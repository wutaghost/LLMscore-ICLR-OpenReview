                                            A BSTRACT

         Watermarking techniques offer a promising way to secure data via embedding
         covert information into the data. A paramount challenge in the domain lies in
         preserving the distribution of original data during watermarking. Our research
         extends and refines existing watermarking framework, placing emphasis on the
         importance of a distribution-preserving (DiP) watermark. Contrary to the current
         strategies, our proposed DiPmark preserves the original token distribution during
         watermarking (stealthy), is detectable without access to the language model API
         or weights (efficient), and is robust to moderate changes of tokens (resilient). DiP-
         mark operates by initially selecting a random assortment of ’green’ tokens prior
         to the generation of a word, then employing a distribution-preserving reweighting
         function to enhance the utilization of these green tokens. The empirical bench-
         marks of our approach underscore its stealthiness, efficiency, and resilience, mak-
         ing it a robust solution for watermarking tasks that demand impeccable quality
         preservation.


1   I NTRODUCTION
In an age where artificial intelligence has transcended the barriers of human-like text generation,
the validity and origin of such content have come under significant scrutiny. The ascent of large
language models (LLMs) and their uncanny capability to emulate human prose present a double-
edged sword. On one hand, the potential applications are vast, from simplifying complex tasks to
augmenting human capabilities. Conversely, there exists a latent threat of misuse, as observed in
scenarios ranging from students seeking undue advantages in academic settings to the propagation
of misinformation on digital platforms such as Reddit, Twitter, and StackOverflow.
As the demarcation between machine-generated and human-penned content blurs, the conventional
heuristic methods to differentiate between them falter. The recent failures of even state-of-the-art
detectors epitomize the dire need for a more robust solution. In this context, watermarking emerges
as a nuanced solution: the deliberate embedding of distinctive signals within generated content to
assert its provenance. By marking content at its source, this technique provides a mechanism to
trace the origin of any disseminated information. Beyond the widely held notion that watermarks
should be identifiable via a secret key, there exist several fundamental characteristics necessary for
an efficient watermark within language models:
       • (Stealthy) The watermark should integrate seamlessly into the generated content, preserv-
         ing the distribution and quality of the original text, making it impossible to discern between
         watermarked and non-watermarked text.
       • (Efficient) With the secret key, detecting watermark within the content (or a fragment of it)
         should be fast and straightforward without accessing the language models and prompts.
       • (Resilient) If the content undergoes moderate changes or modifications, the watermark
         should remain intact and identifiable.
To the best of our understanding, current watermarking techniques do not adhere to the aforemen-
tioned three key properties. These methods either impact the model’s sampling distribution, leading
to reduction in the model’s performance (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023), lack resilience against text al-
terations such as editing or cropping (Christ et al., 2023), necessitate thousands of inference step
during detection process (Kuditipudi et al., 2023), or require the token logits of language model API

Our watermarking framework, in alignment with pre-existing schema Kirchenbauer et al. (2023),
is essentially comprised of two components: a generate function, which transforms a prompt and a
proprietary watermark key into a sample derived from the language model, and a detect function that
aligns a potential watermarked text with the secret key. During the text generation process, language
model providers will reweight the output probability of the current token using a confidential key.
The reweight function maintains stealth, ensuring that each instance of text generation aligns with
the original language model’s distribution. In the detection phase, the presence of a watermark can
be efficiently detected by solely using the secret key and the watermarked text, potentially supple-
mented with standard hash functions found in Python. We also validate the above mentioned three
fundamental properties of our watermark via experimental assessments on major language models,
including BART-large model (Liu et al., 2020) and LLaMA-2 (Touvron et al., 2023).
Significance of preserving text distribution in watermarking. We emphasize the critical signif-
icance of preserving the distribution of text in watermarking, driven by the following compelling
justifications: a) Stealthy Watermarking: A watermark that disrupts the original distribution of a
language model lacks the attribute of stealthiness. Such alterations make it relatively straightfor-
ward to distinguish between watermarked and unwatermarked LMs through multiple instances of
sampling. b) Industry-Level LLM Application: When contemplating the application of a watermark
to industry-standard LLMs like ChatGPT and Bard, the primary consideration is to ensure that the
watermark does not compromise the performance of these foundational LLMs. Any watermark that
interferes with the original text distribution will inevitably impact the quality of generated text, an
outcome that is deemed unacceptable by industry stakeholders.
Our contributions. Our work tackles the problem of designing watermarks for large language
models without affecting its natural performance and advances the state-of-the-art in multiple ways.
        • We propose a novel watermarking framework, DiPmark, that introduces a provably
          distribution-preserving watermarking scheme for language models. Unlike existing meth-
          ods, DiPmark is simultaneously stealthy, efficient, and resilient.
        • We develop an effective watermark detection mechanism for DiPmark. By utilizing hy-
          pothesis testing and a well-defined statistical approach, we can reliably detect the presence
          of the watermark while maintaining a low false positive rate. We also show our detect
          algorithm is provably robust against text modifications.
        • Experimentally, we validate the distribution-preserving property of DiPmark by evaluating
          the generated text quality on machine translation and text summarization tasks. Notably, the
          detection time for 1,000 watermarked sequences produced by LLaMA-2 stands at a mere
          90 seconds without the need of API access. Furthermore, DiPmark exhibits robustness
          even when subjected to 20% to 30% random text modifications, encompassing insertions,
          deletions, and substitutions.
2    R ELATED W ORK
In a recent seminal work, Kirchenbauer et al. (2023) introduced a pioneering watermarking scheme
tailored for LLMs. However, this approach inevitably leads to a pivotal change in the distribution
of the generated text, potentially compromising the quality of the generated content. To maintain
an unaltered output distribution in watermarked content, alternative strategies have been explored.
Christ et al. (2023) and Kuditipudi et al. (2023) employed the inverse sampling method to gener-
ate watermarked token distributions. Notably, Christ et al. (2023)’s method faces resilience issues

under modifications or changes and lacks empirical validation for detectability. Meanwhile, Kudi-
tipudi et al. (2023)’s approach necessitates the secret key distribution during detection, potentially
compromising data security and watermark stealthiness. Moreover, their detection process involves
thousands of resampling steps from the secret key distribution, which is inefficient for lengthy texts.
Hu et al. (2023) used inverse sampling and permutation based reweight methods for watermark-

Our research aligns closely with Kirchenbauer et al. (2023). In their methodology, they employed
watermarking for text derived from a language model by separating the token set into ‘red’ and
‘green’ lists. Building on this foundation, our research retains the red-green list configuration, but
introduces an evolved family of reweighting strategies. This approach ensures: 1) a promoted uti-
lization of green tokens, and 2) equivalency in distribution between a sample from the watermarked
language model and one from the original language model.

3    P RELIMINARY
Notations. To lay a foundation for our ensuing discussion, we first establish a few essential nota-
tions. Let’s represent the vocabulary (or token) set by V and its size or volume by N = |V |. We
further introduce the set V, defined as an aggregation of all conceivable string sequences, even ac-

imminent token xn+1 ∈ V , while considering the current context—-spanning from x1 to xn -—is
represented as PM (xn+1 | x1 , x2 , ..., xn ). Inherent to its design, the LLM adopts an autoregres-
sive mode of operation. This implies that the combined probability of producing several tokens,
specifically from xn+1 to xn+m takes the form:

In the context of watermarking, the server provider will use a set of i.i.d. watermark cipher {θi ∈
Θ, i ∈ N} on the cipher space Θ to generate the text. The cipher θi is usually generated by a
secret key k and a fragment of the previous context, named texture key, si . Each θi is independent
and following the same distribution PΘ . The watermarked output of the LLM follows distribution
PW (xn+1 | x1:n , θn ), which is conditioned on both the cipher θn and the context x1:n . We name
the PW as the reweight strategy of the watermark.
Red-Green list watermark. Building upon the soft/hard red-green list delineated in Kirchenbauer
et al. (2023), the vocabulary set is systematically bifurcated into the red and green lists. This process
subtly accentuates the usage of green tokens during sampling. Specifically, for generating the n-th
token xn , the red and green lists are fashioned by randomly permuting the vocabulary set V . Then,
a predefined separator is subsequently interposed into the permuted list, such that the first γ percent
tokens of the permuted list constitute the red list, whereas the remaining 1 − γ percent form the
green list. In this way, the cipher space Θ encompasses all potential permutations of V , with θn
signifying a distinct permutation of V . During token generation, the likelihood of green list tokens
is augmented to watermark the resultant text. For detection purposes, a user, equipped with the
secret key, can deduce the red-green list and ascertain watermark presence through the discrepancy
in the red and green token counts.

(2023), the authors propose a soft reweight technique. Given an initial token probability p(t), the
watermarked probability for the token, denoted by p′ (t), is formulated as:

4   D I P MARK
Motivation. The reweight strategy presented in Kirchenbauer et al. (2023) disrupts the inherent
text distribution when advocating for the green tokens during sampling. This disruption can lead to
skewed sampling, having a pronounced impact on the quality of the resultant text. Our objective is to
conceptualize a novel reweight strategy that ensures the token distribution remains unaltered during
the watermarking process. Contrary to the approach in Kirchenbauer et al. (2023) that promotes
the use of all tokens from the green list, we emphasize increasing the sum of the probability of the
green-list tokens. In this way, the watermarked text, when exposed to the secret key, will still exhibit
a bias towards the green-list tokens. By adopting this idea, we design a special reweight function,
which preserves the text distribution during watermarking.
Cipher space for watermarking. Our chosen watermark cipher space encompasses the permuta-
tions of the vocabulary set, denoted as Θ = {V1p , ..., VNp ! }, wherein Vip represents a permutation of
V . For the cipher distribution PΘ , we employ a uniform distribution over Θ, signifying that each
permutation is equally probable for selection.
α-reweight. Let θ ∈ Θ be a cipher, constituting a permutation of V . The probabilities of individual
tokens can be organized within the interval [0, 1] according to their respective positions in θ. Given
a fixed constant α in [0, 1], the token probabilities within the interval [0, α] are adjusted to 0, while
                                                        1
those in the interval [α, 1] are scaled by a factor of 1−α . Through this procedure, we elevate the sum
of the probability of green-list tokens for arbitrary permutation separator γ, as the green-list tokens
consistently appear towards the end of the ordered set θ. If we represent θ as {t1 , ..., tN } and denote

PM (·|x) as the original token distribution, let F (i|θ) = 1−α    max{ j=1 PM (tj |x) − α, 0}. The
resultant α-reweight probability distribution is expressed as PW (ti |x, θ) = F (i|θ) − F (i − 1|θ).
We wish to highlight the distinction between the probability quantile α and the red-green list sepa-
rator γ. In accordance with the definition in Kirchenbauer et al. (2023), γ serves as the partition for
the permuted token list. In contrast, α separates the probability interval [0, 1] of the permuted token
list. Thus, both the α-reweight and DiP-reweight (as subsequently defined) remain irrelevant to γ,
while still effectively promoting the utilization of green list tokens.

5   WATERMARK D ETECTION
Given that DiPmark is founded on the principle of promoting the utilization of green-list tokens, we
can leverage a hypothesis test to identify the presence of DiPmark. In the context of a predetermined
red-green list separator γ ∈ [0, 1], we classify the initial ⌈γN ⌉ tokens within the token set permu-
tation as belonging to the red list, while the remaining tokens are categorized as part of the green
list. Given a text sequence x1:n , we establish the null hypothesis H0 : x1:n is generated without any
awareness of the red-green list rule. Here, LG (γ) denotes the count of green tokens within x1:n in
relation to γ. Under the null hypothesis H0 , LG (γ) follows a binomial distribution with√parameters
p = (1 − γ) and n total trials. We introduce Φ(γ, x1:n ) := (LG (γ) − (1 − γ)n)/ n, and by
employing Hoeffding’s inequality, we derive the subsequent concentration bound:
                                                          2
                              Pr(Φ(γ, x1:n ) ≥ t) ≤ e−2t ,     ∀t ∈ R.
We proceed to reject the null hypothesis and detect the watermark if Φ(γ, x1:n ) surpasses a pre-
defined threshold. For instance, setting the threshold as Φ(γ, x1:n ) ≥ 2 results in rejecting H0
(indicating watermark presence) while maintaining a false positive rate below e−8 .
It is noteworthy that we divergepfrom Kirchenbauer et al. (2023) by avoiding the use of the z-test
statistic (LG (γ) − (1 − γ)n)/ nγ(1 − γ). The z-test assumes a Gaussian distribution for the
test statistic, but in cases of small text sequence lengths n, the approximation becomes imprecise,
potentially leading to inaccurate estimation of false positive rates.
As DiPmark can always elevate the sum of the probability of the green list tokens with arbitrary γ,
selecting a suitable γ becomes a significant consideration. To enable fair comparison with the Soft
watermark in Kirchenbauer et al. (2023), we fix γ = 0.5 for both watermarks during the detection
phrase, which means the first half of the permutation set is the red list and the second half is the
green list.
Green token ratio. The term LG (γ)/n signifies the proportion of green tokens within a sequence of
tokens, while 1−γ denotes the expected green token proportion in an unwatermarked sequence. Our
metric, referred to as the “green token ratio”, is defined as the difference between these two values,
expressed as LG (γ)/n − (1 − γ). This ratio quantifies the bias towards green tokens within the text
sequence. It is worth noting that Φ(γ, x1:n ) scales proportionally with the square root of n times
the green token ratio. With a fixed green token ratio, detecting longer sequences using Φ(γ, x1:n )
becomes more straightforward because they will show a lower false positive rate. The validity of
this analysis is also confirmed in Section 6.4.
Detecting efficiency comparison. Similar to the detection algorithms presented in Kirchenbauer
et al. (2023) and Christ et al. (2023), our watermark detection process is highly efficient, requir-
ing only a single pass through the provided text sequence. However, it is worth noting that the
detection algorithm outlined in Kuditipudi et al. (2023) necessitates iterating through the sequence
a staggering 5000 times, which is notably inefficient when compared to our approach. In terms
of computational costs, our detection algorithm proves to be significantly faster. For instance, in
a simulation comparing the two methods, our algorithm only requires 90 seconds to detect 1,000
watermarked sequences generated by LLaMA-2, whereas the algorithm in Kuditipudi et al. (2023)
demands at least 10 hours for the same task.

A    L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK

A prominent limitation of DiPmark lies in its detectability, which is akin to the Soft watermark at
δ = 1.5. This limitation may render it unsuitable for scenarios necessitating both high true negative
rate and high true positive rate.
Future endeavors should focus on enhancing the detectability of distribution-preserving watermarks.
This could be realized by assigning greater weight to the green-list tokens during the watermarking
process. Additionally, a promising avenue for exploration involves the design of a more robust
distribution-preserving watermark, potentially through the integration of multiple detectors. These
directions represent promising opportunities for advancing the efficacy and applicability of water-
marking techniques on large language models.

C    R ELATED W ORK
Reweight-based watermarking framework. In a recent seminal work, Kirchenbauer et al. (2023)
introduced a pioneering watermarking scheme tailored for LLMs, backed by formal guarantees.
Their work demonstrated that watermark embedding could be accomplished by altering the token
distribution during generation, targeting outputs with substantial entropy. However, this approach
inevitably leads to a pivotal change in the distribution of the generated text, potentially compromis-
ing the quality of the generated content.
To maintain an unaltered output distribution in watermarked content, alternative strategies have been
explored. Christ et al. (2023) and Kuditipudi et al. (2023) employed the inverse sampling method
to generate watermarked token distributions. Notably, Christ et al. (2023)’s method faces resilience
issues under modifications and lacks empirical validation for detectability. Meanwhile, Kuditipudi
et al. (2023)’s approach necessitates the secret key distribution during detection, potentially com-
promising data security and watermark stealthiness. Moreover, their detection process involves
hundreds of resampling steps from the secret key distribution, which is inefficient for lengthy texts.
Hu et al. (2023) used inverse sampling and permutation based reweight methods for watermarking,
but the detector requires access of the language model API, undermining its operational efficiency.
Aaronson’s ongoing watermarking project (Aaronson, 2022) employs n-gram hashing for reweight-
ing the next-token distribution, though specific details are currently unavailable.
The landscape also includes several schemes (Abdelnabi & Fritz, 2021; Qiang et al., 2023; Yoo et al.,
2023; Munyer & Zhong, 2023) that incorporate an ML model within the watermarking algorithm
itself. However, these constructions lack formal assurances and rely on heuristic arguments for
satisfying the criteria of Stealthiness, Efficiency, and Resilience.
Our research aligns closely with the findings presented in Kirchenbauer et al. (2023). In their
methodology, they employed watermarking for text derived from a language model by bifurcating
the token set into designated ‘red’ and ‘green’ lists. The division is determined by a random seed
that is contingent on the secret key coupled with a hash of priorly generated tokens. The authors
accentuated the prominence of green tokens during the sampling phase by reweighting the token
log-probabilities. Building on this foundation, our research retains the red-green list configuration,
but introduces an evolved family of permutation-based reweight strategies. This dual approach en-
sures: 1) a promoted utilization of green tokens, and 2) equivalency in distribution between a sample
from the watermarked language model and one from the original language model.

Post-hoc detectors. Post-hoc detection stands as a notable alternative to watermarking, focusing
on the retrospective analysis of machine-generated text. This could be achieved through leverag-
ing features inherent to language models or by refining pre-existing, expansive language models
to function as detectors, as elaborated by (Zellers et al., 2019). Notably, specific implementation
nuances, such as sampling methodologies, can be discerned through reverse engineering the gener-
ated text, a process detailed by (Tay et al., 2020). There are also post-hoc detectors designed for
the modern large language models (Mitchell et al., 2023; Tian, 2023; Kirchner et al., 2023), which
are models specifically trained for the binary detection task. However, there is a growing sentiment
that those detection methodologies are diminishing in efficacy in tandem with the evolution of lan-
guage model capabilities. As Gambini et al. (2022) observed, detection mechanisms that were adept
with GPT-2 have encountered challenges with GPT-3. Besides, the text rephrasing model in (Kr-
ishna et al., 2023) bypassing prevalent post-hoc detectors like GPTZero (Tian, 2023), DetectGPT
(Mitchell et al., 2023), and OpenAI’s proprietary detector (Kirchner et al., 2023). Additionally, a
pertinent observation made by Chakraborty et al. (2023) suggests that as AI-generated content be-
comes increasingly indistinguishable from human-produced text, the demands on post-hoc detectors
to analyze more extended text segments will escalate.
Steganography. Steganography involves embedding concealed messages in channels such as nat-
ural language or images, ensuring only intended recipients can discern the message while others
remain unaware (Hopper et al., 2002). When applied to watermarking, the aim is stealthy. Yet,
known steganography techniques might not achieve this without certain entropy-related assump-
tions. In scenarios where language model prompts can be chosen adversarially, the need for stealthy
persists. This discrepancy arises due to differences in access levels that watermarking and steganog-
raphy have to the model’s output distribution. In steganography, there’s only oracle access to this
distribution. Conversely, our watermarking approach gets a detailed view of the token’s probability
distribution. Hence, while steganography either relies on entropy assumptions (Hopper et al., 2002)
or compromises security with low entropy channels (Dedić et al., 2009), our watermark remains
stealthy irrespective of the text’s entropy. This is achieved by leveraging the full distribution access
and using it as a foundation for embedding watermarks. Kaptchuk et al. (2021) offers encoding sim-
ilar access. However, it presupposes equal decoding access, which is impractical for watermarking
as the detection algorithm won’t typically have the initiating prompt, thus remaining ignorant of the
distribution.

considering the availability of only three tokens. The texture key history resets before generating

cipher, we employ SHA-256 as the hash function and a 1024-bit random bitstring as the key k. The
cipher θ is sampled from Θ using hash(k, s) as the random seed.
Evaluation metrics for text quality. In this part, we introduce the evaluation metrics we used for
evaluating the text quality (Section. 6.1).

       • ROUGE score. For the summarization task, we utilize the ROUGE score (Lin, 2004),

         content from reference summaries.
       • BLEU score. For the machine translation task, we rely on the BLEU score (Papineni
         et al., 2002), emphasizing the lexical similarity between machine-generated translations
         and human reference translations.
       • BERTScore. BERTScore Zhang et al. (2019) computes the similarity of two sentences as
         a sum of cosine similarities between their tokens’ embeddings. We use BERTScore-F1,
         BERTScore-Precision, and BERTScore-Recall for evaluating both text summarization and
         machine translation tasks.
       • Perplexity. In information theory, perplexity is a measurement of how well a probability
         distribution or probability model predicts a sample. It may be used to compare probability
         models. A low perplexity indicates the probability distribution is good at predicting the
         sample. We use perplexity for evaluating both text summarization and machine translation
         tasks.
