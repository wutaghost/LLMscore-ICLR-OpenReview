                                             A BSTRACT

         Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive inferential capa-
         bilities, with numerous research endeavors devoted to enhancing this capacity
         through prompting. Despite these efforts, a unified epistemological foundation
         is still conspicuously absent. Drawing inspiration from Kant’s a priori philoso-
         phy, we propose the UPAR prompting framework, designed to emulate the struc-
         ture of human cognition within LLMs. The UPAR framework is delineated into
         four phases: “Understand”, “Plan”, “Act”, and “Reflect”, enabling the extrac-
         tion of structured information from complex contexts, prior planning of solutions,
         execution according to plan, and self-reflection. This structure significantly aug-
         ments the explainability and accuracy of LLM inference, producing a human-
         understandable and inspectable inferential trajectory. Furthermore, our work of-
         fers an epistemological foundation for existing prompting techniques, allowing
         for a possible systematic integration of these methods. With GPT-4, our approach
         elevates the accuracy from COT baseline of 22.92% to 58.33% in a challeng-
         ing subset of GSM8K, and from 67.91% to 75.40% in the causal judgment task.
         Without using few-shot examples or external tools, UPAR significantly outper-
         forms existing prompting methods on SCIBENCH, a challenging dataset contain-
         ing collegiate-level mathematics, chemistry, and physics scientific problems.

Prompt Engineering emerges as an efficacious approach to augment the performance of LLMs with-
out the necessity for model retraining. This enhancement is achieved by the construction of special-
ized instructive prompts, such as chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2023), self-consistency (Wang et al.,
2023b), and knowledge generation (Liu et al., 2022), among others. These strategies hold the po-
tential to mitigate various shortcomings inherent in contemporary large language models, such as
inexplicability (Zhao et al., 2023a), hallucinations (Bang et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2022; McKenna
et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2023; Rawte et al., 2023), complex causal reasoning (Hobbhahn
et al., 2022; Willig et al., 2022; Zečević et al., 2023; Kıcıman et al., 2023), and limited mathematical
ability (Lu et al., 2023b; Dziri et al., 2023). Nevertheless, the majority of existing prompt method-
ologies encounter three significant challenges: 1. Their primary focus on addressing local reasoning,
overlooking the intricate underlying cognitive structures of human mind. 2. Numerous prompts are
custom-designed for specific structured reasoning processes, neglecting the complexities inherent in
real-world natural language tasks. 3. The foundational basis for most prompt designs predominantly
relies on intuition, lacking a consolidated epistemological perspective. To address these problems,
we turn to epistemology in philosophy.
In the seminal work “Critique of Pure Reason” (Kant et al., 1934), Immanuel Kant delineated an
epistemological hierarchy within the human mind: sensibility, understanding, and reason, as de-
picted in Figure 2. Sensibility constitutes the initial phase, wherein perceptions are amassed as raw
sensory impressions, devoid of structured meaning. Subsequently, the stage of understanding inter-
prets these impressions, bestowing structure and significance upon them. Reason stands as the zenith
of cognitive capabilities, striving to unearth truths transcending empirical experience. Notably, Kant
emphasized that reason must remain rooted in experience to avert “illusions,” signifying logical but
unrealistic knowledge. Although Kant’s philosophy has cast a profound influence across various
academic disciplines (Allison, 2004; Bird, 2013; Strawson & Allais, 2018), discussions regarding
its application within LLMs are notably scant.
Inspired by Kant’s transcendental philosophy (Kant et al., 1934), we propose a prompt framework
to augment the performance of LLMs, as illustrated in Figure 1. Our methodology unfolds in four
sequential stages: Understand, Plan, Act, and Reflect. Succinctly, the “Understand” stage extracts
crucial information from the input text, utilizing a priori human cognitive categories. The “Plan”
stage devises an action plan meticulously tailored to the specific task at hand. The “Act” stage em-
bodies the direct problem-solving process of the LLM. Lastly, the “Reflect” phase enables the LLMs
to provide feedback and refine its output based on the information above. This framework allows
LLMs to generate a multi-level reasoning process and improve its reasoning accuracy by simulat-
ing the cognitive ability of the human mind. In addition, such a reasoning process allows humans
to correct and provide feedback by checking the output of LLMs at different stages. Finally, the
UPAR framework is compatible with various existing prompting techniques, providing possibilities
for future systematic prompting engineering.

2     R ELATED W ORKS

Our prompting framework draws inspiration from Kant’s transcendental philosophy. Therefore in
this section, we provide a succinct review of the existing research on prompts and introduce the
transcendental principles posited by Kant, elucidating potential linkages between them.

2.1     C URRENT P ROMPT R ESEARCH

2.1.1    I NDEPENDENT OF EXTERNAL TOOLS
Numerous studies on prompts have augmented the capabilities of LLMs by delving into single or
multi-step thought processes. The Chain-of-Thought (COT) series obliges LLMs to expound the un-
derlying rational processes (Wei et al., 2023; Kojima et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2023).
Wang et al. (2023b) introduces Self-Consistency, a strategy wherein the LLM iteratively answers the
same query, employing majority voting to diminish answer uncertainties. Zhou et al. (2023a) un-
veils LEAST-TO-MOST, deconstructing intricate problems into sequential sub-tasks. Building upon
these foundations, subsequent research has expanded the paradigm to encompass multi-path infer-
ential frameworks (Dohan et al., 2022). Significant advancements include the Tree of Thoughts (Yao
et al., 2023a), Graph of Thoughts (Besta et al., 2023), and Algorithm of Thoughts (Sel et al., 2023).
These works conceptualize individual LLM computations as fundamental nodes within potential
cognitive trajectories, exploring a more extensive spectrum of cognitive pathways.
Another direction involves utilizing the internal knowledge of LLMs to assist decision-making or
facilitate automatic optimization. Works by Shwartz et al. (2020); Paranjape et al. (2021); Liu et al.
(2022) leverage LLMs to autonomously generate problem-related insights that aid in problem res-
olution. Jung et al. (2022) proposes the MAIEUTIC, constructing a recursive explanation tree and
confining inference within the bounds of a satisfiability problem. Creswell et al. (2022) puts forth
the Selection-Inference model, delineating each reasoning step into selection and inference. Madaan
et al. (2023) permits LLMs to operate iteratively, alternating between outputting answers and evalu-
ations, and refining. Xie et al. (2023) empowers LLMs to conduct fine-grained self-assessments to
enhance multi-step reasoning. Additionally, Yang et al. (2023a); Zhou et al. (2023b) delve into the
potential of employing LLMs for the automatic generation and optimization of prompts.
These methods typically yield substantial advancements for LLMs dealing with zero-shot or low-
shot prompts. However, several limitations persist. Primarily, these studies often concentrate exclu-
sively on local and specific reasoning processes, neglecting the intrinsic human cognitive structures
underpinning language. Concurrently, these manually constructed methods often rely more on intu-
ition than established principles, posing challenges to systematic knowledge in prompt engineering.

2.1.2    U SING EXTERNAL TOOLS
Another exploration involves combining LLMs with external tool using. As an intermediary, LLMs
have the potential to meld the flexibility of natural language systems with the precision of symbolic
language systems, translating natural language problems into the language of external tools. Works
in this direction include SEQZERO (Yang et al., 2022), Rational Meaning Construction (Wong
et al., 2023), Faithful Chain-of-Thought (Lyu et al., 2023), Program of Thoughts (Chen et al., 2022),
Program-aided Language Models (Gao et al., 2023a). The broader concept of tools includes search
engines and knowledge databases and even other neural networks, as demonstrated by the tool li-
brary (Paranjape et al., 2023), React (Yao et al., 2023b; Yang et al., 2023b), LATM (Cai et al., 2023),
Toolformer (Schick et al., 2023), HuggingGPT (Shen et al., 2023), Chameleon (Lu et al., 2023a),
FACTOOL (Chern et al., 2023).
These methods integrate LLMs with external tools to augment their capabilities, achieving state-of-
the-art results across various tasks. However, they overlook the fact that these tools are products
of human thought, not the foundation of thinking. For instance, the human brain, as a large and
highly complex network, does not rely on programming languages to solve basic computational
problems, nor does it exhibit signs of internal formal reasoning systems. These tools are the creations
of human intellect rather than the basis of human reliable thinking. Therefore, in this paper, we
exclusively focus on prompting techniques without external tools. As we observe, tool utilization is
not a precondition for the proper functioning of the human mind, but merely a supplementary aid.

2.2     VIEW FROM PHILOSOPHY

2.2.1    R ATIONALISM

Rationalism in epistemology posits that knowledge fundamentally emanates from logical reasoning
(Markie & Folescu, 2004; Gottlieb & Russell, 2013), frequently neglecting the depth and richness of
experiential knowledge. This unwavering perspective subsequently led to the emergence of dogma-
tism, asserting human rationality as infallible and dismissing the indispensable need for experiential
insights. This stance obstructs a more holistic comprehension of the evolution of knowledge. Anal-
ogously, early AI models, such as Symbolic AI and Expert Systems (Nilsson, 1982; Luger, 2005),
anchored in this rationalistic framework, encountered significant challenges with real-world com-
plexities. Gradually, AI has transitioned from this rigid approach to a more adaptive, data-driven
paradigm, exemplified by the rise of deep learning (LeCun et al., 2015).

2.2.2    E MPIRICISM

Empiricism posits that all human knowledge originates from sensory experience (Sellars et al., 1956;
Markie & Folescu, 2004), asserting that knowledge hinges on external observations rather than in-
trinsic principles. This perspective, however, can inadvertently steer us towards skepticism, where
fundamental notions such as cause and effect might merely represent mental association rather than
objective reality, which nullifies the essential possibility of humans discerning the truth and con-
tradicts the scientific system.Empiricism is manifested in machine learning and deep learning, dis-
ciplines that prioritize data-driven learning over established rules. Despite the success of these
models, their unpredictability and susceptibility to incomprehensible errors, such as adversarial vul-
nerability (Madry et al., 2017) and LLMs’ hallucinations (Rawte et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2023), remain
prominent. We have also observed that large language models, rooted in empiricism, suffer from
difficulties similar to Hume’s problem (Hume, 1875): the lack of genuine understanding regarding
the inner laws of the world. LLMs’ reasoning rests upon patterns and statistical relationships, de-
void of true causality (Hobbhahn et al., 2022; Willig et al., 2022; Zečević et al., 2023; Kıcıman et al.,
2023). This observation compels us to explore a more comprehensive epistemological framework to
alleviate the prevailing issues faced by current LLMs.

2.2.3    K ANT ’ S T RANSCENDENTAL P HILOSOPHY

Kant’s transcendental philosophy is dedicated to resolving the contradiction between rationalism
and empiricism. In “Critique of Pure Reason” (Kant et al., 1934), Kant constructs an intricate
epistemological framework that has fundamentally shaped modern philosophy as we have shown in
Fig. 2. Sensibility lies as the first layer of cognitive capacity. Kant advances that sensibility is the
initial stage in acquiring perceptions, acting as the fundamental channel between the external world
and our cognitive faculties. This perception initially appears as unprocessed sensory data, lacking
intrinsic structure or meaning. Kant’s delineation of two pure forms of sensibility, space and time,
summarizes the transcendental structures we superimpose on experience.
Understanding, as the second layer, epitomizes our capacity to interpret and ascertain meaning from
the raw sensory information. It imparts structure and meaning to our experiences. Kant contends
that there exist fundamental concepts or “categories” that are intrinsically pure, not emerging from
experiential contexts, but rather forming the structural framework we impose upon our experiences.
These a priori categories encompass fundamental relationships as is shown in Table 1, forming the
bedrock for our interpretation and comprehension of the external realm.
Reason transcends sensibility and understanding, representing the apex of cognitive capabilities. It is
devoted to elucidating truths that surpass sensibility and understanding. Kant postulates that reason,
grounded on insights furnished by understanding, continuously aspires for systematic, holistic, and
ultimate elucidations of existential entities.
Kant’s critique also highlights the need for caution and self-reflection in the practice of reason.
Despite reason’s intrinsic drive to unravel ultimate truths, Kant warns against its venture beyond
the boundaries of empirical experience. Kant points out that using reason beyond the confines
of experience could produce “illusions”, knowledge that seems reasonable but is unrealistic, and
advocated rigorous critical examination of our cognitive processes.

3.1   U NDERSTAND

In Kant’s epistemological framework, the a priori categories of understanding serve the process
of human understanding of the world, and are the nodes on the web of knowledge that help us
understand natural phenomena (Hegel, 2010). It is these categories that empower us to organize
and interpret this raw sensory data. From this perspective, we designate the initial phase of LLM’s
problem processing as “understanding”—a restoration of a real-world model through the linguistic
representation. Specifically, drawing upon Table 1, we prompt LLMs to first address these four
questions within the context of time and space:

         Quantity: What entities/events and their quantitative relationships are related to
         the question?

         Quality: What intrinsic properties and external constraints of these entities/events
         are related to the question?

         Relation: What is the relationship between these entities/events?

         Modality: Is possibility/impossibility, inevitable/accidental involved in the enti-
         ties/events related to the question?

We aim to assist the model better understand entities and their relationships by these questions,
especially in the context of time and space. Consider reading a detective fiction: it is crucial to dis-
cern characters, objects, events, their attributes, and spatio-temporal coordinates, and comprehend
their interactions and relationships. These hidden understandings do not always take the form of
language, but allow for more informed conclusions rather than intuitive judgments. Moreover, Shi
et al. (2023) affirmed that even slight irrelevant information in queries can notably detract LLMs’
performance, highlighting the necessity for information sifting. Hence, we set the first step of the
LLM’s work as constructing a world model using language. Denoting u as the understanding, this
process can be formally written as

3.2   P LAN

In Kant’s epistemological framework, reason is situated at the zenith. Reason’s capacity lies in
processing knowledge that the intellect has already refined, and subsequently integrating this into a
systematic comprehension. In this context, we define “reason” as the ability to devise a high-level
plan for a target task, grounded on the original context and the structured knowledge extracted by
the intellect. We also observe that Bubeck et al. (2023) highlights the limitation imposed by the
autoregressive nature of transformer models, as LLMs are constrained in their foresight to “plan
ahead” for complex tasks. This notion resonates with the delineation between System 1 and System
2 models in psychology (Kahneman, 2011), where LLMs, functioning as unidirectional word-by-
word generative models, inherently lack the “slow thinking” component essential for overseeing the
thought process. Consequently, we design the planning stage to compel the model to generate a
high-level plan l before embarking on problem-solving, which is prompted and formalized as:

Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023). Concurrently, certain studies focus on constructing sufficiently
complex LLM thinking paths to fully explore the possible solution space of the problem such as
the Tree of Thoughts (Yao et al., 2023a), Graph of Thoughts (Besta et al., 2023), and Algorithm of

3.4   R EFLECT

In Kant’s critical philosophy, an important aspect is the examination of reason within its own con-
fines: the application of reason must not deviate from experience. Exceeding these bounds may lead
reason astray, giving rise to erroneous “illusions”. This notion resonates with observations made
in LLMs’ hallucinations (Kadavath et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2022; McKenna et al., 2023; Li et al.,
2023; Ji et al., 2023; Rawte et al., 2023). Therefore, we propose the final step as reflection, re-
quiring LLMs to comprehensively review the content they generate, point out possible errors, and
correct them. Many recent methods employ similar self-checking concepts to mitigate the LLMs’
hallucinations, such as Self-Consistency (Wang et al., 2023b), Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023),
DOLA (Chuang et al., 2023), citation generation (Gao et al., 2023b), Retrieval Augmentation (Ren
et al., 2023). Essentially, these techniques can be used to enhance the “reflect” stage in our UPAR
framework. Denoting r as the reflection, “reflect” is prompted and formalized as:

ally constructed few-shot samples; do not perform multiple Q&A iterations; do not manually divide
sub-questions, and only use the entire contents of model’s single output as the final answer.

4.4   E RROR ANALYSIS

According to our experiments, LLMs under UPAR may make er-
rors in the following situations: 1.Understand: LLMs may mis-
understand the ambiguous semantic content and generate a de-
terministic attitude answer rather than seek clarification. 2.Plan:
LLMs may produce illogical plans for solving problems, even under
the premise of correctly understanding. 3.Execution: LLMs can-
not accurately perform computational problems such as floating-
point multiplication, matrix operations, calculus, complex algebraic
equations, etc. Without relying on external tools, LLMs often out-

In order to assess UPAR’s compatibility with existing methods, we further attempted to integrate

answer the same question multiple times and using a majority vote to determine the final result.
Each experiment was conducted five times to conduct SC. It can be observed that the UPAR method
is highly compatible with the SC method, achieving significant performance improvement. For
instance, on GSM8K-Hard, the combination of UPAR and SC improves the accuracy from 47.92%
to 60.42%, while the combination of COT and SC does not significantly improve the performance.
This is usually because the question involves some non-linear reasoning process that cannot be
effectively captured by linear thinking processes, while UPAR alleviates this significantly.

5     CONCLUSION AND DISCUSSION
This paper aims to fill the existing gap in the epistemological foundation in the field of prompting
techniques for Large Language Models (LLMs). Inspired by Kant’s philosophy of a priori knowl-
edge, we introduce the UPAR prompting framework, emulating human cognition structures within
LLMs. The UPAR framework is divided into four phases: “Understand”, “Plan”, “Act” and “Re-
flect”, enabling the extraction of structured information from complex contexts, plan solutions in
advance, execute according to the plan, and self-reflect. This structure significantly enhances the
explainability and accuracy of LLM inference, producing a human-understandable and inspectable
inferential trajectory. Furthermore, Kant’s a priori philosophical epistemological framework brings
a new perspective to the LLMs community, which is currently dominated by empiricism. We believe
that integrating these a priori epistemic structures as additional constraints into LLMs training is a
valuable topic.

    Quality: What intrinsic properties and external constraints of these entities/events are related

    Relation: What is the relationship between these entities/events? [Your answer here]

    Let’s make a briefly plan to solve this question step by step: [Your plan here.]

    Now, let’s execute the plan step by step: [Your solution here.]

    Check your answers and correct possible errors.: [Your reflect here.]

These multi-step thinking processes can be provided to LLMs at once using the [“role”:“system”]
part of OpenAI’s API reference here, or can be asked step by step in multiple rounds of dialogue.

experiments. In all experiments, we use the zero-shot approach, i.e., no samples are manually
constructed.

    Modality: The total cost of the truck is inevitably the sum of the costs of the truck and its

    First, calculate the cost of the leather seats by dividing the cost of the king cab upgrade by

    Then, calculate the cost of the running boards by subtracting $500 from the cost of the leather

    The cost of the leather seats is $7,500 / 3 = $2,500.

    The total cost of the truck is $30,000 + $7,500 + $2,500 + $2,000 + $1,500 = $43,500.
