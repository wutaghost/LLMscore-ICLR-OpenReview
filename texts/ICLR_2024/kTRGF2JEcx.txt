                                                  A BSTRACT

 1            Math word problem (MWP) solving requires generating a reasoning path based
 2            on a given problem description that often contains irrelevant conditions. Existing
 3            chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting methods elicited multi-step reasoning abilities
 4            of large language models (LLMs) to solve MWPs. However, they were seriously
 5            confused by the irrelevant conditions, resulting in low accuracy. In this paper, we
 6            propose a novel approach named I3 C that instructs LLMs to identify and ignore
 7            irrelevant conditions. It identifies a set of irrelevant condition candidates that have
 8            a weak semantic relevance with the question. Then it prompts LLMs to verify the
 9            irrelevant conditions. Lastly it instructs the LLMs with the verification on relevant
10            and irrelevant conditions to avoid confusion and improve reasoning paths. More-
11            over, we propose to select (problem, reasoning paths)-pairs as demonstrations to
12            enhance I3 C with few-shot reasoning. We develop I3 C-Select that selects the most
13            confusing problems based on the semantic relevance measurement. We conduct
14            extensive experiments on six MWP datasets. I3 C can be combined with any CoT
15            prompting methods to improve the performance of solving MWPs. Notably, I3 C-
16            Select achieves an accuracy of 93.7 and 90.9 on GSM-IC2-1K and GSM-ICM-1K,
17            respectively, significantly outperforming the state-of-the-art few-shot prompting
18            method Auto-CoT by +19.4 and +25.7.


19   1   I NTRODUCTION

20   Math word problem (MWP) solving is a task of developing algorithms to generate a reasoning path
21   towards an unknown quantity based on a problem description. This task is challenging as it requires
22   mathematical understanding and multi-step reasoning abilities. Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting
23   methods were able to guide large language models (LLMs) to perform complex multi-step reasoning
24   (Kojima et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a). Adding demonstrations created manually Wei et al. (2022)
25   or retrieved from a large training set Zhang et al. (2023) in CoT prompts enabled few-shot in-context
26   learning and improved accuracy. However, Shi et al. found that LLMs could be seriously confused
27   by irrelevant conditions which are specifications or data presented in a problem that are unrelated to
28   the solution (Kellogg, 2016). For example, as shown in Figure 1a, the third condition “The height of
29   Mary is 5 feet.” was irrelevant to the final question and misled the reasoning and prediction. Shi et al.
30   added a plain instruction “Feel free to ignore irrelevant conditions in the problem description.” in
31   the prompts, but the LLMs could not effectively ignore them in the problem solving process because
32   they were not identified or specified in the instruction.
33   Improving the reasoning on MWPs that have irrelevant conditions is non-trivial. Self-consistency
34   (Wang et al., 2023b) was proposed to repeatedly solve a problem multiple times (e.g., 10 times) and
35   employ a majority vote strategy to determine the most consistent answer as the final answer. How-
36   ever, it was computationally expensive and still confused by the irrelevant conditions. Moreover, the
37   demonstrations would have to be re-designed to obtain the few-shot learning ability of identifying
38   and ignoring the irrelevance, compared to those in (Wei et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023).
39   In this paper, we propose a novel approach named I3 C to instruct LLMs to explicitly Identify and
40   Ignore Irrelevant Conditions in the mathematical reasoning process. It creates effective instructions
41   that can be added to any CoT prompts to improve their generated reasoning paths. Unlike self-
42   consistency, I3 C does not prompt LLMs many times. Its advanced variant I3 C-Select uses the most
43   confusing problems and their generated reasoning paths as demonstrations for few-shot learning.

66    2     R ELATED W ORK
67    2.1   M ATH W ORD P ROBLEM S OLVING

68    Our work is related to existing efforts on solving MWPs. Traditional methods used statistical learn-
69    ing to extract entities, quantities, and operators from a question and generated an arithmetic equation
70    to find the answer (Hosseini et al., 2014; Roy et al., 2015; Zhou et al., 2015; Mitra & Baral, 2016).
71    Later, sequence-to-sequence (Seq2Seq) model and recurrent neural networks directly transformed
72    the question into an arithmetic equation (Wang et al., 2017; 2019). Recently, fine-tuned pre-trained
73    language models have significantly improved the validity of generated equations and accuracy of an-
74    swers (Shen et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2022; 2023). However, these methods require a large amount
75    of human annotations, lacking the ability to generalize to new kinds of MWPs. In this work, we aim
76    to prompt LLMs to answer arbitrary MWPs without human annotations or task-specific fine-tuning.
77    Our approach generates reasoning paths so that researchers can investigate the behaviors of LLMs.

78    2.2   C HAIN - OF -T HOUGHT P ROMPTING M ETHODS

79    CoT prompting methods have enabled LLMs to generate reasoning paths and solve complex MWPs
80    (Kojima et al., 2022). The reasoning paths could be more expressive if the prompts were added
81    with “Let’s think step by step”. To mitigate missing-step errors, Plan-and-Solve (PS) prompting
82    methods instructed the LLMs to devise a plan to break down the entire task into smaller subtasks,
83    and then carry out the subtasks according to the plan (Wang et al., 2023a). Manual-CoT, as a type
84    of few-shot prompting, manually designed demonstrations to elicit multi-step reasoning ability of
85    the LLMs (Wei et al., 2022). Program of Thought (PoT) generated programming language state-
86    ments and used a program interpreter to execute the generated program to get final answers (Chen
87    et al., 2022a). Zhang et al. (2023) designed Auto-CoT that sampled diverse questions from data and
88    minimized the manual effort on finding demonstrations. Aware of irrelevant conditions in the prob-
89    lem description, Instruct-CoT added the instruction “Feel free to ignore irrelevant conditions in the
90    problem description” in the prompt (Shi et al., 2023). These methods do not explicitly specify the
91    irrelevant conditions in the prompt, which makes it difficult for LLMs to identify and ignore irrel-
92    evant conditions in the problem solving process. Our method identifies irrelevant conditions in the
93    problem description, instructs the LLMs to ignore them, and achieves significantly higher accuracy.

94    2.3   I DENTIFY AND I GNORE I RRELEVANT I NFORMATION

95    Jia & Liang (2017) have shown that neural question answering systems are confused when para-
96    graphs contain irrelevant information. Several studies have trained models to identify and filter out
97    the irrelevant information. For example, Roy & Roth (2015) trained a classifier and scored the like-
98    lihood of each quantity in the problem being an irrelevant quantity. Kim et al. (2022) employed a
99    new training loss to remove the attribute-irrelevant information from the semantic encoder output.
100   Li et al. (2022) proposed a multi-scale knowledge-aware transformer to eliminate identity-irrelevant
101   information. Yang et al. (2023) leveraged pre-extracted semantic information to improve the prepro-
102   cessor’s ability to accurately identify and filter out task-irrelevant information. All these methods
103   require massive human annotations. In contrast, our method does not require time-consuming train-
104   ing or fine-tuning. It employs large language models to automatically identify irrelevant conditions
105   and generate instructions to help the models ignore them.

106   3     P ROPOSED A PPROACH
107   3.1   OVERVIEW

108   In this section, we elaborate on how to instruct LLMs to identify and ignore irrelevant conditions in
109   the math word problem description. Given a complex problem, we first identify a set of irrelevant
110   condition candidates that have a weak semantic relevance with the question (§ 3.2). Then we prompt
111   LLMs to verify if the candidates are indeed irrelevant. Putting all the verification results together,
112   we create a novel I3 C instruction to instruct the LLMs to ignore the irrelevant conditions in the
113   problem description. The I3 C instruction can be added to any CoT prompting methods to help LLMs
114   avoid confusion and improve their generated reasoning paths. Furthermore, we develop a few-shot

118   process. We introduce the I3 C instruction in § 3.3 and I3 C-Select method in § 3.5.

192   Overall performance on MWP datasets. As shown in Table 1, I3 C-Select consistently outper-
193   forms the baseline methods across all MWP datasets by a significant margin. Specifically, it im-
194   proves the accuracy over Zero-Shot-CoT by at least +6.6 for all datasets, except for SingleEq,

197   LLMs to identify and ignore irrelevant conditions in the problem description. It is worth noting
198   that even in the SingleEq dataset, using the most confusing problems and their reasoning paths as

280   5     C ONCLUSION
281   In this paper, we present a novel approach named I3 C to instruct LLMs to explicitly identify and
282   ignore irrelevant conditions in the mathematical reasoning process. It first identifies a set of irrele-
283   vant condition candidates that have a weak semantic relevance with the question, and then prompts a
284   LLM to generate verification outputs to verify if candidates are indeed irrelevant. By incorporating
285   all the verification outputs, we obtained the I3 C instruction. The I3 C instruction is a plug-and-play
286   module that can be added to any CoT prompting methods to help LLMs avoid confusion and im-
287   prove their generated reasoning paths. Moreover, we present a novel few-shot prompting method,
288   I3 C-Select, which selects the most confusing problems and their reasoning paths as demonstrations,
289   and adds the I3 C instruction before the demonstrations to construct the prompt. Extensive experi-
290   ments demonstrate that adding the I3 C instruction to CoT prompting methods effectively improves
291   MWP solving performance, achieving new state-of-the-art performance on all MWP datasets.

414   A     A PPENDIX

415   A.1   DATASETS

416   We use six math word problem datasets for assessing prompting method quality. The statistics of
417   the datasets are shown in Table 6. We give a brief description of the datasets used below:

418    − SingleEq (Koncel-Kedziorski et al., 2015) contains a set of grade-school algebra word prob-
419      lems. Every problem may involve multiple math operations including multiplication, division,
420      subtraction, and addition.
421    − AddSub (Hosseini et al., 2014) consists of math word problems on addition and subtraction for
422      third, fourth, and fifth graders.
423    − SVAMP (Patel et al., 2021) consists of one-unknown math word problems which can be solved
424      by expressions requiring no more than two operators.
425    − GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) consists of high quality grade school math word problems created
426      by human problem writers. These problems take between 2 and 8 steps to solve, and solutions
427      primarily involve performing a sequence of elementary calculations using basic arithmetic op-
428      erations to reach the final answer.
429    − GSM-IC (Shi et al., 2023) is an arithmetic reasoning dataset with irrelevant conditions in the
430      problem description. It is divided into two splits: GSM-IC2, consisting of problems requiring
431      two steps to solve, and GSM-ICM, consisting of problems requiring more than two steps to
432      solve. Being mindful of the experiment costs, we uniformly sample 1, 000 examples from the
433      GSM-IC2 dataset (denoted by GSM-IC2-1K) and 1, 000 examples from the GSM-ICM dataset
434      (denoted by GSM-ICM-1K) for evaluation and analysis purposes throughout this paper.

435   A.2   BASELINES

436   As we study how to prompt large language models to solve math word problems, we employ seven
437   prompting baselines. We give a brief description of the baselines used below:

438    − Direct (Kojima et al., 2022) is a baseline that utilizes the symbolic reasoning ability of large
439      language models. By simply adding the sentence “The answer is” after the problem of interest,
440      which instructs the large language model to generate the answer to the problem.
441    − Zero-Shot-CoT (Kojima et al., 2022) is a Chain-of-Thought prompting method. By adding
442      “Let’s think step by step” to the problem to elicit the large language model to generate reasoning
443      path leading to the final answer.
444    − Plan-and-Solve (PS) (Wang et al., 2023a) replaces the sentence “Let’s think step by step” with
445      “Let’s first understand the problem and devise a plan to solve the problem. Then let’s carry out
446      the plan and solve the problem step by step” to address the missing step issue in Zero-Shot-CoT.
447    − Instruct-CoT (Shi et al., 2023) adds the sentence “Feel free to ignore irrelevant conditions in
448      the problem description.” before the problem of interest, which instructs the large language
449      model to ignore irrelevant information in the problem description.
450    − Manual-CoT (Wei et al., 2022) is a few-shot prompting method. By representing manual de-
451      signed demonstrations that solve the corresponding problems with intermediate reasoning steps
452      in the prompts, Manual-CoT elicits multi-step reasoning ability of large language models.
453    − Auto-CoT (Zhang et al., 2023) automatically constructs demonstrations with questions and
454      reasoning paths to eliminate manual designs in Manual-CoT.

      We use accuracy to evaluate the performance of different prompting methods. Since large language
      models cannot perform the computation precisely (especially with high-precision floats), we con-
