                                                 A BSTRACT

 1            Attribute reconstruction is used to predict node or edge features in the pre-training
 2            of graph neural networks. Given a large number of molecules, they learn to cap-
 3            ture structural knowledge, which is transferable for various downstream property
 4            prediction tasks and vital in chemistry, biomedicine, and material science. Pre-
 5            vious strategies that randomly select nodes to do attribute masking leverage the
 6            information of local neighbors. However, the over-reliance of these neighbors
 7            inhibits the model’s ability to learn long-range dependencies from higher-level
 8            substructures. For example, the model would learn little from predicting three
 9            carbon atoms in a benzene ring based on the other three but could learn more from
10            the inter-connections between the functional groups, or called chemical motifs. To
11            explicitly determine inter-motif knowledge transfer of pre-trained model, we define
12            inter-motif node influence measures. Then, we propose and investigate motif-
13            aware attribute masking strategies to capture long-range inter-motif structures by
14            leveraging the information of atoms in neighboring motifs. Once each graph is
15            decomposed into disjoint motifs, the features for every node within a sample motif
16            are masked. The graph decoder then predicts the masked features of each node
17            within the motif for reconstruction. We evaluate our approach on eight molecular
18            property prediction datasets and demonstrate its advantages.


19   1    I NTRODUCTION
20   Molecular property prediction has been an important topic of study in fields such as physical chemistry,
21   physiology, and biophysics (Wu et al., 2017). It can be defined as a graph label prediction problem
22   and addressed by machine learning. However, graph learning models such as graph neural networks
23   (GNNs) must overcome issues in data scarcity, as the creation and testing of real-world molecules is
24   an expensive endeavor (Chang et al., 2022). To address labeled data scarcity, model pre-training has
25   been utilized as a fruitful strategy for improving a model’s predictive performance on downstream
26   tasks, as pre-training allows for the transfer of knowledge from large amounts of unlabeled data. The
27   selection of pre-training strategy is still an open question, with contrastive tasks (Zhu et al., 2021)
28   and predictive/generative tasks (Hu et al., 2020a) being the most popular methods.
29   Attribute reconstruction is one predictive method for graphs that utilizes masked autoencoders to
30   predict node or edge features (Hu et al., 2020a; Kipf & Welling, 2016; Xia et al., 2022). Masked
31   autoencoders have found success in vision and language domains (He et al., 2022; Devlin et al., 2018)
32   and have been adopted as a pre-training objective for graphs as the reconstruction task is able to
33   transfer structural pattern knowledge (Hu et al., 2020a), which is vital for learning specific domain
34   knowledge such as valency in material science. Additional domain knowledge which is important for
35   molecular property prediction is that of functional groups, also called chemical motifs (Pope et al.,
36   2019). The presence and interactions between chemical motifs directly influence molecular properties,
37   such as reactivity and solubility (Frechet, 1994; Plaza et al., 2014). Prior work in message passing for
38   quantum chemistry has shown that long-range dependencies are important for downstream prediction
39   in chemical domains (Gilmer et al., 2017). Therefore, to capture the interaction information between
40   motifs, it is important to transfer inter-motif structural knowledge and other long-range dependencies
41   during the pre-training of graph neural networks.
42   Unfortunately, the random attribute masking strategies used in previous work for graph pre-training
43   were not able to capture the long-range dependencies inherent in inter-motif knowledge (Kipf &

45   feature information for reconstruction (Hu et al., 2020a; Hou et al., 2022). Notably, leveraging
46   the features of local neighbors can contribute to learning important local information, including
47   valency and atomic bonding. However, GNNs heavily rely on the neighboring node’s features rather
48   than graph structure (Yun et al., 2021), and this over-reliance inhibits the model’s ability to learn
49   from motif structures as message aggregation will prioritize local node feature information due to
50   the propagation bottleneck (Alon & Yahav, 2021). For example, as shown on the left-hand side of
51   Figure 1, if only a (small) partial set of nodes were masked in several motifs, the pre-trained GNNs
52   would learn to predict the node types (i.e., carbon) of two atoms in the benzene ring based on the
53   features and structure of the other four carbon atoms in the ring, limiting the knowledge transfer
54   of long-range dependencies. To measure the inter-motif knowledge transfer of graph pre-training
55   strategies, we define five inter-motif influence measurements and report our findings in Sec. 6.
56   Recent successes in vision and language domains have shown the utility of masking semantically
57   related regions, such as pixel batches (Li et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2022; He et al., 2021) and multi-token
58   spans (Levine et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2019; Joshi et al., 2020), and have demonstrated that a random
59   masking strategy is not guaranteed to transfer necessary inter-part relations and intra-part patterns
60   (Li et al., 2022). To better enable the transfer of long-range inter-part relations downstream, we
61   propose a novel semantically-guided masking strategy based on chemical motifs. In Figure 1, we
62   visually demonstrate our method for motif-aware attribute masking, where each molecular graph
63   is decomposed into disjoint motifs. Then the node features for each node within the motif will be
64   masked by a mask token. A graph decoder will predict the masked features of each node within the
65   motif as the reconstruction task. The benefits of this strategy are twofold. First, because all features
66   of the nodes within the motif are masked, our strategy reduces the amount of feature information
67   being passed within the motif and relieves the propagation bottleneck, allowing for the greater
68   transfer of inter-motif feature and structural information. Second, the masking of all intra-motif node
69   features explicitly forces the decoder to transfer intra-motif structural information. A novel graph
70   pre-training solution based on the Motif-aware Attribute Masking strategy, called MoAMa, is able
71   to learn long-range inter-motif dependencies with knowledge of intra-motif structure. We evaluate
72   our strategy on eight molecular property prediction datasets and demonstrate its improvement to
73   inter-motif knowledge transfer as compared to previous strategies.

74   2    R ELATED W ORK
75   Molecular graph pre-training The prediction of molecular properties based on graphs is impor-
76   tant (Wu et al., 2017). Molecules are scientific data that are time- and computation-intensive to
77   collect and annotate for different property prediction tasks (Liu et al., 2023). Many self-supervised
78   learning methods (Hu et al., 2020a; Hou et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2022; Xia et al.,
79   2023) were proposed to capture the transferable knowledge from another large scale of molecules
80   without annotations. For example, AttrMask (Hu et al., 2020a) randomly masked atom attributes for


                                                                               2
81    prediction. GraphMAE (Hou et al., 2022) pre-trained the prediction model with generative tasks to
82    reconstruct node and edge attributes. D-SLA (Kim et al., 2022) used contrastive learning based on
83    graph edit distance. These pre-training tasks could not well capture useful knowledge for various
84    domain-specific tasks since they fail to incorporate important domain knowledge in pre-training. A
85    great line of prior work (Zhang et al., 2021; Rong et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2021) used graph motifs
86    which are the recurrent and statistically significant subgraphs to characterize the domain knowledge
87    contained in molecular graph structures, e.g., functional groups. However, their solutions were
88    tailored to specific frameworks for either generation-based or contrast-based molecular pre-training.
89    Additionally, explicit motif type generation/prediction inherently does not transfer intra-motif struc-
90    tural information and is computationally expensive due to the large number of prediction classes. In
91    this work, we study on the strategies of attribute masking with the awareness of domain knowledge
92    (i.e., motifs), which plays an essential role in self-supervised learning frameworks (Xia et al., 2023).

93    Masking strategies on molecules Attribute masking of atom nodes is a popular method in graph
94    pre-training given its broad usage in predictive, generative, and contrastive self-supervised tasks (Hu
95    et al., 2020a;b; Hou et al., 2022; You et al., 2020; 2021). For example, predictive and generative
96    pre-training tasks (Hu et al., 2020a; Hou et al., 2022; Xia et al., 2023) mask atom attributes for
97    prediction and reconstruction. Contrastive pre-training tasks (You et al., 2020; 2021) mask nodes to
98    create another data view for alignment. Despite the widespread use of attribute masking in molecular
99    pre-training, there is a notable absence of comprehensive research on its strategy and effectiveness.
100   Previous studies have largely adopted strategies from the vision and language domains (He et al.,
101   2022; Devlin et al., 2018), where atom attributes are randomly masked with a predetermined ratio.
102   Since molecules are atoms held together by strict chemical rules, the data modality of molecular
103   graphs is essentially different from natural images and languages. For molecular graphs, random
104   attribute masking results in either over-reliance on intra-motif neighbors (Dwivedi et al., 2023)
105   or breaking the inter-motif connections via random edge masking. In this work, we introduce a
106   novel strategy of attribute masking, which turns out to capture and transfer useful knowledge from
107   intra-motif structures and long-range inter-motif node features.

108   3    P RELIMINARIES
109   Graph property prediction Given a graph G = (V, E) ∈ G with the node set V for atoms and
110   the edge set E ⊂ V × V for bonds, we have a d-dimensional node attribute matrix X ∈ R|V|×d that
111   represents atom features such as atom type and chirality. We use y ∈ Y as the graph-level property
112   label for G, where Y represents the label space. For graph property prediction, a predictor with
113   the encoder-decoder architecture is trained to encode G into a representation vector in the latent
114   space and decode the representation to predict ŷ. The training process optimizes the parameters to
115   make ŷ to be the same as the true label value y. A GNN is a commonly used encoder that generates
116   k-dimensional node representation vectors, denoted as hv ∈ Rk , for any node v ∈ V:
                                  H = {hv : v ∈ V} = GNN(G) ∈ R|V|×k .                               (1)
117   Here H is the node representation matrix for the graph G. Without loss of generality, we implement
118   Graph Isomorphism Networks (GIN) (Xu et al., 2019) as the choice of GNN in accordance with
119   previous work (Hu et al., 2020a). Once the set of node representations are created, a READOUT(·)
120   function (such as max, mean, or sum) is used to summarize the node-level representation into
121   graph-level representation hG for any G:
                                        hG = READOUT(H) ∈ Rk .                                      (2)
122   The graph-level representation vector hG is subsequently passed through a multi-layer perceptron
123   (MLP) to generate the label prediction ŷ, which exists in the label space Y:
                                             ŷ = MLP(hG ) ∈ Y.                                     (3)

124   GNN pre-training Random initialization of the predictor’s parameters would easily result in
125   suboptimal solutions for graph property prediction. This is because the number of labeled graphs
126   is usually small. It prevents a proper coverage of task-specific graph and label spaces (Hu et al.,
127   2020a; Liu et al., 2023). To improve generalization, GNN pre-training is often used to warm-up the
128   model parameters based on a much larger set of molecules without labels. In this work, we focus on
129   the attribute masking strategy for GNN pre-training that aims to predict the masked values of node
130   attributes given the unlabeled graphs.

173   5     P ROPOSED S OLUTION
174   In this section, we present our novel solution named MoAMa for effectively pre-training graph neural
175   networks on molecular data. We will give details about the strategy of motif-aware attribute masking
176   and reconstruction. Each molecule G will have some portion of their node masked according to
177   domain knowledge based motifs. We replace the node attributes of all masked nodes with a special
178   mask token. Then, the GNN in Eq. (1) encodes the masked graph to the node representation space,
179   and an MLP reconstructs the atom types for the attribute masked molecule.

180   5.1   K NOWLEDGE - BASED M OTIF E XTRACTION
181   To leverage the expertise from the chemistry domain, we extract motifs for molecules using the
182   BRICS (Breaking of Retrosynthetically Interesting Chemical Substructures) algorithm (Degen et al.,
183   2008). This algorithm leverages chemical domain knowledge by creating 16 rules for decomposition,
184   the rules of which define the bonds that should be cleaved from the molecule in order to create a
185   multi-set of disjoint subgraphs. Two key strengths of the BRICS algorithm over a motif-mining
186   strategy (Geng et al., 2023) is that no training is required and important structural features, such as
187   rings, are inherently preserved.
188   For each graph G, the BRICS algorithm decomposes the full graph into separate motifs. We denote
189   the decomposition result as MG = {M1 , M2 , ..., Mn }, which is a set of n motifs. Each motif
190   Mi = (Vi , Ei ), for i ∈ {1, 2, ..., n}, is a disjoint subgraph of G such that Vi ⊂ V and Ei ⊂ E. For

219   5.3   D ESIGN S PACE OF THE ATTRIBUTE M ASKING S TRATEGY

220   The design space of the motif-aware node attribute masking includes the following four parts:
221   Masking distribution We investigate the influence of masking distribution to the masking strategy
222   using two factors to control the distribution of masked attributes:
223          • Percentage of nodes within a motif selected for masking: we propose to mask nodes from the
224            selected motifs at different percentages. The percentage indicates the strength of the masked
225            domain knowledge, which affects the hardness of the pre-training task of the attribute
226            reconstruction.
227          • Dimension of the attributes: We propose to conduct either node-wise or element-wise
228            (dimension-wise) masking. Element-wise masking selects different nodes for masking in
229            different dimensions according to the percentage, while node-wise masking selects different
230            nodes for all-dimensional attribute masking in different motifs.
231   Reconstruction target Existing molecular graph pre-training methods heavily rely on two atom
232   attributes: atom type and chirality. Therefore, the reconstructive task could include one or both
233   attributes using one or two different decoders. Experiments will find the most effective task definition.
234   Reconstruction loss We study different implementations of reconstruction loss functions for Lrec .
235   They include cross entropy (CE), scaled cosine error (SCE) (Hou et al., 2022), and mean square error
236   (MSE). GraphMAE (Hou et al., 2022) suggested that SCE was the best loss function, however, it is
237   worth investigating the effect of the loss function choices in the motif-based study.
238   Additionally, attribute masking focuses on local graph structures and suffers from representation
239   collapse (Hu et al., 2020a; Hou et al., 2022). To address this issue, we use a knowledge-enhanced
240   auxiliary loss Laux to complement Lrec . Given any two graphs Gi and Gj from the graph-based
241   chemical space G, Laux first calculates the Tanimoto similarity (Bajusz et al., 2015) between Gi and
242   Gj as Tanimoto(Gi , Gj ) based on the bit-wise fingerprints, which characterizes frequent fragments
243   in the molecular graphs. Then Laux aligns the latent representations with the Tanimoto similarity
244   using the cosine similarity, inspired by previous work (Atsango et al., 2022). Formally, we define:
                          X
                 Laux =       Tanimoto(Gi , Gj ) − cosine(hGi , hGj ) , 1 ≤ i, j ≤ |G|, i ̸= j,       (14)
                          i,j

245   where hGi and hGj are the graph representation of Gi and Gj , respectively. The full pre-training loss
246   is L = βLrec + (1 − β)Laux , where β is a hyperparameter to balance these two loss terms (β = 0.5).
247   Decoder model The decoder trained via Eq. (13) could be a GNN or a MLP. Although the GNN
248   decoder might be powerful (Hou et al., 2022), we are curious if the MLP delivers a comparable or
249   better performance with higher efficiency.

265   pre-training and fine-tuning are 256 and 32, respectively.

266   6.2   BASELINES
267   There are two general types of baseline graph pre-training strategies that we evaluate our work
268   against: contrastive learning tasks, such as D-SLA (Kim et al., 2022), GraphLoG (Xu et al., 2021),
269   and JOAO (You et al., 2021), and attribute reconstruction, including Grover (Rong et al., 2020),
270   AttrMask (Hu et al., 2020a), ContextPred (Hu et al., 2020a), GraphMAE (Hou et al., 2022), and
271   Mole-BERT (Xia et al., 2023). Additionally, we evaluate on motif-based pre-training strategies,
272   MGSSL (Zhang et al., 2021), which recurrently generates the motif tree for any molecule, and MCM
273   (Wang et al., 2022), which uses a motif-based convolution module to generate embeddings.

274   6.3   R ESULTS
275   We report AUC-ROC of different graph pre-training methods in Table 1. MoAMa outperforms all
276   baseline methods on five out of eight datasets. On average, MoAMa outperforms the best baseline
277   method Mole-BERT (Xia et al., 2023) by 1.3% and the best contrastive learning methods D-SLA (Kim
278   et al., 2022) by 1.4%. Even without the auxiliary loss Laux , our motif-aware masking strategy still

280   6.4   A BLATION S TUDIES
281   To verify motif-aware masking parameters, we conduct ablation studies on the selection of masking
282   distributions, reconstruction target attribute(s), reconstruction loss function, and decoder model.
283   Study on Masking Distributions For motif-aware masking, there is the choice of masking the
284   features of all nodes within the motif or choosing to only mask the features of a percentage of nodes
285   within each sampled motif. For our study, we choose a motif coverage parameter to decide what
286   percentage of nodes within each motif to mask, ranging from 25%, 50%, 75%, or 100%.
287   Furthermore, the masking strategy utilized by previous work performs node-wise masking (Hu et al.,
288   2020a; Hou et al., 2022), where all features of a node are masked. An alternative strategy may be
289   element-wise masking, where masked elements are chosen over all feature dimensions and implies
290   that not all features of a node may necessarily be masked. Note that 100% masking will behave the
291   exact same as node-wise masking, as 100% of nodes within a motif will have each feature masked.

297   Study on Reconstruction Targets The choice of attributes to reconstruct for GNNs towards
298   molecular property prediction has traditionally been atom type (Hu et al., 2020a; Hou et al., 2022).
299   However, there are other choices for reconstruction that could be explored. We verify the choice
300   of reconstruction attrbutes by comparing the performance of the baseline model against models
301   trained by reconstructing only chirality, both atom type and chirality using two separate decoders,
302   or both properties using one unified decoder. From Table 2, we note that predicting solely atom
303   type yields the best pre-training results. The second best strategy was to predict both atom type and
304   chirality using two decoders. In this case, the loss of the two decoders are independent, leading to the
305   conclusion that the chirality prediction task is ill-suited to be the pre-training task. Because choice of
306   chirality is limited to four extremely imbalanced outputs, the useful transferable knowledge may be
307   significantly lesser than that of atom prediction, which, for the ZINC15 dataset, has nine types.
308   Study on Reconstruction Loss Functions For the pretraining task, we have three choices of error
309   functions to calculate training loss. A standard error function used for masked autoencoders within
310   computer vision (He et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Germain et al., 2015) is the cross-entropy loss,
311   whereas previous GNN solutions utilize mean squared error (MSE) (Hu et al., 2020b; Park et al.,
312   2019; Salehi & Davulcu, 2019; Wang et al., 2017). GraphMAE (Hou et al., 2022) proposed that
313   cosine error could mitigate sensitivity and selectivity issues:

339   7                                     C ONCLUSIONS
340   In this work, we introduced a novel motif-aware attribute masking strategy for attribute reconstruction
341   during graph model pre-training. This motif-aware masking strategy outperformed existing methods
342   that used random attribute masking, and achieved competitive results with the state-of-the-art methods
343   because of the explicit transfer of long-range inter-motif knowledge and intra-motif structural
344   information. We quantitatively verify the increase in inter-motif knowledge transfer of our strategy
345   over previous works using inter-motif node influence measurements.

467   A.1    I NTER -M OTIF I NFLUENCE E VALUATION C OMPLEXITY

468   In the worst-case, evaluation of inter-motif node influence can be computed between every pair of
469   nodes within a molecule, causing an evaluation complexity of O(n2 ), for n is the number of nodes in
470   a graph G. However, GNN message passing is limited by the number of layers used, k. Therefore,
471   the node influence calculations will only need to be performed on neighbors within a k-hop radius of
472   each other. This means that the time complexity of our evaluation is O(nd¯k ), where n is the number
473   of nodes in the graph, k is the number of layers of our GNN (k = 5), and d¯ is the average degree of a
474   node. d¯k ≤ n as molecular graphs are sparse, so the evaluation is not nearly as inefficient as O(n2 ).

475   A.2    R EGRESSION TASKS

476   We conducted additional evaluations on three regression datasets from MoleculeNet, ESOL, FreeSolv,
477   and Lipophilicty (Wu et al., 2017). We use RMSE to measure the test performance of the best
478   validation step during 3 independent runs.
479   Our method outperforms all baselines on the ESOL dataset and shows comparative results with
480   previous methods when considering average RMSE across all three datasets.

481   A.3    C ASE S TUDY

482   In Figure 3 there are two pairs of molecules, colored in blue and purple, that domain experts suggest
483   to study. They are pairs because they look similar, have mostly similar properties, and have some
484   different properties due to the structural differences. The molecular embedding space is obtained by
485   a two-dimensional t-SNE algorithm on the pre-trained embeddings of the methods. The distributions
486   and average distance between the graph examples are similar across the methods. However, contrastive
487   learning strategies such as GraphLoG and JOAO failed to capture the proximities and put the pairs
488   too distantly from each other. AttrMask based on random masking strategy was not able to learn
489   from the structural difference at a higher level and put the pairs too close to each other. The proposed
490   MoAMa provides a more reasonable set of embeddings for downstream fine-tuning.
