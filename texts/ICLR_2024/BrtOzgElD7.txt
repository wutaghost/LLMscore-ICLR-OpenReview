                                                                           A BSTRACT

               Deployable Large Language Models (LLMs) must conform to the criterion of help-
               fulness and harmlessness, thereby achieving consistency between LLMs outputs
               and human values. Red-teaming techniques constitute a critical way towards this
               criterion. Existing work rely solely on manual red team designs and heuristic adver-
               sarial prompts for vulnerability detection and optimization. These approaches lack
               rigorous mathematical formulation, thus limiting the exploration of diverse attack
               strategy within quantifiable measure and optimization of LLMs under convergence
               guarantees. In this paper, we present Red-teaming Game (RTG), a general game-
               theoretic framework without manual annotation. RTG is designed for analyzing
               the multi-turn attack and defense interactions between Red-team language Models
               (RLMs) and Blue-team Language Model (BLM). Within the RTG, we propose
               Gamified Red-teaming Solver (GRTS) with diversity measure of the semantic
               space. GRTS is an automated red teaming technique to solve RTG towards Nash
               equilibrium through meta-game analysis, which corresponds to the theoretically
               guaranteed optimization direction of both RLMs and BLM. Empirical results in
               multi-turn attacks with RLMs show that GRTS autonomously discovered diverse
               attack strategies and effectively improved security of LLMs, outperforming existing
               heuristic red-team designs. Overall, RTG has established a foundational frame-
               work for red teaming tasks and constructed a new scalable oversight technique
               for alignment. Warning: this paper contains examples that may be offensive or
               upsetting.

To detect toxic content within language models, existing approaches predominantly rely on heuristic
design of adversarial prompts through manual annotation to uncover security vulnerabilities (Xu
et al., 2021; Ross et al., 2021). Alternatively, some methods employ pre-established detectors
based on human experience to detect and filter toxic content (Wu et al., 2021a; Dinan et al., 2019).
Concerning the optimization of language models, prior research has explored techniques such
as prompt engineering and Reinforcement Learning (RL) (Saleh et al., 2020) to improve RLMs.
Additionally, strategies involving self-critique (Bai et al., 2022) and self-alignment (Sun et al.,
2023) have been employed to optimize BLMs. In summary, existing work heavily rely on manual
annotations and heuristic methods within single-turn interactions, lacking a rigorous mathematical
description of red team tasks. This limitation makes it challenging to conduct in-depth theoretical
analyses of offensive behaviors and security improvements, as well as to provide guidance for
optimizing the security performance of language models. Additionally, manual annotations are
constrained in terms of quantity and diversity. LLMs aligned solely based on manual annotations
may potentially cause harm to users in unpredictable ways. The process of manual annotation also
incurs substantial costs and has the potential to impose psychological trauma upon annotators. More
related work will be postponed to the Appendix A.
In this work, we bridge the gap through establishing a connection between natural language dialogue
and multi-turn game optimization. Specifically, we formulate token generation within a single
sentence as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) (Puterman, 1990) to finely optimize the token
generation of RLMs and BLMs, maximizing the reward for sentence generation. Furthermore, we
model the multi-turn dialogue interaction between RLMs and BLMs as an extensive-form adversarial
team game to maximize the payoffs obtained by RLMs and BLMs during the dialogue, achieving
more aggressive RLMs and safer BLMs. This results in a bi-level optimization at both the token
and sentence levels. Due to limitations in the quantity and diversity of annotated samples, manual
annotations and heuristic methods may struggle to discover and optimize certain potential security
vulnerabilities, posing obstacles to the practical deployment of language models. To address this,
we establish an automated and multi-turn offensive-defensive mechanism to solve red team task
to improves security more efficiently through interaction. Specifically, We employ meta-game
methods (Lanctot et al., 2017) within policy space to introduce diversity in sentence generation into
meta-game analysis. This is achieved through Nash solvers and uniform solvers by fictitious play
(Berger, 2007), among others, to perform sub-game solving and continuously reduce exploitability,
aiming to approximate Nash equilibrium in the Red Teaming Game (RTG). Nash equilibrium is
a critical solution concept in game theory and serve as the optimization goals in the game. In
the context of language model dialogue games, introducing Nash equilibrium is significant as it
provides optimization directions for both RLMs and BLMs simultaneously. Additionally, we analyze

2   R ED T EAMING G AME
In this section, we formally introduce Red Teaming Game (RTG), a mathematical model to formulate
the multi-turn dialogue process between RLMs and BLM from the game-theoretic perspective. Then
we formulate the RTG between RLMs and BLM.
RTG is extension of static, normal-form adversarial team games (Von Stengel & Koller, 1997) and
extensive-form game (Ritzberger et al., 2016). It is worth noting that red team can be expanded to
consist of multiple RLMs in RTG. In this work, we mainly discuss the red teaming task of single RLM.
RTG is assumed to take place in an finite-horizon setting in which a red team of identically-interested
RLMs inveigle a BLM to output toxic content in multi-turn dialogue. BLM aims to overcome these
inveiglements and follow the criterion of helpfulness and harmlessness. The two-player extensive-
form game between language models is a instance of RTG when there is only one RLM in the red
team. RTG consists of token-level MDP and sentence-level extensive-form team games. Figure 2
shows the process of RTG. Figure 6 in Appendix shows the bi-level optimization structure in RTG.
Formally, the process of next token prediction through autoregressive in single sentence is defined
as a Markov Decision Process for Token Generation (MDPTG), denoted by T . The interactive
process between RLMs and BLM in multi-turn dialogue is defined as a Extensive-form Team Game
in Dialogue (ETGD), denoted by D. RTG is defined as a bi-level optimization framework with
hierarchical structure, denoted by G. G is a tuple (T , D) with a token-level T and a sentence-level
D. The first level of optimization aims to solve the T by maximizing the cumulative reward of
single sentence generated by LLMs. The second level of optimization focuses on solving the D,
aiming to find equilibrium strategies σ ∗ for both RLMs and BLM in multi-turn dialogues for sentence
generation. We provide a more detailed formulation for each level of optimization in G in Appendix
B.1 and Appendix B.2.
In summary, solving MDPTG is the objective for first level optimization, with the aim of maximizing
the cumulative reward of single sentence generated by RLMs and BLM. Solving ETGD is the
objective for second level optimization, with the aim of solving the ETGD to find Nash equilibrium
strategies σ ∗ . In order to find a better equilibrium, we aim to maximize the utility for RLMs and
minimize the utility for BLM in multi-turn dailogue.

3   G AMIFIED R ED T EAMING S OLVER
Gamified Red Teaming Solver is based on Double Oracle (DO) methods (McMahan et al., 2003;
Dinh et al., 2021; McAleer et al., 2021) and PSRO family (Lanctot et al., 2017), which provide an
iterative framework for calculating approximate Nash equilibrium in normal form meta games. GRTS

an approximate Nash equilibrium. To provide a detailed account of the performance dynamics of
RLM and BLM during a single GRTS solving iteration, we selected one such iteration and plotted
BLM’s rewards per round as a function of training steps (prompt count) in Figure 3 (b). Notably, in
the second (turn 2) and third (turn 3) round of attack, BLM exhibited convergence to similarly high
rewards after approximately 6k prompts, indicating that the blue team, after engaging in the game,
can optimize to the same extent with regard to security vulnerabilities posed by multi-turn attacks. In

higher rewards. It is noteworthy that higher rewards signify increased security, and the first round
of attack originated from a manually annotated static dataset. Although the blue team succeeded in
reducing insecurity caused by the first round of attack, the overall performance gap between the blue
team and the red team in the three turns of attack remained marginal. This suggests that BLM is not
only adept at handling manually designed adversarial prompts but also effectively responds to attacks
from automated RLMs.
Figure 3 (c) and Figure 3 (d) further elucidate changes in cost for BLM and RLM during the RTG
solving process, along with the success rate of attacks on the blue team. It is evident that BLM’s cost
values decrease as the game progresses, corresponding to a reduction in the success rate of attacks
by the red team. When BLM is held constant, RLM gradually strengthens with training, enhancing
its attack success rate and increasing the cost of BLM’s output. Notably, as the dialogue between
BLM and RLM deepens, RLM uncovers more latent security vulnerabilities in BLM. This is reflected
in the latter stages of training, where the red team model exhibits higher attack success rates and
induces greater cost in BLM during the third round of attacks. This underscores the role of multi-turn
structures in assisting BLM in identifying more concealed security vulnerabilities and enhancing
RLMs’ attack capabilities. Such a game process effectively promotes the optimization of both RLM
and BLM.
After introducing semantic diversity into the training, we closely monitored the variations in semantic
diversity generated by the red team during the resolution process of GRTS in in Figure 3 (e). What we
observed was that as the iterative solving progressed within the multi-round attack-defense context,
the semantic diversity of the Red Team decreased to some extent. This reduction was primarily due to
the constraints imposed by the context provided in each iteration. However, it’s worth noting that the
Red Team’s models increasingly delved deeper into single-topic themes and forms of attacks.Figure
4 (e) serves as evidence supporting this observation. It shows a trend of increasing semantic diversity
among different rounds of attack methods and topics employed by the Red Team. This positive trend
indicates the effectiveness of the semantic diversity metric we introduced, as it facilitated the Red
Team in discovering a more diverse set of attack strategies.
Finally, a comparison was made between the cost distribution of BLM before and after the RTG in
Figure 3 (f). It is discernible that after 900 training steps, the mean of the cost distribution shifted
towards values less than 0, indicating an improvement in the safety of BLM’s outputs. Moreover, the
variance of the cost distribution decreased, signifying that BLM’s outputs became more concentrated
in the secure region with cost values less than 0. Overall, through the automated game between RLM
and BLM, we achieved a safer BLM and a more formidable RLM.

4.3   M ULTI - TURN ATTACKS

We conducted a comprehensive analysis of how multi-turn gameplay affects the performance of
both RLM and BLM. In Figure 3 (i), it is observed that, for BLM, as the number of attack rounds
increases, RLM’s attack success rate tends to decrease. However, it is noteworthy that, with training
progression, BLM’s defensive capability against the first turn (turn 1) of attacks surpasses that of
the second and third rounds. This is because the first round of attacks was composed of manually
annotated adversarial prompts, demonstrating the limitations of manually annotated heuristic red
teaming. After the 600th step, the success rates of the second and third rounds of attacks even show
improvement as RLM identifies new security vulnerabilities in BLM and launches targeted attacks.
Figure 3 (g) displays the Pareto optimal curve for BLM, showcasing the trade-off between harmless-
ness and helpfulness after enduring repeated attacks. Points closer to the upper-right corner signify
models that maximize both harmlessness and helpfulness simultaneously. It can be inferred from
Figure 3 (g) that multi-turn interactions between RLM and BLM contribute to enhancing BLM’s
security. The blue points in the figure represent the model’s performance after the third round of
dialogue. Furthermore, it is found that although multi-turn deep conversations enhance BLM’s
security, they simultaneously reduce BLM’s utility, incurring a certain alignment tax. However, it’s
pleasantly surprising to note that multi-turn attack-defense interactions help reduce this alignment tax.
This is evident in Figure 3 (h), where the red curve (turn 3) exhibits higher rewards compared to the
previous two rounds. This increase is attributed to the in-depth, multi-turn dialogues between the red
and blue teams, which provide high-quality language data for the more secure BLM to compensate
for the loss of utility and fundamental capabilities caused by security alignment, thereby mitigating
the negative effects of security alignment. Additionally, it is observed that after multiple rounds of
attack-defense, BLM’s perplexity is at its lowest. This is because, after extensive multi-turn dialogues,
BLM’s ability to counter concealed attack methods improves, and it demonstrates a certain degree of
generalization against unpredictable automated red team attacks. Consequently, it can cope with a
variety of red team attacks with lower perplexity.
Finally, in Figure 3 (j), we visualize the evolution of RTG in the form of a heatmap. The horizontal
and vertical axes of the matrix represent different CKPTs of RLM and BLM during training. Each
value in the matrix represents the win rate resulting from the multi-turn attack-defense interactions
between different pairs of RLM and BLM. The directions in which the red color deepens indicate
RLM’s increasing attack capabilities, while the fading blue direction signifies the improving security
performance of BLM. This heatmap delineates the optimization directions for BLM and RLM,
providing valuable insights for the development of more potent red team attackers and blue team.

5     C ONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK
In this work, we have made a pioneering effort by establishing a rigorous mathematical model named
RTG (Red Teaming Game) for the red teaming task of language models. Furthermore, we have
introduced a solver called GRTS (Game-Theoretic Red Teaming System) that comes with theoretical
guarantees of Nash equilibrium convergence, while incorporating diversity strategies. Through
multi-round attack-defense scenarios, we have successfully achieved simultaneous optimization of
both the Red Team Language Model (RLM) and the Blue Team Language Model (BLM). This
approach has enabled us to comprehensively detect and optimize hidden security vulnerabilities
within BLM. In the future, we plan to delve deeper into the discussion of how diversity measurements
in the semantic space impact the resolution of RTG. Additionally, we aim to elucidate the geometric
structural characteristics of RTG.

A    R ELATED W ORK

Existing work primarily address red teaming tasks in language models through two main directions.
We provide a detailed analysis of how our work addresses the shortcomings of existing work and
makes substantial contributions in the following.
One direction has been dedicated to discovering security vulnerabilities in LLMs through the heuristic,
manual design of prompts (Xu et al., 2021; Ross et al., 2021; Casper et al., 2023) and manual toxicity
detection (Wu et al., 2021a; Dinan et al., 2019; Nie et al., 2019; Stiennon et al., 2020; Shi et al.,
2023). However, this approach has limitations, including constraints on the quantity and diversity of
detected vulnerabilities, as well as an inability to provide clear guidance for optimizing LLMs. For
instance, some studies have detected toxicity by employing either manually crafted test cases (Ribeiro
et al., 2020; Röttger et al., 2020) or supervised test case generation (Bartolo et al., 2021a). Others
have hired annotators from various age and occupational backgrounds to offer diverse attack prompts
for uncovering security concerns in LLMs (Ganguli et al., 2022b; Bartolo et al., 2021b; Wu et al.,
2021b). Additionally, certain research has involved manual template and code creation for generating
test cases targeting specific failed responses (Jiang & Bansal, 2019; Garg et al., 2019; Dixon et al.,
2018; Jia & Liang, 2017; Ribeiro et al., 2020). All of these methods rely on human effort and
creativity to unveil undesirable LLM behaviors, leading to oversights in numerous security scenarios.
In addition, these methods have only considered single-turn attacks, resulting in relatively shallow
interactions with language models and making it difficult to detect and optimize more concealed
security vulnerabilities.
Another direction of efforts focus on autonomously detecting security vulnerabilities within LLMs
through learning. Some efforts involve the generation of test cases through learning, but they rely on
approximately 50,000 manually crafted examples (Bartolo et al., 2021a). Other approaches employ
gradient-based methods to search for adversarial prompts (Behjati et al., 2019; Wallace et al., 2019),
but this can result in adversarial examples lacking natural language fluency, which does not align
with typical human user behavior. Some research utilizes a trained classifier to detect offensive
content (Perez et al., 2022), assessing the toxicity of responses generated by the target LLM in
response to generated test queries. These studies explore zero-shot generation and reinforcement
learning methods to create test cases with varying diversity and difficulty levels. Additionally, prompt
engineering is employed to control the test cases generated by LLMs, uncovering various other
potential harms (Perez et al., 2022). However, the classifier used in this approach is binary, lacking
fine-grained classification of toxic content, which poses challenges for continuous LLM optimization.
Furthermore, the reinforcement learning methods used primarily enhance the offensive capabilities of
RLMs, neglecting the optimization of BLM through learning. Yet, optimizing BLM using RLMs is a
crucial objective in red teaming tasks. Some work adopts a self-critical approach (Bai et al., 2022;
Sun et al., 2023). Self-Alignment (Sun et al., 2023) utilizes a RLMs to generate attack instructions
and enhances diversity through the topic guided red team method. However, this method still relies on
some manual design. In Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022), LLMs self-optimize their behavior based
on a set of human-defined criteria. However, these methods exclusively considers the optimization
of blue team language models without explicitly enhancing the offensive capabilities of red team
language models. Specifically, Constitutional AI employs pre-existing human-labeled red team
prompt datasets and generates additional fixed attack prompts, making it challenging to ensure
robustness when language models face more diverse and powerful red team models. To optimize
BLM, some research has employed unlikelihood training (Welleck et al., 2019; He & Glass, 2019)
to minimize the probability of the original toxic outputs given specific test cases. Unlikelihood
training proves effective in reducing contradictions and offensive language in dialogues (Li et al.,
2019). Additionally, BLM can undergo training using RL (Saleh et al., 2020). However, these
approaches solely concentrate on the optimization of blue team language models, without adopting a
joint optimization framework for both RLMs and BLM. In this work, we introduce a game-theoretic
framework, providing a rigorous mathematical model to formulate the process of dialogue between
RLMs and BLM. We achieve joint optimization of both RLMs and BLM, resulting in more adversarial
RLMs and more secure BLMs.

Two-player Normal-form Games.            A two-player normal-form game, as defined     by Owen (2013),
is denoted as a tuple Π, U Π , where Π = (Π1 , Π2 ) and U Π = U Π1 , U Π2 constitute the tuple
of policy sets and the tuple of payoff tables, respectively. Formally, for each player i ∈ 1, 2, the
function U Πi : Π → R|Π1 |×|Π2 | is defined, with each entry representing the utility associated with
a joint policy. In this game, players endeavor to maximize their respective expected utilities by
selecting policies from a probability mixture (distribution) σi over their respective policy sets. It is
important to note that for all i ∈ 1, 2, the policy σi is drawn from the probability simplex ∆ (Πi ).
For convenience, throughout the subsequent discussion, we employ the notation −i to refer to the
other agent in the game, excluding player i. The concept of a best response to a mixed strategy σ−i
is pivotal in this context, defined as a strategy that yields the highest utility. Mathematically, this
best response can be expressed as BR (σ−i ) = arg max σi′ ui (σi′ , σ−i ), where ui (·, ·) represents
the utility function specific to player i for a given joint policy. In this work, the meta-game in RTG is
based on Two-player Normal-form Games.
Policy Space Response Oracles (PSRO) Double Oracle (DO) methods (McMahan et al., 2003; Dinh
et al., 2021; McAleer et al., 2021) provide an iterative framework for approximating Nash equilibria
in normal-form games. These algorithms iteratively expand restricted policy sets Πri for each player.
During each epoch, a Nash equilibrium σ = (σi , σ −i ) is computed for a restricted game formed
by a tuple of restricted policy sets Πr = Πri , Πr−i . Subsequently, a best response to this Nash
equilibrium is computed
                           for each player i and incorporated into their respective restricted policy set:
Πr = Πri , Πr−i . PSRO (Lanctot et al., 2017) serves as a generalization of DO, where choices in
the restricted game are policies rather than actions. In each epoch, PSRO learns an approximate best
response to a Nash equilibrium through oracles, such as reinforcement learning algorithms. Various
solvers are available for computing Nash equilibria, including α-rank (Omidshafiei et al., 2019), PRD
(Lanctot et al., 2017), and certain linear programming methods (Sandholm et al., 2005). In practice,
PSRO seeks an approximation of the Nash equilibrium with a desired level of precision, denoted as
ϵ ≥ 0 (Shoham & Leyton-Brown,P2008). To assess the quality of this approximation, we employ
NASHCONV(σ), calculated as i ui (BRi (σ−i) , σ−i ) − ui (σ), to measure the exploitability
of σ with respect to an oracle {BR (σ−i )} (Johanson et al., 2011). An exact Nash equilibrium is
achieved when NASHCONV = 0. In this work, GRTS is precisely based on PSRO.

D     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS AND H YPERPARAMETERS

The architectures of RTG and GRTS are based on the Beaver (Dai et al., 2023) and Open-Spiel
(Lanctot et al., 2019), employing DeepSpeed (Microsoft, 2023) ZeRO-3 technique for mixed-precision
parallel training. To further conserve computational resources, we utilize the LoRA (Hu et al., 2021)
technique on all linear layers with a hidden dimension of 128, adapted from the DeepSpeed-Chat
project (Microsoft, 2023).
We employ a total of seven models, namely RLM-sft, RLM-online, Critic-red, BLM-sft, BLM-online,
Critic-blue, and Cost Model. During the training stage, all models utilize stablelm-alpaca-3b model as
the backbone, which is a reproduction version of LLaMA from stability-AI (Taori et al., 2023). The
Cost Model undergoes fine-tuning with a safety dataset (Ji et al., 2023) consisting of 300k samples.
Serving as the payoff function for the RTG framework, the Cost Model assesses the harmfulness
of content generated by the BLM. BLM-sft undergoes fine-tuning using the Alpaca 52k dataset,
while RLM-sft is fine-tuned using the BAD dataset (Xu et al., 2021) and the Anthropic HH dataset
(redteam@anthropic.com, 2023). RLM-learning and BLM-learning are used for online learning
during games, Critic-red and Critic-blue are used to provide value estimates when solving the best
response using PPO (Schulman et al., 2017). Please refer to Table 1 for more key parameters during
the training stage.
During the evaluation stage, both our Cost Model and Reward Model were built upon the Beaver-7B
(Dai et al., 2023) model as their backbone architecture. The Reward Model was specifically employed
to assess the helpfulness of the language models. The evaluation process utilized the SafeRLHF-test
dataset (Ji et al., 2023), consisting of 35k samples.

F    E THIC D ISCUSSION
As the field of LLMs advances, it is imperative to address the ethical considerations and potential
broader impact of our research, especially when it involves the development and use of adversarial
models, such as the Red Team language models described in this study.
Ethical Risks The primary ethical concern in our research revolves around the creation and deploy-
ment of adversarial language models. While our intention was to enhance the security of "Blue Team"
language models by identifying vulnerabilities, we acknowledge that the same techniques can be
exploited for malicious purposes. Adversarial models have the potential to generate toxic or harmful
content, which could be used to manipulate or deceive individuals, spread disinformation, or harass
others online. It is crucial to recognize the responsibility that comes with developing such models
and to ensure that they are used responsibly and ethically.
Broader Impact The broader impact of our research extends beyond the immediate context of
language models. The emergence of more potent adversarial models raises concerns about the
robustness of AI systems and the potential for AI-generated content to be weaponized. This highlights
the importance of ongoing research into the detection and mitigation of adversarial attacks on AI
systems. Moreover, our findings underscore the need for the development of strong ethical guidelines
and regulations to govern the deployment of AI, particularly in situations where AI-generated content
can have real-world consequences.
Responsible AI Development To address these ethical concerns and mitigate potential harm, it is
essential for researchers, developers, and policymakers to collaborate in establishing best practices
and guidelines for the responsible development and use of AI models, especially those with adversarial
capabilities. This includes transparency in model development, robust content filtering mechanisms,
and comprehensive user education on the risks associated with AI-generated content.
In conclusion, our research, while aimed at enhancing the security of language models, underscores
the critical importance of ethics and responsible AI development in the field. We must continue
to explore the ethical implications of our work, engage in ongoing discussions, and take proactive
measures to ensure that AI benefits society while minimizing potential harm.
