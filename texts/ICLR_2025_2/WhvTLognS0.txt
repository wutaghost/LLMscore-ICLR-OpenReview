009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Generalizing deep neural networks to unseen target domains presents a major
012            challenge in real-world deployments. Test-time training (TTT) addresses this is-
013            sue by using an auxiliary self-supervised task to reduce the gap between source
014            and target domains caused by distribution shifts. Previous research relies on the
015            assumption that the adopted auxiliary task would be beneficial to the target task
016            we want to adapt. However, this situation is not guaranteed as each task has a dif-
017            ferent objective, thus adaptation relies on the relation between the tasks. This limi-
018
               tation has motivated us to introduce a more generalized framework: Task Relation
               Learning for Test-time Training (TR-TTT), which can be applied to multiple tasks
019
               concurrently. Our key assumption is that task relations are crucial information for
020
               successful test-time training, and we capture these relations using a Task Relation
021            Learner (TRL). We model task relations as conditional probabilities by predicting
022            the label of a target task based on the latent spaces of other task-specific features.
023            By leveraging these relations, the network can more effectively handle distribu-
024            tion shifts and improve post-adaptation performance across various tasks—both
025            classification and regression—unlike previous methods focused mainly on sim-
026            ple classification. To validate our approach, we apply TR-TTT to conventional
027            multi-task benchmarks, integrating it with the traditional TTT experimental pro-
028            tocol. Our empirical results demonstrate that TR-TTT significantly outperforms
029
               state-of-the-art methods across a range of benchmarks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Assuming the data distributions are identical between training and test-time, deep learning networks
034
      demonstrate robust performance across a range of tasks. Unfortunately, real-world scenarios rarely
035
      allow for such assumptions, making it challenging to apply numerous deep learning methods in
036   practice. Solving the distributional gap between training and test-time has been emerged as a new
037   challenge towards developing deep learning methodologies, motivating the development of domain
038   adaptation or domain generalization. These methodologies, however, adapt or generalize to the fixed
039   target distribution, which leads to the same challenges in real-world scenarios that the aforemen-
040   tioned settings face. To address these challenges, test-time adaptation (TTA) and test-time training
041   (TTT) have emerged as the latest approaches to suppress the performance degradation caused by
042   distributional gaps, aligning the pre-trained networks with target domains during test-time.
043   Both TTA (Wang et al., 2020; Nguyen et al., 2023) and TTT (Sun et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021;
044   Gandelsman et al., 2022; He et al., 2022; Mirza et al., 2023; Osowiechi et al., 2023; 2024) have
045   access to a subset of the target domain during test-time, which allows them to update the network
046   to better adapt to the target domain. In particular, TTT methods include an additional branch for
047   auxiliary tasks, which leverage the information trained on source domain to the adaptation on the
048   target distributions. By selecting self-supervised or unsupervised schemes for these auxiliary tasks,
049   TTT methods effectively improve their ability to handle distributional shifts during test time.
050   However, it is almost impossible to pre-emptively decide which information would be effective
051   in reducing the domain gap, as we do not have access to the ground truth of the target distribution.
052   Thus, most existing TTT approaches rely on assumptions about which information will be beneficial
053   for narrowing the distributional gap, making the selection of the auxiliary task a critical aspect
      of designing a TTT method. However, the chosen auxiliary task does not guarantee performance

      Consequently, previous TTT methods, as illustrated in Fig. 1-(a), exhibit varying adaptation abilities
078   depending on the relations between the main task and the selected auxiliary task. Motivated by
079   this limitation, we take a new approach to TTT, based on the assumption that the relations between
080   tasks are the key factor, robust on domain shift, which is effectively reducing the domain gap. This
081   eliminates reliance on auxiliary tasks, preventing the performance degradation of the main task.
082
      In this context, we propose a new approach for Test-Time Training by learning Task Relations (TR-
083
      TTT) , which effectively reduces the domain gap across multiple tasks, as shown in Fig. 1-(b). We
084   argue that the relations between tasks represent crucial information that can generalize across differ-
085   ent domains. To capture these task relations, we introduce a dedicated branch separate from the main
086   task prediction decoders. We define inter-task relations as conditional probabilities—specifically,
087   the probability of predicting a target task label given the set of all task-specific latent vectors. These
088   task-specific latent spaces are projected from a shared latent space and are optimized for each task.
089   To model these relations, we introduce a Task Relation Learner (TRL), which predicts task labels
090   using task-specific latent vectors. To further enhance the generalizability of the task relations learned
091   by TRL, we incorporate the masking technique motivated by the Masked AutoEncoder (MAE) (He
092
      et al., 2022). We assume that the mutual information encoded by these task relations is preserved
      across domain shifts and that TRL is sufficiently trained to predict task labels effectively, even with
093
      masked latent vectors. At test-time, the predicted output from masked latent vectors is guided by the
094
      predicted output from unmasked latent vectors to reduce the domain gap based on the learned task
095   relations in the source domain.
096
097   To evaluate the influence of different TTT methods on various tasks, we utilize several multi-task
098
      benchmarks that include both classification and dense regression tasks. This setup is more challeng-
      ing than previous TTT research, which typically focuses on simple classification tasks. By allowing
099
      access to multiple task labels in the source domain, each TTT method can learn more generalizable
100
      features across domain gaps. Therefore, we also enable previous TTT methods to access the ground
101   truths for multiple tasks in the source domain. We demonstrate that the proposed TR-TTT outper-
102   forms previous TTT methods by effectively improving the performance across all tasks, as evidenced
103   by experiments conducted on TTT between diverse datasets such as NYUD-v2, Pascal-Context, and
104   Taskonomy. The key contributions of this work are as follows:
105

107   • We propose a novel Test-Time Training method, called TR-TTT, which utilizes task relations as
        key information to address domain shifts during test-time adaptation.

116   2   R ELATED W ORK
117
118   Test-Time Adaptation & Training. Adapting deep neural networks to a target domain is challeng-
119
      ing due to the necessity of additional burdens for collecting and labeling data in that domain. Recent
      research has focused on using unlabeled data to infer the target domain’s distribution, thereby nar-
120
      rowing the gap between source and target domains during adaptation (Liang et al., 2024). Test-time
121
      adaptation (TTA) and test-time training (TTT), which enable online adaptation, show broad appli-
122   cability. A pivotal contribution in this area is TENT (Wang et al., 2020), which uses entropy as an
123   adaptation objective for image classification. In the field of computer vision, various TTA methods
124   have been suggested to adapt off-the-shelf models during the testing phase, focusing on tasks such
125   as image classification (Wang et al., 2022a; Iwasawa & Matsuo, 2021; Chen et al., 2023a), semantic
126   segmentation (Zhang et al., 2022a; Volpi et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2024), and object detection (Fan
127   et al., 2024). However, previous TTA methods primarily enable adaptation for classification tasks,
128   limiting their applicability to a wider range of downstream tasks. On the other hand, TIPI (Nguyen
129   et al., 2023) enforces transformation invariance, a technique commonly used in unsupervised learn-
130
      ing to simulate domain shifts, enabling adaptation for regression tasks, although its effectiveness on
      these tasks has not been fully demonstrated.
131
132   TTT employs a separate self-supervised task branch (Sun et al., 2020) as an auxiliary task for the
133   main task adaptation, drawing inspiration from multi-task learning (Caruana, 1997). Gandelsman
134   et al. (2022) utilizes the masked autoencoder (He et al., 2022), demonstrating its generalizability in
135   handling distribution shifts during deployment. TTT++ (Liu et al., 2021) preserves the statistical
136
      information of the source domain to align the test-time features through contrastive learning. As an
      auxiliary self-supervised branch, TTT-MAE (Gandelsman et al., 2022) adopt Masked Autoencoder
137
      to adapt the network in the test-time domain while the normalizing flow (Rezende & Mohamed,
138
      2015) has been used for Osowiechi et al. (2023). ActMad (Mirza et al., 2023) directly aligns the
139   activation statistics of the test-time domain to the training domain directly, using the L1 norm. For
140   only the classification task, several TTT methods (Su et al., 2022; Hakim et al., 2023; Li et al.,
141   2023) adaptively update prototype clustering for each class, aligning the distribution shift. NC-
142   TTT (Osowiechi et al., 2024), the most recent TTT study, adapt model on new domain by learning
143   to classify noisy views of projected feature maps.
144
      Task Relations. Capturing inter-task relations has been the main approach in the multi-task learn-
145   ing (MTL) domain (Caruana, 1997). In particular, partially labeled MTL addresses the challenge
146   of inferring distribution shifts from one task to another when access to task labels is limited during
147   training. Early studies in this field (Liu et al., 2007; Zhang & Yeung, 2009; Wang et al., 2009),
148   uses semi-supervised learning approaches to infer these task relations. Recent works (Imran &
149   Terzopoulos, 2019; Huang et al., 2020; Latif et al., 2020) have been applied to various domains,
150   including computer vision and speech recognition, utilizing evolving deep neural networks. Zamir
151   et al. (2020); Lu et al. (2021); Saha et al. (2021) directly utilize task relations by leveraging the
152   unique characteristics of each task. Lu et al. (2021) regularizes the results of depth estimation and
153
      normal vector estimation, using the fact that normal vectors can be obtained by differentiating depth
      information. Saha et al. (2021) infers task relations between semantic segmentation and depth esti-
154
      mation, inspired by the human perception process, which uses depth information to infer semantic
155
      details. On the other hand, several studies infer distribution shifts among different tasks without
156   explicitly analyzing task relations. Chen et al. (2020) utilizes consistency loss between similar tasks
157   in a shadow detection problem set. Wang et al. (2022b) uses intra-domain and inter-domain adver-
158   sarial loss to align the learning process of the same task across different domains. Li et al. (2022)
159   learns pairwise task relations by regularizing the outputs of tasks from different paths in a pairwise
160   task mapping. Nishi et al. (2024) constructs the joint-task latent space by encoding and decoding the
161   stacked labels of multiple tasks at once. (Ye & Xu, 2024) enhances the diffusion model (Ho et al.,
      2020) for multi-task learning by sharing the information across the different tasks.

      3.1              P ROBLEM D EFINITION
165
166   Consider a domain defined by the joint distribution pθ (x, y) with random variables {X , Y}, where
167   the input data x ∼ X and the corresponding label y ∼ Y. In the source domain {Xs , Ys }, a deep
168   learning network with parameters θ is trained to learn the conditional distribution pθ (ys |xs ), where
169   xs ∼ Xs and ys ∼ Ys . The goal of Test-Time Training (TTT) is to find the conditional distribution
170   pθ (yt |xt ) in the target domain {Xt , Yt } by adapting the network parameters θ to the target domain,
171
      where Xs ̸= Xt , without direct access to the target domain labels yt ∼ Yt . For classification tasks,
      both domains share the same label space Ys = Yt . We assume a similar setup for regression tasks,
172
      as the accuracy of regression tasks can be evaluated using scale-invariant metrics. Therefore, we use
173
      Y to represent the task label for both domains. Most recent multi-task architectures (Riquelme et al.,
174   2021; Zhang et al., 2022b; Fan et al., 2022; Mustafa et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023b; Huang et al.,
175   2024) use a shared encoder across different tasks, generating a common latent space. We denote this
176   latent space as zs ∼ Zs for the source domain and zt ∼ Zt for the target domain, respectively.
177
178   3.2              C ONNECTING TASK RELATIONS FROM SOURCE TO TARGET
179
180   In a multi-task setting, both the source and target domains are expanded to {X , {Yi }ni=1 }, where
181   n is the number of tasks. Similarly, the latent space that benefits each task varies across tasks.
182   We partition the latent space Z into task-specific subspaces {Zi }ni=1 , where each Zi represents the
183   projection of the shared latent space Z that is advantageous for target task i, resulting in (Z1 ∪ Z2 ∪
184   . . . ∪ Zn ) ⊆ Z. For simplicity, we denote the (Z1 ∪ Z2 ∪ . . . ∪ Zn ) as Ẑ.
185   Since we cannot access the ground truth of the target domain during adaptation, it is nearly impos-
186   sible to predict in advance which specific information from the source domain will be useful for the
187   target domain. As a result, most previous TTT approaches rely on assumptions about which infor-
188   mation would be beneficial across domains and suggest learning strategies tailored to their specific
189   objectives. Motivated by the previous research in multi-task learning, which uses task relation to
190   improve the generalizability of networks, we assume that the relations between tasks are key in-
191   formation that can be generalized across different domains. Our first assumption is that inter-task
192
      relations will remain consistent across domain shifts, as illustrated in Assumption 1.
193   Assumption 1 (Preservation of Task Relations). The mutual information between the latent space
194   and the task labels is preserved across the source and target domains, such that

      3.3   T EST-T IME T RAINING BY L EARNING TASK R ELATIONS
252   Following the previous derivation, we implement a methodology for test-time training (TTT) by
253   capturing task relations on both training and test-time, as illustrated in the Fig. 2. The proposed
254   framework consists of two branches: the branch for the main target task and the other branch,
255   including the TRL, which encourages the framework to learn the generalizable task relations. Since
256   the ideal latent space for capturing task relations may differ from that for predicting outputs, we
257   implement the separate branch to extract each task-specific latent space. The encoder, pθ (z|x),
258   extracts a latent vector, z, from the input image, x. The final main output, {yimain }ni=1 , are derived
259   by passing the latent vector, z, through a decoder, pθ (y|z), and supervised with the ground truth,
260
      {yi }ni=1 . On the other branch, the TRL captures the task relation by predicting the task outputs from
      a set of task-specific latent vectors, {zi }ni=1 . The set of the latent vectors are the projected versions
261
      of the latent vector z that pass through the task-specific projection layer.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
331
332   Datasets. To evaluate the ability to reduce the domain gap on downstream tasks, which include
333   both classification and regression, we utilize several existing multi-task benchmarks. We incorpo-
334   rate NYUD-v2 (Silberman et al., 2012), PASCAL-Context (Mottaghi et al., 2014), and Taskonomy
335   (Zamir et al., 2018) in our TTT evaluation protocols. These datasets contain 4, 5, and 26 vision
336   tasks, respectively. Following the typical protocol for TTT experimental settings, we use the shared
337   task set between each pair of benchmarks, such as depth estimation, semantic segmentation, surface
338
      normal prediction, and edge detection for NYUD-v2 and Taskonomy. Also, for PASCAL-Context
      and taskonomy datasets, we use semantic segmentation, surface normal prediction, and edge detec-
339
      tion. We select commonly used semantic labels for each dataset pair to simulate TTT protocols.
340
      Further details are provided in Appendix B.
341
342   Baselines and Evaluation Protocols. We compare our methods with previous test-time adapta-
343   tion approaches, including TENT (Wang et al., 2020) and TIPI (Nguyen et al., 2023), as well as
344
      test-time training methods such as TTT (Sun et al., 2020), TTT++ (Liu et al., 2021), TTTFlow
      (Osowiechi et al., 2023), ClusT3 (Hakim et al., 2023), ActMAD (Mirza et al., 2023), and NC-
345
      TTT (Osowiechi et al., 2024). To evaluate the adaptation, we cover the domain shifts as follows:

348   multiple tasks in the source domain to learn their relations during training, we also allow the afore-
349   mentioned baselines to access multi-task labels during training for a fair comparison. To achieve
350   this, we similarly incorporate multi-task decoders to facilitate learning during training. This setup
351   maximizes the potential of TTT methods, as learning multiple tasks in the source domain during
352   training enhances generalizability at test-time by learning shared representations across tasks. Dur-
353   ing test-time, we evaluate all tasks simultaneously. For TTA, we adapt the model that was trained
354   on multiple tasks in the source domain. To evaluate overall performance improvements during test

527   5    C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we introduce Task Relation Learning for Test-time Training (TR-TTT) to address the
530   distribution gap between source and target domains during adaptation. We demonstrate that under-
531   standing task relations is crucial for successful adaptation in TTT. By employing a Task Relation
532
      Learner to capture these relations as conditional probabilities, our approach enables the network to
      predict the labels of target tasks using information from other task-specific latent spaces. This in-
533
      novative strategy allows TR-TTT to manage distribution shifts more effectively and enhances post-
534
      adaptation performance across a range of tasks, including both classification and regression. We
535   validated our approach through extensive experiments using conventional multi-task benchmarks
536   integrated with established TTT protocols. The empirical results indicate a significant performance
537   improvement compared to state-of-the-art methods, confirming the effectiveness of our framework.
