011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            In industrial automation technology, radar is one of the crucial sensors in the ma-
014            chine perception stage. However, due to the long wavelength of radar electromag-
015            netic waves and the limited number of antennas, the angle resolution is limited.
016
               Recent advancements have introduced methods that leverage paired LiDAR-radar
               data for training, achieving notable point enhancement effect. However, the re-
017
               quirement for paired data significantly increases the cost and complexity of model
018
               development, limiting model’s widespread adoption and scalability. To address
019            this, we propose an unsupervised radar point cloud enhancement algorithm using
020            diffusion model as prior without paired training data. Specifically, our method for-
021            mulates radar angle estimation recovery into an inverse problem and introduces
022            prior knowledge via a diffusion model when solving it. Experimental results
023            demonstrate that our method achieves high fidelity and low noise performance
024            compared to traditional regularization methods. Compared to paired data training
025            methods, our approach not only delivers comparable performance but also offers
026            greater content control and reduced generation variance. Additionally, it does not
027
               require a huge amount of paired data. To the best of our knowledge, our method is
               the first to enhance radar point cloud by introducing prior knowledge via diffusion
028
               model instead of training on paired data.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Radio Detection and Ranging (Radar) technology has been extensively used in robot and related
034
      technology. In traffic monitoring, radar systems have been employed for vehicle detection (Palffy
035
      et al., 2020) and tracking (Liu et al., 2024; 2023). In the realm of intelligent driving, radar has
036   been integrated with other sensors like LiDAR (Yang et al., 2022) and cameras (Zheng et al., 2023;
037   Xiong et al., 2023) to enhance object detection (Liu et al., 2022; Xiong et al., 2022) and cooperative
038   perception (Huang et al., 2023). However, limited by the number of antennas and hardware noise,
039   the angular resolution of radar is restricted.
040
      To enhance the angular resolution capability of radar, the number of antennas can be increased
041
      using MIMO (Multiple Input Multiple Output) techniques (Bliss & Forsythe, 2003). However, how
042   to further improve radar angular resolution under fixed hardware parameters remains a persistent
043   research challenge for scientists. With the advent of deep learning methods, some studies directly
044   use radar points as input (Jin et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024), employing deep models to fit the
045   mapping function from sparse radar points to dense LiDAR points. However, due to the excessive
046   sparsity of the input information, these approaches exhibit limited performance. To enrich the input
047   information, researchers have used radar signals as input (Prabhakara et al., 2023). When mapped
048   to paired high-resolution LiDAR data, this input signal contains more comprehensive information
049   (Cheng et al., 2021), thereby enhancing the method’s performance. Nevertheless, models based on
050
      direct mapping from paired data suffer from issues such as insufficient generalization and lack of
      detail in reconstruction.
051
052   To address this, methods based on generative models have been proposed, such as diffusion model
053   (Song et al., 2020; Ho et al., 2020). Methods usually set LiDAR point cloud as target data domain,
      radar point cloud (Zhang et al., 2024) or range-azimuth heatmap (Wu et al., 2024; Luan et al.,

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
109
110
      Discriminative Model-based Methods: With the advancement of deep learning technology, re-
111   searchers have been able to leverage the powerful fitting capabilities of neural networks to directly
112   train models that map inputs to outputs. These models are referred to as discriminative models.
113   Some studies have adopted approaches based on radar point input data, where the data is structured
114   in 2D (Jin et al., 2023) or 3D perspectives and then fed into UNet networks (Kim et al., 2024) to
115   train the mapping parameters from radar input to LiDAR output. However, the performance of these
116   approaches is limited by the sparse information contained in the radar points. To improve the results,
117   the input data has been replaced with radar signals, such as time domain signal (Jiang et al., 2023),
118   range-Doppler signal (Cheng et al., 2021; 2022), range-azimuth signal (Prabhakara et al., 2023) or
119
      range-Doppler-elevation-azimuth signal (Han et al., 2024; Roldan et al., 2024a;b). This method also
      employs CNN/Transformer-based network architectures to implement such mappings. Due to the
120
      introduction of richer information, this approach achieves better enhancement effects. Nevertheless,
121
      these models suffer from significant generalization issues and lack of output details.
122
123   Generative Model-based Methods: To address the issue of model generalization, methods based
124   on generative models have been proposed. Generative models can achieve the function of randomly
125
      generating data within a specific domain by learning the mapping from Gaussian distributions to the
      statistical distributions of specific data (Song et al., 2020). As the current state-of-the-art generative
126
      model, the diffusion model accomplishes this mapping through a process of gradually adding noise
127
      to the original data until it becomes Gaussian noise, and then reversing this process via a Markov
128   chain (Ho et al., 2020). During training, the model uses neural networks to learn the parameters of
129   the noise at each step of the noise-adding process. During the denoising process, the neural network
130   is used to progressively remove the noise, generating new data within the target domain (Rombach
131   et al., 2022). Based on this, some studies have set LiDAR as the target data domain for enhanced
132   point clouds and radar as the conditional input (Wu et al., 2024), training various conditioning diffu-
133   sion models. These studies improve inference performance by adding new consistency constraints
134   (Zhang et al., 2024) during training or by setting biased mapping domains (Luan et al., 2024). How-
135   ever, all of these approaches require well-paired data for training, which severely limits the model’s
136
      flexibility and wide-range usage. Therefore, this paper proposes an unsupervised radar point cloud
      enhancement method using a diffusion model as prior without paired training data. Under the in-
137
      verse problem of radar angular estimation reconstruction that we established, the method utilizes a
138
      diffusion model trained in the LiDAR data domain and adds extra gradient constraints (Luo et al.,
139   2020; 2023) from the perspective of inverse problem to achieve radar point cloud enhancement (Rout
140   et al., 2024).

361
362   Dataset: In order to express the effectiveness of our proposed method, an autonomous driving
363
      sensing dataset, RADIal dataset (Rebut et al., 2022), is selected. This dataset is a collection of 2-
      hour of raw data from synchronized automotive-grade sensors (camera, laser, High Definition radar)
364
      in various environments (city-street, highway, countryside road) and comes with GPS and vehicle’s
365
      CAN traces. From the dataset, corresponding LiDAR and radar frames are extracted. The LiDAR
366   data serves as the high-resolution data domain that the radar data is intended to approximate. In
367   order to align with radar range-azimuth data format, LiDAR data are projected into bird-eye’s view

369   is not able to detect ground. Then, a latent diffusion model is trained on this LiDAR dataset as Fig.

404   4.2     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
405
406   First, a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) is trained on an Nvidia-L40 GPU to serve as the initial stage
407   model for the latent diffusion model. Specifically, we employ the VQ-VAE (Van Den Oord et al.,
408   2017) as described in LDM (Rombach et al., 2022). In our experiments, the embedded feature
409
      dimension is set to 4, and the quantized channel number is set to 2048. Additionally, the input
      LiDAR polar BEV data is duplicated three times and concatenated to better leverage the model’s
410
      capabilities. Once the VQ-VAE has converged, the encoder is used to compress the input LiDAR
411
      data into a significantly smaller latent feature map. Subsequently, an unconditioning latent diffusion
412   model is trained on the latent space.
413
414   For the inference phase, as illustrated in Fig. 1, the process begins with a randomly initialized Gaus-
415
      sian noise latent feature map. 1000 steps are then taken to recover the final result. As depicted in
      Algorithm 1, these steps involve alternating between gradient descent based on the diffusion model
416
      denoising prior probability and the measurement function likelihood probability. The measurement
417
      function is constructed to reflect the actual configuration of the radar sensor used in the RADIal
418   dataset, which employs 84 virtual antennas.

518
      This paper proposes a radar enhancement reconstruction algorithm using diffusion model, which
      is capable of utilizing the learned prior knowledge from LiDAR data. Bayesian inference for the
519
      radar enhancement is formulated, and the samples are drawn from the posterior distribution using
520
      the diffusion model as a prior and linear radar imaging equation as a constraint. Through parameter
521   analysis and comparative experiments, the effectiveness and high performance of our method are
522   demonstrated. However, there are still some issues in our method, such as high cost of inference
523   time and multi-modal feature alignment. These issues will be discussed and addressed in future
524   work.
