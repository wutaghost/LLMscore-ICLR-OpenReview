009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Haim et al. [NeurIPS 2022] propose a method to reconstruct training data from
012            trained neural networks with impressive results. While their reconstructed images
013            resemble the original training images, most of them also contain a considerable
014            amount of noise and artifacts. This is especially true, when the network was trained
015            on more than just a few dozen images. To address this, we view the problem as
016
               a specific image restoration task. Since the noise and artifacts are different from
               other types of noise (Gaussian noise, compression artifacts, blurring, or impulse
017
               noise from digital cameras), we create a new dataset specifically for the restoration
018
               of images produced by the reconstruction process proposed by Haim et al. We
019            use this dataset consisting of about 60 million noisy reconstructions of CIFAR-10
020            images to train a diffusion model on the restoration task. Using this method, we
021            obtain reconstructions that are significantly closer to the original training images
022            measured in terms of SSIM and HaarPSI scores.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      To what extent is the training data encoded in the weights of a trained neural network? This is not just
      an important question for our understanding of neural networks, but also a question with potential
028
      privacy implications. Since the work of Fredrikson et al. (2014), model inversion attacks have been
029
      investigated for many years. Fredrikson et al. (2015) for example show that, given a facial recognition
030   model, it is possible to obtain an image depicting a target person. This is done by optimizing for
031   an input of a facial recognition model that maximizes the models certainty that the input shows a
032   particular target person. However, such model inversion attacks fail to produce any meaningful result
033   if a class contains many diverse images that are not very similar to one another (Shokri et al., 2017).
034
      Haim et al. (2022) develop another type of attack that can recover part of the training data of an image
035
      classifier even when each class contains a diverse set of images. They are able to recover a number
036   of training images from the weights of a given trained network. The reconstructed images can be
037   noisy, but clearly resemble specific individual images from the training set (as opposed to simply
038   constructing new realistic images that would belong to a class, but were not in fact used during the
039   training).
040
      We view the removal of noise and artifacts from the reconstructed images produced by Haim et al.
041
      (2022) as a new type of image restoration task and by tackling this new task, we significantly improve
042   the reconstructions obtained by Haim et al. (2022). We create a large dataset consisting of such noisy
043   reconstructions together with their clean counterparts from the original training set and then train a
044   conditional diffusion model on this new image restoration task. This works even when the corruption
045   of the images is severe and even when it would be difficult for a human to interpret the corrupted
046   image.
047
      Previous works have considered using generative tools to guide model inversion attacks. The idea
048   is that an unconstrained optimization over the model input to maximize a certain model output
049   may lead to completely unrealistic results. Instead, the goal is to either constrain the generation or
050   bias it towards specific types of “realistic” inputs. Zhang et al. (2020) use pretrained GANs in an
051   attempt to prevent the model inversion attack from generating unrealistic images. However, while
052   this approach generates realistic images that fit a particular class, just as the classic unguided model
053   inversion attack, it cannot recover images used in training if the images in the class are sufficiently
      diverse. In this sense our results are notably different. While our use of a diffusion model also risks


                                                         1

054
      hallucinations—the production of images that look realistic, but were in fact not part of the training
055   data—we propose a mitigation for this that utilizes the probabilistic nature of the diffusion process.
056
057   Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
058
       • We demonstrate that diffusion models can be very effective at removing noise and artifacts
059
         from reconstructed training data, specifically reconstructions that are produced by solving the
060
         optimization problem based on Karush–Kuhn–Tucker(KKT) points of the maximum-margin
061      problem.
062
       • As far as we are aware, this is the first work that successfully trains and applies a diffusion
063
         model for improving imperfect results of solving a specialized optimization problem, where
064
         the imperfections appear to be a special kind of noise not otherwise familiar and for which
065      off-the-shelf denoisers do not appear suitable for.
066
067
       • We contribute a dataset consisting of about 60 million pairs of reconstructed images of varying
         quality and their estimated (augmented) CIFAR-10 counterpart for the community to use in future
068
         research.

      1.1   R ELATED WORK
072
      We refer to Haim et al. (2022) for a detailed comparison between their approach and other types of
073   attacks.
074
075   Buzaglo et al. (2023) extend the work of Haim et al. (2022) in a number of ways. Firstly, they show
076
      that the approach can also reconstruct part of the training data in the multiclass setting. In fact, they
      demonstrate that trained models become more vulnerable to training sample reconstruction as the
077
      number of classes increases. They also study regression loss functions with weight decay (as opposed
078
      to cross entropy loss) and establish that reconstructions of training samples is still possible. Finally,
079   they also successfully perform reconstructions for models trained on a larger training set (size 5000
080   as opposed to the previous largest of 1000 in Haim et al. (2022)).
081
082
      Building on Haim et al. (2022) and model inversion methods such as that of Tumanyan et al. (2022),
      more recently Oz et al. (2024) show how one may reconstruct higher-resolution training data from
083
      fine-tuned foundation models via the embedding space.
084
085   Feldman (2020) provide a theoretical model demonstrating that for natural data distributions memo-
086   rization of labels is necessary for achieving close-to-optimal generalization error. Hayes et al. (2023)
087   obtain a tight upper bound on the success of any reconstruction attack against DP-SGD, a standard
088
      algorithm for private deep learning.
089   Balle et al. (2022) show that it is feasible for an adversary who knows all the training data points
090   except one to reconstruct the remaining data point.
091
      Jagielski et al. (2023) show that, although non-convex models can memorize data forever in the
092   worst-case, standard image, speech, and language models empirically forget examples as training
093   time increases. Carlini et al. (2023b) identify three log-linear relationships that quantify the degree to
094   which large language models emit memorized training data.
095
      Carlini et al. (2023a) devise a generate-and-filter pipeline, and show that it is able to extract over
096
      a thousand training examples from state-of-the-art diffusion models. Somepalli et al. (2023) study
097
      how to detect reproduction of training images by diffusion models, and investigate how its rates are
098   impacted by factors such as training set size.

101
102   The approach by Haim et al. (2022) relies on the implicit bias of gradient descent. Specifically, Lyu
103   & Li (2020) and Ji & Telgarsky (2020) show that when optimizing the binary cross-entropy loss of
104   a homogeneous neural network using gradient flow, under certain conditions, the direction of the
105   network weights converges to a Karush–Kuhn–Tucker (KKT) point of the maximum-margin problem.

107   i ∈ [n] and that at some point during training, we have mini {yi Φ(θ; xi )} ≥ 1, where Φ(θ; x) is
      the output of the neural network with weights θ on input x. This condition mini {yi Φ(θ; xi )} ≥ 1

      Rather than optimizing for the loss L(λ′ , x′ ) as written, Haim et al. introduce a few additional
131

132   derivative of the Softplus function ln(1 + eαx )/α, with a hyperparameter α. The hope is that this
133   is easier to optimize due to it being continuous, but, especially for large α, otherwise not very
134   different from the derivative of the ReLU function. Secondly, they add terms to the loss function that
135   penalize pixel values outside the range [−1, 1] and values for λ′i smaller than a hyperparameter λmin .
136   Concretely, they add a term of max(p − 1, 0)2 + max(−1 − p, 0)2 for each pixel p of each xi to the
137   loss and a term of 5 · max(−λi + λmin , 0)2 for each λi .

139   2    C REATION OF THE DATASET
140
141   The reconstructions of training data that are obtained by minimizing the loss function stated in
142   section 1.2 contain considerable noise and artifacts. One reason for this is that in practice, networks
143   are not trained to infinity and therefore eq. (1) is not exactly satisfied. We see the task of “cleaning
144   up” such noisy reconstructions as a specific type of image restoration task.
145
      In order to be able to train a model, in our case mainly a diffusion model, on our image restoration
146
      task, we have to create a suitable dataset for this particular task. When, say, training for an image
147   restoration task that aims to remove Gaussian noise, it is possible to generate the corresponding
148   image corruptions on the fly during training. For this, we only require a set of clean images, which
149   we can then augment (through flips, rotations, etc.) and corrupt by adding Gaussian noise to obtain
150   pairs of input images and the corresponding target image. In contrast, we cannot generate the type of
151   corruption occurring in our restoration task on the fly in an efficient way. Instead, we create a dataset
152   consisting of a large number of pairs of images. One image in each pair is an original image from the
153   CIFAR-10 training set and the second image is a corrupted version of the same image resulting from
154   the reconstruction process.
155   To generate such pairs, we first train a neural network on some images of the CIFAR-10 training set
156   and then attempt to reconstructed the training images. If the reconstruction procedure results in some
157   images that, while noisy, can be confidently matched to one of the images the network was trained
158   on, this gives us a pair of noisy reconstructed image and a clean target image from the CIFAR-10
159   training set.
160
161   The training phase. We train a neural network with two hidden layers, without bias terms, consist-
      ing of 1000 neurons each and with ReLU activations. As Haim et al., the weights in the first layer are


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      initialized using Gaussians with standard deviation 10−4 , and all other layers are initialized using
163   Kaiming He initialization (He et al., 2015). The training is done for the binary task of distinguishing
164   animals from vehicles in images and uses binary cross entropy loss. Then we run the reconstruction
165   procedure. As Haim et al., we normalize the training data by averaging all training images and
166   subtracting this average from all training images.
167
168   The reconstruction phase. For all i, we randomly initialize λ′i ∼ U[0, 1) and x′i ∼ N (0, σ 2 I),
169   where σ is a hyperparameter. We set m = 200 (which means we have 2m = 400 many λ′i and
170   x′i ’s). We then optimize the loss function described in section 1.2. Note that, as mentioned before,
171   during this reconstruction phase, we substitute the gradient computation of the ReLU function by the
172   derivative of the Softplus function with the hyperparameter α.
173
174   Improving the data generation speed. One of our main challenge is that training a network and
175   then running the reconstruction procedure of Haim et al. takes a considerable amount of time. In the
176
      main setup of Haim et al., the network is trained on 500 images for one million epochs. Haim et al.
      then perform the reconstruction given this network. A single such reconstruction run alone takes
177
      about 90 minutes when using a single NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3080 Ti GPU and may only produce a
178
      few reconstructions of sufficient quality. Here sufficient quality means that we can match it to one of
179   the images the network was trained on with some level of confidence.
180
181   For this reason, we modify the procedure in order to generate more data for our dataset in a shorter
182
      amount of time. In particular:
183
       • We use single instead of double precision.
184
185    • For both the training of the network and the reconstruction procedure, we use the Adadelta
186
         optimizer (Zeiler, 2012) instead of gradient descent. The Adadelta learning rate is fixed at 1.
187    • We train the network on 300 images (150 for each class) instead of 500.
188    • We train the network for 50,000 epochs instead of one million epochs.
189
       • Instead of setting m equal to the number of images the network was trained on, We run the
190
         reconstruction only with m = 200 (i.e. we use 400 x′i and λ′i values).
191
192    • We fix the hyperparmeters for the reconstruction run as α = 20, λmin = 0.5, and σ = 0.001.
193    • We run the reconstruction for 11,900 epochs instead of 50,000.
194
195   With these changes, the combined training and reconstruction takes a few minutes rather than hours.
196
197   Image augmentation. As is often done when training generative models, we aim to increase the
198   diversity in our training set through image augmentation. While this is often done on the fly while
199   training a diffusion model, as explained above, this is not possible for us. The difficulty is the
200   following: suppose we have an augmentation operator A that transforms a training image I to an
201
      augmented image A(I). Now suppose we get a noisy reconstruction R of I and the pair (R, I) is
      stored in our dataset. Can we now train the diffusion network on an augmented version of I? For
202
      this we would want to obtain a reconstruction of A(I). However, it is not obvious what such a
203
      reconstruction would be. One guess would be A(R), but it is not clear to us that, say, rotating a
204   training image would result in the same kind of rotation in the reconstructions with no other changes
205   to the distribution of the reconstruction. Investigating this would be interesting future work, but for
206   this current work, we are choosing the safe option of performing the data augmentation already when
207   creating our dataset.
208
      Concretely, after sampling 300 images from the CIFAR-10 training set (150 from “vehicle” classes and
209
      150 from “animal” classes), we randomly augment each of the images using the same augmentation
210   pipeline and parameters as Karras et al. (2022) (which in turn borrow the pipeline from Karras et al.
211   (2020)). It randomly applies x-flips, y-flips, isotropic and anisotropic scalings, and fractional rotations
212   and translations. To stop the augmentations from leaking into the image generation when applying
213   the trained diffusion model, the augmentation parameters are given to the diffusion model as an
214   additional conditioning input. When the model is used to generate new images, the augmentation
215   parameters are set to 0 to condition the model to only generate non-augmented images. All this is
      done exactly as in Karras et al. (2022) and we refer to their work for more details.

      Identifying image pairs. During each reconstruction, we save x′i at epochs 100, 200, 300, 500,
217   800, 1400, 2400, 4100, 7000, 10000, and 11900. Then for each of the x′i at each of these points in
218   time, we compute the SSIM score (Wang et al., 2004) to each of the images the network was trained
219   on. If there is an x′i at any of these times that has an SSIM score above 0.4 for some training image T ,
220   but where the SSIM score is at least 0.05 lower for the next best training image, then we say that we
221   are reasonably confident that this x′i represents a corrupted version of T . We therefore add x′i and T
222   to our dataset. In fact, in this case we add all versions of x′i recorded in epochs 100, 200, . . . , 11900
223   even if only one of them produced a confident match with T . By taking even versions of x′i from
224   very early epochs, we hope to also include image pairs in our dataset where one image is a corrupted
225
      version of the other, but where the corruption is so severe that the SSIM score is possibly even
      below 0.4.
226
227
228
      Number of runs, dataset size, and compute. We ran this process of training a network and then
      performing the reconstruction 100k times; each time with a new random subset of 300 images from
229
      the full CIFAR-10 training set (but such that the classes are balanced) and their random augmentations.
230
      Unfortunately, some images are much more likely to be reconstructed than others, which creates big
231   imbalances in the dataset and missing out on including many images of the CIFAR-10 training set
232   completely because they were not reconstructed with suitable quality even once in any of the 100k
233   runs. To mitigate this, in a second phase, we ran the process another 50k times, but this time only
234   sampling from the 4000 CIFAR-10 images least seen so far in the dataset rather than from all 50,000
235   images in the CIFAR-10 training set (which images were least seen is updated after each run). Indeed,
236   while imbalances remain, with this approach we were able to ensure that 42,707 of the 50,000 images
237   in the CIFAR-10 training set appear at least once in the dataset.
238   The total compute for generating the dataset on an internal cluster utilizing NVIDIA A100 GPUs
239   was about 5600 GPU hours. We believe there is room to improve this by using a better sampling
240   strategy when picking images to train the network on. This could make better use of our new
241   gained knowledge about the discrepancies in likelihood of different images having good enough
242   reconstructions in a single run. For example, it may be advisable to avoid repeatedly sampling
243   images for which already many suitable corrupted versions were generated. At the same time, it
244   may also help to not too aggressively sample from the images that have repeatedly failed to produce
245
      any suitable reconstructions over a large number of runs. In our experiments, we saw that otherwise
      there can be many runs not contributing a single new entry to the dataset because all images used
246
      in training the network were chosen from the set of images that are unlikely to produce meaningful
247
      reconstructions.

250   3       T HE CONDITIONAL DIFFUSION MODEL
251
252   For our diffusion model, we use the DDPM++ architecture (Song et al., 2021) and exactly the same
253   method (preconditioning, noise distribution, etc.) as Karras et al. (2022). We only make two notable
254   changes to their code (which is available1 under a CC BY-NC-SA 4.0 license).
255   The purpose of the first change is to condition the model on a given (corrupted) image that is supposed
256   to be restored. For this we use six instead of three input channels, where the three colors of the
257   corrupted image are fed into the network through the three additional input channels. The skip
258   connection in the preconditioner is still restricted to the first three channels, however.
259
      The second change is to use our dataset consisting of pairs of images from the CIFAR-10 training
260
      set and noisy reconstructions of those as described in section 2. This also means data augmentation
261   already has been performed when creating the dataset rather than being done on the fly at the time
262   of training the diffusion model. We emphasize that it is important that our dataset used to train the
263   diffusion model only relies on the training set of CIFAR-10, such that we can later evaluate it using
264   images involving the test set that the diffusion model has not seen during training.
265
266   Dataset balancing. Our dataset containing about 60 million examples contains augmented versions
267   of some CIFAR-10 images much more frequently than others. We balance this as follows: if a
268   CIFAR-10 image appears z times within the dataset, we remove each such occurrence from the

385   otherwise arbitrarily scaled (in particular, their range can be orders of magnitude smaller than the
386   full interval [−1, 1]). We therefore normalize them by multiplying the pixel values with the largest
387   possible constant r, such that after reversing the training data normalization (i.e., after adding the
388   average of all images in the training data), the pixel values are in the interval [0, 1].
389
390   5.1.1    E VALUATING OUR IMAGE RESTORATION PERFORMANCE
391
      For each of the 500 images that the neural network was trained on, we find the best match in terms of
392   SSIM score for 3 each of the following set of images:
393
394     1. The set of all images obtained in all of the reconstruction runs. We call these the plain recon-
395        structions. They tend to be noisy and have artifacts.
396     2. The set of images obtained after applying the Restormer framework by Zamir et al. (2022) with
397        pretrained weights for image deraining, Gaussian denoising, or real image denoising to all plain
398        reconstructions.
399     3. The set of images obtained after applying the trained BF-DnCNN discussed in section 4 to all
400        plain reconstructions.
401
        4. The set of images obtained after applying our diffusion basaed image restoration model once to
402
           each plain reconstruction. By this we mean that we condition our trained diffusion model on the
403
           plain reconstruction and sample an image using 18 sampling steps.
404
405
        5. The set of images obtained as follows: For each plain reconstruction, applying our image
           restoration model 20 times and taking the average of the 20 resulting images.
406
407   We are exploring the last method mainly for the purpose of mitigating hallucinations. Hallucinations
408   can arise when the plain reconstruction we condition on is of very poor quality. In such cases, the
409   diffusion model will produce vastly different outputs each time it is applied with different initial noise.
410   Averaging such different outputs will not result in a clear image and be therefore less misleading than
411   taking the output of only a single application of the diffusion model.
412

415   method, we only show the effect of real image denoising as a representative example. Gaussian
416   denoising and image deraining performed worse (see Figure 2).

553   8    E THICS S TATEMENT
554
555   The risk of the recovery of training data is not new, but is amplified by our work. So far, the method
556   by Haim et al. (2022) has only been used to recover training data from relatively small models that
557   are trained on relatively small datasets. It is currently computationally expensive to scale this up.
558   However, until robust defenses are found and used, it is still advisable to treat the weights of a trained
559   model in the same way as one would responsibly treat the data it was trained on.
560   We also note that, similar to membership inference attacks, training data reconstruction can also
561   potentially be used by copyright holders to investigate whether their works have been used in the
562   training of a model. See for example discussions in Carlini et al. (2023a).
