009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
012            In-context learning (ICL) enables large language models (LLMs) to generalize to
013            new tasks by incorporating a few in-context examples (ICEs) directly in the input,
014
               without updating parameters. However, the effectiveness of ICL heavily relies on
               the selection of ICEs, and conventional text-based embedding methods are often
015
               inadequate for tasks that require multi-step reasoning, such as mathematical and
016
               logical problem solving. This is due to the bias introduced by shallow semantic
017            similarities that fail to capture the deeper reasoning structures required for these
018            tasks. We present GraphIC, a novel approach that leverages graph-based represen-
019            tations of reasoning processes, coupled with Bayesian Networks (BNs) to select
020            ICEs. Graph structures inherently filter out shallow semantics while preserving
021            the core reasoning structure. Importantly, BNs capture the dependency of a node’s
022            attributes on its parent nodes, closely mirroring the hierarchical nature of human
023            cognition—where each thought is shaped by preceding ones. This makes BNs
024            particularly well-suited for multi-step reasoning tasks, aligning the process more
025
               closely with human-like reasoning. Extensive experiments across three types of
               reasoning tasks (mathematical reasoning, code generation, and logical reasoning)
026
               demonstrate that GraphIC outperforms both training-free and training-based mod-
027
               els in selecting ICEs, excelling in terms of both effectiveness and efficiency. We
028            show that GraphIC enhances ICL’s performance and interpretability, significantly
029            advancing ICE selection for multi-step reasoning tasks.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   In-context learning (ICL) (Brown et al., 2020) represents a paradigm in how large language models
035   (LLMs) perform inference by using a small number of in-context examples (ICEs) within the input
036   prompt. This technique enables LLMs to generalize to new tasks or enhance their performance
037   on existing tasks without updating parameters. However, previous studies have highlighted the
038   sensitivity of ICL performance to the specific ICEs selected (Zhao et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022),
039
      underscoring the importance of strategic ICE selection. Consequently, numerous methods have been
      proposed to optimize the selection of ICEs, focusing on improving task performance and ensuring
040
      greater robustness (Liu et al., 2022; Rubin et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2024). These
041
      methods frequently rely on text-based embeddings, where both the query and candidate ICEs are
042   embedded using a language encoder, with similarity scores guiding the selection process.
043
044   Current text-based embedding selection methods primarily focus on capturing semantic-level simi-
045
      larity, demonstrating their utility in tasks such as semantic analysis (Liu et al., 2022) and machine
      translation (Agrawal et al., 2023). However, these approaches encounter significant limitations in
046
      multi-step mathematical and logical reasoning tasks, such as GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and
047
      ProofWriter (Tafjord et al., 2021). The core issue lies in the fact that textual data often encodes
048   a substantial amount of shallow semantic information, which is largely irrelevant to the underlying
049   reasoning processes required for math and logic tasks. This extraneous information introduces bias
050   in the selection of ICEs (An et al., 2023), and can even lead the LLM to adopt misleading rea-
051   soning strategies, thereby degrading task performance. For example, in a problem involving speed
052   calculation (i.e., determining the rate of change of distance over time), text-based embeddings may
053   prioritize examples that focus on solving for time or distance due to their closer semantic similarity,
      as shown in Figure 1 (left). This misalignment steers the LLM away from the correct problem-

083   Extensive research indicates that graph-based representations, in contrast to text, align more closely
084   with human cognitive processes and are better equipped to model multi-step reasoning (Friston,
085   2008; Besta et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2023). Graphs enable the filtering of irrelevant shallow seman-
086   tic content while preserving the core reasoning structure, thus facilitating the development of an
087
      unbiased example retrieval mechanism. More critically, this representation offers a novel and inter-
      pretable approach for constructing example retrievers. When solving multi-step reasoning problems,
088
      the ideal examples are those whose reasoning processes can be directly applied to the query. In other
089
      words, after extracting the thought pattern from these examples, the same logic can be reused to solve
090   the query question and arrive at the correct answer. Graph structures provide an explicit means to
091   model these reasoning processes, enabling us to extract implicit thought patterns and assess their
092   transferability to new problems.
093
      More formally, a reasoning process can be modeled as a graph G with attributes (x1 , . . . , xn ),
094
      where each xi represents the attribute of vertex vi , corresponding to the thought at vi . We fur-
095
      ther assume that the probability density function governing the sequence of thoughts throughout the
096   reasoning process is given by p(x1 , . . . , xn ; G, W ), where W denotes the parameters of the under-
097   lying thought pattern. Given a query question q and its associated reasoning graph Gq , our goal
098   is to retrieve ICEs with parameterized thought patterns W i that maximize the probability density
099   p(xq1 , . . . , xqn ; Gq , W i ), i.e., maximizing the likelihood of solving q correctly. To achieve this, we
100   employ a Bayesian Network (BN) (Pearl, 2014), a type of probabilistic graphical model, to repre-
101   sent the multi-step reasoning process and to parameterize the thought patterns. The motivation for
102   using BN lies in their inherent similarity to human cognition; they assume that the value of each
103   node (thought) is influenced by the values of preceding nodes, mirroring the way new ideas are con-
104
      structed based on prior knowledge. This structure makes BNs particularly well-suited for capturing
      the dependencies and progression of reasoning steps.
105
106   In this paper, we introduce GraphIC, a Graph-based In-Context Example Retrieval Model. The key
107   idea is to leverage graph representations of reasoning processes to enhance the retrieval of ICEs.
      Specifically, we first prompt the LLM to generate “thought graphs” for both the candidate examples

108
      and the query question, where each graph encodes the reasoning steps involved. We then employ
109   a BN to model each thought graph of candidate examples and estimate the associated parameters
110   through maximum likelihood estimation, which we reformulate as a bilinear form maximization
111   problem with a closed-form solution. To better simulate human-like reasoning, we incorporate a
112   personalized PageRank (PPR) mechanism (Page, 1999), reflecting the cognitive tendency to re-
113   visit starting point during reasoning, a characteristic well-aligned with the assumptions behind PPR.
114   Once the thought graphs and their parameters are computed, we select examples whose parameters
115   maximize the probability density of thoughts on the query question’s thought graph. Note that we
116   leverage a graph model to aid ICE retrieval, not for direct problem-solving. The solution is driven
117
      by the LLM’s reasoning capabilities. This approach ensures that the retrieved examples, though not
      necessarily semantically similar to the query, align with the underlying reasoning process. As shown
118
      in Figure 1(right), GraphIC retrieves examples that, while not focusing on semantically similar tasks,
119
      share a common reasoning structure—for instance, solving for rates such as how the number of en-
120   velopes changes over time or how costs vary with the number of toys. This guides the LLM towards
121   the correct solution.

123
      We evaluate GraphIC against 10 baseline models on three multi-step reasoning tasks: mathemat-
      ical reasoning, code generation, and logical reasoning. Results show GraphIC excels in selecting
124
      relevant ICEs, surpassing existing methods in effectiveness and efficiency. Additionally, GraphIC
125
      shows faster performance improvements with more ICEs and exhibits asymmetry, mirroring real-
126   world reasoning. Key contributions are summarized as follows: 1) We introduce a novel graph-based
127   representation, the “thought graph”, to model multi-step reasoning processes. This approach effec-
128   tively filters out irrelevant shallow semantic information while preserving the essential reasoning
129   steps. 2) By leveraging BNs, we design a retrieval mechanism that maximizes the probability density
130   of solving the query problem, providing a more objective-driven and interpretable retrieval process.
131   3) Our experimental results indicate that GraphIC, despite being a training-free model, outperforms
132   both training-free and training-based models across various multi-step reasoning tasks.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
135   Existing ICE selection techniques can be classified as either training-free or training-based, depend-
136   ing on whether a retriever needs to be trained.
137
      Training-free approaches are generally divided into two types: (i) those that use heuristic criteria
138   such as similarity (Liu et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2022), diversity (Cho et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022b;
139   Levy et al., 2023; Hongjin et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023), complexity (Fu et al., 2022), or combi-
140   nations of these (Agrawal et al., 2023; Tonglet et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2023) to select in-context
141   examples (ICEs); (ii) those that leverage feedback from LLMs, such as probability distributions (Wu
142   et al., 2023; Nguyen & Wong, 2023; Li & Qiu, 2023; Yang et al., 2023), perplexity (Gonen et al.,
143   2023), or the model’s generated output (An et al., 2023) to guide the selection process. While
144   training-free approaches avoid the computational and time overhead associated with model train-
145   ing, their relatively simplistic architecture often results in sub-optimal performance compared to
146
      training-based methods.
147   Training-based methods are typically divided into two main categories. The first learns to select
148   individual examples and then extends this to k-shot scenarios (Rubin et al., 2022; Xiong et al., 2024;
149   Gupta et al., 2024). The second models the selection of a group of examples as a whole (Ye et al.,
150   2023; Wang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022a; Scarlatos & Lan, 2023; Lu et al., 2022; Peng et al.,
151   2023; Xu et al., 2024). While training-based approaches usually achieve superior performance, their
152
      reliance on repeated LLM queries and model training makes them both computationally intensive
      and time-consuming.
153
154   Our proposed GraphIC method is not only training-free and inherently efficient but also incorporates
155   an advanced graph-based example retriever specifically designed for multi-step reasoning tasks.
156   This sophisticated design enables GraphIC to achieve a significant performance advantage, even
157   surpassing training-based methods.

      4.1   T HOUGHT G RAPH AND ITS C ONSTRUCTION
220
221   We begin by introducing the concept of a thought graph and its construction, inspired by the hier-
222   archical structure of human cognition in problem-solving. The human thought process, especially
223   when addressing complex problems, can be naturally modeled as a graph-like structure (Friston,
224   2008; Besta et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2023). In this work, we present the “thought graph” as a struc-
225   ture for modeling the cognitive process of LLMs during multi-step reasoning tasks. Formally, a
226   thought graph G is represented as a vertex-attributed graph, where each vertex is associated with
227
      a natural language text, corresponding to the description of the operation performed or the inter-
      mediate conclusion reached at that step. To facilitate computation, we further represent the vertex
228
      attributes as the BERT embedding (Devlin et al., 2019) of corresponding text, denoted as xi .
229
230   Since LLMs are not natively equipped to output graph structures, we propose a methodology to gen-
231   erate these graphs from LLM outputs. As illustrated in Figure 2, we prompt the LLM to generate a
232   “formalized reasoning representation” (FRR), which is subsequently parsed to construct the thought
233
      graph. The detailed prompt and parser pseudo-code are provided in Appendix D.
234   After constructing the thought graph, a straightforward way to select in-context examples (ICEs) is
235   to compare the similarities between graph embeddings. To compute these embeddings, we employ
236   a widely adopted method where each graph embedding is generated through iterative aggregation of
237   node information, as outlined by Togninalli et al. (2019). Specifically, this process is formalized as:

254   framework for modeling the thought graphs. In this section, we outline the construction of BNs for

323   disparity makes the solution for W non-unique. Moreover, storing and computing a matrix of size
      nf × nf is computationally burdensome. To address both uniqueness and computational efficiency,

      5.1   DATASETS AND I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
345   We conduct a comprehensive evaluation of GraphIC model across four multi-step reasoning bench-
346   marks: two for mathematical reasoning (GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and AQUA (Ling et al.,
347   2017)), one for code generation (MBPP (Austin et al., 2021)), and one for logical reasoning
348   (ProofWriter (Tafjord et al., 2021)). For both GSM8K and MBPP, we utilize the original datasets
349
      without further preprocessing. For AQUA and ProofWriter, we refine the original dataset to improve
      the experimental setup, as detailed in Appendix A.
350
351   For GSM8K, AQUA, and ProofWriter, model performance is evaluated based on the accuracy of
352   the LLMs’ final answers. For MBPP, we adopt the pass@1 metric (Chen et al., 2021) to assess the
353   quality of code generation.
354   We employ GPT-4o-mini and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct as LLMs. Unless explicitly mentioned other-
355   wise, all evaluations are conducted under an 8-shot paradigm. We set the temperature to 1e-5. We
356   set iterations H in Equation 5 to 3, with λ values from {0, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3}, based on the LLM and
357   dataset (see Appendix D for details). For GSM8K, AQUA, and ProofWriter, we prompt the LLM
358   to create a formalized reasoning representation (FRR) for thought graph construction, using vertex
359   features from a BERT model. For MBPP, we use the staticfg module to parse Python code
360   and generate the control flow graph, embedding each vertex’s features with CodeBERT (Feng et al.,
361
      2020). Variable names in the code are anonymized with arbitrary symbols like ‘a’, ‘b’, and ‘c’.
362   5.2   BASELINES
363
      Our model, GraphIC, is designed as a training-free retriever for ICE selection. We compare GraphIC
364
      against six training-free retrieval methods spanning random, similarity-based, diversity-based, and
365
      complexity-based approaches, including: 1) Random randomly selects k unique ICEs from the
366   candidate set; 2) BM25 (Robertson et al., 2009) selects the top k examples based on BM25 scoring;
367   3) BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) is a dense retriever using cosine similarity with BERT-base-uncased
368   embeddings; 4) Complex-CoT (Fu et al., 2022) selects k examples based on complexity, quantified
369   by newline characters; 5) Auto-CoT (Zhang et al., 2022b) clusters candidates and selects the closests
370   to each cluster center; and 6) Skill-kNN (An et al., 2023) prompts LLM to generate task-relevant
371   skills for query and candidates, followed by dense retrieval. Since Skill-kNN does not natively sup-
372   port datasets like GSM8K, we manually craft the instructions and examples, detailed in Appendix C.
373   We also compare with four training-based retrievers, which encompass both single-example and
374   combination-example retrieval strategies, including: 1) EPR (Rubin et al., 2022) is trained to
375   retrieve the single most relevant ICE, with top k examples being selected during inference;
376   2) CEIL (Ye et al., 2023) uses determinantal point processes to select ICEs balancing similarity
377   and diversity, where three CEIL models per dataset with scaling factors of 0.01, 0.05, and 0.1 are
      trained and the best results are reported; 3) DQ-LoRe (Xiong et al., 2024) uses dual queries and


                                                         7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      low-rank approximation re-ranking to identify ICEs; and 4) GistScore (Gupta et al., 2024) encodes
379   task-specific information into gist tokens for selecting ICEs. Following Skill-kNN, We use GPT-J-
380   6B (Wang & Komatsuzaki, 2021) as the scoring LLM for EPR, CEIL, and DR-LoRe.

      Assumption of Symmetry. Our findings show that the GraphIC model uses an “asymmetric” ap-
      proach, unlike the common symmetry assumption in most baseline models. To assess the validity of
509
      this assumption, we conduct an experiment examining whether symmetry, in the context of retrieval
510
      models, holds. Here, a retrieval model is considered symmetric if score(i, j) = score(j, i), where
511   score(i, j) represents the model’s assessment of example i as an ICE for example j. For example, the
512   Skill-kNN is symmetric as it uses inner product embeddings for score(j, i), while the Complex-CoT
513   model is asymmetric, calculating score(j, i) based on the complexity of example i.
514
      We randomly select 10 examples from the GSM8K candidate set and use the top-7 performing
515
      models to compute the score matrix S (Sij = score(i, j)), which we then compare against the
516                                     gt
      ground truth matrix S gt . Here, Sij captures the probability that Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct provides the
517
      correct answer when example i is used as an ICE for example j.
518
519   The experimental results show that the ground truth matrix (Figure 5 (a)) is asymmetric, undermin-
520   ing the symmetry assumption. Using symmetric models for inherently asymmetric data introduces
521   significant errors. For instance, the EPR model, trained on correct answer probabilities, struggles
522
      with accuracy due to its symmetry reliance (Figure 5 (f)). In contrast, simple asymmetric methods
      like Complex-CoT (Figure 5 (b)) and BM25 (Figure 5 (d)) perform well, ranking second and fourth
523
      as Table 1 shows, and surpassing many symmetric models. However, their simplistic assumptions
524
      limit their ability to capture ground truth nuances. In contrast, GraphIC (Figure 5 (h)), a sophisti-
525   cated asymmetric model, aligns closely with the ground truth, resulting in superior performance.

527   6          C ONCLUSION
528
529   We introduce GraphIC, a graph-based method for in-context example (ICE) retrieval aimed at en-
530
      hancing LLM performance on multi-step reasoning tasks. By modeling reasoning as “thought
      graphs” and utilizing Bayesian Networks and personalized PageRank, GraphIC selects ICEs that
531
      align with the task’s cognitive structure, overcoming the limitations of text-based embedding meth-
532
      ods. Extensive experiments on four benchmarks show that GraphIC consistently outperforms both
533   training-free and training-based baselines, especially in mathematical and logical reasoning. Our
534   analysis of symmetry assumptions highlights the advantage of asymmetric retrieval models. A limi-
535   tation of the GraphIC model is that, as a training-free framework, it may face difficulties in capturing
536   more intricate thought patterns. Beyond this, GraphIC not only introduces a powerful ICEs retrieval
537   method, but more crucially, it provides a way to represent and understand the reasoning process. This
538   capability can be applied to various domains related to LLM reasoning, such as developing novel
539   graph-based reasoning methods, selecting high-quality and diverse training datasets, and more.

1488   On the refrigerator, MATHEMATICS is spelled out with $11$ magnets, one letter per magnet. Two
            vowels and four consonants fall off and are put away in a bag. If the T’s, M’s, and A’s
1489        are indistinguishable, how many distinct possible collections of letters could be put in
1490        the bag?
1491   Answer:
1492   We count the number of ways to choose the vowels and the consonants separately. There are four
             vowels, of which two are As. If there are no As, then we must choose both the remaining
1493        vowels, so there is $1$ choice; if there is one A, then we can choose the remaining vowel
1494         in $2$ ways; and if there are two As, then there are no vowels left to choose, so there
            is $1$ choice. This makes $1 + 2 + 1 = 4$ distinct pairs of vowels.
1495
1496   There are seven consonants, of which two are Ts and of which two are Ms. Since we must choose
            four consonants, we must use at least one of the Ts and Ms.
1497
1498   If we use one T and no Ms, we have only $1$ choice (use the three remaining consonants); the
            same is true if we use one M and no Ts.
1499   If we use both Ts and no Ms, there are $\tbinom{3}{2} = 3$ choices for the two remaining
1500        consonants; the same is true if we use both Ms and no Ts, or if we use one T and one M.
       If we use both Ts and one M, there are $\tbinom{3}{1} = 3$ choices for the single remaining
1501        consonant; the same is true if we use both Ms and one T.
1502   Finally, if we use both Ts and both Ms, there are no more letters left to choose, so we get
            $1$ more choice.
1503
1504   In total, we have $2(1) + 5(3) + 1 = 18$ distinct collections of consonants.
1505   Therefore, the number of distinct collections of letters is $4 \cdot 18 = \boxed{72}.$

1600   Given that $m$ and $n$ are positive integers such that $m\equiv 6\pmod 9$ and $n\equiv 0\pmod
            9$, what is the largest integer that $mn$ is necessarily divisible by?
1601
1602   Answer:
       If $m\equiv 6\pmod 9$, then we can write $m$ as $9a+6$ for some integer $a$. This is equal to
1603        $3(3a+2)$, so $m$ is certainly divisible by $3$. If $n\equiv 0\pmod 9$, then $n$ is
1604        divisible by $9$. Therefore, $mn$ must be divisible by $3\cdot 9 = 27$.
1605   Note that $m$ can be 6 and $n$ can be 9, which gives us $mn = 54$. Also, $m$ can be 15 and $n$
1606         can be 9, which gives us $mn = 135$. The gcd of 54 and 135 is 27.
1607   Therefore, the largest integer that $mn$ must be divisible by is $\boxed{27}$.
