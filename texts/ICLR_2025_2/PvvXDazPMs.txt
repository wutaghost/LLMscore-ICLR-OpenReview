                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Recent large visual-language action models pretrained on diverse robot datasets
010
               have demonstrated the potential for generalizing to new environments with a few
011            in-domain data. However, those approaches usually predict discretized or contin-
012            uous actions by a small action head, which limits the ability in handling diverse
013            action spaces. In contrast, we model the continuous action with a large multi-
014            modal diffusion transformer, dubbed as Diffusion Transformer Policy, in which
015            we directly denoise action chunks by a large transformer model rather than a small
016            action head. By leveraging the scaling capability of transformers, the proposed ap-
017            proach can effectively model continuous end-effector actions across large diverse
018            robot datasets, and achieve better generalization performance. Extensive experi-
019
               ments demonstrate Diffusion Transformer Policy pretrained on diverse robot data
               can generalize to different embodiments, including simulation environments like
020
               Maniskill2 and Calvin, as well as the real-world Franka arm. Specifically, without
021
               bells and whistles, the proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art performance
022            in the Calvin novel task setting, and the pretraining stage significantly facilitates
023            the success sequence length on the Calvin by over 1.2. The code will be publicly
024            available.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Traditional robot learning paradigm usually relies on large-scale data collected for a specific robot
030   and task, but collecting robot data for generalist tasks is time-consuming and expensive due to the
031   limitations of robot hardware in the real world. Nowadays, the foundational models OpenAI (2022;
032   2023; 2021); Rombach et al. (2021) in Natural Language Process and Computer Vision, pretrained
033
      on broad, diverse, task-agnostic datasets, have demonstrated powerful ability in solving downstream
      tasks either zero-shot or with a few task-specific samples. It is principally possible that a general
034
      robot policy exposed to large scale diverse robot datasets improves generalization and performance
035
      on downstream tasks Brohan et al. (2022; 2023). However, it is challenging to train a general robot
036   policy on a large scale of cross-embodiment datasets with diverse sensors, action spaces, tasks,
037   camera views, and environments.
038
039
      Toward a unified robot policy, existing works directly map visual observation and language instruc-
      tions to actions with large visual-language-action models for robot navigation Shah et al. (2023a;b)
040
      or manipulation Brohan et al. (2022; 2023); Kim et al. (2024); Team et al. (2024), and demonstrate
041
      zero-shot or few-shot generation to new environments. Robot Transformers Brohan et al. (2022;
042   2023); Padalkar et al. (2023) present robot policy based on transformer architecture, and demon-
043   strate robust generalization by training on the large scale of Open X-Embodiment Dataset Padalkar
044   et al. (2023). Octo Team et al. (2024) follows the autoregressive transformer architecture with a
045   diffusion action head, while OpenVLA Kim et al. (2024) discretizes the action space and lever-
046   age the pretrained visual-language model to build VLA model exposed to Open X-Embodiment
047   Dataset Padalkar et al. (2023). Though those Visual-Language-Action (VLA) models Team et al.
048   (2024); Kim et al. (2024) have shown the potential to learn robot policy from the large cross em-
049   bodiment datasets Padalkar et al. (2023), the diversity of robot space among the cross embodiment
050
      datasets still limits the generalization of previous models with autoregressive transformers.
051   Recent diffusion policy Chi et al. (2023); Ze et al. (2024); Ke et al. (2024) has shown its stable ability
052   in robot policy learning for single task immitation learning with UNet or cross attention architecture,
053   and diffusion transformer demonstrates its scalability in multi-modal image generation Peebles &
      Xie (2023). Specifically, Octo Team et al. (2024) presents a generalist policy that denoises the

      Similar to previous robot transformer models Brohan et al. (2022; 2023); Padalkar et al. (2023);
077   Team et al. (2024); Kim et al. (2024), we leverage the transformer as our base module to retain
078   the scalability on the large-scale cross-embodiment datasets. Different from Brohan et al. (2022;

081   MLP to denoise action embedding to continuous actions as illustrated in Figure 1. The Diffusion
082   Transformer Policy retains the scalability of transformer for diffusion, and thus more effectively

086   ers. Extensive experiments demonstrate Diffusion Transformer Policy achieves considerably better
087   performance on two large-scale Sim datasets, Maniskill2 and Calvin, compared to corresponding
088   diffusion action head Team et al. (2024) and discretized action head Brohan et al. (2022) baselines.

      Diffusion Policy Denoise diffusion techniques Ho et al. (2020); Rombach et al. (2022) are pioneer-
095
      ing image generation, and recent Diffusion Policy Chi et al. (2023); Ze et al. (2024); Ke et al. (2024)
096   has exhibited a powerful ability in modeling multimodal actions compared to previous robot policy
097   strategies in both 2D Chi et al. (2023) and 3D observations Ze et al. (2024); Ke et al. (2024). Current
098   diffusion policy approaches usually follow an Unet structure or a shallow cross-attention network
099   for a single manipulation task, leaving large-scale multimodal diffusion policy poorly investigated.
100   For example, 3D diffusion Policy Ze et al. (2024) presents a diffusion approach conditional on a
101   3D point cloud, while 3D diffuser actor Ke et al. (2024) proposes a 3D diffusion strategy based on
102   point cloud with cross-attention. Differently, we present a scalable in-context conditioning diffu-
103   sion transformer architecture for generalist robot policy. Recent generalist policy Octo Team et al.
104
      (2024) conditions the denoise process on the embedding from the Transformer model with a small
      MLP diffuser. By contrast, the diffuser in Diffusion Transformer Policy is a large Transformer
105
      architecture.

      robot policy with foundational multi-modal models for both robot navigation Shah et al. (2023a;b);

122   3     M ETHOD
123
124   We describe the proposed architecture of diffusion transformer policy in this section, a DiT-based
125   generalist diffusion policy model that can be adapted to new environments and embodiment. The
126   model, built from a diffusion transformer architecture, achieves better generalization on both novel
127
      camera views and environments from large amounts of diverse robot data.
128
129   3.1   A RCHITECTURE
130
131
      Instruction Tokenization. The language instructions are tokenized by a frozon CLIP Radford et al.
      (2021) model.
132
133   Image observation Tokenization. The image observations first pass into the DINOv2 Oquab et al.
134   (2023) to obtain the image tokens. Note that DINOv2 Oquab et al. (2023) is trained on the web data
135   which is different from the robot data, we thus jointly optimize the DINOv2 parameters together
136   with Transformers through an end-to-end way.
137   Q-Former. To reduce the computation cost, a Q-Former together with FiLM Perez et al. (2018)
138   conditioning is incorporated to select image tokens from the features of DIDOv2 Oquab et al. (2023)
139   by instruction context.
140
      Action Tokenization. We use the end-effector action and represent each action with a 7D vector,
141   including 3 dimensions for translation vector, 3 dimensions for rotation vector, and a dimension for
142   gripper position. To align the dimension with image and language tokens, we simply pad the action
143   vector with zeros to construct the action token. We only add the noise into the 7D action vector
144   during denoise diffusion optimization.
145
      Architecture. Our core design is the Diffusion Transformer structure Peebles & Xie (2023) which
146
      denoises action token chunks, instead of each single action token, conditioned on image observation
147   and instruction tokens by an in-context conditioning style with a causal transformer network, i.e., we
148   simply concatenate image tokens, language tokens, and timestep token in the front of the sequence,
149   equally treating the noisy action tokens from the image/instruction tokens as illustrated in Figure 2.
150   This design retains the scaling properties of transformer networks. The model, conditioned on lan-
151   guage instructions and image observations with the causal transformer structure, is supervised by
152   the noise that we add to the continuous actions. In other words, we conduct the diffusion objective
153   directly in the action chunk space with a large transformer model, differently from a diffusion action
154   head with a few MLP layers Team et al. (2024).
155   The proposed Diffusion Transformer Policy is a general design that can be scaled to different
156   datasets, and demonstrate excellent performance. Meanwhile, we can also add additional obser-
157   vation tokens and input into the transformer structure. Appendix A provides more details.
158

185
186   We devise the proposed Diffusion Transformer architecture and evaluate the pretraining approach in
187   the large cross-embodiment datasets Padalkar et al. (2023). We use the DDPM Ho et al. (2020) diffu-
188   sion objective in the pretraining stage with T = 1000 for the Open X-Embodiment dataset Padalkar

      4.3   CALVIN
300
301   CALVIN (Composing Actions from Language and Vision) Mees et al. (2022) is an open-source
302   simulated benchmark to learn long-horizon language-conditioned tasks. CALVIN Mees et al. (2022)
303   includes four different scenes tagged as ABCD and presents a novel scene evaluation benchmark,
304   ABC→D, i.e., trained on environments A, B, and C and evaluated on environment D. The goal of
305   CALVIN is to solve up to 1000 unique sequence chains with 5 distinct subtasks. The benchmark
306   requires successfully solving the task sequence with 5 continuous subtasks, and one of the important
307   evaluation indicators is the success sequence length.
308   Setup. In this section, we utilize CALVIN (ABC→D) to evaluate the novel task generalization of
309   Diffusion Transformer Policy architecture. Specifically, we directly apply the proposed method on
310   CALVIN with a single static RGB camera and predict the end-effector action, including 3 dimen-
311   sions for translation, 3 dimensions for Euler angles rotation and 1 dimension for gripper pose. We
312   evaluate Diffusion Transformer Policy and Diffusion Action Head Team et al. (2024) on CALVIN,
313   and leverage the pretrained model on Open X-Embodiment to initialize the model for CALVIN.
314   Optimization Details. While training Calvin, 2 history images are used as input. For each iteration,
315   the model predicts 10 future frames supervised by MSE loss. An AdamW optimizer is used together
316   with a decayed learning rate with half-cycle cosine scheduler after several steps of warming up. The
317   learning rate is initialized as 1e-4. We use 4 NVIDIA A100 GPUs(80GB) to train the model for 15
318   epochs with a global batch size of 128.

526   5              C ONCLUSION , D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
527
528   In this paper, we present a Diffusion Transformer architecture for generalist robot learning, named as
529   Diffusion Transformer Policy. Diffusion Transformer Policy directly utilizes the large transformers
530
      as a denoising network to denoise the continuous actions conditioned on language instruction and
      image observations. The proposed architecture retains the scale attribute of the transformer, thus
531
      is capable of generalizing to different datasets with a unified architecture. Extensive experiments
532
      on Maniskill2, CALVIN, real Frank Arm demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
533   Particularly, the proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art performance in CALVIN (ABC→D)
534   with only a single observation.
535
536
      A limitation of the Diffusion Transformer Policy is that it requires multiple denoising steps during
      inference, which will impede the inference speed in the real application. In this paper, we focus
537
      on the modeling of complex and diverse actions. We think it is possible to improve the finetuning
538
      strategy with a few denoising steps to accelerate the inference speed. Meanwhile, the failure cases
539   indicate it is important for the model to plan a right trajectory and grasp pose for picking object.
