009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                Recent generative models have demonstrated impressive capabilities in generating
012                realistic and visually pleasing images grounded on textual prompts. Nevertheless,
013                a significant challenge remains in applying these models for the more intricate
014                task of story visualization. Since it requires resolving pronouns (he, she, they) in
015                the frame descriptions, i.e., anaphora resolution, and ensuring consistent charac-
016                ters and background synthesis across frames. Yet, the emerging Large Language
017
                   Model (LLM) showcases robust reasoning abilities to navigate through ambiguous
                   references and process extensive sequences. Therefore, we introduce StoryGPT-V,
018
                   which leverages the merits of the latent diffusion (LDM) and LLM to produce im-
019
                   ages with consistent and high-quality characters grounded on given story descrip-
020                tions. First, we train a character-aware LDM, which takes character-augmented
021                semantic embedding as input and includes the supervision of the cross-attention
022                map using character segmentation masks, aiming to enhance character generation
023                accuracy and faithfulness. In the second stage, we enable an alignment between
024                the output of LLM and the character-augmented embedding residing in the input
025                space of the first-stage model. This harnesses the reasoning ability of LLM to
026                address ambiguous references and the comprehension capability to memorize the
027                context. We conduct comprehensive experiments on two visual story visualization
028
                   benchmarks. Our model reports superior quantitative results and consistently gen-
                   erates accurate characters of remarkable quality with low memory consumption.
029
                   Our code will be made publicly available1 .

032   1       I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Image generation algorithms have made significant strides and are on the verge of matching human-
035   level proficiency. Despite this progress, even a powerful image generator suffers from story visual-
036   ization task, which involves generating a series of frames that maintain semantic coherence based on
037   narrative descriptions (Li et al., 2019; Zeng et al., 2019; Maharana et al., 2021; 2022). This challenge
038   arises from the fact that captions for a single image are typically self-sufficient, lacking the conti-
039   nuity needed to capture the narrative of object interactions that unfold through multiple sentences
040   over a sequence of frames. This poses a promising avenue for further research and exploration of
041   story visualization. Such a task demands a model capable of producing high-quality characters and
042
      detailed environmental objects grounded on given text descriptions. Moreover, it requires the ability
      to disambiguate referential pronouns in the subsequent frame descriptions, e.g., “she, he, they”.
043
044   Prior studies (Maharana & Bansal, 2021; Li et al., 2019; Maharana et al., 2022; Song et al., 2020b;
045   Chen et al., 2022a) explore the realm of story visualization but do not take reference resolution (Seo
046   et al., 2017) (i.e., anaphora resolution in the context of natural language processing (Aone &
047   William, 1995; McCarthy & Lehnert, 1995)) into consideration. Story-LDM (Rahman et al., 2023)
048
      first extended story visualization benchmarks with referential text and devises an attention mem-
      ory module that retains visual context throughout the series of generated frames. However, it still
049
      struggles to generate precise characters for referential text since the interaction between current
050
      descriptions and contextual information occurs within the CLIP (Radford et al., 2021b) semantic
051   space, causing a loss in fine-grained language understanding and hindering referencing capabili-
052   ties. Furthermore, the attention memory module requires maintaining all previous images in latent

079   The limitation of previous works leads us to rethink how to achieve accurate and efficient reference
080   resolution toward consistent story visualization. Large Language Models (LLMs) (Radford et al.,
081   2019; Raffel et al., 2020; Brown et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022), trained on extensive text corpora,
082   have exhibited impressive capabilities in deciphering contextual references in natural language de-
083
      scriptions. Prior works (Koh et al., 2023a; Ge et al., 2023) have demonstrated the effectiveness of
      harnessing LLMs for tasks involving image comprehension and generation, where the visual fea-
084
      tures are adapted within LLM’s token space rather than the pixel space. Hence, such a model could
085
      be utilized to efficiently address ambiguous references for story visualization tasks.
086
087   In this work, we aim at story visualization grounded on given co-referential frame descriptions, fo-
088   cusing on delivering high-quality and coherent portrayals of characters. To achieve this, we leverage
089
      a powerful text-to-image model (Rombach et al., 2022) to generate high-quality characters and en-
      vironmental objects grounded on given frame descriptions, coupled with the reasoning ability of
090
      Large Language Models (LLMs) to resolve ambiguous references and improve the cohesiveness of
091
      the context. To improve the generation of highly faithful characters, we enhance the pre-trained
092   Latent Diffusion (LDM) towards character-aware training in the first stage. We first augment the to-
093   ken feature by incorporating the visual representation of the corresponding character. Additionally,
094   we regulate the cross-attention map of the character token to highlight the interaction between the
095   conditional token and specific latent pixels.
096
      In addressing the challenge of ambiguous reference, which cannot be effectively handled by a ro-
097
      bust text-to-image model alone, we leverage an LLM that takes interleaved images and co-referential
098   frame descriptions as input, and aligns its visual output with the character-augmented embedding
099   encoded by first-stage model. Such semantic guidance, along with LLM’s casual modeling, en-
100   ables effective reference resolution and consistent generation. Furthermore, our approach efficiently
101   preserves context by processing images as sequences of tokens in the LLM input space with low
102   memory consumption.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
119
120
      Text-to-image synthesis. Numerous works (Crowson et al., 2022; Gafni et al., 2022; Ding et al.,
      2021) have demonstrated unprecedented performance on semantic generation. Recently, diffusion-
121
      based text-to-image models (Ramesh et al., 2022; 2021; Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022)
122
      have shown significant advancements in enhancing image quality and diversity through the utiliza-
123   tion of diffusion models. However, these text-to-image approaches primarily concentrate on aligning
124   individual-generated images grounded on text descriptions and do not take into account the crucial
125   aspects of character and scene consistency across multiple frames in the story visualization task.
126   Additionally, they lack the capability to effectively resolve co-reference issues within a narrative
127   description.
128
      Multi-modal Large Language Models. Large Language Models (LLMs) wield an extensive repos-
129   itory of human knowledge and exhibit impressive reasoning capabilities. Recent studies (Tsim-
130   poukelli et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022b; Alayrac et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b) utilize pre-trained
131   language models to tackle vision-language tasks, and subsequent studies (Zhu et al., 2023; Zhang
132   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a; Huang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023) further en-
133   hance multi-modal abilities by aligning vision models with LLM input space. In addition to multi-
134   modal comprehension, several works are dedicated to more challenging multi-modal generation
135   tasks. FROMAGe (Koh et al., 2023b) appends a special retrieval token to LLM and maps the hidden
136   representation of this token into a vector space for retrieving images. Several current works (Koh
137
      et al., 2023a; Wu et al., 2023; Zeqiang et al., 2023) learn a mapping from hidden embeddings of
      an LLM represents for additional visual outputs into the input space of a frozen pre-trained text-
138
      to-image generation model (Rombach et al., 2022). In this work, we fed multi-modal LLM with
139
      interleaved image and referential text descriptions as input and aligned the output with a character-
140   aware fused embedding from our first-stage Char-LDM, guiding the LLM in implicitly deducing the
141   references.
142
143
      Story Visualization. StoryGAN (Li et al., 2019) pioneers the story generation task, which proposes
      a sequential conditional GAN framework with dual frame and story level discriminators to im-
144
      prove image quality and narrative coherence. DuCoStoryGAN (Maharana et al., 2021) introduces a
145
      dual-learning framework that utilizes video captioning to enhance semantic alignment between de-
146   scriptions and generated images. VLCStoryGAN (Maharana & Bansal, 2021) used video captioning
147   for semantic alignment between text and frames. Recently, StoryDALL-E (Maharana et al., 2022)
148   retrofits the cross-attention layers of the pre-trained text-to-image model to promote generalizabil-
149   ity to unseen visual attributes of the generated story. These methods do not consider ambiguous
150   references in text descriptions. Story-LDM (Rahman et al., 2023) first introduced reference reso-
151   lution in story visualization tasks and proposed an autoregressive diffusion-based framework with
152   a memory-attention module to resolve ambiguous references. Nevertheless, it struggled with accu-
153   rately resolving references and was memory-intensive, as it required retaining all previous context in
154
      pixel space. In our work, we employ a powerful causal inference LLM for reference resolution, and
      it efficiently maintains context by mapping visual features into several token embeddings as LLM
155
      inputs rather than operating in latent pixel space.

      3.2   C HARACTER - AWARE LDM WITH ATTENTION CONTROL
217
218   Integrate visual features with text conditions. To achieve accurate and high-quality characters in
219   story visualization, we augment text descriptions with visual features of corresponding characters
220   and guide the attention of text conditions to focus more on corresponding character synthesis. Given
221   a text description S, suppose there are K characters that should be generated in image I, images of
222   those characters {Ic1 , ..., IcK }, a list of token indices indicating each character name located in the
223   description, denoted as {i1c , ...iKc }. Inspired by (Wei et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023),
224
      we first utilize CLIP (Radford et al., 2021b) text encoder ψ and image encoder ϕ to obtain text
      embedding and visual features of the characters appear in the image respectively. Then, we augment
225
      the text embedding if the token represents a character name. More specifically, we concatenate
226
      the token embedding and the visual features of the corresponding character and feed them into an
227   MLP to obtain the augmented text embedding. Each augmented token embedding in the augmented
228   embedding c is formulated as below:

      Our first stage Char-LDM focuses solely on the quality of image generation grounded on a sin-
      gle caption. Yet, there remain challenges that surpass the abilities of text-to-image generators in
260
      visualizing a sequence of stories. Firstly, story visualization demands character and background
261
      consistency, an aspect not covered by our first-stage enhancements. Moreover, the inherent nature
262   of lengthy descriptions includes referential terms like he, she, or they, which presents a significant
263   challenge for LDM in achieving accurate inference. In contrast, LLMs can adeptly infer the in-
264   tended character to which the ambiguous text refers. Therefore, to address this issue, we harness the
265   formidable reasoning capabilities of LLM to disambiguate such references.

269   To enable an LLM to autoregressively generate images conditioned on prior context and resolve

      Evaluation metrics. To measure the accuracy of the characters and background in the generated
349   stories, we consider the following evaluation metrics the same as previous story visualization litera-
350   ture (Maharana & Bansal, 2021; Maharana et al., 2022; Rahman et al., 2023): Following (Maharana
351   & Bansal, 2021), we finetune Inception-v3 to measure the classification accuracy and F1-score of
352   characters (Char-Acc, Char-F1) and background (BG-Acc, BG-F1) respectively. In addition, we
353   consider the Frechet Inception Distance (FID) score, which compares the distribution between fea-
354   ture vectors from real and generated images for quality assessment.
355   When assessing text-image alignment, the CLIP (Radford et al., 2021b) score falls short in reliabil-
356   ity since it cannot capture fine-grained details. Therefore we choose the powerful captioning model
357   BLIP2 (Li et al., 2023b) as the evaluation model and fine-tune it on the corresponding datasets. We
358   then employ it as a captioner to predict 5 captions for generated images and 5 captions for ground
359   truth images as a comparison to report the average BLEU4 (Papineni et al., 2002) and CIDEr (Vedan-
360   tam et al., 2015) score to assess text-image alignment.
361   Comparison Approaches. We compare our model with state-of-the-art approaches: VLCStory-
362   GAN (Maharana & Bansal, 2021), StoryDALL-E (Maharana et al., 2022), LDM (Rombach et al.,
363   2022) and Story-LDM (Rahman et al., 2023). Following previous research (Li et al., 2019; Rah-
364   man et al., 2023), we use 4 consecutive frames for evaluation. For StoryDALL-E (Maharana et al.,
365   2022), which takes both story descriptions and the initial frame as input, we use the first frame of
366   a 5-frame story and evaluate using the generated 4 frames. We finetune vanilla Stable Diffusion
367   (LDM) on FlintStonesSV (Gupta et al., 2018) and PororoSV (Li et al., 2019) as a baseline. Since
368   Story-LDM (Rahman et al., 2023) does not provide pre-trained checkpoint or cleaned training code,
369
      we initiate training from pre-trained LDM2 .
370   Implementation Details. For the first stage training, we freeze CLIP (Radford et al., 2021b) text
371   encoder and fine-tune the remaining modules for 25k steps with a learning rate 1e-5 and batch size
372   of 32 on original non-referential text. To enhance inference time robustness and flexibility, we adopt
373   a training strategy that includes 10% unconditional training, i.e., classifier-free guidance (Ho &
374   Salimans, 2022), 10% text-only training, and 80% character-augmented fuse training (section 3.2).
375
      We use the original loss of latent diffusion and Lreg (eq. (2)) loss for the first stage training.

504   Our model could generate longer stories featuring accurate characters, at a faster speed and with
505   lower computational consumption. Our architecture allows our model to retain an extensive con-
506   text requiring minimal computational resources by efficiently mapping visual features into tokens
507   instead of operating in pixel space. fig. 6 shows the comparison between our model and Story-

      LDM (Rahman et al., 2023) w.r.t GPU memory consumption and inference speed for longer-frames

510
      A100) when generating 42 frames. This is because Story-LDM (Rahman et al., 2023) requires the
511   retention of the entire context, e.g., n frames in latent pixel space (n × h × w × d), whereas our

515   mance of Story-LDM (Rahman et al., 2023) significantly decreases when generating longer stories
516   and reaches the memory limit before 50 frames. In contrast, by utilizing the capacity of LLM to
517   retain extensive context, our model upholds accurate character consistency in visualizing lengthy

      5     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we aim at high-quality and consistent character synthesis for story visualization
      grounded on co-referential text descriptions. To accomplish this, we utilize the strengths of the
532
      LDM for generating high-quality images, combined with the reasoning capability of LLM to com-
533
      prehend extended contexts, resolve ambiguities, and ensure semantic consistency in the generation
534   process. We first finetune LDM by guiding the cross-attention map of LDM with character seg-
535   mentation masks, which improves the accuracy and faithfulness of character generation. Next, we
536   facilitate a mapping from the output of LLM to align with the input space of the first stage LDM,
537   thus allowing Multi-modal LLM to both process and produce images. This process leverages the
538   LLM’s logical reasoning to clarify ambiguous references and its capacity to retain contextual infor-
539   mation. Our model reports superior quantitative results and consistently generates characters with
      remarkable quality.

739   A    M ULTI - MODAL S TORY G ENERATION
740
741   Owing to StoryGPT-V design leveraging the advanced capabilities of Large Language Models
742   (LLMs), it exhibits a unique proficiency in that it can extend visual stories. StoryGPT-V is not
743   merely limited to visualizing stories based on provided textual descriptions. Unlike existing models,
744   it also possesses the innovative capacity to extend these narratives through continuous text gener-
745   ation. Concurrently, it progressively synthesizes images that align with the newly generated text
746
      segments.
747   Figure 4 presents an example of a multi-modal story generation. Initially, the first four frames are
748   created according to the text descriptions from the FlintstonesSV (Gupta et al., 2018) dataset (gray
749   part). Subsequently, the model proceeds to write the description for the next frame (blue part), taking
750   into account the captions provided earlier, and then creates a frame based on this new description
751   (blue part). This method is employed iteratively to generate successive text descriptions and their
752
      corresponding frames.
753   Our model represents a notable advancement in story visualization, being the first of its kind to
754   consistently produce both high-quality images and coherent narrative descriptions. This innovation
755   opens avenues for AI-assisted technologies to accelerate visual storytelling creation experiences by
      exploring various visualized plot extensions as the story builds.

852
      benchmark. Therefore, we finetune CLIP (Radford et al., 2021b) on the story visualization datasets.

853
      or image-image similarity (I-I), i.e., the similarity between visual features of generated images and
854
      corresponding ground truth images.
855

857   evaluation model. We finetune BLIP2 on FlintstonesSV (Gupta et al., 2018) and PororoSV (Li
858
      et al., 2019), respectively, and employ it as an image captioner for generated visual stories. We
      avoided direct comparisons to bridge the gap between BLIP2’s predictions and the actual ground
859
      truth captions. Instead, we used the fine-tuned BLIP2 to generate five captions for each ground truth

962
963   We built upon pre-trained Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) v1-57 and use CLIP (Radford

967
      strategy that includes 10% unconditional training, i.e., classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans,
968   2022), 10% text-only training, and 80% augmented text training, which integrates visual features of
969   characters with their corresponding token embeddings.

       E        L IMITATIONS
1005
1006   Our method demonstrates proficiency in resolving references and ensuring consistent character and
1007   background conditions in the context provided by guiding the output of a multi-modal Large Lan-
1008   guage Model (LLM) with character-augmented semantic embedding. However, several limitations
1009   remain. The process involves feeding the previously generated frame into the LLM to produce a
1010   visual output that aligns with the Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) input conditional space. This ap-
1011   proach guarantees semantic consistency, enabling the generation of characters and environmental
1012   objects that resemble their originals. Nonetheless, there are minor discrepancies in detail. This is
1013   because the visual output from the Large Language Model (LLM) is aligned with the semantic em-
1014
       bedding space rather than the pixel space, which hinders the complete reconstruction of all elements
       in the input image. However, the current most powerful multi-modal LLM, i.e., DALL-E 3 (Ope-
1015
       nAI, 2023), could not solve this exact appearance replication in the multi-round image generation
1016
       task (Figure 8), indicating an area ripe for further exploration and research.
