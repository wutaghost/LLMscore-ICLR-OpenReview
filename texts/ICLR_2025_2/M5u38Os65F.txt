009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Matrix-stepsized gradient descent algorithms have been shown to have superior
012            performance in non-convex optimization problems compared to their scalar coun-
013            terparts. The det-CGD algorithm, as introduced by Li et al. (2024b), leverages
014            matrix stepsizes to perform compressed gradient descent for non-convex objec-
015            tives and matrix-smooth problems in a federated manner. The authors establish
016            the algorithm’s convergence to a neighborhood of a weighted stationarity point un-
017            der a convex condition for the symmetric and positive-definite matrix stepsize. In
018
               this paper, we propose two variance-reduced versions of the det-CGD algorithm,
               incorporating MARINA and DASHA methods. Notably, we establish theoreti-
019
               cally and empirically, that det-MARINA and det-DASHA outperform MARINA,
020
               DASHA and the distributed det-CGD algorithms in terms of iteration and com-
021            munication complexities.

032   In this context, each function fi : Rd → R is differentiable and bounded from below. This type
033   of objective function finds extensive application in various practical machine learning algorithms,
034   which increase not only in terms of the data size but also in the model size and overall complexity
035   as well. As a result, most neural network architectures result in highly non-convex empirical losses,
036
      which need to be minimized. In addition, it becomes computationally infeasible to train these mod-
      els on one device, often excessively large, and one needs to redistribute them amongst different
037
      devices/clients. This redistribution results in a high communication overhead, which often becomes
038
      the bottleneck in this framework.
039
040   In other words, we have the following setting. The data is partitioned into n clients, where the i-
041   th client has access to the component function fi and its derivatives. The clients are connected to
042
      each other through a central device, called the server. In this work, we are going to study iterative
      gradient descent-based algorithms that operate as follows. The clients compute the local gradients in
043
      parallel. Then they compress these gradients to reduce the communication cost and send them to the
044
      server in parallel. The server then aggregates these vectors and broadcasts the iterate update back to
045   the clients. This meta-algorithm is called federated learning. We refer the readers to Konečný et al.
046   (2016); McMahan et al. (2017); Kairouz et al. (2021) for a more thorough introduction to federated
047   learning.

053   aims to solve problem (1) using matrix stepsized gradient descent. Under the matrix smoothness
      assumption proposed by Safaryan et al. (2021), the authors demonstrate that the matrix stepsized

      2     BACKGROUND AND MOTIVATION
064
065
      For a given ε > 0, finding an approximately global optimum, that is xε such that f (xε ) −
066   minx f (x) < ε, is known to be NP-hard (Jain et al., 2017; Danilova et al., 2022). However, gradient
067   descent based methods are still useful in this case. When these methods are applied to non-convex
068   objectives, they treat the function f as locally convex and aim to converge to a local minimum.
069   Despite this simplification, such methods have gained popularity in practice due to their superior
070   performance compared to other approaches for non-convex optimization, such as convex relaxation-
071   based methods (Tibshirani, 1996; Cai et al., 2010).
072
073   2.1   S TOCHASTIC GRADIENT DESCENT
074
075   Arguably, one of the most prominent meta-methods for tackling non-convex optimization problems
076   is stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The formulation of SGD is presented as the following iterative
077   algorithm: xk+1 = xk − γg k . Here, g k ∈ Rd serves as a stochastic estimator of the gradient
078
      ∇f (xk ). SGD essentially mimics the classical gradient descent algorithm, and recovers it when
      g k = ∇f (xk ). In this scenario, the method approximates the objective function f using a linear
079
      function and takes a step of size γ in the direction that maximally reduces this approximation. When
080
      the stepsize is sufficiently small, and the function f is suitably smooth, it can be demonstrated that
081   the function value decreases, as discussed in (Bubeck et al., 2015; Gower et al., 2019).
082
083   However, computing the full gradient can often be computationally expensive. In such cases,
084
      stochastic approximations of the gradient come into play. Stochastic estimators of the gradient can
      be employed for various purposes, leading to the development of different methods. These include
085
      stochastic batch gradient descent (Nemirovski et al., 2009; Johnson & Zhang, 2013; Defazio et al.,
086
      2014), randomized coordinate descent (Nesterov, 2012; Wright, 2015), and compressed gradient
087   descent (Alistarh et al., 2017; Khirirat et al., 2018; Mishchenko et al., 2019). The latter, compressed
088   gradient descent, holds particular relevance to this paper, and we will delve into a more detailed
089   discussion of it in subsequent sections.
090
091
      2.2   S ECOND ORDER METHODS
092
093   The stochastic gradient descent is considered as a first-order method as it uses only the first order
094   derivative information. Although being immensely popular, the first order methods are not always
095   optimal. Not surprisingly, using higher order derivatives in deciding update direction can yield to
096   faster algorithms. A simple instance of such algorithms is the Newton Star algorithm (Islamov et al.,
097   2021):
                                                               −1
098                                  xk+1 = xk − ∇2 f (x⋆ )        ∇f (xk ),                          (NS)
099
      where x⋆ is the minimum point of the objective function. The authors establish that under specific
100
      conditions, the algorithm’s convergence to the unique solution x⋆ in the convex scenario occurs at a
101
      local quadratic rate. Nonetheless, its practicality is limited since we do not have prior knowledge of
102   the Hessian matrix at the optimal point. Despite being proposed recently, the Newton-Star algorithm
103   gives a deeper insight on the generic Newton method (Gragg & Tapia, 1974; Miel, 1980; Yamamoto,
104   1987):
105                                                                −1
                                     xk+1 = xk − γ ∇2 f (xk )          ∇f (xk ).                      (NM)
106
107   Here, the unknown Hessian of the Newton-Star algorithm, is estimated progressively along the it-
      erations. The latter causes elevated computational costs, as the inverting a large square matrix is

109   erate with a computationally cheaper estimate (Broyden, 1965; Dennis & Moré, 1977; Al-Baali &
110   Khalfan, 2007; Al-Baali et al., 2014).

113
114   The det-CGD algorithm falls into this framework of the second order methods as well. Proposed
115   by Li et al. (2024b)1 , the algorithm suggests using a uniform “upper bound” on the inverse Hessian
116   matrix. Assuming matrix smoothness of the objective (Safaryan et al., 2021), they replace the scalar
117   stepsize with a positive definite matrix D. The algorithm is given as follows:
118                                          xk+1 = xk − DS k ∇f (xk ).                                  (det-CGD)

121   inverse Hessian. The standard SGD is a particular case of this method, as the scalar stepsize γ can
122   be seen as a matrix γId , where Id is the d-dimensional identity matrix. An advantage of using a
123   matrix stepsize is more evident if we take the perspective of the second order methods. Indeed, the
124   scalar stepsize γId uniformly estimates the largest eigenvalue of the Hessian matrix, while D can
125   capture the Hessian more accurately. The authors show both theoretical and empirical improvement
126   that comes with matrix stepsizes.

157
158
      To eliminate this neighborhood, various techniques for reducing variance are employed. One of
      the simplest techniques applicable to CGD is gradient shifting. By replacing S k ∇f (xk ) with

163   algorithm is an instance of a more commonly known method called SGD⋆ (Gower et al., 2020).
164   However, since the exact optimum x⋆ is typically unknown, this technique encounters similar chal-
165   lenges as the Newton-Star algorithm mentioned earlier. Fortunately, akin to quasi-Newton methods,

167   of research focuses on variance reduction for CGD based algorithms on this insight.
168
      To eliminate the neighborhood in the distributed version of CGD, denoted as det-CGD1, we apply
169   a technique called MARINA (Gorbunov et al., 2021). MARINA cleverly combines the general
170   shifting (Shulgin & Richtárik, 2022) technique with loopless variance reduction techniques (Qian
171   et al., 2021). This approach introduces an alternative gradient estimator specifically designed for
172   the federated learning setting. Thanks to its structure, it allows to establish an upper bound on the
173   stationarity error that diminishes significantly with a large number of iterations. In this paper, we
174   construct the analog of the this algorithm called det-MARINA, using matrix stepsizes and sketch
175   gradient compressors. For this new method, we prove a convergence guarantee similar to the results
176   of Li et al. (2024b) without a neighborhood term.
177   Furthermore, we also propose det-DASHA, which is the extension of DASHA in the matrix step-
178   size setting. The latter was proposed by Tyurin & Richtárik (2024) and it combines MARINA
179   with momentum variance reduction techniques (Cutkosky & Orabona, 2019). DASHA offers better
180   practicality compared to MARINA, as it always sends compressed gradients and does not need to
181   synchronize among all the nodes.

497   8   F UTURE WORK
498
499   i) In this paper, we have only considered (linear) sketches as the compression operator. However,
500   there exists a variety of compressors which are useful in practice that do not fall into this category.
501   Extending det-CGD and det-MARINA for general unbiased compressors is a promising future work
502   direction. ii) Additionally, given recent successes with adaptive stepsizes (e.g., (Loizou et al., 2021;
503   Orvieto et al., 2022; Schaipp et al., 2023)), designing an adaptive matrix stepsize tailored to our

506
      matrix version of the server step size could also be interesting.

872   A.2   A DDITIONAL PRIOR WORK
873
874   Numerous effective convex optimization techniques have been adapted for application in non-convex
875   scenarios. Here’s a selection of these techniques, although it’s not an exhaustive list: adaptivity
876   (Dvinskikh et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020b), variance reduction (J Reddi et al., 2016; Li et al.,
877   2021), and acceleration (Guminov et al., 2019). Of particular relevance to our work is the paper by
878   Khaled & Richtárik (2023), which introduces a unified approach for analyzing stochastic gradient
879
      descent for non-convex objectives. A comprehensive overview of non-convex optimization can be
      found in (Jain et al., 2017; Danilova et al., 2022).
880
881   An illustrative example of a matrix stepsized method is Newton’s method, which has been a long-
882   standing favorite in the optimization community (Gragg & Tapia, 1974; Miel, 1980; Yamamoto,
883   1987). However, the computational complexity involved in computing the stepsize as the inverse of
884   the Hessian of the current iteration is substantial. Instead, quasi-Newton methods employ a readily
885
      computable estimator to replace the inverse Hessian (Broyden, 1965; Dennis & Moré, 1977; Al-
      Baali & Khalfan, 2007; Al-Baali et al., 2014). An important direction of research that is relevant to
886
      our work, studies distributed second order methods. Here is a non-exhaustive list of papers in this
887
      area: (Wang et al., 2018; Crane & Roosta, 2019; Zhang et al., 2020a; Islamov et al., 2021; Alimisis
888   et al., 2021; Safaryan et al., 2022a).
889
890   The Distributed Compressed Gradient Descent (DCGD) algorithm, initially proposed by Khirirat
891
      et al. (2018), has seen improvements in various aspects, as documented in works such as (Li et al.,
      2020; Horváth et al., 2022). Its variance reduced version with gradients shifts was studied by Shulgin
892
      & Richtárik (2022) in the (strongly) convex setting. Additionally, there exists a substantial body of
893
      literature on other federated learning algorithms employing unbiased compressors (Alistarh et al.,
894   2017; Mishchenko et al., 2019; Gorbunov et al., 2021; Mishchenko et al., 2022; Maranjyan et al.,
895   2022; Horváth et al., 2023).
896
897
      Variance reduction techniques have gained significant attention in the context of stochastic batch
      gradient descent that is prevalent in machine learning. Numerous algorithms have been developed
898
      in this regard, including well-known ones like SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013), SAG (Schmidt
899
      et al., 2017), SDCA(Richtárik & Takáč, 2014), SAGA (Defazio et al., 2014), MISO (Mairal, 2015),
900   and Katyusha (Allen-Zhu, 2017). An overview of more advanced methods can be found in (Gower
901   et al., 2020). Notably, SVRG and Katyusha have been extended with loopless variants, namely
902   L-SVRG and L-Katyusha (Kovalev et al., 2020; Qian et al., 2021). These loopless versions stream-
903   line the algorithms by eliminating the outer loop and introducing a biased coin-flip mechanism at
904   each step. This simplification eases both the algorithms’ structure and their analyses, while preserv-
905   ing their worst-case complexity bounds. L-SVRG, in particular, offers the advantage of setting the
906   exit probability from the outer loop independently of the condition number, thus, enhancing both
907   robustness and practical efficiency.
908   This technique of coin flipping allows to obtain variance reduction for the CGD algorithm. A rele-
909   vant example is the DIANA algorithm proposed by Mishchenko et al. (2019). Its convergence was
910   proved both in the convex and non-convex cases. Later, MARINA (Gorbunov et al., 2021) obtained
911   the optimal convergence rate, improving in communication complexity compared to all previous
912   first order methods. Finally, there is a line of work developing variance reduction in the federated
913   setting using other methods and techniques (Chraibi et al., 2019; Hanzely & Richtárik, 2020; Dinh
914
      et al., 2020; Peng et al., 2022).
915   Another method to obtain variance reduction is based on momentum. It was initially studied by
916   Cutkosky & Orabona (2019), where they propose the STORM algorithm, which is a stochastic gra-
917   dient descent algorithm with a momentum term for non-convex objectives. They obtain stationarity
      guarantees using adaptive stepsizes with optimal convergence rates. However, they require the vari-


                                                        17

      ance of the stochastic gradient to be bounded by a constant, which is impractical. Using momentum
919   for variance reduction has since been widely studied (Liu et al., 2020; Khanduri et al., 2020; Tran-
920   Dinh et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022).
