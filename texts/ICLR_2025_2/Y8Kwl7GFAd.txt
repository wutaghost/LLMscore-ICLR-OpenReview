009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011               We introduce RNA-F RAME F LOW, the first generative model for 3D RNA back-
012               bone design. We build upon SE(3) flow matching for protein backbone generation
013               and establish protocols for data preparation and evaluation to address unique
014               challenges posed by RNA modeling. We formulate RNA structures as a set of
015               rigid-body frames and associated loss functions which account for larger, more
016               conformationally flexible RNA backbones (13 atoms per nucleotide) vs. proteins (4
017               atoms per residue). Toward tackling the lack of diversity in 3D RNA datasets, we
018
                  explore training with structural clustering and cropping augmentations. Addition-
                  ally, we define a suite of evaluation metrics to measure whether the generated RNA
019
                  structures are globally self-consistent (via inverse folding followed by forward
020
                  folding) and locally recover RNA-specific structural descriptors. The most perfor-
021               mant version of RNA-F RAME F LOW generates locally realistic RNA backbones of
022               40-150 nucleotides, over 40% of which pass our validity criteria as measured by a
023               self-consistency TM-score ≥ 0.45, at which two RNAs have the same global fold.

026   1       I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Designing RNA structures. Proteins, and the diverse structures they can adopt, drive essential
029   biological functions in cells. Deep learning has led to breakthroughs in structural modeling and design
030   of proteins (Jumper et al., 2021; Dauparas et al., 2022; Watson et al., 2023), driven by the abundance
031   of 3D data from the Protein Data Bank (PDB). Concurrently, there has been a surge of interest in
032   Ribonucleic Acids (RNA) and RNA-based therapeutics for gene editing, gene silencing, and vaccines
033   (Doudna and Charpentier, 2014; Metkar et al., 2024). RNAs play several roles: they are carriers of
034
      genetic information coding for proteins (mRNA) or may remain non-coding (tRNA). There are several
      families of RNA, which we focus on in this work, whose functions depend on their tertiary structure1 .
035
      While there is growing interest in designing such structured RNAs for a range of applications in
036
      biotechnology and medicine (Mulhbacher et al., 2010; Damase et al., 2021), the current toolkit for
037   3D RNA design uses classical algorithms and heuristics to assemble RNA motifs as building blocks
038   (Han et al., 2017; Yesselman et al., 2019). However, hand-crafted heuristics are not always broadly
039   applicable across multiple tasks and rigid motifs may not fully capture the conformational dynamics
040   that govern RNA functionality (Ganser et al., 2019; Li et al., 2023a). This presents an opportunity for
041   deep generative models to learn data-driven design principles from existing 3D RNA structures.
042
      What makes deep learning for RNA hard? The primary challenge is the paucity of raw 3D RNA
043   structural data, manifesting as an absence of ML-ready datasets for model development (Joshi et al.,
044   2023). Protein structure is primarily driven by hydrogen bonding along the backbone, and current
045   geometric deep learning models incorporate this inductive bias through backbone frames to represent
046   residues (Jumper et al., 2021). RNA structure, however, is often more conformationally flexible and
047   driven by base pairing interactions across strands as well as base stacking between rings of adjacent
048   nucleotides (Vicens and Kieft, 2022), all of which can only be learnt implicitly at present2 .
049   Additionally, RNA nucleotides, the equivalent of amino acids in proteins, include significantly more
050   atoms as part of the backbone (13 compared to 4) which necessitates a generalization of backbone
051   frames where the placement of most atoms needs to be parameterized by torsion angles. These

      Our contributions. We develop RNA-F RAME F LOW, the first generative model for 3D RNA
081   backbone design, illustrated in Figure 1. We adapt FrameFlow (Yim et al., 2023a), an SE(3)
082   equivariant flow matching model for proteins to RNA. We introduce RNA-specific modifications
083   to the data preparation and loss formulation, including representing RNA nucleotides as rigid-body
084   frames that parameterize all 13 atoms. We also introduce an evaluation pipeline to benchmark RNA
085   backbone design models’ capabilities at recovering local and global structure. Our best model is
086   trained on RNAs of lengths 40-150 from the PDB and can unconditionally sample locally plausible
087   backbones with over 40% validity as measured by a self-consistency TM-score ≥ 0.45.
088   Through this study, we aimed to evaluate the extent to which generative models for proteins can
089   be adapted for RNA. This brought up critical challenges and limitations of deep learning for RNA
090   modelling, such as a lack of explicit representations of the physical interactions that drive RNA
091   structure as well as biases in 3D RNA datasets, which we have made preliminary efforts towards
092   addressing. Together with our engineering contributions, we hope this work will stimulate future
093   research in generative models for RNA design.

      2     T HE RNA-F RAME F LOW P IPELINE
096
097
      Overview. We are concerned with building a generative model that unconditionally outputs all-
098   atom RNA backbones, sampled from a distribution of realistic 3D RNA structures. Formally, given
099   an RNA sequence length of Nnt nucleotides, we aim to generate a real-valued tensor X of shape
100   Nnt × 13 × 3 representing 3D atomic coordinates for each of the 13 backbone atoms per nucleotide.
101   In the following sections, we will describe how we adapt FrameFlow (Yim et al., 2023a), an SE(3)
102   equivariant flow matching model for protein backbones, to our setting.
103
104   2.1     R EPRESENTING RNA BACKBONES AS F RAMES
105
106   As shown in Figure 1, the RNA backbone consists of nucleotides with a phosphate group
107   (P, OP 1, OP 2, O5′ ), a ribose sugar (C1′ − C5′ , O2′ , O3′ , O4′ ), and a nitrogen atom N at the
      stem of the base. We represent the group of atoms within each nucleotide as a rigid-body frame.


                                                                                 2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Frames enable inferring the positions of all atoms within the nucleotide via a frame center and
109   orientation (described subsequently). However, the 13 atoms per nucleotide in the RNA backbone
110   is significantly greater than protein residues with 4 atoms (Cα , N, C, O). In proteins, it is standard
111   to represent each residue by a frame centered at Cα with vectors along Cα − N and Cα − C, and
112   O is placed assuming an idealized planar geometry (Jumper et al., 2021). No such canonical frame
113   representation exists for RNAs.
114
      RNA frames. We use the C4′ , C3′ , and O4′ atoms to create the frame for each nucleotide, as
115   in Morehead et al. (2023). All other backbone atoms are inferred with 8 torsions Φ = {ϕ1 →
116   ϕ8 }, ϕi ∈ SO(2) that are predicted post-hoc after frame generation. The Gram-Schmidt process is
117   used on v1 , v2 defined by the vectors along the C4′ − O4′ and C4′ − C3′ bonds; C5′ is imputed
118   based the positions of the other 3 atoms and tetrahedral geometry. Given the 8 torsion angles, we
119   autoregressively place non-frame atoms in order of the torsions Φ in Figure 1, constructing the final
120   set of all-atom RNA nucleotides. We describe this imputation of non-frame atoms in Appendix B.4.
121   Choice of frame atoms. We had two main considerations for selecting the atoms to create RNA
122   frames: (1) the atoms should have roughly the same spatial orientation w.r.t. each other; and (2)
123   the atoms should be reasonably close to the centroid in the nucleotide to reduce error accumulation
124   when placing the furthest non-frame atoms. We choose {C3′ , C4′ , O4′ } as these atoms spatially
125   shift the least in naturally occurring RNA (Harvey and Prabhakaran, 1986). The non-frame backbone
126   atoms – the remaining atoms in the ribose ring and the phosphate group atoms – are parameterized
127   by torsion angles to account for their relative conformational flexibility. This choice of frame enables
128   models to learn ring puckering, the planar rotation of the ribose ring about the C4′ − C5′ bond which
129
      affects how the RNA interacts with partners to form complexes (Clay et al., 2017). We are actively
      evaluating alternate choices of RNA frames.

132   2.2   SE(3) F LOW M ATCHING ON RNA F RAMES
133
134   Input. Given a set of 3D coordinates, a simultaneous rotation and translation (r, x) forms an
135
      orientation-preserving rigid-body transformation of the coordinates. The set of all such transforma-
      tions in 3D is the Special Euclidean group SE(3), which composes the group of 3D rotations SO(3)
136
      and 3D translations in R3 .
137
138   We can represent an RNA frame T = (r, x) as a translation x ∈ R3 from the global origin to place
139   C4′ and a rotation r ∈ SO(3) to orient C3′ − C4′ − O4′ . Compared to working with raw 3D
140   coordinates for each backbone atom, using the frame representation entails performing flow matching
141
      on the space of SE(3). This is an inductive bias to reduce the degrees of freedom the generative
      model needs to learn. Instead of predicting 13 correlated 3D coordinates independently (39 quantities)
142
      for each nucleotide, we instead predict one 3D coordinate (of C4′ ) and one 3×3 rotation matrix
143
      (12 quantities). We follow Chen and Lipman (2024) and Yim et al. (2023a)’s framework for flow
144   matching on SE(3), which we summarise subsequently.
145
146   Overview. Flow matching generates or learns how to place and orient a set of N frames T =
147   {T (n) }Nn=1 , where T
                              (n)
                                  = (r(n) , x(n) ), to form an RNA backbone of length N . To do so, we
148
      initialize frames at random in 3D space at time t = 0, and train a denoiser or flow model to iteratively
      refine the location and orientation of each frame for a specified number of steps until time t = 1.
149
150   Suppose p0 (T0 ) and p1 (T1 ) are the marginal distributions of randomly oriented and ground truth
151   frames from our dataset of RNA structures, respectively. Suppose a non-unique time-dependent
152   vector field ut leads to an ODE between the two distributions p0 and p1 , i.e., assume there is a way
153   to map from noisy samples to the corresponding true samples. This solution forms a ground truth
154
      probability path pt between the two distributions at time t ∈ [0, 1], which we can use to transform
155   samples from noise to the true distribution. The continuity equation ∂p∂t = −∇ · (pt ut ) relates the
156
      vector field ut to the evolution of the probability path pt .

158   from p1 (T1 ), we construct a flow Tt by following the probability path pt between T0 and T1 for any
159   time step t sampled from U(0, 1). As shown by Chen and Lipman (2024) for the SE(3) group (and

      Sampling. To generate or unconditionally sample an RNA backbone of length N , we initialize a
239
      random point cloud of frames. We use our trained flow model vθ within an ODE solver to iteratively
240
      transform the noisy frames into a realistic RNA backbone. For each nucleotide, we begin with a noisy
241   frame T0 = (r0 , x0 ) at time step t = 0, and integrate to t = 1 using the Euler method for a specified
242   number of steps NT , with step size ∆t = 1/NT . At each step t, the flow model vθ predicts updates
243   for the frames via a rotation r̂1 and translation x̂1 :

249   Conditional generation. The unconditional sampling strategy described above aims to generate
250
      realistic RNA backbone structures sampled from the training distribution. However using generative
      models in real-world design tasks entails conditional generation based on specified design constraints
251
      or requirements (Ingraham et al., 2022; Watson et al., 2023), which we are currently exploring. For
252
      example, unconditional models can leverage inference-time guidance strategies (Wu et al., 2024), be
253   fine-tuned conditionally (Denker et al., 2024) or in an amortized fashion for motif-scaffolding (Didi
254   et al., 2023). For sequence conditioning and structure prediction, we can incorporate embeddings
255   from language models (Penic et al., 2024; He et al., 2024).

      3      E XPERIMENTS
259
      3D RNA structure dataset. RNAsolo (Adamczyk et al., 2022) is a recent dataset of RNA 3D
260
      structures extracted from isolated RNAs, protein-RNA complexes, and DNA-RNA hybrids from the
261   Protein Data Bank (as of January 5, 2024). The dataset contains 14,366 structures at resolution ≤ 4
262
      Å (1 Å = 0.1nm). We select sequences of lengths between 40 and 150 nucleotides (5,319 in total) as
263   we envisioned this size range contains structured RNAs of interest for design tasks.
264
265   Evaluation metrics. We evaluate our models for unconditional RNA backbone generation, analogous
266
      to recent work in protein design (Yim et al., 2023b;a; Bose et al., 2023; Lin and AlQuraishi, 2023);
      see Figure 2. We generate 50 backbones for target lengths sampled between 40 and 150 at intervals
267
      of 10. We then compute the following indicators of quality for these backbones:
268
269       • Validity (scTM ≥ 0.45): We inverse fold each generated backbone using gRNAde (Joshi et al.,
            2023) and pass Nseq = 8 generated sequences into RhoFold (Shen et al., 2022). We then compute


                                                           5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
            the self-consistency TM-score (scTM) between the predicted RhoFold structure and our backbone
271         at the C4′ level. We say a backbone is valid if scTM ≥ 0.45; this threshold corresponds to
272         roughly the same fold between two RNAs (Zhang et al., 2022). Alternatively, we use an RMSD
273         threshold of 4.3 Å, corresponding to the median RMSD of RhoFold on RNAsolo sequences.
274       • Diversity: Among the valid samples, we compute the number of unique structural clusters formed
275         using qTMclust (Zhang et al., 2022) and take the ratio to the total number of samples. Two
276         structures are considered similar if their TM-score ≥ 0.45. This metric shows how much each
277         generated sample varies from others across various sequence lengths.
278       • Novelty: Among the valid samples, we use US-align (Zhang et al., 2022) at the C4′ level to
279         compute how structurally dissimilar the generated backbones are from the training distribution.
280         For a set of samples for a given sequence length, we compute the TM-score between all pairs
281         of generated backbones and training samples, and for each generated backbone, we assign the
282         highest TM-score. We call the average across this set, pdbTM.
283       • Local structural measurements: We measure the similarity between bond distances, bond
284         angles, and dihedral angles from the set of generated samples and the training set. To do so, we
285
            compute histograms for each of the local structural metrics and use 1D Earth Mover’s distance to
            measure the similarity between generated and training distributions.
286
287   Hyperparameters. We use 6 IPA blocks in our flow model, with an additional 3-layer torsion
288   predictor MLP that takes in node embeddings from the IPA module. Our final model contains 16.8M
289   trainable parameters. We use AdamW optimizer with learning rate 0.0001, β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999.
290   We train for 120K gradient update steps on four NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3090 GPUs for about 18
291
      hours with a batch size B = 28. Each batch contains samples of the same sequence length to avoid
      padding. Further hyperparameters are listed in Appendix B.1.

299

300

      For our best-performing model using diffusion timesteps NT = 50, we plot histograms of bond
345   distance, bond angles, and dihedral angles in Figure 4. We include the Earth Mover’s distance (EMD)
346   between measurements from the training and generated distributions as an indicator of local realism
347   (using 30 bins for each quantity). An ideal generative model will score an EMD close to 0.0 (i.e.
348   consistent with the training set comprising naturally occurring RNA). In Table 2, we observe EMD
349   values from our best-performing model’s backbones being significantly closer to 0.0 compared to
350   MMDiff. We include histograms of local structural descriptors for MMDiff in Appendix D.1.
351   We also show RNA Ramachandran angle plots for generated samples and the training distribu-
352   tion in Figure 4. Keating et al. (2011) introduced η − θ plots, similar to Ramachandran angle
353   plots for proteins, that track the separate dihedral angles formed by {C4′i , Pi+1 , C4′i+1 , Pi+2 } and

356   from naturally occurring RNA structures in the training set.

405   4.4           DATA PREPARATION PROTOCOLS
406
407   Due to the overrepresentation of RNA strands of certain lengths (mostly corresponding to tRNA
408   or 5S ribosomal RNA) in our training set, our models generate close likenesses for those lengths
409
      that achieve high self-consistency but are not novel folds. To avoid this memorized recapitulation
      and promote increased diversity among samples, we sought to develop data preparation protocols to
410
      balance RNA folds across sequence lengths. We identically train RNA-F RAME F LOW on these data
411
      splits for 120K gradient steps, with results reported in Table 3.
412
413       • Structural clustering: We cluster our training set using qTMclust. When creating a training
414         batch, we sample random clusters, and from each cluster, random structures. This ensures batches
415
            do not solely contain samples for a single sequence length or are dominated by over-represented
            folds. There are only 342 structural clusters for the 5,319 samples within sequence lengths 40-150,
416
            highlighting the lack of diversity in RNA structural data. Each batch comprises padded samples
417
            up to a maximum length of 150 from randomly selected clusters across sequence lengths.
418
          • Cropping augmentation: We expand our training set by cropping longer RNA strands beyond
419
            length 150 by sampling a random crop length in [40, 150] and extracting a contiguous segment
420         from the larger chains. As cropped RNA are not standalone molecules and serve only to augment
421         the dataset, we consider a randomly chosen 20% of the training set size to balance uncropped and
422         cropped samples; this gives 1,063 extra cropped samples.
423
      We observe improved diversity and novelty at the cost of reduced validity. Randomly cropping
424
      may introduce subsequences that fold into significantly different structures than the substructure
425
      extracted from the original RNA; these subsequences may even unfold in real life. As a result, the
426   augmented dataset may contain folds that are unstable or implausible. The structure prediction and
427   inverse-folding models may not have encountered these folds loosely recapitulated by our model,
428   resulting in poor validity. We are actively developing principled cropping methods that capture unique,
429   realistic folds. We include additional results on these data preparation protocols in Appendix D.2.

      5    L IMITATIONS AND D ISCUSSIONS
444
445   Altogether, our experiments demonstrate that the SE(3) flow matching framework is sufficiently
446   expressive for learning the distribution of 3D RNA structure and generating realistic RNA backbones
447   similar to well-represented RNA folds in the PDB. Select examples are shown in Figure 6. We have
448   also identified notable limitations and avenues for future work, which we highlight below.
449   Physical violations. While well-trained models usually generate realistic RNA backbones, we do
450   observe some physical violations: generated backbones sometimes have chains that are either too
451   close by or directly clash with one another, are highly coiled, have excessive loops and unrealistically
452   intertwined helices, or have chain breaks. We highlight these limitations in Figure 5. RNA tertiary
453   structure folding is driven by base pairing and base stacking which influence the formation of helices,
454   loops, and other tertiary motifs (Vicens and Kieft, 2022) . Base pairing refers to nucleotides along
455   adjacent chains forming hydrogen bonds, while base stacking involves interactions between rings
456
      of adjacent nucleotide bases along a chain. To our knowledge, all current deep learning models
      operate on individual nucleotides, only implicitly learning base pairing and stacking. Developing
457
      explicit representations of these interactions as part of the architecture may further minimize physical
458
      violations and provide stronger inductive biases to learn complex tertiary RNA motifs. We analyze
459   steric clashes in our generated backbones in Appendix D.4.
460
461   Generalization and novelty. We observed that the best designs from our models (as measured by
462
      scTM score) are sampled at lengths 70-80 and 120-130, and often have closely matching structures in
      the PDB (high TM-scores). This suggests that models can recapitulate well-represented RNA folds in
463
      their training distribution (e.g., both tRNAs at length 70-90 and small 5S ribosomal RNAs at length
464
      120 are very frequent). However, self-consistency metrics were relatively poorer for less frequent
465   lengths, suggesting that models are currently not designing novel folds.
466
467   We would also like to note that the models we use for structure prediction and inverse folding may
468
      be similarly biased to perform well for certain sequence lengths, leading to the overall pipeline
      being reliable for commonly occurring lengths and unreliable for less frequent ones (see Appendix
469
      B.2 for an analysis on RhoFold). We evaluated preliminary strategies for structural clustering and
470
      cropping augmentations during training, which improved the novelty of designed structures but led
471   to fewer designs passing the validity filter. Overall, the relative scarcity of RNA structural data
472   compared to proteins necessitates greater care in preparing data pipelines for scaling up training
473   and/or incorporating inductive biases into generative models, which we hope to continue exploring.
474
475
476
      6    C ONCLUSION
477   We introduce RNA-F RAME F LOW, a generative model for 3D RNA backbone design. Our evaluations
478   show that our model can design locally realistic and moderately novel backbones of length 40 – 150
479   nucleotides. We achieve a validity score of 41.0% and relatively strong diversity and novelty scores
480   compared to diffusion model baselines and ablated variants. While generative models can successfully
481   recapitulate well-represented RNA folds (e.g., tRNAs, small rRNAs), the lack of diversity in the
482
      training data may hinder broad generalization at present. We are actively exploring improved data
      preparation strategies combined with inductive biases that explicitly incorporate physical interactions
483
      that drive RNA structure. We hope RNA-F RAME F LOW and the associated evaluation framework can
484
      serve as foundations for the community to explore 3D RNA design, towards developing conditional
485   generative models for real-world design scenarios.

      Here, we summarize recent developments in deep learning for 3D RNA modeling and design.
759
760   Recent end-to-end RNA structure prediction tools include RhoFold (Shen et al., 2022),
761   RoseTTAFold2NA (Baek et al., 2022a), DRFold (Li et al., 2023b), and AlphaFold3 (Abramson
762
      et al., 2024), each with varying performance that is yet to match the current state-of-the-art for
      proteins. Other approaches use GNNs as ranking functions (Townshend et al., 2021) together with
763
      sampling algorithms (Boniecki et al., 2016; Watkins et al., 2020). However, structure prediction tools
764
      are not directly capable of designing new structures, which this work aims to address by adapting an
765   SE(3) flow matching framework for proteins (Yim et al., 2023a). MMDiff (Morehead et al., 2023), a
766   diffusion model for protein-nucleic acid complex generation, can also sample RNA-only structures
767   in principle. Our evaluation shows that our flow matching model significantly outperforms both the
768   original and RNA-only versions of MMDiff that we re-trained for fair comparison.
769
      Joshi et al. (2023) introduce gRNAde, a GNN-based encoder-decoder for 3D RNA inverse folding, a
770
      closely related task of designing new sequences conditioned on backbone structures. Tan et al. (2023)
771   and Shulgina et al. (2024) have also developed GNNs for 3D RNA inverse folding. We use gRNAde
772   (Joshi et al., 2023) followed by RhoFold (Shen et al., 2022) in our evaluation pipeline to forward fold
773   designed backbones and measure structural self-consistency.
774
      Independently and concurrent to our work, Nori and Jin (2024) propose RNAFlow, an SE(3) flow
775
      matching model to co-design RNA sequence and structure conditioned on protein partners. At each
776
      denoising step, RNAFlow uses a protein-conditioned variant of gRNAde (Joshi et al., 2023) to inverse
777   fold noised structures, followed by RoseTTAFold2NA (Baek et al., 2022a) to predict the structure
778   of the designed sequence. The performance of RNAFlow is upper-bounded by RoseTTAFold2NA
779   as a pre-trained structure generator, which is kept frozen and not developed for designed RNAs
780   which do not have co-evolutionary MSA information. Our work tackles de novo 3D RNA backbone
781   generation, an orthogonal design task of sampling RNA backbone structures. We train RNA structure
782   generation models from scratch, akin to recent developments in protein design (Yim et al., 2023b;a;
783   Bose et al., 2023; Lin and AlQuraishi, 2023). Backbone generation followed by inverse folding has
784   shown experimental success in designing functional proteins (Dauparas et al., 2022; Watson et al.,
785
      2023; Ingraham et al., 2022), as the framework is flexible for including specific structural motifs and
      sequence constraints.
