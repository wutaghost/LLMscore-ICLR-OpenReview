009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Intrinsic capability to continuously learn a changing data stream is a desideratum
012            of deep neural networks (DNNs). However, current DNNs suffer from catastrophic
013            forgetting, which hinders remembering past knowledge. To mitigate this issue,
014            existing Continual Learning (CL) approaches either retain exemplars for replay,
015            regularize learning, or allocate dedicated capacity for new tasks. This paper
016            investigates an unexplored CL direction for incremental learning called Incremental
017            Latent Rectification or ILR. In a nutshell, ILR learns to propagate with correction
018
               (or rectify) the representation from the current trained DNN backward to the
               representation space of the old task, where performing predictive decisions is easier.
019
               This rectification process only employs a chain of small representation mapping
020
               networks, called rectifier units. Empirical experiments on several continual learning
021            benchmarks, including CIFAR10, CIFAR100, and Tiny ImageNet, demonstrate
022            the effectiveness and potential of this novel CL direction compared to existing
023            representative CL methods.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Humans exhibit the innate capability to incrementally learn novel concepts while consolidating
029   acquired knowledge into long-term memories (Rasch & Born, 2007). More general Artificial
030   Intelligence systems in real-world applications would require similar imitation to capture the dynamic
031   of the changing data stream. These systems need to acquire knowledge incrementally without
032   retraining, which is computationally expensive and exhibits a large memory footprint (Rebuffi et al.,
033
      2016). Nonetheless, existing learning approaches are yet to match human learning in this so-called
      Continual Learning (CL) problem due to catastrophic forgetting (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989). These
034
      systems encounter difficulty balancing the capability of incorporating new task knowledge while
035
      maintaining performance on learned tasks, or the plasticity-stability dilemma.
036
037   Representative CL approaches in the literature usually involve the use of memory buffer for rehearsal
038   (Ratcliff, 1990; Chaudhry et al., 2019a; Buzzega et al., 2020; Caccia et al., 2022; Bhat et al.,
039
      2023; Arani et al., 2022), auxiliary loss term for learning regularization (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017;
      Ebrahimi et al., 2020; Zenke et al., 2017; Schwarz et al., 2018), or structural changes such as
040
      pruning or model growing (Rusu et al., 2016; Mallya & Lazebnik, 2018; Fernando et al., 2017; Yan
041
      et al., 2021). These methods share the common objective of discouraging the deviation of learned
042   knowledge representation. Rehearsal-based methods allow the model to revisit past exemplars to
043   reinforce previously learned representations. Alternatively, regularization-based methods prevent
044   changes in parameter spaces by formulating additional loss terms. However, both approaches present
045   shortcomings, including keeping a rehearsal buffer of all past tasks during the model lifetime or
046   infusing ad-hoc inductive bias into the regularization process. Meanwhile, structure-based methods
047   utilize the over-parameterization property of the model by pruning, masking, or adding parameters to
048   reduce new task interferences.
049   This paper studies a novel approach for CL named Incremental Latent Rectification (ILR), where we
050   allow the model to “forget” knowledge of old tasks but then “recall” or rectify such “catastrophic
051   forgetting” during inference using a sequence of lightweight knowledge mapping networks. These
052   lightweight knowledge mapping networks, called rectifiers, help significantly reduce information loss
053   on learned tasks by incrementally correcting the changes in the representation space. Specifically,
      for each new task, we add a small, simple, and computationally inexpensive auxiliary unit that will


                                                         1

054
      rectify the representation from the current task to the previous task. Our method differs from many
055   network expansion methods, where additional parameters are allocated to minimize changes to the
056   old parameters. Instead, we iteratively recover past task representations by backwardly propagating
057   current representations through a series of mapping networks. Through this mechanism, ILR allows
058   the optimal adaptation of a new task (plasticity) while separately mitigating catastrophic forgetting. In
059   addition, different from various CL approaches that heavily modify the training process, ILR imposes
060   minimal changes to new task learning as modifications are mainly performed after the training process
061   has been completed. Hence, ILR can be easily integrated into the existing CL pipelines.
062
063   Contributions. We propose a new direction for CL by sequentially correcting the representation of
064
      the current task into the past task’s representation using a chain of lightweight rectifier units:
065
       • We propose ILR, a novel approach to continual learning that separates catastrophic forgetting
066      mitigation with new task learning via a sequence of lightweight rectifier units.
067
       • To train the rectifier unit, we rely on either data samples from task t − 1 or the current task t;
068
         when such data is unavailable (e.g., due to memory constraint or privacy concerns), a generative
069      model that synthesizes task t − 1’s data can also be utilized. At inference time, for the task-
070      incremental setting, we construct a chain of rectifiers based on the provided task identity and
071      forward the latent representation and inputs to correct the representation. For the class incremental
072      setting, ILR forms the final prediction from an ensemble of predictions based on the reconstructed
073      representations.
074    • We empirically evaluate our approach on three widely-used continual learning benchmarks
075      (CIFAR10, CIFAR100, and Tiny ImageNet) to demonstrate that our approach achieves comparable
076      performance with the existing representative CL directions.
077
078   This paper unfolds as follows. Section 2 discusses the literature on the continual learning problems,
079
      and Section 3 describes our Incremental Latent Rectification method. Finally, Section 4 provides the
      empirical evidence for the effectiveness of our proposed solution.

082   2    R ELATED W ORK
083
084   Catastrophic forgetting is a critical concern in artificial intelligence and is arguably one of the most
085   prominent questions to address for DNNs. This phenomenon presents significant challenges when
086   deploying models in different applications. Continual learning addresses this issue by enabling
087
      agents to learn throughout their lifespan. This aspect has gained significant attention recently (Sun
      et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2021; Kirichenko et al., 2021; Balaji et al., 2020). Considering a model
088
      well-trained on past tasks, we risk overwriting its past knowledge by adapting it for new tasks. The
089
      problem of knowledge loss can be addressed using different methods, as explored in the literature
090   (Yin et al., 2020; Farajtabar et al., 2020; Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Li & Hoiem, 2017; Chaudhry
091   et al., 2019a; Bhat et al., 2023; Rusu et al., 2016; Yan et al., 2021) . These methods aim to mitigate
092   knowledge loss and improve task performance through three main approaches: (1) Rehearsal-based
093   methods, which involve reminding the model of past knowledge by using selective exemplars; (2)
094   Regularization-based methods, which penalize changes in past task knowledge through regularization
095   techniques; (3) Parameter-isolation and Dynamic Architecture methods, which allocate sub-networks
096   or expand new sub-networks, respectively, for each task, minimizing task interference and enabling
097   the model to specialize for different tasks.
098   Rehearsal-based. Experience replay methods build and store a memory of the knowledge learned so
099   far (Rebuffi et al., 2016; Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017; Shin et al., 2017; Riemer et al., 2018; Rios
100   & Itti, 2018; Zhang et al., 2019). As an example, Averaged Gradient Episodic Memory (A-GEM)
101   (Chaudhry et al., 2019a) builds an episodic memory of parameter gradients, while ER-Reservoir
102   (Chaudhry et al., 2019) uses a reservoir sampling method to maintain the episodic memory. These
103   methods have shown strong performance in recent studies. However, they require a significant amount
104
      of memory to store the examples.
105   Regularization-based. A popular early work using regularization is the elastic weight consolidation
106   (EWC) method (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017). Other methods (Zenke et al., 2017; Aljundi et al., 2018;
107   Van et al., 2022; Nguyen et al., 2018; Ahn et al., 2019) propose different criteria to measure the
      “importance” of parameters. A later study showed that many regularization-based methods are


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      variations of Hessian optimization (Yin et al., 2020). These methods typically assume there are
109   multiple optima in the updated loss landscape in the new data distribution. One can find a good
110   optimum for both the new and old data distributions by constraining the deviation from the original
111   model weights.
112
      Parameter Isolation. Parameter isolation methods allocate different subsets of the parameters to each
113
      task (Rusu et al., 2016; Jerfel et al., 2019; Rao et al., 2019; Li et al., 2019). From the stability-plasticity
114
      perspective, these methods implement gating mechanisms that improve stability and control plasticity
115   by activating different gates for each task. Masse et al. (2018) proposes a bio-inspired approach for a
116   context-dependent gating that activates a non-overlapping subset of parameters for any specific task.
117   Supermask in Superposition (Wortsman et al., 2020) is another parameter isolation method that starts
118   with a randomly initialized, fixed base network and, for each task, finds a sub-network (supermask)
119   such that the model achieves good performance.
120
      Dynamic Architecture. Different from Parameter Isolation, which allocates subnets for tasks in a
121   fixed main network, this approach dynamically expands the network structure. Yoon et al. (2018)
122   proposes a method that leverages the network structure trained on previous tasks to effectively learn
123   new tasks, while dynamically expanding its capacity by adding or duplicating neurons as needed.
124   Other methods (Xu & Zhu, 2018; Qin et al., 2021) reformulate CL problems into reinforcement
125   learning (RL) problems and leverage RL methods to determine when to expand the architecture when
126   learning new tasks. Yan et al. (2021) introduces a two-stage learning method that first expands the
127   previous frozen task feature representations by a new feature extractor, then re-trains the classifier
128   with current and buffered data.

131   3     P ROPOSED F RAMEWORK
132
133   We consider the task-incremental and class-incremental learning scenarios, where we sequentially
134   observe a set of tasks t ∈ {1, . . . , N }. The neural network comprises a single task-agnostic feature
135   extractor f and a classifier w with task-specific heads w(t) |N   t=1 . The architecture of f is fixed;
136   however, its parameters are gradually updated as new tasks arrive. At task t, the system receives the
137   training dataset Dttrain sampled from the data distribution Dt and learns the updated parameters of
138   the feature extractor f and w. For easier discussion, the feature extractor and classifier obtained after
139   learning at task t are denoted as ft and wt , respectively. Thus, after learning on task t, we obtain the
140
      evolved feature extractor ft and classifier wt We call the latent space created by the feature extractor
      trained with Dttrain as the t-domain. Catastrophic forgetting occurs as the feature extractor ft′ is
141
      updated into ft , t′ < t, which causes the t′ -domain to be overwritten by the t-domain. This domain
142
      shift degrades the model’s performance over time.
143
144   To overcome catastrophic forgetting, we propose a new CL paradigm: learning a latent rectification
145   mechanism. This mechanism relies on a lightweight rectifier unit rt that learns to align the represen-
146
      tations from the t-domain to the (t − 1)-domain. Intuitively, this module “corrects” the representation
      change of a sample from the old task t−1 due to the evolution of the feature extractor f when learning
147
      the newer task t. These rectifier units will establish a chain of corrections for the representation of
148
      any task’s input, allowing the model to predict the rectified representation better. Figure 1 provides a
149   visualization of the inference process on a task-t sample, after learning N tasks.
150
151   Learning the latent rectification mechanism is central to our proposed framework. In general, each
152
      rectifier unit should be small compared to the size of the final model or the feature extractor f , and
      its learning process should be resource-efficient. The following sections present and describe our
153
      solution for learning this mechanism.

      3.2.1    L ATENT A LIGNMENT
232   For an input x at task t − 1, its feature representation under the feature extractor ft−1 is ft−1 (x).
233   One can heuristically define the (t − 1)-domain as the representation of the input under the feature
234   extractor ft−1 . Unfortunately, the (t − 1)-domain is brittle under extractor update: as the subsequent
235   task t arrives, the feature extractor is updated to ft , and the corresponding feature representation
236   of the same input x will be shifted to ft (x). Likely, the t-domain and the (t − 1)-domain do not
237   coincide, and ft (x) ̸= ft−1 (x).
238   The feature rectifier unit rt aims to offset this representation shift. To do this, rt takes x, and its
239   t-domain representation ft (x) as input, and it outputs the rectified representation that satisfies

463   5     L IMITATIONS
464
465   We have shown the potential and high utility of ILR’s continual learning mechanism in this paper.
466   Nevertheless, ILR also has some limitations. One limitation is that ILR still maintains additional
467   parameters, i.e., the rectifier, which incurs an additional overhead as the number of tasks increases.
468   Inference cost for a long chain would be costly, which can be further explored with modified chaining
469   methods such as skipping (i.e., building a rectifier every two tasks). Additionally, the best performance
470
      is achieved with access to task t − 1’s data. Ideally, we would want to remove this requirement;
      thus, future research should focus on creating the alignment training data. We have attempted to
471
      demonstrate that generative methods are a viable option. Furthermore, since ILR relies on the
472
      task identity to reconstruct the rectifier sequence, application to class-incremental learning settings
473   requires either inferring task identity or forming an ensemble of predictions. The proposed ensemble
474   solution might suffer from over-confident or under-confident classifiers. Class-incremental learning
475   is still an open research, where more effective adaptations of our framework can be discovered.

      6     C ONCLUSION
479
      This work proposes a new CL paradigm, ILR, for task incremental learning. ILR tackles catastrophic
480
      forgetting through its novel backward-recall mechanism that learns to align the newly learned
481   presentation of past data to their correct representations. Unlike existing CL methods, it requires
482   neither a replay buffer nor intricate training modifications. Our experiments validate that the proposed
483   ILR achieves comparable results to the performance of existing CL baselines for task-incremental
484   and class-incremental learning.

772   B       V ERSATILITY OF ILR F RAMEWORK
773
774   In ILR, as the tasks arrive, conventional fine-tuning or training on the new task happens without
775   any CL’s intervention. ILR only augments or adds to this process with a separate training of the
776   backward-recall mechanism. The attractiveness of this framework is twofold. First, ILR allows the
777   best adaptation on the new task to possibly achieve maximum plasticity while the backward-recall
778
      mechanism mitigates catastrophic forgetting. Second, unlike previous CL approaches that heavily
      modify the sequential training process, ILR minimally changes the fine-tuning process, allowing the
779
      users to more flexibly incorporate this framework into their existing machine learning pipelines.
780
781   Relationship to Memory Linking. ILR’s process of mapping newly learned knowledge repre-
782   sentation resembles the popular humans’ mnemonic memory-linking technique, which establishes
783   associations of fragments of information to enhance memory retention or recall. 2 As the model
784
      learns a new task, the feature rectifier unit establishes a mnemonic link from the new representation
      of the sample from the past task to its past task’s correct representation.
