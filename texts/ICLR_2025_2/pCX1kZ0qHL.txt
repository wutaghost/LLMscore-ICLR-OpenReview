                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               We propose a novel ensemble method called Riemann-Lebesgue Forest (RLF) for
010            regression. The core idea in RLF is to mimic the way how a measurable function
011            can be approximated by partitioning its range into a few intervals. With this idea in
012            mind, we develop a new tree learner named Riemann-Lebesgue Tree (RLT) which
013            has a chance to perform Lebesgue type cutting,i.e splitting the node from response
014            Y at certain non-terminal nodes. In other words, we introduce the "splitting type
015            randomness" in our ensemble method. We show that the optimal Lebesgue type
016            cutting results in larger variance reduction in response Y than ordinary CART
017            (Breiman et al., 1984) cutting (an analogue of Riemann partition). Such property is
018
               beneficial to the ensemble part of RLF. We also generalize the asymptotic normality
               of RLF under different parameter settings. Two one-dimensional examples are
019
               provided to illustrate the flexibility of RLF. The competitive performance of RLF
020
               against original random forest (Breiman, 2001) is demonstrated by experiments in
021            simulation data and real world datasets.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Random Forest (Breiman, 2001) has been a successful ensemble method in regression and classi-
027   fication tasks for decades. Combination of weak decision tree learners reduces the variance of a
028
      random forest (RF) and results in a robust improvement in performance. “Feature bagging" further
      prevents RF from being biased toward strong features which might cause fitted subtrees are correlated.
029
      However, the benefit of “feature bagging" may be limited when only small proportion of features are
030
      informative. In that case, RF is likely to learn noisy relationship between predictors and response
031   which in the end makes RF underfit the true functional relationship hidden in the data.
032
033   Many methods have been proposed to tackle this issue. Heaton (2016) proposed to rule out irrelevant
034
      features or perform feature engineering at the beginning of fitting a RF . Another type of ideas is
      to adjust the way RF selecting informative features. Amaratunga et al. (2008), Ghosh & Cabrera
035
      (2021) employed weighted random sampling in choosing the eligible features at each node. Besides
036
      the feature weighting method, Xu et al. (2012) used different types of trees such as C4.5, CART,
037   and CHAID to build a hybrid forest. The resulted forest performs well in many high-dimension
038   datasets. Zhou & Feng (2017) employed a sequential multi-grained scanning to discover the feature
039   relationships.
040
      Most of those methods only deal with classification tasks. In this paper, we fill in the gap by proposing
041
      a novel forest named Riemann-Lebesgue Forest (RLF) which has superior performance than ordinary
042
      Random Forest in regression tasks. The main idea of RLF is to exploit information hidden in the
043   response rather than predictors only. In most types of tree algorithms, people approximate the
044
      regression function in the "Riemann" sense. That means the fitted function fˆ(x) can be written as
045   follows:

186   Another issue for the Riemann-Lebesgue Tree is the prediction. When there comes a new point (x, y),
187   we are not allowed to use the information of its response y. That enforces us to estimate the value of

196   the procedure of fitting a Riemann-Lebesgue Tree. To our best knowledge, this is the first type of

479   5     D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATION
480
481   Theorem 3.1 has shown the benefit of Lebesgue cutting in reducing the L2 error of RLF. Section 4.3
482   further demonstrates the flexibility of RLF in many complicated models and real world datasets with
483   tuned p̃. The asymptotic normality in section 3 is useful for statistical inference such as confidence
484   intervals and hypothesis testings (Mentch & Hooker, 2016). It’s possible to obtain a Berry-Esseen
485   bound of RLF in large N -setting by similar arguments. However, this bound can be worthless in
      practice as it’s requires N ≫ n and we decide not to pursue it in this paper.


                                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Although the experiment results show that tuned p̃ is superior than data-driven p̃, the cross-validation
487   tuning process is less time efficient than data-driven methods in large datasets. How to choose the
488   optimal value of p̃ is an interesting problem. On the other hand, we employ a Bernoulli random
489   variable to determine the splitting type at each non-terminal node. It’s possible that there are other
490   better regularization methods to control the overfitting resulted from Lebesgue splitting. Connecting
491   RLT with boosting would be another riveting direction as RLT is essentially a new kind of base
492   learner in ensemble methods.
493   As we discussed in section 3.3, time efficiency is the main limitation of RLF. For prediction part, we
494   employ random forest locally which is powerful but time consuming for large dataset. Developing
495   a more efficient local model for RLF would benefit RLF in large datasets. The time efficiency of
496   current RLF can be improved by employing less number of subtrees in local forest and setting larger
497   value of control probability p̃ so that each RLT won’t perform too many Lebesgue splittings.
498
      Readers may view the use of Riemann-Lebesgue Tree as counterintuitive if the local models (e.g
499   local forests) can estimate response Y accurately. However, the local models don’t have to be as good
500   as the RLF or RLT since we only require the local models to indicate the direction, not the precise
501   prediction of new points. What’s more, when the response is complicated, as indicated in example
502   2 (mixture model with prior probability), we can employ more Lebesgue type splittings in RLF to
503   partition the range of response Y . As a result, the local distribution of Y becomes simpler which will
504   relax the requirement of the precision of local models. The flexibility of RLF comes from the control
505   probability p̃, which controls the number of Lebesgue type splittings in each RLT and can be tailored
506   to different cases.
507   In more general scenarios, how to cope with possible missing values deserves deep inspection since
508   RLF replies heavily on the information from all variables and it’s possible that the prediction of local
509   RF in RLF could be misled by missing values. Actually, in practice, we can perform imputation by
510   rough average/mode, or by an averaging/mode based on proximities during the data preprocessing,
511   which is out of the scope of our current paper.
