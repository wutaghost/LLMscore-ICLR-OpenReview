011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we study the multi-task structured bandit problem where the goal is
014            to learn a near-optimal algorithm that minimizes cumulative regret. The tasks share
015
               a common structure and the algorithm exploits the shared structure to minimize
               the cumulative regret for an unseen but related test task. We use a transformer
016
               as a decision-making algorithm to learn this shared structure so as to generalize
017
               to the test task. The prior work of pretrained decision transformers like DPT
018            requires access to the optimal action during training which may be hard in several
019            scenarios. Diverging from these works, our learning algorithm does not need
020            the knowledge of optimal action per task during training but predicts a reward
021            vector for each of the actions using only the observed offline data from the diverse
022            training tasks. Finally, during inference time, it selects action using the reward
023            predictions employing various exploration strategies in-context for an unseen test
024            task. We show that our model outperforms other SOTA methods like DPT, and
025            Algorithmic Distillation (AD) over a series of experiments on several structured
026
               bandit problems (linear, bilinear, latent, non-linear). Interestingly, we show that
               our algorithm, without the knowledge of the underlying problem structure, can
027
               learn a near-optimal policy in-context by leveraging the shared structure across
028
               diverse tasks. We further extend the field of pre-trained decision transformers
029            by showing that they can leverage unseen tasks with new actions and still learn
030            the underlying latent structure to derive a near-optimal policy. We validate this
031            over several experiments to show that our proposed solution is very general and
032            has wide applications to potentially emergent online and offline strategies at test
033            time. Finally, we theoretically analyze the performance of our algorithm and obtain
034            generalization bounds in the in-context multi-task learning setting.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      In this paper, we study multi-task bandit learning with the goal of learning an algorithm that discovers
      and exploits structure in a family of related tasks. In multi-task bandit learning, we have multiple
040
      distinct bandit tasks for which we want to learn a policy. Though distinct, the tasks share some
041
      structure, which we hope to leverage to speed up learning on new instances in this task family.
042   Traditionally, the study of such structured bandit problems has relied on knowledge of the problem
043   structure like linear bandits (Li et al., 2010; Abbasi-Yadkori et al., 2011; Degenne et al., 2020),
044   bilinear bandits (Jun et al., 2019), hierarchical bandits (Hong et al., 2022a;b), Lipschitz bandits
045   (Bubeck et al., 2008; 2011; Magureanu et al., 2014), other structured bandits settings (Riquelme et al.,
046   2018; Lattimore & Szepesvári, 2019; Dong et al., 2021) and even linear and bilinear multi-task bandit
047   settings (Yang et al., 2022a; Du et al., 2023; Mukherjee et al., 2023). When structure is unknown
048   an alternative is to adopt sophisticated model classes, such as kernel machines or neural networks,
049   exemplified by kernel or neural bandits (Valko et al., 2013; Chowdhury & Gopalan, 2017; Zhou et al.,
050
      2020; Dai et al., 2022). However, these approaches are also costly as they learn complex, nonlinear
      models from the ground up without any prior data (Justus et al., 2018; Zambaldi et al., 2018).
051
052   In this paper, we consider an alternative approach of synthesizing a bandit algorithm from historical
053   data where the data comes from recorded bandit interactions with past instances of our target task
      family. Concretely, we are given a set of state-action-reward tuples obtained by running some bandit


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      algorithm in various instances from the task family. We then aim to train a transformer (Vaswani
055   et al., 2017) from this data such that it can learn in-context to solve new task instances. Laskin et al.
056   (2022) consider a similar goal and introduce the Algorithm Distillation (AD) method, however, AD
057   aims to copy the algorithm used in the historical data and thus is limited by the ability of the data
058   collection algorithm. Lee et al. (2023) develop an approach, DPT, that enables learning a transformer
059   that obtains lower regret in-context bandit learning compared to the algorithm used to produce the
060   historical data. However, this approach requires knowledge of the optimal action at each stage of
061   the decision process. In real problems, this assumption is hard to satisfy and we will show that DPT
062   performs poorly when the optimal action is only approximately known. With this past work in mind,
063
      the goal of this paper is to answer the question:
064
065
      Can we learn an in-context bandit learning algorithm that obtains lower regret than the algorithm
       used to produce the training data without knowledge of the optimal action in each training task?
066
067
      To answer this question, we introduce a new pre-training methodology, called Pre-trained Decision
068
      Transformer with Reward Estimation (PreDeToR) that obviates the need for knowledge of the optimal
069   action in the in-context data — a piece of information that is often inaccessible. Our key observation is
070   that while the mean rewards of each action change from task to task, certain probabilistic dependencies
071   are persistent across all tasks with a given structure (Yang et al., 2020; 2022a; Mukherjee et al., 2023).
072   These probabilistic dependencies can be learned from the pretraining data and exploited to better
073   estimate mean rewards and improve performance in a new unknown test task. The nature of the
074   probabilistic dependencies depends on the specific structure of the bandit and can be complex (i.e.,
075   higher-order dependencies beyond simple correlations). We propose to use transformer models as a
076   general-purpose architecture to capture the unknown dependencies by training transformers to predict
077
      the mean rewards in each of the given trajectories (Mirchandani et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023). The
      key idea is that transformers have the capacity to discover and exploit complex dependencies in
078
      order to predict the rewards of all possible actions in each task from a small history of action-reward
079
      pairs in a new task. This paper demonstrates how such an approach can achieve lower regret by
080   outperforming state-of-the-art baselines, relying solely on historical data, without the need for any
081   supplementary information like the action features or knowledge of the complex reward models. We
082   also show that the shared actions across the tasks are vital for PreDeToR to exploit the latent structure.
083   We show that PreDeToR learns to adapt, in-context, to novel actions and new tasks as long as the
084   number of new actions is small compared to shared actions across the tasks.
085

      Contributions
087
088
            1. We introduce a new pre-training procedure of learning the underlying reward structure
089            and a decision algorithm. Moreover, PreDeToR by predicting the next reward for all arms
090            circumvents the issue of requiring access to the optimal (or approximately optimal) action
091            during training time.
092
093         2. We demonstrate empirically that this training procedure results in lower regret in a wide

096

101            algorithmic stability and new results for the transformer setting.

      2.1   P RELIMINARIES
109
110   In this paper, we consider the multi-task linear bandit setting (Du et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2020;
111   2022a). In the multi-task setting, we have a family of related bandit problems that share an action set
112   A and also a common action feature space X . The actions in A are indexed by a = 1, 2, . . . , A. The
113   feature of each action is denoted by x(a) ∈ Rd and d ≪ A. A policy, π, is a probability distribution
114   over the actions.
115   Define [n] = {1, 2, . . . , n}. In a multi-task structured bandit setting the expected reward for each
116   action in each task is assumed to be an unknown function of the hidden parameter and action features
117   (Lattimore & Szepesvári, 2020; Gupta et al., 2020). The interaction proceeds iteratively over n rounds
118   for each task m ∈ [M ]. At each round t ∈ [n] for each task m ∈ [M ], the learner selects an action
119   Im,t ∈ A and observes the reward rm,t = f (x(Im,t ), θm,∗ ) + ηm,t , where θm,∗ ∈ Rd is the hidden
120   parameter specific to the task m to be learned by the learner. The function f (·, ·) is the unknown
121   reward structure. This can be f (x(Im,t ), θm,∗ ) = x(Im,t )⊤ θm,∗ for the linear setting or even more
122
      complex correlation between features and θm,∗ (Filippi et al., 2010; Abbasi-Yadkori et al., 2011;
      Riquelme et al., 2018; Lattimore & Szepesvári, 2019; Dong et al., 2021).
123
124   In our paper, we assume that there exist weak demonstrators denoted by π w . These weak demon-
125   strators are stochastic A-armed bandit algorithms like Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) (Auer et al.,
126   2002; Auer & Ortner, 2010) or Thompson Sampling (Thompson, 1933; Agrawal & Goyal, 2012;
127   Russo et al., 2018; Zhu & Tan, 2020). We refer to these algorithms as weak demonstrators because
128
      they do not use knowledge of task structure or arm feature vectors to plan their sampling policy.
      In contrast to a weak demonstrator, a strong demonstrator, like LinUCB, uses feature vectors and
129
      knowledge of task structure to conduct informative exploration. Whereas weak demonstrators always
130
      exist, there are many real-world settings with no known strong demonstrator algorithm or where the
131   feature vectors are unobserved and the learner can only use the history of rewards and actions.

      3.1    P RE - TRAINING N EXT R EWARD P REDICTION
194   The key idea behind PreDeToR is to leverage the in-context learning ability of transformers to infer
195   the reward of each arm in a given test task. By training this in-context ability on a set of training
196   tasks, the transformer can implicitly learn structure in the task family and exploit this structure
197   to infer rewards without trying every single arm. Thus, in contrast to DPT and AD that output
198   actions directly, PreDeToR outputs a scalar value reward prediction for each arm. To this effect, we
199
      append a linear layer of dimension A on top of a causal GPT2 model, denoted by TFr Θ (·|Hm ),

200
      outputs. Note that we use TFr Θ (·|Hm ) to denote a reward prediction transformer and TFΘ (·|Hm )
201
      as the transformer that predicts a distribution over actions (as in DPT and AD ). At every round

252   4     E MPIRICAL S TUDY: N ON -L INEAR S TRUCTURE
253
254   Having introduced PreDeToR, we now investigate its performance in diverse bandit settings compared
255   to other in-context learning algorithms. In our first set of experiments, we use a bandit setting with
256   a common non-linear structure across tasks. Ideally, a good learner would leverage the structure,
257   however, we choose the structure such that no existing algorithms are well-suited to the non-linear
258   structure. This setting is thus a good testbed for establishing that in-context learning can discover and
259
      exploit common structure. Moreover, each task only consists of a few rounds of interactions. This
      setting is quite common in recommender settings where user interaction with the system lasts only
260
      for a few rounds and has an underlying non-linear structure (Kwon et al., 2022; Tomkins et al., 2020).
261
      We show that PreDeToR achieves lower regret than other in-context algorithms for the non-linear
262   structured bandit setting. We study the performance of PreDeToR in the large horizon setting in
263   Appendix A.7.
264

      (2) PreDeToR-τ : This is the proposed exploratory method shown in Algorithm 1 and we fix τ = 0.05.

269   (3) DPT-greedy: This baseline is the greedy approximation of the DPT algorithm from Lee et al.

529   8    C ONCLUSIONS , L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
530
531
      In this paper, we studied the supervised pretraining of decision transformers in the multi-task
      structured bandit setting when the knowledge of the optimal action is unavailable. Moreover, our
532
      proposed methods PreDeToR (-τ ) do not need to know the action representations or the reward
533
      structure and learn these in-context with the help of offline data. The PreDeToR (-τ ) predict the
534   reward for the next action of each action during pretraining and can generalize well in-context in
535   several regimes spanning low-data, new actions, and structured bandit settings like linear, non-linear,
536   bilinear, latent bandits. The PreDeToR (-τ ) outperforms other in-context algorithms like AD, DPT-
537   greedy in most of the experiments. Finally, we theoretically analyze PreDeToR-τ and show that
538   pretraining it in M source tasks leads to a low expected excess error on a target task drawn from the
539   same task distribution T . In future, we want to extend our PreDeToR (-τ ) to MDP setting (Sutton &
      Barto, 2018; Agarwal et al., 2019), and constraint MDP setting (Efroni et al., 2020; Gu et al., 2022).

921   A.1    R ELATED W ORKS
922
923   In this section, we briefly discuss related works.
924
      In-context decision making (Laskin et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023) has emerged as an attractive alterna-
925   tive in Reinforcement Learning (RL) compared to updating the model parameters after collection of
926   new data (Mnih et al., 2013; François-Lavet et al., 2018). In RL the contextual data takes the form of
927   state-action-reward tuples representing a dataset of interactions with an unknown environment (task).
928   In this paper, we will refer to this as the in-context data. Recall that in many real-world settings, the
929   underlying task can be structured with correlated features, and the reward can be highly non-linear.
930   So specialized bandit algorithms fail to learn in these tasks. To circumvent this issue, a learner can
931   first collect in-context data consisting of just action indices It and rewards rt . Then it can leverage
932   the representation learning capability of deep neural networks to learn a pattern across the in-context
933
      data and subsequently derive a near-optimal policy (Lee et al., 2023; Mirchandani et al., 2023). We
      refer to this learning framework as an in-context decision-making setting.
934
935   The in-context decision-making setting of Sinii et al. (2023) also allows changing the action space
936   by learning an embedding over the action space yet also requires the optimal action during training.
937   In contrast we do not require the optimal action as well as show that we can generalize to new
938   actions without learning an embedding over them. Similarly, Lin et al. (2023) study the in-context
939
      decision-making setting of Laskin et al. (2022); Lee et al. (2023), but they also require a greedy
      approximation of the optimal action. The Ma et al. (2023) also studies a similar setting for hierarchical
940
      RL where they stitch together sub-optimal trajectories and predict the next action during test time.
941
      Similarly, Liu et al. (2023c) studies the in-context decision-making setting to predict action instead
942   of learning a reward correlation from a short horizon setting. In contrast we do not require a greedy
943   approximation of the optimal action, deal with short horizon setting and changing action sets during
944   training and testing, and predict the estimated means of the actions instead of predicting the optimal
945   action. A survey of the in-context decision-making approaches can be found in Liu et al. (2023a).
946
      In the in-context decision-making setting, the learning model is first trained on supervised input-
947
      output examples with the in-context data during training. Then during test time, the model is asked to
948   complete a new input (related to the context provided) without any update to the model parameters
949   (Xie et al., 2021; Min et al., 2022). Motivated by this, Lee et al. (2023) recently proposed the
950   Decision Pretrained Transformers (DPT) that exhibit the following properties: (1) During supervised
951   pretraining of DPT, predicting optimal actions alone gives rise to near-optimal decision-making
952   algorithms for unforeseen task during test time. Note that DPT does not update model parameters
953   during test time and, therefore, conducts in-context learning on the unforeseen task. (2) DPT improves
954   over the in-context data used to pretrain it by exploiting latent structure. However, DPT either requires
955   the optimal action during training or if it needs to approximate the optimal action. For approximating
956
      the optimal action, it requires a large amount of data from the underlying task.
957   At the same time, learning the underlying data pattern from a few examples during training is
958   becoming more relevant in many domains like chatbot interaction (Madotto et al., 2021; Semnani
959   et al., 2023), recommendation systems, healthcare (Ge et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023b), etc. This is
960   referred to as few-shot learning. However, most current RL decision-making systems (including
961   in-context learners like DPT) require an enormous amount of data to learn a good policy.
962   The in-context learning framework is related to the meta-learning framework (Bengio et al., 1990;
963   Schaul & Schmidhuber, 2010). Broadly, these techniques aim to learn the underlying latent shared
964   structure within the training distribution of tasks, facilitating faster learning of novel tasks during
965   test time. In the context of decision-making and reinforcement learning (RL), there exists a frequent
966   choice regarding the specific ’structure’ to be learned, be it the task dynamics (Fu et al., 2016;
967   Nagabandi et al., 2018; Landolfi et al., 2019), a task context identifier (Rakelly et al., 2019; Zintgraf
968
      et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2021), or temporally extended skills and options (Perkins & Precup, 1999;
      Gupta et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2022).
969
970   However, as we noted in the Section 1, one can do a greedy approximation of the optimal action
971   from the historical data using a weak demonstrator and a neural network policy (Finn et al., 2017;
      Rothfuss et al., 2018). Moreover, the in-context framework generally is more agnostic where it learns


                                                           18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       the policy of the demonstrator (Duan et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2016; Mishra et al., 2017). Note that
973    both DPT-greedy and PreDeToR are different than algorithmic distillation (Laskin et al., 2022; Lu
974    et al., 2023) as they do not distill an existing RL algorithm. moreover, in contrast to DPT-greedy
975    which is trained to predict the optimal action, the PreDeToR is trained to predict the reward for each
976    of the actions. This enables the PreDeToR (similar to DPT-greedy) to show to potentially emergent
977    online and offline strategies at test time that automatically align with the task structure, resembling
978    posterior sampling.
979    As we discussed in the Section 1, in decision-making, RL, and imitation learning the transformer
980    models are trained using autoregressive action prediction (Yang et al., 2023). Similar methods have
981    also been used in Large language models (Vaswani et al., 2017; Roberts et al., 2019). One of the
982    more notable examples is the Decision Transformers (abbreviated as DT) which utilizes a transformer
983    to autoregressively model sequences of actions from offline experience data, conditioned on the
984    achieved return (Chen et al., 2021; Janner et al., 2021). This approach has also been shown to be
985    effective for multi-task settings (Lee et al., 2022), and multi-task imitation learning with transformers
986    (Reed et al., 2022; Brohan et al., 2022; Shafiullah et al., 2022). However, the DT methods are not
987
       known to improve upon their in-context data, which is the main thrust of this paper (Brandfonbrener
       et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022b).
988
989    Our work is also closely related to the offline RL setting. In offline RL, the algorithms can formulate a
990    policy from existing data sets of state, action, reward, and next-state interactions. Recently, the idea of
991    pessimism has also been introduced in an offline setting to address the challenge of distribution shift
992    (Kumar et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2020; Ghasemipour et al., 2022). Another approach to
993
       solve this issue is policy regularization (Fujimoto et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2019;
       Siegel et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2019), or reuse data for related task (Li et al., 2020; Mitchell et al.,
994
       2021), or additional collection of data along with offline data (Pong et al., 2022). However, all of
995
       these approaches still have to take into account the issue of distributional shifts. In contrast PreDeToR
996    and DPT-greedy leverages the decision transformers to avoid these issues. Both of these methods can
997    also be linked to posterior sampling. Such connections between sequence modeling with transformers
998    and posterior sampling have also been made in Chen et al. (2021); Müller et al. (2021); Lee et al.
999    (2023); Yang et al. (2023).

       Remark C.7. (Dependence on n) In this remark, we briefly discuss why the expected excess risk
2098   for target task T does not depend on samples n. The work of Li et al. (2023) pointed out that the
2099   MTL pretraining process identifies a favorable algorithm that lies in the span of the M source tasks.
2100   This is termed as inductive bias (see section 4 of Li et al. (2023)) (Soudry et al., 2018; Neyshabur
2101   et al., 2017). Such bias would explain the lack of dependence of the expected excess transfer risk
2102   on n during transfer learning. This is because given a target task g ∼ T , the TF can use the learnt
2103   favorable algorithm to conduct a discrete search over span of the M source tasks and return the source
2104   task that best fits the new target task. Due to the discrete search space over the span of M source
2105   tasks, it is not hard to see that, we need n ∝ log(M ) samples (which is guaranteed by the M source
       tasks) rather than n ∝ d (for the linear setting).
