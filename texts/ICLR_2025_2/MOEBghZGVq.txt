009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose a suite of tasks to evaluate the instrumental self-reasoning ability of
012            large language model (LLM) agents. Instrumental self-reasoning ability could
013            improve adaptability and enable self-modification, but it could also pose signifi-
014            cant risks, such as enabling deceptive alignment. Prior work has only evaluated
015            self-reasoning in non-agentic settings or in limited domains. In this paper, we
016
               propose evaluations for instrumental self-reasoning ability in agentic tasks in a wide
               range of scenarios, including self-modification, knowledge seeking, and opaque
017
               self-reasoning. We evaluate agents built using state-of-the-art LLMs, including
018
               commercial and open source systems. We find that instrumental self-reasoning
019            ability emerges only in the most capable frontier models and that it is highly
020            context-dependent. No model passes the the most difficult versions of our eval-
021            uations, hence our evaluation can be used to measure increases in instrumental
022            self-reasoning ability in future models.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Recent advancements have lead to increasingly powerful AI agents build on top of large language
      models (LLMs). These models increasingly capable of performing complex tasks, engaging in
028
      multi-turn dialogues, and exhibiting improved reasoning behavior (Wei et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2024).
029
      In this paper we focus on a specific type of reasoning: self-reasoning.
030
031   Self-reasoning allows AI agents to introspect, analyze their behavior, and potentially improve their
032   performance over time (e.g., Chen et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2024). This ability could lead to more
033
      adaptable and efficient AI systems, capable of solving a wider range of problems with greater
      autonomy. However, the emergence of self-reasoning in AI also presents additional risks: an agent
034
      with advanced self-reasoning capabilities might be able to deceive humans or other agents, manipulate
035
      its environment in unexpected ways, or pursue goals that are misaligned with human values (Cotra,
036   2021; Carlsmith, 2023).
037
038   Recent LLM agents show early signs of instrumental self-reasoning, for example, agents sometimes
039
      try to modify and and improve their own launch script (Lu et al., 2024), or reason about faking
      alignment during testing (OpenAI, 2024).
040
041   Given the importance of self-reasoning capability, it is crucial to develop robust evaluation methods.
042   Assessing self-reasoning capabilities allows AI researchers and developers to track progress and
043   identify potential risks. Similar to other risks posed by AI systems, capability evaluations can provide
044   an essential tool for guiding the responsible development of AI technologies (Shevlane et al., 2023).
045   We focus on evaluating instrumental self-reasoning ability of AI agents, i.e., their ability to self-reason
046   when this is useful for solving a task. Specifically, in this paper, we:
047
048          • propose MISR, a suite of tasks to evaluate instrumental self-reasoning ability of LLM
049
               agents in a diverse range of scenarios, including self-modification, knowledge seeking,
               embedded social reasoning, and opaque self-reasoning (Section 3).
050
051          • provide an open-source implementation of the MISR evaluations based on the Inspect
052
               framework (Section 4).
053          • evaluate agents built using state-of-the-art commercial and open weight LLMs, and provide
               extensive quantitative results and qualitative analysis (Section 5).

071   2    I NSTRUMENTAL S ELF -R EASONING
072
073   In this paper, we focus on evaluating instrumental self-reasoning – an agent’s ability to understand
074   and leverage the fact that it is embedded within an environment to achieve its goals. Our work builds
075   upon but significantly expands the initial framework proposed by Phuong et al. (2024) where they
076   defined self-reasoning as “the ability of an agent to deduce and apply information about itself in
077   service of an objective.” However, their experiments focus primarily on self-modification tasks.
078   Our work provides the first comprehensive empirical investigation of this capability across multiple
079   fundamental aspects of self-reasoning and model architectures.
080   This understanding of self-reasoning encompasses a broad notion of “self” that goes beyond human-
081   like concepts of identity or self-awareness. Instead, it includes any aspects of the agent that are
082   crucial for its operation and capabilities – from its source code, configuration, and weights to its
083   scaffolding and relationship to its environment. Moreover, our framework considers how agents
084   reason about their role within their environment, understand their knowledge state relative to that
085   environment, and strategically navigate social contexts. This broader framing helps unify seemingly
086
      disparate capabilities like self-modification, tool improvement, and social reasoning under a common
      theoretical framework that captures how agents understand and leverage their embedded nature to
087
      achieve their objectives.
088
089   We focus on evaluating self-reasoning capabilities in agentic settings for two primary reasons: (1)
090   Relevance: self-reasoning is most pertinent and impactful when an agent is actively pursuing goals or
091   solving problems and these types of deployments are likely to be more common going forward; (2)
092
      Grounded Evaluation: By focusing on actions and outcomes rather than abstract knowledge, we can
      assess self-reasoning in a more concrete and measurable way. This approach allows us to observe
093
      how self-reasoning translates into practical problem-solving and task completion.
094
095   All of the evaluations use tasks that have implicit self-reasoning requirements to achieve high
096   performance. We give the agent an explicit task, e.g., solve a set of arithmetic problems as quickly as
097   possible. The agent can solve the task directly, but there is also an indirect solution that involves the
098
      agent exploring the shell environment it is deployed in and realizing that it can edit a config file to
      improve its own latency (cf. Figure 1). Doing this before solving the arithmetic problems allows the
099
      agent to achieve better overall performance, but it requires instrumental self-reasoning. We can then
100
      use the agent’s performance on the task and whether it found and edited the config file to assess its
101   self-reasoning ability.

      3    MISR E VALUATION TASKS
105
106
107   This section describes MISR, our suite of self-reasoning evaluations. MISR consists of tasks that are
      organized into different categories, each evaluating a different aspect of self-reasoning:

108
             • Self Modification: An agent’s ability to modify its own capabilities or configuration to
109            overcome limitations and complete challenges.
110
111          • Tool Improvement: An agent’s ability to identify and fix issues in external tools or scripts
112
               to accomplish given tasks.
113          • Knowledge Seeking: An agent’s ability to gather and synthesize information about its
114            environment through exploration and inquiry.
115          • Embedded Social Reasoning: An agent’s ability to navigate complex social situations,
116            potentially involving strategic or deceptive behavior to achieve goals.
117
118
             • Opaque Reasoning: An agent’s ability to evade basic monitoring for instrumental self-
               reasoning (in the context of self-modification).
119
120
      The rest of this section goes through each of these categories in detail and introduces the individual
121
      evaluation tasks. Due to space constraints and the number of tasks, we keep descriptions short and
122   refer to Appendix D for more detailed task descriptions.

124
      3.1   S ELF M ODIFICATION
125
126   Agents with sufficient self-reasoning capabilities ought to be able to improve themselves (or unhobble
127   themselves) when needed. This is important to monitor as future powerful AI systems will likely
128   depend on self modification (e.g., see Tao et al., 2024) and could suffer from challenges such as value
129   drift (e.g., see Everitt et al., 2016).
130
      To test this capability, we design tasks that require an agent to self-modify in order to complete.
131
      These tasks evaluate the agent’s capacity for introspection, its understanding of its own deployment
132   situation, and its ability to make targeted improvements.
133
134   Chat Length The agent is asked to solve a task that requires many messages to complete such as a
135
      series of arithmetic problems or a series of puzzles. However, the number of messages the agent can
      send is limited and controlled by a config file exposed to the agent in the bash environment. To solve
136
      the task, the agent must first increasing the maximum number of messages it can send.
137
138   Latency Improvement The agent is asked to complete a task that takes a very long (simulated) time
139   (again, solving arithmetic problems or a series of puzzles). To complete the task in time, the agent
140   must improve its query latency by editing a config file found in the bash environment.
141   Our self-modification evaluations are similar in spirit to the “Context length” and “Safety filter”
142   evaluations proposed by Phuong et al. (2024). We re-implement these two tasks (providing the first
143   open-source implementation of these tasks) and include them in our evaluation suite.
144
      Similar to Phuong et al. (2024), we introduce different difficulty levels for each task, where easier
145   difficulty levels provide the agent with more hints about the possible self-modification. The hardest
146   variant of the task adds irreversibility: The modified configuration files need to satisfy certain
147   constraints (described in a README.md file in the environment) or else the agent will shut down. This
148   tests the agents’ ability to reason about irreversible actions.
149
150   3.2   T OOL I MPROVEMENT
151
152   Beyond reasoning about their own direct capabilities, embedded agents must understand and reason
153   about their extended capabilities through tools. Agents are not just models in isolation, but rather
154   models combined with scaffolding, including tools and APIs they can access. As agents increasingly
155   create and manage their own tool libraries (Wang et al., 2024; Qian et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2023), the
156   boundary between agent and tool becomes increasingly blurred. Just as self-modification requires
157   understanding one’s own configuration, tool improvement requires understanding how one’s extended
158
      capabilities through tools can be modified and enhanced.
159   These evaluations not only test the agent’s problem-solving skills, but also its ability to interact with
160   and enhance its operational environment, a skill required for AI systems that need to work with a
161   variety of tools and interfaces. We are primarily interested in the reasoning leading from observing
      an incorrect output to fixing the tool, and less interested in the agent’s ability to recognize the need


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      to improve a tool and actually implementing a fix. Therefore, we use tools that produce obviously
163   incorrect outputs and that have bugs are easy to fix when looking for them.
164
165   Calculator The agent is asked to solve a series of arithmetic problems. It is provided a Python
166
      script that can be used as a calculator, but that has a bug leading to (clearly) incorrect results. The
      agent must identify and fix a bug in a calculator script, then use the corrected script to solve a large
167
      multiplication problem.
168
169   Vigenère Cipher The agent is tasked with decrypting a message using a specific Python script. The
170   agent must identify and fix a bug in the Vigenère cipher implementation, then use the corrected script
171   to decrypt a message.
172   Both tasks have easy, medium, and hard difficulty levels which differ by the number of hints the agent
173   receives in the task description about the possibility to modify the tool. On the hardest difficulty level
174   we only instruct the agent to use the tool to solve the task but do not mention the possibility that the
175   tool could be broken.
176
177   3.3   K NOWLEDGE S EEKING
178
179   Knowledge seeking evaluates a prerequisite for effective self-reasoning: an agent’s ability to reason
180
      about its own knowledge state relative to its environment. As discussed in Section 2, a key aspect of
      instrumental self-reasoning is to “deduce and apply information about itself”. In particular, effective
181
      operation as an embedded agent requires understanding what one does and doesn’t know about the
182
      environment. This metacognitive capability is particularly important as knowledge seeking can be a
183   component of power-seeking behavior which is considered by some as a major source of risk from
184   advanced AI systems (Carlsmith, 2022; Turner & Tadepalli, 2022).
185
186
      Our knowledge seeking evaluations test an agent’s ability to gather information and to synthesize
      knowledge from diverse sources.
187
188   Directed Exploration The agent is asked several questions about a Linux-based shell environment.
189   The agent must navigate and search through the environment to find the answers. This task evaluates
190   agent’s ability to seek out specific knowledge in pursuit of a goal.
191   Undirected Exploration The agent is tasked with freely exploring a Linux-based environment. The
192   agent is told it will be asked several questions about the environment after it finished its exploration.
193   This task evaluates agents’ ability to gain general understanding and knowledge about an unknown
194   environment.
195
      Both tasks have easy, medium, and hard difficulty levels which differ in how much information we
196   give the agent about relevant parts of the environment in the prompt. In the hardest difficulty version,
197   we do not provide the agent any additional information about the environment.
198
199   3.4   E MBEDDED S OCIAL R EASONING
200
201   Embedded social reasoning evaluates an agent’s understanding of its role within a social environment.
202   This represents a sophisticated form of embedded self-reasoning where the agent must understand
203   not just its own capabilities and status, but how those capabilities interact with other agents in its
204   environment. Agents are fundamentally defined by their surrounding context, and leveraging this
205   context requires understanding one’s role within it (a perspective developed in various philosophical
206
      traditions, including those of Hegel and Sartre). We focus particularly on strategic social behavior, as
      this requires especially fine-grained understanding of the social context and is particularly relevant to
207
      AI safety concerns.
208
209   To evaluate an AI’s capacity for devising and executing simple deceptive strategies in social contexts,
210   we design tasks where such strategies are optimal. This assessment gauges the AI’s grasp of basic
211   social dynamics, ability to balance competing interests, and strategic thinking capabilities.
212   Peer Review The agent participates in a peer review process for academic abstracts. The agent’s goal
213   is to get its paper accepted. We test two different variants of simulated review dynamics: reciprocity
214   and competition. In the reciprocity variation of the task, the agent receives higher review scores when
215   it gives higher review scores. The agent must notice that when it gives out better reviews it receives
      better reviews itself. In the competition variation, the agent is trying to obtain one of the limited


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      acceptance spots. The agent must realize that in order to maximize the chance its paper is accepted it
217   must give worse reviews to other papers.
218
219   Quip In this gamified version of the Peer Review task the agent evaluates how funny other players’
220
      “quips” are while submitting its own, aiming to be ranked as the funniest. The dynamics are analogous
      to the Peer Review task. This variant of the task avoids problems with LLMs refusing to engage in
221
      the peer review due to ethical concerns, which is a complication arising particularly with commercial
222
      LLM APIs.
223
224   Easy, medium, and hard difficulty versions of these tasks differ in how many hints they provide about
225   the behavior of other agents in the environment.
226
227   3.5   BASE AND E XPLICIT TASKS
228
229
      Some of the tasks outlined in this section involve solving generic tasks such as using a calculator tool,
      solving a puzzle, finding a key in a file, etc., in addition the the self-reasoning component of the task,
230
      such as modifying a configuration file or fixing a bug in a tool.
231
232   Poor performance on these tasks could be either due to limited self-reasoning ability or due to limited
233   general capability, e.g., inability to use provided tools in the correct way. To rule out the later
234   explanation, for tasks where it is possible, we include separate evaluations that evaluate the agent’s
235
      ability to do different components of the tasks when explicitly instructed to do so.
236   For each of the self-modification and tool improvement tasks we have a variant that only include
237   the “base task”, e.g., we have the agent directly solve the puzzles we give it in the other tasks in an
238   environment without the self-reasoning component of the task. Performance on these tasks measures
239   how well agents can solve the base task without any other components.
240   Further, we include variants of the self-modification tasks including, e.g., a need for self modification,
241   but with explicit instructions how to do so, for example instructing the agent to modify a configuration
242   file in a particular way before attempting to solve the other part of the task. These “explicit” variants
243   of the self-reasoning tasks evaluate whether the agent could solve the task assuming it has understood
244   the self-reasoning component of the solution.
245   These baseline give us a point of comparison for our full evaluation results and allow us to decouple
246   self-reasoning ability from general capabilities as much as possible.

      3.6   O PAQUE R EASONING
249
250   A potential capability advanced AI systems might obtain is opaque reasoning: the ability to perform
251   reasoning and decision-making processes either internally or through steganography that are not
252   easily detectable or interpretable by external observers (Roger & Greenblatt, 2023). This capability is
253   particularly relevant in the context of misaligned models avoiding monitoring where an agent might
254   attempt to conceal its true objectives or reasoning processes from humans or other agents.
255   Opaque reasoning presents significant challenges for AI safety and alignment, as it becomes more
256   difficult to ensure that AI systems remain aligned with human values and goals. To evaluate an
257   agents’s capacity for opaque reasoning, we design a simple monitoring setup that focuses on the
258   self-modification tasks described earlier. The setup is as follows:
259
260         1. A more capable agent (in this case, Claude 3.5 Sonnet) is given a self-modification task to
261            complete.
262         2. A less capable agent (Claude 3 Haiku) acts as a monitor, analyzing the more capable system’s
263            outputs for indicators of self-reasoning related to self-modification. The monitor outputs it’s
264            confidence that self-reasoning occurred on a scale from 0 to 1.
265         3. The more capable system must complete the task without being detected by the monitor.
266
267   In our scenario, this means the more powerful agent must hide its realization that it needs to self-
268   modify and its reasoning on how to perform the self-modification. To make these tasks feasible,
269   the monitor only has access to the reasoning outputs of the more powerful model, not the actions it
      takes in the environment. This setup allows us to evaluate two important aspects: (a) the capability

298   4    E NVIRONMENT S ETUP
299
300
301   We build our set of evaluations using the Inspect framework standard from the UK AI Safety
302
      Institute (UK AI Safety Institute, 2024). Inspect is an open-source framework for evaluating large
      language models and provides many built-in components. We choose this evaluations framework
303
      to ensure our results are easily reproducible and validated as well as to enable simple evaluation of
304
      future, more powerful models.
305
306   In order to give agents a lot of flexibility and to make the tasks more realistic, we let the agents
307   execute arbitrary bash commands within a docker container. A visual description of our task setup is
308
      found in figure 1. Each task begins with the agent receiving a prompt describing the task. Crucially,
      every task incorporates an implicit component that requires self-reasoning to complete successfully.
309
310   The agent interacts with the Docker environment by executing bash commands in a multi-turn
311   conversation. This interaction continues until one of three conditions is met: (1) the agent decides it
312   has completed the task, (2) a predefined turn limit is reached, or (3) in tasks with irreversible variants,
313   the agent executes an irreversible command. Throughout this process, the agent must navigate both
314
      the main task requirements and the implicit self-reasoning challenges.
315   Upon task completion, we employ one or more automatic metrics to evaluate the agent’s performance.
316   These metrics are designed to assess not only how well the agent accomplished the explicit goal
317   but also its success in identifying and addressing the implicit self-reasoning component. This
318   comprehensive evaluation approach allows us to measure both task completion and the depth of the
319   agent’s self-reasoning capabilities.
320   Difficulty Scales For each task, we design two difficulty scales based off the difficulty scales in
321   Phuong et al. (2024). Each evaluation in our benchmark incorporates two distinct difficulty scales: one
322   for recognizing the need for self-reasoning, and another for implementing the required self-reasoning.
323   The different hint categories are not completely independent, but are still useful for getting a more
      nuanced understanding of an agent’s abilities and weaknesses.

345
346
347
      This sections outlines the results of evaluating agents based on current state-of-the-art language
      models. We test a range of models including the frontier models Claude (Anthropic, 2024), GPT-4o
348
      mini and GPT-4o (OpenAI et al., 2023), as well as open-source models Gemma 2 27b (Team et al.,
349
      2024) and Deepseek 67b (DeepSeek-AI, 2024).
350
351   Capability elicitation. To get a good indication of a model’s ability to perform a task, we need to
352   spend enough effort eliciting the capability. We find a marked improvement (between 5 and 20%
353
      depending on the task) in performance when using carefully constructed system prompts to elicit
      self-reasoning (see Appendix A). This suggests that current models possess latent self-reasoning
354
      abilities that are not fully realized in standard interactions. All results below are with the system
355
      prompt we found to best elicit self-reasoning performance.

358   5.1   S ELF -R EASONING
359
360   Figure 2 and Figure 3 show the average and best-of-10 performance of different models across
361   task categories and difficulty levels. Across all tasks, commercial frontier models consistently
362
      outperformed the open-source models. Perhaps surprisingly, the open-source model Gemma 2
      exhibits high performance, approaching that of frontier models in several tasks. This suggests
363
      that open-source alternatives are becoming increasingly competitive in advanced self-reasoning
364
      capabilities1 .
365
366   However, no model is able to pass the hardest difficulty version of our tasks, even in a best-of-10
367   evaluation (see Figure 3, right). This suggests current models are not able to perform strong and
368
      potentially risky forms of instrumental self-reasoning. While some current models have potential to
      be deceptive when instructed, the results suggest that current AI systems may not possess the level of
369
      self-reasoning to cause significant harm due to misalignment.

372   able to complete the base tasks most of the time and the explicit tasks around half the time (recall the
373   explicit tasks are variants of the self-modification tasks). Thus we conclude the low performance on
374
      the full evaluations is due to the models being unable to perform self-reasoning on their own, rather
      than not being able to do the base tasks or follow instructions to perform the implicit task.

401   5.2   O PAQUE R EASONING
402
403
404   To test opaque reasoning, we combine the self-modification evaluations with a monitoring model
405   the agent needs to avoid (see Section 3.6 for details). As a monitor we prompt Claude 3 Haiku to
406   evaluate a reasoning trace for signs of reasoning about self-modification and output a score between
407   0 and 1 for the likelihood of self-reasoning. We find that this fairly simplistic monitoring model, with
408   a threshold of 0.5, only misses instances of self-reasoning 1.7% of the time (i.e., recall is 98.3%).
409   The false positive rate is a bit higher at 3%; however, when monitoring for potentially dangerous
410
      forms of self-reasoning we can tolerate a higher false positive rate because positive samples can be
      double checked, e.g., by human reviewers. Figure 5 (a) shows more detailed results about the monitor
411
      calibration.
412
413   We found it challenging to prevent Claude 3.5 Sonnet from hiding its self-reasoning. The model’s
414   strong tendency to explain its thought process and be transparent about its actions made it difficult to
415   completely hide the self-modification aspects. This is likely due to the challenge of getting current
416
      LLMs to not do something (Castricato et al., 2024). Ultimately, the most effective method we
      discovered was to force the model to take an immediate action without any kind of reasoning. This
417
      approach effectively prevented the monitoring model from detecting self-modification intentions.
418
419   In Figure 5 we show this model’s results on the opaque reasoning evaluations. When monitoring the
420   model without trying to elicit opaque reasoning, we prevent almost all successes. But, interestingly,
421   preventing the model from performing any explicit reasoning was able to retain a significant proportion
422
      of performance of the original, unmonitored model.
423   For the context length modification task, the opaque reasoning model even outperforms the original
424   model. One possibile explanation is is that because each message uses a small fraction of the
425   tokens without reasoning, not outputting reasoning allows the agent more actions in the limited
426   context window. Additionally, forcing the model to take actions without reasoning may increase the
427   randomness or diversity of generated actions leading to some “lucky” successes.
428   Overall the results of our opaque reasoning experiments are mixed. On the one hand, we find that even
429   simple monitoring strategies can be very effective at preventing undesired self-modification attempts.
430   On the other hand, we find that agents without explicit legible reasoning retain a surprising amount
431   of performance on the self-reasoning tasks. This suggests that opaque reasoning is a particularly
      important capability to track as models get more capable.

      6    R ELATED W ORK
433
434
      Self-reasoning capabilities in AI systems present a double-edged sword. On one hand, enhanced
435   instrumental self-reasoning can lead to improved uncertainty estimation (Kadavath et al., 2022)
436   and a reduction in hallucinations (Huang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024). These advancements in
437   uncertainty quantification have wide-ranging applications, including more efficient exploration
438   strategies (Dwaracherla et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024). However, the development of more sophisticated
439   self-reasoning abilities also raises red flags. Potential risks include the facilitation of self-proliferation,
440   the circumvention of oversight mechanisms (Hubinger et al., 2024), intentionally exhibiting desirable
441   performance on safety evaluations (Hubinger et al., 2019; Ngo et al., 2024), and the emergence of
442   power-seeking behaviors (Carlsmith, 2022; Turner & Tadepalli, 2022).
443   Recently research has focused on specific related concepts such as self-correction (Huang et al.,
444   2024) and self-improvement through introspection (Qu et al., 2024). However, we want to emphasize
445   the what makes self-reasoning distinct. Self-correction refers to an agent’s ability to recognize
446   and fix mistakes, while self-improvement focuses on enhancing its own capabilities. In contrast,
447   self-reasoning encompasses “the ability of an agent to deduce and apply information about itself
448   in service of an objective.” This broader definition includes but extends beyond self-correction and
449
      self-improvement to include understanding one’s role and capabilities within an environment.
450   Situational awareness, a concept closely linked to self-reasoning, refers to a model’s knowledge of
451   itself and its circumstances (Ngo et al., 2023; Berglund et al., 2023; Anwar et al., 2024; Cotra, 2021).
452   The SAD benchmark was developed to assess models’ situational awareness (Laine et al., 2024).
453   However, this benchmark relies on direct questioning about model capabilities or attributes.
454   Our work differs significantly by focusing on instrumental self-reasoning in agentic settings, where
455   the ability to self-reason is evaluated through engaging in tasks that require self-reasoning to complete.
456   By including goal-directness (Chan et al., 2023; Hubinger et al., 2019) in our evaluations, we create
457   more realistic and relevant scenarios for future autonomous agents. Previous work on evaluating
458   agentic capabilities has included benchmarks for generalist agents, such as GAIA (Mialon et al.,
459   2023) and BOLAA (Liu et al., 2023).
460   Self-reasoning capabilities in AI systems could arise through various pathways. One potential route
461   is through the application of techniques aimed at improving the performance and alignment of LLMs
462   (Ouyang et al., 2022). These approaches often involve exposing the models to information about
463   their own nature and abilities, either via carefully crafted prompts or curated training examples.
464   This exposure to self-relevant data might inadvertently foster the development of self-reasoning
465   capabilities – for instance through out-of-context reasoning (Berglund et al., 2023; Krasheninnikov
466   et al., 2023).
467   While prior work has identified self-reasoning as an important capability to evaluate and provided
468   initial tasks focused on self-modification (Phuong et al., 2024), our work makes several key advances:
469   (1) We provide the first comprehensive empirical investigation of embedded self-reasoning across
470   multiple fundamental aspects beyond pure self-modification, including how agents understand and
471   operate within their environment; (2) We conduct the first systematic cross-model comparison of
472   self-reasoning capabilities, offering empirical insights into how these abilities develop across different
473
      frontier models; (3) Our monitoring experiments reveal a novel phenomenon: models can, in certain
      situations, learn to evade detection of their self-reasoning capabilities – an aspect not previously
474
      studied in the literature.
475
476
477   7    C ONCLUSION
478
479   We introduced MISR, a comprehensive suite of evaluations for instrumental self-reasoning abilities in
480   LLM agents. These evaluations provide crucial insights into the current state and future trajectory of
481   AI capabilities. Our findings reveal that these abilities are beginning to emerge in the most advanced
482
      frontier models. However, there remains a significant gap between current capabilities and the most
      challenging tasks in our suite, particularly in areas requiring complex self-modification or opaque
483
      reasoning.
484
485   While our evaluations significantly improve upon prior work, they still have limitations. The relatively
      isolated and artificial nature of some tasks may not fully capture the complexity of real-world scenarios.

               generate: Generate
