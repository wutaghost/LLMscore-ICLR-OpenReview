                                                 A BSTRACT
013
014
015
               We propose both a theoretical and a methodological framework to address a crit-
               ical challenge in applying deep learning to physical systems: the reconciliation
016
               of non-linear expressiveness with SO(3)-equivariance in predictions of SO(3)-
017
               equivariant quantities, such as the electronic-structure Hamiltonians. Inspired by
018            covariant theory in physics, we present a solution by exploring the mathematical
019            relationships between SO(3)-invariant and SO(3)-equivariant quantities and their
020            representations. We first construct theoretical SO(3)-invariant quantities derived
021            from the SO(3)-equivariant regression targets, and use these invariant quantities
022            as supervisory labels to guide the learning of high-quality SO(3)-invariant fea-
023            tures. Given that SO(3)-invariance is preserved under non-linear operations, the
024            encoding process for invariant features can extensively utilize non-linear map-
025            pings, thereby fully capturing the non-linear patterns inherent in physical sys-
026
               tems. Building on this, we propose a gradient-based mechanism to induce SO(3)-
               equivariant encodings of various degrees from the learned SO(3)-invariant fea-
027
               tures. This mechanism can incorporate non-linear expressive capabilities into
028
               SO(3)-equivariant representations, while theoretically preserving their equivari-
029            ant properties as we prove, establishing a strong foundation for regressing com-
030            plex SO(3)-equivariant targets. We apply our theory and method to the electronic-
031            structure Hamiltonian prediction tasks, experimental results on eight benchmark
032            databases covering multiple types of systems and challenging scenarios show sub-
033            stantial improvements on the state-of-the-art prediction accuracy of deep learning
034            paradigm. Our method boosts Hamiltonian prediction accuracy by up to 40% and
035            enhances downstream physical quantities, such as occupied orbital energy, by a
036            maximum of 76%. Our method also significantly promotes the acceleration per-
037
               formance for the convergence of traditional Density Functional Theory methods.

040   1   I NTRODUCTION
041
042   Electronic structure calculations provide crucial insights into the behavior of electrons in condensed
043   matter, forming the foundation for predicting material properties such as conductivity, magnetism,
044   optical response, and chemical reactivity, playing a key role in applications ranging from semicon-
045
      ductors to renewable energy and catalysis, ultimately helping to reshape our lives. The core of elec-
      tronic structure calculations lies in solving the Hamiltonian matrices, from which critical physical
046
      quantities such as orbital energy, band structure and electronic wavefunction are induced. However,
047
      traditional Density Functional Theory (DFT) methods (Hohenberg & Kohn, 1964; Kohn & Sham,
048   1965) suffer from the extremely time-consuming self-consistent field (SCF) algorithms used to ob-
049   tain the Hamiltonians. These algorithms involve the exhaustive iterative diagonalization of large
050   matrices, with each diagonalization scaling with a high computational complexity of O(N 3 ), where
051   N is the number of atoms in the system. With advantages in computational complexity and general-
052   ization capabilities, the deep learning paradigm has significantly advanced physics research (Zhang
053   et al., 2023). In the task of predicting the Hamiltonians, deep learning approaches (Unke et al.,
      2021; Gu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023b; Gong et al., 2023) bypass the SCF process,


                                                        1
054
      dramatically reducing the computational complexity of solving Hamiltonians, opening up new pos-
055   sibilities for analyzing extremely large atomic systems, enabling efficient materials simulation and
056   design that was previously unimaginable. See Appendix B for more details.
057
058
      However, deep learning methods still face substantial challenges when processing physical systems.
      To align with fundamental physical laws, these methods must strictly adhere to symmetry principles.
059
      For example, physical quantities such as force fields and electronic-structure Hamiltonians must be
060
      equivariant under 3D rotational transformations, i.e., elements from the SO(3) group. Besides, the
061   calculation of physical quantities calls for high numerical accuracy, necessitating that the neural net-
062   works possess strong capabilities to express the complex non-linear mappings from atomic structures
063   to the regression targets. However, it is challenging for deep learning methods to simultaneously en-
064   sure strict SO(3)-equivariance and high numerical accuracy on modeling physical systems. The root
065   cause of this problem lies in the conflict between SO(3)-equivariance and non-linear expressive-
066   ness: specifically, directly applying non-linear activation functions on SO(3)-equivariant features
067   (with degree l ≥ 1) may lead to the loss of equivariance, while bypassing non-linear mappings
068   severely restricts the network’s expressive capabilities and thereby lowering down the achievable
069
      accuracy. This issue is commonly found in physics-oriented machine learning tasks that demand
      both strict equivariance and fine-grained generalization performance, as analyzed by Zitnick et al.
070
      (2022). Some recent efforts have been made to alleviate this issue (Zitnick et al., 2022; Passaro &
071
      Zitnick, 2023; Wang et al., 2024c; Yin et al., 2024) while compromising strict SO(3)-equivariance.
072
073   To address the equivariance-expressiveness dilemma, we make theoretical and methodological ex-
074   plorations on unifying strict SO(3)-equivariance with strong non-linear expressiveness within the
075
      realm of deep representation learning. We are inspired by the insight that invariant quantities in
      transformation (Resnick, 1991) often reflect the mathematical nature of physical laws and can induce
076
      other quantities with equivariant properties, and aim to extend the relationship between invariance
077
      and equivariance from specific physical quantities to representation learning of neural networks.
078   From the perspective of deep representation learning, the attribute that invariance is preserved under
079   non-linear operations is a significant advantage, given its compatibility with non-linear expressive
080   capabilities. Built upon these insights, we propose a solution to the equivariance-expressiveness
081   dilemma by intensively exploring and making use of the intrinsic relationships between SO(3)-
082   invariant and SO(3)-equivariant quantities and representations: we first dedicate efforts to learn-
083   ing high-quality SO(3)-invariant features with ample non-linear expressiveness, and subsequently,
084   we derive SO(3)-equivariant non-linear representations and the target quantities from these SO(3)-
085   invariant ones. Specifically:
086   First, we propose a theoretical construct of SO(3)-invariant quantities, namely tr(Q · Q† ), where
087   tr(·) signifies the trace operation, † denotes the conjugate transpose operation, and Q denotes the
088   SO(3)-equivariant regression targets we aim to predict (e.g. the electronic Hamiltonian in this work).
089   A significant advantage of these SO(3)-invariant quantities lies in the fact that they are directly de-
090   rived from the SO(3)-equivariant target labels and can serve as unique supervision labels for the
091   effective learning of informative SO(3)-invariant features that capture the intrinsic symmetry prop-
092
      erties of the mathematical structure of Q without requiring additional labeling resources.
093   Second, we propose a gradient-based mechanism to induce SO(3)-equivariant representations for
094   predicting the regression target Q in the inference phase from high-quality non-linear SO(3)-
095   invariant features learned under the supervision of tr(Q · Q† ) in the training phase. Taking SO(3)-
096   invariant features as a bridge, this mechanism can incorporate non-linear expressive capabilities into
097   SO(3)-equivariant representations while preserving their equivariant properties, as we prove, laying
098
      a solid foundation for accurately inferring complex SO(3)-equivariant targets.
099   We develop our theory into an SO(3)-equivariant non-linear representation learning method and
100   apply it to the computation of the electronic-structure Hamiltonian. This task poses significant chal-
101   lenges for machine learning techniques due to the intrinsic SO(3) symmetry and high-dimensional
102   complexity of the Hamiltonian, as highlighted by Yin et al. (2024). Our method significantly im-
103   proves the state-of-the-art performance in Hamiltonian prediction on eight databases from the well-
104
      known DeepH and QH9 benchmark series (Li et al., 2022; Gong et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023a).
      It demonstrates excellent generalization performance to both crystalline and molecular systems,
105
      covering challenging scenarios such as thermal motions, bilayer twists, scale variations, and new
106
      trajectories. Furthermore, as observed from the experiments on the QH9 benchmark series, our
107   approach also substantially enhances the prediction accuracy of downstream physical quantities of


                                                         2
108
      Hamiltonian including occupied orbital energy and electronic wavefunction. Moreover, our method
109   also demonstrates superior performance in accelerating the convergence of classical DFT by pro-
110   viding predicted Hamiltonians as initialization matrices. Our leading performance comprehensively
111   demonstrates that our method, while satisfying SO(3)-equivariance, possesses excellent expressive
112   power and generalization performance, providing an effective deep learning tool for efficient and
113   accurate electronic-structure calculations of atomic systems.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
116
117   The SO(3)-equivariant representation learning paradigm (Thomas et al., 2018; Geiger & Smidt,
118   2022) typically developed group theory-based symmetry operators, such as linear scaling, element-
119   wise sum, direct products, direct sums, Clebsch-Gordan decomposition, and equivariant normal-
120   ization, to encode equivariant features. These operators have been used to construct graph neural
121   network architectures for tasks in 3D point cloud analysis (Fuchs et al., 2020), molecular property
122   prediction and dynamic simulation (Musaelian et al., 2023; Liao & Smidt, 2023), as well as Hamil-
123   tonian prediction (Schütt et al., 2019; Unke et al., 2021; Gong et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2023).
124   However, as non-linear activation functions may result in the loss of equivariance, they are restricted
125
      when applied to SO(3)-equivariant features with degree l greater than one. This restriction severely
      limits the network’s capability to model complex non-linear mappings. To alleviate this issue, meth-
126
      ods like DeepH-E3 (Gong et al., 2023), QHNet (Yu et al., 2023b) and Equiformer (Liao & Smidt,
127
      2023) introduced a gated activation mechanism that feeds SO(3)-invariant features (l = 0) into non-
128   linear activation functions and uses these features as linear gating coefficients for SO(3)-equivariant
129   features (l ≥ 1), aiming to enhance their expressive power while maintaining strict equivariance.
130   However, as demonstrated in our numerical study in Appendix H, multiplying SO(3)-equivariant
131   features with linear coefficients may not be fully effective in enhancing non-linear expressiveness.
132
      In order to improve non-linear expressiveness, Zitnick et al. (2022) decomposed SO(3)-equivariant
133
      features into SO(3)-invariant coefficients of spherical harmonic basis functions. These SO(3)-
134   invariant coefficients were processed by non-linear neural networks to enhance expressiveness, with
135   equivariance regained by recombining the updated coefficients with the basis functions. In subse-
136   quent developments (Passaro & Zitnick, 2023; Liao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b;c), this approach
137   has demonstrated a remarkable capacity to fit complex functions. However, as pointed out by exist-
138   ing literature (Zhang et al., 2023), this approach degenerates from continuous to discrete rotational
139   equivariance, losing strict equivariance to continuous rotational transformations due to the decom-
140   position based on inner-product operations with discrete basis functions; Li et al. (2022) proposed a
141   local coordinate strategy, projecting rotating global coordinates onto SO(3)-invariant local ones for
142
      the non-linear neural network to encode. However, this strategy is effective only for global rigid
      rotations and fails to maintain symmetry under non-rigid perturbations like thermal fluctuations or
143
      bilayer twists, as it lacks a neural mechanism to enforce equivariance; Yin et al. (2024) proposed
144
      a hybrid framework consisting of both group theory-guaranteed SO(3)-equivariant mechanisms and
145   non-linear mechanisms to regress Hamiltonians. In this framework, the non-linear mechanisms
146   showed remarkable capability at learning SO(3)-equivariance from the data with the help of the
147   theoretically SO(3)-equivariant mechanisms, and released powerful non-linear expressive capabili-
148   ties to achieve more numerical accuracy. However, the equivariance achieved through data-driven
149   methods does not have strict theoretical guarantee, even with rotational data augmentation. In some
150   applications, the demands for symmetry are extremely high. Even minor deviations from perfect
151   SO(3)-equivariance can result in incorrect physical results. In this paper, we propose a framework
152   that theoretically combines strict SO(3)-equivariance with the non-linear expressiveness of neural
153
      networks to resolve the equivariance-expressiveness dilemma.

182   Due to the intrinsic complexity and non-linearity of physic quantities, neural networks on regressing
183   these quantities are supposed to equip with non-linear mappings to fully capture the intrinsic patterns

192   expressiveness, and effectively apply it to the prediction of SO(3)-equivariant complex physical
193   quantities, i.e., electronic-structure Hamiltonian in this context, is the core problem this paper aims
194
      at solving.

260

262   ing v and f , and applying gnonlin (·) in a stacked manner can yield rich SO(3)-equivariant non-linear

      8   C ONCLUSION
523
524
      We propose a theoretical and methodological framework to tackle the issue of reconciling non-
525
      linear expressiveness with SO(3)-equivariance in deep learning frameworks for physical system
526   modeling, through deeply investigating the mathematical connections between SO(3)-invariant and
527   SO(3)-equivariant quantities, as well as their representations. We first constructs SO(3)-invariant
528   quantities from SO(3)-equivariant regression targets, using them to train informative SO(3)-invariant
529   non-linear representations. From these, SO(3)-equivariant features are derived with gradient oper-
530   ations, achieving non-linear expressiveness while maintaining strict SO(3)-equivariance. We apply
531   our theory and method to the challenging electronic-structure Hamiltonian prediction tasks, achiev-
532   ing dramatic promotions in prediction accuracy across eight benchmark databases. Experimental
533   results demonstrate that this approach not only improves the accuracy of Hamiltonian prediction but
534
      also significantly enhances the prediction for downstream physical quantities, and also markedly
      improves the acceleration ratios for traditional DFT algorithms.

539   This work develops a representation learning method that exhibits strong non-linear expressive ca-
      pabilities while strictly adhering to SO(3) equivariance. This method has demonstrated superior

672   A   D EFINITION OF C ONCEPTS
673
674   This section provides definitions of foundational concepts relevant to the problem addressed in this
675
      paper. For additional background, please refer to the book by Dresselhaus et al. (2007).
676   Definition 1. Group. A set G, denoted as G = {. . . , g, . . . }, equipped with a binary operation ·, is
677
      called a group if it satisfies the following four axioms:
678         1. Closure: For any f, g ∈ G, the result of their operation f · g is also an element of G:
679            f · g ∈ G.
680
681         2. Associativity: For all f, g, h ∈ G, the operation satisfies (f · g) · h = f · (g · h).
682         3. Identity Element: There exists a unique element e ∈ G (called the identity) such that for
683            all f ∈ G, e · f = f · e = f .
684
685         4. Inverse Element: For each f ∈ G, there exists a unique element f −1 ∈ G (called the
686
               inverse) such that f · f −1 = f −1 · f = e.
687
688   Definition 2. SO(3) Group. The special orthogonal group SO(3) is the group of all 3×3 orthogonal
689   matrices with determinant 1. Formally, it is defined as:
690                             SO(3) = {R ∈ R3×3 | RT R = I, det(R) = 1},
691   where RT denotes the transpose of R, and I is the 3 × 3 identity matrix. The elements of SO(3)
692   represent rotations in three-dimensional Euclidean space.
693
694   Definition 3. Group Representation. A representation of a group G on a tensor space T (V ) is a
695   homomorphism ρ from G to the general linear group GL(T (V )), the group of all invertible linear

       I     T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS ON C OMPUTATIONAL C OMPLEXITY
1331
1332   The total number of non-zero Hamiltonian matrix elements to be calculated is proportional to the
1333   number of local atomic pairs in the system, with a complexity of O(N E), where N is the total
1334   number of atoms and E is the average number of neighboring atoms within the cutoff radius per
1335   atom. Since the atomic orbital basis set has a finite range, the Hamiltonian matrix elements vanish
1336   beyond a certain distance. In small systems, where all atoms lie within each other’s cutoff radius,
1337   E scales with N , resulting in a total number of non-zero elements proportional to N 2 . However,
1338   in sufficiently large systems, the finite range of the atomic orbitals ensures that E remains constant,
1339   independent of N . As a result, for large atomic systems, the total number of non-zero elements
1340
       simplifies to O(N ).
1341   The baseline models we select, whether DeepH-E3 or QHNet, are SO(3)-equivariant graph neural
1342   network models with efficient information aggregation and message-passing mechanisms. These
1343   models cleverly balance the locality of Hamiltonian definitions with the long-range interactions
1344   present in the system. As a result, the computational complexity asymptotically scales as O(N )
1345   as N increases, which is consistent with the growth of the scales of non-zero Hamiltonian matrix
1346
       elements. The proposed TraceGrad method directly updates each SO(3)-equivariant feature of the
       baseline models, and the computational amount is proportinal to the number of features of the base-
1347
       line models. Therefore, combining TraceGrad, the computational complexity also scales as O(N ).
1348
1349   Traditional DFT methods require T iterations of diagonalizing N × N matrices, each with a time
       complexity of O(N 3 ), because all occupied states are needed to compute the charge density. As N

1407   Despite the increasing ability of deep learning models to independently handle more electronic-
1408   structure computation tasks, there are still applications with extremely high numerical precision
1409   requirements and very low tolerance for error, where traditional DFT algorithms must perform the
1410   final calculations. In such cases, the predictions from deep models can be used as initial matrices to
1411   accelerate the convergence of traditional DFT algorithms. We evaluate the acceleration performance
1412
       brought by the proposed method for the convergence of classical DFT algorithms implemented

1413
       performance:

1416
1417          • Achieved ratio. This metric calculates the number of DFT optimization steps taken when
1418
                initializing with the Hamiltonian matrices predicted by the deep model compared to using
                initial guess methods like minao and 1e.
1419
1420          • Error-level ratio. This metric measures the number of DFT optimization steps required,
1421            starting from random initialization, to reach the same error level as the deep model’s pre-
1422            dictions, relative to the total number of steps in the DFT process.
1423

1428   mode. It is worth noting that this does not affect the fairness of the comparison, as the achieved
1429   ratio and error-level ratio measure the ratio of iteration counts rather than runtime, and differences
1430   in CPU computation times are negligible in these metrics. From Table 9, we could observe that
1431   the proposed TraceGrad method brings significant improvements to the baseline model QHNet on
1432   the acceleration ratio of DFT calculation, notably reducing the achieved ratio and enhancing the

1590
       SO(3) symmetry with expressiveness to a broader range and more complex groups, e.g., SU(2),
1591   SO(n), and Spin(n), in deep learning research. This would enable deep learning frameworks to
1592   incorporate richer mathematical structures and physical quantities.

       Second, while our theoretical framework, detailed in Theorem 1 and Theorem 2, incorporating con-
       cepts such as SO(3)-equivariant variables Q, SO(3)-invariant features T, SO(3)-equivariant features
1595
       f and v, and SO(3)-invariant features z, along with their mathematical relationships (proven in Ap-
1596
       pendix C), is general in nature and not limited to specific physical quantities, from an application
1597   perspective, the empirical effectiveness of our method beyond predicting Hamiltonian and its down-
1598   stream quantities remains to be validated. In future work, we plan to extend the current methodology
1599   beyond predicting electronic-structure Hamiltonians for predicting a wide range of physical quanti-
1600   ties and properties that exhibit equivariance, such as force constant matrices, Born effective charges,
1601   and more.
1602
       Furthermore, in principle, our approach also has potential to find applications in fields such as
1603   robotics, autonomous vehicles, and motion tracking systems to harmonize SO(3)-equivariance with
1604   non-linear expressiveness. In these areas, data are typically represented as 3D point clouds, which
1605   is conceptually similar to describing the 3D structure of atomic systems using atomic point clouds.
1606   This similarity facilitates the transferability of our method to these domains. For example, in vision
1607   tasks such as autonomous vehicles (Wang et al., 2023), the relative positions of cameras and ob-
1608   jects are not fixed, causing the sampled 3D point clouds to undergo coordinate transformations, with
1609   rotation being a common example. Given the safety-critical nature of these tasks, ensuring the reli-
1610   ability and robustness of pattern recognition systems are of utmost importance. Consequently, there
1611
       is a significant demand for systems that are robust to coordinate transformations of 3D point clouds.
       The mainstream approach has been to approximate SO(3)-equivariance through data augmentation.
1612
       However, this method does not ensure absolute reliability or safety. Our work suggests considerable
1613
       potential for constructing deep models with strong generalization performance, grounded in strict
1614   SO(3)-equivariance, and could contribute to advancements in these fields. Therefore, our next step
1615   may involve applying our method to the autonomous vehicles domain for 3D point cloud object
1616   segmentation and recognition, with the goal of achieving more robust recognition results. Specifi-
1617   cally, in the task of 3D point cloud object recognition, the position vectors of the corner points of
1618   3D bounding boxes relative to their center point are SO(3)-equivariant quantities, corresponding to
1619   Q in this work, while their magnitudes are SO(3)-invariant quantities, corresponding to T in this
       work. These can serve as regression targets and supervision signals for the SO(3)-equivariant and


                                                         30
1620
       SO(3)-invariant branches of our method, respectively, and may enable the learning of informative
1621   SO(3)-equivariant non-linear features for regressing 3D bounding boxes.
