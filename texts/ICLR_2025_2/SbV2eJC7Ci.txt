151   2.2    T HE A DVERSARIAL BANDIT P ROBLEM
152
153   In the adversarial bandit problem, a learner plays a sequential game against nature over T ∈ N
154   rounds. In each round t ∈ [T ], the learner selects (potentially randomly) an action It ∈ [K] and
155
      observes only its loss ℓt (It ). The goal of the learner is to adaptively select actions I1 , . . . , IT ∈ [K]
      such that its cumulative loss is close to the best possible cumulative loss of the best fixed action
156
      i⋆ ∈ [K] in hindsight. Crucially, we place no assumptions on the sequence of losses ℓ1 , . . . , ℓT , and
157
      thus they may be chosen adversarially.
158
159   Before we quantify the performance metric of interest, we provide a formal definition of a bandit
160   online learning algorithm. This definition will be useful for precisely formalizing the notion of
161
      privacy (Section 2.4) and describing our generic transformation of non-private bandit algorithms to
      private ones (Section 3).

314   3.1   P ROOF OF T HEOREM 1
315
316   The conversion behind Theorem 1 is remarkably simple. At a high-level, it just requires simulating
317   the non-private bandit algorithm on noisy batched losses. That is, instead of passing every loss to the
318   non-private bandit algorithm, we play the same arm for a batch size τ , average the loss across this
319   batch, add independent Laplace noise to the batched loss, and then pass this noisy batched loss to the
320
      non-private bandit algorithm. By adding Laplace noise to batched losses as opposed to the original
      losses (as is done by Tossou & Dimitrakakis (2017) and Agarwal & Singh (2017)), the magnitude
321
      of the required noise is reduced by a multiplicative factor of the batch size.
322
323   However, a key issue that needs to be handled when adding noise (whether to batched or un-batched
      losses) is the fact that the losses fed to the non-private bandit algorithm can now be negative and


                                                        6
           Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
           unbounded. Accordingly, in order to get any meaningful utility guarantees, Theorem 1 effectively

           5     D ISCUSSION ON LOWER BOUNDS FOR PRIVATE ADVERSARIAL BANDITS
465
466
           In this work, we provided new algorithms for the private adversarial bandit problem and its expert
           advice counterpart. In the adversarial bandits setting, we provided a generic conversion of a non-
467
           private bandit algorithm into a private bandit algorithm. Instantiating our conversion with existing
468
           bandit algorithms resulted in private bandit algorithms whose worst-case expected regret improve
469        upon all existing work in all privacy regimes. In the bandits with expert advice setting, we provide,
470        to the best of our knowledge, the first private adversarial bandit algorithms by modifying EXP4.
471
472
           An important direction of future work is answering whether it is possible to achieve an additive
           separation in ϵ and T . We note that this is possible in the stochastic bandit setting (Azize & Basu,
473
           2022) as well as the the full-information adversarial online setting (Agarwal & Singh, 2017). To this
474
           end, we end our paper by discussing some road blocks when attempting to derive such guarantees
475        for the adversarial bandit setting.

           5.1   O N THE HARDNESS OF PRIVATIZING EXP3
478
479
           First, we comment on the difficulty of privatizing EXP3. In the full-information setting, a standard
480        privacy analysis for exponential weights shows that for every t ∈ [T ], the per-round privacy loss at
481        time step t is at most 2η, and for η = √ϵT advanced composition yields (ϵ, δ)-differential privacy
482                                p
           with expected regret O( T log(K)/ϵ) (Dwork et al., 2014).
483
484        Unfortunately, it is not easy to bound the per-round privacy loss of EXP3 uniformly across time. This
485        is because EXP3 uses Inverse-Probability-Weighted estimators (Robins et al., 1994) (see Algorithm
           3). Thus the algorithm needs to know not just the arm It but also the probability Pt with which it


                                                            9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      was selected. It is, however, not clear how to account for the privacy cost of releasing Pt and indeed
487   we can construct examples where the per-round privacy loss grows with the time horizon T . We
488   provide a more formal analysis of this issue in Appendix G.1.
489
490   5.2   A LGORITHM - SPECIFIC LOWER BOUNDS
491
492   All existing lower bounds for private bandits are in the stochastic setting and effectively show a
493   lower bound of Ω( Kϵ ) (up to log factors) (Azize & Basu, 2022). In Appendix G.2, we prove a
494   stronger lower bound for a large class of bandit algorithms by exploiting the ability to pick arbitrary
495   sequences of loss functions. Informally, our lower bound holds for any (adaptively) private bandit
496   algorithm that “quickly” reduces the probability of playing a sub-optimal arm.
497   Consider an instance on two arms where arm 1 has loss 12 at each step, while arm 2 has loss 1 at
498   each step. Any algorithm that has regret R must play arm 2 at most O(R) times on this instance.
499   Informally, our lower bound applies to bandit algorithms that drops the probability of playing arm
500   2 to be about R                                                                                  R
                      T within about o(T /R) steps. We note that EXP3 drops this probability to O( T ) in
501   O(log T ) steps. For algorithms of this kind, p our lower bound shows that any ϵ-differentially private
502   algorithm (for ϵ < 1) must incur regret O( T /ϵ). Intuitively, the lower bound follows from the
503   fact that if the loss of arm 2 falls to 0 at step ≈ T /R (while arm 1 is unchanged at 21 ), then an ϵ-
504   differentially private algorithm must pull arm 2 at least 1ϵ times to “notice” this change. Accounting
505   for the accumulated regret in the time it takes to pull arm 2 sufficiently many times, and setting
506   parameters appropriately yields the lower bound.
