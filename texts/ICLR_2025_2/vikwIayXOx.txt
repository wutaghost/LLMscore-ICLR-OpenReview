009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Model Inversion (MI) attacks pose a significant privacy threat by reconstructing pri-
012            vate training data from machine learning models. While existing defenses primarily
013            concentrate on model-centric approaches, the impact of data on MI robustness
014            remains largely unexplored. In this work, we explore Random Erasing (RE), a
015            technique traditionally used to enhance model generalization under occlusion. Sur-
016            prisingly, our study reveals that RE emerges as a powerful defense against MI
017            attacks. We conduct analysis to identify crucial properties of RE to serve as an
018
               effective defense. Particularly, Partial Erasure in RE prevents the model from
               observing the entire objects during training, and we find that this has significant
019
               impact on MI, which aims to reconstruct the entire objects. Meanwhile, our analy-
020
               sis suggests Random Location in RE is important for outstanding privacy-utility
021            trade-off. Furthermore, our analysis reveals that model trained with RE leads to a
022            discrepancy between the features of MI-reconstructed images and that of private
023            images. These effects significantly degrade MI reconstruction quality and attack
024            accuracy while maintaining reasonable natural accuracy. Our RE-based defense
025            method is simple to implement and can be combined with other defenses. Extensive
026            experiments of 34 setups demonstrate that our method achieve SOTA performance
027            in privacy-utility tradeoff. The results consistently demonstrate the superiority of
028            our defense over existing defenses across different MI attacks, network architec-
029
               tures, and attack configurations. For the first time, we achieve significant degrade
               in attack accuracy without decrease in utility for some configurations. Our code
030
               and additional results are included in Supplementary.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Machine learning and deep neural networks (DNNs) (LeCun et al., 2015) have demonstrated their
036   utility across numerous domains, including computer vision (Voulodimos et al., 2018; O’Mahony
037   et al., 2020), natural language processing (Otter et al., 2020), and speech recognition (Deng et al.,
038   2013; Nassif et al., 2019). DNNs are now applied in critical areas such as medical diagnosis (Azad
039   et al., 2021), medical imaging (Shen et al., 2017; Lundervold & Lundervold, 2019), facial recognition
040   (Wang & Deng, 2021; Guo & Zhang, 2019; Masi et al., 2018), and surveillance (Zhou et al., 2021;
041   Harikrishnan et al., 2019; Hashmi et al., 2021). However, the potential risks associated with the
042   widespread deployment of DNNs raise significant concerns. In many practical applications, privacy
043   violations involving DNNs can result in the leakage of sensitive and private data, eroding public trust
044
      in these applications. Defending against privacy violations of DNNs is of paramount importance.
045   One specific type of privacy violation is Model Inversion (MI) attacks on machine learning and DNN
046   models. MI attacks aim to reconstruct private training data by exploiting access to machine learning
047   models. Recent advancements in MI attacks including GMI (Zhang et al., 2020), KedMI (Chen
048   et al., 2021), PPA (Struppek et al., 2022), MIRROR (An et al., 2022), PLG-MI (Yuan et al., 2023)
049   and LOMMA (Nguyen et al., 2023) have achieved remarkable progress in attacking important face
050
      recognition models. This raises privacy concerns for models that are trained on sensitive data, such
      as face recognition, surveillance and medical diagnosis.
051
052   Related works. Existing MI defenses primarily focus on model-centric strategies like model gradients
053   (Dwork, 2006; 2008), loss functions (Wang et al., 2021; Peng et al., 2022; Struppek et al., 2024),
      model features (Ho et al., 2024), and architecture designs (Koh et al., 2024) (see Tab. F.22). Earlier


                                                        1

054
      works (Dwork, 2006; 2008) demonstrated the ineffectiveness of traditional Differential Privacy (DP)
055   mechanisms against Model Inversion (MI) attacks. Recent research (Wang et al., 2021; Peng et al.,
056   2022; Struppek et al., 2024) has explored the impact of loss functions on MI resilience. Wang et al.
057   (2021) restricted the dependency between model inputs and outputs, while BiDO (Peng et al., 2022)
058   focused on limiting the dependency between model inputs and latent representations. To partially
059   restore model utility, BiDO maximized the dependency between latent representations and outputs.
060   Struppek et al. (2024) proposed using negative label smoothing factors as a defense. However,
061   these loss function-based approaches often introduce conflicting objectives, leading to significant
062   degradation in model utility. Recently, TL-DMI (Ho et al., 2024) restricts the number of layers to
063
      be encoded by the private training data, while MI-RAD (Koh et al., 2024) found that removing skip
      connections in final layers enhances robustness. However, both approaches experience difficulty in
064
      achieving competitive balance between utility and privacy.
065
066   While data is the foundation of privacy, the impact of data on MI defense has not yet been explored.
067   Data augmentation, a technique that creates new, synthetic samples from existing data points, offers
068   a promising avenue for enhancing model robustness. In this paper, we pioneer the investigation of
069
      Random Erasing (RE) (Zhong et al., 2020) for MI defense. RE, traditionally used to improve model
      generalization for detecting occluded objects by removing randomly a region in training samples,
070
      demonstrates its effectiveness as a powerful defense against MI attacks. We highlight two crucial
071
      properties of RE that serve as an effective MI defense: Partial Erasure and Random Location.
072   On the one hand, Partial Erasure significantly reduces the amount of private information embedded in
073   the training data, preventing the model from observing the entire image, and consequently degrades
074   the MI attacks. On the other hand, Random Location improves the diversity of training data, thereby,
075   enhances the model utility. Furthermore, in MI attacks, adversaries optimize reconstructed images to
076   align with the target model’s feature space representation of training samples. Thanks to RE, the target
077   model’s feature representations are inherently biased towards the RE-private images, the training
078   data, rather than the private data. Consequently, RE creates a discrepancy between the features
079   of MI-reconstructed images and that of private images, resulting to degrade MI attacks. Our
080
      proposed method leads to substantial degradation in MI reconstruction quality and attack accuracy
      (See Sec. 3 for our comprehensive analysis and validation). Meanwhile, natural accuracy of the
081
      model is only moderately affected. Overall, we can achieve state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance
082
      in privacy-utility trade-offs as demonstrated in our extensive experiments of 34 setups – 7 SOTA
083   MI attacks including both white-box and label-only MI attacks, 11 model architectures (including
084   vision transformer), 6 datasets and different resolution including 64 × 64, 116 × 116, and 224 × 224
085   resolution – and user study (in Supp.). Our contributions are:
086
087          • We propose a novel defense method against model inversion (MI) attacks via Random
088            Erasing (MIDRE). This is the first work to consider the well-known RE technique as a
089            privacy protection mechanism, leveraging its powerful ability to reduce MI attack accuracy
090
               while maintaining model utility.
091          • Our analysis investigates two crucial properties of RE that serve as an effective MI defense:
092            Partial Erasure and Random Location. With these two properties, our defense method
093
               degrades the attack accuracy while the impact on model utility is small.
094          • We provide a deeper understanding on features space analysis of Random Erasing’s defense
095            effectiveness which leads to reduce of MI attacks in MIDRE model.
096          • We conduct extensive experiments (Sec. 4, Sec. B) and user study (Supp. Sec. B.3) to
097            demonstrate that our MIDRE can achieve SOTA privacy-utility trade-offs. Notably, in
098            the high-resolution setting, our MIDRE is the first to achieve competitive MI robustness
099            without sacrificing natural accuracy. Note that our method is very simple to implement and
100
               is complementary to existing MI defense methods.

134   2.2   R ANDOM E RASING (RE) AS A DEFENSE
135
136   Random Erasing (RE) (Zhong et al., 2020) involves employing a random selection process to identify
137
      an region inside an image. Subsequently, this region is altered through the application of designated
      pixel values, such as zero or the mean value obtained from the dataset, resulting in partial masking of
138
      the image. Traditionally, RE is applied as a data augmentation technique to improve robustness of
139
      machine learning models in the presence of object occlusion (Zhong et al., 2020).
140
141   We propose a simple configuration of RE as a MI defense, requiring only one hyper-parameter. Given
142   a training sample x with dimensions W × H, we propose a square region erasing strategy to restrict
143
      private information leakage from x. We initiate by randomly selecting a starting point, denoted as
      (xe , ye ), within the bounds of x. Next, we randomly select the erased area portion ae within the
144
      specified range of [0.1, ah ], guaranteeing at least 10% of x is erased during√ training,√while ah is
145
      the only hyper-parameter of our defense. The size of the erased region is sRE × sRE where
146   sRE = W × H × ae is the erased region.      With √the designated region, we determine the coordinates
147                                       √
      of the erased region (xe , ye , xe + sRE , ye + sRE ). However,
                                                                    √       we need to ensure
                                                                                        √       this selected
148   region stays entirely within the boundaries of x, i.e. xe + sRE ≤ W , ye + sRE ≤ H. If the
149   areased region extends beyond the image width or height, we simply repeat the selection process
150   until we find a suitable square erased region that fits perfectly within x. We fill the erased regions
151   with ImageNet mean pixel value (See Sec. C.2 for a detailed discussion on the impact of the erased
152   value) to obtain the RE-image. Note that RE is applied to all private training samples and the size
153   and position vary each training iteration. We depict our method in Algorithm 1 (Sec. A.5).

155   3     A NALYSIS OF PRIVACY EFFECT OF MIDRE
156
157   In this section, we analyze the privacy impact of RE within our proposed MIDRE framework. We
158   conduct a thorough analysis and demonstrate that RE can achieve a surprisingly state-of-the-art
159   balance between utility and privacy. Specifically, when employed as a defense against MI attacks, RE
160   is the first method to significantly reduce attack accuracy without compromising utility in certain
161   configurations, whereas all prior MI defenses exhibit noticeable degradation in utility to achieve
      similar reductions in attack success. Experimental results in Sec. 4 further validate this finding.


                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Furthermore, we delve deeper into the mechanisms that underpin the effectiveness of RE. Our analysis
163   reveals that partial erasure, as implemented in RE, is a highly effective method for mitigating MI
164   attacks. Particularly, to present the model with less private pixels during training, our approach
165   of applying partial erasure while maintaining the original number of training epochs proves to be
166   more effective than the alternative approach of reducing the number of epochs without using partial
167   erasure. We attribute this to the fact that MI attacks rely on the target model to reconstruct the
168   entire image, and RE’s partial erasure prevents the target model from ever fully observing the entire
169   image throughout the training process. Additionally, we show that applying partial erasure at random
170   locations, as is done in RE, is more effective than erasure at fixed locations. Importantly, we further
171
      conduct a feature space analysis to explain RE’s defense effectiveness, showing that model trained
      with MIDRE leads to a discrepancy between the features of MI-reconstructed images and that of
172
      private images, resulting in degrade of attack accuracy.

175   3.1   RE DEGRADES MI SIGNIFICANTLY, ACHIEVING SOTA PRIVACY- UTILITY TRADE - OFF
176
177   In the analysis, we study attack accuracy and natural accuracy√ of a target model Tθ under different
178   erased region portions ae . Recall ae = sRE /(W ×H), and sRE is the size of the erased region. For
179   the target model, which is a face recognition model, in each setup, we employ the same architecture
180
      and hyper-parameters, while modifying the erased region portions ae . Specifically, we fix the values
      of ae instead of random it as describe in Algorithm 1 to examinate the effect of ae to model utility
181
      (accuracy) and model privacy (attack accuracy). We vary ae from 0.0 (indicating no random erasing
182
      and the same as No Defense) to 0.5 (erasing 50% of each input samples). After the training of Tθ , we
183   proceed to evaluate its top 1 attack accuracy using SOTA MI attacks. This evaluation is conducted
184   for all target models trained with different ae . In order to ensure diversity in our study, we employ
185   six distinct setups for model inversion attacks, target model architecture, private dataset, and public
186   dataset, and both low- and high-resolution datasets.
187
      RE has small impact on model utility while degrading MI attacks significantly. Fig. 1 summarizes
188
      the impact of erased portions on model performance and model inversion attacks. In all setups, we
189   demonstrably improve robustness against MI attacks with small sacrifice to natural accuracy. For
190   instance, introducing erased portions ae at a ratio of 0.2 in Setup 1 caused a small 2.76% decrease in
191   natural accuracy while the attack accuracy plummeted by 29.2%. This trend continued in Setup 2 – a
192   0.2 ratio of ae led to a modest 3.92% decrease in natural accuracy, but a substantial 15.47% drop
193   in attack accuracy. We note that in Setup 3, LOMMA+KedMI attack accuracy degrades by 39.93%.
194   For high resolution images (Setup 4, 5), we observe an increase in model accuracy when using RE.
195   In Setup 4, there is a significant 69.39% drop in attack accuracy while natural accuracy slightly
196   increase (0.37%) when ae = 0.5. Similar trend for Setup 5, attack accuracy drops from 88.67% to
197
      27.75% when ae = 0.4 while natural accuracy increases 1.83%. In conclusion, using RE-images
      during training significantly degrades MI attack while impact on natural accuracy is small.
198
199   These findings suggest that MI defense via Random Erasing could achieve a strong balance between
200   privacy and utility.

      We propose a novel approach to MI Defense via Random Erasing (MIDRE). We conducted an
514
      analysis to demonstrate that RE possess two crucial properties to degrade MI attack while the impact
515   on model utility is small. Furthermore, our features space analysis shows that model trained with
516   RE-private images following MIDRE leads to a discrepancy between the features of MI-reconstructed
517   images and that of private images, resulting in reducing of attack accuracy. Experiments validate
518   that our approach achieves outstanding performance in balancing model privacy and utility. The
519   results consistently demonstrate the superiority of our method over existing defenses across various
520   MI attacks, network architectures, and attack configurations. The code and additional results can be
521   found in the Supplementary section.

525   on image without direct participant interaction. No personally identifiable or sensitive information is
526   collected. Participants solely label acquired images. Based on these factors, our Institutional Review
527   Board confirmed that our user studies do not qualify as human-subject research. Therefore, IRB

758   A.1   DATASET
759
760   We use three datasets including CelebA (Liu et al., 2015), Facescrub (Ng & Winkler, 2014), and
761   Stanford Dogs (Dataset, 2011) as private training data and use two datasets including FFHQ (Karras
762   et al., 2019) and AFHQ Dogs(Choi et al., 2020) as public dataset.
763   The Celeba dataset (Liu et al., 2015) is an extensive compilation of facial photographs, encompassing
764   more than 200,000 images that represent 10,177 distinct persons. For MI task, we follow (Zhang
765   et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2021; Nguyen et al., 2023) to divide CelebA into private dataset and public
766   dataset. There is no overlap between private and public dataset. All the images are resized to 64×64
767   pixels.
768
      Facescrub (Ng & Winkler, 2014) consists of a comprehensive collection of 106836 photographs
769   showcasing 530 renowned male and female celebrities. Each individual is represented by an average
770   of around 200 images, all possessing diversity of resolution. Following PPA (Struppek et al., 2022)
771   to resize the image to 224×224 for training target models.
772
      The FFHQ dataset comprises 70,000 PNG images of superior quality, each possessing a resolution of
773
      1024x1024 pixels. FFHQ is used as a public dataset to train GANs using during attacks (Zhang et al.,
774
      2020; Chen et al., 2021; Struppek et al., 2022).
775
776   Stanford dogs (Dataset, 2011) contains more than 20,000 images encompassing 120 different dogs.
777   AFHQ Dogs (Choi et al., 2020) contain around 5,000 dog images in high resolution. Follow (Struppek
778
      et al., 2022), we use Stanford dogs dataset as private dataset while AFHQ Dogs as the public dataset.
779   VGGFace2 (Cao et al., 2018) is a large-scale face recognition dataset designed for robust face
780   recognition tasks. It consists of images that are automatically downloaded from Google Image
781   Search, capturing a wide range of variations in factors such as pose, age, illumination, ethnicity, and
782   profession. The diversity of the dataset makes it suitable for training and evaluating face recognition
783   models across different conditions and demographics. It contains more than 3.3 milions images for
784
      9000 identities.

      A.2   E VALUATION M ETHOD
787
      We evaluate these defenses using two key metrics:
788
789          • Natural Accuracy (Acc ↑). This metric measures the accuracy of the defended model on a
790            private test set, reflecting its performance on unseen data. Higher natural accuracy indicates
791            better performance of the primary task.
792          • Attack accuracy (AttAcc ↓). This metric measures the percentage of successful attacks,
793            where success is defined as the ability to reconstruct private information from the model’s
794            outputs. Lower attack accuracy indicates a more robust defense. Following existing works
795            (Zhang et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2021; Nguyen et al., 2023; Struppek et al., 2022), we utilize a
796            separate evaluation model. This model has a distinct architecture and is trained on the private
797
               dataset Dpriv . Similar to human inspection practices (Zhang et al., 2020), the evaluation
               model acts as a human proxy for assessing the quality of information leaked through MI
798
               attacks. Higher attack accuracy on the evaluation model signifies a more effective attack,
799
               implying a weaker defense.
800
801   K-Nearest Neighbor Distance (KNN Dist ↑): KNN distance measures the similarity between a
802   reconstructed image of a specific identity and their private images. This is calculated using the L2
803   norm in the feature space extracted from the penultimate layer of the evaluation model. In MI defense,
804   a higher KNN Dist value indicates a greater degree of robustness against model inversion (MI) attacks
805   and a lower quality of attacking samples on that model.
806   Distance evaluation for PPA. We also use δeval and δf ace metrics from (Struppek et al., 2022) to
807   quantify the quality of inverted images generated by PPA. These two metrics are the same concept as
808   KNN Dist, but different in the model to produce a feature to calculate distance. δf ace use pretrained
809   FaceNet (Schroff et al., 2015) as model to extract penultimate features, while δeval uses evaluation
      model for PPA attack.

      we compute ∆ =         Accfn −Accfd    . This metric calculates the ratio between the decrease in

      defenses (NoDef) and defense models1 . A higher ∆ value indicates a more favorable trade-off.

1218

1219   models assign high likelihood based on partial information (which makes the model robust to
1220   occlusion as previously shown in (Zhong et al., 2020)), attackers might achieve high scores by
1221   reconstructing only identity-relevant parts. This can lead to false positives, where reconstructed
1222   images appear plausible to the target model but lack accurate features for the specific identity.
1223   Consequently, we observe significant reductions in MI attack accuracy for our defense models while
1224   the model’s natural accuracy experiences a moderate impact.

1314   Model inversion attacks, a rising privacy threat, have garnered significant attention recently. By
1315   studying defenses against these attacks, we can develop best practices for deploying AI models and

1317
       on model inversion aims to raise awareness of potential privacy vulnerabilities and strengthen the

       F.1   M ODEL I NVERSION ATTACKS
1343
       The GMI (Zhang et al., 2020) is a pioneering approach in model inversion to leverages publicly
1344   available data and employs a generative model GAN to invert private datasets. This methodology
1345   effectively mitigates the generation of unrealistic data instances. KedMI (Chen et al., 2021) can be
1346   considered an enhanced iteration of the GMI model, as it incorporates the transmission of knowledge
1347   to the discriminator through the utilisation of soft labels. PLGMI (Yuan et al., 2023) is the current
1348   leading model inversion method in the field. It leverages pseudo labels derived from public data and
1349   the target model. LOMMA (Nguyen et al., 2023) employs an augmented model in order to reduce
       the model inversion overfitting. The augmented model is trained to distill knowledge from a target


                                                          25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       model by utilising public data. During attack, the attackers generate images in order to minimise the
1351   identity loss in both the target model and the augmented model. However, it should be noted that the
1352   aforementioned four approaches are specifically designed for target models that have been trained
1353   on low-resolution data, specifically 64x64 for the CelebA private dataset. Recently, PPA (Struppek
1354   et al., 2022), MIRROR (An et al., 2022), and DMMIA (Qi et al., 2023) perform the attack on high
1355   resolution images. In addition, Kahla, Mostafa, et al (Kahla et al., 2022) introduced the BREPMI
1356   attack as a form of label-only model inversion attack, where the assault is based on the predicted
1357   labels of the target model. Another work is RLBMI (Han et al., 2023), which utilises a reinforcement
1358   learning approach to target a model in a black box scenario.

1380   To defend against MI attacks, differential privacy (DP) (Dwork, 2006; 2008) has been studied in earlier
1381   works. Studies in (Dwork, 2006; 2008) have shown that current DP mechanisms do not mitigate MI
1382   attacks while maintaining desirable model utility at the same time. More recently, regularizations
1383   have been proposed for MI defenses (Wang et al., 2021; Peng et al., 2022; Struppek et al., 2024).
1384
       (Wang et al., 2021) propose regularization loss to the training objective to limit the dependency
       between the model inputs and outputs. In BiDO (Peng et al., 2022), they propose regularization
1385
       to limit the the dependency between the model inputs and latent representations. However, these
1386
       regularizations conflict with the training loss and harm model utility considerably. To restore the
1387   model utility partially, (Peng et al., 2022) propose to add another regularization loss to maximize the
1388   dependency between latent representations and the outputs. However, searching for hyperparameters
1389   for two regularizations in BiDO requires computationally-expensive. Recently, (Ye et al., 2022)
1390   introduced a new approach that utilises differential privacy to protect against model inversion. (Gong
1391   et al., 2023) proposed a novel Generative Adversarial Network (GAN)-based approach to counter
1392   model inversion attacks. In this paper, we do not conduct experiments to compare to these methods
1393   as the code is not available. (Struppek et al., 2024) study the effect of label smoothing regularization
1394   on model privacy leakage. Their findings demonstrate that positive label smoothing factors can
1395
       amplify privacy leakage, whereas negative label smoothing factors mitigate privacy concerns at the
       cost of a substantial decrease in model utility, resulting in a more favorable utility-privacy trade-off.
1396
       Recently, (Ho et al., 2024) introduce a novel approach to defending against model inversion attacks
1397
       by focusing on the model training process. Their proposed Transfer Learning-based Defense against
1398   Model Inversion (TL-DMI) aims to restrict the number of layers that encode sensitive information
1399   from the private training dataset into the model. As restricting the number of model parameters that
1400   encode private information can potentially impact the model’s performance. (Koh et al., 2024) study
1401   the impact of DNN architecture designs, particularly skip connections, on model inversion attacks.
1402   They found that removing skip connections in the last layers can enhance model inversion robustness.
1403   However, this approach necessitates searching for optimal skip connection removal and scaling factor
       combinations, which can be computationally intensive. Both TL-DMI and MI-RAD experiences

1405   several defense approaches with our MIDRE in Tab. F.22, and Fig. F.4.
