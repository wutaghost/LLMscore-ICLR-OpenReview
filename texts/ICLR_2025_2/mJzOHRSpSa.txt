011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Machine learning models are increasingly adapted in various domains, such as
014            autonomous driving, facial recognition, and malware detection, achieving state-
015
               of-the-art results. However, adversarial example attacks pose a significant threat
               to the reliable deployment of machine learning models in such applications. In
016
               recent years, some powerful adversarial example attacks have been proposed for
017
               the fast and query-efficient generation of adversarial examples, even in black-box
018            scenarios where attackers only have an oracle access to the target model, high-
019            lighting the need for scalable, low-cost, and powerful defenses. In this work, we
020            present two contributions to the domain of black-box attacks and defenses. First,
021            we propose Random Logit Scaling (RLS), a randomization-based defense against
022            black-box score-based adversarial example attacks. RLS is a plug-and-play, post-
023            processing defense that can be implemented on top of any existing ML model with
024            minimal effort. The idea behind RLS is to confuse an attacker by outputting fal-
025            sified scores resulting from randomly scaled logits while maintaining the model
026
               accuracy. We show that RLS significantly reduces the success rate of state-of-
               the-art black-box score-based attacks while preserving the accuracy and mini-
027
               mizing confidence score distortion compared to state-of-the-art randomization-
028
               based defenses. Second, we introduce a novel adaptive attack against AAA, a
029            SOTA non-randomized black-box defense against black-box score-based attacks
030            that also modifies output logits to confuse attackers. With this adaptive attack, we
031            demonstrate the vulnerability of AAA, establishing RLS as the effective SOTA
032            defense against black-box score-based attacks.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   In recent years, due to the remarkable performance of deep neural networks, there has been an
038   increasing demand for their adoption in various domains, such as image classification (Deng et al.,
039
      2009), face recognition (Kim et al., 2022), and malware detection (Raff et al., 2018; Chaganti et al.,
      2022; Deldar & Abadi, 2023). However, it has been shown that deep neural networks are vulnerable
040
      to a range of threats, including poisoning (Zhang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2017b; Xiao et al., 2015),
041
      model extraction (Tramèr et al., 2016), and adversarial example attacks (Goodfellow et al., 2015;
042   Madry et al., 2018; Al-Dujaili & O’Reilly, 2020; Ilyas et al., 2018; Andriushchenko et al., 2020;
043   Ilyas et al., 2019; Cheng et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2019), raising serious concerns regarding their
044   safe deployment. Adversarial examples mislead deep neural networks and other machine learning
045   models by adding a small amount of perturbation to an input, causing the target model to make an
046   incorrect prediction. In the image classification domain, for example, adversarial examples apply a
047   carefully crafted perturbation to an image that causes the label assigned by the model to change to
048   an incorrect one while making sure that the new perturbed image is barely distinguishable from the
049   original image to the human eye.
050   Adversarial example attacks were initially introduced in a white-box setting (Goodfellow et al.,
051   2015; Madry et al., 2018) where it is assumed that the attacker is fully aware of the model’s archi-
052   tecture and parameters. However, a more realistic attack scenario is the black-box threat model (An-
053   driushchenko et al., 2020; Ilyas et al., 2018; 2019; Al-Dujaili & O’Reilly, 2020), where the attacker’s
      knowledge of the model is limited, and they can only observe the model outputs. The black-box

054
      threat model is more representative of real-world scenarios, such as machine learning as a service
055   (MLaaS) deployments. Even in the restrictive conditions of black-box assumption, many powerful
056   adversarial example attacks have been proposed, such as Andriushchenko et al. (2020); Al-Dujaili
057   & O’Reilly (2020), that can generate adversarial examples with only dozens of queries, a modest
058   cost for any capable adversary.
059
      The severe vulnerability of ML models to adversarial examples has led to a plethora of work on
060
      defenses against these attacks. A significant amount of work has been dedicated to improving the
061   adversarial robustness of DNNs using methods such as adversarial training (AT) (Goodfellow et al.,
062   2015; Madry et al., 2018; Li et al., 2022; Rice et al., 2020; Stutz et al., 2020; Singh et al., 2023).
063   However, it has been shown that the improvement in adversarial robustness provided by AT comes
064   with a drop in the model accuracy. Furthermore, the additional overhead of adversarial training
065   makes AT a less practical solution. Ensemble methods (Deng & Mu, 2023; Yang et al., 2021) are
066   another defensive approach against adversarial examples. In this approach, the defender aggregates
067   the predictions of an ensemble of ML models when responding to user queries. A critical disad-
068   vantage of ensemble methods is that their maintenance, storage, computation, and deployment costs
069
      increase with the number of models in the ensemble.
070   As another approach to defeat query-based black-box attacks, several proposals have been made to
071   leverage randomization for defense (Hung-Quang et al., 2024; Qin et al., 2021; Aithal & Li, 2022;
072   Lécuyer et al., 2019; He et al., 2019; Ma et al., 2023). Notably, it has been demonstrated that simply
073   adding random noise to the input is effective against black-box score-based attacks (Qin et al., 2021)
074   at the cost of a slight decrease in the model accuracy; this is a common issue with other proposed
075
      randomization-based defenses. The methods often achieve better robustness at the cost of degrading
      the model accuracy.
076
077   This work presents two key contributions. First, we propose a plug-and-play, zero-effort, and easily
078   deployable defense against black-box score-based attacks, which preserves model accuracy while
079   significantly reducing the effectiveness of state-of-the-art black-box score-based attacks. Second,
080   we introduce the Pendulum attack, an adaptive attack against AAA Chen et al. (2022), a state-
081
      of-the-art (SOTA) non-randomized defense. AAA modifies output logits to reshape the attacker’s
      objective function into a sine-like or linear function with an opposing slope to the original loss, while
082
      also optimizing for reduced calibration error.
083
084   Our proposed defense, Random Logit Scaling (RLS), confuses black-box attackers by outputting
085   confidence scores resulting from randomly scaled logits. We show that our defense substantially
086   reduces the success rate of SOTA attacks compared to state-of-the-art randomization-based defenses
087
      and also AAA by conducting experiments on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet datasets and four image
      classifiers; notably, our defense improves robustness against Square attack Andriushchenko et al.
088
      (2020), one of the most powerful black-box attacks, by up to 80%. Moreover, we show that, unlike
089
      the SOTA randomization-based defenses, our defense maintains accuracy and minimizes distortion
090   to the confidence scores at the same time. RLS allows the deployment of test-time defenses without
091   the additional overhead for training and eliminates the trade-off between robustness and performance
092   by preserving accuracy.
093
      The contributions of this work can be summarized as follows:
094
095          • We propose RLS, a lightweight, zero-effort defensive method against black-box score-
096            based adversarial example attacks and provide an analysis of the effect of logit-scaling on
097            black-box score-based optimizations that attackers use, showing how RLS makes it difficult
098            for the attackers to tackle the optimizations and how it can preserve the model accuracy.
099
100
             • We show that RLS significantly reduces the attack success rate of black-box score-based
101
               attacks through experiments conducted on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 datasets, evaluating
102            ℓ∞ , ℓ2 , and also ℓ0 sparse attacks. In these experiments, we use six state-of-the-art at-
103            tacks and compare RLS with AAA and three SOTA randomization-based defenses. RLS
104            achieves state-of-the-art robustness while maintaining accuracy and disturbing confidence
105            scores less than the state-of-the-art randomization-based defenses. Additionally, we use
106            Expectation over Transformations (EOT) as an adaptive attack against the randomization-
107            based defenses, showing that RLS outperforms other randomization-based defenses against
               the adaptive attacker.

114   2.1   Q UERY- BASED ATTACKS
115   Query-based adversarial attacks involve crafting adversarial examples by repeatedly querying the
116   target model and adjusting the perturbation based on the model output. Query-based attacks can be
117   score-based (Vo et al., 2024; Andriushchenko et al., 2020; Al-Dujaili & O’Reilly, 2020) or decision-
118   based (Vo et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2020); Brendel et al. (2018)). Score-based attacks assume an
119   attacker has access to all or some elements of the probability vector, whereas, in a decision-based
120   attack, the attacker only observes the predicted label. Lacking access to score information, decision-
121   based attacks are often more challenging to conduct, thus increasing the number of queries required
122
      to create adversarial examples and raising the cost of the attack. We only focus on defending score-
      based attacks where powerful adversarial example attacks exist that can generate an adversarial
123
      example with only dozens of queries compared to thousands that decision-based attacks require.
124
125   Black-box score-based attacks do not have access to the model parameters; therefore, they can-
126   not compute the gradient of the loss function with respect to input—the gradient that attackers use
127   to adjust the input in the direction that increases the loss in order to create an adversarial exam-
128
      ple. Black-box attackers try to estimate the gradient or use a proxy of the gradient. Chen et al.
      (2017a) proposed a Zeroth Order Optimization (ZOO) method to estimate the gradient using the tar-
129
      get model’s outputs and then use the estimated gradient to generate an adversarial image. Borrowing
130
      from conventional derivative-free optimization research, Ilyas et al. (2018) proposed using the Natu-
131   ral Evolution Strategies (NES) to estimate the gradient. Bandit optimization with priors (Ilyas et al.,
132   2019) (Bandit-TD) relies on the correlation that the gradients in successive steps of an iterative at-
133   tack demonstrate and that of the neighboring pixels. Bandit-TD exploits these correlations as prior
134   information for more efficient gradient estimation. Al-Dujaili & O’Reilly (2020) proposed Sign-
135   Hunter which improves attack efficiency by eliminating the gradient estimation and only relying
136   on the gradient sign. Andriushchenko et al. (2020) proposed a very powerful and query-efficient
137   adversarial attack that uses random search and adds square-shaped adversarial noises to the inputs
138   to solve the black-box optimization. Square attack demonstrates a high success rate while requiring
139
      only a few dozen queries. BruSLe (Vo et al., 2024) is a SOTA sparse score-based attack that employs
      a Bayesian framework and a learnable mask to identify sparse perturbations under an ℓ0 constraint.

142   2.2   D EFENSE M ETHODS
143
144   Numerous efforts have been made to mitigate the effectiveness of both black-box and white-box
145   adversarial example attacks. One category is adversarial training, where DNNs are trained on a
146   mixture of clean and adversarial samples (Tramèr et al., 2018; Rice et al., 2020; Stutz et al., 2020; Li
147
      et al., 2022). Although adversarial training has been proven effective to some extent, it has several
      limitations, including its high computational cost, reduced accuracy for benign samples, and the risk
148
      of overfitting to adversarial examples. Ensemble methods (Deng & Mu, 2023; Yang et al., 2021)
149
      aggregate the prediction of an ensemble of models in their response but increase the cost of storage
150   and computation. Chen et al. (2022) introduce AAA and propose optimizing output logits so that
151   the output loss takes the shape of a target curve (specifically, a sine function or a linear function
152   with a slope opposite to the true loss function’s slope) to confound the attacker. We show how the
153   deterministic nature of AAA can be used by attackers to evade this defense by proposing a simple
154   adaptive attack against AAA-sine. Additionally, AAA introduces additional overhead due to its
155   logit optimization process, which can be problematic in applications that rely on fast and real-time
156   predictions, e.g., self-driving cars speeding on highways.
157   Randomization-based defenses introduce randomness into the model predictions by randomizing
158   inputs (Qin et al., 2021), outputs (Aithal & Li, 2022), parameters (He et al., 2019; Ma et al., 2023),
159   or the intermediate output features (Hung-Quang et al., 2024) of the model. These methods have
160   been shown to be effective in improving robustness against adversarial examples; however, they can
161   have a negative effect on the model performance by degrading its accuracy. Random Noise Defense
      (Qin et al., 2021) proposes adding a small random Gaussian noise to the input images. Boundary

162
      defense (Aithal & Li, 2022) proposes adding a random noise to logits. Hung-Quang et al. (2024)
163   investigate the effect of adding small random noise to the output of intermediate layers of neural
164   networks and concludes that adding a small Gaussian noise to the outputs of the penultimate layer
165   improves model robustness against adversarial example attacks.

167   3     P RELIMINARIES
168   While white-box attacks assume access to the target model and its parameters, a more realistic threat
169   model is the black-box setting, where the attacker only has an oracle access to the model, meaning
170   the attacker can only observe the output for any query sent to the model. This lack of access to
171   the model parameters prevents black-box attacks from computing the gradient of the loss function
172   with respect to input since the computation is based on backpropagation through the model’s net-
173   work. However, black-box attacks have developed alternative methods to overcome this challenge.
174   Black-box attacks are generally divided into score-based and decision-based attacks, as discussed in
175
      Sections 3.1 and 3.2.
176
177   3.1   B LACK -B OX S CORE -BASED ATTACKS
178
      Black-box score-based attacks assume an attacker can access confidence scores and leverage the
179   scores (Guo et al., 2019; Ilyas et al., 2019; Andriushchenko et al., 2020; Ilyas et al., 2018) to generate
180   adversarial examples. Here, we discuss two common approaches to black-box score-based attacks.
181
182
      Zero-Order Optimization: In black-box settings, since attackers do not have access to the target
      model parameters, they cannot compute the gradient of adversarial loss. An alternative approach
183
      commonly employed is to estimate the gradient using the output values of the black-box model f
184
      for an input x and slightly modified versions of x (Ilyas et al., 2019; 2018). This gradient estimator
185   can be formulated as

192   direction u from a pre-defined distribution, apply it to the sample x, and test the effect of moving

199   3.2   B LACK -B OX D ECISION -BASED ATTACKS
200
      Decision-based black-box attacks differ from score-based attacks in that they assume the black-box
201
      model f solely outputs the model’s final prediction (Brendel et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020). In other
202   words, the model only outputs the label associated with the highest score. Decision-based attacks
203   usually start with a significantly perturbed sample x′ that is misclassified by the target model f , then
204   aim to minimize the distance between x′ and the original sample x while ensuring that the resulting
205   sample is still misclassified as any class other than the true class y for untargeted attacks or as the
206   target class t for targeted attacks.
207
208   4     ATTACKING AAA
209
210   Chen et al. (2022) proposed Adversarial Attack on Attackers (AAA) for defense against black-box
211   score-based attacks. AAA optimizes output logits such that the objective loss function computed by
212   attackers is transformed into a sine-like shape (AAA-sine) or a linear shape with an opposite slope
213   to the true loss. It was shown that AAA-linear is vulnerable to a simple adaptive attack (Chen et al.,
214   2022) (the attacker can simply estimate the gradient and step in the opposite direction or search
215   for the opposite direction in search-based attacks). However, here we propose an effective adaptive
      attack against AAA-sine to show that AAA is vulnerable to adaptive attackers.


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      We propose Pendulum attack, an adaptive attack against AAA-sine that repeatedly changes the di-
217   rection the attacker follows. Both random-search and gradient-based attacks generally follow the
218   direction that minimizes the loss, hence, AAA-sine misleads attackers by reversing the gradient di-
219   rection in some parts of the loss curve, effectively trapping them in local minima. Our Pendulum
220   adaptive attack works as follows: when the attacker fails to find a direction that reduces the loss
221   for k attack iterations, they shift to the opposite direction, one that increases the loss. After several
222   iterations, however, the attacker may become stuck at a local maximum of the sine-like loss curve.
223   To overcome this, after another k iterations with no success in moving up the loss curve, the attacker
224   again changes direction. By iteratively switching directions upon k unsuccessful consecutive attack
225
      iterations, the attacker will be able to climb up the local minima in the loss curve that AAA-sine
      generates and climb back down from local maxima again when it fails to find an upward direction
226
      for k attack iterations. Our results show that the attack success rate of attacks incorporating the
227
      adaptive Pendulum attack improves by more than 35% (as detailed in Section 6.2).

259   5.2   T HE M ETHOD OVERVIEW
260
261
      The intuition behind our proposed defense is that by modifying f (x′ ) in a deceptive way, the attacker
      will not be able to tackle either of the optimizations Eq. (1) or Eq. (2). More concretely, a defender
262
      could blind an attacker by not producing the true logit values, resulting in falsified confidence scores.
263
      Outputting a greater or smaller logit value for each class i leads to a falsified score fi (x) and a false
264   value for the objective functions Eq. (1) and Eq. (2). In other words, masking the scores in a way
265   that falsifies the gaps in Eq. (3) and Eq. (4) undermines an attacker’s effort to minimize the objective
266   values. However, this masking should be done with care since arbitrary modification of logits could
267   lower the model accuracy as the value of each logit directly reflects the ranking of the respective
268   class. A possible approach is to scale output logits by a positive factor m. Scaling logits with a
269   positive value can falsify the values in Eq. (3) and Eq. (4) while preserving the ranking of output
      classes as explained in Sections 5.2.1 and Appendix B.

285
286   Using different values of m to scale the output logits, results in large fluctuations in the value of
287   the objective functions used by the attacker, hence confusing the attacker and making it difficult for
288   both gradient-based and search-based black-box attacks to tackle the optimization problem:
289
      Untargeted Attacks Eq. (4): As long as the untargeted attack is unsuccessful (as long as Eq. (4)
290   is non-negative), maxk̸=y fk (x′ ) represents the runner-up class score, and the top-scoring class is
291   the true class (fy (x′ )). Hence, Eq. (4) effectively subtracts the runner-up class score from the top-
292   scoring class score, i.e., it uses the gap between the two classes to guide the optimization in Eq. (2).
293   However, as the analysis in Appendix B and Appendix C show, scaling the logits with the random
294   coefficient m can cause a falsified lower or higher value for the scores and the gap between the two
295   classes used in Eq. (4).
296   Targeted Attacks Eq. (3): Scaling logits by a positive m affects Eq. (3) similarly to the untargeted
297   attacks. As long as the targeted attack is not successful, maxk̸=t fk (x′ ) represents the top-scoring
298   class, and the target class score ft (x) is a lower-ranked class. As we show in Appendix B, the gap
299   between any two classes is affected by the fluctuations caused by logit scaling in a similar manner.
300
      We investigate the score fluctuations of any two classes in Appendix B and also the fluctuations
301
      to the top-scoring class in Appendix C. As a visual example, Figure 1 demonstrates how scaling
302   the logits with different values of m affects the probability scores of the two top-ranking classes.
303   Here, both a and b are positive values with a < b. As m moves toward b, the gap between the two
304   classes increases. Generally, when a larger m is selected, i.e., when m > 1, the absolute value of
305   all the class logits is increased. As a result, the confidence score of the true class increases. On the
306   other hand, when m approaches zero, the probability vector will get smoothed out, which causes the
307   probability of the top-scoring class to decrease while the lower-scoring classes will generally have
308   higher confidence scores but still lower than the original top-ranked class.

482   For Bandit (Ilyas et al., 2019), Square (Andriushchenko et al., 2020), SignHunter (Al-Dujaili &
483   O’Reilly, 2020), and ZO-signSGD (Liu et al., 2019), RLS consistently outperforms iRND, RFD, and
484   oRND by at least 10% and up to more than 90%. Notably, RLS reduces the attack success rate for
485   the powerful Square attack to only around 21% on VGG-16 and around 40% for both WideResNet-

497   6.5   A DAPTIVE ATTACK AGAINST RLS
498
499   To evaluate the robustness of our defense against adaptive attacks, we use Expectation over Trans-
500   formation (EOT) (Athalye et al., 2018) attack. EOT uses k queries for each sample at each attack
501   iteration and takes an average over the outputs to cancel out the randomness introduced by ran-
502   domized defenses. We report our results in Table 4 for k = 1, k = 5, and k = 10. We use Square
503   attack (Andriushchenko et al., 2020) and limit the number of attack iterations to 1,000 (5,000 queries
504
      for k = 5 and 10,000 queries for k = 10) and use a randomly selected set of 2,000 test samples from
      CIFAR-10 to attack our trained ResNet-18 model. As the results show, our method can withstand
505
      the adaptive attack better. While the adaptive attack can achieve almost 80% success rate against
506
      iRND and 70% against oRND and RFD, our method can still decrease the attacker’s success rate by
507   at least around 45%.
508
509   The results also show that when the defender uses a wider range of m values, the attacker’s success
510
      rate decreases. This is because as the range of possible m values increases, the attacker has to use
      more queries when taking the average to estimate the true confidence scores. In other words, the
511
      attacker has to increase k when the defender uses a wider range of m values. In Appendix E, we
512
      have plotted correlation between true loss and loss under RLS for k = 5, 10, 20, and 100.

515   7     C ONCLUSION
516
517   In this work, we proposed Random Logit Scaling (RLS), a randomization-based, lightweight, and
518   powerful defense against black-box score-based adversarial examples attacks and proposed an ef-
519   fective adaptive attack against AAA. Through extensive experiments on two benchmark datasets,
520
      six SOTA attacks, four state-of-the-art defenses, and four image classifiers, we showed that RLS
      preserves the model accuracy, minimizes distortion to the confidence scores, and outperforms other
521
      defenses against black-box score-based attacks. Furthermore, we showed that RLS is more robust
522
      against the EOT adaptive attack. We also analyzed how scaling logits affects a model and black-box
523   adversaries and how it can be an effective defense against adversarial example attacks. The signif-
524   icant resistance of RLS against the most powerful black-box attacks, combined with its zero-effort
525   implementation and preservation of the model accuracy, makes it a very powerful defense against
526   black-box adversarial attacks.

791   D    A BLATION S TUDY ON RLS D EFENSE C OMPONENTS
792
793   The backbone of RLS lies in two key components: the distribution probability used for sampling the
794   coefficient m and the parameters of candidate distributions. We initially focused on the Gaussian and
795   uniform distributions, and found that uniform distribution works surprisingly well, as demonstrated
796   in Section 6.4, outperforming other defenses by a large margin, notably reducing the success rate of
797   the powerful Square attack by at least 40%.
798   Table 8 summarizes the results of our experiments on the main components of RLS. We run the
799   Square attack (Andriushchenko et al., 2020) against our trained ResNet-18 and use all the 10000
800   testing samples of CIFAR-10 to attack the model. We implement RLS using Gaussian and uniform
801   distributions. The attacker’s query budget is set to 10000 queries, and the detailed settings for the
802   Square attack can be found in Appendix A. It should be noted that since sampling from the Gaussian
803   distribution can result in negative values for m, we have set a minimum sampling threshold of 0.2
804   for all of the experiments conducted using Gaussian distribution.
805   The results show that the uniform distribution outperforms the Gaussian distribution in most cases.
806   However, Gaussian distribution’s effectiveness against adversarial example attacks is comparable
807   to that of uniform distribution, specifically when µ = 1 and σ = 100. The results also indicate
808   that for the uniform distribution, the attack success rate drops as the upper-bound b (lower-bound a)
809   increases (decreases). For all our evaluations in this work, we employed the uniform distribution for
      RLS, leaving a more comprehensive investigation into the choice of distribution for future work.
