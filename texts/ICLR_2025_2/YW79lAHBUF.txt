009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011               Large Language Models (LLMs) can learn new tasks through in-context super-
012               vised learning (i.e., ICL). This work studies if this ability extends to in-context
013               reinforcement learning (ICRL), where models are not given gold labels in context,
014               but only their past predictions and rewards. We show that a naive application of
015               ICRL fails miserably, and identify the root cause as a fundamental deficiency at
016               exploration, which leads to quick model degeneration. We propose an algorithm
017               to address this deficiency by increasing test-time compute, as well as a compute-
018
                  bound approximation. We use several challenging classification tasks to empirically
                  show that our ICRL algorithms lead to effective learning from rewards alone, and
019
                  analyze the characteristics of this ability and our methods. Overall, our results
020
                  reveal remarkable ICRL abilities in LLMs.

023   1       I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Large language models (LLMs) have been shown to exhibit in-context learning (ICL), a form of
026   supervised learning that does not require parameter updates (Brown et al., 2020). ICL relies on includ-
027   ing supervised input-output pairs in the LLM context (i.e., prompt),1 and has been shown as effective
028   with either few (Brown et al., 2020) or many (Bertsch et al., 2024; Agarwal et al., 2024) examples. In
029   this paper, we ask whether the ability to learn in-context extends to the reinforcement learning (RL)
030   paradigm, i.e., whether language models can effectively perform in-context reinforcement learning
031   (ICRL).
032   ICRL is a natural combination of ICL and reinforcement learning (RL). Instead of constructing
033   the LLM context from supervised input-output pairs, the LLM context is constructed using triplets

      consisting of input, model output prediction, and the corresponding rewards. As the number of input
055   examples increases, the model observes more triplets, leading to an online learning scenario. These
056   triplets are followed by a new input, for which the model predicts an output. Figure 1 illustrates ICRL

058   grows over time, and instead of relying on annotated labels and a static dataset, the model is exposed
059   to a stream of inputs and rewards.
060
      We study the ICRL capabilities of Llama 3.1 (Llama Team, 2024) and Phi-3.5-mini (Abdin et al.,
061   2024), using several standard classification benchmarks, focusing on single-step RL (i.e., contextual
062   bandits). Unfortunately, as we show, the naive formulation quickly leads LLMs to degenerate, often
063   by always predicting the same output. We identify two causes for this failure. First, LLMs, even
064   when sampled from, exhibit an inability to explore. Second, they struggle to learn from complex
065   in-context signals, such as when rewards are negative.
066
      We address the exploration deficiency by taking advantage of how LLMs are sensitive to their prompt
067   composition in unexpected ways (Chen et al., 2023; Sclar et al., 2024), and add stochasticity to
068   the prompt construction. Given the difficulty of LLMs to benefit from negative examples, we filter
069   these out from the context, making the prompt more stylistically similar to ICL prompts, and easier
070   to reason about. The stochastic prompt effectively neutralizes degeneration in our experiments.
071   Simplifying the prompt further increases performance, even though it removes informative negative
072   learning signals Overall, these mechanisms expose effective ICRL in LLMs.
073   Our method shows a strong relationship between performance and test-time compute – as compute
074   costs increase, the model also performs better (Snell et al., 2024). Much of the computational cost
075   arises from the stochastic construction of prompts, which occurs for each example and increases in
076   cost as the model observes more examples. To reduce these costs, we show that this process can
077   be approximated, and that the level of approximation is in direct relation to how much compute is
078   allocated to the model.
079   Overall, we show that our approach is able to overcome the exploration degeneration of both Llama
080   and Phi, leading to impressive and consistent gains through ICRL. For example, in the Banking-
081   77 (Casanueva et al., 2020) classification task, Llama improves from 17.2% zero-shot accuracy to
082   66.0% through ICRL. We also show that our approximation of ICRL is effective with both models,
083   although the stronger Llama can absorb a much higher approximation level. Our code, data, and
084   experimental logs will be released upon publication.

087   2    I N - CONTEXT R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
088
089   ICL operates by providing a model with correct demonstrations of a task. A demonstration includes
090   an input (e.g., What is the best football club in Europe?) and its corresponding correct output (e.g.,
091   AC Milan). In its reliance on gold-standard labels, ICL follows the common supervised learning
092   paradigm, although without any change in the model parameters.
093
      However, in-context learning could also be performed differently. Instead of providing models with
094
      correct demonstrations, the model could first try to guess answers, then observe the outcomes (i.e.,
095   rewards) of its predictions, and eventually learn from these signals, in an online learning setting, all
096   within the context. This alternative way of learning in context follows the reinforcement learning
097   paradigm (RL; Sutton & Barto, 2018), where models learn by reinforcing good behaviors and
098   suppressing bad choices.
099
      Formally, we are concerned with an RL scenario where the model π observes an input x(t) ∼ D
100
      sampled from the data distribution D at time t, generates a prediction ŷ (t) , and then observes a reward
101
      r(t) ∼ R(x(t) , ŷ (t) ). We denote the tuple (x(t) , ŷ (t) , r(t) ) as an episode.
102
103   In common RL terminology, the model π is the policy, the input x(t) is the state, and the prediction
104   y (t) is the action. Throughout our formulation, the policy is also conditioned on previous episodes
105   in the form of an LLM context, similar to how supervised examples are provided in ICL. These
106   past episodes are not part of the state. Instead, the context is used to perform in-context policy
107   improvement, similar to how past episodes are used to perform policy improvement in conventional
      RL (e.g., via parameter updates).

      We design several methods to elicit in-context reinforcement learning (ICRL) from LLMs. The
109   naive approach is a straightforward implementation of ICRL following the common ICL recipe
110   (Section 2.1). The explorative approach (Section 2.2) is motivated by the empirical weaknesses we
111   observe in the naive approach (Section 4). Finally, the approximate method comes to reduce some of
112   the computational demands of explorative ICRL.

137   2.2    E XPLORATIVE ICRL
138
      Explorative ICRL addresses the exploration deficiency observed with naive ICRL by leveraging the
139
      sensitivity of LLMs to their prompt. It has been widely observed that changes in prompt composition
140   lead to variance in LLM behavior, including changes in the exact set of examples selected for
141   ICL (Zhang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Levy et al., 2023) or even seemingly
142   meaningless stylistic changes (Sclar et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2022).. Generally, this property of LLMs
143   is not considered positively. However, in the case of ICRL, it provides an opportunity to introduce
144   stochasticity into the process, and thereby introducing a level of exploration. We achieve this by
145   randomly choosing the subset of past episodes to include in the prompt each time the model observes
146   a new input.
147   In addition, we empirically observe that LLMs have a harder time benefiting from negative learning
148   signals (i.e., episodes with negative reward). This has been observed in past feedback-driven
149   continual learning work (Kojima et al., 2021; Suhr & Artzi, 2023). Negative episodes are also not
150   very informative for learning – indicating that one output is bad, essentially encourages an almost
151   uniform distribution over outputs. This leads to the second design decision in explorative ICRL: only
152   include examples with a positive reward in the constructed contexts.
153   Algorithm 2 describes explorative ICRL. For each input, we construct a new context (lines 3–7).
154   We decide what past episodes to include in this context by sampling from a Bernoulli variable
155   parameterized by pkeep (lines 4–7. We sample independently for each past episode. This results in
156   different reasoning for each input, because each is done with a different context. When storing past
157   episodes, we only include episodes with positive reward (lines 13–14).

      Depending on pkeep , explorative ICRL will encounter the issue of the LLM context window saturating
186   much later than naive. However, deploy ICRL for enough interactions, and the context window will
187   saturate, even for the models with the largest context windows. Similar to naive, we downsample the
188   context if it overflows the LLM context window (line 9). We design three strategies to downsample
189   the context if we reach the limit of the LLM context window: (a) unbiased: randomly remove
190   episodes from C (t) until it fits the context window; (b) start-biased: use the longest possible prefix of
191   episodes from C (t) such that it fits the LLM context size; and (c) end-biased: use the longest possible
192   suffix.
193
      A downside of explorative ICRL is the computational cost. While naive ICRL benefits from caching,
194
      caching is not useful for explorative, because the construction of a fresh context C (t) for each episode
195   eliminates this option for explorative ICRL. The probability of encountering the same context twice,
196   or even the same prefix, is exceptionally low even after a few episodes. This means that the context
197   has to be computed from scratch for each input. If pkeep is relatively small, contexts are likely to
198   be much shorter than in naive. Although this can reduce the cost, explorative remains much more
199   computationally demanding than naive.
200
201   2.3    A PPROXIMATE ICRL
202
203   Explorative ICRL addresses the exploration deficiencies of naive ICRL, but incurs high computational
204   costs (Section 2.2). We propose an approximation of explorative ICRL that strikes a balance between
205
      computational cost and learning effectiveness. Similar to explorative ICRL, the approximate version
      also excludes episodes with negative reward and focuses on exploration by stochasticity in the context.
206
207   Algorithm 3 describes approximate ICRL. The core idea behind the approximation is to limit the
208   number of contexts, so we can simply gradually expand them with new episodes, rather than always
209   create and compute new contexts. We maintain K contexts C, which all start empty (line 1). At each
210   time step t, we sample a context C from the K contexts (line 3), and use it for episode t (lines 4–6. If
211   the reward r(t) > 0, we use the episode to expand all contexts stochastically. For each context in C,
212
      we expand it with the t-th episode with a probability of pkeep (lines 8–11).
213   Approximate ICRL introduces stochasticity in two places: sampling the context to use for each
214   episode and the expansion of the stored contexts. In Algorithm 3, we use uniform sampling to choose
215   the context (line 3). This is a uniform approximation of the probability of a context, which can also
      be easily computed exactly using the probabilities of the episodes it contains and pkeep . In practice,

217   have low probability episodes quickly receive very low probability, and are not used. Figure 5b shows

220
      The level of approximation the algorithm provides depends on the resources available. For example,
      one can allocate each context to a compute unit, so a machine with eight compute units (e.g., GPUs)
221
      will support K = 8. Approximate ICRL is a strict approximation of explorative ICRL in the sense
222
      that coupling the exact context sampling strategy and K → ∞ gives explorative ICRL. However,
223   the approximation is limited in handling contexts that extend beyond the LLM window length.
224   Overcoming this while maintaining the efficiency of the approximation is an important direction for
225   future work.

      3   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
229
230   Models We use the instruction-tuned versions of Llama 3.1 8B (Llama Team, 2024) and Phi-3.5-
231   mini 3.8B (Abdin et al., 2024). We chose these model families because, at the time of this work, they
232
      are the only popular open-source large language models that support more than 100k tokens in the
      context, while still having less than 10B parameters. We use both in a chat-like format, with multiple
233
      turns. We compute the maximum number of episodes the context window can take for each model
234
      and task combination. Appendix A.2 reports the exact numbers. Both models are used for the main
235   experiments, but only Llama for a few secondary experiments, due to the computational costs. We
236   use constrained decoding when generating model predictions, similar to recent work on ICL (Bertsch
237   et al., 2024).
238
239   Tasks We follow Bertsch et al.’s (2024) study of many-shot ICL in focusing on five classification
240   problems: Banking-77 (77 labels; Casanueva et al., 2020), Clinic-150 (150 labels; Larson et al.,
241
      2019), NLU (68 labels; Liu et al., 2021), TREC (6 labels: Li & Roth, 2002; Hovy et al., 2001), and
      TREC-fine (50 labels; Li & Roth, 2002; Hovy et al., 2001). Because of the large output spaces (up
242
      to 150 labels in Clinic-150), these tasks are challenging for large language models, as empirically
243
      shown by Bertsch et al. (2024) and replicated in our ICL experiments.
244
245   The datasets are of different sizes. The size of the datasets dictates the number of time steps in our
246   experiments. We randomly sub-sample Banking-77, Clinic-150, and NLU to 10k examples. TREC
247
      and TREC-fine are smaller, so we only use 5k training examples for each. This allows the experiments
      to be of relatively standard length. The training data corresponds to the data distribution D in our
248
      algorithms. We also sub-sample all test sets to 500 examples each, to reduce the computational cost
249
      of experiments. NLU does not provide a standard test set, so we create our own train and test splits.
250   In all experiments, the datasets contain the same examples in the same order.
251
252   Rewards and Prompt Design We use a deterministic binary reward function. Rewards are
253   computed from the gold-standard labels in the dataset. We automatically transform the numerical
254   rewards into a natural language format indicating if the model prediction is correct or not, which
255   is more suitable for LLM reasoning. Appendix A.1 provides more details about our prompting
256   decisions.
257
      Evaluation We report running test accuracy. For test accuracy, we use the held-out test set of each
258
      dataset. We compute it every 500 steps for each test example separately, using the context used to
259   process that step’s training example. In some cases, we also report train accuracy as the running mean
260   accuracy over the most recent 256 episodes.
261
262
      We also report regret, the forgone utility from an actual model prediction in comparison to the oracle
      choice. Intuitively, regret measures how many interactions the model handled poorly throughout the
263
      experiment. In our experiments, regret is the accumulated number of incorrect examples throughout
264
      learning. Regret gives a single number that considers both the final performance and how fast the
265   model reached it. A good system would reach high performance as fast as possible, making fewer
266   mistakes overall (i.e., would have a low regret).
267

269   to the performance on the test set at step zero (i.e., without any in-context examples). We also report
      supervised ICL performance for all tasks to contextualize the results. We generally expect supervised

      In-Context (Supervised) Learning ICL was first demonstrated by Brown et al. (2020), and since
472
      then its causes (Chan et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2022; Olsson et al., 2022; Garg et al., 2022; Von Oswald
473
      et al., 2023; Hendel et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) and the level of learning it displays (Min et al.,
474   2022; Lyu et al., 2023) have been studied extensively. By now, it is well established that LLMs can
475   learn new tasks in context (Garg et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2023b; Kossen et al., 2024;
476   Li et al., 2024). Our work builds on this line of work, and provides the first evidence that LLMs can
477   perform RL in context, and not only supervised learning (i.e., the standard way it is done).
478
      Our study would not be possible without recent increases in the context window length of
479
      LLMs (Llama Team, 2024; Abdin et al., 2024; Gemini Team, 2024). Recent work showed that
480   model performance can continue to increase when including hundreds or thousands of demonstra-
481   tions (Bertsch et al., 2024; Agarwal et al., 2024). We find similar results, as LLMs can continually
482   improve when learning through ICRL until their context does not saturate. Interestingly, while some
483   work (Zhang et al., 2024; Mo et al., 2024; Shinn et al., 2024) find that models can learn from mistakes,
484   our results do not support this. It is possible that models can learn from mistakes only when explicitly
485   reasoning (Kojima et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022) about them (Zhang et al., 2024; Shinn et al., 2024)
      and cannot implicitly leverage negative signals.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      In-Context Reinforcement Learning Likely the closest work to ours is Krishnamurthy et al.
487   (2024). They investigate whether LLMs can solve multi-armed bandit problems, a state-less simpler
488   RL setting than the one we are focused on. Our experimental setup revolves around contextual bandit
489   problems, where the best action depends on the specific input. We observe similar issues to their
490   findings with the Naive approach. They present a set of negative results, and finally are able to elicit
491   effective learning, but through a prompting strategy that cannot generalize beyond their very simple
492   scenario. They conclude LLMs cannot explore, similar to our conclusions from Naive. However, we
493   address this problem by developing Explorative, which includes stochasticity and focuses on positive
494   episodes. Wu et al. (2024) propose a set of benchmarks that includes a simplified multi-armed bandit
495
      problem. The provide baseline performance with a method similar to Naive, showing mixed results
      even given the extreme simplicity of their setting, compared to that of Krishnamurthy et al. (2024).
496
497   Another related line of research is that of Transformers trained to solve sequential decision-making
498   problems (Janner et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022; Laskin et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2022;
499   Lee et al., 2023; Grigsby et al., 2023; Raparthy et al., 2023). In all these cases, Transformers (Vaswani,
500   2017) are trained from scratch. Our focus is different: we study ICRL that emerges from the process
501
      of training LLMs, without fine-tuning the LLM for this purpose.

      6    D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
505
506   We study the potential of LLMs to demonstrate ICRL, and propose several algorithms to elicit this
507   kind of behavior: Naive, Explorative, and Approximate. Naive fails miserably, but this allows us to
508   identify exploration as the key missing ingredient. Explorative introduces stochasticity to the prompt
509   construction, and combined with focusing on positive examples, shows consistent ICRL. However,
510   this comes at a high computational cost. The third algorithm we proposes, Approximate, comes to
511   address this cost, by a strict approximation of Explorative. We provide a detailed analysis of the
512
      various methods, and show the importance of each of our choices, and the sensitivity of the process
      to various settings.
513
514   Our work carries several limitations, all outline important directions for future work. The first is
515   due to our choice of problems to study. We intentionally selected classification benchmarks to
516   simplify the experiments and evaluation in this early stage of studying ICRL. However, this leaves
517   open the question of applicability to more complex problems, where rewards are more nuanced.
518
      For example, summarization and question answering provide much more formidable challenges,
      albeit with complex evaluation challenges. We believe our work enables future work to study these
519
      challenges, and that this is an important direction.
520
521   Another limitation, is our use of a binary reward function. This choice directly falls off our choice
522   of classification problems. It is another important aspect in making our benchmark environment
523   straightforward to experiment with. However, it leaves a very important question open: can ICRL
524
      handle more nuanced reward signals? For example, a reward function that can give all possible real
      number in a specific range. Such a reward function leads to an interesting challenge in decoding it
525
      into language. It is a particularly important question given our findings with regard to the ability of
526
      LLMs to learn in-context from negative rewards. The problem we identified with reasoning about
527   episodes with negative rewards pose another limitation, and lays out an important research question
528   for future work.
529
530
      Our work also lays out open questions as far as the use of computational resources. Our methods
      are relatively compute intensive, especially after the learner observes many episodes. We propose
531
      Approximate to address this, and show how it allows to trade-off compute for robustness. However,
532
      Approximate left open the problem of working with limited context window, a critical problem for
533   deploying these methods for extended periods with many interactions. This, again, is a very important
534   direction for future work.
535
536
      Finally, not a limitation per se, but we kept prompt optimization to a minimum. This was an intentional
      choice, because our goal is to find robust behaviors, and not prompt engineer the problem. However,
537
      this does leave significant room for development, likely improving on the results we observe.
538
539   We hope our work helps to shed light on the capabilities of contemporary LLMs, and that it lays our
      the ground for extensive future work, both research and practice.
