                                                  A BSTRACT
011
012            In goal-conditioned reinforcement learning (RL), the primary objective is to de-
013            velop a goal-conditioned policy capable of reaching diverse desired goals, a process
014
               often hindered by sparse reward signals. To address the challenges associated with
               sparse rewards, existing approaches frequently employ hindsight relabeling, sub-
015
               stituting original goals with achieved goals. However, these methods exhibit a
016
               tendency to prioritize the optimization of closer achieved goals during training,
017            leading to the loss of potentially valuable information from the trajectory and low
018            sample efficiency. Our key insight is that achieved goals, derived from hindsight
019            relabeling, can serve as effective subgoals to facilitate the learning of policies that
020            can reach long-horizon desired goals within the same trajectory. By leveraging
021            these subgoals, we aim to incorporate more longer trajectory information within the
022            same hindsight framework. From this perspective, we propose a novel framework
023            called Goal-Conditioned reinforcement learning with Q-BC (i.e, behavior cloning
024            (BC)-regularized Q) and Subgoals (GCQS) for goal-conditioned RL. GCQS is
025
               a innovative goal-conditioned actor-critic framework that systematically exploits
               more trajectory information to improve policy learning and sample efficiency. As
026
               an extension of the traditional goal-conditioned actor-critic framework, GCQS
027
               further exploits longer trajectory information, treating them as subgoals that guide
028            the learning process and improve the accuracy of action predictions. Experimen-
029            tal results in simulated robotic environments demonstrate that GCQS markedly
030            improves sample efficiency and overall performance when compared to existing
031            goal-conditioned methods. Additionally, GCQS demonstrated competitive per-
032            formance on long-horizon AntMaze tasks, achieving results comparable to such
033            state-of-the-art subgoal-based methods.

115   Goal-conditioned Methods Addressing goal-conditioned RL tasks involves significant complexi-
116
      ties due to the requirement for agents to reach multiple goals concurrently. The major challenge in
      goal-conditioned RL is managing sparse rewards. To address the issue of sparse rewards, the concept
117
      of hindsight was developed, which reinterprets past failures as successes. HER (Andrychowicz et al.,
118
      2017) integrates off-policy learning by incorporating hindsight transitions into the replay buffer. This
119   approach enables agents to learn from their experiences by relabeling the goals they initially aimed
120   for with they actually reached (achieved goals). Based on HER, curriculum hindsight experience
121   replay (CHER) (Fang et al., 2019) and model-based hindsight experience replay (MHER) (Yang et al.,
122   2021) introduce heuristically goal selection from failed attempts and model-based goal relabeling,
123   respectively. Goal-conditioned weighted supervised learning (GCWSL) methods (Chane-Sane et al.,
124   2021; Yang et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2022; Hejna et al., 2023) provide theoretical guarantees that
125   learning from achieved goals (relabeled goals) optimizes a lower bound on the goal-conditioned RL
126   objective. In contrast to these methods, GCQS aims to obtain optimal policies to reach these achieved
127
      goals by employing a Q-BC objective. This method integrates reinforcement learning and imitation
      learning, accelerating the learning process. Experimental results demonstrate its superior sample
128
      efficiency and performance compared to previous goal-conditioned methods.
129
130
      Subgoal Based Approaches Several previous studies have suggested employing subgoals to tackle
131
      goal-reaching tasks (Jurgenson et al., 2020; Chane-Sane et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2021; Islam et al.,
132   2022; Lee et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023b; Kim et al., 2023; Yoon et al., 2024). Our approach diverges
133   from these hierarchical RL methods in that it does not require additional algorithms for subgoal
134   discovery. The closest related work is by Chane-Sane et al. (2021). However, there are significant
135   differences between their method and our GCQS framework. Firstly, Chane-Sane et al. (2021)
136   assumes that the state and goal are identical, which is not applicable in our general goal-conditioned
137   RL environments where states and goals are distinct. Secondly, our method utilizes the relabeled
138   goals within a goal-conditioned RL setting as natural subgoals, thus eliminating the need for separate
139   subgoal discovery mechanisms. This approach has been validated through extensive experimental
140
      evaluations. Moreover, Chane-Sane et al. (2021) lacks a theoretical framework explaining why
      subgoals can enhance policy performance. In contrast, our approach systematically integrates
141
      subgoals into the learning process, demonstrating through empirical evidence how these subgoals
142
      contribute to improved policy efficiency and effectiveness in realizing possible long-horizon tasks.

      Based on the insights and analysis from Section 4, we have developed a novel framework for goal-
      conditioned RL called GCQS. The primary motivation behind GCQS is to leverage more extensive
284
      long trajectories for updates. Overall of this framework is illustrated in Fig. 1. Since we find
285
      that GCWSL underperforms compared to GCAC in our experiments, which may be attributed to
286   GCWSL’s lack of stitching capability (Cheikhi & Russo, 2023; Ghugare et al., 2024). Therefore
287   GCQS integrates the SAC following GCAC. The core of GCQS is grounded in the observation
288   that it is generally more straightforward to identify future achieved goals that lead to the ultimate
289   desired goals, rather than determining the optimal action directly from the initial state. By redefining
290   these achieved goals as subgoals and embedding them within GCAC models, the accuracy of action
291   predictions can be significantly enhanced. This process not only simplifies the learning trajectory but
292   also improves the overall efficiency and effectiveness of the policy learning framework.

294   introduce a policy π(·|s, g ′ ) for reaching achieved goals, as detailed in Section 5.1. Next, we
295   enhance the desired goal-conditioned policy π(·|s, g) by using achieved goals trajectory as subgoals

511
512       To evaluate the significance of subgoals and BC regularization during the stage of learning achieved
513       goals in the GCQS framework, we conducted a series of ablation experiments comparing GCQS
514       variants with HER. In these experiments, the number of subgoals corresponds to all achieved goals,
515       and the parameter β is set to 0.2 by default. We experiment with the following settings:
516
                         • GCQS SAC+Q-BC+Subgoals.
517
518                      • No BC-Regularized Q which is equivalent to remove KL constraints.
519                      • No Subgoals which is equivalent to apply flat goal-conditioned policy.
520
521       The empirical results shown in Fig. 8 demonstrate that subgoals are more pivotal than BC-Regularized
522       Q within the GCQS framework. The GCQS method attains faster learning compared to competitive
523       baseline DDPG+HER, while the state-of-the-art DWSL struggles to learn effectively in these tasks,
524       with the exception of FetchReach. This observation implies that supervised learning (SL) approaches
525
          are suboptimal for relabeled data.
526       Integrating BC-Regularized Q with subgoals leads to substantial performance enhancements. This
527       improvement arises from the synergistic interaction between BC-Regularized Q and subgoals within
528       the GCQS framework. Subgoals offer an improved policy for attaining desired goals, while BC-
529       Regularized Q fine-tunes this policy, thereby efficiently directing the subgoal curriculum.
530
531       7             C ONCLUSION
532
533
          This paper presents GCQS, an advanced GCAC framework for goal-conditioned RL that incorporates
534       a subgoal generation strategy. This approach is motivated by the observation that existing goal-
535       conditioned methods tend to prioritize updates on short-horizon trajectories. A distinctive feature of
536       GCQS is its ability to autonomously generate subgoals using the same relabeling technique applied to
537       the same trajectory, thereby removing the need for additional discovery mechanisms. By leveraging
538       longer trajectories as intermediate subgoals, GCQS enhances the agent’s capacity to predict more
539       accurate actions. Future work will focus on developing more refined techniques for identifying
          subgoals from accomplished outcomes, further optimizing the training of goal-conditioned policies.

849   B.1   P RACTICAL GCQS A LGORITHM
850
851   The complete GCQS algorithm is detailed in Algorithm 1. GCQS extends the SAC framework within
852   GCAC. For each episode, a goal g is sampled from the desired goal distribution, and a trajectory
853
      is collected using the current policy as behavior policy. This trajectory is subsequently stored in
      the replay buffer B. Following data collection, a minibatch m is sampled from the replay buffer.
854
      The future strategy is employed to relabel goals in the minibatch with achieved goals g ′ = ϕ(si ).
855
      After hindsight relabeling, the minibatch m belongs to the relabeled distribution Br and is used to
856   train both the Qπ network and the policy network. The Qπ network is updated according to Eq. (6),
857   and the subgoal policy is trained to minimize the Q-BC objective as described in Eq. (12). Finally,
858   these achieved goals are reused as subgoals to refine the policy by maximizing the KL-divergence
859   regularized Qπ function as described in Eq. (15).

913   current state s, the goal-conditioned value function may struggle to provide a clear learning signal for
914

915   The first is the precise estimation of the value function. As the distance between s and g increases,
916   the precision of the Qπ values tends to decrease. This reduction in precision occurs because the
917   differences in Qπ values for subsequent states Qπ (st+1 , a, g) may be minimal. Consequently,
