011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            CNNs usually work well when they can extract progressively higher-level features
014            through the layers. In small, low-resolution images, the depth of feature extrac-
015            tion is limited, leading to a sparsity in the feature space. Icon detection presents
016            a unique challenge due to the small, feature-sparse nature of the target images,
017
               which often results in limited discriminative features. To address this, we propose
               an icon detection model based on a Siamese network architecture. This approach
018
               draws inspiration from face recognition frameworks. The modified architectures
019
               are aimed at being well-suited for distinguishing subtle differences between icon
020            pairs. Given the relatively sparse feature space of these icons compared to larger
021            images, we explore several enhancements to improve performance. Key inno-
022            vations include the integration of attention mechanisms to focus on informative
023            features, multi-scale feature extraction for better detail capture and contrastive
024            learning. These approaches lean on traditional methods of robust data augmen-
025            tation to enhance performance. Additionally, we investigate dynamic margins in
026            metric learning, adaptations for few-shot learning, and the use of Graph Neural
027            Networks to model icon relationships. Self-supervised pretraining, adversarial
028
               training, ensemble methods, and Neural Architecture Search are employed to fur-
               ther refine and optimize the network. Our comprehensive evaluation demonstrates
029
               significant improvements in icon detection, highlighting the effectiveness of these
030
               advanced techniques in handling small, feature-sparse image data. This solution
031            offers a valuable advancement in high-precision icon recognition, with potential
032            applications in user interface design, software development, and digital asset man-
033            agement.

039   1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041   Icons are fundamental elements of modern digital assets, serving as essential markers in user inter-
042   faces, applications, and automation systems. As machines and automated processes take over rou-
043   tine tasks once performed by humans, the ability to accurately detect and interpret icons becomes
044   increasingly important. This capability is crucial not only for tasks involving traditional icons but
045
      also for recognizing other small, feature-sparse images, which are common in various digital envi-
      ronments. While convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have achieved remarkable success in image
046
      recognition tasks, most of this progress has been focused on images rich in visual features—largely
047
      driven by datasets designed to mimic human perception. These models excel at processing large, de-
048   tailed images where features such as textures, shapes, and colours are abundant. However, they are
049   less effective when applied to smaller images, such as icons, where the visual details are limited and
050   often sparse. This paper addresses the challenges associated with detecting and classifying small
051   images, particularly icons. Unlike traditional models that struggle with such limited information,
052   the proposed model is designed to generalize across different styles and contexts. This adaptability
053   is critical in modern digital ecosystems, where personalization, brand alignment, and user-specific
      customization demand flexible and robust detection systems. To enhance the model’s performance,

      the paper explores several advanced methodologies. Self-supervised pretraining [1]is employed to

072   2     R ELATED WORK
073
074   Pretraining techniques have been widely explored to enhance feature extraction capabilities in var-
075   ious image recognition tasks. One such method, SimCLR [1] (Simple Framework for Contrastive
076
      Learning of Visual Representations), has gained prominence for its self-supervised approach to pre-
      training. SimCLR leverages data augmentations and contrastive learning to enable models to learn
077
      powerful visual representations without the need for labeled data. Initially, SimCLR was employed
078
      in large-scale image datasets like ImageNet, focusing on rich, feature-dense images such as natural
079   scenes and objects. However, its potential in handling small, feature-sparse images like icons has not
080   been thoroughly explored. Our work leverages SimCLR’s capacity to extract robust features from
081   these small-scale images, using self-supervised pretraining to set up the network for downstream
082   tasks, such as icon detection and classification.
083
      Attention mechanisms [2] have also seen significant success in improving the performance of deep
084
      learning models on visual tasks. The Vision Transformer (ViT) [4] demonstrated the effectiveness
085   of using self-attention to capture global dependencies in images, outperforming traditional convo-
086   lutional neural networks on several image classification benchmarks. In addition to ViT, various
087   other models have incorporated attention layers, either as an augmentation to CNNs or as part of
088   transformer-based architectures, to enhance feature extraction from images, making attention par-
089   ticularly useful for images where spatial relationships are critical. In our study, we introduce at-
090   tention both within and outside the Siamese network structure to assess its impact on the model’s
091   performance when dealing with small icons.
092   Siamese networks [3] have long been a staple in the field of metric learning, particularly for tasks
093   like facial recognition, where the goal is to determine whether two images represent the same entity.
094   First introduced by Bromley et al. in 1993 for signature verification tasks, the Siamese network
095   architecture uses two identical subnetworks to compare image pairs, learning a similarity metric
096   between them based on shared weights. This method of contrastive learning, combined with loss
097   functions like contrastive loss, was further popularized by works in facial recognition and one-
098   shot learning. Contrastive learning aims to minimize the distance between embeddings of similar
099
      images while maximizing the distance between dissimilar ones, making it an ideal approach for
      tasks involving small, feature-sparse images such as icons.

104   3.1   S ELF - SUPERVISED PRETRAINING FOR FEATURE EXTRACTION IN SMALL IMAGE
105         DATASETS
106
107   Using SimCLR for unsupervised pretraining in your setup has several benefits, especially when deal-
      ing with small, feature-sparse images like icons. SimCLR is designed for self-supervised learning,

      which means it can learn useful visual representations without requiring labelled data. The network
109   learnt to extract features that generalize well to different images, even though no explicit labels are
110   provided during pretraining. Once the model learned these features, it was fine-tuned on labelled
111   data for the task of icon detection or classification. Small images like icons often contain limited
112   visual information.
113
      Traditional CNNs can struggle with these kinds of images because they are optimized for rich,
114
      detailed features like textures and shapes. SimCLR’s approach of contrastive learning—where the
115   network learns to differentiate between augmented views of the same image—forces the model to
116   focus on even the subtle, distinguishing features in the image. By training the network to maximize
117   similarity between augmented versions of the same icon and minimize similarity between different
118   icons, SimCLR potentially enhanced feature sensitivity. Pretraining with SimCLR allows the model
119   to learn from a larger pool of unlabelled data, which helps reduce the risk of overfitting when training
120   the final model on a smaller labelled dataset.

      3.2   M ULTI - SCALE FEATURE EXTRACTION
124
125
      The model utilizes multi-scale feature extraction, which is crucial for processing icons due to their
      inherent lack of distinguishable features. Icons are often minimalistic, meaning that recognizing
126
      patterns across various scales allows the network to identify the few available distinguishing char-
127
      acteristics, making it better suited for feature-sparse images.
128
129   Different icons can vary significantly in complexity, resolution, and style. By leveraging multi-
130   scale extraction, the model can effectively process both simple and complex icons, leading to better
131
      generalization across different styles. Small images may have minimal distinguishing features, and
      these features can vary in size. For instance, one icon might exhibit very fine details, while another
132
      relies on larger, broader features.
133
134   Using multiple filter sizes (1x1, 3x3, 5x5) in parallel within the same layer enables the network to
135   capture details at different scales. Smaller filters (1x1 or 3x3) are adept at capturing fine details like
136   edges or textures, while larger filters (5x5 or 7x7) can identify broader patterns or shapes, which are
137
      essential for recognizing the overall structure of an icon.

      3.3   I NCORPORATING ATTENTION MECHANISMS IN FEATURE SPARSE SPACE
140
141
      We incorporated an attention mechanism in the network to enhance its ability to focus on salient
142   features within images, particularly when dealing with small, feature-sparse icons. This approach
143   allows the model to dynamically weigh the importance of different parts of an image, improving its
144   capacity to identify critical characteristics.
145
      We conducted a comparison between applying attention to the embeddings generated by the Siamese
146
      network and applying attention before the generation of the final embeddings. The results empiri-
147
      cally indicate that, in this setup, applying attention prior to embedding creation yields better accu-
148   racy.
149
150   The pre-embedding attention mechanism processes the feature maps produced by the convolutional
151
      layers, enabling the model to prioritize specific regions of the image that are more indicative of the
      object’s identity. This strategic focus leads to embeddings that may more effectively capture the
152
      underlying structure and salient characteristics of the icons. We assume that by refining the feature
153
      extraction process before embedding generation, the network can generate richer, more informative
154   representations, thereby enhancing overall performance in icon detection tasks

      5     R ESULTS
325
326
      The performance of the proposed models was evaluated based on various metrics, including ac-
327
      curacy, recall, precision, contrastive loss, and similarity measures such as cosine similarity and
328
      Euclidean distance. The models were tested on a dataset containing icon images, where the posi-
329   tive pairs consisted of different representations of the same object, and the negative pairs comprised
330   distinct objects.
331
332
      5.1     A BLATION S TUDIES
333
334   5.1.1    S IAMESE NETWORK WITHOUT PRE - TRAINING
335
336   The baseline Siamese network was trained on identification of negative and positive pairs. The
337   model demonstrated a satisfactory performance, achieving an accuracy of 81.06% on the validation
338   dataset. However, it struggled with generalization, particularly in distinguishing between similar-
339   looking icons.
340
341   5.1.2    S IAMESE NETWORK WITH S IM CLR PRETRAINING
342
343   The incorporation of SimCLR pretraining significantly enhanced the performance of the Siamese
344
      network. The model trained with SimCLR achieved an accuracy of 88.88% on the validation dataset.
      The pretraining allowed the model to learn robust feature representations, which improved its ability
345
      to differentiate between similar icons. This pretraining exercise allowed the model to leverage more
346
      data which was not labelled and thus could not be incorporated in paired training.
347
348
      5.1.3    ATTENTION
349
350   We further evaluated the performance of the Siamese network that is already coupled with the pre-
351   trained network when an attention was incorporated. This model achieved an accuracy of 93%,
352   indicating that the attention mechanism positively influenced the model’s focus on salient features,
353   leading to improved recognition of icons. The exploration of attention mechanism is split in two
354   parts viz.
355
          a) Appling attention after the Siamese network outputs the embeddings: Applying attention
356   after embedding allows for a reduction in the complexity of the input that the attention mechanism
357   must process. This can lead to faster computation and less memory usage. Since the embeddings
358   might lose some spatial context and feature details, applying attention after embedding may limit
359   the model’s ability to leverage specific spatial features in the original input data
360
         b) Applying attention before the Siamese network output of embeddings: The model learns to
361
      focus on specific regions or aspects of the input that are most informative for the task. This can
362   enhance feature extraction, especially for small or feature-sparse images. The model can adaptively
363   learn which parts of the input contribute most to similarity or dissimilarity, making it potentially
364   more robust to noise or irrelevant features in the input
365
366
      5.2     S UMMARY OF KEY METRICS
367
368   The results indicate that incorporating SimCLR for pretraining of feature sparse small images can
369   significantly improve the performance of Siamese networks for icon recognition tasks. It was one
370   of the most effective enhancements we could make to boost model performance. Furthermore,
371   the addition of attention mechanisms enhanced the models’ ability to focus on crucial features.
372   Incorporating multi scale features which is filters of different dimensions helped the model look at
373   different levels of abstraction of the images.

392   6   C ONCLUSION
393
394   In this study, we explored the effectiveness of various model architectures and training strategies for
395   recognizing small, feature-sparse icons. By employing a Siamese network architecture, we demon-
396   strated the advantages of using a dual approach that combines SimCLR for unsupervised pretraining
397   with traditional supervised learning techniques.
398
      Our results indicate that the SimCLR pretraining significantly enhanced feature representation, al-
399
      lowing the model to better differentiate between similar icons, which is critical in tasks involving
400   minimalistic designs.
401
402   Furthermore, the incorporation of attention mechanisms resulted in notable improvements in accu-
403
      racy, underscoring the importance of focusing on salient features within the images. Our experi-
      ments showed that applying attention before the embedding generation yielded better results com-
404
      pared to applying it afterward, suggesting that early integration of attention enhances the model’s
405
      ability to capture critical information in small, sparse images.
406
407   Overall, this research highlights the potential of combining self-supervised learning methods, adapt-
408   able loss functions, and attention mechanisms to create more effective models for icon recognition.
409
      Future work may involve further optimizing these approaches and exploring additional architectures
      and techniques to enhance performance in diverse visual recognition tasks.
