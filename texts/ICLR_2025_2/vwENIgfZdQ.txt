009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The advances in large vision-language models (VLMs) have sparked a growing
012            interest in generating accurate, complete, and user-friendly image captions to en-
013            hance downstream multi-modality tasks such as text-to-image generation, text-
014            driven object detection, and grounding. However, current VLM-based image cap-
015            tioning methods often miss important details, recognize incorrect objects or re-
016            lationships, and deliver suboptimal captions for downstream applications. One
017            primary reason for this issue is the ambiguous prompts typically used, such as
018
               ”describe this image in detail,” which fail to guide the VLM’s focus on specific
               elements within the image. To address this, we extensively explore the difference
019
               between using ambiguous prompts and decomposing them into a series of specific
020
               questions. We find that asking a series of targeted element-specific questions sig-
021            nificantly enhances the attention of VLMs to important objects, the consistency of
022            the answers under repeated questions, and the alignment with their training data
023            distribution. Building on this insight, we introduce ASSIST, a method that system-
024            atically decomposes image caption prompts into a sequence of focused questions
025            corresponding to distinct image elements. We annotated 100k images using GPT-
026            4V with this approach and fine-tuned a LLAVA model, resulting in a captioner that
027            greatly improves caption accuracy and quality. Our fine-tuned model recognizes
028            ×1.5 more correct objects and achieves ×1.5 higher precision in describing them
029
               on the COCO benchmark compared to vague prompting methods. Additionally,
               our method produces element-specific answers that can be efficiently organized
030
               into graph structures, benefiting tasks like open-vocabulary object detection and
031
               image generation. This leads to significant improvements in the accuracy, preci-
032            sion, and mIoU of state-of-the-art detection models, with recall scores increasing
033            by ×1.7 over previous methods. Experiments across diverse scenarios and bench-
034            marks validate the effectiveness of ASSIST. All code, datasets, and models will
035            be made publicly available.

039
040   Image captioning is a pivotal task in multi-modal understanding, tasked with generating precise
041   and comprehensive descriptions of images. These descriptions are critical not only for enhancing
042   human-computer interaction but also for facilitating deeper integration between visual data and ma-
043   chine learning models, particularly in applications like visual generation and object detection (Liu
044
      et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b; OpenAI, 2023; Podell et al., 2023; Betker et al.,
      2023). An effective image caption should detail key objects, describe their attributes accurately,
045
      and be structured in a user-friendly manner, ensuring seamless utility for downstream models, even
046
      those lacking robust text encoders.
047
048   Despite their capabilities, even state-of-the-art Vision-Language Models (VLMs) such as GPT-4V
049   often fail to capture all essential elements in their captions, resulting in outputs that lack both accu-
050
      racy and completeness (OpenAI, 2023). This shortfall is partially due to the inherently ambiguous
      prompts used in image captioning tasks, which do not specify the desired level of detail or focus,
051
      leading to generic and often unhelpful descriptions.
052

      to posing specific, targeted questions. By decomposing the broad task of captioning into a series of

103   age description tasks, significantly outperforming traditional methods based on manually annotated

140   3                T HE ASSIST P RINCIPLE : A SK S PECIFICALLY I NSTEAD OF A MBIGUOUSLY
141
142   Image captioning aims to gather as much correct information as possible from an image and write it
143   down to optimize downstream tasks’ performance, like object detection or image and video gener-
144   ation. Yet the current prompting ways seem to have some gap from this target. While the previous
145   methods try to add phases like ‘describe in detail’ or ‘describing the content one can determine
146   confidently’ (Chen et al., 2023a) to improve the correctness and completeness of captions, they still
147   struggle to correctly recognize as many as possible existing objects, relations, or other important
148
      information. In this paper, we argue that simply adding more pressure to your VLMs, like asking
      them to be ‘super talent image captioners’ or ’do not lose anything’, may not actually have enough
149
      effects in improving results. Rather, what the VLMs still need is specific questions without any
150
      ambiguity that can be clearly understood and answered.
151
152   So instead of generally asking ‘Create detailed captions describing the contents of the given image’,
153   we propose to decompose it into a series of specific ones such as ‘List all objects in the image’,
154
      ‘Describe the color/attribute/category/location of the first object’, ‘Describe the first object in detail’,
      or ‘Describe the relation between the first object and the second object’.

      Design the Specific Question List The key insight of ASSIST involves decomposing the ambigu-
      ous task of image description into a series of concrete sub-tasks. Besides, we need a complete
256
      question list to cover all possible information in an image. Borrowing the idea from scene graph,
257
      where a graph structural comprising element-wise objects and their relationships (Krishna et al.,
258   2017; Lu et al., 2016; Xu et al., 2017; Johnson et al., 2015; 2018) are commonly used to represent
259   all knowledge in a real-world scenario, we design the sub-tasks following the structure of a scene
260   graph. This includes 1) enumerating all objects within the image and separately describing them,
261   2) identifying the relationships among these objects, and 3) characterizing the style and themes
262   conveyed in the image.
263
      Unify Question List into One Prompt In practice, querying VLMs with specific questions one by
264   one can significantly increase dialogue rounds and thus is deadly expensive in both time and funds.
265   To address this, we propose a method that enables VLMs to answer all questions sequentially within
266   a single dialogue round. Our approach comprises two key steps. First, we design a specific output
267   format that combines answers, using special symbols to separate them for easy parsing. second,
268   we introduce in-context learning (ICL)(Brown et al., 2020) by including hand-crafted examples in
269   the prompt that demonstrate the desired response order and the use of delimiters. In practice, a few
      simplified examples are sufficient and can be integrated into the prompt, allowing the ICL process

353   4.1.1                       A NALYZING OVERALL Q UALITY USING LLM S AND C APTION Q UESTION A NSWERING
354
      Caption Question Answering (CQA) The VQA benchmark is broadly used in evaluating the out-
355   puts of VLMs. Yet they are not suitable for evaluating captioner models, as 1) the query in VQA
356   benchmarks are very different from image caption, thus they may hardly indicate the ability of im-
357   age caption of the captioners; 2) captioner models may sacrifice their general VLM capability to
358   enhance image caption ability. So instead, we remove the image in VQA, and replace it with its
359   caption produced by different caption methods. The caption and original query are then sent to an

515   5   C ONCLUSION
516
517   In this work, we addressed the limitations of current VLM-based image captioning methods, which
518   often fail to capture critical details and relationships, resulting in suboptimal performance for down-
519   stream tasks. Through extensive exploration, we demonstrated that ambiguous prompts like ”de-
520   scribe this image in detail” do not provide sufficient guidance for VLMs to focus on important
521   elements in images. To overcome this, we proposed ASSIST, a method that decomposes image
522   caption prompts into a sequence of specific, element-focused questions, significantly enhancing the
523   model’s ability to recognize and describe objects accurately. By annotating 100k images and fine-
524   tuning a LLAVA model, our approach resulted in substantial improvements in both caption quality
525
      and precision. Our method consistently outperforms vague prompting techniques, achieving a ×1.5
      improvement in object recognition and precision on the COCO benchmark. Additionally, the struc-
526
      tured, element-specific answers generated by ASSIST benefit other tasks, such as open-vocabulary
527
      object detection and image generation, leading to a ×1.7 increase in precision and significant boosts
528   in mIoU for detection models. These findings validate the effectiveness of ASSIST in enhancing
529   VLM-based captioning and its applicability to various multimodal tasks.

      6   L IMITATIONS
533
534
      This paper introduces a method designed to assist smaller models in comprehending complex texts
      and to facilitate their integration with VLMs, achieving remarkable performances across multiple
535
      benchmarks. However, despite these achievements, our approach still faces certain limitations.
536
      Firstly, given the absence of a fully automated method that guarantees reliable quality, our data col-
537   lection process still necessitates human annotation involvement. Secondly, due to cost and resource
538   constraints, the captioner’s localization capabilities remain insufficient, necessitating the combina-
539   tion of a grounding model to obtain high-quality positional information.

      A.1.1    C OMPLETE TEMPLATES FOR IDENTIFYING SPECIFIC QUESTIONS
734
735   In Section Section 3.1, we analyze the different performances of LLaVA in answering specific ques-
736   tions versus abstract questions by examining its training data. A key point of our analysis involves
737   the automated identification and quantification of specific questions from over 3 million training
738   conversations. We apply two main principles for this process.
739   First, we classify the questions of those conversations whose answers are overly brief consisting
740   of a single word or whose questions require a single-word answer as specific questions. This is
741   because abstract questions usually elicit more varied responses that cannot be easily summarized in
742   one word; a single word response typically corresponds to yes/no, numeric, or simple noun answers,
743   which are characteristic of specific questions.
744
      Second, we designed a series of templates for specific questions to facilitate string matching. The
745   templates include three main matching patterns:
746
747           • The questions starting with special words such as "Who", "where" and "how many".
748             These questions typically lead to specific answers. For example, "Who" generally refers
749             to a person, "Where" to a location, and "How many" to a numeric response.
750           • "What" combined with specific terms like "what ... color ..." which specifi-
751             cally asks about color, "what ... time ..." focus on time inquiries, and "what
752             ... type ..." which targets categories. We chose this approach because while
753             "what" can lead to abstract questions, it can also direct queries toward specific details.
754             Various phrasings can ask about color, such as "what color is ..." or "what is
755             the color of ...", allowing us to check for keywords like ”color” when the ques-
                tion begins with "what".

      As discussed in Section 3.2, we design a specific output format for VLMs that sequentially com-
790
      bines the answers to different questions. In this section, we introduce the string format, where an
791   example is shown in Figure A9. Specifically, we label main titles with %% and subtitles with &&.
792   When listing objects, we enclose extra details like category, description, and color in brackets ().
793   Each detail is separated by a semicolon ”;”. We mark the name of an object with <>. During the
794   description of relationships, we use <> for showing objects and [] for the predicate. Additionally,
795   we use <> to highlight important objects within the object, serving multiple purposes. One such
796   function is to post-process the GPT-4V output results. This involves removing foreground informa-
797   tion from the background description by deleting sentences where the foreground objects appear, or
798   similarly, eliminating background information from the foreground description. By using these spe-
799
      cial symbols to separate different sections, we can effortlessly organize the string format output of
      VLMs into a ASSIST-style caption using regular expressions. This makes it easy for downstream

      tasks to extract various pieces of information without any hassle.

881
882   As we’ve mentioned in Section 3.2, creating the ASSIST dataset’s training and test sets involves
883   human annotations.
884
      Collecting training data. In the process of collecting training data, ASSIST significantly reduces
885
      the workload of annotation. It breaks down the complex descriptions into basic elements, for many
886   of which annotators simply need to make a straightforward judgment of right or wrong, a task that is
887   remarkably simple. For large pieces of information such as background or foreground descriptions,
888   annotators are asked to separately determine if each sentence is correct according to the image.
889   Besides, the annotators are asked to add objects missed by GPT-4V. In this process, the structure we
890   designed for objects can help annotators simplify the description process. They only need to fill in
891   the corresponding information according to the structure.
892   Collecting test benchmark. In the method of collecting the test set of ASSIST, annotators are
893   involved in four parts. For the first part, they are expected to correct the result returned by VLMs
894   to recognize the object name given the masked image. In the second and third parts, annotators are
895   asked to separately determine if each sentence is correct. They don’t have to add objects as Segment
896   anything (SAM) Kirillov et al. (2023) in this method has ensured that there will be no omissions. At
897   the last stage, they have to determine if a relationship is correct and add an important relationship
898   omitted by VLMs.

       A.5.2    P OINT QUESTION ANSWERING
1057
1058   Method of applying ASSIST. In our experiment, PointQA is designed to answer questions re-
1059   lated to image regions based on the description of the image. Most descriptions provided by Visual
1060   Language Models (VLMs) cannot accomplish this task as their descriptions lack positional informa-
1061   tion. However, ASSIST provides both the positional information of objects within the image and
1062   their corresponding descriptions. Given a target area, by combining descriptions of different objects
1063   based on their positional relationships, one can create a description relevant to the location. Specifi-
1064   cally, as illustrated in Figure A3, we compute the Intersection Over Union (IOU) between the target
1065   area and the positions of all objects. By combining the descriptions of objects with high overlap, we
1066
       obtain a description that is closely related to the target area. Then, we feed this description to the
       question-answering model to answer the question.

1069   A.5.3    P OINTING QUESTION ANSWERING
1070
1071
       Method of applying ASSIST. The PointingQA task requires selecting the most appropriate region
       from a set of candidate areas based on a textual prompt. VLMs struggle to complete this task be-
1072
       cause they often lack the ability to perceive input location information. However, since ASSIST
1073
       decomposes image descriptions into a series of basic elements, each with its corresponding location,
1074   we can leverage this feature to accomplish the task. As shown in Figure A3, the method is divided
1075   into three steps. First, we calculate the CLIP similarity between each object’s description and the
1076   input textual prompt, obtaining scores for each object. The more relevant an object is to the text de-
1077   scription, the higher its score. Secondly, we calculate scores for each candidate region by weighting
1078   the sum of object scores based on the overlap between the candidate region and the object’s location.
1079   The greater the overlap with the candidate area, the larger the proportion of that object’s score. In
       the third step, the region with the highest score is selected as the answer.

       A.5.4   I MAGE GENERATION
1081
1082   Method of enhancing SDXL by ASSIST. Even as one of the most renowned models for text-
1083   to-image generation, SDXL often struggles to understand complex prompts and generate precise
1084   images accurately. This is primarily because SDXL employs CLIP for text understanding, which
1085   limits its ability to comprehend the text. However, each basic element within a complex prompt
1086
       is not complicated for SDXL to understand and generate. Therefore, by breaking down complex
       texts into basic elements, ASSIST can significantly assist SDXL in simplifying complex tasks.
1087
       Specifically, SDXL can first create the background, then sequentially generate each object, and
1088
       finally assemble the different parts. Currently, there are many methods that can be utilized for
1089   image stitching, such as Anydoor (Chen et al., 2024), Collage Diffusion (Sarukkai et al., 2024), etc.
1090   Sometimes, images can also be directly stitched together and then refined using SDXL as the base
1091   model, with SDEdit (Meng et al., 2021) for refining the images, but this typically requires the images
1092   to be relatively simple. Aside from generating individual parts of the image and then stitching them
1093   together, another approach is to sequentially inpaint (Rombach et al., 2022) objects onto the image
1094   using inpainting methods.
