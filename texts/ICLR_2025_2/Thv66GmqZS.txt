011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) is a widely adopted parameter-efficient fine-tuning
014            method for large language models (LLMs) to adapt to downstream tasks. How-
015
               ever, in scenarios where multiple LoRA models are deployed simultaneously,
               standard LoRA introduces substantial trainable parameters, resulting in significant
016
               memory overhead and inference latency, particularly when supporting thousands
017
               of downstream tasks on a single server. While existing methods reduce stored
018            parameters via parameter sharing, they fail to capture both local and global infor-
019            mation simultaneously. To address this issue, we propose Bi-Share LoRA, which
020            integrates local parameters with intra-layer and inter-layer shared parameters to
021            more effectively capture information at both local and global levels. By shar-
022            ing parameters both within and across layers, our method significantly reduces
023            the number of trainable parameters while preserving or improving model perfor-
024            mance. Additionally, we set a local LoRA to capture local parameters, enabling
025            more precise and fine-grained information extraction at the local level. The final
026
               implementation introduces three parallel sub-LoRAs and designs transformation
               techniques to adapt shared parameters of varying shapes, ensuring compatibility
027
               and efficient sharing. Experiments on the 7B, 8B, and 13B versions of Llama show
028
               that Bi-Share LoRA, with only 44.59% of the parameters of standard LoRA, out-
029            performs LoRA by approximately 0.33% on commonsense reasoning and 2.08%
030            on MMLU benchmarks.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Large language models (LLMs), such as GPT-4o (Openai, 2023) and Claude-3 (Anthropic, 2024),
036   have recently demonstrated remarkable generalization capabilities across a wide range of natural lan-
037   guage tasks (Raiaan et al., 2024; Chang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023). This enhanced performance
038   is largely attributed to the rapid increase in model parameters, for example, GPT-3 (Brown et al.,
039
      2020) contains 175 billion parameters, while the largest version of Llama 3.1 (Touvron et al., 2023)
      features up to 405 billion parameters. However, the ever-increasing size of these models presents
040
      significant challenges for fine-tuning, as full parameter fine-tuning becomes computationally expen-
041
      sive and memory-intensive. To address this issue, Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods
042   have been introduced, achieving performance comparable to full fine-tuning by adjusting only a
043   small subset of parameters (Han et al., 2024) while keeping the majority of the model parameters
044   frozen. Among these methods, LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) stands out by approximating parameter
045   updates using the product of two low-rank matrices and has gained increasing popularity.
046
      However, with the continued scaling up of models’ parameters, fine-tuning LLMs with LoRA would
047
      introduce a considerable number of additional parameters, even when using a relatively low rank.
048   For instance, fine-tuning the Llama 70B model with a LoRA rank of 64 introduces approximately
049   360 million parameters (1.4G of memory), which makes the training process more challenging, an
050   issue that becomes more severe when multiple LoRA services are deployed simultaneously (Wang
051   et al., 2024). For the deployment scenario, LoRA modules are often kept separate from the pre-
052   trained parameters to facilitate multi-task inference services (Chen et al., 2024). Due to the mul-
053   titude of downstream tasks, storing numerous LoRA weight backups consumes significant storage
      space. Additionally, during inference, the excessive quantity of LoRA parameters can occupy sub-

      same layer often process similar features, leading to redundant parameter usage. Therefore, there
081
      is a need to design a new sharing technique that can capture both local and global features,
082   while ensuring the shared parameters can be adapted to all modules with different shapes.

084   In this paper, we first analyze LoRA parameters and identify a high degree of redundancy (Figure
085
      1), and we conduct a preliminary study, as shown in Table 1, which demonstrates that sharing pa-
      rameters within and between layers can achieve comparable performance with fewer parameters.
086
      This indicates that we can eliminate high degree of redundancy in LoRA parameters by shar-
087
      ing them to capture global information. Accordingly, we propose Bi-Share LoRA, a method that
088   combines intra-layer and inter-layer parameter sharing. We decompose the LoRA matrices into
089   three components: local parameters, which capture module-specific information, intra-layer shared
090   parameters, which are shared within the same layer to capture local consistent features, and inter-
091   layer shared parameters, which are shared across layers to capture global patterns. This enables
092   Bi-Share LoRA to learn both local and global information efficiently. Additionally, to tackle the
093   challenge of adapting shared parameters to all modules with different shapes, we present three shape
094   transformation methods: Slice Sharing, Gate Transformation, and Kronecker Extension. To validate
095   the effectiveness of Bi-Share LoRA, we conduct extensive experiments on the Llama model fam-
096
      ily across multiple commonsense reasoning and MMLU benchmarks. Our results demonstrate that
      Bi-Share LoRA achieves significant parameter savings of about 50% while maintaining or even im-
097
      proving the model’s performance compared to standard LoRA and other existing methods. We also
098
      conduct experiments to analyze the rank value benefits and contributions of different configurations
099   for local and shared weights.
100
101   In summary, our contributions are as follows:

114   2.1    L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION
115
      LoRA fine-tuning is employed to recover performance with minimal parameter updates. For an
116
      LLM consisting of l layers, the weight matrix of each layer W is adjusted using an update matrix
117
      ∆W ∈ Rm×n . This matrix is factorized into two low-rank matrices, A and B, where A ∈ Rr×m
118   and B ∈ Rn×r , with r being a hyperparameter shared by all layers. The effectiveness of fine-tuning
119   is highly dependent on rank selection. In this approach, the original weight matrix W remains
120   frozen, while only ∆W , represented by the product AB, is updated. The forward computation can
121   be expressed as:
122                               f (x) = (W + ∆W )x = W x + BAx .                                    (1)
123
      Given that the rank r is typically much smaller than the dimension d, the computational cost is
124   significantly reduced from d2 to 2dr. This optimization can reduce the trainable parameters during
125   the learning process. Typically, the matrix A is initialized by a Gaussian distribution with a small
126   standard deviation, and B is initialized as a zero matrix. Hence, at the beginning of fine-tuning, the
127   model behaves identically to the pre-trained model.
128
129   2.2    M OTIVATION
130
131   In large language models (LLMs), parameter redundancy is a common issue, especially in multi-
132   task learning scenarios. Redundancy commonly occurs within the same transformer block where
133   the attention layer and MLP layer have overlapping functions or learn similar patterns (Lin et al.,
134   2024), and the parameters across different blocks where similar feature representations might be
135
      learned in multiple layers (Li et al., 2024). We also plot the parameter similarity in Figure 1, which
      shows the high similarity across different modules (details refer to Appendix A.2). Addressing both
136
      scenarios of parameter redundancy remains an open problem. We wonder whether sharing these
137
      superfluous parameters, both within a layer (intra-layer sharing) and across layers (inter-layer
138   sharing), can significantly reduce the number of parameters without sacrificing performance? To
139   this end, we conduct a simple experiment on intra-layer sharing, where all modules within the same
140   transformer layer share the same LoRA parameters, and inter-layer sharing, where modules across
141   different layers share the same LoRA fine-tuning parameters. From Table 1 (Individual), we find
142   that the simple sharing may result in some performance degradation. We hypothesize that it may
143   need module-specific parameters to learn the local features.

      3     M ETHOD

      3.1     PARAMETER S HARING
195
196   Parameter sharing not only facilitates learning global information from the dataset but also signif-
197   icantly reduces the number of parameters in the model (Han et al., 2024). Leveraging this, we
198   apply parameter sharing to enhance parameter efficiency in LoRA fine-tuning. Our approach first
199   decomposes the LoRA module horizontally, expanding the optimization search space and allowing
200   for more efficient learning. We divide the LoRA matrix into three sub-LoRA modules: local, intra-
201   layer, and inter-layer which collectively contribute to the parameter updates of the target module.
202
      Intra-Layer Module refers to the sharing of parameters within the same transformer layer, such
203
      that all modules within a single layer (e.g., attention and MLP) share the same LoRA update matrix.
204
      This enables the model to capture consistent patterns and correlations within the layer, thereby
205   improving the coherence of the information processed within each layer.
206
207   Inter-Layer Module denotes global parameter sharing, where the same LoRA update matrix is
208   shared across different layers of the transformer. This facilitates better information flow and fea-
209   ture interaction between layers, which in turn enhance the overall expressiveness and depth of the
210   model’s representations
211
      Local Module refers to the traditional LoRA configuration, where the LoRA update matrix is
212
      applied only to the current module, allowing the model to learn highly specific local features. Dur-
213
      ing training and inference, the parameters for each module are updated according to the following
214   equation:
215
                     f (x) = (W + ∆W )x = W x + (BA)local x + (BA)intra x + (BA)inter x ,                                                                         (2)

      where the distinct rank values of aforementioned three sub-LoRA matrices are denoted as rlocal ,
217   rintra , and rinter , respectively. Specifically, Blocal ∈ Rn×rlocal , Alocal ∈ Rrlocal ×m , Bintra ∈
218   Rn×rintra , Aintra ∈ Rrintra ×m , Binter ∈ Rn×rinter , and Ainter ∈ Rrinter ×m .
219
220
      By introducing this novel decomposition, our Bi-Share LoRA fine-tuning approach is capable of
      learning both localized features and global interactions, providing a balance between task-specific
221
      adaptability and overall model robustness. Figure 2 presents an overview of our Bi-Share LoRA.
222
      This design is particularly beneficial in multi-task scenarios, enhancing the model’s performance
223   and generalization while significantly reducing the computational resources required during training
224   and inference. By decomposing LoRA into multiple smaller LoRA modules, we can assign a higher
225   rank to the shared parameters, allowing the final parameters to achieve a greater overall rank, as
226   discussed further in Section 4.4.

228   3.2     S HAPE T RANSFORMATION
229
230   We initially followed the LoRA setup (Hu et al., 2022) by applying LoRA parameters to the q
231   and v modules in the attention layer, matching the shared parameter size to the qv module, which
232   yielded preliminary results (Table 1). Previous studies suggest that applying LoRA to more modules
233
      improves performance (Dettmers et al., 2023), so we extended it to the FFN layers. However, this
      caused a parameter shape mismatch. In the Transformer block (Vaswani et al., 2017), the qkvo mod-
234
      ules in Llama’s attention layer have a consistent shape of (4096, 4096). Meanwhile, the FFN’s up-
235
      and down-projection modules have dimensions of (4096, 11008) and (11008, 4096), respectively,
236   making it difficult to apply a single shared AB parameters across these varying shapes. Similarly,
237   in Llama3, the Grouped-Query Attention (GQA) changes the shape of k module, further compli-
238   cating the parameter sharing. To address this issue, we develop three transformation methods that
239   adjust shared parameters to size of target modules.
240
241   3.2.1    S LICE S HARING
242
243

      portions (see Figure 3 (a)), which we refer to as the Slice Sharing (SS) method. Specifically, we
244

245
      parameter modules. The shared matrices are then defined with dimensions As ∈ Rr×dim and
246   Bs ∈ Rdom ×r . During forward computation, the shared matrix is automatically sliced to match the
247   parameter dimensions of the target module. The calculation is expressed as:

256   used by all modules, while the remaining parameters are only utilized by larger modules. This limits
257   the efficiency of parameter sharing. To address this, we propose matrix multiplication for dynamic
258   size transformation of shared parameters. By multiplying matrices Ma ∈ Rm×n and Mb ∈ Rn×p ,

409
      Rank Analysis. According to matrix rank theory, the rank of the sum of two matrices is given by
410
      R(A + B) ≤ R(A) + R(B). This implies that decomposing a large LoRA matrix into multiple
411   sub-LoRAs does not increase the overall rank of the matrix. Consequently, the rank of the combined
412   LoRA matrices remains bounded by the sum of their individual ranks. We validate the actual rank
413   of each module and calculate the average ranks across layers for each Shape Transformation method
414   using rank configurations of r = {2, 4, 16}. The results, shown in Figure 4(a), indicate that the Slice
415   Sharing (SS) method achieves a combined rank equal to the sum of local and shared ranks (22), while
416   the Kronecker Extension (KE) reaches approximately 21.53. In contrast, the Gate Transformation
417   (GT) method yields rank value equivalent to the local rank plus 2, likely due to one-rank gates
418   causing some information loss.
419
420   Contribution Analysis. We conduct further experiments to explore the contribution of each sub-
421
      LoRA matrix. By setting the rank of one sub-LoRA matrix to 8 and the others to 0, we examine
      the individual impact of each component. The Kronecker Extension method is used to reshape the
422
      shared parameters. In Figure 4(b), the results reveal the performance preferences of each component
423
      across datasets. Specifically, the local component of LoRA performs best on HellaSwag, ARC-e,
424   BoolQ, and SIQA, while the intra-layer shared component excels on PIQA and ARC-c. Overall,
425   the combination of local, intra-layer, and inter-layer shared parameters yields the best performance
426   across all datasets.

502   5.1      M ULTI -L O RA A RCHITECTURE
503
504   LoRA has demonstrated exceptional resource efficiency and performance in adapting LLMs for
505   specific tasks, driving the demand for a single model capable of handling multiple tasks (Agiza et al.,
506   2024). Several approaches have been proposed to improve their multi-task adaptability. In particular,
507   LoraHub (Huang et al., 2023) assembles LoRA modules trained on different tasks to eliminate the
508
      need for human expertise and assumptions, enabling effective cross-task generalization. Similarly,
      MultiLoRA (Wang et al., 2023) improves adaptability by horizontally expanding LoRA modules and
509
      reducing the dominance of top singular vectors observed in LoRA. Building on these advancements,
510
      HydraLoRA (Tian et al., 2024) introduces an asymmetric architecture that shares a common matrix
511   across tasks while using task-specific matrices for different sub-domains, further enhancing both
512   fine-tuning and inference efficiency.
513
514   5.2      PARAMETER S HARING OF L ORA
515
516   Recent advances in LoRA-based fine-tuning methods have explored various strategies for sharing
517   LoRA weights to enhance parameter efficiency across multiple tasks. VeRA (Kopiczko et al., 2024)
518   proposes sharing random matrices across all layers, reducing the number of parameters, but it results
519   in some performance trade-offs and increased inference latency due to its high-rank requirements.
520   In addition, Tied-LoRA (Renduchintala et al., 2024) takes a different approach by sharing LoRA
521
      matrices specifically across query, key, and value projection layers, using additional scaling vectors
      to differentiate the modules. However, its requirement for identical matrix shapes limits flexibility.
522
      In contrast, PRoLoRA (Wang et al., 2024) employs an intra-layer sharing mechanism with learn-
523
      able parameters, but it only reduces parameters without capturing global features. Additionally,
524   VB-LoRA (Li et al., 2024) introduces a “divide-and-share” approach that partitions shared vectors
525   into a vector bank, addressing the limitations of traditional low-rank decomposition across matrix
526   dimensions, modules, and layers. However, this approach selects vectors uniformly without ac-
527   counting for the internal structure of the model, leading to suboptimal utilization of the model’s
528   internal information

      6      C ONCLUSION
531
532
      This paper presents Bi-Share LoRA, a method that enhances the parameter efficiency of large lan-
533   guage models (LLMs) by combining shared intra-layer, inter-layer parameters, and local parameters.
534   This approach reduces the number of trainable parameters while boosting model efficiency. Experi-
535   ments on various Llama models demonstrate that Bi-Share LoRA significantly cuts down parameter
536   usage by 56.40% and improves average performance by 0.33% on commonsense reasoning tasks
537   and 2.08% on MMLU benchmark. Overall, Bi-Share LoRA effectively reduces redundancy and
538   enhances model adaptability across diverse tasks. In the future, we will explore more intelligent pa-
539   rameter sharing modes that can selectively select different modules or different layers for parameter
      sharing, ultimately further improving the performance of the model.

      A.1     PARAMETER -E FFICIENT F INE -T UNING (PEFT)
759
760   Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) is crucial for large language models (LLMs) as it re-
761   duces computational costs while preserving performance (Ding et al., 2023). Specifically, Adapters
762   (Houlsby et al., 2019), inserted between layers, allow task-specific fine-tuning with minimal train-
763   able parameters while keeping most weights fixed. Similarly, Prefix Tuning (Li & Liang, 2021)
764   adds learnable tokens at each transformer layer to guide task-specific behavior without modifying
765
      core parameters. In contrast, Prompt Tuning (Lester et al., 2021) optimizes a small set of prompts
      attached to the input, leaving the model’s architecture unchanged. LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) repa-
766
      rameterizes weight matrices into low-rank forms, significantly reducing trainable parameters while
767
      maintaining performance. Some studies build upon LoRA to further improve and optimize its per-
768   formance (Shi et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024; yang Liu et al., 2024; Koohpayegani
769   et al., 2024). Likewise, we propose Bi-Share LoRA, building on LoRA, which shares parameters
770   both within and across layers to further enhance efficiency and adaptability.
771

      mutual information, which quantifies the relationship between them and can be computed using the
800

805
      significant overlap in their information, implying redundancy. The calculated mutual information

1104   A.14    DATASETS AND B ENCHMARKS
1105
1106
       BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019) is a dataset for yes/no question answering. It consists of naturally
       occurring questions paired with passages extracted from Wikipedia. It is part of the SuperGLUE
1107
       benchmark, a suite of challenging NLP tasks. It is used to assess a model’s ability to perform
1108
       reading comprehension and binary classification based on the context provided.
1109
1110   PIQA (Bisk et al., 2020) is a dataset designed to evaluate commonsense reasoning about physical
1111   interactions. It contains multiple-choice questions related to everyday physical tasks, asking models
1112
       to choose the most plausible way of completing or describing an action. It is used as a benchmark
       for evaluating the commonsense reasoning abilities of language models, particularly in the context
1113
       of tasks requiring physical understanding.
1114
1115   HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019) is a large-scale dataset for evaluating commonsense reasoning
1116   and natural language inference. The task involves selecting the most plausible continuation of a
1117   given story or event description from multiple choices. It is used to benchmark models on their
1118
       ability to perform commonsense reasoning, particularly in cases where the correct answer requires
       understanding context, sequencing, and implications.
1119
1120   WinoGrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2021) is a large-scale dataset for commonsense reasoning, specif-
1121   ically designed to address the limitations of the Winograd Schema Challenge. The task involves
1122   resolving pronoun references in sentences, where the correct interpretation requires commonsense
1123   knowledge. It is used as a benchmark for evaluating models on their ability to perform pronoun
1124
       resolution and commonsense reasoning.
1125   ARC-easy (Clark et al., 2018) and ARC-challenge (Clark et al., 2018) are part of the AI2 Rea-
1126   soning Challenge, designed to evaluate a model’s ability to answer multiple-choice questions that
1127   require complex reasoning and background knowledge. They are used as a benchmark for testing ad-
1128   vanced question-answering systems, especially those requiring sophisticated reasoning, knowledge
1129   integration, and inference capabilities.
1130   OpenbookQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018) is a multiple-choice question-answering dataset that focuses
1131   on elementary science questions. The dataset comes with an ”open book” of scientific facts, and
1132   models must combine this knowledge with reasoning to answer the questions correctly. It is used
1133   as a benchmark for evaluating a model’s ability to perform open-domain question answering, where
       success requires not just knowledge retrieval but also reasoning and application of that knowledge.

       Social QA (Sap et al., 2019) , often abbreviated as SIQA, is composed of question-answer pairs that

1136   of models to engage in information-seeking conversations, where the model must ask clarifying
1137   questions to a human user to gather information and then provide an answer to the original query.

       MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021) is a benchmark designed to assess a model’s world knowledge and

1143   answer choices, and the task is to predict the correct one based on the given instruction.
