009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Several recent studies have investigated low-precision accumulation, reporting im-
012            provements in throughput, power, and area across various platforms. However,
013            the accompanying proposals have only considered the quantization-aware train-
014            ing (QAT) paradigm, in which models are fine-tuned or trained from scratch with
015            quantization in the loop. As models continue to grow in size, QAT techniques
016            become increasingly more expensive, which has motivated the recent surge in
017            post-training quantization (PTQ) research. To the best of our knowledge, ours
018
               marks the first formal study of accumulator-aware quantization in the PTQ set-
               ting. To bridge this gap, we introduce AXE—a practical, low-overhead framework
019
               of accumulator-aware extensions designed to endow overflow avoidance guaran-
020
               tees to existing layer-wise PTQ algorithms. We theoretically motivate AXE and
021            demonstrate its flexibility by implementing it on top of two state-of-the-art PTQ
022            algorithms: GPFQ and OPTQ. We further generalize AXE to support multi-stage
023            accumulation for the first time, opening the door for full datapath optimization and
024            scaling to large language models (LLMs). We evaluate AXE across autoregressive
025            language generation models, and observe significant improvements in the trade-
026            off between accumulator bit width and model accuracy over baseline methods.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Modern deep learning models have scaled to use billions of parameters, requiring billions (or even
032   trillions) of multiply-accumulate (MAC) operations during inference. Their enormous size presents
033
      a major obstacle to their deployment as their compute and memory requirements during inference
      often exceed the budgets of real-world applications. As a result, model compression has emerged as
034
      an important active area in deep learning research, with quantization being among the most prevalent
035
      techniques studied and applied in practice (Wu et al., 2020; Nagel et al., 2021; Gholami et al., 2022).
036
037   Quantization techniques commonly reduce inference costs by restricting the precision of its weights
038   and activations. Although substituting the standard 32-bit floating-point operands for low-precision
039
      counterparts can drastically reduce the cost of multiplications, this only accounts for part of the core
      MAC operation; the resulting products are often still accumulated at 32 bits. Recent studies have
040
      demonstrated that also restricting the precision of the accumulator can yield significant benefits (see
041
      Section 2.2). However, exploiting such an optimization is non-trivial in practice as reducing the
042   width of the accumulator exponentially increases the risk of numerical overflow, which is known to
043   introduce arithmetic errors that significantly degrade model accuracy (Ni et al., 2020).
044
045
      To address this, recent work has proposed an accumulator-aware quantization paradigm that en-
      tirely eliminates the risk of numerical overflow via strict learning constraints informed by theoret-
046
      ical guarantees (Colbert et al., 2023). The resulting scope of investigations has been limited to the
047
      quantization-aware training (QAT) setting in which models are trained from scratch or fine-tuned
048   from checkpoints with quantization in the loop (Colbert et al., 2023; 2024). With the rising training
049   costs of modern deep learning models (e.g., large language models), it is important to develop meth-
050   ods that are equally as effective in the post-training quantization (PTQ) setting, where pre-trained
051   models are directly quantized and calibrated using relatively modest resources. However, control-
052   ling the accumulator bit width in such a scenario is non-trivial. In this work, we characterize and
053   address these challenges, introduce a practical framework for their investigation, and establish a new
      state-of-the-art for accumulator-aware weight quantization in the PTQ setting.

113   represented in the quantized domain, and s is a strictly positive scalar that corresponds to the reso-
114   lution of the quantizer. Scaled values are commonly rounded to the nearest integer, denoted by ⌈·⌋,
115   and elements that exceed the representation range of the quantized domain Ab are clipped.

119   (QAT) and post-training quantization (PTQ). QAT methods train or fine-tune a neural network with

      quantization in the loop, which often requires significant compute resources and sufficiently large
      datasets. Our work focuses on PTQ methods, which directly cast and calibrate pre-trained mod-
121
      els and often rely on little to no data without end-to-end training. PTQ methods tend to follow a

      and activations to low-precision integers, which can realize throughput gains from both accelerated

      2.2   L OW-P RECISION ACCUMULATION
140
141   The majority of neural network quantization research targeting compute acceleration emphasizes
142   low-precision weights and activations. While this can significantly reduce the costs of multiplica-
143   tions, the resulting products are often still accumulated using high-precision additions. As lower
144   precision integer representations continue to increase in popularity (Dettmers & Zettlemoyer, 2023;
145   Ma et al., 2024), one can expect a focus skewed towards weight and activation quantization to yield
146   diminishing returns as high-precision additions can bottleneck throughput, power, and area (Ni et al.,
147
      2020; de Bruin et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2021; Colbert et al., 2024). For example, Ni et al. (2020) show
      that when constraining weights and activations to 3-bit × 1-bit multipliers, the cost of 32-bit accu-
148
      mulation consumes nearly 75% of the total power and 90% of the total area of their custom MAC
149
      unit; they report up to 4× power savings and 5× area reduction when reducing to 8-bit accumulation.
150
151   Reducing the accumulator bit width is non-trivial in practice as it exponentially increases the risk
152   of numerical overflow, often introducing arithmetic errors that degrade model accuracy (Ni et al.,
153
      2020; Colbert et al., 2023). Existing methods to prepare quantized neural networks (QNNs) for low-
      precision accumulation often aim to either reduce the risk of numerical overflow (Xie et al., 2021;
154
      Li et al., 2022; Azamat et al., 2022) or mitigate its impact on model accuracy (Ni et al., 2020; Sakr
155
      et al., 2019; Blumenfeld et al., 2024). These empirical approaches rely on several assumptions that
156   limit their real-world applicability. For one, empirical estimates of overflow rely on a priori knowl-
157   edge of the input distribution, which is impractical to assume in many real-world scenarios and can
158   even introduce vulnerabilities (Baier et al., 2019). Furthermore, overflow behavior can vary across
159   platforms and programming languages, so designing methods to mitigate the detrimental impact of
160   one particular overflow behavior (e.g., wraparound two’s complement arithmetic) limits portability
161   across applications and accelerators. Finally, empirical approaches are unable to support applica-
      tions that require guaranteed arithmetic correctness, such as encrypted inference (Lou & Jiang, 2019;


                                                                                                                                           3

      Stoian et al., 2023), and are known to break down when overflows occur too frequently (Ni et al.,
163   2020). To address these concerns, recent work has proposed to avoid overflow altogether using
164   accumulator-aware quantization (A2Q) (Colbert et al., 2023; 2024).

      3     ACCUMULATOR -AWARE P OST-T RAINING Q UANTIZATION
200
201
      The standard problem for neural network quantization aims to map high-precision values (e.g., 32-
202
      bit floating-point) to low-precision counterparts (e.g., 4-bit scaled integers) while locally minimizing
203   the discrepancy between the output of the original model and that of the compressed one, as formal-
204   ized by Eq. 2 in Section 2.1. In the post-training quantization (PTQ) setting, one often assumes the
205   quantizer parameters (i.e., scaling factor s and zero point z) are fixed and that the individual weights
206   can move freely, as in Lybrand & Saab (2021); Frantar et al. (2022); Hubara et al. (2021). Build-
207   ing from this, we formalize accumulator-aware post-training quantization as a constrained variant
208   of the standard reconstruction problem in which the optimal quantized weights q ∗ minimize local
209   quantization error while also satisfying a strict ℓ1 -norm constraint, as defined below.
210
211                             \bm {q}^* = \argmin _{\bm {q}} \frac {1}{2} \Vert \bm {X}^T \bm {w} - \bm {\Tilde {X}}^T \bm {q} \Vert ^2_2 \quad \textrm {s.t.} \quad \Vert \bm {q} \Vert _1 \leq Z \label {eq:a2q-reconstr-problem}    (5)
212
213   To approximately solve this accumulator-constrained reconstruction problem, we introduce AXE,
214   a practical low-overhead framework of general accumulator-aware extensions that endow guaran-
215   teed overflow avoidance to layer-wise quantization algorithms that greedily assign bits element-by-
      element (e.g., GPFQ and OPTQ). AXE is built on two accumulator-aware constraints: (1) a soft

      global constraint that discourages the underlying algorithm from opportunistically selecting quan-
217   tized weights with high magnitudes; and (2) a strict local constraint that greedily limits the range of
218   each selected quantized weight while error is iteratively corrected. In its standard form, AXE applies
219   these constraints per-channel (or per-neuron) so that each dot product in the network is guaranteed
220   to independently avoid overflow. Furthermore, without violating our constraints, we generalize our
221   framework to also support multi-stage accumulation in the form of tiled dot products by applying
222   our constraints in finer granularities. We first theoretically justify our solution using GPFQ, then
223   provide accumulator-aware variants of both GPFQ and OPTQ.

      3.3   AXE: ACCUMULATOR -AWARE E XTENSIONS
352
353   While the theoretical justification we presented is tied to the formulation of GPFQ and its deriva-
354   tions, we can extract our constraints to construct a generalized framework that enables the investiga-
355   tion of accumulator-aware PTQ with any iterative algorithm that sequentially assigns bits, assuming
356   the algorithm is also amenable to activation quantization. Our framework consists of two steps:
357   (1) accumulator-aware projection based on our soft ℓ1 -norm regularization penalty; and (2) greedy
358   accumulator-aware clipping based on our strict range limits. We further generalize AXE to support
359   multi-stage accumulation, which has implications for tiled dot products and SIMD parallelization.
360   In Appendix A, we present the pseudocode for our accumulator-aware variants of GPFQ and OPTQ.
361   In both cases, λ is derived per-channel before quantization. Adjusted weight values are greedily
362   projected on the ℓ1 ball accordingly, then clipped to the difference between the cumulative sum
363   of positive and negative elements and their respective limits. The resulting set of quantized weights
364   Q ∈ AK×C
             M     is then guaranteed to avoid overflow when accumulating its inner product with any X̃ ∈
        K×D
365   AN      into P -bit signed registers. Unlike the base GPFQ and OPTQ algorithms, our accumulator-
366   aware variants require quantized activations to calculate the accumulator-aware limits in Eq. 20.
367   Multi-Stage Accumulation. Our accumulator-aware constraints can be generalized to target cus-
368   tomized datapaths beyond user-specific accumulator bit widths. Unlike A2Q and A2Q+, which
369   assume a monolithic accumulator for each dot product (Colbert et al., 2023; 2024), we generalize
370   our framework to support multi-staged accumulation as visualized in Figure 2. In such a scenario,
371   our constraints are enforced on the quantized weights in tiles of size T so that each partial dot prod-
372   uct can be concurrently computed by an atomic MAC unit. We refer to the accumulator of this
373   atomic MAC unit as the “inner” accumulator and denote its bit width as PI . Conversely, we refer
374   to the accumulator of the resulting partial sums as the “outer” accumulator and denote its bit width
375
      as PO . Given that a K-dimensional dot product is executed in tiles of size T , where each tile is
      constrained to a PI -bit accumulator, we can calculate the minimum bit width required to guarantee
376
      overflow avoidance for the outer accumulator as:

380   FBGEMM (Khudia et al., 2018a), XNNPACK (Dukahn & Barchard, 2021), and Ryzen AI (AMD,

382   to realize performance benefits, albeit without any theoretical guarantees of overflow avoidance. For
383   example, Khudia et al. (2018b) use FBGEMM to realize nearly a 2× throughput uplift on compute-

387
      pre-trained model for low-precision multi-staged accumulation while guaranteeing overflow avoid-

      As discussed in Colbert et al. (2024), the ℓ1 -norm of an unconstrained weight vector inherently
456
      grows as its dimensionality increases. This suggests that accumulator-aware quantization scales
457   well to strictly deeper neural architectures since the constraints tighten with width rather than depth;
458   experimental results on the ResNet family support this hypothesis (Colbert et al., 2024). However,
459   this also suggests that accumulator-aware quantization scales poorly in neural network families that
460   grow in width, as is the case in transformer architectures (Zhang et al., 2022a; Biderman et al.,
461   2023). Thus, to scale our accumulator-aware PTQ framework to billion-parameter language models,
462   we turn to our multi-stage accumulation variant of AXE (see Section 3.3). Here, one assumes the
463   partial sums of a dot product are concurrently computed in fixed-length tiles of size T . Our goal in
464   this setting is to minimize perplexity for a target inner accumulator bit width PI that is assumed to
465
      be universal across all tiles. Thus, our accumulator width is constant even as models grow wider.
466   Rather than exploring the full quantization design space, we focus on 4-bit weights and 8-bit acti-
467   vations (W4A8) to maximize utility across platforms with a reasonable number of experiments, as
468   prior studies have suggested this configuration is generally useful (Dettmers & Zettlemoyer, 2023;
469   Li et al., 2024). We evaluate AXE on top of both GPFQ and OPTQ using tiles of 128 elements
470   under 16-bit accumulator constraints (note that PI∗ = 20 when T = 128 for W4A8 via Eq. 3).

472

504   5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSIONS
505
506   As neural networks continue to increase in size, and their weights and activations are increasingly
507   being represented with fewer bits, we anticipate the accumulator to play a larger role in hardware-
508
      software co-design (see Section 2.2). While prior work on accumulator-aware quantization has been
      limited to the QAT setting (see Section 2.3), ours marks the first to extend accumulator-awareness to
509
      the PTQ setting. To do so, we introduce AXE—a practical low-overhead framework of accumulator-
510
      aware extensions designed to endow overflow avoidance guarantees to any layer-wise PTQ algorithm
511   that greedily assign bits element-by-element. We demonstrate the flexibility of AXE by presenting
512   accumulator-aware variants of GPFQ and OPTQ with principled overflow avoidance guarantees.
513   Furthermore, unlike prior accumulator-aware quantization methods, which assume a monolithic ac-
514   cumulator, we generalize AXE to support multi-stage accumulation for the first time.
515
      Our experiments in Section 4.1 show that AXE significantly improves the trade-off between accu-
516
      mulator bit width and model accuracy when compared to existing baselines. As has been shown
517   before in the QAT setting (Colbert et al., 2023; 2024), exposing control over the accumulator bit
518   width allows one to reduce P further than what is attainable via naı̈ve bit width manipulations
519   while also maintaining model accuracy. Moreover, we observe that AXE universally yields marked
520   improvement over EP-init across both models and datasets, establishing a new state-of-the-art for
521   accumulator-aware quantization in the PTQ setting. Although EP-init and AXE are both derived
522   from the same convex optimization problem (see Eq. 14), EP-init is a vector projection that is ap-
523   plied after quantization and relies on the round-to-zero rounding function to ensure the ℓ1 -norm
524   constraints are respected. Previous reports had suspected EP-init is limited by this reliance on round-
525
      to-zero (Colbert et al., 2023; 2024); we provide an ablation study in Appendix C.2 that supports this
      hypothesis but also suggests error correction is critical. For GPFQ, we observe error correction to
526
      be more important than round-to-nearest, but we observe the opposite for OPTQ, although a more
527
      exhaustive analysis in future work may uncover more insights.
528
529   Our experiments in Section 4.2 show that our generalized multi-stage accumulation enables
530   accumulator-aware weight quantization for billion-parameter LLMs. We observe that the gap be-
531
      tween the constrained and unconstrained quantized models shrinks as model size increases, preserv-
      ing both perplexity and zero-shot reasoning. However, we also observe that the gap between the
532
      quantized models and their 32-bit floating-point counterparts begins to increase with model size.
533
      This is consistent with the findings of Li et al. (2024), who conclude that while larger models tend
534   to have a higher tolerance for weight quantization, they also tend to have a lower tolerance for acti-
535   vation quantization. Thus, there exists two diametrically opposing trends in superposition. For the
536   Pythia model suite, we observe Pythia-1B to be the equilibrium point where the costs of weight and
537   activation quantization are balanced. While it is orthogonal to the scope of this study, we expect
538   the emerging rotation-based quantization schemes (e.g., QuaRot (Ashkboos et al., 2024) or Spin-
539   Quant (Liu et al., 2024)) to impact this equilibrium point and reduce the gap between quantized
      models and their 32-bit floating-point counterparts. We leave such investigations for future work.

      C.1   H YPERPARAMETERS & Q UANTIZATION S CHEMES
867
868   Below, we provide a detailed description of the quantization schemes and the specific hyperparam-
869   eters used in our experiments. As discussed in Section 4, we consider pre-trained autoregressive
870   language models that are respectively made publicly available via the HuggingFace (Wolf et al.,
871   2020) libraries. All models are quantized via the Brevitas (Pappalardo, 2023) quantization library
872   using a single AMD MI210 GPU with 64 GB of memory.
873
      We leverage the unmodified implementations of the various LLMs discussed in Section 4 as pro-
874   vided by HuggingFace (Wolf et al., 2020), as well as their pre-trained floating-point checkpoints
875   and datasets (Lhoest et al., 2021). We use drop-in replacements for all linear layers in the networks
876   except the embedding layer or final prediction head, leaving them at 32-bit floating-point. As is
877   common practice (Frantar et al., 2022), we build our calibration set using 128 samples randomly se-
878   lected from the WikiText2 dataset (Merity et al., 2016) without replacement using a fixed sequence
879   length of 2048 tokens for all models except GPT2 (Radford et al., 2019), which is restricted to a
880   maximum sequence length of 1024 by the library.
881   Quantization Scheme. As discussed in Section 2, we adopt the standard uniform integer quantizer
882   parameterized by scaling factor s and zero-point z. We quantize activations asymmetrically, tuning
883   z to the lowest 99-th percentile based on the calibration data. While AXE is not reliant on symmetric
884   weight quantization, we eliminate zero-points in all weight quantizers such that z = 0, as is common
885   practice so as to avoid computational overhead of cross-terms (Nagel et al., 2021; Zhang et al.,
886   2022b). Throughout our experiments, we adopt 32-bit floating-point scaling factors that take the
887   form of Eq. 26, where max(w) is calculated per-channel for the weights and per-tensor for the
888
      activations quantized to b bits.
889                                                s = \frac {\max ( \bm {w} )}{2^{b-1} - 1} \label {eq:scaling_factor_eq}    (26)
890
891
892   Quantization Process. To quantize our models, we first load the pre-trained checkpoint and merge
893
      batch normalization layers if they exist, then we apply SmoothQuant (Xiao et al., 2023) before
      calibrating the scaling factors and zero-points. We then apply either GPFQ (Lybrand & Saab, 2021)
894
      or OPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022) (with or without AXE) before finally applying bias correction (Nagel
895
      et al., 2019). When sequentially quantizing weights element-by-element, we do so in descending
896   order according to the diagonal value of the Hessian proxy (2XX T by our notation in Section 2),
897   which was originally implemented in IST-DASLab (2022) and reported to yield superior results
898   in Lin et al. (2023); Chee et al. (2024). When evaluating EP-init in the PTQ setting, we do so after
899   OPTQ or GPFQ but before bias correction. Because bias correction does not adjust weight values,
900   this allows us to at least perform some form of error correction with EP-init while still ensuring
901   guaranteed overflow avoidance.
