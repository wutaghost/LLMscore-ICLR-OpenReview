009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               The proliferation of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) in the past several years calls
012            for rigorous and comprehensive evaluation methods and benchmarks. This work
013            analyzes existing VLM evaluation techniques, including automated metrics, AI-
014            based assessments, and human evaluations across diverse tasks. We first introduce
015            Robin - a novel suite of VLMs that we built by combining Large Language Models
016            (LLMs) and Vision Encoders (VEs) at multiple scales, and use Robin to identify
017            shortcomings of current evaluation approaches across scales. Next, to overcome the
018            identified limitations, we introduce CHIRP - a new long form response benchmark
019            we developed for more robust and complete VLM evaluation. We provide open
020
               access to the Robin training code, model suite, and CHIRP benchmark to promote
               reproducibility and advance VLM research.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025   Recently, a lot of significant advances have been made in Vision-Language Models (VLMs), driven
026   by breakthroughs in computer vision and natural language processing Chen et al. (2022); Li et al.
027   (2023b); Liu et al. (2023b); Sun et al. (2023). However, existing VLM benchmarks, often designed
028   for specific tasks (e.g., VQAv2 Goyal et al. (2017)), struggle to accurately reflect real-world VLM
029   performance and capture nuanced differences between models Hsieh et al. (2024). This is particularly
030   evident when evaluating models with significant architectural variations, where standard benchmark
031   scores remain similar despite noticeable differences in human-perceived model quality.
032   To address this issue, we introduce CHIRP, a hybrid VLM benchmark that combines automated
033   metrics’ scalability with human evaluators’ nuanced judgment. We argue that this approach is crucial
034   for capturing the complexities of VLM behavior, which traditional benchmarks often fail to represent.
035
      To demonstrate the limitations of existing benchmarks and the efficacy of our proposed method, we
036   introduce Robin, a suite of VLMs trained at various scales, inspired by the Pythia language model
037   suite Biderman et al. (2023). By systematically varying the Vision Encoder (VE) and the Large
038   Language Model (LLM) sizes, we will show that while benchmark scores remain largely unaffected,
039   human evaluations reveal significant differences in the models’ outputs quality.
040
      Our findings underscore the need for more robust and human-centric VLM evaluation methodologies.
041
      CHIRP paves the way for developing more reliable and informative VLM benchmarks, ultimately
042   leading to the creation of more effective and impactful VLMs.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
055
056   Scaling Suites. Scaling laws have recently emerged as one of the central research areas in large
057   foundation models Aghajanyan et al. (2023); Isik et al. (2024). These laws enable performance
058   prediction based on variations in compute time, dataset size, and model parameters, facilitating
059   efficient resource allocation by extrapolating results from small-scale experiments.
060
      Kaplan et al. Kaplan et al. (2020) pioneered the application of scaling laws to language models,
061
      demonstrating a power-law relationships between loss and model size, dataset size, and compute
062   time. This has led to practical applications, such as the Pythia suite Biderman et al. (2023), which
063   comprises of identically trained language models with varying parameter sizes, empirically verifying
064   these scaling laws.
065
      Cherti et al. Cherti et al. (2023) investigated the scaling laws of the CLIP vision encoders, training
066
      and comparing different sizes of the CLIP vision encoders on the same data. These models indeed
067
      verified the aforementioned scaling laws and have become a very popular suite of models.
068
069   AI-based Evaluation. The advent of powerful foundation models like GPT-4V offers a new way
070   to evaluate weaker models, moving beyond traditional, rigid metrics such as exact string matching,
071   as done in Hudson & Manning (2019); Mishra et al. (2019); Singh et al. (2019). Early evidence
072   from benchmarks like MM-Vet Yu et al. (2023) and VQA tasks Agrawal et al. (2016) suggests that
073   evaluating with stronger models offers a promising path towards more comprehensive and insightful
074   evaluation, surpassing the limitations of static, string-based methods Ji et al. (2023); Lee et al. (2024).
075   This shift towards leveraging the semantic understanding of LLMs for evaluation promises to unlock
076   a better understanding of model capabilities.
077   Zheng et al. Zheng et al. (2023) introduce two benchmarks, MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena, to explore
078   the feasibility of employing LLMs as judges. Their findings indicate that advanced LLMs, such
079   as GPT-4, closely align with human preferences, achieving over 80% of agreement Rafailov et al.
080   (2024). Similarly, AlpacaEval Li et al. (2023a) utilizes LLMs to assess instruction-following models.
081   Wu et al. Wu & Aji (2023) focused on the bias in evaluations conducted by both human and LLM
082   annotators, particularly noting a preference for flawed content if it avoids brevity or grammatical
083   errors, and introduced the Multi-Elo Rating System (MERS) for more nuanced assessments. A
084   study by Koo et al. Koo et al. (2023) pointed out significant biases of LLMs evaluators, with an
085   average Rank-Biased Overlap (RBO) score of 49.6%, suggesting a misalignment between machine
086   and human preferences.

      To rigorously assess the reliability of existing benchmarks, we sampled 100 random questions from
163   GQA as well as 100 from TextVQA. These questions require the model to observe the image and
164   answer objective facts. We examined the questions, the provided ground truth answers, and model

168
      If we want toqestimate the error of this value, the actual percentage of incorrect prompts p̂ is
169   p̂ ∈ p ± z ∗ p∗(1−p) n   . For a 95% confidence interval: z = 1.96, and with our sample size n of
170   100, we measured p = 0.09, we are 95% certain: 3.4% ≤ p̂ ≤ 14.6%. This equates to 770,769
171   to 3,309,773 questions of the 22,669,678 GQA questions being incorrect. Although this is a large
172   spread, this result rmeains quite significant, as most improvements on State of The Art (SoTA) models
173   are very small, regularly under 3% Li et al. (2023b). These findings lead to the conclusion that if 2
174
      models score within 3% of each other on GQA, they could very well be equal in actual performance
      on it. Representative examples of the aforementioned questions are shown in Appendix B.4.1.

176   Conducting the same study for TextVQA, we identified only 5 problematic questions in the sample
177   that either did not require reading the text in the image, or were too vague and did not correspond

179   0.73% ≤ p̂ ≤ 9.27%. Although SoTA models are indeed close in performance, we are not as

      confident as in the case of GQA. However, two SoTA models scoring within 0.7% of each other

      5.1   L ONG VS S HORT R ESPONSES (LV SR)
193
194   Most benchmarks were evaluated on short responses; with explicit instructions to "respond with one
195   word or phrase". However, we hypothesize that short responses do not convey sufficient information
196   to evaluate model performance in detail. To test this theory, we allowed models to generate longer
197   responses without prompting for brevity. We then collected, manually evaluated, and compared these
198   LvSR to see if they offered a more nuanced assessment of the models.
199   The GQA benchmark provides an evaluation script that grades responses using string matching
200   on single phrase responses. On the sample of 100 GQA questions, we prompted and manually
201   graded our models for LvSR to see if new trends across the LLM size ablation appear with longer
202   responses, the results of which are shown in Figure 3. For sufficiently large models, we did not
203   notice a significant improvement in overall model accuracy. However, models often got different
204   questions correct when responding with LvSR. To show this, we calculated a superscore, in which
205   responses were marked correct if either the long or short response was correct (See Figure 3). The
206
      improved results of the superscore indicate that while long and short responses achieve a similar
      overall accuracy, they tend to be accurate for a different set of questions. This suggests that evaluating
207
      long and short responses demonstrate different model skills. In Appendix B.4.3, we’ve included
208
      examples of differing responses when prompting for long and short answers.

211

215   ground truth, which can cause issues with exact string matching based evaluation. These issues can
      be especially prevalent with non instruction tuned models, or small scale models.

412   6.5.1     C OHEN ’ S K APPA
413
414   Cohen’s Kappa Cohen (1960) is a method used for calculating inter-rater reliability, that takes into
415   account random chance agreement. A Cohen’s Kappa score of 1 indicates a complete agreement
416   between reviewers, while a Kappa of 0 indicates no agreements other than a random chance of
417
      agreement. Further details on the calculation of Cohen’s Kappa can be found in Appendix C.2.5.
      Looking at Table 2, the results indicate that both GPT-4V (S) and GPT-4V (R) have higher agreement
418
      with human surveys compared to LLaVA-34B. We also note that GPT-4V (R) exhibits the most
419
      agreement to the human surveys of the both of them. However, according to Landis & Koch’s
420   interpretation of Cohen’s Kappa Landis & Koch (1977), GPT-4V (R) only achieves “slight” to “fair”
421   agreement. Despite the low overall agreement, GPT-4V evaluations still exhibit very similar trends to
422   human evaluations.

465   6.5.3    C ONTRADICTIONS
466   One hypothesis for why trends are better captured using AI evaluations is that a single AI evaluator
467   is more consistent than the combined evaluations of many different humans, as different humans
468   may have different preferences or leniency. We tried negating this by aggregating multiple human
469   surveys together however it is possible this still influenced the results. To evaluate the consistency of
470   AI versus human evaluators, we introduce a concept to measure contradictions in their rankings. A
471   contradiction occurs when an evaluator’s preferences form a cycle, such as preferring A over B, B
472   over C, but then C over A. A more exhaustive explanation along which sample graphs is given in
473   Appendix C.2.8. This inconsistency suggests a lack of transitivity in their judgments. By counting
474
      these contradictions, we can determine how reliably an evaluator ranks models.
475   The results presented in Figure 7, indicate that human and LLaVA-34B based evaluations tend to
476   have the most contradictions, requiring more runs to average out human or model inconsistencies.
477   GPT-4V (R) however is the model with the least contradictions, leading us to the conclusion that a
478   single run is sufficient as the model is highly consistent in its responses.
479
480   6.6     O BSERVATIONS AND I NSIGHTS
481
482
      Although AI-based evaluations don’t consistently agree with human evaluations on a case-by-case
      basis, GPT-4V (R) displays both higher agreement with humans preferences and less contradictions
483
      than GPT-4V (S).
484
485   In general, GPT-based evaluations tend to produce lower variance results which correlate better with
      training loss, as shown Appendix C.2.6. We hypothesize that these smoother results are attributed to


                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      the fact that GPT employs a more consistent approach to grading across evaluations, whereas multiple
487   different human evaluators lead to more variability, as indicated by the higher rates of contradictions.
488   This variability could be affecting our ability to accurately measure how well human evaluations
489   correlate with training loss and we hope to address this in future work.
490
      Another possibility is that AI evaluations favor models with larger LLMs because the LLMs generate
491
      preferable strings of words irrespective of the content of the image. However, we rule out this possi-
492
      bility by showing that GPT-4V (R) preferences do not align with the more likely logit probabilities of
493   question-answer strings in Appendix C.2.7.
494
495   Furthermore, there seems to be an ideal ratio of VE size to LLM size that provides an optimal model,
496
      which will be the preferred model for that LLM size. Although this was hinted at in both the loss
      and previous benchmarks, this relationship was rather faint and is made more apparent in the human
497
      preferences result in CHIRP. This can be seen in the complete graph of human preferences shown in
498
      Appendix C.2.9.
499
500   Ultimately, both human and AI evaluations show that performance on CHIRP correlates with loss
501   more than other evaluation tasks. We take this as evidence that the CHIRP benchmark assesses a
502
      valuable and unique skill that other benchmarks do not test for. This makes CHIRP a useful addition
      to the suite of benchmarks that is currently used to evaluate VLMs.
503
504
505
      6.7   L IMITATIONS
506
507
      Although the CHIRP benchmark revealed scaling trends in our models that other benchmarks did
508   not, it has several notable limitations. First, it heavily relies on the strong language proficiency of the
509   evaluator, to evaluate a models’ perceptual capabilities.
510
511
      Second, the benchmark is not very extensive as it only contains 104 questions on 104 images.
      However, the small size is a deliberate choice based on the cost of evaluations. As grading the
512
      responses requires VLM or human evaluations, cost is a major consideration when deciding the size
513
      and 104 was seen as a good balance between evaluating the models performance and the cost or
514   evaluating. This is in line with other small, high quality, and well respected datasets like MM-Vet
515   Yu et al. (2023), 218 questions on 200 images, and LLaVA-Bench Liu et al. (2023b), 60 questions
516   on 24 images, which both require LLM evaluations, which itself is cheaper than VLM or human
517   evaluations.
518
      Finally, models are benchmarked via pairwise matchups. Therefore models can only be compared
519
      via a direct matchup or mutual matchups. This requires more work when validating a new model,
520   requiring matchups which each of the most performant models, however we believe this is a valuable
521   trade-off for a considerably more accurate evaluation and ranking.
522
523
524
      7     C ONCLUSIONS
525
526
527   In this paper, we explore the limitations of existing vision-language model (VLM) benchmarks, and
528   introduce CHIRP, a novel benchmark designed to address these shortcomings. Our analysis reveals
529   that a longer-form benchmark with open-ended questions quantifies multimodal understanding in
530
      ways that existing benchmarks do not. While current benchmarks evaluate contextually relevant
      responses, they often fail to capture the subtleties that humans value in long-form content.
531
532   Expensive evaluations. We acknowledge that generating and evaluating long-form responses,
533   especially with human evaluators, can be resource-intensive. To mitigate this challenge, we have
534   designed CHIRP to remain effective even at a smaller scale. Additionally, our findings suggest that
535   AI evaluations can serve as a reliable proxy for human assessments, demonstrating similar overall
536
      trends in the same unique skill we aim to test for.
537   As VLMs continue to advance towards and beyond human-level performance on quantitative tasks,
538   we emphasize the need to assess models on qualitative tasks that reflect the nuances of human
539   preferences. Our work demonstrates that CHIRP is a viable benchmark for evaluating skills that have
      not been previously reported.

      A     M ODEL TRAINING SETUP
649
650
      A.1    P ROCESS AND DATA
651
652   In order to maximize comparability between models, we train all of them with the same hyper-
653   parameters and data. The training of our VLMs is broken down into two phases: pretraining and
654   finetuning. During pretraining, only the MLP projection is unfrozen, with both vision and language
655   models frozen. The dataset used for this step is the LLaVA Visual Instruct Pretrain LCS-558K Liu
656   et al. (2023a), which is a subset of the LAION/CC/SBU dataset, filtered with a more balanced
657
      concept coverage distribution. Following this, we do a finetuning step where we tuned all three
      components: the MLP projection, language model and vision encoder. The data that was used for
658
      this part of training is the LLaVA Visual Instruct 665K Liu et al. (2023a). This dataset contains 150K
659
      GPT-generated multimodal instruction-following data, in addition to using images from the Coco
660   2017 dataset Lin et al. (2015), the GQA dataseet Hudson & Manning (2019), the OCR-VQA dataset
661   Mishra et al. (2019), the TextVQA dataset Singh et al. (2019), and the VisualGenome dataset Krishna
662   et al. (2016).
663
      In the LLaVA 1.5 model release Liu et al. (2023a), the authors showed that when doing the finetuning
664
      of the language model, there was little difference between doing a full finetuning as opposed to a
665
      Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) Hu et al. (2021) finetuning. Therefore we trained all our models using
666   a LoRA finetuning for the language model.

      B.2   V ERIFYING THE RESULTS ON AN INDEPENDENT S OTA MODEL
919
920   While other experiments in this paper are done using our Robin scaling suite with based on the Pythia
921   LLMs, this section was done using LLaVA1.5-7B Liu et al. (2023a), in order to make sure that our
922   results translated across models. We manually graded LLaVA1.5-7B responses on our sample of
923   GQA questions. Using the known evaluations, we graded the accuracy of automated, LLM, and VLM
924   evaluation methods. Results of grading accuracy on LLaVA1.5-7B are presented in the confusion
925   matrix 13.
926   Some small, but important, usage details we noticed: The LLMs have a very low false positive rate,
927   especially in contrast to their false negative rate. This suggests that for actual deployment, we could
928   employ a two phase strategy, in which we assume the LLM is correct when it marks a long response
929   as correct. When the LLM responds false, we fallback to a VLM. This strategy eliminates some VLM
930   false negatives. The accuracy of this strategy is 88%, beating the other methods shown in Table 6.
931   We tried this strategy on our Robin models across the LLM size ablation. The results on GQA and
932   textVQA are presented in Figure 14. Our results indicate that the joint LLM and VLM strategy
933   provides a risk averse method of evaluation. Regardless of if the LLM or VLM evaluations are
934   more accurate, the combined method provides a middle ground evaluation which performs slightly
935   better on benchmarks where the LLM evaluates well, as GQA, but poorly when the VLM evaluation
936   consistently outperforms the LLM evaluation, as in TextVQA.

1356
1357   Question Categories. We identified 8 distinct categories of questions that demand comprehensive
1358   image analysis. For each category, we prompted GPT-4 to come up with questions and corresponding
1359   image descriptions. After refining these by hand, we pass the image descriptions to Dall-E 3 to
1360   generate the described image. We will aslo iterate the description untill obtaining an image of high
1361   quality. We present the distribution of questions across different categories in Figure 18. The exact
1362   categories and their explanations are as follow:
1363
       • Descriptive Analysis: This category involves questions that test the model’s ability to identify and
1364
         describe the physical elements in an image, including color, position, and interaction and also to
1365     recognize specific details.
1366
       • Inferential Reasoning: It examines the model’s ability to infer things from the image, includ-
1367
         ing predicting possible subsequent events, making assumptions about previous contexts, and
1368
         hypothesizing alternative scenarios that contradict the present one in the image.
1369
1370
       • Contextual Understanding: This category tests the model’s awareness of the importance of
         context in image comprehension. This might involve understanding geographical or temporal
1371
         aspects that bear upon the image.
1372
1373   • Emotional and Psychological Understanding: It measures the model’s ability to gauge emotions
1374
         and psychological states from an image. This incorporates interpreting the visible emotional
         expressions of characters in the image and hypothesizing about their mental state.
1375
1376   • Ethical Evaluations: Questions in this category check how the model deals with the ethical
1377     implications of images. Can it recognize potential ethical concerns and judge the public display
1378
         acceptability of an image with respect to generally accepted ethical guidelines?
1379   • Abstract Understanding: These questions gauge the model’s capacity for abstract thought — can
1380     it identify underlying themes or messages in the image that aren’t immediately visible? Can it
1381     engage in philosophical interpretation?
1382   • Creative and Subjective Analysis: This category gauges the model’s creativity and its ability to
1383     express subjective views on the image. It includes crafting extended narratives based on the image
1384     scenery and presenting a personal point of view for the image.
1385   • Visual Aesthetics Evaluation: This category examines the model’s ability to evaluate the visual
1386     aesthetics of an image including aspects like balance, symmetry, colour composition, lighting, etc.

1408   We evaluated pairwise comparison of responses on each of the following criteria:

1415     describing objects or elements that don’t exist in the image?

       C.2.5    C OHEN ’ S K APPA C ALCULATION
1675
1676   We calculate Cohen’s Kappa to compare AI evaluations with human evaluations, whilst accounting
1677   for the random chance of agreement. We compare only the matchups that were sampled in the
1678   human surveys against the AI evaluations of those same matchups. Cohen’s Kappa (κ) is calculated
1679   according to the formula:
                                                        po − pe
1680                                                κ=
1681
                                                         1 − pe
1682   po is the relative agreement: the proportion of matchups where the different evaluators agree on their
1683   preference.
1684   pe is the hypothetical probability of chance agreement: we calculate this term for all combinations
1685   of matchups separately. Let pe(a,b) be the probability pe for an individual matchup (a, b). Namely,
1686   for each matchup of models a and b, and an evaluator E, the proportion of matchups where model a
1687   is preferred will be represented as pE (a|a, b). The probability of two evaluators E and F agreeing
1688   randomly for a matchup (a, b) is then:
1689
                            pe(a,b) = pE (a|a, b) ∗ pF (a|a, b) + pE (b|a, b) ∗ pF (b|a, b)
1690
1691   pe is then calculated by taking the weighted average over the frequency of matchups f(a,b) present in
1692   the survey:
1693
                                                      X
1694                                           pe =           f(a,b) ∗ pe(a,b)
1695                                                  (a,b)

1735   C.2.8   F URTHER E XPLANATIONS ON C ONTRADICTIONS
1736
       Because human surveys were limited to 5 pairwise comparisons per question per category, we only
1737
       calculate contradictions using those same 5 comparisons in AI evaluations. In order to evaluate
1738
       logical contradictions, we start by building a directed graph where each model is a node and the link
1739   between 2 nodes is the user preference. For instance, if the model based on Pythia 6.9B is preferred
1740   over the model based on Pythia 2.8B, there will be a directional link from the Pythia 2.8B based
1741   model to the Pythia 6.9B based model. A logical contradiction is when a cycle is created in the graph.
1742
       Figure 23 illustrated this, with a contradiction in sub-figure a as users indicated they preferred the
1743
       model based on Pythia 6.9B, over the one based on Pythia 1.4B, over the one based on Pythia 410M,
1744
       which implies that the model based on Pythia 6.9B should be preferred over the model based on
1745   Pythia 410M. However, human evaluations showed that the model based on Pythia 410M is preferred
1746   over the one on Pythia 6.9B, hence the contradiction.
1747
1748
       Note that contradictions themselves have nothing to do with the sizes of the models, but rather if
       there was an inconsistency in the transitivity of preferences for a given question.
