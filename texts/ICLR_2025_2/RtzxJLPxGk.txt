009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            As deep learning models expand, adaptive learning rate algorithms such as Adam
012            face significant memory consumption challenges due to the need to store of opti-
013            mizer states, including first and second moment data. Existing memory-efficient
014            methods such as Adafactor and CAME often compromise approximation accu-
015            racy with their constant rank-1 matrix factorization techniques. In response, we
016            introduce Adapprox, a novel optimizer that employs adaptive randomized low-rank
017            matrix approximation to more effectively and accurately approximate the second
018
               moment. This method dynamically adjusts the rank used for approximation across
               iterations and weight matrices, mitigating the increase in computation burden while
019
               maintaining comparable accuracy. In experiments with GPT-2 and BERT, Adap-
020
               prox achieves substantial memory savings compared to AdamW and surpasses
021            other memory-efficient counterparts in convergence iterations and downstream task
022            performance, with only a modest increase in the overall latency.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   The emergence of large language models (LLMs) presents substantial memory consumption chal-
028   lenges (Steiner et al., 2023; Shazeer & Stern, 2018; Luo et al., 2023). For instance, BERT (Devlin
029   et al., 2018) consists of up to 300 million parameters, while GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) includes as
030   many as 175 billion parameters. Among the most popular optimization algorithms for pretraining
031   LLMs, Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) and its variant, AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2018), intensify
032   these challenges. These optimizers require additional memory to store both first and second moments
033
      for each parameter, a feature that contributes to their faster convergence speeds compared to SGD.
034   To address these challenges, significant efforts have been made to develop memory-efficient opti-
035   mizers that aim to preserve the advantages of adaptive learning rates while minimizing the memory
036   footprint of optimizer states (Shazeer & Stern, 2018; Anil et al., 2019; Li et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023).
037   Given the insight that second moment matrices exhibit low-rank characteristics, as shown in Figure 1,
038   applying low-rank matrix approximation techniques can significantly reduce memory footprint during
039
      LLM training. Adafactor (Shazeer & Stern, 2018) reduces memory usage by offering an option
      to omit the first moment and employing a constant rank-1 matrix approximation technique (Anil
040
      et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2023) to compress the second moment. However, this approach often results
041
      in diminished training effectiveness due to approximation errors. To mitigate these shortcomings,
042   CAME (Luo et al., 2023) builds on Adafactor by introducing a confidence-based scaling factor
043   that matches the dimensions of the second moment. This factor modulates the update step size,
044   slowing it when confidence is low and accelerating it when confidence is high. However, CAME still
045   employs the same factorization method to store confidence statistics to reduce the additional memory
046   consumption, thereby inheriting the fundamental challenges of Adafactor.
047
      Therefore, a key focus is to enhance the method of approximating the second moment to address
048   these ongoing challenges. Figure 1 highlights the shortcomings of the fixed rank-1 approximation.
049   Specifically, the presence of multiple dominant singular values indicates that a rank-1 approximation
050   cannot capture the full complexity of the matrix, leading to reduced accuracy. Although some
051   methods in the literature employ singular value decomposition (SVD) for low-rank approximation by
052   truncating the smallest singular values (Jha & Yadava, 2010; Tsybakov et al., 2011; Kishore Kumar
053   & Schneider, 2017), this technique can be prohibitively time-consuming when iteratively applied to a
      large number of high-dimensional matrices during LLM training. To overcome the computational

075
         iterations and weight matrices, enabling more accurate capture of second moment matrices’ features.
076      Specifically, Adapprox assesses approximation accuracy by computing the error ratio between the
077      Euclidean distances of the second moments, which are updated with the true squared gradients and
078      their approximated counterparts. Beginning with an initial rank setting based on memory constraints
079      and an upper bound, this adaptive process ensures that if the error ratio exceeds a predefined threshold,
080      the rank is increased in the next training step to improve accuracy. Conversely, if the error ratio
081      stays below the threshold, the rank is reduced, optimizing computational efficiency and memory
082      usage without sacrificing precision. Additionally, to ensure stable rank adaptation, we introduce a
083      rank moment that employs an exponential averaging mechanism across iterations to determine the
084
         new rank. Our experimental results show that our method maintains the initial rank scale during the

085

090
        lower validation loss, and enhanced performance in downstream tasks compared to the established

091
        latency with Adapprox due to the introduced randomized low-rank approximation steps, which is a
092

093     terms of both efficiency and effectiveness.

098      Low-Rank Matrix Approximation. Low-rank matrix approximation aims to represent a matrix using
099      lower-rank matrices, seeking more efficient data representation while retaining as much information
100      as possible. For example, a rank-k approximation can represent an m × n matrix while reducing the
101      memory footprint from O(mn) to O(k(m + n)). This technique is utilized across a wide spectrum
102      of applications, including principal component analysis (Shen & Huang, 2008; Papailiopoulos et al.,
103      2013), image processing (Haeffele et al., 2014; Guo et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2017), and various
104
         machine learning scenarios (Paterek, 2007; Li et al., 2016).
105     Memory Efficient Optimizers. Memory-efficient optimizers aim to reduce memory usage by
106     compressing optimizer states during training, ideally without affecting performance. To achieve
107     this goal, various strategies have been proposed, including low-rank matrix approximation (Shazeer
        & Stern, 2018; Luo et al., 2023) and quantization techniques (Li et al., 2023). Notably, these two

      categories of methods are orthogonal and can be integrated seamlessly. Adafactor (Shazeer & Stern,
109   2018) and CAME (Luo et al., 2023) are notable examples that utilize low-rank matrix approximation,
110   significantly reducing memory usage, though this can come at the expense of accuracy due to their
111   rank-1 factorization approaches. Quantization techniques, as employed in 8-bit Adam (Dettmers
112   et al., 2021) and 4-bit Adam (Li et al., 2023), offer another avenue for memory savings by reducing
113   the precision of stored values without drastically affecting performance. In this paper, we focus on
114   enhancing low-rank matrix approximation techniques to reduce memory usage while maintaining
115   high accuracy and performance during LLM training.

      5     D ISCUSSION
508
      While Adapprox and Adafactor achieve significant memory savings through the low-rank approxima-
509
      tion of the second moment and by omitting the first moment, our experiments demonstrate that the
510
      absence of the first moment has a notable impact on convergence speed and overall performance (see
511   Appendix A). Consequently, we recommend retaining the first moment unless memory constraints
512   are extremely prohibitive. In the future, we aim to further reduce the time cost of Adapprox by
513   decreasing the frequency of rank adjustments in the later stages of training, where we observe that
514   the rank remains nearly constant (as shown in Figure 3).

516
      6     C ONCLUSION
517
518
      In this paper, we present Adapprox, a novel optimizer engineered to mitigate memory consumption
519
      challenges inherent in training large-scale models. We employ S-RSI, which leverages randomized
520   low-rank matrix approximation to reduce the memory footprint of the second moment. We observe
521   that the time cost of S-RSI is less significant in practice than theoretically expected, attributed to
522   the parallel computation capabilities of GPUs. Furthermore, we enhance S-RSI with an adaptive
523   rank selection mechanism, introducing AS-RSI that dynamically adjusts the rank value throughout
524   the training iterations. These components are integrated to formulate Adapprox. Our empirical
525   evaluations, encompassing both pretraining and downstream tasks for GPT-2 117M/345M and BERT
526   345M models, corroborate the efficacy of Adapprox. In comparison to AdamW, Adapprox delivers
527   substantial memory savings via its low-rank approximation strategy. Although there is a minor
528
      trade-off in memory efficiency compared to state-of-the-art memory efficient optimizers such as
      Adafactor and CAME, Adapprox surpasses these competitors in several critical performance metrics,
529
      including validation loss and perplexity during pretraining, along with accuracy in downstream tasks.
530
      These results position Adapprox as a balanced solution for memory-efficient training, successfully
531   balancing efficiency with minimal accuracy sacrifices.
