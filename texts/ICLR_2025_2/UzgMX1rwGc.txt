009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The diversity and scale of annotated real-world 3D datasets limit the performance
012            of monocular 3D detectors. Although data augmentation holds potential, creating
013            realistic, scene-aware augmentations for outdoor environments presents a signifi-
014            cant challenge. Existing augmentation methods majorly focus on realistic object
015            appearance by advancing the rendering quality. However, we show that object
016            placement is equally important for downstream 3D detection performance. The
017            main challenge, however, for realistic placement, is to automatically identify the
018
               plausible physical properties (location, scale, and orientation) for placing objects
               in real-world scenes. To this end, we propose Smart-Placement, a novel 3D scene-
019
               aware augmentation method for generating diverse and realistic augmentations. In
020
               particular, given a background scene, we train a placement network to learn a dis-
021            tribution over plausible 3D bounding boxes. Subsequently, we render realistic
022            cars from 3D assets and place them according to the locations sampled from the
023            learned distribution. Through extensive empirical evaluation on standard bench-
024            mark datasets - KITTI and NuScenes, we show that our proposed augmentation
025            method significantly boosts the performance of several existing monocular 3D de-
026            tectors, setting a new state-of-the-art benchmark, while being highly data efficient.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030   Monocular 3D object detection has rapidly progressed recently, enabling its use in autonomous navi-
031   gation and robotics Huang et al. (2022); Ma et al. (2021). However, the performance of 3D detectors
032   relies heavily on the quantity and quality of the training dataset. Given the considerable effort and
033   time required to curate extensive, real-world 3D-annotated datasets, specialized data augmentation
034   for 3D object detection has emerged as a promising direction.
035
      However designing realistic augmentations for 3D tasks, is non-trivial, as the generated augmenta-
036   tions must adhere to the physical constraints of the real world, such as maintaining 3D geometric
037   consistency and handling collisions. Existing techniques Ge et al. (2024); Lian et al. (2022) for 3D
038   augmentation use relatively simple heuristics for placing synthetic objects in an input scene. For
039   instance, in the context of road scenes, a recent approach Li et al. (2023) generates realistic cars and
040   places them on the segmented road region. However, such heuristics result in highly unnatural scene
041   augmentations (Fig. 1), resulting in a marginal improvement in 3D detection performance. In this
042   work, we ask the following two crucial questions: (1) What key factors are essential for generating
043   realistic augmentations to improve monocular 3D object detection?, and (2) How can these factors
044
      be integrated to generate effective scene-aware augmentations?
045   For the first question, we discover two critical factors responsible for generating effective 3D aug-
046   mentations:
047
      1. Object Placement: Plausible placement of augmented objects, with appropriate physical prop-
048   erties (location, scale, and orientation), is essential for rendering realistic scene augmentations. For
049   instance, in road scenes, a car should be placed on the road, be of appropriate size based on the
050   distance from the camera, and follow the lane orientation. Augmentations that respect such phys-
051   ical constraints generalize better to real scenes by faithfully modelling the true distribution of the
052   vehicles in the real world. To give an example of how such an augmentation looks, we compare our
053   proposed augmentation approach against heuristic-based placement from Lift3D Li et al. (2023) in
      Fig. 1. Given the same rendering, our generation looks much more plausible regarding car place-

123   2   R ELATED W ORK
124   Object Placement. There are numerous works Zhang et al. (2020); Zhu et al. (2023); Arroyo
125   et al. (2021); Paschalidou et al. (2021); Wei et al. (2023) which aim to predict object placement by
126   learning a transformation or the bounding box parameters directly for a given background image.
127   Paschalidou et al. (2021); Wei et al. (2023) learns the distribution of indoor synthetic objects. Sun
128   et al. (2020); Lee et al. (2018) learns the plausible locations for humans and other outdoor objects
129   in a 2D manner. Few works aim to learn the arrangement conditioned on the scene-graph Luo et al.
130   (2020); Jyothi et al. (2019); Yang et al. (2021). Zhang et al. (2020); Sun et al. (2020); Lee et al.
131
      (2018) are train a deep network adversarially in order to learn plausible 2D bounding box locations.
      Similarly, ST-GANLin et al. (2018) learns to predict the geometric transformation of a bounding
132
      box in the given scene using adversarial training. Li et al. (2019) uses a variational autoencoder to
133
      predict a plausible location heatmap over the scene but is limited to placement in restricted indoor
134   environments.
135
136   Monocular Object Detection The current monocular 3D detection methods can be grouped as
137
      image-based or pseudo-lidar-based. Image-based detectors Brazil & Liu (2019); Liu et al. (2020);
      Mousavian et al. (2016); Roddick et al. (2018); Simonelli et al. (2019c;a); Wang et al. (2021); Liu
138
      et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2021) estimate the 3D bounding box information for an object from a sin-
139
      gle RGB image. Due to the lack of depth information, these methods rely on geometric consistency
140   in order to predict the class and the location of the object. Some works Li et al. (2020); Liu et al.
141   (2020); Ma et al. (2021) use the prediction of key points of 3D bounding boxes as an intermediate
142   task in order to improve it’s performance on 3D monocular detection. In this work, we aim to im-
143   prove the performance of image-based monocular detection models since RGB images are the most
144   commonly used modality and easy to acquire with low acquisition costs, unlike LIDAR and depth
145   sensors.
146   Scene Data Augmentation. Multiple works use 2D data augmentation techniques to improve the
147   performance of perception tasks Shorten & Khoshgoftaar (2019). However, these augmentations
148   cannot be lifted directly to 3D without violating the geometric constraints. To alleviate this problem,
149   a recent method augments the training dataset for the task of 3D monocular detection Li et al. (2023);
150   Lian et al. (2022); Tong et al. (2023a); Dokania et al. (2022). Lian et al. (2022) learns to paste
151   cars on roads using the copy-paste operation by considering the cars’ relative scale and pose. An
152   interesting approach is taken by Dokania et al. (2022), where they model a synthetic urban scene
153   from real-world distributions using available annotations to mimic the semantic properties of the
154
      real world. Li et al. (2023) learns a neural radiance field to generate realistic 3D cars with GAN
      augmented views. Tong et al. (2023a) learns the location to place 3D cars, but the placed cars look
155
      unrealistic. All these methods use heuristics such as lane segments to place cars; however, we aim to
156
      learn the distribution over car locations, scale, and orientation from the real-world object detection
157   dataset.

      Insight-1: Unlike the object-based augmentations suitable for broad image classification tasks, en-
179
      hancing structured tasks as 3D object detection requires careful consideration of object-background
180   and object-object interactions for generation of plausible scene-based augmentations.
181
182   Remarks: Synthetic object-based augmentation for image classification typically involves placing
183
      objects on any suitable background. This method may not always respect the interaction between
      the object and the background, its impact on the classification task remains minimal. In contrast,
184
      for scene-based augmentation, which is crucial in tasks like 3D detection, the interactions between
185
      objects and backgrounds, as well as between objects, becomes pivotal. For example, implausible
186   placements such as a car in a sky background, two cars occluding each other’s 3D volume, or a car-
187   oriented perpendicular to lanes on the road, need to be avoided. While one might argue that random
188   placement could aid in a 3D object detection task by helping the model distinguish objects from
189   the background, empirical evidence suggests otherwise. Hence, it’s crucial to devise a placement-
190   based augmentation method that respects the scene-prior, thereby instilling this understanding into
191   the detector model during training.
192   Insight-2: The distribution of augmented samples for a given real sample xr , denoted as
193   q(xaug |xr ), can be enhanced by better scene-prior modeling; this leads to augmented scenes that
194   closely align with the real distribution, fostering a robust model that is resilient to failures and can
195   achieve superior performance with fewer real samples.
196
197   Remarks: The equation q(xaug |xr ) = q(xaug |z, xr )q(z|xr ) represents the distribution of aug-
198
      mented samples for a given real sample xr . Here, q(x|z, xr ) represents a pipeline that generates the
      augmented scene image upon applying an effective placement-based augmentation. Here, q(z|xr )
199
      denotes the scene-prior related latent factor z given the real image. This factor can model the distri-
200
      bution of plausible location, orientation, and scale to place objects given the scene layout. Improved
201   modeling of the scene prior ensures that the augmented scene closely matches the real distribution.
202   Training with such augmentations imbues the model with a strong understanding of the scene prior,
203   enhancing its robustness and reliability. We demonstrate that this strategy enables efficient training,
204   yielding superior performance with fewer real samples compared to the baseline.
205

      Approach overview. Our method for 3D augmentation consists of two stages. First, we train the
      placement model that maps a monocular RGB image to a distribution over plausible 3D bounding
207
      boxes (Sec. 3.1). Subsequently, we sample a set of 3D bounding boxes from this distribution to
208
      place cars. In the second stage, we render realistic cars following the sampled 3D bounding box and
209   blend them with the background road scene. (Sec. 3.2).

      The complete architecture for placement is shown in Fig. 2a. We build SA-PlaceNet using the
      backbone of MonoDTR Huang et al. (2022). MonoDTR is designed to perform monocular 3D
253
      object detection and is trained with auxiliary depth supervision. However, depth is not required
254
      during inference. We adapt the architecture of MonoDTR to learn the mapping from background
255   road images I to a set of 3D bounding boxes B. Following Huang et al. (2022), we define bounding
256   box b ∈ B as 8 dimensional vector b = [bx , by , bz , bh , bw , bl , bθ , bα ], where (bx , by , bz ) are 3D
257   locations, (bh , bw , bl ) are height, width, and length of the box, and bθ and bα are orientation angles.
258   Note that bα can be computed deterministically from bθ and hence we have only 7 variables defining
259   a given bounding box. As a convention, we consider the xz plane as the road plane.
260
      Dataset preparation. There is no existing real-world dataset that provides plausible placement
261   annotations for a given road scene. Instead, we take advantage of the KITTI Geiger et al. (2013)
262   dataset with 3D object detection annotations. We preprocess the dataset by inpainting the foreground
263   cars in the scene using off-the-shelf inpainting Rombach et al. (2022). Through this process, we
264   obtain an image dataset (I) with no cars on the road and a set of corresponding 3D bounding boxes
265   (B). Next, we obtain depth images Id for the inpainted images using Ranftl et al. (2021). The
266   obtained paired dataset, D = {I, Id , B}, is used to train the SA-PlaceNet.

303   3.1.2 D ISTRIBUTION OVER 3D BOUNDING BOXES .
304   Geometry-aware augmentation enables the generation of diverse placement locations, but it learns a
305   direct mapping from the input image to a point estimate of bounding boxes. To learn a continuous
306   representation in the output space, we map the input image to the distribution of 3D boxes. This
307   improves the coverage of plausible locations and enables diverse bounding box sampling from a
308   predicted set of mean boxes. Specifically, we approximate each predicted bounding box b as a
309
      multi-dimensional Gaussian distribution with mean µb and a fixed covariance matrix as αI, where
      α is used to control the spread as shown in Fig. 2a. We empirically observed that having a fixed
310
      covariance improves training stability. Having a higher α value results in strong augmentations,
311
      where the sampled car is far away from the mean location, resulting in a weaker training signal.

      5 C ONCLUSION
531
      This work proposes a novel scene-aware augmentation technique to improve outdoor monocular 3D
      detectors. The core of our method is an object placement network, that learns the distribution of
532
      physically plausible object placement for background road scenes from a single image. We utilize
533
      this information to generate realistic augmentations by placing cars on the road scenes with geo-
534   metric consistency. Our results with scene-aware augmentation on monocular 3D object detectors
535   suggest that realistic placement is the key to substantially improving the augmentation quality and
536   data efficiency of the detector. The primary limitation of our approach is the dependency on the
537   off-the-shelf inpainting method for data preparation for the training of the placement network. Also,
538   our current framework does not consider more nuanced appearance factors in augmentations such
539   as the lighting of the scene. In conclusion, we provide important insights for designing scene-based
      augmentations for 3D object detection.

904   A.3.2    BASELINE METHODS
905
906   Geometric Copy-paste (Geo-CP). To augment a given scene, a car is randomly sampled from the
907   database, and its 3D parameters are altered before placement. Specifically, the depth of the box
908
      (z coordinate) is randomly sampled, and corresponding x and y are transformed using geometric
      operations. Other parameters, such as bounding box size and orientation, are kept unchanged. The
909
      sampled car is then pasted using simple blending on the background scene.
910
911   CARLA Dosovitskiy et al. (2017). To compare the augmentations generated by simulated road
912   scene environments, we use state-of-the-art CARLA simulator engine for rendering realistic scenes
913   with multiple cars. It can generate diverse traffic scenarios that are implemented programmatically.
914
      However, it’s extremely challenging for simulators to capture the true diversity from real-world road
      scenes and they often suffer from a large sim2real gap.
915
916   Rule Based Placement (RBP). We create a strong rule-based baseline to show the effectiveness
917   of our learning-based placement. Specifically, we first segment out the road region with Han et al.
      (2022) and sample placement locations in this region. To get a plausible orientation, we copy the


                                                       17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      orientation of the closest car in the scene, assuming neighboring cars follow the same orientations.
919   We used our proposed rendering pipeline to generate realistic augmentations.
920
921   Lift-3D Li et al. (2023) proposed a generative radiance field network to synthetize realistic 3D cars.
922
      Lift3D trains a conditional NeRF on multi-view car images generated by StyleGANs. However,
      the car shape is changed following the 3D bounding box dimensions. The generated cars are then
923
      placed on the road using a heuristic based on road segmentation. We used a single generated 3D car
924
      provided in the official code to augment the dataset as the training code is unavailable. Specifically,
925   road region is segmented using off-the-shelf drivable area segmentor Han et al. (2022). Next, the 3D
926   bounding box of cars is sampled from a predefined distribution of box parameters as given in Tab.8,
927   and the ones outside the drivable area are filtered out. For a sampled 3D bounding box parameters
928   b=[bx , by , bz , bw , bh , bl , bθ ], we render the car at adjusted orientation angle θ̃ using Eq. 7. We place
929   the camera at the fixed height of 1.6m, with an elevation angle of 0. Also, we used (bw , bh , bl ) to
930   render the car of a particular shape. We render the car image for 512x512 resolution using volume
931   rendering and the defined camera parameters. Along with the RGB image, Lift3D also outputs the
932   segmentation mask for the car which is used to blend it with the background. Fig. 12 shows some
933   sample renderings from Lift3D.

1062   software. We employ a random sampling technique to select a 3D car model from this extensive
1063   dataset, which is then loaded in the Blender Community (2018) environment. To ensure consis-
1064   tency in the car shapes, we initially calculated the average dimensions of the cars within the dataset.

1066   this random sampling procedure until the specified conditions are satisfied. Following that, we align

       A.7.1    C ONTROL N ET Z HANG & AGRAWALA (2023) BASED RENDERING .
1093
1094   We leverage a state-of-the-art image-to-image translation method to convert the synthetic ShapeNet
1095   renderings into realistic cars that blend well with the background scene. We use edge-conditioned
1096   ControlNet, which takes an edge image and a text prompt to generate images following the edge map
1097   and the prompt. Specifically, we utilize an edge detector to create edge maps for synthetic car images
1098   rendered using ShapeNet Chang et al. (2015), preserving the car’s structure while maintaining its
1099   original orientation and scale. These edge maps, generated through the Canny Edge Detection algo-
1100
       rithm Canny (1986), serve as input for the edge-conditioned ControlNet Zhang & Agrawala (2023),
       enabling the rendering of realistic cars using the prompt ‘A realistic car on the street’. Furthermore,
1101
       given an edge map and hence a ShapeNet-rendered car, we can obtain various realistic renderings
1102
       at each iteration, facilitating diverse scene generations (Fig. 16). We further enhance ControlNet’s
1103   backbone diffusion model using LoRA Hu et al. (2022) on a subset of ‘car’ images from the KITTI
1104   dataset. This process enables the generation of natural-looking car versions that seamlessly blend
1105   with the background scene. Finally, we integrate the ControlNet-rendered car and its shadow base
1106   into the predicted location within the scene to achieve a realistic rendering.
