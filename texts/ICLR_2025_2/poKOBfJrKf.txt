011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have become increasingly popular for effec-
014            tively modeling data with graph structures. Recently, attention mechanisms have
015
               been integrated into GNNs to improve their ability to capture complex patterns.
               This paper presents the first comprehensive study revealing a critical, unexplored
016
               consequence of this integration: the emergence of Massive Activations (MAs)
017
               within attention layers. We introduce a novel method for detecting and analyzing
018            MAs, focusing on edge features in different graph transformer architectures. Our
019            study assesses various GNN models using benchmark datasets, including ZINC,
020            TOX21, and PROTEINS. Key contributions include (1) establishing the direct link
021            between attention mechanisms and MAs generation in GNNs, (2) developing a
022            robust definition and detection method for MAs based on activation ratio distribu-
023            tions, (3) introducing the Explicit Bias Term (EBT) as a potential countermeasure
024            and exploring it as an adversarial framework to assess models robustness based
025            on the presence or absence of MAs. Our findings highlight the prevalence and
026
               impact of attention-induced MAs across different architectures, such as Graph-
               Transformer, GraphiT, and SAN. The study reveals the complex interplay between
027
               attention mechanisms, model architecture, dataset characteristics, and MAs emer-
028
               gence, providing crucial insights for developing more robust and reliable graph
029            models.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as a powerful tool for learning representations of
035   graph-structured data, demonstrating remarkable success across various applications such as social
036   network analysis (Min et al., 2021), recommendation systems (Gao et al., 2022) and molecular
037   biology (Zhang et al., 2021). Central to the recent advancements in GNNs is the integration of
038
      attention mechanisms, which enable the models to focus on the most relevant parts of the input
      graph, thereby enhancing their ability to capture intricate patterns and dependencies.
039
040   Despite the substantial progress, the phenomenon of Massive Activations (MAs) within attention
041   layers has not been thoroughly explored in the context of GNNs. MAs, characterized by exceedingly
042   large activation values, can significantly impact the stability and performance of neural networks.
043   In particular, understanding and mitigating MAs in GNNs is crucial for ensuring robust and reliable
044
      model behavior, especially when dealing with complex and large-scale graphs.
045   In this paper, we aim to bridge this gap by systematically investigating the occurrence and implica-
046   tions of MAs in attention-based GNNs. We focus on edge features in graph transformers, a state-of-
047   the-art GNN architecture, and analyze how these features contribute to the emergence of MAs. Our
048   study reveals that certain graph structures on edge configurations are more prone to inducing MAs,
049   which in turn affects the overall performance and interpretability of the models.
050   To address these challenges, we propose a novel methodology for detecting and analyzing MAs in
051   GNNs. Our approach involves a comprehensive evaluation of various GNN architectures, including
052   GraphTransformer (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2021), GraphiT (Mialon et al., 2021), and SAN (Kreuzer
053   et al., 2021), across multiple benchmark datasets, like ZINC (Irwin et al., 2012), TOX21 (Mayr
      et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2016) and OGBN-PROTEINS (Hu et al., 2020), which differs from their


                                                       1

054
      downstream tasks like graph regression, multi-label graph classification, and multi-label node clas-
055   sification. We introduce specific criteria for identifying MAs and conduct extensive ablation studies
056   to elucidate the role of edge features in this context.
057
058
      This study represents the first comprehensive investigation of MAs in GNNs, laying the groundwork
      for future research. Our findings suggest that the scope of MAs analysis can be expanded to include
059
      a wider range of architectures and the evaluation of state-of-the-art attack methods, ultimately en-
060
      hancing our understanding of MAs’ influence on GNN performance and robustness. This is crucial
061   for developing more robust and reliable graph transformer models, especially given the increasing
062   popularity and widespread adoption of transformers in various applications today.
063
064
      Our contributions are threefold:
065
             • We provide the first systematic study on MAs in attention-based GNNs, highlighting their
066
               prevalence and impact on model performance.
067
068          • We propose a robust detection methodology for MAs, accompanied by detailed experimen-
069            tal protocols and ablation studies.
070          • We introduce the Explicit Bias Term (EBT) as a potential countermeasure for MAs, and
071            we exploit it in an adversarial framework, called Explicit Bias Attack, to demonstrate the
072            effectiveness of the MAs in compromising GNNs robustness.
073
074   Through this work, we aim to shed light on a critical yet understudied aspect of attention-based
075   GNNs, offering valuable insights for the development of more resilient and interpretable graph-
076   based models.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
079
080
081
      GNNs have become effective instruments for studying and extracting insights from graph-structured
      data, with usages spanning fields like fraud detection (Motie & Raahemi, 2023), traffic prediction
082
      (Wang et al., 2022) and recommendation systems (Wu et al., 2021). The evolution of GNNs has been
083
      marked by significant advancements in their architectures and learning mechanisms, with a recent
084   focus on incorporating attention mechanisms to enhance their expressive power and performance.
085   The introduction of attention in GNNs was largely inspired by the success of transformers in natural
086   language processing (Vaswani et al., 2017). Graph Attention Networks (GATs) (Veličković et al.,
087   2017) were among the first to incorporate self-attention into GNNs, allowing nodes to attend differ-
088   ently to their neighbors based on learned attention weights. This innovation significantly improved
089   the model’s ability to capture complex relationships within graph structures.
090   Building upon the success of GATs, several variants and extensions have been proposed. GraphiT
091   (Mialon et al., 2021) introduced a generalization of transformer architectures to graph-structured
092   data, incorporating positional encodings and leveraging the power of multi-head attention mecha-
093   nisms. Similarly, the Structure-Aware Network (SAN) (Kreuzer et al., 2021) proposed a novel at-
094   tention mechanism that explicitly considers the structural properties of graphs, leading to improved
095   performance on various graph-based tasks.
096   Recent studies on Large Language Models (LLMs) and Vision Transformers (ViTs) have revealed
097   the presence of MAs within their internal states, specifically in the attention layer’s output (Xiao
098   et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024). This phenomenon prompted investigations into the role of these
099   activations in model behavior, performance, and potential vulnerabilities. Similar observations were
100   made in Vision Transformers (ViTs) (Darcet et al., 2023; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), suggesting that
101   the presence of MAs might be a common feature in transformer-based architectures across different
102   domains. These findings have led to a growing interest in understanding the implications of MAs
103   for model interpretability, robustness, and potential vulnerabilities to adversarial attacks.
104   The study of internal representations in deep learning models has been a topic of significant interest
105   in the machine learning community. Works such as Bau et al. (2020) have explored the interpretabil-
106   ity of neural networks by analyzing activation patterns and their relationships to input features and
107   model decisions. However, the specific phenomenon of MAs in GNNs has remained largely unex-
      plored until now, representing a crucial gap in our understanding of these models.

108
      The intersection of adversarial attacks and GNNs is another relevant area of study that relates to the
109   investigation of MAs. Previous work has explored various attack strategies on graph data, including
110   topology attacks, feature attacks, adversarial training and hybrid approaches (Sun et al., 2022a;
111   Gosch et al., 2024). However, the potential vulnerabilities introduced by MAs represent a novel
112   direction for research in this field. Understanding how MAs might be exploited or manipulated by
113   adversarial inputs could lead to the development of more robust GNN architectures.
114
      However, in the broader context of neural network analysis, techniques for probing and interpreting
115   model internals have been developed. Methods such as feature visualization (Olah et al., 2017) and
116   network dissection (Bau et al., 2017) have provided insights into the functions of individual neurons
117   and layers in convolutional neural networks. Adapting and extending these techniques to analyze
118   MAs in GNNs could provide valuable insights into their role and impact in possible future works.
119
      Finally, the study of attention mechanisms in various neural network architectures has also yielded
120
      insights that may be relevant to understanding MAs in GNNs. Work on attention flow (Abnar &
121   Zuidema, 2020) and attention head importance (Michel et al., 2019) in transformer models has
122   shown that not all attention heads contribute equally to model performance, and some may even be
123   pruned without significant loss of accuracy. These findings raise questions about whether similar
124   patterns might exist in graph transformer models and how they might relate to the presence of MAs.

      3.1     C HARACTERIZATION OF M ASSIVE ACTIVATIONS
137
138   MAs in GNNs refer to specific activation values that exhibit unusually high magnitudes compared to
139   the typical activations within a layer. These activations are defined by the following criteria, where
140   an activation value is intended to be its absolute value:
141
      Magnitude Threshold: An activation is classified as massive if its value exceeds a predetermined
142   threshold. This threshold is typically set to a value that is significantly higher than the average
143   activation value within the layer, ensuring that only the most extreme activations are considered.
144
145
      Relative Threshold: In the paper by Sun et al. (2024), MAs were defined as at least 1,000 times
      larger than the median activation value within the layer. This relative threshold criterion helped
146
      differentiate MAs from regular high activations that might occur due to normal variations in the data
147
      or model parameters.
148
149   The formal definition was represented as:
150
                              MAs = {a | a > 100 and a > 1000 × median(A)}
151
152   where A represents the set of activation values in a given layer.
153
      However, in contrast to previous studies that employed a fixed relative threshold, our approach
154
      adopts a more rigorous method. We estimate MAs by comparing the distributions of activation
155   ratios between a base, untrained model with Xavier weight initializations (Glorot & Bengio, 2010),
156   and a fully trained model. This method ensures a more precise identification of MAs based on
157   empirical data rather than an arbitrary fixed threshold. In this way, the untrained model serves as a
158   reference for identifying unusual activations that emerge during training.

      5   E XPLICIT B IAS ATTACK
411
412
      The study of adversarial attacks on GNNs has become increasingly important as these models are
      deployed in critical applications. While various attack strategies have been explored (Zügner et al.,
413
      2018; Sun et al., 2022b), the vulnerability introduced by MAs remains largely unexplored. Un-
414
      derstanding how MAs can be exploited by adversaries is crucial for developing more robust GNN
415   architectures and their downstream tasks. In this section, we propose the Explicit Bias Attack, a
416   gradient-based method designed to exploit MAs and assess model robustness. Our approach is in-
417   spired by gradient ascent attacks previously applied to image classifiers (Goodfellow et al., 2014)
418   and adapted for graph data (Dai et al., 2018). By analyzing the effectiveness of gradient ascent
419   attack with and without the presence of EBT and MAs, we aim to provide insights into the role of
420   these activations in model fragility.
421   Therefore, inspired by previous section, we exploited EBT as computed in Equations (2) and (3) to
422   analyze the importance of MAs for a gradient ascent attack at test time, where noise (added to the
423   input feature embedding) is learned to directly maximize the loss function. The effectiveness of an
424   attack is evaluated by comparing the average test loss before and after the attack (i.e., with random
425   and optimized noise, with the same standard deviations, respectively), using a gain defined as
426
                                                               optimized noise loss − random noise loss

      6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
459
460   This paper presents the first comprehensive study of MAs in attention-based GNNs. We have intro-
461   duced a novel methodology for detecting and analyzing MAs, focusing on edge features in various
462   graph transformer architectures across multiple benchmark datasets. Our findings reveal that MAs
463
      are prevalent across different models and datasets, and demonstrate that they could be effectively
      leveraged by adversaries to degrade the performance of GNNs.
464
465   We showed that the introduction of Explicit Bias Terms (EBT) can effectively mitigate the occur-
466   rence of MAs, leading to more stable activation distributions. However, our results also showed that
467   this mitigation does not always translate to improved test performance, highlighting the complex
468   role of MAs in GNNs’ behavior.
469   Furthermore, we introduced the Explicit Bias Attack, a gradient-ascent adversarial framework, that
470   demonstrates how MAs, if not mitigated by EBT, can expose models to vulnerabilities in their tasks.
471   This further points out the importance of considering these activations in the context of model ro-
472   bustness.
473   Future research will expand this analysis to a wider range of architectures and advanced attack meth-
474   ods, further clarifying the influence of MAs on GNN performance and robustness, and potentially
475   leading to more interpretable and stable graph-based models. Specifically, future research could
476   explore:
477
478         • Customized Adversarial MAs: Developing more adversarial techniques to regulate and
479           attack these activations to enhance model stability and performance, like injecting fake
480           MAs or exploiting state-of-the-art graph attack methods.
481
            • Downstream-driven MAs: Leveraging MAs for specific downstream task, investigating
482
              how to harness these significant activations to improve models and their interpretability on
483           specific assignments such as link prediction or drug design.
484
485         • Comparative Analysis: Extending the study to additional models and datasets to general-
              ize the findings further and uncover broader patterns.

748   This section provides additional details on the Kolmogorv-Smirnov (KS) test (Chakravarti et al.,
749   1967) used to analyze the distribution of activations. The KS test is a non-parametric test that
750   compares the cumulative distribution functions of two samples. It is used to compare a sample with
751   a reference probability distribution (one-sample KS test) or to compare two samples (two-sample
752
      KS test) with each other.

754   and after training (i.e. base against trained model), identifying Massive Activations (MAs). We
755   primarily used the one-sample KS test to assess the goodness of fit between our observed activation
      distributions and a theoretical gamma distribution.
