                                                 A BSTRACT
013
014
015
               Momentum-based gradients are critical for optimizing advanced machine learn-
               ing models, as they not only accelerate convergence but also help gradient-based
016
               optimizers overcome stationary points. While most state-of-the-art momentum
017
               techniques rely on lower-power gradients, such as the squared first-order gradi-
018            ent, there has been limited exploration into the potential of higher-power gradi-
019            ents—those raised to powers greater than two, such as the third-power first-order
020            gradient. In this work, we introduce the concept of high-order momentum, where
021            momentum is constructed using higher-power gradients, with a specific focus on
022            the third-power first-order gradient as a representative example. Our research of-
023            fers both theoretical and empirical evidence of the benefits of this novel approach.
024            From a theoretical standpoint, we demonstrate that incorporating third-power gra-
025            dients into momentum can improve the convergence bounds of gradient-based op-
026
               timizers for both convex and smooth nonconvex problems. To validate these find-
               ings, we conducted extensive empirical experiments across convex, smooth non-
027
               convex, and nonsmooth nonconvex optimization tasks. The results consistently
028
               showcase that high-order momentum outperforms traditional momentum-based
029            optimizers, providing superior performance and more efficient optimization.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Optimization problems in machine learning are commonly tackled using gradient-based optimizers,
035
      which rely on either full gradients—computed from the entire dataset—or stochastic gradients, de-
036   rived from mini-batches. While full gradients guarantee eventual convergence, stochastic gradients
037   offer enhanced computational efficiency (Hazan et al., 2007; Nemirovski et al., 2009; Rakhlin et al.,
038   2011). Over the past decade, research has shown that combining full gradients, stochastic gradients,
039   noisy stimuli, batch strategies, sampling, and momentum techniques in gradient-based optimizers
040   can lead to favorable convergence, expected accuracy, and improved robustness (Shalev-Shwartz &
041   Zhang, 2013; Zhang et al., 2012; Johnson & Zhang, 2013; Defazio et al., 2014; Arjevani & Shamir,
042   2015; Lin et al., 2015; Allen-Zhu, 2017; Haji & Abdulazeez, 2021).
043   Momentum, one of the most influential techniques, is widely used in gradient-based optimizers to
044   further improve performance (Liu et al., 2020; Loizou & Richtárik, 2020; Haji & Abdulazeez, 2021).
045   Intuitively, momentum addresses the issue of slow convergence in later stages of optimization, such
046   as near (δ, ϵ)-Goldstein stationary points (Clarke, 1974; 1975; 1981; 1990; Jordan et al., 2023),
047   where gradients oscillate within a narrow range. Momentum helps by driving gradients away from
048   these oscillations and toward the global optimum, making it especially effective for nonsmooth
049   nonconvex objectives, such as those found in Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) (Mai & Johansson,
050
      2020; Wang et al., 2021; Wang & Wen, 2022; Jordan et al., 2023).
051   Due to these advantages, leading optimizers like Adam, STORM, and ST ORM + (Kingma & Ba,
052   2014; Cutkosky & Orabona, 2019; Levy et al., 2021) incorporate momentum to achieve higher accu-
053   racy and reduce the likelihood of getting trapped in stationary points. For instance, Adam uses two
      momentum terms—first-order and squared first-order gradients—to optimize objective functions,

054
      often outperforming alternatives like AdaGrad and SGD (Kingma & Ba, 2014; Lydia & Francis,
055   2019; Chandra et al., 2022; Beznosikov et al., 2023). STORM, which uses a stochastic recursive
056   momentum term based on squared gradients, has been shown to achieve better accuracy than Adam
057   when optimizing ResNet (Cutkosky & Orabona, 2019), and the more recent ST ORM + enhances
058   this approach with adaptive learning rates, eliminating the need for parameter tuning (Levy et al.,
059   2021).
060
      While first-order and squared gradients dominate current momentum-based approaches, exploring
061   higher-order momentum holds great potential. For instance, incorporating third-power gradients
062   could further enhance the convergence bound of gradient-based optimizers. In this work, we in-
063   troduce the High-Order Momentum Estimator (HOME) optimizer, a framework designed to ex-
064   plore and advance high-order momentum techniques. Our focus is on HOME-3, which leverages
065   third-power gradients to enhance momentum, such as (f ′ )3 . First, we present a theoretical analysis
066   showing that HOME-3 significantly improves convergence bounds for both convex and smooth non-
067   convex optimization problems. We then extend our numerical experiments to nonsmooth nonconvex
068   problems, where HOME-3 consistently outperforms other momentum-based optimizers. Finally, we
069
      use statistical techniques to quantify the performance of HOME-3, validating both the effectiveness
      and robustness of third-power gradients in momentum.
070
071   Contributions: In this work, the potential contributions of HOME are categorized as follows:
072
      Third-Order Momentum Enhances Convergence Bound for Convex Problems (Theorem 4.1): Based
073   on the assumptions and properties of convex objective functions (see Assumption 2.1), the proposed
074   HOME-3 optimizer, incorporating a third-power gradient, enhances the convergence bound to O(1/
075   T 5/6 ). Detailed proof of Theorem 4.1 can be found in Appendix A of the Supplementary Material.
076
077   Third-Order Momentum Advances Convergence Bound for Smooth Nonconvex Problems (Theorem
078
      4.2): According to the assumptions and properties of smooth nonconvex functions (see Assumption
079
      2.2), the HOME-3 optimizer advances the convergence bound to approximately O(1/T 5/6 ). The
      proof for Theorem 4.2 is provided in Appendix A of the Supplementary Material.
080
081   High-Order Momentum Enhances Convergence for Nonsmooth Nonconvex Problems (Theorem
082   4.4): We empirically investigate the performance of high-order momentum optimizers on nonsmooth
083   nonconvex problems, as illustrated in Figure 3 . To further validate the performance of HOME-3,
084   we employ a deep neural network, since the objective function of a multi-layer deep neural network
085
      is typically nonsmooth and nonconvex (Jordan et al., 2023). The results, shown in Figures 3 and 4,
      indicate that HOME-3 outperforms other peer momentum-based optimizers. Additionally, we ex-
086
      plore the advantages of coordinate randomization in Lemma 4.3 and Theorem 4.4, demonstrating
087
      that it preserves the convergence bound of the original gradient-based optimizer.
088
089   Related Work: In the field of convex and smooth nonconvex optimization, Kingma’s work on
090   Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) demonstrated that momentum, built on the first-order and squared gra-
091   dients, can achieve a convergence bound of O(1/T 1/2 ) for convex problems. Similarly, STORM,
092   which uses a recursive stochastic momentum, obtains a convergence bound of O(1/T 1/3 ) for
093
      smooth nonconvex problems (Cutkosky & Orabona, 2019). More recently, ST ORM + achieved
094
      a bound of O(1/T 1/2 + σ 1/3 /T 1/3 ) (Levy et al., 2021). Notably, in both convex and smooth non-
095
      convex scenarios, HOME-3 achieves a superior convergence bound of O(1/T 5/6 ).

527
528   This work introduces an innovative high-order momentum technique that utilizes high-power gra-
529   dients to significantly enhance the performance of the gradient-based optimizer. Our contributions
530   are both theoretical and empirical. On the theoretical side, we demonstrate that high-order momen-
531   tum with high-power gradients improves the convergence bound of optimizers in both convex and
532   smooth nonconvex cases, achieving an upper bound of O(1/T 5/6 ). Empirically, extensive exper-
533   iments showcase that HOME-3 consistently delivers superior reconstruction accuracy across con-
534   vex, smooth nonconvex, and nonsmooth nonconvex problems, underscoring its robustness. Looking
535
      ahead, an exciting direction for future research is determining the optimal order of momentum for
      complex objective functions, which will be pivotal in efficiently optimizing Large Language Models.
