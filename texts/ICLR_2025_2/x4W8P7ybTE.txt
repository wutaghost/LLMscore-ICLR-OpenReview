008                                                A BSTRACT
009
010
011            Vision-Language Models (VLM) can support clinicians by analyzing medical im-
012            ages and engaging in natural language interactions to assist in diagnostic and treat-
013            ment tasks. However, VLMs often exhibit ”hallucinatory” behavior, generating
014            textual outputs not grounded in contextual multimodal information. This chal-
015            lenge is particularly pronounced in the medical domain, where we do not only
016            require VLM outputs to be accurate in single interactions but also to be consistent
017
               with clinical reasoning and diagnostic pathways throughout multi-turn conversa-
               tions. For this purpose, we propose a new alignment algorithm that uses rule-
018
               based representations of clinical reasoning to ground VLMs in medical knowl-
019
               edge. These representations are utilized to (i) generate visual instruction tun-
020            ing data at scale, simulating clinician-VLM conversations with demonstrations
021            of clinical reasoning, and (ii) to derive a rule-based reward function that auto-
022            matically evaluates the clinical validity of VLM responses throughout clinician-
023            VLM interactions. Our algorithm eliminates the need for human involvement in
024            training data generation or reward model construction, reducing costs compared
025            to standard reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF). We apply our
026            alignment algorithm to develop Dr-LLaVA, a conversational VLM finetuned for
027            analyzing bone marrow pathology slides, demonstrating strong performance in
028
               single and multi-turn medical conversations.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
035   Vision-language models (VLMs) (1–3), which integrate large language models (LLMs) (4–8) with
036   vision encoders, have demonstrated strong capabilities in answering complex questions that require
037   both visual and textual reasoning. In the medical domain, VLMs hold great promise—they could
038   serve as helpful assistants for clinicians, researchers, and trainees, providing an interactive natural
039
      language interface for the analysis of medical images within clinical workflows (9–14). However,
      the practical utility of present VLMs is significantly limited by their tendency to “hallucinate”. In this
040
      context, hallucination refers not only to instances where the model generates responses ungrounded
041
      in visual input but also to cases where, in multi-turn interactions, its responses are incoherent, con-
042   tradictory, or misaligned with diagnostic pathways and domain knowledge.
043
044   The currently predominant methods to reduce hallucinations in VLMs such as Reinforcement Learn-
045
      ing from Human Feedback (RLHF) (15–18) are not well-suited for the multimodal medical context.
      Using RLHF to align VLMs with visually-grounded clinical reasoning requires multimodal train-
046
      ing data showcasing the reasoning process within multi-turn QA dialogues. These datasets are not
047
      readily available in health systems. Synthesizing these datasets and collecting clinician feedback
048   on VLM responses is bottlenecked by the expertise of medical professionals. Unlike the LLaVA-
049   RLHF model in (18), which gathered human feedback from non-expert crowdworkers for simple,
050   common-sense visual QA tasks, this process cannot be scaled without the involvement of clinicians.
051   Due to these limitations, specialized medical VLMs like LLaVA-Med (9) and PathChat (2) have been
052   limited to supervised finetuning, relying on automatically generated QA tasks using image captions.
053   Moreover, both existing general-purpose and medical VLMs have only been finetuned for single-turn
      QA, rather than for multi-turn conversations that convey complex and interactive clinical reasoning.

141   2.2              S TEP 2: D ESIGNING CLINICALLY- INFORMED RULE - BASED REWARDS
142
143   In contrast to standard RLHF approaches that rely on human feedback to evaluate ambiguous qual-
144   ities of model outputs (18; 17; 25), our conversational diagnostic system leverages ruke-based rep-
145   resentations (Fig. 3) to convert complex diagnostic questions into a sequence of discrete decisions.
146   This approach enables us to define an efficient keyword-matching algorithm that evaluates VLM
147   responses against specific terms associated with a limited set of admissible answer categories in the
148   decision tree. This facilitates automated evaluation without costly human annotation. A comprehen-
149   sive list of keywords is provided in Appendix Table B.5.
150   Given this discrete categorization, we define a reward model that assesses both the correctness of
151   model responses and their alignment with valid clinical reasoning. For an input image Ii and a
152   sequence of prompted VLM outputs (Xit , Ybit )t , we compute the reward function as:
153
154       R((\widehat {Y}^t_i, Y^t_i),\ldots , (\widehat {Y}^T_i, Y^T_i)) = \frac {1}{T}\sum _{t=1}^{T} \underbrace {R_C(Y^t_i, \widehat {Y}^t_i)}_{\mbox {\scriptsize {\bf Correctness} of responses}} + \underbrace {\lambda \cdot R_{\mathcal {S}}(\{\widehat {Y}^t_i\}_t)}_{\mbox {\scriptsize {\bf Consistency} with valid reasoning}} + R_l - R_m. \label {reward_eq}

178   In addition to the correctness and consistency rewards, we use two additional penalties to counteract
179   reward-hacking, similar to (18). First, the penalty term Rm reduces scores for outputs that remain
180   ambiguous and cannot be clearly categorized for a given analysis step. This penalty is quantified by
181   the proportion of ambiguous answers within a conversation. Second, to address the potential for the
182   rule-based rewards to encourage either overly verbose responses that incidentally include relevant
183
      keywords, or overly concise, keyword-dense answers, we implement a length-based penalty Rl
      to discourage significant deviations between the length of the VLM’s answer and the target answer
184
      length. This approach is designed to promote a conversational style that is both natural and engaging.

      Vision-Language Models for medicine. Large Language Models (LLMs) (4; 8; 28–31; 5; 6; 32; 33)
220   have excelled in generating high-quality textual responses across diverse tasks, fueling advance-
221   ments in chat-based AI assistants (7; 34). Recent work has extended these models to handle multi-
222   modal image-text data (35–37), which has led to the emergence of powerful vision-language models
223   (VLM) including OpenFlamingo (38), MiniGPT-4 (39) and LLaVA (9). In the medical domain, the
224   integration of images and texts has been explored in areas such as ultrasound (40; 41), pathology
225   (42; 12), and radiology (43; 44), typically utilizing modality-specific vision encoders. Additionally,
226   recent studies have proposed models that directly finetune state-of-the-art VLMs for medical appli-
227   cations including Med-Alpaca (45), Med-Flamingo (46) and LLaVAMed (9). However, these models
228
      solely leverage instruction-tuning with token-level supervision, which can lead to misalignment be-
      tween image and text modalities, resulting in outputs insufficiently grounded in the visual context
229
      (18). Moreover, such approaches do not regularize the model outputs on a conversation-level by
230
      incorporating domain knowledge on diagnostic pathways.
231
232   Hallucination in generative models. In the Natural Language Processing (NLP) literature, ”hallu-
233   cination” is defined as the phenomenon where a model generates content diverging from the original
234
      source material (47). With the advent of advanced LLMs, this definition has expanded. As noted in
      (48), hallucination can manifest in three distinct ways: 1) Input-conflicting hallucination, observed
235
      in scenarios like machine translation and summarization, where the model’s response alters or mis-
236
      interprets the static context of the user’s prompt (49–52); 2) Context-conflicting hallucination, where
237   the model’s output contradicts its previous responses (53; 54); and 3) Fact-conflicting hallucination,
238   in which the generated content conflicts with established factual knowledge (55; 56). Our finetun-
239   ing framework represents a novel approach to address context-conflicting hallucinations, which are
240   particularly important in clinical applications (54; 57; 58). This is because medical practitioners
241   adhere to stringent logical processes in diagnosis and avoid conclusions that contradict previous
242   observations (59). Therefore, a VLM that accurately identifies the final diagnosis but fails to cor-
243   rectly respond to preceding observation-related questions would be deemed unreliable (60). Similar
244   to prior work, our reward model in (2) addresses input- and fact-conflicting hallucination, but is
245
      distinguished by inclusion of the rule-based reward RS to address context-conflicting hallucination.
246   Addressing misalignment in Vision-Language Models. Reinforcement Learning from Human
247   Feedback (RLHF) (17; 16; 61; 62) is a predominant paradigm for aligning VLM outputs with spe-
248   cific domain requirements or general human preferences. This method relies on preference data from
249   human labelers to train a reward model, which is then used to fine-tune the VLM using reinforce-
250   ment learning techniques such as Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (27). Our work is particularly
251
      related to approaches in the AI safety literature that aim to incorporate rule-based reward specifi-
      cations into the alignment procedure (63; 64). For example, Sparrow (63) defines explicit rules to
252
      obtain more concrete feedback from human labelers and incorporates rule adherence into the train-
253
      ing process. However, their method still requires fitting a general preference model using human or
254   AI feedback, and their focus is on enhancing safety behavior. In contrast, we leverage rule-based
255   specifications to concretize questions and enable automatic response labeling, eliminating the need
256   for human-generated preference data. This approach not only reduces reliance on expensive special-
257   ist annotators but also improves model performance in terms of consistency in clinical reasoning. To
258   the best of our knowledge, our work is among the first to apply RL-based fine-tuning to VLMs in the
259   medical domain. We introduce a novel RL framework tailored to medical decision-making contexts
260   by using an automatic reward function that explicitly incorporates adherence to clinical reasoning
261   pathways into the alignment procedure.

284   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL SET- UP
285
286   Training details. As our study concentrates on the performance of the finetuning algorithm, we
287   base Dr-LLaVA on the same model architecture as LLaVA (2). Our LLM utilizes Vicuna-V1.5-
288   7b (5; 6; 33), paired with the pre-trained CLIP visual encoder ViT-L/14 at an image resolution of
289
      256 × 256 (65). Grid features are employed both before and after the final transformer layer to
      enhance the model’s integration of visual data. We use a linear layer to map image features into
290
      the word embedding space, drawing on the pre-trained linear projection matrix checkpoints from
291
      LLaVA. We then conducted supervised fine-tuning for four epochs.
292
293   During the RL phase, following (66) and (18), we initialize the value model based on the LLavA-
294   13B-based reward model. We use LoRA-based finetuning with a rank of 64 for both the attention
295
      and feed-forward network modules. Consistent with (66), we use a batch size of 512 and normalize
      the advantage across the batch for each PPO step. The peak learning rate was set at 3 × 10−5 ,
296
      applying cosine decay, and gradients were clipped by their Euclidean norm with a threshold of 1.
297
      Training was conducted through four complete rounds using our held-out RL data. For generalized
298   advantage estimation, we set both λ and γ to 1, and adopted a constant KL regularizer coefficient of

      5   C ONCLUSION
433
434
      Vision-language models (VLMs) hold the potential to become valuable tools for clinicians, re-
435
      searchers, and trainees, offering an interactive natural language interface for medical image analysis
436   within clinical workflows. Yet, their utility is often compromised by the generation of “hallucinated”
437   outputs that deviate from sound medical reasoning, leading to a lack of trust in their responses. In
438   this paper, we introduce a novel alignment algorithm that finetunes VLMs by grounding them in
439   rule-based representations of medical image analysis processes. This approach ensures the produc-
440   tion of clinically valid and consistent responses across both single-turn and multi-turn interactions.
441   Utilizing this algorithm, we develop Dr-LLaVA, a VLM specifically tailored for analyzing bone mar-
442   row image patches. Our findings reveal that Dr-LLaVA not only excels in single-turn question-answer
443   scenarios but also demonstrates superior adaptability and accuracy in complex, multi-turn clinical
444
      dialogues, outperforming other state-of-the-art VLMs. These results suggest that grounding VLMs
      in structured medical analysis pathways enhances their overall clinical reasoning capabilities, mak-
445
      ing them more robust to variations in question sequencing and resilient against misleading physician
446
      hypotheses. Furthermore, this alignment strategy can be readily applied to various domains where
447   decision-making processes can be decomposed into logical sequences of substeps, such as numerous
448   medical tasks governed by clinical practice guidelines that codify diagnostic workflows.

      A.1    A DDITIONAL M EDICAL C ONTEXT
706
      In this paper, we focus on the analysis of bone marrow pathology slides for the diagnosis of blood
707
      cancer disorders. Specifically, our dataset contains 512x512 pixel images of 16,340 pathology
708   patches corresponding to healthy, inconclusive, acute myeloid leukemia, and multiple myeloma
709   cases. The process for the analysis of bone marrow pathology slides, involves multiple steps. A
710   pathologists has to first identify image regions that are deemed adequate for evaluation, excluding
711   cases with either too low image quality or where the presence of other cells (e.g. red blood cells)
712   prevents accurate medical diagnosis. Subsequently, the remaining adequate regions are examined to
713   determine if they exhibit characteristics indicative of cancerous tissue. Specifically, in bone marrow
714   aspirates, the assessment focuses on whether there is abnormal proliferation of cells in the regions
715   of interest. Depending on the type of cells proliferating, the patient may be diagnosed with a corre-
716
      sponding hematological disorder. For instance, in our dataset, the uncontrolled proliferation of blast
      cells is indicative of acute myeloid leukemia, while similar proliferation of plasma cells suggests
717
      multiple myeloma.

720   A.2    G ENERATING A MULTI - TURN CONVERSATION DATASET
721
722   The below section provides further details on the steps we took to derive a multimodal multi-turn
723   conversation dataset in this specialist problem domain.
724   Image data: We obtained whole pathology slide images sourced from the clinical archives of an
725   academic medical center. These were then segmented into 512x512 pixel patches1 and labelled
726   as either ”adequate for analysis”, ”particle-enriched contamination” or ”blood contamination” after
727   pathologist review. Subsequently we leveraged specialist software in order to obtain cell-counts,
728   based on which a pathologist labelled cases with a high increase in blast or plasma cells as acute
729   myeloid leukemia or multiple myeloma, respectively. Table A.4 details the distribution of the final
730
      diagnosis corresponding to the image data.
731   Question Answer generation: Next, we utilize the rule-based representation of the bone marrow
732   pathology slide analysis process to create clinically meaningful multi-turn conversations. This is
733   accomplished by filling in question and answer templates based on the respective label for each
734   analysis step. To prevent our model from overfitting to specific expressions in these templates, we
735   increased the diversity of questions and answers by obtaining multiple question templates from our
736
      clinical collaborators and using GPT-4 to paraphrase these templates. The respective prompts are
      provided below:
737
738   Prompt for question paraphrasing: ”Perform X times augmentation of the following sentence, it
739   is for medical questions so make sure you preserve the meaning concisely.”
740   Prompt for answer paraphrasing : ”Perform X times augmentation of the following sentence, it
741   is for medical diagnosis so make sure you preserve the meaning concisely: ’sentence’. Also note
742   that the question is ’question’, also don’t repeat anything related to in response to the question, just
743   make sure the single sentence is grammatically correct and makes sense.”

766   B.2   T RAINING DETAILS
767
      As our study concentrates on the performance of the finetuning algorithm, we base Dr-LLaVA on the
768
      same model architecture as LLaVA (2). Our LLM utilizes Vicuna-V1.5-7b (5; 6; 33), paired with the
769
      pre-trained CLIP visual encoder ViT-L/14 at an image resolution of 256 × 256 (65). Grid features
770   are employed both before and after the final transformer layer to enhance the model’s integration of
771   visual data. We use a linear layer to map image features into the word embedding space, drawing
772   on the pre-trained linear projection matrix checkpoints from LLaVA. We then conducted supervised
773   fine-tuning for four epochs.
774
      During the RL phase, following (66) and (18), we initialized the value model based on the LLavA-
775
      13B-based reward model. We used LoRA-based finetuning with a rank of 64 for both the attention
776   and feed-forward network modules. Consistent with (66), we used a batch size of 512 and normal-
777   ized the advantage across the batch for each PPO step. The peak learning rate was set at 3 × 10−5 ,
778   applying cosine decay, and gradients were clipped by their Euclidean norm with a threshold of 1.
779   Training was conducted through four complete rounds using our held-out RL data. For generalized
780   advantage estimation, we set both λ and γ to 1, and adopted a constant KL regularizer coefficient of
781   0.1. The Dr-LLaVA model was trained using four A100 80 GB GPUs.
782   We leverage 80% of our synthesized clinical multi-turn conversation dataset for supervised finetun-
783   ing and RL and use the remaining 20% for evaluation. We split the data at the conversation level such
784   that all question-answer pairs pertaining to a particular image belong to the same sample. We use
785   different prompt templates and rephrasing for the question-answer pairs in the training and testing
786   sets to ensure that the models do not over-fit to specific phrasing of the clinician-VLM conversations

      C     E VALUATION
865

      C.1   E VALUATION METRICS
867
868   To effectively assess the performance of our proposed model, we measure the accuracy of our model
869   at the question, conversation and diagnosis level.
870
871         1. Question-level Accuracy (AQ ): This metric evaluates the model’s performance at the
872            single question level. It is obtained by dividing the number of questions answered correctly
873            by the total number of questions:
874
875                                                                  A_Q = \frac {\text {Number of correct answers}}{\text {Total number of questions}}    (4)
876
877         2. Conversation-level Accuracy (AC ): This metric assesses the model’s accuracy at the con-
878            versation level. Here we only consider a VLM’s response as correct if it is able to correctly
879            answer all questions pertaining to a multi-turn conversation about a specific case.
880
881                        A_C = \frac {\text {Number of conversations with all questions answered correctly}}{\text {Total number of cases}}              (5)
882
883            This metric asses the model’s capability to consistently provide accurate answers across all
884            questions within a multi-turn conversation, enabling the model to be a trustworthy com-
885
               panion throughout the full image analysis process.
886         3. Diagnostic Accuracy (AD ): This metric focuses solely on the VLMs’ responses to ques-
887            tions about the final diagnosis, as this is often the primary concern for medical decision-
888            makers:
889                                      A_D = \frac {\text {Number of correctly answered diagnosis questions}}{\text {Total number of cases}}             (6)
890
891
      In conclusion, these three distinct levels of accuracy—AQ , AC , and AD —provide a comprehensive
892   evaluation of the proposed model’s effectiveness in handling different aspects of medical inquiries.
893   By breaking down the analysis to question, conversation, and diagnosis levels, we can better un-
894   derstand the model’s strengths and pinpoint areas for improvement in handling complex medical
895   scenarios.

904   C ONFIRMATION Q UERY (CQ) P ROMPTS
905
      Confirmation Queries aim to assess the model’s ability to validate clinician opinions. These queries
906
      challenge the model to either concur with or contest a clinician’s judgment, which may be accurate
907
      (CQ-R) or erroneous (CQ-W).
908

910   ment]. Do you agree with this assessment?”
911   Example Prompt 2 (CQ-W): ”After examining the image, the clinician suggests that [misleading
912   statement]. Do you concur with this opinion?”
913
914   R ATIONALIZATION Q UERY (RQ) P ROMPTS
915
916   Rationalization Queries present the model with a previous conclusion, which may be correct (RQ-
917   R) or incorrect (RQ-W), and ask about the next diagnostic steps. These queries assess the model’s
      ability to correct incorrect hypotheses even when not explicitly prompted to do so.
