011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            3D Single Object Tracking (3D SOT) in LiDAR point clouds plays a crucial role
014            in autonomous driving. Current approaches mostly follow two paradigms, i.e.,
015
               Siamese matching-based and motion-centric. However, LiDAR point clouds lack
               enough appearance information, while the motion-centric trackers suffer from
016
               complex model structures. To address these issues, we present a novel and con-
017
               ceptually simple tracking framework dubbed SCtrack, which jointly explores the
018            matching and motion clues in point clouds. Specifically, SCtrack embeds point
019            clouds into spatially structured features and conducts space correlation along the
020            aligned spatial region. The target relative motion is directly inferred from the cor-
021            related features. In contrast to prevalent PointNet-based features, our spatially
022            structured representation inherently models motion clues among the consecutive
023            frames of point clouds, thereby being complementary to appearance matching.
024            To better utilize the aligned structured features, we employ a strategy of varied-
025            size space regions that adapt to different target shapes and locations during space
026
               correlation. Without bells and whistles, SCtrack achieves leading performance,
               with 89.1%, 71.5%, and 62.7% precision on KITTI, NuScenes, and Waymo Open
027
               Dataset, and runs at a considerably high speed of 60 Fps on a single RTX3090
028
               GPU. Extensive studies validate the effectiveness of our SCtrack framework. The
029            code will be released.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   3D single object tracking (SOT) based on point clouds is a fundamental task with enormous poten-
035   tial for various applications, including autonomous driving and robotics (Lee & Hwang, 2015; Liu
036   et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2022). Early efforts (Kristan et al., 2015; 2016; Bertinetto
037   et al., 2016b; Danelljan et al., 2017; Li et al., 2018; Xie et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2022) focus on visual
038   object tracking that uses RGB images obtained by cameras. Recently, with the development of 3D
039
      sensors, such as LiDAR, 3D data is easy to acquire and set up for 3D object tracking. However,
      due to the sparsity and irregular distribution of 3D points, existing popular schemes on 2D visual
040
      tracking cannot be directly applied to 3D single object tracking. As a result, accurately and effi-
041
      ciently analyzing point cloud data to track objects in complex scenes is still a challenging and open
042   problem.
043
044   Many current tracking approaches (Wang et al., 2021c; Cui et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2021; Shan
045
      et al., 2021; Hui et al., 2021; 2022) use a point-wise representation by taking raw point clouds
      as input. For instance, P2B (Qi et al., 2020) and its subsequent works use a point-based network
046
      with a Siamese architecture for feature extraction. This is followed by a point-wise appearance-
047
      matching module that propagates target cues and a target prediction network, as shown in Fig. 1
048   (a). M2-Track (Zheng et al., 2022) proposes a motion-centric approach that first identifies the target
049   points by segmenting them from their surroundings using a PointNet (Qi et al., 2017a) segmentation
050   network. Then, it leverages motion clues to refine the localization of the target, as shown in Fig. 1
051   (b). Although these approaches (Giancola et al., 2019; Qi et al., 2020; Fang et al., 2020; Wang et al.,
052   2021c; Cui et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2021; Shan et al., 2021; Hui et al., 2021; 2022) have shown
053   excellent performance in tracking benchmarks, motion-centric trackers often necessitate complex
      model structures. In the meantime, Siamese matching trackers suffer from insufficient appearance

107   2D Single Object Tracking. 2D Single object tracking in videos is closely related to 3D point cloud
      tracking tasks. The dominant 2D object tracking paradigm is Siamese tracking (Bertinetto et al.,


                                                                      2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2016a; Li et al., 2018; Zhang & Peng, 2019; Xu et al., 2020), which regards the tracking problem
109   as an appearance-matching problem between consecutive video images. Due to the rich appearance
110   clues and complex motion patterns in natural 2D video data, most 2D trackers (Chen et al., 2021;
111   Yan et al., 2021; Cui et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2022) focus on enhancing appearance
112   matching. The representative trackers are based on transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017; Dosovitskiy
113   et al., 2021). For example, TransT (Chen et al., 2021) conducts the cross-attention (Vaswani et al.,
114   2017) module multiple times to improve the matching quality. Stark (Yan et al., 2021) manages a
115   dynamic template to obtain a better appearance-matching result over time. Despite their great suc-
116   cess, the 2D tracking techniques largely overlook the motion clues, hindering their direct application
117
      in 3D point cloud tracking. Realizing that point cloud data lacks appearance information, we jointly
      model the motion and appearance clues instead of solely exploiting one clue.
118
119   3D Single Object Tracking. 3D Single Object Tracking shares a similar task definition with 2D
120   object tracking. Consequently, classical 2D tracking techniques such as Siamese network (Bertinetto
121   et al., 2016a) and region proposal networks (Ren et al., 2015; Li et al., 2018), are widely transferred
122   to the 3D point cloud tracking domain. The pioneering 3D SOT work, P2B (Qi et al., 2020) follows
123
      the Siamese paradigm and develops a Point-to-Box network for target box regression. With a simple
      Siamese tracking pipeline, most 3D SOT methods (Qi et al., 2020; Hui et al., 2021; Xu et al.,
124
      2023b) focus on enhancing target-matching modules and prediction networks. Among them, the
125
      transformer-based methods (Hui et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2021; Shan et al., 2021) are most widely
126   explored to enhance the point cloud matching for tracking. For example, PTTR (Zhou et al., 2021)
127   proposes a Point Relation Transformer to match the appearance clues in point clouds of the template
128   and search region. Later, CXTtrack (Xu et al., 2023a) conducts transformer matching in both local
129   and global contexts to exploit the appearance clues more comprehensively. MBPtrack (Xu et al.,
130   2023b) further improves the appearance-matching paradigm by using a memory network to leverage
131   the historical point clouds. SyncTrack (Ma et al., 2023) performs synchronizing feature extraction
132   and matching inspired by the one-stream pipeline (Ye et al., 2022). Thus, most 3D methods are
133   developed under the Siamese appearance-matching paradigm. Differently, M2Track (Zheng et al.,
134
      2022) firstly builds a pure motion-centric paradigm rather than exploiting appearance clues solely.
      M2Track models the motion clues by processing two consecutive frames in two stages: A target
135
      segmentation to classify the foreground object and a target prediction refinement to localize the
136
      target precisely.
137
138   Despite their great success, most 3D trackers improve tracking performance by developing more
139   complex appearance-matching modules because they overlook the role motion clues. The point
140
      cloud data modality is generally textureless, unordered, and sparse, which makes appearance-
      matching less effective than that of 2D object tracking in videos. However, the motion-centric
141
      tracker M2Track suffers from a complex two-stage model pipeline. In this work, we joint exploit
142
      appearance matching and motion modeling by constructing a conceptually simple framework, which
143   fully leverages spatially structured feature representation to guide accurate target localization.
144
145   Transformer in 2D/3D Vision. Transformers have shown great performance and wild applications
146
      in various vision tasks. The attention scheme (Vaswani et al., 2017) exhibits a stronger global
      modeling ability than the traditional CNN (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; He et al., 2016) architecture in
147
      the visual domain. ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) first conducts a comprehensive exploration of the
148
      transformer-based backbone network for the fundamental image classification task. Then, vision
149   transformers are modified and adapted to various 2D vision tasks. SwinT (Liu et al., 2021) and
150   PVT (Wang et al., 2021a) adopt the hierarchical structure with an approximation attention scheme
151   for dense visual prediction tasks, such as object detection (Lin et al., 2014) and segmenation (Wang
152   et al., 2021b). In the 3D vision task, the transformer also shows superiority in modeling the point
153   cloud data. The point transformer (Zhao et al., 2021) is the pioneering work to apply transformer
154   in 3D point cloud understanding task. Later, the succeeding works apply a transformer to more
155   widely 3D tasks, such as 3DETR (Misra et al., 2021) for detection, Segformer3D (Perera et al.,
156   2024) for segmentation, and PointformerV3 (Wu et al., 2024) for indoor/outdoor point cloud tasks.
157
      Transformer also demonstrates the effectiveness in processing consecutive point clouds, such as 3D
      video detection and tracking (Yin et al., 2021).

160   Shan et al., 2021; Nie et al., 2023) particularly address the appearance matching problem, largely
161   neglecting the motion clues in consecutive point clouds. In this work, we first embed the point

      3.1       P ROBLEM D EFINITION
193
194
      The 3D Single Object Tracking (SOT) task definition is to find a specific object by giving the tar-
195   get point clouds in the first frame. The target state is generally described by a seven-dimensional
196   3D bounding box (x, y, z, w, l, h, θ) ∈ R7 . Specifically, {xyz, wlh, θ} describes the target center,
197   object size, and orientation angle, respectively. Considering that most objects in point cloud track-
198   ing scenarios are rigid, the trackers only need to predict the relative translation (∆x, ∆y, ∆z) and
199   changing angle ∆θ. A typical working pipeline of 3D SOT trackers is described as follows: Given
200   the template point cloud Pt=1 ∈ RN ×3 (N is the number of point clouds) and initial bounding box
201   B1 in the first frame, the tracker predicts the relative movement for incoming search region point
202   clouds Pt=i ∈ RN ×3 (i is the current time index). The final predicted 3D bounding box Bi for
203
      localizing the current target is obtained by a rigid body transformation, which applies the relative
      movement (∆x, ∆y, ∆z) to the previous 3D bounding box Bi−1 .
204
205
206   3.2       J OINT M ATCHING AND M OTION M ODELING P IPELINE
207
208   We propose to unify the independent feature extraction and matching in the Siamese paradigm and
209   remove the two stages of the motion-centric paradigm with one single backbone. Within a backbone
210   network, we first use the structured embedding module to process unstructured points. Then, we
211   adopt a space-correlated transformer for feature correlation and motion modeling. Finally, the target
212
      position is directly predicted from the extracted features. Our SCtrack can realize synchronized
      matching and motion modeling.
213

      Figure 3: The details of spatially structured embedding module. The structured features of the
228   template and search region are squeezed, concatenated, and merged to capture the target motion
229   clues. In contrast to the popular point-wise features, spatially structured feature representation can
230   preserve more spatial information for predicting relative target motion in two consecutive frames.
231
232   To accurately capture the relative motion of the tracked target, we subdivide the 3D space into
233   equally spaced voxels given a pair of point clouds from the template and search regions. Suppose
234   the point cloud encompasses 3D space with range D, H, W along the z, y, x axes respectively.
235   We define each voxel of size vD , vH , and vW accordingly. The resulting 3D voxel grid is of size
236   D′ = D/vD , H ′ = H/vH , W ′ = W/vW . Due to the sparsity and highly variable point density
237
      throughout the space, we group the points according to the voxel they are located in. Then, we
      randomly sample a fixed number, N , of points from those voxels containing more than N points.
238
      To encode the shape of the surface contained within the voxel, we adopt the hierarchical feature
239
      encoding layer in VoxelNet Zhou & Tuzel (2018) to process each voxel. Then, we obtain voxel
240   features VF{C,D,H,W } with the grid size {vD , vH , vW } and channel dimension C, repectively. We
241   further feed voxel features VF{C,D,H,W } into 3D convolutional layers, which consist of stacked
242   3D convolution, BN Ioffe & Szegedy (2015) layer, and ReLU Real et al. (2019) layer. The 3D
243   convolutional layers aggregate voxel-wise features within a progressively expanding receptive field,
244   adding more context to the shape description. The detailed architectural variants of the embedding
245   module are in supplementary materials.
246   Comparisons with point-wise feature embedding. The mainstream 3D single object trackers com-
247   monly adopt point-wise feature representation extracted by PointNet Qi et al. (2017a;b). However,
248   the PointNet-based features emphasize appearance matching more while neglecting the spatial re-
249   lationship among points. In contrast, our spatially structured representation inherently reflects the
250   spatial relationships among corresponding points, thereby guiding a more accurate relative motion
251   of the target between two consecutive frames for 3D tracking tasks.

361   The total epoch number is 120 and the batch size is set to 32. Following the common training
362   settings (Xu et al., 2023a; Nie et al., 2023), we construct template/search region inputs for two con-
363
      secutive frames. Specifically, we randomly crop search regions that are located around the previous

364
      found in supplementary materials. We also adopt a simple data augmentation strategy to improve the
365
      tracking robustness. The random data augmentation simulates the change in target movement and

      5                L IMITATION AND D ISCUSSION
521
522
      Although SCtrack has proven the effectiveness of structured embedding in 3D SOT, its application
      to other Siamese-based and motion-based 3D trackers still needs to be validated and explored. Com-
523
      pared to the motion-centric tracking paradigm, our joint appearance matching and motion modeling
524
      backbone is conceptually simple and consists of two modules: a structured embedding module and
525   a space-correlated transformer. However, our tracking model could be improved by unifying these
526   two modules and embedding the space correlation operator into the structured embedding stage,
527   resulting in a more straightforward model pipeline.

      6                C ONCLUSION
531
      This paper introduces SCtrack, a conceptually simple tracking framework. SCtrack provides 3D sin-
532
      gle object tracking area with a joint exploring appearance matching and motion modeling paradigm.
533
      It explores the spatially structured representation of point clouds instead of widely used point-wise
534   representations in most 3D SOT methods. Moreover, in contrast to the complex matching-based
535   fusion module, SCtrack uses a space-correlated transformer module to capture the appearance and
536   motion information of targets in varying sizes and shapes. Extensive experiments demonstrate our
537   SCtrack framework is efficient, achieving superior performance over all previous state-of-the-art
538   trackers. We expect this work could attract more attention to the balance between leveraging ap-
539   pearance and motion clues on 3D single object tracking task. A deeper exploration of the joint
      backbone may leave as future work.

      B.1     S TRUCTURED EMBEDDING MODULE .
767
768   We present the network details of the structured embedding module as follows: Our experimental
769   setup is based on the LiDAR specifications of the KITTI dataset (Geiger et al., 2012). The detailed
770   information are as follows: The range of point clouds for this task is set as [−4.8, 4.8]×[−4.8, 4.8]×
771   [−1.5, 1.5] meters along the X, Y , and Z axes, respectively. Any points that go beyond the bound-
772   aries of the image will be removed. The voxel size is set to vD = 0.15, vH = 0.075, vW = 0.075
773   meters. We have set T to 35, which represents the maximum number of randomly sampled points
774   in each non-empty voxel. Our approach involves using two voxel encoding layers which is inspire
775
      by VoxelNet (Zhou & Tuzel, 2018): VE-1 (7, 32) and VE-2 (32, 128). The final channel dimension
      of the feature maps output to R128 by VE-2. We use Conv3D (ci n, co ut, k, s, p) to represent an 3D
776
      convolution operator. Here, ci n and co ut denote the number of input and output channels, and k,
777
      s, and p represent the kernel size, stride size, and padding size, respectively. The 3D convolutional
778   filters in our voxel encoding layers are stacked as follows: Conv3D (128, 64, 3, (2,1,1), (1,1,1));
779   Conv3D (64, 64, 3, (1,1,1), (0,1,1)); Conv3D (64, 64, 3, (2,1,1), (1,1,1)) The input to the space-
780   correlated transformer is a feature map of size 3 × 384 × 16 × 16 after reshaping. These dimensions
781   correspond to the channel, height, and width of the 3D tensor.
782
783   B.2     S PACE - CORRELATED TRANSFORMER .
784
785   In this section, we give details of the hyper-network and sampling process. The proposed hyper-
786   network module differs from previous work (Liu et al., 2021) as it controls the regression distance
787   without relying on a predefined parameter. Our space-correlated transformer follows the similar
788   learned window scheme in VSA (Zhang et al., 2022). Specifically, we utilize the hypernetwork to
789
      predict the scales and offsets of the corresponding region, which are denoted as sw and ow in R2 .
      The normalization is achieved by multiplying the ratio between the window size and the image size,
790
      which are also referred to as sw and ow for simplicity. This normalization allows the hyper-network
791
      module to learn how to expand and move the current region toward the optimal attention region
792   using the window as a reference. It then computes the tokens’ new coordinates within that region.
793
794
      B.2.1    T EMPLATE AND SEARCH REGION INPUT.
795
796   In our method, we define search regions for two consecutive frames at timestamps t − 1 and t by
797   focusing on the previous target bounding box within a 3D space. Section 4.1 of the main manuscript
798   outlines the specific range values of 3D spaces for different object categories. To ensure a fair
799   comparison, we employ the same approach as existing methods (Qi et al., 2020; Zheng et al., 2022)
800
      by sampling 1024 points using farthest point sampling (Qi et al., 2017b) within the derived search
      regions as inputs for SCtrack. For SCtrack, instead of reducing the number of points in the point
801
      clouds, we partition the search regions into 128×128×20 voxel grids, which are then used as inputs
802
      for the model.

950   D.2    M OTION EXPLOITATION IN SC TRACK AND OTHER 3D SOT TRACKERS .
951
952   The difference in the inference phase between the 3D SOT trackers and the 2D SOT trackers (Hui
953   et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2023a;b; Zheng et al., 2021; 2022) lies in the selection of the template. The
954   appearance information in 2D images is far more rich than the 3D point cloud data. Thus, 2D SOT
955
      trackers generally take the first frame of the video as the template and adopt a fixed template strategy
      for subsequent video frames. In 3D SOT trackers, the predicted target location is center-cropped as
956
      the new template for next-frame prediction, which means the template is kept updating every time
957
      stamp. Thus, 3D SOT trackers only need to predict the relative motion of the tracked target between
958   the current frame and the last frame. The reason is the point cloud data lacks enough appearance
959   information, so exploiting motion clues is vital to locating the tracked target. Fig. 8 shows how we
960   exploit the motion clues. The motion-centic M2track in 3DSOT leverages motion clues by using a
961   direct spatial concatenation and a segmentation network to crop the aligned region. This complex
962   operation can be simplified by our varied-size space region alignment. Our Method further conducts
963   feature matching to exploit appearance clues as a complementary. Besides, our joint modeling
964   method is under a conceptually simple and neat (one-stage) network.
