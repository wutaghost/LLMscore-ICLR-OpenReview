011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Whole-Slide-Images (WSIs) have generated significant interests in cancer re-
014            search community, owing to their availability and the rich information that they
015            provide. Previous Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) methods often neglect the
016
               topological structure of tissues which is closely related to tumor evolution. Some
               attempts with transformer-based MIL methods take spatial relation into account
017
               with a trade-off of computational complexity. We propose Projection-gated
018
               Graph Attention Network (Pg-GAT), a lightweight model that effectively lever-
019            ages graph neural network to provide structural prior, learns spatial and contex-
020            tual relations through graph attention, and mitigates tissue morphology redun-
021            dancy with differentiable projection-gated pooling, maintaining a data-adaptive
022            decision boundary. In addition, Pg-GAT outputs region-of-interest (ROI) with
023            respect to the graph-level prediction with post-hoc graph explainer, offering tu-
024            mor localization and model interpretability. We evaluate our method on lymph
025            node metastasis datasets (CAMELYON16 and CAMELYON17) and non-small
026            cell lung cancer (TCGA-NSCLC), achieving AUCs of 97.6% and 95.6% and
027
               99.6% respectively, outperforming state-of-the-art methods. Code is avaliable at

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The growing availability of Whole-Slide-Images (WSIs) is transforming the field of digital pathol-
      ogy. However, due to the gigapixel resolution of WSIs, manual annotation and analysis remain
034
      prohibitively time-consuming. Recent advancements in artificial intelligence (AI) have enabled sig-
035
      nificant progress in automating WSI analysis, with multiple instance learning (MIL) being the key
036   paradigm for whole-slide-level analysis. MIL approaches divide WSIs into smaller patches, which
037   are then further analysed via convolutional neural networks (CNNs). However, conventional MIL
038   methods often treat all patches from a WSI as instances within a ”bag” and assign a positive label
039   to the entire bag based on the presence of a single positive patch, overlooking important contextual
040   and spatial dependencies between patches.
041
      Attention-based MIL methods are proposed to tackle the missing contextual information problem by
042   learning patch level attention based on extracted patch features. AB-MIL (Ilse et al., 2018) learns the
043   attention of each patch with respect to the slide-level classification. CLAM (Lu et al., 2021) extends
044   AB-MIL with an extra patch clustering branch with pseudo patch label generated by the attention
045   model. Similarly DS-MIL (Li et al., 2021) incorporates a branch of max-pooling to identify critical
046   patches along side the patch attention learning branch. CAMIL (Fourkioti et al., 2023) introduces a
047   context-aware neighbor-constrained mask which is a static 1-hop similarity weighted adjacency ma-
048   trix in graph construction. However, these methods still overlook the spatial relationships between
049   patches, which are crucial for accurate tissue profiling.
050   Several other researches leverage transformers (Vaswani, 2017) to incorporate spatial information
051   with positional encoding. TransMIL (Shao et al., 2021) creates artificial 2D square feature map as
052   positional encoding with zero-padding during squaring process, introducing extra non-informative
053   input and alters the tissue spatial structure representation. GTP (Zheng et al., 2022a) constructs a
      graph with the 2D locations of patches and applys a transformer block with graph adjacency matrices

054
      as positional encoding. This method employs a single layer of graph convolution network (GCN)
055   layer, followed by a computationally expensive min-cut pooling, hindering the scalability.
056
057   We observe several limitations in existing spatial-aware and context-aware MIL methods: a) they
058
      reply on spectral graphs, requiring extra storage for large adjacency matrix. b) they fail to fully
      leverage the potential of attention mechanism within graph models. Instead computationally ex-
059
      pensive transformer is often adopted. Motivated by this, we seek to explore the efficacy of modern
060
      spatial graph models with integrated attention mechanism for WSIs analysis.
061
062   In this paper we propose a novel framework for WSIs analysis, namely Projection-gate Graph
063   Attention Network (Pg-GAT), by exploiting graph structure with attention and empirically chosen
064
      n-hop neighborhood. We argue that graph intrinsic characteristics is capable of capturing spatial
      relations in tissue regions. By incorporating differentiable projection-gated topk pooling in a hier-
065
      archical manner, our model efficiently removes morphology redundancy and offers multi-resolution
066
      field of view (FOV). Besides being computationally lightweight, Pg-GAT also demonstrates the WSI
067   representation learning efficacy on three benchmark dataset. Furthermore, Pg-GAT can identify tu-
068   mor regions, offering model interpretability with post-hoc GNNExplainer.

071   2   R ELATED W ORK
072
073   Graph-based WSIs representation learning can be broadly categorized into two approaches: cell-
074   based and patch-based. Cell-based methods (Pati et al., 2022; Nair et al., 2022; di Villaforesta et al.,
075   2023; Alzoubi et al., 2024) rely on precise cell segmentation and effective cell-level feature extrac-
076   tion, which introduces extra uncertainty during preprocessing. In contrast, patch-based approaches
077   offer increased robustness. For instance, GTP (Zheng et al., 2022a) constructs a graph with patches
078   as nodes, connecting them based on Euclidean distance, utilizing a single graph convolutional net-
079   work (GCN) layer followed by a transformer block that incorporates the graph adjacency matrix
080   as positional encoding. Similarly, CAMIL (Fourkioti et al., 2023) employs a neighbor-constrained
081
      matrix that functions as a static 1-hop neighbor similarity matrix in the graph domain. Both methods
      require substantial storage for large adjacency matrices due to dense matrix multiplication.
082
083   Graph pooling is a critical operation for aggregating node-level information to the graph level. In
084   MIL, the class distribution of patches within a single whole-slide image (WSI) is often imbalanced,
085   and traditional pooling techniques such as global mean, max, or sum pooling can lead to undesir-
086   able shifts in the decision boundary. Differentiable pooling methods enable graph neural networks
087
      (GNNs) to learn the distribution of node classes via backpropagation. DiffPool (Ying et al., 2018)
      computes soft cluster assignments to coarsen nodes into clusters at each layer. Min-cut pooling
088
      (Bianchi et al., 2020), based on the graph min-cut problem, also performs clustering for pooling.
089
      Both methods, however, operate on adjacency matrices, resulting in significant storage overhead.
090   More computationally efficient alternatives exist, such as TopK pooling (Gao & Ji, 2019), which
091   uses a 1D projection of node features as a gating criterion, and SAGPool (Lee et al., 2019), which
092   replaces the 1D projection with a GNN layer. ASAP (Ranjan et al., 2020) further extends this by
093   scoring nodes with a GCN after an initial node clustering, incorporating node aggregation and edge
094   weights into the pooling process. However, there is functional overlap between pooling methods
095   like SAGPool, ASAP, and GNNs themselves, which may not necessarily lead to improved model
096   performance.
097   In contrast to convolutional neural networks (CNNs), where a single convolution operation is of-
098   ten applicable to all image data, the graph domain exhibits greater structural flexibility, making it
099   challenging to design a universal GNN architecture. However, with a deep understanding of GNN
100   architectures and key engineering principles—such as message passing, graph pooling, and domain-
101   specific data characteristics, straightforward and effective GNN models can therefore be developed
102   with recent advances in graph research.
103   In our proposed Pg-GAT, local information is captured through attention-guided message passing,
104   while global information is aggregated using differentiable graph pooling, addressing the class im-
105   balance problem in WSIs by incorporating both spatial and contextual relationships. Unlike some
106   existing MIL approaches, which apply hierarchical aggregation across multiple image resolutions
107   at the input stage, Pg-GAT performs in-graph hierarchical aggregation. This ensures that the entire
      process remains intrinsic to the graph representation.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
109
110
      Pg-GAT operates on the principle that graphs inherently capture spatial relations, while message
111
      passing facilitates context exchange among neighboring nodes. Graph pooling mechanisms enable
112   the extraction of global relations. The overall framework of Pg-GAT is depicted in Figure 1.
113
114         1. WSIs are divided into non-overlapping patches. A grid graph is constructed with each patch
115            as a node. Node features are extracted via a feature encoder and edges are formed based on
116            the Euclidean coordinates. This initial grid graph serves as the structural prior.
117         2. Pg-GAT takes grid graphs as inputs. Graph attention mechanism learns the interaction im-
118            portance between nodes/patches with respect to the graph label. Node features are updated
119            via message passing, enhancing the contextual awareness in local neighborhood.
120
            3. Projection-gated topk pooling step projects each node feature onto 1D vector with a learn-
121
               able projection vector p. Projection score y serves as the gating criterion, leading to the
122            removal of redundant nodes and their corresponding edges after each pooling operation.
123            This step is crucial for learning the global structure.
124
            4. Global pooling is applied at each hierarchy, and the slide representation is subsequently
125
               aggregated and fed into a classifier for final prediction.

      3.2     P ROJECTION - GATED G RAPH ATTENTION N ETWORK
182
183   Our key assumption is that the intrinsic properties of graphs are more effective and elegant in han-
184   dling the spatial structure of WSIs compared to positional encodings used in transformers. Given
185   the highly imbalanced distribution of patch classes within a WSI, pooling is crucial for capturing
186   the correct global landscape in slide-level predictions. Projection-gated topk pooling introduces
187   sparsity and hierarchy among the nodes, which we argue is effective in removing morphological
188   redundancies and aggregating data-adaptive, skewed global information.

234   Nodes share similar degrees. Therefore there is less flexibility compared to more complex graphs
235   such as those in protein structures or social networks. As we show in the results section, project-
236   ing node features onto 1D scalar values as pooling gating criterion is an efficient way to learn a
237   meaningful projection direction in pathology WSIs domain.

422   6   C ONCLUSION
423
424
      In this work, we proposed Pg-GAT, a novel graph-based framework for WSI analysis that incorpo-
425
      rates spatial- and context-awareness with in-graph hierarchical aggregation, emulating the decision-
426   making process of pathologists. Pg-GAT captures node interactions using an initial Euclidean grid
427   graph as a structural prior and enhances contextual awareness within local neighborhoods through
428   graph attention. The differentiable projection-gated pooling mechanism enables the model to learn
429   data-adaptive decision boundaries, which is particularly important in handling imbalanced class dis-
430   tributions typical in the WSIs domain. We demonstrated the effectiveness of our approach on three
431   benchmark datasets using accuracy and AUC metrics, offering model interpretability with tumor
      localization, as well as its computational efficiency through model complexity analysis.

      We use PyTorch (2.2.2), NVIDIA RTXA5000. One GPU is used for each training. We intend to

555   A.2     DATASET
556
      CAMELYON16 (Bejnordi et al., 2017) and CAMELYON17 (Bandi et al., 2018) are breast can-
557
      cer lymph node metastasis dataset, both with lesion level annotation by pathologists. For CAME-
558   LYON17, there are also lesion sub-level labels:
559
560           • macro: Metastases greater than 2.0 mm
561
              • micro: Metastases greater than 0.2 mm or more than 200 cells but smaller than 2.0 mm
562
563           • itc: Isolated tumor cells. Single tumour cells or a cluster of tumour cells less than 0.2 mm
564
                or fewer than 200 cells are not precisely a metastasis but are instead classified as single
                tumour cells or a cluster of tumour cells smaller than 0.2 mm or less than 200 cells
565
566   Classifying a whole slide as a tumor slide becomes more challenging when the tumor region is
567   confined to very small sub-level areas.
568
569
      TCGA-NSCLC lung cancer dataset, from The Cancer Genome Atlas Program, consists of two types
      lung cancer, lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) and lung squamous cell carcinoma (LUSC), but does
570
      not have tumor region annotation.
571
572
573   A.3     DATA S PLITS
574
      We perform 5-fold cross validation, 270 samples with 80% train and 20% validation split and 129
575
      test samples from official grand challenge on CAMELYON16. On CAMELYON17, we perform
576   4-fold cross validation, 506 samples with 70% train 15% validation and 15% test split with the same
577   sub-level lesion label distribution. On TCGA-NSCLC we perform 5-fold cross validation, 920 sam-
578   ples with 70% train 15% validation and 15% test split. All with standalone test set. CAMELYON
579   dataset is with experts annotations of tumor regions, thus is used for downstream ROI investigation.
580
581   A.3.1    WSI PREPROCESSING FEATURE EXTRACTION
582
583   We adopt the standard WSIs preprocessing (Lu et al., 2021), segmenting the tissue region with Otsu
584
      thresholding, then dividing the remaining images into none-overlapping 224 × 224 patches. To
      minimize computational overhead and take advantage of the rich feature representations acquired
585
      from prior training, we utilize UNI (Chen et al., 2024) pathology foundation model, which utilizes
586
      self-supervised learning DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023) for pathology slide feature learning, and is not
587   trained on public dataset CAMElYON16, CAMELYON17 and TCGA, thus there is no data leakage
588   risk in our evaluation. Same feature encoder is applied to all experiments.
