009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal learning plays a crucial role in enabling machine learning models to
012            fuse and utilize diverse data sources, such as text, images, and audio, to support a
013            variety of downstream tasks. A unified representation across various modalities is
014            particularly important for improving efficiency and performance. Recent binding
015            methods, such as ImageBind (Girdhar et al., 2023), typically use a fixed anchor
016            modality to align multimodal data in the anchor modal embedding space. In this
017            paper, we mathematically analyze the fixed anchor binding methods and uncover
018
               notable limitations: (1) over-reliance on the choice of the anchor modality, (2)
               failure to capture intra-modal information, and (3) failure to account for inter-
019
               modal correlation among non-anchored modalities. To address these limitations,
020
               we propose CentroBind, a simple yet powerful approach that eliminates the need
021            for a fixed anchor; instead, it employs adaptively adjustable centroid-based anchors
022            generated from all available modalities, resulting in a balanced and rich representa-
023            tion space. We theoretically demonstrate that our method captures three crucial
024            properties of multimodal learning: intra-modal learning, inter-modal learning,
025            and multimodal alignment, while also constructing a robust unified representation
026            across all modalities. Our experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets
027            demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method, showing that adaptive anchor
028            methods outperform all fixed anchor binding methods as the former captures more
029
               nuanced multimodal interactions.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Multimodal alignment is defined as identifying and exploiting relationships and correspondences
034
      between multiple modalities (e.g., text, image, audio) viewing common phenomena to establish
035
      meaningful connections between their representations (Baltrušaitis et al., 2018). This process allows
036   machine learning models to analyze heterogeneous data holistically, facilitating comprehensive
037   decision-making. A common approach is learning a shared embedding space (Tu et al., 2022; Girdhar
038   et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2024b; Zhu et al., 2024), which aims to project data from multiple modalities
039   into a common embedding space by clustering similar items together for direct comparison and
040   linkage. This approach leverages well-trained single-modal embeddings, aligning them with auxiliary
041   objective functions like contrastive loss (Oord et al., 2018) or triplet loss (Wang et al., 2020b) to
042   minimize distances between similar items and maximize distances between dissimilar ones across
043   modalities.
044   Instead of training separate models for each modality, ImageBind (Girdhar et al., 2023) pairs images
045   with other modalities and projects them into a common image embedding space. Similarly, Zhu
046   et al. (2024) shows that pairing texts with other modalities (LanguageBind) improves retrieval
047   task performance when language is specified as the anchor modality. This approach has inspired
048   various “-Bind” methods tailored to align different modalities for specific domains, such as molecular
049   modeling (Xiao et al., 2024), medical imaging (Gao et al., 2024), brain signals (Yang et al., 2024b),
050
      and music selection for videos (Teng et al., 2024). These models commonly use image or text
      as the anchor embedding due to the abundance of data, with other modalities projected into this
051
      anchor representation.
052
053   We define the aforementioned approaches as Fixed-Anchor-Bind (FAB IND) methods, where the
      embedding space of the primary anchor modality remains fixed during the alignment process. Many

082
083   Issues with fixed anchor binding. First, selecting which modality to serve as an anchor is crucial
084   but challenging, as it depends on both embedding quality and task suitability. Common choices like
085
      images or text can be suboptimal, especially when no single modality has the dominant information
      about the source. Second, fixing an anchor can result in loss of semantic information that is well
086
      represented only in other modalities. For instance, while text may describe ‘a dog barks loudly,’ sound
087
      could reveal mood, and an image could add facial expression, a holistic combination that a fixed
088   alignment might miss. Third, optimizing only for anchor-to-other-modality overlooks information
089   complementarity between non-anchored modalities. These issues are the primary motivation for
090   the proposed C ENTRO B IND alternative to fixed modality anchoring methods. We formally analyze
091   deficiencies of FAB IND approaches in Section 2.
092
093   Adaptive anchor alignment. We propose an alternative to fixed anchor alignment by replacing
094   fixed anchors with “adaptive” anchors computed from paired samples. Our proposed method,
095   C ENTRO B IND, described in Section 3, removes the need for selecting a fixed anchor modality,
096   instead calculates the centroid over the aggregate of all modality’s representations and generates an
097
      multimodal anchor representation, as shown in Figure 1a.1 Encoders are then trained to minimize
      the ensemble of InfoNCE loss (Oord et al., 2018) between the representation of this adaptive anchor
098
      and the fixed modality representations. The main intuition is that a desirable anchor should be
099
      representative of all modalities, capturing the most comprehensive information, with well-trained
100   encoders producing representations that naturally cluster around this shared centroid, reflecting their
101   underlying semantic alignment.
102

      nents of multimodal learning: 1) capturing intra-modal mutual information, 2) capturing inter-modal

      mutual information, and 3) performing multimodal alignment by maximizing embedding similar-
105
      ity measures. By incorporating these elements, C ENTRO B IND outperforms other multimodality

174   2.2      B INDING REPRESENTATION SPACES
175
176   In addition to the objective of capturing intra and shared information, multimodal learning often takes
177   into account multimodal alignment (Radford et al., 2021; Duan et al., 2022). Without multimodal
178
      alignment, each modality can only access its own embedding structure depending on its encoder.
      For example, embeddings of cat and dog images, respectively, locate around (1, 0) and (0, 2) in
179
      R2 , whereas embeddings of cat and dog text can lie around (0, 2) and (1, 0). Such a misalignment
180
      can happen even for sufficient encoders (Definition 1), since the mutual information is invariant to
181   one-to-one mappings (Polyanskiy & Wu, 2024).
182
183   To align multimodal embedding spaces, a unified representation space (Radford et al., 2021; Zhou
184
      et al., 2023) or multimodal alignment (Wang et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2024c) have been proposed
      for multimodal representation learning, in which embeddings of multimodal features having similar
185
      semantic should near each other in embedding space. Several FAB IND methods have been proposed
186
      (see Appendix A for a summary of FAB IND methods) that include ImageBind (Girdhar et al., 2023).
187   ImageBind sets the image modality as the fixed anchor modality, and then InfoNCE loss is minimized
188   between the embeddings of the anchor modality and the other modalities. FAB IND (e.g., ImageBind)
189   aims to find encoders fiFB for all modalities, except the anchor modality, such that

231   does not guarantee shared information between non-anchored modalities I(fi (Xi ); fl (Xl )), i, l ̸= 1,

234
235   The above Propositions reveals several limitations in FAB IND. Firstly, achieving maximum shared
236   information requires sufficient anchor representation, which depends on having both an informative
237   modality and a sufficient encoder. Without these conditions, FAB IND may not effectively capture
238
      shared information. Secondly, even with sufficient anchor representation, FAB IND may not provide
      encoders with maximum intra information. This is because its objective function (4) does not take
239
      into account intra information. Thirdly, the objective function of FAB IND (4) focuses solely on
240
      learning shared information between pairs of anchor and non-anchored modalities, while disregarding
241   shared information among non-anchor modalities. This implies that FAB IND may not capture shared
242   information among non-anchored modalities. This limitation renders FAB IND less effective when
243   crucial shared information exists among non-anchored modalities. Lastly, the representation produced
244   by FAB IND may not approximate an ideal representation, such as the “Platonic representation”
245   of (Huh et al., 2024). While integrating all modalities is needed in order to effectively represent
246   the information in all modalities, FAB IND falls short of this ideal. In the sequel, we introduce an
247   alternative multimodal representation that, fully leverages fine-grained, sample-level information in
248   all modalities.
249   In summary, FAB IND exhibits the following weaknesses:
250
251       P1: over-reliance on a single anchor modality;
252
          P2: failure to capture intra information;
253
254       P3: absence of shared information among non-anchored modalities;
255
256   Next, we propose our method C ENTRO B IND, which does not require selecting a single anchor
257   modality and is thus capable of capturing intra and shared information.

      3   T OWARD A DESIRABLE UNIFIED REPRESENTATION SPACE
260
261
      The main intuition deriving C ENTRO B IND is as follows: 1) A desirable multimodal embedding
262
      should not favor any specific modality; 2) A desirable unified embedding should attain the highest
263
      alignment in similarity. To this end, we generate an anchor representation that is the centroid of
264   the multiple modality representations. Then, we train the encoders toward minimizing the InfoNCE
265   loss between anchor and other modalities, similarly to FAB IND. We note that other dynamic
266   anchors, such as median and weighted average (refer to Appendix C.1 for further discussion), are
267   also possible alternatives to the centroid. However, we focus on C ENTRO B IND, as the centroid
268   represents the geometric center, aligning with the objective in achieving multimodal alignment in the
269   embedding space (e.g., Rdz ). Next, we formally defind C ENTRO B IND, and show that the method
      aligns multimodal representations and simultaneously maximizes intra and shared information.

      Synthetic datasets. We employ a latent variable model (Bishop & Nasrabadi, 2006) for generating
      synthetic multimodal datasets. A latent variable model is a statistical model for data X ∈ Rdx , under
424
      which X is generated according to a conditional probability distribution PX|Z , where Z ∈ Rdz is the
425
      latent variable. In terms of the representation learning framework, Z can be seen as a low dimensional
426   representation of X. We assume that the class label Y ∈ [K] and the latent variable Z are jointly
427   distributed according to PZ,Y . In our setting, we exploit Gaussian mixture model (GMM) (Bishop &
428   Nasrabadi, 2006) for the latent variable Z, and we generate M modalities Xi = gi (Z) + N, i ∈ [M ]
429   with random noise N and some non-linear projections gi : Rdz → Rdx . We choose the projections in
430   a way such that each model can be ranked in ascending order, i.e., X1 is the worst, and X4 is the best
431   modality in terms of their inherent correlation with the latent variable. The class label Y is set to the
      component id of GMM (for details, see Appendix C.1).

531   5    C ONCLUSIONS
532
533
      In this paper, we analyze the limitations of fixed-anchor-bind methods (FAB IND), including over-
534   reliance on the choice of anchor modality, and failing to capture both intra and shared information
535   among non-anchored modalities. To overcome such shortcomings, we propose C ENTRO B IND, which
536   aligns multimodal embeddings to adaptive anchors constructed by centroids of the embeddings, hence
537   removing the need for anchor modality. Moreover, we theoretically study C ENTRO B IND, showing
538   that it captures intra- and shared information. Extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world
539   datasets show that C ENTRO B IND significantly outperforms FAB IND, providing a robust unified
      representation space and validating our analysis on C ENTRO B IND and FAB IND.

      A     R ELATED WORK
811
812
813   A.1   M ULTIMODAL LEARNING
814
815
      Multimodal learning has gained significant attention in recent years due to its potential to enhance
      machine learning models by leveraging diverse data modalities, such as text, images, audio, and video.
816
      By combining these modalities, multimodal learning seeks to mimic human-like perception, thereby
817
      improving performance across a wide range of applications, from healthcare to natural language
818   processing. Common supervised multimodal learning tasks include audio-visual classification (Peng
819   et al., 2022; Feichtenhofer et al., 2019; Zhu & Rahtu, 2022), visual question answering (Antol et al.,
820   2015; Guo et al., 2021), and vision-language tasks (Xu et al., 2015; Radford et al., 2021), as well as
821   more complex vision-audio-language tasks (Aytar et al., 2017; Harwath et al., 2018).
822
      Typically, these models integrate unimodal features extracted by modality-specific encoders (Seichter
823
      et al., 2021; Nagrani et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2020a; Peng et al., 2022). For
824   instance, Madaan et al. (2024) introduce inter- and intra-modality modeling frameworks that treat
825   the target as a composition of multiple modalities. Similarly, Du et al. (2023) propose a late-fusion
826   approach for supervised multimodal tasks, demonstrating that insufficient feature extraction from
827   individual modalities negatively affects the model’s generalization ability. Additionally, Zhang et al.
828   (2024) address joint optimization by alternating between unimodal learning scenarios and integrating
829   modality-specific encoders with a unified head shared across all modalities.
830
831
      A.2   M ULTIMODAL ALIGNMENT
832
833
      Multimodal learning addresses four key challenges (Liang et al., 2024c; Baltrušaitis et al., 2018; Liang
834
      et al., 2024d): managing interactions among redundant, unique, and synergistic features (Dumas
835   et al., 2017; Liang et al., 2024a;b), aligning fine-grained and coarse-grained information (Wang
836   et al., 2023; 2024a), reasoning across diverse features (Yang et al., 2023), and integrating external
837   knowledge (Shen et al., 2022; Lyu et al., 2024). Among these challenges, multimodal alignment is
838   one of the core challenges that many researchers aim to solve.
839
      A common method in multimodal alignment is using cross-modal alignment by using attention
840
      mechanisms between pairwise modalities, such as vision-language (Tan & Bansal, 2019) and vision-
841   language-audio (Tsai et al., 2019). Another effective approach is leveraging graph neural networks
842   to align multimodal datasets (Yang et al., 2021; Wilf et al., 2023). For instance, Yang et al. (2021)
843   transforms unaligned multimodal sequence data into nodes, with edges capturing interactions across
844   modalities over time. Wilf et al. (2023) build graph structures for each modality—visual, textual, and
845   acoustic—and create edges to represent their interactions.
846
      To enhance the generalizability of cross-modal representations, Xia et al. (2024) employ a unified
847   codebook approach, facilitating a joint embedding space for visual and audio modalities. Another
848   prominent method (Radford et al., 2021) achieves cross-modal alignment by leveraging large collec-
849   tions of image-text pairs, making it a widely adopted strategy in multimodal learning (Zhang et al.,
850   2022; Guzhov et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023).
851
852
853
      A.3   B INDING METHODS
854
855
      Recent studies have focused on aligning multimodal datasets by leveraging binding properties
      in various modalities. ImageBind (Girdhar et al., 2023) aligns multimodal data by using image
856
      representation as the anchor and aligning each modality embedding with the image embedding.
857
      Similarly, LanguageBind (Zhu et al., 2024) uses language representation as the anchor, aligning other
858   modalities into the language space. PointBind (Guo et al., 2023) learns a joint embedding space
859   across 3d point, language, image, and audio modalities by designating the point space as the central
860   representation. Thanks to the efficacy of such a binding idea with a fixed anchor, several “-Bind”
861   approaches have been studied in numerous domains (Teng et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2024; Gao et al.,
862   2024; Yang et al., 2024b; Balemans et al., 2024; Dhakal et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024a) While these
863   methods demonstrate strong performance in zero-shot cross-modality retrieval and classification
      tasks, they are constrained by their reliance on an existing single anchor modality.


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Several approaches have integrated additional knowledge into multimodal representation spaces to
865   address this limitation. Freebind (Wang et al., 2024a) introduces bi-modality spaces to enhance a
866   pretrained image-paired unified space. It generates pseudo-embedding pairs across diverse modality
867   pairs and aligns them with the pre-trained unified space using contrastive learning. Omnibind (Wang
868   et al., 2024b) leverages multiple pretrained multimodal models to construct pseudo item-pair retrievals
869   based on top-1 recall across various modality combinations using pairwise cross-modal alignment.
870   Both methods show promising results in cross-modal retrieval by incorporating extra spaces into
871   existing pairwise binding spaces. However, they still rely on fixed (pre-trained) representation spaces.
872   Unibind (Lyu et al., 2024) highlights the imbalanced representation when using image-centered
873   representation spaces. To address this, Unibind employs large language models (LLMs) to create a
874   unified and balanced representation space. It constructs a knowledge base with multimodal category
875   descriptions, establishes LLM-augmented class-wise embedding centers, and aligns other modalities
876   to these centers through contrastive learning. This approach attempts to balance representations
877   across modalities but still depends heavily on large-scale pretrained LLMs and centers alignment
878   around a single unified space, namely, text (language).
879   ViT-Lens (Lei et al., 2024) builds upon the Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) and
880   multimodal foundational models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to align multiple modalities. It
881   extends ViT by incorporating an additional embedding layer and attention layer for each modality,
882   which are trained via contrastive learning involving embeddings generated by the CLIP and the
883   ViT models. This approach generalizes FAB IND by allowing more than one fixed anchor modality;
884   specifically, image and text in this case. C ENTRO B IND could also adopt a similar strategy, leveraging
885
      the powerful ViT model for modality alignment while adaptively computing anchors based on their
      centroids.

998    C.1    E XPERIMENTS WITH SYNTHETIC DATASETS
999
1000   Synthetic datasets. We employ a latent variable model (Bishop & Nasrabadi, 2006) for generating
1001   synthetic multimodal datasets. A latent variable model is a statistical model for data X ∈ Rdx ,
1002   under which X is generated according to a conditional probability distribution PX|Z , where Z ∈ Rdz
1003
       is the latent variable. In terms of the representation learning framework, Z can be seen as a true
       representation of X. Moreover, we assume that the class label Y ∈ [K] and the latent variable Z are
1004
       jointly distributed according to PZ,Y .
1005
1006   For the marginal distribution of Z, we make use of a Gaussian mixture model (GMM) (Bishop
1007   & Nasrabadi, 2006), and hence the probability density function (PDF) of Z is a weighted sum of
1008   Gaussian densities. In particular, the PDF of Z is defined as follows:
1009                                                      K
                                                          Y
1010                                          pZ (z) =          πy N (z; µy , Σy ),                                                (26)
1011                                                      y=1
1012
       where K is the number of mixture components, πy = Pr(Y = y) is the component prior probability,
1013
       and N (z; µy , Σy ) denotes Gaussian PDF with mean µy ∈ Rdz and covariance matrix Σy ∈ Rdz ×dz .
1014
       This leads to the conditional PDF of Z as pZ|Y (z|y) = N (z; µy , Σy ).
1015
1016   Once a latent variable z is generated from GMM in (26), we generate data samples

1197   Since the median is more robust to outliers than the average (Lopuhaä & Rousseeuw, 1991), we
1198
       additionally evaluate the case of a median-based dynamic anchor. In high-dimensional spaces, rather
       than in the univariate case, a coordinate-wise median can be used as a naive generalization of the
1199
       univariate median to the multivariate setting, preserving its robustness to outliers. We assess the
1200
       dynamic anchor binding method using the coordinate-wise median approach (denoted as MB in
1201   Figure 9). Specifically, for the median anchor, we compute the jth coordinate of the ith anchor as
1202   ai,j = Median(z 1,i,j , z 2,i,j , · · · , z M,i,j ), where z m,i,j denotes the jth coordinate of the embedding
1203   for the ith sample in modality m. For improved readability, we summarize the final accuracies for
1204   each method and modality in Table 4.
1205
       This scenario, where modal distributions are uneven, is commonly referred to as the modality
1206
       imbalance problem (Du et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024). Intuitively, in the presence
1207   of modality imbalance, the centroid may produce suboptimal dynamic anchor constructions, and
1208   other methods, such as weighted average, might yield better results. Nevertheless, C ENTRO B IND
1209   consistently performs better or comparably to weighted average methods, demonstrating its robustness
1210   to the modality imbalance problem.
1211
       From these experiments, we conjecture that the specific dynamic anchor generation method may not
1212
       significantly impact final performance, provided that all encoders are well-trained during the process.
1213
1214   Addressing the modality imbalance problem typically requires additional information, such as domain
1215   knowledge, labels, or downstream task insights. Since this work focuses on multimodal alignment
1216
       under contrastive learning, we do not assume such information is available. We therefore leave the
       exploration of the modality imbalance problem for dynamic anchor generation as a direction for
1217
       future work.
