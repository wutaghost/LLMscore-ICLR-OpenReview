      Mini-batch methods are among the most successful tools for handling huge datasets for machine
      learning. Notable examples include Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) and mini-batch k-means
030
      (Sculley, 2010). Mini-batch k-means is one of the most popular clustering algorithms used in prac-
031
      tice (Pedregosa et al., 2011).
032
033   While k-means is widely used due to itвҖҷs simplicity and fast running time, it requires the data to be
034   linearly separable to achieve meaningful clustering. Unfortunately, many real-world datasets do not
035
      have this property. One way to overcome this problem is to project the data into a high, even infinite,
      dimensional space (where it is hopefully linearly separable) and run k-means on the projected data
036
      using the вҖңkernel-trickвҖқ.
037
038   Kernel k-means achieves significantly better clustering compared to k-means in practice. However,
039   its running time is considerably slower. Surprisingly, prior to our work there was no attempt to speed
040   up kernel k-means using a mini-batch approach.

      LloydвҖҷs algorithm The most popular algorithm for (non kernel) k-means is LloydвҖҷs algorithm,
055   often referred to as the k-means algorithm (Lloyd, 1982). It works by randomly initializing a set of
056   k centers and performing the following two steps: (1) Assign every point in X to the center closest
057   to it. (2) Update every center to be the mean of the points assigned to it. The algorithm terminates
058   when no point is reassigned to a new center. This algorithm is extremely fast in practice but has a
059   worst-case exponential running time (Arthur & Vassilvitskii, 2006; Vattani, 2011).
060
      Mini-batch k-means To update the centers, LloydвҖҷs algorithm must go over the entire input at
061   every iteration. This can be computationally expensive when the input data is extremely large. To
062   tackle this, the mini-batch k-means method was introduced by Sculley (2010). It is similar to LloydвҖҷs
063   algorithm except that steps (1) and (2) are performed on a batch of b elements sampled uniformly
064   at random with repetitions, and in step (2) the centers are updated slightly differently. Specifically,
065   every center is updated to be the weighted average of its current value and the mean of the points (in
066   the batch) assigned to it. The parameter by which we weigh these values is called the learning rate,
067   and its value differs between centers and iterations. The larger the learning rate, the more a center
068   will drift towards the new batch cluster mean.

      In Section 5 we go on to provide theoretical guarantees for our algorithm. This is somewhat tricky
092   for mini-batch algorithms due to their stochastic nature, as they may not even converge to a local-
093   minima. To overcome this hurdle, we take the approach of Schwartzman (2023) and answer the
094   question: how long does it take truncated mini-batch kernel k-means to terminate with an early
095   stopping condition. Specifically, we terminate the algorithm when the improvement on the batch
096   drops below some user provided parameter, Пө. Early stopping conditions are very common in prac-
097   tice (e.g., sklearn (Pedregosa et al., 2011)).
098   We show that applying the k-means++ initialization scheme (Arthur & Vassilvitskii, 2007) for our
099   initial centers implies we achieve the same approximation ratio, O(log k) in expectation, as the
100   full-batch algorithm.

      Until recently, mini-batch k-means was only considered with a learning rate going to 0 over time.
      This was true both in theory (Tang & Monteleoni, 2017; Sculley, 2010) and practice (Pedregosa
139
      et al., 2011). Recently, (Schwartzman, 2023) proposed a new learning which does not go to 0 over
140                                                         2 4
      time, and showed that if the batch is of size в„Ұ(k(d/Пө)
                                                    e        ) , mini-batch k-means must terminate within
141
      O(d/Пө) iterations with high probability, where d is the dimension of the input, and Пө is a threshold
142
      parameter for termination.
143
144   A popular approach to deal with the slow running time of kernel k-means is constructing a coreset of
145   the data. A coreset for kernel k-means is a weighted subset of X with the guarantee that the solution
146
      quality on the coreset is close to that on the entire dataset up to a (1 + Пө) multiplicative factor. There
      has been a long line of work on coresets for k-means an kernel k-means (Schmidt, 2014; Feldman
147
      et al., 2020; Barger & Feldman, 2020), and the current state-of-the-art for kernel k-means is due to
148
      (Jiang et al., 2021). They present a coreset algorithm with a nearly linear (in n and k) construction
149   time which outputs a coreset of size poly(kПөвҲ’1 ).
150
151   In (Chitta et al., 2011) the authors only compute the kernel matrix for uniformly sampled set of m
152
      points from X. Then they optimize a variant of kernel k-means where the centers are constrained to
      be linear combinations of the sampled points. The authors do no provide worst case guarantees for
153
      the running time or approximation of their algorithm.
154
155   Another approach to speed up kernel k-means is by computing an approximation for the kernel
156   matrix. This can be done by computing a low dimensional approximation for П• (without computing
157   П• explicitly)(Rahimi & Recht, 2007; Chitta et al., 2012; Chen & Phillips, 2017), or by computing a
158
      low rank approximation for the kernel matrix (Musco & Musco, 2017; Wang et al., 2019).
