009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement learning agents often struggle with sample inefficiency, requiring
012            extensive interactions with the environment to develop effective policies. This
013            inefficiency is partly due to the challenge of balancing exploration and exploitation
014            without the abstract reasoning and prior knowledge that humans use to quickly
015            identify rewarding actions. Recent advancements in foundation models, such as
016            large language models (LLMs) and vision-language models (VLMs), have shown
017            human-level reasoning capabilities in some domains but have been underutilized in
018
               directly selecting low-level actions for exploration in reinforcement learning. In this
               paper, we investigate the potential of foundation models to enhance exploration in
019
               reinforcement learning tasks. We conduct an in-depth analysis of their exploration
020
               behaviour in multi-armed bandit problems and Gridworld environments, comparing
021            their performance against traditional exploration strategies and reinforcement
022            learning agents. Our empirical results suggest foundation models can significantly
023            improve exploration efficiency by leveraging their reasoning abilities to infer
024            optimal actions. Building on these findings, we introduce Foundation Model
025            Exploration (FME), a novel exploration scheme that integrates foundation models
026            into the reinforcement learning framework for intelligent exploration behaviour.
027            We use VLMs and demonstrate that they can infer environment dynamics and
028            objectives from raw image observations. This means FME only requires the action
029
               space as environment-specific manual text input. We find that agents equipped with
               FME achieve superior performance in sparse reward Gridworld environments and
030
               scale to more complex tasks like Atari games. Moreover, the effectiveness of FME
031
               increases with the capacity of the VLM used, indicating that future advancements
032            in foundation models will further enhance such exploration strategies.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Reinforcement learning enables agents to learn optimal policies through interactions with an envi-
038   ronment by observing states, taking actions, and receiving rewards. The goal is to find a policy that
039   maximizes the expected cumulative rewards. Despite its broad applicability in fields like healthcare,
040   robotics, logistics, finance, and advertising, reinforcement learning often suffers from sample ineffi-
041   ciency, requiring vast amounts of data and numerous trial-and-error iterations to develop effective
042   policies. A crucial factor of being sample efficient is balancing exploration, the act of seeking new
043   knowledge about potential rewards, with exploitation, the use of known rewarding actions.
044   Scalable exploration strategies in reinforcement learning typically introduce random actions or rely
045   on heuristics. While these methods are general and widely applicable, they can incur redundant
046   environment interactions, especially in cases where the path to rewards would be apparent to a
047   human observer. For instance, in environments where visual cues clearly indicate the goal, such as
048   navigating a maze with visible exits, traditional methods may waste time exploring irrelevant paths.
049   This inefficiency stems from the absence of abstract reasoning and prior knowledge in conventional
050
      reinforcement learning algorithms, limiting their effectiveness in complex environments.
051   Recent advances in foundation models, particularly large language models (LLMs) and vision-
052   language models (VLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in retaining vast amounts of
053   knowledge and exhibiting human-level reasoning when trained on massive datasets of text and
      images (Brown et al., 2020; Team et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Jiang et al.,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2024; Team et al., 2024). Prior works have explored leveraging foundation models in reinforcement
055   learning, often focusing on text-based environments or using them as auxiliary components for tasks
056   like reward shaping and goal generation (Klissarov et al., 2024; Du et al., 2023). When used for
057   decision-making, foundation models are frequently equipped with hand-crafted abstract skills to
058   enable autonomous action (Wang et al., 2024b). However, these approaches often involve significant
059   prompt engineering and impose constraints that limit the agents’ capabilities and generalization
060   potential through reliance on text-based interfaces or predefined skill sets. The lack of involvement
061   of foundation models in low-level decision-making means that their fundamental capacity to address
062   traditional reinforcement learning exploration challenges is currently unexplored.
063   In this paper, we aim to fill this gap by conducting a fundamental study of the potential of foundation
064   models in traditional reinforcement learning settings. We analyse foundation models’ exploration
065   behaviour in multi-armed bandit settings and compare their performance to traditional exploration
066   strategies. Our findings reveal that these models can outperform well-established exploration algo-
067   rithms in certain settings. We extend this analysis to Gridworld environments, where we observe
068   efficient navigation behaviour of foundation models in fully observable deterministic settings but sub-
069
      optimal performance when stochasticity and partial observability are introduced. These experiments
      highlight the complementary strengths and weaknesses of reinforcement learning algorithms and
070
      foundation models in decision-making, underlining the need for a hybrid approach.
071
072   To this end, we propose FME (Foundation Model Exploration), a multi-step adaptable exploration
073   scheme that integrates foundation models with reinforcement learning agents by letting the foundation
074   model control the agent’s behavior for certain periods of time. We demonstrate that, by using
075
      VLMs, we can equip an arbitrary reinforcement learning agent with FME without extensive prompt
      engineering or having to provide explicit environment descriptions. Simply by specifying the action
076
      space in natural language, the VLM can use its prior knowledge and reasoning to understand the
077
      environment’s dynamics and objectives from visual inputs, enabling it to produce effective sequences
078   of actions that assist the agent in accumulating rewards.
079
080   We evaluate and analyze FME in various environments, including sparse-reward Gridworlds and
081
      complex domains like Atari games. Our experiments demonstrate that FME can help agents explore
      significantly more efficiently in accumulating rewards. We also find that the performance gains
082
      increase as larger foundation models are used, suggesting that the approach benefits from the
083
      increased capacity. Lastly, using FME, researchers can provide manual priors in natural language to
084   the agent, such as hints of achieving the environment’s objective.
085
086   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
087
             • We conduct the first fundamental study of the capacity of foundation models in traditional
088            reinforcement learning exploration challenges, providing insights into their strengths and
089            limitations in such settings.
090
             • We propose FME, the first method to utilize VLMs for enhancing the exploration efficiency
091
               of reinforcement learning agents through a temporally extended and adaptable exploration
092            scheme that requires minimal prompt engineering.
093
             • We empirically demonstrate the effectiveness of FME in improving exploration efficiency in
094
               Minigrid and Atari games.

102   2   R ELATED WORKS
103
104   Foundation models as auxiliary components. In the context of reinforcement learning, LLMs
105   have been used to shape the behaviour of the agents by reward shaping and goal generation. Klissarov
106   et al. (2024) construct an intrinsic reward model that encodes the preference of an LLM between two
107   observations in a text-based environment. Ma et al. (2024a) use foundation models to write high-
      and low-level code for robotic control movements to help with exploration in continuous control

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      domains. Triantafyllidis et al. (2023) use LLMs to rate actions in robotic manipulation tasks and use
109   these ratings as intrinsic rewards to enhance exploration. If the underlying code of the environment
110   is available, Ma et al. (2024b) demonstrates that an LLM can directly program a reward function
111   for a given task description. Du et al. (2023) utilize an LLM to generate plausible goals for guiding
112   the training of a goal-conditioned reinforcement learning agent. Similarly, Yang et al. (2023) train
113   a multi-modal reinforcement learning agent that learns to imitate an expert LLM policy defined
114   via PDDL. Rocamonde et al. (2024) use a VLM as zero-shot reward models for robotic control
115   environments. Zhang et al. (2024) use LLMs for task-selection in open-ended learning settings.
116   Finally, Xie et al. (2024) prompts LLMs to generate reward codes that are used to shape rewards for
117
      continuous control reinforcement learning agents. However, these approaches primarily leverage
      foundation models as auxiliary tools to influence agent behaviour indirectly through reward shaping
118
      or goal generation, rather than integrating them directly into the agent’s action selection process. In
119
      contrast, our work directly incorporates foundation models into the agent’s decision-making process,
120   allowing them to select low-level actions to enhance exploration efficiency.
121
122
      Foundation models for decision-making. The reasoning abilities of large language models (LLMs)
123
      have been leveraged to produce high-level plans, which are executed through low-level skills to
124   interact with the environment (Wang et al., 2024b; Lin et al., 2023). These low-level skills can be
125   manually implemented behaviours (Zhu et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2022a), pre-trained
126   machine learning models (Wang et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2022b; Song et al., 2023), or accessed via
127   programming APIs (Wang et al., 2024a; Liang et al., 2022). While utilizing these skills reduces the
128   computational cost of querying LLMs, the constraints they introduce can limit the agent’s capabilities
129   and generalization (Song et al., 2023). While these methods demonstrate the potential of foundation
130   models in decision-making, they often rely on hand-crafted low-level skills or predefined behaviours
131   to interact with the environment. In contrast, our approach differs by introducing a hybrid approach
132
      that eliminates the need for predefined skills or extensive prompt engineering by directly utilizing
      VLMs to infer environment dynamics and objectives from raw visual inputs.

135   3     E XPLORATION S TUDY
136
137   In this section, we perform an in-depth analysis of the exploration behaviour of foundation models
138   in traditional reinforcement learning settings. Our goal is to understand their fundamental capacity
139   for handling the exploration-exploitation trade-off and to identify their strengths and limitations
140   compared to traditional reinforcement learning algorithms. To achieve this, we choose to study two
141   well-established environments: multi-armed bandit problems and Gridworld environments. The
142   multi-armed bandit problem (used in Section 3.1) represents one of the most fundamental and widely
143   studied paradigms for evaluating exploration strategies in reinforcement learning. This framework
144
      provides a simplified setting that focuses solely on the exploration-exploitation trade-off, making it
      ideal for analyzing the exploratory decision-making capabilities of foundation models in a controlled
145
      environment. Gridworld environments (used in Section 3.2), on the other hand, introduce spatial
146
      navigation and state transitions, offering a more complex setting to examine the decision-making and
147   exploration abilities of foundation models.

330   3.3   C ONCLUSION
331
332   The results observed in Section 3.1 suggest that foundation models’ vast prior knowledge and reason-
333   ing capabilities offer promising potential for balancing exploration and exploitation in traditional
334   reinforcement learning problems. Section 3.2 highlighted both approaches’ potentially comple-
335   mentary strengths and weaknesses. Reinforcement learning agents often explore inefficiently as
336   they cannot perform high-level reasoning but can learn complex optimal policies with long-term
337
      dependencies. Foundation models struggle to produce such optimal policies and, when using LLMs,
      are limited to text-based environments that require effortful prompt engineering. However, they can
338
      provide zero-shot efficient exploration behaviour using their prior knowledge and reasoning abilities.
339
      These observations highlight the need for a hybrid approach that utilizes the complementary strengths
340   of both methodologies.

397
      Environment description. To evaluate and study the efficacy of FME, we first analyze its perfor-
398
      mance in Minigrid (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2023). In Minigrid environments, the objective typically
399
      is for the agent to navigate towards a reward location within a sparse reward gird world. We consider
400   two instances that allow an in-depth analysis of the exploration behaviour of agent. The first instance
401   is MiniGrid-Empty-5×5, where the agent navigates to the reward location in an empty 5 × 5 grid.
402   The agent has access to an image of the environment and has access to the action space A = [turn
403   left, turn right, move forward]. In the second instance, there is a door between the agent and the
404   reward location, as well as a key that can be used to unlock the door. In this case, A = [turn left, turn
405   right, move forward, pickup, drop, toggle].
406
407   Experiments. We study the performance of a DQN agent equipped without FME, equipped with
408   FME powered by the VLM GPT-4o-mini, and equipped with FME powered by the VLM GPT-4o.
409   We make use of ϵ = 0.01, H = 10, and N = 10. For each DQN variant, we run the Empty instance
410   for 5000 timesteps and the DoorKey instance for 10000 timesteps for five different seeds. The
411   results presented in Figure 5 show that FME substantially affects the exploration behaviour, allowing
412
      significantly faster acquisition of positive rewards in both environments. Furthermore, we observe a
      significant gap in performance between the different VLM model sizes.
413
414
415   Analysis. As seen in Figure 5, the Minigrid environments are intuitively easy to solve from a human
416   perspective. Similarly, the foundation models were often able to infer the objective without any
417
      explicit text information and suggest effective actions. Note that although we use Gridworlds of the
      smallest size, FME’s advantage gap will likely increase as the environment size increases. However,
418
      we also observed that the models struggled with certain ambiguities. First, the actions turn left and
419
      turn right rotate the agent in the respective orientation from the agent’s perspective, but the VLMs
420   occasionally interpreted these orientations from the image perspective (i.e. the inverse). Furthermore,
421   in some cases, it was unclear to the VLMs which direction the agent was currently facing, as it would
422   occasionally reason that the triangle’s hypotenuse may indicate it. Finally, in the DoorKey instances,
423   it was not always evident to the model that the yellow block represented a door.

483   5   C ONCLUSION
484
485   In this paper, we explored the potential of foundation models, specifically LLMs and VLMs, to address
      the exploration challenges inherent in reinforcement learning. Through an in-depth analysis of their


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      behaviour in traditional bandit settings and Gridworld environments, we found that foundation models
487   exhibit promising capabilities for balancing exploration and exploitation. Notably, in certain multi-
488   armed bandit settings, GPT-4 demonstrated superior exploration efficiency compared to classical
489   methods like Thompson Sampling and UCB, highlighting the potential of LLMs in decision-making
490   tasks that require strategic exploration. However, our experiments in Gridworld settings revealed
491   limitations in the ability of foundation models to reach optimal policies in more complex and
492   stochastic environments. While LLMs could effectively navigate deterministic settings with known
493   objectives, they struggled in environments where the reward locations were randomized and not
494   directly observable. In contrast, the reinforcement learning agents were able to learn such complex
495
      optimal policies but would gather the required data inefficiently. These findings underscored the
      need for a hybrid approach that leverages the strengths of both foundation models and traditional
496
      reinforcement learning agents.
497
498   Building upon these insights, we proposed FME, an exploration approach that integrates foundation
499   models into the reinforcement learning framework by letting a foundation model control the agent’s
500   behavior for certain periods of time. We showed that by using VLMs, FME provides intelligent
501
      exploration guidance without requiring explicit environmental descriptions or extensive prompt
      engineering, relying solely on the action space as a variable input. Our empirical evaluations in sparse
502
      reward Minigrid environments demonstrated that an arbitrary reinforcement learning agent can use
503
      FME in addition to a conventional exploration policy to facilitate significantly better sample efficiency.
504   Moreover, we showed that FME scales effectively to more complex domains, such as Atari games,
505   where it enabled agents to discover rewards more efficiently in both sparse and dense reward settings.
506   An important observation from our experiments is that the performance of FME improves with the
507   capacity of the underlying VLM. Larger models tend to possess more extensive prior knowledge
508   and reasoning capabilities, which seems to translate to more effective exploration behavior. This
509   suggests that as foundation model research progresses and more powerful models become available,
510   the benefits of integrating foundation models into reinforcement learning for exploration purposes
511   will likely increase.
512   Despite these promising results, our work also highlights several limitations and avenues for future
513   research. One challenge is ensuring that the foundation models correctly interpret environmental
514   cues, especially in visually complex or ambiguous settings. Misinterpretations can lead to suboptimal
515   action choices and hinder the overall performance of the agent. Incorporating further mechanisms to
516   provide temporal context or disambiguate visual inputs may help address these issues. Additionally,
517   exploring alternative methods for integrating foundation models, such as different triggering mecha-
518
      nisms or more profound synergy between the model and the agent’s policy, could further enhance
      exploration efficiency. Potentially interesting directions would be letting the foundation model
519
      decide autonomously if it should take control based on its confidence level or using other uncertainty
520
      measures commonly used in reinforcement learning to trigger FME. Larger scale experiments to
521   evaluate FME, which will certainly be interesting, will also become more feasible as the cost of using
522   foundation models decreases.
523
524
      Lastly, although we did not explore providing explicit instructions or descriptions to the VLMs in
      this paper, it is worth considering that incorporating manual prior knowledge could further enhance
525
      the performance of foundation models in reinforcement learning tasks. Relying on such manual
526
      input reduces the generality of the solution and makes the agent dependent on specific instructions
527   tailored to particular environments, which is why we refrained from doing so in this paper. However,
528   by supplying additional information (which is extremely challenging with traditional reinforcement
529   learning), researchers might enable foundation models to interpret environmental cues and objectives
530   better, potentially overcoming some limitations observed in more complex settings in our experiments.
531   Future research could investigate effectively integrating such information without compromising the
532   agent’s generalization capabilities.
533   In conclusion, our study demonstrates that foundation models have significant potential to augment
534   exploration in reinforcement learning. By combining the high-level reasoning and prior knowledge
535   of VLMs with the learning capabilities of reinforcement learning agents, FME offers a promising
536   direction for developing more sample-efficient and intelligent exploration strategies. As foundation
537   models continue to evolve, their integration into reinforcement learning frameworks could play a
538   crucial role in overcoming the exploration-exploitation trade-off, paving the way for more efficient
539   and effective learning in complex environments.

      The methodologies proposed in this paper are primarily benign in isolation but, crucially, when
907   future LLMs and other foundation models are leveraged as (assisting) action selectors in sequential
908   decision-making problems for real-world applications, specific cautionary measures are necessary.
909   For instance, the use of these models in dynamic, real-time decision-making environments such as
910   autonomous driving would introduce significant ethical and societal challenges. To address these
911   risks, rigorous validation processes to ensure that models behave as intended in varied and unforeseen
912   circumstances should be used, similar to the extensive experimentation performed in this paper where
913   we pinpoint and investigate the errors made by these models.
