009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite their success, Transformers suffer from quadratic complexity in the se-
012            quence length, limiting their applicability to long-range dependency problems
013            and making them expensive to train and run. After many proposals to address
014            this issue, the Long Range Arena (LRA) was suggested as a benchmark to eval-
015            uate the performance of new models in long-range dependency modeling tasks.
016            The Transformer and its variants performed poorly on this benchmark, and a new
017            series of architectures such as State Space Models (SSMs) gained some traction,
018            greatly outperforming Transformers in the LRA. Recent work has shown that with
019
               a denoising pretraining phase, Transformers can achieve competitive results in the
               LRA with these new architectures. In this work, we discuss and explain the superi-
020
               ority of architectures such as MEGA and SSMs in the Long Range Arena, as well
021
               as the recent improvement in the results of Transformers, pointing to the positional
022            and local nature of the tasks. We show that while the LRA is a benchmark for
023            long-range dependency modeling, in reality most of the performance comes from
024            short-range dependencies. By using rotary embeddings and training techniques to
025            mitigate its data inefficiency, the Transformer is also able to reach state-of-the-art
026            performance without a separate pretraining phase. What is more, with the same
027            techniques, we are able to remove all restrictions from SSM convolutional ker-
028            nels and learn fully parameterized convolutions without decreasing performance,
029            suggesting that the design choices behind SSMs merely added inductive biases
030
               and learning efficiency for these particular tasks. Our insights indicate that LRA
               results should be interpreted with caution and call for a redesign of the benchmark.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al.) has revolutionized sequence modeling, and has been
037   the basis for many state-of-the-art breakthroughs in NLP, computer vision, and reinforcement learn-
038
      ing. Despite its success, it is not without limitations. Being reliant on the attention mechanism, the
      Transformer suffers from quadratic complexity in the input sequence length, which makes it difficult
039
      to scale to long sequences and expensive to train and run. Addressing this issue has become a hot
040
      topic in the deep learning community, and many variations of the Transformer have been proposed
041   to improve its scalability.
042
043   In this work, we focus our attention on the Long Range Arena benchmark (Tay et al.), which was
044
      designed to evaluate the ability of models to capture long-range dependencies. This benchmark
      encompasses tasks in the image, text, and mathematical domains, and includes sequences of up to
045
      16,384 elements. The Transformer architecture and its variants have been shown to perform poorly
046
      on these tasks, which has motivated the development of new architectures.
047
048   These new architectures, such as State Space Models (SSMs) (Gu et al., b), were synthesized to a
049   common underlying idea by Li et al.. They can all be reformulated as a long-convolution, with a
050
      kernel size matching the full sequence length. This kernel should be efficiently parameterized, that
      is, scale sublinearly with the input sequence length, and should include a time decay mechanism,
051
      reducing the weight of elements based on their distance to the current token.
052
053   Although these architectures greatly outperformed the Transformer on the Long Range Arena bench-
      mark, the success of the Transformer in real-world settings has not been replicated. This raises

072   suggests a bias towards locality, which is counterintuitive to modeling long-range dependencies.

      Recent work by Amos et al. has shown that, in fact, the Transformer can achieve competitive
074
      performance on the LRA benchmark with appropriate training strategies. In particular, they used
075   a pretraining phase with a denoising objective, using the same data as the downstream tasks. In
076   this work, we achieve similar results without the need for a separate pretraining phase, reducing
077   the computational burden and the risk of representation collapse during the fine-tuning stage. We
078   use different data augmentation strategies for the image and mathematical domains, and a denoising
079   objective in a multitask learning setting for the text domain. We show that with these strategies,
080   the Transformer can achieve competitive performance on the LRA benchmark. We hypothesize the
081   reasons for the necessity of these strategies in the case of the Transformer: very high-dimensional
082   and insufficient data, and a very expressive architecture with poor inductive biases for the tasks.
083   Amos et al. showed that when using their pretraining strategy, a vanilla diagonal linear RNN can
084   achieve state-of-the-art performance on the LRA benchmark. We further show that, using our strate-
085   gies, an unrestricted and fully parameterized long convolution can achieve similar results as well.
086   This suggests that restrictions to the kernel such as those synthesized by Li et al. are not necessary
087   when enriching the data with proper strategies, but were only a way to add inductive biases and learn
088   more efficiently in these particular tasks.
089   We discuss the reasons behind these results, arguing that the tasks are mainly positional and local.
090   We measure the importance of locality and short-range dependencies by training convolutional mod-
091   els of increasing receptive fields. As shown in table 1, with small kernels of size 61 we get close to
092   state-of-the-art results in all tasks. Particularly concerning is the case of text, where kernels of size
093   5 suffice. This heavily favors long-convolution-based architectures with time-decay mechanisms.
094   Rotary embeddings add similar biases to the Transformer, and our ablation study shows its critical
095
      importance in achieving state-of-the-art results in the LRA.
096   Our results call the validity of the LRA as a long-range dependency modeling benchmark into ques-
097   tion and indicate that it is important to analyze performance on the LRA with caution, taking into
098   account the inductive biases of the models and the nature of the tasks.
099   Our contributions can be summarized as follows, in order of importance:
100
101            • We discuss the positional and local nature of the tasks in the LRA benchmark, and provide
102              empirical evidence that one can reach close to state-of-the-art results with small convolu-
103              tions that bound the range of dependencies that can be modeled. This explains the great per-
104              formance of models such as MEGA or SSMs, and the poor performance of Transformers.
105              It also questions the validity of the LRA as a long-range dependency modeling benchmark.
106            • We show that the restrictions on the kernel synthesized by Li et al. are not necessary when
107              enriching the data or training process with proper strategies, but merely added inductive
                 biases and learning efficiency in these particular tasks.

121
122   The attention mechanism, paramount in the Transformer architecture, suffers from quadratic com-
123
      plexity in the sequence length. Indeed, given queries Q ∈ RL1 ×Dqk , keys K ∈ RL2 ×Dqk , and values
      V ∈ RL2 ×Dv , where L1 and L2 are the sequence lengths, and Dqk and Dv are the dimensions of
124
      the query/key and value vectors, respectively, the output of the attention module is given by
125
                                                                   !
126                                                         QK T
127                                       Y = softmax p              V.                               (1)
                                                             Dqk
128

      The attention matrix A = softmax QK T / Dqk is of size L1 × L2 , yielding a computation time
                                                   p
129
130   complexity of O(L1 L2 Dqk ) and a naive space complexity of O(L1 L2 ). In reality, we can avoid
131   quadratic memory complexity by recomputing the attention matrix during the backward pass (Dao
132   et al.).
133
      Reducing the cost of running the Transformer and allowing it to process long sequences is a hot
134
      topic in Deep Learning. From hardware usage optimizations such as the I/O optimizations in Flash
135   Attention (Dao et al.) or precision reductions and quantization (Gholami et al.), to architectural mod-
136   ifications like the Linear Transformer (Katharopoulos et al.), the Reformer (Kitaev et al.), blockwise
137   attention (Qiu et al.; Dai et al.) or sliding-window attention (Zaheer et al.), we have seen a large
138   body of work on this very topic.
139
140   2.2   L ONG R ANGE A RENA
141
142   To evaluate the performance and efficiency of the different architectural proposals for long-sequence
143   modeling, Tay et al. proposed a benchmark with tasks in the text, image, and math domains, called
144   the Long Range Arena. These tasks were designed to include long sequences, ranging from 1000 to
145
      16000 elements, while keeping the computational burden reasonably low. It includes six different
      datasets.
146
147
      ListOps This synthetic task involves calculating the result of nested mathematical operations in
148           parentheses, which form a tree-like structure. Both operands and results are always
149           integers between 0 and 9, and sequences can be as long as 2000 tokens between op-
150           erations and operands. Four different operations can appear: minimum (MIN), maxi-
151           mum (MAX), median (MED), and sum modulo 10 (SM). The following is a short example:
152           MIN [MAX [1, 2, 3] , 4, 5] = 3.
153   Text Classification The IMDB sentiment analysis task, involving the classification of movie re-
154           views as positive or negative. In this benchmark, proposals are required to tokenize the text
155           at the byte level, instead of the usual subword tokenization. They should also use sequence
156           lengths of 1000, 2000, 3000 or 4000 bytes.
157   Document Retrieval This task involves finding a similarity score between two documents. The
158           dataset is the ACL Anthology Network, where the model has to predict whether two papers
159           have a citation link. The documents are tokenized at the byte level as well, this time re-
160           quiring a fixed sequence length of 4000 bytes. Inter-token interaction between documents
161           is not allowed for this task, this is, each document needs to be summarised to a fixed-size
              vector representation and then compared.


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Image Classification The CIFAR-10 image classification task. Images are 32 × 32 pixels, and the
163          task is to classify them into one of ten classes: airplane, automobile, bird, cat, deer, dog,
164          frog, horse, ship, and truck. In this benchmark, images are given in grayscale, and they
165          must be encoded as a one-dimensional sequence of individual pixels, totaling 32 × 32 =
166          1024 elements. No information about the two-dimensional structure of the image can be
167          provided to the model.
168   Pathfinder A synthetic task that involves identifying whether two white points in a black back-
169           ground are connected by a path of dashed white lines. The images are 32 × 32, and the
170           encoding restrictions are the same as in the Image Classification task.
171   Pathfinder-X An extreme version of the previous task, with larger images of 128 × 128 = 16384
172           pixels.
173
174   The Transformer achieved very poor results in this benchmark, both in the vanilla version and in
175   the proposed variants to handle long sequences. In fact, results across variants were fairly similar in
176   most tasks. The largest differences were found in the ListOps task, and we offer an explanation in
177   Appendix B.

179   2.3     N EW ARCHITECTURES
180
181   Motivated by the poor performance of the Transformer in the Long Range Arena, several works
182
      have been published proposing new architectures that greatly improved their performance and com-
      putational efficiency. In this section, we review some of the most relevant.
183

      2.3.1    S TATE S PACE M ODELS
185
186   In 2022, Gu et al. (b) proposed a new architecture, S4, based on the discretization of a differential
187   equation model called the State Space Model (SSM). For a 1D input signal u(t), the differential
188   equation is the following:

189                                       x (t) = Ax(t) + Bu(t)

190                                       y(t) = Cx(t) + Du(t)

      where x is the hidden state, u is the input signal, and y is the output signal. The elements A, B, C,
192
      and D are the parameters of the model. The system is discretized with a fixed time step ∆, and the
193   D parameter is removed, as it can be seen as a skip connection. The discretized system is then:
194

196                                            yt = Cxt

200
      reduces expressiveness, as nonlinearities will only appear in token-wise operations. However, it also
      has great properties. In addition to the recurrent formulation that allows for very fast auto-regressive
201
      inference, the recurrence can be expanded to

      When the input signal is a vector, the parameters A, B, and C are matrices. In this case, the
208
      exponential of the matrix A is not straightforward, but the kernel can be efficiently computed by
209
      parameterizing the matrix A as the sum of a normal diagonal matrix minus a low-rank matrix. This
210   architecture pushed the state-of-the-art average accuracy in the Long Range Arena from 59.37% to
211   86.09%.
212
213   2.3.2    S YNTHESIZING SSM S
214
215   Posterior work has been published that synthesizes the key ideas that make the S4 model work.
      Orvieto et al. developed a similar linear recurrent model with a diagonal parameterization with


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      complex eigenvalues in the unit disk. They thus removed the discretization step or the normal
217   minus low-rank decomposition. Furthermore, independent processing of each dimension in the
218   diagonalized space allows us to process the convolution in O(L log L) by using the Fast Fourier
219   Transform, where L is the sequence length. They achieved performance comparable in the long
220   range arena to that of the S4 model.
221
      Note that S4 has a convolutional mode that uses a kernel of the same size as the input signal. Li et al.
222
      tried to generalize these architectures by creating a convolutional model directly and trying to pin
223   down the properties that these long convolutions should have to achieve good performance. They
224   found two sufficient conditions. First, the kernel should be parameterized in a way that makes the
225   effective number of parameters scale sublinearly with sequence length. Second, the kernel should
226   satisfy a decaying structure, that is, the weights assigned to distant tokens should be smaller in
227   magnitude than those assigned to closer tokens. They developed the SGConv architecture, which is
228   a simple convolutional model that satisfies these properties, and achieved comparable performance
229   in the Long Range Arena. The authors showed that the S4 model fulfilled these properties, and the
230   model developed by Orvieto et al. clearly does too, as the exponential of a matrix with complex
231
      eigenvalues of norm less than 1 will decay in magnitude, and the number of parameters is linear in
      the hidden dimension and independent of the sequence length.
232

      2.3.3    MEGA
235   At the time of writing, the current state of the art in the Long Range Arena is MEGA (Ma et al.),
236   which alternates between the attention mechanism and an exponential moving average (EMA) across
237   the sequence length. More formally, given input embeddings x1 , . . . , xL in RD , the output of the
238   EMA component is
239                                   yt = α ⊙ xt + (1 − α ⊙ δ) ⊙ yt−1 ,                               (4)
240   where δ ∈ (0, 1)D is a damping vector and α ∈ (0, 1)D is a component-wise decay factor. The
241   EMA operation is also a form of diagonal linear recurrence with eigenvalues of norm less than 1,
242   like the one in Orvieto et al.. This yields a convolutional kernel that meets the properties discussed
243   by Li et al.. The MEGA model achieved state-of-the-art performance in the Long Range Arena,
244   with an average accuracy of 88.21%.
245
246   2.4     I MPROVING THE T RANSFORMER IN THE L ONG R ANGE A RENA
247
248   Amos et al. managed to achieve close to state-of-the-art performance in the Long Range Arena with
249   the rotary Transformer by pretraining the models with a denoising objective, using the data from the
250   downstream task. Similar to the pretraining of BERT (Devlin et al.), the model is asked to predict
251
      randomly masked tokens in the sequence.
252   Using this pretraining procedure, they also achieved competitive performance with a simple diagonal
253   linear recurrent model, without the need for any complex parameterization or initialization as in the
254   S4 model or the model by Orvieto et al..
255
256   2.5     G MLP
257
258   Liu et al. tried to achieve BERT’s performance on standard text tasks using only MLP networks. To
259   do this, they developed the gMLP architecture, alternating between channel-wise and spatial-wise
260
      or sequence-wise projections. Let X ∈ RL×D be the input data, where L is the sequence length and
      D is the embedding dimension. The gMLP layer is defined as follows:
261
262           Z = σ(XU ),     Z1 , Z2 = split(Z, axis=“D”),     Ẑ = Z1 ⊙ (W Z2 + b) ,      O = ẐV,      (5)
263
264   where U ∈ RD×H and V ∈ RH×D are projections in the channel dimension, acting on each token
265   independently, and W ∈ RL×L and b ∈ RL are the weights and biases of a linear projection across
266
      the sequence dimension. The function σ is a non-linear activation function such as the GELU.
267   They managed to achieve competitive performance with BERT on the GLUE benchmark. The au-
268   thors observed that the learned spatial projection matrices W were, in fact, Toeplitz matrices, which
269   resulted in a long convolutional operation. Unlike the long convolutions in the models described in
      the previous sections, the kernels in gMLP are fully learned and unrestricted.

      3   T RAINING METHODS
271
272
      As we hypothesized that the reasons for the poor performance of the Transformer in the LRA are
273
      the lack of inductive bias, and very high-dimensional and insufficient data, we apply techniques to
274   avoid overfitting and prevent the model from learning spurious correlations.
275
276   CIFAR-10. Images are amenable to very natural data augmentation techniques. In the CIFAR-10
277         classification task, we apply a strategy derived from the one discovered in for AutoAug-
278         ment (Cubuk et al.). The specific techniques can be found in the source code files indicated
279         in appendix A.3.
280   Pathfinder. The LRA dataset provides three sets of examples of different difficulty, depending on
281           the path lengths and amount of distracting paths. The common procedure is to train only
282           on the difficult set. We try training on all three sets, without augmentation. The advantage
283           of this over using augmentation is that the dataset is more varied and that the model is able
284           to find the signal faster in the easier sets. Further discussion is provided in Appendix C.
285           To make the comparison fair and maintain the number of training steps, we reduce the
286           number of epochs from the usual 200 to 67. Validation and test sets are drawn from the
287           difficult set, and we remove subsets of similar sizes from the easy and intermediate ones to
288           have exactly three times the number of training samples.
289   ListOps. As each mathematical operation in the task is commutative, we can shuffle the order of
290           the operands without altering the result. In other words, at each node of the operation tree,
291           we can apply a random permutation to the children nodes. Using this technique, we can
292           generate many more training samples from the same input data.
293   Text tasks. Since data augmentation is not as straightforward in textual data, we apply a denoising
294           objective where we mask some tokens and ask the model to predict them. This is similar
295           to the masked language modeling objective in BERT (Devlin et al.) and what Amos et al.
296           did in their work. However, instead of training for this task in a separate pretraining phase,
297           we use a multi-task setting, where the total loss is the (unweighted) sum of the denoising
298           loss and the task-specific loss. This has the advantage of reducing computational costs (as
299           computations up to the final heads are shared), as well as removing the risk of representation
300
              collapse during fine-tuning.
301           Using this auxiliary task presents several advantages. First, since masking is done dynami-
302           cally and randomly, the model sees different samples at each epoch, which helps to prevent
303
              overfitting. Second, a token-wise classification task such as the denoising one provides a
              large number of training samples per batch. Finally, since each text is tokenized at the byte
304
              level, the model needs to learn to aggregate neighboring bytes to form meaningful word
305
              embeddings. The MLM objective is known to produce a reliance on neighboring tokens to
306           predict the masked ones, thus resulting in useful representations for the main task.

365   6     D ISCUSSION ON THE POSITIONAL AND LOCAL BIASES OF THE L ONG
366         R ANGE A RENA TASKS
367
368   Our results indicate that the differences in performance between the Transformer and new archi-
369   tectures such as SSMs in the LRA are likely a byproduct of insufficient datasets and the greater
370   inductive biases of the latter. If we look closely at each task, we can deduce that they are mainly
371   positional. In particular, we expect a strong encoding of relative positions to be effective and more
372   efficient to learn. Further, some of the tasks might benefit greatly from a bias towards locality. Both

      similar biases.

401   7   A BLATION STUDY: T RANSFORMER PERFORMANCE
402
403   In this section we study the importance of correct positional encoding in increasing the performance
404   of the Transformer. Table 4 shows the results. We compare rotary, summed sinusoidal and summed
405   learned embeddings. The learned embeddings are very inferior to the other two, and rotary em-
406   beddings are superior to the summed sinusoidal ones, with small margins except for the ListOps
407   task. These results were expected because rotary and sinusoidal embeddings do not include train-
408   able parameters, model relative positions, and add a time-decay mechanism. The superiority of
409   rotary embeddings might come from the fact that they are applied only to key and query vectors,
410
      without distorting intermediate representations or value vectors. Summed embeddings add this dis-
      tortion, and they get entangled with the rest of the information across layers. In table 4 we also
411
      find the impact of removing our training techniques. While rotary embeddings—together with bet-
412
      ter hyperparameters—clearly improve the originally reported performance by the Transformer, not
413   using our training techniques causes a massive drop in accuracy.

416   8   M EASURING THE IMPORTANCE OF LOCALITY IN THE L ONG R ANGE
417       A RENA
418
419   The fact that a locality bias could be helpful in the LRA is concerning in a benchmark for long-range
420   dependency modeling. To discern the importance of locality on each task, we train a convolutional
421   model with small kernel sizes. This experiment should provide a baseline for the performance we
422
      can expect with a fixed bound on the range of dependencies that can be modeled. The details of the
      model can be found in appendix A.2, and the results are shown in table 1.
423
424   In general, we are able to get fairly close to state-of-the-art performance in each task with a small
425   distance bound of 30 tokens per layer. The Pathfinder task appears to be the least reliant on very
426   short-range dependencies. The extreme cases are the textual tasks, where a distance bound of 2
427   tokens per layer is sufficient to achieve state-of-the-art performance. It appears that aggregating
428
      bytes into meaningful word embeddings is enough to make predictions about most of the samples.
429   Our results show the great importance of short-range dependencies in these tasks, especially in the
430   textual ones. This raises doubts about the relationship between the results in the LRA and the
431   ability to model long-range sequences, especially in the case of models with local inductive biases,
      including recent architectures such as SSMs or MEGA, and Transformers with positional encodings


                                                        8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      that add time-decay mechanisms. The evaluation of models in the LRA should include an analysis
433   of the fairness of the results based on the biases of the model and the nature of the benchmark.

436   9   C ONCLUSIONS
437
438   Our study provides critical insights into the nature of the tasks in the Long-Range Arena (LRA)
439   benchmark, challenging its utility for long-range dependency modeling evaluation. Specifically,
440
      we identify that the tasks in the LRA benchmark largely benefit from positional and local depen-
      dencies. Our experiments demonstrate that small convolutional networks—limited in the range of
441
      dependencies they model—can closely match state-of-the-art results. This finding explains the suc-
442
      cess of models like MEGA and structured state-space models (SSMs) on the benchmark and raises
443   questions about the adequacy of the LRA in truly testing long-range dependencies.
444
445   We also establish that the constraints on kernel design, as suggested by Li et al., are not strictly
446
      necessary to achieve strong performance. Instead, enriched data and optimized training strategies
      can yield similar benefits, with the proposed kernel constraints mainly providing inductive biases and
447
      learning efficiency in specific tasks. This opens new pathways for more adaptable model designs for
448
      long-range dependency modeling.
449
450   Our analysis highlights that Transformers’ historically weak performance on LRA tasks stems pri-
451   marily from a lack of inductive biases, rather than an inability to model dependencies. By intro-
452
      ducing rotary embeddings for local and positional biases and refining training to prevent overfitting,
      they also manage to achieve state-of-the-art results.
453
454   Our findings point to a significant re-evaluation of both model architectures and the LRA benchmark
455   for long-range dependency modeling. We underscore the need for benchmarks that genuinely assess
456   long-range dependencies, but leave the design of such a benchmark for future work. As general
457   guidelines, synthetic tasks such as Pathfinder seem to be the most robust to short-range dependencies
458
      and allow us to modulate the range of dependencies required to solve the tasks. Natural language
      seems to be an inconvenient modality, as it may prove difficult to create tasks that require long-range
459
      dependency modeling but low computational resources, which was one of the intentions behind the
460
      LRA. The restriction to simplified synthetic languages is probably the best choice.

744   C    R EMARKS ON TRAINING FOR THE PATHFINDER TASK
745
746   For this task, we decided to learn from the easy and intermediate sets instead of using augmentation,
747   or both. Of course, this is because we found better empirical results with this approach. The main
748   consideration is that the Transformer seemed to need several passes over the most difficult examples
749
      to correctly learn them. The model seems to learn faster this way. However, not using any augmen-
      tation or other sets leads to overfitting. A good balance is having extra data or a finite set of possible
750
      augmentations. We recommend using a subset of the dihedral group D4 of reflections and rotations,
751
      as they do not cause any distortion in the images due to low resolution. Depending on the ability of
752   the model to overfit, one might decide to use more or less augmentations, as well as other training
753   sets.
