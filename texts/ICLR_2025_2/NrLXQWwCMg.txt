009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Implicit Neural Representations (INRs) employ neural networks to represent con-
012            tinuous functions by mapping coordinates to the corresponding values of the target
013            function, with applications e.g., inverse graphics. However, INRs face a chal-
014            lenge known as spectral bias when dealing with scenes containing varying fre-
015            quencies. To overcome spectral bias, the most common approach is the Fourier
016
               features-based methods such as positional encoding. However, Fourier features-
               based methods will introduce noise to output, which degrades their performances
017
               when applied to downstream tasks. In response, this paper addresses this prob-
018
               lem by first investigating the underlying causes through the lens of the Neural
019            Tangent Kernel. Through theoretical analysis, we propose that using Fourier fea-
020            tures embedding can be interpreted as fitting Fourier series expansion of the target
021            function, from which we find that it is the insufficiency in the finitely sampled fre-
022            quencies that causes the generation of noisy outputs. Leveraging these insights,
023            we introduce bias-free MLPs as an adaptive linear filter to locally suppress unnec-
024            essary frequencies while amplifying essential ones by adjusting the coefficients at
025            the coordinate level. Additionally, we propose a line-search-based algorithm to
026            adjust the filter’s learning rate dynamically, achieving Pareto efficiency between
027
               the adaptive linear filter module and the INRs. Extensive experiments demonstrate
               that our proposed method consistently improves the performance of INRs on typ-
028
               ical tasks, including image regression, 3D shape regression, and inverse graphics.
029
               The full code will be publicly available.

034   Implicit Neural Representations (INRs), which fit the target function using only input coordinates,
035   have recently gained significant attention. By leveraging the powerful fitting capability of Multilayer
036   Perceptrons (MLPs), INRs can implicitly represent the target function without requiring their ana-
037   lytical expressions. The versatility of MLPs allows INRs to be applied in various fields, including
038   inverse graphics (Mildenhall et al., 2021; Barron et al., 2023; Martin-Brualla et al., 2021), image
039
      super-resolution (Chen et al., 2021b; Yuan et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2023), image generation (Sko-
      rokhodov et al., 2021), and more (Chen et al., 2021a; Strümpler et al., 2022; Shue et al., 2023).
040
041   However, MLPs face a significant challenge known as the spectral bias, where low-frequency sig-
042   nals are typically favored during training (Rahaman et al., 2019). A common solution is to project
043   coordinates into the frequency domain using Fourier features, such as Random Fourier Features and
044   Positional Encoding, which can be understood as manually set high-frequency correspondence prior
045
      to accelerating the learning of high-frequency targets. (Tancik et al., 2020). This projection is widely
      applied in the INRs for novel view synthesis (Mildenhall et al., 2021; Barron et al., 2021), dynamic

126   2     R ELATED WORKS
127
128   2.1   I MPLICIT N EURAL R EPRESENTATIONS
129
      Implicit Neural Representations are designed to learn a continuous representations of target func-
130
      tions by taking advantages of the approximation power of neural networks. Their inherent contin-
131
      uous property can beneficial in many cases like video compression (Chen et al., 2021a; Strümpler
132   et al., 2022), 3D modeling (Park et al., 2019; Atzmon & Lipman, 2020; Michalkiewicz et al., 2019;
133   Gropp et al., 2020; Sitzmann et al., 2019) and volume rendering (Pumarola et al., 2021; Barron
134   et al., 2021; Martin-Brualla et al., 2021; Barron et al., 2023). However, simply employing MLPs
135   may result in spectral bias, where oversmoothed outputs are generated due to the inherent tendency
136   of MLPs to prioritize learning low-frequency components first. Consequently, many studies have
137   focused on these drawbacks and explored various methods to address this issue. The most straight-
138   forward way to address this issue is by projecting the coordinates into the higher dimension (Tancik
139   et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021). However, these methods can lead to noisy outputs if there is a
140
      mismatch in the projection variance. To address this, Landgraf et al. (2022) propose dividing the
      Random Fourier Features into multiple levels of detail, allowing the MLPs to disregard unnecessary
141
      high-frequency components. Another type of approach to mitigating the spectral bias introduced by
142
      the ReLU activation function, as proposed by Sitzmann et al. (2020), Saragadam et al. (2023), and
143   Shenouda et al. (2024), is to modify the activation function itself by using alternatives such as the
144   Sine function, Wavelets, or a combination of ReLU with other functions. There are also efforts to
145   modify network structures to mitigate spectral bias (Mujkanovic et al., 2024). Lindell et al. (2022)
146   introduce a network design that treats MLPs as filters applied to the input of the next layer, known as
147   Multiplicative Filter Networks (MFNs). Additionally, based on the discrete nature of signals like im-
148   ages and videos, grid-based approaches (e.g., Grid Tangent Kernel (Zhao et al., 2024), DINER (Xie
149   et al., 2023), and Fourier Filter Bank (Wu et al., 2023)) have been proposed to address spectral bias,
150   as the grid property allows for sharp changes in features, which facilitates learning fine details.
151
152   2.2   N EURAL TANGENT K ERNEL
153
      Deep neural networks are powerful across various domains but remain a black box that lacks in-
154
      terpretability. Therefore, many researchers have dived into explaining the mechanism of the neural
155
      networks in recent years. Lee et al. (2020) propose a Neural Network Gaussian Process (NNGP),
156   modeling a two-layer neural network using a frozen first layer as the kernel, transforming it into
157   kernel regression. Jacot et al. (2018); Arora et al. (2019b) introduce Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK)
158   by linearizing the MLPs, extendable to multiple layers via induction, offering insights like spec-
159   tral bias (Bietti & Mairal, 2019) and data distribution effects (Basri et al., 2020). Convolutional
160   Neural Tangent Kernel (CNTK) (Arora et al., 2019b) generalizes these ideas to CNNs, enhancing
161   researchers’ understanding of different phenomena in deep learning (Tachella et al., 2021; Ulyanov
      et al., 2018; Cao & Gu, 2019; Advani et al., 2020).

168   be represented by a single formula with slight variations in their implementation.

526   7   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
527
528   Building on insights from the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK), we analyze the high-frequency noise
529   in Fourier Features, which arises due to limited frequency sampling. This understanding motivates
530   the development of our proposed method, which incorporates a line-search algorithm to achieve a
531   Pareto-efficient balance between frequency learning and noise reduction. By applying our method
532   to a range of tasks, including image regression, 3D shape regression, and Neural Radiance Fields
533   (NeRF), we consistently outperform baseline models. Our approach excels at capturing high-
534   frequency details while effectively mitigating noise, leading to more accurate reconstructions. The
535   method demonstrates robust performance in both low- and high-frequency regions, ensuring more
536
      precise and stable outputs in complex tasks.
537   Limitations: Despite the improvements, our method does not completely resolve finite sampling
538   issues from the root. Additionally, while the line-search algorithm enhances the performance of the
539   adaptive linear filter, it may lead to slower convergence and occasional instability during the early
      stage of training. Addressing these challenges is part of our future work.
