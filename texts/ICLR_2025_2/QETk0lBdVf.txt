009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Video sequences offer valuable temporal information, but existing large multimodal
012            models (LMMs) fall short in understanding extremely long videos. Many works
013            address this by reducing the number of visual tokens using visual resamplers.
014            Alternatively, in this paper, we approach this problem from the perspective of
015            the language model. By simply extrapolating the context length of the language
016
               backbone, we enable LMMs to comprehend orders of magnitude more visual tokens
               without any video training. We call this phenomenon long context transfer and
017
               carefully ablate its properties. To effectively measure LMMs’ ability to generalize
018
               to long contexts in the vision modality, we develop V-NIAH (Visual Needle-In-
019            A-Haystack), a purely synthetic long vision benchmark inspired by the language
020            model’ s NIAH test. Our proposed Long Video Assistant (LongVA) can process
021            2000 frames or over 200K visual tokens without additional complexities. With
022            its extended context length, LongVA achieves state-of-the-art performance on
023            Video-MME and MLVU among 7B-scale models by densely sampling more input
024            frames.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Driven by the progress of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Team, 2023; Touvron
029
      et al., 2023; Team, 2024a; Ormazabal et al., 2024; Mistral, 2024; Cohere, 2024), multiple studies
030   are conducted to extend their capability to understand images and videos (Li et al., 2023b; Dai
031   et al., 2023; Team, 2024b; Liu et al., 2023c). With modality alignment and visual instruction tuning,
032   these Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) have shown impressive abilities such as captioning and
033   visual question-answering. While current LMMs have demonstrated promising performance on
034   tasks involving single images and short videos (Song et al., 2024b; Lin et al., 2023a; Maaz et al.,
035   2023; Zhang et al., 2023), effectively processing and understanding extremely long videos remains a
036   significant challenge (Wang et al., 2024b).
037   One primary reason for this challenge is the excessive number of visual tokens generated by the
038   vision encoder. For instance, LLaVA-1.6 (Liu et al., 2024b) can produce 576 to 2880 visual tokens for
039   a single image. The number of visual tokens increases significantly with the addition of more frames.
040   To address this problem, numerous methods have been proposed to reduce the number of visual
041   tokens. One popular direction is to modify the visual resampler that connects the vision encoder
042   and LLM, aiming to extract fewer tokens (Li et al., 2023b;d; Cai et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024).
043   Alternative approaches (Chen et al., 2024a; Shang et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024b)
044
      employ heuristic techniques to prune or merge the visual features. However, despite these efforts,
      Table 1 demonstrates that the majority of current LMMs are still limited in their ability to process a
045
      large number of frames effectively.
046
047   Another issue hindering the development of high-performance long video LMMs is the lack of
048   high-quality long video datasets. In Table 2, we list the average video length of existing video
049   instruction tuning data. Most datasets consist of video clips within 1 minute. Even if some datasets do
050
      contain longer videos, the corresponding text pairs are generated by annotating only several frames
      within that video, lacking long and dense supervision signals.
051
052   Given the circumstance, in this paper, instead of reducing the visual tokens, we identify the more
053   critical issue limiting the visual context length in existing LMMs: the context length of the language
      model backbone. Given a language model, we first extend its context length by training on longer

093   Vision Language Connector in Large Multimodal Models Existing studies explore different
094   architectures to extract and inject visual features into LLMs. One line of work (Alayrac et al., 2022;
095   Li et al., 2023a; Awadalla et al., 2023; Laurençon et al., 2023), pioneered by Flamingo (Alayrac et al.,
096
      2022), adopts a resampler to compress the visual feature and inserts cross-gated attention layers into
      the LLM. Some other works still use a reampler (Li et al., 2023b; Zhu et al., 2023; Team, 2024b) while
097
      directly feeding the image feature into the input layer of the language model. The LLaVA series (Liu
098
      et al., 2024b; 2023b;c) use a simple and scalable design to directly project the image features into
099   language model without any pooling or resampling. When the field moves from image-only models
100   to include multi-image and video inputs, more modifications to the visual language connector were
101   proposed. Zhang et al. (2024b) and Cai et al. (2024) use a simple average pooling. Jin et al. (2024)
102   dynamically drop the visual tokens. Cheng et al. (2024) adopt a spatial-temporal convolution to better
103   capture the dynamics of video data and reduce feature size. Our proposed context transfer from text
104   to image is orthogonal to those works and can further enable LMMs to understand more frames.
105
106   Context Extrapolation in Transformer Transformer does not directly work on sequences longer
107   than its training length. To alleviate that, various RoPE-based (Su et al., 2023) extension tech-
      niques (Chen et al., 2023a; bloc97, 2024; Rozière et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2024)

122   Video Language Benchmarks Recent years have witnessed significant progress in Video Question-
123   AnsweringAntol et al. (2015). To accurately measure the progress of the video LMMs’ performance,
124   researchers have developed various benchmarks encompassing a broad spectrum of tasks. These
125   range from fundamental visual perception tasks such as activity recognitionYu et al. (2019a), concept
126
      detection (Xu et al., 2017), and counting (Jang et al., 2017), to more complex visual reasoning
      tasks including compositional (Grunde-McLaughlin et al., 2021), causal (Xiao et al., 2021a; Yi
127
      et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2021), and situated reasoning (Wu et al., 2021). However, most of those
128
      benchmarks focus on short videos, lacking data and metrics to test LMMs’ capability over a long
129   context. Inspired by the NIAH test (Gregory, 2024) in the language model community, we proposed
130   V-NIAH to benchmark LMMs’ ability over long visual inputs with the minimum overhead of data
131   collection and human annotation. Several concurrent works also developed multimodal versions of
132   the Needle-in-a-haystack test (Wang et al., 2024c; Zhou et al., 2024a; Song et al., 2024a; Wang et al.,
133   2024a). However, they only measure on several hundreds of frames and lack a strong baseline to
134   properly analyze the properties of visual context length.

      3     L ONG V IDEO A SSISTANT
137

      As in Figure 1, this paper centers around the hypothesis that if the modality of vision and language
139   can be truly aligned, the capability to handle long contexts could also transfer from text to vision,
140   and this could happen even without explicit long video training. Our methodology is thus very
141   straightforward. Given a language model, we first perform long context training purely on language
142   to extend its text context (Section 3.1). We then detailed how we augment this language model with
143   long visual capabilities by training solely on short image data in Section 3.2.
144
145   3.1    T RAINING L ONG L ANGUAGE M ODEL
146
147   We use Qwen2-7B-Instruct (Team, 2024c) as the backbone language model and perform continued
148   pretraining with a context length of 224K1 over a total of 900M tokens. We follow Xiong et al. (2023)
149   to increase RoPE (Su et al., 2023) base frequency during the continued pertaining and specifically set
150
      it to 1B. A constant learning rate of 1e-5 is maintained for a batch size of one million tokens across
      1,000 training steps. Following Fu et al. (2024b), we construct the dataset used for long context
151
      training from Slimpajama (Cerebras, 2023) by upsampling documents longer than 4096 and keeping
152
      the domain mixture ratio unchanged. Multiple documents are packed into a single sequence separated
153   by a BOS token.
154
155   We employed several optimization strategies to perform training on such long sequences. These
156
      includes FlashAttention-2 (Dao, 2023), Ring Attention (Liu et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2023c), activation
      checkpointing, and parameter offload (Rajbhandari et al., 2020). To balance the load across different
157
      GPUs, we shard the sequence in a zigzag way (Zhu, 2024) in ring attention. The resulting training
158
      framework is memory efficient and maintains very high GPU occupancy. Note that we do not use any

      3.2   A LIGNING L ONG L ANGUAGE M ODEL U SING S HORT V ISION DATA
196
197
      Inspired by the AnyRes encoding scheme in LLaVA-NeXT (Liu et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024a), we
198   designed UniRes that provides a unified encoding scheme for both images and videos, as shown in
199   Figure 2. Unlike AnyRes which retains a small base image and flattens ViT patches across the grids,
200   UniRes removes the base image, flattens patches within each grid, and 2x2 pool the visual features by
201   default (Appendix B). This approach allows us to maintain consistent representation when extending
202   image data into videos where multiple frames are viewed as multiple grids in a row.
203
      Specifically, UniRes divides an input image of resolution a × b into smaller grids, each with a
204   resolution of 336 × 336 pixels. This results in (a//336) × (b//336) grids. For very high-resolution
205   images, we limit the maximum number of grids to 49, resizing images larger than this threshold. Each
206   grid is separately encoded using CLIP-ViT-L-336px (Radford et al., 2021) and then projected
207   through a 2-layer MLP to match the LM’s input dimension, resulting in 576 features per grid. We
208   then apply 2x2 average pooling, finally converting an a × b image into (a//336) × (b//336) × 144
209   tokens. During inference, this visual encoding scheme allows videos to be represented as very long
210   images (even though we do not train on videos). An N -frame video is treated as an image of size
211   336 × (336 × N ), divided into N grids where each grid corresponds to a video frame. Using CLIP
212
      encoding, MLP projection, and average pooling, an N -frame video is encoded into 144N visual
      tokens.
213
214   To clearly ablate the long context transfer phenomenon from language to vision, we adopt a train
215   short, test long protocol where we only use image-text data during training, but test on long videos.
      We trained our model using the same data recipe and two-stage training approach as LLaVA-1.6.

246   4   V-NIAH
247
248   To measure the context length of language models on extremely long input, earlier works calculate
249
      perplexity scores over long documents. Recently, many have started using the Needle-in-a-Haystack
      (NIAH) test to benchmark LLMs’ ability to retrieve long context information precisely. We note that
250
      there is so far no benchmark to measure the visual context length of LMMs. To evaluate LongVA’s
251
      capacity to locate and retrieve long-range visual information, we extend the NIAH test from text to
252   video and propose V-NIAH.
253
254   As shown in Table 8, we designed 5 video question-answering problems as the needle and inserted
255
      each as a single frame into hours-long videos. We sampled the videos at 1 FPS as the visual input.
      The image of the needle is sourced from existing VQA benchmarks or AI-generated to avoid any
256
      contamination. The AI-generated images and questions are purposely chosen to be "counterfactual"
257
      or "counter-commonsense", ensuring the model cannot answer based on language knowledge alone.
258   Each question includes a "locating prompt" so that a capable system or human can locate the needle
259   frame from the video haystack and answer the question.
260
261
      When testing LongVA with visual inputs of up to 3000 frames, one difficulty we encountered was
      that processing a 200K-token input requires up to 100GB of GPU memory for the KV cache for
262
      a 7B LM like LLaMA. Even with advanced LM serving systems like vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023)
263
      with tensor parallelism to shard the KV cache across multiple GPUs, the sampling process remains
264   extremely slow due to limited memory and batchsize. To address this, we used "perplexity-based"
265   evaluation to measure the correctness of the model output. We first encode all frames and save their
266   corresponding visual embeddings. During the evaluation, we only load the language model from
267   LongVA and concatenate the visual embeddings, question tokens, and answer tokens for a single
268   forward pass with ring attention. This approach makes the workload compute-bound and eliminates
269   the need to cache the KV state. The model’s output is considered correct only if the highest output
      logits index of all tokens in the answer span matches the correct answer.

343   5.1      V-NIAH R ESULTS
344
345   Long context transfers from language to vision Figure 4 shows the V-NIAH performance of
346   LongVA and other LMMs. Specifically, Figure 4 (iii) demonstrates that the visual context length
347   of LLaVA-NeXT-Video-32K (Zhang et al., 2024b) is constrained by the 32K context length of its
348   language backbone, Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 (Jiang et al., 2023), equivalent to approximately 200
349
      frames. Beyond this limit, the V-NIAH accuracy drops significantly. As a stronger baseline, we
      include the results of LLaVA-NeXT-Video-32K enhanced with a training-free length extrapolation
350
      algorithm (bloc97, 2024) by increasing its RoPE base frequency. We empirically determine the
351
      optimal extrapolation frequency by choosing from [3M, 10M, 30M, 100M, 300M, 1B]. As indicated
352   in Figure 4 (iv), although this training-free extrapolation allows the model to process information
353   across an extended context, the improvement is marginal. These findings led us to develop LongVA,
354   a model that unlocks the visual context by extending the language model purely on text. As shown in
355   Figure 4 (i), LongVA can almost perfectly retrieve information and answer the needle question for
356   input frames fewer than 2000. Although we only trained LongVA’s language backbone on a context
357   length of 224K (equivalent to 1555 frames), it generalizes well beyond that, maintaining satisfactory
358   performance within 3000 frames. Those results clearly corroborate of hypothesis of long context
359   transfer.
360   Unified encoding enables better visual context extrapolation We also present the V-NIAH heatmap
361   of LongVA trained with AnyRes encoding scheme, keeping all other factors unchanged in Figure 4
362   (ii). LongVA-AnyRes demonstrates strong retrieval capabilities. However, its performance still lags
363   behind LongVA trained with UniRes. We believe that the unified representation of images and videos
364   in UniRes, where a video is encoded in the same way as a long image, enhances the long context
365   transfer from language to vision. This approach also facilitates effective training with short vision
366
      data (images) and enables zero-shot understanding of long videos during inference.

532   8      C ONCLUSION
533
534   This work addresses the challenges of understanding long videos in Large Multimodal Models. By
535   extending the language model on text and then aligning this extended model with visual inputs, we
536   significantly improved the capability of LMMs to handle long videos thanks to the long context
537   transfer phenomenon. Our model, LongVA, shows improved performance with more input frames and
538   achieves state-of-the-art results on Video-MME. Additionally, we introduce a synthetic benchmark,
539   V-NIAH, to effectively measure the visual context length of video LMMs. We hope this work inspires
      further research in the field of long video LMMs and multimodal agents.

      to-evaluate variant (AI, 2023) that involves identifying and retrieving random numbers associated

          book>.\n Based on the content of the book, Question: What is the
886
          special magic Singapore number? Answer: The special magic Singapore

      We insert a needle with the key Singapore and a 7-digit randomly sampled magic number as the value
890
      into the haystack of Paul Graham’s Essays. The needle has the following format:
891
892   \nThe special magic {City} number is: {XXXXXXX}.\n

893
894   We iterate over various document depths (where the needle is placed) and context lengths to measure
895   the performance. For each depth and context length, we conducted the test 5 times, each time with a
