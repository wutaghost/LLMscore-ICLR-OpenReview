                                                      A BSTRACT
012
                 Prior work in mechanistic interpretability has analyzed how neural networks solve
013
                 modular arithmetic tasks, but conflicting interpretations have emerged, question-
014              ing the universality hypothesis—that similar tasks lead to similar learned circuits.
015              Revisiting modular addition, we identify that these discrepancies stem from overly
016              granular analyses, which obscure the higher-level patterns that unify seemingly
017              disparate solutions. Using a multi-scale approach—microscopic (neurons), meso-
018              scopic (clusters of neurons), and macroscopic (entire network)—we show that all
019              scales align on (approximate) cosets and implement an abstract algorithm resem-
020              bling the approximate Chinese Remainder Theorem. Additionally, we propose a
021              model where networks aims for a constant logit margin, predicting O(log(n)) fre-
022              quencies—more consistent with empirical results in networks with biases, which
023
                 are more expressive and commonly used in practice, than the n−1 2 frequencies de-
                 rived from bias-free networks. By uncovering shared structures across setups, our
024
                 work provides a unified framework for understanding modular arithmetic in neural
025              networks and generalizes existing insights to broader, more realistic scenarios.

041   1    I NTRODUCTION
042
043   The universality hypothesis (Olah et al., 2020) is the claim that ‘Analogous features and circuits
044   form across models and tasks.’ This hypothesis lays at the foundation of current efforts in mech-
045   anistic interpretability because it gives hope that reverse engineering neural networks will reveal
046   interpretations that can be applied to a large class of networks. Mechanistic interpretability aims to
047   uncover the precise mechanisms by which models transform inputs into outputs, offering insights
048   into how specific neurons, layers, or circuits contribute to a network’s overall function. Understand-
049   ing how these networks organize information, form abstractions, perform computations, and make
050
      predictions is essential to building more trustworthy and reliable systems; moreover, such findings
      can guide the development of theory.
051
052   We build on the work of Nanda et al. (2023a), Chughtai et al. (2023b), Stander et al. (2023), Zhong
053   et al. (2024), which mechanistically studied and reverse-engineered neural networks trained on mod-
      ular addition and other group operations. These works found conflicting algorithms–either due to

054
      random variation in the training process or human interpretation itself–eventually raising doubts
055   about the universality of neural network representations when trained on group-theoretic tasks. It
056   is necessary to walk through the history of this problem in order to situate our work. The study of
057   modular addition by Nanda et al. (2023a) was an attempt to mechanistically explain the surprising
058   “grokking” phenomenon observed in Power et al. (2022), where algorithmic tasks were found to
059   suddenly generalize long after memorizing the training set. By reverse engineering a model trained
060   to grok modular addition, Nanda et al. (2023a) discovered that it was possible to specify the com-
061   putations performed by a specific network in a human-understandable way. This key result opened
062   the door to a flood of research on the topic. Initially, Nanda et al. (2023a) identified a ‘Fourier
063
      multiplication’ algorithm, which later was generalized to the more general ‘Group Composition via
      Representations’ (GCR) framework (Chughtai et al., 2023b), claimed to be universal for all groups.
064
      However, subsequent studies have challenged this universality. Networks trained with different hy-
065
      perparameters sometimes learned entirely different algorithms (e.g. The Clock, The Pizza, or made
066   use of Lissajous-like curves) (Zhong et al., 2024). More definitively, Stander et al. (2023)–using
067   the exact same experimental setup as Chughtai et al. (2023b)–showed that GCR fails to describe
068   networks trained on the symmetric group, where coset-based structures (circuits) emerged instead.
069   Consequently, Stander et al. (2023) and Zhong et al. (2024) provide evidence that even on something
070   as simple as finite groups, there may be no universality in the algorithms neural networks learn.
071
      This paper revisits the following fundamental question in
072   mechanistic interpretability: do neural networks trained
073   on group-theoretic tasks, such as modular addition, con-
074   verge to universal structures, and if so, what form do
075   these structures take? Focusing on modular addition, we
076   embark on a classification of network structures, with
077   the aim to find a scale at which studying the network
078   makes our interpretation robust to changes in hyperpa-
079   rameters and random initializations. Our analysis reveals
080
      that regardless of whether the modulus is prime or com-
      posite, neural networks may be implementing a “macro-
081
      scopic” algorithm for modular addition through a pro-
082
      cess resembling the Chinese Remainder Theorem, where
083   neurons are organized into superimposed clusters repre-
084   senting (approximate) cosets of (approximate) subgroups.
085   Within the context of modular addition, this macroscopic
086   view both accounts for the variation seen in Nanda et al.
087   (2023a) and Zhong et al. (2024) and also naturally draws
088   a connection with the coset structures identified in the
089   symmetric group (Stander et al., 2023), which previously
090   disproved GCR’s (Chughtai et al., 2023b) claims of uni-
091
      versality. While we do not claim to have established uni-
      versality across all groups, our findings restore hope that

124   2     R ELATED W ORK
125
126   Mechanistic interpretations of group-theoretic tasks, grokking and universality. Popularized
127   by works such as Cammarata et al. (2020), mechanistic interpretability has not only studied toy
128   settings like Nanda et al. (2023b); Quirke et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023), but even tackled frontier
129   models and shed light into the origins of in-context learning (Olsson et al., 2022). The universality
130   hypothesis (Olah et al., 2020), (Li et al., 2015) asserts that different neural networks trained on
131   similar tasks will converge to similar features and circuits; it is a key open question in the field of
132
      mechanistic interpretability. A common thread in attempts to interpret neural networks is the crucial
      need for caution and precision in the interpretation process (Adebayo et al., 2018; Bolukbasi et al.,
133
      2021; Sun et al., 2023; Poursabzi-Sangdeh et al., 2021; Jain & Wallace, 2019; Doshi-Velez & Kim,
134
      2017). This has led to attempts to automate circuit discovery (Conmy et al., 2023) and understand
135   the dynamics of circuit formation during training (Hoogland et al., 2024)
136
137   Learning mathematical tasks. Mathematical tasks are valuable testbeds for studying neural net-
138
      works due to their structural sparsity, which isolates key variables and simplifies analysis, and the
      rich theoretical frameworks available to align human intuition with model representations. These
139
      tasks have driven progress in understanding network properties, representations, and dynamics (El-
140
      hage et al., 2022). Modular addition, the focus of this paper, has been extensively studied: Liu
141   et al. (2022) link grokking to structured representations; Doshi et al. (2023) analyze generalizable
142   representations under corrupted labels; Ding et al. (2024) model interactions of competitive and co-
143   operative representations as linear differential equations; and He et al. (2024) explore the emergence
144   of in-context learning and skill composition in modular arithmetic.
145
      Theoretical models of grokking modular addition. Previous work, such as Gromov (2023), an-
146
      alyzed two-layer quadratic networks without biases for modular arithmetic, showing that solutions
147   rely on Fourier features and deterministically use all (n − 1)/2 frequencies. Morwani et al. (2024)
148   extended this by proving such solutions maximize the margin, while Mohamadi et al. (2023) linked
149   margin maximization to the grokking phenomenon in similar bias-free networks. In contrast, our pri-
150   marily empirical work, supported by a heuristic theoretical model, incorporates biases and matches
151   experimental observations, showing that biased networks use only O(log(n)) frequencies to solve
152   modular addition, highlighting the subtleties of biases both in theory and in training dynamics for
153   this problem.

157   3.1   G ROUP ( REPRESENTATION ) THEORY AND THE C HINESE R EMAINDER T HEOREM
158
159   A group (G, ◦) is the data of a set G with an associative binary operation ◦ : G×G → G, an identity
160   element e, and the existence of inverse elements. Associativity implies that f ◦ (g ◦ h) = (f ◦ g) ◦ h
161   for any f, g, h ∈ G. The identity e is characterized by e ◦ g = g ◦ e = g for any g ∈ G. Finally,
      for g ∈ G, the existence of inverses asserts that there is an element g −1 ∈ G such that g ◦ g −1 = e.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      A subset H of G is a subgroup if it is closed under the group operation ◦ and forms a group on its
163   own. Subgroups partition G into equally sized disjoint subsets called cosets. A left coset is the set
164   gH = {gh : h ∈ H}, and similarly, Hg is a right coset.
165
166
      For example (Z, +), the integers under addition, forms a group, and the even integers form a sub-
      group. The cyclic group Cn = {0, 1, ..., n − 1} under addition modulo n is a central object of study
167
      in this paper. Subgroups of Cn take the form {0, m, 2m, . . . , (n/m − 1)m} for divisors m of n,
168
      which corresponds to “wrapping” Cn at a coarser granularity.
169
170   A group homomorphism is a map f : G → H preserving the group operation: f (g ◦G h) =
171   f (g) ◦H f (h). If f is bijective, it is called an isomorphism.
172   A group representation is a homomorphism ρ : G → GL(V ), where GL(V ) is the group of
173   invertible linear transformations on a vector space V over R or C. Representations link group
174   theory to linear algebra, enabling intuitive analysis via matrices. For example, the cyclic group
175   Cn has complex representations ρ(k) = exp(2πi mk      n ) for m | n. These encode rotations in the
176   complex plane and correspond to the discrete Fourier transform (DFT). Real representations,
177   such as rotation matrices in R2 , are derived from these complex representations. In this paper, we
178
      draw heavily on the connection between representations of Cn and the DFT to analyze how neural
      networks encode modular addition.
179
180   The Chinese remainder theorem (CRT) states that if an integer n has factorisation n = q1 q2 · · · qj
181   into pairwise coprime integers qi , then for any integers m1 , . . . , mj , the system of congruences
182   k ≡ mi (mod qi ) for i = 1, . . . , j has a unique solution modulo n. In the language of groups, one
183   would say that the map Cn → Cq1 × ... × Cqr mapping k to the vector (k mod q1 , k mod q2 , ..., k
184
      mod qr ) = (k1 , ..., kr ) is a group isomorphism. One way of finding the solution of this set of
      congruence relations is to find the preimage of each reduction mod qi and taking their intersection.
185
      This intersection of sets will contain a single element which is the sought solution.
186
187

189   neural networks we study. Think of each congruence k ≡ mi (mod qi ) as defining a signal over
190
      Cn . To each congruence condition k ≡ mi (mod qi ), associate the function fi : Cn → R defined
191   by fi (k) = cos(2πi qi (k−m

      Example: Addition modulo 91. Consider n = 91 with prime factors 7 and 13. Suppose we are
196
      solving (a + b) mod 91 = 10. Using CRT and cosets:
197
198   Compute 10 mod 7 = 3. The set of integers congruent to 3 mod 7 (a coset) is {3, 10, 17, 24, ...}
199   Compute 10 mod 13 = 10. The set of integers congruent to 10 mod 13 (a coset) is {10, 23, 36, ...}

      The only number in the intersection of these two sets is 10, i.e. the unique solution modulo 91. To
      use this to construct what the neural network is learning when trained on modular arithmetic, we do
202
      the following: 1) fit a sine wave through these cosets with a frequency such that it peaks on each
203
      value in the coset (in this case that frequency is the prime factor); 2) fit a sine wave through every
204   coset we did not mention, e.g. (mod 3 = 0, mod 3 = 2, etc); 3) when we cluster all sine waves
205   (neurons) with the same frequency, we get three clusters and the behavior shown in Figures 6, 18
206   and 32! This connects the CRT to the neural network’s learned representation of modular addition.

      Approximate cosets. While CRT relies on exact cosets, neural networks often learn more flexible
208
      structures that we term approximate cosets. Pick any c, v ∈ Cn and 1 ≤ k ≤ n, then the set
209
      {c, c+v, c+2v, ..., c+kv} is an approximate coset. In fact, we can show that the set of values a with
210   cos(2πf a/p) > d for a fixed value d (i.e. exceeds a fixed threshold) forms an approximate coset.
211   To see this, set v to be the modular inverse of f , that is, vf = 1 mod n, and pick the first positive
212   integer c such that cos(2πc/n) > d but cos(2π(c+1)/n) ≤ d. Then we have cos(2πx/n) > d ⇐⇒
213   x ∈ {−c, ..., c}, which means that cos(2πf a/n) > d ⇐⇒ a ∈ {−cv, −cv+v, −cv+2v, ..., cv}. If
214   you introduce a phase shift to the cosine, then that just changes the starting point of the approximate
215   coset. Section 5.1 goes into more detail for the case when the frequency does not divide n, as will
      be the case most of the time, and shown in Fig 3 .

      3.2   TASK DESCRIPTION AND MODEL
237
      The task is to learn the labeling function ◦ : Cn × Cn → Cn , corresponding to addition modulo n
238   in the cyclic group Cn . The dataset is the n2 pairs (a, b) with label c = a + b mod n. We train
239   128-neuron (with biases) 1-hidden-layer ReLU networks with two embedding matrices (dimension
240   32), one for a and one for b, with n output logits. The training set is 5096 randomly selected pairs for
241   n = 91 and 4717 pairs for n = 89. The inputs a and b to the neural network select a column from
242   their respective embedding matrix, then these columns are concatenated, and the neural network
243   receives this concatenated vector as input. We train models using the Adam optimizer (Kingma &
244   Ba, 2015), with a learning rate of 0.008 and L2 weight decay of 0.0001 (Note we use ∥θ∥22 , not the
245   square root, where θ is the weight vector of the network).

249   Diverging observations have been made across works. Despite these differences, a closer exam-
250   ination reveals that these networks exhibit invariances at three key points: embeddings, neuron

      While these invariances provide a unifying thread, the lack of a cohesive framework to explain
255   how they arise has led to confusion and conflicting interpretations. In the next section, we present

257   unified CRT-inspired model.

462
463   Our experimental findings show that the neurons concentrate on the complex representations of
464   the group thus learning periodic functions; see Fig. 2. Simple neurons concentrate on one complex
465   representation and fine-tuning neurons concentrate on multiple complex representations (Figs 2, 22).
466   Additionally, fine-tuning neurons have additive or subtractive relations with each other, see Fig. 29,
467   while simple neurons try to avoid these relations Fig. 4b. Furthermore, we introduced a model for a
468   simple neuron that explains that the simple neurons are actually activating on (approximate) cosets.
469   The best way to see this is to relate it to the CRT which uses set intersection on cosets (see 3.1),
470
      whereas the network superimposes linear combinations of cosets onto the logits by using different
      frequencies, thus giving the correct logit the highest value (guaranteeing its selection by argmax).
471
      Furthermore, we show how using cosets in this way models the constructive and deconstructive
472
      interference, explaining why the incorrect logits have low values instead of just why the correct logit
473   has a high value, see Fig. 6 and Section 5.2. We’d also like to point out that the pizza interpretation
474   is an implementation of the CRT see Fig. 18, e.g. see Fig. 1 in Zhong et al. (2024), “Same-label
475   predictions are spread over two slices of pizza” proceeds to list all points in the coset a + b ≡ 5
476   mod 12, and see Section B.1.1.
477
      Furthermore, the circular, non-circular and Lissajous-like curves found in Zhong et al. (2024) are
478
      predicted by our simple neuron model. Given two neurons in different clusters, i.e. with different
479   frequencies (f1 , f2 ), a parametric plot of their activations (cos(2πf1 t), cos(2πf2 t)) traces out a

      6    C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
487
488
      We have unified experimental findings with a model for an approximate CRT, demonstrating that
489   the following are different expressions of the same overall phenomenon: each neuron learns a pro-
490   jection of a representation, i.e., a phase-shifted representation, or an approximate coset of a specific
491   subgroup (learning a coset if the frequency is a prime factor). Furthermore, we demonstrate that
492   superimposing neurons to form clusters reveals that neurons come together to construct cosets that
493   are approximately equivariant to changes to the inputs of the network independently of whether the
494   network is a clock Figs. 6 and 32 or pizza 18. Thus, we show that the algorithm the model is using
495   to minimize the loss is reminiscent of the CRT—using linear combinations of cosets. Thus, it is the
496   case that the results found by Nanda et al. (2023a); Chughtai et al. (2023b); Zhong et al. (2024) are
497
      all true, yet simultaneously conflict with eachother. This is because the explanations are not robust
      at multiple scales, giving rise to an interesting question: should we consider an interpretation to be
498
      good only if it’s true at more than one scale of abstraction?
499
500   Furthermore, the result of Stander et al. (2023) that networks trained on permutation groups are
501   learning cosets and not representations, is no longer a conflicting piece of evidence for a universality
502   hypothesis that neural networks learn similiar structures when trained on similar classes of data
503
      (finite groups). We restore hope for the universality hypothesis to be true by showing that cosets –
      not GCR – are core features in networks learning the cyclic group across a variety of hyperparameter
504
      conditions. Our work revisits the conjecture, which was believed to be refuted, and shows that both
505
      solutions can be unified, reopening the conjecture for consideration.
506
507   Reopened-conjecture 2: There is universality in the structures neural networks uncover when
508   trained on group operations. This universality involves coset circuits and approximate cosets.
509
510   It’s worth noting that researchers working on
511   new approaches to a theory for deep learning
512   have been in search of a model that appears
513   to have learned features of an error-correcting
514   code (Murfet, 2024). The aforementioned su-

      these features are contained. We can see in

521   logit output and the second largest output. This
522   is reinforced by Fig. 27 finding that all weights
523   in a cluster can have a substantial amount of
524   multiplicative noise injected, without destroy-
525   ing test accuracy.
