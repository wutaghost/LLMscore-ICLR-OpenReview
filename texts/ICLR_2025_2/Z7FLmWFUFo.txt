011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Exploration remains a critical challenge in reinforcement learning (RL), with
014            many existing methods either lacking theoretical guarantees or being computa-
015            tionally impractical for real-world applications. We introduce Litee, a lightweight
016
               algorithm that repurposes the value network in standard deep RL algorithms to
               effectively drive exploration without introducing additional parameters. Litee uti-
017
               lizes linear multi-armed bandit (MAB) techniques, enabling efficient exploration
018
               with provable sub-linear regret bounds while preserving the core structure of ex-
019            isting RL algorithms. Litee is simple to implement, requiring only around 10 lines
020            of code. It also substantially reduces computational overhead compared to pre-
021            vious theoretically grounded methods, lowering the complexity from O(n3 ) to
022            O(d3 ), where n is the number of network parameters and d is the size of the em-
023            bedding in the value network. Furthermore, we propose Litee+, an extension that
024            adds a small auxiliary network to better handle sparse reward environments, with
025            only a minor increase in parameter count (less than 1%) and additional 10 lines
026            of code. Experiments on the MiniHack suite and MuJoCo demonstrate that Litee
027
               and Litee+ empirically outperform state-of-the-art baselines, effectively bridging
               the gap between theoretical rigor and practical efficiency in RL exploration.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Exploration remains a fundamental challenge in reinforcement learning (RL), particularly in envi-
      ronments with sparse rewards or complex dynamics. Although algorithms such as DQN [26], PPO
034
      [34], SAC [13], DDPG [24], TD3 [12], and IMPALA [10] have demonstrated impressive perfor-
035
      mance on tasks like Atari games [25; 26], StarCraft [37], and Go [35], they often depend on rudi-
036   mentary exploration strategies. Common approaches, such as ϵ-greedy policies or injecting noise
037   into actions, are typically inefficient and can struggle in scenarios with delayed or sparse rewards.
038
039
      Various exploration methods have been proposed to improve performance and address the challenge
      of reward sparsity. For decades, exploration strategies with proven optimality in tabular settings have
040
      been available [20]. More recently, methods with provable regret bounds have been developed for
041
      scenarios involving function approximation, including linear functions [27; 28; 18; 19; 1], kernels
042   [40], and neural networks [40]. However, while linear and kernel-based approaches make strong
043   assumptions about the structure of RL functions, provable methods based on neural networks often
044   suffer from prohibitive computational costs—specifically O(n3 ) complexity, where n is the number
045   of parameters in the RL network—making these methods impractical for real-world applications.
046
      A more practical approach to exploration relies on heuristics, leading to the development of several
047
      empirically successful methods, such as Pseudocount [5], ICM [29], RND [6], RIDE [30], NovelD
048   [42], AGAC [11], and E3B [14; 15]. These methods typically use internally generated bonuses to
049   incentivize agents to explore novel states based on specific metrics. For instance, RND [6] utilizes
050   the prediction error of a randomly initialized target network as the exploration bonus, while RIDE
051   [30] combines the errors from forward and inverse dynamics models. However, these methods
052   lack theoretical guarantees and are primarily driven by intuitive heuristics. Furthermore, they often
053   require the training of additional networks beyond the standard value or policy networks in RL
      algorithms, which makes them computationally expensive.

071
072   In this work, we aim to combine the strengths of both theoretically grounded and empirically effec-
073   tive exploration methods. Provably efficient exploration strategies that leverage function approxi-
074   mation [18; 19; 40; 27; 28; 32] are fundamentally rooted in the theory of contextual Multi-Armed
075   Bandits (MAB) [22; 9; 2; 38; 43; 44]. Building on this foundation, we hypothesize that advanced
076   techniques from neural MAB can be effectively adapted for exploration in deep RL. Empirical re-
077   sults indicate that decoupling deep representation learning from exploration strategies, such as Upper
078
      Confidence Bound (UCB) or Thompson Sampling in linear MAB [41; 31; 39], shows promise for
      achieving efficient exploration in neural MAB.
079
080   Motivated by these insights, we propose Litee: a Lite exploration algorithm for deep RL. Unlike
081   existing methods [5; 6; 29; 29; 30; 14; 15], which require training additional embedding networks
082   for state representation, Litee directly utilizes the state embeddings of the existing value network in
083   the RL algorithm, applying linear MAB techniques for exploration. As a result, Litee introduces
084
      no new parameters beyond those already present in the original algorithm, demonstrating that RL
      algorithms inherently possess strong exploration capabilities when their learned networks are effec-
085
      tively leveraged. Moreover, Litee is simple to implement—requiring only around 10 lines of code.
086
      For more complex tasks, where learning from sparse rewards is especially challenging, Litee can
087   be enhanced by incorporating a small auxiliary network to accelerate the learning process. This
088   extended version, Litee+, results in only a minimal increase in parameter count (less than 1%) and
089   implementation effort (approximately 10 additional lines of code).
090
      We evaluated Litee+ and Litee on the MiniHack and MuJoCo benchmarks to assess their effective-
091
      ness in both sparse and dense reward environments. Litee either outperforms or at least matches the
092
      performance of state-of-the-art baseline methods such as PPO [34], SAC [13] and TD3 [12], which
093   are not specifically designed for exploration. In contrast, Litee+ consistently outperforms E3B [14],
094   the state-of-the-art exploration method for MiniHack, across all evaluated tasks, demonstrating su-
095   perior reliability and effectiveness in diverse reinforcement learning settings.
096
      In summary, we make three key contributions in this paper. First, we propose Litee, a lightweight
097
      exploration algorithm that integrates seamlessly with existing RL algorithms without introducing
098
      additional parameters, and extend it to Litee+ for improved performance in sparse-reward environ-
099   ments. Second, we provide theoretical guarantees, showing that any RL algorithm enhanced with
100   Litee achieves a sub-linear regret bound over episodes. Finally, we validate the effectiveness of
101   Litee and Litee+ through experiments on the MiniHack and MuJoCo benchmarks, demonstrating
102   their superior performance in both sparse and dense reward settings.

152   An episodic Markov Decision Process (MDP) is formally defined as a tuple (S, A, H, P, r), where
153   S denotes the state space and A is the action space. Integer H > 0 indicates the duration of each
154   episode. Functions P : S × A × S → [0, 1] and r : S × A → [0, 1] are the Markov transition and
155   reward functions, respectively. During an episode, the agent follows a policy π : S × A → [0, 1].
156
      At each time step h ∈ [H] in the episode, the agent observes the current state sh ∈ S and selects an
      action ah ∼ π(·|sh ) to execute, then the environment transits to the next state sh+1 ∼ P(·|sh , ah ),
157
      yielding an immediate reward rh = r(sh , ah ).

175   3.2    Litee: E XPLORATION WITH VALUE N ETWORK UNDER U NCERTAINTY
176
177   For the state-action pair (sh , ah ) at time step h, the approximated action-value Q(sh , ah ) is subject
178   to an uncertainty term β(sh , ah ). This uncertainty arises from the novelty or limited experience with
179   the particular state-action pair. Similar to MAB problems, it is essential to account for this uncer-
180   tainty when utilizing the latest approximated action-value function. Incorporating the uncertainty
181   term encourages exploration, ultimately improving long-term performance. Thus, the action-value
182
      function adjusted for uncertainty is given by Equation 2, where α ≥ 0 is the exploration coefficient:
183
184                                      Q(s, a) = θ T ϕ(s, a|W ) + αβ(·, ·).                                   (2)
185
186   However, defining β(·, ·) remains a significant challenge. Traditional MAB methods often attempt
187   to address this by either assuming a linear action-value function or relying on algorithms that require
188
      O(n3 ) computation time in terms of the number of parameters n in the action-value network. Both
      of these approaches have inherent drawbacks. Linearity may fail to capture the complexity of real-
189
      world tasks. On the other hand, algorithms with cubic computation time become impractical.
190
191   To overcome these limitations, we draw inspiration from Neural-LinUCB [39] and Neural-LinTS
192   [31], which effectively decouple representation learning from exploration. Building on this idea,
193   Litee adopts a similar approach, decomposing the action-value function into two distinct compo-
194
      nents. This decomposition follows the standard value network structure (Equation 1), while provid-
      ing a flexible and computationally efficient framework for balancing exploration and exploitation:
195
196
       • Network ϕ(s, a|W ) extracts the embeddings of state-action pair (s; a);
197
198
       • Q(s, a) = θ T ϕ(s, a|W ) is linear in the embedding of (s, a) with parameter θ.
199
      Consequently, MAB theory with the linearity assumption can be applied to the embedding ϕ(s, a)
200   for ∀s ∈ S and ∀a ∈ A. Simultaneously, the action-value function retains its representational
201   capacity through the neural network ϕ(s, a), ensuring promising empirical performance.

529   6   C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this paper, we introduced a lightweight exploration module, Litee, which seamlessly integrates
532   with existing reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms without adding extra parameters, making it
533   computationally efficient. Litee utilizes the state embeddings from the RL value network to drive
534   exploration, leaving the rest of the RL algorithm unchanged. We provided theoretical guarantees for
535   Litee, establishing a sub-linear regret bound in terms of the number of interaction episodes, demon-
536   strating its sample efficiency. For more complex tasks, we extended Litee to Litee+, incorporating
537   a small auxiliary network to accelerate learning with only a minimal increase in parameters. Our
538
      experiments on two benchmarks, MiniHack and MuJoCo, evaluated Litee in both sparse and dense
      reward settings, and the results demonstrate that Litee consistently outperforms state-of-the-art base-
539
      lines, bridging the gap between theoretical rigor and practical efficiency in RL exploration.
