009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            With the escalating demand for privacy-preserving machine learning, federated
012            learning (FL) stands out by enabling collaboration among decentralized entities.
013            Utilizing graph representations of data enhances learning for graph-level tasks,
014            crucial for FL with data distributed across local repositories. Despite its bene-
015            fits, stringent privacy regulations often compromise FL’s performance. Previous
016
               methods aimed at ensuring privacy introduce performance degradation and com-
               putational overhead. In response to these challenges, we propose using graph
017
               coarsening—a simple yet effective method—to enhance the security and privacy
018
               of FL on graph data. Our approach posits that graph coarsening alone can suffice
019            for privacy guarantees, as model parameters obtained from training on the coars-
020            ened graph effectively conceal sensitive information susceptible to privacy attacks.
021            Through comprehensive application and analysis, we demonstrate the efficacy
022            of graph coarsening within an FL setup, taking both the graph matrix and node
023            features as input, and jointly learning the coarsened graph matrix and feature matrix
024            while ensuring desired properties. The resultant coarsened graph representations
025            are then utilized to train model parameters, subsequently communicated within an
026            FL framework for downstream tasks such as classification. Extensive experimenta-
027
               tion across various datasets confirms that graph coarsening ensures privacy while
               enhancing performance with minimal trade-offs compared to traditional differential
028
               privacy (DP) methods without adding extra complexity overhead.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Federated learning (FL) is a distributed machine learning approach enabling multiple decentralized
034   entities to collaboratively train a shared model without exchanging their local data, thus preserving
035   data privacy by keeping raw data localized and only sharing model updates [41]. This paradigm
036   offers enhanced data privacy, reduced latency, and the ability to leverage diverse datasets from
037   multiple sources, resulting in robust and generalizable models [43]. However, FL faces significant
038   privacy challenges, as the exchange of model updates can still inadvertently leak sensitive information
039
      through inference attacks [26]. Addressing these concerns, methods like differential privacy (DP)
      [34; 13], secure multi-party computation [28; 16], and homomorphic encryption [24; 15] have been
040
      proposed, each with its trade-offs [19] such as reduced model performance, increased computational
041
      complexity, and added communication overhead [37; 33]. Graph representation is crucial in capturing
042   relationships between entities, aiding in tasks like graph classification and prediction [40]. For
043   instance, in molecular research [9], graphs predict properties or classify enzymes [12], while in social
044   networks [22], graphs facilitate community detection. In the context of graph data, FL involves clients
045   each with a local graph represented by a set of nodes and edges [11]. Each client collaboratively
046   trains a global graph neural network (GNN) model by sharing model parameters or gradients rather
047   than raw graph data [3].
048   Privacy attacks in FL threaten the confidentiality of graph data, even when raw data isn’t exchanged.
049   Adversaries can infer sensitive information from shared model updates, such as through gradient
050   inversion attacks where gradients are used to reconstruct private data [31]. For example, in a federated
051   graph machine learning scenario where healthcare institutions collaborate to predict patient outcomes,
052   an attacker could infer sensitive patient information from the model updates [30; 17; 14]. This
053   highlights the urgent need for robust privacy-preserving techniques in FL. Current methods for
      privacy preservation in FL are categorized into data manipulation and model gradient manipulation


                                                         1
054
      techniques [32; 30]. Data manipulation includes secure multi-party computation (SMPC) and
055   homomorphic encryption (HE), which ensure computations are carried out without revealing raw
056   data [7]. Model gradient manipulation, particularly DP, adds controlled noise to model updates,
057   with differential privacy stochastic gradient descent (DP-SGD) being a notable method [35; 27].
058   However, these methods face significant limitations: the trade-off between privacy and model accuracy
059   increased computational complexity, and potential performance degradation [1]. For instance, in fraud
060   detection using graph data, the noise added by DP can impair the detection accuracy, revealing the
061   limitations of DP in complex FL scenarios [25]. Addressing these challenges is crucial to advancing
062   privacy-preserving FL for graph data.
063   Data-based approaches for privacy preservation in FL, including data condensation and data reduction,
064   have been effective for non-graph and graph data [10]. Data condensation (DC) involves summarizing
065   a large dataset into a smaller synthetic dataset that retains essential statistical properties, providing
066   privacy by limiting the impact of individual samples on model parameters, effectively offering DP
067   [8]. Data reduction techniques reduce the dimensionality or amount of data, thereby minimizing the
068   exposure of sensitive information [5]. However, these methods face limitations when applied to graph
069
      data due to its intricate structure. To address this, we propose using graph coarsening as a privacy
      measure. Graph coarsening simplifies a graph by merging nodes and edges, creating a smaller version
070
      that preserves essential structural properties [4]. Reconstructing the original graph from its coarsened
071
      version is challenging, thus protecting sensitive information. In our approach, clients coarsen their
072   local graphs before training local models, which are then shared with the server for aggregation,
073   maintaining privacy with minimal performance trade-offs. We focus on featured graph coarsening
074   (FGC) [18] due to its simplicity and flexibility. FGC ensures that coarsened graphs retain necessary
075   information, allowing for effective model training while ensuring privacy. This method integrates
076   seamlessly into the FL framework, providing a robust solution for privacy preservation in federated
077   graph machine learning.
078   Our main contributions are summarized below:
079
080          • We introduce Graph Coarsening for Privacy-Preserving Federated Learning (CPFL), a
081            novel framework integrating graph coarsening techniques into FL to enhance privacy while
082            maintaining performance balance.
083          • CPFL ensures privacy without additional communication overhead by training local models
084
               on coarsened graphs, minimizing data transmission between clients and the server.
085
             • We built the connection between graph coarsening and differential privacy and validated our
               approach’s robust privacy preservation and effectiveness across various scenarios, including
086
               cross-domain datasets in multi-client and multi-dataset settings.

      2.2   G RAPH C OMPRESSION : P RIVACY-P RESERVING A PPROACHES IN FL
135
      In FL, multiple client devices collaboratively train a shared model while keeping their local data
136   decentralized, enhancing data privacy by preventing direct data sharing. However, privacy risks
137   persist as model updates can leak sensitive information through gradient inversion attacks [36]. To
138   mitigate these risks, various data manipulation techniques, such as random sparsification and dataset
139   condensation, have been proposed [23; 42]. Random sparsification involves randomly removing
140   edges to achieve anonymity and can ensure DP while approximating the original graph’s spectrum
141   [6]. However, there is a trade-off between the degree of sparsification and the preservation of critical
142   information, which impacts the graph’s utility. Excessive edge removal can compromise the graph’s
143   structural integrity and informative value. Dataset condensation, on the other hand, transforms the
144
      dataset into a smaller, abstract representation while preserving essential features [8; 39]. In FL,
      condensed local graphs on client devices reduce the risk of sensitive information leakage during
145
      aggregation. By synthesizing representative data points instead of sharing actual data, condensation
146
      maintains privacy and mitigates re-identification risks.
147
148   Despite the advantages, these techniques have limitations. Sparsification selects existing graph
149   elements, which may reduce interpretability and relevance to the original graph. Condensation,
150
      while effective, can struggle to balance the trade-off between privacy and the preservation of key
      information, leading to potential losses in data utility. Additionally, condensation methods can
151
      introduce artifacts that deviate from the original data’s natural structure, impacting downstream
152
      machine learning tasks [10].
153
154   Graph coarsening presents a compelling alternative to sparsification and condensation for privacy
155   preservation in FL. Graph coarsening aggregates graph elements into supernodes and superedges,
156
      making it challenging to reconstruct the original graph, thus enhancing privacy. The coarsened graph
      retains the critical information necessary for effective model training while significantly reducing the
157
      risk of sensitive information leakage. Moreover, graph coarsening can mitigate the issue of relating
158
      back to the original graph, which is a common concern with sparsification and condensation methods.
159
160   One established method for graph coarsening is FGC [18]. Given an original graph G = (V, E, X, L),

185   3.1 P RIVACY AND T RADE - OFFS IN G RAPH DATA R EDUCTION T ECHNIQUES
186   In FL, preserving data privacy while maintaining the utility of graph data for downstream tasks is
187   a significant challenge. DP [27] provides a mathematical framework to ensure that the inclusion
188   or exclusion of a single element (such as a node or edge in a graph) does not significantly affect
189   the outcome of an analysis. Formally, a randomized algorithm M satisfies (ϵ, δ)-DP if, for any two
190   neighboring graphs G and G′ (differing by one node or edge), and for any subset S of the output
191
      space, the following inequality holds:
192
193
                                      P [M (G) ∈ S] ≤ eϵ P [M (G′ ) ∈ S] + δ                                  (6)
194
195   Here, ϵ represents the privacy budget, quantifying the allowed privacy loss, and δ accounts for the
196   probability of the privacy guarantee being broken.
197
      However, traditional reduction techniques like sparsification and condensation, while enhancing
198
      privacy, often compromise the utility of the graph data. In tasks such as protein-protein interaction
199
      networks, road networks, and social network analysis, removing nodes or edges can strip away
200   critical information, degrading the graph’s utility. Condensation methods synthesize fake elements,
201   potentially distorting the graph’s original structure and affecting tasks that rely on precise interactions,
202   such as drug discovery.
203
      One promising solution here is graph coarsening which retains essential structural properties and
204
      minimises the risk of reconstructing the original graph. It preserves data utility while enhancing
205
      privacy, making it ideal for FL environments where both are critical. Visualization of graph coarsening
206   is given in Figure 1.

      4.2   C LASSIFICATION ACCURACY: CCPL VS DP-SDG
379
380   We present classification accuracy using graph coarsening and differentially private stochastic gradient
381   descent (DP-SGD) [27] in FL settings. DP-SGD, the gold standard for privacy-preserving machine
382   learning, adds calibrated noise to gradients to protect individual data points, quantified by the privacy
383   budget (ϵ)—lower values indicate stronger privacy. We evaluated performance based on varying
384   coarsening ratios (r) and privacy budgets (ϵ), also assessing effectiveness in heterogeneous data
385   settings by combining datasets from Molecules, Proteins, and Social Networks.
386   Our evaluation shows that graph coarsening often outperforms DP-SGD in maintaining classification
387   accuracy across different datasets and settings. For example, in single-data multi-client settings (given
388   in Table 1) like the PROTEINS dataset, graph coarsening achieved 0.62 accuracy with FedProx,
389   compared to 0.62 with DP-SGD and 0.75 with the original dataset. In the IMDB-(B) dataset, graph
390   coarsening achieved 0.72 with FedAvg, compared to 0.47 with DP-SGD. In the multi-data multi-
391   client setting given in Table 2, for the Molecules group, although DP values indicated that FedProx
392   handled privacy better with a DP of 0.67, the consistent performance of graph coarsening techniques
393   underscores their viability in heterogeneous settings. In multi-data multi-client multi-domain settings
394
      shown in Table 3, graph coarsening showed significant advantages, such as in the MIX 2 setting

      a compelling choice for privacy-preserving FL, especially where high model performance is critical.

411   4.3   E VALUATING U TILITY OF CPFL FOR P RIVACY E NHANCEMENT
412
413   The two subfigures, 4 (a) and 4 (b), illustrate how the percentage of nodes and edges concealed varies
414   with increasing coarsening ratios (r) across three datasets: MUTAG, PROTEINS, and Reddit (M). As
415   the coarsening ratio increases, a higher percentage of nodes and edges are concealed, indicating a
416   reduced level of granularity in the graph representation. Concealing a greater proportion of nodes
417
      and edges by increasing the coarsening ratio directly reduces the level of detail in the graph. This
      results in a decrease in L2 -sensitivity, as each node or edge has a diminished influence on the graph’s
418
      overall structure. Lower L2 -sensitivity means that the impact of modifying or omitting a single node
419
      or edge is minimized, making it harder for an adversary to distinguish whether a particular node or
420   edge was part of the original graph. In differential privacy, the parameter ϵ quantifies the level of
421   privacy provided. Lower L2 -sensitivity translates to a smaller ∆f (the sensitivity parameter), which
422   means that for a fixed amount of noise, the effective ϵ value will decrease. As the figures show that a
423   higher coarsening ratio results in more concealed nodes and edges, it implies a reduced sensitivity,
424   thereby strengthening privacy guarantees (lower ϵ).
425
      L2 -sensitivity measures the maximum change in a function’s output when a single input data point
426   is modified. For graph data, it indicates how much model output or gradient values change when
427   a specific node or edge is altered. By coarsening the graph, the number of nodes and edges is
428   reduced, effectively lowering L2 -sensitivity, which decreases the potential for inferring specific
429   details from the original graph structure. In the context of differential privacy, lower L2 -sensitivity
430   means that achieving a given privacy level (ϵ) requires less noise, thus preserving model utility.
431   As observed in Figure 4(c), L2 -sensitivity decreases consistently with higher coarsening ratios for
      various datasets, confirming that graph coarsening can act as a natural privacy mechanism. This

      In this work, we introduced a graph coarsening technique as a privacy measure in federated learning
518   environments for graph data. This approach facilitates private and secure collaborative training of
519   advanced graph models, such as neural networks for classification, without the need for direct data
520   exchange. Our extensive experiments show that the tradeoff associated with this privacy measure
521   is minimal compared to traditional methods like differential privacy. Moreover, since this measure
522   is applied as a preprocessing step, it incurs minimal computational overhead, ensuring that the
523   performance of round-wise communication in federated learning remains unaffected. While our study
524   primarily focuses on protecting against graph reconstruction attacks, future research should investigate
525   other potential attacks in federated learning for graph data. We believe this work lays the groundwork
526
      for further studies on privacy-preserving techniques through client-side data manipulation and the
      development of methods to evaluate their effectiveness.

      A.1    DATASET D ESCRIPTION

706   We have utilized a diverse set of datasets from three distinct subcategories: Molecules, Proteins, and
707   Social Networks. Each dataset varies in terms of the number of classes, graphs, average nodes, and
708   average edges, providing a broad spectrum for analysis and classification tasks.
709   In the Molecules subcategory, we have the following datasets: Mutag, BZR, COX2, DHFR, and
710   PTC_MR. Each of these datasets contains 2 classes. Mutag consists of 188 graphs with an average of
711   17.93 nodes and 19.79 edges per graph. BZR has 405 graphs, averaging 35.75 nodes and 38.36 edges
712   per graph. COX2 includes 467 graphs with an average of 41.22 nodes and 43.45 edges per graph.
713   DHFR comprises 756 graphs, with averages of 42.43 nodes and 44.54 edges per graph. PTC_MR has
714   344 graphs, with an average of 14.29 nodes and 14.69 edges per graph.
715   For the Proteins subcategory, we employed the ENZYMES, DD, and PROTEINS datasets. EN-
716   ZYMES is the most diverse with 6 classes, encompassing 600 graphs, each averaging 32.63 nodes
717   and 62.14 edges. DD has 2 classes and includes 1178 graphs with a high average of 284.32 nodes
718   and 715.66 edges per graph. The PROTEINS dataset also has 2 classes, consisting of 1113 graphs,
719   with averages of 39.06 nodes and 72.82 edges per graph.

      In the Social Networks subcategory, we used datasets such as COLLAB, Reddit (M), IMDB (B),
721   IMDB (M), and Reddit (B). COLLAB has 3 classes and includes 5000 graphs with an average of
722   74.49 nodes and 2457.78 edges per graph. Reddit (M) is more diverse with 5 classes, consisting of
723   5000 graphs with an average of 508.29 nodes and 594.87 edges per graph. IMDB (B) includes 2
724   classes and 1000 graphs, each averaging 19.77 nodes and 96.53 edges. IMDB (M) has 3 classes,
725   encompassing 1500 graphs with averages of 13 nodes and 65.94 edges. Lastly, Reddit (B) contains 2
726   classes and 2000 graphs, each averaging 429.63 nodes and 497.75 edges.

749   Single Data Multi-Client (SDMC) Setting In the SDMC setting, we have a single graph dataset
750   distributed across multiple clients. Each client holds a portion of the graph data. The goal is to train a
751   graph machine learning model while respecting data privacy and without centralizing the data.
752
      Multi Data Multi-Client (MDMC) Setting In the MDMC setting, we have multiple graph datasets,
753
      each owned by different clients. The goal is to train a federated graph machine learning model that
754
      can generalize across different graphs while maintaining data privacy.
