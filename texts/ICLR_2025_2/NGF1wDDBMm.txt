009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Machine unlearning (MU) aims to remove the influence of specific data from trained
012            models, addressing privacy concerns and ensuring compliance with regulations
013            such as the “right to be forgotten.” Evaluating strong unlearning, where the
014            unlearned model is indistinguishable from one retrained without the forgetting
015            data, remains a significant challenge in deep neural networks (DNNs). Common
016
               black-box metrics, such as variants of membership inference attacks and accuracy
               comparisons, primarily assess model outputs but often fail to capture residual
017
               information in intermediate layers. To bridge this gap, we introduce the Information
018
               Difference Index (IDI), a novel white-box metric inspired by information theory.
019            IDI quantifies retained information in intermediate features by measuring mutual
020            information between those features and the labels to be forgotten, offering a more
021            comprehensive assessment of unlearning efficacy. Our experiments demonstrate
022            that IDI effectively measures the degree of unlearning across various datasets and
023            architectures, providing a reliable tool for evaluating strong unlearning in DNNs.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      Machine unlearning (MU) seeks to remove the impact of specific data samples from a trained model,
028
      addressing privacy issues such as “right to be forgotten” (Cao and Yang, 2015; Voigt and Von dem
029
      Bussche, 2017). In addition to privacy, MU is also emerging as a tool to eliminate the influence of
030   corrupted or outdated data used during training (Nguyen et al., 2022; Kurmanji et al., 2023). The
031   most straightforward approach to MU is exact unlearning, where the model is retrained from scratch,
032   excluding the data that need to be forgotten. Although this method ensures complete data removal,
033   it is computationally expensive and not scalable (Aldaghri et al., 2021; Bourtoule et al., 2021; Yan
034   et al., 2022). Consequently, research has shifted towards approximate unlearning, which aims to
035   replicate the effects of retraining in a more efficient manner.
036   The goal of MU is to create an unlearned model that is indistinguishable from a model retrained
037   from scratch, referred to as strong unlearning. This objective has become particularly crucial with
038   the rise of open-source models like Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) and LLaMA (Touvron
039   et al., 2023), which are widely used and fine-tuned by various users. For unlearning algorithms to
040   be practically useful, they must be capable of fully eliminating traces of private data and preventing
041   potential exploitation. While (ϵ, δ)-certified unlearning methods (Zhang et al., 2024b; Mu and
042   Klabjan, 2024) provide theoretical guarantees, they are often impractical for large-scale models. As a
043   result, most approximate unlearning methods rely on heuristic approaches, lacking formal guarantees.
044
      Thus, these methods must undergo empirical evaluation to demonstrate their effectiveness.
045   However, current evaluations, primarily based on black-box approaches such as membership infer-
046   ence attacks (MIA) (Shokri et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2022) and accuracy comparisons, focus on
047   output similarity rather than internal model changes. Although these metrics may capture weak
048   unlearning (Fan et al., 2024; Jia et al., 2023; Chundawat et al., 2023b; Foster et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
049   2023), they may not be sufficient for assessing strong unlearning. In this work, we investigate whether
050
      relying solely on outputs can truly reflect complete influence removal, considering that outputs can
      be superficially adjusted without impacting internal representations (Kirichenko et al., 2023).
051
052   Surprisingly, our experiments reveal that even minimal changes to the model, such as modifying
053   only the final layer while preserving all information in the intermediate layers, can still satisfy the
      black-box evaluation metrics, exposing their limitations in assessing strong unlearning. This finding

054
      also raises critical concerns about whether current MU methods genuinely achieve information
055   removal comparable to retraining from scratch, despite yielding similar model outputs.
056
057   Consequently, motivated by the Information Bottleneck principle (Tishby et al., 2000; Tishby and
058
      Zaslavsky, 2015), we introduce the information difference index (IDI), a novel white-box metric
      designed to quantify residual information in intermediate layers after unlearning. IDI measures
059
      the mutual information (Shannon, 1948) between intermediate features and the forgetting labels,
060
      providing an interpretable value to assess the effectiveness of unlearning algorithms. We observe that
061   IDI remains robust despite the inherent stochasticity of the unlearning process, and is model-agnostic,
062   ensuring adaptability to various architectures. Additionally, estimating IDI with a data subset yields
063   reliable results, enhancing its practicality. To our knowledge, IDI is the first robust white-box metric
064   designed to evaluate unlearning quality, addressing a crucial yet underexplored aspect of the field.
065
      Through the application of IDI, we find that many recent MU methods, despite their strong per-
066
      formance on black-box metrics, still retain significant information about the forgetting data within
067   intermediate layers. Building on the insights gained from IDI, we introduce COLapse-and-Align
068   (COLA), a simple method that first collapses feature representations to be forgotten and then re-aligns
069   retain features to address residual information in unlearning processes. Despite its simplicity, COLA
070   serves as a useful benchmark, demonstrating notable improvements in IDI scores compared to other
071   methods on datasets such as CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet-1K, as well as architectures like
072   ResNet-18, ResNet-50, and ViT. Notably, COLA achieves this without access to the full training
073   dataset, unlike several existing methods. The ability of IDI to capture COLA’s impact on intermediate
074   features further underscores its value as a robust efficacy metric.
075   We summarize our contributions as follows: First, we identify the limitations of existing black-
076   box metrics, which overlook residual information in intermediate layers. Second, we introduce
077   the information difference index (IDI), an interpretable white-box metric that quantifies mutual
078   information between intermediate features and labels. Third, we validate the robustness of IDI
079   through extensive experiments on diverse datasets and model architectures. Finally, using the
080   COLapse-and-Align (COLA) method as a baseline, we show that IDI effectively captures residual
081
      information in intermediate features, proving its value as a reliable metric for unlearning quality.

083   2     P ROBLEM S TATEMENT AND P RELIMINARIES
084
085   2.1   P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
086
087   Let D = {(xi , yi )}N i=1 denote a training dataset comprising N image-label pairs (xi , yi ). In a
088   supervised learning setup, D is partitioned into two subsets: the forget set Df , containing the data
089   points to be removed, and the retain set Dr = D ∖ Df , containing the data points to be preserved. The
090   initial model θo , referred to as the Original model, is trained on the full dataset D using empirical
091   risk minimization. The Retrain model θr is trained from scratch on only the retain set Dr . The
092   unlearned model θu is obtained by applying a machine unlearning (MU) algorithm to the Original
093
      model θo , aiming to remove the influence of Df . The goal of MU is for θu to closely approximate θr ,
      ensuring the unlearned model behaves as though Df had never been used in training, while preserving
094
      the training methodology across θo , θr , and θu .
095
096   MU is often studied in the context of image classification (Shaik et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2022),
097   where it is typically classified into two scenarios based on the nature of the forget set: class-wise
098   forgetting, where all samples from a specific class are targeted, and random data forgetting, where
099
      samples are selected indiscriminately across all classes.
100   Throughout the paper, within a given model θ, we define the head as the last few layers responsible
101   for classification; typically one to three linear layers. The encoder, on the contrary, encompasses the
102   remainder of the network, which usually consists of convolutional layers or transformer encoders.
103
104   2.2   P RELIMINARIES
105
106   Machine Unlearning (MU). Exact unlearning, which involves creating Retrain, guarantees the
107   information removal from the forget set but is computationally expensive (Bourtoule et al., 2021;
      Yan et al., 2022; Aldaghri et al., 2021; Brophy and Lowd, 2021). To address this, approximate


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      unlearning methods have been developed, focusing on efficiency rather than strict theoretical guaran-
109   tees. Specifically, strong unlearning, where the unlearned model is indistinguishable from Retrain,
110   has been explored through the application of differential privacy (DP) (Dwork and Roth, 2014)
111   inspired techniques, which aim to achieve parameter-level indistinguishability (Dwork and Roth,
112   2014; Ginart et al., 2019; Neel et al., 2021; Sekhari et al., 2021; Ullah et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2020).
113   However, applying such techniques to deep neural networks (DNNs) remains challenging due to their
114   vast number of parameters and non-convex loss landscapes (Qiao et al., 2024). As a result, recent
115   studies typically assess the similarity of model outputs (i.e., predictions), using weak unlearning as a
116   practical proxy for strong unlearning (Xu et al., 2023). While empirically ensuring strong unlearning
117
      is challenging, it is critical for deploying unlearning algorithms to meet legal requirements like
      GDPR (Voigt and Von dem Bussche, 2017), the “right to be forgotten”, and prevent retention of
118
      sensitive data, particularly with the growing use of open-source models like CLIP (Radford et al.,
119
      2021), Stable-Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), and LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023), where data could
120   unintentionally persist and be exploited. Our work focuses on developing a robust empirical metric to
121   evaluate unlearning algorithms, distinct from verification (Zhang et al., 2024a; Sommer et al., 2022),
122   which focuses on real-world attack scenarios to validate the effectiveness of unlearning.
123
124   Evaluation Criteria in MU. As the goal of MU is to remove the influence of specific data while
125   preserving the others, the unlearning algorithms are typically evaluated on three criteria: Efficacy,
126   Accuracy, and Efficiency (Hayes et al., 2024). Efficacy measures how closely the unlearned model
127   approximates Retrain, which is key to unlearning quality. Accuracy ensures task performance remains
128   intact after unlearning, while efficiency ensures the unlearning process is faster than retraining.
129   Accuracy and efficiency can be easily evaluated using existing metrics. Accuracy consists of three
130   categories: unlearning accuracy (UA), remaining accuracy (RA), and testing accuracy (TA). UA
131   measures performance on Df as U A(θu ) = 1 − AccDf (θu ), RA on Dr as RA(θu ) = AccDr (θu ),
132   and TA measures generalization to unseen data as T A(θu ) = AccDtest (θu ). Performance levels
133
      comparable to Retrain across these metrics indicate better unlearning. To simplify comparisons,
      Cotogni et al. (2023) proposed AUS, which combines UA and TA into a single accuracy measure.
134
      In terms of efficiency, runtime efficiency (RTE) measures the time an algorithm takes to complete
135
      unlearning, with lower RTE indicating more efficient unlearning (Fan et al., 2024; Jia et al., 2023).
136   However, assessing unlearning efficacy, or determining whether the unlearned model has fully
137   removed the influence of specific data to the same extent as Retrain, remains a significant challenge in
138   complex DNNs. The efficacy metrics are divided into two categories: black-box metrics, which focus
139   solely on model outputs (i.e., predictions), and white-box metrics, which examine internal dynamics
140   such as parameters, gradients, and features. While black-box metrics are typically used due to their
141   convenience, no universally accepted standard exists, leaving room for more reliable assessment.
142
143   Black-box Efficacy Metrics. Variants of membership inference attacks (MIA) (Shokri et al., 2017;
144   Carlini et al., 2022) are the most widely used black-box metrics (Shen et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024;
145   Fan et al., 2024; Jia et al., 2023; Foster et al., 2024). MIA determines whether specific data were
146   part of the training set by training an auxiliary classifier, with attack success rates on the forget set
147   that are close to those of Retrain being preferred. It is worth noting that recent works often use a
148
      combination of UA, RA, TA, MIA and RTE as metrics for evaluating unlearning performance across
      the three criteria (Chen et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024; Jia et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024), collectively
149
      referred to as the ‘full-stack’ evaluation scheme (Jia et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024). Other metrics,
150
      such as Jensen-Shannon divergence (JSD), and ZRF (Chundawat et al., 2023b; Poppi et al., 2024)
151   compare the output logits between the unlearned model and Retrain (or a random model for ZRF).
152   Additionally, time-based metrics like Anamnesis Index (AIN) (Chundawat et al., 2023a; Tarun et al.,
153   2023a) and relearn time (RT) (Tarun et al., 2023b; Golatkar et al., 2020a;b; 2021) measure the time (or
154   epochs) required for the unlearned model to regain performance on the forget set. Black-box metrics,
155   though convenient, overlook internal behaviors and cannot verify strong unlearning by ensuring
156   forgetting data’s influence is fully removed. Section 3 highlights their limitations.
157
158   White-box Efficacy Metrics. In contrast, white-box metrics offer a more detailed evaluation by
159   analyzing internal model dynamics to track residual influence. Previous studies have measured
160   parameter-wise distances (e.g., ℓ2 -distance, KL-divergence) between the unlearned model and
161   Retrain (Golatkar et al., 2020a; Wu et al., 2020). However, this approach is computationally expensive
      and unreliable due to the inherent randomness in DNN training (Hayes et al., 2024; Goel et al., 2022).


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Becker and Liebig (2022) proposed a Fisher information based metric, but their experimental results
163   were inconsistent with theoretical intuition. Graves et al. (2021) applied model inversion attacks to
164   reconstruct images from the forget set, but this method relies on qualitative comparisons, making
165   it difficult to compare across different algorithms. Although robust white-box metrics are currently
166   lacking and challenging to develop, they are crucial for validating approximate methods, which
167   often lack guarantees of complete information removal. Without such metrics, these methods cannot
168   be trusted in privacy-sensitive applications that demand a high level of confidence in information
169   removal. To address this critical need, our work proposes a reliable and practical white-box metric.

      Current MU efficacy evaluations, which rely primarily on black-box metrics, overlook residual
287
      information in intermediate layers, as shown in Section 3. To address this, we measure residual

      4.1   Q UANTIFYING R ESIDUAL I NFORMATION WITH M UTUAL I NFORMATION

293   To quantify the relationship between high dimensional intermediate features and data labels, we utilize
294   Shannon’s mutual information (MI), a robust measure that effectively captures variable dependencies
                                                                        (u)    (r)
295   across various dimensional complexities. For an input X, let Zℓ and Zℓ denote the features from
296   the ℓ-th layer of the total L-layer encoder in the unlearned model and Retrain, respectively. Let

408   5.1   P ROPOSED BASELINE : COL LAPSE AND A LIGN (COLA)
409
      As discussed in both Section 3 and 4.2, we observed residual information in the intermediate layers
410
      of several unlearned models, despite their outputs being similar to those of Retrain. To address this,
411
      we propose a robust two-step unlearning framework, COLlapse and Align (COLA), consisting of a
412   collapse phase and an alignment phase to directly remove residual information at the feature level.
413
414   During the collapse phase, COLA eliminates feature-level information by applying supervised
415
      contrastive loss (Khosla et al., 2020) to encoder outputs. Rather than dispersing features from the
      forget set, which could harm model performance, COLA applies the loss to the retain set, promoting
416
      tight intra-class clustering. As these clusters shrink, features from the forget set are forced to collapse
417
      into the clusters of the retain set, achieving catastrophic forgetting. After feature collapsing, the
418   alignment phase optimizes the entire model using cross-entropy loss on the retain set to align the
419   encoder and head. For an illustration of COLA, as well as COLA+, a method tailored for random
420   data forgetting, and detailed loss formulations, refer to Appendix C.6.
421

483   IDI as a Real-World Efficacy Metric. Accuracy metrics (UA, RA, TA) and efficacy metrics
484   (MIA, JSD), commonly used in recent unlearning studies, require the presence of Retrain as a gold
485   standard to compare model outputs. While this approach is crucial for advancing MU methods in
      controlled experimental settings, where the field of unlearning for DNNs is still in its infancy, it

486

487   black-box metrics, the original formulation of IDI (see Equations 1 and 2) uses Retrain as a reference

489   model as the reference. Although the absence of Retrain changes the interpretation of IDI (i.e., an
490   IDI of zero means complete unlearning as Retrain), it still provides valuable insights relative to the
491   chosen reference. This adaptability enhances the IDI’s practicality, making it useful for evaluating
492   unlearned models even in real-world scenarios. A detailed explanation and examples are provided

      We highlight the limitations of relying on black-box metrics to assess unlearning efficacy in typical
534   approximate unlearning studies. Although intermediate features capable of reconstructing forgotten
535   information persist, these metrics fail to capture the key aspects required for strong unlearning,
536   often misleading evaluations. To address this, we introduce the Information Difference Index (IDI)
537   from an information-theoretic perspective, alongside the contrastive-based COLA baseline for direct
538   feature-level unlearning. Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate the validity and practicality

704
      Another scenario in machine unlearning (MU) is random data forgetting, which involves forgetting a
705   randomly selected subset of data across multiple classes. This differs from the class-wise forgetting
706   task, which aims to forget entire data from single or multiple classes.

708   A.1    I NFORMATION D IFFERENCE I NDEX FOR R ANDOM DATA F ORGETTING

713   each class is targeted for forgetting. As a result, no single class is completely removed. To address
714
      this, we transform the binary label Y into a multiclass label YC , which reflects the ground-truth class
      label of each sample. Consequently, we define the IDI for random data forgetting as follows:

      A.2    COLA+
727
728   The core idea behind COLA is to induce catastrophic forgetting within the model’s encoder in the
729   collapse phase, making the influence of the forget set vanish implicitly. This approach is effective
730   for class-wise forgetting tasks, where the forget set includes distinct classes. However, it may be
731   less effective for random data forgetting, where the forget set and retain set samples generally share
732   the same classes and are not easily distinguishable. To address this, we aim to explicitly remove
733   the information of the forget set through pseudo-labeling. This variant, called COLA+, assigns the
734
      second-highest predicted label to the forget set samples before unlearning with supervised contrastive
      loss (Khosla et al., 2020). This pseudo-labeling effectively collapsing the forget set features into
735
      the retain set clusters of other classes, while reducing the confusion of the knowledge of the retain
736
      set. The results of the COLA+ experiment on the random data forgetting task are presented in
737   Appendix D.5.

1365   Our work on improving machine unlearning focuses on foundational research aimed at enhancing
1366   privacy and data removal. However, there is a potential risk that our methodology could be misused
1367   to evade data retention policies or obscure accountability. Despite this possibility, it is unlikely that
1368   our work will introduce new harmful practices beyond what existing unlearning methods already
1369   permit, as we are not introducing new capabilities. Therefore, while there might be concerns related
1370   to privacy, security, and fairness, our work does not pose a greater risk compared to other foundational

       research in machine unlearning.

1373   G         L IMITATIONS
1374
1375   Our methodology accomplishes its main objective, but there are a few limitations we point out.
1376   Although our IDI successfully investigates hidden information in intermediate features, its compu-
1377   tation requires multiple training runs, which can be computationally intensive. For instance, The
1378   computation of IDI for ResNet-50 on the CIFAR-100 dataset takes approximately 40-50 minutes.
1379   However, one can mitigate this by computing mutual information for only the last few layers, as
1380
       the early stages of the encoder are largely similar for both the Retrain and Original models. Thus,
       this approach requires fine-tuning only the later layers, reducing the overall computational burden.
1381
       Additionally, by adjusting the forget-to-retain ratio, it is possible to improve efficiency and possibly
1382
       decrease the processing time to merely 3-4 minutes.
