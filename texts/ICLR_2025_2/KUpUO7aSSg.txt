009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Object detection has wide applications in agriculture, but the trained models often
012            struggle to generalize across diverse agricultural environments. To address this
013            challenge, we propose DODA (Diffusion for Object-detection Domain Adaptation
014            in Agriculture), a unified framework that leverages diffusion models to gener-
015            ate domain-specific detection data for multiple agricultural scenarios. DODA
016            incorporates external domain embeddings and an improved layout-to-image (L2I)
017            approach, allowing it to generate high-quality detection data for new domains
018
               without additional training. We demonstrate DODA’s effectiveness on the Global
               Wheat Head Detection dataset, where fine-tuning detectors on DODA-generated
019
               data yields significant improvements across multiple domains (maximum +15.6
020
               AP). DODA provides a simple yet powerful approach to adapt object detectors to
021            diverse agricultural scenarios, lowering barriers for more plant breeders growers to
022            use detection in their personalized environments.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Object detection has been widely used in various aspects of agriculture, such as yield estimation
028   (Wang et al., 2022b;c), disease identification (Wu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2020), and decision-
029   making support (Bazame et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023b). These applications can improve the
030   efficiency and profitability of plant breeders and farmers to improve their efficiency and profits.
031   However, these models are built for specific settings, farms, crop varieties and management systems
032   and excel primarily in their specific settings. However, agricultural scenarios are very diverse, and
033
      domain shifts caused by factors such as crop varieties, growth stages, cultivation management, and
      imaging pipeline, making directly applying a given model to new environments unfeasible.
034
035   The diversity of agricultural scenes makes domain adaptation (DA) a key focus in this field. DA can
036   be divided into instance-level DA and image-level DA (Ma et al., 2022) (see related work for more
037   details). Instance-level DA (Ma et al., 2021) typically involves training a discriminator to separate
038   domain-specific features, aligning the features across source and target domains. On the other hand,
039
      image-level DA (Gogoll et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021) aligns the image styles of the two domains
      through GAN (Goodfellow et al., 2014) or Fourier transform (Yang & Soatto, 2020), but the general
040
      shape and distribution of objects (both foreground and background) in the transformed image are
041
      basically fixed. To achieve optimal results, additional instance-level DA is often required to address
042   the remaining differences (Ma et al., 2022). For new scenes, instance-level DA and GAN-based
043   methods require retraining the entire network, which limits scalability and substantial technical
044   barriers remain for non-experts.
045
      Recently, diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022) have attracted attention for their
046
      ability to generate high-fidelity and novel images that do not appear in the training set. A growing
047
      number of studies explore the potential for diffusion models to address data-related challenges,
048   including visual representation learning (Tian et al., 2024; 2023), classification (Azizi et al., 2023;
049   Sarıyıldız et al., 2023), and semantic segmentation (Schnell et al., 2023; Tan et al., 2023; Xie et al.,
050   2023). Existing diffusion-based methods to generate detection data can be categorized into three
051   types. 1. Copy-paste Synthesis (Ge et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023): foreground and background
052   are synthesized separately (or just the foreground) and then combined, which often results poor
053   image consistency. 2. Direct Image Generation (Zhang et al., 2023b; Feng et al., 2024): images
      are generated via a text-to-image model, with labels obtained from a detector or module, which

      To address these challenges, we propose DODA, a unified framework for generating high-quality
079
      detection data across diverse agricultural domains. By conditioning the diffusion model with external
080   domain embeddings, DODA decouples the learning of domain-specific features from the model,
081   allowing it to generate images for target domains without additional training. Furthermore, we found
082   that existing L2I methods are overly complicated, leading to poor alignment between layout and
083   image features. For these reasons we introduce a new layout-image-to-image (LI2I) technique to
084   simplify the process. In our method, the layouts are directly represented as images and encoded with
085   a simple vision encoder. This approach greatly improves control over the generated image layout,
086   which is crucial for the label accuracy. On the COCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014), our LI2I method
087   (42.5 mAP) achieves a significant performance improvement, outperforming the previous SOTA L2I
088
      method (GeoDiffusion (Chen et al., 2023), +14.8 mAP) and nearly matching the performance on real
      images (45.2 mAP). To further improve the quality of generated detection data, we suggest dividing
089
      DODA’s training into pre-training and post-training, and pre-training the model on a larger set of
090
      unlabeled agricultural images. To support this, we collected an additional 65k agricultural images.
091   Extensive experiments on the Global Wheat Head Detection (GWHD) dataset (David et al., 2021),
092   which is the largest agricultural detection dataset and includes diverse sub-domains, show consistent
093   improvements across multiple domains (as shown in Fig. 1 right), with a maximum AP increase of
094   15.6. These results highlight DODA’s potential to lower the barriers for growers using detection in
095   their personalized environments.
096
      The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as:
097
098          • We decouple the learning of domain-specific features from the diffusion model by incorpo-
099            rating external domain informations, enabling the diffusion model to generate images for
100            target domains without requiring additional training.
101

               on the COCO dataset show that our LI2I method generates images with highly accurate
103

104
               images.
105
106          • We demonstrate that pre-training with more unlabeled data significantly enhances the quality
107            of generated data. To facilitate this, we collected and cleaned 65k unlabeled images, doubling
               the size of the GWHD for pre-training.

      2     R ELATED WORK
114
      Detection and counting in agriculture. The number, size, and appearance of specific plant organs
115
      directly influence yield. Accurately and efficiently quantifying these traits benefits breeders selecting
116
      superior varieties and growers optimizing management. Manual data collection, however, is slow and
117   labor-intensive. Therefore, automating these tasks has become a major research focus. An effective
118   method is to use neural networks to generate density maps, and count the plants from the density
119   maps (Hobbs et al., 2021a; Osco et al., 2020; Hobbs et al., 2021b). Detection and segmentation can
120   provide additional contour information, enabling the estimation of crop size, health, and maturity.
121   These techniques have gained popularity, with models developed for crops such as wheat (Khaki et al.,
122   2022), maize (Zou et al., 2020), sorghum (Ghosal et al., 2019), and cranberries (Akiva et al., 2020).
123   Many studies rely on images from individual experimental fields. However, genetic differences,
124   environmental variation and differences in image pipeline lead to huge differences in the agricultural
125
      images(Ghosal et al., 2019). This limited these models to generalize to new agricultural scenes.
126   Global Wheat Head Detection dataset. The GWHD (David et al., 2021) dataset is one of the largest
127   agricultural detection datasets, specifically focused on close-range wheat head detection. It consists
128   of 47 sub-domains, each with certain differences, such as location, imaging pipeline, collection time,
129   wheat development stages, and wheat varieties. This division allows the development and evaluation
130   of a robust domain adaptation algorithm that perform well under different agricultural environments.
131   Domain shift and adaptation. Domain shift (DS) includes image-level DS (overall differences in
132   factors such as lighting and color, which subtly affect the distribution of features) and instance-level
133   DS (differences in object pose, category, and position) (Ma et al., 2022). In object detection, DS often
134   causes detectors trained on a source domain to perform poorly when applied to a new target domain.
135   To address this problem, few-shot domain adaption (FDA) (Gao et al., 2023; Nakamura et al., 2022)
136   investigates improving model performance on the target domain by using few labeled target images.
137
      In contrast, unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA) (Khodabandeh et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021a) aims
      to improve performance using only unlabeled target images. The focus on this paper is UDA.
138
139   Layout-to-image generation. The category and position information of all objects in an image is
140   referred to as layout. Layout-to-image is the task of synthesizing images that align with the given
141   layout. There are two ways to represent layout: 1. Bounding box, which defines the position of an
142   object by four vertices. 2. Mask, which defines the shape and position of an object by the semantic
143
      mask. Methods based on bounding box (Chen et al., 2023; Rombach et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023)
      rely on a text encoder to encode the input layout. However, this encoding approach often struggles to
144
      align the layout with the image features, leading to weaker control over the layout. On the other hand,
145
      mask-based methods (Mask-to-Image , M2I) (Zhang et al., 2023a) allow for more precise control
146   over the layout. Despite this advantage, generating masks algorithmically is challenging and lacks
147   flexibility, making M2I less suitable for data generation.

      3.3   I NCORPORATING D OMAIN E MBEDDING FOR D OMAIN -AWARE I MAGE G ENERATION
194
195   We expect to incorporate external domain embeddings to decouple the learning of domain-specific
196   features from diffusion, thus enabling domain-aware image generation. These domain embeddings
197   should satisfy two key criteria: 1. They must effectively guide the diffusion model to generate images
198   that align with the target domain. This alignment should be at both the image and instance level. 2.
199   They should be easily obtainable for various domains, including unseen ones.
200   Ma et al. (2022) demonstrated that combined instance and image level DA produces better results
201   than either method alone. This suggests that the features extracted by the model encompass both
202   image-level and instance-level domain characteristics, which is exactly what we want! Furthermore,
203   David et al. (2021) used a ResNet (He et al., 2016) trained on ImageNet to extract features from the
204   GWHD dataset, to suggest that dimensionality reduction can distinguish training and test set features.
205   Similarly, as shown in Fig. 2a, our finer-grained test indicates that simple dimensionality reduction
206   can differentiate features from different domains.This suggests that domain-specific features reflect
207
      unique domain characteristics, even without prior training on those domains.
208   Based on the above, we propose using a pre-trained vision encoder to extract features as domain
209   embeddings. The embeddings can then be integrated into the U-Net via cross-attention:

265   3.4   E NCODING L AYOUT I MAGES WITH V ISION M ODEL FOR S IMPLER AND B ETTER
266         A LIGNMENT
267
268   Generating images from layouts is a key focus in image generation. Existing methods (Zheng et al.,
269   2023; Chen et al., 2023; Rombach et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a) first represent the layout as text, and
      encode it with a language model, then fuse the layout embedding into the diffusion model through


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      cross-attention. We refer to these methods collectively as LT2I (layout-text-to-image). From our
271   perspective, LT2I disrupts the layout’s spatial relationships in multiple ways: 1. Since, LT2I represents
272   the layout as text, which is embedded as discrete tokens before being fed into the transformer, The
273   transformer is forced to learn to reconstruct the spatial relationships from the discretized tokens.
274   2. Before cross-attention, features from the diffusion model are flattened, which degrades spatial
275   information and makes alignment more difficult.
276
      We propose to address these problems by simplifying the process. As shown in the lower left of
277   Fig. 2c, inspired by (Zhang et al., 2023a; Mou et al., 2024), we represent the layout as image, which
278   naturally has accurate spatial information, eliminating the need of learning. The layout encoder
279   then interprets the hierarchical and positional relationships between bounding boxes, and converts
280   rectangles into object shapes. Finally, we fuse the features through addition to maximally retain
281   spatial information. Corresponding to LT2I, we refer to this method as LI2I (layout-image-to-image).
282

302   the discrete input to spatial relationships. The layout encoder consists of a stack of time-dependent

      Layers to Merge Layout Embeddings. We observed that shallow U-Net layers produce noisy,
306
      localized features. As the layers depend, the features become increasingly abstract and holistic,
307

308   the layout embeddings with the features of deeper layers (layers in the U-Net decoder) to better

532   5     C ONCLUSION
533
534   This paper presents DODA, a framework that incorporates domain features and image layout condi-
535   tions to extend a diffusion model, enabling it to generate detection data for new agricultural domains.
536   With just a few reference images from the target domain, DODA can generate data for it without
537   additional training. Extensive experiments demonstrated the effectiveness of DODA-generated data in
538   adapting detectors to diverse agricultural domains, as demonstrated by significant AP improvements
539   across multiple domains. The simplicity and effectiveness reduce barriers for more growers to use
      object detection for their personalized scenarios.
