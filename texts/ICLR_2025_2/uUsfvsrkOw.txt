011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Facial video-based remote physiological measurement (rPPG) has gained promi-
014            nence for its ability to non-invasively estimate vital signs such as heart rate (HR).
015
               The foundation of rPPG lies in using a camera to record facial videos at a cer-
               tain frame rate, allowing the capture of rapid skin color changes necessary for
016
               HR measurement. Inspired by this property, we identified a new task, that is,
017
               to embed malicious information into facial videos by subtly modulating frames
018            and generating frames corresponding to the modified rate. With this task, we
019            can mislead state-of-the-art rPPG HR methods through natural and imperceptible
020            frame modulation changes, aiming for two objectives: testing the resilience of
021            rPPG methods against frame modulation variations and safeguarding heart rate
022            data, which is crucial for individual privacy. However, such a task is non-trivial and
023            should be capable of automatically adapting to different input videos and generating
024            natural, imperceptible frame modulation perturbations along with frames corre-
025            sponding to the modified rate. To address these challenges, we propose Continuous
026
               Representation-driven Video Resampling (CRVR), which targets precise manipula-
               tion of frame timing to subtly skew perceived HR measurements. Specifically, the
027
               CRVR method consists of two modules: Variable Frame Rate Video Resampling
028
               (VFRVR), which automatically determines the optimal resampling strategy for
029            each frame, and Continuous Video Frame Generation (CVFG), which generates
030            frames corresponding to the modified rate and seamlessly injects them back into
031            the video. Extensive testing on UBFC-rPPG and PURE datasets reveals that our
032            CRVR method successfully produces realistic, imperceptible adversarial videos that
033            effectively mislead three different rPPG-based heart rate detection technologies.

036   1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Remote photoplethysmography (rPPG) has been widely investigated as a prominent non-invasive
039
      technique for heart rate (HR) estimation using facial videos (Chen & McDuff, 2018; Yu et al., 2019;
      Liu et al., 2020; Gideon & Stent, 2021; Lu et al., 2021). The rPPG circumvents the discomfort
040
      and potential allergic reactions caused by skin-contact electrodes and wires, presenting a practical
041
      substitute for conventional contact-based devices such as electrocardiograms, thereby enhancing
042   user comfort and accessibility (Krittanawong et al., 2021). The foundation of rPPG, as shown in
043   Figure 1(a), lies in using a camera to record facial videos at a certain frame rate, allowing the capture
044   of rapid skin color changes necessary for HR measurement. This process is influenced by the video’s
045   frame rate, meaning even minor adjustments can impact the accuracy of the extracted signal.
046
      Inspired by this property, we identified a new task: embedding malicious information into facial
047
      videos by subtly altering the frame rate and generating corresponding frames for the modified rate, as
048   shown in Figure 1(b). The goal of this task is to mislead state-of-the-art rPPG heart rate detection
049   methods through natural, imperceptible frame modulation changes, with two primary objectives in
050   mind: first, to evaluate the robustness of existing rPPG methods against frame modulation variations,
051   and second, to protect heart rate data, which is a critical aspect of individual privacy. Achieving
052   this task is particularly challenging, as it requires the system to automatically adapt to various input
053   videos, ensuring that the alterations remain visually imperceptible while maintaining a natural flow.
      The solution must be capable of generating seamless frame modulation perturbations and producing

077   To address this, we explored a range of existing attack methods and their variations. Our findings
078   revealed that these conventional approaches often lead to significant discrepancies between consecu-

084   two further challenges must be addressed to ensure the method’s effectiveness. The second challenge
085   is generality, which requires that the resulting HR from the attacked video significantly differs from
086   the true HR detected by rPPG, thus protecting user privacy. The third challenge is controllability,
087   meaning that the attack must enable us to manipulate the targeted HR value, either increasing or
088   decreasing it as needed, thereby providing precise control over the outcome of the attack.
089   To tackle these challenges, we propose Continuous Representation-driven Video Resampling (CRVR),
090   which targets precise manipulation of frame timing to subtly skew perceived HR measurements.
091   Specifically, the CRVR method comprises two modules: Variable Frame Rate Video Resampling
092   (VFRVR) and Continuous Video Frame Generation (CVFG). VFRVR utilizes a deep neural network
093   to predict the optimal adversarial frame timing adjustments based on the characteristics of each frame,
094   maintaining temporal consistency while altering the perceived frame sequences. CVFG generates
095   frames corresponding to the modified rate seamlessly injects these adjusted frames back into the
096   video to ensure a smooth and natural appearance.
097   Extensive experiments on the UBFC-rPPG and PURE datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of
098   our CRVR method. The results show that CRVR successfully generates realistic and imperceptible
099   adversarial videos that mislead three different rPPG-based heart rate detection technologies. The
100   experimental results show that our method is effective in protecting user privacy, and highlight the
101   critical vulnerabilities in rPPG HR measurement systems that rely on visual inputs.

105   2.1      R PPG M EASUREMENT
106
107   Facial video-based remote physiological measurement (rPPG) has advanced significantly, enabling
      non-invasive extraction of heart rate and other vital signals from facial videos. Historically, rPPG


                                                                             2
108
      techniques have evolved through two major phases. Initially, conventional methods analyzed subtle
109   color variations in specific facial regions to estimate heart rate, relying on mathematical models
110   such as blind signal separation (Poh et al., 2010), least mean square (Li et al., 2014), majority
111   voting (Lam & Kuno, 2015), and self-adaptive matrix completion (Tulyakov et al., 2016). These
112   approaches were grounded in skin reflection models (Wang et al., 2016; De Haan & Jeanne, 2013)
113   and focused on signal extraction from predetermined areas of the face. However, these traditional
114   methods often struggled with robustness and generalizability across diverse conditions and subjects,
115   limited by their foundational mathematical assumptions. The emergence of deep learning ushered in
116   a transformative phase in rPPG research, leading to the development of more adaptive and powerful
117
      models. Contemporary deep learning-based approaches, such as DeepPhys (Chen & McDuff, 2018),
      MTTS (Liu et al., 2020), and BigSmall (Narayanswamy et al., 2024), have significantly enhanced the
118
      accuracy of heart rate detection.
119
120
      2.2   G ENERAL A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS
121
122   With the rapid development of deep learning, adversarial attacks have emerged as a significant
123   threat to deep neural networks. To probe their vulnerabilities, researchers have developed various
124   methods designed to deceive applications such as image classification and face recognition systems.
125   Techniques like the Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) (Goodfellow et al., 2015), iterative FGSM
126   (Kurakin et al., 2017), and natural transformation-based attacks (Shamsabadi et al., 2020) have shown
127   that deep learning models are susceptible to imperceptible perturbations. Furthermore, methods such
128   as Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) (Madry et al., 2018), the Carlini-Wagner attack (Carlini &
129
      Wagner, 2017), and DeepFool (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2016) have expanded the arsenal available for
      generating adversarial examples, proving their effectiveness across various domains. Recent advances
130
      have extended adversarial attacks to video-based systems. For instance, Universal Adversarial
131
      Perturbations (UAPs) have been adapted to target video models, misleading video classification
132   systems through subtle, imperceptible changes between frames (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2017;
133   Papernot et al., 2017). Additionally, methods like temporal perturbations (Li et al., 2019) and
134   spatiotemporal adversarial attacks (Liu et al., 2022) have been developed to exploit the unique
135   dynamics of video data. Black-box attacks, which generate adversarial examples by repeatedly
136   querying video models to construct perturbations leading to misclassification without accessing the
137   model’s internal parameters, are also evolving (Ilyas et al., 2018; Andriushchenko et al., 2020).
138
139   2.3   V IDEO F RAME I NTERPOLATION
140
141   Video Frame Interpolation (VFI) is an established area of study within video processing, involving the
142
      synthesis of intermediate frames that do not originally exist between two consecutive video frames.
      This technology is utilized across various practical applications in video processing including creating
143
      slow-motion effects (Bao et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2018), enhancing the frame rate (Bao et al., 2018;
144
      Castagno et al., 1996), compressing video (Wu et al., 2018), generating new viewpoints (Flynn et al.,
145   2016), restoring video quality (Kim et al., 2020; Tian et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019; Werlberger
146   et al., 2011), and aiding in intra-prediction for video encoding (Choi & Bajić, 2019; Wu et al., 2015).

      3.3   L IMITATIONS AND M OTIVATION
203
      Limitations. The previously mentioned methods, including Random Noise Addition, PGD, and
204   EMA-VFI, have two major limitations: ❶ They lack temporal smoothness, resulting in unnatural
205   temporal changes between video frames that may reveal signs of tampering. ❷ They introduce
206   significant visual alterations, such as noise perturbations and interpolated frames of reduced quality,
207   leading to unnatural visual effects. These limitations increase the risk of the rPPG system detecting
208   the presence of an attack. Furthermore, the limited research focused on adversarial attacks in the
209   rPPG domain underscores the importance and urgency of this study.
210   Motivation. We aim to conduct an effective adversarial frame modulation attack on facial videos
211   for rPPG HR detection. This study is driven by three key motivations: ❶ To explore the impact of
212   adversarial frame modulation on rPPG heart rate detection, thereby safeguarding heart rate data to
213   protect individual privacy. ❷ To predict the optimal adversarial timing adjustment for each frame
214   based on the characteristics of the original video, achieving an effective attack while ensuring natural
215   temporal transitions in the video. ❸ To preserve the video’s structural integrity and maintain high
      visual quality, ensuring that adversarial modifications are not easily perceptible to human observers.

      5     E XPERIMENTAL E VALUATION
358
359
      In this section, we conduct a series of experiments to evaluate CRAD’s attack efficacy under various
360
      previously discussed settings. It should be emphasized that the results for each scenario are averaged
361   across three trials to minimize the impact of different random seeds. In line with the design specifica-
362   tions of rPPG HR measurement, we have configured the reconstruction range to focus on the facial
363   region rather than the entire image, significantly reducing time costs.
364   Datasets. We conduct our experiments on two public datasets: UBFC-rPPG (Bobbia et al., 2019),
365   PURE (Stricker et al., 2014). UBFC-rPPG consists of 42 facial videos with simultaneously recorded
366   PPG signals and heart rates. We follow (Lee et al., 2020) to discard subjects of indices 11, 18, 20
367   and 24 because their heart rates were inappropriately recorded. PURE contains 60 facial videos from
368   10 subjects. During the data collection process, subjects were asked to perform six kinds of head
369
      motions (small rotation, medium rotation, slow translation, fast translation, talking and steady) in

428   to ensure the frame rate as a prerequisite, you can only regenerate the intermediate frames by the
429   previous and next frames to conduct attack tests. In this case, the heart rate will hardly change. In
430   practical applications, the adversarial effect of this method is limited because it is not enough to
431   achieve continuous misleading of heart rate measurement without attracting attention. Our method
      consistently leads to the highest MAE and RMSE in almost all cases, demonstrating its effectiveness

474   6         C ONCLUSIONS
475
476   In this study, we presented a novel adversarial approach to facial video-based remote physiological
477
      measurement (rPPG) heart rate estimation through the Continuous Representation-driven Video
      Resampling (CRVR) method. This method effectively embeds malicious information into facial
478
      videos by subtly modulating frames, producing natural and imperceptible perturbations that mislead
479
      state-of-the-art rPPG heart rate methods. Extensive evaluations on the UBFC-rPPG and PURE
480   datasets demonstrate that CRVR can consistently generate adversarial videos that appear realistic
481   while misleading multiple rPPG-based heart rate detection algorithms. This work reveals critical
482   vulnerabilities in current rPPG systems, highlighting the need for enhanced robustness against adver-
483   sarial attacks to protect privacy and ensure accurate physiological assessments. Future research should
484   focus on developing effective countermeasures and exploring practical applications to strengthen the
485   security and reliability of remote physiological monitoring technologies.
