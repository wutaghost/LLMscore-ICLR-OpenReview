009                                                           A BSTRACT
010
011
                Large Language Models’ knowledge of how to perform cyber-security attacks,
                create bioweapons, and manipulate humans poses risks of misuse. Previous work
012
                has proposed methods to unlearn this knowledge. Historically, it has been unclear
013
                whether unlearning techniques are removing information from the model weights
014             or just making it harder to access. To disentangle these two objectives, we pro-
015             pose an adversarial evaluation method to test for the removal of information from
016             model weights: we give an attacker access to some facts that were supposed to
017             be removed, and using those, the attacker tries to recover other facts from the
018             same distribution that cannot be guessed from the accessible facts. We show that
019             using fine-tuning on the accessible facts can recover 88% of the pre-unlearning
020             accuracy when applied to current unlearning methods, revealing the limitations of
021             these methods in removing information from the model weights.
022
023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025
      During pretraining, Large Language Models (LLMs) acquire many capabilities, both intended and
      unintended (Wei et al., 2022). These capabilities have raised concerns about LLMs acquiring dan-
026
      gerous capabilities that can be exploited by malicious actors, such as assisting in cyber-attacks or
027
      creating bioweapons (Fang et al., 2024). Acknowledging these threats, the Executive Order on Arti-
028   ficial Intelligence (White House, 2023) has emphasized the importance of responsible development
029   of AI models.
030
      To address these concerns, LLMs are typically trained to refuse to engage in dangerous activities.
031   Refusal is vulnerable to jailbreak techniques (Wei et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b)
032   and other attacks. We can address these vulnerabilities by ensuring that dangerous knowledge is
033   not present in the weights. Filtering out dangerous knowledge from the training data of LLMs and
034   rerunning pretraining is impractical given the size of the pretraining datasets. Machine unlearning
035   was suggested to remove harmful knowledge from models (Si et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b), offering
036   a stronger safety assurance relative to refusal.

      The evaluations of unlearning methods are mostly output-based, which fails to determine if the
055   knowledge is removed from the model weights. Lynch et al. (2024b) showed that even after applying
056   the unlearning method suggested by Eldan & Russinovich (2023), information could be recovered
057   from the model using multiple methods, including simply changing the format of questions. Even
058   when applying RMU (Li et al., 2024b) (a state-of-the-art unlearning technique that targets removing
059   harmful knowledge), harmful information can still be recovered using jailbreaks (Li et al., 2024a).
060   To develop reliable unlearning methods, we need to develop robust evaluations to guide the research
061   process.
062   Our contributions:
063
064         1. We present a framework for evaluating the extent to which unlearning methods re-
065            move knowledge from the weights. We create new datasets and modify existing ones to
066            fit the desired criteria of our framework. Using our framework and these datasets, we are
067            able to quantify the amount of knowledge that was hidden but not removed from model
068
               weights.
069         2. We run evaluations on common unlearning methods. This includes Gradient Ascent,
070            RMU, and training on incorrect facts. We show that after performing our attack to recover
071
               hidden information, we can recover at least 88% of the pre-unlearning accuracy for all
               the unlearning methods we evaluate when the unlearning maintains good performance on
072
               non-unlearned tasks.
073
074         3. We stress-test our approach in situations where hidden knowledge is present but poten-
075
               tially harder to recover.

077   2    R ELATED W ORK
078
079   Refusal in LLMs Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al.,
080   2023) is used to mitigate harmful behaviors in language models, but RLHF is not able to protect
081   against jailbreaks (Wei et al., 2023), in-context learning attacks (Anil et al., 2024), few-shot fine-
082   tuning (Qi et al., 2023), and unforeseen misbehavior (Roose, 2023).
083
084   Unlearning in LLMs Several unlearning methods were introduced with the hope of solving the
085
      shortcomings of RLHF. Gradient Ascent modifies the standard training procedure by negating the
      loss term, which increases the loss for the information that needs to be unlearned (Jang et al., 2022).
086
      Eldan & Russinovich (2023) introduced a method to unlearn information about the Harry Potter
087
      universe by estimating the output of the model if it hadn’t been trained on Harry Potter-related data
088   and training on this estimated output. Li et al. (2024b) introduced Representation Misdirection for
089   Unlearning (RMU) that unlearns knowledge by perturbing the activations of the model in a subset of
090   the models’ layers for harmful prompts while preserving the activations for non-harmful prompts.
091
092   Black-box unlearning evaluations Previous work has measured the success of unlearning using
093   performance on a task related to the unlearned information, or output similarity to that of a model
094   that was not trained on the information to be unlearned (Nguyen et al., 2022; Lynch et al., 2024b;
095   Liu et al., 2024a), but these approaches measure the propensity of the LLM to use the unlearned
096   knowledge, failing to capture hidden knowledge. Liu et al. (2024a) suggests two metrics to assess
097
      unlearning effectiveness: evaluating unlearning on harder cases (e.g., jailbreaks, queries in different
      languages) and Membership Inference Attacks (MIA) (Shokri et al., 2016). Since it is not possible
098
      to try all jailbreaks, evaluating jailbreak robustness is difficult: even if some attacks fail, others may
099
      succeed. For example, Li et al. (2024a) demonstrates that RMU (Li et al., 2024b) could be jailbroken
100   using hand-crafted attacks, despite its high robustness against many automated attacks. MIA do
101   not measure the absence of knowledge about a particular fact, but the likelihood that a particular
102   datapoint is absent from the training corpus, which is not the relevant metric for the purpose of
103   preventing LLM misuse.
104
105   White-box unlearning evaluations Past work has introduced white-box unlearning evaluations
106   like linear probes and relearning time. Some of the white-box approaches include linear probes and
107   relearning with fine-tuning. Linear Probes may recover the information present in the activations
      (Lynch et al., 2024b), but are not powerful enough to detect information present in the weights.

      3.1     U NLEARNING AS R EMOVING I NFORMATION F ROM THE W EIGHTS
149
150   While unlearning is used in previous work to imply both removing information and making it harder
151   to access, removing information is a stronger guarantee, as making information harder to access is
152   vulnerable to attacks that make the information easily accessible again like jailbreaking and fine-
153   tuning (Li et al., 2024a). We aim to measure how much an unlearning technique removes target
154   information from the weights.
155
      More precisely, for an unlearning technique that removes information about a certain question q,
156   if the answer to the question was different, the weights after unlearning should not be predictably
157   different; they can be different due to the stochasticity of the training process, but not due to the
158   answer changing. For example, if we consider the question ”was the World Health Organization
159   (WHO) founded in 1948 or 1949?”, if the correct answer to the question was the counter-factual
160   1949 (the correct answer is 1948), the weights should not be different. Formally, if Y is the random
161   variable corresponding to the answer to q (a binary random variable in our WHO example), and θ is
      the model weights after the initial training process (θ is a random variable since it depends on Y ),


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      then an unlearning process U fully removes the information about q from the weights if and only if
163   the mutual information between U (θ) and Y is 0: I(U (θ), Y ) = 0.
164
165   Facts can often be guessed based on more general information (e.g., knowing what the WHO is and
166
      having a basic intuition about historical dates rules out the WHO being created a million years ago).
      Our formalization only applies to questions that are practically impossible to guess (e.g., whether
167
      the WHO was founded in 1948 or 1949).

170   3.2   E STIMATING THE P RESENCE OF I NFORMATION
171
172   We introduce a new approach based on an adversarial setup to evaluate the presence of information
173   in the weights. The developers of an unlearning method identify a set of independent facts the model
174   contains that should be removed from the model weights after unlearning, and which have negligible
175   mutual information given the rest of the training data (i.e. given all but one of these facts and the
176   rest of the training data, it is realistically infeasible to guess the remaining one without additional
177   information). These facts are randomly split into train and validation subsets, T and V . An attacker
178   then tries to recover the facts V using (1) the model weights θ and (2) the facts T . If the unlearning
179   process is successful, neither the model weights nor the facts T alone should enable the recovery of
180
      V by the attacker. Any facts V that the attacker recovers indicate that these facts were hidden, rather
      than removed.
181
182   Because unlearning was performed on T and V , access to T allows for the creation of attacks that
183   can revert the hiding behavior that some unlearning methods may lead to in the model, and because
184   the facts in T and V are independent, we do not need to worry about “reteaching” the model the
185   facts, which is a concern if we perform attacks that use access to V , like relearning time (see Section
186
      2).
187   The developers of the unlearning method try to find an unlearning technique U ∗ that minimizes the
188   recovered accuracy on V :
189
190
191                   U ∗ = argmin max E(V,T )∼splits [AccuracyV (AttackT (UT ∪V (θ)))]
192                             U     Attack

193
194
      That is, finding an unlearning technique that minimizes the maximum expectation of accuracy on V
195
      after the attack on the unlearned model.
196
197   The attack we study in this work is Retraining on T (RTT) which is illustrated in Figure 1: the
198   attacker trains the unlearned model on the facts they have access to, T . After performing RTT,
199   we can use accuracy on V to approximate the mutual information introduced in section 3.1: if the
200
      accuracy on V is high, mutual information has to be high. If the accuracy is close to random chance,
      mutual information is probably low. Training on T might reveal information that was hidden by
201
      increasing the model’s propensity to output the unlearned facts without teaching the model the facts
202
      again. If an unlearning technique leads to information being harder to access, RTT should make
203   the facts T easier to access. Making the facts T easier to access can transfer to making V easier to
204   access since the unlearning technique was applied to both. We test how reliable this transfer is in
205   section 6.
206
      As we previously mentioned, in order for the proposed metric to be a reliable measure of unlearning,
207
      T and V should have minimal shared information; training the model on T should not increase
208
      accuracy on V for a model that was not trained on either T or V .

      unlearning.

      4.1   DATASETS
217
218   Our framework requires datasets for RTT and evaluation that ideally should have the following
219   properties:
220
221         1. The dataset has little shared information among facts: Learning some of the facts should
222            not help in learning the rest if the information is not already present in the weights.
223         2. Models perform well on the dataset before unlearning: This means we do not need to fine-
224            tune the models on the information, which may result in a different response to unlearning
225            compared to information learned in pretraining.
226         3. The data resembles what unlearning is used for in practice.
227
228   We create several datasets that differ in how much they fulfill each of these properties. For each of
229   these datasets, we also have retain datasets that unlearning methods use to ensure the model does
230   not unlearn capabilities we want it to keep:
231
             • Years: A dataset of major events in the 20th century and the years they happened in. The
232
               dataset is randomly split into 5 splits. We use 4 of them as T and 1 as V, testing multiple
233            times for different choices of T and V. For the retain dataset, we use Fineweb-edu (Penedo
234            et al., 2024).
235
             • MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021b;a): By default, MMLU has 58 subsets. We categorize
236
               them into 10 categories such that there’s little shared information between these categories.
237            We use 4 of these categories for T, 1 for V, and the other 5 as the retain dataset.
238
239
             • WMDP-Deduped: A filtered version of WMDP (Li et al., 2024b) with lower leakage
               among questions. The original dataset is not suitable for the purpose of our evaluations
240
               since it contains skill-based questions and questions using the same pieces of information.
241
               We compare WMDP and WMDP-Deduped in Appendix I. We split it into 5 splits, using 4
242            of them for T and 1 for V. For the retain dataset, we use Fineweb-edu (Penedo et al., 2024).
243
             • Random Birthdays: A dataset with randomly generated names and randomly generated
244
               years of birth. As it is randomly generated, we first fine-tune the models on the dataset,
245
               unlike the other 3 datasets. We use 4 splits for T and 1 split for V. We use a subset of the
246            MMLU categories for the retain dataset. The creation of the Random Birthdays dataset
247            was inspired by Maini et al. (2024), and we use it to test unlearning methods when we are
248            confident that the facts are independent. We test that the facts are indeed independent and
249            show the results in Appendix E.
250
251   For each of these datasets, we use two formats: plain-text and multiple-choice questions (MCQ).
252   Because unlearning is supposed to unlearn facts and not just a specific format, we perform unlearn-
253
      ing on a dataset using the plain text format, but RTT and evaluation using the MCQ format. The
      plain-text format is generated from the MCQ using GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), and we provide ex-
254
      amples in Appendix K. Figure 4 shows how the format of the unlearning dataset affects unlearning
255
      performance.
256
257   During evaluations, we measure the forget accuracy (the accuracy on the domain that should have
258   been unlearned), and the retain accuracy (the accuracy on a domain where performance should
259
      remain high) on multiple-choice questions with 4 choices, where random guessing would yield 25%
      accuracy.
260

262   4.2   U NLEARNING
263
      We mainly use Llama 3 (8b) (Llama Team, 2024) for our experiments, but we find similar results
264   with other models. We use the plain-text data format for unlearning. The main unlearning methods
265   we test are:
266
267         1. Gradient Difference (GD) (Liu et al., 2022): Gradient Ascent on the forget dataset and
268            Gradient Descent on the retain dataset. LossGD = −LossForget + α ∗ LossRetain Where α
269            is the retain coefficient. Using a retain coefficient of 0 corresponds to using the Gradient
               Ascent unlearning method.


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
            2. RMU (Li et al., 2024b): An unlearning technique that perturbs the activations of the model
271            in a subset of the models’ layers for harmful prompts while preserving the activations for
272            non-harmful prompts.
273
274
            3. Random Incorrect Answer (RIA): For each question with multiple choice answers, we cre-
275
               ate a plain-text formatted datapoint for each incorrect choice and perform gradient descent
276            on these texts.
277
278
      Unlearning is only useful when the model maintains performance on other non-unlearned tasks. We
279
      therefore configure the unlearning strength for each unlearning method to get a balance of low forget
280
      accuracy and high retain accuracy. For RMU, we configure the α hyperparameter as introduced by
281   (Li et al., 2024b), which scales the retain loss before creating the final loss. For the other unlearning
282   methods, we use a similar hyperparameter: a coefficient we multiply the retain loss by. We consider
283   the results for unlearning that lead to a drop in the retain accuracy less than or equal to 5% of the
284   retain accuracy of the original model in Section 5, in addition to other retain accuracy drops in
285   Appendix G.

288   4.3   R ETRAINING ON T AND E VALUATION
289
290
      We perform RTT using the MCQ format of the facts. We experiment with a variety of learning rates
291   and run RTT with two random choices evaluation split (V ). In each run, we fine-tune the model on
292   the other 4 remaining splits. We report the mean accuracy over the two runs and use the learning
293   rate with the highest validation accuracy.
294
      Across datasets, each split across has 157 datapoints. We use 4 split for T (628 total datapoints) and
295
      1 split for V . We use the same RTT hyperparameters for all datasets and unlearning methods. These
296
      hyperparameters and uncertainty estimations can be found in Appendix A. We also experiment with
297   multiple options for the loss and discuss results in Appendix B.

423   6.1                     H IGH G RANULARITY K NOWLEDGE H IDING
424
425   When models hide facts, they can hide them at multiple levels of granularity. For example, in the
426   case of the random birthday dataset, the model could learn to (a) hide information about all birthdays
427   (low granularity) or (b) only hide the information about the birthdays of specific individuals but not

475   7     D ISCUSSION
476
477   7.1       L IMITATIONS
478
479   RTT is expensive Relative to simple accuracy evaluation on a benchmark, our approach requires
480   fine-tuning with hyperparameter search which is more expensive. Looking for methods other than
481   RTT to recover unlearned information requires even more effort.
482
483   RTT does not ensure information is removed Our evaluation does not guarantee that informa-
484   tion is removed from the weights; rather, it sets a higher bar than previous evaluation methods for
485   unlearning. For example, if an unlearning technique leads to hiding information such that the hiding
      of each fact happens in a separate part of the model (e.g., different layers), we expect that RTT may


                                                                    9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      not recover accuracy on V even though the information is still present in the weights. Still, it works
487   well for current unlearning methods (as we show in Appendix C).
488
489   Low-leakage datasets are hard to build Our evaluation only works on a set of facts that have
490   low leakage. Such property may not be available depending on the goal of the unlearning. This also
491   means that our evaluation does not cover evaluating unlearning capabilities. For example, if the goal
492   is to unlearn the capability of coding, it’s hard to construct T and V with low leakage.
493
494   Removing information is a property stronger than strictly required Ensuring safety may only
495   require making information hard enough to access. For example, jailbreak robustness could in prin-
496   ciple be achieved without removing information from model weights, but jailbreak robustness is
497
      hard to assess directly, resulting in overestimates of jailbreak robustness (Li et al., 2024a). The alter-
      native we suggest likely provides better safety guarantees, but future work may find less conservative
498
      targets that provide strong enough guarantees.
499
500
      7.2   R ECOMMENDATIONS
501
502   In the light of our work and inspired by Carlini et al. (2019), we make the following recommen-
503   dations for future research on addressing dangerous capabilities and knowledge of Artificial Intelli-
504   gence models:
505
506
            1. Indicate whether the purpose of the proposed method is to remove information or make the
               information harder to access in the model.
507
508         2. When the goal is removing capabilities and/or knowledge, evaluate the proposed method
509
               against attacks that aim to recover them, like using the RTT attack presented in this paper.
510         3. Release the models the proposed method was applied to and the code base used to apply
511            the method to facilitate evaluating the robustness of the method by other researchers.
512
513   8     C ONCLUSION
514
515   In this paper, we propose focusing on developing unlearning methods that target removing informa-
516   tion from models over making information harder to access. To help in distinguishing between the
517   two cases, we propose RTT as a method for evaluating the effectiveness of an unlearning technique
518   for removing information and test some notable unlearning methods against our evaluation. The
519   tested unlearning methods remove a small ratio of information in our experiments, especially when
520
      these methods maintain good retain accuracy. We end with recommendations for future work on
      addressing dangerous knowledge and capabilities in models.

      H    S TRESS -T ESTING H YPERPARAMETERS
919
920
      When performing the stress-test described in section 6, we first fine-tune the original model on the
921   Random Birthdays dataset while freezing the second half of the model. We then perform unlearning
922   using GD while freezing the first half of the model. Performing this unlearning required an extensive
923   hyperparameter search. We end up using the Lion optimizer (Chen et al., 2023), a 6.4e-6 learning
924   rate, 11 epochs, and a retain coefficient of 1. In addition, we restrict the loss to be on the number
925   tokens, to help the model focus on the tokens that contain information that should be unlearned.

      I   WMDP VS WMDP-D EDUPED
928
929
      We create WMDP-Deduped to create a version of WMDP that has low shared information among
930   facts. We do this by removing skill-based questions (which use the same templates and rely on
931   similar facts) using keyword matching (e.g. ”What is the output”), and removing duplicate questions
932   using the cosine similarity of questions and answers separately, using OpenAI’s text-embedding-3-
933   large. We use an aggressive threshold of 0.7 for WMDP-cyber and 0.6 for WMDP-bio. We manually
934   check that pairs of questions just below these thresholds do not seem to be about the same facts.
935
      We do not include any of the 408 questions from WMDP-chem both for simplicity and because the
936   original WMDP paper focuses on unlearning in cybersecurity and biosecurity but not in chemistry.
937
