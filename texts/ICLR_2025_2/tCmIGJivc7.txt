011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Cross-lingual in-context learning (XICL) shows promise for adapting large lan-
014            guage models (LLMs) to low-resource languages. Previous methods typically
015            rely on off-the-shelf similarity-based approaches or task-specific retrievers trained
016
               with LLM feedback for demonstration selection. However, these methods often
               overlook important factors beyond a single criterion or can be resource-intensive.
017
               To address these challenges, we propose a novel approach called Topic-XICL,
018
               which leverages a latent topic model for demonstration selection. We assume that
019            latent topic variables encapsulate information that more accurately characterizes
020            demonstrations. By training this topic model on rich-resource language data with
021            a compact LLM, we obtain more relevant demonstrations through topic inference
022            and apply them for in-context learning across various LLMs. We evaluated our
023            method on three multilingual tasks (XNLI, XCOPA, and TyDiQA-GoldP) using
024            three models with 7 to 8 billion parameters (BLOOM, Qwen1.5, and Llama3.1).
025            Our approach outperformed the baselines—random selection, semantic similar-
026            ity, and clustering-based methods—on TyDiQA-GoldP, XCOPA, and XNLI by
027
               3.32%, 2.47%, and 1.77%, respectively, while requiring only moderate additional
               resources.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in natural language
      understanding across a wide range of NLP tasks in English (Lai et al., 2023; Bang et al., 2023;
034
      Zhang et al., 2023). Recent advancements have extended their multilingual functionalities (Shi
035
      et al., 2023; Cahyawijaya et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024); however, achieving
036   robust performance across multiple languages often requires substantial amounts of data for training
037   or fine-tuning. In-context learning (ICL) (Brown et al., 2020; Scao et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2022) has
038   emerged as a promising approach to improve the performance of LLM in low-resource languages.
039
      The impressive comprehension abilities of LLMs in English have fueled interest in Cross-lingual In-
040
      Context Learning (XICL)(Winata et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2022; Asai et al., 2023; Cahyawijaya et al.,
041
      2024; Zhang et al., 2024), which leverages demonstrations from high-resource languages to guide
042   learning in low-resource languages. However, the effectiveness of XICL relies heavily on the selec-
043   tion of demonstration examples (Zhao et al., 2021; Perez et al., 2021; Qin et al., 2023; Cahyawijaya
044   et al., 2024). Researchers have proposed two primary approaches for demonstration selection: using
045   off-the-shelf retrievers(Nie et al., 2023; Chang & Fosler-Lussier, 2023; Winata et al., 2023; Li et al.,
046   2023; Cahyawijaya et al., 2024), such as BM25 or Sentence-BERT (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019),
047   and training task-specific retrievers (Shi et al., 2022) using specially designed task signals, like feed-
048   back from LLMs. While task-specific retrievers may produce better results for certain LLMs, they
049   often require access to model parameters or detailed output distributions, which can be costly to
050
      obtain and are typically unavailable for black-box LLMs (Sun et al., 2022). In contrast, off-the-shelf
      methods offer a lightweight solution by exploiting semantic similarity between input-label pairs, but
051
      they tend to overlook task-specific information and diversity.
052
053   As noted in Qin et al. (2023), the choice between similarity and diversity in demonstrations varies
      by task: diversity works better for tasks like commonsense reasoning and question answering, while

076   Xie et al. (2022) examined in-context learning from a Bayesian inference perspective, while Wang
077
      et al. (2023) treated LLMs as topic models, applying this theory effectively in demonstration selec-
      tion for classification tasks. Building on this, we extend Wang et al. (2023)’s approach to cross-
078
      lingual in-context learning across various tasks, proposing a demonstration selection algorithm
079
      based on topic inference (Topic-XICL), as shown in Fig. 2. Our method consists of two phases:
080   latent topic learning and demonstration selection. In the latent topic learning phase, demonstra-
081   tion candidates from a rich-resource language are clustered into topics using the K-means algorithm,
082   based on multilingual representations, and a topic model is trained using an LLM to capture nuanced
083   topic information. Specifically, the candidate data for a task are grouped into n topics, and for each
084   topic, we introduce c new tokens to extend the LLM’s vocabulary. These tokens are concatenated
085   with the input, allowing the LLM to update token embeddings and improve predictions. In the
086   demonstration selection phase, topic inference is performed on the candidate data to select the k
087   most representative examples for each topic. For each target language input, its topic is determined
088
      by calculating semantic similarity with the candidate data, using the corresponding representative
      examples of its topic as context.
089
090   We trained the latent topic model on BLOOMZ-1b7 (Muennighoff et al., 2023) (with 1.7 billion
091   parameters) and conducted cross-lingual ICL on two multilingual sentence-level tasks and one cross-
092   lingual reading comprehension task. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
093
             • We propose a cross-lingual demonstration selection algorithm based on topic inference
094
               (Topic-XICL), extending Bayesian inference theory to practical applications in cross-
095
               lingual ICL.
096
097
             • Intuitively, the Bayesian theorem is primarily suited for classification tasks. To our knowl-
               edge, we are the first to apply it to non-classification tasks on XICL, and we have experi-
098
               mentally validated its effectiveness.
099
100          • Our topic-based demonstration selection method outperforms the strongest baselines across
101
               three models—BLOOM, Qwen1.5, and Llama3.1—by 3.32%, 2.47%, and 1.77% on aver-
               age for TyDiQA-GoldP, XCOPA, and XNLI, respectively.

104   2   R ELATED W ORK
105
106   Cross-lingual In-context learning The cross-lingual nature of multilingual language models fur-
107   ther enables the possibility of learning from a different language in-context without parameter up-
      dates, as demonstrated by the XICL method (Winata et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2022). Winata et al.


                                                                                                    2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (2021) first showed that, given a few English examples as context, multilingual pre-trained language
109   models (such as GPT (Radford et al., 2019) and T5 (Raffel et al., 2020)) can predict not only English
110   test samples but also non-English ones. Lin et al. (2022) also found that their XGLM demonstrates
111   strong cross-lingual capability, where using English prompts together with non-English examples
112   yields competitive zero- and few-shot learning performance. Cahyawijaya et al. (2024) extensively
113   studied XICL on some low-resource languages from four aspects: cross-lingual alignment, align-
114   ment formatting, label configuration, and cross-lingual retrieval, highlighting the importance of ad-
115   vancing ICL research. Our research mainly focuses on the aspect of cross-lingual retrieval to select
116   demonstrations for XICL.
117   Cross-lingual Demonstration Selection Different rich-resource language demonstrations yield
118   varying XICL outcomes for target languages. Current cross-lingual retrieval methods fall into two
119   categories: using off-the-shelf multilingual representations and leveraging LLM feedback signals.
120   For example, Nie et al. (2023) conducts cross-lingual retrieval from labeled or unlabeled high-
121   resource languages based on the semantic similarity of multilingual embeddings. Li et al. (2023)
122   extended this to focus on zero-shot settings, revealing limitations for complex generation tasks.
123
      Tanwar et al. (2023) augmented prompts with cross-lingual semantic similarity demonstrations and
      in-context label alignment, but Cahyawijaya et al. (2024) identified shortcomings and introduced
124
      translation pairs for alignment. Additionally, Winata et al. (2023) emphasized semantic similarity
125
      by selecting the nearest examples from various sub-datasets for classification tasks. In contrast, Shi
126   et al. (2022) proposed a retrieve-rerank framework for cross-lingual Text-to-SQL, using a bi-encoder
127   to identify relevant exemplars, and then training a retriever by distilling the LLM’s scoring function.
128
129
      Training retrievers on specific task data and LLMs can be advantageous, but managing inaccessible
      parameters of black-box models is challenging. Our method trains using only accessible LLMs.
130
      Semantic similarity alone may not suffice for complex tasks, so we expect to integrate richer infor-
131
      mation into ”latent topics,” such as article types in question-answering tasks, question types, and
132   the structural relationship between answers and articles. We use LLMs to mine this latent topic
133   information and select demonstrations to enhance cross-lingual in-context learning.
134
135
      In-Context Learning with Bayesian inference Xie et al. (2022) provided a latent topic inter-
      pretation to explain in-context learning, showing that the in-context learning predictor approaches
136
      the Bayes optimal predictor as the number of demonstrations increases, assuming both pre-training
137
      and task-specific data follow Hidden Markov Models (HMM). However, the Markovian assumption
138   about data generation limits empirical validation to synthetic data and toy models, raising questions
139   about its applicability to natural language.
140
141
      To bridge the gap between theoretical understanding and real-world LLM algorithms, Wang et al.
      (2023) developed a practical demonstration selection algorithm. Our method extends Wang et al.
142
      (2023) to an XICL setting. Unlike their approach, which treats each classification data as a topic, we
143
      perform semantic clustering on each task’s data to obtain topics, making our approach applicable
144   to a wider range of tasks. To our knowledge, this is the first attempt to use Bayesian theory for
145   demonstration selection beyond classification.

153   tasks. First, we introduce the notations of problem setting and theoretical analysis of the problem.
154   Then we describe the pipeline to learn latent topic embedding in Section 3.2 and the algorithm of
155   demonstration selection in Section 3.3.

      4.2      E XPERIMENTAL S ETTING
280
281   We employ the K-means algorithm with random initial center points to cluster the training set D,
282   using three seed values [32, 44, 100] and reporting the average results and standard deviation per
283   language for k = [2, 3, 4]. Each training data representation is obtained using multilingual Sentence-
284   BERT2 . As for hyper-parameters, the number of cluster classes n = 20 and the length of each topic
285   token sequence c = 10 are used for XNLI, and n = 20 and c = 15 are for TyDiQA-Gold, while
286   n = 5 and c = 15 are set for XCOPA (with only 500 English training dataset). The guidelines for
287   the hyper-parameters section can be seen in A.
288   We leverage the Bloomz-1b73 model to learn the topic token embeddings and compute the prob-
289   ability of each candidate. BLOOMZ-1b7 (Muennighoff et al., 2023) is a multilingual supervised
290   fine-tuning version of BLOOM, which may be more efficient for learning the topic of a task. Greedy
291   Search is employed for decoding answers in each task. For XCOPA, the gold output is changed to
292   ”1” or ”2”. For two-sentence tasks, we set the output length to 1 to obtain the answer label. For the
293   QA task, the maximum output length is 16 and the answer is extracted by regular matching, and the
294   metric is an Exact Match (EM) score. The prompts used for each task are detailed in Appendix B.

296   4.3      BASELINES
297
298
      We use the same set of demonstrations for three LLMs, each with about 7 billion parameters, in-
      cluding BLOOM(Scao et al., 2022), Qwen1.5(Team, 2024), and Llama3.1(Dubey et al., 2024). We
299
      consider the following demonstration selection methods as baselines:
300
301   ICL random: Random select k demonstrations from D for each test example. We also set three
302   seeds to obtain the average results.
303   ICL sem: We use the same sentence-BERT to calculate the cosine similarity between the inputs of
304   the source and target language. We select the top k demonstrations from D for each test example.
305
      ICL cluster: Since our method first clusters D and then selects demonstrations, for ICL cluster,
306
      we randomly sample k instances from each category of the clustered data as demonstrations for all
307
      test examples within that category. The topic classification method for the test set follows the same
308   procedure described in Section 3.3.

527   posing a challenge for automatic multilingual prompt design. Our primary focus is on comparing
528   the performance of ICL sample selection, while prompt selection will be explored in future work.

532
533
      This work presents Topic-XICL, a novel demonstration selection algorithm for cross-lingual in-
534   context learning that leverages latent topic inference. By integrating richer diversity information
535   from latent topic variables based on a compact LLM, our method addresses the limitations of tra-
536   ditional similarity-based and task-specific retrievers. We validate its effectiveness across three task
537   categories and three models, particularly in low-resource languages, demonstrating that latent topic
538   variables effectively capture valuable diversity information for cross-lingual in-context learning.
539   This approach provides a generalizable framework for enhancing XICL with only moderate addi-
      tional resources.

731   A    E MPIRICAL GUIDELINES F OR H YPER - PARAMETER S ELECTION
732
733   Regarding the choice of the number of topics (n) and tokens (c), there are empirical guidelines. For
734   tasks with a large amount of English candidate data (greater than or equal to 2000), the number of
735   clustering categories is set to n = 20, and for tasks with other data sizes, it is selected from (5,
736   10, 15), such as XCOPA with only 500 training data, which chooses n = 5. As for the topic tag
737   sequence length, it is set to c = 10 for general classification tasks, and c = 15 for tasks that require
738   reasoning or understanding of longer texts.

747
748
      All 24 languages in the three datasets are not always pre-trained on the three baseline LLMs. Based
749
      on the language distribution in the pre-training data for each model, we selected some languages as
750   low-resource or unseen languages, as shown in Table 4. For BLOOM (Scao et al., 2022), English
751   training data accounts for 30.4% of the total, with pre-training data covering 46 natural languages.
752   We define languages accounting for less than 0.1% as low-resource languages, and languages with-
753   out training data are unseen languages. In Qwen 1.5 (Team, 2024), which boasts 7 billion parame-
754   ters, the model supports a diverse set of 12 languages from Europe, East Asia, and Southeast Asia.
755   We classify languages not included in their support as low-resource languages, as there is no clear
      distribution across all languages, making it difficult to define extremely low-resource languages.
