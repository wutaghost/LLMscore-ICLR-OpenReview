009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Drawing inspiration from human learning behaviors, this work proposes a novel
012            approach to mitigate catastrophic forgetting in Prompt-based Continual Learning
013            models by exploiting the relationships between continuously emerging class data.
014            We find that applying human habits of organizing and connecting information
015            can serve as an efficient strategy when training deep learning models. Specifi-
016            cally, by building a hierarchical tree structure based on the expanding set of la-
017            bels, we gain fresh insights into the data, identifying groups of similar classes
018
               could easily cause confusion. Additionally, we delve deeper into the hidden
               connections between classes by exploring the original pretrained model’s behav-
019
               ior through an optimal transport-based approach. From these insights, we pro-
020
               pose a novel regularization loss function that encourages models to focus more
021            on challenging knowledge areas, thereby enhancing overall performance. Ex-
022            perimentally, our method demonstrated significant superiority over the most ro-
023            bust state-of-the-art models on various benchmarks. Our code is available at
024            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/HierC-089B/.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Continual Learning (CL) (Wang et al., 2024; Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017) is a research direction
030   focused on realizing the human dream of creating truly intelligent systems, where machines can
031   learn on the fly, accumulate knowledge, and operate in constantly changing environments as a hu-
032   man’s companion. Despite the impressive capabilities of A.I systems, continual learning remains
033
      a challenging scenario due to the tendency to forget obtained knowledge when facing new ones,
      known as catastrophic forgetting (French, 1999). In dealing with this challenge, traditional CL
034
      methods often rely on storing past data for replaying during new tasks, which can raise concerns
035
      about memory usage and privacy. To overcome this limitation, recent methods proposed leveraging
036   the generalizability of pre-trained models (Han et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2022) as frozen backbones to
037   solve sequences of CL tasks (Wang et al., 2022c; Smith et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024).
038
039
      While these pre-trained-based methods have demonstrably achieved impressive results, only con-
      sider forgetting caused by changes in learned prompts or differences between the (prompt-based)
040
      models chosen for training and testing (Wang et al., 2023; Tran et al., 2023; Zhanxin Gao, 2024).
041
      Further completing those arguments, we show that forgetting of old knowledge also comes from the
042   uncontrolled growth of new classes in the latent space. That is, models are confused in distinguish-
043   ing between old and new classes, which many methods overlook when training tasks independently
044   (Smith et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022d). Furthermore, we find that current approaches only utilize
045   limited information from the training dataset and treat class labels equally during training, result-
046   ing in missing opportunities to further enhance model representations and mitigate forgetting more
047   effectively.
048   In addition, we find that human natural learning behavior has many valuable aspects, especially the
049   habit of analyzing data, organizing them in a meaningful way, and finding connections between old
050   and new knowledge (Schön, 1983; Bransford et al., 2000; Sweller, 1988; Mayer, 2005), thereby
051   improving the ability to understand, remember, and reproduce information. Inspired by this, we
052   investigate the characteristics of current common benchmark datasets as well as the behavior of
053   pre-trained-based CL models, showing that the incoming data classes over time can always be cat-
      egorized into consistent groups. Each such group usually includes class data with similar semantic


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      characteristics, which is easy to get confused by models and should be paid special attention to
055   during training.
056
057   Therefore, we propose a training strategy that constantly considers emerging data in groups, follow-
058
      ing a hierarchical tree-like taxonomy developed based on expert/domain knowledge. During training
      new tasks, the model references information from old classes in the tree. Especially, the feature ex-
059
      tractor is encouraged to maximally contrast and distinguish concepts/labels within the same group,
060
      promoting the learning of common features that can be transferred to new concepts/labels in the
061   same group in future tasks. This strategy not only mitigates forgetting when new classes emerge
062   but also consolidates domain-specific knowledge. Furthermore, we observe that images belonging
063   to concepts/labels within the same group in the hierarchical taxonomy share strong visual and se-
064   mantic correlations, leading to overlapping representations in the latent space, which compromises
065   performance. By encouraging the feature extractor to separate and contrast the representations of
066   images in these concepts/labels more distinctly, we effectively reduce the overlap of easily confused
067   classes, thereby improving performance.
068   Contribution. We name our method as Exploiting Hierarchical Taxonomies in Prompt-based
069   Continual Learning (TCL), and summarize our main contributions as follows:
070
071          • We introduce a new perspective to explain the reason for catastrophic forgetting in
072            pretrained-based Continual learning models, which potentially comes from the uncon-
073            trolled growth of incoming classes on the latent space.
074          • Originating from the research findings of Cognitive Science, we propose a novel approach
075            to reduce forgetting by exploiting relationships between data. By dynamically building
076            label-based hierarchical taxonomies and leveraging the initial behavior of the pretrained
077            model, we can identify the challenging knowledge areas that further focus, and determine
078            how to learn them during the sequence of tasks. Based on this taxonomical structure, our
079            testing strategy further improves model performance.
080          • We empirically evaluate the effectiveness of our method against current state-of-the-art
081            pre-trained-based baselines across various benchmarks.
082
083   Organization. The rest of the paper is structured as follows. In Section 2, we present related work.
084   Then in Section 3, we formulate the problem and introduce a new perspective to explain the cause of
085   forgetting in CL models. Section 4 transitions from the motivation provided by Cognitive Science
086
      insights to the proposed training and testing strategy, emphasizing the importance of exploiting
      relationships between class data. Section 5 presents the experimental results, and finally, we discuss
087
      the limitations and suggest future directions in Section 6.

090   2   R ELATED WORK
091
092   Class Incremental Learning. This is one of the most challenging and widely studied CL scenarios
093   (Van de Ven & Tolias, 2019; Wang et al., 2023), where task identity is unknown during testing, and
094   data of previous data is inaccessible during current training (Masana et al., 2023; Rebuffi et al.,
095   2017; Hou et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2022). This work follows the setting of CIL and proposes a novel
096
      approach to mitigate forgetting and improve performance for prompt-based CL models.
097
098   Prompt-based Continual Learning. This line of work exploits the power of pre-trained backbone
099
      to quickly adapt to the sequence of downstream tasks by updating just a small number of parameters
      (prompts). Initial work like Wang et al. (2022d;c); Smith et al. (2023) typically assign a set of
100
      prompts to tasks, enhancing the adaptability of the backbone to downstream tasks. However, the
101
      absence of explicit constraints can lead to feature overlapping between classes from different tasks.
102   Therefore, recent methods employ some types of contrastive loss (Wang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023)
103   or utilize Vision Language models (Wang et al., 2022a; Nicolas et al., 2024) to better separate
104   features from tasks. However, they treat all classes equally during training, missing the opportunity
105   to learn in challenging areas where classes have many similarities and are easily confused. In this
106   work, we propose a novel approach to exploit the relationships within data, allowing the model to
107   recognize groups of these classes, and develop a deeper understanding of the respective knowledge
      areas, thereby reducing forgetting and enhancing its ability to learn new tasks.

      3.2   F ORGETTING IN P ROMPT- BASED C ONTINUAL L EARNING
124
125   In CL models, changes in the dataset, including inputs X in the input space X and labels Y in the
126   label space, Y lead to the changes of model’s behavior (feature shift) and thus model’s performance
127   on previously learned tasks to decrease significantly (i.e., catastrophic forgetting). Current prompt-
128   based CL methods, which leverage the power of pretrained models, attribute forgetting/feature shift
129   either to (I) changes in parameters from the backbone when using the common prompt pool P for
130   all tasks (Wang et al., 2022d;c) or to (II) the inherent mismatch between the models used at training
131   and testing. Specifically, let t̂(x) and t(x) as the chosen promptID and the ground-truth promptID
132   for x, respectively. We may have fθ̂t = fΦ,Pt̂ ̸= fθt = fΦ,Pt because there are chances that
133   the promptID t̂ ̸= t, where t̂ is predicted by pretrained backbone fΦ (Figure 1a), as discussed and
134   analyzed in Zhanxin Gao (2024); Tran et al. (2023). To complement these views, below we provide
135   an empirical study to offer new insights about the reason for forgetting in this type of model, which
136
      arises from overlapping between old and new class representations.
137   Firstly, to completely eliminate concerns about changing learned parameters, we consider methods
138   that propose using a distinct set of prompts Pt to a specific task t. Then, the remaining potential
139   factor of forgetting by feature shift is the difference between the prompt chosen at inference time and
140   the one used during training (i.e., Pt ̸= Pt̂ ). Thus, we conduct experiments on HiDE (Wang et al.,
141   2023) (i.e., the latest SOTA in prompt-based CL) to measure the differences between the features
142
      formed when using these two prompts. Particularly, we consider x ∈ D1 that belongs to the first task
143
      and measure the L2 Wasserstein distance W2 (Q, Q̂) (Kantorovich, 1939) between Q (i.e., the latent
144   distribution corresponding to Pt , which consists of fΦ,Pt(x) (x) for x ∼ D1 ) and Q̂ (i.e., the latent
145   distribution corresponding Pt̂ after learning the last task, consists of fΦ,Pt̂(x) (x) for for x ∼ D1 ).
146   The results in Table 1 show that the difference of these distributions is apparently negligible in many
147   cases.
148   For a closer look, besides the main classification head hψ used for all classes so far, at the end of
149   each task t, we set up a specific classifier st optimized on the frozen latent space of Dt and then kept
150   fixed. From now on, we refer to the accuracy measured on Dt using st as ’within task accuracy’,
151   and the accuracy using hψ as ’true accuracy’ of this task. The results in Figure 1b, show that
152   within task accuracy of the first task stays almost unchanged, which concurs with our observation
153   on the negligible shift between fΦ,Pt̂(x) (x) and fΦ,Pt(x) (x). Meanwhile, we observe a significant
154   decrease in the corresponding true performance in Figure 1c, raising the question of whether we
155   have overlooked additional factors contributing to final forgetfulness (Figure 1d), beyond the issue
156
      of selecting incorrect task prompts during inference.

158   number of classes increase, making the space fuller and increasing the possibility of overlap be-

160   sentations in Figure 4 and the respective illustration in Figure 5. In particular, after Task 1, we have
161   representations of ”oak tree”, ”mouse” and ”porcupine” located in quite separate locations. How-
      ever, when Task 2 and then Task 3 arrive, the appearance of ”willow tree” and ”pine tree” makes the

200   4        P ROPOSED METHOD
201
202   In the previous section, we noted that increased overlap in data representations as more tasks arrive is
203   one of the main reasons for greater confusion in predictions, leading to performance degradation. It
204   is crucial to identify easily confused classes/concepts to effectively enhance their distinguishability.
205
      Inspired by cognitive science studies (Schön, 1983; Bransford et al., 2000; Mayer, 2005), showing
      that organizing concepts in a tree-like taxonomy of visually and semantically related items aids
206
      memory and retrieval, we propose using expert/domain knowledge to structure the concepts/labels of
207
      continual learning (CL) tasks in a hierarchical taxonomy. Interestingly, we find that concepts/labels
208   within the same group in this taxonomy tend to be visually and semantically similar, potentially
209   causing more overlap in the latent space and confusion for the CL classifier. Motivated by this
210   observation, we propose group-based contrastive learning to maximize the distinguishability of these
211   concepts/labels.

213   4.1               M OTIVATION
214
215   Insights from Cognitive Science. Research in Cognitive Science highlights the importance of
      reflection, organization, and linking information as critical components of effective learning. Studies


                                                                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      show that when learners take time to reflect on their experiences, they deepen their understanding
217   and enhance retention (Schön, 1983). This reflective practice encourages individuals to connect new
218   information with existing knowledge, fostering a more integrated learning experience (Bransford
219   et al., 2000). Moreover, organizing information into coherent structures, such as outlines or concept
220   maps, allows learners to see relationships between concepts, making it easier to retrieve information
221   later (Mayer, 2005). Linking new material with relevant prior knowledge—often referred to as
222   associative learning—further strengthens memory retention (Sweller, 1988) and also benefits future
223   learning.
224
225   Our Approach. It is evident that besides reflection, comparison of old and new information, the
226   key factor in learning efficiently is to organize and exploit them in an insightful way, where concepts
227   are linked and arranged according to their semantic meanings. This observation motivates us to de-
228   velop a deep learning classifier that learns labels or concepts structured in a hierarchical taxonomy.
229   The aim is to enable the classifier to grasp relevant concepts more effectively, helping to mitigate
230   the challenge of catastrophic forgetting. More specifically, we propose structuring the data labels
231   in a hierarchical taxonomy, which can be dynamically constructed using domain expertise, adapt-
232   ing as needed based on the specific context and evolving understanding of the domain. Based on
233
      this structure, we establish a reference framework for the relationships between classes, identifying
      which classes belong to the same group with many shared characteristics, easily confused due to
234
      overlap, and require more focus (see Figures 3, 4). This approach not only helps the model better
235
      avoid forgetting, but also reinforces knowledge to facilitate future learning.

      Taking the learning process of Split-CIFAR100 as an example, when training on task t = 3, we
271   can construct a tree-like taxonomy of concepts/labels, as shown in Figure 3. We observe that the
272   concepts/classes under the same leaf in the tree-like taxonomy (e.g., oak tree, willow tree, and pine
273   tree in the ”plants” leaf group) exhibit stronger visual and semantic correlations. Consequently, as
274   shown in Figure 4a, their features in the feature space become more overlapping compared to those
275   from other leaf groups (e.g., otter and hamster under the ”four-legged” leaf group), leading to more
276   confusion and performance degradation when predicting these concepts/classes. This highlights the
277   impact of organizing concepts/labels in a tree-like taxonomy, where data examples within the same
278   leaf group share stronger visual and semantic relationships, causing greater overlap and increased
279
      confusion during predictions. Linking to our analysis in Section 3.2, the tree-like taxonomy of
      concepts/labels serves as a tool to help identify easily confused classes/concepts, facilitating the
280
      subsequent process of making them more distinct and separable in the feature space.
281
282   Our approach aims to train the backbone network so that all class representations must be distinct,
283   especially those within each leaf group, to achieve maximum distinguishability. For the leaf group
284   of four-legged animals, we assume that ”mouse” and ”porcupine” arrive in Task 1, while ”otter” and
285
      ”hamster” are in Task 3. When learning Task 1, to classify ”mouse” and ”porcupine”, the backbone
      is encouraged to capture the essential features of four-legged mammals to efficiently differentiate
286
      between these two animals. We hope that the knowledge learned from ”mouse” and ”otter” in Task 1
287
      can be beneficial for the next tasks. Then in Task 3, we again learn to distinguish ”hamster”, ”otter”
288   and these old ones in this group. In this way, the mechanism helps further strengthen the learning of
289   more efficient and robust features for the Mammals group.
290
291
      To summarize, by grouping and categorizing, we expect the model to concentrate more on the de-
      tailed features of each leaf group. This enhances its ability to distinguish related objects and rein-
292
      forces the model’s knowledge of each group. Therefore, this strategy not only alleviates the forget-
293
      ting of old knowledge—often caused by new classes that are difficult to distinguish from old ones
294   within the same leaf group, but also enables active knowledge transfer between tasks.

      6            C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION DISCUSSION
524
525
      In this work, we demonstrate the importance of organizing and exploiting data meaningfully rather
526
      than lumping it together for training. Organizing data into a tree-like taxonomy based on label in-
527   formation gives us a new perspective on the data. Particularly, we can divide them into small groups
528   containing the classes that are likely to confuse models. This approach encourages the model to
529   focus and build deeper knowledge for each group, thereby reducing forgetting and motivating more
530   effective learning in subsequent tasks. Additionally, we introduce a new perspective by leverag-
531   ing the initial behavior of pretrained models, providing an additional information channel to further
532   improve performance. Besides, our testing strategy has shown positive effects by exploiting group
533   knowledge during inference. Finally, experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of these
534   components and our superiority over state-of-the-art baselines.
535   Despite this novel perspective, the quality of the hierarchical taxonomy depends on the quality of
536   expert knowledge. For example, if similar image classes are not assigned to the same leaf group in
537   this label-based taxonomy, the constraint we put on each such group may not perform as expected.
538   Furthermore, although the testing strategy shows positive results, to exploit group knowledge more
539   efficiently, it is necessary to further investigate to understand the characteristics and hidden structure
      of data.

705   A     E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
706
707   A.1   DATASETS
708
      We adopt the following common benchmarks:
709
710          • Split CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009): This dataset includes images from 100 differ-
711            ent classes, each being relatively small in size. The classes are randomly organized into 10
712            sequential tasks, with each task containing a unique set of classes.
713          • Split ImageNet-R (Krizhevsky et al., 2009): This dataset contains images from 200 exten-
714            sive classes. It includes difficult examples from the original ImageNet dataset, as well as
715            newly acquired images that display a variety of styles. The classes are randomly divided
716            into 10 distinct incremental tasks.
717          • 5-Datasets (Ebrahimi et al., 2020): This composite dataset incorporates CIFAR-10
718            (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), MNIST (LeCun et al., 1998), Fashion-MNIST (Xiao et al.,
719            2017), SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011), and notMNIST (Bulatov, 2011). Each of these is
720            treated as a separate incremental task, enabling the evaluation of the impact of substantial
721            variations between tasks.
722          • Split CUB-200 (Wah et al., 2011): This dataset contains fine-grained images of 200 distinct
723            bird species. It is randomly divided into 10 incremental tasks, each with a unique subset of
724            classes.
725
726   A.2   BASELINES
727
728
      In the main paper, we use CL methods with pre-trained ViT as the backbone. We group them into
      (a) the group using a common prompt pool for all tasks, and (b) the group dedicating distinct prompt
729
      sets for each task:
730
731   (1) L2P (Wang et al., 2022d): The first prompt-based work for continual learning (CL) suggested
732   using a common prompt pool, selecting the top k most suitable prompts for each sample during
733   training and testing. This approach might facilitate knowledge transfer between tasks but also risks
734
      catastrophic forgetting. Unlike our approach, L2P doesn’t focus on training classifiers or setting
      constraints on features from old and new tasks during training, which may limit the model’s pre-
735
      dictability.
736
737   (2) DualPrompt (Wang et al., 2022c): The prompt-based method aims to address L2P’s limitations
738   by attaching complementary prompts to the pre-trained backbone, rather than only at input. DualP
739   introduces additional prompt sets for each task to leverage task-specific instructions alongside invari-
740
      ant information from the common pool. However, like L2P, it does not focus on efficiently learning
      the classification head. Additionally, selecting the wrong prompt ID for task-specific instructions
741
      during testing can negatively impact model performance.
742
743   (3) OVOR (Huang et al., 2024): while using only a common prompt pool for all tasks, this work
744   introduces a regularization method for Class-incremental learning that uses virtual outliers to tighten
745   decision boundaries, reducing confusion between classes from different tasks. Experimental results
746
      demonstrate the role of representation learning, which focuses on reducing overlapping between
      class representations.
747
748   (4) S-Prompt++ (Wang et al., 2022b): S-Prompt was originally proposed for domain-incremental
749   learning, training a separate prompt and classifier head for each task. During evaluation, it infers
750   the domain ID using the nearest centroid from K-Means applied to the training data. To adapt S-
751   Prompt to class-incremental learning (CIL), S-Prompt++ uses a common classifier head for all tasks.
752
      However, it shares limitations with DualP, such as efficient learning of the classification head and
      predicting appropriate prompts during testing.
753
754   (5) CODA-Prompt (Smith et al., 2023): This prompt-based approach uses task-specific learnable
755   prompts for each task. Similar to L2P, CODA employs a pool of prompts and keys, computing a
      weighted sum from these prompts to generate the real prompt. The weights are based on the cosine


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      similarity between queries and keys. To avoid task prediction at the end of the task sequence, the
757   weighted sum always considers all prompts. CODA improves over DualP and L2P by optimizing
758   keys and prompts simultaneously, but it still hasn’t addressed the drawbacks mentioned for DualP.
759
760
      (6) HiDe-Prompt (Wang et al., 2023): a recent SOTA prompt-based method that decomposes learn-
      ing CIL into 3 modules: a task inference, a within-task predictor and a task-adaptive predictor. The
761
      second module trains prompts for each task with a contrastive regularization that tries to push fea-
762
      tures of new tasks away from prototypes of old ones. To predict task identity, it trains a classification
763   head on top of the pre-trained ViT. TAP is similar to a fine-tuning step that aims to alleviate classifier
764   bias using the Gaussian distribution of all classes seen so far. However, this method does not declare
765   the relationship between data during training, thereby missing the opportunity to improve model
766   performance.
767
      (7) CPP (Li et al., 2024): This recent SOTA also uses a contrastive constraint to control features of
768
      all tasks so far during representation learning and achieves roughly equivalent performance to HiDE
769   on the same settings. Nevertheless, this method still has the advantages that we pointed out in HiDE,
770   which we propose to address in our work.

773   A.3    M ETRICS
774
775
      In our study, we employed two key metrics: the Final Average Accuracy (FAA) and the Final Forget-
      ting Measure (FFM). To define these, we first consider the accuracy on the i-th task after the model
776
      has been trained up to the t-th task, denoted as Ai,t . The average accuracy of all tasks observed
777                                                  Pt
778
      up to the t-th task is calculated as AAt = 1t i=1 Ai,t . Upon the completion of all T tasks, we
      report the Final Average Accuracy as FAA = AAT . Additionally, we calculate the Final Forgetting
779                                    1
                                           PT −1
780
      Measure, defined as FFM = T −1         i=1 maxt∈{1,...,T −1} (Ai,t − Ai,T ). The FAA serves as the
781
      principal indicator for assessing the ultimate performance in continual learning models, while the
      FFM evaluates the extent of catastrophic forgetting experienced by the model.

784   A.4    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
785
786   Our implementation basically aligns with the methodologies employed in prior research Wang et al.
787   (2022d;c); Smith et al. (2023). Specifically, our framework incorporates the use of a pre-trained
788   Vision Transformer (ViT-B/16) as the backbone architecture. For the optimization process, we uti-
789   lized the Adam optimizer, configured with hyper-parameters β1 set to 0.9 and β2 set to 0.999. The
790
      training process was conducted using batches of 24 samples, and a fixed learning rate of 0.03 was
      applied across all models except for CODA-Prompt. For CODA-Prompt, we employed a cosine
791
      decaying learning rate strategy, starting at 0.001. Additionally, a grid search technique was imple-
792
      mented to determine the most appropriate number of epochs for effective training. Regarding the
793   pre-processing of input data, images were resized to a standard dimension of 224 × 224 pixels and
794   normalized within a range of [0, 1] to ensure consistency in input data format. The detailed values
795   of the parameters can be found in our source code.
796
      In Table 2 of the main paper, the results of L2P, DualPrompt, S-Prompt++, CODA-Prompt, and
797
      HiDe-Prompt on Split CIFAR-100 and Split ImageNet-R are taken from (Wang et al., 2023). Their
798
      results on the other two datasets are produced from the official code provided by the authors. For
799   CPP and OVOR, the reported results are also reproduced from their official code. It’s worth noting
800   that the reported forgetting of HiDE is reproduced from their official code.
