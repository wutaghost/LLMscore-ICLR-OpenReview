010                                              A BSTRACT
011
012            This research explores the potential of Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Large Lan-
013            guage Models (LLMs) in understanding and simulating complex human behav-
014            iors, specifically in the context of historical international conflicts. We intro-
015            duce WarAgent, an LLM-powered multi-agent AI system, to simulate the de-
016            cisions and consequences of participating countries in three specific historical
017            conflicts. In addition, we propose standard evaluation protocols for LLM-based
018            Multi-agent Systems (MAS) simulation. Our study provides a nuanced analysis
019            of the strengths and limitations of current MAS systems in simulating complex
020
               collective human behaviors under diverse settings of international conflicts. The
               emergent interactions among agents in our simulations offer fresh perspectives
021
               on the triggers and conditions leading to war. Our findings offer data-driven and
022
               AI-augmented insights that can help redefine how we approach conflict resolution
023            and peacekeeping strategies. While we acknowledge the potential of AI in provid-
024            ing data-driven insights, we caution against over-reliance and emphasize the need
025            for careful interpretation in conflict resolution and peacekeeping strategies. The
026            implications of this work extend beyond computer simulation, offering a potential
027            avenue for using AI to better understand human history. Code and data are avail-
028            able at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/WarAgent-0FF0.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   In the wake of revolutionary advancements in Artificial Intelligence (AI), particularly the emergence
035   of LLMs (OpenAI, 2023), we stand on the brink of a paradigm shift in computational social science
036   research. In this study, we explore LLMs on social computing (Stauffer, 2002; Sun, 2006; Conitzer
037   et al., 2020) and develop a novel framework of an LLM-based Multi-Agent System (MAS) (Nayak
038   et al., 2023), specifically for the simulation of historical events. By creating a dynamic environment
039
      where agents of countries, each embodying the characteristics and decision-making processes of
      historical actors, engage in conflict or cooperation, we can explore the vast array of possibilities
040
      that could have shaped the evolution of international conflicts in the past, which have established
041
      the current global order. In this sense, our simulation provides multifaceted ways to explore these
042   age-old puzzles that are central to the safety and warfare of humanity.
043
044   Traditional methods of studying conflict through historical analysis, while insightful, are limited
045
      by their static nature and the bias of hindsight. The application of simulations in social science
      has a rich history, but the fidelity and scope of these simulations have evolved dramatically. Early
046
      attempts were often limited by computational power and simplistic models (Smith, 1970; Hermann
047
      & Hermann, 1967). More recent simulations employ LLMs that can model complex behaviors and
048   interactions, such as the virtual town simulation of human behavior (Park et al., 2023), the Werewolf
049   game simulation (Xu et al., 2023), the auction arena simulation (Chen et al., 2023), and the complex
050   task-solving simulation (Ge et al., 2023). These approaches have laid the groundwork for using AI
051   to model more intricate systems, such as international relations and conflicts. However, there has not
052   been research on how to apply these advanced technologies to simulate the nuanced and multifaceted
053   nature of international diplomacy and war, which is where our research positions itself: our paper
      aims to build the first LLM-based multi-agent system simulation of historical events.

079   At the core of our investigation are critical questions that challenge traditional understandings of
080   historical conflicts. By addressing the following three questions, our research uses the microcosm
081   of World War I (WWI), World War II (WWII), and Warring States Period (WSP) in Ancient China to
082   gain insights about international conflict dynamics. The research questions include:
083
084   RQ1, Simulation Effectiveness How effectively and accurately can LLM-based multi-agent sim-
085   ulations replicate the historical evolution of strategic planning and decision-making processes? By
086   comparing the outcomes of our simulations with documented historical events and trends, we can
087   measure the accuracy of the simulation. Only a system that upholds validity is capable of facil-
088   itating comprehensive analysis and addressing subsequent research questions. In addition, given
089
      extensive knowledge that LLM learn from pretrain data, we experiment on whether WarAgent is
      doing authentic simulation or merely retrieving information from its memory.
090
091
      RQ2, Casus Belli Are certain triggers of war more critical than others, and can they be identified
092
      through LLM-based multi-agent system simulations? We aim to isolate and analyze the various
093
      triggers of war to determine if certain triggers are more decisive than others in precipitating conflict.
094
095
      RQ3, War Inevitability Are historical inevitabilities truly unavoidable? We seek to uncover the
096
      conditions that lead to war (or peace) through LLM-based multi-agent system simulations. This
097   question strikes at the heart of deterministic versus contingent views of history.

100   2   BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
101
102   Multi-Agent Simulation Recent developments in Multi-Agent Systems (MAS) have opened new
103   avenues in AI research. These systems, which coordinate and communicate among multiple agents,
104
      provide an innovative platform for examining emergent communication within agent communities
      tasked with specific problem-solving.
105
106   The existing MAS landscape is broadly categorized into three types: reasoning-enhancement sys-
107   tems, NPC (Non-Player Character) multi-agent players, and production-enhancement sys-
      tems. In reasoning-enhancement systems, several noteworthy contributions have been made (Du


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2023; Chan et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023) where the concept of debate was
109   proposed and multiple agents debate with each other in order to enhance the factuality, faithfulness,
110   and reliability of the reasoning process. In the domain of NPC, several works such as generative
111   agents (Park et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023) simulate human behavior such as ev-
112   eryround behaviors and bargaining games for interactive applications. In production-enhancement
113   systems, notable examples include MetaGPT (Hong et al., 2023), a specialized LLM application
114   based on a multi-agent conversation framework (Li et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b)
115   for automatic software development by assigning different roles to GPTs to develop software appli-
116   cations collaboratively.
117   Our research extends the exploration of MAS to historical event simulations. We leverage World War
118   I (1914 - 1918), World War II (1939 - 1945), and the Warring States Period in ancient China (770 BC
119   – 221 BC) as examples. Our research reveals how MAS can provide valuable insights into historical
120   occurrences and hypothetical “what-if” scenarios, thus introducing a novel, quantitative dimension
121   to the field of humanities. This work underscores the broader impact of AI beyond LLM itself
122   and production, highlighting its potential to enhance our understanding of humanity. In responsible
123
      hands, AI can contribute significantly to larger social good and a brighter future for all.
124
125   Traditional Historical Simulation Tools The academic study of history simulation has undergone
126
      several stages: human simulation, human-program hybrid simulation, and computer simula-
      tion. Dickson (Dickson, 2002) outlines a human simulation of the United States’ journey toward
127
      participation in WWI in an educational scenario. In the 1960s, human-program hybrid systems
128
      were developed. The Inter-Nation Simulation model (Guetzkow et al., 1963) is employed in var-
129   ious studies (Hermann & Hermann, 1967), playing a pivotal role in simulating international con-
130   flicts. This method integrates human decision-making with computerized calculations, creating a
131   dynamic hybrid simulation environment. In the 2000s, computing power was leveraged to build the
132   next-generation historical simulation. The Army One Semi-Automated Forces OneSAF Objective
133   System (OOS) (Tollefson et al., 2008) is a Computer-Generated Force (CGF) that has been designed
134   to represent a full range of operations, systems and control processes from the entity level to the
135   brigade level (Kelly et al., 2006). (Hill et al., 2004) presents a JAVA-based multi-agent modeling
136   and simulation paradigm with game theory for an in silico historical analysis of the Bay of Biscay
137
      submarine war during WWII.
138   Our simulation leverages cutting-edge LLMs, which is presently recognized as the most promising
139   AI paradigm. This marks our inaugural endeavor in employing MAS to model historical events, the
140   first step in the field.

142   3   WAR AGENT S ETTING
143
144   This research centers on the simulation of international conflicts where each country is played by
145   one LLM-based agent, specifically on the outbreak of WWI, WWII, and WSP. We focus on ex-
146   amining the dynamics of diplomacy and international relations and the likelihood of war initiation.
147   Background details of the three historical events are presented in Appendix A. In this section, we
148
      introduce the basic settings in the system, the profile definitions of country agents, by elaborating
      on the dimensions of their profiles as well as the action space available to these agents.
149
150
      Country Agent’s Profile The characterization of each agent in the model requires the delineation
151
      of a comprehensive profile. In the case of a country agent, this profile is to be constructed around six
152
      fundamental dimensions: Leadership, Military Capability, Resources, Historical Background, Key
153   Policy, and Public Morale. Each aspect contributes to a multifaceted understanding of the agent’s
154   potential behavior and decision-making processes within the simulation. An example is presented
155   in Appendix B.
156
157   Action Space Our framework is designed with the specific objective of examining the onset of
158   wars, and accordingly, it includes a suite of actions that shape international relations between coun-
159   tries, categorized into seven distinct groups: Wait for action–An agent may opt to take a passive
160   stance during certain rounds, observing the actions of others and changes in the broader context.
161   General mobilization–This action involves preparing a nation’s military forces for potential con-
      flict, a precursor step required before engaging in war. Declare war–A country can formally initiate


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      hostilities against another. Military alliance–This is a formal agreement between two or more
163   nations to provide mutual support in case of conflict, bringing with it a shared responsibility for
164   collective defense and strategic cooperation. An agent can request an alliance for mutual defense,
165   which others can accept or reject. Non-intervention treaty–This is a diplomatic agreement where
166   signatory states commit to abstain from interfering in each other’s internal affairs, entailing a re-
167   sponsibility to respect sovereign integrity and political independence. The procedure of signing a
168   non-intervention treaty is similar to forming a military alliance: an agent first requests; the target
169   agent may accept or reject. Peace agreement–This is a negotiated settlement between conflicting
170   parties that formally ends hostilities and establishes the framework for future relations. Parties in
171
      conflict can propose and either accept or reject a peace agreement to conclude hostilities and out-
      line the basis for their future relationship, with options for publication or betrayal of the agreement.
172
      Send message–Aside from formal actions, agents can communicate informally through messages
173
      to discuss various matters.
174
175   Each action is defined to be public or non-public. Public actions, such as “Publish Military Alliance”
176   are broadcasted to all country agents within the simulation, reflecting actions in real-world politics
177
      that are openly disclosed and known internationally. On the other hand, private actions such as
      “Request Military Alliance” are communicated only to the targeted country, mirroring confidential
178
      or behind-the-scene diplomatic maneuvers in real-world international relations. By implementing
179
      this feature, the simulation ensures that each country agent operates based on its own knowledge,
180   which may be complete or limited depending on the nature of the actions taken by others.

      3.1   A NONYMIZATION OF H ISTORICAL E VENT
184
185
      Since LLMs are pre-trained on vast corpora of textual data with a substantial repository of knowl-
186
      edge, there exists a possibility that these models recall and subsequently reproduce the actual his-
187   torical trajectory during simulation. To circumvent this potential issue, we anonymize country
188   names and introduce minor modifications to historical facts. These modifications are designed to
189   ensure that these alterations do not materially affect the simulation’s efficacy, thereby maintain-
190   ing the integrity and originality of the simulation outcomes (Details in Appendix C). We compare
191   anonymized simulations and de-anonymized simulations by experiments and verify the effectiveness
192   of the method and also the authenticity of our simulation.

      4     WAR AGENT A RCHITECTURE
217
218
      This section provides a comprehensive introduction to the architecture of the WarAgent, detailing its
219
      core components and the information flow among agents. WarAgent is built upon four foundational
220   building blocks: (1) Country agents, (2) Secretary agents, (3) Board, (4) Stick. The section then
221   shifts to explore the mechanisms of information exchange within the MAS, particularly focusing
222   on (1) Agent-Secretary interaction and (2) Agent-Agent interaction. This exploration aims to study
223   how agents communicate, interact, and share information.
224
225   4.1   B UILDING B LOCKS
226
227   Country Agents Each country agent is defined by its corresponding country profile. In each round,
228   the agent reacts to the current situation by generating actions available from the action space, guided
229   by meticulously structured prompts which directs the agents to analyse who their potential enemies
230   and potential allies are based on the principle of “the enemy of an enemy is a friend” in order to
231   maximize its self-interest.
232
      Secretary Agents While LLMs are powerful tools in facilitating MAS, they are not infallible. They
      often exhibit limitations, such as a tendency towards hallucination and a lack of solid logical rea-
233
      soning, particularly in long contexts with complex, extended scenarios (Liu et al., 2023a; Yang
234
      & Ettinger, 2023). Therefore, the presence of a secretary agent serves as a necessary safeguard,
235   providing a fundamental check against hallucinations. Each country agent has a designated “secre-
236   tary agent” to verify the appropriateness and basic logical consistency of their actions. This role is
237   twofold. Firstly, the secretary agent ensures that all action generated are valid in the provided action
238   space, including the correct name of the action and correct formatting of inputs. Secondly, the agent
239   is responsible for verifying the basic logical coherence of these actions. For instance, it would be il-
240   logical and inadmissible for Austria-Hungary to “Accept a Military Alliance” from Britain if Britain
241   had not initiated the process by sending a “Request for Military Alliance” to Austria-Hungary.
242   Board The Board is designed to manage international relationships. It acts as a dynamic recording
243
      platform that collects and displays the relational dynamics of the ongoing situation in each simula-
      tion round. It further ensures that the agents’ decisions are based on the most up-to-date available
244
      information. As shown in Figure 2(a), the Board class can track the following four types of inter-
245
      national relationships showing by different colors: War Declarations: indicate conflicts or wars
246   between countries, marked in red. Military Alliances: denote formal military partnerships between
247   countries, marked in green. Non-intervention Treaties: represent agreements of non-interference in
248   international affairs, marked in blue. Peace Agreements: represent formal agreements to cease hos-
249   tilities and maintain peace between countries, marked in yellow. In this example: German Empire
250   declares war on Great Britain and France; Serbia and Russia are Military Alliance; Austria-Hungary
251   and France sign a Non-Intervention Treaty; the United States and Ottoman Empire reach a Peace
252   Agreement.
253   Stick The Stick functions as an internal record-keeping system for each country that represents the
254
      domestic status such as whether a country’s army is mobilized. It can help to ensure that the country
      agent’s actions align with its predefined protocols and standards.
255
256
257   4.2   AGENT I NTERACTION
258
      As shown in Figure 2(a), the system’s agent interaction is bifurcated into two primary segments: (1)
259
      internally, each country agent interacts with its corresponding secretary agent in every round, and
260   (2) externally, each country agent interacts with other country agents across multiple rounds.
261
262
      Agent-Secretary Interaction Figure 2(b) shows the Agent-Secretary interaction. In each round
263
      of the simulation, there is a designated interaction between each country agent and its corresponding
264   secretary agent. The country agent presents a proposed plan of action, which the secretary agent then
265   evaluates for format, content, and logical coherence. Should the secretary agent find discrepancies
266   or areas for improvement, it offers suggestions and engages in a dialogue with the country agent for
267   revision. This iterative process is capped at a maximum of four rounds of exchanges. If agreement
268   is not reached within these exchanges, the secretary agent takes the initiative to directly amend
269   the proposal. This dynamic of “country agent–secretary agent” internal interaction is a consistent
      feature across all external rounds and is applicable to all country agents.


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Agent-Agent Interaction Figure 2(c) illustrates the Country Agent-Country Agent interaction. It
271   should be noted that the secretary agents do not participate in the interactions that occur between
272   country agents. For clarification, “agent” in this context specifically denotes a country agent.
273
274
      In our framework, the very initial actions proposed by each agent are precipitated by a triggering
      event (denoted in the blue box in Figure 2(c)). Within the historical context, a triggering event refers
275
      to an incident that initiates a sequence of reactions among various countries, ultimately culminating
276
      in a significant historical development. In our simulation, a triggering event serves as the initial
277   scenario to which all agents respond. For instance, in the case of WWI, the assassination of Arch-
278   duke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary is widely recognized as the triggering event (Mombauer,
279   2013).
280
      In the first round of the simulation, each participating agent reacts to the trigger event in unison
281
      by variety of actions such as general mobilization and interactive communication through assorted
282
      messages and requests directed at all other agents. Subsequently, agents assimilate communications
283   from the preceding phases, specifically those aimed directly at them or those disseminated publicly.
284   We leverage the Board to record relationships such as “Military Alliance” formed between agents
285   and Stick to record agent-internal information such as “General Mobilization”. Note that during
286   each round, a multitude of new communications and evolving scenarios emerge as all country agents
287   engage in simultaneous interaction, generating a substantial volume of text so that the agents can
288   potentially become overwhelmed and lost in the middle. To address this challenge, agents rely on the
289   Board and Stick for latest situation. Thus for each round, agents only have access to the one-previous
290   round of conversation and information on the Board and Stick, turning the multi-turn conversation
291
      into a semi-Markovian process.

300   5.1       E VALUATION M ETRIC
301
      Here are the evaluation methods for each research question: for RQ1, we mainly use board-based
302
      accuracy to compare the simulated results with historically true results, and use human analysis to
303
      manually verify the logical consistency across rounds in the simulation. For RQ2 and RQ3, we
304   apply counterfactual analysis by altering the original settings and conducting observational analysis
305   on the difference in the simulation results.
306
307
      Here we introduce more details for the three aspects of Board-based Accuracy: Given the often
      contentious and debatable nature of historical events, this evaluation adopts descriptions and sum-
308
      maries from a widely recognized historical masterpieces (Keegan, 2014; Churchill, 1948; Chan,
309
      1999) as basis for historical facts. This approach provides a standardized and widely accepted ref-
310   erence point for comparing simulated situations with real historical events. For Alliance Accuracy
311   Score: Alliance formation is a transitive process, which means that if country A forms an alliance
312   with country B and B forms an alliance with country C, then countries A and C naturally become
313   alliances1 . Thus alliance formation among a group of countries can be seen as a partition of a set.
314   To evaluate the congruence of simulated alliances with historical alliances, we employ the mutual
315   information score of two partitions (Vinh, 2010). This metric offers a quantifiable measure of
316   the similarity between the two sets of partitions—the simulated and the historical. We adopt the
317   standard implementation from SciPy in Python. For War Declaration and General Mobilization
318
      Accuracy Score: We employ the Jaccard set similarity index as the primary metric for calculating
      the accuracy of the simulation in these two domains. The Jaccard index (Rajaraman & Ullman,
319
      2011), a widely recognized statistical tool for measuring the similarity and diversity of sample sets,
320
      quantifies the degree of commonality between the respective sets. This method involves comparing
321   the similarity between two sets of pairs, specifically in the context of war declarations, and two sets
322   of singletons in the case of general mobilization.

      6.1   D ESIGN OF M ECHANISM
649
650   The present study conducts simulations encompassing brief durations from the precipitating event
651   to the onset of warfare. These periods include approximately one and a half months for World War
652   I (June 28th to August 4th, 1914), half a month for World War II (September 1st to September 18th,
653   1939), and a maximum of one year for the Warring States Period (WSP) around 323 BC, given the
654   paucity of precise date documentation. Consequently, the interaction mechanism elucidated in this
655   paper pertains solely to these specific, brief timeframes, rather than offering a comprehensive simu-
656
      lation of historical events. For the execution of alternative historical simulations, the implementation
      of more intricate designs would be requisite.

659
      6.2   C AN W E T RUST S IMULATION R ESULTS ?
660   Since the early 20th century, computers have played a pivotal role in social science research, par-
661   ticularly in social simulation. In these simulations, computers emulate human reasoning processes
662   and the resultant scenarios. The objective is to generate simulations that allow for inductive analysis
663   based on either a strictly defined set of rules or, as in our case, the intelligence derived from LLMs
664   trained on extensive human-generated documents and corpus.
665
      However, criticisms have been around for computer simulation and we list 4 points beloe:
666   Simplicity: The view that simulations are overly simplistic representations of human society.
667   Limited Insight: Concerns that simulations fail to enlighten researchers about unprogrammed hu-
668   man interactions.
669   Relevance to Real World: The difficulty in relating abstract simulation results to the complexities
670   and variations of actual societies.
671   Verification Challenges: The notion that simulation results are unverifiable and thus meaningless.
672   In response to these criticisms, our stance is as follows:
673
674
675
      Comparison with Social Science Theories: Contrary to the criticism that simulations oversim-
      plify social phenomena, we argue that traditional social science theories often present an even more
676
      simplified view of social dynamics. These theories are frequently derived from linear models or
677
      small-scale laboratory experiments that fail to capture the intricate dynamics produced by interac-
678   tions among large populations. In contrast, our AI and LLM-based simulation results offer a more
679   complex and nuanced understanding of these dynamics, providing a richer and more comprehensive
680   model of social behavior.
681   Value in Unverifiability: The challenge of verifying simulation results with real-world experiments
682   or empirical data does not diminish their value. Given the inherent difficulties in conducting large-
683   scale social experiments, simulations emerge as a crucial tool for exploring hypothetical scenarios.
684   They enable researchers to model and analyze the potential outcomes of various policies or social
685   changes, offering insights that would be otherwise unattainable due to ethical or practical constraints.
686
      Role in Policy Discussion: The difficulty in verifying social science theories does not render them
      useless. Discussions about societal policies, such as crime approaches which rely on unverified the-
687
      ories, are essential for democratic governance and policy formulation. These debates often rely on
688
      theoretical frameworks and hypotheses without possibility of being verified quantitatively in real
689   world, still hold great values to the society.
690   Simulations as Suggestive Tools: We posit that simulation results should be interpreted as informa-
691   tive suggestions or rationales rather than definitive conclusions. These results provide policymakers,
692   historians, and students with valuable hypothetical insights into the potential outcomes of various
693   actions and policies. By offering a range of possible scenarios and outcomes, simulations serve as a
694   useful tool in decision-making processes, aiding in the evaluation of different strategies and policies.
695   It is ultimately up to human judgment to interpret these results and make informed decisions. Simu-
696   lations, therefore, should be viewed as valuable aids in the decision-making process, contributing to
697
      a deeper understanding of complex social dynamics.
698   In summary, while acknowledging the limitations and criticisms of computational social simulations,
699   we emphasize their significant role as a complementary tool in computational social science research
700   and policy analysis, providing unique insights and perspectives that enhance our understanding of
701   complex social systems.

781   Leadership encompasses the political institutions responsible for decision-making within a nation,
782   contextualized by specific historical periods. For instance, before WWI, Britain exemplified a con-
783   stitutional monarchy replete with democratic structures, distinguished by pragmatic and stoic leader-
784   ship. In contrast, Prussia operated under an autocratic imperial regime, with a foreign policy geared
785   toward aggression and military expansion, pivotal to its national ethos.
786   Military capability comprises quantitative data such as the size of its standing army, naval tonnage,
787   and a qualitative assessment of its overall military strength, including any particular dominance in
788   specific branches, such as naval or aerial forces. A conclusion regarding military might is essential,
789   as it correlates with a nation’s propensity to engage in or declare war; countries with robust military
790   capabilities are typically less hesitant to partake in military conflicts.
791
      Resources encompass critical elements such as geography, population, Gross Domestic Product
792   (GDP), terrain, and climate conditions. Population size and GDP are particularly salient indicators
793   of a country’s strength and serve as pivotal considerations in the strategic decision-making processes
794   of the agent. These factors provide a measure of the nation’s potential economic and logistical
795   support for its objectives, influencing its capacity to project power and sustain military and political
796   efforts.
797
      Historical background incorporates the legacy of prior conflicts of interest and unresolved issues
798   between nations, which can considerably influence current policies. Historical enmities and ter-
799   ritorial disputes often leave indelible marks on a nation’s current posture and potential alignment
800   within the global arena. An illustrative case is the aftermath of the Franco-Prussian War, where
801   France’s loss of the Alsace-Lorraine region—an area rich with iron mines critical to its industrial
802   development—engendered a fervent desire for retribution against Prussia. They shape a country’s
803   strategic alliances and influence its broader diplomatic and military engagements.
804   Key policy outlines the principal objectives pursued by nations. For instance, in the historical
805   context, post-unification Prussia harbored ambitions of becoming the preeminent European power,
806   seeking to eclipse Britain’s supremacy in territories and colonies. Conversely, Britain was intent
807   on maintaining its status as “the empire on which the sun never sets.” This led to strategic policies
808   such as the “Arms Race Act,” a stipulation mandating that for every warship built by Prussia, Britain
809   would respond by constructing two, exemplifying the competitive dynamics and the centrality of
      naval power to their geopolitical strategies.

1385       Intermediate Summary Our findings indicate that historical background, key policy, and public
1386       morale play significant roles in determining a nation’s propensity for war. In the experiments that
1387       examine the cases of France and the German Empire, historical grievances and nationalistic senti-
1388       ments, deeply rooted in past conflicts and territorial disputes, significantly influenced their military
1389
           engagements. For instance, the Franco-Prussian War of 1870-71, which led to the unification of
           German Empire and the loss of Alsace-Lorraine for France, created lasting enmity and a desire for
1390
           retribution in France. This historical context set the stage for future conflicts, as France sought to
1391
           regain its lost territory and prestige. In the experiments that examine key policy and public morale
1392       of United States, the effect is immediate. Across all simulations, this adjustment resulted in the
1393       United States proactively seeking alliances, specifically with Britain and France. The establishment
1394       of these alliances marked a significant shift in the United States’ international posture, leading to
1395       its active involvement in WWI. This scenario illustrates the potential consequences of a strategic
1396       realignment in U.S. foreign policy, highlighting how such changes can substantially alter a nation’s
1397       role and actions in global conflicts.
1398       Meanwhile, military capability and resources, though influential, do not singularly dictate a coun-
1399       try’s decision to engage in war. The German Empire, with its significant military advancements
1400       and resources, could have pursued a more aggressive expansion policy. However, it was often the
1401       historical and diplomatic contexts, such as alliances and mutual defense pacts, that played a more
1402       decisive role in its military actions. Similarly, France, despite its relative military inferiority to Ger-
1403       man Empire in certain periods, was motivated by historical factors to pursue a robust military policy,
           leading to its involvement in World War I.

1407       the importance of understanding historical contexts to fully grasp the dynamics of international con-

2066        I     L IMITATIONS AND R ESEARCH O UTLOOK
2067
2068        We recognize the limitations of the current framework in fully capturing the complexity of interna-
2069        tional relations, leading to directions for future research.
2070
2071        I.1     L IMITATIONS
2072
2073
            WarAgent is the first LLM-based Multi-Agent System (MAS) that simulates historical events. This
            simulation seeks to capture the complex web of factors influencing diplomatic interactions through-
2074
            out history, yet it must be noted that our current model falls short of encompassing the full spectrum
2075
            of these intricacies. At present, we face a number of challenges in accurately replicating the nuanced
2076        dynamics of historical diplomacy. The following points outline some of these key limitations:
2077
2078        (1) One significant aspect is the variance in communication technologies across different nations,
2079
            leading to time lags in message transmission. Historically, the dispatch of ambassadors was a time-
            intensive process, with durations varying significantly based on distance. This factor played a crucial
2080
            role in shaping diplomatic relations, as the timing of message delivery could impact the outcomes
2081
            of diplomatic exchanges.
2082
2083        (2) Moreover, the realm of espionage adds another layer of complexity. In historical contexts, spies
2084        were often deployed to intercept and decipher messages, with different countries experiencing vary-
2085
            ing degrees of success and exposure in this regard. This aspect influenced the flow and integrity of
            information among nations.
2086
2087        (3) Another critical factor is the varying levels of message publicity. Unlike the binary distinction
2088        of private and public messages in our current model, historical diplomatic communications existed
2089        on a spectrum of publicity, influenced by various strategic and contextual factors.
2090        (4) Lastly, the mobilization of armies varied significantly among countries. Different nations had
2091        disparate capabilities and timescales for readying their military forces. This variance could critically
2092        impact the timing and feasibility of war declarations, significantly influencing the course of interna-
2093        tional conflicts and relations. Our simulation framework, in its current state, may not fully account
2094        for these nuanced and time-sensitive processes.
2095
2096        I.2     R ESEARCH O UTLOOK
2097
2098
            WarAgent marks the start of research that applies LLM-based MAS systems to simulate and examine
            complex human society behaviors, especially in historical and international relation settings. This
2099
            advancement shed light on the potential applications for historical simulations extending far beyond
2100
            the WarAgent system itself. We propose several avenues for future exploration:
2101
2102        I.2.1    ROUND - BASED VS . T IME - BASED S IMULATION
2103
2104        Currently, our framework operates on a round-based system, implying a synchronous mode of sim-
2105        ulation as opposed to an asynchronous one. In this format, each country agent is constrained to
            one-way communication to any other country agent per round.


                                                              39
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2106
       However, historical developments often unfolded asynchronously, characterized by varying frequen-
2107   cies of communication and activity among different nations. While our system allows agents to opt
2108   for “Wait without Action,” providing a rudimentary representation of asynchronous interactions
2109   (whereby some countries are more active than others), this mechanism fails to capture the complex-
2110   ity of historical communication patterns. For instance, in the lead-up to WWI, Austria-Hungary and
2111   the German Empire engaged in intensive private communications before declaring war on Serbia, a
2112   level of interactional detail that our current model cannot adequately replicate. Addressing this lim-
2113   itation by developing a more nuanced time-based simulation approach could significantly enhance
2114   the accuracy and depth of our historical simulations.
2115
2116
       I.2.2    S TOPPING C RITERIA
2117   Historical simulation inherently embodies a sequential and potentially unending process, mirroring
2118   the continuous flow of time. In the context of our research, we have not implemented a predefined
2119   condition to systematically terminate the simulation. Instead, we rely on observational analysis to
2120   discern whether a specific event transpires over a span of approximately 5 to 10 rounds, serving as a
2121   de facto endpoint.
2122
       Nonetheless, the establishment of systematic criteria for terminating a simulation presents itself as
2123   a compelling research query. One conceivable approach involves the application of “Board Connec-
2124   tivity”. This method entails concluding the simulation when all boards representing different agents
2125   become part of a connected graph, and this configuration remains unchanged for a predetermined
2126   number of rounds.
2127
       Additionally, other criteria could include the achievement of a specific historical outcome or the
2128
       stabilization of agent interactions within certain parameters. For instance, the simulation could be
2129   designed to end when a pre-established peace treaty is signed, or when a certain level of economic or
2130   military equilibrium is reached among the participating agents. Such criteria would not only provide
2131   a clear conclusion to the simulation but also offer valuable insights into the dynamics and conditions
2132   that lead to these outcomes.
2133
       Exploring these various stopping criteria could yield a richer understanding of the complex interplay
2134
       of historical events and offer a more nuanced perspective on the factors that drive historical change.
2135   This exploration, in turn, could enhance the predictive capabilities of our simulation models, allow-
2136   ing for more accurate and insightful historical analyses.
2137
2138   I.2.3    N EW R ESEARCH Q UESTIONS
2139
2140   This project answers whether LLM-based MAS can simulate historical events and international
2141
       conflicts, and provides relevant counterfactual analysis. Beyond this core investigation, numerous
       other research inquiries offer unique perspectives on historical dynamics. For instances:
2142
2143           1. Correlation between diplomatic communication and conflict likelihood: One intriguing
2144              question is whether there is a correlation between increased diplomatic communications
2145              and a reduced likelihood of conflicts. This aspect could involve examining historical sce-
2146              narios where heightened diplomatic dialogue either preceded peace or failed to prevent
2147              war. The simulation could analyze patterns of communication, the tone and content of
2148              diplomatic exchanges, and their impact on de-escalating potential conflicts.
2149           2. Influence of non-state actors in geopolitical dynamics: Additionally, the impact of non-state
2150              actors, such as multinational corporations or terrorist groups, on geopolitical dynamics can
2151              be a significant area of study, especially in the context of modern history.
2152           3. Effectiveness of international treaties and agreements in resolving long-standing disputes:
2153              The simulation could also be used to assess the effectiveness of various international
2154              treaties and agreements in resolving disputes and the conditions under which these agree-
2155              ments hold or fail.
2156           4. Game theory in alliance formation and deterrence strategy: Incorporating game theory
2157              could involve analyzing how states assess the benefits and risks of forming alliances. This
2158              approach could provide a deeper understanding of the strategic calculations behind alliance
2159              formation, how these alliances influence global power dynamics, and under what conditions
                  they may lead to either stability or escalation of conflicts.

                                                          40
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025
