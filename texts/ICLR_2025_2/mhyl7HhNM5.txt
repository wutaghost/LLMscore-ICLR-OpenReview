011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            NLP benchmarks rely on standardized datasets for training and evaluating models
014            and are crucial for advancing the field. Traditionally, expert annotations ensure
015            high-quality labels; however, the cost of expert annotation does not scale well
016
               with the growing demand for larger datasets required by modern models. While
               crowd-sourcing provides a more scalable solution, it often comes at the expense
017
               of annotation precision and consistency. Recent advancements in large language
018
               models (LLMs) offer new opportunities to enhance the annotation process, par-
019            ticularly for detecting label errors in existing datasets. In this work, we consider
020            the recent approach of LLM-as-a-judge, leveraging an ensemble of LLMs to flag
021            potentially mislabeled examples. Through a case study of four datasets from the
022            TRUE benchmark, covering different tasks and domains, we empirically analyze
023            the labeling quality of existing datasets, and compare expert, crowd-sourced, and
024            our LLM-based annotations in terms of agreement, label quality, and efficiency,
025            demonstrating the strengths and limitations of each annotation method. Our find-
026            ings reveal a substantial number of label errors, which, when corrected, induce a
027
               significant upward shift in reported model performance. This suggests that many
               of the LLMs so-called mistakes are due to label errors rather than genuine model
028
               failures. Additionally, we discuss the implications of mislabeled data and propose
029
               methods to mitigate them in training to improve model performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Natural Language Processing (NLP) benchmarks have long served as a cornerstone for advancing
035
      the field, providing standardized datasets for training and evaluating methods and models (Wang
036   et al., 2019; Hendrycks et al., 2021; Srivastava et al., 2023). These datasets have been developed
037   over the years for various tasks and scales, annotated using different schemes. Initially, human
038   domain expert annotation was preferred, as these experts possess the skills necessary to determine
039   correct labels accurately. However, as models have increased in size (Devlin et al., 2019; Raffel
040   et al., 2020; Brown et al., 2020), the demand for larger datasets has also grown (Kaplan et al., 2020).
041   Since expert annotation is cost-prohibitive, it does not scale well to meet these new demands. The
042   demand for large quantities of annotated data quickly and cost-effectively has led researchers to
043   adopt crowd-sourcing, often sacrificing expertise for scale.
044   That way or another, constructing datasets heavily involves making compromises in annotation,
045   trading off between scale, efficiency and expertise. Even when annotated by experts, datasets can
046   naturally contain labeling errors, arising from factors such as task subjectivity, annotator fatigue,
047   inattention, insufficient guidelines, and more (Rogers et al., 2013; Reiss et al., 2020; Sylolypavan
048   et al., 2023). Mislabeled data is even more pronounced when non-expert annotators are involved
049   (Kennedy et al., 2020; Chong et al., 2022b). Widespread mislabeled data is particularly concerning
050
      because both the research community and the industry rely heavily on benchmarks. In training data,
      label errors harm model quality and hinder generalization, while in test sets, they lead to flawed
051
      comparisons, false conclusions, and prevent progress.
052
053   Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) (Ouyang et al., 2022; Chiang & Lee, 2023;
      Li et al., 2023; Gat et al., 2024) present new opportunities to improve the annotation process, specifi-


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      cally in detecting label errors within existing datasets. Rather than re-annotating entire datasets (e.g.,
055   through experts or crowd-workers), we consider the recent approach of LLM-as-a-judge (Zheng
056   et al., 2023), and propose a simple yet effective method by leveraging an ensemble of LLMs to flag
057   a set of potentially mislabeled examples. These can then be sent to experts for re-annotation and
058   correction, or even get filtered during training.
059
      Specifically, we construct an ensemble model using multiple LLMs with diverse prompts, gathering
060
      both their predicted labels and corresponding confidence scores. These predictions are contrasted
061   with the original labels, and instances where the LLMs strongly disagree with the original label (i.e.,
062   show high confidence in a different label) are flagged as potential mislabeling cases. Additionally,
063   we not only explore the role of LLMs in detecting errors but also evaluate their performance as
064   annotators, comparing them with expert and crowd-sourced annotations. We assess these approaches
065   in terms of agreement, label quality, and efficiency, highlighting their strengths and limitations.
066
      We aim to answer the following questions through a comprehensive end-to-end study: (1) To which
067   extent current benchmarks include mislabeled data? (2) Can LLMs detect label errors? (3) How do
068   expert, crowd-sourced, and LLM-based annotations compare in quality and efficiency? and (4) What
069   are the implications of mislabeled data on model performance and can we mitigate their impact?
070
      To this end, we choose the TRUE benchmark (Honovich et al., 2022) – A collection consolidating
071
      11 existing datasets annotated for factual consistency in a unified format – as a case-study and
072
      empirically investigate its labeling quality. Specifically, we analyze four datasets from TRUE with
073   binary factual consistency annotation originating from different tasks. This enables us to explore
074   multiple tasks and domains while benefiting from a uniform labeling schema.
075
076
      This paper presents both methodological and empirical contributions. We propose a straightfor-
      ward approach for detecting potential mislabeled examples, revealing a substantial number of label
077
      errors in existing datasets, ranging from 6% to 21%. Additionally, we demonstrate that the preci-
078
      sion of LLMs in identifying errors improves with their confidence in an incorrect label; when their
079   confidence exceeds 95%, over two-thirds of those labels are, in fact, errors. Moreover, we show
080   that LLM-based annotations not only excel in error detection but also perform similarly to, or better
081   than, traditional annotation methods, offering better trade-offs between quality, scale, and efficiency.
082   Finally, we empirically illustrate the negative impact of mislabeled data on model training and eval-
083   uation. We propose a simple, fully automated method for addressing label errors, improving the
084   performance of fine-tuned models by up to 4%. In evaluation, we found that mislabeled data can
085   significantly distort reported performance; LLMs may perform up to 15% better. This indicates that
086   many so-called prediction errors are not genuine errors but are instead human annotation mistakes.

091   2.1   T RADITIONAL A NNOTATION A PPROACHES
092
093
      Crowd-Sourcing. Crowd-sourcing has been widely used to annotate large-scale NLP datasets (Ra-
      jpurkar et al., 2016; Williams et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022) because it enables the rapid collection
094
      of labeled data at scale. However, the reliability of crowd-sourced annotations has been questioned,
095
      as quality control remains a challenge, with labeling inconsistencies becoming more frequent as
096   dataset complexity increases (Lu et al., 2020; Allahbakhsh et al., 2013). One of the key advantages
097   of crowd-sourcing has traditionally been its ability to handle tasks requiring human creativity or
098   subjective judgment — areas where models have historically struggled. However, this advantage
099   is fading, as models now approach near-human performance on such tasks (Chiang & Lee, 2023;
100   Chen & Ding, 2023), and crowd workers increasingly rely on models for assistance, diminishing the
101   human element in the process (Veselovsky et al., 2023b;a).
102   Human Experts. Expert annotation is a reliable approach for NLP tasks that require domain-
103   specific expertise (e.g., medical or legal domains) and for tasks that demand deep cognitive en-
104   gagement, such as those requiring training to understand complex guidelines or intelligent, attentive
105   annotators. However, this approach is slow and expensive compared to crowd-sourcing (Snow et al.,
106   2008; Chau et al., 2020), limiting its scalability for the large datasets needed to train modern LLMs.
107   Managing the trade-off between cost-effectiveness and annotation quality is critical, especially in
      tasks that require domain-specific accuracy (Chau et al., 2020). Maintaining inter-annotator agree-


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      ment among experts presents an additional challenge, further driving up costs (Baledent et al., 2022).
109   Hybrid approaches, combining expert and crowd-sourced annotations, can mitigate this trade-off,
110   though expert involvement remains essential for high-quality labels (Nguyen et al., 2015).
111
112
      2.2   LLM AS AN A NNOTATOR
113
114   As shown in recent studies (Gilardi et al., 2023; Kholodna et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023), LLMs can
115   be integrated into the annotation process, as they are fast, relatively cheap, and obtain decent per-
116   formance. Despite the promise of LLMs acting as annotators, LLMs make mistakes, and therefore
117   their annotations can not be considered as Gold labels (Chen et al., 2024; Bhat & Varma, 2023).
118   Still, incorporating LLMs in the labeling process offers cost and scalability benefits.
119
      In this study we propose to utilize LLM annotations, alongside with their confidence, in order to
120   automatically detect errors in existing labeled datasets. Specifically, we propose a general schema
121   for re-classification via LLMs, described as follows. We re-label the dataset via LLM, and obtain a
122   predicted probability for each class. When using a model with access to the parameters, these prob-
123   abilities can be extracted directly at inference time. When using models available via public APIs,
124   probabilities may be given as token log-probabilities (if available through the API) or approximated
125   via sampling. After annotating via LLMs, examples for which there is a strong disagreement be-
126   tween the LLM annotation and the original label (i.e., high LLM probability for another label), are
127   flagged as potentially mislabeled. If in a test set, the flagged examples could then be re-examined
128
      by human experts to determine their true label. If in a training set, the same solution may apply, but
      such examples could also be filtered or have their label changed according to the LLMs prediction.
129
130   To overcome the variance in LLM-generated answers, we suggest averaging over various prompts,
131   and over different models, obtaining one strong LLM ensemble. As shown in Appendix A, this
132   approach not only reduces variance but also enhances model performance and detection capabilities.

      3.1   DATA
137
138   As a case-study, we choose to explore the extensive and widely used TRUE benchmark (Honovich
139   et al., 2022), which is typically used as an evaluation set (Steen et al., 2023; Gekhman et al., 2023;
140   Wang et al., 2024; Zha et al., 2023). It consists of 11 datasets from various NLP tasks such as
141   summarization and knowledge-grounded dialogue. This benchmark is unique in its approach of
142   bringing multiple datasets and tasks into a unified schema of binary factual consistency labels. Each
143   dataset is transformed from its original structure (e.g., a source document and a summary) into two
144   input texts, Grounding and Generated Text, and a binary label indicating whether the generated text
145   is factually consistent w.r.t the grounding. This enables us to examine multiple tasks and domain
146
      under the same umbrella at once, while maintaining a unified binary-label schema.
147   Specifically, we focus on four TRUE datasets, one from each task (summarization, dialogue, fact
148   verification, paraphrasing). Each of these datasets have been annotated with different guidelines, for
149   a different purpose, and with a slightly different annotation procedure:
150   MNBM (Maynez et al., 2020): Summarization. This dataset provides annotations for hallucina-
151   tions in generated summaries from the XSum dataset (Narayan et al., 2018). Grounding refers to the
152   source document that the summary is based on, while Generated Text consists of model-generated
153   summaries, which may include hallucinated information not present in the source. Three human
154   annotators, trained for the task through two pilot studies, annotated the dataset for the existence of
155   hallucinations. In TRUE, the binary annotations were determined by majority vote.
156   BEGIN (Dziri et al., 2022): Dialogue. This dataset evaluates groundedness in knowledge-
157   grounded dialogue systems, where responses are expected to align with an external Grounding
158   source, typically a span from Wikipedia. Generated Text refers to model-generated dialogue re-
159   sponses that were fine-tuned on datasets like Wizard of Wikipedia (Dinan et al., 2019). Data was
160   annotated into entailment/neutral/contradiction labels, by three human annotators, trained for the
161   task through two pilot studies, aggregated by majority vote. In TRUE, binary annotations were then
      determined by the entailment/not-entailment partition.


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      VitaminC (Schuster et al., 2021): Fact Verification. This dataset is based on factual revisions
163   of Wikipedia. The evidence, or Grounding, consists of Wikipedia sentences, either before or af-
164   ter these revisions. Most human involvement came from creating Generated Text rather than the
165   annotation process, with annotators writing claim/evidence pairs derived from Wikipedia revisions,
166   inherently generating labeled data for fact verification. Synthetic examples from the FEVER dataset
167   (Thorne et al., 2018) were also included. Additionally, three annotators reviewed 2,000 examples,
168   presumably to ensure data quality.
169   PAWS (Zhang et al., 2019): Paraphrasing. This dataset consists of paraphrase and non-
170   paraphrase pairs. Grounding refers to source sentences drawn from Quora and Wikipedia,
171   while Generated Text was automatically generated through controlled word swapping and back-
172   translation. Five human annotators annotated the dataset with binary labels w.r.t paraphrasing cor-
173   rectness. The dataset includes both high- and low-agreement annotations.
174
      For each of the four datasets, we randomly sampled 1000 examples (or the whole dataset if the
175   number of examples is smaller than 1000). These examples are annotated via LLMs as described in
176   subsection 2.2. We set an evaluation (i.e., test set) based on 160 randomly sampled examples from
177   each dataset (a total of 640), while the rest remain for training and validation (they will be relevant
178   for subsection 6.1). In addition to the LLMs annotation, the evaluation set is also re-annotated by
179   human experts, which are two of this paper’s authors, fully familiar with the task, and by three
180   human annotators per example via crowd-sourcing.

238
      tifying the extent to which mislabeled data exists across various datasets. Following the procedure

239
      tions with the original labels, to find disagreements. As shown in Table 2, the disagreement rate is
240

291   5     C OMPARING A NNOTATION A PPROACHES
292
293   As discussed in section 2, we focus on three main annotation approaches, each with its own benefits
294   and drawbacks. These approaches differ in how they manage the trade-offs between label quality,
295   scalability, and cost. In the following section, we discuss and compare their characteristics.
296
297   5.1   A NNOTATION Q UALITY
298
299   When annotating or validating a dataset, one of our main concerns is the quality of the labels, or
300   in other words, establishing a reliable gold standard. However, each annotation approach produces
301   different labels. To estimate the quality of these approaches, we measure the agreement between
302   different annotations using the weighted F1-score (which accounts for both classes). Note that this
303
      metric is not symmetric, meaning that treating one annotation as the true label and the other as the
      prediction, or vice versa, can result in different scores.
304
305   Figure 2 presents the F1-score between each pair of annotation approaches. As the figure shows,
306   LLMs have disagreements with the original labels (0.72). Yet, as discussed in subsection 4.1, the
307   original labels themselves contain mistakes, so this disagreement does not necessarily indicate poor
308   performance of the LLMs. When considering the Gold as the true label, LLM performance increases
309
      to 0.83. This suggests that LLMs, despite their discrepancies with the original labels, perform
      closer to the truth than initially reported. The Gold label, obtained by experts, has high agreement
310
      with both the Original and LLM labels. On the other hand, the MTurk-Majority approach performs
311
      poorly, with near-random F1-scores compared to both the original and gold labels, and even when
312   compared to its stricter variant, MTurk-Strict. The results indicate that basic crowd-sourcing, without
313   additional training to enhance crowd-workers into specialized sub-experts, performs significantly
314   worse compared to other approaches, including LLM-based methods.
315
      Crowd-sourcing. For crowd-sourcing, the reported F1-score does not provide the complete pic-
316
      ture. When we focus on individual annotators, we see that those who annotate more examples
317
      generally deliver higher-quality annotations, achieving greater accuracy when compared to both the
318   original and gold labels (see Figure 3). This phenomenon can be explained by two hypotheses: (1)
319   a learning process— as the annotators see more examples, they improve at the task, or (2) users
320   who dedicate time to annotating multiple examples are likely those who either read the guidelines
321   carefully and strive to perform the task to the best of their ability, or are naturally proficient at the
322   task and therefore continue annotating. Even though annotators who label more instances tend to
323   provide higher-quality annotations, they are less common—most annotators tend to stop after only a
      few examples. This distribution of annotators results in overall insufficient annotation quality. Pre-

349   workers, experts, and even LLMs— in this study, we use an ensemble of different LLMs and
350   prompts. The use of multiple annotators, similar to an ensemble, is meant to overcome the variance
351   between individuals, which can arise from the subjective nature of NLP tasks, different interpreta-
352   tions of instructions, lack of experience, task difficulty, and cognitive bias (Uma et al., 2021).
353   As such, a common practice in the NLP community is to report Inter Annotator Agreement (IAA)—
354   a set of statistical measures used to evaluate the agreement between individuals. Typically, IAA can
355   be viewed as an adjustment of the proportion of pairwise agreements, where 0.0 indicates random
356   agreement. We focus on Fleiss’s κ (Fleiss, 1971), as it accounts for label imbalance and multiple (>
357   2) annotators. High IAA, or low variance between independent annotators, is considered an indicator

409

410
      makes it harder to identify consistent patterns. The impact depends on various factors , such as the
411
      fraction of mislabeled data and the training procedure. In this subsection, we show that addressing
412   this issue, even heuristically, significantly improves model’s performance on a test set.
413
414   Handling Label Errors. In order to handle label errors in the training set, and reduce its effect
415
      on model performance, we propose two manipulations. For both manipulations, we use similar
      methodology as discussed in this paper, and flag examples where the LLMs p strongly disagree
416

417
      flagged examples out, which maintains a ”cleaner” yet smaller training set. The second manipulation
418   is label flipping for flagged examples, which maintains the same amount of data, but may also cause
419   harm if flipping too many correct labels.

      7     R ELATED W ORK
496
497
      7.1   LLM S IN THE A NNOTATION L OOP
498
      LLMs have been increasingly utilized as annotators in various NLP tasks, offering potential benefits
499   in efficiency and scalability. Several studies have demonstrated that LLMs can effectively generate
500   annotations from scratch, sometimes outperforming human annotators or crowd workers (He et al.,
501   2023; Gilardi et al., 2023; Törnberg, 2023). However, LLMs are not flawless and cannot be consid-
502   ered gold-standard annotators when used alone. They may produce incorrect annotations, especially
503   in complex (Chen et al., 2024), social (Felkner et al., 2024), or low-resource (Bhat & Varma, 2023)
504   contexts. These studies showed that LLMs can exhibit poor performance and biases, highlighting the
505   necessity of human oversight to ensure quality or fairness. To address this issue, several approaches
506   for collaborative (Kim et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023) or active learning (Zhang et al., 2023; Kholodna
507
      et al., 2024) were suggested, where LLMs and humans are both part of the annotation procedure.
508
509   7.2   H ANDLING L ABEL E RRORS
510
511   Label errors (also referred to as label noise) in training and evaluation datasets can significantly
512   impair NLP model performance and reliability (Frénay & Verleysen, 2014). Fine-tuned models have
513
      been employed to detect mislabeled data by identifying examples with high loss or low confidence
      (Chong et al., 2022a; Hao et al., 2020; Pleiss et al., 2020; Northcutt et al., 2019). For example, Chong
514
      et al. (2022a) demonstrated that fine-tuned pre-trained language models can effectively detect label
515
      errors by ranking data points based on the training loss. Once these high-loss or low-confidence
516   examples are flagged, they are typically filtered out (Nguyen et al., 2019; Northcutt et al., 2019),
517   corrected automatically (Pleiss et al., 2020; Hao et al., 2020), or re-labeled by human annotators
518   (Northcutt et al., 2021) to verify and improve dataset quality.
519
520
      7.3   FACTUAL C ONSISTENCY E VALUATION
521
522   Factual consistency evaluation (FCE) refers to the task of verifying that generated text remains true
523   to the facts in the source content, addressing factual inaccuracies in models’ outputs. It has been
524   applied to tasks like summarization (Kryscinski et al., 2019; Xie et al., 2021; Gekhman et al., 2023)
525   and dialogue (Honovich et al., 2021; Xue et al., 2023), which are prone to suffer from hallucinated
526   outputs. Benchmarks like the TRUE Honovich et al. (2022) standardize evaluation across datasets.
527   Common methods include entailment-based models (Laban et al., 2022) and QA-based approaches
528   such as Q2 (Honovich et al., 2021) and QAFactEval (Fabbri et al., 2021), with recent advancements
529
      like WeCheck (Wu et al., 2023) improving efficiency through weakly supervised learning.
530
531   8     D ISCUSSION
532
533
      Labeling errors are a persistent issue in NLP datasets, negatively affecting model fine-tuning and
534   evaluation. Our findings demonstrate that LLMs, particularly when highly confident, can effectively
535   detect these errors, outperforming crowd workers in accuracy, consistency, and cost-efficiency. As
536   LLM capabilities advance, their role in refining data quality will become central to improving NLP
537   benchmarks. Future work could explore applying LLM-based error detection to a broader range
538   of datasets and tasks, as well as refining methods for optimizing label correction strategies. We
539   encourage researchers to adopt our methods and critically evaluate existing datasets to drive more
      robust, reliable results in the field.

      We address several ethical considerations related to human annotators and the research community.

554   these platforms for income. While we explore the use of LLMs to enhance or potentially replace

       A                    T HE P OWER OF E NSEMBLE
1030
1031   As mentioned in subsection 3.2, we treat the LLM annotations as an ensemble of 2 models combined
1032
       with 4 different prompts, in order to ensure greater stability in the results. Where one LLM may
       succeed, the other may fail, and averaging all their probabilities enables us to have more confidence
1033
       in the final answer. In this subsection, we further analyzed the performance of LLMs by varying
1034
       the size of the LLM ensemble, examining how this affects the model performance. We evaluate two
1035   aspects of model performance. First, we assess how closely the ensemble’s annotations match the
1036   gold labels — essentially, how much we can trust the LLM annotations. We measure this aspect of
1037   label quality using the ROC AUC compared to the gold labels. The second aspect is the ensemble’s
1038   ability to detect label errors. For this, we compute the F1-score by averaging the recall of errors and
1039   the precision of correctly identifying a candidate as a true error.
1040
       Results are shown in Figure 5. For both aspects, we see a clear trend. As we increase the number
1041   of models in the ensemble, the performance increases. In terms of ROC AUC w.r.t the gold labels
1042   (left plot), this suggests better annotation quality, while the right plot, a higher F1 score indicates a
1043   stronger error detector, either by recalling more errors or improving precision, or through a balance
1044   of both. Additionally, for both measures the variance decreases as the ensemble size grows, which
1045   indicates more stable and consistent annotations and error detections. Although not yet discussed in
1046   the context of error detection with LLMs, these results align with previous work showing the power
1047   of ensemble (Dietterich, 2007). These observations justify our choice to use an ensemble of models
1048   rather than a single one.

1337   E.1           C LOPPER -P EARSON
1338
1339   As mentioned in subsection 4.1, we we employed the Clopper-Pearson exact method (Clopper &
1340   Pearson, 1934) to construct a 95% confidence interval for the binomial proportion, adjusted by a
1341
       finite population correction (FPC). As we only have a subset of examples we re-annotated by LLMs
       or experts, we can not precisely determine what is the error rate in the full dataset, but only construct
1342
       a confidence interval based on the re-annotated subset. The Clopper-Pearson method provides an
1343
       exact confidence interval for a binomial proportion, which means it gives a reliable estimate even
1344   with small sample sizes. By applying the finite population correction (FPC), we adjust the interval
1345   because our sample is drawn from a limited population. This adjustment helps refine the estimate
1346   by taking into account the size of the overall dataset compared to the sample.
