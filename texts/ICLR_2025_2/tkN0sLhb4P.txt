011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Multivariate time series regression, encompassing forecasting and interpolation,
014            is crucial for numerous real-world applications, particularly in healthcare, climate
015            science, ecology, and others. While recent work has focused on improving model-
016            ing for time series regression, two main limitations persist. First, the prevalence of
017            irregularly sampled time series with missing values poses significant challenges.
018
               For instance, healthcare applications often involve predicting future or missing ob-
               servations from irregular data to enable continuous patient monitoring and timely
019
               intervention. As current approaches mainly rely on the assumptions of regular
020
               time series such as strong periodicity, when applied to irregular ones they exhibit
021            performance degradation. Second, while some state-of-the-art methods (SOTA)
022            do model irregularity and perform regression tasks on irregular data, they are of-
023            ten trained in a fully supervised manner. This limits their ability to generalize
024            easily to different domains (e.g., training and testing datasets with different num-
025            bers of variables). To address these challenges, we propose GITaR, a Generalized
026            Irregular Time Series Regression model via masking and Reconstruction pretrain-
027            ing mechanism, aiming to capture the inherent irregularity in time series and learn
028            robust, generalizable representations without supervision for downstream regres-
029
               sion tasks. Comprehensive experiments on common real-world regression tasks
               in healthcare, human activity recognition, and climate science underline the su-
030
               perior performance of GITaR compared to state-of-the-art methods. Our results
031
               highlight our model’s unique capability to generalize across different domains,
032            demonstrating the potential for broad applicability in various fields requiring ac-
033            curate temporal prediction and interpolation.

      Multivariate time series regression, i.e., regression tasks that encompass interpolation and forecast-
      ing, aim to predict the continuous and numerical values based on their relationship within or beyond
040
      the existing time range (Tan et al., 2021). This type of regression is particularly challenging when
041
      observation data are unevenly sampled and contain missing values, a common issue in many fields.
042   Modeling irregular time series is essential in various real-world applications, including healthcare
043   monitoring, climate science, ecology and more Shukla and Marlin (2021a). For instance, in inten-
044   sive care units (ICUs), patient data is often collected at varying intervals and from multiple sensors,
045   resulting in irregular time series. These sensors typically monitor physiological features such as
046   heart rate, blood pressure, and respiratory rate. Modeling such irregular times in the past 24 hours
047   to predict the subsequent 24 hours is particularly valuable for continuous patient health condition
048   monitoring and inference.
049   While advanced deep learning architectures have significantly improved time series analysis, they
050   often rely on the assumption of regular spacing between observations, which limits their realistic
051   applicability to irregular time series data. For example, TS2Vec (Yue et al., 2022) assumes strong
052   auto-correlation in time series, and PatchTST (Wu et al., 2022) initially segment time series into sub-
053   series level patches and assumes observations are continuous without missing. Both methods, how-
      ever, struggle with irregular time series. As illustrated in Figure 1a, these state-of-the-art (SOTA)

091
      Recent advances in self-supervised learning (SSL) have shown promise in modeling time series data
092
      without explicit supervision, potentially improving generalization capabilities (Zhang et al., 2024b).
093
      However, most SSL approaches still focus on regular time series and cannot be easily adapted to
094   irregular data (Yang et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024b), as we will show in our
095   evaluation. One recent study, Primenet (Chowdhury et al., 2023) targets irregular time series SSL
096   and designs suitable augmentations combined with contrastive learning to learn the characteristics of
097   irregularly sampled data during the pretraining stage. However, its applicability to regression tasks
098   remains less explored. Moreover, PrimeNet still struggles to generalize across different domains
099   due to its modeling of intercorrelations within specific numbers of training variables, limiting its
100   effectiveness beyond the validation datasets used in the study.
101   Given these challenges and limitations in existing approaches, we aim to address two critical re-
102   search questions:

107          • How can we develop a generalized SSL modeling approach that can adapt to different

      We propose GITaR, an irregular-sensitive reconstruction-based pretraining mechanism to learn
109   more generalizable and robust representations. Among various SSL pretext tasks, masking and
110   reconstruction-based methods have emerged as particularly powerful. These approaches, which
111   primarily focus on reconstructing missing parts of the data, have demonstrated strong predictive
112   capabilities and have been successfully applied in recent large pretrained models (Brown et al.,
113   2020). This approach appears especially suitable for regression tasks, as the underlying principle
114   of masking and reconstruction closely aligns with the interpolation task of predicting missing or
115   future values based on partial data (He et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2024). Specifically, to leverage the
116   temporal properties of irregular time series, GITaR first introduces an irregular-sensitive masking
117
      strategy and time-sensitive patching segmentation. Further, we apply an irregular-temporal encoder
      that transforms patched segments into latent space representations for learning local and global tem-
118
      poral dynamics. Specifically, within the encoder, the channel-independent design of our approach
119
      caters to domains with varying numbers of variables or irregularity ratios, enhancing its generaliza-
120   tion capabilities across different domains.
121
122   To support the practical evaluation of GITaR, we conduct experiments on 4 naturally occurring ir-
123
      regular and asynchronous time series from healthcare to climate sensing. Our contribution includes:
124          • We highlight that irregular time series are common and present a significant challenge
125            in real-world applications and find that existing time series modeling algorithms undergo
126            substantial performance degradation when applied to irregular time series regression.
127
             • We propose GITaR, a pioneering pretraining framework uniquely designed for irregular
128            multivariate time series regression. Our approach introduces a novel irregular-sensitive
129            masking and patching technique coupled with an adaptive irregular-temporal encoder, en-
130            abling simultaneous learning of local irregularities and global temporal correlations, which
131            is effective for various regression tasks. The results showcase the SOTA performance of
132            GITaR again baselines with an average 5.68% improvement on irregular time series fore-
133            casting tasks and an average 6.73% improvement on irregular time series interpolation
134            tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness to varying irregular patterns.
135          • GITaR demonstrated remarkable cross-domain generalization capabilities across four di-
136            verse real-world datasets, spanning varying numbers of channels, irregular ratios and pat-
137            terns, temporal resolutions, and domain-specific challenges, establishing GITaR as a ver-
138            satile foundational model for irregular time series regression. Its adaptability to unseen
139
               irregular patterns makes it particularly valuable for real-world applications where data dis-
               tributions may unexpectedly shift.

142   2   R ELATED W ORK
143
144   Multivariate Time Series Modeling for Regression Tasks. Regression tasks such as forecasting
145   or interpolation aim to predict the unseen or missing observation values in time series (Wen et al.,
146   2023; Zhang et al., 2024b). Recent advanced work has shown promising results either trained in
147
      a supervised manner or self-supervised manner, such as Informer (Zhou et al., 2021), Crossformer
      (Zhang and Yan, 2023), PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023) and TS2Vec (Yue et al., 2022). However, these
148
      approaches often focus on regular time series forecasting tasks, via modifying the native structure
149
      of Transformers to encode temporal dependencies for regular long-term time series forecasting.
150   They neglected the intrinsic characteristics of irregularity and lack of synchronization inherent in
151   multivariate irregular time series data. Therefore, they cannot be readily generalized to multivariate
152   irregular time series analysis.
153
      Irregular Time Series Modeling. Irregular time series are characterized by varying time intervals
154
      between adjacent observations (Shukla and Marlin, 2021a). Early methods rely on set-based ap-
155
      proaches, incorporating the time index as an additional feature and using recurrent networks to learn
156   irregular temporal time dynamics (Che et al., 2016; Schirmer et al., 2022). Another line of work
157   leverages ODEs by parameterizing the governing function in ODEs with neural networks. Addi-
158   tionally, these methods combine ODEs with recurrent structures to learn the underlying dynamics
159   of time series, inherently addressing irregularity (Chen et al., 2019; Rubanova et al., 2019). Atten-
160   tion mechanism in Transformers have also been improved to process irregular time series (Vaswani
161   et al., 2023). For example, mTand (Shukla and Marlin, 2021b) first replaces the positional en-
      coding with fixed continuous time embedding and then maps the irregular input into regular latent


                                                        3

162
      space. To account for the inter-channel dependencies in the multivariate time series, graph-based
163   approaches (Zhang et al., 2022; 2024a) have also been explored by treating each variable as a node
164   in the graph and learning the edge weights to capture the multivariate correlation. However, they are
165   primarily trained under fully or semi-supervised paradigms (Tipirneni and Reddy, 2022b; Zhang
166   et al., 2023), requiring large amounts of high-quality labeled or observed data. In contrast, ap-
167   proaches like PrimeNet (Chowdhury et al., 2023) and PATIS (Beebe-Wang et al., 2023) leverage
168   self-supervised settings to learn representations of irregular time series and improve downstream
169   tasks with limited labeled data. However, current SSL methods remain less explored for regression
170   tasks. Moreover, they employ domain-specific model structures by applying channel-dependent
171
      structures directly to the input to learn the correlations between multivariates, potentially hindering
      their adaptability to diverse datasets with different numbers of variables and tasks.
172
173   In contrast, our work seeks to fill these gaps by designing a generalized SSL pretext task for irregular
174   time series regression, not only effectively capturing the intrinsic characteristic in irregular time
175   series but also learn more robust and generalizable representations.

178
179   In this section, we present the details of our proposed GITaR, a masking and reconstruction pretrain-
180   ing mechanism designed to effectively handle irregularities in real-world time series data and learn
181   robust representations that can generalize across various domains.

      3.1   P RELIMINARIES
184
185   A multivariate irregular time series is denoted as O = (O1 , O2 , ..., OD ), where Oi represents
186   the ith variable among D variables. Each Oi is a univariate irregular time series represented as
187   Oi = (oit1 , oit2 , ..., oitN ), recorded at time stamp tn , n ∈ [1, Ni ]. Unlike regular time series, the
                                   i
188   time interval between two consecutive measurements ∆t = tn+1 − tn is not constant. The number
189   of observations Ni may also vary between different variables. We first synchronize and align the
190   D variables through the upsampling of each Oi to the maximum sample rate, ensuring a consistent
191   number of samples N for each variable. To preserve the irregularity, we simultaneously introduce
192
      additional mask variables M and a time index T to account for irregularities. Specifically, each mul-
      tivariate irregular time series can be represented as O = (O1 , O2 , ..., OD ) = (T, X, M) where:
193
      T ∈ RN ×D represents the union of timestamps at which any of the D variables have been sampled.
194
      N denotes the total number of unique timestamps in the series. X ∈ RN ×D constitutes the obser-
195   vation values at the recorded times. M ∈ {0, 1}N ×D is a binary masking matrix indicating whether
196   a variable has an observation at a specific sampled time. In specific, for each variable i at time tn , if
197   the variable is sampled at time stamp Mtin = 1, otherwise Mtin = 0.

      3.2   OVERALL ARCHITECTURE
200
201   The overview of GITaR is illustrated in Figure 2. The primary objective of GITaR is to learn a recon-
202   struction model R that effectively captures the underlying temporal dynamics in irregular time series
203   data while reconstructing masked portions of the input data. The choice of a reconstruction-based
204   approach is grounded in the fundamental concept of masking and prediction, which aligns closely
205   with the goals of time series regression tasks and facilitates learning complex temporal dynamics.
206   GITaR incorporates two key components: an irregular-sensitive masking and patching module, and
207
      an irregular-temporal encoder module. The first module preserves the original irregular patterns
      while masking and segments the multivariate time series into synchronized, channel-independent
208
      patches. This approach ensures effective learning of local semantics (i.e., irregular patterns) within
209
      patches and enhances generalization capability via channel-independent design, reducing process-
210   ing complexity, and mitigating long-range information loss. The second module aims to: i) learn
211   the local semantic embeddings for each patch through the irregular patch encoder, and ii) capture
212   the global temporal correlations among patches via the global temporal encoder. The irregular
213   patch encoder transforms each irregular patch p into a irregular-aware embedding Zpi using contin-
214   uous time embeddings and irregular time attention mechanisms, leading to a sequence of regularly
215   spaced embeddings. This process ensures local irregularity representation learning. Concurrently, a
      global temporal encoder captures long-range dependencies among Zpi . These embeddings are then

      3.3   I RREGULAR - SENSITIVE M ASKING AND PATCHING
237
238   To design a more accurate masking and reconstruction SSL framework tailored for irregular time
239   series data, we introduce an irregular-sensitive masking and patching strategy to capture the intrinsic
240   irregularity within the time series, deviating from common random masking techniques (He et al.,
241   2021; Huang et al., 2023) that can distort temporal dependencies in irregular data. Our strategy
242   preserved irregularity patterns and applied channel-independent processing, ensuring application
243   ability to various variables and enabling generalization across different domains.
244   Irregular-sensitive Masking. The irregular-sensitive masking data augmentation aims to maintain
245   the irregular patterns in the original time series. Specifically, recognizing that different regions
246   of the time series may have varying sampling densities, we mask a fixed timespan qn within each
247   variate rather than a constant number of observations. This approach ensures that in densely sampled
248   regions, more observations are masked, while in sparsely sampled regions, fewer observations are
249   masked. As each variable may have a different sampling frequency and observation gap, we apply
250
      the masking strategy independently for each univariate Oi .
251   Time-sensitive Patching. In irregular time series data, due to its sparsity, some regions might have
252   more dense observations while others do not. Therefore, simply mapping the whole time series into
253   latent space might lose such local information. We introduce a time-sensitive patching mechanism
254   during the data processing stage to capture local information. Different from the standard time
255   series patching (Nie et al., 2023) that segments regular time series into equal length of subseries-
256
      level patches, our approach aims to preserve the synchronization among channels, leading to patches
      that span fixed time horizons but contain varying numbers of samples. We, therefore, segment
257                                                                               p=P
      each univariate time series into fixed time horizon patches: Oi = [Opi ]p=1      = (Tpi , Xpi , Mpi ) for
258
      each univariate i, where P is the number of resulting patches. This patching strategy allows us to
259
      preserve the temporal structure of the irregular time series, enabling efficient processing and local
260   feature extraction and maintaining synchronization across multiple channels.

263
264   After the masking and patching stage, each univariate irregular time series has been transformed into
265   a series of patches. However, the irregularity challenge, i.e., the unevenly sampled observations,
266   persists within each chunked patch. To enable the reconstruction model to capture such irregular
267   data, we first design the irregular patch encoding mechanism to learn the embeddings that encode
268   the local irregularity within each chunked patch effectively. Furthermore, to learn the temporal
269   dependencies among these patches, we design the global temporal encoding mechanism to capture
      the correlation across multiple patches.

478
479   We have presented GITaR, a novel generalized masking and reconstruction pretraining framework
480   for irregular time series regression. The design of the irregular-sensitive masking and time-patching,
481   the irregular-time encoder, and the global temporal encoding module has shown promise in captur-
482   ing the underlying dynamics of irregular time series for effective representation learning. More
483   importantly, combined with the channel-independent design, our GITaR has demonstrated superior
484   performance across various domains, effectively leveraging it as a foundational model for multi-
485   ple tasks or data types involving irregular time series. This work potentially provides a robust and
      generalised framework for a range of real-world time series applications.

633   A.2   E XPERIMENTAL P ROTOCOLS
634
635   Interpolation Task: For the interpolation task on the PhysioNet dataset, we aim to predict missing
636   values within a given time series. In specifically, we use 8000 data cases. To evaluate the model’s
637   performance under different conditions, we vary the number of observed points used for prediction.
638   Specifically, we test scenarios where 50% to 90% of available data points are used to predict the
639   remaining values, during the test phase.
640   Forecasting Task: For the forecasting task, we aim to use the previous data to predict the subsequent
641   future observation values across various variables. Specifically, during the test time, for Physionet
642   and MIMIC, we use the first 24 hours to predict the next 24 hours. For Human Activity, we use the
643   first 3000 milliseconds to predict the next 1000 milliseconds. For USHCN, we use two years of data
644   to predict the following year.

647
      We employ the following state-of-the-art time series modeling algorithms for comparison.

648
      PatchTST: (Nie et al., 2023) enhances the original Transformer architecture by introducing sub-
649   series level patching and channel-independence, effectively capturing cross-time dependencies for
650   time series forecasting.
651
652
      Crossformer: (Zhang and Yan, 2023) modifies the vanilla Transformer architecture by implement-
      ing Cross-Time Attention and Cross-Dimension Attention, enabling it to capture both temporal-wise
653
      and channel-wise correlations in forecasting tasks.
654
655   mTAND: (Shukla and Marlin, 2021b) introduces an irregular time series attention mechanism. This
656   method replaces traditional positional embedding with continuous-time embedding, learning corre-
657   lations between observed data and continuous-time steps to map irregular data onto a fixed, regular
658
      representation space.
659   T-patchGNN: (Zhang et al., 2024a) tackles irregular time series forecasting through a multi-step
660   approach. It first employs a transformable patching strategy to segment irregular time series data
661   into uniform temporal resolutions. Subsequently, it utilizes a transformable time-aware convolution
662   network to map the irregular data to a latent space, and finally leverages a graph neural network to
663   learn inter-channel correlations.
664   TS2Vec: (Yue et al., 2022) performs hierarchical contrastive learning over augmented context views,
665   which enables a robust contextual representation for each timestamp. The method is for regular time
666   series classification, regression and anomaly detection task.
667
      PrimeNet: (Chowdhury et al., 2023) propose time-sensitive contrastive learning and data recon-
668   struction to learn from data irregularity patterns. The method is for irregular time series classification
669   and interpolation.

      A.4    I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
673
      Our implementation consists of two primary stages: representation learning and task-specific fine-
674
      tuning. During the representation learning stage, we pretrain the model using mask and reconstruc-
675   tion tasks to develop robust representations applicable to various downstream tasks. For each dataset
676   and task, we utilize the entire training set to train the representation model. Subsequently, we fine-
677   tune this model based on the specific task and dataset requirements before evaluating it against the
678   test set to obtain the final representation. To optimize performance, we conduct a comprehensive grid
679   search over key hyperparameters. The batch size is varied among [32, 64, 128], while the learning
680   rate is tested at [0.01, 0.001, 0.0001]. For the model architecture, we explore different configura-
681   tions: the number of heads (H) in mTAND is varied among [1, 2, 3], and the number of Transformer
682   encoder layers is tested at [2, 4, 6]. To preserve the irregularity within each univariate time series,
683
      we implement a masking strategy controlled by the timespan parameter qn. This parameter, varied
      among [2, 3, 4], ensures that the masking ratio for each univariate series remains between 30% and
684
      50%. The choice of patch value, critical for handling data sparsity, is selected from [32, 64, 128]
685
      based on the temporal density characteristics of the data. All experiments are run 5 times and the
686   results are reported with mean and standard deviation. All models are implemented using PyTorch,
687   and the experimental evaluations are conducted on NVIDIA A100-SXM-80GB GPUs.
