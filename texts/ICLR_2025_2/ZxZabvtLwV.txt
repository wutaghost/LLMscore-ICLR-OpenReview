009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Mapping a protein sequence to its underlying biological function is a critical prob-
012            lem of increasing importance in biology. In this work, we propose ProtEx, a
013            retrieval-augmented approach for protein function prediction that leverages exem-
014            plars from a database to improve accuracy and robustness and enable generalization
015            to unseen classes. Our approach relies on a novel multi-sequence pretraining task,
016
               and a fine-tuning strategy that effectively conditions predictions on retrieved ex-
               emplars. Our method achieves state-of-the-art results across multiple datasets and
017
               settings for predicting Enzyme Commission (EC) numbers, Gene Ontology (GO)
018
               terms, and Pfam families. Our ablations and analysis highlight the impact of condi-
019            tioning predictions on exemplar sequences, especially for classes and sequences
020            less well represented in the training data.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Proteins perform a wide array of functions within organisms, captured by biologists using ontologies
026   such as Gene Ontology (GO) terms (Ashburner et al., 2000), Enzyme Commission (EC) numbers (Tip-
027
      ton & Boyce, 2000), and Pfam families (Bateman et al., 2004). Mapping proteins to such functional
      annotations can address key problems in biology, medicine, and chemistry (Price et al., 2018; Durairaj
028
      et al., 2023). However, given the expense of wet lab experiments, and the rapid growth of protein
029
      sequence databases (Uniprot, 2023), it is critical to extend coverage using computational protein
030   function predictions. Protein function prediction techniques largely fall into two categories, with
031   different strengths and weaknesses. Homology-based approaches align the query protein to annotated
032   sequences via methods such as BLAST (Altschul et al., 1997) or profile hidden Markov models (Eddy,
033   1998) allowing propagation of a label to the query. More recently, deep learning approaches directly
034   predict a protein’s function from its amino acid sequence.
035
      While these approaches have been successful, key obstacles remain. A critical challenge is out-of-
036   distribution generalization across both label and sequence space. For example, recent expansions of
037   the Pfam database were driven by the identification of new classes (Mistry et al., 2021), for which
038   there are few annotated sequences. Moreover, a substantial number of proteins belong to the “dark
039   matter" of the protein universe (Durairaj et al., 2023), i.e. their sequences are dissimilar from those
040   of any characterized proteins, posing considerable challenges for both homology-based and deep
041   learning methods.
042   In this work, we propose ProtEx, a method for Protein function prediction via retrieved Exemplars.
043   ProtEx is a semiparametric approach that combines aspects of non-parametric similarity search
044   based methods and parametric deep learning models to achieve increased accuracy and robustness.
045   ProtEx is inspired by retrieval augmented methods in natural language processing and vision,
046   e.g. (Lewis et al., 2020; Pasupat et al., 2021; Izacard et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023a; Long et al., 2022),
047   which show advantages over fully parametric models in capturing tail information (Kandpal et al.,
048   2023) and performing few-shot tasks through conditioning on task exemplars (Min et al., 2022; Chen
049   et al., 2022).
050   As shown in Figure 1, given a query protein and candidate label, ProtEx first uses a homology-based
051   retriever (such as BLAST) to obtain a class-conditioned set of positive and negative exemplars from
052   the training set. Our neural model is trained to make a task-based comparison across the set of
053   exemplars and the query to output a binary decision of whether the query sequence has the same
      label as the positive exemplars. To enable the model to efficiently learn the relationship between

082   2       BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
083
084   We review traditional methods for protein similarity search, neural models for protein function
085   prediction, and related work on retrieval-augmented models from other domains.
086
087   Protein Similarity Search Proteins with similar sequences often perform similar functions, and
088
      for a given query sequence, homologous sequences can be retrieved from a database using a variety
      of similarity-based search methods (Altschul et al., 1997; Johnson et al., 2010; Remmert et al., 2012).
089
      These enable homology-based inference (Loewenstein et al., 2009), where functional labels from
090
      retrieved homologs are transferred to the query. ProtEx can be seen as performing homology-based
091   inference where information from the homologs is aggregated using a learned, non-linear model. A
092   ubiquitous tool for similarity search is BLAST (Altschul et al., 1990; 1997), which identifies and
093   scores local sequence alignments using an empirically-derived substitution cost matrix (Henikoff
094   & Henikoff, 1992). Our method leverages BLAST and BLAST-inspired techniques to retrieve
095   exemplars (see §4). BLAST also serves as a strong baseline for homology-based inference when
096   used in isolation.
097
098   Neural Models for Protein Function Prediction Recent work has shown that deep models map-
099   ping a protein sequence to functional predictions can outperform traditional alignment-based tech-
100   niques. Models can be fit from scratch (Kulmanov et al., 2018; Ryu et al., 2019; Cao & Shen, 2021;
101
      Bileschi et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2022; Sanderson et al., 2023) or fine-tuned from a model pretrained on
      unlabeled protein sequences (Strodthoff et al., 2020; Dohan et al., 2021; Villegas-Morcillo et al., 2021;
102
      Yuan et al., 2023; Dickson & Mofrad, 2023). The amino acid sequence largely specifies a protein’s
103
      structure and function (Anfinsen, 1973), and hence is often used as input to models. However, other
104   approaches also encode protein structures (Sokolov & Ben-Hur, 2010; Roy et al., 2012; Konc et al.,
105   2013; Gligorijević et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Lai & Xu, 2022), with broad coverage due to
106   advancements in protein structure prediction (Jumper et al., 2021; Baek et al., 2021). Using structures
107

      in addition to or instead of protein sequences, either for prediction or retrieval, is largely orthogonal
109   to the contributions of this paper, and future extensions to ProtEx could incorporate such techniques.
110   Disordered proteins, which are not well characterized by a static structure would need to be handled
111   carefully, however (Ruff & Pappu, 2021). We do not consider prediction approaches based on protein
112   interaction networks (Mostafavi et al., 2008; You et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2019; Kulmanov et al.,
113   2024), which have demonstrated high accuracy where such networks are already established, but
114   have limited overall coverage.
115   Particularly relevant are recent methods combining neural models and retrieval for protein function
116   prediction. Notin et al. (2022) combines likelihood scores from a protein language model and
117   an MSA to predict the functional effects of mutations. Hamamsy et al. (2023) distills a protein
118   similarity function into a neural network, and Dickson & Mofrad (2023) fine-tunes a retrieval model
119   for homology-based inference. The ProtIR method of Zhang et al. (2024) uses transductive learning
120   where predictions from a retriever and neural classifier are iteratively updated towards agreement. In
121   contrast to retrieval methods, which form predictions by aggregating pairwise similarity scores over
122   sequences, ProtEx predicts conditioned on multiple labeled exemplar sequences. This also differs
123
      from methods that use the correlation structure in aligned sets of homologous sequences (MSAs),
      without accompanying annotations of their function, to inform, e.g., structure prediction (Marks et al.,
124
      2011; Jumper et al., 2021; Baek et al., 2021) or representation learning (Rao et al., 2021).
125
126
127
      Retrieval-Augmented Models and In-Context Fine-tuning Retrieval augmented neural models
      have shown success for a variety of text generation and classification tasks, with particular strength in
128
      recalling long tail knowledge, where even large parametric language models (LMs) struggle (Kandpal
129
      et al., 2023). Examples include retrieval-augmented language models, and models for machine
130   translation, question answering, semantic parsing, and text classification (Khandelwal et al., 2020;
131   2021; Karpukhin et al., 2020; Guu et al., 2020; Lewis et al., 2020; Izacard et al., 2023; Pasupat et al.,
132   2021; Yu et al., 2023a; Chalkidis & Kementchedjhieva, 2023). Retrieved context or exemplars come
133   from large-scale unlabeled collections or labeled sets for supervised learning (Wang et al., 2022; Lewis
134   et al., 2021). Computer vision and multimodal models also benefit from retrieval augmentation (Long
135   et al., 2022; Ramos et al., 2023). A focus in NLP have been methods to adapt a pretrained parametric
136   language model into one that can integrate information from retrieved sequences, and joint training
137   of a retriever and generation model. In this work, we use pre-existing protein similarity models for
138
      retrieval and pre-train a protein sequence model to make inferences from multiple proteins.
139   ProtEx is also related to in-context tuning methods for few-shot tasks (Min et al., 2022; Chen et al.,
140   2022), where pretrained language models are meta-trained to make predictions given an input and
141   task-relevant exemplars. These works show strong performance on unseen tasks, enabled by the LM’s
142   ability to make predictions from an input and a few in-context exemplars. Similarly, ProtEx can be
143   seen as meta-training on multiple binary classification tasks with in-context exemplars and, as shown
144
      in Section 4.5, can adapt to new labels without additional fine-tuning.

      Given a protein represented as a sequence of amino acids, x, our goal is to predict a set of associated
149   labels, y ⊂ L, where L is a set of protein function labels, such as EC numbers, GO terms, or
150   Pfam labels. Core to our approach is to condition model predictions on a set of annotated exemplar
151   sequences, which are retrieved from a training set, as visualized in Figure 2.
152
153
      3.1   OVERVIEW
154
155   To predict a set of labels y given x, we first determine a set of candidate labels, L̂x ⊆ L, and then
156   make an independent binary prediction for each candidate label l ∈ L̂x , conditioning on exemplars
157   that are selected based on both x and l. Due to our model’s limited context window, we can only
158   condition on a limited number of exemplars per prediction. Therefore, our approach has several
159   advantages compared to generating y based on x and a single limited set of exemplars. First, we
160   can focus each prediction on the exemplars that are most relevant towards understanding the class
161   boundary of a specific label l. Second, we can consider a larger and more diverse set of candidate
      labels than those corresponding to a limited number of exemplars. Finally, our approach can be seen

235   3.2    T RAINING
236
237
      Model training consists of two stages. First, we pre-train using unlabeled sequence pairs. Then, we
      fine-tune for a specific task by constructing positive and negative examples given a set of labeled
238
      sequences. In both stages, models are trained to maximize the likelihood of a target sequence given
239
      an input sequence, given our generic encoder-decoder architecture (Raffel et al., 2020).
240
241
      Pre-training Our pre-training task shown in Figure 3. Prior work has primarily pre-trained on
242   single unlabeled sequences, then fine-tuned with a single sequence as input to improve protein
243   function prediction (Dohan et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2023). In contrast, our goal is to train models that
244   implicitly compare a query sequence with exemplar sequences. We thus propose a new pre-training
245   objective over multiple sequences. We sample pairs of sequences from UniRef90 (Suzek et al., 2015),
246   approximating a uniform distribution over sequence similarity buckets. We then implement a version
247   of the span denoising objective of Raffel et al. (2020). We mask the sequences by randomly replacing
248   approximately 10% of residues with a placeholder index. The prediction target consists of predicting
249   the masked residues as well as the similarity score between the two sequences. As a simple and easy
250
      to compute measure of sequence similarity, we use Levenshtein distance normalized by the sequence
      length, bucketed to the nearest multiple of 5. Intuitively, the prediction target encourages the model
251
      to learn to approximately align and compare the two input sequences. We then expect the model to
252
      implicitly learn a more task-specific notion of similarity during fine-tuning. Pre-trained checkpoints
253   are shared across all tasks that we study. See Appendix A.2 for details and §4 for analysis.
254

      For the Pfam dataset, we use an alternative to BLAST for selecting similar sequences, detailed in
401
      Appendix B.5. The Pfam dataset is considerably larger than the other datasets (see Table 1), and
402
      running BLAST for all examples in the training set can take considerable time. Moreover, BLAST
403   does not achieve as strong of a result for Pfam classification as it does for the EC and GO tasks.
404   Therefore, we implemented an alternative retrieval system that can be more easily parallelized and
405   customized than BLAST. For every sequence, we select a set of similar sequences for each class
406   independently. For efficiency, we randomly select up to a maximum number of sequences per class in
407   the training set, and then rank these sequences according to a local alignment score that is similar to
408   the one computed by BLAST. Based on analysis of the effect of restricting the number of classes
409   (see Appendix C.4.1), we opted to consider all classes as candidate labels. We also evaluated this
410   strategy for EC prediction although it did not perform as well as our BLAST-based approach (see
411
      Appendix C.3.2). We use 4 positive exemplars and zero negative exemplars, since we found in our
      early experiments that additional positive exemplars added more benefit.

468   Generalization to New Labels We tested the ability of ProtEx to make predictions for new classes
469
      not seen during fine-tuning. On the EC and GO clustered splits we randomly removed 10% of classes

471

472   performance decrease when candidate labels are not included in the model input (see Appendix C.3.1),
473   further indicating that the model is conditioning its predictions on the exemplar sequences as opposed
474   to directly representing the sequence to class relationship in the model parameters.
475
476   Pre-training Analysis We show results for ablating the key elements of our pre-training recipe
477   in Table 7. Notably, there is a drop in end task performance when pre-training with only a single
478   sequence as input, as commonly done in prior work, as opposed to pre-training over sequence pairs.
479   This indicates that our pre-training task is useful for retrieval-augmented models that are fine-tuned
480   to make comparisons across multiple sequences. See Appendix B.4 for further details.
481
482   Model Architectures and Scaling In Appendix C.2 we compare Small and Base sized models,
483   finding that there is a modest benefit to increasing model size from Small to Base. We also compare
484   the Fusion-in-Decoder (FiD) approach introduced by Izacard & Grave (2021) with a standard
485   Transformer, finding that this may be viable path towards scaling to more exemplars, but that there is
      a performance drop, indicating that cross-attention between exemplar sequences is beneficial.

       5              L IMITATIONS AND D ISCUSSION
511
512   Computational Requirements While we use a model size and number of pre-training steps
513   comparable to or less than prior work (Appendix B.3), the cost of inference with our method is
514
      potentially larger due to encoding multiple protein sequences and making independent predictions
      for each class. To mitigate these factors, we have considered the FiD architecture (Appendix C.2)
515
      and a candidate label generator (§3), which offer a path towards more efficient inference. Regardless,
516
      improvements in accuracy and robustness can justify an increase in computational cost for some
517   applications, given the much greater cost of running wet-lab experiments to annotate protein function.
518
519
520
      Scope and Future Work In this work we focused on training and inference procedures that
      can effectively condition predictions on retrieved exemplars, using a general-purpose Transformer
521
      architecture and standard methods for retrieval (e.g. BLAST). Going forward, our approach could
522
      potentially be further improved using enhanced similarity search techniques such as those based
523   on protein structure (Zhang et al., 2022; Hamamsy et al., 2023; Van Kempen et al., 2024) and
524   more specialized architectures. Finally, we focused on predicting EC, GO, and Pfam labels. Other
525   tasks such as fitness prediction (Romero et al., 2013) or generating free-text descriptions of protein
526   function (Gane et al., 2022; Abdine et al., 2024) could be of interest for future work.
527
528
      Broader Impact and Ethical Considerations Our method enables more accurate and robust
529   prediction of protein functional annotations. Any computationally derived annotation should be
530   verified by wet lab experiments where possible, especially for critical applications. Our method
531   extends a long history of prior work that develops such tools and the community’s safeguards for how
532   to apply them in an ethical manner applies here as well.
533
534
535    6              C ONCLUSION
536
537   We proposed ProtEx, a semiparametric approach that combines aspects of homology-based similarity
538   search with pre-trained neural models. ProtEx achieves state-of-the-art results on EC, GO, and Pfam
539   classification tasks. Our work highlights the potential of retrieval-augmented methods for improving
      the accuracy and robustness of protein function prediction.

      A.1       M ODEL I NTERFACE
867
868   We use the character-based vocabulary of Xue et al. (2022), which ensures that amino acid sequences
869   are tokenized into their individual amino acid residues. We represent labels as short character
870   sequences such as EC:1.2.3.4. We use single characters to indicate the start and end of amino
871   acid sequences, and to indicate whether an exemplar sequence is positive or negative.
872
      The training target is a single character sequence, p or n, for positive and negative examples,
873   respectively. At inference time, we determine the score based on the probability assigned to the single
874   character sequence p.

      A.2       P RETRAINING D ETAILS
877
878   As our fine-tuned models need to make comparisons between query and exemplar sequences of
879   varying similarities, we construct the dataset such that all similarity ranges are well represented in
880   the pre-training data. For each pair, we sampled one sequence uniformly from UniRef90. Then,
881   we sampled a second sequence, approximating a uniform distribution over similarity buckets. The
882   resulting distribution of normalized Levenshtein similarities in the pretraining data is shown in
883   Table 8.
884   The process is loosely analogous to some methods explored in NLP. For example, Sellam et al.
885   (2020) pre-trained models to compute BLEU scores over pairs of strings. BLEU is a deterministic
886   measure of string similarity. Models were then fine-tuned on human labeled data to learn a more
887   task-specific notion of similarity. Pre-training on a context that includes related sequences is perhaps
888   also analogous to the in-context pre-training method proposed by Shi et al. (2024), which includes
889   a language modeling objective over related documents, showing this is useful relative to randomly
890
      selected documents, for various downstream tasks.

1043
1044
       The computational cost of self-attention in a standard Transformer scales quadratically with input
       length. To more efficiently encode exemplars, we studied the Fusion-in-Decoder (FiD) approach
1045
       introduced by Izacard & Grave (2021). We apply this approach to encode the query and each exemplar
1046
       in a separate encoder, effectively masking attention between exemplars. Notably, this model variant
1047   consists of the same set of parameters and can be initialized from the same pre-trained checkpoint. We
1048   compare the performance of the standard and FiD architecture for Small (60M) models in Table 11.
1049
1050
       The FiD architecture performs only slightly worse than the standard Transformer, indicating this may
       be one path towards more efficiently encoding a larger number of exemplars. On the other hand, the
1051
       drop in performance suggests there is value in attention across exemplar sequences.
1052
1053   Another potential alternative would be a specialized architecture such as MSA Transformer (Rao
1054   et al., 2021). This would require a couple of modifications to the MSA Transformer architecture.
1055   First, MSA Transformer would need to be adapted to include functional labels along with unlabeled
1056
       sequences. Second, the architecture requires that all sequences are aligned as a preprocessing step.
       Intuitively, our pre-training and fine-tuning procedures are designed to teach the model to implicitly
1057
       align sequences without relying on heuristic alignments. Notably, the main difference between
1058
       MSA Transformer and a standard Transformer is the more restricted attention operations allowed in
1059   MSA Transformer. As we have shown, using a Fusion-in-Decoder Transformer, which has a more
1060   restricted attention mechanism, leads to a modest drop in performance. Therefore, this would be a
1061   concern for any architecture that similarly restricts the attention mechanism such as MSA Transformer.
1062   Regardless, such specialized architectures could be a path to explore for future work.
