009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In dynamic real-world settings, models must adapt to changing data distributions,
012            a challenge known as Test Time Adaptation (TTA). This becomes even more chal-
013            lenging in scenarios where test samples arrive sequentially, and the model must
014            handle open-set conditions by distinguishing between known and unknown classes.
015            Towards this goal, we propose ROSITA, a novel framework for Open set Single Im-
016            age Test Time Adaptation using Vision-Language Models (VLMs). To enable the
017            separation of known and unknown classes, ROSITA employs a specific contrastive
018
               loss, termed ReDUCe loss, which leverages feature banks storing reliable test sam-
               ples. This approach facilitates efficient adaptation of known class samples to do-
019
               main shifts while equipping the model to accurately reject unfamiliar samples.Our
020
               method sets a new benchmark for this problem, validated through extensive ex-
021            periments across diverse real-world test environments.. Our code is anonymously
022            released at https://github.com/anon-tta/ROSITA.git

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026   Over the past decade, substantial advancements have been achieved in various computer vision
027   tasks Deng et al. (2009); Ren et al. (2015); He et al. (2017); Everingham et al. (2010). However,
028   these achievements are predominantly realized under the assumption that both training and test
029   data originate from the same distribution. In contrast, the real world is dynamic and ever-changing,
030   making such assumptions often untenable. Distribution gaps between training and test data manifest
031   in diverse forms Hendrycks & Dietterich (2019); Peng et al. (2019b), including domain shifts and
032   semantic shifts. Domain shifts emerge from variations in lighting, weather, camera specifications,
033
      or geographical locations between the train and test datasets. Semantic shifts occur when a model,
      initially trained on a specific set of classes, encounters previously unseen classes during testing.
034
      Hence, navigating deep learning models through these dynamic test environments is imperative.
035
036   Researchers have been tackling the robustness of models to domain shifts, by diving into paradigms
037   like Unsupervised Domain Adaptation Ganin et al. (2016), Source-Free Domain Adaptation Liang
038   et al. (2020); Yang et al. (2022). More recently, the problem of Test Time Adaptation (TTA) Wang
039
      et al. (2021); Schneider et al. (2020); Niu et al. (2022) and Continuous Test Time Adaptation
      (CTTA) Döbler et al. (2023) has come to the forefront. TTA is characterized by three key factors: (1)
040
      No access to source data; (2) No ground truth labels for test data; (3) An online adaptation scenario
041
      where the model encounters test samples only once, reflecting the online nature of real-world.
042
043   Another facet of distribution gaps lies in semantic shifts Li et al. (2023); Lee et al. (2023). While
044   TTA methods have predominantly focused on closed-set scenarios, the real world seldom operates
045
      within such constraints. A classic example is that of autonomous driving Wang et al. (2022),
      where models trained for specific geographical locations are deployed elsewhere. For instance,
046
      a model trained to recognize only vehicles commonly seen in urban areas—such as car, truck,
047
      motorcycle—may incorrectly classify a bicycle as a motorcycle when deployed in rural settings. In
048   these new environments, the model must be able to identify elements that are not relevant to its
049   training as unknown, rather than misclassifying them as part of the known set of categories. This
050   underscores the importance of Open Set Adaptation. Though this has only recently been explored in
051   the context of TTA Li et al. (2023); Lee et al. (2023), current TTA and CTTA methods Wang et al.
052   (2021); Döbler et al. (2023) generally rely on accumulating a batch of images to update the model,
053   which may not be feasible in scenarios where test samples arrive individually. This highlights the
      growing need for efficient Single Image Test Time Adaptation methods.

      Parallel to the recent advances in TTA, there has been tremendous progress in the development of
055   large scale Vision Language Models (VLM) like CLIP Radford et al. (2021). Having trained on large
056   scale web scrapped image-text pairs, these VLMs Radford et al. (2021) have demonstrated impressive
057   zero shot generalization capabilities, making it a natural candidate for TTA. Recently, Shu et al.
058   (2022); Samadh et al. (2023); Karmanov et al. (2024) have shown that these VLMs can be adapted on
059   each image during inference, further improving the zero shot generalization performance.
060
      Current VLM based works Shu et al. (2022); Karmanov et al. (2024) address Single Image TTA in
061   closed-set setting and do not explicitly handle open set scenarios. Recent CNN based open-set TTA
062   works Li et al. (2023); Lee et al. (2023) operate on batches of test images. In this work, we address
063   both the challenges and establish a benchmark for Open set Single Image Test Time Adaptation using
064   VLMs. We refer to the classes of interest with respect to a particular downstream classification task
065   (say 10 classes of CIFAR-10) as desired classes and the rest as undesired classes (say 10 digits of
066   MNIST). In such scenarios, it is necessary to filter out undesired class samples, preventing them from
067   negatively impacting model adaptation during test-time adaptation (TTA). To achieve this, we employ
068   a Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) Fisher (1936); Li et al. (2023)-based class identifier, which
069
      first determines whether a test sample belongs to a desired or undesired class. Samples identified as
      belonging to the desired classes are then classified accordingly into one of the desired classes. The
070
      challenges we address are twofold: (1) Enabling TTA of VLMs where samples arrive sequentially,
071
      and (2) handling open-set scenarios where test samples may belong to either Desired or Undesired
072   classes. To tackle these challenges, we utilize Reliable samples to differentiate Desired vs Undesired
073   classes through a Contrastive loss, termed ReDUCe, within the framework of Open set Single Image
074   Test time Adaptation (ROSITA). Our contributions are summarized as follows:
075
076          • To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to tackle the challenging and realistic problem
077            of Open set Single Image Test Time Adaptation using VLMs, setting a new benchmark.
078          • We provide a comprehensive analysis on continuous adaptation of VLMs during test time
079            and identify LayerNorms to be the optimal set of parameters to adapt the model.
080          • Our framework, ROSITA, adapts a VLM to recognize desired class samples with domain
081            shifts while enabling it to effectively differentiate unfamiliar samples by saying “I don’t
082            know.” This distinction between desired and undesired class samples is achieved using our
083            ReDUCe loss, which dynamically contrasts these classes to enhance separability.
084          • We demonstrate the effectiveness of our method through extensive experiments across a
085            diverse array of domain adaptation benchmarks, simulating various real-world test environ-
086            ments, with samples from single domain, continuous and frequently changing domains. We
087            also experiment varying the ratio of desired and undesired class samples in the test stream.

      2.1    P ROBLEM S ETUP
091
092   Test stream. The model encounters a single test sample xt at time t, sampled from Dt = Dd ∪ Du
093   comprising of: (i) Desired class samples: Dd = {xt ; yt ∈ Cd }, with domain shift and belonging to
094   one of the Cd desired classes, for example, Cd = {car, bus, ..., motorcycle}; (ii) Undesired class
095   samples: Du = {xt ; yt ∈ Cu }, which have semantic shift (irrelevant classes) such that Cd ∩ Cu = ϕ.
096   Goal. Given a test sample xt arriving at time t, the goal is to be first recognize if it belongs to a
097   desired class or not, constituting a binary classification task. If xt is identified as a desired class
098   sample, a subsequent |Cd |-way classification is performed, else the prediction is “I don’t know”. In
099   essence, the overall process can be viewed as a |Cd | + 1 way classification problem.
100
      Open set Single Image TTA scenarios. We simulate several test scenarios inspired from the real
101
      world to evaluate the effectiveness of our method. (1) Single domain: We extend the standard TTA
102   scenario where the test samples come from an unseen domain Dd (say snow corruption of CIFAR-
103   10C) by incorporating undesired samples Du (say MNIST). (2) Continuously changing domains:
104   Here, Dt changes with time as (Dd1 ∪ Du ) → (Dd2 ∪ Du ) . . . → (Ddn ∪ Du ), where Ddi is the ith
105   domain encountered. (3) Frequently changing domains: Here, we significantly reduce the number of
106   samples per domain in continuous open set TTA. Lesser the samples per domain, more frequently the
107   domain of the test stream changes, simulating very dynamic open set test scenarios. (4) Vary the ratio
      of samples from Cd to Cu in the test stream.

      Open set TTA in the context of pretrained VLMs. TTA has traditionally focused on improving
109   the performance of CNNs, where models trained on clean data struggle in unseen environments
110   such as noisy or weather-affected conditions. However, these models are trained specifically on a
111   dataset to recognize a desired set of classes Cd . Recently VLMs such as CLIP Radford et al. (2021)
112   have demonstrated impressive zero-shot generalization performance across diverse domains without
113   any specific retraining. Due to the contrastive pretraining of (image, text) pairs in VLM, text-based
114   classifiers can be obtained for free by embedding text prompts of the form “A photo of a {class
115   name}” through its text encoder. Image features can then be matched with text-based classifiers to
116   perform |Cd |-way classification. This makes CLIP a natural candidate for TTA scenarios.
117   In the context of CLIP, it is non-trivial to define classes or domains as unseen, given its exposure to a
118   vast array of visual data including variations, corruptions, and styles. In general, CLIP can be used to
119   classify an image by making a choice from the given set of desired classes. However, it lacks the
120   ability to explicitly say I don’t know when presented with a sample which does not belong to the set
121   of desired classes. Also, despite CLIP’s strong zero-shot performance on clean data, its performance
122   on corrupted/style-shifted datasets like ImageNet-C/R is still subpar (Shu et al., 2022; Karmanov
123
      et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024), highlighting the need for handling severe domain shifts better. This
      makes the problem highly relevant and worth addressing.
124
125   To address this, we establish a strong benchmark by adapting current TTA methods based on CLIP,
126   as well as open-set TTA approaches designed for CNNs to evaluate CLIP’s performance in open-set
127   settings. Further, we introduce a novel framework called ROSITA, which achieves state-of-the-art
128   results, surpassing prior methods and setting a new standard for open-set TTA.

      2.2   BASELINES
130
131   We perform experiments using CLIP Radford et al. (2021) and MaPLe Khattak et al. (2023) backbones.
132   CLIP consists of a Vision (FV ) and Text (FT ) encoder, trained using contrastive learning on image-
133   text pairs. MaPLe backbone uses multimodal prompts to adapt CLIP for downstream tasks.
134
      Classification using VLMs. Given a test image xt and a set of desired classes Cd = {c1 , c2 , . . . cN },
135   we construct the text-based classifier by first prepending each class name with a predefined text
136   prompt pT = “A photo of a”. This forms class-specific text inputs {pT , ci }, which are then passed
137   through the text encoder to obtain text embeddings ti = FT ({pT ; ci }) for each ci ∈ Cd . As a result,
138   we get the text-based classifier {t1 , t2 , . . . t2 }. Finally, the class prediction is made by identifying
139   the text embedding ti that has the highest similarity to the image feature ft .
140
      Desired vs Undesired Class Identifier. In real-world, a deployed model may encounter instances
141   from both desired and undesired classes. We equip all methods Shu et al. (2022); Karmanov et al.
142   (2024); Zhang et al. (2024) with an LDA based parameter-free class identifier Fisher (1936); Li et al.
143   (2023) to reject undesired class samples. Subsequently, the model is adapted during test time.
144
      Benchmark for Open-set Single Image TTA. We adapt the single image closed-set TTA baselines
145
      ZSEval Radford et al. (2021), TPT Shu et al. (2022), PAlign Samadh et al. (2023), TDA Karmanov
146
      et al. (2024), DPE Zhang et al. (2024) for our problem setting. We also adapt TPT and PAlign for
147   continuous model update by adapting prompts, which we refer as TPT-C and PAlign-C respectively.
148   The test samples recognized to belong to Cu are not used to update the model as they can adversely
149   affect its performance on desired classes. We adapt two recent CNN based open-set TTA works
150   (K+1)PC Li et al. (2023), UniEnt Gao et al. (2024) for VLMs. We refer to Li et al. (2023) as
151   (K + 1)P C, as they perform (K + 1)-way Prototypical Classification. We equip all these baselines
152   (Appendix B) with the same LDA based desired vs undesired class identifier for fair comparison.
153   We first present our preliminary analysis on continuous adaptation of VLMs. We then describe the
154   LDA based Cd vs Cu class identifier Li et al. (2023) and the proposed ROSITA framework.

161   has only been explored very recently Shu et al. (2022); Karmanov et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024)

204   Therefore, adapting the image encoder allows for more effective handling of domain shifts while
205   retaining the class-level discriminative information from the text modality. This ensures that the

      model can be updated continuously without the need for resets, ultimately enhancing its performance

      This problem can be viewed as a binary classification problem between desired and undesired samples
217   based on the score st . Defining a threshold to discriminate between the two can be particularly
218   challenging in the TTA scenario as the samples are only accessible in an online manner. To circumvent
219   this issue, following Li et al. (2023), we store the scores in a score bank S, which is continuously
220   updated in an online manner to store the latest |S| scores, approximating the latest distribution
221   of scores of the test data. Given this, the optimal threshold can be estimated by performing 1D

223   threshold that minimizes the variance of scores of samples from Cd and Cu . For a threshold τ , let

236   3   P ROPOSED ROSITA F RAMEWORK
237
238   Given a single test sample xt at time t, it is first identified as a desired or undesired class sample as
239
      described above. This is important, since, using undesired class samples can have a negative impact
      on model adaptation. In this work, we propose a test time objective that can leverage both desired
240
      and undesired class samples through feature banks to enhance the discriminability between them.
241
242   Reliable samples for TTA. We first identify a test sample xt as a reliable desired or undesired class
243   sample based on its score st . As we have access to an approximate distribution of the scores as
244   described in Section 2.4, we leverage the statistics µd and µu estimated through LDA to identify
245
      reliable samples. A test sample xt is said to be a reliable sample belonging to desired classes Cd if
      its score st > µd and a reliable sample from any of the other classes Cu if its score st < µu . We
246
      leverage Reliable samples to differentiate Desired vs Undesired class samples through a Contrastive
247
      (ReDUCe) Loss for Open-set Single Image Test time Adaptation, illustrated in Figure 2.
248
249   ReDUCe Loss. A contrastive objective typically needs positive and negative features, the goal
250   being to maximize the similarity between a sample and its positive (could be augmentation Chen
251
      et al. (2020) or nearest neighbours Dwibedi et al. (2021)), while minimizing its similarity with the
      negatives. Such objectives (Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2020; Khosla et al., 2020; Dwibedi et al.,
252
      2021) have been extensively used to learn good image representations in a self-supervised way. While
253
      self-supervised learning assumes access to abundant data in an offline manner giving the freedom
254   to carefully choose positives and negatives, this problem is set in an online scenario, where the test
255   samples arrive one at a time and are accessible only at that instant. This challenging setting makes it
256   non trivial to use objectives by Dwibedi et al. (2021). To circumvent this issue of lack of abundant
257   test data, we propose to store two dynamically updated feature banks Md and Mu of sizes Nd and
258   Nu , to store the features of reliable samples from Cd and Cu respectively. We propose a ReDUCe
259   objective to contrast a reliable sample from Cd by choosing its positives and negatives as the K
260   nearest neighbours from Md and Mu respectively and vice versa for a reliable sample from Cu .
261   The buffer size for Md is set as |Cd | × K, where |Cd | is the number of desired classes and K is the
262
      number of neighbours retrieved. The feature banks Md or Mu are updated with a feature ft if it is
      detected as a reliable sample from Cd and Cu respectively.

391   Key factors distinguishing ROSITA from prior works.
392
      1. Enhanced Use of LDA Statistics to identify Reliable samples: Apart from the threshold τt , ROSITA
393
      leverages the score statistics µd and µu provided by the LDA class identifier, combined with the
394
      novel ReDUCe loss function, to adapt the model. This synergy enhances the discriminability between
395   desired (Cd ) and undesired (Cu ) class samples, offering a clear advantage over baselines that use the
396   same LDA identifier but fail to exploit this additional information (Figure 3).
397
398
      2. Bridging CNN and VLM-Based TTA Insights: ROSITA integrates key insights from CNN-based
      TTA methods such as normalization layer updates with vision-language models (VLMs) (Section
399
      2.3). While simple in hindsight, this baseline was overlooked in prior VLM-based TTA works Shu
400
      et al. (2022); Karmanov et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024). ROSITA highlights how these learnings
401   can translate effectively to VLMs, underscoring their utility as a foundational approach for TTA.
402
403   3. Holistic Design for Open-set TTA: ROSITA introduces the ReDUCe loss to distinctly separate
404
      desired (Cd )and undesired (Cu ) class samples using compact feature banks. Although it is inspired
      by contrastive learning frameworks Chen et al. (2020; 2022), it is specifically designed for open-set
405
      TTA: (i) Reliable samples from Cu use nearest Cu samples as negatives, and vice versa (ii) Unlike the
406
      Cd +1-way classification in Li et al. (2023), ROSITA forces Cd features to form distinct clusters and
407   pushes Cu features away. (iii) The feature banks are populated only with reliable samples, ensuring
408   robust updates during adaptation (see Appendix C.5). This approach addresses the significant overlap
409   of zero-shot scores st between Cd and Cu in vision-language models, reducing misclassification and
410   boosting discriminability.

527   the text encoder also depends on the number of classes. On the other hand, ROSITA requires two
528   forward passes and one backward pass through the vision encoder for reliable test samples. For e.g.,
529   for ImageNet-C dataset with 1000 classes, ZSEval, TPT, TDA and ROSITA require 5.71 GB, 23.24
530   GB, 5.71 GB and 5.73 GB GPU memory (refer Appendix C.8) to perform a single image based
531   model update. Hence, ROSITA is computationally very efficient, similar to that of ZSEval.
532
533   6    C ONCLUSION
534   In this work, we propose ROSITA, a novel framework to address the challenging problem Open
535   set Test Time Adaptation (TTA) on a single image basis. ROSITA effectively distinguishes between
536   samples from desired classes vs others by leveraging two dynamically updated feature banks. The
537   proposed ReDUCe loss facilitates effective model adaptation by using reliable, while mitigating
538   any negative impact of undesirable samples in the test stream. Through extensive experimentation
539   on diverse domain adaptation benchmarks, we demonstrate the effectiveness of ROSITA in several
      scenarios inspired by the dynamic real world environment. We discuss the limitations in B.5.

653   Here, we delve deeper into the ReDUCe loss function in ROSITA, breaking down its key components
654   and mathematically demonstrate why the proposed objective improves the separation of Cd and Cu
655   samples. We’ll focus on contrastive loss components LD and LU which are designed to improve
656
      discriminability.
657   ReDUCe loss in a nutshell. A test sample xt arrives at time t with feature representation ft . Two
658   feature banks, Mw and Ms store reliable sample features from Cd and Cu respectively. ReDUCe
659   loss aims to pull the test sample’s feature ft towards its positive samples z + , which are its K nearest
660   neighbors Qd = kN N (ft ; Md ) if it is a reliable Cd sample or Qu = kN N (ft ; Mu ) if it is a reliable

662
      neighbors from the undesired feature bank Mu if it is a reliable Cd sample or from the desired feature
      bank Md if it is a reliable Cu sample. The features ft , z + , z − are all unit norm vectors.

729   Interpretation of the Gradients.
730
731          • Both the gradient terms in Equations 12 and 14 have two components: Positive term z + and
732            Negative term p (z − ) z − . The positives and negatives are suitably chosen from the desired
733            and undesired feature banks.
734          • Positive term z + : The term z + pulls the test feature ft closer to its feature vectors z + . This
735            term represents the attraction force that encourages Cd samples to cluster together in LD
736            and Cu samples to cluster together in LU .
737          • Negative term p (z − ) z − : The negative samples z − exert a repulsive force, pushing ft away
738            from them. The strength of this repulsion is controlled by the softmax probabilities p (z − ),
739            where higher similarity between ft and z − increases the repulsion force. This inherently
740            models the degree of hard negatives from the negative feature bank.
741          • The overall gradient update encourages ft to move closer to its positives while moving away
742            from its negatives, enhancing the separation between samples from Cd and Cu classes.

      B     BASELINES

758
      B.1    V ISION L ANGUAGE M ODELS
759
760
      CLIP Radford et al. (2021) is a multimodal VLM consisting of two modules: Vision encoder and
761   Text encoder denoted as FV and FT respectively. During pre-training, the two modules are jointly
762   trained in a contrastive self-supervised fashion to align massive amounts of web scrapped image-text
763   pairs. CLIP has demonstrated impressive zero-shot performance across a wide variety of datasets.
764
      MaPLe Khattak et al. (2023) is a multimodal prompt learner model that simultaneously adapts
765
      both the vision and text encoders while finetuning CLIP for downstream tasks. They use learnable
766
      text prompts pT and bridge the two modalities using visual prompts obtained as pV = Proj(pT ).
767   Learnable tokens are also introduced in the deeper layers of both image and text encoders, to enable
768   progressive adaptation of the features.
769
770
      B.2    M ETHODS
771
772
      ZSEval (Radford et al., 2021): Given a test image xt , the image feature is extracted from the
773   vision encoder as ft = FV (xt ). For a C-class classification problem, the classifier is obtained by
774   prepending a predefined text prompt pT =”A photo of a”, with the class names {c1 , c2 , . . . cC } to
775   form class specific text inputs {pT , ci } for i ∈ {1, . . . C}. These texts are then embedded through
776   the text encoder as ti = FT ({pT ; ci }) to get the text classifiers {t1 , t2 , . . . tC }. The class prediction
777   is made by identifying the text feature ti which has the highest similarity with the image feature ft .
778
      TPT Shu et al. (2022) aims to improve the zero shot generalization ability of CLIP by providing
779   custom adaptable context for each image. This is done by prepending learnable text prompts pT
780   to the class names instead of a predefined text prompt. The text classifiers ti = FT ({pT ; ci }), i ∈
781   {1, 2, . . . C} are now a function of these learnable prompts, which are specially adapted for each test
782   image using an entropy minimization objective as arg minpT Lent . The entropy is obtained using the
783   average score vector of the filtered augmented views.
784
      PromptAlign (PAlign) (Samadh et al., 2023) leverages multimodal prompt learner model
785   MaPLe Khattak et al. (2023) to facilitate the adaptation of both vision and language encoders
786   for each test sample. They align the token distributions of source and target domains, considering
787   ImageNet as a proxy for the source dataset of CLIP. The vision and language prompts of MaPLe are
788   optimized with the objective arg min{pV ,pT } Lent + Lalign for each sample xt .
789
      TPT-C Shu et al. (2022)/PAlign-C (Samadh et al., 2023): We adapt TPT and PAlign for continuous
790
      model update, which we refer as TPT-C and PAlign-C respectively. The prompts {pT } and {pV , pT }
791   in TPT and PAlign are continuously updated with the test stream with their respective test objectives.
792
793   (K+1)PC (Li et al., 2023): This was the first work exploring open world TTA, however it was done
794
      in the context of CNNs and not VLMs. Also, the test samples come in batches, while we perform
      single image TTA. We adapt this method for our problem setting as follows: As we use VLMs, we
795
      use the text prototypes (instead of the source prototypes). The prototype pool is dynamically updated
796
      by adding features of reliable test samples recognized to belong to undesired classes. The vision
797   encoder is updated using a (K+1) way prototypical cross entropy loss.
798
799   TDA (Karmanov et al., 2024): TDA is a training-free dynamic adapter for test-time adaptation in
800
      vision-language models, utilizing a lightweight key-value cache for efficient pseudo label refinement
      without backpropagation.
801
802   DPE (Zhang et al., 2024):DPE accumulates task-specific knowledge by dynamically evolving two
803   sets of prototypes, textual and visual, during test time. These prototypes are refined to capture
804   increasingly accurate multi-modal representations for target classes. To ensure consistency between
805   modalities, DPE incorporates learnable residuals for each test sample, aligning textual and visual
806
      prototypes for improved representation alignment.
807   UniEnt Gao et al. (2024): This is a very recent work addressing open-set TTA in the context of CNNs.
808   They use a Distribution Aware Filter (DAF) based on Gaussian Mixture Modeling of the scores to
809   distinguish between desired and undesired class samples. They employ entropy minimization and
      entropy maximization objectives for desired and undesired class samples respectively.

1091   Why is ReDUCe loss better than Unified entropy objective for Open-set TTA of VLMs?
1092
1093         • Both LDA Li et al. (2023) and DAF Gao et al. (2024) were proposed for CNN based open-set
1094
               TTA where a source model is trained on say clean data and is adapted to new domains,
               with the observation that the feature-prototype similarity scores st can distinguish desired
1095
               and undesired class samples. In the case of VLMs, the source model is trained on a large
1096
               scale dataset and is adapted to potentially unseen/corrupted/covariate-shifted data. The
1097           prior that the feature-prototype similarity scores st can distinguish desired and undesired
1098           class samples does not translate to VLMs as the scores overlap significantly, as observed in
1099           ZSEval histogram plots in Figures 3 5 6.
1100
             • In the case of CNNs, where the the initial scores are well separated and model has access
1101           to a batch of test samples at a time, UniEnt leverages this to further aid the separation
1102           of desired and undesired class samples in the batch through the UniEnt objective. In the
1103           case of VLMs, the scores are not well separated initially. This results in the means µd and
1104           µu in the case of LDA to be very close leading to misclassification of Cd and Cu class
1105           samples using the estimated threshold τt . Similarly, in the case of DAF, the two components
1106           of GMM would not be very distinctive to well distinguish desired and undesired class
1107           samples. This misclassification can result in entropy minimization being applied on Cu
1108           samples and entropy maximization on Cd samples, which is undesirable. Employing UniEnt
1109
               objective with several misclassified samples may not actually separate desired and undesired
               classes, as also empirically observed in Tables 1 2 3 (UniEnt has high FPR rate in general).
1110
               Entropy maximization of Cu samples does not explicitly enforce the separation of desired
1111
               and undesired class samples in the feature space.
1112
1113
             • The LD and LU loss components of ReDUCe loss explicitly enforce the separation desired
               and undesired class samples in the common VL latent space, while the LRe loss aims to only
1114
               align the desired class samples to align with the text prototypes. With time, the model is
1115
               adapted such that undesired class samples are away from the desired class samples and also
1116           the text prototypes. This ReDUCe loss addresses the challenges in single image open-set
1117           TTA in a holistic manner, resulting in better performance.
1118
             • On adopting UniEnt objective to single-image TTA, either entropy minimization or max-
1119           imization loss would be active based on whether a test sample is identified as desired or
1120           undesired class sample, which is a limitation, as the objective cannot enforce distinction
1121           between the two types of features.
1122
             • In the case of CNNs, where the the initial scores are well separated and model has access
1123           to a batch of test samples at a time, UniEnt leverages this to further aid the separation of
1124           desired and undesired class samples in the batch through the UniEnt objective. In the case of
1125           VLMs, the scores are not well separated initially, hence the ReDUCe loss components (with
1126           the help of feature banks) is the driving force to better separate the desired and undesired
1127           class samples in the common latent space, resulting in lower FPR rates as a consequence.
