009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Instruction tuning—supervised fine-tuning using instruction-response pairs—is a
012            foundational step in transitioning pre-trained Large Language Models (LLMs)
013            into helpful and safe chat assistants. Our hypothesis is that establishing an ade-
014            quate output distribution can enable such a transition given the capabilities inher-
015            ent in pre-trained LLMs. To verify this, we propose Response Tuning (RT), which
016
               eliminates the instruction-conditioning step in instruction tuning and solely fo-
               cuses on response distribution supervision. Our experiments demonstrate that RT
017
               models, trained only using responses, can effectively respond to a wide range of
018
               instructions and exhibit helpfulness comparable to that of their instruction-tuned
019            counterparts. Furthermore, we observe that controlling the training response dis-
020            tribution can substantially improve their user preference or elicit target behaviors
021            such as refusing assistance for unsafe queries. Our findings illuminate the role of
022            establishing an adequate output distribution in alignment, highlighting the poten-
023            tial of the extensive inherent capabilities of pre-trained LLMs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Large Language Models (LLMs) are pre-trained to predict the next token using massive amounts
028
      of web-crawled text, implicitly learning a wide range of tasks (Radford et al., 2019; OpenAI, 2023;
029
      Dubey et al., 2024). To align them with human needs, LLMs typically undergo Instruction Tuning
030   (IT) (Mishra et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022a; Sanh et al., 2022)—a supervised fine-tuning process
031   using instruction-response paired data. This conditional supervision enables them to follow natural
032   language instructions and responsibly handle unsafe queries, facilitating their real-world applica-
033   tions (Wang et al., 2023a;b; Ivison et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024;
034   Bianchi et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024).
035
      We note that LLMs might acquire abilities to follow instructions and identify unsafe queries during
036   pre-training, as well as the extensive knowledge required to perform specific tasks (Radford et al.,
037   2019; Brown et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2024). For instance, they may implicitly learn instruction-
038   following dynamics from texts in community threads, such as StackOverflow, and develop the ability
039   to identify unsafe requests from news articles or legal documents—all of which are common sources
040   in typical pre-training datasets (Elazar et al., 2024). Moreover, previous observations suggest that
041   output space information plays a crucial role in canonical NLP tasks (Min et al., 2022; Kung &
042   Peng, 2023). Our hypothesis is that establishing an adequate response distribution can surface these
043   capabilities, enabling pre-trained LLMs to function as desired chat assistants.
044   In this study, we explore the impact of establishing response distribution in transforming pre-trained
045   LLMs into helpful and safe assistants. To this end, we propose Response Tuning (RT), which focuses
046   solely on response distribution supervision. Specifically, RT omits the instruction-conditioning pro-
047   cess and the model is not supervised from instruction-response mappings (see Figure 1). Instead,
048   it trains the model to construct responses and learn their distribution. This omission allows us to
049   examine the isolated effect of response supervision.
050   We first investigate whether establishing an output distribution alone can enable LLMs to gener-
051   ate aligned responses without additional supervision from instruction-response mappings. To this
052   end, we evaluate the instructability of RT models trained on four recent LLMs using three differ-
053   ent datasets: Llama-3.1-8B (Dubey et al., 2024), Gemma-2-2B and Gemma-2-9B (Riviere et al.,
      2024), and Mistral-7B-v0.3 (Jiang et al., 2023), utilizing only the responses from Alpaca (Taori

090

104   2      R ELATED W ORK
105
106   Instruction tuning. Instruction Tuning (IT) is a process of supervised fine-tuning LLMs using
107   instruction-response pairs, where the model is trained to produce responses (assistant outputs) con-
      ditioned on paired instructions (user inputs). Recent studies, expanding from earlier research that


                                                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      focused on cross-task generalization across canonical NLP tasks (Weller et al., 2020; Mishra et al.,
109   2022; Wei et al., 2022a; Sanh et al., 2022), have shifted their focus to generalization for unseen user
110   instructions, demonstrating remarkable success in improving the usability of LLMs. Notable con-
111   tributions include synthetic data generation frameworks (Wang et al., 2023b; Honovich et al., 2023;
112   Ding et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024), human-involved conversation collection schemes (Conover et al.,
113   2023; Köpf et al., 2023; Chiang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024).
114   Recently, a line of research has explored the superficial nature of the post-training stage, questioning
115   the necessity of large-scale data (Zhou et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024) or parameter
116   updates (Lin et al., 2024). However, few controlled studies have systematically examined the impact
117
      of specific learning signals on the development of instruction-following assistants.
118   Large Language Model safety. Instruction-following LLMs are susceptible to malicious use with-
119   out proper safeguards, and their risks become more nuanced and pronounced as their capabilities
120   grow (Bommasani et al., 2021; Hendrycks et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2023). This has motivated
121   studies on safety measures to mitigate their potential risks in real-world applications (Perez et al.,
122   2022; Bai et al., 2022b; Dubey et al., 2024; Mu et al., 2024). These measures typically aim to make
123   the model refuse to answer unsafe queries—such as requests for assistance in committing illegal
124   activities—while retaining their helpfulness in appropriate contexts. A common practice to achieve
125   this is incorporating unsafe instruction-refusal response pairs into the IT stage (Touvron et al., 2023;
126
      Bianchi et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024). However, as with instruction-following alignment, it re-
      mains unclear which cues mainly elicit the desired behaviors.
127
128   Supervision from input-output pairs. Min et al. (2022) show that the input-output mapping mat-
129   ters substantially less than the label space information in the in-context demonstrations of canonical
130   NLP tasks (e.g., classification). Taking a step further, later works examine the role of task defi-
131   nitions given in the prompts of the IT dataset and demonstrate that models trained with minimal
132   (only label space) or misleading task definitions have little effect on their task generalization per-
133   formance (Kung & Peng, 2023). However, those findings are limited to conventional label spaces,
134   which correspond to predefined categories or outputs expected in given NLP tasks (e.g., yes/no,
135
      positive/negative), rather than the open-ended output space in open-domain IT.
136   Our approach. To address these gaps, we aim to examine the impact of supervision from the re-
137   sponse distribution in alignment. Specifically, we focus on open-domain, open-ended IT—a widely
138   adopted practice in aligning both open-source and proprietary LLMs (Wang et al., 2023a; Köpf
139   et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023; Dubey et al., 2024; Riviere et al., 2024). Our fo-
140   cus lies on two foundational objectives of LLM alignment: transforming pre-trained LLMs into
141   instruction-following and safe assistants.

143   3   R ESPONSE T UNING (RT)
144
145   To verify our hypothesis, we propose Response Tuning (RT) to examine the impact of establish-
146   ing a response space in alignment. All scripts and resources used in training can be found in the
147   supplementary materials.
148
149
      Training data. We adopt the chatbot-style schema proposed by Wang et al. (2023a), which separates
      user instructions and assistant responses using special tokens: <|user|> and <|assistant|>.
150
      However, in RT, we omit both the <|user|> token and the user instructions during training.
151
      Therefore, the training data consists only of the <|assistant|> token followed by the train-
152   ing response.
153
154   Training. RT employs standard teacher forcing and computes the loss only on the response tokens
155   that appear after the <|assistant|> token. The loss function for the autoregressive language
156   model is defined as:

      Here, θ represents the model parameters, l is the total number of tokens in a training example, and
163   ti denotes the i-th token in the sequence. The indicator function δi equals 1 if the i-th token belongs
164   to the response set R (i.e., the assistant’s response), and 0 otherwise. This formulation mirrors the

168   distribution of responses independently.
169
      Inference. We input a sequence that starts with the instruction delimiter (<|user|>), followed
170
      by the user’s instruction, and then the response delimiter (<|assistant|>). The model then
171
      generates the assistant’s response after the <|assistant|> token. Although the RT models are
172   trained without paired instructions, they can leverage their inherent capabilities to generate desired
173   responses. We will demonstrate this in the subsequent experiment sections.

      Establishing a response space alone enables LLMs to behave as chat assistants. Figures 2, 3
      and 4 depict the human acceptability, response quality, and pairwise helpfulness assessment results,
301
      respectively. The independent assessment results show that, while the base models are not capable
302
      of functioning as instruction-following models, the RT models—trained solely on responses without
303   paired instructions—can generate appropriate responses to a wide range of instructions. A majority
304   of the responses generated by RT models are rated as Acceptable, with many achieving the Excellent
305   rating. They perform comparably to their IT counterparts in response quality evaluations, achieving
306   similar scores across all metrics of the JustEval benchmark.
307
      The pairwise evaluation results also indicate that while RT models are competent, their outputs
308
      are slightly less preferred compared to their IT counterparts. Our manual investigation of cases
309   where IT model outputs are preferred over those of RT models reveals that IT models often produce
310   outputs with a style more closely aligned with the given instructions. This suggests that the explicit
311   instruction-response mapping enables the model to select a response format that is better tailored
312   to the instruction, based on the established response distribution. These findings suggest that
313   the instruction-response dynamics are largely acquired during pre-training, and just establishing a
314   response space can elicit those dynamics to generate aligned responses. The examples of the model
315   outputs are available in Appendix G.
316

317

318   els across all tested tasks. Additionally, a similar but slight level of performance discrepancies—

320   RT models after fine-tuning. These findings suggest that RT models largely retain the core capa-

347
348   5.1     R EFINING R ESPONSE ATTRIBUTES
349
350   Motivation. Our preceding experiments demonstrate that establishing a response distribution alone
351   can enable LLMs to behave as instruction-following assistants. In this section, we investigate
352   whether refining the training response distribution can further enhance user preference, which is
353   one of the essential objectives of LLM alignment. While previous works have shown that tech-
354   niques like feedback learning or completely regenerating responses in the IT dataset using other
355   aligned LLMs can improve user preference (Bai et al., 2022a; Peng et al., 2023; Ivison et al., 2024),
356
      we are interested in verifying whether adjusting the attributes of existing response distributions in
      IT or RT data can yield similar benefits.
357
358
359   5.2     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
360
361   Response refinement. We focus on refining the fundamental characteristics of the response dis-
362   tribution, specifically considering three core elements that correlate with user preference: clarity,
363   structure, and tone. For the refinement, we utilize a strong instruction-following LLM, Llama-3.1-
364   70B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024), with a manually designed refinement prompt. The responses from
365
      the Alpaca, Dolly, and LIMA datasets are refined according to the following guidelines:
366       • Clarity: Make the response easy to understand. It should be direct and to the point, avoiding
367         complex language that might confuse the user
368
          • Structure: Organize the content in a logical and coherent manner. The response should flow
369
            naturally, making it easy for the user to follow along and grasp the key points.
370
371       • Tone: Adjust the tone to be friendly, conversational, and engaging. The response should feel
372         approachable and enjoyable, as if having a pleasant conversation with the user.
373
      The prompt and the example of refined data are available in Appendix B and F.
374
375   Evaluation. We conduct simulated preference evaluations using the GPT-4 judge of AlpacaEval (Li
376   et al., 2023) and report length-controlled win rates (Dubois et al., 2024). To directly measure the
377   gains from response distribution refinement, we compare both the IT and RT models trained using
      the refined datasets with those trained on the original datasets.

                               S AFETY A LIGNMENT
423
424
425
       Motivation. Similar to instruction-following alignment, recent work demonstrates that incorporat-
426    ing only a small set of safety-focused examples—unsafe queries paired with refusal responses—into
427    IT data can enable models to reject unsafe inputs (Bianchi et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024). On the
428    other hand, our previous experiments have shown the abilities like instruction-following are also ac-
429    quired during pre-training. Based on these observations, we hypothesize that the abilities required to
430    identify the unsafe queries are also acquired during pre-training. Therefore, we investigate whether
431    properly establishing the response space—by including refusal responses that demonstrate how to
       handle unsafe queries—can induce responsible behaviors even without using paired data.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      6.1      E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
433
434
      Response tuning with refusals. We incorporate explanatory refusals—responses that indicate an
435
      inability to assist while providing a rule explaining how the model should handle unsafe queries—
436   into the response subsets of Alpaca, Dolly, and LIMA datasets. These refusals are obtained from
437   existing safety-focused pairs using keyword searches (Bianchi et al., 2024). To control their propor-
438   tion, we add 100, 200, and 500 examples into randomly sampled sets of 1,0002 responses from the
439   base RT dataset.
440
441   Evaluation. We compare safety-enhanced RT models with their IT counterparts trained using the
442
      same responses but with paired instructions. We measure the Refusal Rate (RR) against unsafe
      queries using benchmarks such as AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023), MaliciousInstruct (Huang et al.,
443
      2024), HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024), and XSTest (Röttger et al., 2024). To consider exag-
444
      gerated safety (over refusal) behaviors, we also measure the False Refusal Rate (FRR) using benign
445   queries from XSTest that are lexically similar to unsafe ones (Röttger et al., 2024). Existing methods
446   for evaluating refusals often involve significant costs, require human effort, or cannot measure FRR.
447   Therefore, we utilize a cost-efficient, open-source LLM—Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct (Dubey et al.,
448   2024)—to classify whether a response is a refusal or compliance, i.e., whether the response declines
449   to assist or attempts to provide the requested information. However, our initial manual inspection
450   found that simply instructing the model to perform this classification yields poor performance due
451   to tricky cases:
452     • Deceptive compliance for unsafe queries: Cases where the model (1) starts with disclaimers,
453       warnings, or expressions of hesitation but still attempts to provide the requested information;
454       (2) initially refuses to answer but continues to provide the requested unsafe information; (3)
455       assumes hypothetical scenarios and provides the unsafe information requested.
456     • Soft refusals for unsafe queries: Cases where the models provide alternative suggestions or
457       advice instead of directly refusing the request.
458
      To address these issues, we designed a universal and reliable judge prompt (see Appendix B). This
459
      judge is validated using a set of 120 examples consisting of 60 compliance and 60 refusal responses,
460   each containing 30 of the edge cases described above. Our engineered prompt, which induces chain-
461   of-thought reasoning (Wei et al., 2022b), achieved 98.33% accuracy on this test set. We use this
462   judge for all safety evaluations.
463
464
      6.2      R ESULTS
465
466
      Response supervision alone can elicit responsible behaviors. Figure 5 shows the evaluation re-
467
      sults for the Gemma-2-9B model trained based on LIMA. The results for the other models and
468
      datasets are available in Appendix D. Notably, RT models trained with refusals exhibit substantially
469   higher RR compared to those trained without refusals, indicating that they are able to handle unsafe
470   queries as suggested in training responses. We also find that their FRR falls within a reasonable
471   range. Although they require more data to achieve a safety level close to IT counterparts, their re-
472   fusal rates are largely on par with IT counterparts that are additionally supervised from mappings
473   between unsafe queries and refusals. These results indicate that complex capabilities, such as safety
474   assessment, are largely acquired during pre-training. LLMs can generalize the guidance for han-
475   dling queries embedded in the training response distribution even without supervision from explicit
476   instruction-response mappings.
477
      The gap in refusal rates between RT and IT models shrinks with model scale. Our experiments
478   using Gemma-2-9B and Gemma-2-2B models reveal that the difference in safety performance be-
479   tween RT and IT models diminishes as the size of the base model increases. While smaller models
480   like Gemma-2-2B show a noticeable gap—with IT models outperforming RT models in RR—larger
481   models such as Llama-3.1-8B and Gemma-2-9B exhibit minimal differences. In some cases, the
482   larger RT models achieve refusal rates comparable to their IT counterparts. This suggests that larger
483   models have a greater capacity to internalize and generalize safety behaviors from limited response
484   supervision. Please refer to Appendix D for the detailed numerical report.

505
      Motivation. We further validate our hypothesis—that establishing an appropriate output space alone
506
      can enable LLMs to behave as desired chat assistants—in an in-context learning setting. To this end,
507   we test whether untuned base LLMs can helpfully and safely respond to user queries given only
508   response demonstrations.
509
510   Experimental setup. We remove instruction-response mappings from URIAL (Lin et al., 2024),
511   which consists of 4 instruction-response pairs including one pair of unsafe instruction and refusal.
512   We refer to this new version as URIAL-R. We then evaluate these models using two different base
513
      LLMs, Llama-3.1-8B and Gemma-2-9B, with the JustEval benchmark. We employ greedy decod-
      ing with a maximum generation length of 2,048 tokens. To further elucidate the importance of
514
      response supervision, we include zero-shot templated prompting (Lin et al., 2024) as a baseline in
515
      our evaluation. The prompts and details of the setup can be found in Appendix B.
516
517   Results. Figure 6 presents the evaluation results. The findings indicate that the base LLM, when
518   provided with the URIAL-R prompt containing only response demonstrations, performs compara-
519   bly to the model prompted with the original URIAL, which includes instruction-response pairs. The
520   scores across all metrics are similar between the two prompts. Additionally, URIAL-R substan-
521   tially outperforms the zero-shot prompting baselines across all metrics. These results demonstrate
522
      that response demonstrations alone can elicit helpful and safe assistant behaviors in LLMs, high-
      lighting the impact of supervision from response distribution and latent capabilities of pre-trained
523
      LLMs. This reinforces our earlier conclusion that the capabilities required to behave as chat assis-
524
      tants are largely inherent in pre-trained LLMs and can be activated without explicit supervision from
525   instruction-response mappings.

528   8   C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we explore the role of establishing an output distribution in transforming pre-trained
531
      LLMs into instruction-following and safe assistants. We propose Response Tuning (RT), a method
      that focuses solely on response distribution supervision by eliminating the instruction-conditioning
532
      step in instruction tuning. Our extensive experiments demonstrate that RT models, trained only
533
      on responses without paired instructions, effectively respond to a wide range of user queries and
534   responsibly handle unsafe requests by generalizing embedded behavioral guidance in the response
535   distribution. These findings suggest that many of the capabilities required for alignment are already
536   inherent in pre-trained models and can be activated by establishing a proper response distribution.
537   Our work emphasizes the impact of establishing distribution in alignment and highlights the poten-
538   tial of inherent capabilities in LLMs. Future work could build on these findings to develop more
539   effective alignment strategies. For a detailed discussion of limitations and future directions, we refer
      readers to Appendix A.

921   A       L IMITATIONS & F UTURE W ORK
922
923   The primary aim of our work is to provide insights into the mechanisms by which language models
924
      develop the capabilities needed to handle instructions helpfully and safely. RT serves as a tool in
      our study, intentionally designed as a simplified ablation of IT. However, as a standalone method,
925
      it exhibits limited performance compared to IT. Future work could build on the insights gained
926
      from RT to develop alignment strategies for LLMs that more effectively and efficiently harness
927   their inherent capabilities. To further stimulate discussion, we outline the practical aspects of RT
928   in Appendix E, offering a foundation for future exploration and refinement. Additionally, our study
929   focuses on the core objectives of LLM alignment—instruction-following and safety. Future work
930   could explore controlling response distribution to achieve more complex alignment objectives, such
931   as mitigating sycophancy or social bias (Perez et al., 2023; Sharma et al., 2024). Additionally,
932   our work relies on empirical results from the ablation study; direct investigations into the inherent
933   capabilities of pre-trained LLMs, such as extracting their semantic features (Bricken et al., 2023;
934   Templeton et al., 2024), could further clarify the role of the alignment stage and potentially improve
935
      efficiency. Moreover, future research might adopt analytical approaches for automatic selection or
      fine-grained control over the response distribution to achieve better alignment.
