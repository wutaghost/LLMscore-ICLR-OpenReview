009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Entity alignment (EA) aims to merge two knowledge graphs (KGs) by identifying
012            equivalent entity pairs. Existing methods can be categorized into symbolic and
013            neural models. Symbolic models, while precise, struggle with substructure het-
014            erogeneity and sparsity, whereas neural models, although effective, generally lack
015            interpretability and cannot handle uncertainty. We propose NeuSymEA, a proba-
016            bilistic neuro-symbolic framework that combines the strengths of both methods.
017            NeuSymEA models the joint probability of all possible pairs’ truth scores in a
018
               Markov random field, regulated by a set of rules, and optimizes it with the varia-
               tional EM algorithm. In the E-step, a neural model parameterizes the truth score
019
               distributions and infers missing alignments. In the M-step, the rule weights are
020
               updated based on the observed and inferred alignments. To facilitate interpretabil-
021            ity, we further design a path-ranking-based explainer upon this framework that
022            generates supporting rules for the inferred alignments. Experiments on bench-
023            marks demonstrate that NeuSymEA not only significantly outperforms baselines
024            in terms of effectiveness and robustness, but also provides interpretable results.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Knowledge graphs (KGs) are crucial for organizing structured knowledge about entities and their
030   relationships, enhancing search capabilities across various applications. They are widely used in
031   question-answering systems (Bast & Haussmann, 2015; Dong et al., 2023), recommendation sys-
032   tems (Catherine & Cohen, 2016), social network analysis (Tang et al., 2008), Natural Language
033
      Processing (Weikum & Theobald, 2010), etc.. Despite their utility, real-world KGs often face issues
      like incompleteness, domain specificity, and language constraints, which hinder their effectiveness
034
      in cross-disciplinary or multilingual contexts. To address these issues, entity alignment (EA) aims
035
      to merge disparate KGs into a unified, comprehensive knowledge base by identifying and linking
036   equivalent entities across different KGs. For example, aligning entities between a biomedical KG
037   and a pharmaceutical KG allows for mining cross-discipline relationships through the aligned en-
038   tities, such as identifying the same drugs and their effects on different diseases to enhance drug
039   repurposing efforts. This alignment enables more nuanced exploration and interrogation of inter-
040   connected data, providing richer insights into how entities function across multiple domains.
041
      Entity alignment models aim to determine the equivalence of two entities by assessing their align-
042   ment probability. Existing methods can be broadly categorized into symbolic models and neural
043   models. Symbolic models (Suchanek et al., 2012; Jiménez-Ruiz & Cuenca Grau, 2011; Qi et al.,
044   2021) provide interpretable and precise inference by mining ground rules, but they struggle with
045   aligning long-tail entities, especially those without aligned neighbors. In such cases, the lack of
046   supporting rules leads to low recall. Conversely, neural models, such as translation models (Chen
047   et al., 2017; Sun et al., 2018) and Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) (Mao et al., 2020; 2021;
048   Wang et al., 2018; Mao et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024), excel in recalling similar entities by embedding
049   them in a continuous space, yet they often fail to distinguish entities with similar representations,
050
      causing a drop in precision as the entity pool grows. Neuro-symbolic models aim to combine the
      strengths of both approaches, offering the interpretability and precision of symbolic models along-
051
      side the high recall capabilities of neural models.
052
053   However, neuro-symbolic reasoning in entity alignment (EA) faces several challenges. First, com-
      bining symbolic and neural models into a unified framework is suboptimal due to the difficulty in

054
      aligning their objectives. Current approaches either use neural models as auxiliary modules for
055   symbolic models to measure entity similarity (Qi et al., 2021) or employ symbolic models to refine
056   pseudo-labels (Liu et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024b). Second, in EA task, the search space for rules
057   is large, as the EA task requires deriving ground rules from both intra-KG and inter-KG structural
058   patterns, leading to an exponentially large search space with increasing rule length. Finally, generat-
059   ing interpretations for EA remains underexplored. Effective interpretations should not only generate
060   supporting rules but also quantify their confidence through rule weights.
061   To overcome these challenges, we propose NeuSymEA, a neuro-symbolic framework that combines
062   the strengths of both symbolic and neural models. NeuSymEA models the joint probability of truth
063   score assignment for all possible entity pairs using a Markov random field, regulated by a set of
064   weighted rules. This joint probability is optimized via a variational EM algorithm. During the E-
065   step, a neural model parameterizes the truth scores and infers the missing alignments. In the M-step,
066   the rule weights are updated based on both observed and inferred alignments. To leverage long
067   rules without suffering from the exponential search space, we employ logic deduction to decompose
068   rules of any length into shorter, unit-length rules. This allows for efficient inference and weight
069
      updates for long rules. After training, the learned rules are adapted into an explainer, enhancing
      interpretability. Specifically, we reverse the logic deduction process to calculate the weights of
070
      long rules based on the learned weights of their shorter counterparts. Additionally, an explainer is
071
      introduced to efficiently generate supporting rules with quantified confidence through breadth-first
072   search, as explanations for inferred alignments. Our contributions are summarized as below:
073
074          • A principled neuro-symbolic reasoning framework: NeuSymEA seamlessly integrates
075            neural and symbolic models through a principled variational EM framework, combining
076            their strengths for effective entity alignment.
077          • Efficient optimization via logical decomposition: We introduce a logic deduction mech-
078            anism that decomposes long rules into shorter ones, significantly reducing the complexity
079            of rule inference and enabling efficient reasoning over large knowledge graphs.
080          • Interpretable inference: The explainer utilizes learned rules to generate support paths for
081            interpreting both aligned and misaligned pairs. It offers two modes: (1) Hard-anchor
082            mode—generates supporting paths from prealigned anchor pairs; and (2) Soft-anchor
083            mode—incorporates inferred anchor pairs for more informative interpretation.
084          • Empirical validation and superior results: NeuSymEA demonstrates state-of-the-art
085            performance on benchmark datasets, delivering both robust alignment accuracy and in-
086            sightful rule-based interpretations.

090   2.1   P ROBLEM STATEMENT
091
092   A knowledge graph G comprises a set of entities E, a set of relations R, and a set of relation triples
093   T where each triple (ei , rk , ej ) ∈ T represents a directional relationship between its head entity
094
      and tail entity. Given two KGs G = {E, R, T }, G ′ = {E ′ , R′ , T ′ }, and a set of observed aligned
      entity pairs O = {(ei , e′i )|ei ∈ E, e′i ∈ E ′ }ni=1 , the goal of entity alignment is to infer the missing
095
      alignments by reasoning with the existing alignments. This problem can be formulated in a proba-
096
      bilistic way: each pair (e, e′ ), e ∈ E, e′ ∈ E ′ is associated with a binary indicator variable v(e,e′ ) .
097
      v(e,e′ ) = 1 means (e, e′ ) is an aligned pair, and v(e,e′ ) = 0 otherwise. Given some observed align-
098
      ments vO = {v(e,e′ ) = 1}(e,e′ )∈O , we aim to predict the labels of the remaining hidden entity pairs
099
      H = E × E ′ \O, i.e., vH = {v(e,e′ ) }(e,e′ )∈H .

141     3.1      VARIATIONAL EM
142
143     Given a set of observed labels vO , our goal is to maximize the log-likelihood of these labels, i.e.,
144     log pw (vO ). Directly optimizing this objective is intractable because it requires computing an in-
145     tegral over all the hidden variables. Instead, we optimize the evidence lower bound (ELBO) of the
146     log-likelihood as follows:
147                     pw (vO ) ≥ Eq(vH ) [log pw (vO , vH ) − log q(vH )] = ELBO(q, vO ; w),           (3)
148     here, q(vH ) is a variational distribution of the hidden variables vH . This inequality holds for all q
149     because pw (vO ) = ELBO(q, vO ; w) + DKL (q(vH )∥pw (vH | vO )), where DKL (q(vH )∥pw (vH |
150     vO )) ≥ 0 is the KL-divergence between q(vH ) and pw (vH | vO ). Under this framework, the
151     log-likelihood pw (vO ) can be optimized using an EM algorithm, an efficient method to find the
152     maximum likelihood where the model depends on unobserved hidden variables: during the E-step,
153     we fix w and update the variational distribution q; during the M-step, we update w to maximize the
154     log-likelihood of all the entity pairs, i.e., Eq(vH ) [log pw (vO , vH )], as illustrated in Figure 1.
155     Explicitly representing the variational distribution q is parameter intensive, which requires ≈ |E||E ′ |
156     variables because the observed pairs are very sparse. To this end, we parameterize q with a neural
157     model as qθ , with θ being the parameters of the neural model.
158

235   4.2   I NFERENCE WITH INTERPRETABILITY
236
237   To predict new alignments, there are two approaches: using the symbolic model or the neural model.
238   The symbolic model infers alignment probabilities with the optimized weights w. Due to scalability
239   concerns, symbolic methods generally adopt a lazy inference strategy that only preserves the confi-
240
      dent pairs implied by the neighbor structure during inference. On the other hand, the neural model
      computes similarity scores for all entity pairs (e, e′ ) ∈ H using the learned parameters θ, generating
241
      a ranked candidate list for each entity.
242
243   The evaluation of these models thus differs. Symbolic models are generally evaluated by precision,
244   recall, and F1-score for their binary outputs, while neural models are assessed using hit@k and
245   mean reciprocal ranks (MRR) for their ranked candidate lists. Following the practices in Qi et al.
246
      (2021) and Liu et al. (2022), we unify the evaluation metrics by treating the recall metric of symbolic
      models as equivalent to hit@1, facilitating comparison with neural models.
247
248   To enhance the interpretability of predictions, we adapt the optimized symbolic model into an ex-
249   plainer. For any given entity pair, the explainer generates a set of supporting rule path pairs that
250   justify their alignment, each associated with a confidence score indicating its significance. The ex-
251   plainer operates in two modes: (1) hard-anchor mode, which generates supporting paths only from
252
      prealigned pairs, and (2) soft-anchor mode, which includes paths from both prealigned and inferred
      pairs, providing more informative interpretations.
253
254   By integrating a breadth-first search algorithm (detailed in Appendix A.3), the explainer efficiently
255   generates high-quality interpretations. For truly aligned pairs, it typically produces high-confidence
256   interpretations, while for non-aligned pairs, the interpretations may result in an empty set (indicating
257   no supporting evidence) or have low confidence scores. See Figure 3 for a visualized comparison.

      full version of DBP15K contains significantly more entities than the condensed version, exacer-

450   6             R ELATED WORK
451
452   Neuro-symbolic reasoning on knowledge graphs. Neuro-symbolic methods aim to merge sym-
453   bolic reasoning with neural representation learning, leveraging the precision and interpretability of
454   symbolic approaches alongside the scalability and high recall of neural methods. In KG completion
455   task, Guo et al. (2016) and Guo et al. (2018) employ horn rules to regularize the learning of KG
456
      embeddings; Cheng et al. (2022) and Cheng et al. (2023) model the rule-based predictions as dis-
      tributions conditioned on the input relational sequences, and parameterize these distributions using
457
      a recurrent neural network; Qu et al. (2020) and Cheng et al. (2022) enhances rule grounding by
458
      augmenting the fact set using a pre-trained KG-embedding model; Qu & Tang (2019), Zhang et al.
459   (2020) and Chen et al. (2024a) models the joint probability of neural model and the symbolic model
460   with a Markov random field, and employ gradient descent for weight updates. Despite extensive
461   advancements of neuro-symbolic reasoning in KG completion(DeLong et al., 2024; Cheng et al.,
462   2024), these studies only consider single-KG structures, thus cannot be directly adopted to entity
463   alignment which requires consideration of both intra-KG and inter-KG structures.
464
      Entity alignment. Recent models have sought to combine symbolic and neural approaches for
465   entity alignment. For instance, Qi et al. (2021) enhances probabilistic reasoning by utilizing KG
466   embeddings, employing a KG-embedding model to measure similarities during both updating and
467   inference processes. Liu et al. (2022) implements self-bootstrapping with pseudo-labeling in a neu-
468   ral framework, using rules to choose confident pseudo-labels. However, it relies solely on unit-length
469   rules, which restricts its effectiveness for long-tail entities. Recently, Tian et al. (2024) proposes to
470   generate interpretations to explain entity alignment, but their interpretations are subgraphs extracted
471   by semantic matching using a pre-trained neural model.
472   In contrast, our work measures agreement between symbolic and neural models by modeling joint
473   probability within a Markov random field, optimizing both components toward a unified objective
474   within a variational EM framework. We employ logic deduction to scale reasoning with long rules
475   of any length. These learned rules provide interpretations during inference.

478
479
      In this work, we presented NeuSymEA, a neuro-symbolic framework for entity alignment that ef-
480   fectively integrates symbolic methods with neural approaches within a principled variational EM
481   framework. By unifying these traditionally distinct methodologies, NeuSymEA addresses the chal-
482   lenges of substructure heterogeneity, sparsity, and uncertainty. Our empirical results demonstrate
483   that NeuSymEA not only outperforms existing approaches but also provides interpretable alignment
484   predictions through its path-ranking-based explainer. These findings underscore the potential of
485   this unified neuro-symbolic framework to advance knowledge fusion by enabling effective entity
      alignment with uncertainty-aware interpretations.

621   In variational inference, the process of learning and inferring long rules (Eq. 7) is simplified by
622   decomposing them into unit-length rules (Eq. 8). Consequently, rule weight learning (Eq. 10) is
623   only conducted for unit-length rules. The inference process for an L-length rule is then estimated by
624   iteratively applying inference steps with unit-length rules (Eq. 9) for L iterations. This strategy ef-
625
      fectively avoids the exponential search space associated with longer rules, making the computational
      complexity of the inference linear with respect to the rule length L.
626
627   Each iteration of reasoning with unit-length rules comprises an inference step (Eq. 9) and a rule-
628   weight learning step (Eq. 10). These steps require computing the matching probability for all
629   possible entity pairs and relation pairs, respectively. As a result, the computational complexity of
630   the inference step and the weight updating step are O(|E||E ′ |) and O(|R||R′ |), respectively.

632   |R||R′ |)). Given that entity sizes are typically much larger than relation sizes, this complexity can
633   be approximated as O(L · |E||E ′ |).
