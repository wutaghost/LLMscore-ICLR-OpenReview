011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT demonstrate significant potential in
014            the medical field, often evaluated using multiple-choice questions (MCQs) similar
015            to those found on the USMLE. Despite their prevalence in medical education,
016
               MCQs have limitations that might be exacerbated when assessing LLMs. To
               evaluate the effectiveness of MCQs in assessing the performance of LLMs, we
017
               developed a fictional medical benchmark focused on a non-existent gland, the
018
               Glianorex. This approach allowed us to isolate the knowledge of the LLM from
019            its test-taking abilities. We used GPT-4-Turbo and Claude 3.5 Sonnet to generate
020            two comprehensive textbooks on the Glianorex in both English and French and de-
021            veloped corresponding multiple-choice questions in both languages. We evaluated
022            various open-source, proprietary, and domain-specific LLMs using these questions
023            in a zero-shot setting. The models achieved average scores around 64%, with
024            minor performance differences between larger and smaller models. Performance
025            was slightly higher in English than in French. Fine-tuned medical models showed
026            some improvement over their base versions in English but not in French. The high
027
               performance across models suggests that traditional MCQ-based benchmarks may
               not accurately measure LLMs’ clinical knowledge and reasoning abilities, instead
028
               highlighting their pattern recognition skills. This study underscores the need for
029
               more robust evaluation methods to better assess the true capabilities of LLMs in
030            medical contexts.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Large Language Models (LLMs), such as ChatGPT, have demonstrated significant potential in the
036   medical field, with studies evaluating their performance on tests originally designed for humans,
037   including the USMLE (Jin et al., 2020; Pal et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2019; Nori et al., 2023). Furthermore,
038   domain-specific research shows that these models perform well on specialized medical exams in
039   areas such as pediatrics, radiology, ophthalmology, plastic surgery, and oncology (Rydzewski et al.,
040   2024; Bhayana et al., 2023; Barile et al., 2024; Mihalache et al., 2023; Humar et al., 2023). The
041   common reliance on multiple-choice questions in these assessments reflects their widespread use as a
042   testing method for medical students globally (Al-Wardy, 2010).
043   However, multiple-choice questions (MCQs), while easy to administer and grade, have notable limita-
044   tions, often promoting surface learning and pattern recognition over deep understanding (Veloski et al.,
045   1999). Few studies address the potential issues unique to LLMs, such as the reliance on statistical
046   patterns rather than genuine understanding. For instance, Meerkat-7b improved its performance by
047   18.6% on medical benchmarks through training Mistral 7b on synthetic questions, outperforming
048   Meditron-7b based on Llama2 7b, which saw only a 10.5% improvement despite using a much larger
049   and high-quality dataset of clinical guidelines and articles (Kim et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023). This
050
      discrepancy highlights that extensive multiple-choice question-based training can be more effective
      than using comprehensive medical content, raising concerns about the true depth of understanding
051
      being assessed.
052
053   These potential issues are particularly relevant for LLMs, which depend heavily on large datasets that
      might contain statistical patterns. This dependency can result in models arriving at correct answers

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      for incorrect reasons, such as identifying skin cancer based on extraneous features like a ruler in the
055   image (Narla et al., 2018). To address these concerns, this study proposes evaluating LLMs using a
056   multiple-choice question test based on entirely fictional medical knowledge. By doing so, we aim
057   to determine whether traditional evaluations are sufficient for assessing the clinical knowledge and
058   reasoning abilities of LLMs for the medical domain, free from the influence of pre-existing data.

060   1.1   R ELATED WORK
061
062   Evaluating medical knowledge and clinical skills remains an active research area, with new methods
063   such as oral and competency evaluations proposed to better assess medical students and residents
064   (Veloski et al., 1999; Prediger et al., 2020; Goins et al., 2023). Globally, medical evaluations heav-
065   ily rely on MCQs, like the USMLE in the United States, which significantly influences residency
066
      placements (Gauer & Jackson, 2017). LLMs are similarly evaluated using MCQs to assess their
      medical knowledge. Google introduced MultiMedQA with their Med-PaLM model, combining
067
      several existing medical benchmarks, and this has become a standard for evaluating medical pro-
068
      ficiency in AI models (Singhal et al., 2023; Pal et al., 2024). Recently, Google incorporated more
069   manual evaluations by medical doctors for their Med-Gemini model (Saab et al., 2024). MultiMedQA
070   includes the following benchmarks:
071
072   MedQA-USMLE This subset of the MedQA dataset was sourced from the National Medical Board
073   Examination, the organization responsible for the United States Medical Licensing Examination
074   (NBME, 2024). The dataset is composed of a total of 12723 questions split into a training set of
075   10178 samples, a validation set of 1273 questions and a test set of 1273 questions. The questions
076   have 4 options with only one correct answer (Jin et al., 2020). Most questions present a clinical
077   vignette and require the test taker to apply clinical or foundational science knowledge to select the
078   best answer.
079
080   MedMCQA The Multi-Subject Multi-Choice Dataset for Medical domain is composed of 194k
081   multiple choice questions obtained from the All India Institute Of Medical Science (AIIMS) and
082   National Eligibility cum Entrance Test Postgraduate (NEET PG) entrance exam (AIIMS, 2024;
083
      NBEMS, 2024). These questions are split into 3 subsets, one training subset composed of 183k
      samples, a validation subset of 4.18k samples and a test subset comprising 6.15k samples with the
084
      specificity of not containing the correct answer to prevent contamination and manipulation of results.
085
      The questions have 4 options each and can be either single or multiple choice. Most questions are
086   straight forward knowledge recall and do not use clinical vignettes.
087
088   PubMedQA This biomedical question answering dataset was created using PubMed (NLM, 2024)
089   article abstracts from which the authors derive a question, a context, a long answer and a yes/maybe/no
090   answer. It comprises 3 subsets, one expert-annotated subset of 1k samples, an unlabeled subset of
091   61.2k samples and an artificially generated subset of 211.3k samples. The generated samples are
092   used to train models, while 500 samples of the expert-annotated subset are used to test the models.
093   This benchmark was designed to evaluate the reasoning capabilities of models when presented with
094   the abstract and a question pertaining to this abstract (Jin et al., 2019).
095
096   MMLU-Medical The Massive Multitask Language Understanding dataset contains 57 tasks, of
097   which 6 tasks are used to assess medical knowledge (Clinical knowledge, Medical genetics, Anatomy,
098   Professional Medicine, College Biology and College medicine) (Hendrycks et al., 2021). These tasks
099
      were collected by students from publicly available online resources, including USMLE questions
      as well as undergraduate level questions. The dataset contains 1227 questions split into 25 training
100
      samples, 118 validation samples and 1044 test samples. The questions have 4 options with only one
101
      correct answer and are a mix of clinical vignettes and recall questions.

104   2     M ETHODS
105
106   We devised a novel approach to assess the relevance of MCQs and LLMs’ utilization by generating
107   entirely fictional content. We performed this process twice, once using GPT-4 and a second time using
      Claude 3.5 Sonnet. In each iteration, we co-created a fictional gland named the Glianorex, located in


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the mediastinum and purported to regulate emotions. This ensured no prior knowledge existed about
109   the Glianorex, isolating the models’ reasoning capabilities from memorized information.
110
111   Knowledge For both GPT-4 and Claude 3.5 Sonnet, we began by generating a comprehensive
112   textbook on the Glianorex, detailing its history, physiology, anatomy, and pathology. We structured
113   each textbook using a top-down approach, defining key chapters and subchapters to provide a coherent
114   framework for the respective model. To maintain consistency across the chapters, we generated
115   summaries of key points, such as the anatomical location of the Glianorex and the roles of its
116   hormones, Equilibrion and Neurostabilin, for both textbooks.
117
118   Questions Based on these fictional textbooks, we used GPT-4 and Claude 3.5 Sonnet separately
119   to generate MCQs. For each model, these questions contained four choices with only one correct
120   answer, adhering to a format similar to that of the USMLE to ensure uniformity. To facilitate the
121   creation of these questions, we designed a prompt for each model that included the table of contents
122
      and a paragraph from the respective textbook. See Table 1. In addition, we included a random gender
      and age between 12 and 90 in 50% of the prompts for both models to ensure variability in clinical
123
      vignettes. This approach guided both models to generate questions in a JSON format consistent with
124
      existing medical benchmarks. For each model, we used a temperature of 1 and ran 4 generations
125   per paragraph to ensure some variability in questions. After generation, the order of options was
126   randomized to ensure a balanced distribution of correct labels amongst the 4 options.
127
128   Multilingual To study the influence of language on test taking abilities, we used GPT-4 to translate
129   the generated textbooks and questions using a simple one-shot prompt per paragraph and question
130   asking the model to translate to French.
131
132   Validation We engaged two medical doctors who took at least one step of the USMLE in the past
133   five years, to assess question quality. They evaluated 100 random English questions on a 7-point
134   Likert scale and answered them to establish a human baseline. We conducted a keyword search
135   for ”context” across all questions to identify potential incompleteness. Additionally, a medical
136   doctor manually verified the consistency of Introduction, Anatomy, and Biochemistry chapters in
137
      both English and French GPT-4 textbooks to assess both language quality and consistency of the
      translation.
138
139
      Models To evaluate the performance of LLMs, we selected a diverse set of models, includ-
140
      ing both proprietary and open-source options. We included commonly used foundational mod-
141
      els as reported in Table 2. Additionally, we included two fine-tuned medical domain models
142   based on mistralai/Mistral-7B-v0.1 to assess the influence of domain-specific training
143   on this fictional benchmark. First, internistai/base-7b-v0.2 (Apache 2.0) which we
144   trained on a mixture of general data, medical textbooks, and MCQs, demonstrating improved
145   performance on medical evaluations compared to its base model (Griot et al., 2024). Then,
146   dmis-lab/meerkat-7b-v1.0 (Creative Commons Attribution Non Commercial 4.0), which
147   was trained exclusively on multiple-choice questions, some of which were generated from medical
148   textbooks (Kim et al., 2024). The latter training approach showed a significant performance increase
149   on the benchmarks using a relatively small amount of training data compared to continued pretraining
150
      on large datasets of medical data as shown by Meditron and PMC-LLaMA (Chen et al., 2023; Wu
      et al., 2024).
151
152
153
      Evaluation The evaluation was conducted using the lm-evaluation-harness in a zero-shot setting,
      meaning that the models were presented with the questions and choices without any additional
154
      training specific to the Glianorex content (Gao et al., 2023). The task was modeled after the MedQA
155
      4 options task, using a log likelihood approach to measure the models’ accuracy. The standard
156   error of the mean was then multiplied by 1.96 to obtain the 95% confidence interval assuming a
157   normal distribution of errors. Additionally we assessed the statistical significance of the performance
158   against a random model using a cumulative distribution function on a binomial distribution. Model
159   comparisons were conducted using a two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA), followed by Tukey’s
160   honestly significant difference (HSD) test for post-hoc pairwise comparisons of performance. We run
161   these evaluations on a virtual machine with 4 NVIDIA GPU A100 80GB on Microsoft Azure, for a
      total runtime of 4 hours including model download time.

224   3.1   DATASET
225
226
      The resulting fictional textbooks on the Glianorex were generated using the proposed structure
      for both GPT-4 and Claude 3.5 Sonnet. Each textbook contains detailed sections on the anatomy,
227
      physiology, biochemistry, pathology, and diagnostic tools related to the Glianorex. For both models,
228
      the textbooks were produced in English and French, each containing approximately 35,000 words.
229   We then reused paragraphs of the English textbooks to generate multiple-choice questions in English,
230   followed by a translation step to obtain the same questions in French. The GPT-4 process resulted in
231   264 questions per language, while the Claude 3.5 Sonnet process produced 224 questions per language.
232   For both models, examples of these questions included complex scenarios requiring multiple steps of
233   reasoning. Each question adhered to a four-option format similar to MedQA-USMLE standards, with
234   one correct answer.
235   Human validation of data quality did not reveal major flaws. Two medical doctors evaluated a sample
236   of 100 English questions, assigning high quality scores (6.94 and 6.86 out of 7), comparable to
237   standard board exams. Manual verification identified only 8 questions (4 per language, ¡1% of
238   total) as incomplete due to missing context. A thorough review of key textbook chapters revealed
239   consistent structure and content across languages, with only minor, inconsequential variations in
240   French abbreviation usage.

243
244   All of the models achieved relatively high scores averaging at 63.8%, as illustrated in Figure 1. This
245   score is to put in perspective compared to medical doctors obtaining on average 27% which is within
246   the expected results of answering randomly. A statistically significant difference was noted between
247   the top-performing models and the lowest-performing models as shown in Table 3. The performance
248   differences when isolating languages were also significant and more frequent in English as shown
249
      in Tables 6 and 7. We also calculated Cohen’s d between all model pairs which revealed a range
      of effect sizes, indicating varying degrees of performance differences between the models (Cohen,
250
      2013). Most of the comparisons show very small or negligible effect sizes, with many pairs having
251
      a Cohen’s d close to 0 as shown in Table 8. For instance, pairs such as 01-ai/Yi-1.5-34B
252   - 01-ai/Yi-1.5-9B (d = 0.002) and 01-ai/Yi-1.5-34B - gpt-3.5-turbo-0125
253   (d = 0.030) suggest negligible differences. This pattern is consistent across most pairs, indicating that
254   the models’ performances are closely aligned. However, a few pairs demonstrate more noticeable dif-
255   ferences, such as dmis-lab/meerkat-7b-v1.0 - gpt-4o-2024-05-13 (d = 0.343), and
256   gpt-4-turbo-2024-04-09 - mistralai/Mistral-7B-v0.1 (d = 0.254), suggesting
257   small performance disparities. Overall, the analysis reveals that while some variation exists, the effect
258   sizes for most model comparisons are small. Additionally, the average score for English questions was
259   65.7%, while for French questions, it was 61.8%. Most models showed better performance in English
260
      than in French on this benchmark, with the exception of meta-llama/Meta-Llama-3-70B,
      gpt-4-turbo-2024-04-09, and Qwen/Qwen1.5-32B achieving similar performances in
261
      both languages.

345   4     D ISCUSSION
346
347   The results of this study highlight several key insights into the capabilities and limitations of LLMs
348   in handling MCQs based on fictional medical knowledge. Despite the novelty and complexity of
349   the fictional organ, the Glianorex, and the associated textbook, all evaluated models performed
350   achieved high scores. This finding suggests that LLMs are adept at recognizing patterns and applying
351
      test-taking strategies, even in unfamiliar contexts.
352
      Benchmarking The high performance across various foundational models in English, regardless of
353
      their architecture, size, or specialization, indicates that traditional MCQ-based benchmarks may not
354
      be sufficient for assessing the true understanding and clinical reasoning abilities of LLMs. These
355   benchmarks appear to test the models’ ability to identify patterns and associations rather than their
356   genuine comprehension of the material. Consequently, relying solely on MCQs for evaluating LLMs
357   in medical and other specialized domains might lead to an overestimation of their actual capabilities.
358   This finding is additionally supported by research demonstrating that models are becoming less
359   reliable as they are scaled up(Zhou et al., 2024). Using adversarial benchmarks such as the one
360   introduced in this study could help identifying reductions in reliability during development.
361
362   Training The improved performance of finetuned models Internist.ai and Meerkat over their
363   base versions underscores the impact of domain-specific training on enhancing LLM capabilities.
364   However, this improvement was predominantly observed in English, which raises questions about the
365
      multilingual generalization capabilities of these models.
366
367
      Language The difference in performance between English and French underscore the models’
      reliance on language processing capabilities to infer correct answers from multiple-choice options.
368
      The less imbalanced French distribution suggests that the models’ inferential strategies are less
369
      effective when applied to a language they may process less fluently. This variability provides valuable
370   insights into the strengths and limitations of current language models in dealing with fictional
371   knowledge across different languages.

436   4.2   F UTURE DIRECTION
437
438   The findings of this study suggest several avenues for future research. First, there is a need to
439   develop alternative evaluation methods that go beyond multiple-choice questions to assess the
440   deeper understanding and reasoning capabilities of LLMs. These methods could include open-ended
441
      questions, scenario-based assessments, and interactive simulations that require models to apply their
      knowledge in more complex and realistic contexts. While these evaluations are not sufficient to
442
      ensure safety and clinical relevance, they would provide a more realistic view of the capabilities on
443
      specific domains which could lead to clinical trials.
444
445   Second, further exploration of multilingual training and evaluation is necessary to ensure that LLMs
446   can perform consistently across different languages. This is particularly important in the medical
447
      field, where accurate comprehension and communication in multiple languages can have significant
      implications for patient care.
448
449   Lastly, investigating the underlying mechanisms that contribute to the enhanced performance of
450   finetuned models could provide valuable insights into effective training strategies. Understanding
451   how additional domain-specific training improves test-taking abilities and clinical reasoning can
452   guide the development of more advanced and capable LLMs.

455   5     L IMITATIONS
456
457   Knowledge coherence We performed a partial coherence check on the generated textbook to .
458   This oversight could result in inconsistencies or contradictions within the text, potentially creating
459   questions with multiple plausible correct answers depending on the chapter context provided to the
460   model during question generation. However, since the LLMs had no prior exposure to this fictional
461   gland, these potential inconsistencies do not undermine the overall conclusions about the models’
462
      performance and does affect the internal validity of the study.
463
464   Sample size The study generated 488 questions per language, for a total of 976 questions. This
465   sample size is relatively small but is within the same order of magnitude of established multiple-choice
466   benchmarks included in MedQA-USMLE. Despite the limited number of questions, the statistical
467   analysis suggests that the observed performance differences are unlikely to be due to chance.
468
469
      Synthetic biases We used GPT-4 and Claude 3.5 Sonnet to generate the multiple-choice questions,
470
      which could introduce hidden patterns that the models might exploit and affect the external validity
471   of this work. To reduce this potential bias, we used two models and a high temperature and generated
472   multiple samples per paragraph. While it is challenging to detect and eliminate all potential patterns, it
473   is important to note that LLMs are trained on extensive datasets and learn a compressed representation
474   of the data which implies that any bias introduced by the models were likely present in the real-world
475   data. Thus, this limitation reflects a realistic scenario where models encounter and utilize inherent
476   patterns in real-world data and does not alter the construct validity of the benchmark. Furthermore,
477   the difficulty experienced by medical doctors in answering these questions suggests that any patterns
478   present are not easily discernible, even to domain experts. This reinforces that the performance of
479
      LLMs on this benchmark cannot be attributed to simple, exploitable patterns, as such patterns were
      not apparent even to highly trained professionals.

483   and fine-tuned medical models, the selection was not exhaustive. There may be other models with
484   different architectures or training methodologies that could yield different results. To minimize this
485   bias, we selected models based on their current popularity as being a representative sample of LLMs
      currently in use. This selection

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      This study demonstrates that LLMs can achieve high scores on multiple-choice questions based on
489
      entirely fictional medical knowledge, even without prior exposure to the content. By using a novel
490   approach of creating a fictional gland, the Glianorex, and generating a comprehensive textbook and
491   related multiple-choice questions, we have isolated the models’ reasoning capabilities from their
492   memorization of real-world data. The findings reveal that models of different architectures, sizes,
493   and specializations perform similarly, suggesting that they rely on pattern recognition and test-taking
494   strategies rather than genuine understanding and memorization of the material.
495
      Our results call into question the effectiveness of current multiple-choice question-based benchmarks
496   for evaluating the clinical knowledge and understanding of LLMs. The similar performance across
497   models indicates that traditional multiple-choice questions may not adequately distinguish between
498   superficial pattern matching and deep comprehension. This study highlights the need for developing
499   more robust evaluation methods that better assess the true understanding and reasoning capabilities
500   of LLMs in the medical domain.
501
      In conclusion, while LLMs show promise in handling medical multiple-choice questions, our findings
502   suggest that current benchmarks may not fully capture their clinical knowledge and reasoning abilities.
503   Future research should explore alternative evaluation methods that go beyond current multiple-choice
504   questions to provide a more accurate assessment of Large Language Models’ capabilities in medicine
505   and other specialized fields.
