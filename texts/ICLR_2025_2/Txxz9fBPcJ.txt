009                                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011           Accurate performance prediction of Deep Learning (DL) models is essential for
012           efficient resource allocation and optimizations in various stages of the DL system
013           stack. While existing approaches can achieve high prediction accuracy, they lack
014           ability to quickly adapt to new hardware environments or emerging workloads.
015           This paper leverages both Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and Large Language
016
              Models (LLMs) to enhance the accuracy and adaptability of DL performance pre-
              diction. Our intuition is that GNNs are adept at capturing the structural infor-
017
              mation of DL models, naturally represented as graphs, while LLMs provide gen-
018
              eralization and the ability to quickly adapt to various tasks thanks to extensive
019           pre-training data. We empirically demonstrate that using GNN-derived graph em-
020           beddings as inputs to an LLM outperforms traditional representations, including
021           high-level text summary and lossless semi-structured text (e.g., JSON), for this
022           task. Furthermore, we propose a structured pre-training strategy to enable model
023           adaptation to new hardware environments, significantly reducing the need for ex-
024           tensive retraining. Our experiments validate the effectiveness of this approach,
025           showing an 8.8 percentage-point improvement in accuracy over a state-of-the-art
026           GNN baseline. Notably, when adapted to new hardware with few samples, our
027
              method achieves a remarkable 30–70 percentage-point increase in accuracy com-
              pared to the GNN baseline.

049   1   I NTRODUCTION
050
051   Performance prediction for Deep Learning (DL) models is essential for all sorts of optimization
052   methods in the DL system stack: from Neural Architecture Search, to model partitioning and shard-
053   ing, to low-level compiler optimizations. Performance prediction involves estimating various op-
      erational metrics — such as inference time, memory usage, and power consumption — that are


                                                                              1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      crucial for efficient hardware utilization and scheduling. Since DL models are computation graphs,
055   researchers have employed Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to extract information from the DL
056   model for various optimization decisions given hardware components Phothilimthana et al. (2023);
057   Panner Selvam & Brorsson; 2023); Liu et al. (2022).
058
      Unfortunately, aforementioned GNN-based approaches require comprehensive retraining to accom-
059
      modate new hardware environments or DL architectures, often requiring large labeled datasets.
060
      These requirements can hinder a rapid adaptation and optimization, limiting the flexibility of these
061   models when new architectures or configurations emerge. Fortunately, the recent successes of Large
062   Language Models (LLMs) in various domains have underscored their capability to understand and
063   generate complex systems Team et al. (2024); Singhal et al. (2023); Wayne et al. (2023); Wu et al.
064   (2023); Li et al. (2023). This includes not only natural language but also structured data such as
065   code, configuration settings, and textual descriptions of hardware configurations and DL architec-
066   tures. Given their extensive pre-training on diverse datasets, LLMs can generalize effectively when
067   fine-tuned on specific tasks. Their generalization capability makes them good candidates for en-
068   hancing DL performance prediction.
069   However, employing LLMs in the performance prediction domain poses challenges, primarily due
070   to the need for representing DL models in a format that LLMs can efficiently process. Prior works
071   have considered using high-level descriptions to represent programs and graphs as text inputs for
072   compiler optimizations and performance predictions Cummins et al. (2023); Jawahar et al. (2023).
073   Nonetheless, these representations often fail to maintain the full structural intricacies of DL models,
074   losing crucial connectivity and hierarchical information. An alternative representation is to use
075
      structured text format (e.g. JSON, XML, Protobuf, etc.), which maintains detailed information of
      node features and their connections. However, DL models can contain tens-of-thousands of nodes
076
      Phothilimthana et al. (2023), which can hinder the processing efficiency and scalability when used
077
      with LLMs.
078
079   Recent research has explored the use of GNNs as encoders to convert graph data into embeddings
080   as inputs to LLMs, thereby effectively bridging the gap between graph data and the textual input
081
      preferred by LLMs. However, these studies primarily focus on graph-based question answering,
      rather than directly on performance prediction Perozzi et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024).
082
083   In line with Perozzi et al. (2024) findings, We hypothesize that graph embeddings, derived from
084   GNNs, represent DL models more effectively for performance prediction than conventional text rep-
085   resentations because the graph embeddings could better capture structures and connectivity. Based
086   on this hypothesis, we propose the GNN-LLM model for DL performance prediction, as illustrated
087
      in Figure 1. Our experiment has confirmed that using graph embeddings significantly outperforms
      using a semi-structured text format (JSON) and a high-level text format in both accuracy and com-
088
      putational efficiency. Specifically, our approach surpasses a JSON format by approximately 6% in
089
      accuracy and is 21 times faster in terms of training time. Likewise, our approach surpasses high-level
090   text by 134% in accuracy and is 2 times faster in terms of training time, demonstrating a substantial
091   improvement over text-based representation.
092
093
      To enhance the adaptability and accuracy of the model, we further develop a structured pre-training
      strategy that obviates the need for extensive retraining from scratch. The approach begins by training
094
      a GNN using a mask autoencoding technique on unlabeled DL models, inspired by Hou et al. (2022)
095
      research. In this initial phase, the GNN learns to capture DL graph structures and node information.
096   Subsequently, we refine the integration between the DL graph data and the LLM by fine-tuning
097   the projection layer and the LLM through a graph-to-text task. This graph-to-text translation will
098   enable the LLM to comprehend DL graph structures and improve the model’s ability to adapt to new
099   hardware with minimal training samples for downstream performance prediction tasks. Finally, all
100   components are fine-tuned for the final performance prediction task.
101
      In the evaluation, our method achieves a 8.8 percentage-point increase in accuracy over the state-of-
102   the-art GNN baseline on the NNLQP multi-platform dataset, and a remarkable 30–70 percentage-
103   point increase in accuracy when adapted to new hardware with few samples. The results confirms
104   our method’s efficacy in enhancing both the accuracy and adaptability of performance predictions
105   across varied computational environments.

121   2.1     DL M ODELS AS C OMPUTATIONAL G RAPHS & G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS (GNN S )
122
123   DL models can be represented as directed acyclic computational graphs, where nodes correspond to
124   mathematical operations and edges represent data flow between these operations. The input features
125
      of every node include the op code (e.g., einsum, relu, etc), the output data type (e.g., float32, uint8,
      etc) and the shape of the output tensor – See Phothilimthana et al. (2023) for comprehensive list of
126
      node-wise features.
127
128   GNNs are designed to operate on graph-structured data. Let graph of n nodes be represented with
129   a node feature matrix X ∈ RN ×. and an adjacency matrix A ∈ {0, 1}N ×N . GNNs use an iterative
130   message passing process to generate embeddings for nodes. During message passing, each node
131
      updates its embedding by aggregating information from its neighbors. GNN layer can be written as:

132                                                             (l−1)
                                   H(l) = TRANSFORM Hi                ,A                            (1)

134   where H(l) is the node embedding matrix at the l-th layer and H(l) = X. Through multiple message-
135   passing layers, each node aggregates information from a wider neighborhood, capturing both imme-
136   diate and distant neighbor information. GNNs have excelled in tasks such as node classification,
137   link prediction, and graph-level classification. There are many possible choices for TRANSFORM
138   function Kipf & Welling (2017); Veličković et al. (2018); Hamilton et al. (2017); Xu et al. (2019).

146   2.2.1     P RE - TRAINED L ARGE L ANGAUGE M ODELS
147
148
      Pre-trained LLMs are advanced neural networks for natural language processing tasks. They lever-
      age the Transformer architecture Vaswani et al. (2017), which uses self-attention mechanisms to
149
      manage long-range dependencies in text. LLMs are pre-trained on extensive corpora to predict sub-
150
      sequent tokens, enabling them to capture intricate linguistic patterns. This pre-training is followed
151   by finetuning task-specific datasets to adapt to various applications like text classification and trans-
152   lation.
153
154   2.2.2     PARAMETER -E FFICIENT F INE -T UNING
155
156   With the rapid increase in the size of state-of-the-art LLMs, traditional fine-tuning has become
157   resource intensive. Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) aims to adapt models to new tasks by
158
      updating only a small subset of parameters Xu et al. (2023).
159   Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA): LoRA introduces low-rank matrices into model layers, repre-
160   sented as ∆W = BA, where B and A are trainable low-rank matrices. This approach reduces
161   the computational burden by updating fewer parameters while keeping the main model’s parameters
      frozen, thus preserving the pre-trained knowledge Hu et al. (2021).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Soft Prompting: Soft prompts are learnable vectors integrated into the model’s input to guide its
163   behavior toward specific tasks. This method updates only a small number of parameters, making it
164   computationally efficient and preserving the broad knowledge of the model Bulat & Tzimiropoulos
165   (2023).

174
175   We consider the following methods to represent DL models for processing by LLMs.
176
177   Graph Representation. This method first encodes a DL model in the Open Neural Network Ex-
178   change (ONNX) format, represented as a graph with node feature matrix X formulated as:

183   the output shape. The operation ⊕ represents a vector concatenation. This method is adapted from
184   the framework established in Liu et al. (2022). Subsequently, we feed the node feature matrix X and
185   the adjacency matrix A into the GNN. The GNN then produces a graph embedding for input into
186   the LLM, along with prompt’s token embeddings, to predict the model’s performance.
187
188   High-level Text Representation. We use a predefined template that captures essential computa-
189   tional and structural properties of a DL model. This includes overall model statistics — such as
190   FLOPs, parameter count, and batch size — offering insights into the model’s complexity and ca-
191   pacity. We also include layer-specific statistics, detailing each layer’s FLOPs and parameter counts.
192
      These elements together offer a holistic view of a DL model’s architecture and its computational
      behavior. To predict its performance, we simply tokenize and apply a conventional word encoder on
193
      the textual prompt for LLM processing.
194
195
      Semi-Structured Text Representation. We adopt a semi-structured JSON format to comprehen-
196
      sively encapsulate a DL architecture. This format itemizes each node’s characteristics, including the
197   operator type, input and output shapes, computation complexities, and node attributes. Addition-
198   ally, it capture node connectivity. For LLM processing, we tokenize and apply a conventional word
199   encoder on the semi-structured description.

248   3.3   T RAINING S TRATEGY: 3- STAGE TRAINING
249
      We hypothesize that directly fine-tuning both LLMs and GNNs for performance prediction tasks,
250
      starting from scratch, may not yield optimal adaptability for new tasks. The challenge lies in the
251
      initial lack of domain-specific knowledge, which is crucial for the model to effectively process and
252   predict the DL performance metrics. To address this, we propose a novel structured pre-training
253   methodology, designed to enhance the model’s intrinsic understanding of DL graph structures be-
254   fore fine-tuning for performance prediction. The pre-training strategy comprises the three stages as
255   shown in Figure 2.
256
257   Stage 1. GNN Pre-training. We employ the Graph Maked Auto Encoder technique (GraphMAE)
258   for GNN pre-training Hou et al. (2022). We use GIN as both encoder and decoder. Given a DL
259   graph with X and A, we mask a portion of X using a learnable mask vector to produce X.      e The
260   GIN encoder processes (X,  e A) to generate latent embeddings Z, effectively capturing the obscured
261   structural details. The GIN decoder reconstructs the node features from Z to X, b aimed at closely
262   approximating the original X. Reconstruction accuracy is quantified using Scaled Cosine Error
263   (SCE), which evaluates alignment in both direction and magnitude of the feature vectors. Using
264   GIN for both encoding and decoding optimizes the preservation and reconstruction of local graph
265   structures, essential for understanding DL graphs. The SCE, by assessing both vector orientation
266   and length, enhances model sensitivity to structural and feature variations, preparing it for robust
267   performance on subsequent tasks.
268
269   Stage 2. Graph-Text Adaptation. For this stage we update only projection layer and LLM
      weights. The projection layer Wp adapts the graph embeddings GraphToken for integration with

295
      For GNN pre-training, we use a dataset containing 20,000 unlabeled DL graphs, Liu et al. (2022),
296
      including of ten DL model families (ResNets, EfficientNets, MobileNetV2s, MobileNetV3s, Mnas-
297   Nets, SqueezeNets, VGGs, Alexnets, NasBench201s and GoogleNets). These ONNX models are
298   transformed into node feature matrices and adjacency matrices, then converted these into the Py-
299   Torch Geometric data format (PyG), as detailed in Section 3.1, for GNN pre-training (Section 3.3).
300   This extensive set of graphs allows our GNN to capture a wide range of node and edge features,
301   providing robust initial embeddings.
302

303   structured the dataset into {(G, Q, A)} format: G represents the DL model’s graph structure in PyG
304   format, Q is a textual prompt (Summarise the graph), and A is the summary of DL architecture,
305   which is the response from the LLM (as referenced in the sample prompt in Appendix A.3). The
306   summary provides comprehensive details, including the total number of nodes, edges, model com-
307   plexity, and statistics for each layer. The dataset comprises 20,000 prompts.
308
      For performance prediction fine-tuning, we use the NNLQP Multi-platform dataset, which includes
309   ten DL model families across nine computational architectures (cpu-openppl-fp32, hi3559A-nnie11-

311   nnie12-int8, atlas300-acl-fp16, mul270-neuware-int8). This dataset contains DL graphs, platform
312   IDs, and inference latency metrics. It consists of 7,396 graphs for training and 3,201 for testing.
313   During the forward pass, queries Q are provided to the LLM, combined with G, to predict inference
314   times. The predicted inference latency A is directly derived from the LLM’s output.

493   The field of performance prediction for DL models has witnessed growing interest in recent years.
494
      Early work by Qi et al. (2017) proposed an analytical model to estimate DL model training time.

495
      sumption, utilizing analytical models to estimate resource utilization during training. To improve
496
      prediction accuracy, researchers have explored machine learning approaches. Bouhali et al. (2021)
497   used an MLP-based regressor with features like trainable parameter counts, but it was constrained by
498   a shallow understanding of DL layers’ dynamics. Others, such as Justus et al. (2018), Gianniti et al.
499   (2018), Zhang et al. (2021), Lee et al. (2021) and adopted a layer-by-layer technique, they predicted

523   6   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
524
525   This paper has investigated the integration of GNNs and LLMs to enhance the accuracy and adapt-
526   ability of DL performance prediction. Our empirical evaluations have demonstrated that graph
527   embeddings, derived from GNNs, are more effective inputs for LLMs than traditional text-based
528
      representations, leading to significant improvements in both accuracy and computational efficiency.
      Additionally, we have proposed a structured pre-training strategy that enables model adaptation to
529
      new hardware environments with minimal retraining, further enhancing the practicality and efficacy
530
      of our approach. We believe that our research offers a promising direction for advancing the field of
531   DL performance prediction and its applications in various stages of the DL system stack.

705   metrics:
706   Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE): This metric quantifies the average of the absolute
707   percentage differences between each predicted value and its corresponding actual value. It is defined
708   mathematically as:

      Here, yi represents the actual value and yi′ represents the predicted value. MAPE is a non-negative
712
      number, where a smaller value indicates a more accurate model.
713
714   Accuracy within a delta threshold (Acc(δ)): This metric measures the percentage of predicted
715   values that are within a specified percentage (δ) of the actual values. It is defined as:
716                                             n

                                             1X

      better predictive performance of the model.
