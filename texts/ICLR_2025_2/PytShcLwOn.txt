011                                                      A BSTRACT
012
013                We introduce a novel task of generating realistic and diverse 3D hand trajectories
014                given a single image of an object, which could be involved in a hand-object in-
015                teraction scene or pictured by itself. When humans grasp an object, appropriate
016                trajectories naturally form in our minds to use it for specific tasks. Hand-object
017
                   interaction trajectory priors can greatly benefit applications in robotics, embodied
                   AI, augmented reality and related fields. However, synthesizing realistic and ap-
018
                   propriate hand trajectories given a single object or hand-object interaction image
019
                   is a highly ambiguous task, requiring to correctly identify the object of interest and
020                possibly even the correct interaction among many possible alternatives. To tackle
021                this challenging problem, we propose the SIGHT-Fusion system, consisting of a
022                curated pipeline for extracting visual features of hand-object interaction details
023                from egocentric videos involving object manipulation, and a diffusion-based con-
024                ditional motion generation model processing the extracted features. We train our
025                method given video data with corresponding hand trajectory annotations, without
026                supervision in the form of action labels. For the evaluation, we establish bench-
027                marks utilizing the first-person FPHAB and HOI4D datasets, testing our method
028
                   against various baselines and using multiple metrics. We also introduce task sim-
                   ulators for executing the generated hand trajectories and reporting task success
029
                   rates as an additional metric. Experiments show that our method generates more
030
                   appropriate and diverse hand trajectories than baselines and presents promising
031                generalization capability on unseen objects. The accuracy of the generated hand
032                trajectories is confirmed in a physics simulation setting, showcasing the authen-
033                ticity of the created sequences and their applicability in downstream uses.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
037
038
      As our hand grasps an object, we immediately plan out potential maneuvers to manipulate it for our
039
      intentions. Consider pouring some juice from a bottle into a cup – it can be as straightforward as ro-
040   tating your wrist. At a granular level, it requires a continuous adjustment of the hand translation and
041   orientation to transfer exactly the desired amount of liquid into the target receptacle. Humans’ hand
042   motion planning systems are remarkably robust in adapting to unseen objects, emulating movements
043   observed from others, and devising paths from visual cues alone. Robotic agents and AI systems
044   alike could benefit immensely from a similar ability to synthesize 3D hand trajectories from an image
045   depicting a hand-object interaction scenario, be it to anticipate human behavior, generate realistic
046   animations, or interact with the physical world. Following this realization, we wish to investigate
047   the generation of high-quality hand trajectories from visual representations of objects.
048   In this paper, we propose the new task of Single-Image Conditioned Generation of Hand Trajectories
049   (SIGHT). Given a single first-person image showing a hand interacting with an object, our goal is to
050   generate plausible and diverse 3D hand trajectories that meaningfully complete the action initiated
051   in the image. Additionally, we explore the generation of appropriate interaction trajectories from
052   standalone images of previously unseen objects without a hand manipulating the object.

068   Previous studies on hand-object interactions concentrate on detecting and segmenting (Darkhalil
069   et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2022a)) pairs of hands and objects interacting within images, or aim
070   to reconstruct the 3D models of objects and the hands interacting with them from images (Hasson
071   et al. (2019); Ye et al. (2022)) or videos (Fan et al. (2024); Ye et al. (2023a)), but do not generate
072   trajectories. Furthermore, the field of human motion generation has hitherto focused on whole-body
073   motion synthesis (Guo et al. (2022; 2020b); Tevet et al. (2022)), with little attention paid to syn-
074   thesizing realistic, task-appropriate and interactive hand motions. The conditional information in
075   previous whole-body motion synthesis work is restricted to either action labels or textual descrip-
076
      tions of motions, inconveniently requiring manual text annotations or action descriptions alongside
      the motion data. In contrast, our main focus is on generating dynamic 3D hand trajectories from a
077
      single static image. We utilize an underexplored and more easily affordable form of conditioning
078
      motion generation models to generate motions for an important yet neglected facet of human mo-
079   tion. Our aim is to reduce the amount of conditional information provided, restricting it solely to
080   the static hand-object interaction or standalone object portrayed in the input image. Diverging from
081   prior hand-object interaction tasks, the new proposed task, SIGHT, has a more generic but challeng-
082   ing setup, considering the multitude of possible hand-object interactions and the minimal guidance
083   information provided.
084
      SIGHT comes with several challenges. When generating trajectories from first-person hand-object
085   interaction images, the initial step in addressing this task is to detect the interacting hand and object,
086   as images captured from a real-world first-person perspective often exhibit significant clutter in the
087   background. Moreover, generating future hand trajectories from images capturing static moments of
088   interaction involves disambiguating the intended action, with objects often having multiple possible
089   uses corresponding to different hand trajectories. The points of contact between the hand and the
090   object play a key role in differentiating between similar actions, although hand and object often
091   occlude each other in the images. Further, synthesizing sequences of hand trajectories necessitates
092   the generation of smooth, natural and anatomically plausible motions that lead to the successful
093
      completion of the initiated actions for contacted objects. Finally, an interaction trajectory generation
      system integrated into the real world must operate robustly even when presented with previously
094
      unseen objects, complicating the inferral of object-appropriate trajectories.
095
096   We present SIGHT-Fusion to address these challenges. Our key insight is in the meaningful iden-
097   tification of the contacted object in presence of distracting information in the input image’s back-
098   ground so as to maximally disambiguate the conditioning of the motion generator. Further, we find
099
      that egocentric videos of people interacting with diverse objects can serve as an effective source of
      training data for reasoning about interaction trajectories fitting previously unseen object instances.
100
      The effective use of visual hand-object interaction features enables our model to infer the underlying
101
      intentions and generate realistic hand trajectories for the intended interactions depicted in a single
102   image. More specifically, we propose SIGHT-Fusion, a conditional motion generation diffusion
103   model that learns to generate realistic and diverse 3D hand trajectories. The extracted hand-object
104   interaction features are used as the conditional input. Through training, SIGHT-Fusion learns the
105   distribution of possible hand movements given a static interaction moment and acquires the ability
106   to distinguish between potential plausible motion sequences by leveraging fine-grained details con-
107   tained in the contact regions. Using a general-purpose vision foundation models for the extraction
      of object features, SIGHT-Fusion effectively generalize to object instances not seen during training.

108
      We set up comprehensive baselines and metrics utilizing the FPHAB (Garcia-Hernando et al. (2018))
109   and HOI4D (Liu et al. (2022)) datasets for the newly proposed SIGHT task. Extensive experiments
110   show that SIGHT-Fusion is able to generate more natural and diverse hand trajectories than base-
111   lines. We also observe promising potentials for our learned system to generalize to unseen object
112   instances. We further introduce a task-oriented metric to execute the generated hand trajectories
113   in physical simulation and evaluate if they could lead to successful task execution. Results show
114   that our method is able to produce trajectories that can accomplish downstream tasks successfully.
115   Lastly, we empirically validate system designs including the usefulness of various feature types in
116   improving the quality of our result.
117   In summary, the contributions in this paper are as follows: 1) We introduce a novel task of generating
118   3D hand trajectories given a single image of an object, in the two settings of either being depicted
119   by itself or being interacted with by a human hand. 2) We set up comprehensive benchmarks for
120   the new task, including various baselines and metrics. We further propose a task success rate met-
121   ric for evaluation using physical simulation. 3) We develop SIGHT-Fusion to tackle the proposed
122   problem with a novel pipeline to extract hand-object interaction features from the single image input
123
      and a conditional diffusion-based hand trajectory generative model. 4) Experiments show superior
      performance compared with baselines and ablated versions of our system.

130   The field of learning hand-object interactions, particularly in first-person videos, has seen growing
131   interest. We briefly discuss existing methods for hand-object interaction segmentation, 3D hand
132   reconstruction, and motion generation.
133   Hand-Object interaction segmentation. Advancing the domain of interaction detection, Shan et
134   al. (Shan et al. (2020)) introduce a method based on Faster-RCNN (Ren et al. (2015)) to detect
135   hands and objects from RGB images, utilizing RoI features for predicting hand sides, bounding
136   boxes, and contact states. EgoHOS (Zhang et al. (2022a)), adopting a similar framework, enhances
137   the methodology by training on diverse first-person datasets and adding the capability to predict
138   the contact boundary between hands and objects. In a different approach, COHESIV (Shan et al.
139   (2021)) generates detailed pixel-wise features, enabling the segmentation of images into classes of
140   people, objects, and backgrounds. This segmentation process is augmented by a user-defined query
141
      point on the visible hand, utilizing optical flow and regressed hand poses to improve accuracy.
142   Whole-Body motion generation. Recent work in motion generation has relied heavily on Vari-
143   ational Auto Encoders (VAE) and Diffusion models. Studies have explored conditional genera-
144   tion using text (Karunratanakul et al. (2023); Kim et al. (2023); Tevet et al. (2023); Zhang et al.
145   (2022b)), audio (Yi et al. (2023)), both (Dabral et al. (2023); Zhou & Wang (2022)) and categorical
146   actions (Zhao et al. (2023)). Tevet et al. (Tevet et al. (2023)) expand upon previous work (Kim et al.
147
      (2023); Zhang et al. (2022b)) by introducing a classifier-free diffusion model MDM for text-driven
      full-body human motion generation. Other works such as (Diomataris et al. (2024)) also generate
148
      full-body human motions using c-VAEs. GMD (Karunratanakul et al. (2023)) further enhances it
149
      by adding spatial constraints. Most works such as (Petrovich et al. (2022b)) use VAE’s to generate
150   diverse SMPL body shapes from texts, while others (Yi et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023)) use a
151   Vector Quantised VAE (VQ-VAE) to generate human poses from text and speech respectively. A
152   hybrid approach combining VAEs and diffusion models by (Chen et al. (2023)) de-noise conditional
153   latent vectors and decode them to produce human body poses.
154
      Hand motion generation. A novel two-step method is introduced in Ye et al. (2023b) for synthe-
155
      sizing hand-object interaction images from a single RGB image of an object using diffusion models.
156   This method, however, generates static snapshots rather than a continuous motion. Other works
157   such as HMP (Duran et al. (2023)) try to generate 3d hand poses even if the hand is occluded. All of
158   these approaches have the disadvantage that they generate snapshots rather than full motions which
159   is what has been done for full body movements. Bao et al. (2023) tries to predict hand movement
160   for a VR/AR setting however it only predicts the general motion and not the precise movement of
161   individual joints. AI often has problems with generating accurate and precise hands, which is often
      used as a telltale sign to differentiate actual images from generated ones.

201   3.1     TASK DEFINITION
202
      We consider two variations of our task: in our first setup, the input I to the hand motion generator
203
      M is a first-person image showing a hand enacting a certain action on an interacted object, such
204   as opening or pouring out a bottle. In our second setup, I consists of a close-up image showing
205   an object without an interacting hand. For both cases, the expected output is a sequence of hand
206   poses H1:f = H1 , . . . , Hf , where f is a predefined number of frames. Each Hi corresponds to
207   a representation of a 3D human hand for a single timestep. Our work represents the Hi using
208   SMPL-X (Pavlakos et al. (2019)) and reasons about the right hand, in concordance with the almost
209   exclusively right-handed object interactions seen in most publically available video datasets.
210
211   3.2     E XTRACTING ACTION -I NFORMATIVE F EATURES
212
213   Given an input image, the SIGHT task requires extracting highly informative visual features to guide
214   the motion synthesis. When generating hand trajectories from images depicting hand-object inter-
215   action scenarios, correctly identifying the object of interest in the input frame presents a challenge
      due to background clutter and occlusion caused by the hand. The synthesis of a correct motion is


                                                         4
216
      even more difficult when generating trajectories from images of standalone objects not including
217   manipulating hands, as no interaction cues are given.
218
219   Object features. We first use VISOR-HOS (Darkhalil et al. (2022)) to detect interacted objects O
220
      in the input images I, and then crop to the bounding box of O before extracting visual features. See
      the Supp. Mat. for details about the training of VISOR-HOS. The visual object features are obtained
221
      using CLIP (Radford et al. (2021)) to leverage the generalizability of foundation model features,
222
      helping us reason about objects not seen during training.
223
224   Part features. Furthermore, we investigate providing the motion generator with additional con-
225   ditioning information in the form of features local to the contact point between the hand with the
226
      object. We extract features from high-level CLIP 2D feature grids and use them as part features. Our
      hypothesis is that part features help to disambiguate different actions that are associated to different
227
      object parts. We begin by dilating the HOS hand mask and intersecting the dilated mask with the
228
      HOS object mask. The resulting mask is then cropped to the region of O and resized to match the
229   dimension of the 2D CLIP feature grid. An average feature vector is computed by taking the mean
230   of all grid patches within the resized mask region. This vector is linearly projected to the input
231   dimension of our Transformer (Vaswani et al. (2017))-based motion generator and prepended to the
232   input motion sequence.

234   3.3   L EARNING TO G ENERATE H AND T RAJECTORIES
235
236   Denoising diffusion models, originally proposed for image synthesis (Rombach et al. (2022); Sa-
237   haria et al. (2022)), have been successfully adapted to whole-body human motion generation (Tevet
238
      et al. (2022); Karunratanakul et al. (2023); Yuan et al. (2023)). These models surpass traditional
      methods based on GANs (Barsoum et al. (2018); Guo et al. (2020b)) and VAEs (Petrovich et al.
239
      (2021; 2022a)) by delivering superior quality and diversity in generated motions. Given the shared
240
      requirement of reasoning about temporal sequences of joints, we adapt the state-of-the-art Human
241   Motion Diffusion Model (MDM) (Tevet et al. (2022)) for the SIGHT task of 3D hand trajectory
242   generation. Originally, MDM synthesizes whole-body human motions from text inputs or learned
243   embeddings encoding action labels. The motion synthesis process for a sequence of N frames, rep-
244   resented by J joints with R features each, starts with a randomly sampled latent code xT ∼ N (0, I)
245   where xi ∈ RN ×J×R ∀i ∈ {1, ..., N }, and uses a transformer G to iteratively diffuse the final
246   motion x0 in T steps.
247   We employ a Transformer encoder model to iteratively diffuse the synthesized motion, starting from
248   Gaussian noise. During each diffusion iteration, the Transformer receives as input the conditioning
249   information contained in the initial tokens, followed by tokens representing the motion sequence
250   from the previous diffusion step, or Gaussian noise for the initial step (see Figure 2). Our work does
251   not consider the prediction of manipulation trajectories for multiple objects being manipulated by
252   the actor simultaneously, as this is a highly specific setup with little publicly available data.
253   We repurpose MDM for hand trajectory generation by replacing the original whole-body kinematic
254   tree with the representation of the right hand used by OpenPose (Cao et al. (2017)) and SMPL-X
255   (Pavlakos et al. (2019)), yielding J = 17 joints. We also replace the original textual encoder used for
256   conditioning inputs with CLIP’s visual encoder. We maintain the 6-dimensional pose representation
257   used by MDM to encode motions, i.e. R = 6, as suggested by (Zhou et al. (2019)).
258

259   (2022) while dropping the foot contact loss. Specifically, let c be the conditioning information, F K

263   the input conditioning information c with xt to form the input of G, which is trained to predict the
264   final motion sequence from this condition-augmented intermediate representation. Our optimized
265   loss term L hence consists of:

      4.1   DATASETS
287
      To establish a comprehensive evaluation for our newly introduced task, we adapt two first-person
288   video datasets for our benchmark. We also introduce new dataset splits to test adaptability to unseen
289   scene backgrounds and new object instances.
290
291
      FPHAB (Garcia-Hernando et al. (2018)) The FPHAB dataset contains first-person videos captur-
      ing 45 activities (defined as action-object pairs, e.g. stir cup), involving 26 objects. Each activity is
292
      recorded multiple times by six different subjects, all using their right hands. Motion-captured hand
293
      poses are provided together with the video data. Following the human motion generation literature,
294   we first merge all actions of the same verb, disregarding the corresponding objects (to be elaborated
295   in Sec. 4.2). We further eliminate videos without any object manipulation, such as those where a
296   simple handshake is performed. We note that most of the frames in FPHAB contain hand-object
297   interactions and pure object frames without occlusions are rare to find.
298
      Preprocessing. Notably, the hands of all subjects in FPHAB exhibit significant occlusion due to
299
      motion capture markers, which leads to a performance deterioration of the off-the-shelf hand-object
300   interaction detector used in our pipeline (Darkhalil et al. (2022)). To address this issue, we inpaint
301   away these markers using a video inpainting method (Li et al. (2022)) prior to processing the dataset.
302   Please refer to Supp. Mat. for detailed evaluation and visualizations.
303
      Data split. We use the cross-subject split proposed in the original work, with subjects 1, 3 and 4
304
      used for training, and the three others used for testing. As all actions, object categories and object
305
      instances appear in both the training and test sets, this original FPHAB split poses a simple test
306   verifying the generalizability of models to novel views of object instances and actions already seen
307   in the training set. However, the high number of objects and the association of multiple actions with
308   the same object category make inferring the correct action to synthesize appropriate hand trajectories
309   particularly challenging for this dataset.
310
      HOI4D (Liu et al. (2022)) The HOI4D dataset consists of first-person videos of hands interact-
311
      ing with everyday objects from 16 categories. The number of object instances varies within each
312   category, ranging from 31 to 47, and the action tasks associated with these objects vary between 2
313   to 6 per category. Altogether, the dataset defines 31 action tasks. As many tasks are highly similar
314   to each other or only involve a displacement of the object, we merge/rename certain tasks and drop
315   others to extract 13 actions from the original 31 tasks. Details of this grouping are provided in the
316   Supp. Mat. As no public data split is available for HOI4D, we define our own splits with the aim of
317   testing several dimensions of the generalizability for methods addressing the SIGHT task.
318   Instance split. For each object category and each action, there exist videos of at least 2 different
319   object instances in HOI4D. This lends itself well to grouping the videos corresponding to different
320   object instances of the same category and showing the same action being enacted. We then split
321   each group into one subgroup with instances to use only for the training set, and another subgroup
322   with instances to use only for the test set. Our proposed instance split thus allows a meaningful
323   evaluation of cross-instance action knowledge transfer. Simultaneously, more advanced reasoning is
      required for successful cross-instance transfer, as object instances in the test set may look different


                                                          6
324
      from those seen during training. Unlike in FPHAB, the videos in HOI4D do not start with the
325   manipulated object already in hand. We thus construct the test frames for the instance split by
326   cropping the first video frame to the yet ungrasped object of interest. Altogether, the instance split
327   allows us to evaluate the setting of generating 3D hand trajectories for an unseen object, without
328   interaction cues provided by the presence of a hand in the image. A detailed description of the
329   proposed instance split is provided in the Supp. Mat.
330
      Location split. We further wish to investigate generalization ability for hand-object interaction
331   scenes explicitly featuring hands in view. Since the HOI4D videos feature objects recorded at dis-
332   tinctive locations associated with specific actions, the background becomes more visible in the input
333   image when expanding to a larger region to include the hand into the input image. We hence de-
334   sign the location split by dividing the HOI4D videos into training and test sets according to their
335   recording location, i.e. the environment. Doing so reduces the possibility of data leakage through
336   the image’s background. The location split hence provides an evaluation for the scenario of encoun-
337   tering both seen and novel objects in environments that are unseen during training.
338   In-contact frame selection. When constructing the test sets of our datasets in scenarios where the
339   object and hand are in contact, we use VISOR (Darkhalil et al. (2022)) to detect the frame where the
340   actor’s hand first contacts any object in each video. We then manually verify that the actor is indeed
341   contacting the desired object. If necessary, we select a later frame that shows actual contact with
342   the desired object. This manual adaptation is needed for only a few videos and is done to ensure the
343   reliability of the test data used for evaluating methods.

345   4.2   E VALUATION OF G ENERATED H AND T RAJECTORIES
346
      Evaluation metrics. Following the combination of metrics that are commonly used in the human
347
      motion generation literature (Guo et al. (2020b); Tevet et al. (2022)), we measure a method’s success
348
      on our task based on the accuracy, diversity, and fidelity to ground-truth of its generated trajectories.
349
350          • Accuracy (ACC): Given that each generated hand trajectory is supposed to depict a
351            specific action, we use the accuracy of an action classifier working on hand trajectories
352            to measure the accuracy of the trajectories produced by a motion generation method M.
353            This formulation encourages the development of methods adept at matching their output
354            trajectories to the hand-object interaction in the image given visual information alone, with
355            no explicit knowledge of the desired output action.
356
357          • Diversity (DIV): The diversity of M ’s outputs is calculated through the Fréchet Inception
358            distances (FIDs) (Heusel et al. (2017)) between action classifier features extracted from
359            generated (gen.) and ground-truth (GT) motion groups. The diversity of the generated
360
               trajectories corresponds to the FID of one half of a trajectory group to the other half. It is
               desirable in moderation: too little diversity corresponds to a method simply (re-)producing
361
               a few learned trajectories, while too much results in the hand trajectories becoming erratic.
362
               Hence, a diversity score close to that of the GT trajectories is desirable.
363
364
             • Fidelity (FID): The fidelity of hand trajectories generated by M is calculated through the
365
               Fréchet Inception distances, similar to the diversity metric. Lower FIDs between generated
366
               and ground-truth sequences translate to a motion generation method producing trajectories
367            more similar in distribution to real reference trajectories.
368
369   Implementation details. For each run, consistent with the human motion generation literature (Guo
370   et al. (2022; 2020a); Tevet et al. (2022)), we select the checkpoint achieving the lowest FID metric on
371   the test set, so as to produce the trajectories most similar to the test set trajectories. Hyperparameters
372   are provided in Supp. Mat.
373   Baselines. We compare our method against several strong baselines. As the proposed SIGHT task
374   is novel and no existing approach is suitable for solving the task, we adapt state-of-the-art baselines
375   from the whole-body motion generation literature. We evaluate both image-conditioned and text-
376   conditioned setups. The image conditioning features are produced as described in Sec. 3.2. The text
377   conditioning is obtained by prompting LLaVa (Liu et al. (2023)), a state-of-the-art visual question
      answering model, with the question “What is the hand in the image doing?” given the input image

527   5   C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work, we introduce the novel task of generating natural and diverse hand trajectories con-
530   ditioned on single image inputs for hand-object interaction. Our work delves into the develop-
531
      ment of methodologies for this challenging problem, presenting not only straightforward baselines,
      but also examining advanced approaches by proposing the novel method SIGHT-Fusion. Various
532
      hand-object interaction features are extracted from the single image input, and a diffusion-based
533
      conditional hand trajectory generative model is trained without the need for supervision by action
534   labels. We set up comprehensive baselines and metrics on the FPHAB and HOI4D datasets, and
535   also propose novel physical simulation environments for additional evaluation. Experiments show
536   our superior performance over baseline methods and promising results in generalizing to unseen
537   objects. Ablation studies further assess and validate the effectiveness of our usage of part features
538   as conditional information during the motion synthesis. We hope that our work will invite greater
539   interest in the SIGHT task, and that our general-purpose pipeline will be of use in related problems
      such as action anticipation and hand trajectory reconstruction.
