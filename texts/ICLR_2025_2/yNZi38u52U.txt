009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we introduce the concept of model cautiousness, which stresses the
012            importance of aligning a model’s confidence with its accuracy in in-distribution
013            (ID) scenarios while adopting a more uncertain approach in out-of-distribution
014            (OoD) contexts. Model cautiousness is framed as a spectrum between justified
015            confidence and complete ignorance, induced by the inability to clearly define a
016            model’s domain of expertise. We propose a rigorous post-hoc approach to ob-
017            tain a cautious model that merges the confidence scores of the primary confi-
018            dence model and a model discriminating between ID and OoD inputs. A metric
019
               to measure the cautiousness error of a confidence model is introduced. We further
               present a simple method for discriminating ID from OoD inputs and providing a
020
               meaningful confidence estimate that an input is OoD. Finally, we benchmark our
021
               approach across 12 question-answering and 37 vision datasets, demonstrating its
022            effectiveness in enhancing model cautiousness compared to standard calibration
023            procedures.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      As machine learning models become increasingly involved in high-stakes domains, the question
029
      of when they are equipped to make critical decisions becomes more pressing. This query lies at
030   the intersection of philosophy and practical application, requiring careful consideration. Intuitively,
031   models that consistently produce accurate predictions in their areas of expertise should exhibit high
032   confidence in their outputs. This is because a strong track record indicates that the model has effec-
033   tively learned from relevant data and can generalize its knowledge to similar situations.
034
      When a model is asked to make a statement or decision outside its domain of expertise, the situation
035
      becomes more complex. The model’s opinion may be less valuable in such cases, as it lacks the
036   necessary knowledge and experience to make informed judgments. The question arises: should we
037   trust the model’s statement, even if we know it lacks expertise in the given topic? If the model offers
038   a confident opinion outside its expertise and later turns out to be correct, can we justify its initial
039   confidence? Or should it have expressed uncertainty due to its lack of expertise?
040
      Within a model’s domain of expertise, it is reasonable to require the confidence exhibited by the
041
      model to match the accuracy of the model itself. In other words, the more accurate the model is, the
042   more confident we should expect it to be. It is well established in the literature, however, that even
043   within the model’s domain of expertise, models’ confidence and accuracy often do not align (Guo
044   et al., 2017). In fact, models often exhibit a largely overconfident behaviour, with a significant risk of
045   incurring into false positive decisions. Among the plethora of uncertainty quantification techniques
046   developed to properly quantify the model’s uncertainty (Abdar et al., 2021), calibration stands out as
047   a simple, usually post-hoc, family of techniques which require the model’s confidence and accuracy
048   to match (Guo et al., 2017). The confidence of a calibrated model therefore retains a notion of
049   probability, which provides an interpretable measure of risk to the user. However, as we move
050
      further from the model’s expertise, blindly trusting a model because it exhibits good calibration
      properties may still be dangerous, as it would encourage to trust models because they happen to be
051
      right, without considering whether they have a solid basis upon which to make a statement.
052
053   In this paper, we introduce the concept of model cautiousness, which emphasizes the importance
      of aligning a model’s confidence with its accuracy when handling in-distribution (ID) data, while

054
      gradually adopting a more uncertain stance as it encounters out-of-distribution (OoD) data. Cau-
055   tiousness can be viewed as a spectrum, ranging from justified confidence to complete uncertainty.
056   This gradual transition arises from the inherent inability in defining a model’s domain of expertise,
057   making it difficult to distinguish between instances where the model is interpolating within familiar
058   data and those where it is extrapolating beyond its knowledge.
059
      Given the challenges of implementing safety mechanisms in real production environments, partic-
060
      ularly when multiple stakeholders control different parts of the product pipeline, we propose an
061   operational definition of cautiousness that avoids imposing overly restrictive requirements for de-
062   ployment. Similar to calibration, we approach cautiousness as a post-hoc adjustment to the confi-
063   dence model, allowing it to be developed independently of the primary model. Unlike traditional
064   post-hoc calibration methods, which typically require only confidence scores and target variables
065   from a calibration dataset, our approach additionally leverages the embeddings of calibration inputs
066   to build a model capable of distinguishing between ID and OoD inputs (see Section 3.1). We do not
067   require a specific distribution of OoD data, nor do we rely on the synthetic generation of such data,
068   which could undermine the robustness of the pipeline. However, since qualitative OoD data, even if
069
      synthetic, can enhance the ability to differentiate between ID and OoD inputs, we encourage the use
      of such resources if feasible. Nonetheless, our preferred approach remains agnostic, with minimal
070
      assumptions, to lower the barriers to adopting model cautiousness.
071
072   In this paper, we make the following contributions:
073
074         1. We provide a formal definition of model cautiousness and introduce an error metric to
075            quantify the degree of cautiousness in a confidence model.
076         2. We propose a simple method for discriminating between ID and OoD inputs, along with a
077            reliable confidence estimate for identifying OoD data.
078
            3. We outline a simple yet rigorous method for achieving model cautiousness by combining
079
               the confidence scores from both the primary model and the discrimination model into a
080            cautious confidence score.
081
082
            4. Finally, we assess the cautiousness of our approach across 37 vision datasets and 12
               question-answering datasets. Our results show that our method significantly improves cau-
083
               tiousness compared to models calibrated using standard techniques on the vast majority of
084
               datasets.

088
      To illustrate the importance of model cautiousness, we begin with a motivating example. Figure 1
089
      (left) illustrates two distinct clusters of data, which we refer to as “moons”. The data points in the
090   upper moon are represented in orange, similar to the blob of data located above, while the points in
091   the lower moon are depicted in blue. A classification model is trained using these two moons of data,
092   without any exposure to the blob during the training phase. The model manages to classify perfectly
093   between the two moons. By doing so, it also happens to perfectly classify the out-of-distribution
094   blob of data. The background color indicates the model’s confidence, which approaches 1 as we
095   move away from the decision boundary.
096   Intuitively, the confidence of the classification model is said to be calibrated when the confidence
097   scores align with the model’s accuracy (for further details, see Section 2). For instance, if the model’s
098   accuracy is nearly 1 for certain data, we would anticipate the confidence to also be close to 1. Thus,
099   if we were to assess the calibration error for the blob of OoD data, we would conclude that the model
100   is well calibrated with respect to this distribution, as both confidence and accuracy are nearly 1.
101
      Nevertheless, even though the model is well calibrated, its high confidence for the OoD blob of
102   data may be viewed as undesirable and potentially risky in various practical applications. This
103   concern arises because the model has not been exposed to any data in that specific region of the input
104   space. Ideally, we would prefer a situation akin to Figure 1 (right), where the model demonstrates
105   high confidence near the training data but adopts a more cautious stance as we move further into
106   the OoD area. The left and right images in Figure 1 were generated using histogram binning, a
107   conventional calibration method described in (Zadrozny & Elkan, 2001; Detommaso et al., 2024a),
      and a cautiousness approach, which we will discuss in the following section.

126   2    BACKGROUND ON C ALIBRATION
127
128   Let us denote input variables by X ∈ X and target variables by Y ∈ Y. For simplicity, in this work
129   we restrict the focus to binary targets only, that is Y := {0, 1}, but the discussion naturally extends
130   to more general cases. We denote random variables via upper case letters, and reserve lower case
131   letters for their corresponding realizations.
132   Consider a model f (x) representing the confidence that Y equals 1 for a given input x. The follow-
133   ing definition introduces calibration, a minimal consistency requirement that endows a confidence
134   model with a notion of probability.
135
      Definition 2.1 (Calibration). We say that a model f is calibrated with respect to the distribution of
136   (X, Y ) if and only if
137
138
                                            P(Y = 1|f (X) = p) = p,
139   for all p ∈ [0, 1] within the support of the distribution of f (X).
140
141   In words, calibration requires that the confidence in a positive outcome matches the fraction of times
142   the positive outcome arises, for all confidence levels. If, for instance, we are confidence that an event
143   happens with 80% probability, then the fraction of independent times the event arises, over an infinite
144   number of trials, should be in fact 80%. While calibration is often thought of as a distribution-free
145   condition, we remark that Definition 2.1 is equivalent to asking that Y |f (X) ∼ Bernoulli(f (X)).
146   Definition 2.2 (Expected Calibration Error). We introduce the Expected Calibration Error (ECE)
147   defined by
148
                               ECE(f ) := EP P(Y = 1|f (X) = P ) − P ,                            (1)
149
150   where P is distributed as f (X).
151
152   The ECE is a popular measure of calibration error firstly introduced in (Guo et al., 2017). It is
153   immediate to see that if f is calibrated, than the ECE is zero. The ECE has been critized in multiple
154   works for being impossible to compute in practice and not robust to approximations (Błasiok et al.,
155   2023). Several variants have been proposed to improve over the ECE (Nixon et al., 2019). In this
156   work, we will propose a novel definition of calibration error that is built upon the definition of ECE
157   in (1), but we remark that the same concept can be directly applied to any of its variations.
158   As discussed in Section 1.1, evaluating the ECE against OoD data can lead to scenarios where a
159   model appears perfectly calibrated, resulting in a very low ECE, yet the model may exhibit high
160   confidence in its OoD predictions. This situation can create a misleading sense of security, poten-
161   tially leading to unsafe deployment of models in critical applications. The next section presents a
      solution to this issue.

242
243   Our approach to construct a model g that is able to discriminate between ID and OoD inputs is
244   pragmatic. Given a calibration dataset, we first fit a multivariate distribution over the embeddings of
245   the calibration inputs. At prediction time, we reconduct the prediction problem to a one-dimensional
246   statistical test, and exploit the p-value to quantify the probability that an input is ID.
247   Concretely, given the calibration data D = {(xi , yi )}N   i=1 , let zi denote the embeddings of the cal-

294   4    E XPERIMENTS
295
296   The purpose of this section is to show the effectiveness of our methods in quantifying model cau-
297   tiousness, and to show how our method significantly improves cautiousness against just a calibrated
298   model. We benchmark on 37 vision datasets and 13 question-answering datasets - see a detailed list
299   of the datasets in Section 4.1. For every vision dataset, we use a CLIP ViT 32b model (Radford
300   et al., 2021) with Quick GELU (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016) to compute confidence scores for each
301   class. For each question-answering dataset, we use a Google Gemma-2b model (Team et al., 2024)
302   to compute confidence scores for each answer. Let us denote by fj (X) the confidence score asso-
303
      ciated to the j-th class/answer given the image/question x, and by yj a binary variable indicating
      whether the class/answer j is correct. Because for simplicity in this work we restricted the scope to
304
      binary classification, we introduce ι(x) := arg maxj fj (x), whence we define the confidence score
305
306
      f˜(x) := fι(x) (x) and the corresponding binary target y := yι(x) .
307   We form pairs of datasets, of which one is considered as ID and the other OoD. The ID dataset is
308   randomly split 50/50 into calibration and holdout datasets. Each model f˜(x) is calibrated using the
309   calibration dataset, to produce a final calibrated model f (x). The same calibration dataset is used
310   to fit a discrimination model g(x). We form a test dataset by combining the holdout ID dataset with
311   the OoD in a 50/50 split. Metrics reported in this section are computed over this test dataset unless
312   differently specified. Results are on average over 5 different runs, resulting from 5 different random
313   splits of the ID dataset.

315   answering datasets, where g represents the confidence of the X2-95 discrimination model. The
316   notation (f, 0) refers to a case where the discrimination model is entirely absent, meaning all inputs
317   are predicted as ID with full confidence. This is equivalent to relying solely on the calibrated model

452   5     R ELATED W ORK
453
454   Several works in the literature focus on providing calibration or conformal prediction guarantees
455   under covariate shift assumptions (Tibshirani et al., 2019; Park et al., 2020; Jonkers et al., 2024).
456   This is relevant to our work since shifted covariates are considered OoD. However, these approaches
457   differ fundamentally as they aim to ensure OoD calibration, while the goal of cautiousness is to
458   ensure that the system remains uncertain when faced with OoD inputs. Additionally, the success
459   of these methods often depends on estimating a density ratio, which can be unreliable in practice
460   (Sugiyama et al., 2010).
461   Another branch of the literature focuses on OoD detection. In ODIN (Liang et al., 2017; Hsu et al.,
462   2020), the authors attempt to detect OoD inputs using a softmax approach with temperature scaling
463   (Guo et al., 2017) and input preprocessing. Other works use distance functions to measure how far
464   an input is from the training distribution, with the Mahalanobis distance (Lee et al., 2018) being the
465   most common. This distance is equivalent to the negative log-probability density function (PDF) of
466   a Gaussian distribution. A related approach, the relative Mahalanobis distance (Ren et al., 2021),
467
      computes the maximum ratio between the Gaussian likelihood for a specific class and the likeli-
      hood for the entire dataset. Other works based on the nearest neighbor distance (Sun et al., 2022;
468
      Detommaso et al., 2022) calculate the distance between an input and the closest training input, but
469
      these methods require knowledge of the training inputs and can be computationally expensive. DUQ
470   (Van Amersfoort et al., 2020) introduces a radial basis function to estimate confidence, while DDU
471   (Mukhoti et al., 2023) uses a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) over classes, similar to our approach
472   in Section 3.1. In (Venkataramanan et al., 2023), a GMM with a shared covariance matrix is used,
473   and OoD confidence is derived using a Chi-squared approach. This method is similar to X2-α, but
474   we utilize separate covariance matrices as in (Mukhoti et al., 2023), and introduce a guardrail quan-
475   tile in (9), which is shown in Section 4 to improve performance. Another related line of work is
476   SNGP (Liu et al., 2020), where the authors introduce a decomposition similar to ours in (5). How-
477   ever, they do not impose a full uncertainty requirement for OoD inputs as we do in (2), and their
478
      approach is not post-hoc, requiring modifications to the confidence model.
479
480   6     C ONCLUSION
481
482   In this work, we introduced the concept of model cautiousness, which requires a model to be cali-
483   brated for in-distribution (ID) data while remaining uncertain for out-of-distribution (OoD) inputs.
484   The balance between these two states is rigorously determined based on the performance of an aux-
485   iliary model that discriminates between ID and OoD inputs. We also introduced a metric to measure
      cautiousness error and proposed a method for discriminating ID versus OoD inputs, which pro-

486
      vides meaningful confidence estimates. Our approach was evaluated across numerous vision and
487   question-answering datasets, showing its effectiveness in improving model cautiousness compared
488   to standard calibration methods.
