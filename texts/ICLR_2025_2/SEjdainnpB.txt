011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Unlike Poker where the action space A is discrete, differential games in the phys-
014            ical world often have continuous action spaces not amenable to discrete abstrac-
015            tion, rendering no-regret algorithms with O(|A|) complexity not scalable. To
016
               address this challenge within the scope of two-player zero-sum (2p0s) games with
               one-sided information, we show that (1) a computational complexity independent
017
               of |A| can be achieved by exploiting the “Cav u” property of behavioral strate-
018
               gies in incomplete-information games and the Isaacs’ condition that commonly
019            holds for control systems, and that (2) the computation of the two equilibrium
020            strategies can be decoupled under the Isaacs’ condition. We provide computa-
021            tional complexity of the resultant algorithm for approximating continuous-action
022            mixed strategies (CAMS). Empirically, we demonstrate correctness of CAMS us-
023            ing a homing game where the Nash equilibrium exists analytically, and scalabil-
024            ity through the same game with higher-dimensional actions. Codes available in
025            anonymous repo.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029   The strength of game solvers has grown rapidly in the last decade, beating elite-level human players
030   in Chess (Silver et al., 2017a), Go (Silver et al., 2017b), Poker (Brown & Sandholm, 2019; Brown
031   et al., 2020b), Diplomacy (, FAIR), Stratego (Perolat et al., 2022), among others with increasing
032   complexity. These successes motivated recent interests in solving differential games in continuous
033
      time and space, e.g., competitive sports (Wang et al., 2024; Ghimire et al., 2024), where critical
      strategic plays should be executed precisely within the continuous action space and at specific mo-
034
      ments in time (e.g., consider set piece scenarios in soccer). However, existing no-regret solvers, e.g.,
035
      CFR+ (Tammelin, 2014) and its variants (Burch et al., 2014; Moravčı́k et al., 2017; Brown et al.,
036   2020b; Lanctot et al., 2009), and last-iterate online learning algorithms, e.g., variants of follow the
037   regularized leader (FTRL) (McMahan, 2011; Perolat et al., 2021) and of mirror descent (Sokota
038   et al., 2022; Cen et al., 2021; Vieillard et al., 2020), are designed for discrete actions and have com-
039   putational complexities increasing with respect to the size of the action space A. Thus applying
040   these algorithms to differential games would require either insightful action and time abstraction or
041   enormous compute, neither of which are readily available.
042   As a one step towards addressing this challenge, our study focuses on games with one-sided informa-
043   tion, which represent a variety of attack-defence scenarios: Both players have common knowledge
044   about the finite set of I possible payoff types and nature’s distribution over these types p0 . At the
045   beginning of the game, nature draws a type and informs Player 1 (P1) about the type but not P2. As
046   the game progresses, the public belief about the chosen type is updated from p0 based on the action
047   sequence taken by P1 via the Bayes rule. P1’s goal is to maximize the expected payoff over p0 . This
048   game is proved to have a value under Isaacs’ condition (Cardaliaguet, 2009). Due to the zero-sum
049   nature, P1 may need to delay information release or manipulate P2’s belief to take full advantage of
050
      information asymmetry; and P2’s strategy is to optimize the worst-case payoff. Real-world exam-
      ples of the game include man-on-man matchup in sports where the attacker has private information
051
      on which play is to be executed, and missile defense where multiple potential targets are concerned.
052
053   The two differences between our game and commonly studied imperfect-information extensive-form
      games (IIEFGs) (Sandholm, 2010; Perolat et al., 2022; , FAIR) are that: (1) IIEFGs often have belief

      spaces (e.g., belief about opponent’s hands in Poker) larger than their abstracted action spaces (e.g.,
055   betting categories in Poker), and (2) information asymmetry in our games is only one-sided. This
056   paper investigates the potential computational advantages from exploiting these differences via the
057   following insights: (1) At any infostate, P1’s (resp. P2’s) behavioral strategy is I (resp. I + 1)-
058   atomic and convexifies the primal (resp. dual) value with respect to the public belief. With this,
059   we can reformulate the convex-concave minimax problem of size O(|A|) at each infostate into a
060   nonconvex-nonconcave problem of size O(I 2 ). When I 2 ≪ |A|, and in particular when |A| = ∞,
061   the latter of a much smaller scale becomes more efficient to solve in practice. And (2) due to the one-
062   sidedness of information, the equilibrium behavioral strategies of P1 and P2 can be solved separately
063
      through primal and dual formulations of the game. In each formulation, the opponent plays pure best
      responses under Isaacs’ condition. This formulation avoids recurrent learning dynamics between the
064
      pair of strategies without regularization (Perolat et al., 2021).
065
066   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
067         • We show that when the imperfect information of a 2p0s differential game is only about
068           the payoff type and is one-sided, and when Isaacs’ condition (see equation 2) holds, the
069           game can be solved with a computational complexity independent of the size of the action
070           space. To our best knowledge, this is the first time the utility of these properties in jointly
071           addressing the scalability challenge is explained in the context of imperfect-information
072           games.
073         • We propose a continuous-action mixed strategy (CAMS) algorithm that demonstrates supe-
074           rior strategy approximation accuracy and computational cost against SOTA game solvers
075           including CFR+ (Tammelin, 2014), MMD (Sokota et al., 2022), and Deep-CFR (Brown
076
              et al., 2019). CAMS also approximates reasonable strategies for games that are intractable
              for IIEFG solvers.

079   2   R ELATED W ORK
080
      2p0s games with incomplete information. Harsanyi (1967) introduced a Bayesian game frame-
081
      work to solve incomplete-information normal-form games by transforming the game into an
082   imperfect-information one involving a chance mechanism. The seminal work of Aumann et al.
083   (1995) extended this idea to repeated games and established the connection between value convex-
084   ification and belief manipulation. Within the same framework, Blackwell’s approachability the-
085   orem (Blackwell, 1956) naturally becomes the theoretical support for the optimal strategy of the
086   uninformed player (P2). Building on top of Aumann et al. (1995), De Meyer (1996) introduced
087   the concept of a dual game in which the behavioral strategy of the uninformed player becomes
088   Markov. This concept later helped Cardaliaguet (2007); Ghimire et al. (2024) to establish the value
089   existence proof for 2p0s differential games with incomplete information. Unlike repeated games in
090
      which belief manipulation occurs only in the first round of the game, differential games may have
      multiple critical collocation points in the joint space of time, state, and public belief where belief
091
      manipulations are necessary to achieve Nash, depending on the specifications of system dynamics,
092
      payoffs, and state constraints (Ghimire et al., 2024). For this reason, scalable value and strategy
093   approximation for 2p0s differential games with incomplete information has not yet been achieved.
094
095   Imperfect information extensive-form games. IIEFGs represent the more general set of simul-
096   taneous or sequential multi-agent decision-making problems with finite horizons. Since any 2p0s
097   IIEFG with finite action sets has a normal-form formulation, a unique Nash equilibrium always
098   exists in the space of mixed strategies. Significant efforts have been taken to find equilibrium of
099
      large IIEFGs such as poker (Koller & Megiddo, 1992; Billings et al., 2003; Gilpin & Sandholm,
      2006; Gilpin et al., 2007; Sandholm, 2010; Brown & Sandholm, 2019), with a converging set of
100
      algorithms that are no-regret, average- or last-iterate converging, and with sublinear or linear con-
101
      vergence rates (Zinkevich et al., 2007; Abernethy et al., 2011; McMahan, 2011; Tammelin, 2014;
102   Johanson et al., 2012; Lanctot et al., 2009; Brown et al., 2019; 2020a; Perolat et al., 2021; Sokota
103   et al., 2022; Perolat et al., 2022; Schmid et al., 2023) (see summary in Tab. 1). These algorithms
104   all have computational complexities increasing with |A|, provided that the equilibrium behavioral
105   strategy lies in the interior of the simplex ∆(|A|) (see discussion in Appendix C). Critically, this
106   assumption does not hold for differential games equipped with the Isaacs’ condition, in which case
107   the equilibrium strategy is mostly pure along the game tree, and is atomic on A when mixed, as we
      will elaborate in Sec. 3.

510   7   C ONCLUSION
511
512   Unlike IIEFG where mixed strategies have to be approximated over the entire action space across the
513   game tree, we showed that differential games with one-sided information on game type enjoy a much
514   simpler strategy structure when the Isaacs’ condition holds: The strategy of the informed (resp. un-
515   informed) player has at most I (resp. I + 1) pure action branches at each infostate. We demonstrated
516   the clear advantage of using this structural property in solving games with continuous action spaces,
517   against SOTA IIEFG solvers, in terms of computational cost and solution quality. To the authors’
518   best knowledge, this is the first method that enables tractable solve of incomplete-information games
519
      with continuous action spaces without problem-specific abstraction and discretization.

1081
1082   Data Sampling: At each time-step, we first collect training data by solving the optimization prob-
1083   lem (P1 or P2 ). Positions are sampled uniformly from [-1, 1] and velocities from [−v̄t , v̄t ] com-
1084   puted as v̄t = t × umax , where umax is the maximum acceleration. For the unconstrained game,
1085   umax = 12 for both P1 and P2. For the constrained case, uxmax = 6, uymax = 12 for P1 and
1086   uxmax = 6, uymax = 4 for P2. During training, the velocities are normalized between [-1, 1]. The
1087   belief p is then sampled uniformly from [0, 1]. For the dual value, we first determine the upper
1088
       and lower bounds of p̂ by computing the sub-gradient ∂p V (t0 , ·, ·) and then sample uniformly from
       [p̂− , p̂+ ].
1089
1090
       Training: We briefly discuss the training procedure of the value networks. As mentioned in the
1091
       main paper, both the primal and the dual value functions are convex with respect to p and p̂ respec-
1092   tively. As a result, we use Input Convex Neural Networks (ICNN) (Amos et al., 2017) as the neural
1093   network architecture. Starting from T − τ , solutions of the optimization problem P1 for sampled
1094   (X, p) is saved and the convex value network is fit to the saved training data. The model parameters
1095   are saved and are then used in the optimization step at T − 2τ . This is repeated until the value func-
1096   tion at t = 0 is fit. The inputs to the primal value network are the joint states containing position
1097   and velocities of the players X and the belief p.
1098   The process for training the dual value is similar to that of the primal value training. The inputs to
1099   the dual value network are the joint states containing position and velocities of the players X and
1100   the dual variable p̂.

1105
       states x1 , x2 of the players P1 and P2, the constraint is given by the function c(x1 , x2 ) = r −
1106
       ||(px1 , py1 ) − (px2 , py2 )||2 . P1 must always maintain a radial distance of r from P2, else P1 receives
1107   a +∞ penalty and P2 receives a reward of −∞ (both want to minimize their costs).
1108

1111
