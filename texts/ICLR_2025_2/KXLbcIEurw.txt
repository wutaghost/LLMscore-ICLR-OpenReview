009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have increased the demand for personalized and
012            stylish content generation. However, closed-source models like GPT-4 present
013            limitations in optimization opportunities, while the substantial training costs and
014            inflexibility of open-source alternatives, such as Qwen-72B, pose considerable
015            challenges. Conversely, small language models (SLMs) struggle with understand-
016
               ing complex instructions and transferring learned capabilities to new contexts,
               often exhibiting more pronounced limitations. In this paper, we present a novel
017
               collaborative training framework that leverages the strengths of both LLMs and
018
               SLMs for style article generation, surpassing the performance of either model
019            alone. We freeze the LLMs to harness their robust instruction-following capa-
020            bilities and subsequently apply supervised fine-tuning on the SLM using style-
021            specific data. Additionally, we introduce a self-improvement method to enhance
022            style consistency. Our new benchmark, NoteBench, thoroughly evaluates style-
023            aligned generation. Extensive experiments show that our approach achieves state-
024            of-the-art performance, with improvements of 0.78 in ROUGE-L and 0.55 in
025            BLEU-4 scores compared to GPT-4, while also maintaining a low hallucination
026            rate in terms of factual accuracy and faithfulness.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Stylish article generation is an emerging and significant research topic in AI-generated content
031   (AIGC). A critical challenge in this domain is the comprehensive understanding of various writ-
032   ing styles, including tone, vocabulary, and structural nuances. This complexity poses difficulties for
033   models, making it difficult to effectively replicate a specific style while ensuring that the generated
034   content remains coherent and contextually relevant. Previous works primarily focus on stylish arti-
035   cle transfer while lacking attention to content generation. Specifically, studies such as Horvitz et al.
036   (2024b) and Khan et al. (2024) often transform approximately dozens of words of text from one style
037   to another without creating any new content. Recently, large language models (LLMs) pre-trained
038   on vast amounts of data have demonstrated significant potential in various generation tasks, such as
039
      writing (Coenen et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2022), mathematics (Yang et al., 2024;
      Shao et al., 2024), and coding (Team et al., 2024; DeepSeek-AI et al., 2024; Hui et al., 2024). These
040
      models excel at following specific instructions and executing generative tasks, offering promising
041
      solutions for stylish article generation.
042
043   Existing works that employ LLMs in generating stylish articles can be categorized into two ap-
044   proaches: training-free and training-based methods. The training-free approach (e.g., prompt engi-
045
      neering, and in-context learning) typically involves crafting specific prompts or examples to guide
      LLMs in adopting a particular style while generating articles. This method heavily relies on the
046
      world knowledge and comprehension capabilities embedded within the LLMs. Moreover, these
047
      models, such as GPT, LLaMA, and Qwen, are primarily trained on general data, which may limit
048   their effectiveness in stylish generation tasks. Conversely, the training-based approach enhances
049   the LLMs’ stylistic generation capabilities through supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on targeted stylish
050   data. However, this adjustment to a pre-trained LLM often results in catastrophic forgetting (Dou
051   et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024), diminishing the model’s instruction-following capabilities and lead-
052   ing to a loss of world knowledge. Additionally, applying SFT to LLMs presents challenges: for
053   instance, some models are closed-source, and others are too large to fine-tune effectively. Smaller
      language models (SLMs) could serve as a potential alternative, but they typically exhibit inferior ca-

087   We propose a novel style-aligned generation framework that incorporates collaborative training of
088   small language models (SLM) and large language models (LLM). In this framework, we first freeze
089   the LLM and then train the SLM. The frozen LLM, which possesses instruction-following abilities
090   and world knowledge, serves as an interface to process user instructions for the SLM. The training
091   process for the SLM consists of two stages: style supervised fine-tuning (S-SFT) and content direct
092
      preference optimization (C-DPO). In the S-SFT stage, we filter style-consistent data to fine-tune
      the SLM, thereby enhancing its capacity to generate stylish articles. To improve style consistency,
093
      we introduce a self-improved approach to filter out training pairs with low style similarity. In the
094
      C-DPO stage, we curate content preference data to further mitigate the hallucination issues in the
095   SLM. Finally, given a user instruction, the LLM generates a neutral article, and then the SLM
096   injects the article with a specific style, achieving superior performance in both contextual and stylish
097   refinement.
098
      We introduce a new benchmark named NoteBench to evaluate performance. Specifically, we define
099
      a style imitation task in which the content summary and stylish references are treated as inputs,
100
      while the stylish content serves as the output. We employ Rouge (Lin) and BLEU (Papineni et al.)
101   metrics to assess style consistency and use GPT-4 to evaluate the degree of hallucination regarding
102   factual accuracy and faithfulness. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our collaborative training
103   approach for SLMs and LLMs surpasses existing state-of-the-art (SOTA) LLMs when compared to
104   prompt engineering and vanilla SFT on the same SLM. For instance, we achieve improvements of
105   0.78 in ROUGE-L and 0.55 in BLEU-4 compared to GPT-4, and 2.86 in ROUGE-L and 1.92 in
106   BLEU-4 with vanilla SFT. Additionally, our method maintains a low hallucination rate in compari-
107   son to GPT-4 while significantly surpassing vanilla SFT, with a decrease of 29.1%.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
122
      One relevant research topic in stylish article generation is text style transfer, which aims to transfer
123
      an article from a source style into a target style while preserving the original meaning. Unsupervised
124
      style transfer reconstructs texts and style representations from non-parallel examples (Krishna et al.,
125   2020; Riley et al., 2021; Horvitz et al., 2024a). Krishna et al. (2020) first employs paraphrasing
126   to neutralize the style article text and trains the model to re-stylize it. Riley et al. (2021) propose
127   a style transfer approach that enables a single model to generate various styles by conditioning on
128   different target styles. Several studies also leverage LLMs for style transfer through zero-shot or
129   few-shot in-context learning (Patel et al., 2023; Reif et al., 2022). However, they primarily focus
130   on converting pre-existing sentences or passages, overlooking the collaboration between LLMs and
131   SLMs, as well as the challenges posed by information expansion in the creation of new content.
132   Stylish article generation is also prevalent in role-playing, where LLMs are utilized to portray spec-
133   ified characters by imitating their tones and behaviors. RoleLLM (Wang et al., b) first proposes a
134   benchmark for aligning LLMs with specific character language styles, demonstrating that injecting
135   style information into LLMs using auto-annotated data is effective. CharacterLLM (Shao et al.)
136   further advances the concept of personified LLMs in more practical scenarios. By editing char-
137   acter profiles, these models are trained to provide the experiences and knowledge inherent to the
138   characters. DITTO (Lu et al.) proposes a self-alignment method that uses self-generated datasets
139
      to augment its role-playing capabilities. Several works also focus on evaluating performance in
      role-playing (Wang et al., 2024; Jiang et al.). However, they primarily enhance persona-related
140
      performance, often centering on celebrity personas and overlooking the nuanced expressions and
141
      writing styles of real-world users.
142
143   Alignment technology is widely used to adapt the base LLM to better align with human intentions,
144   including human values (Gabriel, 2020), domain knowledge (Zhang et al., 2024), and task-solving
145
      abilities (Sun et al., 2024). Preference datasets are obtained through human feedback (Ouyang et al.,
      2024) or LLM annotations (Wang et al., 2023). Training methodologies, such as Proximal Policy
146
      Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017), Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al.,
147
      2024), and Prospect Theory Optimization (KTO) (Ethayarajh et al., 2024) use human preference data
148   to improve model performance. In this task, we aim to align the models with stylish text generation,
149   specifically by composing content from relatively short summaries while maintaining factuality and
150   faithfulness.

      3.1   TASK D EFINITION
155
156   To emphasize stylish content generation, we streamline the general AI content generation process
157   and define the style imitation task. We simplify the process of topic conception and related informa-
158   tion collection to the summary part, which contains sufficient topics and information for generating
159   an article. Nevertheless, the summary is relatively less stylish and structured, enabling the model
160   to generate a more characterized article through the style reference part. The task emphasizes the
161   content fidelity of the summary and style consistency with the reference text, allowing us to focus
      on generating stylistically consistent text without delving into other complex AI content generation

162
      techniques. Notably, the style reference text is provided by the complete content rather than a col-
163   lection of explicitly defined style types, which requires the model to implicitly mimic the style from
164   the reference text.

      Data Curation We collect articles from open-source forums, including product feature introduc-
169   tions and user experience sharing. These articles span a variety of topics such as fashion, daily
170   life, and travel, originating from users of diverse backgrounds. We cluster the articles by user and
171   sort them by publication date. This ensures that only the content previously posted by each user is
172   referenced, maintaining chronological consistency.

211   3.3   C OLLABORATIVE T RAINING
212
213   We introduce a collaborative training framework that combines a frozen LLM for processing raw
214   user instructions with an SLM for generating stylish articles. This approach allows the SLM to
215   produce results that are both contextually appropriate and stylistically superior, thereby surpassing
      the performance of either model when used individually.

      Simulating Intention with LLM. We adopt an inverse generation approach (Riley et al., 2021)
217   to align user-generated content with user intentions. Specifically, we instruct the SOTA LLM to
218   extract a summary and generate neutral text that simulates user intention. This simulates a scenario
219   where users instruct the LLM to create content while seeking additional stylish refinement. For
220   summary extraction, we instruct the LLM to include all key information from the original content,
221   such as entities, time, and price. For neutral text generation, akin to paraphrase generation (Kr-
222   ishna et al., 2020), we instruct the LLM to retain essential details while minimizing stylish expres-
223   sions—avoiding specialized vocabulary, emojis, and uncommon punctuation. Finally, the extracted
224   summaries, generated neutral text, and original content are organized into a training dataset. The
225
      detailed prompts and examples are provided in Appendix A.
226
227   Imitating Style with SLM. We conduct style supervised fin-tuning (S-SFT) to generate stylish
228   content. Specifically, we incorporate both neutral text and style reference articles as inputs. This
229   combination offers both style and content guidance, facilitating more effective stylish content gen-
230   eration. By using neutral text as an input, we can ensure that essential information is preserved
231
      while allowing the stylish elements to be infused. To further enhance this process, we include the
      original summary to compensate for any potential loss of information that may occur during the
232
      LLM’s processing. This ensures that critical content details are not overlooked, allowing the SLM
233
      to generate outputs that maintain both the richness of the original information and the desired stylish
234   features. Ultimately, this approach promotes a balance between informative content and stylistic
235   refinement, ensuring high-quality results that meet user expectations. We use the cross-entropy loss
236   on the generated tokens, as shown below:

253

258   reward hacking (Pan et al., 2024), we not only verify the hallucinated content but also instruct the

      Evaluation Benchmarks We use NoteBench to evaluate the performance of different methods on
      the style imitation task, which consists of 249 users and 732 articles. To assess style consistency, we
316
      employ ROUGE and BLEU as evaluation metrics. BLEU assesses the precision of the generated text
317
      by focusing on the overlap of n-grams between the generated output and reference texts. In contrast,
318   ROUGE measures recall by evaluating the similarity between the model’s responses and the target
319   answers, identifying the proportion of overlapping words or phrases. To control hallucinations, we
320   prompt GPT-4o to evaluate the hallucination rate of generated content, focusing on two key aspects:
321   Factual Hallucinations: These occur when there is a discrepancy between the generated content
322   and real-world facts, resulting in inaccuracies or fabrications; Faithful Hallucinations: These arise
323   when the generated content diverges from user instructions or the context provided by the input, as
      well as when there is a lack of self-consistency within the content.

      5   C ONCLUSION
475
476   In this paper, we have addressed the challenges of stylish article generation by proposing a novel
477
      collaborative training framework that leverages both large language models (LLMs) and small lan-
      guage models (SLMs). Our approach effectively balances style-following capabilities with con-
478
      tent generation through a two-stage process of style supervised fine-tuning (S-SFT) and content
479
      direct preference optimization (C-DPO). By freezing the LLM to maintain world knowledge and
480   instruction-following capabilities while fine-tuning the SLM, we significantly enhance the stylistic
481   and contextual refinement of generated articles. Our experiments, evaluated on the newly intro-
482   duced NoteBench benchmark, demonstrate that our method surpasses existing state-of-the-art mod-
483   els in both style consistency and hallucination mitigation. These results affirm the efficacy of our
484   collaborative training framework in generating coherent and contextually relevant stylish content.
485   This work paves the way for further advancements in AI-generated content (AIGC), particularly in
      refining models’ stylistic and content generation capabilities.
