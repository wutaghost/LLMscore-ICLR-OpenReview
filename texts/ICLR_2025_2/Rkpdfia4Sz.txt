009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            A central challenge in machine learning is discovering meaningful representations
012            of high-dimensional data, commonly referred to as representation learning. How-
013            ever, many existing methods lack a theoretical foundation, leading to unreliable
014            representations and limited inferential capabilities. In approaches where certain
015            uniqueness of representation is guaranteed, such as nonlinear Independent Com-
016
               ponent Analysis, variables are typically assumed to be continuous. While recent
               work has extended identifiability to binarized observed variables, no principled
017
               method has been developed for scenarios involving discrete latent variables. In
018
               this paper, we show how identifiability can be achieved when both latent and ob-
019            served variables are discrete. We propose general identification conditions that do
020            not depend on specific data distributional assumptions or parametric model forms.
021            We further show how multi-domain information can be leveraged in this context
022            to relax the constraints. The effectiveness of our approach is validated through
023            experiments on both simulated and real-world datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Learning effective representations without supervision has always been critical to the performance
      of downstream deep learning tasks. In recent years, numerous advanced methods for representation
029
      learning have emerged, ranging from earlier models like Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) and Gen-
030
      erative Adversarial Networks (GANs) to more recent innovations such as Diffusion Models Kingma
031   (2013); Goodfellow et al. (2014); Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015); Ho et al. (2020). These well-known
032   unsupervised methods aim at learning an accurate posterior distribution over a lower-dimensional
033   unobserved variable. It is hoped that by aligning observed distributions with the predicted ones, the
034   learned posterior will correspond to the underlying distribution of statistically independent sources
035   of variation. However, few of them have theoretical guarantees in terms of the learned representa-
036   tions, without which the results can be unreliable Arora et al. (2017); Dai & Wipf (2019). Given
037   this limitation, recent developments in the field have focused on establishing the reliability of the
038   learned representations by ensuring that they capture the true explanatory factors behind the ob-
039
      served data—a concept known as identifiability. With identifiability, we can guarantee that in the
      large sample limit, the probabilistic model learns a unique representation corresponding to the true
040
      latent factors. Identifiability is therefore crucial in representation learning, as it provides reliable
041
      interpretability, supports accurate inference, and enhances the usefulness of the learned representa-
042   tions for downstream tasks.
043
044   Among the representation learning methods that guarantee the identifiability of models, most fo-
045
      cus on the linear setting. Well-known methods include Independent Component Analysis (ICA)
      Hyvärinen & Oja (2000), Factor Analysis (FA) Spearman (1904), dictionary coding Olshausen &
046
      Field (1997), and latent class models Goodman (1974). For instance, in ICA, the observed data is
047
      considered a mixture of independent, unobserved components, and the goal is to ”demix” these ob-
048   servations to recover the latent variables, up to some ambiguity (please refer to Appendix A.1.1 for
049   more details). Recent advancements have extended ICA to nonlinear settings, where deep neural net-
050   works are employed to approximate the nonlinear mixing process. Notable examples include iVAE
051   (Identifiable Variational Autoencoders) and Invariant Causal Representation Learning Hyvärinen &
052   Morioka (2019); Khemakhem et al. (2020); Schölkopf et al. (2021). Identifiability in nonparametric
053   settings poses a significant challenge, as nonlinear transformations can obscure the original sources
      of variability. For instance, when variables are continuous, it is well-known that the model be-

      comes severely unidentifiable if the observations are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.)
055   Hyvärinen & Pajunen (1999). To address this issue, additional constraints are typically required to
056   ensure identifiability. One widely adopted approach is to leverage multi-domain information. Here,
057   “multi-domain” refers to distinct scenarios or conditions under which the distributions of variables
058   differ. This means that with the inclusion of domain information, represented by a fully observed
059   random variable u (e.g., temporal or contextual information), we assume that each latent variable
060   is statistically dependent on u. Importantly, the mixing function that maps the latent variables to
061   the observed variables remains fixed across domains. With additional assumptions, such as suffi-
062   cient variability in the domain information, model identifiability can be achieved in these settings.
063
      Khemakhem et al. (2020); Hyvärinen & Morioka (2019; 2016)
064   However, such method is only limited to continuous variables. There have been several recent works
065   that extend ICA to cases where the observed variables are discretized from latent continuous factors,
066   such as in binary ICA Hyvärinen & Hoyer (2001). However, none of these provides any guarantees
067   when both the latent and observed variables are discrete.
068   The inability to handle discrete latent variables poses significant challenges, especially in real-world
069   applications. In practice, many scenarios feature only discrete variables, with latent variables
070   considered to be discrete based on prior knowledge. For example, in disease diagnosis, physicians
071   often make initial judgments about potential diseases based on a patient’s symptoms. Here,
072   “whether the patient has a disease” and “whether she exhibits symptoms” can be viewed as discrete
073   latent and observed variables, respectively, both taking values of True/False. In Figure 1, the patient
074   may have conditions like Gastritis, Asthma, or Anemia, represented as latent binary variables that
075
      are causally connected to symptoms, also modeled as binary variables (X1 , X2 , X3 , X4 ) in the
      figure. The goal is then to properly infer the unknown diseases from the observed symptoms.
076
      Other real-world examples of discrete-to-discrete model structures include topic modeling , where
077
      words in a document are generated from a distribution over discrete topics, with each document
078   represented as a mixture of topics and each topic as a distribution over a finite vocabulary Blei
079   et al. (2003); Goodman (1974); Pritchard et al. (2000); Collins & Lanza (2010). Another example
080   is survey data, where responses to questions (e.g., agree/disagree levels) are discrete, and the
081   underlying latent traits (such as personality or attitudes) are also modeled as discrete variables.
082   This approach is commonly used when researchers believe the population consists of distinct,
083   unobserved subgroups that explain the differences in survey responses, as seen in methods like
084   Latent Class Analysis (LCA) Lazarsfeld & Henry (1968); Collins & Lanza (2010).
085   In this paper, we provide concrete identifiability guarantees for cases where both observed and latent
086   variables are discrete. We derive identifiability results under various settings. First, we demonstrate
087   that identifiability is readily achievable under the nonlinear ICA framework, which assumes an in-
088   vertible mapping between the observed and latent variables. Next, we extend this framework to
089   accommodate arbitrary mappings and establish sufficient conditions for identifiability in these more
090   general settings. In each case, we show how incorporating multi-domain information can signifi-
091   cantly relax the constraints on model parameters. Finally, we empirically validate our theoretical
092
      results using simulated data and demonstrate their practical applicability on multiple real-world
      datasets.

      2    P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
109
110   Different from the continuous setting, where the goal is often to recover the values of latent variables
111   and the functional relations between latent and observed variables—relying on strong structural as-
112   sumptions such as the invertible mixing function and the smoothness of probability density functions
113   (pdfs)—the goal in discrete settings typically focuses on identifying relations at the distributional
114   level, emphasizing the recovery of relevant distributions through discrete probability mass functions
115   (pmfs) rather than the specific values of the latent variables, which can be flexibly assigned. This
116   distinction is well-established in models such as LCA and finite mixture models Lazarsfeld & Henry
117   (1968); McLachlan & Peel (2000).
118   In this paper, the observed variables X are modeled as a mixture of conditional distributions based
119   on the possible values of the latent variables Z. For K independent domains where the random
120   variable u takes values uk , with k ∈ {1, · · · , K}, we assume that the latent variables are dependent
121   on u. Similar to the continuous case, we assume that the marginal distributions P (Z | u) vary across
122   domains, while the generating mechanism P (X | Z) remains fixed across all domains. Our primary
123   goal is to identify both P (X | Z) and P (Z | u).
124   In line with Latent Class Models (LCM) Goodman (1974); McCutcheon (1987); Vermunt & Magid-
125   son (2002), we aim to establish state-level identifiability (Definition 1). Inspired by nonlinear ICA,
126   we adapt the use of domain information to address the identifiability problem in a more general
127   setting. Without loss of generality, we consider both the observed and latent variables to be binary,
128   as categorical variables can be transformed into binary form using appropriate encoding techniques.
129   The specific model settings and necessary assumptions are presented as follows.

225
      In this section, we aim to relax the constraint of having an invertible mapping between X and Z.
226
      While such a constraint is beneficial for ensuring identifiability, as shown in the previous section,
227   it is often impractical in real-world scenarios. In practice, the distributions of X and Z may not
228   align, and their support sizes may differ significantly. To address this, we present identifiability
229   results under the more flexible assumption that the mapping between observed and latent variables
230   can be arbitrary. A formal definition of this flexible mapping is provided in Appendix A.2. We
231   establish sufficient conditions for both local and strict identifiability and demonstrate how multi-
232   domain information can help alleviate constraints on model parameters.

638   Here we provide additional information on nonlinear ICA and latent class models, which are closely

      related to the topic in this paper.

      A.1.1    ICA
642   ICA has been widely explored in the past decades, due to its ability in recovering the latent
643   distributions and their structure connected to observed variables. ICA under linearity assumptions
644   was first studies, which assumes that the latent variables {s1 , ..., sd } are transformed through an
645   unknown mixing matrix A to the observed data {x1 , ..., xn } = As. Linear ICA is able to recover
646   the mixing matrix A and the s up to permutation and scaling, with the assumptions that s are
647   independent and there is no more than one source following Gaussian distributions.



                                                       12
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


648
      Nonlinear ICA generalizes the above results by considering the invertible mapping x = f (s). How-
649   ever, it is shown that there is always a transformation such that z = g(x) has a uniform distribution
650   with independent zi ∈ z. It seems that the latent factors are not identifiable given the nonlinear
651   structure. However, it is then discovered that identifiability can be established if there is a nonsta-
652   tionary temporal structure of the observed data, or the independent components. It is then general-
653   ized into the identifiability given just auxiliary variables that can modulates the latent components.
654   In their setting, the components
                                  Q       are dependent on the auxiliary variable but independent of each
655   other, where p(s | u) = i pi (si | u). The auxiliary variable here can be widely defined, such as
656   the index, class label, or domain knowledge. The theory shows that the f and s are identifiable up
657
      to component-wise invertible transformations under some regularity constraints. Domain informa-
      tion is then widely used in various ICA identification tasks, with which we can separate the static
658
      parts from the changing parts and recover them. However, there are very few ICA research done on
659
      discrete variables. One reason is that, in the discrete case, some classical assumptions underlying
660   ICA can be violated. For example, most ICA assumes smooth probability dense functions and are
661   second-order differentiable. This obviously does not hold in the discrete case. Also, most of the
662   ICA methods aim at recovering the values of latent variables, but for discrete variables we are only
663   interested in recovering the distributions since the values of discrete variables do not make much
664   sense. In conclusion, for discrete variables we must establish different principles for identifiability.
665
666   A.1.2     L ATENT C LASS M ODELS
667

669

670
      assume that the latent variables are categorical. Our argument in this paper borrows ideas from
671   the recent developments in the identifiability of cognitive diagnosis models (CDMs). In CDM, each

      latent classes is denoted as C = 2D and the latent class membership as c ∈ 0, · · · , 2D − 1 .

677   summarized as η = P (Z = c), the conditional probabilities θjxj c = P (Xj = xj | c) for getting
678   response value xj in item j. Without loss of generosity, in this paper we treat the variables as binary
679   (ref here). It also assumes that conditional on the latent class variables, the manifest variables are

760   can be uniquely solved up to permutation and scaling of columns, if the conditions in 4 holds.
761   Provided that the sum of each column in A1 , · · · , AN is 1 in our case, the scales for the matrices are
762
      fixed. Then the state-level identifiability of P (Xi | Z), i ∈ {1, · · · , N } and P (Z | u) are ensured.
763
764
      A.3.5    P ROOF OF T HEOREM 5
765   Proof. Under Assumption 6, we arrange the probabilities P (Z = z | u) in ascending order, where
766   z ∈ {0, 1}D represents the possible states of the latent variables. Assume without loss of generality
767   that P (Zj = 0 | u) ≤ P (Zj = 1 | u). We relabel the latent variables Z1 , Z2 , . . . , ZD such that Zi
768   represents the latent variable with the i-th smallest value of P (Zi = 0 | u) across all variables.
769
      With this ordering, we can express the ratio of the minimum to the maximum probability over the
770   latent space as:
771                                    min P (Z)       P (Z1 = 0 | u)
772                                                 =                 .
                                       max P (Z)       P (ZD = 0 | u)
773
774   By exploiting this ordered structure and the fact that the latent variables are independent, we can
775   systematically identify the marginal distributions P (Zj | u) for all j, up to the state level. This
776   identification is based on the relative ordering of the probabilities and ensures that we can recover
777   the marginal distributions of all independent latent variables.
