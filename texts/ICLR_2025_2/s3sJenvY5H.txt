009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Due to the difficulty of acquiring extensive real-world data, robot simulation has
012            become crucial for parallel training and sim-to-real transfer, highlighting the im-
013            portance of scalable simulated robotic tasks. Foundation models have demon-
014            strated impressive capacities in autonomously generating feasible robotic tasks.
015            However, this new paradigm underscores the challenge of adequately evaluating
016
               these autonomously generated tasks. To address this, we propose a comprehensive
               evaluation framework tailored to generative simulations. Our framework segments
017
               evaluation into three core aspects: quality, diversity, and generalization. For
018
               single-task quality, we evaluate the realism of the generated task and the complete-
019            ness of the generated trajectories using large language models and vision-language
020            models. In terms of diversity, we measure both task and data diversity through
021            text similarity of task descriptions and world model loss trained on collected task
022            trajectories. For task-level generalization, we assess the zero-shot generalization
023            ability on unseen tasks of a policy trained with multiple generated tasks. Exper-
024            iments conducted on three representative task generation pipelines demonstrate
025            that the results from our framework are highly consistent with human evaluations,
026            confirming the feasibility and validity of our approach. The findings reveal that
027
               while metrics of quality and diversity can be achieved through certain methods, no
               single approach excels across all metrics, suggesting a need for greater focus on
028
               balancing these different metrics. Additionally, our analysis further highlights the
029
               common challenge of low generalization capability faced by current works. Our

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Embodied artificial intelligence (EAI) is crucial to enable intelligent agents to understand and inter-
036   act with the physical world. However, creating such agents with physical forms and universal func-
037   tionalities necessitates extensive data, which is prohibitively expensive to acquire manually (Dasari
038   et al., 2020; Srivastava et al., 2021; Mu et al., 2021). Although multiple attempts have been made
039
      toward massive real-world data collection (Brohan et al., 2023b;a), training in simulated environ-
      ments still plays a key role in various robotic tasks (Wang et al., 2023a; Huang et al., 2021; Lin et al.,
040
      2021; Yuan et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2020). Consequently, the acquisition of a substantial number of
041
      robotic tasks in simulation, which heavily rely on foundation models, is of significant importance.
042
043   Foundation models (OpenAI, 2023; Team et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a) have exhibited remark-
044   able proficiency in various robotics-related tasks, including coding (Rozière et al., 2023), 3D gen-
045
      eration(Deitke et al., 2022; 2023), scene comprehension (Mohiuddin et al., 2024), planning (Huang
      et al., 2023b; 2024), and reward formulation (Ma et al., 2023). Notably, recent works have demon-
046
      strated the potential of leveraging such capabilities of foundation models to generate robotic tasks in
047
      simulation (Wang et al., 2023b; 2024; Katara et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Hua et al., 2024). In gen-
048   erative simulation, foundation models such as large language models and vision-language models
049   are prompted to output necessary task information (e.g., code, language descriptions), an appropri-
050   ate scene, and successful trajectories for novel tasks at scale. However, despite these advancements,
051   concerns have been raised regarding aspects such as the quality and reality of the generated tasks
052   and whether the generated data can boost policy performance (Hua et al., 2024). Therefore, there
053   is an urgent need for a comprehensive evaluation framework for generative simulation pipelines,
      which has so far been absent.

085
086   However, evaluating the generated tasks faces challenges similar to those encountered in assessing
087
      images (Salimans et al., 2016; Heusel et al., 2017) and texts (Wang et al., 2018), i.e., it is hard to
      quantify the realism of the generated tasks, thus hindering traditional evaluation mechanisms such
088
      as success rate from reflecting the quality and value of the generated tasks and data. In this paper,
089
      we propose a novel evaluation framework (see Fig 1) tailored to generative simulation pipelines.
090   Our framework is concerned with three key perspectives: (1) the quality of single-task generation,
091   which typically involves the alignment score of generated task scenarios with the real world and the
092   completeness of generated task trajectories; (2) the task and data diversity concerned with generated
093   tasks as well as generated trajectories; (3) the task-level generalization ability of a policy trained on
094   a bunch of generated tasks.
095
      Specifically, in terms of single-task quality, we leverage vision-language models to understand
096   scene/trajectory images and output the scene alignment scores and task completion rates, which
097   have been measured by human subjective judgment or hard-coded functions in previous works. Be-
098   sides thorough single-task evaluation, we also incorporate multi-task evaluation on diversity and
099   generalization, which are not extensively investigated in prior studies. For diversity, we first mea-
100   sure task diversity by examining the text similarity between the language descriptions of generated
101   tasks. We then train a world model with trajectory data collected from these tasks and evaluate
102   trajectory diversity based on the model’s prediction loss. For generalization, we train an imitation
103   learning policy based on a variety of generated tasks and measure its efficacy on unseen tasks to
104
      gauge its task-level generalization capability.
105   In our experiments, we study 3 notable projects, namely, GenSim (Wang et al., 2024), RoboGen
106   (Wang et al., 2023b), and BBSEA (Yang et al., 2024), in the hope of establishing a reference for
107   subsequent research towards this direction. By comparing our evaluations with those from humans,
      we find that our evaluations reach consistent conclusions with the human experts on the vast ma-

      jority of tasks. According to our evaluation results, RoboGen’s tasks exhibit the highest single-task
109   quality while also holding an advantageous position in the textual task diversity of task descriptions.
110   In terms of trajectory diversity, both GenSim and BBSEA have demonstrated superior results. Al-
111   though currently none of the pipelines possess sufficiently excellent generalization capabilities, the
112   tasks from GenSim still show a certain degree of potential in the direction of generalization. These

114   rics, none consistently outperform across all criteria, emphasizing the need for intensified efforts to
115   balance these metrics effectively. Furthermore, our study also emphasizes the prevalent issue of low
116   generalization capability encountered by current methodologies.
117   The main contributions of our work can be summarized as follows:
118
119   • We propose a novel framework to evaluate generative robotic simulation methods, providing re-
120     searchers with tools to assess and improve their future works in this area.
121   • We develop an autonomous pipeline that quantitatively assesses the quality of an individual task
122     using foundation models, which have previously been performed by human efforts.
123   • We introduce metrics for diversity and generalization of generated tasks and data to evaluate the
124     value of multiple generated tasks and the extensive data derived from them.

      2.1   F OUNDATION M ODELS
129
130   In our article, we utilize large language models such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), released by OpenAI,
131   which have had a profound impact on the field of natural language processing. Previous work has
132   applied these large language models to the domain of robotics, specifically in policy learning (Driess
133   et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023c) and motion planning (Huang et al., 2022). Researchers have also
134   explored using language models to generate code and rewards (Huang et al., 2023c; Wang et al.,
135   2023b), aiding solvers in learning policies from tasks. Furthermore, vision-language models and
136   multimodal foundational models have demonstrated remarkable potential (Zhang et al., 2023a; Team
137   et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023). Model GPT-4-vision (Zhang et al., 2023a) have exhibited capabilities
138
      in spatial understanding and basic assessment, making the automatic evaluation of tasks feasible. In
      prior work, vision-language models have been employed in the robotic task generation pipeline to
139
      verify the quality of the generated tasks (Wang et al., 2023b; 2024).
140
141
      2.2   G ENERATIVE ROBOTICS TASKS AND DATASETS IN E MBODIED AI
142
143   In recent research, foundational models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities (OpenAI, 2023;
144   Zhang et al., 2023a; Team et al., 2023), leading to the emergence of autonomously generated robotic
145   tasks in the field of robotics. Typically, such generative models utilize large language models to
146   create a basic framework for generating tasks, which involves submitting the required 3D models
147   through text-to-3D model (Li et al., 2023b) conversion, text-to-image (Mid) and image-to-3D mod-
148   els (Liu et al., 2023) processes, or searching and generating the necessary three-dimensional models
149   in extensive 3D model repositories like Objaverse (Deitke et al., 2022; 2023). These models are
150   then assembled into tasks within simulators, and methods such as reinforcement learning or trajec-
151
      tory optimization are employed to learn the trajectories needed to solve the tasks. Researchers have
      also explored tasks in other directions; for instance, the creators of Robogen have expanded task
152
      types to include soft materials and humanoid robots (Wang et al., 2023b), while the developers of
153
      Gensim have opted to deploy tasks on real robots, completing the generated tasks in the real world
154   (Wang et al., 2024). Therefore, when evaluating the quality of generated tasks, it is also necessary
155   to consider the diverse directions of exploration being pursued by different researchers.
156
157   2.3   B ENCHMARKS ON M ESHES AND L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL
158
159   Recent work has bridged gaps in the evaluation of three-dimensional models and large language
160   models. For instance, T3Bench (He et al., 2023) introduced the use of multiple foundational mod-
161   els to establish an evaluation system for metrics such as the quality and alignment of 3D models.
      The methods used in this work to assess the quality and alignment of 3D models have inspired our

162
      approach to evaluating the alignment of task scenarios in robotic tasks. Additionally, in the eval-
163   uation of large language models (Zhang et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2023a), previous studies have
164   discussed assessing various metrics across multiple scenarios to identify potential issues of hallu-
165   cination and errors within models. These evaluation standards provide a valuable perspective for
166   assessing robotic tasks, aiding in a more appropriate evaluation of such tasks.

168   2.4   E VALUATION OF TASK D IVERSITY
169
170   Learning a range of skills is crucial for building generalist robot agents that can autonomously oper-
171   ate in a complex environment. Therefore, we expect task generation to produce tasks with varying
172   goals, dynamics, and environment setups such that collectively learning these tasks promotes gener-
173   alization, robustness (Tobin et al., 2017), and even unseen skills (Eysenbach et al., 2018). However,
174
      evaluating such diversity of generated tasks remain unclear. RoboGen (Wang et al., 2023b) proposed
      to compare the Self-BLEU score and Embedding Similarity (Zhu et al., 2018) of the descriptions
175
      generated alongside the tasks. While such language-based diversity metrics consider high-level se-
176
      mantic information, they are strongly coupled with the language models used, which are known to
177   be subject to alignment issues. In this work. we propose to evaluate task diversity as the coverage of
178   skill or dynamics space, where high diversity facilitates better transfer or generalization to a held-out
179   set of tasks. Recent model-based skill learning methods (Hafner et al., 2023; Hansen et al., 2024)
180   are capable of learning highly complex dynamics and task information on a wide range of tasks. We
181   leverage them for diversity evaluation.

      3.1   I NTRODUCTION TO G ENERATIVE S IMULATION
186
187   Generative simulation represents a field of studies that utilize foundation models, particularly gener-
188   ative models pre-trained on internet-scale data, to acquire massive tasks and data in robot simulation.
189   In generative simulation, large language models are first prompted to provide the basic framework
190   for a novel task, such as the task description, assets, task code, etc. The task is then loaded into the
191   simulation to construct a scene. We further query foundation models to provide objectives for task
192   solutions, e.g. goals for planning or rewards for RL. Through RL training or motion planning, the
193   pipeline will produce trajectory data for the previously generated task. To summarize, the perfor-
194
      mance of a generative simulation pipeline is fundamentally determined by key aspects such as the
      basic task framework, solution objectives, and the specific implementations of solution generation.
195
196
197   3.2   OVERVIEW
198
      We divide our evaluation work into three parts, as visualized in Fig 2. In the first part (Sec 3.3), we
199
      assess the quality of a single generated task through foundational models, especially large language
200
      models and vision-language models, and statistical methods. In the second part (Sec 3.4), we re-
201   spectively measure the diversity of generated task descriptions and trajectory data with a language
202   model and a world model. In the third part (Sec 3.5), we evaluate the generalization capability of an
203   imitation learning policy distilled from a large number of generated tasks.

214   2023a), to generate textual descriptions of rendered scene images, followed by large language mod-
215   els (LLMs) such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) to assess the consistency between the textual descriptions
      and the task descriptions. The other directly employs multi-modal LLMs like GPT-4V (Zhang et al.,

      3.4      TASK AND DATA D IVERSITY
255
256
      The generated tasks are expected to be diverse so that training on these tasks grants agents a range
257   of skills and the ability to operate in various situations. However, a concrete definition of diversity
258   is hard: in what sense are tasks distinct or similar? In this work, we are concerned with diversity
259   from the following perspectives: (1) task diversity, a high-level diversity as identified by LLMs; and
260   (2) trajectory diversity, a low-level diversity in terms of the dynamics of the collected data.
261
      Text-based task diversity. Since LLMs generate tasks including the task descriptions and possibly
262
      scene configurations and goals, they are supposed to have an internal understanding of diversity at
263   a high level. For example, “stack-blocks-tower” differs from “align-balls” semantically in terms of
264   the action (verb) and the object of interest. Therefore, the similarity between embeddings of task
265   descriptions can be considered as the similarity between tasks. Specifically, following (Zhu et al.,
266   2018), we compute the diversity of a task set with text embeddings {ei }Ni=1 as:

273   Dynamics-based trajectory diversity. Though straightforward, task description diversity itself
274
      does not sufficiently characterize the actual learning experience of tasks, e.g., different interaction
      dynamics will take place when training on different generated tasks. Ideally, a diverse set of tasks
275
      should cover a large space of dynamics to promote the agent’s robustness under different scenarios.
276
      Therefore, we propose to evaluate such diversity through prediction error of dynamics models. Dy-
277   namics prediction error has been associated with novelty and widely adopted to promote exploration
278   (Pathak et al., 2017; Burda et al., 2018). A high dynamics prediction error indicates unfamiliar (and
279   thus novel) dynamics being experienced. We leverage a latent dynamics model pθ (ot+1 |ot , at ) fol-
280   lowing DreamerV3 (Hafner et al., 2024), where ot and at are the observation and action at time
281   step t. The model is trained on trajectories collected from the generated tasks and then evaluated to
282   compute the prediction errors. As will be discussed in Section 4.2, this approach helps us to identify
283   tasks that render notably similar dynamics and are therefore not diverse.

285   3.5     TASK G ENERALIZATION
286
287   Generalization can be an ambiguous yet vital metric for evaluating the capabilities of generalist robot
288
      agents. In this paper, we define generalization as the capability to solve tasks within the same distri-
      bution, specifically whether an agent trained on the generated tasks can address similar scenarios and
289
      objectives albeit with varying initial states and minor low-level variations. To quantitatively exam-
290
      ine this capability, we first train an imitation learning policy with trajectories collected by either the
291   oracle policies or policies learned from the generation pipeline. The trained policy is subsequently
292   evaluated with new task scenarios including varied object instances, appearance, and initial poses.
293   The policy uses the state-of-the-art algorithm called Diffusion Policy (Chi et al., 2023) as the back-
294   bone and takes as input RGB observations, and the proprioceptions. Although BAKU(Haldar et al.,
295   2024) meets our needs, choosing the more widely known diffusion policy method for our evaluation
296   is reasonable. A typical indicator of low generalization is when the trained policy performs well on
297   the training data, confirming correct algorithm implementation, yet struggles to adapt to the varied
298   tasks during evaluation.

      4.3    G ENERALIZATION
502
      We train a Diffusion Policy for each task group using 40 trajectories, identical to the data for dy-
503   namics model training, and assess their performance on the same task group under variations such as
504   scene configurations, object colors, and initial robot states. As indicated in Table 3, GenSim demon-
505   strates reasonable generalization performance, despite the challenge posed by randomizing object
506   colors, which complicates the effectiveness of an RGB-based policy. Conversely, agents trained on
507   RoboGen and BBSEA tasks exhibit poor generalization. For RoboGen, the primary issue is that
508   the training trajectories all begin from the same initial state, and the RL solutions do not generate
509   high-quality data. In the case of BBSEA, the problem often lies in the repetition of similar tasks,
510   which restricts task-level generalization capabilities. Moreover, significant task variations can result
511
      in out-of-distribution challenges that adversely affect agent performance.

513   5     C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
514
515   In this paper, we propose a novel evaluation framework for generative simulation, which includes
516   three fundamental metrics: quality, diversity and generalization. We evaluate three representative
517   generative simulation pipelines based on our proposed method. Results indicate that while various
518   pipelines excel in terms of quality and diversity, there remains significant potential for improvement
519
      in their generalization capabilities. We hope that future work in generative simulation can make
      advancements and improvements in these three areas, especially in terms of generalization.
520
521   Moreover, we identify and outline some common drawbacks and failure cases across current gener-
522   ative simulation pipelines as follows for instructions to encourage further exploration:
523
524
      • Low-quality task descriptions: Although task proposal is not a bottleneck for generative simu-
        lation in general, we still observe some vague and repeated task descriptions that fail to express
525
        the details of the generated tasks. Such ambiguity may cause suboptimal results in the evaluation
526
        of text-based task diversity, as well as harm the performance of a language-conditioned policy.
527
      • Trajectory data with limited diversity: The task solution in some methods only considers lim-
528
        ited task and scene variations, which will affect both the trajectory diversity and task-level gen-
529
        eralization capability. Typical cases include insufficient intra-task randomization, relatively fixed
530     task domain, or identical semantics between different tasks, leading to very similar trajectories.
531     We advocate an appropriate dynamics model trained along with the task generation process to
532     inspect and improve the diversity regarding dynamics coverage in future works.
533
      • Task-specific design data collection and imitation learning: We remark that, on designing the
534     task generation pipeline, generalization could be considered and improved in various ways, e.g.,
535     action space with good abstraction (control by end-effector poses, joint positions, or primitive
536     actions), data augmentation, and unified goal specification. For example, in GenSim, actions are
537     abstracted as high-level waypoints, and each task trajectory contains only a few such high-level
538     actions. This design benefits its generalization evaluation based on imitation learning. We aim to
539     devise a generally applicable protocol, with the diffusion policy not tuned or adopted specifically,
        and advocate efficient domain-specific designs for better generalization performance.

1130   A.3.2      T RAJECTORY C OLLECTION
1131
1132   Trajectories are collected for both dynamics model learning and imitation learning. GenSim imple-
1133   ments an oracle agent for generating demonstrations. The oracle agent’s action specifies the target
       end-effector pose command, which is executed by a low-level Inverse Kinematics controller with


                                                              21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       joint commands. Therefore, we collect transitions of both high-level (target end-effector pose as ac-
1135   tions) and low-level (joint commands as actions) with the observations being RBG image and robot
1136   joint states. We collect for each task 40 trajectories with manually varied random seeds.
1137
1138
       RoboGen decomposes a long-horizon task into multiple stages, each solved by motion planning
       or reinforcement learning. In this work, we are only concerned with the sub-tasks that require
1139
       reinforcement learning. Specifically, we run their pipeline to train a policy for each task and subse-
1140
       quently collect trajectories using that policy. The action space includes joint and gripper commands
1141   and the observation space includes RGB images and robot joint states. We collect for each task 40
1142   trajectories with manually varied random seeds.
1143
1144
       BBSEA generates trajectory demonstration by querying ChatGPT to output parameterized action
       primitives, which are then executed by low-level controllers. Since BBSEA does not have officially
1145
       released tasks, we run the pipeline for generation and collection for each of the 32 scenes, giving
1146
       256 trajectories in total. BBSEA’s proposed pipeline additionally filters success trajectories. But
1147   here we use all trajectories for learning the dynamics model.
1148
1149   A.3.3   DYNAMICS M ODEL T RAINING D ETAILS
1150
1151   We adopt a popular community implementation (Hafner et al., 2024). For all experiments, the model
1152   is trained for 10 epochs with a batch size of 8. The data was chunked into sequences of size 40/40/20
1153   for GenSim/RoboGen/BBSEA. All other hyperparameters are kept as default. Since DreamerV3 is
1154
       designed for reinforcement learning from visual observations, its model architecture is expressive
       and robust to different domains. Data augmentation could be used to improve its robustness to
1155
       aspects such as the variation in color and appearance further to obtain a lower prediction error.
1156
       However, we do not incorporate that in this paper for simplicity.

       A.3.4   I MITATION L EARNING M ODEL T RAINING D ETAILS
1159
1160   For GenSim and RoboGen, the implementation is adapted from the official release of Diffusion
1161   Policy (Chi et al., 2023). We use the configuration provided by the authors of Diffusion Policy for
1162   image-state observation. For all experiments, the policy is trained for 8000 epochs. For BBSEA, we
1163   use the image-language diffusion policy implementation provided by the authors.
