009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have demonstrated significant progress in visual perception
012            tasks due to their ability to capture fine-grained, object-centric features through
013            large-scale vision-language pretraining. While their success in image-based tasks
014            is well-established, extending this capability to the domain of video understand-
015            ing remains a key challenge. In this work, we explore the potential of diffusion
016            models for video understanding by analyzing the feature representations learned
017            by both image- and video-based diffusion models, alongside non-generative, self-
018
               supervised approaches. We propose a unified probing framework to evaluate six
               models across four core video understanding tasks: action recognition, object
019
               discovery, scene understanding, and label propagation. Our findings reveal that
020
               video diffusion models consistently rank among the top performers, particularly
021            excelling at modeling temporal dynamics and scene structure. This observation
022            not only sets them apart from image-based diffusion models but also opens a new
023            direction for advancing video understanding, offering a fresh alternative to tradi-
024            tional discriminative pre-training objectives. Interestingly, we demonstrate that
025            higher generation performance does not always correlate with improved perfor-
026            mance in downstream tasks, highlighting the importance of careful representation
027            selection. Overall, our results suggest that video diffusion models hold substantial
028            promise for video understanding by effectively capturing both spatial and tempo-
029
               ral information, positioning them as strong competitors in this evolving domain.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Beyond generating high-fidelity images, diffusion models have achieved significant breakthroughs
034
      in visual perception. Their success is largely attributed to the large-scale vision-language pretaining,
035
      which allows them to capture detailed, object-centric features. This has positioned them as strong
036   candidates for tasks such as image segmentation (Zhao et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023) and classifica-
037   tion (Li et al., 2023). This raises a natural question: Can diffusion models’ success in images extend
038   to the more complex domain of video understanding?
039
      Video understanding presents unique challenges absent in the image domain, particularly in cap-
040
      turing temporal dynamics and motion patterns. While image diffusion models have demonstrated
041
      success in video-level tasks like mask propagation (Tang et al., 2023), they do not explicitly model
042   time and thus struggle with higher-level video understanding. In contrast, video diffusion mod-
043   els (Blattmann et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023b) are inherently designed to capture spatial-temporal
044   dynamics, making them far better suited for these tasks. As illustrated in Figure 1, where we visual-
045   ize video representations using K-Means clustering and three-channel PCA for several widely used
046   visual foundation models, video diffusion models excel at capturing motion dynamics - a critical
047   capability that sets them apart from their image-based counterparts. Additionally, video diffusion
048   models retain a high-level structured representation of the video input, further enhancing their posi-
049   tion as strong contenders for advanced video understanding tasks.
050   To investigate this advantage in more depth, we propose a unified probing framework to analyze
051   feature representations across a range of visual models. Our study spans six models, including
052   both image- and video-based architectures, as well as non-diffusion (Oquab et al., 2023; Bardes
053   et al., 2024a) and diffusion-based approaches. In the diffusion category, we further evaluate both
      UNet-based (Blattmann et al., 2023a; Rombach et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023b) and diffusion-

089
090
             • Motion and temporal dynamics: Video diffusion models demonstrate exceptional profi-
               ciency in capturing motion patterns and temporal dynamics, a capability that significantly
091
               contributes to their strong performance in video understanding tasks.
092
093          • Comparison with image diffusion models: While image diffusion models learn object-
094            centric and semantic-aware representations, video diffusion models retain a high-level
095
               structured representation while effectively modeling spatio-temporal information. This en-
               ables them to generally outperform their image-based counterparts, particularly in tasks

097
098          • Role of training data: The scale and nature of the training data play a key role in model

100

101
102          • Generation v.s. perception: Interestingly, in diffusion models, a higher capacity for gen-
103            eration does not always correlate with improved performance in visual perception tasks.
104

               no universal metric for selecting a representation exists as of yet.
105
106   Overall, our results suggest that video diffusion models hold substantial promise for video under-
107   standing by effectively capturing both spatial and temporal information, positioning them as strong
      competitors in this evolving domain.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Diffusion models, inspired by principles of heat and anisotropic diffusion, have emerged as a pow-
111
      erful class of generative models for image and video synthesis (Perona & Malik, 1990; Weickert
112
      et al., 1998). Recent advancements have positioned diffusion models as state-of-the-art across un-
113   conditional (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020a;b; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Bond-Taylor et al.,
114   2022) and conditional image synthesis tasks (Nichol et al., 2021; Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia
115   et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022; Ho & Salimans, 2022; Wang et al.,
116   2022; Zhang & Agrawala, 2023). Notably, Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) (Ho
117   et al., 2020) introduced the use of neural networks for modeling the denoising process, optimizing
118   with a weighted variational bound. The Denoising Diffusion Implicit Model (DDIM) (Ho et al.,
119   2020) enhanced this by incorporating a non-Markov sampling strategy to accelerate inference. Sta-
120   ble Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) extended the diffusion-denoising process into the latent space
121
      of a pre-trained autoencoder (Kingma & Welling, 2013), enabling more efficient large-scale model
      training. More recently, Transformer-based models have been introduced to further scale up training,
122
      achieving superior performance (Peebles & Xie, 2023; Esser et al., 2024).
123
124   The extension of diffusion models from image to video generation (Ho et al., 2022b; He et al.,
125   2022; Luo et al., 2023b) represents a significant advancement, encompassing both text-to-video
126   (T2V)(Blattmann et al., 2023b; Qing et al., 2023; Khachatryan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023c;
127
      Jain et al., 2023) and image-to-video (I2V) generation(Guo et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023a; Zhang
      et al., 2023b; Ni et al., 2023).These efforts largely build upon pre-trained image-level diffusion
128
      models, such as Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), by training the additional video backbone
129
      with extra video data (Ho et al., 2022a; Wang et al., 2023b; Guo et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2023a;
130   Girdhar et al., 2023; Blattmann et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2024). Some approaches avoid retraining
131   entirely by utilizing training-free algorithms for video generation from image models (Wu et al.,
132   2023; Singer et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2023) or employing training-free algorithms for direct video
133   generation from image models (Wu et al., 2023; Singer et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2023). Most recently,
134   Sora (Brooks et al., 2024) and its open-sourced couterparts (Zheng et al., 2024; Lab & etc., 2024)
135   demonstrated leading video generation capabilities with the more advanced architecture of diffusion
136   transformer (Peebles & Xie, 2023). Among them, ModelscopeT2V (Wang et al., 2023b), Stable
137   Video Diffusion (SVD) (Blattmann et al., 2023a), and OpenSora (Zhang et al., 2023b) have open-
138
      sourced their large-scale pre-trained model which serves as our backbones for this study.
139   Diffusion models for visual perception. Diffusion models have also demonstrated strong semantic
140   correspondence in their feature spaces (Hertz et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a).
141   This has spurred a line of research that utilizes diffusion models for visual perceptual tasks, through
142   either training diffusion-based models for specific tasks such as segmentation (Xu et al., 2023; Zhao
143   et al., 2023; Ozguroglu et al., 2024), depth estimation (Saxena et al., 2023b;a; Guizilini et al., 2024)
144   or open-world novel view synthesis (Liu et al., 2023). ther work leverages pre-trained frozen diffu-
145   sion models for perceptual learning (Tang et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2023a; Hedlin
146
      et al., 2023; Namekata et al., 2024; Khani et al., 2023), or explores their use in data augmentation for
      discriminative tasks (Trabucco et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2023; Burg et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2021).
147
148   Among them, DIFT (Tang et al., 2023) proposes a general pipeline to extract features from real im-
149   ages with diffusion models, for which we adopt for our evaluation pipeline. Chen et al. (2023b) and
150   Nag et al. (2023) leverage diffusion models for video-related tasks, but they do not leverage a video
151   diffusion model with spatial-temporal reasoning modules. VD-IT (Zhu et al., 2024) designs a novel
152
      architecture with video diffusion models as the backbone for referring object segmentation. Lexi-
      con3D (Man et al., 2024) conducted a comprehensive study of visual foundation models, including
153
      diffusion-based ones, on 3D scene understanding. Unlike previous work, this study addresses the
154
      general video understanding with diffusion models across multiple tasks, each with a distinct focus.

      3.2   V IDEO U NDERSTANDING P ROBING F RAMEWORK
226
227   We show our unified probing framework in Figure 2. We fetch the video representation from the
228   denoising module and apply a lightweight task head afterward for different tasks.
229
230   Diffusion features. We extract video features with diffusion models following DIFT (Tang et al.,
231   2023). The process begins by adding noise at timestep T to the real video latent (Equation 2),
232
      moving it into the zT distribution. This noisy video latent, along with T, is then passed to ϵv θ.
      Instead of using the final output of ϵvθ , which predicts the noise, we extract features from intermediate
233
      layer activations that effectively capture the video’s underlying representations. These intermediate
234
      representations form the diffusion features. For features from image diffusion models, we follow
235   a nearly identical process, except that we handle the videos frame by frame. Additionally, during
236   feature extraction, we introduce a fixed ”null-embedding” as the condition for ϵvθ . For language-
237   based models, this embedding is obtained by passing an empty prompt to the text encoder. For
238   image-based models, we use an all-zero conditional image.
239
240
      Adaptation for downstream tasks. After extracting features from diffusion models, we use a
      lightweight task head (much fewer than 1% of the backbone’s parameters) to interpret these features
241
      and generate annotations for the specified tasks, as demonstrated by the object discovery task in
242
      Figure 2. It is important to note that the diffusion backbone remains frozen during our evaluations
243   unless otherwise specified. In addition, our study also includes non-diffusion feature extractors,
244   where we simply replace the diffusion features with representations from other visual models.

251   Baseline models. We perform our video understanding analysis with six visual foundation mod-
252   els. DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023) is a self-supervised approach that employs a conservative learn-
253
      ing algorithm, leveraging both image-level and patch-level similarity matching to produce robust
      visual features. VJEPA (Bardes et al., 2024b) learns comprehensive video representations by re-
254
      constructing from masked video patch features, enabling it to capture spatiotemporal dynamics.
255
      Stable Diffusion (SD)(Rombach et al., 2022) is a text-to-image diffusion model operating in the la-
256   tent space. Through large-scale vision-language generative pretraining, it learns rich object-centric
257   representations suitable for various perception tasks. Stable Diffusion 3 (SD3)(Esser et al., 2024)
258   utilizes a DiT-based (Peebles & Xie, 2023) architecture to fuse multi-modal embeddings and incor-
259   porates rectified flow for more efficient training, therefore yielding enhanced performance compared
260   with SD. Building upon SD, ModelScopeT2V (Wang et al., 2023b) further adds temporal modeling
261   units to produce promising results in text-to-video generation. Stable Video Diffusion (SVD) is an
262   image-to-video generation model that trains its temporal-spatial modules on a large-scale dataset,
263   excelling at capturing object motion during video generation. Detailed configurations of these fea-

268   global, video-level representations; (2) Object discovery is a self-supervised instance segmentation
269   task that focuses on identifying and tracking dynamic objects in videos. It evaluates the model’s
      ability to extract dense, fine-grained features necessary for distinguishing object-centric details; (3)

458   4.3            A BLATION S TUDY
459
460   Generation v.s. perception. A natural question arises: does a model with superior generation
461
      capacity inherently perform better in visual perception tasks? Directly comparing the generation
      capacity of all diffusion-based models in our evaluation is challenging, as they are designed for
462
      different tasks – some are text-conditioned, others image-conditioned, and they span both image
463
      and video generation. To address this, we conducted an ablation study using different checkpoints
464   of the same model, based on the assumption that later versions exhibit improved generation capacity.
465   We evaluated four versions of SD (v1.4, 1.5, 2.0, and 2.1) and two versions of SVD (v1 and v1.1)
466   across the four tasks, with the results presented in Figure 4. Interestingly, we found that within
467   diffusion models, better generation capacity does not always translate to superior performance in
468   visual perception tasks. Different versions of the same model may excel in different downstream
469   tasks, indicating that no universal metric for selecting a representation exists as of yet.
470
      Noisy steps and block index. We ablate the effects of noise level selection and block index in SVD
471
      for action recognition on HMDB51 and label propagation on DAVIS17, as shown in Table 4. Our
472   results indicate that, compared to the block index, the noise level plays a relatively smaller and more
473   task-specific role. Pilot experiments are recommended to determine the optimal noise level when
474   applying video diffusion models to new tasks. In contrast, the block index has a significant impact
475   on downstream task performance. Features extracted from earlier blocks capture more abstract,
476   high-level information, making them well-suited for classification tasks. Meanwhile, features from
477   later blocks are more detail-oriented, which is advantageous for dense prediction tasks. This finding
478   aligns with insights from image diffusion models, as revealed by Tang et al. (2023).
479
      Inference cost. We report the inference time and memory usage for a single batch of size
480   [6, 256, 256] on the MOVi-E dataset, using an NVIDIA A100 GPU in Table 5. The baseline model,
481   DINOv2, has an inference time of 0.224 seconds and consumes 2.6 GB of GPU memory. Notably,
482   the memory consumption for ModelScope is an outlier, due to the lack of optimization in its public
483   implementation. In general, diffusion-based and video-based models require more computational
484   resources, though these costs remain acceptable. The exception is SD3, which employs a DiT-based
485   architecture. This observation is consistent with our earlier conclusions and highlights the need for
      developing more efficient and effective feature extraction methods for DiT-based models.

522   5      D ISCUSSIONS
523
524
525   Limitation. In this work, we aim to analyze the strengths and limitations of video diffusion models
526   for a variety of video understanding tasks. Our probing framework is less powerful thus the per-
527   formances are still not optimal. More advanced frameworks with video diffusion models can fully
528
      explore their potential for video understanding. Moreover, though the diffusion backbones used in
      this paper are the open-soured ones, their performances are still far from state-of-the-art (Brooks
529
      et al., 2024). With the current rapid development of video diffusion models and the trend of open
530
      source, we believe the performance of leveraging video diffusion models for video understanding
531   tasks can be greatly improved in the future.
532
533   Two feasible future work of this study include: (1) exploring suitable finetuning strategy for video
534
      diffusion models; and (2) designing a more advanced feature extraction pipeline with newly intro-
      duced DiT-based models.
535
536   Conclusion and social impact. This paper showcases the untapped versatility of diffusion models,
537   encouraging the vision community to expand their application beyond generation to video under-
538   standing tasks. By pushing the boundaries of what is possible with video diffusion models, the
539   findings in this paper can further inspire future explorations with video diffusion models in both
      generative and video analysis aspects.

      A.1     BACKBONE M ODEL I MPLEMENTATIONS
867
868   We implement our backbone models including both diffusion-based ones and discriminative ones
869   with public implementations of DIFT1 (Tang et al., 2023), ModelScopeT2V2 (Wang et al., 2023b),
870   Stable Video Diffusion3 (Blattmann et al., 2023a), Stable Diffusion 34 (Esser et al., 2024), DI-
871   NOv25 (Oquab et al., 2023), and VJEPA6 (Bardes et al., 2024b). We will release our code for
872   reproduction upon publication.
873
874   A.2     ACTION R ECOGNITION
875
876   Datasets. We evaluate action recognition with top 1 and top 5 accuracy on two widely used datasets,
877
      UCF101 (Soomro et al., 2012) and HMDB51 (Kuehne et al., 2011). Both datasets are relatively
      small-scaled datasets containing 101 and 51 action categories respectively. UCF101 and HMDB51
878
      contain around 9.5k/3.5k train/val videos and 3.5k/1.5k train/val videos, respectively. We center-
879
      cropped the video frames to 224 × 224 for evaluation and uniformly sampled 16 frames for each
880   video for training. We build all the compared models by applying a dense layer on the averaged
881   representation for all the video tokens to make the final prediction. Following previous works (Tong
882   et al., 2022; Bardes et al., 2024b), we report the averaged results among the 3 test splits for both
883   datasets.
884
885
      Training details. We remove the data augmentation for training. Otherwise, we follow the same
      training procedure as VideoMAE (Tong et al., 2022). We train all the models for 100 epochs for
886
      UCF101 and 50 epochs for HMDB51. We use AdamW for optimization with a maximum learning
887
      rate of 5e-4. We use the same learning rate scheduler as the object discovery task. It takes about 1
888   day to train our model for UCF101 and 6 hours for HMDB51 with NVIDIA A-100 GPUS.
889
890   A.3     O BJECT D ISCOVERY
891
892   Datasets. We evaluate the object discovery task on two widely-used photo-realistic synthetic
893   datasets, MOVi-C and MOVi-E (Greff et al., 2022). Both datasets feature multiple objects exhibit-
894   ing rigid motion. MOVi-C solely focuses on moving objects without camera movement, whereas
895   MOVi-E includes a mix of moving and static objects, complemented by linear camera motion. Both
896   datasets contain 9,750 videos for training and 250 videos for validation. Each video contains 24
897   frames under the resolution of 256 × 256. We use the original resolution as the input but the mask
898   evaluations are conducted under the resolution of 32 × 32, which is consistent with previous object
899
      discovery models (Kipf et al., 2022; Bao et al., 2023; Singh et al., 2022; Zadaianchuk et al., 2023;
      Aydemir et al., 2023). We take video foreground adjust random index (FG. ARI) and video mean
900
      best overlap (mBO) as metrics.
901
902   Baselines. We build our main model and the additional three variants with different pre-trained
903   feature extraction backbones upon the public implementation of MoTok7 (Bao et al., 2023). We
904   replace their Resnet (He et al., 2016) feature extractor backbone with the other pre-trained feature
905   extractors. For VJEPA, we repeat each frame twice to match the shape.
906
      Training details. We build all the baseline models with 15 slots following the setting of
907   VideoSAUR (Zadaianchuk et al., 2023). We train all the baseline models for 500 epochs with a
908   batch size of 48. We use AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) for optimization with a gradient clip
909   with norm 0.1. We apply a Cosine annealing learning rate scheduler with the largest learning rate
910   as 5e-5 and warm-up steps as 3000. It takes about 3 days to train our model with NVIDIA A-100
911   GPUS.

      A.4     S CENE U NDERSTANDING
919
920   Datasets. We conduct scene understanding on CityScape (Cordts et al., 2016) datasets. We select
921   video semantic segmentation and monocular depth estimation as the target task. CityScape dataset
922   contains 5,000 labeled frames with a train, val, and test split. However, the labels for the test set are
923   not of the same quality as the other two. Therefore, we evaluate on the val set, following previous
924   work (Luo et al., 2020). The original resolution of CityScape is 1024 × 2048, we downsample them
925   to 256 × 512 to run the evaluation. We train each model with a video clip of 16 frames.
926
      Training details. We train each model for 100 epochs with a batch size of 24. We use
927
      AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) for optimization with a gradient clip with norm 0.1. We
928   apply a Cosine annealing learning rate scheduler with the largest learning rate as 5e-5 and warm-up
929   steps as 3000. It takes about 1 day to train our model with NVIDIA A-100 GPUS. We did not in-
930   clude any data augmentation in our training. We randomly select a video clip that contain the labeled
931   one during training, while in inference, we start from a fixed frame where the labeled one is in the
932   middle.

      around 40 frames to 110 frames. We evaluate our model on the resolution of 512 × 896 by resizing

940

941
      aspect ratio. We report region-based similarity J and contour-based accuracy F (Perazzi et al.,
