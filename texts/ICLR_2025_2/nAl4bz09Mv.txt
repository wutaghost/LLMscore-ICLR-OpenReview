                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have shown excellent performance on various image generation
012            tasks, but the substantial computational costs and huge memory footprint hinder
013            their low-latency applications in real-world scenarios. Quantization is a promising
014            way to compress and accelerate models. Nevertheless, due to the wide range and
015            time-varying activations in diffusion models, existing methods cannot maintain
016            both accuracy and efficiency simultaneously for low-bit quantization. To tackle
017            this issue, we propose DilateQuant, a novel quantization framework for diffu-
018
               sion models that offers comparable accuracy and high efficiency. Specifically, we
               keenly aware of numerous unsaturated in-channel weights, which can be cleverly
019
               exploited to reduce the range of activations without additional computation cost.
020
               Based on this insight, we propose Weight Dilation (WD) that maximally dilates
021            the unsaturated in-channel weights to a constrained range through a mathemati-
022            cally equivalent scaling. WD costlessly absorbs the activation quantization errors
023            into weight quantization. The range of activations decreases, which makes acti-
024            vations quantization easy. The range of weights remains constant, which makes
025            model easy to converge in training stage. Considering the time-varying activa-
026            tions, we design a Temporal Parallel Quantizer (TPQ), which sets time-step quan-
027            tization parameters and supports parallel quantization for different time steps by
028            utilizing an indexing approach, significantly improving the performance and re-
029
               ducing time cost. To further enhance performance while preserving efficiency, we
               introduce a Block-wise Knowledge Distillation (BKD) to align the quantized mod-
030
               els with the full-precision models at a block level. The simultaneous training of
031
               time-step quantization parameters and weights minimizes the time required, and
032            the shorter backpropagation paths decreases the memory footprint of the quanti-
033            zation process. Extensive experiments demonstrate that DilateQuant significantly
034            outperforms existing methods in terms of accuracy and efficiency.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Recently, diffusion models have shown excellent performance on image generation (Li et al., 2022;
      Zhang et al., 2023b;c), but the substantial computational costs and huge memory footprint hinder
040
      their low-latency applications in real-world scenarios. Numerous methods (Nichol & Dhariwal,
041
      2021; Song et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2022) have been proposed to find shorter sampling trajectories
042   for the thousand iterations of the denoising process, effectively reducing latency. However, complex
043   networks with a large number of parameters used in each denoising step are computational and
044   memory intensive, which slow down inference and consume high memory footprint. For instance,
045   the Stable-Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) with 16GB of running memory still takes over one
046   second to perform one denoising step, even on the high-performance A6000.
047
      Model quantization is one of the most popular compression methods. By quantizing the weights and
048   activations with low-bit integers, we can reduce memory requirements and accelerate computational
049   operations. The effects become more noticeable as the bit-width decreases. For example, employing
050   8-bit models can achieve up to a 4× memory compression and 2.35× speedup compared to 32-bit
051   full-precision models on a T4 GPU (Kim et al., 2022). Adopting 4-bit models can further deliver
052   an additional 2× compression and 1.59× speedup compared to 8-bit models. Thus, quantization
053   is a highly promising way to facilitate the low-latency applications of diffusion models on source-
      constrained hardware.

073   Unfortunately, while previous methods (Xiao et al., 2023c; Li & Gu, 2023; Xiao et al., 2023b; Li
074   et al., 2023b) of quantization have achieved remarkable success in single-time networks, the wide
075   range and time-varying activations caused by the unique temporal network of diffusion models make
076   them fail. Specifically, since the diffusion models infer in pixel space or latent space, the absence
077   of layer normalization results in a wide range of activations, complicating activation quantization.
078   For example, in the same UNet network, the range of activations is almost 2.5× larger than that of
079   the segmentation models (Ronneberger et al., 2015), as shown in Figure 2(a). Equivalent scaling
080
      techniques address the wide range of activations by shifting the quantization difficulty from activa-
      tions to weights. Some methods (Xiao et al., 2023a; Shao et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
081
      2023a) utilizing equivalent scaling have shown success in large language models (LLMs) by tack-
082
      ling outliers in certain channels, but these methods are not appropriate for diffusion models, where
083   outliers exist in all channels, as shown in Figure 2(b). Unconstrained scaling of all outlier chan-
084   nels significantly alters the weight range, making it difficult for model to converge in training stage.
085   In addition, the temporal network induces a highly dynamic distribution of activations that varies
086   across time steps, as shown in Figure 2(c), further diminishing the performance of quantization.
087   Numerous PTQ methods (Li et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2024) have been explored to enhance results
088   based on the properties of diffusion models, none of them break through the 6-bit quantization for
089   activations. And the QAT methods(Esser et al., 2019) retrain the whole model separately for each
090   time step using the original datasets, which is not practical due to the significant time and resources.
091   In this paper, we propose DilateQuant, a novel quantization framework that can achieve QAT-like
092   performance with PTQ-like efficiency. Specifically, we propose a weight-aware equivalent scaling
093   algorithm, called Weight Dilation (WD), which searches for unsaturated in-channel weights and
094   dilates them to the boundary of the quantized range, using the max-min values of the out-channel
095   weights as constraints. WD narrows the range of activations while keeping the weights range un-
096   changed, making activation quantization easier and ensuring model convergence during the training
097
      stage. This approach effectively alleviates the wide range activations. To address the difficulty of
      quantization for time-varying activations, previous methods (He et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024) set
098
      multiple activation quantizers for one layer and trains them individually using different time-step
099
      calibration sets, which is data- and time-inefficient. On the other hand, we design a Temporal Paral-
100   lel Quantizer (TPQ), which sets time-step quantization parameters and supports parallel quantization
101   for different time steps by utilizing an indexing approach, significantly improving performance and
102   training efficiency, as evidenced by a 160× reduction in calibration and a 2× reduction in training
103   time compared to the SoTA method (He et al., 2023) for DDIM on CIFAR-10. To further enhance
104   performance while preserving efficiency, we introduce a Block-wise Knowledge Distillation (BKD)
105   to avoids data- and time-consuming retraining of the whole model, distilling the full-precision model
106   to its quantized counterpart at block level using a data-free approach. Additionally, it further min-
107   imizes the time and memory footprint required by training the time-step quantization parameters
      simultaneously and using the shorter backpropagation paths, respectively.

138   2.1   D IFFUSION MODEL ACCELERATION
139
      While diffusion models have generated high-quality images, the substantial computational costs and
140
      huge memory footprint hinder their low-latency applications in real-world scenarios. To reduce the
141
      inference computation, numerous methods have been proposed to find shorter sampling trajectories,
142   efficiently accelerating the denoising process. For example, (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021) shortens the
143   denoising steps by adjusting variance schedule; (Song et al., 2020) generalizes diffusion process to a
144   non-Markovian process by modifying denoising equations; (Lu et al., 2022) uses high-order solvers
145   to approximate diffusion generation. These methods have achieved significant success, obtaining
146   comparable performance with nearly 10% of the denoising steps. However, they involve expensive
147   retraining and complex computations. Conversely, we focus on the complex networks of diffusion
148   models, accelerating them at each denoising step with a quantization method, which not only reduces
149   the computational cost but also compresses the model size.
150
151   2.2   M ODEL QUANTIZATION
152
153
      Model quantization, which represents the original floating-point parameters with low-bit values,
      compresses model size and accelerates inference. Depending on whether the model’s weights
154
      are fine-tuned or not, it generally falls into two categories: Post-Training Quantization (PTQ)
155
      and Quantization-Aware Training (QAT). PTQ calibrates the quantization parameters with a small
156   dataset and does not require fine-tuning the model’s weights, making it data- and time-efficient. The
157   reconstruction-based PTQ techniques, such as BRECQ (Li et al., 2021), utilize gradient descent
158   algorithms to optimize quantization parameters, which have yielded remarkable results in conven-
159   tional models. Nevertheless, the unique temporal networks of diffusion models cause them to fail.
160   To address the issues, PTQ4DM (Shang et al., 2023) and Q-diffusion (Li et al., 2023a) design a
161   specialized calibration dataset, and EDA-DM (Liu et al., 2024) refines the reconstruction loss. Al-
      though these PTQ methods enhance results based on the properties of diffusion models, none of


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      them break through the 6-bit quantization. On the other hand, QAT retrains the whole model after
163   the quantization operation, maintaining performance at lower bit-width. However, the significant
164   training resources (original dataset, training time, and GPU consumption) make it not practical for
165   diffusion models. For instance, the recent work TDQ (So et al., 2023) requires 200K training iter-
166   ations on a 50K original dataset. To efficiently quantize diffusion models to lower precision, Effi-
167   cientDM (He et al., 2023) fine-tunes all of the model’s weights with an additional LoRA module,
168   while QuEST (Wang et al., 2024) selectively trains some sensitive layers. Unfortunately, although
169   they achieve 4-bit quantization of the diffusion models, both of them are non-standard (please refer
170   to Appendix E for detail). Hence, the standard quantization of low-bit diffusion models with high
171
      accuracy and efficiency is still an open question.

202
      shifts the quantization difficulty from activations to weights. For a linear layer in diffusion model,
                                            o             i           i  o

                    i

207                                            Y = (X/s)(s · W )                                          (5)

304   4.2            T EMPORAL PARALLEL Q UANTIZER

306   Previous methods (He et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024) utilize multiple activation quantizers for a
307   layer to quantize activations at different time steps. However, since each quantizer is independent,
308   these methods optimize each quantizer individually using time-step calibration sets, which is data-
309   and time-inefficient. For example, EfficientDM uses 819.2K samples for a total of 12.8K iterations

      4.3     B LOCK - WISE K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
330
331   QAT significantly alleviates accuracy degradation in low-bit cases, but it has several limitations
332   for diffusion models: (1) QAT typically requires original training data, which can sometimes be
333   challenging or even impossible to obtain due to privacy or copyright concerns; (2) QAT involves
334   end-to-end retraining of the whole complex networks, which is training-unstable and time-intensive.
335   To address these limitations, inspired by the reconstruction method in PTQ (Li et al., 2021), we
336   propose a novel distillation strategy called Block-wise Knowledge Distillation (BKD). Assume the
337   target model for quantization has K blocks (B1 , . . . , BK ), and the input samples of model are x,
338   which is generated by the full-precision model. BKD trains the quantized network block-by-block
339   and aligns it with full-precision network at block level. More specifically, assume that block Bk
340   is going to be quantized, and its quantized version is Bˆk . We update the quantization parameters
341   (∆Tx , zxT , ∆w ) and weights (w) of Bˆk using the mean square loss L:
342

         L∆Tx ,zxT ,∆w ,w = M SE Bk · Bk−1 · Bk−2 · ... · B1 (x) − B̂k · B̂k−1 · B̂k−2 · ... · B̂1 (x)  (17)
343
344   As can be seen, (1) BKD does not rely on original training data; (2) BKD shortens the gradient back-
345   propagation path by aligning blocks, which enhances training stability and decreases the memory
346   footprint of the quantization process. In addition, BKD trains quantization parameters and weights
347   in parallel, which not only further saves training time but adapts the weights to each time step.

      6      C ONCLUSION
476
      In this work, we propose DilateQuant, a novel quantization framework for diffusion models that
477
      offers comparable accuracy and high efficiency. Specifically, we find the unsaturation property of
478
      the in-channel weights and exploit it to alleviate the wide range of activations. By dilating the
479   unsaturated channels to a constrained range, our method costlessly absorbs the activation quantiza-
480   tion errors into weight quantization. Furthermore, we design a flexible quantizer that sets time-step
481   quantization parameters to time-varying activations and supports parallel quantization for training
482   process, significantly improving the performance and reducing time cost. We also introduce a novel
483   knowledge distillation strategy to enhance performance, which aligns the quantized models with
484   the full-precision models at a block level. The simultaneous training of parameters and shorter
485   backpropagation paths minimize the time and memory footprint required. Exhaustive experiments
      demonstrate that DilateQuant significantly outperforms existing methods in low-bit quantization.

630   diffusion models and does not quantize the decoder models. We empoly channel-wise asymmet-
631

632

804   G    D IFFERENT EQUIVALENT SCALING ALGORITHMS FOR DIFFUSION MODELS
805
806   In this section, we start by analyzing the differences between LLMs and diffusion models in terms
807   of the challenges of activation quantization. As shown in Figure 2(b), the activation outliers of the
808   diffusion models are present in all channels, unlike in LLMs where the activation outliers only exist
809   in fixed channels. Additionally, the range of activations for diffusion models is also larger than that
      of the LLMs. Therefore, it is essential to scale the number of channels as much as possible for the


                                                              15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      diffusion models. Some equivalent scaling algorithms are proposed to smooth out the activation
811   outliers in LLMs, and these methods have achieved success. SmoothQuant (Xiao et al., 2023a)
812   scales all channels using a hand-designed scaling factor. AWQ (Lin et al., 2024) only scales a few of
813   channels based on the salient weight. OmniQuant (Shao et al., 2023) proposes a learnable equivalent
814   transformation to optimize the scaling factors in a differentiable manner. DGQ (Zhang et al., 2023a)
815   devises a percentile scaling scheme to select the scaled channels and calculate the scaling factors.
816   OS+ conducts channel-wise shifting and scaling across all channels.
817   Unfortunately, when we applied methods similar to these previous equivalent scaling algorithms
818   to diffusion models, we find that none of them work. Specifically, we employ these five methods
819   for diffusion models as follows: (1) For the method similar to SmoothQuant, we scale all channels
820   before quantization using a smoothing factor α = 0.5; (2) For the method similar to AWQ, we scale
821   1% of channels based on the salient weight, setting smoothing factor the same as SmoothQuant;
822   (3) For the method similar to OmniQuant, we modify the scaling factors to be learnable variants
823   and train them block by block with a learning rate of 1e-5; (4) For the method similar to DGQ, we
824   scale the top 0.5% of quantization-sensitive channels, setting scaling factor based on the clipping
825
      threshold. (5) For OS+, we perform shifting and scaling across all channels, consistent with the
      original work. However, as shown in Table 9, all of these methods result in higher FID and sFID
826
      scores compared to no scaling. The reason for this result is that although the range of activations
827
      decreases, the range of weights also increases significantly, making it more difficult for the model
828   to converge during the training stage. In contrast, the Weight Dilation algorithm we proposed scales
829   the number of channels as much as possible. It searches for unsaturated in-channel weights and
830   dilates them to a constrained range based on the max-min values of the out-channel weights. The
831   algorithm reduces the range of activations while maintaining the weights range unchanged. This
832   effectively makes activation quantization easier and ensures model convergence, reducting the FID
833   and sFID scores to 9.13 and 6.92 in 4-bit quantization, respectively.

850   tion. We start with the principle of quantization to achieve hardware acceleration. A matrix-vector
851   multiplication, y = W x + b, is calculated by a neural network accelerator, which comprises two
852   fundamental components: the processing elements Cn,m and the accumulators An . The calcula-
853   tion operation of accelerator is as follows: firstly, the bias values bn are loaded into accumulators;
854   secondly, the weight values Wn,m and the input values xm are loaded into Cn,m and computed in
855   a single cycle; finally, their results are added in the accumulators. The overall operation is also
856   referred to as Multiply-Accumulate (MAC):

863   the speed of inference. Quantization transforms floating-point parameters into fixed-point parame-
      ters, which not only reduces the amount of data transfer but also the size and energy consumption
