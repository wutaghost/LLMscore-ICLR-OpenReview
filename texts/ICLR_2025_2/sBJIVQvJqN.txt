011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            We propose Word-Frequency-based Image-Text Pair Pruning (WFPP), a novel
014            data pruning method that improves the efficiency of VLMs. Unlike MetaCLIP,
015            our method does not need metadata for pruning, but selects text-image pairs to
016            prune based on the content of the text. Specifically, WFPP prunes text-image
017            pairs containing high-frequency words across the entire training dataset. The
018
               effect of WFPP is to reduce the dominance of frequent words. The result a better
               balanced word-frequency distribution in the dataset, which is known to improve
019
               the training of word embedding models. After pre-training on the pruned subset,
020
               we fine-tuned the model on the entire dataset for one additional epoch to achieve
021            better performance. Our experiments demonstrate that applying WFPP when
022            training a CLIP model improves performance on a wide range of downstream tasks.
023            WFPP also provides the advantage of speeding up pre-training by using fewer
024            samples. Additionally, we analyze the training data before and after pruning to
025            visualize how WFPP changes the balance of word frequencies. We hope our work
026            encourages researchers to consider the distribution of words in the training data
027            when pre-training VLMs, not limited to CLIP.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large-scale pre-trained Vision-Language Models (VLMs) are gaining popularity, because of their
033
      remarkable zero-shot transferability (Radford et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023b; Zhai et al., 2022; Jia et al.,
      2021; Kim et al., 2021). This makes the VLM a foundational model with wide applicability in a
034
      variety of downstream tasks (Rombach et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022). The success of VLMs
035
      rests on two key points: (1) Large-scale image-text pair datasets crawled from the Internet (Sharma
036   et al., 2018; Changpinyo et al., 2021; Thomee et al., 2016; Schuhmann et al., 2022). (2) Large-scale
037   transformers used as image and text encoder (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Vaswani et al., 2017).
038
039
      Despite the importance of the scale of large-scale datasets, previous work has shown that pruning
      the training dataset can lead to improvements. MetaCLIP (Xu et al., 2024) employs a strategy that
040
      leverages metadata associated with text-image pairs in order to create a subset of CLIP training
041
      data. MetaCLIP pruning results in a new dataset that is balanced at the level of metadata categories
042   (called “entries"), such that the number of texts associated with any given category does not exceed a
043   threshold. The improvements of pruning are accompanied by the computational speed-up that results
044   when the size of the training dataset is reduced.
045
      In this paper, we propose that the decision to prune an image-text pair should be based directly
046
      on information about the frequency of words in that pair. Our method, Word-Frequency-based
047
      Image-Text Pair Pruning (WFPP) is inspired by observations of the importance of balancing word
048   frequencies for training word embedding models. Specifically, Mikolov et al. (2013) introduced a
049   technique that subsamples frequent words in text data, in order to speed up the training and enhance
050   the quality of word representations. Like Mikolov et al. (2013), we consider a dataset to be better
051   balanced in terms of word-frequency when the frequencies of frequent words are less dramatically
052   higher than the frequencies of infrequent words. Our idea is also consistent with work that has
053   demonstrated that neural networks tend to learn more from the majority class due to the higher
      number of examples available (Ross & Dollár, 2017; Buda et al., 2018).

079   WFPP uses a simple yet effective text-level score based on word probabilities to prune image-text
080   pairs from the data set in which the text contains frequent words. Word balance could also be
081   improved by removing individual words from the training data, such as proposed by Liang & Larson
082   (2023). However, such an approach does not remove images, which is disadvantageous since the
083   image encoder usually accounts for a large portion of the training time. WFPP in contrast selects
084
      entire image-text pairs for removal. Note that the WFPP manner of removing texts does not impact
      the overall vocabulary richness, measured in vocabulary size, which is important to maintain.
085
086   Figure 1 demonstrates that our method effectively speeds up zero-shot classification tasks by 1.3×
087   while maintaining the performance of CLIP trained on the full training set. Moreover, our findings
088   demonstrate that WFPP outperforms CLIP in a variety of downstream tasks, including zero-shot
089   classification and zero-shot image-text retrieval across multiple datasets.
090   WFPP offers two advantages over the data pruning proposed by MetaCLIP. First, WFPP selects
091   image-text pairs on the basis of an individual score. In contrast, MetaCLIP selects image-text pairs
092   on the basis of the metadata category they are associated with, meaning that the selection process is
093   less specific. Second, WFPP selects image-text pairs directly using the content of the training data
094   and without the need for a list of metadata categories. Often, the data collection process for training
095   sets involves the use of queries, which can be adopted as the metadata categories for the training
096
      examples, as in CLIP. However, it is not a given that the dataset was created in this way. Further, the
      set of categories use to collect one dataset might not be optimal to subsample another dataset, e.g.,
097
      with a different topical distribution. The contributions of this work can be summarized as follows:
098
099
             • Image-text-pair-level pruning: We introduce an image-text pair pruning method based
100
               on word frequency, which substantially reduces the computational requirements while
101            pre-training VLMs without compromising performance.
102
103          • Word balance: Our approach contributes to the evidence on the importance of word balance,
104            the importance of good design decisions for large-scale datasets, rather than just scaling
105            them up.
106
107          • We provide extensive experiments and analyses that CLIP trained on a dataset sampled with
               our approach outperforms CLIP trained on an unsampled dataset.

111   2       R ELATED WORK
112
113   Due to its remarkable zero-shot transferability, the visual-semantic embedding model as a foundational
114   model has received sustained attention from researchers. In this section, we describe the pre-training
115
      methods for vision-language models and discuss related works to accelerate their pre-training.
116   Vision-Language Models: DeViSE (Frome et al., 2013) learns visual-semantic embedding from
117   labeled embeddings which are generated from pre-trained skip-gram on 5.7 million documents (5.4
118   billion words) extracted from wikipedia.org. The semantic knowledge learned from language provides
119   the zero-shot prediction capability of a visual model, which improves performance on unseen data.
120   To enhance visual-semantic embeddings and achieve good zero-shot performance, CLIP and ALIGN
121
      scale the data to 400M to learn the better visual-semantic embedding that achieves remarkable
      zero-shot performance across 27 datasets (Radford et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021). These models are
122
      pre-trained by contrastive learning, which pushes positive image-text pairs closer to each other and
123
      separates negative image-text pairs, aligning the vision and language by acquiring a visual-semantic
124   embedding from the natural language supervision. However, pre-training VLMs on large-scale data
125   are quite expensive, demanding thousands of GPU days (Cherti et al., 2023; Radford et al., 2021).
126
127
      Efficient Language-Image Pre-training for CLIP: Several methods have been proposed to enhance
      the efficiency of CLIP models. DeCLIP Li et al. (2022b) leverages additional self-supervised losses
128
      to improve image and text representations which extends CLIP by incorporating intra-modal self-
129
      supervision, cross-modal multi-view supervision, and nearest-neighbor supervision. FILIP Yao et al.
130   (2022) introduces a cross-modal late interaction module that refines the contrastive objective by using
131   token-wise maximum similarity between visual and textual tokens. UniCLIP Lee et al. (2022) unifies
132   inter-domain (image-text) and intra-domain (image-image, text-text) contrastive losses into a single
133   universal embedding space, capturing comprehensive relationships across and within modalities.
134   Knowledge distillation approaches such as TinyCLIP Wu et al. (2023) and MoPE-CLIP Lin et al.
135   (2024) transfer knowledge from large pre-trained models to smaller ones via cross-modal distillation
136   to reduce the pre-training budget. Additionally, methods like MobileCLIP Vasu et al. (2024) and
137   ALIP Yang et al. (2023) generate synthetic captions using models pre-trained on large datasets
138
      (CoCa Yu et al. (2022) and OFA_base Wang et al. (2022), respectively) to improve CLIP pre-training.
      Although these proposed pre-training strategies and synthetic caption methods are efficient, they may
139
      still exhibit imbalanced word distributions of pre-training data. Applying WFPP to these methods
140
      can further improve training efficiency. Moreover, Fast Language-Image Pre-training (FLIP) (Li
141   et al., 2023b) removes a large portion of image patches to speed up pre-training VLMs. FLIP uses
142   ViT as an image encoder, reducing computation by 2-4× by removing 50%-75% patches of the
143   image while obtaining better accuracy than the unmasked model (Li et al., 2023b). In addition, they
144   randomly masked 50% of the text to pre-train the VLMs, However, this approach does not work
145   well in text encoders, and the performance of zero-shot classification on ImageNet is decreased.
146   Resource-efficient CLIP (RECLIP) (Li et al., 2023a) employs a smaller version of images for the
147   initial pre-training of CLIP and subsequently fine-tunes the models using larger versions of the
148   images. The pre-training RECLIP with an image size of 64 × 64 reduces compute resource usage by
149
      approximately 80% while still outperforming CLIP on image-text retrieval tasks (Li et al., 2023a).
      Subsampling of Frequent Words for Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training (SW-CLIP) (Liang &
150
      Larson, 2023) proposed a frequency-based word subsampling technique to reduce text length by half
151
      for pre-training VLMs, but does not remove image-text pairs.
152
153   Data Puning in VLMs: Several data pruning methods have been developed for Natural Language
154   Processing Sorscher et al. (2022); Marion et al. (2023) and Computer Vision Tan et al. (2024).
155
      However, in this work, we focus on multimedia data pruning. Metadata-Curated Language-Image
      Pre-training (MetaCLIP) (Xu et al., 2024) creates a balanced subset based on the metadata distribution
156
      to pre-train VLMs. To balance the training data, MetaCLIP selects image-text pairs from the data
157
      pool where the text contains a metadata entry. These metadata entries consist of four components:
158   WordNet synsets, Wiki unigram, Wiki bigram, and Wiki titles. MetaCLIP utilizes a rich metadata
159   source with 500k entries covering a wide range of concepts. However, it only seeks a balance at
160   the level of the entries (metadata categories), which could lead to certain words being under or

      In this section, we present WFPP, a method designed to enhance the pre-training of Vision-Language
180   Models (VLMs) by strategically selecting image-text data from the dataset based on word frequency.
181   Following the data pruning process, we can effectively pre-train the VLM using a reduced portion of
182   the dataset without compromising its performance.
183
      Following our proposed principles for building more balanced data, we remove as much text as
184
      possible from the dataset that contains higher-frequency words. The removal probability of a text is
185
      defined by the joint probability of the words in the text. This approach maintains the diversity of the
186   data when filtering the information, as well as maintaining a balance between frequent and infrequent
187   words.

      4.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
240
      Dataset In our experiments, we utilize CC3M and CC12M to pre-train our model which includes
241
      about 3M and 12M image-text pairs (Sharma et al., 2018; Changpinyo et al., 2021). These datasets
242
      were chosen because they collect a large number of different image-text pairs, providing diverse
243   content for pre-training effective VLMs. We employed various methods for subsampling the dataset,
244   including random selection, and frequency-based sampling. This comparative analysis aims to
245   illustrate the advantage of more diverse data over less diverse data in the context of pre-training
246   models. As a result, we have successfully downloaded 2.72 million data items for CC3M, and 9.30
247   million data items for CC12M (Sharma et al., 2018; Changpinyo et al., 2021). In these datasets,
248   each image has an associated text. We also use the COCO (Lin et al., 2014) and Flick30K (Young
249   et al., 2014) to evaluate the zero-shot retrieval performance, and in these datasets, each image has five
250   associated texts to describe the context of the image.
251   Architecture For the image encoder, we used ViT-B-16 (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) to encode the image
252   and the input size of the image is 224. We use a Transformer-based model (Vaswani et al., 2017) as
253   the text encoder and the text length is 32 (Li et al., 2023b). Following CLIP and OpenCLIP (Radford
254   et al., 2021; Cherti et al., 2023), we compute the similarity score based on the cosine similarity
255   between image and text embeddings. The model is pre-trained by InfoNCE loss (Oord et al., 2018),
256   and the similarity scores are scaled by a learnable temperature parameter (Radford et al., 2021).
257   Training and Fine-tuning We first pre-trained the model on the entire dataset, as well as on random
258   and WFPP subsets, for 30 epochs. Then, we fine-tuned the models pre-trained on the subsets using
259   the entire dataset for an additional epoch. This additional epoch of fine-tuning aims to bridge the
260   distribution gap between the pre-training and inference stages and to account for any unknown
261   concepts that may have been present in the initially removed data. The value of t in Eq. 2 is set to
262   10−7 , and the details of pre-training and fine-tuning configuration are shown in Table 2.

315
      from the CC12M dataset allows us to attain comparable performance without needing to fine-tune

520   5     C ONCLUSION AND O UTLOOK
521
522   In this paper, we introduce WFPP, a novel data-pruning method to enhance vision-language pre-
523   training. By pruning image-text pairs based on word frequencies in the corpus, we reduce the size of
524   the training dataset reducing the necessary computation to pre-train the model. We demonstrate that
525
      pruning improves the word-frequency balance and we claim that this is the reason it is possible to
      prune data without impacting performance. In fact, across a wide range of tasks and datasets, our
526
      experiments demonstrate that after data pruning with WFPP, CLIP is actually able to achieve better
527
      performance that CLIP trained on unpruned data. WFPP also outperforms MetaCLIP pruning, which
528   similarly aims to yield a balanced subset over the metadata distribution.
529
530   Moving forward, using WFPP to prune very large-scale datasets such as LAION-400M (Schuhmann
531
      et al., 2021) would be an interesting direction to explore. As shown in Figure 1, the improvement in
      zero-shot classification accuracy on ImageNet-1K increases substantially when 60% instead of 50%
532
      of CC12M data is retained after pruning. When more than 60% is retained, the rate of improvement
533
      falls off. We believe that between 50% to 60%, we are observing the linear scaling law demonstrated
534   in CLIP (Radford et al., 2021; Cherti et al., 2023), but after that CC12M offers insufficient image-text
535   pairs containing low-frequency words in order to maintain this rate of improvement. If we are
536   right, it means that starting with a larger data set like LAION-400M, we could improve zero-shot
537   classification accuracy would already exceed 40% with 280 million. The broader implication is
538   that WFPP would maintain and possibly enhance its ability to improve performance as size of the
539   dataset before pruning is increased. It would also be worthwhile to investigate alternative methods
      for pruning image-text pair data, such as term frequency-inverse document frequency (TF-IDF).

832   A.2   T HRESHOLD S ELECTION FOR WFPP
833
834   Based on the idea that high-frequency and low-frequency words should be treated differently, we
835   select the threshold using Equation 2. We aim to decrease the sampling probability of high-frequency
836   words while retaining low-frequency words. For words with frequency f (wi ) less than t (i.e., very
837   rare words), we set P (wi ) to 1. These very rare words do not affect the probability of the text being
838   discarded. Specifically, for the cc12m dataset, setting t to 1e-6 means words with count less than
839
      206 are assigned P (wi ) = 1; setting t to 1e-7 corresponds to words with a count less than 20 being
      assigned P (wi ) = 1; when t is set to 1e-8, all words are considered by Equation 2 (since no words
840
      have frequency less than t). We also provide zero-shot classification accuracy with three different
841
      thresholds: 1e-6, 1e-7, and 1e-8, As shown in Table 16. When we set the threshold t to 1e-6, the
842   zero-shot classification accuracy is lower than when t is 1e-7 or 1e-8, but it still outperforms the
843   random pruning method. The performances for thresholds 1 × 10−7 and 1 × 10−8 are similar.

845   A.3   SYNTHETIC CAPTIONS
846
847   Some works utilize synthetic image captions to pre-train CLIP Vasu et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2023).
848   While these synthetic captions are of higher quality than web captions, they do not ensure a balanced
849   word distribution. To address this imbalance, WFPP can be applied to the synthetic captions datasets.
850   As shown in Table 17, we generated synthetic captions for the CC3M dataset using BLIP Li et al.
851
      (2022a). We then pruned 50% of the synthetic data using random pruning and WFPP to pre-train
      CLIP. Our results show that WFPP outperforms the random pruning method by 0.4% in zero-shot
852
      classification accuracy on the zero-shot ImageNet-1k classification.
