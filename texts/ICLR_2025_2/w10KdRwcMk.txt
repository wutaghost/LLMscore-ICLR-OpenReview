010                                             Abstract
011
012
              The Information Bottleneck (IB) framework offers a theoretically optimal
013
              approach to data modeling, though it is often intractable. Recent efforts have
014
015           optimized supervised deep neural networks (DNNs) using a variational upper
016           bound on the IB objective, leading to enhanced robustness to adversarial
017           attacks. In these studies, supervision assumes a dual role: sometimes as
018           a presumably constant and observed random variable, and at other times
019           as its variational approximation. This work proposes an extension to the
020
              IB framework, and consequently to the derivation of its variational bound,
021
              that resolves this duality. Applying the resulting bound as an objective for
022
023           supervised DNNs induces significant empirical improvements, and provides
024           an information theoretic motivation for decoder regularization.
025
026
027   1   Introduction
028
029   The Variational Information Bottleneck, VIB, (Alemi et al. 2017) adapts the theoretically
030
      optimal1 , yet mostly intractable, Information Bottleneck, IB, (Tishby et al. 1999) to super-
031
032
      vised DNNs. However, the IB is a method for unsupervised learning, and requires knowledge
033   of the underlying joint distribution p(x, y) (Slonim 2002). This requirement is relaxed in
034   the original VIB derivation, resulting in a duality in the usage of the downstream RV Y ,
035   which is treated both as an observed RV when sampled from the training data, and as a
036   variational approximation when optimized over. This work proposes a new adaptation of
037   the IB and VIB frameworks for supervised tasks, and consequently an information-theoretic
038
      motivation for decoder regularization.
039
040   We begin by laying down what IB is, and how it can be adapted to DNNs. Classic
041   information theory provides rate-distortion (Shannon 1959) for optimal compression of data.
042
      However, rate-distortion regards all information as equal, not taking into account which
043
      information is more relevant to a specified downstream task, without constructing tailored
044
045   distortion functions. The Information Bottleneck (IB) (Tishby et al. 1999) resolves this
046   limitation by defining mutual information (MI) between the learned representation and
047   a designated downstream random variable (RV) as a universal distortion function. Yet,
048   learning representations using the IB method is possible given discrete distributions, and
049   some continuous ones, but not in the general case (Chechik et al. 2003). Moreover, MI is
050
      either difficult or impossible to optimize over when considering deterministic models, such as
051
052      1
           Optimal data modeling with the IB method is established under the assumption that optimizing
053   a precision-complexity trade-off will yield a model that is closer in nature to the real underlying
      process, and that mutual information is a sufficient metric for this purpose (Slonim 2002).

054
      DNNs (Saxe et al. 2018; Amjad & Geiger 2020). Nonetheless, the promise of the IB remains
055
      alluring, and recent efforts utilized VAE (Kingma & Welling 2014) inspired variational
056
057   methods to approximate upper bounds on the IB objective, allowing its utilization as a loss
058   function for DNNs, where the underlying distributions are both continuous and unknown
059   (Alemi et al. 2017; Fischer 2020; Cheng et al. 2020). These approaches learn representations
060   in supervised settings, without knowledge of the underlying distribution p(x, y), utilizing
061   the learned variational conditional p(y|x) to approximate MI. In contrast, non variational
062
      IB methods learn representations in unsupervised settings, where the stochastic process
063
      underlying the observed data is known (Tishby et al. 1999; Chechik et al. 2003; Painsky &
064
065   Tishby 2017). Nonetheless, when deriving the variational IB objectives, previous research
066   considered the learned representation as the only optimized RV, when in practice a variational
067   classifier is also optimized. This work proposes a modification of the IB and variational
068   IB objectives, by setting the downstream RV as a parameterized model in the problem
069   definition. We believe our modification is a better adaptation of the IB for supervised tasks,
070
      and show empirical evidence of improved performance across several challenging tasks over
071
072
      different modalities. Finally, we use our findings to propose a novel information theoretic
073   interpretation of overfitting in supervised DNNs.

078   2     Related work
079
080   2.1   Deterministic Information Bottleneck
081
082   Classic information theory offers rate-distortion (Shannon 1959) to mitigate signal loss during
083
      compression: A source X is compressed to an encoding Z, such that maximal compression is
084
      achieved while keeping the encoding quality above a certain threshold. Encoding quality is
085
086
      measured by a task specific distortion function: d : X × Z 7→ R+ . Rate-distortion suggests a
087   mapping that minimizes the rate of bits to source sample, measured by I(X; Z), that adheres
088   to a chosen allowed expected distortion D ≥ 0. The Information Bottleneck (IB) (Tishby
089   et al. 1999) extends rate-distortion by replacing the tailored distortion functions with MI
090   over a target distribution: Let Y be the target signal for some specific downstream task, such
091
      that the joint distribution p(x, y) is known, and define the distortion function as MI between
092
      Z and Y . The IB is the solution to the optimization problem Z : min I(X; Z) subject to
093                                                                         p(z|x)
094   I(Z; Y ) ≥ D, that can be optimized by minimizing the IB objective LIB = βI(X; Z)−I(Z; Y )
095   over p(z|x). The solution to this objective is a function of the Lagrange multiplier β, and
096   is a theoretical limit for representation quality, given mutual information as an accepted
097
      metric, as elaborated in more detail in Appendix B. The IB is in fact an unsupervised soft
098
099
      clustering problem, where each data point x is assigned a probability z to belong to different
100   clusters, given the joint distribution of the input and target tasks p(x, y) (Slonim 2002).
101   Chechik et al. (2003) showed that computing the IB for continuous distributions is hard
102   in the general case, and provided a method to optimize the IB objective in the case where
103   X, Y are jointly Gaussian and known. Painsky & Tishby (2017) offered a limited linear
104
      approximation of the IB for any distribution by extracting the jointly Gaussian element of
105
      given distributions. Saxe et al. (2018) considered the application of the IB objective as a
106
107
      loss function for DNNs, and concluded that computing mutual information in deterministic
      DNNs is problematic as the entropy term H(Z|X) for a continuous Z is infinite. Amjad &

      Geiger (2020) extended this observation and pointed out that for a discrete Z MI becomes a
109
      piecewise constant function of its parameters, making gradient descent limited and difficult.
110
111   Considering the supervised problem, Geiger & Fischer (2020) suggested to consider the
112
      classification output as an additional random variable, leading to an extended Markov chain
113
      underlying the problem: Y ↔ X ↔ Z ↔ Ỹ . A similar approach has also been suggested by
114
115
      Piran et al. (2020) where a dual IB formulation was suggested that although still considers
116   the minimization of I(X; Z) replaces the constraints to one that takes into account Ỹ . The
117   approach suggested here follows these ideas, but adds the additional objective of reducing
118   overfitting during the classification step.

      2.2   Variational Information Bottleneck
122
123   Alemi et al. (2017) introduced the Variational Information Bottleneck (VIB) - a variational
124   approximation for an upper bound to the IB objective for DNN optimization. Bounds for
125   I(X, Z) and I(Z, Y ) are derived from the non negativity of KL divergence, and are used to
126   form an upper bound for the IB objective. Variational approximations are then used to replace
127   intractable distributions in the upper bound. Using the reparameterization trick (Kingma &
128
      Welling 2014), a discrete empirical estimation of the variational upper bound is used as a
129
      loss function for classifier DNN optimization, resulting in a loss function that is equivalent
130
131   to the β-autoencoder loss (Higgins et al. 2017). VIB was evaluated over image classification
132   tasks, and displayed substantial improvements in robustness to adversarial attacks, while
133   inflicting a slight reduction in test set accuracy, when compared to equivalent deterministic
134   models. The improved robustness is attributed to an improvement in representation quality,
135   and subsequently better generalization. Achille & Soatto (2018) extended VIB with a total
136
      correlation term, designed to increase latent disentanglement. Fischer (2020) proposed an IB
137
      based loss function named Conditional Entropy Bottleneck (CEB), in which the conditional
138
139   mutual information of X and Z given Y is minimized, instead of the unconditional mutual
140   information. The CEB loss, LCEB = minI(X; Z|Y ) − γI(Y ; Z), is designed to minimize all
                                             Z
141   information in Z that is not relevant to the downstream task Y , by conditioning over Y .
142   CEB is equivalent to IB for γ = β − 1 following the chain rule of mutual information (Cover
143
      1999) and the IB Markov chain, as established in Appendix B. However, its variational
144
      approximation, VCEB, differs from VIB in the way the marginal is approximated. Geiger &
145
146   Fischer (2020) showed that VCEB is a tighter variational approximation for IB under certain
147   conditions, but not in the general case. Later work (Fischer & Alemi 2020) evaluated VCEB
148   on the ImageNet-A and ImageNet-C datasets, two flavors of ImageNet (Deng et al. 2009) that
149   assess model performance on challenging edge cases and robustness to common corruptions,
150   respectively. Results showed improved generalization, calibration, and robustness to the
151
      targeted PGD (Madry et al. 2018) attack, in particular when model size was increased.
152
153
154   2.3   Information theoretic regularization
155
156   Label smoothing (Szegedy et al. 2016) and entropy regularization (Pereyra et al. 2017)
157   regularize classifier DNNs by increasing classifier entropy, either by inserting a scaled
158
      conditional entropy term to the objective, or by smoothing the training labels. Applying
159
      either methods improved test accuracy and model calibration on various challenging tasks.
160
161
      Alemi et al. (2018) extended the information plane (Tishby et al. 1999) to VAE (Kingma &
      Welling 2014) settings, measuring distortion as MI between input and reconstructed images,


                                                    3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      and rate as KL divergence between variational representation and marginal. The limits of
163
      representation quality in VAEs are looser than the theoretical IB limits, and heavily depend
164
165   on the chosen variational families of the marginal and decoder distributions. The closer
166   the families are to the true distributions, the tighter the gap to the theoretical IB limit
167   for representation quality. Alemi et al. (2018) also showed that the ELBO loss is prone
168   to produce low quality representations: provided a strong enough decoder, the ELBO KL
169   regularization term might induce completely uninformative representations, that are then
170
      overfitted by the powerful decoder, as elaborated in detail in Appendix B. In the current
171
      study, a conditional entropy term (Pereyra et al. 2017) emerges during the derivation of our
172
173   proposed adaptation of the IB objective, providing a possible remedy to the discrepancies in
174   the ELBO loss, and subsequently VIB and VCEB loss, described in (Alemi et al. 2018).

179   3.1   Problem Definition
180
181   As elaborated in Section 2.1, the IB objective, LIB = I(X; Z) − βI(Z; Y ), is computed over
182   the joint distribution p(x, y, z). When p(x, y) is given, this expression is optimized over the
183   distribution p(z|x, y), as proposed by Tishby et al. (1999):
184
185                                              min I(Z; X)
186                                             p(z|x,y)

187                                             s.t. I(Z; Y ) ≥ D1                                (1)
188
189   However, as mentioned, adapting IB to supervised tasks admits the learned classifier as a new
190   RV to the optimization problem (Geiger & Fischer 2020; Piran et al. 2020). Thus, we consider
191   the extended Markov chain Y ↔ X ↔ Z ↔ Ỹ for supervised IB, distinguishing between
192
      the true unknown RV Y , and the learned classifier Ỹ . We follow this approach, and also
193
      assume that Ỹ and Y share the same support. The IB framework connects the underlying
194
195   joint distribution of the input and objective data, p(x, y), with a learned representation Z.
196   We claim that when applying IB to supervised tasks, one must also consider the connection
197   to the classifier defined by the output RV Ỹ . Thus, we also want to consider the joint
198   distribution over the pair Z, Ỹ during optimization. Following the IB method logic, we seek
199   a Ỹ that will minimize mutual information with Z, whilst keeping below a defined distortion
200
      metric with the true Y . That is, we seek a second bottleneck that minimizes passage of
201
202
      information between Z and Ỹ , so as to limit it to the minimum required to ensure that Ỹ
203   is similar enough to Y , given the transition through both X and Z. Since in this case we
204   are optimizing over the joint conditional distribution p(z, ỹ|x, y), instead of the conditional
205   p(ỹ|z, x, y), this problem is not simply an IB problem over the Markov chain Y ↔ Z ↔ Ỹ .
206   Moreover, contrary to the standard IB, X plays a significant role, controlling the distribution
207   of Z, and the entire chain of four random variables must be taken into consideration. We
208
      thus define a second bottleneck for the true distribution p(x, y) and modeled distribution
209
210
      c(ỹ|z)p(z|x, y). We choose KL divergence as a distortion metric, as we assume Y and Ỹ
211   share the same support. For some positive scalar D2 we have:

345   3.4      Motivation
346
347   Tishby et al. (1999) proposed that representations are optimal if they contain just enough
348   information for a required downstream task, and proposed the information bottleneck as
349   a method to obtain such representations. However, in the supervised case an additional
350
      information processing stage is added, where representations are decoded by a learned
351
352
      decoder2 , in a joint training process. As mentioned in Section 2.3, Alemi et al. (2018)
353   observed that the ELBO loss function (Kingma & Welling 2014) may learn uninformative
354   representations even when strong KL regularization is imposed, since an overpowerful decoder
355   can overfit the learned embeddings. This observation holds for all VIB loss functions (Alemi
356   et al. 2017; Fischer 2020; Cheng et al. 2020), as VIB is equivalent to the ELBO loss, as
357
      shown in (Alemi et al. 2017). Our proposed extension to the IB and VIB frameworks asks
358
      to resolve this conflict. By appending an additional bottleneck between the representation
359
360
      Z, and learned classifier Ỹ , we learn a classifier that holds the minimal information about
361   the representation that is required to meet a designated distortion target over the true
362   downstream RV. Extending the work in (Alemi et al. 2018). We propose to define a decoder
363   Ỹ as overfitting, if a substantial amount of its information about Z lacks relevance about Y .
364   The conditional MI I(Z; Ỹ |Y ) measures the amount of information Z and Ỹ share, that is
365
      uninformative about about Y . Hence, we have that Ỹ overfits Z if:
366

370                                      H(Ỹ |Y ) ≫H(Ỹ |Z)                                                        (16)

375   to the loss function: −λH(Ỹ |Z), inducing an increase in the right hand side of Equation 16.

401   4   Experiments
402
403   We follow the experimental setup proposed by Alemi et al. (2017), extending it to NLP
404   tasks as well. We trained image classification models on the ImageNet 2012 dataset (Deng
405
      et al. 2009), and text classification models on the IMDB sentiment analysis dataset (Maas
406
      et al. 2011). For each dataset, we compared a competitive Vanilla model with VIB models
407
408   trained over 8 different β values ranging from 10−4 to 0.5, a VCEB model trained with
409   ρ values ranging from 1 to 7, and an SVIB model trained with different combinations of
410   β and λ values. All models were trained over a frozen encoder of the vanilla model, to
411   allow faster experimentation. Each model was trained and evaluated 5 times per setting.
412   Models were evaluated over test set accuracy and robustness to various adversarial attacks,
413
      showing consistent performance. For image classification, we employed the untargeted Fast
414
415
      Gradient Sign (FGS) attack (Goodfellow et al. 2015), as well as the targeted CW L2 attack
416   (Carlini & Wagner 2017), (Kaiwen 2018). For text classification, we used the untargeted Deep
417   Word Bug attack (Gao et al. 2018), (Morris et al. 2020) as well as the untargeted PWWS
418   attack (Ren et al. 2019). The empirical results presented in Figure 2 confirms that while
419   VIB, VCEB and SVIB models mostly decrease test set accuracy compared to the vanilla
420   model, they significantly improve robustness to the applied adversarial attacks. SVIB attains
421
      significantly higher test set accuracy over VIB and VCEB, notably outperforming the vanilla
422
423
      model for IMDB, while scoring the highest robustness in all attacks, apart from the CW
424   attack. A comparison of the best VIB, VCEB and SVIB models further substantiates these
425   findings, with statistical significance confirmed by a p-value of less than 0.05 on a Wilcoxon
426   rank sum test. We note that our experiments compare identical models, varying only in
427   objective functions and scaling parameters. This design highlights performance differences
428
      solely due to these factors. Methods like training from scratch to boost overall performance
429
      were omitted to ensure a robust comparison. Elaboration on the experimental setup, detailed
430
431
      results, and further insights from the experiments are available in Appendix C. Code to
      reconstruct the experiments is provided in the supplementary materials of this submission.

      5   Discussion
487
488
489
      The IB is a special case of rate-distortion, and was initially designed to optimize compressed
490   representations. Applying the IB objective for supervised tasks results in optimization of a
491   classifier distribution as well, and requires a reformulation of the initial problem to include
492   both representation and classification. We propose Supervised IB (SIB), an extension to the
493   original IB that considers the classifier distribution as well, and adds an additional bottleneck
494
      to mitigate information flow between representations and classifier. We derive a tractable
495
      variational approximation for SIB, SVIB, and show that it outperforms VIB and VCEB in
496
497
      terms of classification accuracy and robustness to adversarial attacks, over high dimensional
498   tasks of different modalities, with high statistical significance. We use previous information
499   theoretic frameworks for deep learning (Alemi et al. 2018; Pereyra et al. 2017; Szegedy et al.
500   2016) to interpret our findings, and propose a definition for decoder overfitting, and a new
501   motivation for conditional entropy regularization. While other advancements have been
502
      achieved in recent years, (Fischer 2020; Cheng et al. 2020; Achille & Soatto 2018), none
503
      propose a reformulation for IB, as is required in our opinion.
504
505   This study opens many opportunities for further research: Applying SVIB in self-supervised
506   learning, and in particular measuring whether representations learned with SVIB capture
507
      better semantics than representations learned with non IB inspired loss functions, empirical
508
509
      studies with a full covariance matrix SVIB, a GMM model SVIB, adding β and λ annealing
510   to SVIB, and combining SVIB with CEB are left for future work.

      Notation
705
706
      We denote random variables (RVs) with upper cased letters X, Y , and their realizations in
707
708
      lower case x, y. Denote discrete Probability Mass Functions (PMFs) with an upper case P (x)
709   and continuous Probability Density Functions (PDFs) with a lower case p(x). Subscripts are
710   written where the RVs identities are not clear from the context, and hat notation denotes
711   empirical measurements.
712
713   Let X, Y be two observed random variables with a true and unknown joint distribution
714   p(x, y), and true marginals p(x), p(y). We can attempt to approximate these distributions
715   using a model pθ with parameters θ, such that for generative tasks pθ (x) ≈ p(x), and
716   for discriminative tasks pθ (y|x) ≈ p(y|x), using a dataset of N i.i.d observation pairs
717   S = {(x1 , y1 ), ..., (xN , yN )} to fit our model. One can also assume the existence of an
718
      additional unobserved RV Z ∼ p(z) that influences or generates the observed RVs X, Y .
719
720
      Since Z is unobserved, it is absent from the dataset S, and so cannot be modeled directly.
      Denote pθ (x) = pθ (x|z)pθ (z) dz = pθ (x, z) dz the marginal, pθ (z) the prior as it is not
                         R                      R
721
722   conditioned over any other RV, and pθ (z|x) the posterior following Bayes’ rule.

724   Variational approximations
725
726   When modeling an unobserved variable of an unknown distribution, we encounter a problem
      as the marginal pθ (x) = pθ (x, z) dz doesn’t have an analytic solution. This intractability
                                  R

728
      can be overcome by choosing some tractable parametric variational distribution qϕ (z|x) to
729
      approximate the posterior pθ (z|x), such that qϕ (z|x) ≈ pθ (z|x), and estimate pθ (x, z) or
730
731
      pθ (x, z|y) by fitting the dataset S (Kingma & Welling 2019).

733   Learning tasks
734
735   Vapnik (1995) defines supervised learning as follows:
736
737         • A generator of random vectors x ∈ Rd , drawn independently from an unknown
738           probability distribution p(x).
739         • A supervisor who returns a scalar output value y ∈ R, according to an unknown
740
              conditional probability distribution p(y|x). We note that these probabilities can
741
742
              indeed be soft labels, where y is a continuous probability vector, rather the more
743           commonly used hard labels.
744         • A learning machine capable of implementing a predefined set of functions, f (x, θ) :
745
              Rd × Θ 7→ R, where Θ is a set of parameters.
746
747   The problem of supervised learning is that of choosing from the given set of functions, the
748
      one that best approximates the supervisor’s response, based on observation pairs from the
749
      training set S, drawn according to p(x, y) = p(x)p(y|x).

752
      tation of a set of unlabeled data points {x1 , ..., xN }, xi ∈ Rd , which in some sense reveals
753
      their hidden structure. This representation can be used further to achieve a variety of goals,
754
755
      including reasoning, prediction, communication etc. In particular, unsupervised clustering
      partitions the data points into exhaustive and mutually exclusive clusters, where each cluster

      The information plane
817

819

820
821   and can thus be plotted as a function of β and of Z’s cardinality, over a Cartesian system
822   composed of the axes I(X; Z) (rate) and I(Z; Y ) (distortion). We denote the resulting curve
823   the information curve, and its Cartesian system the information plane (Tishby et al. 1999),
824   as illustrated in Figure 3. When β approaches 0 the distortion term is nullified and we learn

826

827
      representation that has the maximal possible information over the downstream task, but
828
829   minimal compression. The region above the information curve is unreachable by any possible
830   representation. The different bifurcation of the information curve, illustrated in Figure 3,
831   correspond to the different possible cardinalities of the compressed representation.

1028
1029   Image classification models were trained on the first 500,000 samples of the ImageNet
1030   2012 dataset (Deng et al. 2009), and text classification over the entire IMDB sentiment
1031   analysis dataset (Maas et al. 2011). For each dataset, a competitive pre-trained model
1032   (Vanilla model) was evaluated and then used to encode embeddings. These embeddings were
1033   then used as a dataset for a new stochastic classifier net with either a VIB or a SVIB loss
1034
       function. Stochastic classifiers consisted of two ReLU activated linear layers of the same
1035
       dimensions as the pre-trained model’s logits (2048 for image and 768 for text classification),
1036
1037   followed by reparameterization and a final softmax activated FC layer. Learning rate was
1038   10−4 and decaying exponentially with a factor of 0.97 every two epochs. Batch sizes were 32
1039   for ImageNet and 16 for IMDB. All models were trained using an Nvidia RTX3080 GPU
1040   with approximately 1-2 days per a single experiment run. Beta values of β = 10−i for
1041   i ∈ {1, 2, 3} were tested, and we used a single forward pass per sample for inference, since
1042
       previous studies indicated that these are the best range and sample rate for VIB (Alemi et al.
1043
1044
       2017; 2018). Each model was trained and evaluated 5 times per β value, with consistent
1045   performance. Statistical significance was demonstrated in all comparisons using the Wilcoxon
1046   rank sum test with all metrics compared attaining a p-value of less than 0.05. Rank sum
1047   was computed as follows: A sorted vector of results was prepared for each compared
1048   metric, where each entry featured the attained result in each of the 5 i.i.d. experiments
1049   per algorithm, and a boolean indicator value for the algorithm type. For example, let r :=
1050
       ((0.94, 1) (0.935, 1) (0.93, 1) (0.93, 1) (0.925, 1) (0.92, 0) (0.915, 0) (0.915, 0) (0.91, 0) (0.89, 0))
1051
1052
       be a sorted vector of (test accuracy, algorithm) tuples, 1 being SVIB, 0 VIB. We compute
1053   the rank-sum as follows:

                           2                          12                          4.78
