011                                                A BSTRACT
012            A fully disentangled variational auto-encoder (VAE) aims to identify disentangled
013            latent components from observations. However, enforcing full independence be-
014
               tween all latent components may be too strict for certain datasets. In some cases,
               multiple factors may be entangled together in a non-separable manner, or a sin-
015
               gle independent semantic meaning could be represented by multiple latent com-
016
               ponents within a higher-dimensional manifold. To address such scenarios with
017            greater flexibility, we propose the Partially Disentangled VAE (PDisVAE), which
018            generalizes the total correlation (TC) term in fully disentangled VAEs to a partial
019            correlation (PC) term. This framework can handle group-wise independence and
020            can naturally reduce to either the standard VAE or the fully disentangled VAE.
021            Validation through three synthetic experiments demonstrates the correctness and
022            practicality of PDisVAE. When applied to real-world datasets, PDisVAE discov-
023            ers valuable information that is difficult to find using fully disentangled VAEs,
024            implying its versatility and effectiveness.
025
026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027   Disentangling independent latent components from observations is a desirable goal in representa-
028   tional learning (Bengio et al., 2013; Alemi et al., 2016; Schmidhuber, 1992; Achille & Soatto, 2017),
029   with numerous applications in fields such as computer vision and image processing (Lake et al.,
030   2017), signal analysis (Hyvärinen & Oja, 2000; Hyvarinen & Morioka, 2017), and neuroscience
031   (Zhou & Wei, 2020; Yang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024; Calhoun et al., 2009). To disentangle
032   latent components in an unsupervised manner, most models employ techniques that combine opti-
033
      mizing a variational auto-encoder (VAE) (Kingma, 2013) with an additional penalty term known as
      total correlation (mutual information) (Kraskov et al., 2004), classified as fully disentangled VAEs
034
      (Higgins et al., 2017; Kim & Mnih, 2018; Chen et al., 2018).
035
036   Variants of fully disentangled VAEs include modifying the extra penalty term of the VAE loss func-
037   tion (Meo et al., 2024; Hsu et al., 2024), using auxiliary information to supervise the latent (Ahuja
038   et al., 2022), or designing particular decoder structure (Bhowal et al.). These related works are sum-
039   marized in Tab. 3 in Appendix. A.5. Regardless of the techniques employed in these works, they all
040   result in the latent components being fully independent of each other.
041
      However, enforcing full independence among all latent components can be an overly strong assump-
042   tion for certain datasets. For instance, consider the location coordinates (x, y) of a set of points in a
043   2D plane. If the points are uniformly distributed within a square [−1, 1]×[−1, 1], the location distri-
044   bution can be expressed as p(x, y) = p(x)p(y), indicating that x and y are independent components.
045   However, if the points are distributed in an irregular shape, such as a butterfly, the (x, y) coordinates
046   become entangled, resulting in p(x, y) ̸= p(x)p(y). In this case, the location information cannot be
047   decomposed into two independent components but must be jointly represented by (x, y) together. If
048   the points also have attributes independent of their location, such as RGB color represented by a 3D
049   vector, we then encounter the group-wise independence, where a rank-2 entangled group (location)
050
      is independent of a rank-3 entangled group (color).
051   To deal with such group-wise independence, we propose the partially disentangled VAE (PDisVAE).
052   • First, it generalizes the total correlation (TC) penalty term in the loss function of fully disentangled
053   VAEs to partial correlation (PC). PC explicitly penalizes group-wise independence while permitting
      within-group entanglement. This unified formulation of PC encompasses both the standard VAE

      and fully disentangled VAEs.
055   • Second, we revisit the batch approximation method used for computing PC and TC. The existing
056   batch approximation method proposed by Chen et al. (2018) for computing TC in fully disentangled
057   VAEs exhibits a high variance in the estimator. Since accurate batch approximation is critical for
058   the success of the method, we derive the optimal importance sampling (IS) batch approximation
059   formula and provide a theoretical proof of its optimality.

118   3.1 P ROBLEM DEFINITION
119   Although several approaches have been introduced in Sec. 2, a common issue among them is they are
120   all trying to find “fully disentangled (independent)” latent space. However, if the true latent variables
121   are partially disentangled by groups, applying a fully disentangled method is hard to successfully
122
      recover the underlying latent structure accurately.
123   We first formally define partial disentanglement (independence). Still, assume latent z ∈ RK , but
124   now the latent dimensions are disentangled by G groups, while each group has its internal within-
125   group rank H, satisfying K = G × H. For simplicity, we also denote the g-th group as zg =
126   (z(g−1)H+1 , . . . , zgH ), so that z = (z1 , . . . , zG ). For simplicity and without loss of generality,
127   we assume all groups have the same group rank H, but this requirement can be easily relaxed to
128
      arbitrary group ranks in different groups. Then, the partially disentangled latent can be formulated

139
      To identify partially independent component groups as defined above, one might consider a straight-
140
      forward approach: using existing methods to impose marginal independence on between-group com-
141
      ponents. For instance, if we have (z1 , z2 ) ⊥ z3 , one might attempt to apply existing algorithms to
142   require z1 ⊥ z3 and z2 ⊥ z3 . However, this is generally NOT correct. Specifically, the former
143   is a sufficient but not necessary condition ( =⇒ ) for the latter. A simple counterexample is the
144   distribution p(z1 , z2 , z3 ), where p(0, 0, 1) = p(0, 1, 0) = p(1, 0, 0) = p(1, 1, 1) = 0.25. It can be
145   verified that (z1 , z2 ) ̸⊥ z3 , while z1 ⊥ z3 and z2 ⊥ z3 . For more detailed explanations regarding
146   marginal independence and group-wise independence, please refer to Appendix A.1. Therefore, we
147   must explicitly enforce (z1 , z2 ) ⊥ z3 .

165   3.3 T HE BEHAVIOR OF PD IS VAE
166   In the previous subsection, we introduced PDisVAE to identify group-wise independence but did
167   not discuss what to expect within the groups discovered by PDisVAE. Here, we will outline three
168   potential relationships that the latent components within a group could exhibit. To illustrate, let us
169
      consider a discovered latent pair (ẑi , ẑj ); the three cases of interest are illustrated in Fig. 1.
      • Case 1: Non-separable dependent. Consider we have the true latent (zi , zj ) from the equations
170
      shown in the right plot of case 1, where both the mean and variance of the Gaussian zj are dependent
171
      on zi . This makes zi and zj highly entangled with each other in one group and it is impossible to
172   further separate them independently by any linear transformation. Then, PDisVAE should identify
173   a group (ẑi , ẑj ) that cannot be further separated independently through any linear transformation.
174   Furthermore, we should be able to align the estimated (ẑi , ẑj ) with the true (zi , zj ) via a linear trans-
175   formation. In this case, the within-group TC cannot become zero under any linear transformation.
176   • Case 2: Rank-deficient. Consider that PDisVAE has identified an estimated group (ẑi , ẑj ) in
177   the left plot of case 2. Although they are dependent, they exhibit a clear linear relationship, which
178   means they can be reduced to a single effective component, zi , while zj serves as a dummy latent
179   component. For example, if we have three latent components such that (z1 , z2 ) ⊥ z3 , and we apply
180
      PDisVAE with K = 4 = G × H = 2 × 2, we would expect to find a dummy component z4 ≈ 0
      in the second group, resulting in (z1 , z2 ) ⊥ (z3 , z4 ≈ 0). To verify the presence of a dummy latent,
181
      one could apply principal component analysis (PCA) to the group and identify a significantly small
182
      principal component, or conduct a normality test to detect Gaussian noise.
183   • Case 3: Independent. In this example, ẑi and ẑj are irreducibly dependent on each other. How-
184   ever, it is possible to further separate them into independent components via a linear transformation,
185   resulting in the right plot that zi and zj become uniform distributions independent of each other.
186   Consequently, ẑi and ẑj identified by PDisVAE should be allocated to two different groups rather
187   than the same group. In this case, the within-group TC can be reduced to zero after a particular lin-
188   ear transformation. This indicates that as long as PDisVAE accurately identifies enough independent
189   groups, the latent components within each group should not be independent of one another.

      4 E XPERIMENTS
224
      Alternative methods for comparison. For evaluating the proposed PDisVAE, we compare it with
225
      the following three baselines:
226
      • Standard VAE (Kingma, 2013): Theoretically, standard VAE does not have disentaglement ability.
227   • ICA: This is the logcosh-priored VAE for doing non-linear generative ICA inspired by Hyvärinen
228   & Oja (2000).
229   • β-TCVAE (Chen et al., 2018): This method penalizes an extra total correlation (TC) term to
230   achieve full disentanglement. It is theoretically equivalent to FactorVAE (Kim & Mnih, 2018).
231   • PDisVAE: Our proposed method penalizes the partial correlation (PC) term to achieve partial
232   disentanglement. This is the only general method that can deal with group-wise independent la-
233   tent. It reduces to the standard VAE when the number of groups G = 1; and reduces to the fully
234   disentangled VAE when G = K, i.e., the number of groups equals the latent dimensionality.
235   We will begin by using two synthetic datasets to understand and rigorously validate the PDisVAE.
236   Then, we will apply these methods to pdsprites, face images (CelebA), and neural data.

529   5          D ISCUSSION
530
531
      In this work, we propose the partially disentangled variational auto-encoder (PDisVAE) which is
      a more flexible method that can deal with group independence (partial disentanglement) in data,
532
      which is often a more realistic assumption than full independence (fully disentanglement) in a lot
533
      of applications. PDisVAE is a generalized method, which naturally reduces to standard VAE and
534   fully disentangled VAE, by setting the number of groups to 1 or equal to the latent dimensionality.
535   PDisVAE allows the existence of dummy latent components in groups if the number of learned latent
536   components is less than the specified group rank. A potential limitation of PDisVAE is its need for
537   an adequate number of groups and components to accurately express the disentangled latent space if
538   the data requires, but we may not have guidance on this information. To address this, we might either
539   try different configurations or develop techniques for group rank auto-reduction during training to
      enhance the performance, which could be a further direction.

       A.5    R ELATED WORKS
973
974    Realizing there are a lot of methods related to latent disentanglement, we provide Tab. 3 with a list
975    to summarize their contributions and differences.
976
977
                                              Table 3: Related works
978
                                                      full disentanglement    partial disentanglement
979
980          By prior (not flexible)                           [1]                      [4]
981          By extra penalty to loss (flexible)             [2][3]                Our PDisVAE
982
             By auxiliary information (supervised)             [7]
             Others                                     [5][6][8][9][10]
983
984
985    • [1] ICA (Hyvärinen & Oja, 2000): Traditional ICA uses a non-Gaussian prior to achieving full
986    disentanglement since independence is non-Gaussian from the statistical perspective. However, the
987    choice of the non-Gaussian prior is critical and might be too rigid, hurting the flexibility of the
988    method.
989
       • [2] FactorVAE (Kim & Mnih, 2018) [3] β-TCVAE (Chen et al., 2018): These two papers start
       from the statistical definition of full independence to add an extra total correlation to achieve full
990
       independence rigorously. The only difference between these two papers is their implementations of
991
       minimizing TC.
992    • [4] ISA-VAE (Stühmer et al., 2020): ISA-VAE realized the commonly existing group-wise inde-
993    pendence (partial disentanglement) in the real-world data. It utilizes a group-wise independent prior
994    called Lp -nested distribution to achieve the partial disentanglement. However, they did not validate
995    their approach on partially disentangled synthetic datasets, but merely evaluated their approach us-
996    ing fully disentangled assumptions for dsprites and CelebA datasets.
997    • [5] β-VAE (Burgess et al., 2018): Directly penalize the KL divergence of the VAE ELBO loss,
998    in which TC (in Eq. (10)) is implicitly penalized. This approach has been proven to be worse than
999    β-VAE and FactorVAE.
1000
       • [6] (Locatello et al., 2019): This research presented common challenges in finding disentangled
       latent through an unsupervised approach, implying supervision with semantic latent labels might be
1001
       necessary under the assumption of full latent disentanglement. This also gives us a hint that full
1002
       disentanglement might be a strong and inappropriate assumption and could result in poor latent in-
1003   terpretation.
1004   • [7] (Ahuja et al., 2022): This paper uses weak supervision from observations generated by sparse
1005   perturbations of the latent variables, which requires auxiliary information to the latent variables.
1006   • [8] (Meo et al., 2024): This paper replace the traditional TC term with a novel TC lower bound to
1007   achieve not only disentanglement but generalized observation diversity.
1008   • [9] (Bhowal et al.): This paper claims that VAE with orthogonal structure could also achieve latent
1009   full disentanglement.
1010   • [10] (Hsu et al., 2024): The full disentanglement is achieved by a technique called latent quantiza-
1011
       tion. The approach is quantizing the latent space into discrete code vectors with a separate learnable
       scalar codebook per dimension. Besides, weight decay is also applied to the model regularization
1012
       for better full disentanglement.

1028   Tab. 4 presents the partial correlation (PC), latent R2 , MSE, and mutual information gap (MIG)
1029   evaluated for the partial disentanglement (group-wise independence) in the estimated latent.
1030
       Since only ISA (Stühmer et al., 2020) and our PDisVAE explicitly require group-wise independence
1031

1032   with ISA, PDisVAE is more flexible since we achieve partial disentanglement by adding a PC penalty
1033   term to the loss function. Via the PC penalty, the latent distribution is estimated through the aggre-
1034   gated posterior q(z) from the learned decoder, rather than a fixed Lp -nested prior in ISA. Therefore,
1035   PDisVAE obtains more accurate and partially disentangled latent when evaluated with the true latent
