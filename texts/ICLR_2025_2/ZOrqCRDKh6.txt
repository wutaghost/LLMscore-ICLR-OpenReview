009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The Tsetlin Machine (TM) architecture has recently demonstrated effectiveness in
012            Machine Learning (ML), particularly within Natural Language Processing (NLP).
013            It has been utilized to construct word embedding using conjunctive propositional
014            clauses, thereby significantly enhancing our understanding and interpretation of
015            machine-derived decisions. The previous approach performed the word embed-
016            ding over a sequence of input words to consolidate the information into a co-
017            hesive and unified representation. However, that approach encounters scalability
018
               challenges as the input size increases. In this study, we introduce a novel approach
               incorporating two-phase training to discover contextual embeddings of input se-
019
               quences. Specifically, this method encapsulates the knowledge for each input
020
               word within the dataset’s vocabulary, subsequently constructing embeddings for
021            a sequence of input words utilizing the extracted knowledge. This technique not
022            only facilitates the design of a scalable model but also preserves interpretability.
023            Our experimental findings revealed that the proposed method yields competitive
024            performance compared to the previous approaches, demonstrating promising re-
025            sults in contrast to human-generated benchmarks. Furthermore, we applied the
026            proposed approach to sentiment analysis on the IMDB dataset, where the TM em-
027            bedding and the TM classifier, along with other interpretable classifiers, offered a
028            transparent end-to-end solution with competitive performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Recent advancements in the development of language processing applications have significantly
      propelled the field of Artificial Intelligence (AI). A pivotal innovation in this domain is the applica-
034
      tion of Large Language Models (LLMs), which primarily utilize embeddings during the early stages
035
      of the model development. During training, dense vectors are extracted for each word in a space
036   that conveys the word’s context and location from the training dataset. These embeddings are then
037   leveraged across various architectures to construct diverse applications.
038
039
      A novel methodology in this context involves enhancing ML through the use of logical propositions
      Granmo (2018). This approach enables the representation of a target word by a set of words crucial
040
      in shaping its meaning. One key distinction of embeddings generated using logical propositions, as
041
      opposed to traditional Deep Learning (DL) methods, is their interpretability. The output, represented
042   by clauses, can be understood and analyzed in line with the original logical framework. In previous
043   work Bhattarai et al. (2024), embeddings were derived for a vector of target words. That method
044   produces a fused output that obscures the individual contributions of each input word, thus forfeiting
045   the interpretability advantage inherent in the original TM algorithm.
046
      An experiment utilizing the One Billion Word dataset Chelba et al. (2013) and the Tsetlin Machine
047
      Auto-Encoder (TM-AE) model Bhattarai et al. (2024) revealed that training the model on 100 input
048   target words required approximately 9 hours and 35 minutes. Notably, the embedding generated
049   during each training session is specific to the particular set of input target words and cannot be
050   reused in scenarios involving the addition, removal, or substitution of any element within the input
051   array. Consequently, the time-intensive training process is bound to the initial input string, limiting
052   its reusability in other applications. This scenario highlights the scalability challenges associated
053   with the TM-AE model Bhattarai et al. (2024) when applied to downstream tasks, underscoring the
      need for further optimization and improvements to enhance its efficiency.

      This research aims to introduce a novel approach for scalable knowledge extraction from any word
055   within the training vocabulary. The objectives and contributions are threefold:
056
057         1. To collect and encapsulate the knowledge for each target word in the dataset’s vocabulary
058            in a manner that facilitates the construction of a scalable model for downstream tasks.
059         2. To build embeddings for a vector of target words using the extracted knowledge while
060            preserving the interpretability properties of the original TM algorithm.
061         3. To apply these embeddings in data augmentation, enabling the evaluation of embedding
062            quality by testing on unseen data and analyzing the effect of variations in the trained dataset,
063            thereby enhancing model classification robustness.

      2     BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
067
      In the realm of AI applications, NLP has witnessed remarkable advancements over the past decade.
068
      Prominent algorithms such as Word2Vec and GloVe have played pivotal roles in this progress Gold-
069   berg & Levy (2014); Pennington et al. (2014). Despite the promising results achieved by these
070   embedding algorithms, there remains a pressing need for further improvements. For instance, Fast-
071   Text focuses on subword information, thereby enhancing its capability to handle rare words and
072   misspellings Bojanowski et al. (2017), while ELMo generates contextualized word embeddings by
073   considering the entire sentence Peters et al. (2018).
074
      A novel approach to representing ML through a logical structure is the TM, which emphasizes trans-
075   parency and the ability to provide justifications for outcomes. TM have seen significant advance-
076   ments through various contributions. Maheshwari et al. (2023) introduced REDRESS, a method for
077   generating compressed models tailored for edge inference using TM. Sharma et al. (2023) enhanced
078   the robustness and pattern recognition capabilities of TM through the Drop Clause technique. Seraj
079   et al. (2022) explored the application of TM to solve contextual bandit problems. Yadav et al. (2021)
080   demonstrated the human-level interpretability of TM in aspect-based sentiment analysis. Abeyrathna
081   et al. (2021) developed a massively parallel and asynchronous TM architecture for efficient scaling.
082   Additionally, Abeyrathna et al. (2023) proposed a method to build concise logical patterns by con-
083
      straining the clause size in TM.
084   In the field of NLP, several models have leveraged TM for various applications. For instance, in
085   the work Yadav et al. (2022), robust and interpretable text classification models were developed
086   by learning logical AND rules with negation. Another study Saha et al. (2023) used TM to dis-
087   cover interpretable rules for tasks such as sentiment analysis, semantic relation categorization, and
088   dialogue-based entity identification. The work Zhang et al. (2023) applied TM in sentiment analysis
089
      and spam review detection for Chinese text, aiming to strike a balance between interpretability and
      accuracy when compared to DL models. Additionally, a TM-AE was designed to identify word em-
090
      beddings Bhattarai et al. (2024) using the Coalesced Tsetlin Machine (CoTM) structure Glimsdal &
091
      Granmo (2021), which incorporates voting on a set of outputs with weighted clauses representing
092   their contributions to the algorithm. That embedding method has demonstrated superior performance
093   compared to DL alternatives.

      3.1   C OALESCED T SETLIN M ACHINE
102
103   The process starts with preparing the input X, which is a binary vector encapsulating the context
104   information to be trained on. It is twice the length of the vocabulary (V ) of the training dataset
105   to accommodate all original features and their negation. These original and negated features are
106   called literals, and the original features are basically the vocabulary in the dataset. The model can
107   be trained using unlabeled document inputs. If the target output is 1, the input X is created from
      features found in documents containing the target word. If the output is 0, the input X is created


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      from features found in documents that do not contain the target word. The features mentioned in
109   the documents are activated in X by setting their value to 1, while the rest of the features are set to
110   0. Negated features are represented by reversed values. It is important to note that the construction
111   of X is independent of the frequency with which a word appears in a given document; its mere
112   presence, even if mentioned only once, is sufficient.
113
      To explain the formulation of the input X, let us look at an example. We assume that the vo-
114
      cabulary contains eight words: word1 , word2 , ..., word8 . Suppose we have two documents:
115   document1 , which contains the words [word3 , word2 , word4 ] and document2 , which contains the
116   words [word6 , word3 ] and we want to form X for the target word word3 . For the first part of X, we
117   select the features corresponding to the words in document1 and document2 and set their values
118   to 1. The rest of the features are set to 0. Thus, we have: Xoriginal = [0, 1, 1, 1, 0, 1, 0, 0], where
119   Xoriginal is a binary vector of length 8. For the second part of X, we take the negation of Xoriginal .
120   This means setting the value of each feature to the opposite of its value in Xoriginal . Thus, we have:
121   Xnegation = [1, 0, 0, 0, 1, 0, 1, 1]. Finally, we concatenate Xoriginal and Xnegation to form X, as
122   X = Concat[Xoriginal , Xnegation ], namely, X = [0, 1, 1, 1, 0, 1, 0, 0, 1, 0, 0, 0, 1, 0, 1, 1]. This X
123
      represents the active literals required to train the TM for the target word3, and it has a length of 16
      literals. The two phases used in this work are distinguished by the approach employed in forming
124
      X, to be explained in the next section.
125
126   The process of evaluating the clauses begins with the input X, where the literals of the input are
127   matched with the propositional logic represented by the conjunctive clauses. These clauses are
128   different forms of description of the target word, composed of literals. The literals memorized in
129
      a specific clause are determined during the training process. More specifically, an election process
      occurs throughout the training period of the model to update the depth of memorizing or forgetting
130
      the literals in memory and thus include or exclude them from the clause. Each literal corresponds to a
131
      Tsetlin automaton (TA), which decides to memorize or forget the literal based on the reward/penalty
132   dynamics that it receives. In more details, each TA is assigned to a specific literal in X and evaluates
133   its state across a range of 2N levels, where level 1 corresponds to the most extensive forgetting and
134   level 2N represents the highest degree of memorization. Fig. 1 illustrates the operation of the TM,
135   highlighting how the TA collaborate to evaluate the input X, thereby forming the clause. The input
136   X consists of V features, resulting in a total of 2V literals. In the figure, both x1 and ¬xV have
137   successfully progressed beyond the N state, thereby contributing to the formation of the clause,
138   represented by the blue area. For more details on the clause formation, see Granmo (2018).

      3.2   T SETLIN M ACHINE AUTOENCODER
190
191
      In the architecture proposed in Bhattarai et al. (2024), the input X is constructed by randomly
192   shuffling a set of target words. The process for preparing the input X is outlined in Algorithm 2 in
193   the appendix. This algorithm involves combining input documents, where the model receives the
194   documents in their vectorized form, with the vocabulary serving as a reference to track the position
195   of each word within the encoding throughout the training process.
196
      The TM-AE processes a sequence of input target words, aggregating them without repetition to
197
      form the basis for training and determining their embeddings. This type of embedding integrates the
198   contextual information of each word in the input with the other words. Given the random mixing
199   and the number of examples r applied in each epoch, each word has an equal probability of being
200   at position j in the input vector W , which has a length of k, P (wi is at position j) = k1 , where P
201   represents the probability, wi denotes the i-th word, j indicates the position within the input vector,
202   and k is the total length of the input vector W .
203
      This homogeneous formation of the input contributes to the merging and mixing of information,
204   making it challenging to track and interpret the output. Despite the transparent nature of the TM
205   structure and its ability to clearly explain the reasoning behind any output, the process of shuffling
206   input target words confuses the model, compromising transparency and interpretability. Another
207   issue with that training method is that the larger the input vector, the longer the training time.
208
      In practical NLP applications, the embedding method in Bhattarai et al. (2024) is not scalable or
209
      reusable because the output cannot be effectively leveraged. For example, in LLMs such as GPT-
210   3, one of the initial steps after receiving the input vector is to generate embeddings for each word.
211   These embeddings include context information extracted from documents that the model was trained
212   on, and are stored as dense vector representations in a high-dimensional space. Unlike GPT-3,
213   which retains these embeddings for efficient reuse across tasks, the TM-AE model Bhattarai et al.
214   (2024) generates embeddings specific to the input target words and can not be used for different
215   input combinations. In this work, the aforementioned problems were identified, and a TM-AE was
      employed in two phases, each with a distinct structure to construct the input X and then trained

239   3.3   P HASE 1
240
241   The first phase involved the original TM-AE architecture, with the key difference being that the
242   model was trained with the target words individually, meaning each training instance involved a
243   single word. This approach offered several advantages:
244
245         1. Extracting the knowledge in a pure form for the target word, thereby maintaining trans-
246            parency in interpreting any training outputs.
247
248         2. Enabling the storage of training results for use in other applications, similar to models like
249            Word2Vec. In this research, the stored results were used as a dataset for the second phase.
250
251
            3. Facilitating the application of real-world scenarios that can be updated in the future. If it
252            becomes necessary to update the context information for a word in the vocabulary, it is
253            straightforward to retrain only that specific word.
254
255
      The training results from this phase consist of conjunctive clauses that describe the target word
256   using specific literals in propositional logic. For example, training on the word “car” resulted in
257   1600 clauses, such as “driver AND road.” The process of forming the input X in this manner is
258   characterized by the randomness inherent in selecting documents that either support the target word
259   (resulting in a training outcome of 1) or do not support it (resulting in a training outcome of 0). This
260   randomness facilitates the extraction of different forms of contextual information for the target word
261   from both supporting and non-supporting documents without the interference of other words, as was
262   the case in the previous work.
263   To formalize this, for each word wordk , the number of documents selected is the minimum between
264   a window size a and |Dwordk |, i.e., the actual number of available documents. Therefore, the number
265   of documents picked for wordk is given by min(a, |Dwordk |). The total number of words extracted
266   from the picked documents depends on the number of documents selected and the words contained
267   in each document. Let di represent a document, Words(di ) be the number of words in document
268   di , and Dpicked be the set ofPdocuments selected for wordk . The total number of words extracted
269   for wordk is calculated as di ∈Dpicked Words(di ), which sums up the number of words from each
      document in the selected set to build X.

305   3.4   P HASE 2
306
307   The purpose of training in Phase 2 is to find embedding for a set of input target words. In the second
308
      phase, we utilize the knowledge and vocabulary index obtained from the first phase to generate the
      input X and then do the training. Algorithm 1 describes the construction method. The knowledge
309
      for each target word consists of a set of clauses C. These clauses are divided into positive weight
310
      clauses that vote for the target word and negative weight clauses that vote against it.
311
312   Suppose we have an input vector W ords = [word1 , word2 , word3 ]. In each epoch, and with each
313   example of r, the target words in W ords are randomly arranged to obtain a high level of homo-
314
      geneity in mixing the context information. For example, if the first example has the order W ords =
      [word1 , word2 , word3 ], the second example can have the order W ords = [word2 , word3 , word1 ].
315
      This ensures that each target word has an equal probability of appearing in any position in the vector.
316
317   For each word in the input vector, the knowledge associated with that word is retrieved Cwordk .
318   Using another random process, training is performed on a target with an output of 1 or 0, retrieving
319   positive or negative clauses, respectively. Based on the user-set window size a, a subset of these
320
      clauses is selected Sc , and all the literals within those clauses are extracted. These literals act as
      documents in the first phase, where all the clauses associated with them are fetched. Finally, the
321
      literals collected from these clauses, after taking a subset Sl of size a, are collected to construct X.
322
323   The second phase can be viewed as a clause-to-clause encoding process, where the input and output
      are clauses. This hierarchical tree of knowledge fetching is interpretable and transparent, which


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      maintains the reasoning behind the output. It is important to note that in this phase, there is no need
325   to compute the negation of features, as the results from Phase 1 already encompass these literals.
326   This eliminates redundant calculations and ensures that both positive and negative representations
327   of the features are inherently captured within the clause construction process.
328
      The main difference between the first phase and the second phase lies in the construction of the
329
      input X and the source of information. The output of the second phase is an embedding of the input
330
      target words based on the knowledge collected in the first phase. This embedding is then utilized to
331   measure similarity with a dataset prepared by humans, as will be further demonstrated in the subse-
332   quent section. In future applications, the first phase can be utilized for other operations depending
333   on the specific application, as demonstrated in our experimental results section, particularly in data
334   augmentation tasks for sentiment analysis.
335
      To demonstrate the output of Phase Two in terms of transparency and interpretability, we can use the
336
      words “drive” and “road” as an example. The output of the first phase for the word “drive” includes
337   clauses such as “vehicle AND license” or “road AND safe,” while the knowledge associated with the
338   word “road” includes clauses like “vehicle AND smooth” and “driver AND traffic.” After embedding
339   and training in the second phase, the resulting output for the word “drive” might include clauses
340   such as “vehicle AND (license OR smooth)” or “(road AND safe) OR (driver AND traffic).” The
341   interpretability of this output is evident in the ability to trace the reasoning behind the inclusion of
342   specific words in the second phase. These words emerge from the integration of clauses derived from
343   the knowledge of common words, which themselves are interpretable and transparent, providing
344   clarity regarding their presence in the final output.

403   in future updates, updating a single word may not require new training for the entire first phase, but
404   only for the targeted words. For example, let’s suppose we have a database related to healthcare
405   where the word “cancer” is a target word. If a new study comes out that shows a strong correlation
406   between a certain food and cancer, the knowledge associated with the word “food” may need to be
407   updated to reflect this new information. This would only require updating the “food” knowledge
408
      rather than retraining the entire first phase.
409   Notably, in terms of Cosine similarity, the two-phase TM-AE achieved 0.91 on the RG65 dataset,
410   surpassing all Word2Vec (0.84), FastText (0.69) and the TM-AE (0.87). This indicates that the
411   two-phase approach effectively captures semantic relationships when similarity is assessed through
412   vector alignment. However, its performance in Spearman and Kendall correlations is comparatively
413   weaker. For instance, in the WS-353 dataset, it scores 0.41 and 0.29, respectively, which is lower
414
      than Word2Vec’s scores of 0.58 and 0.41. This suggests that while the two-phase model produces
      closely aligned embeddings, it may not accurately reflect the rank order of word pairs as judged
415
      by humans. Furthermore, the two-phase model exhibits variable performance across datasets. For
416
      example, it achieves a high Cosine similarity of 0.97 in the MTURK287 dataset but experiences
417   a decline in Spearman correlation to 0.40. Overall, while the two-phase TM-AE shows promise
418   in generating aligned embeddings, further improvements should focus on optimizing the knowl-
419   edge extraction phase to ensure better alignment with human semantic evaluations across diverse
420   datasets.

480   We proposed a novel two-phase approach to word embedding based on the TM, designed to im-
481   prove scalability for NLP tasks. By leveraging CoTM, the model captured contextual knowledge

483   similarity benchmarks and sentiment analysis where embedding quality was validated through data
484   augmentation. Together with TM classifier, we introduced, for the first time, an end-to-end scalable,

      6.2   F UTURE W ORK
567
568   In this work, we have presented an optimal approach for initiating the development of a practical em-
569   bedding that can be leveraged in various applications. The application of sentiment analysis through
570   data augmentation (4.2) represents the first instance in which the embedding method proposed in
571   the first phase was utilized. Looking ahead, our future efforts will focus on enhancing the efficiency
572   of the embedding, improving model performance, and addressing the current limitations associated
573   with the slow implementation.
574   We also recognize that the embedding requires further expansion to better capture contextual infor-
575   mation. Achieving this will involve revising the manner in which the TM constructs clauses, as the
576   current embedding heavily relies on this process. Such enhancement will allow for improvements in
577   the second phase, specifically in calculating word similarity, as discussed in this study. Additionally,
578   ensuring a deeper and more structured first phase will significantly contribute to applications tailored
579   to domain-specific tasks. For example, in medical NLP, the ability to incorporate embeddings for
580   newly introduced terms without the need to retrain the entire model would enhance scalability and
581
      adaptability.
582   Furthermore, we anticipate that our approach will have applications in Knowledge Graph Construc-
583   tion, where the interpretability of propositional clauses would be particularly valuable. Such inter-
584   pretability makes the resulting embeddings well-suited for tasks such as semantic search, question-
585   answering, and reasoning.
