010
011
                Large Language Models (LLMs) exhibit impressive problem-solving skills across
012             many tasks, but they still underperform compared to humans in various down-
013             stream applications, such as text-to-SQL. On the BIRD benchmark leaderboard,
014             human performance achieves an accuracy of 92.96%, whereas the top-performing
015             method reaches only 72.39%. Notably, these state-of-the-art (SoTA) methods pre-
016             dominantly rely on in-context learning to simulate human-like reasoning. How-
017             ever, they overlook a critical human skill: continual learning. Inspired by the edu-
018             cational practice of maintaining mistake notebooks during our formative years,
019             we propose LPE-SQL (Leveraging Prior Experience: An Expandable Auxil-
020
                iary Knowledge Base for Text-to-SQL), a novel framework designed to augment
                LLMs by enabling continual learning without requiring parameter fine-tuning.
021
                LPE-SQL consists of four modules that i) retrieve relevant entries, ii) efficient sql
022
                generation, iii) generate the final result through a cross-consistency mechanism
023             and iv) log successful and failed tasks along with their reasoning processes or
024             reflection-generated tips. Importantly, the core module of LPE-SQL is the fourth
025             one, while the other modules employ foundational methods, allowing LPE-SQL
026             to be easily integrated with SoTA technologies to further enhance performance.
027             Our experimental results demonstrate that this continual learning approach yields
028             substantial performance gains, with the smaller Llama-3.1-70B model with sur-
029             passing the performance of the larger Llama-3.1-405B model using SoTA meth-
030             ods.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Text-to-SQL, the task of converting natural language queries into structured SQL commands, has
035   garnered significant attention due to its potential to simplify database interactions. Recently, in-
036   context learning (ICL) with large language models (LLMs) has emerged as the leading approach
037   for this task (Maamari et al., 2024). Unlike traditional fine-tuning, ICL supplies instructions and a
038   few demonstration examples directly in the model’s input prompt, enabling models to generate SQL
039   queries more effectively and efficiently (Rajkumar et al., 2022). Given that LLM performance is
040   highly sensitive to the quality of these examples, creating optimal examples has become a critical
041   area of research (Errica et al., 2024).
042   Current efforts to create demonstration examples for text-to-SQL rely on two primary approaches.
043   The first involves manually annotating a small, fixed set of examples that are reused across
044   queries (Pourreza & Rafiei, 2024). While straightforward, this approach often lacks flexibility and
045   struggles with generalization. The second approach pre-generates a large pool of demonstration ex-
046   amples in advance1 , using retrieval techniques like similarity search to select relevant examples for
047   each query. These examples, typically derived from training data, pair natural language questions
048   with corresponding SQL queries (Poesia et al., 2022). The retrieval-based method using training
049
      data offers more adaptability and leads to performance improvements. Furthermore, Rajkumar et al.
      (2022); Chang & Fosler-Lussier (2023a) demonstrate that providing a small number of in-domain
050
      examples, i.e., those from the same database as the test query, in the prompt results in better per-
051
      formance. This is because in-domain examples contain more relevant details and context, thereby

055   Chang & Fosler-Lussier (2023b) propose synthesizing such data by adapting templates from external
056   databases and populating them with columns and values from the target database, thereby lowering
057   data acquisition costs. However, the absence of specific domain knowledge, such as the database
058   schema or common query patterns, limits the model’s ability to generate accurate SQL queries in
059   cross-domain scenarios.
060
      In practical settings, particularly within enterprise environments, multiple distinct business systems,
061   such as flight booking and concert scheduling systems, often coexist (Panetto & Cecil, 2013), each
062   with vastly different database structures (Panetto & Cecil, 2013). This variation complicates SQL
063   query generation and demands greater adaptability and generalization from LLMs. We conducted
064   extensive experiments comparing LLM performance on in-domain versus out-of-domain tasks for
065   text-to-SQL, as detailed in Tables 1 and 3. The results indicate that LLMs perform significantly bet-
066   ter on in-domain tasks, while their performance declines substantially when confronted with entirely
067   different database structures. This underscores the current limitations of LLMs’ generalization abil-
068   ities in cross-domain scenarios. Consequently, we argue that for widely-used, high-accuracy tasks
069
      like text-to-SQL, effectively leveraging in-domain data is crucial for reducing the high generaliza-
      tion demands placed on LLMs, ultimately leading to improved performance and greater societal
070
      impact.
071
072   While the advantages of using in-domain data are evident, concerns may arise regarding the cost of
073   acquiring such data for text-to-SQL tasks. Based on practical scenarios and our investigation, we
074   provide two key reasons to demonstrate the abundance of in-domain data for text-to-SQL in real-
075
      world applications, which has been overlooked in previous work. i) According to the annual “Top
      10 Programming Languages” report (IEEE Spectrum, 2023), SQL continues to dominate the “Jobs
076
      list”, highlighting its widespread use across various enterprise roles, including Business Intelligence
077
      (BI) analysts, developers, database administrators (DBAs), product management, operations, com-
078   pliance, and business strategy. This inevitably generates a large amount of in-domain data related
079   to enterprise content and business. ii) Although LLMs excel at natural language tasks, their ability
080   to generate complex SQL queries remains inferior to that of human experts. Consequently, LLMs
081   are often used as assistive tools, generating initial SQL queries that users must review and modify.
082   These correct SQL queries are often crafted by experts and accumulated as problems are solved.
083   This valuable repository of correct query data, however, remains underutilized, even though it holds
084   significant potential to enhance LLM performance.
085   Building on the above analysis and inspired by mistake notebooks, i.e., a learning method com-
086   monly employed by students to track and learn from errors in exams or exercises (Yu et al., 2022),
087   we propose a similar strategy for LLMs in the context of text-to-SQL tasks, shown in Fig. 1, referred
088   to as LPE-SQL. In this approach, after each task, regardless of whether the generated SQL query
089   is correct or erroneous, the results are logged into either a correct notebook or a mistake notebook.
090   The correct notebook captures successful queries along with their reasoning paths, while the mistake
091   notebook documents errors along with a reflection-generated tip designed to prevent the model from
092
      repeating similar mistakes in the future. By referencing these prior experiences, the model not only
      avoids previous errors but also reinforces its understanding by leveraging successful reasoning pat-
093
      terns. This iterative process allows the LLM to optimize its performance over time, improving both
094
      the accuracy of query generation and its ability to learn from accumulated experience. In enterprise
095   settings, this feedback-driven mechanism holds the potential to significantly narrow the performance
096   gap between LLMs and human experts in complex SQL generation, while also maximizing the value
097   of existing data resources.

138   2       R ELATED W ORK
139
140   SoTA Text-to-SQL approaches typically use a multi-module method consisting of demonstration
141   selection, SQL generation, and a correction module (Maamari et al., 2024). Below, we discuss each
142   module and relevant research.
143   Demonstration Selection. LLMs exhibit superior performance in Text-to-SQL tasks through in-
144   context learning, where only a few examples are provided within input prompts. However, due to
145   the high sensitivity of LLMs to prompt design, the success of in-context learning relies heavily on
146   selecting appropriate examples (Errica et al., 2024). This module focuses on identifying the most
147   effective examples from the knowledge base to mitigate this limitation. Many previous works have
148   utilized the training set as a knowledge base, employing complex, multi-step retrieval strategies to
149   select suitable examples (Zhang et al., 2023; Talaei et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024). In
150
      contrast, Rajkumar et al. (2022) and Chang & Fosler-Lussier (2023a) found that performance can
      be significantly improved by using a small number of in-domain examples directly in the prompt,
151
      where question-SQL pairs correspond to the test database. Furthermore, Chang & Fosler-Lussier
152
      (2023b) argues that obtaining in-domain data is often challenging and proposes a hybrid knowledge
153   base that combines synthetic in-domain data with out-of-domain examples. Nevertheless, both their
154   experimental results and our findings (reported in Table 1) indicate that synthetic in-domain data is
155   notably less effective than real in-domain data in enhancing performance. Unlike prior work, our
156   approach utilizes a continuously expandable knowledge base that incorporates real in-domain data
157   and out-of-domain data. We employ a straightforward, similarity-based matching retrieval method,
158   which reduces the complexity of the approach while improving efficiency.
159   SQL Generation. The SQL generation phase often involves more than simply producing a can-
160   didate query from an input context. Prior works enhance this process by breaking it into multiple

      subtasks, solving them incrementally, and then merging the results (Talaei et al., 2024; Maamari
163   & Mhedhbi, 2024; Pourreza & Rafiei, 2024). Common subtasks include schema selection, which
164   encompasses both table and column selection. The objective here is to narrow the schema to in-
165   clude only the necessary tables and columns required for generating the SQL query. Another critical
166   aspect is identifying query features and classifying them for targeted handling. For instance, Pour-
167   reza & Rafiei (2024) categorizes queries into three classes—easy, non-nested complex, and nested
168   complex—tailoring prompts accordingly for each type. In our approach, we simplify this stage by
169   directly instructing the LLM to generate SQL using straightforward prompts. This significantly
170   reduces the complexity of the method and enhances the efficiency of SQL generation.
171   Correction. Two widely employed techniques at this stage are self-consistency (Wang et al., 2022)
172   and self-debugging (Chen et al., 2023). For self-consistency, a notable example is MCS-SQL (Lee
173   et al., 2024), which introduces a strategy that generates multiple prompts by varying the selec-
174   tion method and sequence of several demonstration examples, sampling multiple responses from
175   LLMs to mitigate sensitivity. Their framework produces three distinct prompts per step, with each
176   prompt executed 20 times by the LLM. In contrast, our approach executes the LLM only once per
177
      prompt, significantly reducing execution time and API costs, while presenting a greater challenge to
      our methodology. Regarding self-debugging, existing approaches typically rely on execution-based
178
      feedback (Andrew et al., 2024) or model-based feedback (Askari et al., 2024; Talaei et al., 2024)
179
      to correct generated SQL queries. For clarity, we integrate this strategy within the SQL generation
180   module of our methodology.

184   3     M ETHODOLOGY
185
186   In this section, we introduce LPE-SQL, a continual learning framework for Text-to-SQL that utilizes
187   real in-domain data without requiring parameter fine-tuning. It leverages human learning strategies,
188   like mistake notebooks, to create a dynamic knowledge base for enhanced performance. Specifically,
189   we give a detailed description of our proposed method, as shown in Fig. 1, the LPE-SQL method
190   consists of four components: i) Demonstration Selection: demonstrations are selected based on
191   question similarity from correct and mistake notebooks, with varying proportions prepended to the
192   prompt for in-context learning. ii) SQL Generatation: these demonstrations, combined with the
193   database schema and task-specific instructions as prompt, are used by the LLM to generate SQL
194
      queries. iii) Cross-consistency: multiple SQL results are compared to ensure consistency across
      different prompts, selecting the most stable result for increased robustness. iv) Rethink and Update
195
      Notebook: the generated SQL is evaluated against the ground truth; if correct, the model logs its
196
      thought process in the correct notebook. If incorrect, the model reflects on the failure and generates
197   improvement tips for the mistake notebook, promoting continuous learning and refinement. We will
198   discuss each component in detail.
199
200
201   3.1   D EMONSTRATION S ELECTION
202
203
      Given a few demonstration examples, LLMs can leverage them to generate SQL queries with a more
204   standardized format and improved accuracy (Poesia et al., 2022). Demonstration examples selection
205   plays a crucial role in few-shot learning, significantly impacting the performance of LLMs (Liu
206   et al., 2021).
207
      We adopt a hybrid strategy for demonstration selection, wherein we choose a total of k demonstra-
208
      tion examples from the knowledge base based on their embedding similarity to the test sample. The
209
      correct rate denotes the proportion of examples drawn from the correct notebook among the k to-
210   tal examples. Specifically, we construct prompts by selecting examples from both the correct and
211   mistake notebooks at three distinct ratios: correct rate = 1, where all k examples originate from the
212   correct notebook; correct rate = 0.5, featuring an equal distribution of examples from both note-
213   books; and correct rate = 0, where all examples are sourced from the mistake notebook. These
214   examples, along with the database and the user’s query, are integrated to form the final prompt. By
215   varying the correct rate, we aim to thoroughly investigate and leverage the unique contributions of
      both the correct and mistake notebooks. The experimental analysis is reported in Section 5.

      3.2   SQL GENERATATION
217
218   After the Demonstration Selection module, we utilize three distinct prompts. For each prompt,
219   we employ a straightforward approach that enables the LLMs to directly generate a single SQL
220   query. Following prevous work (Andrew et al., 2024), if an error message occurs during SQL
221   execution, we use the error message as feedback. We take the database, user question, initial SQL,
222   and error message as input, and ask LLMs to output the corrected SQL. This correction process is
223   not illustrated in the SQL Generation module in Fig. 1.
224
225   3.3   C ROSS - CONSISTENCY
226
227   Traditional self-consistency approaches in text-to-SQL tasks typically increase the randomness of
228
      LLM outputs by setting higher temperatures, thereby generating diverse SQL queries. These diverse
      queries are then aggregated through majority voting to determine the most consistent result (Lee
229
      et al., 2024). However, this method has notable drawbacks: higher temperatures can exacerbate
230
      model hallucinations (Renze & Guven, 2024), and the necessity for multiple API calls leads to
231   increased costs in terms of token usage and runtime.
232
233   To address these limitations, we propose a novel cross-consistency method that utilizes diverse
234
      prompts generated during the Demonstration Selection and SQL Generation stages. After select-
      ing demonstrations with varying correct rates (as described in Section 3.1), we construct distinct
235
      prompts. Each prompt instructs the LLM to generate a SQL query at a lower temperature, ensuring
236
      high quality and stability while reducing hallucinations. Importantly, only one API call is made
237   per prompt, minimizing costs in terms of token usage and runtime. The resulting SQL queries are
238   executed against the database, and their results are compared for consistency.
239
240
      3.4   R ETHINK AND U PDATE N OTEBOOK
241
242   The Rethink and Update Notebook module serves as a pivotal component of our LPE-SQL method-
243   ology, facilitating continuous learning through systematic evaluation and knowledge accumulation.
244   This module comprises two essential processes: Rethink and Update Notebook.
245
      Rethink: In the Rethink phase, the system evaluates the SQL queries produced by the SQL Gen-
246
      eration module, comparing their execution results against those of the ground truth SQL to ensure
247   alignment and accuracy. This evaluation mirrors the educational practice of maintaining a mistake
248   notebook to enhance learning outcomes. Specifically, when execution results mismatch, the model
249   performs reflective analysis to identify discrepancies and provide targeted improvement suggestions.
250   Conversely, when match of both execution results, the model constructs a detailed reasoning process
251   inspired by the “chain-of-thought” methodology (Wei et al., 2022). We expect this reasoning pro-
252   cess to contribute to multi-step reasoning necessary for more complex tasks, thereby reducing the
253   occurrence of hallucinations.
254   Update Notebook: The Update Notebook process operationalizes the insights gained from the
255   Rethink phase by systematically integrating them into the relevant notebooks. This step involves
256   updating the mistake notebook with reflective feedback and enhancing the correct notebook with
257   validated reasoning chains, ensuring that accumulated knowledge is readily accessible for future
258   tasks. By maintaining an up-to-date knowledge base of both successful and erroneous instances, the
259   system leverages historical data to inform and improve future SQL query generation, fostering an
260   environment of continual improvement and robustness.

262   4     EXPERIMENTAL SETUP
263
264   Datasets and Evaluation Metrics. We conduct our experiments on the BIRD dataset (Li et al.,
265   2024), which is recognized as one of the most challenging benchmarks for Text-to-SQL tasks. BIRD
266   consists of 12,751 unique question-SQL pairs derived from 95 comprehensive databases across 37
267   diverse domains, including education, healthcare, and more. Specifically designed to replicate real-
268   world database scenarios, BIRD incorporates external knowledge and provides detailed descriptions
269   of both databases and columns to address potential ambiguities in query generation. If not explicitly
      stated, all evaluations of LPE-SQL were conducted on the full development set.

      To assess model performance, we utilize execution accuracy (EX), a metric that compares the exe-
271   cution results of the predicted SQL queries with those of the reference queries on the corresponding
272   database instances. This approach measures the functional correctness of queries, accommodating
273   variations in valid SQL formulations that yield the same results.
274
      Large Language Models. We evaluate our approach using several leading LLMs, including
275
      Llama3.1-70B (Dubey et al., 2024), CodeLlama-34B (Roziere et al., 2023)3 , and GPT-3.5-turbo-
276
      0125 (GPT-3.5). Llama3.1 represents one of the most widely-used general-purpose open-source
277   LLMs, while CodeLlama is a specialized variant of Llama2 (Touvron et al., 2023), further trained
278   on code-specific datasets. GPT-3.5, a closed-source model, is among the most prominent LLMs in
279   the field. Together, these models cover a broad spectrum of application scenarios. GPT-3.5 is ac-
280   cessed via OpenAI APIs, while the Llama3.1-70B and CodeLlama-34B model is downloaded from
281   hugging face to perform experiments locally.
282
      Due to the high computational restrictions and API cost of the most advanced models, such as
283   Llama3.1-405B and GPT-4o, we were unable to include them in our experiments. However, it is
284   noteworthy that the SoTA method by Maamari et al. (2024), which utilized Llama3.1-405B, achieved
285   an EX score of 59.18 on a subset of the BIRD development set. In contrast, our approach, using
286   the significantly smaller Llama3.1-70B model with INT4 quantization, achieved a notably higher
287   score of 61.22 on the same evaluation set. This substantial performance improvement highlights the
288   effectiveness of our method, with further analysis provided in the Section 5.
289   Implementation Details. For the initialization of the knowledge base, unless explicitly stated, we
290   randomly selected 1,000 question-SQL pairs from the training set, leveraging our method to populate
291   both the correct and mistake notebooks. For subsequent accumulation strategies of the knowledge
292   base, we have two approaches: dynamically accumulating examples during the evaluation process
293   following the LPE-SQL method, and not accumulating at all. Additionally, we drew inspiration from
294   the work of Pourreza & Rafiei (2024) to create four manually annotated demonstration examples.
295   Using the FAISS (Douze et al., 2024) library, we leveraged the similarities between the sentence
296   embeddings4 of the target and candidate questions to identify the most similar entries from both the
297
      correct and mistake notebooks, with the quantity determined by specific experimental settings. To
      minimize the randomness of the LLM outputs, we set the temperature to 0. All prompt templates
298
      are detailed in Appendix.

      RQ2: What is the impact of the correct notebook and mistake notebook on handling SQL

      generation with varying difficulty? We conducted experiments in scenarios more aligned with the

352
      Specifically, in Table 2, Llama-3.1 and CodeLlama consistently outperform the mistake notebook
353
      alone across most difficulty levels when using the correct notebook, particularly showing an average
354   improvement of 4.49% on challenging tasks. This demonstrates that these models excel at learning
355   the thought processes from the correct notebook to execute complex reasoning more effectively.
356   In contrast, GPT-3.5 performs better using only the mistake notebook, indicating its strength in
357   leveraging error experiences for reasoning tasks.
358
      When both notebooks are used together, Llama-3.1 and CodeLlama show notable improvements
359
      in handling challenging tasks, benefiting from the integration of correct thought processes and er-
360   ror feedback, which helps reduce hallucinations and avoid repeated mistakes. Conversely, GPT-3.5

362   lenging tasks compared to when it relies solely on the mistake notebook. This suggests that GPT-3.5
363   tends to learn from past errors more than from correct reasoning paths, particularly in more complex
364   tasks, indicating a nuanced distinction in its learning process.
365
      Through the analysis presented, we find that Llama-3.1 and CodeLlama achieve more consistent
366   results, compared to GPT-3.5, which often exhibits an opposing trend. This discrepancy may arise
367   from the more similar structure and training data of Llama-3.1 and CodeLlama, as opposed to GPT-
368   3.5’s differing characteristics. Consequently, these models demonstrate distinct learning patterns.
369   Exploring these distinct learning patterns could be a valuable direction for future research.
370
      RQ3: What is the role of cross-consistency, and is it necessary? From Table 2, it is evident that
371
      using both the correct and mistake notebooks in total yields superior results for Llama3.1-70B and
372   GPT-3.5 compared to employing only the correct or mistake notebooks. However, CodeLlama-34B
373   achieves its best performance solely with the correct notebook. Additionally, at more granular levels
374   of difficulty, the differences among the models vary significantly depending on the correct rate. This
375   suggests that the impact of the correct and mistake notebooks differs across various LLMs, likely
376   due to variations in architecture, training data, or parameter settings, leading to distinct preferences
377   in information processing. Consequently, without a clear understanding of the specific effects of the
      correct and mistake notebooks on a given LLM, achieving stable performance becomes challenging.

      RQ4: How does performance improve as the number of entries accumulated in the notebook
408
      increases? To answer RQ4 and comprehensively demonstrate the performance of LPE-SQL, we
409   compared it with the current SoTA method in the BIRD benchmark (Maamari et al., 2024). For
410   compare, we used the same evaluation set as theirs, which consisted of 10% of the entries from each
411   database in the BIRD dev set. We conducted comparisons on Llama-3.1-70B using three different
412   notebook configuration strategies, where all strategies initialized the notebook with 1,000 examples
413   collected from the training set. The differences between the strategies are as follows: i) no fur-
414   ther examples are accumulated during evaluation, ii) examples are continuously accumulated into
415   the notebook during evaluation, and iii) to show performance improvement as the notebook grows,
416   we accumulated the remaining examples from the BIRD dev set into the notebook, while contin-
417
      uously accumulating examples during evaluation. The results are shown in Table 3. Experimental
      results demonstrate that as the number of in-domain data entries accumulated in the notebook in-
418
      creases, Llama-3.1-70B demonstrates steady improvements in EX scores across various difficulty
419
      levels, with particularly notable gains at the challenging level, where performance doubles when
420   utilizing a notebook rich in in-domain data. This strongly highlights the value of continual learn-
421   ing. Furthermore, we compared our approach with the SoTA method proposed by Maamari et al.
422   (2024), which employs a comprehensive and iterative strategy. This method includes generating a
423   candidate SQL query, then applying corrections through iterative re-generation based on database
424   execution errors (Wang et al., 2018), revisions guided by database administrator instructions (Talaei
425   et al., 2024), and model-based feedback akin to Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2024). It also incorporates
426   self-consistency (Wang et al., 2022) to generate multiple responses and selects the most consistent
427   result throughout the entire pipeline for augmentation, SQL generation, and SQL correction. In con-
428
      trast, our approach, while only utilizing fundamental methods in each module, leverages continual
      learning and surpasses the SoTA method by 2.04%.

      6      C ONCLUSION
465
466
      In this paper, we present LPE-SQL, a novel continual learning framework for Text-to-SQL tasks
467
      that utilizes real in-domain data without the need for parameter fine-tuning. Our approach draws
468   inspiration from human learning strategies to create a dynamic knowledge base composed of a cor-
469   rect notebook and a mistake notebook. Extensive experiments conducted on the challenging BIRD
470   dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of our method, as the smaller Llama-3.1-70B model outper-
471   forms the larger Llama-3.1-405B model using SoTA methodtechniques. Furthermore, we note that
472   different large language models display varying learning patterns with the correct and mistake note-
473   books. Leveraging these distinct patterns could further enhance performance, presenting a valuable
474   area for exploration.
475
476   7      L IMITATIONS
477
478   This work has several limitations and areas for improvement. Since advancing the SoTA was not
479   our primary goal and due to the high cost of APIs for advanced models like GPT-4o, we did not
480   evaluate our approach with the most powerful LLMs. Additionally, we did not incorporate schema
481   linking, a common technique for recognizing tables and columns, nor did we use methods like high-
482   temperature self-consistency to generate multiple candidate SQL queries from a single prompt. As a
483   result, the full potential of our approach remain unexplored. We believe that our continuous learning
484   framework for integrating in-domain data into the correct and mistake notebooks is applicable not
485
      only to Text-to-SQL tasks but also to other reasoning tasks, such as mathematical reasoning, which
      we will continue to explore.
