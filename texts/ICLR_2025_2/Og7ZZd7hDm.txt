011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated learning is a popular distributed learning paradigm in machine learning.
014            Meanwhile, composition optimization is an effective hierarchical learning model,
015            which appears in many machine learning applications such as meta learning and
016
               robust learning. More recently, although a few federated composition optimiza-
               tion algorithms have been proposed, they still suffer from high sample and com-
017
               munication complexities. In the paper, thus, we propose a class of faster adaptive
018
               federated compositional optimization algorithms (i.e., MFCGD and AdaMFCGD)
019            to solve the nonconvex distributed composition problems, which builds on the
020            momentum-based variance reduced and local-SGD techniques. In particular, our
021            adaptive algorithm (i.e., AdaMFCGD) uses a unified adaptive matrix to flexibly
022            incorporate various adaptive learning rates. Moreover, we provide a solid theo-
023            retical analysis for our algorithms under non-i.i.d. setting, and prove our algo-
024            rithms obtain a lower sample and communication complexities simultaneously
025            than the existing federated composition optimization algorithms. Specifically, our
026            algorithms obtain lower sample complexity of Õ(ϵ−3 ) with lower communication
027            complexity of Õ(ϵ−2 ) in finding an ϵ-stationary solution. We conduct numerical
028            experiments on robust federated learning and distributed meta learning tasks to
029            demonstrate the efficiency of our algorithms.

119   2   R ELATED W ORKS
120   In this section, we overview some representative composition optimization, federated optimization
121   and adaptive optimization methods, respectively.

123   2.1 C OMPOSITION O PTIMIZATION
124   Composition optimization has been widely applied to many applications such as reinforcement
125   learning Wang et al. (2017b), model-agnostic meta Learning Tutunov et al. (2020) and risk man-
126
      agement Huo et al. (2018). Recently, many compositional gradient-based methods have recently
      been proposed to solve these composition optimization problems. For example, stochastic com-
127
      positional gradient methods Wang et al. (2017a;b); Ghadimi et al. (2020) have been proposed to
128
      solve these problems. Subsequently, some variance-reduced compositional algorithms Huo et al.
129   (2018); Lin et al. (2018); Zhang & Xiao (2019) have been proposed for composition optimization.
130   Tutunov et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2020b) presented a class of momentum-based compositional
131   gradient methods for stochastic composition optimization. More recently, Jiang et al. (2022) pro-
132   posed a class of efficient momentum-based variance reduced methods for non-convex stochastic
133   composition optimization. Huang & Gao (2022) studied the stochastic composition optimization on
134   Riemannian manifolds.
135   For the distributed setting, Huang et al. (2021a) firstly studied federated learning algorithm for the
136   general distributed composition optimization. Meanwhile, Wang et al. (2021) studied personal-
137   ized federated learning algorithm based on the composition optimization. Subsequently, Gao et al.
138   (2022); Tarzanagh et al. (2022) proposed some accelerated federated learning algorithms for the
139   distributed composition optimization.
140
      2.2 F EDERATED L EARNING
141
      Federated Learning (FL) McMahan et al. (2017); Li et al. (2021a); Zhang et al. (2022) is a promising
142
      distributed machine learning framework for collaboratively training the global model without shar-
143
      ing the local data to obtain the privacy-preserving learning solutions, and is widely used in many
144   applications such as healthcare informatics Xu et al. (2021) and automatic diagnosis of COVID-19
145   Yang et al. (2021). McMahan et al. (2017) first studied FL and proposed the FedAvg algorith-
146   m for FL based on local-SGD algorithms Stich (2019), where each client conducts multiple steps
147   of SGD with its local data and then sends the learned model to the server for averaging. Sub-
148   sequently, Li et al. (2019); Karimireddy et al. (2019); Deng & Mahdavi (2021) have studied the
149   convergence properties of the local-SGD and FedAvg algorithms or their variations. To acceler-
150   ate the vanilla local-SGD and FedAvg algorithms, various accelerated FL algorithms Yuan & Ma
151   (2020); Karimireddy et al. (2020); Khanduri et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2020a) have been develope-
152
      d and studied. For example, Karimireddy et al. (2020) proposed a stochastic controlled averaging
      algorithm for FL by adopting the variance-reduced technique of SARAH Nguyen et al. (2017)/SPI-
153
      DER Fang et al. (2018). Subsequently, Khanduri et al. (2021) proposed a faster federated algorithm
154
      based on momentum-based variance reduced technique of STORM Cutkosky & Orabona (2019) and
155   ProxHSGD Tran-Dinh et al. (2022), which obtains lower sample and communication complexities
156   simultaneously.
157
158
      To solve the data heterogeneity in FL, Mohri et al. (2019); Deng et al. (2020b) proposed some ef-
      fective robust FL algorithms by learning the worst-case loss based on the minimax optimization
159
      problems. To further incorporate personalization in FL, some personalized federated learning mod-
160
      els Fallah et al. (2020); Deng et al. (2020a); Li et al. (2021b) have been developed and studied. For
161   example, Li et al. (2021b) proposed an effective and efficient personalized FL algorithm (i.e., Ditto)
      by learning a regularized local model for each client.

      2.3 A DAPTIVE O PTIMIZATION M ETHODS
163
      Adaptive optimization methods Duchi et al. (2011); Kingma & Ba (2014) are a class of efficient
164
      optimization methods due to using adaptive learning rates in machine learning, and they have been
165   widely studied in machine learning community. For example, AdaGrad Duchi et al. (2011) is the first
166   adaptive gradient method. Adam Kingma & Ba (2014) is a popular variation of AdaGrad algorithm
167   based on the momentum technique, which is the default optimization algorithm for training large-
168   scale machine learning models. Meanwhile, some variants of Adam algorithm Reddi et al. (2019);
169   Chen et al. (2019) have been proposed to obtain a convergence guarantee under the nonconvex
170   setting. To further improve the performance of Adam algorithm, recently some new its variants
171   such as AdamW Loshchilov & Hutter (2018) have been developed. More recently, some accelerated
172   adaptive gradient methods Cutkosky & Orabona (2019); Huang et al. (2021b) have been proposed
173
      based on the momentum-based variance reduced techniques. In parallel, some adaptive gradient
      methods Reddi et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2020c) are proposed for distributed optimization. For
174
      example, Reddi et al. (2020) proposed a class of adaptive federated algorithms for FL by using
175
      adaptive learning rates at the server side.

454   6   C ONCLUSION
455
456   In the paper, we proposed a class of faster adaptive momentum-based federated compositional gra-
457   dient descent methods to solve the nonconvex distributed composition problems based on the local-
458   SGD and momentum-based variance reduced techniques. In particular, our adaptive algorithm (i.e.,
459   AdaMFCGD) uses a unified adaptive matrix to flexibly incorporate various adaptive learning rates
460   further accelerate algorithm. Moreover, we established a solid convergence analysis framework for
461   our methods, and proved that they obtain lower sample and communication complexities simultane-
462   ously than the existing federated composition optimization methods.

1987   are dominated by a different class, which is referred to as the dominant class. In our experiments,
1988   each client has a different dominant class, for example, one client has 60% samples from the air-
1989   plane class and the remaining 40% samples from other classes. The hyper-parameter χ controls the
1990   percentage of samples from the dominant class over each client. The dominant class is different for
1991   different clients.
1992   In the experiments, we utilize a grid search approach to determine the optimal hyper-parameters for
1993   all methods, and the search space is described in subsection B.2. As each client’s data distribution
1994   is constructed to be heterogeneous, tuning a personalized model for each client can offer additional
1995   benefits. For selecting the learning rate, we typically set the learning rate to 0.05 at inner loops and
1996   the learning rate to 0.1 at outer loops. Additionally, we randomly select five clients to participate in
1997   training per epoch, and we set the asynchronization step q to 5 if it is not specified. The total number
