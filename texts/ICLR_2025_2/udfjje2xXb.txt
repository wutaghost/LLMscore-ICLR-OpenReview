                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Graph neural networks (GNNs) excel in learning from network-like data but often
010            lack interpretability, making their application challenging in domains requiring
011            transparent decision-making. We propose the Kolmogorov–Arnold Network for
012            Graphs (KANG), a novel GNN model leveraging spline-based activation func-
013            tions on edges to enhance both accuracy and interpretability. Our experiments
014            on five benchmark datasets demonstrate that KANG outperforms state-of-the-art
015            GNN models in node classification, link prediction, and graph classification tasks.
016            In addition to the improved accuracy, KANG’s design inherently provides insights
017            into the model’s decision-making process, eliminating the need for post-hoc ex-
018
               plainability techniques. This paper discusses the methodology, performance, and
               interpretability of KANG, highlighting its potential for applications in domains
019
               where interpretability is crucial.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Neural networks and deep learning have clearly revolutionized countless fields, encompassing the
025   domains of image, text, audio, and medical data. The basic building block of neural network models
026   is the multilayer perceptron (MLP) (Haykin, 1998). Very recently, a promising alternative to MLPs
027   was proposed, namely the Kolmogorov–Arnold Network (KAN) (Liu et al., 2024). MLPs are proved
028
      to be universal function approximators (Hornik et al., 1989), whereas KANs are inspired by the
      Kolmogorov–Arnold representation theorem (Kolmogorov, 1956; 1957), which states that a smooth
029
      multivariate function can be written as the finite sum of the composition of univariate functions; this
030
      has a similar structure to a two-layer neural network.
031
032   The idea of Liu et al. (2024) is to generalize this two-level structure to more levels. This extension
033   allows the definition of a novel framework that proved increased accuracy and interpretability with
034
      respect to standard MLPs. Indeed, lack of interpretability in neural networks is a major issue, which
      limits its usage in domains in which interpretable results are crucial (e.g., medical and financial sce-
035
      narios). To cope with this flaw, research focuses on the development of explainability techniques
036
      that try to unveil what was learned by those models in terms of salient features (Burkart & Huber,
037   2021; Saleem et al., 2022). In the domain of neural networks, models specialized in learning from
038   graph-structure data have emerged. Graph neural networks (GNNs) (Scarselli et al., 2008) are able
039   to leverage the connectivity structure of network-like data, learning representative topology-aware
040   embeddings. In literature, we find different architectures addressing different aspects and character-
041   istics of graph data. Graph convolutional networks (GCNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2017) extend the con-
042   cept of convolution to graph structures, aggregating information from each node’s neighbors using
043   spectral graph convolutions. GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017) is another GNN model that gen-
044   erates node embeddings by sampling and aggregating information from neighbor nodes. It is based
045
      on an inductive learning procedure that allows embedding generation also for previously unseen
      nodes. Graph attention networks (GATs) (Velickovic et al., 2018) incorporate attention mechanisms
046
      into GNNs, allowing the model to focus on the most salient parts of a node’s neighborhood. An
047
      additional GNN model is offered by GINs (graph isomorphism networks) (Xu et al., 2019). Such
048   models are designed to have an effective discriminative ability, theoretically proven to be as pow-
049   erful as the Weisfeiler–Lehman graph isomorphism test (Weisfeiler & Leman, 1968). GNNs have
050   also been extended to relational structures, with the introduction of relational graph convolutional
051   networks (RGCNs). They are designed to handle graphs with multiple types of edges and nodes
052   by incorporating relations explicitly into the model. Furthermore, variational graph autoencoders
053   (VGAE) (Kipf & Welling, 2016) aim to encode graph data into a latent space and then reconstruct
      the graph from the generated embeddings.

      Similarly to classic multilayer perceptron models, GNNs are not immune to the interpretability
055   curse; to shed light on their predictions, several explainability methodologies have been developed.
056   Such methods explain GNN predictions in terms of importance subgraphs built of salient nodes,
057   edges, node features, connected subgraphs, or a combination of those elements. The pioneering
058   work in eXplainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) for GNNs is GNNExplainer (Ying et al., 2019),
059   which determined explanations by generating important subgraphs using a mask on the adjacency
060   matrix able to maximize the mutual information between the prediction and the distribution of the
061   possible explanation subgraphs. GraphSVX (Duval & Malliaros, 2021) in another method, which
062   determines explanation in terms of important nodes and node features relying on the theoretical
063
      background behind Shapley values (Shapley, 1953). It uses a decomposition technique relying on
      a surrogate linear model for approximating Shapley values. A methodology targeting edges as a
064
      means for determining explanation subgraphs is EdgeSHAPer (Mastropietro et al., 2022). It em-
065
      ploys Monte Carlo sampling to approximate Shapley values determining salient edges forming rel-
066   evant subgraphs driving predictions. One additional XAI tool is SubgraphX (Yuan et al., 2021).
067   Also exploiting Shapley value approximation, it looks for explanations only in terms of connected
068   subgraphs by using a Monte Carlo Tree Search approach.
069
      In this work, we extend the Kolmogorov–Arnold representation theorem to GNNs, introducing the
070
      Kolmogorov–Arnold Network for Graphs (KANG). Our main contributions are as follows:
071
072          • Novel GNN Architecture: We propose KANG, a novel GNN model that employs spline-
073            based activation functions on graph edges. This design enhances the model’s flexibility and
074            interpretability while retaining the efficiency of message-passing mechanisms in GNNs.
075          • Enhanced Interpretability: KANG provides inherent interpretability by design, eliminat-
076            ing the need for external explainability techniques. This feature is crucial for applications
077            in domains requiring transparent decision-making processes.
078          • Performance Improvement: We demonstrate that KANG outperforms state-of-the-art
079            GNN models in node classification, link prediction, and graph classification tasks on bench-
080            mark datasets (Cora, PubMed, CiteSeer, MUTAG, and PROTEINS).
081
082
      Recently, the integration of KANs with graph-structured data has attracted growing interest from
      researchers (Kiamari et al., 2024; Bresson et al., 2024; Zhang & Zhang, 2024). Specifically, Kiamari
083
      et al. (2024) proposed two KAN-based architectures: one where node embeddings are aggregated
084
      before applying the learnable spline-based KAN layers, and another where the KAN layers are
085   applied prior to aggregation. They compared their models to GCNs using a reduced subset of the
086   Cora dataset features (200 out of 1433). Bresson et al. (2024) introduced two GNN variants utilizing
087   KAN layers for node representation updates: KAGIN (based on GIN) and KAGCN (based on GCN).
088   Additionally, Ahmed & Sifat (2024) applied a similar architecture to molecular data for protein-
089   ligand affinity prediction.
090
      In the following sections, we detail the KANG1 architecture and its components, present exper-
091   imental results to validate our approach, and discuss the interpretability of KANG. Our findings
092   suggest that KANG outperforms existing GNNs while providing interpretable outcomes.

      2.3     I NTERPRETABILITY OF KANG
217
218   KANG allows for understanding its predictions without relying on external explainers, which may
219   need to be trained (e.g., GNNExplainer) or whose computations are expensive (e.g., Shapley value-
220   based explainers). KANG’s interpretability involves determining the influence of input features and
221   edge importance by analyzing the information flow across the graph.
222
223   2.3.1    F EATURE I NFLUENCE
224
      Motivated by previous studies (Baehrens et al., 2010; Simonyan et al., 2014; Hechtlinger, 2016;
225
      Sundararajan et al., 2017), to compute the incluence of features in KANG, we leverage the interac-
226
      tion between the gradients and the spline weights, capturing both feature sensitivity and nonlinear
227   transformations. Specifically, let x ∈ RN ×F represent the input features for N nodes, each with F
228   features. Given a hidden layer, the first step involves calculating the gradient of the output prediction
229   with respect to each input feature, which gives us a matrix G ∈ RN ×F , where each element gi,f
230   reflects the sensitivity of the output for node i to its corresponding feature f .
231
      However, gradients alone do not provide a complete picture of how the features are processed by
232
      KANG, and can fail to determine a correct measure of feature importance (Sundararajan et al.,
233   2017; Shrikumar et al., 2017). In our model, the spline function weights Sspline ∈ RH×F ×B , where
234   H is the number of hidden units at the current hidden layer, F is the input feature size, and B
235   is the number of spline basis coefficients, modulate the feature transformation along the edges in
236   a nonlinear fashion. These splines are critical because they allow KANG to adaptively adjust how
237   features are processed based on the local graph structure, capturing important nonlinear interactions.
238   We aggregate the spline weights by averaging over the B coefficients, resulting in a reduced matrix
        (l)
239   Smean ∈ RH×F , which can then be interpreted as a set of adaptive nonlinear weights acting on each
240   feature across the hidden dimensions.

255   2.3.2    E DGE I MPORTANCE
256
257   In KANG, edge importance captures how information flows between nodes, influencing predictions.
258   By focusing on edge importance, we capture the interaction between nodes as modulated by the
259   internal spline weights, which are central to the nonlinear transformations occurring along the edges.
260
      For a given node i, the importance of an edge (i, j), where j ∈ N (i) is a direct neighbor of i, is
      determined by analyzing how the features of node j are transformed and passed along the edge to
261
      node i.
262
263   This approach is particularly insightful because the spline weights Sspline play a crucial role in mod-
264   ulating the feature propagation along edges, allowing for adaptive nonlinear transformations of fea-
265   tures as they flow through the graph. This makes the spline weights crucial for interpreting edge
266
      importance, as they capture complex feature interactions.
267   The spline weights in KANG serve a dual purpose: they not only determine how features are trans-
268   formed between nodes but also govern the overall influence of edges on the model’s prediction.
269   When computing the influence of features on a node’s prediction, the spline-modulated transfor-
      mations provide insight into how individual features contribute to the target node. However, this

370   3.3   I NTERPRETABILITY
371
372   The added value of KANG lies not only in its higher accuracy but also in its inherent interpretability.
373   As pointed out in Section 2.3, the interpretability of KANG is two-fold: it provides 1) a means for
374   node feature influence and 2) a measure for edge importance, accounting for the information flow
375   throughout the graph. As a representative example, we determined the most influencing features for

462   4     C ONCLUSIONS
463
464   In this paper, we propose KANG, a novel GNN architecture inspired by the Kolmogorov–Arnold
465   theorem and based on the recently introduced KAN model. The added value of KANG is two-fold.
466   First, it is more accurate than established state-of-the-art GNNs in node and graph classification
467   and link prediction tasks. Second, thanks to the usage of splines and simple aggregation functions,
468   KANG models are more interpretable. While KANG provides significant advancements in terms
469   of interpretability, this is only a preliminary step. Future work should focus on enhancing the inter-
470
      pretability capabilities and exploring their application to even more complex graph structures, and
      compare the outcomes with state-of-art explainability strategies. Moreover, we showed that KANG
471
      can scale to large graphs.
472
473   We want highlight that KANG is not fully interpretable in every scenario, particularly in deeper
474   networks where some information may be lost. However, KANG provides significantly greater
475   transparency than other models by offering direct interpretability from the model itself, without
476
      relying on external explainability methods.
477   Looking forward, several research directions could further enhance KANG’s performance and ap-
478   plicability. First, optimizing computational efficiency is essential, particularly by reducing memory
479   usage and improving training and inference speed through techniques such as more efficient spline
480   implementations, approximation methods, and parallelization. Second, incorporating edge features
481   is a priority for extending KANG’s capabilities. Developing methods to integrate edge information
482
      into the spline-based message passing and aggregation process could significantly improve predic-
      tive power.
483
484   Furthermore, applying KANG to real-world problems—such as biomedical research, where inter-
485   pretability is crucial, or financial analytics, where it can aid in regulatory compliance—could demon-
      strate its practical utility and further validate the results with the aid of domain experts. Finally,


                                                                      9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      hybrid models combining KANG with advanced GNN architectures, such as attention mechanisms
487   or variational techniques, may further boost performance while preserving interpretability.
488
489   By addressing these limitations and pursuing these research directions, KANG can be further ex-
490
      tended into a more robust and versatile tool for graph machine learning applications.
