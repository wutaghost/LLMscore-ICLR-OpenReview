009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has emerged as a powerful method for
012
               enhancing the output quality of large language models (LLMs). However, existing
013
               retrievers are not specifically optimized for LLMs, and retraining them requires
014            substantial resources. Furthermore, current approaches are often constrained to
015            either improving the relevancy of retrieved documents or refining the documents
016            post-retrieval. Various stages within the typical RAG pipeline present challenges
017            in aligning LLMs with retrievers. To address these issues, we propose Gaske-
018            tRAG, a novel approach that introduces a gasket between the retriever and the
019            LLM to improve their collaborative performance. By employing innovative tech-
020            niques, we gather high-quality preference data and use the gasket to optimize both
021            retrieval ranking and document refinement simultaneously. Our approach circum-
022
               vents the need for constructing complex training and inference pipelines. In a fair
               comparison against the latest RAG methods across multiple test datasets, Gaske-
023
               tRAG demonstrated a clear advantage. Our code and data are available anony-
024
               mously at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/9668.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Large language models (LLMs) often struggle with outdated knowledge, and updating them through
031   retraining is both costly and inefficient. Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) addresses this issue
032   by retrieving passages relevant to a given query, allowing LLMs to incorporate up-to-date informa-
033   tion and provide more accurate answers. RAG has demonstrated remarkable effectiveness across
034   various NLP tasks (Yasunaga et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024b; Xiong et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a;
035   Yue et al., 2024).
036
      However, since the retriever and the LLM are typically trained separately, a disconnect exists be-
037   tween them, making it challenging for them to collaborate effectively (Ke et al., 2024). To be
038   specific, retrievers are generally trained based on human preferences, designed to retrieve and rank
039   documents in a way that aligns with human habits. However, the preferences of LLMs do not com-
040   pletely align with those of humans. Additionally, the documents or passages returned by retrievers
041   often contain irrelevant information, referred to as noise. LLMs are highly sensitive to such noise
042   (Xu et al., 2024c; Fang et al., 2024). Similarly, retrievers, constrained by their training data and
043   model architecture, also exhibit different preferences when processing queries. Therefore, both re-
044   trievers and LLMs exhibit their own preference biases when dealing with human-written queries and
045
      documents. When integrated into the RAG pipeline, these biases affect the overall performance, a
      phenomenon we refer to as the preference gap. Tan et al. (2024) argue that LLMs tend to favor con-
046
      tent they generate themselves over retrieved information, which highlights this gap. Existing work
047
      aimed at improving RAG performance generally focuses on either enhancing the retriever’s ability
048   to retrieve more relevant documents (Liao et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024; Yoon et al., 2024a) or re-
049   fining the retrieved documents to filter out the noise (Xu et al., 2024c; Qian et al., 2024; Yoon et al.,
050   2024b). Moreover, some studies employ dynamic retrieval methods to balance LLM knowledge
051   with retrieved information, aiming to generate more precise outputs (Asai et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
052   2024b). However, this optimization of individual components in the RAG pipeline is based on hu-
053   man preferences, leveraging the human definition of ”relevance” while overlooking the preferences
      of the retriever and LLM.

054
      Narrowing this preference gap can help further improve the performance of the RAG system. We
055   propose GasketRAG, which introduces an intermediate model called gasket, trained using prefer-
056   ence data collected from both the LLM and the retriever. Gasket serves as an information bottle-
057   neck to control the behavior of both the retriever and the LLM, aligning them with the ultimate
058   goal—generating accurate answers. The Gasket model selects useful context from the passages re-
059   turned by the retriever, using this context to enhance the original query. The retriever then performs
060   a second round of retrieval based on the enhanced query. Gasket subsequently filter the context,
061   which is finally passed to the LLM to generate the final answer. We designed a method for col-
062   lecting high quality preference data that allows the Gasket model to be trained offline. We train the
063
      Gasket model using a weighted Kahneman-Tversky optimization (KTO) (Ethayarajh et al., 2024).
      This new practice significantly enhances the stability and data-efficiency of our approach.
064
065   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
066
067          • We propose a novel method, GasketRAG, which uses an intermediate model to control the
068
               data flow in the RAG pipeline, taking into account the preferences of both the LLM and the
               retriever, thereby improving their collaborative performance in generating answers.
069
070          • Our preference collection method ensures high data quality and training efficiency, avoiding
071            the complexity and instability of joint training.
072          • We meticulously designed experiments to conduct a fair comparison between GasketRAG
073            and the latest RAG methods.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
076
077
      Existing RAG optimization methods can be categorized into three main types: retriever optimization,
078
      refinement, and adaptive RAG.
079
080
      Retriever Optimization D2LLMs (Liao et al., 2024) combine the efficiency of bi-encoders with
081
      the nuanced understanding of LLMs in semantic search by decomposing and distilling an LLM
082   cross-encoder into a bi-encoder. Search-Adaptor (Yoon et al., 2024a) customizes the embeddings
083   generated by LLMs for retrieval tasks. Landmark Embedding (Luo et al., 2024) introduces a three-
084   stage method to train LLaMA-2 (Touvron et al., 2023), enabling it to embed sentences within a win-
085   dow of context for retrieval purposes. ARL2 (Zhang et al., 2024a) aligns retrievers with LLMs by
086   leveraging LLM-labeled relevance training data. Additionally, Zhang et al. (2024b) train a multi-task
087   embedder using a rank-aware reward that incorporates LLM feedback. However, all of these meth-
088   ods require retraining the retriever, which is computationally expensive. In contrast, our approach
089   can utilize any off-the-shelf retriever, eliminating the need for retriever retraining and significantly
090
      reducing computational costs.
091   Beyond retriever training, some studies focus on rewriting queries to enhance retrieval quality. For
092   instance, CONQRR (Wu et al., 2022) and RRR (Ma et al.) apply reinforcement learning to optimize
093   query rewriting models based on feedback. Without requiring additional training, LLM4CS (Mao
094   et al., 2023) prompts the LLM to generate multiple query rewritings and synthesizes their embed-
095   dings as input for the retriever. While query rewriting can help address the alignment issue between
096
      the retriever and LLM, it falls short in performing fine-grained filtering of retrieval results, leaving
      noise in the retrieved documents unaddressed.
097
098
099
      Refinement Refinement involves extracting useful information from lengthy retrieved documents.
      Zhu et al. (2024a) propose filtering noise by minimizing the mutual information between the refined
100
      text and the retrieved passages, while maximizing the mutual information between the refined text
101
      and the true answer. Similarly, CFIC (Qian et al., 2024) trains a refiner by selecting sentences based
102   on the generation probabilities of prefix tokens. BGM (Ke et al., 2024) selects documents from the
103   retrieved set and aligns them with downstream task metrics. Beyond refinement, Info-RAG (Xu
104   et al., 2024c) integrates the knowledge from the retrieved passages with the LLM’s parameters to
105   improve its robustness when dealing with noisy retrieval results. Fang et al. (2024) also aim to
106   enhance LLM robustness by constructing a noisy dataset and using adversarial training. However,
107   these methods focus exclusively on making the LLM a better document reader, while overlooking
      the retrieval preferences of the upstream retriever.

127   Adaptive RAG This approach often involves constructing search paths or iteratively interacting

134   decompose complex questions into simpler sub-questions, repeating the RAG process until a final
135   answer is reached. However, these methods often require numerous iterations and tend to overlook
136   alignment between the retriever and LLM during each RAG operation. In contrast, our approach
137   focuses on optimizing every minimal unit within the RAG pipeline, ensuring better coordination
138   and performance throughout the entire process.

153   3.2   OVERVIEW
154
155   GasketRAG improves the synergy between the retriever and the LLM generator used for answer
156   generation. It trains a key sentence selector to achieve the purpose of the data-flow control, called
157   the gasket model. This helps the retriever accurately find useful passages and provides the LLM-
158
      adapted context, enabling the LLM to generate correct answers. We first collect preference data from
      the LLM and retriever, then train the gasket model offline. Finally, the trained model is integrated
159
      into the pipeline to work collaboratively with the LLM and retriever.
160
161   Figure 1 depicts how GasketRAG works. A gasket model G is inserted into the RAG pipeline. Given
      a query q, the retriever R returns top-k passages. Then, all the sentences in the passages are assigned

163   S2 , ..., SIDn ⊕ Sn }, ⊕ denotes the concatenation operation. The gasket model will select the sen-

172   3.3   P REFERENCE C OLLECTION
173
174   Preference learning refers to the task of predicting an order relation over a collection of objects.
175   In the RAG pipeline, learning the LLM’s preference involves understanding how to craft prompts
176   that guide the LLM to generate the desired answer. Learning the retriever’s preference focuses on
177   determining how to enhance queries so that the retriever produces high-quality retrieval results. The
178   desired answer is well-defined because we can easily obtain the ground truth. However, the re-
179   triever’s output usually lacks explicit labels. Labels for relevant documents are scarce and annotated
180
      by human. Using LLMs to directly annotate relevant documents is computationally expensive and
      carries a risk of bias since it is not aligned with the final objective. Therefore, we use an approach
181
      to implicitly collect retriever preferences and directly align them with the output objectives of the
182
      LLM.
183
184
185   LLM preference Given the query q and the selected relevant sentences y = {s1 , s2 , ..., sm }, the
186   selection will be labeled as preferred if the LLM answers the query correctly otherwise dispreferred.
187
      Once an LLM preference dataset Dl = {(qi , yi , ai )}|ni=0 , where ai ∈ {−1, +1} , is collected, the
      gasket model learns a policy πθ to minimize the loss (Eq. 1).
188
189
190   Retriever preference Similarly, the sentence selection will be considered as preferred if the aug-
191   mented query leads to better results in the second retrieval. However, golden passages are not always
192   easily obtainable and often rely on manual annotation, which can result in significant workload and
193   cost. Therefore, we indirectly collect the retriever’s preferences by comparing the answers gener-
194   ated by the LLM using the results from the two retrieval iterations and analyzing the distribution
195
      of useful information within the retrieved results. The details will be explained later. Similarly, the
      retriever preference dataset is denoted by Dl = {(qi , yi , ai )}|ni=0 , ai ∈ {−1, +1}.
196
197   However, directly synthesize the two datasets Dl and Dr to train the gasket model would introduce
198   noise into the preferences of the LLM and retriever. Therefore, we extended the preference label a ∈
199   {strong preferred, weak preferred, weak dispreferred, strong dispreferred}, changing it from binary
200   to a four-value system. If both the LLM and retriever prefer the gasket selection result, it’s labeled
201
      as strong preferred. If only the LLM prefers it, it’s labeled as weak preferred. If only the retriever
      prefers it, it’s labeled as weak dispreferred. If neither prefer it, it’s labeled as strong dispreferred.
202
      Thus, we combine Dl and Dr to create a unified dataset D, which integrates the preferences of both
203
      the LLM and the retriever.
204
205   Figure 2 presents the workflow of preference data collection. Given a query q, we first sample mul-
206   tiple different sentence lists Y = {yi |yi ∼ G(q, T op-k)}|ni=0 from the gasket. The LLM then gen-
207
      erates answers based on these sentence lists, which are used to preliminarily label them as preferred
      or dispreferred. Next, we assign weights (strong or weak) to these labeled sentence lists. Similar to
208
      the inference process, each sentence list enters a second iteration. These sentence lists are added to
209
      the original query, forming a corresponding number of augmented queries {qi′ }|ni=0 . The retriever
210   uses the augmented queries to retrieve relevant passages, and the gasket generates new sentence lists
211   Y ′ = {yi′ }|ni=0 . Note that for each augmented query, only one sentence list is generated. Next,
212   re-generated sentence lists Y ′ will be divided into two groups based on the corresponding label of
213   y (preferred or dispreferred). In the preferred group, for each y ′ , we calculate the average sentence
214   index number. A lower average index number indicates that the retriever ranks useful information
215   higher, thus reflecting better retrieval quality. The first and last y ′ in the sorted group are selected as
      strong preferred and weak preferred, respectively.

249   3.4   G ASKET O PTIMIZATION
250
251
      The gasket model is tasked with two optimization objectives: providing knowledge aligned with the
      preferences of the LLM, and offering query background aligned with the preferences of the retriever.
252
      We focus the tasks of achieving these two objectives within a single model, rather than using two
253
      models for joint training, which greatly simplifies the overall pipeline and reduces the difficulty of
254   optimization. Sentence IDs serve as an information bottleneck, restricting the action space of the
255   gasket, enabling it to effectively control the behavior of both the LLM and the retriever, and making
256   it easier for the gasket itself to be optimized from preference data.
257
258   Weighted Kahneman-Tversky Optimization We use the Kahneman-Tversky Optimization
259   (KTO), which directly maximizes the utility of generations instead of the log-likelihood of pref-
260   erences, to optimize the model. This also simplifies the framework’s complexity and improves data
261   utilization efficiency. Recalling the loss in Eq. 1, KTO uses a biased method to estimate the expec-
262   tation EQ of the reward function rθ . The estimation is defined as

284   et al., 2017) and PopQA (Mallen et al., 2023); 2) Multi-hop QA: HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018) and
285   WikiMultiHopQA (Ho et al., 2020); 3) Fact-checking: PubHealth (Kotonya & Toni, 2020) and
286   StrategyQA (Geva et al., 2021). We only used the training data from TriviaQA and HotpotQA to
287   optimize the gasket model, while other test datasets are treated as independent evaluations. Due to
288   the time-consuming testing process, we only use the first 1000 queries from each dataset.
289
290   Preference Data We used the training sets of TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017) and HotpotQA (Yang
291   et al., 2018) to collect preference data, utilizing only the queries and answers from these datasets.
292

      follow Zhang et al. (2024c) to finetune LLaMA-3-8B as the answer generator. Since the LLM
293
      sometimes does not follow the exact standard answer format, to avoid labeling correct answers as
294
      dispreferred due to differences in expression or output format, we used not only Exact Match for
295   evaluation but also leveraged ChatGPT to assess the correctness of the answers. For each query, we
296   generate 5 samples. If all the answers are correct, we consider the query too simple and not requiring
297   optimization, so it is discarded. Similarly, if all 5 samples are labeled as dispreferred and the answers
298   remain incorrect after the second iteration, we consider the query too difficult to effectively train the
299   gasket model and discard it as well. Table 1 presents the statistics of the collected preference data.
300   The difficulty is determined by how many incorrect answers are found among the five sentence
301   selection samples, presented as a percentage. During the data collection process, we observed that
302   TriviaQA generated more samples with entirely correct answers (which were discarded) compared to
303
      HotpotQA, indicating that TriviaQA is easier than HotpotQA. Therefore, considering data collection
      efficiency and balancing difficulty, we collected only 5k samples from TriviaQA and 12k samples
304
      from HotpotQA.

      4.2     BASELINES
325
326   We compare GasketRAG with several baselines: 1) Direct, where we prompt the LLM to answer
327   the question without retrieval; 2) NaiveRAG, a standard RAG process, where the retrieved pas-
328   sages are passed to the LLM without augmentation; 3) Rewrite-Retrieve-Read (RRR) (Ma et al.),
329   a method for aligning the retriever and LLM through query rewriting.; 4) Iter-RetGen (Shao et al.,
330   2023), which synergizes retrieval and generation in iterations; 5) ActiveRAG (Jiang et al., 2023),
331   a method that predicts the next sentence to anticipate future content and uses it as a query to re-
332
      trieve documents, regenerating the sentence if it contains low-confidence tokens; 6) SelfAsk (Press
      et al., 2023), which improves chain-of-thought, where the model proposes follow-up sub-questions
333
      to solve before arriving at the final answer. 7) SelfRAG (Asai et al., 2023), enhancing RAG per-
334
      formance through adaptive retrieval and self-reflection; 8) SearChain (Xu et al., 2024b), which
335   builds a reasoning chain to iteratively propose unsolved sub-questions and verify the answer with
336   the retrieval information.
337
338
      SelfRAG requires an LLM generator with additional special tokens, meaning the model undergoes
      extra finetuning. To ensure a fair comparison, we follow Zhang et al. (2024c) to finetune unified
339
      generators using the same training data as Asai et al. (2023). We also use ChatGPT as a generator
340
      to evaluate the different methods. SearChain was reproduced using ChatGPT as the backend.
341
342
      Implementation Details The gasket model training runs on a 4-GPU H100 node. The base model
343
      is LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct1 . For sufficient KL-divergence estimate in a KTO step, the batch size
344   is set to 2 per GPU with 4 gradient accumulation steps. Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) is utilized
345   where the rank and the scaling factor are 16, targeting all linear layers. The learning rate is 1e − 5,
346   with a warmup ratio of 0.1. For preference data collection, GPT-3.5-turbo serves as the gasket model
347   to generate sentence selections and as the discriminator to evaluate the correctness of the responses.
348   ColBERTv2 (Santhanam et al., 2022) is employed as the retriever. We use the 2018 Wikipedia
349   corpus provided by Karpukhin et al. (2020), where the documents are chunked into passages with a
350   maximum length of 100 words. For all methods we use the top-10 retrieved passages.

      4.5     E FFECTIVENESS OF W EIGHTED KTO
420
421   We retrained the gasket model with the exact same settings, but ignore the sample weights during
422   loss calculation. Figure 3 exhibits the difference between weighted and non-weighted KTO meth-

424   By further distinguishing between weak and strong preferences in the binary preference data, the
425   distraction of weak preference samples is reduced, allowing the gasket model to converge more
426   effectively. Additionally, the non-weighted trained gasket model still shows improvements over
427   NaiveRAG.
428   We also train a gasket model with SFT. The result (Figure 3) demonstrates a significant performance
429   degradation with SFT. SFT provides an unbiased estimation of the target preference, capturing gen-
430   eral trends in labeled data. However, it offers a biased estimation for the model, as it does not
431   account for the model’s inherent limitations or specific dynamics. This limitation means that SFT
      cannot enable the model to precisely adapt to subtle differences in preferences.

524   5     C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this paper, we propose a new method, GasketRAG, which systematically aligns all components
527   of the RAG pipeline in an end-to-end manner. By collecting preference data between the LLM and
528   retriever, we perform Weighted KTO training to obtain a gasket model that effectively coordinates
529   the RAG process. Our approach eliminates the need for complex joint training and costly data anno-
530   tation. Through rigorous and fair comparisons in our experiments, the results show that GasketRAG
531
      significantly outperforms other methods. By comparing with strong LLMs without preference train-
      ing, we demonstrate the importance of aligning LLMs with retrievers to address the preference gap.
532
      We trained gasket models based on LLMs of various parameter scales, showing that even a much
533
      smaller gasket model can achieve performance surpassing other RAG methods. Additionally, we re-
534   veal the high data efficiency of GasketRAG, achieving training objectives with only a small amount
535   of preference data. Due to resource limitations, we have not yet explored GasketRAG’s performance
536   on more complex and domain-specific tasks, which will be left for future work.
