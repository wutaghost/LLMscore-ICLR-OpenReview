009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Many healthcare sensing applications utilize multimodal time-series data from
012            sensors embedded in mobile and wearable devices. Federated Learning (FL),
013            with its privacy-preserving advantages, is particularly well-suited for health ap-
014            plications. However, most multimodal FL methods assume the availability of
015            complete modality data for local training, which is often unrealistic. Moreover,
016            recent approaches tackling incomplete modalities scale poorly and become ineffi-
017            cient as the number of modalities increases. To address these limitations, we pro-
018
               pose FLISM, an efficient FL training algorithm with incomplete sensing modali-
               ties while maintaining high accuracy. FLISM employs three key techniques: (1)
019
               modality-invariant representation learning to extract effective features from clients
020
               with a diverse set of modalities, (2) modality quality-aware aggregation to prior-
021            itize contributions from clients with higher-quality modality data, and (3) global-
022            aligned knowledge distillation to reduce local update shifts caused by modality
023            differences. Extensive experiments on real-world datasets show that FLISM not
024            only achieves high accuracy but is also faster and more efficient compared with
025            state-of-the-art methods handling incomplete modality problems in FL.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In healthcare sensing, many applications leverage multimodal time-series data from an array of sen-
031   sors embedded in mobile and wearable devices (Ramachandram & Taylor, 2017). For example,
032   mobile devices equipped with motion and physiological sensors capture multimodal data to de-
033
      tect eating episodes (Shin et al., 2022), monitor physical activities (Reiss & Stricker, 2012), track
      emotional states (Park et al., 2020), and assess stress levels (Schmidt et al., 2018). Thanks to its
034
      privacy-preserving characteristics, Federated Learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017) is particularly
035
      suited for these applications, supporting local model training without sharing raw data with a central
036   server. Despite this benefit, FL encounters challenges involving multimodal health sensing data.
037
038   One major problem is incomplete modalities (Vaizman et al., 2018; Feng & Narayanan, 2019; Li
039
      et al., 2020a) where factors such as limited battery life, poor network connections, and sensor mal-
      functions prevent users from utilizing all modalities for local training, leading to variations in the
040
      multimodal data availability across FL clients. In centralized machine learning, this issue is often
041
      addressed using statistical techniques (Yu et al., 2020) or deep learning-based imputation meth-
042   ods (Zhao et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023). However, the privacy-preserving nature of FL limits
043   the direct exchange of raw data between clients and the server, making it difficult to apply existing
044   approaches in an FL setting.
045
      A way to handle incomplete modalities in FL is to train separate encoders for each available modality
046
      during local client training and use the extracted features from these encoders to train a multimodal
047
      fusion model. This way of training, known as intermediate fusion, has been widely adopted in recent
048   studies that address incomplete modalities in FL (Feng et al., 2023; Ouyang et al., 2023). Another
049   approach uses deep imputation (Zheng et al., 2023), where cross-modality transfer models trained
050   on complete data are used to impute missing modalities. Although these approaches offer flexibility
051   in adapting to varying modalities, they suffer from high communication and computation costs,
052   limiting their scalability as the number of modalities increases. As discussed in Appendix A.1.1,
053   this lack of scalability is a significant challenge in multimodal healthcare sensing FL, where the
      variety of personal devices and sensors continues to grow. Experiment results in Appendices A.1.2


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      and A.1.3 confirm that current approaches, including intermediate fusion and deep imputation, scale
055   poorly and remain resource-inefficient.
056
057   Unlike intermediate fusion, early fusion combines multimodal streams early at the input level. It
058
      is particularly well-suited for multimodal time-series healthcare sensing applications, as it captures
      intricate modality relationships (Pawłowski et al., 2023) and enhances efficiency by training a single
059
      model (Snoek et al., 2005). However, the standard method of imputing missing modalities in early
060
      fusion using raw statistics is not feasible in FL setting, as the server cannot access clients’ raw data.
061   This restriction leaves zero imputation as the only option. Nevertheless, without properly addressing
062   incomplete modalities, zero imputation alone leads to distribution drifts and significant performance
063   drops, as evidenced in Appendix A.2.
064
      We present FLISM (Federated Learning with Incomplete Sensing Modalities), an efficient FL algo-
065
      rithm for multimodal time-series healthcare sensing tasks with incomplete modalities. Our goal is to
066
      leverage the efficiency of early fusion while maintaining high accuracy. Achieving this is challeng-
067   ing due to several factors: (1) early fusion with zero imputation alone can cause the model to learn
068   biased relationships between modalities; (2) the quality of local modality data varies across clients,
069   resulting in a suboptimal global model, and (3) clients, especially those with limited modalities, may
070   deviate significantly after local updates. To overcome these challenges, we propose three key tech-
071   niques: modality-invariant representation learning to extract robust features from diverse modalities
072   in early fusion, tackling (1); modality quality-aware aggregation to prioritize updates from clients
073   with higher-quality modality data, addressing (2); and global-aligned knowledge distillation to re-
074   duce the impact of drifted local updates, solving (3).
075   We conducted extensive experiments and evaluated the performance of FLISM against six baselines
076   using four real-world multimodal time-series healthcare sensing datasets. The results demonstrate
077   the effectiveness of our method in both accuracy and efficiency. FLISM improves accuracy across
078   all early fusion baselines, with F1 score gains from .043 to .143, and outperforms intermediate fusion
079   methods with F1 score improvements from .037 to .055, while being 3.11× faster in communication
080   and 2.14× more efficient in computation. FLISM also surpasses deep imputation methods, achiev-
081
      ing a .073 F1 score increase and reducing communication and computational costs by 77.50× and
      35.30×, respectively.

      Multimodal Healthcare Sensing. Multimodal learning is becoming more prevalent in healthcare
088
      sensing, with applications ranging from physical activity tracking (Reiss & Stricker, 2012) and eat-
089
      ing episode detection (Shin et al., 2022) to emotion and stress assessment (Park et al., 2020; Yu &
090   Sano, 2023). To effectively utilize multimodal data, recent studies have proposed advanced training
091   methods, including enhancing data representations by optimizing cross-correlation (Deldari et al.,
092   2022) and incorporating self-supervised learning to build foundational model for healthcare sensing
093   tasks (Abbaspourazad et al., 2024). However, transmitting raw health data to a centralized server
094   raises significant privacy concerns.
095
      Federated Learning. Federated Learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017) offers a promising solution
096   for healthcare applications, as it enables local training on client devices without the need to send
097   sensitive raw data to a central server. However, most existing approaches (Xiong et al., 2022; Le
098   et al., 2023) assume complete modality data for local training, which is often unrealistic due to
099   factors such as battery constraints, network issues, and sensor malfunctions. In contrast, FLISM
100   works flexibly with incomplete modalities across various multimodal healthcare sensing tasks.
101
      FL with Incomplete Modalities. Research in multimodal FL with incomplete modalities has re-
102   cently gained attention. For instance, Ouyang et al. (2023) uses a two-stage training framework,
103   while Zheng et al. (2023) introduces an autoencoder-based method to impute missing modalities.
104   Feng et al. (2023) uses attention-based fusion to integrate outputs from separately trained uni-modal
105   models. Although these methods demonstrate effectiveness across various tasks, they often en-
106   counter challenges with efficiency and scalability as the number of modalities grows. In contrast,
107   FLISM utilizes early fusion to enhance efficiency while incorporating techniques to maintain high
      accuracy.

150   3.2   T HE FLISM A PPROACH
151
152   We introduce FLISM, an efficient FL algorithm for multimodal time-series healthcare sensing with
153
      incomplete modalities (overview shown in Figure 1). FLISM combines the resource efficiency
      of early fusion with high accuracy by addressing three key challenges. First, early fusion with
154
      zero imputation can lead to biased modality relationships. We solve this problem with Modality-
155
      Invariant Representation Learning (MIRL, §3.2.1), which extracts robust features from incomplete
156   modality data. Second, the quality of modality data differs among clients, and simply aggregat-
157   ing their updates can result in a suboptimal global model. To counter this, we propose Modal-
158   ity Quality-Aware Aggregation (MQAA, §3.2.2), which prioritizes contributions from clients with
159   higher-quality modalities. Finally, clients with limited or lower-quality data may experience sig-
160   nificant deviations during local updates. We address this issue with Global-Aligned Knowledge
161   Distillation (GAKD, §3.2.3), which aligns local model predictions with that of the global model to
      minimize update drift. Complete pseudocode of FLISM is given in Algorithm 1.

212   sensors for human activity recognition. Similarly, clients have varying sets of available modalities to
213   perform FL; some possess higher-quality or more informative modalities, while others have limited
214   or lower-quality modalities. Consequently, it is essential to prioritize updates from clients with more

245   3.2.3   G LOBAL -A LIGNED K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
246
247   When clients have less informative or limited modality sets, their local model updates can become
248
      significantly biased (Karimireddy et al., 2020). This bias can degrade the global model’s perfor-
      mance and its ability to generalize across different sets of modalities, especially when training
249
      rounds involve clients with lower-quality local modality data. To mitigate the impact of these biased
250
      updates, we employ Global-Aligned Knowledge Distillation (GAKD, Figure 1–⃝          3 ). GAKD lever-
251   ages knowledge distillation (KD) (Hinton et al., 2015) to reduce the influence of less informative
252   modality data on the global model. Specifically, during local training we distill knowledge from the
253   global model to ensure that local model predictions align closely with those of the global model.
254   This is because the global model contains a more comprehensive and generalized knowledge as it
255   aggregates diverse data from all clients across various modalities.
256

348   FLISM achieves noticeable performance improvement over all baselines. It consistently outper-
349   forms all early fusion methods, achieving average F1 score improvements of .043, .043, and .143
350   over FedAvg, FedProx, and MOON, respectively. FedAvg employs zero-imputation and conducts
351   standard FL training. Although simple and efficient, zero-imputation distorts the data distribution,
352   causing the model to learn incorrect relationships between modalities. Both FedProx and MOON
353
      incorporate techniques to handle clients with highly heterogeneous data, primarily addressing the
      standard non-IID (label skew) problem in FL. However, these methods perform similarly or even
354
      worse than the FedAvg when faced with incomplete modalities. This indicates that the data hetero-
355
      geneity targeted by existing approaches differs from the challenges posed by incomplete modalities,
356   rendering these techniques ineffective in such cases. In contrast, FLISM learns effective features
357   with incomplete modalities and builds a more robust global model by prioritizing the clients with
358   highly informative modality data, resulting in enhanced accuracy.
359
      Compared with the intermediate fusion algorithms, FedMM and Harmony, FLISM shows average
360
      F1 score improvements of .037 and .055, respectively. Note that FedMM and Harmony perform
361
      similarly or slightly better than FLISM on datasets with complementary modalities (e.g., RealWorld
362   contains ten modalities collected from two unique sensors at five body locations). This is because
363   intermediate fusion can leverage complementary embeddings during fusion, such as an accelerome-
364   ter from the waist compensating for one from the chest. Nevertheless, intermediate fusion struggles
365   with datasets that have more diverse and non-complementary modalities, such as Sleep-EDF and
366   WESAD. In contrast, FLISM employs early fusion to capture relationships between modalities at
367   an early stage and incorporates components specifically designed to handle incomplete modalities.

      A     M OTIVATIONAL E XPERIMENTS
703
704
      We present the results of motivational experiments that illustrate the impact of incomplete modalities
705
      in healthcare sensing applications in FL settings. Our analysis shows that current approaches become
706   increasingly ineffective, inefficient, and struggle to scale as the number of modalities grows.
707
708
      A.1     S CALABILITY AND E FFICIENCY C HALLENGES
709
710   A.1.1    D EMAND FOR S CALABILITY IN M ULTIMODAL H EALTHCARE S ENSING FL
711
712   In healthcare sensing applications, leveraging various input modalities, from physiological sensors
713   for emotional assessment (Kreibig, 2010), sleep tracking (Goldberger et al., 2000), to wearable
714   devices for dietary monitoring (Merck et al., 2016; Bahador et al., 2021), enhances app perfor-
715
      mance (Ramachandram & Taylor, 2017). Additionally, there has been a recent expansion in both the
      variety of personal devices (such as smartwatches, bands, glasses, and rings) (Oura Ring, 2015; Ap-
716
      ple Vision Pro, 2024) individuals own and in the spectrum of sensors (encompassing motion sensors
717
      and physiological sensors such as photoplethysmography (PPG) and electrodermal activity (EDA)
718   sensors) (Schmidt et al., 2018) that are incorporated into these devices. This highlights the need for
719   a scalable FL system to support multimodal healthcare sensing applications with tens of modalities.
720   Yet, existing systems struggle to scale efficiently with an increase in the number of modality sources.
721
      Below, we demonstrate that existing methods addressing missing modalities in FL such as interme-
722
      diate fusion (Salehi et al., 2022; Xiong et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2023) and deep imputation (Zhao
723
      et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023), show limited scalability and inefficiency in resources and compu-
724   tation.
725

      A.1.2    R ESOURCE C OSTS OF I NTERMEDIATE F USION
727
728   Most existing works (Xiong et al., 2022; Salehi et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2023) tackle the missing
729   modality problem in FL using intermediate fusion. In contrast to early fusion, where all sensing
730   modalities are combined at the input stage to train a unified feature extractor and classifier, inter-
731   mediate fusion trains individual unimodal feature extractors for each modality. This approach leads
732
      to significant scalability and efficiency challenges as the number of modalities in a multimodal task
      increases. To better assess the differences in efficiency and scalability, we compared the MACs
733
      (Multiply-Accumulate operations) and the number of trainable parameters between early and inter-
734
      mediate fusion mechanisms. We specifically focused on MACs and the number of model parameters
735   because these metrics are key indicators of a model’s resource consumption, including CPU, GPU,
736   and memory usage, and therefore directly influencing model’s practical deployment.

      A.2     P ERFORMANCE D EGRADATION IN E ARLY F USION
795
796
      In contrast to intermediate fusion and deep imputation, early fusion is far more efficient because
797
      it requires training just one model (Snoek et al., 2005). It is particularly advantageous for mul-
798   timodal time-series healthcare sensing, as it can accurately capture complex inter-modal relation-
799   ships (Pawłowski et al., 2023). However, it faces challenges when dealing with incomplete modali-
800   ties in federated learning (FL). Imputing missing modalities using raw data statistics (Zhang, 2016;
801   Van Buuren, 2018) is not feasible in FL environments, as the server lacks access to clients’ raw
802   data. As a result, zero-imputation becomes the only viable option (Van Buuren, 2018), leading to
803   performance degradation.
804   To evaluate the performance with zero-imputation in the absence of modalities, we conducted ex-
805   periments on two representative mobile sensing datasets, RealWorld (Sztyler & Stuckenschmidt,
806   2016) and WESAD (Schmidt et al., 2018), each featuring ten modalities, such as accelerometer,
807   gyroscope, temperature, electrocardiogram, electrodermal activity. We simulated conditions where
808   p% of clients possess incomplete modality data. We allowed p% of clients (with 40 ≤ p ≤ 80) to
809   randomly omit up to M −1 modalities from their training data, where M represents the total number
      of available modalities.

      D.2   BASELINES
922
923   FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017) represents the foundational approach to FL, enabling decentralized
924   training without sharing raw data. As a baseline framework, FedAvg is crucial for assessing the
925   lowest achievable accuracy, especially in scenarios lacking specific mechanisms to address missing
926   modalities.
927
      FedProx (Li et al., 2020b) was proposed to address system and statistical heterogeneity. It enhances
928
      performance by adding a proximal term to the local training loss function to minimize the discrep-
929   ancy between the global and local models.
930
931   MOON (Li et al., 2021) targets local data heterogeneity problem. It incorporates contrastive learn-
932
      ing into FL to reduce the gap between the global and local model’s embeddings while increasing
      the disparity from the embeddings of the previous local model. MOON has demonstrated supe-
933
      rior performance over other FL methods across different image classification tasks, showcasing its
934
      effectiveness.
935
936   FedMultiModal (Feng et al., 2023) (abbreviated as FedMM) is designed to tackle missing modal-
937   ity issues in multimodal FL applications. Initially, FedMM conducts unimodal training for each
938
      available modality across all clients. It then merges these unimodal representations through a cross-
      attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017).
939
940   Harmony (Ouyang et al., 2023) is proposed to manage incomplete modality data in multimodal FL
941   tasks. It structures the FL training process into two distinct stages: initial modality-wise unimodal
942   training and a second stage dedicated to multimodal fusion. Additionally, Harmony incorporates
943   modality biases in the fusion step to address local data heterogeneity.
944   AutoFed (Zheng et al., 2023) is framework for autonomous driving that addresses heterogeneous
945   clients in FL, including the problem of missing data modalities. AutoFed pre-trains a convolutional
946   autoencoder to impute the absent modality data. The autoencoder is pre-trained using a dataset with
947   complete modality data. However, AutoFed cannot be directly applied to scenarios involving more
948   than two modalities and incurs significant overhead for pre-training with an increasing number of
949   modalities. Therefore, we implemented AutoFed+ to adapt to datasets with more than two modalities
950
      (details are provided in Appendix D.4).
951
952   D.3   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
953
      We use a 1D convolutional neural network (CNN) as the encoder architecture, following the design
954
      for sensing tasks (Haresamudram et al., 2022). For a fair comparison, we standardized the encoder
955
      models across all methods. We set random client selection rates from 30% to 50% based on the total
956   dataset users. Standard settings include a learning rate of 0.01, weight decay of 0.001, and a batch
957   size of 32, with SGD as the optimizer. After a grid search to fine-tune the hyperparameters for each
958   baseline, we adjusted the MOON’s learning rate to 0.001 and its batch size to 64. We performed all
959   experiments with five seeds and reported the average values.
960

962
963   AutoFed+ consists of two phases: (1) pre-training M (M − 1) autoencoders and ranking, and (2) the
964   main FL training. Initially, we sample 30% of clients, ensuring they have complete modalities for
965   pre-training purposes. The remaining 70% of the clients participate in the main FL training, with
966   incomplete modality ratios p assigned as in our main evaluation experiments.

968   validation set used to rank the imputation models. This ranking is essential because, in the main FL
969   training, if a client k has mk available modalities and M − mk missing modalities, then for each
970   missing modality, there are mk candidate imputation models available to fill the gaps. These models
971   are ranked based on validation loss, computed via distance between original and generated modality
      data. We perform imputation model pre-training for 50 global rounds with three local epochs and

       F    D ISCUSSIONS
999
1000
       We outline discussions and promising directions for future research.
1001
1002   Server Aggregation in Extreme Scenarios. In §3.2.2, we introduced the Modality Quality-Aware
1003   Aggregation (MQAA) to prioritize client updates with high quality modality data. However, in
1004   extreme cases where high quality updates are limited or absent, the global model may struggle to
1005
       generalize, and overemphasis on specific modalities or demographics could lead to unfair outcomes.
       To address this, MQAA could be extended to include constraints that limit the influence of a single
1006
       or a group of clients. Additionally, adopting a more refined client selection strategy that ensures
1007
       diverse client inclusion can prevent overreliance on high-quality clients. Nevertheless, we consider
1008   such scenarios unlikely. In our experiments with real-world multimodal healthcare sensing datasets,
1009   our method performed effectively, indicating that such extreme cases are rare.
1010
1011
       Extension to High-dimensional Modalities. Recently, the study of large multimodal models, in-
       corporating modalities such as images and text, has gained significant attention (Radford et al., 2021;
1012
       Saito et al., 2023). Although FLISM performs well in accuracy and system efficiency, it mainly fo-
1013
       cuses on 1D time-series multimodal sensing applications. This focus stems from observations that
1014   early fusion is more efficient than intermediate fusion. We plan to extend FLISM to include high-
1015   dimensional modalities, such as images and audio. This could involve utilizing small pre-trained
1016   models to extract features from these modalities, aligning the extracted features, and proceeding
1017   with the FLISM training. This approach could broaden the applicability of FLISM to a wider range
1018   of multimodal integration scenarios, enhancing its versatility and effectiveness.
1019
       Runtime Handling of Incomplete Modalities. We focused on scenarios involving static modal-
1020   ity drops. This approach stems from our observation that dropping the entire modality throughout
1021   the FL training causes the highest accuracy degradation. However, we acknowledge that dynamic
1022   modality drops, where modalities might become unavailable at various points during application
1023   runtime, is also a critical aspect. The Modality-Invariant Representation Learning (§3.2.1), a com-
1024   ponent of FLISM, is designed to accommodate extensions for simulating various dynamic drop
1025   scenarios. Further development of the method to specifically cater to dynamic drop scenarios at
       runtime is an area for future exploration.


                                                         19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       System Heterogeneity-Aware Client Selection. Although FLISM achieves a balance between sys-
1027   tem efficiency and model accuracy, it overlooks individual user system utilities, such as WiFi con-
1028   nectivity, battery life, and CPU memory. As highlighted in our motivation, the number and type of
1029   modalities available for local training vary by user, and the system utilities can change dynamically.
1030   Building on the Modality Quality-Aware Aggregation (§3.2.2), we can devise an additional client
1031   selection method that accounts for device utility to enhance convergence speed. Future research
1032   could explore adapting the method to accommodate system heterogeneity.
