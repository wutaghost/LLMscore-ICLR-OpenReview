008                                                                               A BSTRACT
009
010                                           We study the depth of grade-school math (GSM) problem-solving capabilities of
011                                           LLMs. To this end, we evaluate their performance on pairs of existing math word
012
                                              problems together so that the answer to the second problem depends on correctly
                                              answering the first problem. Our findings reveal a significant reasoning gap in
013
                                              most LLMs, that is performance difference between solving the compositional
014
                                              pairs and solving each question independently. This gap is more pronounced in
015                                           smaller, more cost-efficient, and math-specialized models. Moreover, instruction-
016                                           tuning recipes and code generation have varying effects across LLM sizes, while
017                                           finetuning on GSM can lead to task overfitting. Our analysis indicates that large
018                                           reasoning gaps are not because of test-set leakage, but due to distraction from
019                                           additional context and poor second-hop reasoning. Overall, LLMs exhibit sys-
020                                           tematic differences in their reasoning abilities, despite what their performance on
021                                           standard benchmarks indicates.

        1                              I NTRODUCTION
049    The strong performance of large language models (LLMs) on high-school and college-level math
050    reasoning benchmarks (OpenAI, 2023b; Google, 2024; AI@Meta, 2024), has led to the common
051    belief that LLMs have “mastered” grade-school math, particularly as measured by the GSM8K
052    benchmark (Cobbe et al., 2021). This apparent mastery of grade-school math problems raises a
053    deeper question: do LLMs truly grasp the underlying concepts or do they mostly rely on super-
       ficial pattern recognition? For example, a recent examination on private “held-out” grade-school

072   In this work, we perform a case study to probe the brittleness of their reasoning abilities and to
073   evaluate how well LLMs can combine learned concepts to solve new problems (Hupkes et al., 2020)
074   To do so, we introduce Compositional GSM, a two-hop version of GSM8K at the same math diffi-
075   culty level, where each problem chains two test questions together such that the answer to the first
076   question is used as a variable in the second question (Figure 2). As LLMs can easily solve grade-
077
      school math problems, they should also be capable of solving combinations of those problems. As
      such, we measure the gap between their performance on solving the questions individually and on
078
      compositional GSM. Specifically, we benchmark frontier open-weights and closed LLMs, including
079
      Gemini, Gemma2, LLAMA3, GPT, Phi, Qwen2.5, and Mistral families. Here are our key findings:
080
081   • Most models exhibit a clear gap between their performance on GSM8K and compositional
082     GSM (Figure 1,3), which undermines their reliability and reasoning ability.
083
      • This reasoning gap is particularly evident in small, more cost-efficient (Figure 4), and math-
        specialized models (Figure 6), reducing their utility in practice.
084
      • Despite similar settings, instruction-following tuning impacts LLMs of varying sizes in signifi-
085
        cantly different ways (Figure 5), calling for re-examination of standard training recipes.
086   • Finetuning with either human or synthetic data on GSM8K problems results in task-specific over-
087     fitting with longer training (Figure 7).
088   • Smaller models benefit more from generating code solutions rather than natural language to solve
089     compositional problems, emphasizing systematic differences in reasoning abilities (Figure 8).
090   • Our analysis (in §3.6) indicates that large reasoning gaps are not due to test-set leakage, but the
091     result of distraction from additional context and poor second-hop reasoning
092   Our objective is not simply to introduce yet another reasoning benchmark, but to provide a case study
093   for deeper insights into LLM’ reasoning and a reassessment of how we evaluate these abilities.

139   3                   E XPERIMENTS & R ESULTS
140
141   Setup We evaluate each model on three test sets: 1) the original GSM8K test split, 2) the modi-
142   fied GSM8K test split which are the questions with X being substituted, and 3) the compositional
143   GSM test set. Each test set has 1200 examples. Following Zhang et al. (2024), we evaluate all LLMs
144
      with a standard 8-shot prompt (Appendix D) for the original and modified GSM8K test splits. We
      also created a similar 8-shot prompt (Appendix E) for the compositional GSM questions. No elab-
145
      orate prompting method is needed with this format. We evaluate GPT-4o, GPT-4o mini (OpenAI,
146
      2023a), LLAMA3-70B and 8B (PT and IT) (AI@Meta, 2024), Phi 2, Phi-3-mini-instruct (Abdin
147   et al., 2024), Gemini 1.0, 1.5 Flash and 1.5 Pro (Google, 2023; 2024), Gemma2 9B and 27B (PT
148   and IT) (Gemma Team et al., 2024), Mistral-7B (PT and IT), Mixtral-8x7B (PT and IT) (Jiang et al.,
149   2024), and math-specialized LLMs including Numina-7B (Beeching et al., 2024), Mathstral-7B,
150   Qwen2.5-7B and Qwen2.5-72B (Yang et al., 2024a). All models are sampled with temperature 0,
151   and pass@1 (Chen et al., 2021) is used to measure the performance on each test split. Some of
152   the models required a preamble prefixed to the 8-shot prompt for desired output formatting (Ap-
153   pendix B). We test both cases and report the best performance for each model.
154   We find that most LLMs fall below expectation on compositional GSM, exhibiting a large reasoning
155   gap as shown in Figure 3. Specifically, cost-efficient and smaller LLMs exhibit a much larger gap
156   than closed-source frontier LLMs, which we examine in details in the following sections.
157

        We compare pretrained and instruction-tuned versions of small and large models in three LLMs

256     3.3                     M ATH -S PECIALIZATION D OES N OT I MPROVE R EASONING G AP
257
258     Math-specialized LLMs are tailored to solve math reasoning problems. Such LLMs have an exten-
259     sive data coverage for diverse mathematical domains, raising the question: do they generalize to
260     held-out math reasoning tasks or overfit to standard benchmarks? To answer this question, we eval-
261     uated four state-of-the-art mathematical LLMs, namely NuminaMath-7B-CoT, Mathstral-7B, and
262     Qwen2.5-Math-7B-IT and 72B-IT on GSM8K and compositional GSM (Figure 6).
263
        We observe that these math-specialized LLMs exhibit reasoning gaps comparable to other models of
264     similar size within our analysis. For instance, Qwen2.5-Math-7B-IT achieves above 80% accuracy
265     on difficult high-school competition level questions in MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), but solves
266     less than 60% of the compositional grade-school math problems. This results is surprising, as most
267     questions in the MATH test set are significantly more challenging than simply chaining two grade
268     school questions together. Moreover, the large difference in compositional GSM between Qwen2.5-
269     Math-IT 72B and 7B models, despite nearly similar GSM8K performance, reinforces our findings
        in Sec 3.1 that smaller LLMs exhibit systematic differences in their reasoning capabilities.

290   3.4                               F INETUNING C AN L EAD TO TASK OVERFITTING
291
292   Supervised finetuning LLMs is a common strategy to improve their performance on reasoning
293
      tasks (Zelikman et al., 2022; Singh et al., 2023). In this section, we explore how it impacts the perfor-
      mance on compositional GSM. To do so, we finetune Gemma2 27B PT on the original GSM8K train-
294
      ing dataset with human-written solutions, as well as synthetic data (Yuan et al., 2023), to identify any
295
      difference in the characteristics of these two sources. For synthetic data, we collect self-generated
296   solutions that result in correct final answers for all GSM8K training queries. See Appendix C for
297   details of data generation and training for this set of experiments.
298
299
      When finetuning on either human or synthetic data (Figure 7), compositional GSM performance
      increases with some training (up to 100 steps), but drops with more training steps (400 steps) while
300
      GSM8K test performance keeps increasing, which suggests task-specific overfitting. Moreover,
301
      training on synthetic data generally leads to a higher performance on both GSM and compositional
302   GSM. We did not observe further improvements on either test splits after 400 training steps. Based
303   on this result, we hypothesize that the trend of using increasingly larger training datasets for over-
304   training small models beyond compute-optimal scaling (Sardana and Frankle, 2023; Touvron et al.,
305   2023; Gadre et al., 2024) – often heavily composed of synthetic data (AI@Meta, 2024) – may
306   primarily target performance on standard benchmarks, potentially at the expense of overall general-
307   ization and effectiveness across a wider range of tasks.

      3.5                               R EASONING IN NATURAL L ANGUAGE versus C ODE
310
311
      Breaking down natural language solutions into executable code can improve reasoning abilities of
312   LLMs (Gao et al., 2023; Gou et al., 2023). To this end, we evaluate whether compositional problem-
313   solving ability of LLMs improves when generating Python code instead of natural langauge CoT
314   solutions. For code generation, we utilize a compositional 8-shot prompt (Appendix F), where the
315   answers are written as two functions, one which solves the first question solve q1(), and solution()
316   which solves the second question with a X = solve q1() line at the beginning.
317
      We report our results in Figure 8 for three families of open-weight LLMs: LLAMA3-8B and 70B,
318   Gemma2-9B and 27B, and Mistral 7B and Mixtral-8 × 7B. We find that code generation generally
319   improves performance on compositional GSM problems, albeit not uniformly. Comparing relative
320   improvements, smaller models benefit substantially more from generating code solutions, again
321   highlighting the systematic differences in their reasoning. While code generation may help reduce
322   the gap for certain models, the primary aim of this study is not to “solve” compositional GSM as
323   a benchmark. Further, often what matters most is not the final answer itself, but the interpretative
      process by which it was derived in natural language, making it applicable across a variety of contexts.

      Mathematical Reasoning Robustness. Our work is heavily inspired by the study of robustness of
420   math reasoning capabilities of LLMs via rewrites of GSM8K test queries (Zhang et al., 2024), or
421   by employing functional variants of MATH problems (Srivastava et al., 2024). While these works
422   argue for the possibility of test set leakage and memorization, our results in Figure 9 suggest that
423   these issues are not a major concern in our setup. Others have investigated the robustness of math
424   reasoning abilities of LLMs via adversarial examples (Anantheswaran et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a),

      (2024) for comprehensive surveys on LLM reasoning.

463   5         D ISCUSSION
464
465
      Our case study on compositional GSM demonstrates that most LLMs have still not “mastered”
466
      grade-school math reasoning, despite what their high performance on prevalent math reasoning
467
      benchmarks would suggest. Instead, LLMs may be exploiting superficial patterns in their train-
468   ing data, leading to an overestimation of their reasoning capabilities. Stress-testing LLMs with tasks
469   like compositional GSM or counterfactual tasks is crucial for differentiating true understanding from
470   superficial pattern matching (McCoy et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023), highlighting the need for more
471   “out-of-distribution” tasks to assess reasoning capabilities of LLMs (Shapira et al., 2023; Shah et al.,
472   2024; Lewis and Mitchell, 2024; Li et al., 2024b).
473
      A key finding of our work is that small and cost efficient LLMs, which are broadly accessible and
474   crucial for real-world applications (Wan et al., 2024), exhibit larger reasoning gaps. Our analysis
475   on these models uncovers their systematic differences in learning dynamics and flaws in reasoning
476   capabilities, despite similar training settings and comparable performances on common benchmarks
477   to larger, more expensive models. This raises the question of whether small and cost-efficient models
478   are fundamentally limited in their ability to achieve such generalizations (Grosse et al., 2023).
479
      Mathematical reasoning is inherently contextual and compositional, yet current evaluation methods
480   fail to capture this complexity. Our compositional testing approach on grade-school math (GSM)
481   reasoning has yielded significant insights, and we envision future work exploring the application of
482   this testing approach to additional tasks and benchmarks, such as those from MATH (Hendrycks
483   et al., 2021), or by extending our framework to multimodal reasoning problems. Our case study
484   should not be viewed as an endpoint or merely as a tool for generating additional training data to
485   “solve” compositional GSM problems, but as a catalyst to gain insights about the nature of reasoning
      of current LLMs as well as to re-evaluate how we assess “reasoning”.
