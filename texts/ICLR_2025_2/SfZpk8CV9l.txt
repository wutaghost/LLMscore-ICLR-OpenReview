037                                                                       A BSTRACT
038
039                 Recent advancements in multimodal foundation models have yielded significant
040                 progress in vision-language understanding. Initial attempts have also explored
041                 the potential of multimodal large language models (MLLMs) for visual content
042                 generation. However, existing works have insufficiently addressed the varying
043                 granularity demands of different image generation tasks within a unified MLLM
044
                    paradigm — from the diversity required in text-to-image generation to the precise
                    controllability needed in image manipulation. In this work, we propose PUMA,
045
                    emPowering Unified MLLM with Multi-grAnular visual generation. PUMA uni-
046                 fies multi-granular visual features as both inputs and outputs of MLLMs, ele-
047                 gantly addressing the different granularity requirements of various image genera-
048                 tion tasks within a unified MLLM framework. Following multimodal pretraining
049                 and task-specific instruction tuning, PUMA demonstrates proficiency in a wide
050                 range of multimodal tasks, including image understanding, diverse text-to-image
051
                    generation, editing, inpainting, colorization, and conditional generation. This
                    work represents a significant step towards a truly unified MLLM capable of adapt-
052
                    ing to the granularity demands of various visual tasks. The code and model will
053                 be released upon acceptance.

      Unifying multimodal understanding and generation capabilities within a single model is a critical
      milestone toward artificial general intelligence (AGI). Towards this goal, recent advancements (Liu
060
      et al., 2024b; Zhu et al., 2023a) in multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have made signif-
061   icant progress in integrating visual reasoning and understanding with natural language interfaces.
062   However, developing a unified framework that excels at both comprehending and generating multi-
063   modal content remains a significant challenge in the field of artificial intelligence.
064
      Recent studies (Sun et al., 2023; Ge et al., 2024b) have explored MLLM’s potential for visual gen-
065   eration, beyond the previously well-explored visual understanding and reasoning with MLLMs.
066   These approaches enable MLLMs to process image-text inputs and produce either textual outputs
067   or semantic-level visual tokens. In the case of image generation, these visual tokens are subse-
068   quently transformed into pixel-space images using diffusion-based decoders. Such unified frame-
069   works empower MLLMs to perform a wide spectrum of tasks within a single framework, ranging
070   from detailed visual analysis to creative image synthesis.
071   However, existing MLLM-based methods (Sun et al., 2023; 2024b) face a common challenge in
072   the trade-off between diversity for text-to-image generation and high controllability for tasks such
073   as image editing. Previous methods mostly rely on single-granular features extracted from a vi-
074   sual encoder and neglect the varying granularity requirements of different tasks. On the one hand,
      generating diverse images reflecting the real world from text descriptions requires features that en-
075
      code coarse semantic concepts. Such features are fed as conditions into the diffusion-based image
076
      decoder, allowing the diffusion model to generate diverse images that semantically align with the
077   text prompt. On the other hand, tasks demanding precise control over output images, such as image
078   editing and inpainting, require the LLMs to predict fine-grained features that encode rich, detailed
079   visual information for the image decoder. This dichotomy presents a significant challenge for cur-
080   rent MLLM-based methods, which typically generate single-granular feature representations for all
081   tasks. As a result, models optimized for diverse image generation often lack the fine-grained con-
082
      trollability necessary for detailed downstream tasks such as editing, while those focused on precise
      controllability produce less varied outputs for the task of text-to-image generation. Although re-
083
      cent work like SEED-X (Ge et al., 2024b) attempts to bypass this issue by leveraging condition
084   images directly input to the diffusion-based decoder for fine-grained control, a unified solution to
085   the multi-granularity problem remains underexplored.
086
      Towards the multi-granular feature demands of various tasks, we propose a novel paradigm
087
      emPowering Unified MLLM with Multi-grAnular visual generation (PUMA). PUMA facilitates
088   seamless integration of image generation and understanding processes, while simultaneously han-
089   dling multiple feature granularities — from coarse-grained abstractions to fine-grained details —
090   within a single framework. By leveraging multi-scale features, our approach empowers MLLMs to
091   excel in diverse image generation and controllable downstream tasks, within a unified framework.
092   Our method comprises three key modules: 1) An image encoder that extracts multi-granular rep-
093   resentations, which serve as the foundation for visual generation and understanding; 2) An autore-
094   gressive MLLM that processes and progressively generates multi-scale image features; and 3) A set
095   of dedicated diffusion-based image decoders that decode images from MLLM-generated features
096   at multiple granularities. To optimize this framework, we employ a two-stage training strategy:
097   first fine-tuning the set of pre-trained diffusion models as our image decoders, where each model
      reconstructs or generates images conditioned on the corresponding feature granularities from the
098
      encoder; then training the autoregressive MLLM with regression loss supervised by the multi-scale
099
      encoder features to process and generate multi-granular image features. PUMA leverages large-
100   scale pre-training followed by task-specific instruction tuning on a collection of linguistic-visual
101   datasets, enabling our model to handle various tasks including image understanding, text-to-image
102   generation, editing, inpainting, colorization, and conditional generation.
103
      In summary, we introduce a novel multi-granularity paradigm for MLLMs that addresses the lim-
104   itations of existing single-scale methods. By simultaneously processing and generating features at
105   multiple granularities, our approach enables a unified framework to handle a wide range of tasks,
106   from diverse image generation to precise editing and highly controllable generation. This unified
107   framework represents a significant advancement towards more versatile and capable MLLMs, con-
      tributing to the broader goal of achieving AGI in multimodal domains.

110   2.1   M ULTIMODAL U NDERSTANDING
111   The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) has catalyzed significant progress in mul-
112   timodal large language models (MLLMs) for multimodal understanding tasks Dai et al. (2023); Li
113   et al. (2024b); Zhang et al. (2023a); Chen et al. (2024); Lin et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024b); Li
114   et al. (2024a). Pioneering works such as LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b) and MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al.,
115   2023a) have demonstrated remarkable performance across diverse image understanding tasks, in-
116   cluding visual question answering (VQA), visual reasoning, optical character recognition (OCR),
      and object grounding. These approaches typically employ visual encoders, such as the CLIP en-
117
      coder (Radford et al., 2021), to extract continuous image features, which are then projected into the
118
      LLM’s embedding space for subsequent tasks. While successfully unifying various image under-
119   standing tasks within a single model, these methods mostly adhere to a multimodal-input, text-output
120   paradigm. Consequently, they excel at text-based responses to visual inputs but cannot generate mul-
121   timodal outputs beyond text, limiting their applicability in tasks requiring visual content generation.
122
123   2.2   U NIFIED U NDERSTANDING AND G ENERATION FOR MLLM S
124   Recent research has focused on equipping MLLMs with multimodal output capabilities (Wu et al.,
125   2023; Tang et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2024a; Zhu et al., 2023b). GILL (Koh et al., 2024) pioneered
126   the integration of image generation abilities into MLLMs. Subsequently, SEED-LLaMA (Ge et al.,
127   2023) and Emu (Sun et al., 2023) further advanced image generation and understanding capabilities
128   within MLLMs, while DreamLLM (Dong et al., 2023) proposed an end-to-end training approach
      for enhanced performance.
129
130   More recent works, such as SEED-X (Ge et al., 2024b) and Emu2 (Sun et al., 2024b), have scaled up
      MLLMs for unified generation, adopting continuous feature-based methods. These approaches uti-
131
      lize pre-trained vision encoders to extract continuous semantic features, which MLLMs then autore-
132
      gressively regress. Specialized diffusion model-based decoders transform these MLLM-generated
133   features into pixel-space images. However, the single-scale image feature generation pipeline em-
134   ployed by these methods struggles to address tasks with varying granularity demands, making it
135   challenging to balance diverse image generation with fine-grained control for manipulation tasks.
136   SEED-X attempts to address the multi-granularity issue by introducing conditional image input to
137   the diffusion-based decoder for fine-grained control. However, this approach limits its applicability
138
      to image editing tasks encountered during decoder training. Consequently, a unified solution to the
      multi-granularity problem remains underexplored. In contrast, our work proposes a novel multi-
139
      granularity paradigm that addresses these limitations by simultaneously handling multiple levels of
140   feature granularity within a single, unified framework.
141
      Alternative approaches have also been investigated. Chameleon (Team, 2024) explored using dis-
142   crete image tokens to bridge image understanding and generation, but the vector quantization process
143   leads to information loss, hindering high-performance image understanding. TransFusion (Zhou
144   et al., 2024) and show-o (Ge et al., 2023) proposed transforming the MLLM backbone itself into
145   a denoiser in a diffusion-based or demasking-based approach. However, these methods require nu-
146   merous denoising steps for each image generation, resulting in substantial computational costs given
147
      the scale of current MLLM backbones. VAR (Tian et al., 2024) is another track of generation frame-
      work that implements hierarchical autoregressive with discrete tokens for image generation, but it
148
      only discusses image generation and cannot unify multimodal tasks.
149
150   3     M ETHOD
151
      Existing approaches typically optimize for either fine or coarse-grained features, resulting in a trade-
152
      off between precise control and generation diversity. To overcome this limitation, we propose
153   PUMA, a unified multi-granular MLLM paradigm. Our approach simultaneously processes multi-
154   ple levels of feature granularity within a unified MLLM framework, facilitating seamless transitions
155   across a wide spectrum of multimodal tasks.
156
      Our framework consists of three key components: an image encoder (Sec. 3.1), a set of image
157
      decoders conditioned on different granular features (Sec. 3.2), and a multi-granular autoregressive
158   MLLM (Sec. 3.3). These components work synergistically to extract, process, and generate multi-
159   scale image features, adapting to various task-specific granularity requirements. To optimize our
160   MLLM, we employ a two-stage process of pretraining and instruction tuning (Sec. 3.4), enabling it
161   to perform a wide range of tasks including image understanding, generation, editing, and conditional
      image generation.

      3.1     I MAGE E NCODING AND M ULTI - GRANULAR F EATURE E XTRACTION
188
189   Our unified multi-granularity paradigm leverages a semantic image encoder to extract multi-scale
190   features, forming the foundation for diverse visual task processing. We employ a CLIP (Radford
      et al., 2021) semantic image encoder to process input images x and generate the initial set of high-
191
      resolution features f0 ∈ RH×W ×C , with H and W representing the spatial dimensions of the
192
      highest resolution feature grid, and C denoting the channel dimension. In our setting, the feature
193   size is H = W = 16, thus the highest resolution feature f0 has 256 visual tokens.
194
195
      To obtain multi-granular representations, we derive lower resolution features through successive
      applications of 2D average pooling with kernel size 2 and stride 2:
196
197                                                   fi = AvgPool(fi−1 ),             i = 1, 2, ..., N                                                  (1)
198
199
      where N is the number of additional granular levels. This process generates a series of feature
      grids at progressively coarser resolutions, ranging from fine-grained features preserving detailed
200
      spatial information and local textures, through mid-level features capturing object parts and regional
201   structures, to features representing coarse-grained semantic concepts. These features are denoted as
202   f0 , f1 , f2 , f3 , and f4 , which have 256, 64, 16, 4, and 1 visual tokens respectively.
203
204   3.2     M ULTI -G RANULAR V ISUAL D ECODING
205
      Image features at different granularities encode varying levels of information. We employ diffusion-
206
      based models as decoders due to their flexible capability to handle multi-scale features. When
207
      processing coarse-grained semantic features, the decoders can effectively synthesize missing fine-
208   grained information with their learned image priors and generate diverse, semantics-aligned images.
209   On the other hand, when handling fine-grained features, they accurately reconstruct precise image
210   details. This versatility in generating or reconstructing images across different granularities makes
211   diffusion-based models suitable for our multi-granularity approach.
212   We develop a set of dedicate diffusion-based image decoders D0 , D1 , ..., DN corresponding to the
213   feature scales f0 , f1 , ..., fN . These decoders enable the visual decoding of images at various levels of
214   granularity. We formulate the image decoding process for each granularity level i as x̂i = Di (fi , z),
215   where x̂i is the decoded image, fi is the feature map at granularity level i, and z is a random noise
      vector for the diffusion process.

      3.3   P ROGRESSIVE M ULTI -G RANULAR I MAGE M ODELING IN AUTOREGRESSIVE MLLM
252
253   Driven by the goal of utilizing a unified framework capable of adapting to a wide range of visual-
254   linguistic tasks with varying granularity requirements, we design an autoregressive MLLM to pro-
255   cess and generate both text tokens and multi-granular image features.
256   Our autoregressive MLLM, denoted as M , processes text and multi-granular image features pro-
257   gressively, as illustrated in Fig. 2. The model processes features token by token, predicting each
258   token sequentially within each granularity level, and progressing from the coarsest level N to the
259   finest level 0. This approach allows the model to refine its predictions as more detailed information
260
      becomes available.
261   We structure the input sequence as a concatenation of text tokens and flattened image feature tokens
262   from multiple granularity levels. This progressive approach enables the model to capture dependen-
263   cies across different scales, from coarse global structures to fine local details.
264   The MLLM is trained using an autoregressive next token prediction objective, combining both text
265   and image losses:

      3.4   M ULTIMODAL P RETRAINING AND I NSTRUCT T UNING
276   To demonstrate the effectiveness of our unified multi-granularity paradigm, we implement a com-
277   prehensive two-stage training pipeline for PUMA: multimodal pretraining followed by task-specific
278   instruct tuning. This approach allows our model to first acquire broad multimodal capabilities before
279   specializing in targeted visual-linguistic tasks during the subsequent instruct tuning stage.
280   Multimodal Pretraining: Our multimodal pretraining leverages a diverse set of large-scale
281   datasets: Laion-2B (Schuhmann et al., 2022), Laion-Aesthetics (Burger, 2023), GRIT (Peng et al.,
282   2023), The Pile (Gao et al., 2020), OCR-VQA-200K (Mishra et al., 2019), and LLaVAR (Zhang
283   et al., 2023b). This combination of datasets provides a rich mixture of image-text pairs, textual data,
284   and specialized visual question-answering samples. To enhance the model’s bidirectional under-
285   standing of image-text relationships, we employ a dynamic training strategy that randomly alternates
286
      between text-to-image and image-to-text tasks for each image-text pair.
287   Instruct Tuning: Following pretraining, we conduct targeted instruct tuning to adapt our model
288   to specific visual-linguistic tasks. To evaluate PUMA’s performance across different task types, we
289   fine-tune four dedicated models for the four types of tasks, each initialized from the pretraining
290
      checkpoint.
291   High-quality Text-to-Image Generation: We utilize Laion-Aesthetics (Burger, 2023) and JourneyDB
292   (Sun et al., 2024a) to focus on generating aesthetically pleasing and diverse images.
293   Precise Image Manipulation: Training on the SEED-Edit (Ge et al., 2024a) dataset enables accurate
294   and controlled image editing.
295
      Conditional Image Generation: The subset of MultiGen-20M dataset (Qin et al., 2023) including
296
      canny-to-image, inpainting, and colorization is employed to equip the model with the ability to
297   generate images under specific conditions and constraints.
298
299
      Image Understanding: Fine-tuning on the subset of LLaVA-OneVision (Li et al., 2024a) and Cam-
      brain (Tong et al., 2024) to enhance the model’s image comprehension capabilities. Data about
300
      math/reasoning and cross-duplicated data in the two datasets are removed.

      4.2.1   F INE - GRAINED I MAGE R ECONSTRUCTION
356
357   Fine-grained image reconstruction is crucial for preserving image details, yet it has posed significant
358   challenges for models like SEED-LLaMA (Ge et al., 2023), SEED-X (Ge et al., 2024b), and Emu2
359
      (Sun et al., 2024b). While SEED-LLaMA and SEED-X struggle with detailed reconstruction, limit-
      ing their precise image manipulation capabilities without additional techniques such as conditional
360
      image input (as used in SEED-X), Emu2 attempts to improve reconstruction by scaling up its image
361   encoder to 4 billion parameters. Our approach achieves superior reconstruction quality with a more
362   efficient architecture. We employ the CLIP-Large encoder (0.3 billion parameters), which is over
363   10 times smaller than Emu2’s, and implement fine-grained level image embedding with 256 tokens.
364   As demonstrated in Tab. 1, our method using f0 scale features achieves 18.16 PSNRr and 0.2215

      diversity in outputs. To quantify this semantics-guided diversity, we decode twice to obtain two
373
      images from the same image input using different random seeds and measure their differences,

377   input, as illustrated in Fig. 5. This demonstrates our approach’s effectiveness in balancing semantic
      accuracy with generative diversity, a crucial factor in tasks like text-to-image generation.

531   5     C ONCLUSION
532
533   In this paper, we introduce PUMA, a novel unified multi-granular MLLM that unifies various gran-
534
      ular tasks in visual generation and understanding. By leveraging multi-granular representations,
      PUMA effectively addresses the challenge of balancing diversity and controllability in image gen-
535
      eration tasks. Our approach demonstrates superior performance across a spectrum of visual tasks,
536   including diverse text-to-image generation, image editing, inpainting, colorization, conditional gen-
537   eration, and understanding. PUMA’s ability to adapt to varying granularity requirements within a
538   single framework represents a significant advancement in MLLM capabilities. This work opens up
539   new possibilities for more versatile and powerful multimodal AI systems, contributing to the broader
      goal of achieving artificial general intelligence in multimodal domains.

      A     PUMA T RAINING

      A.1     V ISUAL D ECODING T RAINING
759
760   A.1.1    DATASET D ETAILS
761
762   For training the image decoding process, we leverage three large-scale datasets: Laion-2B (Schuh-
      mann et al., 2022), Laion-Aesthetics (Burger, 2023), and JourneyDB (Sun et al., 2024a). To ensure
763
      high-quality generation capabilities, we apply a resolution-based filtering criterion, selecting only
764
      images with resolutions of 512 × 512 pixels or larger. We only use center crop as the data augmen-
765   tation method.
766
767   A.1.2    T RAINING S ETTINGS
768
769   We train five dedicated image decoders for the f0 , f1 , f2 , f3 , and f4 scale features respectively. The
770   image encoder is the frozen CLIP-L image encoder (Radford et al., 2021). Each image decoder is
771
      initialized from the SDXL model. The VAE (Kingma, 2013) remains frozen throughout the training
      process. The corresponding image features are input to the diffusion model through the cross-
772
      attention mechanism, replacing the original text embedding input. We train the decoders using
773   AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov, 2017) with a maximum learning rate of 8e-5, using linear learning
774   rate decay and a gradient clipping value of 1.0. The training batch size is 1,024. The training steps
775   for the five features are 40, 000, 30, 000, 20, 000, 15, 000, and 10, 000 respectively, with features
776   containing more visual tokens using longer training steps. We use noise off value of 0.1 and random
777   drop of 10% of the input image to blank image for classifier-free guidance.
778
779   A.2     MLLM T RAINING
780
781
      A.2.1    T RAINING O BJECTIVE
782   PUMA employs a unified framework with supervision on both text tokens and image features. For
783   text tokens, we use cross-entropy classification loss, while for image features, we adopt MSE re-
784   gression loss. To balance the contribution of text and image outputs, we apply a loss ratio of 0.02 for
785   text and 1.0 for image features. Within the image feature regression loss, we use different ratios for
786   the progressively generated 5 scales of image features (f4 , f3 , f2 , f1 , and f0 ), with ratios of 1024.0,
787
      512.0, 64.0, 8.0, and 1.0 respectively. This scaling compensates for the varying number of tokens
      at each feature scale, with larger ratios for scales with fewer tokens. The training loss objective
788
      remains consistent across both the pretraining and instruction tuning phases.
789
790
      A.2.2    P RETRAINING DATASET D ETAILS
791
792   During PUMA’s pretraining phase, we utilize a diverse set of datasets including Laion-2B (Schuh-
793   mann et al., 2022), Laion-Aesthetics (Burger, 2023), GRIT (Peng et al., 2023), The Pile (Gao et al.,
794   2020), OCR-VQA-200K (Mishra et al., 2019), and LLaVAR (Zhang et al., 2023b). For the image-
795   text pair data in Laion-2B, Laion-Aesthetics, and GRIT, we randomly assign 50% of the samples
      to text-to-image training and 50% to image-to-text training, fostering both image generation and
796
      understanding capabilities. We employ center crop as the primary image augmentation technique.
797   To train on the GRIT dataset for object grounding, we append 224 additional position tokens to
798   the MLLM’s codebook, representing object positions with bounding box coordinates [x min,
799   y min, x max, y max]. We construct the training sequences by appending the tokens <s>
800   and </s> to denote the beginning and end of each sequence. At the beginning and end of each
801   image feature sequence, we include the special tokens [IMG] and [/IMG] to indicate the visual
802   position.
803
804   A.2.3    P RETRAINING S ETTINGS
805
      We conduct pretraining for 100K steps using the AdamW optimizer with a batch size of 2048. The
806   maximum learning rates are set to 1e-4 for the projector and 3e-5 for the LLaMA backbone. We
807   employ a 2,000-step warm-up period, cosine learning rate decay, and gradient clipping at 5.0 during
808   pretraining. To optimize memory usage and computational efficiency, training is accelerated using
809   DeepSpeed ZeRO Stage 3. The entire pretraining process is carried out on 256 NVIDIA V100 GPUs
      over a period of 10 days.

      A.2.4    I NSTRUCT T UNING S ETTINGS
811
812   High-quality Text-to-Image Generation: We utilize Laion-Aesthetics (Burger, 2023) and JourneyDB
813   (Sun et al., 2024a) with a data ratio 1:1 to instruct tune the text-to-image generation model based on
814   the previous pretraining checkpoint. We use training batch size 2048 and train for 20,000 steps with
815
      the max learning rate 1e-5, warm up 1,000 steps, and cosine learning rate decay. Random crop with
      fixed aspect ratio is adopted as the image augmentation.
816
817   Precise Image Manipulation: We train the image manipulation task with SEED-Edit Ge et al.
818   (2024a). It contains seven different operations: background alteration, comprehensive image
819
      changes, style alteration, object removal, object addition, localized modifications, and color/texture
      alterations. We train with batch size 1024 and train for 10,000 steps. The max learning rate is
820
      1e-5, warm-up is 500 steps, and cosine learning rate decay is adopted. We apply random crop
821   with fixed aspect ratio on the accordingly input image and output image. The sequence of the image
822   manipulation sample is like “<s>[IMG]embedding of origin image[/IMG]instruct
823   editing prompt[IMG]embedding of edited image[/IMG]</s>”.
824
      Conditional Image Generation: We train on the subset of MultiGen-20M dataset (Qin et al.,
825   2023) including canny-to-image, image inpainting, and colorization. We use the training batch
826   size 1, 024 and train for 20, 000 steps. The max learning rate is 1e-5, warm-up is 500 steps, and
827   cosine learning rate decay is adopted. We apply center crop as the image augmentation. The
828   sequence of the conditional image generation is like “<s>[IMG]embedding of origin
829   image[/IMG]instruct conditional generation prompt[IMG]embedding of
830   edited image[/IMG]</s>”. The “instruct conditional generation prompt”
      contains the caption of the target image and with a 50% probability contain the task instruction like
831
      “Please convert the canny image to a natural image”.
832
833   Image Understanding: We train image understanding task on the subset of LLaVA-OneVision (Li
834   et al., 2024a) and Cambrain (Tong et al., 2024). Data about math/reasoning and cross-duplicated
      data in the two datasets are removed. We train with the batch size 512 and train all data for 1
835
      epoch. The max learning rate is 1e-5 with the warm-up 500 steps. Cosine learning rate decay is
836
      adopted. We apply resizing as the image augmentation. Supervision is only applied to the output
837   text tokens. We use the system message “A chat between a curious user and an
838   artificial intelligence assistant. The assistant gives helpful,
839   detailed, and polite answers to the user’s questions.”

      B     E VALUATION D ETAILS
842
843   B.1     I MAGE R ECONSTRUCTION E VALUATION
844
845   To evaluate the reconstruction performance of different scales of features and our baselines, we use
846   the ImageNet validation set, comprising 50,000 images. Each image is resized to a rectangular
847   shape before being input into each image encoder. We assess reconstruction precision by computing
848
      PSNRr and LPIPSr , which measure the difference between the reconstructed image and the original
      image.
849
850   Given the inherent randomness in the decoders, we measure reconstruction diversity by reconstruct-
851   ing each original image twice using different random seeds. We then calculate PSNRd and LPIPSd
852
      to quantify the difference between these two reconstructed images. Higher diversity is beneficial for
      downstream tasks such as text-to-image generation.
853
854   For PSNR and LPIPS evaluations, we use a resolution of 256 × 256 to align with the evaluation
855   settings in previous works. For LPIPS evaluation specifically, we employ AlexNet as the feature
856
      extractor.
857
858   B.2     T EXT- TO - IMAGE G ENERATION E VALUATION
859
      We evaluate text-to-image generation on the COCO 30K validation set (Lin et al., 2014). We use
860   CLIP-I and CLIP-T scores to measure the consistency between the generated image and the ground
861   truth image and caption, respectively. CLIP-Base-32 serves as the feature extractor for these metrics.
862   To assess generation diversity, we calculate LPIPSd between two images generated using the same
863   input prompt but different random seeds. The LPIPSd measurement details are consistent with those
      described in Sec. B.1.

892   C     A BLATION OF D IFFERENT S CALE F EATURES AS I NPUT ON I MAGE
893         U NDERSTANDING TASK
894
895   Given that PUMA adopts a unified multi-granular image feature as both input and output for the
896   MLLM backbone, we conducted an ablation study to investigate the influence of different scales of
897   image feature input on image understanding tasks. For a fair comparison, we adopted the standard
898   LLaVA-1.5-7B pretraining and finetuning setting, only changing the image encoder to a 224-input
899   CLIP-Large with different granularities of features.
900   Tab. 5 presents the results of this ablation study. The findings demonstrate that finer-grained features
901   generally lead to better performance in image understanding tasks. Notably, utilizing all image
902   features from f4 to f0 (the PUMA setting) achieves comparable performance to using all 256 visual
903   tokens of the finest scale (f0 ). These results validate that the unified visual input and output format
904   of PUMA provides a robust foundation of visual features for image understanding tasks, effectively
905
      balancing performance across different granularities.
906

908   D     S ELECTION OF 5 S CALE F EATURES IN T EXT- TO - IMAGE G ENERATION
909
910   PUMA generates images at 5 granularity levels, allowing users to select the output that best meets
911   their requirements. In our evaluation of diverse text-to-image generation, we produce 5 image out-
912   puts for each input prompt, corresponding to the 5 feature scales. To assess performance, we select
913   the image with the highest CLIP-I and CLIP-T scores among the 5 outputs and compute the average
914   maximum value. Tab. 6 presents the CLIP-I and CLIP-T scores for each of the 5 feature scales.
915   The results demonstrate that different granularity levels excel in various aspects of image genera-
916   tion. Notably, the ability to select the best output from multiple scales (PUMA 5-scale Max) yields
917   significantly improved CLIP-I and CLIP-T scores compared to any single scale, highlighting the
      advantage of PUMA’s multi-granular approach.
