009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large Vision Language Models (LVLMs) are increasingly integral to healthcare
012            applications, including medical visual question answering and imaging report
013            generation. While these models inherit the robust capabilities of foundational Large
014            Language Models (LLMs), they also inherit susceptibility to hallucinations—a
015            significant concern in high-stakes medical contexts where the margin for error
016            is minimal. However, currently, there are no dedicated methods or benchmarks
017            for hallucination detection and evaluation in the medical field. To bridge this
018            gap, we introduce Med-HallMark, the first benchmark specifically designed for
019            hallucination detection and evaluation within the medical multimodal domain. This
020
               benchmark provides multi-tasking hallucination support, multifaceted hallucination
               data, and hierarchical hallucination categorization. Furthermore, we propose the
021
               MediHall Score, a new medical evaluative metric designed to assess LVLMs’
022
               hallucinations through a hierarchical scoring system that considers the severity and
023            type of hallucination, thereby enabling a granular assessment of potential clinical
024            impacts. We also present MediHallDetector, a novel Medical LVLM engineered for
025            precise hallucination detection, which employs multitask training for hallucination
026            detection. Through extensive experimental evaluations, we establish baselines
027            for popular LVLMs using our benchmark. The findings indicate that MediHall
028            Score provides a more nuanced understanding of hallucination impacts compared to
029            traditional metrics and demonstrate the enhanced performance of MediHallDetector.
030            We hope this work can significantly improve the reliability of LVLMs in medical
031
               applications. All resources of this work have been released.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
035
      Large Vision Language Models (LVLMs) Liu et al. (2023b); Dai et al.; Li et al. (2023b); gpt (2023)
036
      inherit the powerful world knowledge of Large Language Models (LLMs) and integrate visual
037   information, enabling them to tackle complex visual-language tasks. However, they also inherit the
038   common problem of hallucinations, where models generate information that is irrelevant or factually
039   incorrect compared to the input. In LVLMs, the hallucination phenomenon is further exacerbated by
040   the lack of visual feature extraction capability, misalignment of multimodal features, incorporation of
041   additional information, and et.al.
042
      Unlike general applications, where hallucinatory contents are somewhat tolerant, hallucinations
043   in the medical domain can disastrously mislead clinical diagnosis or decision-making. Therefore,
044   mitigating medical hallucinations in LVLMs is of paramount importance. However, compared with
045   the significant concentration given to hallucination problems in the general domain, the medical
046   domain so far does not even have a specific method and benchmark for detecting hallucinations,
047   which severely hinders the development of the medical capacity of LVLMs and results in a scarcity
048   of Medical LVLMs (Med-LVLMs) or LVLMs with great medical competence.
049   In the current rare series of Med-LVLMs Li et al. (2023a); Lau et al. (2018); Thawkar et al. (2023), they
050   still face two major problems: benchmark and evaluation method. From the benchmark dimension, the
051   evaluation of the medical capabilities of existing LVLMs is still performed on outdated benchmarks
052   Johnson et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2021); Lau et al. (2018); Wang et al. (2017); Pelka et al. (2018). The
053   problem of data leakage during pre-training of large models makes the results obtained from testing
      on these traditional benchmarks no longer reliable. Furthermore, these traditional medical datasets

054
      either have very short answers or unstructured image reports, making a direct evaluation of LVLM
055   outputs extremely difficult, as the outputs are typically well-ordered long texts.
056
057   From the evaluation dimension, traditional NLP task metrics such as METEOR, BLEU and et al.
058
      often fail to directly reflect the factual correctness of a language model’s output, typically measuring
      only shallow similarities to the ground truth. Accuracy, while indicating whether generated content is
059
      correct, evaluates at a coarse semantic level and cannot distinguish between degrees of hallucinations
060
      in the output. Existing methods like CHAIR Rohrbach et al. (2018) and POPE Li et al. (2023c),
061   designed for general LVLM hallucination evaluation, are limited to ’object hallucinations’ in general
062   domains and cannot accommodate the multi-layered complexities of hallucinations in the medical
063   field. Furthermore, they are often constrained to fixed benchmarks or specific types of questions.
064
      To address these issues and enable researchers to evaluate LVLMs’ medical outputs reasonably, we
065
      propose solutions from three dimensions: data, evaluation metrics and detection methods. Firstly,
066
      we introduce a hierarchical categorization of hallucinations specific to the medical domain and
067   develop Med-HallMark, the first benchmark for hallucination detection in medical multimodal fields
068   which provides multi-tasking hallucination support, multifaceted hallucination data, and hierarchical
069   hallucination categorization. Furthermore, we propose the MediHall Score, a new evaluation metric
070   specifically designed for the medical domain, which calculates the hallucination score of the LVLM
071   outputs through hierarchical categorization, providing an intuitive numerical representation of the
072   rationality of the medical texts. Finally, we present MediHallDetector, the first multimodal medical
073   hallucination detection model designed to detect hallucinations in model output texts with fine
074   granularity. It employs unique design features and customized training methods to enhance its
075
      scalability and hallucination detection capabilities. We not only provide the baseline performance
      of the most popular LVLMs with medical capacity on the Med-HallMark but also demonstrate the
076
      rationality of the medical multimodal hallucination detection method in this paper as well as the
077
      superiority of the hallucination detection model MediHallDetector.
078
079   In general, the main contributions of this paper are as follows:
080   (i) We introduce the first benchmark dedicated to hallucination detection in the medical domain,
081   Med-HallMark, and provide baselines for various LVLMs.
082
      (ii) We propose the first hallucination detection model, MediHallDetector, and demonstrate its
083
      superiority through extensive experiments.
084
085   (iii) We present a new hallucination evaluation metric, MediHall Score, and show its effectiveness
086   relative to traditional metrics through qualitative and quantitative analysis.

      2    R ELATED WORK
090
091   Large Vision Language Models Hallucinations. Although LVLMs have shown significant capabili-
092   ties on a range of multimodal tasks, they still suffer from inevitable performance bottlenecks due
093
      to hallucinatory interference Bai et al. (2024); Ji et al. (2023). The hallucination in LVLMs stands
      for the generation of hallucinatory descriptions that are inconsistent with relevant images and user
094
      instructions, containing incorrect objects, attributes, and relationships related to the visual input, thus
095
      significantly limiting the usage of LVLMs. Previous studies have shown that even with the current
096   state-of-the-art (SOTA) LVLMs, at least 30% of the hallucinatory text still exists in the form of
097   nonexistent objects, unfaithful descriptions, and inaccurate relationships Gunjal et al. (2024). The
098   hallucination problem is further exacerbated when LVLMs are applied to medical scenarios, not only
099   because most models lack sufficient medical knowledge, but also because the medical issues are
100   more complex and fine-grained. However, there is no current work that systematically investigates
101   hallucinations in LVLMs in medical scenarios.
102   Hallucinations Detection and Evaluation. Hallucination detection Liu et al. (2023a) is an important
103   step in addressing potential hallucination disturbances in LVLMs. Current efforts Gunjal et al. (2024);
104   Xiao et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024); Zhou et al. (2023); Zhao et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024c) can
105   be categorized into two groups: approaches based on off-the-shelf tools and training-based models. In
106   the former, closed-source LLMs or visual tools can be appropriately used for hallucination assessment.
107   In contrast, training-based approaches aim to detect hallucinations incrementally from feedback.
      However, detection methods dedicated to the medical domain remain unproposed. Some work Chen

133   3         M ED -H ALL M ARK
134
135
      To investigate domain-specific hallucination dilemmas in medical texts, we present Med-HallMark,
      the first hallucination detection dataset serving the multi-modal healthcare domain. As shown in
136
      Figure 1, Med-HallMark provides a comprehensive hallucination awareness benchmark through three
137
      significant characteristics, including multi-task hallucination support, multifaceted hallucination data,
138   and hierarchical hallucination categorization.
139
140
      3.1           M ULTI - TASK H ALLUCINATION S UPPORT
141
142   Med-HallMark implements support for the following three key dimensions: medical multimodal task
143   types, hallucination detection formats and multidimensional hallucination detection. Specifically,
144   Med-HallMark covers two primary medical multimodal task types: Medical Visual Question An-
145   swering (Med-VQA) and Imaging Report Generation (IRG) tasks. The former is Question-Answer
146   (QA) pairs that examine the LVLM’s understanding of an image text from a single fine-grained
147   perspective, and the latter is the instruction pairs that require the LVLM to describe a medical image
148   from a global perspective; Med-HallMark accommodates different hallucination detection needs by
149   including both open-ended and closed-ended questions. This allows users to leverage metrics such as
150
      POPE Li et al. (2023c) and CHAIR Rohrbach et al. (2018) for closed-ended question hallucination
      detection, as well as calculate conventional BertScore and ROUGE metrics for open-ended questions
151
      from a global perspective. Our benchmark also supports hallucination detection across four refined
152
      dimensions, as illustrated in Figure 1. The questions can be categorized into four types, conventional
153   medical questions, confidence-weakening questions, counterfactual questions, and image depiction
154   questions (More details in Supplementary material) for users to measure the model’s performance
155   from different aspects.
156
157   3.2           M ULTIFACETED H ALLUCINATION DATA
158
159   Our benchmark includes multifaceted hallucination data, detailed across three primary dimensions:
160   ground truth (GT), LVLM outputs for prompts and annotations of the LVLM-generated content. The
161   GT provide a reliable standard against which to evaluate LVLM performance. The LVLM outputs
      for prompts may be correct or may contain hallucinations. Med-HallMark includes fine-grained

      annotations for all LVLM responses, detailing both the type of hallucination and its correctness. In
163   fine-grained single-dimension VQA scenarios, each response is labeled with a single hallucination
164   category. In coarse-grained multi-dimension IRG scenarios, the LVLM outputs are segmented into
165   sentences and annotated at the sentence level. This detailed annotation process allows for a thorough
166   evaluation of model performance across different medical tasks.

      3.3   H IERARCHICAL H ALLUCINATION C ATEGORIZATION
169
170   In the realm of general LVLMs, hallucinations are commonly categorized into object hallucinations,
171   attribute hallucinations and relational hallucinations Bai et al. (2024). However, this categorization
172   fails to address the unique challenges posed by medical text hallucinations adequately. For instance,
173   in the prompt, "What is the function of the organ on the bottom of this image?", an LVLM might
174   respond, "Its function is to store urine produced by the kidneys." when the ground truth is, "Digest
175   food, absorb water, excrete body waste." In this example, it is challenging to discern whether the
176   LVLM’s error stems from a misidentification of the organ at the bottom of the image or a fundamental
177   misunderstanding of stomach function. Therefore, the traditional categorization of hallucinations is
178
      neither suitable for complex problem scenarios nor for medical text hallucinations.
179   To address this gap, we propose a novel hierarchical method specifically designed for medical
180   text hallucinations, which classifies hallucinations based on the severity of their impact on clinical
181   diagnosis or decision-making, as illustrated in Figure 1. With the assistance of experienced clinicians,
182   we finely categorize the sentence-level hallucination outputs of LVLMs into five distinct levels:
183   Catastrophic Hallucinations: These involve grossly incorrect judgments, such as misjudging the
184   global health status of the image, misidentifying organs, fabricating organs, fabricating pathologies or
185   lesions on "normal" images, or making incorrect descriptions of the image based on previous errors.
186
      Critical Hallucinations: These generally involve incorrect descriptions of organ functions or
187
      pathological categories, fabricating "other types of lesions" on "abnormal" images, resulting in
188   "misanalyses" or "omissions", and incorrect descriptions of the causes of pathologies.
189
190   Attribute Hallucinations: These manifest as incorrect judgments or descriptions of the size, shape,
191
      location, and number of organs and pathologies and affect diagnostic accuracy to some extent.
192   Prompt-induced Hallucinations: These hallucinations are induced by prompts containing confused
193   information, often arising from a lack of plausibility or factuality in the prompt and testing the
194   model’s robustness in specific contexts.
195   Minor Hallucinations: These are often related to judgments about the modality of medical images
196   and how they are collected, which do not seriously affect clinical diagnosis and treatments.

      4   M EDI H ALL S CORE
247
248
      In traditional NLP tasks, metrics often fail to directly reflect the factual correctness of a language
      model’s output, typically measuring only shallow similarities to the ground truth. Accuracy, while
249
      indicating whether generated content is correct, evaluates at a coarse semantic level and cannot
250
      distinguish between degrees of hallucinations in the output. Existing methods like CHAIR and POPE,
251   designed for general LVLM hallucination evaluation, are limited to ’object hallucinations’ in general
252   domains and cannot accommodate the multi-layered complexities of hallucinations in the medical
253   field. Furthermore, they are often constrained to fixed benchmarks or specific types of questions.
254
      This fine-grained metric MediHall Score is based on the hierarchical categorization of medical text
255
      hallucinations introduced in subsection 3.3, which evaluates hallucinations at a fine-grained level and
256
      considers two different dimensions of the evaluation scenarios: Med-VQA tasks and IRG tasks.
257
258   For Med-VQA tasks, where the LVLM centres on the question and the response is from a fine-grained
259   dimension, the MediHall Score assesses the entire answer to determine the hallucination category
260
      and calculates the corresponding score. In IRG tasks, the LVLM’s response typically encompasses
      descriptions from multiple dimensions, with each sentence potentially containing different types of
261
      hallucinations. As illustrated in Figure 1, the MediHall Score evaluates hallucinations at the sentence
262
      level and aggregates these to compute the overall score for the entire response. Hallucination scores
263   are assigned based on the identified category: Catastrophic Hallucinations (Hc = 0.0), Critical
264   Hallucinations (Hcr = 0.2), Attribute Hallucinations (Ha = 0.4), Prompt-induced Hallucinations
265   (Hp = 0.6), Minor Hallucinations (Hm = 0.8), and Correct Statements (Hs = 1.0).
266
      For fine-grained Med-VQA scenarios, the hallucination category and corresponding score Hi are
267
      determined for each answer. The MediHall Score for a single answer is thus Hanswer = Hi .
268
269   In coarse-grained IRG scenarios, the score is calculated by averaging the hallucination scores of
      all sentences within a report. Let n be the number of sentences and Hj be the hallucination score

306   Destination Setup: We require the MediHallDetector M to be able to classify its hallucination levels
307   based on the input image I, the original prompt P , the LVLM answer A and the ground truth GT ,
308   which can be denoted as Htype ←  − M (I, P, A, GT ). This destination brings about four requirements
309   for the model: (1). It must accurately differentiate between various levels of hallucinations. (2). It
310   should follow instructions correctly. (3). It must categorize hallucinations in LVLM outputs based on
311   the medical images. (4). The model must maintain flexibility to adapt to different scenarios. These
312   requirements guided the design of the MediHallDetector.

317   LLaVA1.5-7BLiu et al. (2024) to leverage its robust foundational capabilities.

320   hallucination evaluation instruction pair data. Traditional medical image-text task data, sourced from
321   SLAKE, VQA-RAD, MIMIC-Test and OpenI datasets shown in Figure 1, helps adapt the general
322   LVLM to the medical domain. Med-HallMark data improves the model’s robustness and prevents

456   As shown in Figure 3, regardless of whether the LVLM’s answer A aligns with the ground truth GT ,
457   the METEOR metric shown in Table 4 fails to directly reflect this alignment.
458   ROUGE score evaluates the presence of matching n-grams or subsequences between two texts.
459   ROUGE-1/2/L matches single words, bigrams, and the longest common subsequence, respectively.
460   Although this can detect some content alignment between A and GT , it is prone to extreme cases. For
461   example, in the confidence-weakening question shown in Figure 3(b), the model correctly identifies
462   the largest organ in the image as the lung. However, the A "Lung." fails to match "lung" in GT due
463   to the punctuation, resulting in a failed direct match. Additionally, because A contains only one
464   word, ROUGE-2/L cannot be computed. BLEU has similar issues; without any shared n-grams or
465
      subsequences between A and GT, BLEU also scores zero. While BLEU does account for significant
      length differences between A and GT, making it somewhat more versatile than ROUGE, it still weakly
466
      measures factual correctness.
467
468   BertScore mitigates some of the shortcomings of ROUGE and BLEU but still does not intuitively
469   reflect the factual accuracy or degree of hallucination in medical texts. In ID1/2 where the LVLM
470   answers are entirely correct, the BertScore (%) is 66.73 and 46.11, respectively, indicating a significant
471
      and unwarranted disparity. In contrast, ACC directly shows the correctness of A but is only suitable for
      evaluating short, fine-grained texts. When A includes complex, multi-dimensional content or belongs
472
      to long texts, ACC fails to intuitively measure the LVLM output. For instance, in counterfactual
473
      questions, while the LVLM recognizes that the image is a chest X-ray, it does not explicitly state the
474   absence of kidneys, failing to address all aspects of the question. Hence, evaluating the correctness of
475   such a response solely based on ACC is inadequate. In IRG tasks, where the model needs to analyze
476   image content from various dimensions, mere correctness does not capture the model’s judgment of
477   factuality across all dimensions.
478
      In comparison, MediHall Score calculates metrics based on hallucination detection models that
479
      classify hallucination levels according to image facts and textual annotations. The calculation method
480   varies according to the requirements of different scenarios. Under conventional and confidence-
481   weakening questions, if A aligns perfectly with the facts, the MediHall Score is 1.0. For counterfactual
482   questions, the model’s response is affected by the inherent confusion of the question. Thus, even
483   though the answer is not comprehensive, it is categorized as a prompt-induced hallucination, yielding
484   a MediHall Score of 0.6. In the multi-dimensional IRG scenario, MediHall Score evaluates sentence-
485   level hallucinations and aggregates these scores to derive the final score for the response. More
      examples are provided in the supplementary materials.

      6.3        E VALUATIONS OF THE M EDI H ALL D ETECTOR ’ S P ERFORMANCE
496
497   To demonstrate the superiority of MediHallDetector as a hallucination detection model, we uniformly
498   sampled 300 inferences from InstructBLIP-13b, LLaVA1.5-13b, and mPLUG-Owl2 across the four
499   tasks in Med-HallMark. These samples were manually annotated for hallucination types following
500   the construction methods outlined in Section 5, forming a test set representative of human preferences
501   for hallucination detection models.
502   Comparison of different hallucination detection models: We compared our model’s performance
503   with two of the most powerful LLMs: GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 in the context of medical hallucination
504   hierarchy. As depicted in the radar chart in Figure 2(c), the chart illustrates the alignment of detection
505   models with human preferences across six different hallucination levels. GPT-3.5 predominantly clas-
506   sified responses as Critical and Attribute hallucinations, and only correctly identified approximately
507   1.14% of these instances, indicating a poor understanding of the nuanced definitions of different
508   hallucination levels and an inability to reasonably assess the correctness of A in more complex
509
      hallucination classification scenarios. While GPT-4 performed better at detecting the correctness of
      A in complex prompts, they still struggled with the hierarchical categorization of hallucinations. This
510
      was particularly evident in distinguishing prompt-induced hallucinations.
511
512   Figure 2(b) compares MedHallDetector with GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 across two dimensions: evaluation
513   time and multi-round evaluation consistency. The results show that GPT models tend to produce
514   varying conclusions in each evaluation, whereas MedHallDetector maintains a high consistency
515
      rate of 93.88% across three evaluation rounds. Additionally, MedHallDetector achieves the shortest
      average inference time per evaluation.
516
517   Ablation studies of different SFT methods: We conducted ablation studies to evaluate the perfor-
518   mance of MediHallDetectors SFT with different strategies, comparing their ACC (%) and Recall (%)
519   against human preferences across various hallucination categories. The results are summarized in
520   Table 5. In this study, a-f represent the following configurations: baseline model, fine-tuning only
521
      the connector, using only traditional task data, using only instruction data, using only MedihallMark
      data, excluding traditional task data, and models fine-tuned with different data at each of three stages,
522
      respectively. From these experiments, we derived the following insights: (1). When fine-tuning
523
      only the connector with all data, the model fails to adapt effectively to the medical domain. (2).
524   Performing SFT in a single phase with data from three different tasks allows each type of training
525   data to effectively contribute, leading to incremental improvements in MediHallDetector’s perfor-
526   mance. (3). Sequentially using different task data for SFT across multiple stages is unnecessary.
527   Instead, mixing different task data in a single SFT phase maximizes the performance enhancement of
528   MediHallDetector.

667   Conventional Medical Questions: In this paper, conventional medical questions refer to the original
668   questions we constructed based on the original medical image constructs. Conventional questions
669   ask information about the image from a single dimension and are usually clearly expressed without
670   any confrontational or model-confusing vocabulary, such as those illustrated in Figure 3 of the main
671   paper as well as in Figure 10.
672   Confidence-weakening Questions: Confidence-weakening questions are constructed based on
673   Qtrue′ , as introduced in Section 3.4 of the main paper. The purpose of these questions is to add
674   prefixes that reduce the model’s confidence, encouraging it to respond more cautiously and thus test
675   its inferential abilities under conditions of uncertainty. We manually created 10 different confidence-
676   weakening prefixes, as illustrated in Figure 4. Each confidence-weakening question is formed by
677   randomly combining Qtrue′ with one of these prefixes.
678   Counterfactual Questions: Counterfactual questions present prompts that typically include premises
679   contrary to the factual content of the image. These counterfactual premises often involve incorrect
680   descriptions of various dimensions of a medical image, such as shape, size, location, number, or
681   pathology. The questions are then based on these incorrect descriptions. As illustrated in Figure 3 of
682   the main paper and Figure 12, counterfactual questions can be understood as adversarial descriptions
683   designed to test whether the model can detect discrepancies between the prompt and the fundamental
684   facts depicted in the image. This type of question evaluates the model’s ability to search for image
685   features based on textual information and determine whether the model truly understands the image
686
      rather than merely fitting the training data domain.
687   In this study, we introduce a method for rapidly constructing counterfactual question-answer pairs.
688   Specifically, we leverage GPT-4’s in-context learning capabilities to generate these pairs without
689   using image information. By incorporating the original questions and their correct answers into
690   instructions, as shown in the prompt context in Figure 5, GPT-4 automatically generates counterfactual
691   question-answer pairs. These pairs, denoted as Qcounter and GTcounter , are subsequently reviewed
692
      and verified by human annotators to ensure accuracy.
693   Image Depiction Questions (IRG): The medical image report generation (IRG) task can be regarded
694   as a type of image depiction question. In this study, we employ five different manually designed
695   instructions to guide the model in performing IRG tasks, as illustrated in Figure 7. These instructions
696   are crafted to ensure that the model provides comprehensive descriptions of the medical images.
697   For image sampling, in the original dataset with the medical reports, many of the images were of low
698   quality, resulting in a report that could not accurately describe the corresponding medical images in
699   detail. Therefore, we performed the following image sampling method in this dataset: (1) Removing
700   images with lower resolution; (2) Removing images with smaller image sizes; (3) Selecting 1,800
701   high-quality data evenly from different patients in different years to avoid the source of the data being
      concentrated within a particular year.

759     Now you are an intelligent AI assistant evaluating the medical performance of a large visual language model (LVLM) in a medical multimodal
       question and answer task, and you need to judge the correctness of the LVLM outputs as well as the type of hallucinations based on the image, the
760
       question, the response of the LVLM as well as the ground-truth of the image-question and answer pairs (if there are any). The way in which the
761    hierarchy of the hallucinations is classified is as follows:
762    1. **Catastrophic Hallucinations**: Mostly wrong <judgments>, usually involving misjudging the global health status of the image, misidentifying
763    organs, fabricating organs, fabricating pathologies or lesions on "normal" images, or making incorrect descriptions of the image based on previous
       errors, which typically have disastrous impacts on clinical decision-making.
764
765    2. **Critical Hallucinations**: Typically involving incorrect <descriptions> of organ functions or pathological categories, fabricating "other types
       of lesions" on "abnormal" images, resulting in "misanalyses" or "omissions", and incorrect descriptions of the causes of pathologies; these
766    hallucinations are serious but slightly less severe than catastrophic hallucinations, still significantly affecting clinical diagnosis and decision-making.
767    3. **Attribute Hallucinations**: Manifest as incorrect judgments or descriptions of the size, shape, location, and number of organs and pathologies,
768    affecting diagnostic accuracy to some extent but not resulting in disastrous impacts.

769    4. **Prompt-induced Hallucinations**: This type of hallucination is manifested in the input model of the prompt contains certain interference
       information, can be divided into two kinds: the first is the input contains a prefix that interferes with the degree of confidence of the model; the other
770    is the prompt contains the description of the factual content of the image is opposite to that of the model caused by misleading. These types of
771    illusions are usually caused by a lack of plausibility or factuality in the prompt, and examine the robustness of the model in a particular context.

772    5. **Minor Hallucinations **: These hallucinations are often manifested as judgements about the modality of medical images, the way they are
773    collected, and they do not have serious consequences for clinical diagnosis and treatment.

774    6. **Correct Statements**: No hallucinations are present; the statement semantically matches the ground truth.
