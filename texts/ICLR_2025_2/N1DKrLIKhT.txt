009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Monotonic multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) are crucial in applications requiring
012            interpretable and trustworthy machine learning models. This particularly applies                 FIX
013            to domains, in which decisions must adhere to specific input-output relationships.
014            Traditional approaches that build monotonic MLPs with universal approximation
015            guarantees often rely on constrained weights and bounded activation functions,
016            which suffer from optimization issues. In this work, we prove that non-negative
017            constrained weights MLPs with activations that saturate on alternating sides are
018
               universal approximators for the class of monotonic functions. Given this new re-                 FIX
               sult, we show that non-positive constrained weights MLPs with convex monotone
019
               activations, contrary to their non-negative constrained counterpart, are universal
020
               approximators. Despite such guarantees, we also show that such classes of MLPs
021            are hard to optimize. Therefore, we propose a novel parameterization that elim-
022            inates the need for weight constraints. This approach allows the networks to dy-                 FIX
023            namically adjust activations based on weight signs, which enhances optimization
024            stability and performance. Experiments demonstrate that our approach maintains
025            theoretical guarantees and outperforms existing monotonic architectures in ap-                   FIX
026            proximation accuracy.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Monotonic neural networks represent a pivotal shift in deep learning. They bridge the gap between         FIX
032   high-capacity non-linear models and the need for interpretable, consistent outputs in various ap-
033
      plications. Monotonic MLPs preserve input-output monotonic relationships, which makes them
      particularly suitable for domains that require justified and transparent decisions (Gupta et al., 2016;
034
      Nguyen & Martı́nez, 2019). In general, the enforcement of constraints on the model architecture
035
      guarantees certain desired properties, such as fairness or robustness.                                    FIX
036
037   Furthermore, explicitly designing the model with inductive biases that exploit prior knowledge has
038   been shown to be fundamental for efficient generalization (Dugas et al., 2000; Milani Fard et al.,
039
      2016; You et al., 2017). For this reason, the use of monotonic networks can both help for perfor-
      mance improving (Mitchell, 1980) and for data efficiency (Veličković, 2019).                            FIX
040
041   Recent works in this field usually fall into one of the following two categories: ‘soft monotonicity’
042   and ‘hard monotonicity’. Soft monotonicity employs optimization constraints (Gupta et al., 2019;
043   Sill & Abu-Mostafa, 1996), usually as additional penalty terms in the loss. This class of approaches
044   benefits from its simple implementation and cheap computation. They exploit the power of Multi-           FIX
045
      Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) to be able to approximate arbitrary functions. However, they often suffer        FIX
      from in-distribution guarantees since penalties are usually applied to dataset samples. For this rea-
046
      son, they struggle to generalize the constraint out-of-distribution. Hard monotonicity instead gives
047
      guarantees by construction by imposing constraints in the model architecture (Wehenkel & Louppe,
048   2019; Nolte et al., 2023). Such guarantees usually come at the cost of effectiveness, leading to
049   vanishing gradient dynamics due to the usage of sigmoid-like activations or dead neuron dynamics
050   due to the usage of ReLU6. The simplest way to do so is to constrain the MLP weights to be non-           FIX
051   negative and to use monotonic activations (Daniels & Velikova, 2010). The proposed methods in the         FIX
052   literature that exploit this parametrization (Daniels & Velikova, 2010; Wehenkel & Louppe, 2019)
053   require the usage of bounded activations, such as sigmoid and hyperbolic tangent. Even though the
      usage of bounded activations with MLPs with constrained weights allows proving their universal ap-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      proximation abilities for monotonic functions,they are also well-known to be hard to optimize due         FIX
055   to vanishing gradients. This shortcoming is even more evident in monotonic NNs with non-negative          FIX
056   weights, where bounded activations make the initialization even more crucial for optimization. As
057   discussed in Section 4.1, poor initialization may lead to saturated activations at the beginning of
058   training, thus slowing it down significantly. Indeed, most recent advances in NNs use activations         FIX
059   in the family of rectified linear functions, such as the popular ReLU activation (Vaswani, 2017; He       FIX
060   et al., 2016). However, ReLU activations are problematic for non-negative-weight MLPs as they can
061   only approximate convex monotonic functions (Daniels & Velikova, 2010; Mikulincer & Reichman,
062   2022). Indeed, any non-negative-weight MLP that uses a convex activation can only approximate             FIX
063
      convex functions, which severely limits its application. For this reason, many approaches in the          FIX
      literature still rely on including bounded activations like sigmoid, which are known to be universal
064
      approximators for the class of monotonic functions (Daniels & Velikova, 2010).                            NEW
065
066   The primary aim of this work is to extend the theoretical basis of monotonic-constrained MLPs,
067   by showing that using activations that saturate on one side, such as ReLU, it is still possible to
068   achieve universal approximation. To showcase that these new findings are not just theoretical tools,
069
      we create a new architecture that only uses saturating activations with comparable performances to
      state-of-the-art architectures. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
070
071          • We show that constrained MLPs that alternate left-saturating and right-saturating mono-
072            tonic activations are universal approximators for the class of monotonic functions. We also
073            demonstrate that this can be achieved with a constant number of layers, which matches the
074            best-known bound for threshold-activated networks.                                               FIX
075
             • Contrary to the non-negative-constrained formulation, we prove that an MLP with 2n layers
076            (n ≥ 2), non-positive-constrained weights, and ReLU activation is a universal approxima-         FIX
077            tor. More generally, this holds true for any saturating monotonic activation.
078
             • We propose a simple parametrization scheme for monotonic MLPs that (i) can be used
079
               with saturating activations (ii) does not require constrained parameters, thus making the
080            optimization more stable and less sensitive to initialization (iii) does not require multiple
081            activations, and (iv) does not require any prior choice of alternation of any activation and
082            its point reflection.
083
084   Our approach will focus primarily on ReLU activations, which are widely used in the latest advance-
085   ments in deep learning. However, the results apply to the broader family of monotonic activations
086   that saturate on at least one side. This includes most members of the family of ReLU-like activations
087
      such as exponential, ELU (Clevert et al., 2016), SeLU (Klambauer et al., 2017), SReLU (Jin et al.,
      2016), and many more.

090   2     R ELATED WORK
091
                                                                                                                NEW
092   Monotonicity in neural network architectures is an active area of research that has been addressed
093   both theoretically and practically. Prior work can be broadly classified into two categories: archi-
094   tectures designed with built-in constraints (hard monotonicity) and those employing regularization
095   and heuristic techniques to enforce monotonicity (soft monotonicity). Our contribution falls into the
096
      former category, but without the complexity that comes from existing methods.
097
098   2.1   H ARD M ONOTONICITY
099
      Hard-monotonicity aims at building MLPs with provably monotonicity for any point of the input
100
      space. They do so by constructing the MLP so that only monotonic functions can be learned. Ini-
101
      tial attempts were Deep Lattice Networks (You et al., 2017) and methods constraining all weights
102   to have the same sign exemplify this approach (Dugas et al., 2009; Runje & Shankaranarayana,
103   2023; Kim & Lee, 2024). However, constraining the parameters to be non-negative violates the
104   original MLP formulation, thus invalidating the universal approximation theorem. Indeed, the ar-
105   chitecture’s universal abilities are proven only under the condition that the threshold function is
106   used as activation and that the network is at least 4 layers deep (Runje & Shankaranarayana, 2023).
107   Furthermore, the non-negative parameter constraint also creates issues from an initialization stand-
      point, as the assumption for popular initializers might be violated for positive semidefinite matrixes.


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Only recently, new architectures have been proposed with novel techniques: adapting the Deep
109   Lattice framework to MLPs (Yanagisawa et al., 2022), working with multiple activations (Runje
110   & Shankaranarayana, 2023), constraining the Lipschitz constant (Raghu et al., 2017). However,
111   Runje & Shankaranarayana (2023) requires the usage of multiple activations, and an a priori split
112   of the layer neurons between them, which might be sub-optimal or require additional tuning, and
113   Nolte et al. (2023) relies on very specific activations to control such property, as reported by the
114   authors. In contrast, our work aims to overcome these drawbacks by enhancing flexibility without
115   compromising the monotonicity constraint.
116
117   2.2   S OFT M ONOTONICITY
118
119
      Soft-monotonicity aims to build monotonic MLPs by working on the training instead of architecture,
      either using heuristics or regularizations. Techniques such as the point-wise penalty for negative gra-
120
      dients (Gupta et al., 2019; Sill & Abu-Mostafa, 1996) and using Mixed Integer Linear Programming
121
      (MILP) for certification (Liu et al., 2020) have been proposed. These methods maintain considerable
122   expressive power but do not guarantee monotonicity. Additionally, the computational expense re-
123   quired for certifications, such as those using MILP or Satisfiability Modulo Theories (SMT) solvers,
124   can be prohibitively high.

      3     M ONOTONE MLP
127
128
      A function f : Rd → R is said to be monotone non-decreasing with respect to xi , if given
129
      x0i , x1i ∈ R, i ∈ [1, d], has the following property:
130
131                       x0i ≤ x1i ⇒ f (x1 , . . . , x0i , . . . , xd ) ≤ f (x1 , . . . , x1i , . . . , xd )    (1)
132   And similarly, a function f : Rd → R is said to be monotone non-increasing with respect to xi if:
133
                          x0i ≤ x1i ⇒ f (x1 , . . . , x0i , . . . , xd ) ≥ f (x1 , . . . , x1i , . . . , xd ).   (2)
134
135   Observation 1. Given f (x), g(x) monotonic non-decreasing, and h(x), u(x) monotonic non-
136   increasing, f ◦ g is monotonic non-decreasing, f ◦ h is monotonic non-increasing, and u ◦ h is
137   monotonic non-decreasing.
138
      In this work, we will only focus on parametrizing non-decreasing functions, as the monotonicity can
139
      be reversed by simply inverting the sign of the inputs1 .
140
141   An MLP is defined as a parametrized function fθ obtained as composition of alternating affine
142   transformations lθ(i) and non-linear activations σθ(i) :
143                                          fθ (x) = lθ(1) ◦ σθ(1) . . . σθ(n − 1) ◦ lθ(n) .                    (3)
144
145   A straightforward approach to building a provable monotonic MLP that respects Equation 1 is to
146   impose constraints on its weights and activations, forcing each term in Equation 3 to be monotonic.              FIX
147   Indeed, monotonic activations are, by definition, monotonic. Instead, for affine transformations                 FIX
148   lθ(i) (x) = W (i) x + b(i) , we only need to enforce the Jacobian to be non-negative, which is simply the
149   matrix W (i) .
150   To optimize the MLP with unconstrained gradient-based approaches, the non-negative weight con-
151   straint is obtained using reparametrization, i.e., lθ(i) (x) = g(W (i) )x + b(i) for some differentiable
152   g : R → R+ . Typical reparametrizations use absolute value or squaring.

      3.3   N ON - POSITIVE CONSTRAINED MONOTONIC MLP
325
326   Consider the simple modification to the standard constrained MLP approach described in Equation
327   3. However, instead of constraining the weights to be non-negative, they are constrained to be non-            FIX
328   positive. This simple modification might seem inconsequential. However, we will show that this
329   structure can create more expressive networks than the original, given an even number of layers or,
330   equivalently, an odd number of hidden layers. Indeed, we will show that a non-positive constrained
331   MLP with 3 hidden layers satisfies the conditions of Theorem 1, as long as the activation function
332
      saturates on at least one side. This also includes convex activations like ReLU, which provably do             FIX
      not yield universal approximators in the non-negative constrained weight setting.
333
334   Note that an MLP defined according to Equation 3 is still monotone for an even number of non-
335   positively constrained layers and monotonic activations, by Observation 1. Therefore, it is still

372
373   Historically, the first works that proposed a monotonic neural network formulation relied on the
374   fact that forcing the parameters of the network to be non-negative, specifically the matrixes W in
375   the affine transformations, combined with bounded activations, is a sufficient condition to guaran-
376   tee that the overall induced function is monotonic (Daniels & Velikova, 2010; Sill & Abu-Mostafa,
377   1996). Recently, Runje & Shankaranarayana (2023) showed a way to build effective monotonic
      MLPs with such a technique by exploiting multiple activations. However, even though using con-

      strained weights and bounded activation is easy to implement and can be optimized with any un-
379   constrained gradient optimizer, it might lead to vanishing gradient dynamics. Instead, we will show
380   how to address this issue while also tackling the necessity of alternate activations to have universal
381   approximation capabilities, working on the architecture of the MLP.
382
383   4.1   VANISHING G RADIENT IN C ONSTRAINED MLP S WITH BOUNDED ACTIVATIONS
384
385   As reported in Section 3, a naive approach to ensure monotonicity is to have monotonic activations
386   and to impose monotonicity to the weights, constraining them to be non-negative. For this rea-
387   son, such networks’ affine transformations are usually parametrized as l(x) = g(W )x + b, for some
388
      transformation g : R → R+ . Note that the bias can be any value, as it is a constant and thus does
      not affect the gradient.
389
390   Such networks employed bounded activations, like sigmoid, tanh, or ReLU6, to have convex-
391   concave activations. This peculiarity makes them very sensitive to initialization and can potentially
392   lead to vanishing gradient dynamics(Glorot & Bengio, 2010). To see why constraining weights to              FIX
393   be non-negative exacerbates this condition, consider a monotonic MLP with sigmoidal activations,
394
      initialized with random weights according to known, widely used initializers, such as Glorot, where
      each matrix is sampled from a symmetric distribution around zero with some variance. Instead, bi-
395
      ases are initialized to zero, as usually done. Let’s assume to use g(x) = |x|, but the same reasoning
396
      can be applied to any other mapping g. At this point, the MLP comprises layers of the following
397   form σ(x) = σ(|W |x + b). Now, let’s consider the second layer of such MLP. Since the first layer
398   has applied the sigmoid activation, then σ (1) (x) ∈ (0, 1). Because of this, |W (2) |σ (1) (x) will be a
399   product of all non-negative terms. Therefore, its result can become significantly large. Then, when
400   applying the sigmoid activation of the second layer, it will most likely saturate due to the large
401   positive values returned from the affine transformation. Going on with this reasoning for multiple
402   layers, such behavior will be exacerbated. Appendix A.4 shows one example of such behavior for a
403   very simple function. The same behavior occurs for ReLU6 MLPs, where the gradient might even                FIX
404   become exactly 0, and for tanh MLPs if, for example, the dataset is normalized, which is one of the
405
      most commonly used data-preprocessing.
406   One possible solution might beusing BatchNormalization layers (Ioffe, 2015). BatchNorm has al-              FIX
407   ready shown its effectiveness in tackling initialization and optimization problems. Indeed, Batch-
408   Norm is comprised only of the following transformation:

525   6   C ONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORKS
526
527
528   In this work, we proved that MLPs with non-negative constrained weights and alternating activations
529   that saturate at least on one side are universal approximators for the class of monotonic functions.     FIX
530   In addition, we show that a specific case of such a setting is defined by a network with mono-
531
      tonic convex activation and constrained non-positive weights. We then use this result to present
      a new parametrization that relaxes the need for activation alternation and weight constraint while
532
      still allowing for monotonic convex activation, which was impossible earlier. With this parametriza-
533
      tion, the layer can choose which activation to use based on the parameters’ signs. We then use           FIX
534   our monotone fully connected layer to build MLPs, we show that we can achieve state-of-the-art
535   performances. Even though this work proves that any monotonic saturating activation can be used
536   to build monotonic MLPs, it’s still an open question whether non-saturating activations, such as
537   Leaky-ReLU, can be used to build monotonic MLPs. After that, activation must only be monotonic           FIX
538   to be used in monotonic MLPs. Furthermore, batch normalization has proven highly effective in the
539   unconstrained case. Still, it has never been used as a possible solution to the initialization problem
      for the monotonic case.

866   To showcase the effectiveness of the proposed method to the bounded-activation counterpart, in
867   Figure 4 we compare them on a simple synthetic example. In particular, the models are asked to ap-
868   proximate f (x) = cos(x) + x, a simple 1D monotonic function with multiple saddle points. For this
869   reason, it is fundamental for the approximation model to be very flexible. To showcase the different
870   performances, we will test 4 models. The first model to test is an unconstrained NN, which shows
871   that an unconstrained model can learn such a function. The second model is a monotonic NN with
872
      non-negative and ReLU activations, which shows that, as shown in theory, it cannot approximate
      nonconvex function. The third model is a monotonic NN with non-negative and sigmoid activations.
873
      This model, instead, is shown to be a universal approximator for monotonic functions but suffers
874
      from vanishing gradients. Lastly, the fourth model is the proposed parametrization, specifically the
875   post-activation setting, as described in Section 4.2.

878

879
      function struggles to be optimized due to the complications of using sigmoid activations. Instead,
880

1028

1030

1031   such a category, it is not the only one, and many other widely used ReLU-like activations satisfy the
1032   minimal assumptions of Theorem 1. For this reason, we will now analyze many other activations
1033   and report whether they comply with our construction. In particular, we report in Table 3 multiple

1038   activations, it also can be used with bounded activations, such as sigmoid and tanh, but that might
1039   bring almost no additional advantage over the weight-constrained counterpart. Any activation that
1040   saturates at least one side can be used, given that it is monotonic. Still, the real advantage comes
