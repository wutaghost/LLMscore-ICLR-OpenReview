011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent studies have combined 3D Gaussian and 3D Morphable Models (3DMM)
014            to achieve real-time, high-quality rendering of controllable head avatars. Sev-
015            eral techniques have attempted to express dynamic textures in facial animation
016            when modeling 3D avatars. However, accurately capturing and displaying ex-
017            pressive appearance dynamics while maintaining temporal and spatial efficiency
018
               remains a technical challenge. To this end, we propose a novel method for 3D
               facial avatar modeling that utilizes an expressive and compact model representa-
019
               tion, capturing dynamic facial information accurately while ensuring efficiency.
020
               We encode texture-related attributes of the 3D Gaussians in the tensorial feature
021            representation. Specifically, we store color information of the neutral expression
022            in static tri-planes; and represent dynamic texture details for different expressions
023            using lightweight 1D feature lines, which are then decoded into opacity changes
024            relative to the neutral face. Experiments show that this design introduces nonlin-
025            ear expressiveness to the model, enhancing its performance, while the compact
026            representation maintains real-time rendering capabilities and significantly reduces
027            storage costs. This approach thus broadens the applicability to more scenarios.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      Photorealistic 3D avatars generation is a fundamental research topic within the field of computer
033   graphics and computer vision, encompassing a variety of applications including movies, gaming,
034   and AR/VR etc. A high-quality 3D human head avatar needs to meet several requirements: 1)
035   achieve photorealistic high-fidelity rendering from arbitrary viewpoints; 2) generate animations of
036   various expressions conveniently; and 3) ensure real-time rendering with reduced memory and stor-
037   age. However, achieving the above quality and performance poses significant technical challenges.
038
      Some early methods are based on 3D face Morphable Models (3DMM) Blanz & Vetter (1999); Thies
039   et al. (2016) to achieve 3D avatar generation, which can easily control facial expressions using
040   low-dimensional PCA coefficients, but the rendering effect lacks realism. With the development
041   of NeRF Mildenhall et al. (2021), some approaches combine NeRF and 3DMM to achieve high-
042   quality, animatable head avatars, but the volumetric rendering process of NeRF requires substantial
043   computation, making it difficult to achieve real-time performance. Recently, 3D Gaussian Splatting
044   (3DGS) Kerbl et al. (2023) has gained widespread attention for its high-quality rendering and real-
045   time performance, with some works attempting to combine 3DGS with 3DMM to achieve real-time,
046   highly realistic, and animatable head avatars. It is important to model the dynamic facial textures and
047
      details that change with expressions, but representing such details inevitably introduces additional
      time and memory costs. Some methods Ma et al. (2024) explicitly store a set of Gaussian splats for
048
      each expression, resulting in significant storage requirements. Alternatively, some methods Xu et al.
049
      (2024) employ MLPs to implicitly model dynamic textures, followed by super-resolution techniques
050   to enhance detail rendering, but at the cost of not being able to render in real-time.
051
052   To address these issues, we propose a high-fidelity 3D head avatar reconstruction method that takes
053
      multi-view face videos as inputs and outputs an animatable 3D head avatar with dynamic facial
      textures and details, while maintaining space efficiency and real-time speed.

092   2.1   DYNAMIC S CENE R EPRESENTATION
093
094   NeRF Mildenhall et al. (2021) and 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) Kerbl et al. (2023) are widely used
095   for novel view synthesis and multi-view reconstruction for static scenes. To adapt these methods for
096   dynamic scenes, most approaches reconstruct a scene at a specific moment as the canonical frame,
097   and use additional representations to capture dynamic changes. Some methods Park et al. (2021b;a);
098   Wu et al. (2024) use MLPs to model displacement relative to the canonical frame. Other methods use
099
      spatially or temporally discrete representations. To reduce the storage demands of high-dimensional
      data, compact formats like K-Plane Fridovich-Keil et al. (2023) or Tensor4D Shao et al. (2023)
100
      compress 4D space into multiple 2D planes. Low-dimensional parametric models, such as sparse
101
      control points Huang et al. (2024); Jiang et al. (2024) and parametric curves Wang et al. (2021); Li
102   et al. (2024), are also used to enhance training stability. In the fields of 3D human body and face
103   reconstruction, some mature shape prior models for the human body Loper et al. (2023) and face Li
104   et al. (2017); Paysan et al. (2009) use low-dimensional parameters to accurately describe geometry
105   and motion, making them easy to control. Many methods combine these priors with NeRF and
106   3DGS for animatable human or head avatars. However, the low-dimensional parameters limit their
107   expressiveness, and some new representations Li et al. (2023) are proposed to capture finer details.
      We will discuss head avatars reconstruction in more details in the next section.

      2.2   A NIMATABLE H EAD AVATAR
109
110   Some 3D animatable head avatar reconstruction methods Zielonka et al. (2023); Gafni et al. (2021);
111   Xu et al. (2023) use NeRF to implicitly represent geometry and take tracked 3DMM coefficients as
112   inputs. In contrast, 3DGS uses explicit geometric primitives that can be directly bound to a mesh.
113   GA Qian et al. (2024) defines Gaussian splats in the local coordinate system of the mesh triangles,
114   allowing splats to move with the mesh. However, since GA uses the same set of Gaussian splats for
115   all expressions, it fails to accurately model dynamic details that change with expressions. 3DGS-
116
      Blendshapes Ma et al. (2024) optimizes a set of 3D Gaussians splats for each blendshape, enabling
      linear interpolation of Gaussian attributes using blendshape coefficients, but this demands significant
117
      storage. Moreover, linear interpolation struggles to capture complex dynamic details. GHA Xu et al.
118
      (2024) uses a coarse mesh and two MLPs to predict geometry and color offsets, and converts 3D
119   Gaussian features into RGB images via a super-resolution module. NPGA Giebenhain et al. (2024b)
120   leverages the rich latent expression space and detailed motion prior of MonoNPHM Giebenhain et al.
121   (2024a) and employs a screen-space CNN to suppress small-scale artifacts. GHA Xu et al. (2024)
122   and NPGA Giebenhain et al. (2024a) achieve high-quality detailed rendering, but fail to achieve real-
123   time rendering speed. In contrast, we achieve high-quality rendering with dynamic details, real-time
124   speed, and compact storage simultaneously, by combining different representations including mesh,
125   triplanes and feature lines to capture different aspects of 3D animatable avatar.
126
127   2.3   C OMPACT 3D S CENE R EPRESENTATIONS FOR NOVEL VIEW SYNTHESIS .
128
129
      NeRF uses deep MLPs to model scenes, but suffers from slow inference and scalability issues for
      large, unbounded areas. Some methods Müller et al. (2022); Chan et al. (2022) split the large
130
      MLP into spatially discrete features and tiny MLP decoders, improving inference speed and training
131
      efficiency. Other approaches decompose large NeRFs into smaller ones Yang et al. (2021) to reduce
132   storage needs. 3DGS is efficient in rendering but requires storing a large number of Gaussian splats
133   with their attributes for accurate scene representation. Recent approaches address this issue by using
134   region-based vector quantization Niedermayr et al. (2024), K-means-based codebooks Navaneet
135   et al. (2023), or learned binary masks for each Gaussian. They also replace spherical harmonics
136   (SHs) with grid-based neural networks Lee et al. (2024); Zou et al. (2024) within an adjusted training
137   framework to minimize model size. In this paper, we leverage a lightweight feature line for each
138   expression blendshape, to enable a compact representation for dynamic facial textures.

140   3     M ETHOD
141
142   Our method takes multi-view face videos as inputs and outputs an animatable head avatar with
143   dynamic textures. As shown in Fig. 2, our method uses 3DGS to model the dynamic head avatars,
144   and stores large-scale geometry movements of head, head appearance in the canonical space, and
145   dynamic texture variations for each blendshape using three different structures, i.e., mesh, triplane,
146   and feature lines, respectively. 1) Geometry movements bound to mesh: For each frame in the
147   video, we track a FLAME Li et al. (2017) mesh from multi-view observations and known camera
148   parameters by a photometric head tracker Qian et al. (2024). We follow Qian et al. (2024) to generate
149   geometry attributes (including position, rotation and scaling) of splats via deformed mesh for each
150
      frame. 2) Appearance in canonical space stored in a Triplane: Instead of using SH to represent view-
      dependent color as in original 3DGS, a triplane is used to store radiance around the 3D head in the
151
      canonical space. The features sampled from the triplane, along with the view direction transformed
152
      into the canonical space, are fed into a tiny MLP decoder to obtain the color of the Gaussian. 3)
153   Dynamic textures stored in Feature Lines: We utilize a feature line per expression blendshape to
154   store dynamic textures. The tracked expression coefficients of each frame are used to interpolate the
155   feature lines. An MLP decoder then maps the features sampled from the interpolated feature line
156   into an opacity offset, which is added to canonical opacity. Finally, the aforementioned gaussian
157   attributes are combined to render the image, which is compared with the ground truth.

      3.2   A PPEARANCE IN C ANONICAL S PACE VIA T RIPLANE
217
218   In 3DGS, 48 out of the total 59 parameters for each Gaussian are used for spherical harmonics (SH,
219   up to 3 degrees) to capture view-dependent color. Based on the observation that neighboring splats
220   have similar appearance, instead of storing 48 SH parameters for each splat, we use a triplane to
221   store implicit encodings, and a tiny MLP decoder to decode the encodings to RGB colors, which
222   compresses the model size and accelerates the inference speed.
223   The triplane T consists of three orthogonal feature planes aligned with the axes: {Txy , Txz , Tyz } ∈
224   R3×nf ×nf ×nd1 , where nf × nf is the spatial resolution of the 2D feature plane, and nd1 is the
225   feature dimension. For any given position p in canonical space, the corresponding feature vector is
226   obtained by projecting the position onto the axis-aligned planes (the x − y, x − z and y − z planes),
227   interpolating to obtain the features on each feature plane and concatenating the interpolated features,
228   which is formulated as:
229
                        t(p) = interp(Txy , pxy ) ⊕ interp(Txz , pxz ) ⊕ interp(Tyz , pyz ),
230
231   where interp represents trilinear interpolation, ⊕ represents concatenation, and pxy , pxz , pyz refer
232   to the projected position on each plane.
233   Note that the triplane is defined in the canonical space, which means the global space corresponding
234   to the neutral expression. Meanwhile, we call the global space corresponding to the non-neutral
235   expression in each frame as the deformed space. Given a splat with the position µ, rotation r, and
236   scale s of defined in the local space, it can be transformed into the canonical space and deformed
237   space of the current frame based on the canonical transformation (Rc , Tc , kc ) and the deformed
238   transformation (Rd , Td , kd ) respectively. The local coordinate system can also serve as a bridge
239   to transform between the deformed space and the canonical space. The transformation from view
240
      direction in the deformed space (denoted as vd ) to view direction in the canonical space (denoted as
      vc ) can be formulated as:
241
                                                  vc = Rc Rd−1 vd .
242
243   Finally a tiny MLP decodes t(p) and vc into RGB value c, which we use as the first three compo-
244   nents of degree-1 spherical harmonics for 3DGS rendering. Additionally, since the majority of facial
245   information is concentrated in the frontal view, with less information available from the side views,
246   we utilize a larger feature dimension in the Txy while employing lower dimension for Txz , Tyz to
247   achieve storage compression.

294   3.4   T RAINING
295
      Class-balanced Sampling. In the training dataset, most images exhibit small facial movements,
296
      with only a small portion showing significant movements, which makes the reconstruction of large
297   expressions less accurate. However, the distribution of the training data is unknown, and simply
298   increasing the sampling probability for images with large expressions may introduce bias against
299   images with smaller expressions. Instead, we propose a class-balanced sampling method based on
300   pre-computed FLAME parameters to prioritize frames with larger expressions during training.
301
      First, we measure the similarity between two frames by comparing the differences in vertex dis-
302
      placements of the FLAME mesh. Given that different regions of the face exhibit varying degrees of
303   motion, e.g., the lips moving significantly more than the eyes, we empirically increase the weight
304   of the vertices around the eyes. We denote the FLAME mesh for frame i as Mi , and the similarity
305   score between frame i and frame j is calculated as dist(i, j) = ||Mi − Mj ||22 ∗ w, where w denotes
306   the weight of each vertex. Next, we perform spectral clustering on the similarity matrix to catego-
307   rize all frames into n classes. Finally, we randomly sample from each category, ensuring a uniform
308   sampling probability distribution between categories and equal probability for samples within each
309   category.

      4.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
360
361   We implemented the code using PyTorch and trained for 600,000 iterations using the Adam opti-
362   mizer for each subject. Both the triplane and feature lines consist of two components: the neural
363   grid feature and the MLP decoder. We trained triplane and feature line blendshapes with the same
364   learning rates, setting learning rate of feature to 1e − 3 and the MLP learning rate to 1e − 4. Each
365   plane of the triplane is 64 × 64 in size, with the feature dimension of the Txy being 64, and the
366   feature dimensions of the Txz , Tyz planes set to 32. The MLP decoder following triplane consists
367
      two-layer with 128 dimensions per layer, using ReLU as the activation function. Additionally, we
      apply position encoding to improve the resolution of view direction. We assign feature lines with a
368
      spatial resolution of 64 and feature dimension of 32 to the first 80 PCA expression bases and 16 key
369
      jaw rotation bases. The decoder for the feature lines is a two-layer MLP with 128 dimensions per
370   layer. The triplane requires 2.08M of storage, the feature line requires 2.41M, and the other Gaussian
371   attributes (including position/rotation/scaling and canonical opacity) average 5.2M per subject.

      4.5   C ONCLUSION
487
488   In this paper, we propose a 3D Gaussian Splatting head avatar modeling method that balances dy-
489   namic details capturing with real-time performance and low storage. The use of compact tensorial
490   feature representations (static triplane for canonical appearance, and lightweight 1D feature lines for
491   dynamic textures) allows for accurate expression modeling, leading to improved rendering quality.
492   Experimental results demonstrate that our approach not only enhances the expressiveness of head
493   reconstruction but also maintains real-time rendering speed and minimal storage, making it suitable
494
      for a wide range of practical applications.

      A.1   BASELINES
651
652   We conducted comparative experiments with three baseline methods: GA, GHA, and GSBlend-
653   shapes. Both GHA and our method utilize multi-view videos from the Nersemble dataset, but the
654   input resolutions differ. GHA processes 2K resolution images, while our input images are down-
655   sampled by a factor of four. To ensure fairness in testing, we first downsampled the 2K images by
656   four times and then upsampled them back to their original size as the input for GHA.
657   GSBlendshapes is a monocular facial video reconstruction method, requiring the use of a metrical
658   tracker to regress FLAME coefficients and camera parameters from the images, which serve as the
659   model’s input. In our approach, the input consists of multi-view videos along with camera parame-
660   ters and FLAME coefficients tracked from the multi-view data. To align the inputs, we concatenate
661   the multi-view videos into a single video and use the multi-view tracked FLAME coefficients in
662   place of the metrical tracker’s results. It is important to note that the metrical tracker uses the 2020
663
      version of FLAME, with two additional expression bases for describing closed eyes, while we use
      the 2023 version of FLAME.
664
665   To align the inputs, we use FLAME 2023 and the tracked coefficients from multi-view videos to
666   generate meshes, then optimize the FLAME 2020 parameters by calculating the mesh vertex posi-
667   tions loss. Note that the parameters output by the metrical tracker do not include the hair offset or
668   neck motion, so we calculate the loss using only the facial region vertices. First, we compute the
669
      shape coefficients using the neutral expression, then iteratively regress the expression coefficients,
      eye rotation, jaw rotation for each frame.
