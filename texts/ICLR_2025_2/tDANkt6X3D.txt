                                                    A BSTRACT
012
               Fill-in-the-Middle (FIM) has become integral to code language models, enabling
013            generation of missing code given both left and right contexts. However, the current
014            FIM training paradigm, which reorders original training sequences and then
015            performs regular next-token prediction (NTP), often leads to models struggling to
016            generate content that aligns smoothly with the surrounding context. Crucially, while
017            existing works rely on rule-based post-processing to circumvent this weakness,
018
               such methods are not practically usable in open-domain code completion tasks as
               they depend on restrictive, dataset-specific assumptions (e.g., generating the same
019
               number of lines as in the ground truth). Moreover, model performance on FIM tasks
020            deteriorates significantly without these unrealistic assumptions.
021            We hypothesize that NTP alone is insufficient for models to learn effective planning
022            conditioned on the distant right context, a critical factor for successful code infilling.
023            To overcome this, we propose Horizon-Length Prediction (HLP), a novel training
024            objective that teaches models to predict the number of remaining middle tokens (i.e.,
025            horizon length) at each step. HLP advances FIM with lookahead planning, enabling
026
               models to inherently learn infilling boundaries for arbitrary left and right contexts
               without relying on dataset-specific post-processing. Our evaluation across different
027
               models and sizes shows that HLP significantly improves FIM performance by up
028            to 24% relatively on diverse benchmarks, across file-level and repository-level,
029            and without resorting to unrealistic post-processing methods. Furthermore, the
030            enhanced planning capability gained through HLP boosts model performance on
031            code reasoning. Importantly, HLP only incurs negligible training overhead and no
032            additional inference cost, ensuring its practicality for real-world scenarios.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large Language Models (LLMs) trained on massive source code data have demonstrated significant
037   progress in coding-related tasks (Lozhkov et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024; DeepSeek-AI et al., 2024;
038   Hui et al., 2024). While natural language generation predominantly follows a Left-to-Right (L2R)
039   approach, Fill-in-the-Middle (FIM), or infilling, is ubiquitous in code completion scenarios. This
040   prevalence stems from the iterative nature of coding, which involves frequent edits and insertions
      rather than a single left-to-right pass (Bavarian et al., 2022; Fried et al., 2023). In an infilling task,
041
      the model is asked to generate the missing code in the middle, conditioned on both the preceding (left)
042   and the succeeding (right) contexts.
043
044   The common practice to achieve FIM capability with uni-directional auto-regressive models is
      to reorder the original sequence of prefix-middle-suffix into either prefix-suffix-middle (PSM) or
045
      suffix-prefix-middle (SPM). This reordering allows the middle part to be predicted while conditioned
046   on both left and right contexts as past tokens (Bavarian et al., 2022; Fried et al., 2023). Typically.
047   an <end_of_insertion> (i.e., <eoi>) token is appended to the end to indicate the generation
048   boundary. At training time, the prefix, middle, and suffix are determined by a random split, and the
049   reordered sequence is fed to the model for standard next-token prediction (NTP).
050   A key challenge in FIM is to seamlessly connect the generated middle to the given suffix considering
051   both fluency and semantics, a difficult task for models to learn in practice. As pointed out by the
052   previous work (Bavarian et al., 2022), though model is trained to generate <eoi> when middle ends
053   and connects to suffix, it often fails to do so at the right place during test time, resulting in generation
      that does not connect well to suffix. We hypothesize that this challenge stems from the fundamental

      difference in prediction horizon compared to standard NTP. In NTP, the model only needs to consider
055   a horizon of one token at a time. In contrast, FIM requires the model to plan for a much longer horizon,
056   i.e., the entire length of the missing middle section. This extended horizon is crucial because the model
057   must generate a sequence that not only follows the left context but also smoothly transitions to the right
058   context, which may be many tokens away. Standard NTP training does not adequately prepare models
059   for this long-horizon planning task. Consequently, models often struggle to maintain coherence over
060
      the longer sequences required in FIM, particularly when approaching the transition to the right context.
      Without effective long-horizon planning, generation frequently falters towards the end of the infill,
061
      failing to create a smooth connection with the given suffix.

150   Post-processing methods adopted by recent FIM benchmarks typically assume a certain completion

154

155   A more practical and effective solution is to let the model itself decide when to stop by learning from

237   our analysis (§2) that LLMs trained with NTP alone do not learn this task well in practice. This is likely

239   and thus infilling which typically requires planning over a much longer horizon length could not be
240   learnt effectively.
241   Horizon-Length Prediction (HLP). To mitigate this issue, we propose adding an auxiliary training
242   objective, namely horizon-length prediction (HLP), to improve the planning capabilities of LLMs over
243   long horizons. Specifically, given the hidden state of current token, the model is tasked by HLP to
244   predict the number of future tokens required to complete middle, as shown in Figure 3.
245   In detail, the FIM-style document D′ defined in Eq. (1) contains three different parts, including prefix,
246   suffix, and middle, and HLP is applied to middle part as it is the major focus of FIM tasks. Recall that
247   middle has M tokens, ranging from xP +1 to xP +M . At each position t ∈ [1,M ] in middle, HLP aims
248   to predict the number of future tokens required to complete the middle, which is M −t. Considering
249   that the context window size of LLMs can be infinite in theory and so is M −t, instead of formatting
      HLP as a classification task with a fixed set of discrete values as labels, we make it a regression problem

      Why should HLP work? Theoretically, next-token prediction (NTP) is myopic in the sense that it does
266   not take into account future constraints when predicting the immediate next token, as it only optimizes
267   for local token-by-token decisions. While this is less of a problem for left-to-right code generation, in
268   FIM there is always such a constraint that the middle part must connect smoothly to the right context.
269   Standard NTP only tackles this constraint at the very last <EOI> token. However, at test time, the <EOI>
      token can sometimes be completely missing due to accumulated errors in preceding tokens which are

      not trained with any end-of-insertion information (Gong et al., 2024). In contrast, with HLP, every
271   token gets a sense of how far away it is from connecting to the right context (i.e., global sequence-level
272   planning), leading to a more reliable closing of the infilling generation. In other words, while NTP
273   only provides a sparse signal from <EOI> token prediction at the end of generation, HLP enables a
274   consistent optimization by providing an auxiliary learning signal at every generation step.
275   Overhead Analysis. While HLP introduces the additional hlp_head during training, the number
276   of added parameters is < 0.01% of the base model, which incurs almost zero training overhead.
277   Furthermore, the additional head can be discarded during inference, leading to no inference overhead.

      6    R ELATED W ORK
487
488   Fill-in-the-Middle for Code Language Models The unprecedented success of causal language
489   models such as GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) in natural language has inspired researchers to develop
490   similar decoder-only models for programming languages. These models are trained on massive source
491   code data for applications such as code generation. While early models such as Codex (Chen et al.,
492   2021) and CodeGen (Nijkamp et al., 2023) only support Left-to-Right (L2R) generation, Fill-in-
493
      the-Middle (or infilling) has attracted increased attention because right context naturally carries an
      indispensable part of information for completing code in the middle (Fried et al., 2023; Bavarian et al.,
494
      2022). Subsequently, FIM training has become a common practice widely adopted by most code
495   LLMs, such as StarCoder (Li et al., 2023; Lozhkov et al., 2024), DeepSeek-Coder (Guo et al., 2024;
496   DeepSeek-AI et al., 2024), and Code Llama (Rozière et al., 2023).
497
      Existing models generally tackle the infilling problem by breaking a code snippet into prefix-middle-
498
      suffix, and reordering them into prefix-suffix-middle (PSM) or suffix-prefix-middle (SPM). The trans-
499   formed sequences are fed to the model during training for standard next-token prediction (NTP). We
500   point out that the infilling task cannot be effectively learned with NTP alone, as it requires planning
501   capability for the model to fluently and meaningfully connect the middle completion to the suffix
502   through forward looking during auto-regressive decoding.
503   An alternative approach is to train two language models in different directions, with one generating
504   from left to right and the other from right to left, and have the two generations meet in the middle
505   (Nguyen et al., 2023). Nevertheless, the L2R model does not have access to the right context, and vice
506   versa, which impedes holistic planning that takes into account the context from both sides.
507   Planning and Lookahead in Language Generation Standard decoder-only models are trained with
508   next-token prediction and used to sequentially predict one token at a time, conditioned only on past
509   tokens, in an auto-regressive manner. One drawback of this paradigm is that models are not aware of
510   future tokens during decoding. The token that maximizes the conditional probability at current step
511   may lead to suboptimal continuation, and consequently the model can fail to compose a fluent and
      sensible generation that meets human requirements. Various decoding techniques have been proposed
512
      to address the problem through tree search with lookahead heuristics, particularly for constrained
513   generation problems (Lu et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2024). While these methods are training-free, they
514   inevitably incur additional cost of inference complexity.
515
      Apart from those, Gloeckle et al. (2024) proposed to predict multiple tokens from a single hidden state
516
      during both training and inference, which was shown to achieve stronger performance on coding tasks
517   with no computation overhead. While multi-token prediction enhances models’ planning capability
518   within the n tokens predicted together (n ≤ 8), we argue that with a small n, the limited horizon is
519   usually insufficient for planning in the case of infilling as the connection from middle to suffix only
520   happens towards the end of the generation. In contrast, HLP adopts a global and arbitrary long horizon
521   over all future tokens by counting the remaining generation budget, which more effectively helps
522
      models to close the generation fluently with early planning.
523
524   7    C ONCLUSION
525
526   Fill-in-the-Middle is ubiquitous in code completion, and therefore, has become an important considera-
527   tion in the development of code language models. The current FIM training paradigm splits and reorders
      original training sequences (prefix-middle-suffix) into FIM-style sequences (prefix-suffix-middle/PSM
528
      or suffix-prefix-middle/SPM), and performs standard next-token prediction. However, this approach
529   frequently results in models struggling to generate content that smoothly aligns with the right context.
530   While existing FIM benchmarks frequently rely on different post-processing methods to circumvent
531   this problem, we emphasize that such methods typically require dataset-specific assumptions, which
532   are impractical in real-world scenarios. To address this limitation and enhance the infilling capability
533   of code language models, we propose Horizon-Length Prediction (HLP). HLP teaches models to
534
      predict the portion of remaining tokens at every step. Experiments across different model families
      and sizes show that HLP improves infilling performance on diverse FIM benchmarks, across file-level
535
      and repository-level, and without using any dataset-specific post-processing. Moreover, the enhanced
536   planning capability acquired through HLP training also boosts models’ performance on code reasoning
537   tasks, suggesting that HLP may broadly improve language models’ reasoning capabilities. Besides,
538   HLP is also efficient as it does not cause any inference overhead and the training overhead is negligible
539   as well. Our work marks a significant advancement in developing more effective code language models
      for real-world applications.

695   A.1    E FFECT OF HLP ON L EFT- TO -R IGHT P ERFORMANCE
696
      While HLP have significantly improved the FIM performance of LLMs, we also study its impact on the
697
      L2R code completion. To this end, we evaluate L2R performance on HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021)
698   and MBPP (Austin et al., 2021) with DeepSeek-Coder-Base 1.3B. We further employ HumanEval+
699   and MBPP+ from EvalPlus Liu et al. (2023) for more rigorous evaluation with better test coverage.
700   As shown in Table 8, with HLP applied to FIM data only (i.e., HLPFIM ), the performance on L2R
701   tasks sometimes shows a slight degradation. We hypothesize that applying HLP to middle only causes
      unbalanced training on prefix and suffix parts.

716   To mitigate such effect, we need to devise another HLP task that can be applied to L2R training (i.e.,

718   have any clear signals, as it is often possible to add additional contents (e.g., another line of code or a
719   new helper function) to the end of document fluently without any restrictions.
720
      Therefore, instead of taking the entire code file as prediction horizon, we ask the model to predict the
721
      number of future tokens required to complete current line in L2R training, which is a natural semantic
722   unit in code. Furthermore, to avoid conflicts between HLPFIM and HLPL2R , we use two independent
723   hlp_heads to let the model learn HLPFIM and HLPL2R separately. As shown in Table 8, by applying
724   HLPFIM and HLPL2R simultaneously, the performance degradation on L2R tasks is recovered, with the
725   improvement on FIM tasks largely retained. These results demonstrate the generalizable effectiveness
726   of HLP and shows the huge potential of applying the idea of HLP to more general training scenarios.
