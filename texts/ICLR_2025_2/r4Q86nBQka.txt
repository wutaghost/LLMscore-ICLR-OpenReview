009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011            In this empirical article, we introduce INNAprop, an optimization algorithm that
012            combines the INNA method with the RMSprop adaptive gradient scaling. It
013            leverages second-order information and rescaling while keeping the memory re-
014            quirements of standard DL methods as AdamW or SGD with momentum. After
015            giving geometrical insights, we evaluate INNAprop on CIFAR-10, Food101, and
016            ImageNet with ResNets, VGG, DenseNet, and ViT, and on GPT-2 (OpenWeb-
017            Text) train from scratch and with LoRA fine-tuning (E2E). INNAprop consistently
018
               matches or outperforms AdamW both in training speed and accuracy, with minimal
               hyperparameter tuning in large-scale settings. Our code is publicly available at
019
               https://github.com/innaprop/innaprop.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024
      As deep learning models grow in size, massive computational resources are needed for training,
025
      representing significant challenges in terms of financial costs, energy consumption, and processing
026   time (Susnjak et al., 2024; Varoquaux et al., 2024). According to the UN’s Environment Programme
027   Training, the Big Tech sector produced between two and three percent of the world’s carbon emissions
028   in 2021; some estimations for the year 2023 go beyond 4%, see the latest Stand.earth reports, and also
029   (Schwartz et al., 2020; Strubell et al., 2020; Patterson et al., 2021) for related issues. For instance,
030   training GPT-3 is estimated to require 1,287 megawatt-hours (MWh) of electricity, equivalent to the
031   annual usage of over 100 U.S. households (Anthony et al., 2020; Patterson et al., 2021). Similarly,
032   the financial cost of specialized hardware and cloud computing is extremely high. OpenAI claimed
033   that the training cost for GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) exceeded 100 million dollars. The PaLM
034
      model developed by Google AI was trained for two months using 6144 TPUs for 10 million dollars
      (Chowdhery et al., 2023). All this implies a need for faster and more cost-efficient optimization
035
      algorithms. It also suggests that early stopping (Prechelt, 2002; Bai et al., 2021) in the training phase
036
      is a desirable feature whenever possible.

043   Continuous dynamical systems as optimization models. To achieve higher efficiency, it is
044
      necessary to deeply understand how algorithms work and how they relate to each other. A useful way
      to do this is by interpreting optimization algorithms as discrete versions of continuous dynamical
045

046
      2008; Attouch et al., 2016; Aujol et al., 2019). In deep learning, this approach is also quite fruitful; it
047   has, in particular, been used to provide convergence proofs or further geometric insights (Davis et al.,
048   2020; Bolte & Pauwels, 2020; Barakat & Bianchi, 2021; Chen et al., 2023a).

096   Relation with existing work. To improve the efficiency of stochastic gradient descent (SGD), two
097   primary strategies are used: leverage local geometry for having clever directions and incorporate mo-
098   mentum to accelerate convergence. These approaches include accelerated methods (e.g., Nesterov’s
099   acceleration (Nesterov, 1983; Dozat, 2016), momentum SGD (Polyak, 1964; Qian, 1999; Sutskever
100   et al., 2013), and adaptive methods (e.g., Adagrad (Duchi et al., 2011), RMSProp (Tieleman et al.,
101   2012)), which adjust learning rates per parameter.
102   Adam remains the dominant optimizer in deep learning. It comes under numerous variants proposed
103   to improve its performance or to adapt it to specific cases (Dozat, 2016; Shazeer & Stern, 2018;
104   Reddi et al., 2019; Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017; Zhuang et al., 2020). Adafactor (Shazeer & Stern,
105   2018) improves memory efficiency, Lamb (You et al., 2019) adds layerwise normalization, and Lion
106   (Chen et al., 2023b) uses sign-based momentum updates. AdEMAMix (Pagliardini et al., 2024)
107   combines two EMAs, while Defazio et al. (Defazio et al., 2024) introduced a schedule-free method
      incorporating Polyak-Ruppert averaging with momentum.

      One of the motivations of our work is the introduction of second-order properties in the dynamics akin
109   to Newton’s method. Second-order optimizers are computationally expensive due to frequent Hessian
110   computations (Gupta et al., 2018; Martens & Grosse, 2015). Their adaptation to large scale learning
111   settings require specific developments (Jahani et al., 2021; Qian et al., 2021). For example, the Sophia
112   optimizer (Liu et al., 2023), designed for large language models, uses a Hessian-based pre-conditioner
113   to penalize high-curvature directions. In this work, we draw inspiration from INNA (Castera et al.,
114   2021), based on the continuous time dynamics introduced by (Alvarez et al., 2002), which combines
115   gradient descent with a Newtonian mechanism for first-order stochastic approximations.
116   Our proposed method, INNAProp, integrates the algorithm INNA, which features a Newtonian effect
117   with cheap computational cost, with the gradient scaling mechanism of RMSprop. This preserves
118   the efficiency of second-order methods and the adaptive features of RMSprop while significantly
119   reducing the computational overhead caused by Hessian evaluation. Specific hyperparameter choices
120   for our method allow us to recover several existing optimizers as special cases.
121
122   Contributions. They can be summarized as follows:
123
124    • We introduce INNAprop, a new optimization algorithm that combines the Dynamical Inertial
125
            Newton (DIN) method with RMSprop’s adaptive gradient scaling, efficiently using second-order
            information for large-scale machine learning tasks. We obtain a second-order optimizer with
126
            computational requirements similar to first-order methods like AdamW, making it suitable for
127
            deep learning (see Section 2.2 and Appendix B).
128
       • We provide a continuous-time explanation of INNAprop, connecting it to second-order ordinary
129
            differential equations (see Section 2 and Equation (5)). We discuss many natural possible
130
            discretizations and show that INNAprop is empirically the most efficient. Let us highlight a key
131
            feature of our method: it incorporates second-order terms in space without relying on Hessian
132         computations or inversions of linear systems which are both prohibitive in deep learning.
133
       • We show through extensive experiments that INNAprop matches or outperforms AdamW in
134
            both training speed and final accuracy on benchmarks such as image classification (CIFAR-10,
135
            ImageNet) and language modeling (GPT-2) (see Section 3).
136
137   We describe our algorithm and its derivation in Section 2. Hyperparameter tuning recommendations
138   and our experimental results are provided in Section 3.

234   3        E MPIRICAL EVALUATION OF INNA PROP
235
236   We conduct extensive comparisons of the proposed algorithm and the AdamW optimizer, which
237   is dominantly used in image classification (Chen et al., 2018; Zhuang et al., 2020; Touvron et al.,
238   2021; Mishchenko & Defazio, 2023) and language modeling tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Hu et al.,
239
      2021; Liu et al., 2023). Hyperparameter tuning (Sivaprasad et al., 2020) is a crucial issue for this
      comparison, and we start with this. As a general rule, we strive to choose the hyperparameters that
240
      give a strong baseline for AdamW (based on literature or using grid search). Unless stated differently,
241
      our experiments use the AdamW optimizer 1 with its default settings as defined in widely-used
242   libraries (Paszke et al., 2019; Bradbury et al., 2018; Abadi et al., 2016): β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999,
243   λ = 0.01 and ϵ = 1e − 8. For INNAprop, unless otherwise specified, the default settings for the
244   RMSprop component align with those of AdamW: σ = 0.999 and ϵ = 1e − 8.
245
      The INNAprop method and the AdamW optimizer involve different classes of hyperparameters; some
246
      of them are common to both algorithms, and some are specific.
247
248   Our hyperparameter tuning strategy for both algorithms is summarized in Table 1.
249   We begin this section with the tuning of parameters α, β for INNAprop on CIFAR10 with VGG
250   and ResNet architectures and then use these parameters on larger datasets and models. We use as
251   much as possible the step size scheduler and weight decay settings reported in the literature for the
252   AdamW optimizer, which we believe to be well-adjusted and provide adequate references for each
253   experiment. These are used both for AdamW and INNAprop. With this protocol, we only perform
254   minimal hyperparameter tuning for INNAprop for larger-scale experiments. This is due to constrained
255   computational resources. We aim to demonstrate the typical performance of the Algorithm 1, rather
256   than its peak performance with extensive tuning.

      4                        C ONCLUSION
521
      INNAprop is an optimizer that leverages second-order geometric information while maintaining
522
      memory and computational footprints similar to AdamW. Experiments on text modeling and image
523
      classification show that INNAprop consistently matches or exceeds AdamW’s performance.
524
525   We systematically favored AdamW through the choice of recommended hyperparameters (schedulers,
526   learning rates, weight decay). Hyperparameter tuning for friction parameters (α, β) was conducted
527
      using a grid search on CIFAR-10 (see Figure 18). Further experiments in that direction could greatly
      improve the efficiency of INNAprop.
528
529   For language models, INNAprop with (α, β) = (0.1, 0.9) performs consistently well across all
530   training durations, both for pre-training from scratch and for fine-tuning. We recommend that value
531   for LLMs. Early training achieves notable successes (refer to Table 4).
532   In image classification, (α, β) = (0.1, 0.9) accelerates short-term learning, while higher values like
533   (α, β) = (2.0, 2.0) improve test accuracy during longer training runs. Moreover, (α, β) = (2.0, 2.0)
534   is effective for fine-tuning, offering a good balance between convergence speed and final accuracy.
535
      These experiments illustrate consistent performances of the proposed method over a diversity of
536
      benchmarks, architecture, and model scales, making INNAprop a promising competitor for the
537   training of large neural networks. Future research will be focused on the design of schedulers for the
538   hyperparameters α and β.
