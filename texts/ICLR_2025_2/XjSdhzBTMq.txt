009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper introduces a learning-to-rank (LTR) framework to address the problem of
012            pipeline selection in automated machine learning systems. The traditional approach
013            to AutoML involves learning to predict the performance of various pipelines on a
014            given task based on data acquired from previous tasks (i.e., meta-learning), which
015            can be complex due to the need for different models for each task-specific metric.
016            The proposed framework aims to select the best pipeline based on ranking rather
017            than estimating a target metric, aligning more closely with the ultimate goal of
018
               the task (i.e., selecting pipeline candidates in order, from more to least promising).
               This approach enables more robust, metric-agnostic solutions that are easier to
019
               compare using ranking metrics like NDCG and MRR. The paper evaluates LTR
020
               strategies on public OpenML datasets, demonstrating a clear advantage for ranking-
021            based methods. Additionally, the integration of LTR with Bayesian optimization
022            and Monte Carlo tree search is explored, leading to improvements in the ranking
023            metrics. Finally, the study found a strong correlation between ranking metrics (e.g.,
024            NDCG and MRR) and AutoML metrics, such as the task objective metric and the
025            time to find the best solution, providing insights into how ranking-based methods
026            could enhance AutoML systems.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Machine learning (ML) has become an essential component of countless scientific and industrial
032   endeavors, ranging from healthcare (Waring et al., 2020) to e-commerce (Micu et al., 2019). However,
033
      applying machine learning in the real world is far from straightforward, as it involves multiple
      steps such as data preparation (preprocessing), feature and model selection, hyperparameter tuning,
034
      performance evaluation, etc. (Barbudo et al., 2023; Vazquez, 2022). The complexity in the design
035
      of machine-learning solutions led to the development of automated Machine Learning (AutoML)
036   techniques that seek to relieve ML practitioners from repetitive and time-consuming tasks that have a
037   large impact on the final performance of ML systems (Karmaker et al., 2021).
038
039
      While AutoML helped widen the adoption of machine learning solutions at scale by simplifying
      the process of finding optimal configurations, it faces significant challenges. One such challenge
040
      is efficiently leveraging past experiences to improve performance on new tasks. This problem led
041
      to the development of meta-learning algorithms and techniques (Vanschoren, 2019) to capture the
042   knowledge gained from solving a variety of tasks to help find optimal solutions for novel (future) ones.
043   Meta-learning algorithms rely on meta-models to predict the performance of a given configuration
044   (pipeline) on a specific dataset. This prediction serves as an approximation of the actual performance
045   that the model would demonstrate if it were trained with the specified data. The benefit of this
046   approach is that it prevents from actually training the model on the data in question, saving time
047   and costs. Note that predicting pipeline performance involves predicting specific metrics for the
048   different types of ML tasks (classification, regression, etc.), often requiring different models for each
049   task-specific metric being optimized (Karmaker et al., 2021).
050   We can recognize two broad strategies in the design of an AutoML system. One is to build an optimal
051   machine learning solution from a pool of simpler components (Ren et al., 2021), and the other is to
052   select it from a set of predefined options or configurations (Yang et al., 2020). When the problem
053   is about choosing among given solutions, it can be viewed as a ranking or recommendation task,
      where the goal is to rank the set of possible configurations according to their effectiveness in solving

054
      the task. However, in many cases, the efforts of AutoML systems are focused on predicting the
055   performance of configurations rather than tackling the selection or decision-making problem directly.
056   For instance, when Bayesian optimization is used (Hutter et al., 2011), the selection of configurations
057   often involves choosing the next most promising configuration based on a surrogate model. This
058   model is designed to capture the relationship between the configuration and the target metric, to
059   inform the selection process about which configuration to evaluate next. We argue that the selection
060   problem could be addressed more effectively by framing it as a decision-making problem, thereby
061   facilitating a more direct approach to determining the most valuable solutions.
062   Previous works explored different approaches to navigate the complex combinatorial space of
063   available options (models, preprocessing steps, hyperparameters, etc.) in AutoML. Drawing parallels
064   to finance portfolio management, some methods (Fusi et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2019) optimize
065   model and pipeline selection based on historical performance, akin to optimizing a collection of
066   assets. Other methods (Feurer et al., 2022; Laadan et al., 2019; 2020), directly adopt ranking and
067   meta-learning strategies, demonstrating improvements in the efficiency of the AutoML workflow
068   through data-driven decision-making and the pre-ranking of machine learning pipelines. However,
069
      although these approaches emphasize the importance of classifying information to speed up the
      search process and improve the efficiency of the AutoML system, they do not consider the underlying
070
      ranking problem explicitly.
071
072   This paper addresses some of these challenges by introducing a simple strategy for incorporating
073   learning to rank (LTR) (Liu et al., 2009) into the pipeline selection problem in AutoML. Here, the
074   aim is to learn to select the best candidate rather than estimating the target metric, which we believe
075
      is closer to the ultimate goal of the pipeline selection task. This approach allows us to generate
      more robust solutions that are agnostic to the target metric and easier to compare. Through the use
076
      of ranking metrics such as NDCG and MRR, we explore the effectiveness of different approaches,
077
      both score- and ranking-based. We also explore the use of LTR in classical sequential optimization
078   techniques, such as Bayesian optimization and Monte Carlo tree search. The approaches are compared
079   using public datasets from OpenML showing clear improvements for the ranking-based approaches.
080
081
      The contributions of the paper are:
082
             • The introduction of learning to rank (LTR) into the AutoML pipeline selection problem,
083            aiming to more accurately reflect the ultimate goal of selecting the best candidate pipeline.
084            This approach produces solutions that are robust and agnostic to the target metric, thereby
085            simplifying comparisons.
086
             • Evaluation of the effectiveness of different AutoML approaches using ranking metrics such
087
               as NDCG and MRR. This includes exploring the relationship between the ranking metrics
088            and typical AutoML metrics, such as the improvement in the objective metric and the time
089            it takes to find the best solution.
090
             • Demonstrated improvements of ranking-based approaches over score-based ones through
091
               empirical evidence gathered from experiments on public datasets from OpenML. This
092
               underscores the efficiency and improved decision-making provided by adopting a ranking
093            perspective in the AutoML workflow.
094
095   The rest of the paper is structured as follows. Section 2 introduces the learning to rank framework
096   applied to AutoML, outlining some key concepts. Section 3 details the experimental setup, including
097   the design and objectives of our study. Section 4 discusses the results of our experiments, providing
098   insights into the performance of the LTR framework in the context of AutoML. Section 5 revisits
099   related work. Section 6 discusses the findings and limitations of the approach. Finally, Section 7
100
      concludes the paper and suggests avenues for future research.

102   2   L EARNING TO R ANK FOR AUTOMATED M ACHINE L EARNING
103
104   Learning to rank is an important concept in the field of machine learning that involves training
105   models able to predict the relative order of a set of items. This technique is commonly used in search,
106   recommendation systems, and other cases where prioritizing items based on their relevance is critical
107   (Liu et al., 2009; Xia, 2019; Karatzoglou et al., 2013). This can be exploited in the context of AutoML
      through the concept of meta-learning (Vanschoren, 2019).


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      To prevent AutoML systems from repeatedly tackling similar tasks starting from scratch each time,
109   meta-learning methods leverage data acquired from previous experience (meta-learning data) to
110   improve performance on novel tasks (represented with meta-features). In our case, this corresponds
111   to optimizing the procedure by which we select optimal pipelines for a wide variety of problems. We
112   propose to do so by using ranking information instead of task-specific performance scores. In this
113   way, the process of pipeline selection becomes independent of the absolute scores observed in the
114   meta-learning data as well as from the different performance values achieved on each task.
115   In what follows, we first characterize the different approaches into score-based and ranking-based.
116   While the former appears as the most common approach in AutoML, the latter provides a less
117   explored and attractive alternative that will later prove to be effective.
118
119
      2.1   S CORE -BASED M ODELS
120
121   Score-based models represent most traditional approaches in the AutoML frameworks. These models
122   aim to predict the target metric of a problem, such as classification accuracy or mean regression
123   error. The predicted score is then used to select the most promising configuration or pipeline. This
124   aligns with the pointwise approach in LTR theory, where the input space consists of individual
125   configurations, and the output space is the score of these configurations on the target task.
126   This can be formalized as follows. Let C = {C1 , C2 , . . . , Cn } represent the set of all possible
127   configurations or pipelines in the AutoML search space. The goal is to predict the performance
128   score of each configuration Ci for a new given dataset D. To do so, we rely on a (parametric) score
129   prediction model f : C × D → R that takes a candidate configuration Ci and dataset D as inputs and
130   predicts a score f (Ci , D) that relates to the predictive performance of Ci on D. The score predicted
131   by f is specific to each task (accuracy, F1-score, regression errors, etc.). We assume we know the
132   true value of this score for each pipeline-dataset combination in the meta-training dataset. We denote
133   the ground-truth scores as s(Ci , D). Training the meta-model is based on regression towards this
134
      metric by optimizing a suitable loss, such as the mean squared error of the scores, using a training
      dataset (e.g. mean squeare error of the scores) consisting of triplets of the form {(Ci , Dj , sij )}, with
135
      Ci ∈ C, Dj ∈ D, and sij = s(Ci , Dj ).
136
137
138   2.2   R ANKING -BASED M ODELS
139
140
      Ranking-based models focus on directly learning the ranking order of different configurations. These
      models are trained using explicit ranking information. The two primary formulations in this category
141
      are the pairwise and listwise approaches.
142
143   In the pairwise approach, the input space consists of pairs of configurations, P = {(Ci , Cj )|Ci , Cj ∈
144   C}, where C represents the set of all available pipeline configurations. In this case, we rely on a
145   preference function g : P × D → {0, 1} that given a pair of candidate configurations (Ci , Cj ) and
146
      a dataset D, it predicts which one is better: g((Ci , Cj ), D) = +1 if Ci is better than Cj , and 0
      otherwise. The formulation could be extended to include a third category in which both configurations
147
      are deemed equally good. The binary case, however, has the advantage that it can be easily tackled as a
148
      binary classification problem. The model is trained using configuration pairs along with their relative
149   ground-truth performances. For a new given dataset D′ , configurations are ranked by comparing
150   pairs and aggregating these pairwise preferences to form a global ranking. Common loss functions
151   for the pairwise approach include hinge loss (Cao et al., 2006) or logistic loss (Burges et al., 2005),
152   which explicitly account for a correct ordering of the pairs.
153
      In the listwise approach, on the other hand, the input space corresponds to permutations of the set of
154
      all configurations C. Instead of directly working with the set of all permutations of C, we can think
155
      of a ranking function h : C × D → R|C| that takes as input the set of available configurations and a
156   dataset D, and outputs a vector of scores of dimensionality |C|, whose i-th entry corresponds to the
157   relevance of configuration Ci for solving the problem. Sorting the elements of this vector provides
158   the permutation indices that rank the elements of C according to their relevance for D. Training such
159   a model involves using the entire set of configurations C and their rankings for different datasets. The
160   model learns to predict these rankings as accurately as possible. There are two types of output spaces
161   used in the listwise approach. In the first, for a new dataset D′ , the model predicts a ranking position
      for each configuration. The configurations are then ranked based on these positions (Taylor et al.,


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      2008). In the second, the output space contains the ranked list (or permutation) of the configurations
163   (Cao et al., 2007). In this work, we will focus on the first one. By transforming the input and output
164   spaces from a score into ranking positions, we have a simple way to compare pipeline selection
165   approaches with and without ranking information. Finally, different losses can be used to train a
166   ranking model listwise (Cao et al., 2007; Yue et al., 2007; Taylor et al., 2008), all of which extend the
167   ranking formalism to account for a large set of candidate objects.
168
169   2.3   I NTEGRATING LTR INTO S EQUENTIAL O PTIMIZATION F RAMEWORKS
170
171   LTR approaches described so far require evaluating the whole set of configurations during inference.
172   With large configuration spaces, this might result in excessive computations and time. Sequential
173   model-based optimization (SMBO) stands as an alternative formulation that seeks to improve the
174
      efficiency of searching for optimal configurations in complex spaces. SMBO approaches rely on
      discovering new configurations by using an estimate of the target metric as a comparison reference.
175
      In the Bayesian optimization (BO) approach proposed by Hutter et al. (2011), a surrogate model is
176
      trained to estimate the value of the target metric for a given configuration. In this way, configuration
177   pipelines are sampled through the use of an acquisition function, which guides the search for the
178   optimal solution by quantifying the expected utility of selecting a particular configuration. The
179   configuration that exhibits the highest estimated value according to this function is then selected.
180
      In our work, we consider the selection problem as a ranking process that we can directly integrate into
181
      the model. For instance, if our surrogate model is a regression of the accuracy for a classification task,
182
      we can replace it with a regression that estimates the ranking position of the configuration. It is also
183   possible to use a pairwise model that, from a sample of configurations, orders them by comparing
184   pairs and selects the best one. By replacing the surrogate model with a pre-trained ranking model we
185   expect to improve overall performance. We call this variant of the BO algorithm BO-Rank.
186
      Similarly, we can also reformulate model-free approaches such as Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)
187
      Vazquez et al. (2022). In MCTS, the search space is encoded as a tree whose leaves encode the set of
188
      possible configurations. Configurations are compared using a selection policy, which is computed
189   based on the target metric for the task, often referred to as the reward. In this case, we can replace
190   the objective function with a ranking objective, i.e. by using the ranking position as the reward from
191   which the selection policy is computed. This allows us to precompute the partial ranks for each
192   configuration and thus avoid the need to compute estimates of the target metric on a validation set as
193   in the traditional MCTS. We call this variant of the algorithm MCTS-Rank.
194
      Finally, we also note that our approach could also be applied in the case of model-based MCTS by
195   using a ranking model as a surrogate during the simulation step. However, for the sake of simplicity,
196   we leave this to be explored in future works.

199   3     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
200
201   Our evaluation encompassed a comprehensive comparison of four categories of selection approaches:
202
203
            1. Random Selection: A baseline approach involving the random selection of pipelines,
               serving as a control to gauge the effectiveness of more sophisticated methods.
204
205         2. Average Best: This approach involves selecting the best pipeline by ranking them based on
206            their performance in the training data.
207         3. Regression: This category uses regression to predict the performance metric, treating the
208            problem as the prediction of a score Hutter et al. (2011); Feurer et al. (2015), or to predict
209            the rank of pipelines in a list, treating the problem as the prediction of a position.
210         4. Sequential Optimization: We consider the problem of Sequential Model-Based Optimiza-
211            tion (SMBO) using classical optimization frameworks, Bayesian Optimization (BO) and
212            Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS).
213
214   Each category was analyzed in two flavors: score-based and ranking-based (except for the Random
215   Selection baseline). In the case of Average Best (Avg), one version creates a list of the best pipelines
      ordered by average score, while the other orders them by average rank. For the regression-based


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      approaches, regressors from scikit-learn and LightGBM (LGBM) were used with default hyperparam-
217   eters to avoid bias from hyperparameter tuning. The regressors utilized were LinearRegression (LR),
218   Lasso, Ridge, RandomForestRegressor (RF), GradientBoostingRegressor (GB), and LGBMRegressor
219   (LGBM).
220
      Additionally, for the SMBO frameworks, BO and MCTS were employed. These frameworks were
221
      selected for their efficacy in navigating large search spaces, providing a robust platform to evaluate
222
      the comparative performance of the proposed ranking methodologies. This experiment allows us to
223   evaluate and compare the effectiveness of different approaches in the context of continuous pipeline
224   selection within traditional frameworks, offering insights into the potential of these methods to
225   enhance and optimize existing techniques.
226
      For BO, we consider a straightforward setting in which we initialize the process with a pre-trained
227
      (meta-learned) surrogate model. Specifically, we first train a linear regression model on training tasks,
228
      where it learns to either estimate the score of the pipelines (score-based regression) or their ranking
229   position (ranking-based regression). In each iteration, we take a pipelines sample (10 for AMLB and
230   100 for OpenML-Weka) and employ a greedy approach to select the best next pipeline to evaluate as
231   the acquisition function. After evaluating a pipeline, the surrogate model is retrained with the new
232   information, and the pipeline is removed from the pool of possible options. The algorithm runs until
233   there are no more pipelines available. The ordered list of selected pipelines is considered the final
234   ranking of pipelines to evaluate.
235   Similarly, for MCTS, we use the model-free variant; that is, no surrogate model is used. Instead, we
236   consider, in one case, a pre-calculated (meta-learned) average score of the pipelines as the reward
237   function, and in the other case, the average ranking position. In each iteration of MCTS, we use a
238   greedy approach for the rollout policy and the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) for the selection
239   policy (with C = √12 ) to discover the next pipeline to evaluate. After evaluating a pipeline, it is
240   pruned from the pool of possible options. The algorithm runs until there are no more pipelines
241   available. The ordered list of selected pipelines is considered the ranking of pipelines to evaluate.
242

      The second and third scenarios consist of tasks from the Automated Machine Learning Benchmark

      To collect pipeline performances for the AMLB scenarios, we executed 2160 different pipelines for
264   classification and 1485 for regression. For the purpose of this experiment, we executed all pipelines in
265   a search space defined by a simple grammar (Appendix C) as in Vazquez et al. (2022). The execution
266   was carried out on an Amazon EC2 R5 spot instance (8 vCPUs and 64GB of RAM), and results were
267   saved, with the sole restriction that each pipeline must be completed within one minute. Each pipeline
268   was then identified by a randomly assigned ID. We used standard train-test splits, as provided by each
269   OpenML task. For classification and regression problems, balanced accuracy and root mean square
      error (RMSE) were used as metrics, respectively.

      To describe the datasets, we computed the set of meta-features proposed by Rivolli et al. (2022)
271   with the Python Meta-Feature Extractor (Alcobaça et al., 2020)1 . These meta-features encode
272   different characteristics useful for representing machine learning datasets. We applied PCA to
273   reduce the dimensionality of such representations to three dimensions. We found that this value
274   preserves most of the discriminative power of the original representation while considerably reducing
275   the number of dimensions. Additionally, we considered an additional meta-feature to capture the
276   average performance of each configuration. This additional meta-feature corresponds to the average
277   performance score for score-based models and the average rank for ranking-based ones. The average
278   is computed on the training dataset.
279   For learning to rank (i.e., meta-learning), we used task-level cross-validation with 10 folds. Addi-
280   tionally, we ran experiments with 10 different seeds. For evaluation, we considered two classical
281   ranking metrics: Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) and Mean Reciprocal Rank
282   (MRR). NDCG measures the quality of the ranking system in terms of the position of the top-K
283   elements in a ranked list, with a diminishing value for elements further down the list. In contrast,
284   MRR considers the position of the highest-ranked pipeline, computed as the inverse of the position of
285
      the best configuration.
286   In addition, we measured important AutoML system metrics across the multiple tasks: the average
287   score (SCORE) representing the mean value of the objective metric (such as accuracy or RMSE);
288   the time to find the best solution (TTB) measuring how long it takes for the system to identify the
289   best-performing pipeline within the predefined search space; and the average rank (AVG RANK)
290   reflecting the ranking of each approach performance relative to others (score- vs ranking-based)
291
      where a lower rank indicates better performance (e.g., the best-performing framework on a task is
      assigned a rank of 1, the second-best a rank of 2, etc.).

      Across all datasets, there is a strong positive correlation between NDCG@1 and SCORE@1. For
433   instance, in the AMLB Classification dataset, the correlations between these metrics are particularly
434   high, with values such as 0.95 between NDCG@1 and SCORE@1. This suggests that these metrics
435   are closely aligned in assessing the quality of the top-ranked predictions. The implication is that im-
436   provements in one of these metrics are likely to be reflected in the others, indicating their consistency
437   in evaluating the effectiveness of ranking at the top position.
438
      Another interesting finding is that TTB@1 shows a strong negative correlation with MRR@1,
439   particularly in the AMLB datasets where it was evaluated. For example, in AMLB Regression,
440   the correlation between TTB@1 and MRR@1 is -1. This negative correlation indicates that when
441   MRR@1 improves, TTB@1, which focuses more on the time to find the best solution, tends to
442   decrease. This is expected since MRR tends to position the best result at the top, and optimizing
443   it may lead to finding it faster in terms of time. RANK@1 exhibits a negative correlation with
444   NDCG@1, MRR@1, and SCORE@1 across all datasets. In the AMLB Classification dataset, for
445   instance, RANK@1 correlates negatively with NDCG@1 (-0.67) and MRR@1 (-0.66). This inverse
446   relationship implies that as the average rank (RANK@1) improves (lower rank value), the other
447
      metrics show better performance (higher values), reinforcing the idea that these metrics are effective
      in capturing the quality of the ranking, particularly at the top positions.
448
449   The correlations exhibit some variation across different datasets. For example, in the OpenML-WEKA
450   dataset, the correlation between MRR@1 and RANK@1 is 0.32, much weaker and with the opposite
451   sign compared to the AMLB datasets. This variability could suggest that the relationships between
452   these metrics may be affected by the length of the models evaluated (only 20 in OpenML-WEKA),
453
      but this is speculative.
454   In general, improvements in ranking-based strategies compared to those that rely on score-based
455   models are clear. The underperformance of score-based models, although better than random selection,
456   highlights the advantages of using ranking information. The results suggest that the choice between
457   ranking-based and score-based variants can significantly influence the consistency and robustness of
458   performance across tasks for optimal pipeline selection in AutoML problems.

460   5    R ELATED W ORK
461
462   Pipeline selection is an important problem of AutoML. Significant approaches have been developed
463   to efficiently navigate the combinatorial space of possible models, preprocessing steps, and hyperpa-
464   rameters (Hutter et al., 2011; Feurer et al., 2015; Vanschoren, 2019). In particular, Vanschoren 2019
465   categorizes meta-models into performance predictors and ranking generators. We extend this idea by
466   showing that transitioning from score-based to ranking-based methods in classical approaches like
467   BO and MCTS could lead to improvements in pipeline selection.
468   Furthermore, some other approaches have evaluated the possibility of using ranking techniques
469   to improve the selection of pipelines. For instance, AutoFolio (Lindauer et al., 2015) uses several
470   algorithms based on performance scores to train a model based on portfolio training data, like pairwise
471   classifiers. While Autofolio proposes some transformations to the target like log (Xu et al. (2008))
472   or z-score normalization (Collautti et al. (2013)) to improve the selection, none of these consider
473   the ranking position. Similarly, other works (Sun & Pfahringer, 2013; Tornede et al., 2020) use
474   comparisons as a way to learn better models for algorithm selection. However, comparisons are
475   primarily score-based between performance scores and not between ranking positions.
476   An interesting aspect is that, in practice, the pipeline selection problem can occur before the system’s
477   search process. For example, AutoGluon (Erickson et al., 2020) employs an ensemble strategy to
478   combine predictions from different types of preselected fixed pipelines. This methodology leverages
479   the strengths of various models to improve overall performance, albeit within the limitations of the
480   preselected pipeline space. On the other hand, GramML (Vazquez et al., 2022) adopts a different
481   strategy by learning to delineate the space while exploring it using MCTS. It also extends the
482
      search approach to preselected hyperparameters within fixed and predefined ranges (Vázquez et al.,
      2023). Preselection allows a priori knowledge of the size of the space and the constituent pipelines,
483
      facilitating a more directed and potentially efficient search.
484
485   The idea of working with predefined pipeline spaces resemble to the concept of portfolio management
      in finance, where a collection of assets is optimized to achieve the best possible return for a given level


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      of risk. Thus, approaches like Probabilistic Matrix Factorization (Fusi et al., 2018) and Oboe (Yang
487   et al., 2019), seek to optimize the selection of models and pipelines based on historical performance
488   data. These systems leverage collaborative filtering techniques to predict the performance of various
489   models on new datasets, thereby guiding the selection process in a data-driven manner. By building
490   on the successes and failures of past model applications, portfolio-based approaches aim to streamline
491   the AutoML workflow and enhance decision-making efficiency.
492
      Another innovative direction in AutoML research involves using ranking information to improve the
493   cold start problem and meta-learning strategies. Auto-sklearn 2.0 (Feurer et al., 2022), for example,
494   uses portfolio information to prioritize models and configurations that are likely to perform well,
495   based on historical performance on similar tasks. This approach helps to quickly identify promising
496   starting points for the BO process, reducing the time and resources required for model selection. It
497   also uses preselected pipelines to address the cold start problem.
498
      Similarly, RankML (Laadan et al., 2019) explicitly focusing on a meta-learning approach for the pre-
499   ranking of machine learning pipelines. This methodology aims to leverage accumulated knowledge
500   to predict the efficacy of different pipelines before detailed evaluations, potentially saving significant
501   computational resources and accelerating the AutoML process.
502
      MetaTPOT (Laadan et al., 2020) takes this concept to enhances the evolutionary algorithm-based
503
      optimization tool (TPOT, Olson & Moore (2016)) by incorporating meta-learning techniques that
504
      use ranking information to guide the search process. By learning from the performance rankings of
505   pipelines across a variety of datasets, MetaTPOT seeks to improve upon its predecessor by identifying
506   efficient pipelines more quickly.
507
508
      The approaches described above recognize the importance of using ranking information, whether to
      preselect the system’s components, order them to expedite the search, or as a form of meta-learning
509
      to enhance system efficiency. However, the problem of learning to rank and the importance of using
510
      positional information has not been addressed, to our knowledge, until this work. We believe this
511   work presents a novel conceptual framework as a basis for learning to rank in AutoML that can
512   potentially be transferred to many other approaches.

      6    C ONCLUSIONS AND D ISCUSSION
517
518   This work presents a simple strategy to frame the pipeline selection task in automated machine
519   learning (AutoML) as a Learning-to-Rank (LTR) problem. Based on the hypothesis that incorporating
520   the positional information of pipelines from previous results can improve selection outcomes, this
521   work compares traditional pipeline selection approaches (score-based) against a ranking-based
522   counterpart by transforming the prediction objective from a target metric to a ranking position.
523   The results of the experiments demonstrate a marked improvement of the ranking-based approach
524   over the score-based approach. The superior performance underscores the importance of considering
525   the positional information rather than focusing on isolated metric predictions regarding to the ranking
526   problem. Furthermore, it was found that metrics commonly used in ranking problems correlate
527   with metrics more typical of AutoML, such as the objective metric and the time taken to find the
528   best solution. This finding corroborates the hypothesis that ranking optimization strategies are more
529   aligned with the intrinsic structure of selection problems in AutoML.
530   However, this study primarily focuses on demonstrating the effectiveness of ranking-based approaches
531   in scenarios where all pipelines are known, which means that the search space is finite and predefined.
532   While we believe this scenario addresses a significant portion of the pipeline selection problem, as
533   predefined pipelines are common in many practical tools, further studies are needed to extend these
534   findings to more diverse scenarios, including feature selection and hyperparameter optimization.
535   Additionally, evaluating more complex and computationally expensive LTR algorithms, such as
536
      pairwise methods, was beyond the scope of this work.
537   Lastly, while the tasks and pre-computed pipelines used for evaluation may not capture the full range
538   of possibilities in the AutoML landscape, we believe that by leveraging classification and regression
539   tasks from OpenML and established benchmarks like AMLB and ASLib, the results are generalizable
      and can be built upon in future research.
