009                                            A BSTRACT
010
011
012           We introduce PokéChamp, a Large Language Model (LLM) powered game-
013           theoretic aware agent for two-player competitive Pokémon battles, that uses an
014
              LLM prior and collected high-Elo human data to model minimax search with-
              out any additional neural network training. PokéChamp uses a depth-limited
015
              minimax search online where the LLM replaces three key components: 1) action
016
              sampling from the LLM guided by prompts (including from a damage calcula-
017           tion tool), 2) opponent-modeling via the historical likelihood of actions from our
018           dataset to model the effect of LLM-predicted opponent actions, and 3) state value
019           calculation for the LLM to reflect on each intrinsic state. PokéChamp outper-
020           forms all existing LLM-based (76%) and rule-based bots (84%) by an enormous
021           margin, including winning consistently (64%) against prior human-parity work
022           run with a frontier model, GPT 4-o, while using an open-source 8 billion param-
023           eter Llama 3.1 model. PokéChamp achieves expert performance in the top 10%
024           of players on the online ladder against competitive human players at an Elo of
025
              1500. Finally, we collect the largest Pokémon battling dataset, including 1 mil-
              lion+ games with 150k+ high Elo games, prepare a series of battling benchmarks
026
              based on real player data and puzzles to analyze specific battling abilities, and
027
              provide crucial updates to the local game engine. Our code is available online.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   A desirable element of a language agent is to be able to approach Nash solutions for competitive
058
      tasks. Previous work in reinforcement learning Campbell et al. (2002); Silver et al. (2017; 2016);
      Brown & Sandholm (2019; 2018); Vinyals et al. (2019); Berner et al. (2019) finds the best per-
059
      formance through tabula rasa self-play or train large language models via imitation learning and
060
      human-regularized self-play (FAIR). Language agents have the ability to leverage prior information
061   about background and task strategies and apply it to new situations. Recent advances in these text
062   agents have shown that they lack basic planning capabilities Topsakal & Harper (2024), perform
063   worse than hardcoded heuristic bots for games Küttler et al. (2020), and have issues understanding
064   the basic game mechanics Hu et al. (2024b).
065
      Let us consider competitive Pokémon as an example. With a vast number of Pokémon species that
066
      each have their own unique abilities, moves, typing, special mechanics, and range of stats, the total
067   number of possible states is on the order of 10354 The-Third-Build (2022) and this is only for turn
068   1. Information about the opponent’s team is only partially observable, which keeps the search space
069   from shrinking once the opponent’s team is revealed. Pokémon battles can last anywhere from 6
070   to over 100 turns, meaning exhaustive self-play can be quite computationally intractable. We argue
071   that an informative prior can help limit minimax search to the space of human strategies. Large
072   language models (LLMs) have the potential to be trained on text datasets that include information
073   about Pokémon, but are not exhaustive since they are not the focus of training. Thus, we want an
074   agent that can harness the prior of large language models to: (1) propose optimal actions to provide
075
      highly likely, diverse actions for potential strategies, (2) accurately model the opponent based on
      their move history, team, and strategy based on their skill-level, and (3) reflect on an internally
076
      planned game trajectory without requiring a terminal win/lose state.
077
078   Thus, to achieve these mechanisms, we introduce PokéChamp, a competitive language agent
079   that achieves human-expert level performance in the two-player turn-based battles in Pokémon.
080   PokéChamp uses a large language model to power generative minimax tree search through the
081
      following elements: (1) action branching from tool-use and LLM sampling; (2) LLM-based
      opponent-modeling via information from our collected historical data of real Pokémon battles
082
      to provide likely team compositions, which is prompted to the LLM to provide opponent actions;
083
      and (3) using a score rating guide prompt and the internally predicted state from our world model
084   engine, provide an LLM-generated value calculation of leaf nodes. Based on the depth of our
085   tree, the sum of the values of the leaf nodes is backpropagated up through the tree to output the most
086   likely action. The LLM acts as a black box, in which it can be switched out based on one’s compute
087   availability and used with better frontier LLMs as they are developed. Our approach prevents the
088   need for additional training or fine-tuning on Pokémon-specific data.
089
      In order to successfully plan k steps into the future battle, our agent needs a proper world model.
090   Due to partial observability, we cannot just use the game engine as the world model. Rather, we de-
091   veloped our own local game engine in order to adequately address the intrinsic planning capabilities.
092   We developed a tool, which we simply call the damage calculator (dmg calc), that mathematically
093   calculates the core game engine capabilities in combination with loading historical data from real
094   player games in order to load likely stats for the opponent’s team. The historical data comes from
095   our Pokémon battling dataset, which, as of this writing, contains over 1 million games from various
096   Elos and game modes.
097   We use our dataset, which is the first and largest Pokémon battling dataset, to establish a set of
098   benchmark puzzles to understand key mechanics and strategies commonly found in battles. We
099   establish a move prediction and opponent modeling benchmark based on high-elo human data to
100   show the limitations of prompting LLMs. Our 1v1 benchmark provides an opportunity to evaluate
101   the competency of our bot to choose the best moves for individual matchups.
102   Empirically, PokéChamp demonstrates expert-level performance in Pokémon battles. Our agent is
103   able to choose optimal actions with a short minimax planning lookahead. We evaluate PokéChamp
104   in an arena setup against other competitive Pokémon bots, including heuristic bots and an LLM-
105   based agent PokéLLMon Hu et al. (2024b), in two highly played game modes: Generation 8 Random
106   Battles (gen8randombattles) and Generation 9 OverUsed Meta (gen9ou). PokéChamp outperforms
107   all other bots and AI agents, with the largest margin being in gen9ou with a 76% winrate against
      the strongest LLM-based bot and an 84% winrate against the strongest heuristic bot. Our method

      3     P OKÉ C HAMP
188
189
      PokéChamp consists of three novel components that perform a minimax tree search: (1) policy
      sampling via a combination of LLM and tool-use, (2) opponent modeling via historical data and
190
      LLM generation, and (3) an LLM-generated value function. To output the best action, the value is
191
      recursed through the tree to choose the action with the maximum value for player moves and the
192   minimum value for opponent moves. Full prompts are available in the Appendix. See figure 2.
193

      3.1   ACTION S AMPLING
195
196   Using the input prompt, PokéChamp generates an edge for both action types: move and switch.
197   The top move choice by the damage calculator are top switch choice from the Abyssal bot, which
198   rates matchups based on a fixed set of rules, is also used as a branch of the search tree. The edges
199   of the minimax search tree are made of action samples from the LLM. The input prompt consists of
200   the following features:
201
             • LLM Team strategy: The LLM is asked to generate an overall team strategy based on the
202
               summary of the player’s team and the opponent’s team;
203
204
             • State: This includes the available team, items, opponent’s visible team, etc;
205          • Battle history: This includes the information from the last N turns.;
206          • Damage calculation: For each of the current Pokémon’s damaging and stat raising moves,
207            the number of turns to KO the opponent’s Pokémon is calculated. Additionally, the number
208            of turns including switching to another Pokémon and then KO’ing the opponent’s current
209            Pokémon is calculated. This can be thought of as a depth-first search heuristic with a cutoff
210            at KO;
211          • Available actions: The current pokémon has up to 4 available moves, up to 5 available
212            switch options, and a special mechanic such as dynamax or terastallize, if available.
213

241   3.2   O PPONENT M ODELING
242
243   Unlike with the agent’s team, the opponent’s A attack and D defense is unknown. Using historical
244   data, we can estimate the likelihood of each set of stats the opponent may choose for their EV/IVs.
245   We can either choose the most likely option or sample from the set with probability equal to their
246   likelihood. The prompt for generating the likely opponent actions from the LLM is similar to the
247   action sampling. Afterwards, we can use our proprietary damage calculator to predict next turn state
248   given the edges from the player action and opponent action. Perfect prediction is not possible to be
249   perfect due to stochastic transition function that includes the random element of move damage and
250
      accuracy and moves. We choose to be optimistic and choose the best option assuming maximum
      damage, which means the high end of the random spectrum and 100% accuracy.

      3.3   VALUE F UNCTION
254
255
      Live games provide each player with 150 seconds for the entire match. An additional up to 15
256   seconds is added to each player’s clock after their turn. Due to time limitations, we are unable to
257   perform exhaustive minimax tree search across all player and opponent action possibilities. Thus,
258   we must perform a value calculation at leaf nodes to estimate the utility of a state. We ask the LLM
259   to provide a score for the internal state based on the following prompt features:
260
261          • Add points: based on the effectiveness of current moves, number of player’s Pokémon
262            remaining, and overall player’s likelihood to win.
263
264          • Subtract points: based on excessive switching, effectiveness of opponent’s current moves,
265            faster opponent speed, number of opponent’s remaining Pokémon, and the strength of the
266            remaining Pokémon.
267
268   After the value is generated by the LLM, the value is propagated up the tree to the root, which
269   chooses the player actions with the highest score and the opponent actions that correspond to the
      lowest scores , which satisfy equation 1.

      4.2   H UMAN ACTION P REDICTION
307
      The replay data provides information about battles from the perspective of the opponent. This means
308   that not all stats are available. Thus, in order to perform prediction, we must first reverse engineer
309   the moves, team, and stats from the replay data. Afterwards, we can fill in additional switching and
310   move options from what is historically likely. Feeding this information into our prompt generator
311   will give our bot the ability to predict player and opponent actions.
312
      We compare the accuracy of these actions with the historical data across various Elos. Our results
313
      are shown in table 3.3. The player prediction accuracy for PokéChamp varies between 26% and
314   30% as Elo increases while the opponent prediction accuracy is lower, between 13% and 16%. This
315   shows that predicting opponent actions is more difficult given our state information. The opponent
316   prediction performs a little better than random, while the player prediction performs much better
317   than random. However, there is still quite an improvement to be made. There may also be more than
318   one correct action possible. Many strategies may be equally optimal option. Rather, the performance
319   may simply have to do with player preference.

460   and over 2000 games are active at any given time. Each battle has a time limit of 2 minutes and 30
461   seconds with an additional 15 seconds gained each turn. Each season, players have their Elo reset to
462   1000, the default rating, ensuring that each player’s Elo corresponds to an accurate skill level.
463
      PokéChamp battles real opponents on the ladder for a total of 50 games in the Gen 9 OU meta. The
464
      fastest turns of our method take under 10 seconds to run. However, when there are full actions our
465   available for the player and opponent, our method can take as long as one minute (depending on the
466   speed of the LLM calls). Thus, 33% of the total games were lost due to running out of time on the
467   clock. In table 5, we show the results of the battles. Of the finished games, PokéChamp achieves
468   a 76% winrate against real human opponents. With the opponent’s average rating of 1300, we have
469   an estimated Elo rating of 1500, which is in the top 10% of all players.
470
      PokéChamp performs particularly poorly against two key strategies: stall and excessive switching.
471   We provide additional discussion regarding these strategies in the appendix and in figure 7. See
472   more details in sections D.1 and D.2. This is most likely due to the limited lookahead in order to

      Previous work in competitive games, such as Chess Campbell et al. (2002); Silver et al. (2017),

482   by training exhaustively tabula rasa. Other prior efforts solve competitive fighting games such as
483   Streetfighter Li et al. (2024) through multi-agent reinforcement learning, simulating a ladder-style
484   tournament where AI agents compete in one-on-one matches. This trend has continued to find
485   human-level performance in more realistic game simulations such as Gran Turismo Sophy Wurman
      et al. (2022), which outperformed world-class human drivers in the racing game Gran Turismo

489   work, PokéChamp does not train exhaustively Pokémon. In fact, our method does not explicitly

      6.2   L ANGUAGE AGENTS FOR G AMES
494
      There is growing interest in language agents for games Hu et al. (2024a), but they still fundamen-
495
      tally fail at basic planning algorithms. LLMs are still unable to play the Nash strategy for Tic-Tac-
496   Toe Topsakal & Harper (2024), which is a simple 3 × 3 solved game. This shows the need for addi-
497   tional planning augmentation for LLMs. Nethack Küttler et al. (2020) is a roguelike game designed
498   to test open-ended and long context reinforcement learning agents. However, an LLM-powered
499   Nethack agent Jeurissen et al. (2024) still performs poorly compared to an extensive heuristic bot.
500   Other work on LLMs for games uses prompting algorithms to overcome planning limitations of
501   LLMs for Pokémon Hu et al. (2024b), StarCraft Ma et al. (2023), and mixed cooperative-competitive
502   games such as Avalon Shi et al. (2023); Stepputtis et al. (2023). In another direction, open-world
503   games are being explored by LLMs such as Voyager Wang et al. (2023) learning skills in Minecraft
504
      or Spring Wu et al. (2024), which understands how to play a game by reading the strategy guide.
      LLMs have also been finetuned specifically based on human game data in Diplomacy (FAIR). Us-
505
      ing finetuning and reinforcement learning with LLMs has seen interest in model distillation Nalty
506
      & Rosenthal, two-player online reinforcement learning for finetuning LLMs Zhou et al. (2024),
507   and using an LLM to generate reward feedback for a reinforcement learning agent Klissarov et al.
508   (2023).

511   6.3   P ROMPTING AND P LANNING
512
513
      Recent advancements in prompting techniques and planning algorithms have significantly enhanced
      the reasoning capabilities of large language models. Chain-of-thought prompting Wei et al. (2022)
514
      improves models’ reasoning by providing step-by-step examples, while self-consistency Wang et al.
515
      (2022), boosts performance by sampling multiple reasoning paths. The Tree of Thoughts Yao et al.
516   (2024) framework generalizes this approach, enabling models to explore and evaluate multiple rea-
517   soning paths. In a similar vein, the ReAct Yao et al. (2022) framework interleaves reasoning traces
518   with text actions, enhancing performance on tasks requiring both reasoning and interaction. Other
519   work has demonstrated that language models can serve as both a world model and reasoning agent
520   in their Reasoning via Planning (RAP) approach Hao et al. (2023). This concept is further ex-
521   plored in work Zhao et al. (2023) which leverages language models as commonsense knowledge
522   sources for large-scale task planning. The integration of search algorithms with language models
523   has also shown promise, as evidenced by the TS-LLM Feng et al. (2023) framework, which applies
524
      AlphaZero-like tree search to guide model decoding and training. Additionally, researchers have
      explored reinforcement learning techniques to improve models’ self-correction abilities, as seen in
525
      the SCoRe Kumar et al. (2024) method and the Reflexion Shinn et al. (2024) framework, which uses
526
      linguistic feedback and episodic memory to enhance performance without extensive fine-tuning.

529   7     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we introduce PokéChamp, which augments minimax tree search with the following
532
      LLM-based components: (1) action sampling, (2) opponent modeling, and (3) a state value func-
533
      tion. PokéChamp achieves state-of-the-art performance against heuristic and LLM-based bots and
534   expert performance against real players on the online ladder. Further performance enhancements are
535   currently limited by the accuracy of opponent modeling and the method’s online computational bud-
536   get. By increasing the breadth and depth size of the search, we expect to see further improvement’s
537   to the performance of the method. Additionally, our work can be taken advantage of adversari-
538   ally due to static opponent modeling. Our work leaves open challenges in opponent modeling and
539   generative minimax planning for future work exploring competitive multiagent settings and future
      superhuman performance in Pokémon battling.
