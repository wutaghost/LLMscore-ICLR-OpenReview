                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) provide explicit interpretations for deep neural
               networks through concepts and allow intervention with concepts to adjust final
011
               predictions. Existing CBMs assume concepts are conditionally independent given
012
               labels and isolated from each other, ignoring the hidden relationships among con-
013            cepts. However, the set of concepts in CBMs often has an intrinsic structure where
014            concepts are generally correlated: changing one concept will inherently impact its
015            related concepts. To mitigate this limitation, we propose Graph CBMs: a new
016            variant of CBM that facilitates concept relationships by constructing latent concept
017            graphs, which can be combined with CBMs to enhance model performance while
018            retaining their interpretability. Empirical results on real-world image classification
019            tasks demonstrate Graph CBMs are (1) superior in image classification tasks while
020            providing more concept structure information for interpretability; (2) able to utilize
021
               concept graphs for more effective interventions; and (3) robust across different
               training and architecture settings.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Deep neural networks have shown supremacy and efficiency in various tasks in Computer Vision
      He et al. (2016); Dosovitskiy et al. (2021); Tolstikhin et al. (2021), Natural Language Processing
028
      Vaswani et al. (2017); Devlin et al. (2019); Brown et al. (2020); Raffel et al. (2020), and Graphs
029
      Kipf & Welling (2017); You et al. (2020b); Rong et al. (2020b). Despite their great performance
030   and expressivity, most deep learning models are often regarded as black-box models since they lack
031   interpretability. However, in areas such as medical applications, there is a surging need to explain the
032   prediction of deep learning models to make them more transparent and reliable.
033
      To gain better interpretability, Concept Bottlenectk Models (CBMs) Koh et al. (2020) is proposed
034
      to map inputs’ hidden representations to a concept score space, in which each hidden neuron in the
035
      concept bottleneck layer corresponds to an interpretable concept with a confidence score. Unlike
036   End-to-End training that maps the input (e.g. pixel values in images) directly to target labels, CBMs
037   make a prediction based on the representations inside concept space. In this manner, we can tackle
038   the black-box problem and intervene in concept score vectors to adjust the final prediction without
039   changing the model’s parameters.
040
      However, limitations still exist inside current CBMs. One major limitation is missing correlations
041
      among concepts: the existence of a group of concepts can reinforce or diminish other concept scales.
042   For example, when one is prompted with concepts like ’furs’ and ’whiskers’, one may anticipate
043   concepts like ’tails’ or ’paws’ shown up; while if concepts were ’wings’ and ’beaks’, then ’feather’
044   would be more possibly present. Previous methods Espinosa Zarlenga et al. (2022); Kim et al. (2023);
045   Yuksekgonul et al. (2023) have proposed many variants of CBMs to have a more sophisticated module
046   to generate concept scores. However, most still ignore the structure and interactions of the latent
047   concept.
048   Motivated by a lack of internal concept structure, in this paper, we aim to capture the hidden
049   interactions within the concept space while preserving the essence of CBMs. We propose Graph
050   Concept Bottleneck Models (Graph CBM) which uses a latent concept graph to explicitly enhance
051   the concept interaction and update concept scores by aggregating information from its neighborhood.
052   Observing that the activated concept graph can be regarded as another view of an image, we design a
053   contrastive learning mechanism to learn the latent graph; updated concept scores are then fed into a
      classification module for final prediction. Based on different backbones for the classification module,

054
      we developed four variants: 1) Graph LF-CBM and Graph PCBM for label-free settings; 2) Graph
055   CBM and Graph CEM for concept-supervised settings.
056
057   To summarize our contribution:
058
             • We propose Graph CBMs that utilize a learnable graph structure to allow interactions among
059
               concepts. Empirical results have shown their effectiveness in both target prediction and
060            intervention when compared to SOTAs.
061
             • We design a contrastive learning framework to automatically learn the latent concept graph.
062
               Our Graph CBMs have demonstrated robust performance across different settings and
063            backbones, and Graph CBMs can still be effective when there are concept interventions.

066   2     R ELATED W ORK
067
068   Concept Bottleneck Models: Koh et al. (2020) first presents the idea of Concept Bottleneck Models,
069
      tackling the black-box property in the End-to-End training paradigm by mapping inputs to certain
      concepts and then classifying concept vectors. Espinosa Zarlenga et al. (2022) further investigates
070
      CBMs and proposes a new architecture called Concept Embedding Models (CEM). Kim et al. (2023)
071
      continue to work on the CEMs by modeling probabilistic embeddings in the concept embedding
072   space and the class embedding space. Havasi et al. (2022) addressed the issues of insufficient concept
073   set and inexpressive predictor. Xu et al. (2024) applies energy-based models to define joint energy
074   of (input, concept, class) tuples. While these works show some promising results, they require
075   collecting concept labels to train the models, which may be expensive and time-consuming with
076   manual specifications. To address this shortcoming, recent works Oikarinen et al. (2023); Yang et al.
077   (2023) leverage large pretrained models to enable automation.
078   Yang et al. (2023) leverages a language model to define a large space of possible bottlenecks and
079   filters the concept set with a submodular utility that selects discriminative and diverse information.
080   Yuksekgonul et al. (2023) introduces Post-hoc Concept Bottleneck models (PCBM) which mitigate the
081   requirement of dense concept annotations and avoid training a backbone from scratch by only training
082   the last FC layer along with an optional residual fitting layer. Label-free CBMs (LF-CBM) Oikarinen
083   et al. (2023) transform any neural network into an interpretable CBM without labeled concept data
084   and use a sparse prediction layer to enhance model accuracy and interpretability simultaneously. On
085   the other hand, the above methodologies ignore the interaction between concepts during inference
086
      and intervention, our architecture is orthogonal to them and can be integrated with them efficiently.
087   Graph Structure Learning: Graph Structure Learning (GSL) is a standing problem that finds useful
088   graph structures from data. It has been explored in different contexts and domains. Early efforts
089   come from the signal processing community Dong et al. (2019). Another field of study is grounded
090   by probabilistic graphical models. For example, Yu et al. (2019) intends to learn a faithful directed
091   acyclic graph (DAG) from samples of a joint distribution and propose DAG-GNN that naturally
092
      handles discrete variables and vector-valued ones. In the era of graph neural networks, the latent
      graph is often learned together with the graph neural network parameters Franceschi et al. (2019);
093
      Kipf et al. (2018); Shang et al. (2020); Ma et al. (2023); Kazi et al. (2022). For example, NRI
094
      Kipf et al. (2018) adopts a latent variable approach to learn the latent interaction graph of dynamic
095   systems; Franceschi et al. (2019) formulates graph learning as a bi-level optimization problem,
096   and learns discrete probability distribution on the edges of the graph. Wei et al. (2022) construct
097   contrastive views for HyperGraphs via studying graph structures in the HyperGraph setting. In our
098   case, we define a learnable deterministic graph structure that can be trained together with graph neural
099   networks by contrastive learning.

145   3.2      G RAPH C ONCEPT B OTTLENECK M ODELS
146
147
      The main motivation of the Graph CBM is to bring latent graph structures to the concept space.
      Latent graphs exist in various scenarios and inferring the latent graph topology (or called graph
148
      structure learning) has been demonstrated beneficial in different applications, such as time series
149
      Shang et al. (2020), physical systems Kipf et al. (2018), computer vision Kazi et al. (2022), and it is
150   even useful when the data already has a graph structure Franceschi et al. (2019). In the context of
151   concept bottleneck models, it is clear that many of the concepts for image classification have intrinsic
152   interactions, and the concept scores are influenced by each other. A concept graph captures such
153   interactions by viewing concepts as nodes and connecting related pairs through edges. The natural
154   interpretability of graphs also provides another perspective to explain how the model learns to make
155   predictions through concepts.
156   To learn the latent graph, we employ a contrastive learning framework, with fundamental assumptions
157   that 1) the structure of the concept graph shall be label-agnostic; 2) each image can be seen as a
158   combination of a set of activated concepts in the concept graph. Thus, the activated concept graph
159   provides an augmented view of input images, and the representation from the activated concept graph
160   and the image encoder should have the maximum mutual information. Once the latent graph is learned,
161   it can be used to update the concept scores and project them to the label space for classification. The
      framework is shown in figure 1.

      3.2.1      C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING FOR G RAPH S TRUCTURE L EARNING
178
179   Graph structure learning is often in a self-supervised Kipf et al. (2018) or a semi-supervised learning
180   manner Franceschi et al. (2019). As an intrinsic structure, we expect that the concept graph should
181   not depend too much on the labels; otherwise, the model can easily learn shallow and superficial
182   dependencies among concepts and labels because the objective function acts on a Cartesian space of
183   concept and label. Thus, having a label-agnostic means to learn the latent graph is crucial. We intend
184   to discover the hidden correlations inside concept space solely, so we propose a contrastive learning
185   mechanism to self-supervisedly learn the graph. The essential assumption behind CBMs is to view
186
      images as a combination of concepts; in other words, the concept graph representation and the image
      representation for the same image should be regarded as a positive pair in contrastive learning.
187
188   Given a concept graph gi for each image vi , each node j of the graph is a concept, with the text
189   description tj and a concept score cji . Although the graph structure A is unified at the dataset level
190   and fixed for all images, the concept scores decide a different subset of concepts that are activated by
191   each image. That means for each image we can have a graph with different node activations. Thus,
192   the graph representation should be an augmented view of the image representation zvi .
193   To encode this concept graph gi , we use a graph neural network (GNN) and integrate concept
194   scores and descriptions in the nodes. In detail, we first use the text encoder Et to get the concept
195   embeddings zT = Et (T ). After getting the concept score vector ci , we will use it to weight the
196   concept embedding for node initialization in the GNN: zT,i = ci zT . The zT,i reflects the concept
197   description and activation information. We feed zT,i into the GNN to obtain the graph embedding.
198   To prevent overfitting and over-smoothing, we employ the DropEdge Rong et al. (2020a) to randomly
199   drop out some edges and sample a subgraph in each layer of the GNN. For simplicity, at each layer, we
200
      use the graph convolutional network (GCN) Kipf & Welling (2017) to update the node representation:

246   3.2.2   L ABEL -F REE & C ONCEPT-S UPERVISED OBJECTIVES
247
      When datasets lack concept annotations for each instance, we would refer to them as label-free
248
      settings. Once we have the updated concept scores c′i , we can use them to build the Graph CBM.
249   There are different ways to build the layer from concepts to labels based on different backbones. One
250   way is to use the new concept scores to replace the concept matrix in LF-CBM. Then we follow the
251   design of LF-CBM and train a backbone model (RN50) with a transformation layer to get the concept
252   bottleneck layer and the final sparse prediction layer. We call this model Graph LF-CBM.
253
      In addition to separating the contrastive learning phase and the concept-based classification phase,
254
      we can also combine the losses and train them together:
255
                               L = CE(ŷi , yi ) + α(Lemb + Lscore ) + βL-1(A),                                (7)
256
257   α and β are two hyperparameters adjusting the impacts of regularization. Instead of using the complex
258   design of LF-CBM, here we build a linear classification layer from c′i to the label: yi = f2 (c′i ). It can
259
      be seen as a variant of PCBM with concept graphs, so we call it Graph PCBM.
260   When there are concept supervisions, i.e., concept-supervised settings, the model learns the concept
261   information purely according to the ground truth annotations. In this case, we do not need a language
262   encoder to extract concept embeddings, so the Lcontrast only depends on Lscore (equation 4) and
263   L-1(A) (L-1 normalization controls latent graph complexity). We express the final loss function for
264   models with concept annotations below:
265                          L = CE(ŷi , yi ) + BCE(ĉi , c′i ) + αLscore + βL-1(A),                    (8)
266   where the BCE(·, ·) is the binary cross-entropy loss. Since concept annotations capture the concept
267   relations or interactions, the BCE term will impact the latent graph to realize the ground truth
268   relations to some extent, and the Lscore also regularizes the latent graph by viewing the c′i as a
269   hidden representation of image vi . Combining them, the model can learn a good quality latent graph
      structure.

286   Datasets: For evaluating Graph CBMs, we choose various real-world datasets ranging from common
287   objects to dermoscopic images. As the number of concept-supervised datasets is limited, We adopt
288   label-free settings Oikarinen et al. (2023) and PCBM Yuksekgonul et al. (2023) for other general
289   image classification tasks, in which we use multi-modal encoders to generate concepts for each
290   downstream dataset. We introduce 1) common objects: CUB Wah et al. (2011), Flower102 Nilsback
291   & Zisserman (2008), CIFAR-10 & CIFAR-100 Krizhevsky et al. (2009), and AwA2 Xian et al. (2018);
292   and 2) medicial domains: HAM10000 Tschandl (2018) and ChestXpert Irvin et al. (2019). Dataset
293
      details can be found in appendix A.1.
294   Since many datasets do not contain concept annotations, we will use LLMs to generate and filter the
295   concept set for each dataset. In CUB and CIFAR datasets, we use the same concept sets provided
296   by Oikarinen et al. (2023). Furthermore, we found that concepts can be redundant and noisy in the
297   CIFAR datasets. To mitigate these effects, we apply the submodular filtering algorithm proposed by
298   Yang et al. (2023) to shrink the concept set to 30 concepts for CIFAR-10. For Flower and HAM10000,

      we use the selected concepts in Yang et al. (2023) and filter them with LLMs to reduce the number of

301   Baseline: We choose the state-of-the-art CBM models that do not need concept annotations during
302   training, i.e., LF-CBM2 Oikarinen et al. (2023) and Post-hoc CBMs (PCBM) Yuksekgonul et al.
303   (2023). For multi-modality encoders, we choose the standard CLIP Radford et al. (2021). For
304   Label-free CBMs, we use CLIP(ViT-B/16) image encoder as the backbone for FLower, HAM10000,
305
      CIFAR, and CUB. The numbers of concepts for datasets are as follows: 370 for CUB, 108 for Flower,
      48 for HAM10000, 30 for CIFAR-10, and 50 for CIFAR-100. For CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 with
306
      the PCBM backbone, we perform submodular filtering to reduce the number of concepts from 143
307

308   concept-supervised setting, standard CBMs Koh et al. (2020) and CEMs Espinosa Zarlenga et al.
309   (2022) serve as our baseline, and we still use CLIP(ViT-B/16) to extract features on CUB (112

393
            implicitly modeling concept correlation through joint energies or conditional probabilities, the latent

394
            learned representations. The latent graph not only helps to learn a better model at training time but
395
            also makes the intervention more effective (see section 4.5).
396
397         The method of using latent graphs is independent of model architecture and orthogonal to baseline
398         approaches. As shown by performance enhancements in tables 1 and 2, learning latent graphs offers a
399
            robust generalization ability across different model architectures and training settings, which further
            supports the expectation of latent graph effectiveness on other SOTAs (in A.7, we provide a case
400
            study on CDM to further support this statement). More importantly, unlike Havasi et al. (2022); Xu

            et al. (2024); Vandenhirtz et al. (2024) that capture concept correlations only on datasets with concept

      5     L IMITATIONS
524
525
      One limitation of our Graph CBMs is that we only learn the latent interactions of the concepts.
526   However, the learned graphs cannot show the hierarchical structure or more complex relationships
527   among concepts. We believe utilizing external knowledge about concept relationships should be
528   useful in building more interpretable concept graphs. We will also extend the work and explore the
529   hierarchies of concepts instead of simply interacting with them.

      6     C ONCLUSIONS
532
533
      In this paper, we have presented Graph CBMs: a simple yet effective method to integrate graph
534   structures for capturing intrinsic correlations among concepts. Graph CBMs are orthogonal to
535   previous CBM methods and can be plugged into those models to facilitate task performances while
536   retaining interpretability. Graph-specific intervention policies also assist us to better sample concepts
537   that should be intervened. In addition, our learnable graph performance can match up with that of
538   a ground true concept graph. Future work may include 1) combining external knowledge graphs
539   to assume the graph distribution and do the graph sampling, and 2) applying latent graph ideas to
      develop explanations for individual neurons inside CBMs Oikarinen & Weng (2024).

            A.6                                      L ATENT G RAPHS O FFER ROBUSTNESS UNDER L ABEL -F REE S ETTINGS
935
           In this section, we further investigate the effect of latent graphs on concepts (nodes), and we design a
936        simple experiment to test such impacts. We first partition concepts into connected concepts (node
937        degree ≥ 1) and isolated concepts, and we also keep the whole concept set as a baseline group for
938        random attacks; then, we will randomly mask or perturb the same number of concepts in these groups
939        separately; lastly, we let our model predict those corrupted concepts. We choose the CUB as the
940        testing dataset for this experiment4 .
941
          As shown in figure 7, attacking connected concepts does not harm the model performance as much
942       as attacking isolated concepts or random concepts. We hypothesize that the latent graph structure
943       provides much better robustness towards connected concepts, while the isolated ones cannot benefit
944       from it. When masking or perturbing a concept, there will be information missing for the final
945       prediction layer. Nevertheless, the latent graph structure can aggregate neighborhood information
946       to recover the masked or perturbed concept so that the final prediction layer is still able to make a
947       reasonable prediction.
