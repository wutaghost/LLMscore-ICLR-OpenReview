011             One of the inherent challenges in deploying transformers on time series is that
012             reality only happens once; namely, one typically only has access to a single
013             trajectory of the data-generating process comprised of non-i.i.d. observations. We
014             derive non-asymptotic statistical guarantees in this setting through bounds on the
015             generalization of a transformer network at a future-time t, given that it has been
016             trained using N ⩽ t observations from a single perturbed trajectory of a bounded
017             and exponentially ergodic Markov process. √     We obtain a generalization bound

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032   Transformers Vaswani et al. (2017) have become the main architectural building block in deep
033   learning-based state-of-the-art foundation models Bommasani et al. (2021); Zhao et al. (2023);
034   Wei et al. (2022). Transformers are primarily deployed on sequential learning tasks which have
035   complex temporal relationships, and thus, transformers are trained on non-i.i.d. data. The i.i.d.
036
      assumption is typically made (e.g. Neyshabur et al. (2015); Bartlett et al. (2017); Zhang et al. (2024))
      to derive theoretical statistical guarantees, but in practice, it is rarely satisfied; e.g. in natural language
037
      processing (NLP) (Zhou et al., 2021), physics Paul and Baschnagel (2013), medical research Beck
038
      and Pauker (1983), reinforcement learning Sutton and Barto (2018), optimal control Touzi (2013),
039   and in finance Föllmer and Schied (2011). This creates a mismatch between available statistical
040   guarantees in deep learning (which often rely on the i.i.d. assumption or they do not provide explicit
041   constants for transformers trained on non-i.i.d. data) and how transformers are used in practice.
042

      bound is that it relies on a recently well-studied optimal transport-theoretic notion of exponential
093
      ergodicity, which is easily verified, or already known, for most data-generating processes. Indeed,

      formers either concern: in-context learning for linear transformers Zhang et al. (2024); Garg et al.
      (2022), transformers Von Oswald et al. (2023); Akyürek et al. (2023) trained with gradient descent,

      Generalization bounds for multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) have been actively studied for years. For
      classification problems, these generalization bounds often rely on bounding the VC-dimension of
113
      classes of MLPs, depending on their depth, width, norm bounds on their parameters and activation
114
      functions Bartlett et al. (1998; 2019), or similar quantities. In regression problems, one instead
115   controls the Rademacher complexity of similar classes of MLPs Bartlett et al. (2017); Neyshabur
116   et al. (2019); Yin et al. (2019), due to the results such as Koltchinskii (2001); Bartlett and Mendelson
117   (2002), or turns to instance-dependent bounds which control the path-norm of the MLP Neyshabur
118   et al. (2015); Golowich et al. (2020); Galanti et al. (2024) and local variants of these quantities;
119   e.g. Bartlett et al. (2005) or Hou et al. (2023b). Our generalization bounds also partially borrow ideas
120   from both of these directions, but instead, we use high-order sensitivities (partial derivatives) of our
121   transformer networks
                       √       to obtain tighter bounds for large enough N . This does not yield a faster rate,
122   since the O(1/ N ) rate is generally optimal, by the central limit theorem, but it allows us to better
123   control the constants in the generalization bound and thus yields more precise bounds. Thus, a key
124   part of our technical contributions is the computation of these higher-order derivatives (C s -norms,
125
      see Definition 3) both of the transformer and the MLP models using smooth activation functions.
126   These statistical learning results assume that the data samples are i.i.d. However, time-series data
127   is rarely i.i.d, they are often generated by Markov process or at least embeddable into a Markovian
128   setting Cuchiero and Teichmann (2019; 2020a). Though there are generalization bounds for non-i.i.d.
129   relying on martingale arguments e.g. Kontorovich (2014) and concentration of measure phenomena
130   for martingale sums e.g. Bercu et al. (2015); Boucheron et al. (2013) those results primarily focus
131
      on Lipschitz functions; thus, they do not consider higher-order derivatives. Our results add to this
      literature since we rely on the concentration of measure phenomena for Markov processes with respect
132
      to smooth counterparts of the 1-Wasserstein distance (a tool used in many martingale arguments,
133
      e.g. Kontorovich and Raginsky (2017), for Lipschitz classes).
134
135   There are several results in the literature addressing learning with non-i.i.d. data satisfying a mix-
136   ing/ergodicity condition dating back, at least, to Yu (1994). However, none of these results provide
137
      explicit generalization bounds for transformer classes as they either rely on bounding the Rademacher
      complexity of the transformer class, e.g. in applying Mohri and Rostamizadeh (2008), or they rely
138
      on computing the cardinality of delta nets Ziemann and Tu (2022), both of which necessitate the
139
      computation of the worst-case Lipschitz (or C s norm) of any transformer in the hypothesis class
140   using (van der Vaart and Wellner, 2023, Theorem 2.7.4) and (Lorentz et al., 1996, Equation (15.1.8)).
141   These highly technical computations of the worst-case C s norm case of any transformer our hypothe-
142   sis class was never computed before our Theorems 6 and 7. Alternatively, prior results impose strong
143   assumptions on the data-generating process Simchowitz et al. (2018); Foster et al. (2020).
144
      We require that the data-generating Markov process has an exponentially contracting Markov kernel
145
      Kloeckner (2020). For Markov chains, i.e. finite-state space Markov processes, this means that the
146   generator (Q-matrix) of the Markov chain has a spectral gap. These spectral gaps are actively studied
147   in the Markov chain literature Mufa (1996); Kontoyiannis and Meyn (2012); Atchadé (2021); Paulin
148   (2015); Kloeckner (2019) since these have a finite mixing time, meaning that the distribution of such
149   Markov chains approaches their stationary limit after a large finite time has elapsed; i.e. they have
150   well-behaved (approximate) mixing times Montenegro et al. (2006); Hsu et al. (2015); Wolfer and
151   Kontorovich (2019); Zamanlooy (2024). We rely on actively-studied optimal transport-theoretic
152   notions of mixing since it is easily verified, or already known, for most data-generating processes
153   than more classical notions; e.g. Kuznetsov and Mohri (2017); Mohri and Rostamizadeh (2010).
154   Our generalization bounds rely on concentration of measure arguments for the “smooth” integral
155   probability metrics (IPMs) studied in Kloeckner (2020); Riekert (2022), by refining the arguments
156   of Hou et al. (2023b); Benitez et al. (2023); Kratsios et al. (2024) to the non-i.i.d. and smooth setting.
157   In the i.i.d. case, our computation of the maximum C s -norm (R ⩾ 0) of the class TC (Theorem 6)
158   can be used to relate the rate at which measure concentrates to other bounds based on classical
159   quantities such as the Rademacher complexity of the class of C s -functions on Rd ; which is bounded
160
      by R, see e.g. (Sriperumbudur et al., 2012, Theorem 3.3).

524   4 C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS , AND F UTURE W ORK
525   We provided a theoretical foundation for the future-generalization of transformer trained on a single
526   perturbed realization of a time-series trajectory (Theorem 1). Our results thus help provide insight on
527
      the reliability of LLMs outside the i.i.d. framework and their principled use in time-series analysis.
528   We obtain explicit estimates on the constants in these generalization bounds which relied on explicitly
529   bounding all the higher-order derivatives of transformers; in terms of their number of attention
530   heads, activation functions, depth, width, and weights constraints (Theorems 6 and 7). These bounds
531   can equally be used in conjunction with classical tools, e.g. Rademacher or VC-type bounds in the
532   i.i.d. setting, or other applications where one needs to understand the higher-order sensitivities of
533
      transformers to their inputs.
534   Several dynamical systems and financial markets have long-term memory and thus are non-Markovian.
535   In future work, it would be interesting to extend our results to cover such settings as well. It would
536   be interesting to extend our generalizations bounds to the fully non-Markovian setting, where every
537   Markovian lift of X· is infinite-dimensional. However, one would have to extend the concentration of
538   measure result used in Benitez et al. (2024) to allow for non-i.i.d. data or use a local Glivenko-Cantelli
539
      theorem such as Cohen and Kontorovich (2023). One would only need more general concentration
      inequalities than Proposition 5, which is already extended well beyond the standard i.i.d. setting.

1217   aim of to make each of these concrete by numerically estimating them, which we report in a series of
1218   tables. Importantly, we see how subtle choices of the activation function used to define the transformer
1219   model can have dramatic consequences on the size of these constants, which could otherwise be
1220   hidden in big O notation.
1221   Interestingly, in Tables 1 and 2, we see that the softplus activation function produces significantly
1222   tighter bounds than the tanh activation function through much smaller constants, and the GeLU and
1223   SWISH activation functions are a relatively comparable second-place.

1228   bound lies within the constraints imposed on the parameters. Despite the seemingly conservative
1229   nature of the chosen parameter-bounds, it is important to acknowledge their alignment with the
