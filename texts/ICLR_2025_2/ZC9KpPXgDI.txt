      The development of Artificial Intelligence (AI) has four stages: (1) prediction AI, e.g., ResNet
      (He et al., 2016) in image classification; (2) generation AI, e.g., ChatGPT (Achiam et al., 2023) in
034
      language generation; (3) autonomous agent AI, Voyager (Wang et al., 2023a) autonomously plays
035
      Minecraft game (Fan et al., 2022); (4) Artificial Generalization Intelligence (AGI). Humans have
036   made rapid progress in generative AI, and we are excitingly heading to the third stage, the era of AI
037   agent (Liu et al., 2023). One prevalent application of AI agents is customized large generative mod-
038   els (LGMs) agents (OpenAI, 2024a), e.g., AgentGPT (GitHub, 2024a), SuperAGI (GitHub, 2024d),
039   MetaGPT (Hong et al., 2024b;a), GPT Researcher (GitHub, 2024c) and many so on. In particular,
040   recently, Apple Inc. introduced Apple Intelligence (Apple, 2024), signaling the integration of LGMs
041   into physical devices. This innovation allows devices to use personal information for real-life as-
042   sistance, such as entering passport numbers when booking flights or informing users of their latest
043   meetings. With increased AI capabilities, privacy concerns become significant, as the more personal
044
      information devices handle, the greater the potential privacy risks.
045   One fundamental technique used in LGMs is cross-attention (Vaswani et al., 2017), which is an
046   essential module in retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020), system prompt,
047   guided stable diffusion, and many so on. In RAG, to be more professional, the LGMs answer
048   user input queries by using a domain-specific database under cross-attention, which may contain
049   specific privacy data and knowledge so that the LGMs gain additional power. For system prompts,
050
      based on cross-attention, some customized long prompts, e.g., user information or concrete rules,
      are concatenated before user input to follow human instructions better, which are commonly used in
051
      ChatGPT (GitHub, 2024b), Claude3 (Anthropic, 2024) and other commercial LGMs.

      as they contain sensitive information about users and companies. These data and prompts are the

      1.1   R ELATED W ORK
112
113   Differential Privacy in Data Structure and Attention. Differential privacy (DP) is a flourishing
114   and powerful technique that has enormous applications in the topic of private machine learning.
115   In the era of Large Generative Models (LGMs), there are three primary approaches to ensuring
116   privacy: (1) during the pre-training stage: to protect training data (Abadi et al., 2016; Ponomareva
117   et al., 2023), (2) during the adaptation stage: to protect target data (Behnia et al., 2022; Singh et al.,
118
      2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Yu et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2022a; Huang et al., 2024),
      (3) during the inference stage: to protect user/system prompts (Edemacu & Wu, 2024) and RAG
119
      data (Lewis et al., 2020). To protect training data, DP-SGD (Abadi et al., 2016) uses DP optimizer
120
      to ensure data privacy, severing as the traditional baseline method. Recently, numerous works have
121   aimed to improve this method by integrating DP in both the pre-training and fine-tuning stages
122   of LGMs (Yu et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Golatkar et al., 2022; Behnia et al., 2022; Shi et al.,
123   2022a; Mattern et al., 2022; Singh et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b). However, DP-
124   SGD confines differential privacy to the optimizer. In contrast, we propose a novel approach that
125   integrates DP directly into the attention mechanism, supported by strong theoretical analysis and
126   guarantees. Given the resource-intensive nature of training LGMs, our technique offers a practical
127   alternative for models trained with standard SGD, which lack inherent privacy guarantees. In such
128   cases, applying DP-SGD would require retraining the models, which is computationally expensive,
129
      whereas our method avoids this additional cost.
130   To protect user/system prompts, Edemacu & Wu (2024) provides a survey on both DP and non-
131   DP methods. In the use of LGMs, prompting methods almost become a standard way for infer-
132   ence (Schulhoff et al., 2024). Given the billions of prompt interactions daily, ensuring privacy is
133   essential (Mai et al., 2023). We refer readers to Appendix A for more related works.
134   Roadmap. In Section 2, we present the preliminary of differential privacy (DP) and cross-attention.
135   In Section 3, we present the main result of our cross-attention theorem (Theorem 3.1). In Sec-
136   tion 4, we outline the main results of our algorithms. In Section 5, we discuss DP-related topics and
137   potential extensions. In Section 6, we conclude our paper.

351   4.2   DPT REE , DPT REE D ISTANCE , AND DPT REE H IGH D IM
352
353   The unweighted distance query has been explored in prior works (Huang & Roth, 2014; Backurs
354   et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a). Specifically, Huang & Roth (2014) leverages online learning tech-
355
      niques to approximate the sum of distances, while Backurs et al. (2024) introduces a DP data struc-
      ture based on a node-contaminated balanced binary tree. Furthermore, Liu et al. (2024a) presents a
356
      new data representation in tree nodes, where each node stores the sum of distances from one point
357
      to multiple points. In contrast, we focus on the weighted distance query, generalizing their results.
358
359   We design a basic data structure DPT REE (Algorithm 2) that answers summation queries by a sum-
360   mation segment tree with truncated Laplace noise (Definition 2.4). The algorithm first builds a
361
      binary summation tree in an array and then adds truncated Laplace noises to each node. During a
      query, the algorithm traverses each layer of the binary structure based on the input y, aggregating
362
      values from sibling nodes by accessing at most O(log n) nodes along the path. It then returns the
363
      accumulated left and right sums as the query result (Algorithm 2). See more details in Section C.
364
365   We then design DPT REE D ISTANCE, a one-dimensional weighted ℓpp distance data structure detailed
366   in Algorithm 4 and 5. Initialization involves assigning each data point to the nearest bin and aggre-
367
      gating their weighted polynomial terms into multiple arrays (illustrated in Figure 1), which are then
      used to initialize several instances of our DPT REE. At query time, the algorithm retrieves aggregated
368
      weights from each DPT REE corresponding to the query point and combines them using binomial
369
      coefficients and distance powers to compute the one-dimensional weighted ℓpp distance. Guided by
370   Eq. (3), we design DPT REE H IGH D IM (Algorithm 6), which extends DPT REE D ISTANCE to higher
371   dimension by constructing independent data structures for each coordinate. See details in Section E
372   and F.

477   5     D ISCUSSION
478
479   How do we extend to self-attention and other data structures? As self-attention is a more
480   fundamental module in LGMs, we would like to extend our data structure to this setting. However,
481   the challenge we faced was the dynamic update in tree nodes for each query for self-attention, which
482
      our current analysis does not support. How we can solve this challenge is crucial, and we leave it as
      our future direction.
483
484   Moreover, we observe that Li et al. (2015) introduces the DP matrix mechanism, which offers an
485   alternative to our currently used binary tree data structure. A preliminary idea for extending this
      is as follows: consider A = exp(QK ⊤ /d) as defined in Definition 1.1, where Q of size m × d


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      represents the query matrix with m linear queries, and K serves as the database. Leveraging the
487   results from Li et al. (2015), we could design an alternative algorithm to enhance the current binary
488   tree data structure, DPT REE. We leave this exploration for future work.
489
490   Why not add noise to some other places? Where and how to add DP noises is an impor-
491   tant
      Pn problem to ask during the DP algorithmd design. In this paper,n we consider the problem of
492      i=1 wi exp(⟨xi , y⟩/d) where y, xi ∈ [0, R] and w ∈ [−Rw , Rw ] (Definition 4.1). Notice that
493   the only place where we add noises is in the most basic building block data structure DPT REE (Al-
494   gorihtm 2). From Lemma C.3 and the way we initialize DPT REE in Algorithm 4, we see that the
495   sensitivity ∆ of this problem is 2Rw .
496   A simple method for adding noise involves adding n noises to a length n array, with each item
497   wi exp(⟨xi , y⟩/d) for i ∈ [n]. However, this approach increases the error by a factor of n by basic
498   composition (Fact B.8) and also makes the model dependent on the number of queries. Besides,
499   it only supports a single query and requires rebuilding the tree for each new query, rendering it
500   impractical. In contrast, our current noise-adding technique (Lines 8 and 14 of Algorithm 2) utilizes
501   a summation tree such that the error only increases by a factor of poly log n. This method also
502
      supports multiple queries, eliminating the need to rebuild the tree each time.
503
      How to remove the relative error parameter α? The relative error parameter α in Theorem 3.1
504
      appears because of the (1 + α)-approximation introduced in Algorithm 4 to reduce the number of
505
      required iterations from naive O(n) to O(log(n)/α). However, we notice that a recent work (Liu
506   et al., 2024a) does not utilize (1 + α)-approximation and still achieves O(log n) iteration number.
507   They introduce a new tree node representation where each node stores the sum of distances from one
508   point to multiple points, enabling the answer to be divided into only log n values, each combining
509   two distance values, two count values, and y itself. Our DPT REE algorithms can be integrated with
510   their method, thus removing parameter α.
511
512   6   C ONCLUSION
513
514   To our knowledge, we are the first work to provide differential privacy for cross-attention. This
515   paper presents the DPT REE data structures, which provide a differential privacy guarantee for the
516   cross-attention module in large generative models. This is achieved by transforming the cross-
517   attention mechanism into a weighted distance problem. Furthermore, our algorithm is robust to
518   adaptive queries, allowing users to interact with the model arbitrarily without extracting sensitive
519   information from the system prompts or RAG data. Our results may inspire more privacy algorithm
520   design in large generative models.

1039   Differential Privacy Guarantee Analysis. Ever since Dwork et al. (2006) proposes the notion of
1040   differential privacy (DP), it has become one of the most essential standards of privacy protection in
1041   both theoretical and empirical ways (Dwork, 2008; Li et al., 2017; Zhao & Chen, 2022; Ponomareva
1042   et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023). DP provides a powerful, robust, and quantifiable privacy definition,
1043   allowing algorithm design with concrete privacy and accuracy guarantee (Hay et al., 2009; Esfandiari
1044   et al., 2022; Andoni et al., 2023; Li & Li, 2023b; Huang & Yi, 2021; Ghazi et al., 2023; Backurs
1045   et al., 2024; Cohen-Addad et al., 2022a; Epasto et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2022; Hopkins et al., 2023;
1046
       Narayanan, 2022; 2023; Jung et al., 2019; Li & Li, 2024; Fan & Li, 2022; Fan et al., 2024; Li & Li,
       2023a; Cherapanamjeri et al., 2023; Cohen-Addad et al., 2022b; Dong et al., 2024; Farhadi et al.,
1047
       2022; Gopi et al., 2021; 2023; Li et al., 2022; Gopi et al., 2022; Eliáš et al., 2020; Song et al., 2023b;
1048
       Dinur et al., 2023; Woodruff et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023a; Gao et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024a; Li
1049   et al., 2024b). Additionally, new mechanisms have been proposed beyond the traditional Laplace,
