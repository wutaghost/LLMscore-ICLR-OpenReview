011                                                         A BSTRACT
012
013                Large language models (LLMs) excel at addressing general human queries, yet they can
014                falter or produce unexpected responses in specific scenarios. Gaining insight into the
015                internal states of LLMs is key to understanding their successes and failures, as well as
016                to refining their capabilities. Recent efforts have applied sparse autoencoders to learn a
017                feature basis for explaining LLM hidden spaces. However, current post-hoc explanation
018
                   methods can not effectively describe the semantic meaning of the learned features, and it
                   is difficult to steer LLM behaviors by manipulating these features. Our analysis reveals
019
                   that existing explanation methods suffer from the frequency bias issue, i.e., they tend to
020
                   focus on trivial linguistic patterns rather than semantics. To overcome this, we propose
021                explaining the learned features from a fixed vocabulary set to mitigate the frequency bias,
022                and designing a novel explanation objective based on the mutual information theory to
023                better express the meaning of the features. We further suggest two strategies to steer LLM
024                representations by modifying sparse feature activations in response to user queries during
025                runtime. Empirical results demonstrate that our method generates more discourse-level
026                explanations than the baselines, and can effectively steer LLM behaviors to defend against
027                jailbreak attacks in the wild. These findings highlight the value of explanations for steering
028                LLM representations in downstream applications.1

031   1       I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong capabilities in responding to general human re-
034
      quests (Achiam et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024). Meanwhile, we still often observe failed
      or unexpected responses in certain situations (Ji et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024). Gaining insight into the
035
      factors behind their successes and failures is crucial for further improving these models. A straightforward
036
      way to understand LLM behaviors is directly studying their hidden activations or internal weights. However,
037   it is non-trivial to interpret the hidden states of modern LLMs because of their polysemantic nature (Arora
038   et al., 2018; Scherlis et al., 2022), where each dimension of the spaces encodes multiple pieces of unique
039   features. This property allows LLMs to encode more features than the dimensions of their hidden space, but
040   it presents significant challenges for human interpretation and understanding.
041
      Researchers have made significant efforts to overcome the polysemantic challenge. Linear probing (Camp-
042
      bell et al., 2023; Burns et al.; Marks & Tegmark, 2023; Gurnee et al., 2023) is a conventional technique to
043   detect whether an LLM learns a particular feature of interest. Unfortunately, the feasibility of this technique
044   is bounded by its requirement of an annotated dataset with samples including or excluding certain features.

059   To reduce the need for annotated datasets, researchers (Cunningham et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Freire
060   et al., 2024; Bricken et al., 2023) are switching to decomposing the hidden spaces of LLMs in an unsuper-
061   vised way. In this context, recent research has explored the sparse autoencoder (Olshausen & Field, 1997;
062   Makhzani & Frey, 2013) technique, demonstrating their effectiveness in learning a number of sparse features
063   as a basis to reconstruct the hidden spaces of advanced LLMs with hundreds of billions of parameters from
064   Anthropic (Templeton et al., 2024), OpenAI (Gao et al., 2024), and Google (Lieberum et al., 2024). These
065   sparse features are expected to be interpretable, since each feature should only react to a specific kind of
066   content, showing a monosemantic nature instead of a polysemantic one.
067   However, researchers find that the learned sparse features have not shown strong enough explainability to
068   meet our expectations, i.e., understanding LLM encoded features and even steering LLM behaviors. Specifi-
069   cally, Makelov et al. (2024) and Chaudhary & Geiger (2024) designed dedicated tasks to test whether sparse
070   autoencoders could detect sufficient features for certain tasks. However, they found that sparse autoencoders
071   cannot capture enough relevant features to meet these goals, even for simple and experimental-level tasks
072   with clear training samples. Meanwhile, researchers (Gao et al., 2024) also observed that many learned
073
      sparse features from advanced LLMs could not be effectively explained with current techniques. These
      headwinds undermine confidence in extending such techniques to real-world applications.
074
075   In this work, we enhance the interpretability and usability of sparse autoencoder features by introducing a
076   new post-hoc explanation method and strategies to steer LLM representations with these features. We first
077   formalize the text generation process with the topic model (Blei & Lafferty, 2006; Arora et al., 2016), reveal-
078   ing that sparse autoencoders learn both discourse topics and linguistic patterns as features simultaneously,
079
      with linguistic patterns being less semantically critical but often dominating. To address this issue, we pro-
      pose to leverage a fixed vocabulary set to collect explanations and ensure that critical information on learned
080
      features is captured based on a mutual information-based objective. We also explore steering LLM repre-
081
      sentations by modifying the activation of explained features during runtime. Figure 1 shows some examples
082   of explanations generated by our method compared to other explainers, and Figure 2 visualizes our pipeline
083   to steer LLMs with explained features. Experiments on open-source LLMs show that our method provides
084   more meaningful discourse-level explanations, and they are practically usable for downstream tasks. We
085   summarize our contributions as follows:
086
      • Our theoretical analysis identifies a key challenge in explaining learned features from sparse autoencoders,
087
        i.e., the frequency bias between the discourse and linguistic features.
088
089   • We propose leveraging a fixed vocabulary set to mitigate the frequency bias for explaining learned features.
090
        Experimental results show that our method provides more discourse-level explanations than the others.
091   • We propose steering LLM representations by modifying their activations in response to user inputs during
092     runtime. We apply this approach with our explanations to prevent real-world jailbreak attacks, and show
093     that the steered LLM achieves a significant safety improvement while baseline explanations fail.

      2     P RELIMINARY

      2.1     P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
111
112   Let V denote the vocabulary set, and X be a text of length N , where each token xn ∈ V is the n-th token
113   of X. Given a large language model f , the embedding of X at the l-th layer is denoted as X(l) ∈ RN ×D ,
114   where D is latent dimension. In the rest of this paper, we omit superscript (l) for simplification of notations.
115   Our goal is to interpret these embeddings by extracting semantic features from the latent space. We assume
116   that there are C learned feature vectors W ∈ RC×D , so that X can be decomposed as a linear combination
117   of these feature vectors, i.e., X ≈ AW, where A ∈ RL×C are weights of the linear combination for the
118   given instance X. Let Wc denote the c-th row of W. After the decomposition, X is explainable if we could
119   understand the semantic meaning of each learned feature vector Wc . To achieve this, we aim at seeking a
120   set of words Ic ⊂ V to explain each learned feature Wc with natural language.
121
122   2.2     L EARNING AND I NTERPRETING LLM S WITH S PARSE AUTOENCODERS
123
124   Sparse autoencoders have shown great promise to learn the feature vectors for latent representation decom-
125   position and explaining LLMs in practice (Gao et al., 2024; Lieberum et al., 2024). A standard sparse
126   autoencoder (Olshausen & Field, 1997) is a two-layer multi-layer perceptron X̂ = σ(XW) · W′⊤ , where
127
      W, W′ ∈ RD×C are trainable parameters and σ refers to the ReLU activation function. Typically, a tight
      weight strategy is applied, i.e., W′ = W, and the trained weights W are considered as the learned feature
128
      vectors. The traditional training objective of sparse autoencoders can be written as ||X − X̂||2 + λ||A||1 ,
129
      where A = σ(XW) and λ ∈ R+ is a hyper-parameter to balance the impact of the sparsity constraint. The
130   Top-K sparse autoencoder (Makhzani & Frey, 2013) replaces the ReLU function with the Top-K activation,
131   enforcing each reconstruction to apply with no more than K learned features. Recent studies (Templeton
132   et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024; Lieberum et al., 2024) have shown that Top-K sparse autoencoders can be
133   used to learn sparse features for reconstructing token-level representations from LLMs, where these sparse
134   features are expected to be interpretable by humans.
135
      However, there are limited explorations on collecting a natural language explanation Ic for each of the
136   learned feature vectors Wc . The most intuitive strategy (Bricken et al., 2023) is collecting some N-gram
137   spans that could best activate the feature vector Wc over a large corpus. Some researchers (Gao et al., 2024)
138   leverage the Neuron-to-Graph (N2G) algorithm (Foote et al., 2023) to refine the N-gram spans for more
139   precise interpretations. However, it has been found (Gao et al., 2024) that these methods still fail to generate
140   explanations for a large number of learned features from sparse autoencoders trained for LLMs.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
142
143
      This section first theoretically studies the properties of text generation for learning sparse autoencoders, com-
144   paring them to traditional image generation scenarios. With these insights, we propose a mutual information-
145   based post-hoc method to explain the semantics of feature vectors learned by a trained sparse autoencoder.
146   Finally, we design two strategies to steer LLM representations with the explained features.
147
148   3.1   L EARNING S PARSE F EATURES FROM T EXTUAL DATA
149
150   Conventional sparse autoencoders (Olshausen & Field, 1997) are developed based on an assumption for
151   image data, where each image is a linear combination of features. A sparse autoencoder learns an over-
152   complete set of visual features, so that any image can be decomposed and reconstructed with the learned
153
      features. Early works (Bricken et al., 2023; Cunningham et al., 2023) borrow this framework from image
      data to textual data, assuming that each token is linearly related to a set of features. However, they ignore
154
      some natures of textual data, leading to a suboptimal solution to learning sparse features (Gao et al., 2024).
155
156   To start with our theoretical analysis, we consider the text generation task as a dynamic process under
157   the topic-model assumption (Steyvers & Griffiths, 2007; Arora et al., 2016; 2018), where each word xn is
158   generated at the n-th step. This topic model describes a dynamic process in which a person first comes up
159
      with a topic cn they want to express in mind and then selects a word xn that best represents the topic to
      say. It means that, in topic models, text generation begins with a predetermined “mind” or theme, guiding
160
      word selection to align with that central idea. Similarly, autoregressive language models (Radford et al.,
161
      2019) generate text by sequentially predicting each word based on prior context alone, achieving coherence
162   through accumulated context rather than a predefined topic, thus allowing the theme to emerge organically
163   without explicit guidance. Formally, this dynamic process can be driven by the random walk of a discourse
164   vector ecn ∈ Rd representing what it talks about. The discourse vector ecn does a slow random walk at each
165   step n, i.e., ecn = ecn−1 + eϵn , where eϵn ∼ N d (0, σ). Also, at each step, a word xn ∈ V is sampled based
166   on the discourse vector ecn . To this end, the text generation process for a sequence of words X is given by:

179   Equation 2 reveals some critical characteristics of textual data that is different from image data. Firstly, there
180
      is a shared discourse topic c0 across all words xn from the same sentence X, for n = 1, ..., N . However,

181

182
      features learned for discourse semantical topics (i.e., ec0 ) and linguistic patterns (i.e., eϵn ). In other words,
183   each learned sparse feature may store both discourse and linguistic information, where the latter is less useful
184   for steering LLMs than the previous one. Additionally, discourse topics are rarer than linguistic patterns,
185   as each instance has N times more linguistic patterns than discourse topics, we call it the frequency bias.
186   This issue leads to the sparse features that prioritize capturing the linguistic patterns, raising the challenge
187   of interpreting the discourse topics encoded within LLMs.

      3.2   E XPLAINING L EARNED F EATURES WITH NATURAL L ANGUAGE
189
190   To interpret the learned features {wc }Cc=1 , existing works (Bricken et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024) typically
191   enumerate a large number of texts, and then treat those whose hidden representations could most activate
192   the learned features as the interpretations. This method works well for interpreting the learned linguistic
193   patterns as they are frequently presented in the corpus, while it is hard to discover the learned discourse
194   topics because the more frequent linguistic patterns dominate (see discussions in Sec. 4.2.2), leading to fail
195   of explaining the amount of learned features (Gao et al., 2024). Since our goal is to understand and control
196
      LLM behaviors, we aim to interpret those discourse topics within a feasible budget cost.
197   To tackle the challenge of frequency bias, we propose to leverage a fixed vocabulary set V of a general
198   corpus instead of its raw texts. Specifically, our goal is to seek a K-word set Ic ⊂ V that can describe
199   most information of the c-th feature vector Wc . Mathematically, we let C denote the knowledge encoded
200   by Wc and measure the information of C described by a given word set V ′ ⊂ V based on their mutual
201   information (Cover, 1999). To this end, the objective of constructing Ic is defined as

245   4       E XPERIMENTS
246
247   This section investigates two research questions. RQ1: Does the proposed method generate more discourse-
248
      level explanations than traditional methods? RQ2: Whether these discourse-level explanations are useful in
      steering LLM behaviors? To answer these questions, we first train a Top-K sparse autoencoder for open-
249
      sourced LLMs as our foundation (Sec. 4.1). We then compare the explanations of the trained sparse autoen-
250
      coder with our proposed and other explanation methods for RQ1 (Sec. 4.2). We finally explore the usability
251   of these explanations for downstream tasks, i.e., jailbreak defense, for RQ2 (Sec. 4.3).
252
253   4.1 G ENERAL S ETTINGS
254
      Language Models. In this work, we study LLMs from the Mistral family (Jiang et al., 2023) as it has
255
      demonstrated its strong usability in the wild. In particular, we choose the Mistral-7B-Instruct model. We
256
      follow the settings from previous work (Lieberum et al., 2024) to select our target layer. In their work,
257   they train SAEs with hidden representations from the 10th, 21st, and 32nd layers of Gemma2-9B-Instruct.
258   Considering Gemma2-9B-Instruct consists of 42 layers, these numbers roughly refer to the first 24%-th,
259   50%-th, and 76%-th layers, respectively. In addition, since our goal is to steer LLM predictions and re-
260   searchers (Nostalgebraist, 2020) have observed that LLMs begin performing next-word prediction tasks in
261   their shallow layers, we seek a shallow layer to leave room for changing LLM predictions. To this end, we
262   choose the most shallow layer from (Lieberum et al., 2024), i.e., the 24%-th layer of the entire model, which
263   refers to the 8th layer of Mistral-7B-Instruct with 32 layers in total. Without specifics, the greedy search
264   decoding with a maximum of 512 new tokens is applied to our experiments for reproducibility.
265
266
      Datasets. Since our goal is to develop sparse autoencoders for understanding and controlling LLMs for
      different applications, we select various instruction-tuning datasets for training our backbone sparse autoen-
267
      coder. In specific, we contain the training subset of the ShareGPT (RyokoAI, 2023), UltraChat (Ding et al.,
268
      2023), HH-RLHF (Bai et al., 2022), WebGLM-QA (Liu et al., 2023), Evol-Instruct (Xu et al., 2023), and
269   HelpSteer2 (Wang et al., 2024) datasets. For the UltraChat dataset, we randomly sample 400K instances
270   from its training subset. We also drop duplicate prompts across different datasets. To this end, we have
271   retained about 711K unique user queries covering diverse topics and user intents. We randomly select 90%
272   of samples to form our training set, and the rest is our validation set. Overall, we collect 113M tokens for
273   training and 12M tokens for validating, with an average length of 177.9 tokens per query.
274

313   4.2     E VALUATING E XPLANATIONS OF S PARSE F EATURES
314
315   Exactly measuring the explanation quality of features from sparse autoencoders is still an open question (Ra-
316   jamanoharan et al., 2024b). One that is commonly applied is conducting human studies (Bricken et al., 2023;
317   Rajamanoharan et al., 2024a; Gao et al., 2024; Rajamanoharan et al., 2024b), where the human subjects are
318   asked to determine whether an explanation is meaningful or not. We follow this paradigm to evaluate the

445   5   R ELATED W ORKS
446   Modern large language models have shown promising text-generation abilities, prompting researchers to
447   explore their internal mechanisms. One approach (Belinkov et al., 2018; Jawahar et al., 2019; Rogers et al.,
448   2021) develops contrastive datasets to probe hidden states for specific features, but it is limited by the poly-
449   semantic nature of neurons (Elhage et al., 2022; Olah et al., 2020), making the explanations non-concise and
450   difficult to apply in downstream tasks. To overcome this, researchers (Bricken et al., 2023) propose learning
451   orthogonal basis vectors to better understand LLMs. Early works (Beren & Black, 2022; Wu et al., 2024)
452   applied singular vector analysis to identify concise, interpretable directions in neuron activations. Soon af-
453   ter, sparse autoencoders (Bricken et al., 2023; Cunningham et al., 2023) were introduced, allowing for a
454
      more flexible settings. Sparse autoencoders, initially used to analyze image data (Olshausen & Field, 1997;
      Makhzani & Frey, 2013), are now being applied to LLMs. Researchers from Anthropic (Bricken et al.,
455
      2023) and EleutherAI (Cunningham et al., 2023) demonstrated that activations from smaller models like
456
      GPT-2 and Pythia yield highly interpretable features. Subsequent studies showed these features help inter-
457   pret model behaviors in tasks like indirect object identification (Makelov, 2024), translation (Dumas et al.),
458   and circuit detection (Marks et al., 2024). Recent works (Templeton et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024; Lieberum
459   et al., 2024) confirm this technique’s success with larger LLMs. Our study follows this path, and advances
460   by developing a method for generating discourse-level explanations to steer LLM representations.
461
462   6   C ONCLUSIONS
463
      This study steps a solid stamp toward understanding and steering LLM representations in the wild. Our
464
      theoretical analysis first reveals a frequency bias between discourse and linguistic features learned by sparse
465   autoencoders. To eliminate this bias, we propose seeking words from a fixed vocabulary set and designing
466   a mutual-information-based objective to ensure the collected words capture the features’ meanings. Ad-
467   ditionally, we demonstrate that our steering strategies effectively enhance the safety of LLMs using our
468   mutual-information-based explanations, while baseline methods fail to achieve the same. Overall, this study
469   underscores the importance of discourse-level explanations in effectively controlling LLM behavior.

      B        T RAINING S PARSE AUTOENCODERS ON M ISTRAL -7B
800
801
      Our training procedures and hyper-parameter settings majorly follow the previous works (Bricken et al.,
802
      2023; Gao et al., 2024; Lieberum et al., 2024). Specifically, we initialize C = 216 feature vectors for a
803
      Top-K sparse autoencoder with Kaiming initialization (He et al., 2015). Here, C = 216 is set according
804   to the scaling law between the number of features C and the number of training tokens Z found by Gao
805   et al. (2024), i.e., C = O(Z γ ), where γ ≈ 0.60 for GPT2-small and γ ≈ 0.65 for GPT-4.5 . To prevent
806   dead neurons, we also apply the tied-weight strategy as suggested by Gao et al. (2024). We use Adam
807   optimizer (Kingma, 2014) with a constant learning rate of 1e−3 and epsilon of 6.25e−10 to train a total of
808   4 epochs. The hyper-parameters β1 and β2 of the optimizer are 0.9 and 0.999 following Gao et al. (2024),
809   respectively. We set the batch size as 512 queries, leading to around 90K tokens per gradient update, which
810   is the same volume as Gao et al. (2024). The mixed precision training strategy (Micikevicius et al., 2017)
811   is also applied to speed up the training process as Lieberum et al. (2024) found that it only shows a slightly
812
      worse impact on the model performance. Top-K sparse autoencoder has an initial sparsity K = 200, and it
      gradually decreases to the target sparsity K = 20 in the first 50% training samples of the first epoch. The
813
      training process runs on one Nvidia A6000 GPU with CUDA 12.6 and takes about 16 hours per epoch.

871   We provide a case study in Figure 4 on defending against jailbreak attacks using our proposed method.
872   Specifically, we follow the aware security strategy introduced in Section 4.3.1 to perform the jailbreak de-
873   fense. The attacking prompt comes from the Salad-Bench (Li et al., 2024) with a role-play attacking strategy,
874   where the attacker asks the LLM to play in an “opposite mode” so that it will be misleading to generate some
875   dangerous advice to the users about using the microwave. In specific, we could observe that the original LLM
876
      follows the instructions from the attacker to suggest that the user blow up items in the microwave within the
      “opposite mode” (e.g., “[AntiGPT]”). There is no doubt that this response is harmful and unsafe to the users,
877
      indicating a successful attempt from the attacker.
878
879   However, by constantly enforcing the security-aware features to be activated at a level of β = 1, we observe
880   that the original response becomes less harmful, where the LLM specifies that the blow-up items should be
881   some foods, such as “marshmallows, popcorn, and hot dogs”. Finally, when we enforce the activations to a
882
      more significant level, i.e., β = 3, the LLM entirely rejects the harmful premise of the prompt, providing a
      response that strictly adheres to safety guidelines. Specifically, the LLM refuses to engage with the idea of
883
      “blowing up items” in a microwave, emphasizing the importance of following safety protocols and avoiding
884
      any unsuitable items. By activating security-related features more strongly, the method demonstrates the
885   capability not only to mitigate harmful responses but also to completely align the model’s output with ethical
886   and safety standards. This case study illustrates the effectiveness of our strategy in steering the LLM’s
887   behavior towards responsible and safety-conscious outputs.
