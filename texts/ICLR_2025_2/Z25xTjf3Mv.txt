010                                                A BSTRACT
011
012
               Partial label learning (PLL) is a challenging real-world problem in the field of
013            weakly supervised learning, in which each data instance contains a set of candidate
014            labels with multiple ambiguous labels and one gold label. Although recent progress
015            in PLL using deep representation learning has led to significant advances, the
016            methods continue to experience significant performance drop on data with high
017            label ambiguity and fine-grained categories. By casting PLL into a satisfiability
018            problem and incorporating a loss based on this reduction, we show that the accuracy
019            of those techniques can be further improved. We establish several key theoretical
020            properties of the proposed SATisfiability-based (SAT) loss and its learning error
021
               bound. Our extensive empirical comparison reveals that the proposed loss improves
               over existing PLL techniques by up to 25.12% on multi-class benchmarks and
022
               12.50% on fine-grained categorized benchmarks.

      What is the root cause of this undesired behavior? Both P I CO (and its successor P I CO+ ) and
102   PA P I put emphasis on prototype learning, simply using the cross entropy of the pseudo labels as
103   a classification loss term. However, choosing the cross entropy loss on highly noisy pseudo labels
104   may have catastrophic consequences: it is already known that the cross entropy loss is not robust to
105   noise (Feng et al., 2021). To tackle noisy labels, an approach would be to employ loss functions that
106   are tolerant to noisy labels (Wang et al., 2022c). However, all these techniques work under certain
107   assumptions on how noise is modeled. These assumptions are violated in the standard PLL setting:
      we only know that the gold label is in the set of candidate labels, with no other information.

      To overcome the limitations of P I CO and PA P I, and motivated by the observations that the classifier
109   was always more correct than prototype-based classification at the beginning and sometimes this
110   advantage was even maintained until convergence, we remove P I CO’s and PA P I’s cross entropy-based
111   classification loss term and incorporate techniques for training neural classifiers subject to logical
112   constraints (Wang et al., 2023). In particular, we show that the PLL constraints can be naturally
113   encoded into formulas in propositional logic. These formulas are subsequently used to compute losses
114   capturing the degree to which the classifiers’ scores abide by them, hereby transforming PLL into a
115   satisfiability problem. We define this logic-based formulated loss as SATisfiability-based (SAT) loss
116   and provide theoretical justification for its effectiveness on difficulty scenarios as high label ambiguity
117
      and fine-grained candidate categories. Further, we empirically demonstrate that satisfiability-based
      training can serve as a standalone loss function or enhance the state-of-the-art PLL methods beyond
118
      P I CO+ and PA P I via seamless integration. As shown in Figure 2(c) and (f), integrating SAT loss
119
      effectively enhanced the classification (Test Accuracy) and label disambiguation performance (Mean
120   Max Confidence) of P I CO+ and PA P I . Based on the comparison between Figure 2(a)(b) and (d)(e),
121   it also improved the consistency between the classifier and the prototype predictions as a better
122   classifier generates high-quality representations. We summarize our main contributions as below:
123
124   Methodology. We are the first to thoroughly assess satisfiability-based learning in the context of
125   PLL and its merits when integrated with state-of-the-art PLL techniques focusing on learning better
126   representations (Wang et al., 2022b;c; Xia et al., 2023a; Wu et al., 2022a). The obtained empirical
127   results are state-of-the-art in PLL. The proofs of all formal statements, as well as more details on our
128   empirical analysis are in the appendix.
129
130   Theory. Beyond empirically assessing satisfiability-based PLL, we theoretically analyze the proper-
131   ties of the proposed method. We prove several natural properties of satisfiability-based PLL: that
132
      the loss is non-increasing the larger candidate label sets become, see Corollary 5.2, and that it favors
      low entropy classifiers, Proposition 5.3. In addition, we provide the Rademacher-style error bound,
133
      showing the learnability and generalization ability of SAT loss, see Theorem 5.4.
134
135
      Experiments. We validate the performance of logic-inspired PLL training empirically, via the
136
      most thorough, to our knowledge, experimental evaluation. Our assessment shows that satisfiability-
137   based training leads to consistently higher classification accuracy over all settings compared with
138   previous state-of-the-art techniques. Its integration brings up to 25.12% increase on CIFAR-100 for
139   P I CO+ and 12.50% improvement on CUB-200 for PA P I.

      2    R ELATED WORK
143
144
145   The literature on PLL is very rich. We quickly summarize the most well-known theoretical results.
146   The small ambiguity degree assumption (Cour et al., 2011a; Cabannes et al., 2020) has been central
147   to showing several formal properties including learnability (Liu & Dietterich, 2014) and classifier
148   consistency (Lv et al., 2020; Cabannes et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022a). Regarding the latter, the
149   authors in (Lv et al., 2020) show classifier consistency via training under the minimal loss, while the
150   authors in (Cabannes et al., 2020) show classifier consistency via the infimum loss. Both the minimal
151
      and the infimum losses can be seen as a special case of Semantic Loss (SL) (Xu et al., 2018b). In
      addition, similarly to (Lv et al., 2020; Cabannes et al., 2020), we prove classifier consistency and
152
      provide Rademacher-style error bounds, see Section 5.
153
154
      Risk-consistent PLL techniques have been also proposed (Feng et al., 2020; Wen et al., 2021),
155
      where a PLL technique is risk-consistent (Xia et al., 2019) if by increasing the size of PLL samples
156   from P (X, S), the empirical risk based on those samples converges to the expected risk based on
157   fully-supervised supervised samples from P (X, Y ). Classifier- and risk-consistent techniques were
158   proposed in (Feng et al., 2020), named CC and RC, respectively, based on the assumption that the
159   PLL training samples abide by a specific data generation process. This research was later extended
160   by (Wu & Sugiyama, 2021). The authors in (Wen et al., 2021) introduced a family of loss functions
161   named leveraged weighted loss (LWC) and proved risk and Bayes consistency under a data generation
      process that extends the one from (Feng et al., 2020). Differently from (Feng et al., 2020; Wen et al.,


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      2021), our work makes no assumption on the data generation, while the empirical results demonstrate
163   superior classification accuracy against CC, RC, and LWC, see Section 6.
164
165   Advancements in deep learning have further expanded the PLL exploration (Xu et al., 2021; Wang
166   et al., 2022b;c; Wu et al., 2022a; Xia et al., 2023a). The authors in (Xu et al., 2021) introduced
167   VALEN, a technique based on variational inference, for PLL settings in which the candidate labels
168   in s are dependent on the features of x. The key idea is to associate each x with a latent label
169   distribution over the labels in s, representing the probability to which each l ∈ s describes the features
170   of x. Recently, (Wang et al., 2022b;c) adopted contrastive representation learning (Chen et al.,
171   2020) for PLL, significantly improving the state-of-the-art results on several image classification
172   benchmarks. Despite its remarkable performance, the authors in (Xia et al., 2023a) observed that
173
      P I CO’s contrastive learning component and its label disambiguation process that is based on the
      class embedding prototypes may negatively affect the training process. Motivated by these finds,
174
      the authors proposed PA P I (Xia et al., 2023a). PA P I gets rid of contrastive learning and relies on
175
      aligning the distribution over the pseudo-labels of x with a distribution over the distances of x’s
176   feature embedding to the representative feature embeddings of each class. In addition, PA P I relies on
177   the classifier’s predictions for label disambiguation and not to the class prototypes. Another recently
178   proposed PLL technique that relies deep learning is DPLL (Wu et al., 2022a). Both P I CO and
179   PA P I rely on the cross-entropy loss for label disambiguation. Our work instead shows that via
180   replacing this loss term with ℓSAT , we can have major improvements in the accuracy of the learned
181   classifiers, see Section 6.
182
183   For completeness, we conclude with work on other learning settings. One of the closest fields is
184   neuro-symbolic learning, whose key focus is the integration of deep learning with logical reasoning.
185   Works as Tsamoura et al. (2021); Buffelli & Tsamoura (2022) explore frameworks that combine
186
      neural network outputs with symbolic reasoning processes, enhancing the system’s ability to perform
      tasks requiring complex reasoning or knowledge representation. Despite its promise, neuro-symbolic
187
      learning faces challenges, particularly in seamlessly integrating neural and symbolic components
188
      and scaling these systems for complex, real-world applications. There are many existing works
189   (Xu et al., 2018a; Manhaeve et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2021) explored utilizing
190   logic constraints to train neural networks. A few of the close works deployed logic constraints into
191   multi-instance partial label learning learning (Zombori et al., 2023), which are different from what we
192   emphasized. In this work, we aim to demonstrate that training neural network with logic constraints
193   can enhance their robustness in handling label ambiguity under standard partial label learning.
194
195   Complementary to ours is the work in (Ferber et al., 2019; Vlastelica et al., 2019; Paulus et al., 2021)
196   that integrates combinatorial solvers into deep models. The integration of combinatorial optimization
197   methods, as explored in these works, provides a direct link to PLL under logic constraints. Specifi-
198   cally, decision-focused learning methods like MIPaaL (Ferber et al., 2019) and CombOptNet (Paulus
199
      et al., 2021) align well with PLL, where selecting the correct label from a set of candidate labels
      can be framed as an optimization problem. The incorporation of integer programming and combi-
200
      natorial solvers as layers in deep networks, as demonstrated in these papers, suggests that similar
201
      approaches could be applied to PLL, especially under the scenario where candidate label sets need to
202   be disambiguated efficiently.

      5     T HEORETICAL ANALYSIS
319
320   In this section, we provide a theoretical justification to show why the proposed SAT loss can benefit
321   label disambiguation under challenging scenarios such as high label ambiguity and fine-grained
322   categories. The primary challenge brought by high label ambiguity is that each data instance will have
323   a larger set of candidate labels. On the other hand, compared to standard datasets like CIFAR-10,
      fine-grained categorization data involves data that comes from the same super-category, such as


                                                                            6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      different species of birds. While this is more reflective of real-world scenarios, it also leads to the
325   model assigning similar prediction probabilities to multiple candidate classes, making it difficult to
326   distinguish between them.
327
328   To quantify the label ambiguity level in a given dataset DN , we adopt the concept of small ambiguity
329   degree, which was widely used in PLL literature (Liu & Dietterich, 2014; Lv et al., 2020; Cabannes
330   et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022a).

339   alongside the ground truth. Next, we present an extensive analysis of learning via minimizing ℓSAT
340   to show the properties of SAT loss can mitigate these challenges.

      non-increasing as the small ambiguity degree increases.

362
363   Fine-grained categorized data (Wah et al., 2011; Nilsback & Zisserman, 2008) is extremely challeng-
364   ing for previous PLL methods (Wang et al., 2022b;c; Xia et al., 2023a) using cross entropy loss for
365   label disambiguation, as the high similarity among the candidate labels causing the pseudo labels
366   generated by these methods become noisy and unreliable.
367
368   The advantage of SAT loss on fine-grained categorization data can be attributed to its two key
369   characteristics. On the one hand, SAT loss gets rid of the pseudo label, which may lead to erroneous
370   guidance by the representation learning module or self-supervised manner. On the other hand, SAT
371
      loss forces the classifier to concentrate the prediction probability on one single candidate label during
      the training process. To show that, we provide the second important property of SAT loss below, the
372
      low entropy preference.

375

      7     C ONCLUSIONS
532
533
      To conclude, this paper introduces a novel approach to Partial Label Learning (PLL) by framing it as
534   a satisfiability problem and introducing a SATisfiability-based (SAT) loss function. Through both
535   theoretical and empirical analyses, we demonstrate that this method offers substantial improvements in
536   classification accuracy, especially in scenarios with high label ambiguity and fine-grained categories.
537   Our SAT loss does not rely on pseudo labels and helps the model focus on low-entropy solutions,
538   resulting in better label disambiguation. This work provides a solid foundation for future research on
539   applying logical constraints to weakly supervised learning, with potential extensions to more complex
      and real-world datasets.
