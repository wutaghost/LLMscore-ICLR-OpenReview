009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The well-known vulnerability of Neural Networks to adversarial attacks is con-
012            cerning, more so with the increasing reliance on them for real-world applica-
013            tions like autonomous driving, medical imaging, and others. Multiple previous
014            works have proposed defense methods against adversarial attacks, including ad-
015            versarial training, adding random noise to images, frequency pooling, and oth-
016
               ers. We observe from several such works, that there are two main paradigms
               for mitigating adversarial attacks. First, effective downsampling leads to learn-
017
               ing better feature representations during training, thus improving the performance
018
               on attacked and non-attacked samples. However, these methods are expensive.
019            Second, perturbing samples with for example random noise helps in mitigating
020            adversarial attacks as they stymie the flow of gradients to optimize the attacks.
021            However, these methods lower the network’s performance on non-attacked sam-
022            ples. Thus, in this work, we combine the best of both strategies to propose a
023            novel Monte-Carlo sampling-based approach for downsampling called Stochastic
024            Downsampling. We combine bi-linear interpolation with Monte Carlo integration
025            for performing downsampling. This helps us mitigate adversarial attacks while
026            preserving the performance of non-attacked samples, thus increasing reliability.
027
               Our proposed Stochastic Downsampling operator can easily be integrated into
               any existing architecture, including adversarially pre-trained networks, with some
028
               finetuning. We show the effectiveness of Stochastic Dowsampling over multiple
029
               image classification datasets using different network architectures with different
030            training strategies. We provide the code for performing Stochastic Downsampling
031            here: Anonymous GitHub Repository.

128   2   R ELATED W ORK
129
130   Following, we discuss prior works done toward defense from adversarial methods
131
132   Gradient Obfuscation All white-box adversarial attacks attempt to optimize the attack noise by
133   back-propagating the loss gradients to the input image. However, if this flow of gradients were to be
134   disturbed, it would interfere with the optimization ability of the attack and thus such methods would
135   be hacks that work at adversarial defense. This is known as “Obfuscated Gradients” as shown by
136   Athalye et al. (2018). They categorized obfuscation of gradients into three types, Shattered Gra-
137   dients, where the gradients are incorrect or non-existent, Exploding & Vanishing Gradients, and
138   Stochastic Gradients, which causes incorrect estimation of the gradients. Our proposed Stochas-
139
      tic Downsampling might appear similar to the Stochastic Gradient type of Obfuscated Gradients
      method, however, as we sample multiple points from the valid data space (i.e. within the variance
140
      of correct sampling), we simply change the direction of the gradients within their correct range
141
      rather than making them incorrect. Thus, Stochastic Downsampling is more than just a gradient
142   obfuscation method but is an efficient sampling method with stochasticity.
143
144   Byun et al. (2022); Nguyen et al. (2023); Li et al. (2019) proposed adding noise at various stages of
145
      the model. For our comparative analysis, we take inspiration from them and include “AddNoise”, as
      a method for comparison. Here, in each forward pass on the model, we add noise to feature maps,
146
      after downsampling them. The noise itself can be sampled from a Gaussian or a uniform distribution
147
      and has the same spatial resolution as the feature maps to which it is added.
148
149   While moderately effective in disturbing gradient flow and thus weakening the adversarial attacks,
150   these methods lead to a significant drop in clean performance. This is explained by Zhang et al.
151
      (2019) and Tsipras et al. (2019), which show there exists a trade-off between robustness and clean
      performance of a model. However, we demonstrate that Stochastic Downsampling can achieve a
152
      significantly better trade-off than some simple hacks, helping the model extract meaningful repre-
153
      sentations during downsampling.
154

      Adversarial Training Adversarial training is one of the most promising methods to enhance the
156   model’s robustness, especially in the presence of adversarial attacks (Goodfellow et al., 2015; Ku-
157   rakin et al., 2017; Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2016; Carlini & Wagner, 2017; Rony et al., 2019) but also
158   to enhance general model robustness (Croce et al., 2020). During adversarial training, the model is
159   confronted with adversarial samples by adding an additional loss term (Liu et al., 2023; Kurakin
160   et al., 2017), showing augmented inputs (Geirhos et al., 2018) or adding additional external or gen-
161   erated inputs. One widely used additional data source is using ddpm (Gowal et al., 2021; Rade &
      Moosavi-Dezfooli, 2021; Rebuffi et al., 2021) dataset which is generated by Ho et al. (2020) and

                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      includes one million additional samples for CIFAR-10. For the evaluation and collection of robust
163   models RobustBench (Croce et al., 2020) provides a compressive overview of recent adversarially
164   trained models and their performance on AutoAttack (Croce & Hein, 2020a) and Common Corrup-
165   tions (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019; Kar et al., 2022).
166
      However, most of these methods to enhance the model’s robustness rely heavily on additional data
167
      or data augmentation which takes much longer to train. In the case of traditional adversarial training,
168
      one needs even several forward and backward passes to calculate the adversarial noise depending
169   on the adversarial attack used to generate the perturbations. Perturbations generated using several
170   iterations (Kurakin et al., 2017) provide stronger robustness than perturbations generated with a
171   single iteration (Goodfellow et al., 2015). Summarizing, adversarial training mostly comes at an
172   increased amount in computations needed due to more samples and a harder learning problem this
173   can increase the training time by a factor between seven and fifteen (Kurakin et al., 2017; Wang
174   et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2019; Grabinski et al., 2022a)
175
176
      Anti-Aliasing Sampling for increased Robustness Prior works on inherently improving robust-
177
      ness via Anti-Aliasing Sampling include aliasing-free downsampling like Frequency Low Cut (FLC)
178   Pooling (Grabinski et al., 2022a), aliasing- and sinc-artifact-free pooling (ASAP) (Grabinski et al.,
179   2023), BlurPooling (Zhang, 2019) or adaptive BlurPooling (Zou et al., 2020). While BlurPooling
180   and adaptive BlurPooling use blurring before downsampling to reduce aliasing and ensure greater
181   shift invariance, FLC Pooling and ASAP ensure aliasing-free downsampling, leading to higher na-
182   tive robustness and a reduced risk of catastrophic overfitting in FGSM adversarial training. In Gra-
183   binski et al. (2022b), the authors show a strong negative correlation between aliasing after down-
184   sampling and the robustness of a network. Thus, ensuring aliasing-free downsampling increases a
185   network’s robustness.

      6     C ONCLUSION
468
469
470
      Adversarial attacks pose a threat to Deep Learning based methods. It is of paramount importance that
      reliable DL-based methods can defend against such threats to a reasonable extent. However, most
471
      adversarial defense methods inherently create a challenging trade-off between i.i.d. performance and
472
      robustness. Stochastic Downsampling eases this challenge by significantly improving the trade-off
473   by increasing reliability with only a marginal drop in i.i.d. accuracy and generalization ability of DL-
474   based methods. Stochastic Downsampling is easy to incorporate in pre-trained models and requires
475   very limited finetuning to perform at its peak efficiency. The gains from Stochastic Downsampling
476   are consistent across model architectures, and datasets. We show that despite being very similar
477   to a gradient obfuscation based defense method, Stochastic Downsampling does not provide a false
478   sense of security. This work is a step in the direction of sampling-based adversarial defense methods
479   that help extract meaningful representations during downsampling, even under adversarial attacks.
480
481
482   L IMITATIONS
483
484   There is still a marginal drop in clean performance, ideally this should also be avoided. While
485   Stochastic Downsampling mitigates adversarial attacks better than other defense methods, there is
      still significant room for improvement.

768   A.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
769
770   Here we provide an overview of the implementation details, we provide additional implementation
771   details in Appendix A.
772
      Downstream Tasks. The majority of our downstream tasks are performed for image classifica-
773   tion. We perform classification on the commonly used datasets ImageNet-1k, ImageNet-100 and
774   CIFAR-100. Additionally, we perform evaluations on images corrupted using 2D Common Cor-
775   ruptions(Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019), and images perturbed using Adversarial attacks like PGD,
776   APGD (Wong et al., 2020) and AutoAttack.
777
      Datasets. We use the 15 corruptions, with 5 severity levels each, from 2D Common Corrup-
778
      tions(Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019) (2D-CC), to generate the Common Corruptions version of the
779   respective dataset. We denote this dataset by appending ‘-C’ to the end of the name of the respective
780   dataset, for example, 2D Common Corruptions on ImageNet-1k results into ImageNet-1k-C. We
781   perform our experiments on the following datasets:
782
      ImageNet-1k(Russakovsky et al., 2015): This is a subset of the larger ImageNet-22k dataset(Deng
783
      et al., 2009), with 1000 object classes, and 1,281,167 training images, 50,000 validation images.
784
785   ImageNet-100: This is a commonly used(Hoffmann et al., 2021; Tsai et al., 2021; Ge et al., 2021;
786   Lee et al., 2022; Saikia et al., 2021) subset of ImageNet-1k with 100 classes, such that it has 130,000
787   training images and 5000 validation images, used for faster processing and inference.
788   CIFAR-100(Krizhevsky et al., 2009): This dataset contains 60,000 32×32 images, split into 50,000
789   training images and 10,000 validation images, equally distributed over 100 object classes.
790
      Adversarial Attacks. We use PGD(Kurakin et al., 2017), APGD(Wong et al., 2020) and AutoAt-
791                                                                  4
      tack(Croce & Hein, 2020a), with ℓ∞ -norm bounded ϵ = 255          and α=0.01 for all our experiments.
792   PGD and APGD are white-box attacks, meaning they require an undisturbed flow of gradients for
793   optimizing their attack. However, AutoAttack, as proposed comprises of APGD-CE (non-targeted
794   APGD attack with Cross Entropy loss), APGD-T (targeted APGD attack), FAB(Croce & Hein,
795   2020b) and Square(Andriushchenko et al., 2020) Attacks, from these, Square Attack is a black-box
796   attack that does not require a flow of gradients through the ML model. Additionally, since Stochastic
797   Downsampling is essentially a gradient obfuscation method, we also test against Square Attack (An-
798   driushchenko et al., 2020) alone, as it is a black-box attack and does not use the gradient information
799   of a model to optimize the attack.
800   Metrics. For independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) (non-attacked and non-perturbed) sam-
801   ples, we report the i.i.d. Accuracy (i.i.d. Acc). For evaluations against adversarial attacks, we report
802   the accuracy after the respective attack. For samples from 2D Common Corruptions variant of the
803   respective datasets, we report the mean Corruption Error (mCE), this is the mean error by the method
804   on all the corrupted samples. All numbers are reported in percentages.
805   Architectures. To demonstrate the versatility of Stochastic Downsampling, we have considered
806   multiple architectures and their variants. For experiments with ImageNet-1k, and ImageNet-1k-
807   C, we use ResNet18, ResNet50 and ResNet101 (He et al., 2016), ConvNeXt-T, ConvNeXt-S, and
808   ConvNeXt-B (Liu et al., 2022). We use RobustBench(Croce et al., 2020), to get the adversarially
809   trained weights. For ImageNet-100 and CIFAR-100 (and their 2D-CC counterparts) experiments, we
      use ConvNeXt-T (tiny) (Liu et al., 2022). Additionally, for comparison, we also use the architectural

891   11               resolution: Tuple[int, int],
      12               spp: int = 16,
892   13               reduction: Literal["mean", "sum", "min", "max", "prod"] = "mean",
893   14               jitter_type: Literal["uniform", "normal"] = "uniform",
      15               normal_std: float = 1,
894   16               ):
      17        super().__init__()
895   18        if (not isinstance(resolution, tuple)
896   19           and not isinstance(resolution, list)):
      20           resolution = (resolution, resolution)
897   21        if len(resolution) != 2:
      22           raise ValueError(f"Resolution must be a tuple of length 2, got {resolution}")
898   23
      24        self.resolution = resolution
899   25        self.spp = spp
900   26        self.reduction = reduction
      27        self.jitter_type = jitter_type
901   28        self.normal_std = normal_std
      29        if self.jitter_type == "uniform":
902   30           self.jitter_fn = torch.rand
903   31        elif self.jitter_type == "normal":
      32           self.jitter_fn = lambda *args, **kwargs : self.normal_std*torch.randn(*args, **kwargs) + 0.5
904   33        else:
      34           raise NotImplementedError(f"Jitter type {jitter_type} not supported")
905   35
      36     def forward(self, x: torch.Tensor, jitter_array=None):
906   37        """
907   38        Downsamples x to the target resolution
      39        :param x: high-res input feature map, shape (batch_size, C, H, W)
908   40        :return: downsampled image, shape (batch_size, C, resolution[0], resolution[1])
      41        """
909   42        b, c, h, w = x.shape
910   43        resolution, spp = self.resolution, self.spp
      44
911   45        step_x = (1 + 1) / resolution[1]
      46        step_y = (1 + 1) / resolution[0]
912   47        pixel_pos_x = torch.arange(-1, 1, step_x, device=x.device)
      48        pixel_pos_y = torch.arange(-1, 1, step_y, device=x.device)
913   49        pixel_pos = torch.stack(torch.meshgrid(pixel_pos_x, pixel_pos_y, indexing='xy'), dim=2)
914   50
      51        # add subpixel jitter
915   52        if jitter_array is not None:
      53           jitter = jitter_array
916   54        else:
917   55           jitter = self.jitter_fn((spp, resolution[0], resolution[1], 2), device=x.device)
      56
      57        jitter[..., 0] *= step_x
