009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Long-term Time Series Forecasting (LTSF) is critical for numerous real-world
012            applications, such as electricity consumption planning, financial forecasting, and
013            disease propagation analysis. Time series, generated from continuous real-world
014            processes sampled at multiple scales, pose significant challenges for LTSF. These
015            challenges arise from the need to capture long-range dependencies between in-
016            puts and outputs, driven by the complex temporal dynamics of multi-scale, multi-
017            periodic data. While current time-domain multiscale models effectively capture
018            temporal variations, they often fall short with multi-scale datasets. These models
019
               primarily focus on temporal patterns, frequently overlooking critical frequency-
               specific features, such as harmonics and periodic behaviors, which are better rep-
020
               resented in the frequency domain. In this paper, we introduce MMFNet, a novel
021
               model designed to enhance long-term multivariate forecasting by leveraging a
022            multi-scale masked frequency decomposition approach. MMFNet captures fine,
023            intermediate, and coarse-grained temporal patterns by converting time series into
024            frequency segments at varying scales while employing a learnable mask to filter
025            out irrelevant components adaptively.
026            Extensive experimentation with benchmark datasets shows that MMFNet not only
027            addresses the limitations of the existing methods but also consistently achieves
028            good performance. Specifically, MMFNet achieves up to 6.0% reductions in the
029            Mean Squared Error (MSE) compared to state-of-the-art models designed for mul-
030            tivariate forecasting tasks.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Time series forecasting is pivotal in a wide range of domains, such as environmental monitor-
036   ing (Bhandari et al., 2017), electrical grid management (Zufferey et al., 2017), financial analy-
037   sis (Sezer et al., 2020), and healthcare (Zeroual et al., 2020). Accurate long-term forecasting is
038   essential for informed decision-making and strategic planning. Traditional methods, such as autore-
039
      gressive (AR) models (Nassar et al., 2004), exponential smoothing (Hyndman & Athanasopoulos,
      2008), and structural time series models (Harvey, 1989), have provided a robust foundation for time
040
      series analysis by leveraging historical data to predict future values. However, real-world systems
041
      frequently exhibit complex, non-stationary behavior, with time series characterized by intricate pat-
042   terns such as trends, fluctuations, and cycles. Those complexities pose significant challenges to
043   achieving accurate forecasts (Makridakis et al., 1998; Box et al., 2015).
044
045
      Long-term Time Series Forecasting (LTSF) has seen significant advancements in recent years, driven
      by the development of sophisticated models, such as Transformer-based models (Zhou et al., 2021;
046
      Wu et al., 2021; Nie et al., 2024) and linear models (Zeng et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Lin
047
      et al., 2024). Transformer-based architectures have demonstrated exceptional capacity in capturing
048   complex temporal patterns by effectively modeling long-range dependencies through self-attention
049   mechanisms at the cost of heavy computation workload, particularly when facing large-scale time
050   series data, which significantly limits their practicality in real-time applications. In contrast, the
051   linear models provide a lightweight alternative for real-time forecasting. In particular, FITS demon-
052   strates superior predictive performance across a wide range of scenarios with only 10K parameters
053   by utilizing a single-scale frequency domain decomposition method combined with a low-pass filter
      employing a fixed cutoff frequency (Xu et al., 2024).

054
      Current methods often overlook the multiscale periodic nature of time series data. Time series
055   are generated from continuous real-world processes sampled at various scales. For example, daily
056   data capture hourly fluctuations, while yearly data reflect long-term trends and seasonal cycles.
057   This inherent multi-scale, multi-periodic characteristic presents a significant challenge for model
058   design, as each scale emphasizes distinct temporal dynamics that need to be effectively captured.
059   Centered Kernel Alignment analysis has shown the ability to produce diverse representations across
060   layers is particularly beneficial for tasks requiring the capture of irregular patterns Kornblith et al.
061   (2019). These diverse representations are instrumental in managing variations across scales and
062   periodicities.
063   Current time-domain multiscale models like TimeMixer (Wang et al., 2024), though effective at
064   capturing temporal variations across resolutions, has several limitations, particularly for datasets
065   with multi-scale and multi-periodic properties. It primarily focuses on temporal patterns, often
066   overlooking critical frequency-specific features such as harmonic or periodic behaviors, which are
067   better captured in the frequency domain. For example, seasonal or cyclic trends are more apparent
068   in frequency representations but can be difficult to disentangle in the time domain. Additionally,
069
      time-domain methods are sensitive to noise, as they work directly on raw signals, allowing noise to
      propagate across scales and obscure meaningful patterns, especially at coarser resolutions. Further-
070
      more, while these methods enhance temporal resolution, they frequently struggle to capture long-
071
      term dependencies, as dividing data into scales can result in a loss of the broader context necessary
072   for understanding long-range interactions.
073
074   In this paper, we present MMFNet, a novel model designed to enhance LTSF through a multi-
075
      scale masked frequency decomposition approach. MMFNet captures fine, intermediate, and coarse-
      grained patterns in the frequency domain by segmenting the time series at multiple scales. At each
076
      scale, MMFNet employs a learnable mask that adaptively filters out irrelevant frequency components
077
      based on the segment’s spectral characteristics. MMFNet offers two key advantages: (i) the multi-
078   scale frequency decomposition enables MMFNet to effectively capture both short-term fluctuations
079   and broader trends in the data, and (ii) the learnable frequency mask adaptively filters irrelevant
080   frequency components, allowing the model to focus on the most informative signals. These features
081   make MMFNet well-suited to capturing both short-term and long-term dependencies in complex
082   time series, positioning it as an effective solution for various LTSF tasks.
083
      In summary, the contributions of this paper are as follows:
084
085   • To our knowledge, MMFNet is the first model that employs multi-scale frequency domain decom-
086     position to capture the dynamic variations in the frequency domain;
087
      • MMFNet introduces a novel learnable masking mechanism that adaptively filters out irrelevant
088     frequency components;
089
090
      • Extensive experiments show that MMFNet consistently achieves good performance in a variety of
        multivariate time series forecasting tasks, with up to a 6.0% reduction in the Mean Squared Error
091
        (MSE) compared to the existing models.

094   2   P RELIMINARIES
095
096   Long-term Time Series Forecasting. LTSF involves predicting future values over an extended
097   time horizon based on previously observed multivariate time series data. The LTSF problem can be
098   formulated as:
099                                      x̂t+1:t+H = f (xt−L+1:t ),                                (1)
100   where xt−L+1:t ∈ RL×C denotes the historical observation window, and x̂t+1:t+H ∈ RH×C rep-
101   resents the predicted future values. In this formulation, L is the length of the historical window,
102   H is the forecast horizon, and C denotes the number of features or channels. As the forecast hori-
103   zon H increases, the models face challenges to accurately capture both long-term and short-term
104   dependencies within the time series.
105
106   Single-Scale Frequency Transformation (SFT). SFT refers to the process of converting the time-
107   domain data into the frequency domain at a single, global scale without segmenting the time series.
      Such a transformation is typically performed using methods, such as the Fast Fourier Transform


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (FFT), which efficiently computes the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT). SFT decomposes the en-
109   tire signal into sinusoidal components, enabling the analysis of its frequency content. Each fre-
110   quency component can be expressed as:
111
112
                                                Xk = |Xk |ejϕk ,                                         (2)
113   where |Xk | represents the amplitude and ϕk the phase of the k-th frequency component. While
114   the frequency decomposition provides valuable insights into periodic patterns and trends, traditional
115   approaches assume stationarity and operate on a global scale, limiting their capacity to capture
116
      the complex, non-stationary characteristics frequently observed in real-world time series. Current
      frequency-based LTSF models, such as FITS (Xu et al., 2024), implement this method by performing
117
      frequency domain interpolation at a single scale, which can be formulated as:
118
119                                      x̃t+1:t+H = g(F(xt−L+1:t )),                                    (3)
120   where F denotes the Fourier transform, and g represents the filtering operation applied uniformly
121   across the signal. Although SFT is capable of capturing broad temporal patterns, such as long-term
122   trends through low-pass filtering or short-term fluctuations through high-pass filtering, its global
123   application treats the entire signal uniformly. This uniform treatment may result in the loss of
124   important local temporal variations and non-stationary behaviors occurring at different scales.

128   3.1   OVERVIEW
129
130   To overcome the limitations of SFT, we propose the Multi-scale Masked Frequency Transformation
131
      (MMFT). MMFT performs frequency decomposition across multiple temporal scales, enabling the
      model to capture both global and local temporal patterns. Formally, the MMFT problem can be
132
      expressed as:
133
                                       x̃t+1:t+H = h({Fs (xt−L+1:t )}Ss=1 ),                             (4)
134
      where Fs denotes the frequency transformation at scale s, and h represents the aggregation and
135
      filtering operation applied to the learnable frequency masks at various scales. Unlike SFT, which
136
      applies a single transformation to the entire time series, MMFT divides the signal into multiple
137   scales, each subjected to frequency decomposition. At each scale, a learnable frequency mask is ap-
138   plied to retain the most informative frequency components while selectively discarding noise. This
139   multi-scale approach allows the model to adapt to non-stationary signals, capturing complex de-
140   pendencies that span different temporal ranges. By leveraging frequency decomposition at multiple
141   scales and applying adaptive masks, MMFT enhances long-term forecasting accuracy by focusing
142   on both short-term fluctuations and long-term trends within the data. This method increases the
143   model’s flexibility and robustness, particularly for non-stationary and multivariate time series. Fur-
144   ther analysis of the differences between SFT and MMFT can be found in Appendix A.
145   MMFNet enhances time series forecasting by incorporating the proposed MMFT method to cap-
146   ture intricate frequency features across different scales. The overall architecture of MMFNet is
147   depicted in Figure 1. The model comprises three key components: Multi-scale Frequency Decom-
148   position, Masked Frequency Interpolation, and Spectral Inversion. Multi-scale Frequency Decom-
149   position normalizes the input time series, divides it into segments of varying scales, and transforms
150   these segments into the frequency domain using the DCT. Masked Frequency Interpolation applies
151
      a self-adaptive, learnable mask to filter out irrelevant frequency components, followed by a linear
      transformation of the filtered frequency domain segments. Finally, Spectral Inversion converts the
152
      processed frequency components back into the time domain via the Inverse Discrete Cosine Trans-
153
      form (iDCT) (Ahmed et al., 1974). The outputs from different scales are then aggregated, resulting
154   in a refined signal that preserves the essential characteristics of the original input.

      3.3   M ASKED F REQUENCY I NTERPOLATION
217
218   Masked Frequency Interpolation leverages a learnable mask to adaptively filter frequency compo-
219   nents across different scales in the frequency domain, followed by reconstruction through a linear
220   layer neural network. This approach enables the model to learn and apply scale-specific filtering
221   strategies tailored to diverse datasets. The process consists of two primary steps: Masking and
222   Interpolation.
223
224   Masking. Traditional methods often employ fixed low-pass filters with a predefined cutoff fre-
225   quency to filter frequency components. These approaches assume that certain frequencies are uni-
226   versally important or irrelevant across the entire time series, an assumption that may not hold for
227   non-stationary data where the relevance of frequency components varies over time. Moreover, over-
228
      filtering can lead to the loss of critical details, resulting in oversimplified representations and di-
      minished model performance in tasks such as forecasting and signal analysis. To address these
229
      limitations, MMFNet employs an adaptive masking technique to capture dynamic behaviors in the
230
      frequency domain. Given the frequency segments XDCT , a learnable mask is generated to adap-
231   tively filter the frequency components. The mask adjusts the significance of different frequency
232   components by attenuating or emphasizing them based on their relevance to the task. This filtering
233   process is applied via element-wise multiplication, represented as:
234
235                                       Xmask DCT = XDCT ⊙ M,                                          (6)
236
      where ⊙ denotes element-wise multiplication, M represents the learnable mask, and Xmask DCT
237   is the resulting masked frequency representation. During training, the mask is iteratively updated
238   based on the loss function, allowing MMFNet to focus on the most relevant aspects of the frequency
239   domain representation. This adaptive mechanism improves the model’s capacity to capture mean-
240   ingful patterns while minimizing the influence of irrelevant or noisy information.
241
242   Interpolation. In this step, the masked frequency segments Xmask DCT are transformed into pre-
243   dicted frequency domain segments Xpred DCT through a linear layer. This linear transformation
244   maps the filtered frequency components to the target frequency representations aligned with the
245   model’s forecasting objectives. Specifically, a fully connected (dense) layer is applied to the masked
246   frequency components, and this operation can be expressed as:
247
                                      Xpred DCT = W · Xmask DCT + b,                                     (7)
248
249   where W denotes the weight matrix of the linear layer, and b is the bias term. The linear layer
250   is designed to learn a projection that aligns the filtered frequency components with the target pre-
251   diction space. This transformation further refines the frequency domain information, producing
252   Xpred DCT , which is essential for reconstructing accurate time-domain predictions. By leveraging
253   the refined frequency information and reducing the influence of irrelevant frequency components,
254
      this step improves the overall prediction accuracy.

      5.1        L ONG - TERM T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING
465
466   LTSF is a critical area in data science and machine learning and focuses on predicting future val-
467   ues over extended periods. Such a task is challenging due to the inherent seasonality, trends, and
468   noise in time series data. In addition, time series data is often complex and high-dimensional Zheng
469   et al. (2024; 2023). Traditional statistical methods, such as ARIMA (Contreras et al., 2003) and
470   Holt-Winters (Chatfield & Yar, 1988), are effective for short-term forecasting but frequently fall
471   short for longer horizons. Machine learning models, such as SVM (Wang & Hu, 2005), Random
472   Forests Breiman (2001), and Gradient Boosting Machines (Natekin & Knoll, 2013), offer improved
473   performance by capturing non-linear relationships but typically require extensive feature engineer-
474
      ing. Recently, deep learning models, such as RNNs, LSTMs, GRUs, and Transformer-based models
      (Informer and Autoformer), have demonstrated notable efficiency in modeling long-term dependen-
475
      cies. Furthermore, the hybrid models that combine statistical methods with machine learning or
476
      deep learning techniques have shown improved accuracy. State-of-the-art models, such as FED-
477   former (Zhou et al., 2022b), FiLM (Zhou et al., 2022a), PatchTST Nie et al. (2024), and SparseTSF,
478   leverage frequency domain transformations and efficient self-attention to improve prediction perfor-
479   mance.
480
481   5.2        M ULTISCALING M ODEL
482
483   In the field of computer vision, several multi-scale Vision Transformers (ViTs) have leveraged hi-
484   erarchical architectures to generate progressively down-sampled pyramid features. For instance,
485   Multi-Scale Vision Transformers (Fan et al., 2021) enhance the standard Vision Transformer archi-
      tecture by incorporating multi-scale processing, allowing for improved detail capture across varying


                                                                                    9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      spatial resolutions. Pyramid Vision Transformer (Wang et al., 2021) integrates a pyramid struc-
487   ture within ViTs to facilitate multi-scale feature extraction, while Twins (Dai et al., 2021) com-
488   bines local and global attention to effectively model multi-scale representations. SegFormer (Xie
489   et al., 2021) introduces an efficient hierarchical encoder that captures both coarse and fine features,
490   and CSWin (Dong et al., 2022) further improves performance by using multi-scale cross-shaped
491   local attention mechanisms. In the context of time series forecasting, TimeMixer (Wang et al.,
492   2024) represents a significant advancement with its fully MLP-based architecture, which employs
493   Past-Decomposable-Mixing and Future-Multipredictor-Mixing blocks. This architecture enables
494   TimeMixer to effectively leverage disentangled multi-scale time series data during both past extrac-
495
      tion and future prediction phases.
496
497   5.3   T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING IN THE F REQUENCY D OMAIN
498
      Recent advancements in time series analysis have increasingly utilized frequency domain informa-
499
      tion to reveal underlying patterns. For instance, FNet (Lee-Thorp et al., 2021) adopts an attention-
500   based approach to capture temporal dependencies within the frequency domain, thereby eliminating
501   the need for convolutional or recurrent layers. Models such as FEDformer (Zhou et al., 2022b) and
502   FiLM (Zhou et al., 2022a) improve predictive performance by incorporating frequency domain infor-
503   mation as auxiliary features. FITS (Xu et al., 2024) also demonstrates strong predictive capabilities
504   by converting time-domain forecasting tasks into the frequency domain and utilizing low-pass filters
505   to reduce the number of parameters required. However, many of these techniques rely on manual
506   feature engineering to identify dominant periods, which can constrain the amount of information
507   captured and introduce inefficiencies or risks of overfitting.
508
509   5.4   M ASKED M ODELING
510
      Masked language modeling and its autoregressive variants have emerged as dominant self-
511
      supervised learning approaches in natural language processing. These techniques enable large-scale
512
      language models to excel in both language understanding and generation by predicting masked or
513   hidden tokens within sentences (Devlin et al., 2018; Radford et al., 2018). In computer vision,
514   early approaches, such as the context encoder (Pathak et al., 2016), involve masking specific re-
515   gions of an image and predicting the missing pixels, while Contrastive Predictive Coding (van den
516   Oord et al., 2018) uses contrastive learning to improve feature representations. Recent innova-
517   tions in MIM include models like iGPT (Chen et al., 2020), ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), and
518   BEiT (Bao et al., 2022), which leverage Vision Transformers and techniques, such as pixel clus-
519   tering, mean color prediction, and block-wise masking. In the realm of multivariate time series
520   forecasting, masked encoders have recently been employed with notable success in classification
521
      and regression tasks (Zerveas et al., 2021). For example, PatchTST uses a masked self-supervised
      representation learning method to reconstruct the masked patches and showcases its effectiveness
522
      in time series data (Nie et al., 2024). However, the application of masked modeling techniques in
523
      linear time series forecasting remains relatively under-explored.

728   Single-scale frequency domain decomposition provides a global representation of time series data
729   by analyzing the overall frequency spectrum of the entire sequence. While effective for capturing
730   broad trends or global patterns, this method suffers from a significant limitation: the inability to
731   localize specific frequency components to particular segments of the sequence. This drawback is
732   especially problematic for non-stationary time series data, where frequency characteristics evolve
733   over time. For instance, high-frequency noise or transient events might be confined to specific
734   cycles or segments of the sequence. As illustrated in Figure 2, single-scale analysis often produces
735
      similar spectra for different segments, losing crucial location-specific information (highlighted by
      the red circle). This lack of localized detail hinders accurate forecasting, particularly in complex
736
      multivariate scenarios where capturing subtle temporal and spectral variations is essential.
737
738   To address these challenges, MMFNet introduces Multi-scale Frequency Masking, which over-
739   comes the limitations of single-scale methods by enabling localized frequency domain analysis. By
740   segmenting the sequence and performing frequency decomposition at multiple scales, MMFNet cap-
741
      tures both global patterns and localized high-frequency variations. This approach ensures that criti-
      cal frequency features—such as transient events or high-frequency noise within specific cycles—are
742
      preserved and effectively utilized for forecasting, rather than being obscured in a single-scale global
743
      analysis. As shown in Figure 3, MFT enables MMFNet to retain essential location-specific fre-
744   quency details across segments, allowing for the identification and modeling of hierarchical and
745   nested frequency structures in the data.
746
747
      MMFT, the core of MMFNet, demonstrates superiority over traditional single-scale frequency de-
      composition (SFT) by excelling at capturing temporal patterns at fine, intermediate, and coarse-
748
      grained scales. Unlike SFT-based models such as FiTS, which often lose location-specific infor-
749
      mation during global frequency analysis, MMFT preserves this information through its multi-scale
750   approach. By maintaining a hierarchical representation, MMFT ensures that both global trends
751   and localized variations are accurately captured, enhancing the model’s ability to manage complex
752   temporal dependencies. Additionally, MMFT incorporates a dynamic masking mechanism, which
753   adaptively filters out irrelevant or noisy frequency components. This ensures that the model fo-
754   cuses on meaningful features while suppressing noise, enhancing its robustness in complex or noisy
755   datasets. In contrast, traditional SFT models lack this adaptive capability, making them less effective
      in distinguishing signal from noise, especially in datasets with high variability or non-stationarity.

1466   G    U PDATED I NTRODUCTION
1467
1468   Time series forecasting is pivotal in a wide range of domains, such as environmental monitor-
1469   ing (Bhandari et al., 2017), electrical grid management (Zufferey et al., 2017), financial analy-
1470   sis (Sezer et al., 2020), and healthcare (Zeroual et al., 2020). Accurate long-term forecasting is
1471   essential for informed decision-making and strategic planning. Traditional methods, such as autore-
1472   gressive (AR) models (Nassar et al., 2004), exponential smoothing (Hyndman & Athanasopoulos,
1473
       2008), and structural time series models (Harvey, 1989), have provided a robust foundation for time
       series analysis by leveraging historical data to predict future values. However, real-world systems
1474
       frequently exhibit complex, non-stationary behavior, with time series characterized by intricate pat-
1475
       terns such as trends, fluctuations, and cycles. Those complexities pose significant challenges to
1476   achieving accurate forecasts (Makridakis et al., 1998; Box et al., 2015).
1477
1478   Long-term Time Series Forecasting (LTSF) has seen significant advancements in recent years, driven
1479
       by the development of sophisticated models, such as Transformer-based models (Zhou et al., 2021;
       Wu et al., 2021; Nie et al., 2024) and linear models (Zeng et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Lin
1480
       et al., 2024). Transformer-based architectures have demonstrated exceptional capacity in capturing
1481
       complex temporal patterns by effectively modeling long-range dependencies through self-attention
1482   mechanisms at the cost of heavy computation workload, particularly when facing large-scale time
1483   series data, which significantly limits their practicality in real-time applications. In contrast, the
1484   linear models provide a lightweight alternative for real-time forecasting. In particular, FITS demon-
1485   strates superior predictive performance across a wide range of scenarios with only 10K parameters
1486   by utilizing a single-scale frequency domain decomposition method combined with a low-pass filter
1487   employing a fixed cutoff frequency (Xu et al., 2024).

1511   Current methods often overlook the multiscale periodic nature of time series data. Time series
       are generated from continuous real-world processes sampled at various scales. For example, daily


                                                        28
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1512
       data capture hourly fluctuations, while yearly data reflect long-term trends and seasonal cycles.
1513   This inherent multi-scale, multi-periodic characteristic presents a significant challenge for model
1514   design, as each scale emphasizes distinct temporal dynamics that need to be effectively captured.
1515   Centered Kernel Alignment analysis has shown the ability to produce diverse representations across
1516   layers is particularly beneficial for tasks requiring the capture of irregular patterns Kornblith et al.
1517   (2019). These diverse representations are instrumental in managing variations across scales and
1518   periodicities.
1519   Current time-domain multiscale models like TimeMixer (Wang et al., 2024), though effective at
1520   capturing temporal variations across resolutions, has several limitations, particularly for datasets
1521   with multi-scale and multi-periodic properties. It primarily focuses on temporal patterns, often
1522   overlooking critical frequency-specific features such as harmonic or periodic behaviors, which are
1523   better captured in the frequency domain. For example, seasonal or cyclic trends are more apparent
1524   in frequency representations but can be difficult to disentangle in the time domain. Additionally,
1525   time-domain methods are sensitive to noise, as they work directly on raw signals, allowing noise to
1526   propagate across scales and obscure meaningful patterns, especially at coarser resolutions. Further-
1527
       more, while these methods enhance temporal resolution, they frequently struggle to capture long-
       term dependencies, as dividing data into scales can result in a loss of the broader context necessary
1528
       for understanding long-range interactions.
1529
1530   Current frequency domain decomposition methods apply a single scale frequency decomposition,
1531   which offers a global perspective of time series data in the frequency domain, it lacks the ability to
1532   localize specific frequency components within the sequence as shown in figure 9 on Traffic dataset7 .
1533
       Additionally, the low-pass filter employed by FITS may inadvertently smooth out crucial short-term
       fluctuations necessary for accurate predictions. The fixed cutoff frequency of the low-pass filter may
1534
       not be universally optimal for diverse time series datasets, further limiting its adaptability.
1535
1536   In this paper, we present MMFNet, a novel model designed to enhance LTSF through a multi-
1537   scale masked frequency decomposition approach. MMFNet captures fine, intermediate, and coarse-
1538   grained patterns in the frequency domain by segmenting the time series at multiple scales. At each
1539
       scale, MMFNet employs a learnable mask that adaptively filters out irrelevant frequency components
       based on the segment’s spectral characteristics. MMFNet offers two key advantages: (i) the multi-
1540
       scale frequency decomposition enables MMFNet to effectively capture both short-term fluctuations
1541
       and broader trends in the data, and (ii) the learnable frequency mask adaptively filters irrelevant
1542   frequency components, allowing the model to focus on the most informative signals. These features
1543   make MMFNet well-suited to capturing both short-term and long-term dependencies in complex
1544   time series, positioning it as an effective solution for various LTSF tasks.
