009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
                  Recent advancements in Machine Unlearning (MU) have introduced solutions to
012
                  selectively remove certain training samples, such as those with outdated or sen-
013
                  sitive information, from trained models. Despite these advancements, evaluation
014               of MU methods have been inconsistent, employing different trained models and
015               architectures, and sample removal strategies, which hampers accurate compari-
016               son. In addition, prior MU approaches have mainly focused on singular tasks or
017               modalities, which is not comprehensive. To address these limitations, we develop
018               MU-Bench, the first comprehensive benchmark for MU that (i) unifies the sets
019               of deleted samples and trained models, and (ii) provides broad coverage of tasks
020               and data modalities, including previously unexplored domains such as speech and
021               video classification. Our evaluation show that R AND L ABEL (Graves et al., 2021)
022
                  and S AL U N (Fan et al., 2024b) are the most effective general MU approaches on
                  MU-Bench, and BAD -T (Chundawat et al., 2023) and SCRUB (Kurmanji et al.,
023
                  2023) are capable of achieving random performance on the deletion set. We analyze
024
                  several under-investigated aspects of unlearning, including scalability, the impacts
025               of parameter-efficient fine-tuning and curriculum learning, and susceptibility to
026               dataset biases. MU-Bench provides an easy-to-use package that includes dataset
027               splits, models, and implementations, together with a leader board to enable unified
028               and scalable MU research1 .

032   1       I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Machine Unlearning (MU) aims at selectively removing a small portion of training data–and the
035   influence of the samples–from a trained model. MU is essential for protecting sensitive information
036   and discarding outdated samples. Recent works have studied machine unlearning in various contexts,
037   including classification tasks on image (Guo et al., 2020; Tang et al., 2023) and graph (Chien et al.,
038   2023; Cheng et al., 2023) data, multimodal tasks (Cheng & Amiri, 2023), generation tasks (Chen &
039   Yang, 2023; Gandikota et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024b), and federated learning (Wang et al., 2022).
040   Despite these advancements, existing approaches to machine unlearning face several challenges: (1):
041   MU systems are evaluated under inconsistent settings, using different trained models (from which
042   data is deleted) and metrics, which can lead to unfair comparisons and hinder the development of
043   robust unlearning approaches (Fan et al., 2024b); (2): evaluation tend to focus on specific tasks,
044   modalities, and architectures, which limits our understanding on the effectiveness of these models
045   across different settings (Wang et al., 2023; Chundawat et al., 2023).
046   To address these limitations, we introduce MU-Bench, a comprehensive machine unlearning bench-
047   mark consisting of multiple tasks, data modalities, base models, standardized evaluation metrics, all
048   compiled into an easy-to-use package with a leader board to enable robust and scalable MU research.
049   To the best of our knowledge, this benchmark represents the first effort to benchmark existing MU
050   approaches across a wide range of settings.

      2.1       P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
096
097
      Machine unlearning Let DTrain denotes the training dataset, Df ⊆ DTrain the subset to be
098   unlearned, and Dr = DTrain \Df the remaining dataset post-unlearning. Given a model f trained
099   on DTrain , machine unlearning seeks to remove the influence of Df from f without affecting the
100   knowledge it gained from Dr , without retraining from scratch. We term f as the original model and
101   f ′ as the model post-unlearning. A successful unlearned model f ′ should be minimally impacted by
102   Df , while maintaining the performance of f on the original downstream test set DTest .
103
104   Evaluation Metrics Evaluating the efficacy of unlearning is crucial for identifying models that
105   are more secure and retain no/less memory of deleted data. While previous studies have employed
106   different metrics, we propose a set of metrics that do not require model retraining: performance
107   on test set DTest (↑), performance on deletion set Df (↓), performance on remaining set Dr (↑),
      unlearning time (↓), and success rate of membership inference attack (↓).

127   Toward a retrain-free evaluation Early works in machine unlearning research often considered
128   the model retrained from scratch on Dr as the gold standard for f ′ , which is now recognized as an
129   inappropriate design choice due to several issues: First, evaluating f ′ based solely on its closeness
130
      or similarity to the retrained model can lead to false negatives. This is because the parameters of f ′
      may fall onto different distributions than the retrained model, but still achieve competitive unlearning
131
      performance. On the other hand, the parameters of two models can match even with completely
132
      different training datasets (Lamproudis et al., 2022). Second, retrained models cannot guarantee
133   the privacy of deleted data in practice, often maintaining undesired high performance on Df , as
134   demonstrated by previous work (Cheng et al., 2023). Third, obtaining a precise Dr can be impractical
135   in cases where the goal of unlearning is to remove toxic content (Zhang et al., 2023; Ilharco et al.,
136   2023) or abstract concepts (Gandikota et al., 2023). Such abstract concepts may not correspond
137   to identifiable data samples. Finally, retraining a model from scratch on Dr can be impractical or
138   even impossible due to confidentiality constraints, proprietary data concerns, or because the data
139   may no longer be available. In addition, retraining is often expensive, especially for large datasets
140   or complex tasks such as multimodal learning or large language models (LLMs). Based on the
141
      above shortcomings, we advocate for a retrain-free evaluation of unlearning systems, a method that is
      increasingly recognized in recent works (Chundawat et al., 2023).

144   2.2    DATASETS AND TASKS
145
146   We adopt nine publicly available datasets covering a diverse set of discriminative and generative tasks
147   and data modalities. As Table 1 shows, the discriminative tasks include CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky,
148   2009) for image classification, IMDB (Maas et al., 2011) for sentiment classification, DDI (Segura-
149   Bedmar et al., 2013) for relation extraction in the biomedical domain, NLVR2 (Suhr et al., 2019) for
150   visual reasoning, Speech Commands (Warden, 2018) for keyword spotting, and UCF101 (Soomro
151   et al., 2012) for action classification. The generative tasks include SAMSum (Gliwa et al., 2019) for
152
      text summarization, Biography (adapted from Min et al. (2023), see below) for text generation, Tiny
      ImageNet (Le & Yang, 2015) for text-to-image generation.
153
154   We build a new dataset for evaluating machine unlearning in large language models (LLMs), focusing
155   on the removal of personal information, as a common unlearning request. This is a crucial tasks
156   because for example, on social media, user can choose to delete their accounts or privatize them,
157   resulting in a critical and perhaps legal impetus for machine unlearning. The dataset contains factual
158
      descriptions of 183 celebrities, obtained from (Min et al., 2023), to enable machine unlearning of
      personal data from LLMs.
159
160   These datasets were chosen for their relevance to practical machine unlearning tasks, their variety,
161   including both well-established and under-explored datasets, and their capacity to highlight differ-
      ences between unlearning methods across diverse tasks and modalities (as they have non-saturated

      2.3         U NIFIED U NLEARNING
166
167   To address inconsistencies in the evaluation of MU approaches, we unify critical aspects such as the
168   choice and size of deleted samples (Df ), and the baseline model (f ) from which data is removed. This
169   unification allows for meaningful comparison and democratizes access through open-source tools.
170
171   Deleted Samples For each dataset, we randomly sample 1-10% of the training data as Df , with
172   increments of 1% to covers both typical and extreme evaluation settings. This approach reflects typical
173   and realistic settings where a small portion of data is deleted (Golatkar et al., 2020; Chundawat et al.,
174   2023; Cheng et al., 2023), and challenges the limits of unlearning methods without fundamentally
175   altering the data distribution, as would be the case with more extensive data removal.
176
177   Original Model For each dataset, we train a set of commonly-used models on different architectures
178
      and scales, from which Df is deleted, to allow for robust and relevant comparisons. We train a total of
      20 architectures and 34 scales, such as ResNet (He et al., 2016) (18, 34, 50 layers), ViT (Dosovitskiy
179
      et al., 2021) (Small, Base, Large), Swin-Transformer (Liu et al., 2021) (Tiny, Small, Base), MobilNet
180
      V2 (Sandler et al., 2018) for image classification; and HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021b) (Base, Large,
181   X-Large), Whisper (Radford et al., 2023) (Tiny, Small, Base), Wav2Vec2.0 (Baevski et al., 2020b)
182   (Base, Large) for the audio classification. Additional details are provided in Appendix A.3.
183
184   Example Usage We include the datasets, standardized data splits, evaluation scripts, and
185   unlearning methods within an easy-to-use Python package and integerate them with commonly-used
186   packages such as PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019), Huggingface Transformers (Wolf et al., 2020), and
187   Diffusers (von Platen et al., 2022), containing pretrained diffusion models for image and speech
188   data. Users can initiate an unlearning experiment with minimal adjustment to existing script. All
189   original model checkpoints are released for standardized unlearning and fair comparisons. We also
190   host and maintain a leaderboard to rank methods overall and on individual tasks and architectures.
191   For example, to remove 5% of training data from a BERT-base model trained on IMDB using
192
      BAD -T (Chundawat et al., 2023), only a minimal script modification is required. This setup simplifies
      the unlearning process and enables rapid comparison against methods and architectures.

255   Extension to generative tasks Even though many unlearning methods are designed for and
256   evaluated on classification tasks, they can be applied to generative tasks with minimal modifications.
257   For example, in case of R AND L ABEL, data pairs (x, y) ∈ Df can be altered to (x, y ′ ) where
258   y ′ ∈ Dr , y ′ ̸= y. For BAD -T, the method can be adjusted to match the predictions of each token
259   when measuring the teacher-student divergence.

336   data including Df and Dr , while BAD -T and SCRUB remove knowledge of all data. In addition,
337   we find that N EG G RAD show very different performance pattern on generative tasks compared to
338   discriminative tasks, with non-random performance when a small portion of examples are deleted,
339
      see Figure 13.
340   For text-to-image generation, we find all methods can effectively reduce the clip score between

342   generated images are not from the orginal classes, we use a trained image classifier to classify the

344   Additional results on training time and membership inference attack are shown in Appendix A.5.

347   4                   D ISCUSSION AND A NALYSIS
348
349   What is the deletion capacity of each method? We define deletion capacity as the amount of data
350
      a model can forget without degrading performance on DT . R AND L ABEL and S AL U N have relatively
      larger deletion capacity than SCRUB, while BAD -T has the smallest capacity. These results suggest
351
      that task loss is a potentially better way of knowledge measurement than matching logits in BAD -T.
352
      Another reason is the computation cost of BAD -T restricts its capability of forgetting more samples.
353   Furthermore, we find that the deletion capacity of the same MU method varies across different
354   tasks, modalities, and network architectures. S AL U N has large deletion capacity on image and text
355   classification datasets, but much smaller capacity on multimodal tasks, shown in Figures 7–10.
356
357   Does unlearning amplify biases? A less explored aspect of unlearning in existing works is does
358   MU amplify or restrict the model’s dependence on biases in MU. To answer these questions, we
359   evaluate the zero-shot transfer performance of f and f ′ on test examples that are adversarial or
360   from shifted distributions, specifically, CIFAR100-C (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019) for CIFAR100,
361   Rotten Tomatoes (Pang & Lee, 2005) for IMDB, extra test set from (Suhr et al., 2019) for NLVR2,
362   UCF101-DS (Schiappa et al., 2023) for UCF101, and XSum (Narayan et al., 2018) for SAMSum.
363   The results show that N EG G RAD significantly affects models’ capability on transfer test sets, while
364
      other methods we evaluated do not strongly influence models’ dependence on biases, see Figure 4.
365
366
      Does unlearning follow scaling laws? Scaling is a critical aspect to understand the limitations
      of an unlearning method. The results show that R AND L ABEL, S AL U N, N EG G RAD, and BAD -T
367
      have a better predictability of performance on Df , given the amount of compute (FLOS), while
368
      the performance of SCRUB depends on the switch between max steps and min steps. In addition,
369   N EG G RAD and SCRUB have faster speed in decreasing performance on Df . BAD -T has relatively
370   slower speed, due to the fact that it simultaneously iterate through Df and Dr at every optimization
371   step, which leads to more computing cost than other methods.
372
373   Does unlearning benefit from curriculum learning? The effect of curriculum learning (Bengio
374   et al., 2009; Sukhbaatar et al., 2018) (CL) in MU is an overlooked aspect in existing literature. MU
375   models often sample batches randomly with no specific order and treat inputs with equal weight. We
376   experiment with one common curriculum learning approach SuperLoss (Castells et al., 2020), which
377   implements the core principle of curriculum learning. Specifically, it weights training losses based on
      sample difficulty, weighing down the contribution of samples with large training loss (potentially

401    Does unlearning benefit from parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT)? Despite recent advance-
402    ments of parameter-efficient fine-tuning (He et al., 2022), most MU methods optimize the entire
403    network parameters, which results in significant cost. Only a few approaches have adopted a
404
       parameter-efficient strategy (Chen & Yang, 2023; Cheng et al., 2023). Since PEFT only updates a
       small portion of the model, it is intuitive to assume that PEFT can maximally retain the knowledge
405
       from the original model without compromising unlearning. To validate this hypothesis in the context
406
       of MU, we experiment with LoRA (Hu et al., 2022a). The results show that most methods can benefit
407    from PEFT, where the performance gap on Df is less than 10 points in accuracy. However, the amount
408    of trainable parameters in MU is much larger than that of fine-tuning. As the trainable parameters are
409    less than 50% of the original size, the performance on Df is close to that of Dr . Such performance
410    persists even with larger learning rate and longer training time, indicating unlearning Df cannot be
411    achieved below the threshold of 50%, see Figure 6. This minimum trainable threshold (Hu et al.,
412    2022b; Su et al., 2023) is much larger than non-MU tasks with as low as a few thousand parameters,
413    since selective knowledge removal is a more challenging task. Meanwhile, the performances on
414    DTest and Dr are not affected, indicating LoRA forgets less and slower in MU.
415
416   Which design choices are effective for machine unlearning? For discriminitive tasks, corrupting
417   gradient is a less effective approach compared to corrupting data (R AND L ABEL, etc.) and model
418   (BAD -T). Corrupting gradients can discard learned knowledge and therefore we suggest not using it in
419
      isolation without other constraints. However, this approach has a greater potential for generative tasks.
      It is generally more effective to simultaneously iterate through Df and Dr (BAD -T) or randomly
420
      iterate through the training set (R AND L ABEL, S AL U N), than to clearly separate Df and Dr . For
421
      example, SCRUB takes a few passes on Df to forget the deletion set before learning on Dr to
422   retain non-deleted data. On the other hand, simultaneous processing of Df and Dr lead to higher
423   computational cost. Using representation or task loss as a measurement of learned knowledge can
424   adapt to both discriminative and generative tasks, while using logits (BAD -T) has a much more
425   restricted application merely on classification tasks.
426
427    Is a lower performance on Df always better? Previous works focus on driving the performance
428    on Df to as low as possible. We suggest that excessively low score on Df might reveal information
429    or indicate its existence, which may be taken advantaged by adversary. Moreover, unlearning does
430    not mean a model should completely lose its capability of handling specific samples in Df . Instead,
431    a balanced approach where the unlearned model maintains a reasonably low performance on Df is
       preferable. Recent works are focusing on this direction, such as zero-retrain evaluation (Chundawat

      Conclusion We propose MU-Bench, the first comprehensive machine unlearning (MU) benchmark
      that spans various tasks and data modalities, including those previously unexplored in MU. We
450
      introduce a unified taxonomy of existing MU works, which highlights their unique design choices and
451
      establishes connections between them. We also conduct extensive experiments with commonly-used
452   and recent MU algorithms using MU-Bench, discovering that audio and video tasks require more
453   focused development of MU techniques. In addition, we explore several overlooked yet crucial aspects
454   of unlearning, such as bias, parameter-efficiency, curriculum learning, and deletion capacity. Finally,
455   we develop an open-source package of MU-Bench to provide unified data splits, and implement a
456   suite of commonly-used MU methods and their design choices to enable fast experimentation and
457   fair comparisons across MU methods. The package along with a leaderboard are structured to easily
458   incorporate new datasets and tasks in future. We will continue to expand MU-Bench by incorporating
459   more datasets and tasks.
460
461   Future Works There are several venues for future work including: (a): MU methods for under-
462   investigated tasks and modalities: existing unlearning methods are primarily developed for text or
463   image data types. Our experiments on MU-Bench show that current models severely underperform
464   in audio and video contexts. A promising area of research is to extend MU to these data modalities
465   and tasks through focused development of MU techniques to ensures comprehensive MU capability.
466   (b) Efficient MU methods: existing unlearning methods require extensive training, either tuning the
467   entire model or training on large portions of the dataset. Meanwhile, most methods do not benefit
468
      from PEFT. Future research can focus on developing more efficient MU methods using approaches
      like zero-shot methods, sparse methods, and curriculum learning methods to speed up the unlearning
469
      process. (c) Explainability: understanding why certain samples are more easily forgotten than
470
      others could shed light on inner working of MU methods and improve MU performance. Therefore,
471   investigating the complexities of samples that affect their retention or deletion is a promising area
472   of research. (d) Evaluation: current evaluation of MU is still in its early stage and demands more
473   attention. Refining current evaluation strategies and metrics will be crucial for advancing the field. (e)
474   Theoretical guarantee of MU: most current non-DP-based MU approaches do not provide theoretical
475   guarantees. A critical future directions is to develop theoretical frameworks that provide bounds
476   performance bounds for MU.
477
478
      Limitations While our work marks significant progress, it has the following limitations: (a):
479
      Not all MU algorithms are evaluated: due to the significant cost and resource constraints, we
480   focused on a selection of recent, well-performing and representative approaches rather than an
481   exhaustive examination of all MU models. (b): Breadths of experiments. Our investigation into
482   parameter-efficient fine-tuning and curriculum learning were limited to specific methods like LoRA
483   and SuperLoss, though other more effective approaches exist. (c): Not all tasks are included. There
484   are some relevant tasks that are not currently included in MU-Bench, such as those related to graphs,
485   recommendation, or retrieval tasks. We plan to expand the range of tasks and datasets in ongoing
      development of MU-Bench.

1006
       A.1   R ELATED WORK
1007
1008   Categorization of unlearning methods Exact unlearning methods divide the remaining data into
1009   several shards and train a separate model on each subset of data. Then all models are combined to
1010   make a prediction. They work under different scenarios, including on images (Bourtoule et al., 2021;
1011   Wu et al., 2020b;a; Liu et al., 2022b; Dukler et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023), on graphs (Chen et al.,
1012   2021). Differential Privacy-based methods adopt a one-shot weight update followed by added noise
1013   to model weights, whose probability distribution is indistinguishable from that of a model retrained
1014   from scratch with theoretical guarantee Golatkar et al. (2020); Guo et al. (2020); Neel et al. (2021);
1015
       Brophy & Lowd (2021); Wu et al. (2023); Izzo et al. (2021); Suriyakumar & Wilson (2022); Liu et al.
       (2023a). Teacher-student unlearning methods formulates unlearning as selectively transferring the
1016
       knowledge into the unlearned model (student). Usually, the teacher on the non-deleted data is the
1017
       original model, while the teacher on deleted data is opposite to the original model Wang et al. (2023);
1018   Kurmanji et al. (2023); Chundawat et al. (2023); Cheng et al. (2023); Fan et al. (2024b); Tarun et al.
1019   (2023a).
1020
1021   Unlearning for discriminative tasks Unlearning works in discriminative tasks covers image
1022   classification (Foster et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Jia et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022b), text
1023   classification Li & Liu (2023); Mehta et al. (2022); Cha et al. (2024); Kang et al. (2024), node / edge
1024   classification on graph-structured data (Chen et al., 2022b; Chien et al., 2023; Cong & Mahdavi,
1025   2023; Cheng et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2023; Sinha et al., 2023), regression (Tarun
       et al., 2023b), image retrieval (Zhang et al., 2022a), multimodal classification tasks (Cheng & Amiri,


                                                        19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       2023; Poppi et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b), Bayesian models Nguyen et al. (2020), recommender
1027   systems Chen et al. (2022a); Li et al. (2022; 2023), k-means (Pan et al., 2023), and intelligent
1028   agents (Liu et al., 2022a). Many other works focus on class unlearning, i.e. removing all samples
1029   with a specific class (Chen et al., 2023a). However, discriminative tasks on audio and video have
1030   been limitedly studied, which this work bridge the gap.
1031
1032   Unlearning for generative tasks Unlearning for generation models centers on removing copy-
1033   righted, private, NSFW, or biased content from generative models, including diffusion mod-
1034   els (Gandikota et al., 2023; ?; 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Fuchi & Takagi, 2024; Fan et al., 2024b),
1035   image-to-image models (Li et al., 2024a), text summarization models (Chen & Yang, 2023), trans-
1036
       lation models (Wang et al., 2023), and text generation models (Lu et al., 2022; Jang et al., 2023;
       Kassem et al., 2023; Chen & Yang, 2023).
1037
1038
       Unlearning in LLMs Recently, more attention has been paid to unlearning in LLMs. Most
1039
       works focus on gradient ascent to forget copyrighted content (Eldan & Russinovich, 2023). Yao
1040
       et al. (2024) designed two additional losses: 1) predicting if answer is gramatically correct, and 2)
1041   maintaining performance. SOUL (Jia et al., 2024) leverages second-order optimization techniques.
1042   Other approaches include sparsity Ma et al. (2023) and operations on gradient Ullah et al. (2021);
1043   Hoang et al. (2024). Applications of unlearning include removing bias Setlur et al. (2022); Chen
1044   et al. (2023b), alleviating backdoor attack Wei et al. (2023), conducting data poinson attack Di et al.
1045   (2023).
1046
1047   Unlearning evaluation Evaluation of MU include the effectiveness of exact / DP-based unlearn-
1048   ing (Thudi et al., 2022), adversarially trained models Liu et al. (2023b), adversarially evaluation (Goel
1049   et al., 2022), red-teaming tool for concept removal Tsai et al. (2024), verification Sommer et al.
1050
       (2022), sequential deletion (Gupta et al., 2021), vulnerability to attack ZHAO et al. (2023), trade-off
       with reverting decisions Pawelczyk et al. (2023), different choices of deleted points (Fan et al., 2024a),
1051
       theoretical capacity of deletion (Liu et al., 2023a), under shallow models Schelter et al. (2021); Ginart
1052
       et al. (2019), under zero-shot setting Chundawat et al. (2022).
1053
1054   Task-specific MU benchmarks In general, datasets and benchmarks for unlearning is under-
1055   explored. Most works draw samples as deleted data from existing datasets and choose different subsets
1056   from paper to paper. UnlearnCanvas is a benchmark for unlearning for diffusion models (Zhang
1057   et al., 2024). TOFU (Maini et al., 2024) is a benchmark for unlearning fictitious author profiles in
1058   LLMs. Conversely, we test LLMs with unlearning real profiles, as such information appears in the
1059   pretraining corpus of the LLMs, which aligns with the unlearning setting.
