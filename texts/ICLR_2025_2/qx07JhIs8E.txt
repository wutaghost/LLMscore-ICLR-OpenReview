009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper examines the phenomenon of probabilistic robustness overestima-
012            tion in TRADES, a prominent adversarial training method. Our study reveals
013            that TRADES sometimes yields disproportionately high PGD validation accuracy
014            compared to the AutoAttack testing accuracy in the multiclass classification task.
015            This discrepancy highlights a significant overestimation of robustness for these in-
016            stances, potentially linked to gradient masking. We further analyze the parameters
017
               contributing to unstable models that lead to overestimation. Our findings indicate
               that smaller batch sizes, lower beta values (which control the weight of the robust
018
               loss term in TRADES), larger learning rate, and higher class complexity (e.g.,
019
               CIFAR-100 versus CIFAR-10) are associated with an increased likelihood of ro-
020            bustness overestimation. By examining metrics such as the First-Order Stationary
021            Condition (FOSC), inner-maximization, and gradient information, we identify the
022            underlying cause of this phenomenon as gradient masking and provide insights
023            into it. Furthermore, our experiments show that certain unstable training instances
024            may return to a state without robust overestimation, inspiring our attempts at a so-
025            lution. In addition to adjusting parameter settings to reduce instability or retrain-
026            ing when overestimation occurs, we recommend incorporating Gaussian noise in
027            inputs when the FOSC score exceed the threshold. This method aims to mitigate
028
               robustness overestimation of TRADES and other similar methods at its source,
               ensuring more reliable representation of adversarial robustness during evaluation.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Adversarial robustness has emerged as a critical measure of security in machine learning models,
034   particularly for deep neural networks. These models, while achieving state-of-the-art accuracy on
035   various tasks, often exhibit vulnerabilities to adversarial examples—inputs specifically crafted to
036   cause the model to make errors (Szegedy et al., 2014). Adversarial training (Goodfellow et al.,
037   2015; Madry et al., 2018), a method that involves training a model on adversarial examples, has
038   been developed as a primary defense mechanism to enhance adversarial robustness.
039   In a more recent development, Zhang et al. (2019) proposed a novel adversarial training method,
040   TRADES (TRadeoff-inspired Adversarial DEfense via Surrogate-loss minimization). Consider a
041   data distribution D over pairs of examples x ∈ Rd and labels y ∈ [k]. As usual, fθ represents
042   the classifier, CE is the cross-entropy (CE) loss, and KL is the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence.
043   For a set of allowed perturbations S ⊆ Rd that formalizes the manipulative power of the adversary,
044   TRADES has the loss minimization defined as:

045                                                      1
                         min E(x,y)∼D CE(f (x), y) + max KL(fθ (x), fθ (x + δ)) .                    (1)
046                        f                             λ δ∈S
047
048   Within this approach, the inner maximization seeks to increase the KL-divergence between the logits
049   generated from the original and adversarial examples, while the outer minimization aims to balance
050
      accuracy and robustness by utilizing a hyperparameter λ and adjusting the trade-off between the per-
      formance on natural and adversarial examples. This approach has been successful and is commonly
051
      used today as one of the bases for adversarial training, often serving as a baseline.
052
053   However, the loss design of TRADES is similar to the logit pairing method (Kannan et al., 2018),
      which is an abandoned approach due to its potential to cause gradient masking (Athalye et al., 2018)

054
      (a typical flaw in defense methods that appear to fool gradient-based attacks but do not actually
055   make the model more robust). In the past, many analyses (Mosbach et al., 2018; Engstrom et al.,
056   2018; Lee et al., 2021) have been conducted on this issue, but none of the works have experimented
057   on TRADES specifically. As a result, TRADES is still trusted and widely used today.
058
      Through our study, under certain hyperparameter settings in multi-class classification tasks, we
059
      found that some training instances may lead to robustness overestimation, where AutoAttack (Croce
060
      & Hein, 2020b) test accuracy is significantly lower than PGD-10 (Madry et al., 2018) validation
061   accuracy. After analyzing the loss landscapes and the convergence capability of multi-step adver-
062   sarial examples of these instances, we observed the occurrence of gradient masking, a phenomenon
063   where the adversarial robustness of a model is inaccurately gauged, potentially compromising the
064   effectiveness of the defense in practical scenarios.
065
      Moreover, by examining the inner maximization and batch-level gradient information, we experi-
066
      mentally explained the reasons behind this phenomenon. We also found that robustness overesti-
067   mation may occasionally end by the model itself, returning to stability —a phenomenon we refer to
068   as “self-healing”. Inspired by some of the characteristics of self-healing, we proposed an actively
069   healing solution by simultaneously quantifying the degree of gradient masking and adding Gaus-
070   sian noise in inputs if needed, which allows errors during training to be detected and corrected in
071   real-time without the need for retraining. The contributions of this paper are as follows.
072
073         1. We identify the adversarial robustness overestimation and instability in TRADES and the
074
               impact of hyperparameters in multi-class classification. By examining the loss landscape
               and the convergence capability of adversarial examples, we interpret this issue as gradient
075
               masking.
076
077         2. We analyze inner-maximization metrics and gradient information to elucidate the underly-
078            ing causes of the observed phenomena and propose methodologies to address these instabil-
079
               ities. Additionally, we discover that some training instances have the potential to self-heal
               and resolve the issues mentioned above.
080
081         3. We propose a healing solution that enables real-time examination and correction of poten-
082            tial instability during TRADES training.
083
      Our findings aim to refine the understanding and application of current adversarial training frame-
084
      works, paving the way for more secure machine learning deployments.

087   2   R ELATED W ORK
088
089   To defend against various adversarial attacks (Carlini & Wagner, 2017; Madry et al., 2018; Croce &
090   Hein, 2020a; Andriushchenko et al., 2020; Croce & Hein, 2020b), adversarial training (Goodfellow
091   et al., 2015; Madry et al., 2018) has been demonstrated to be effective in enhancing the robust-
092   ness with AutoAttack (Croce & Hein, 2020b) serving as a reliable evaluation method. (Croce et al.,
093
      2021) Adversarial training comes in different variants, including PGD-AT (Madry et al., 2018), ALP
      (Kannan et al., 2018), and LSQ (Shafahi et al., 2019), MMA (Ding et al., 2020), among others. On
094
      the other hand, Tsipras et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2019) have identified a trade-off between robust-
095
      ness and accuracy, a phenomenon well-explained in theoretical aspects. Based on this, Zhang et al.
096   (2019) developed TRADES, which currently serves as a classical baseline in adversarial training.
097   Then, subsequent works include Wang et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2020); Blum et al. (2020); Jin
098   et al. (2022; 2023) have improved upon TRADES and achieved promising results.
099
      However, in adversarial training, logit pairing methods (such as the previously mentioned ALP
100
      (Kannan et al., 2018) and LSQ (Shafahi et al., 2019)) have been analyzed as unreliable because
101
      they lead to robustness overestimation. (Mosbach et al., 2018; Engstrom et al., 2018; Lee et al.,
102   2021) This typically arises from gradient masking (or obfuscated gradients) (Athalye et al., 2018;
103   Goodfellow, 2018; Boenisch et al., 2021; B.S. et al., 2019; Ma et al., 2023), which results in black-
104   box attacks performing unexpectedly better than white-box attacks. This is unacceptable because
105   the model does not provide genuinely reliable robustness. Therefore, for a more comprehensive
106   robustness evaluation, in addition to using different attacks for testing, directly quantifying (Wang
107   et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2021) or visualizing (Li et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2020) the
      relationship between the testing model and adversarial examples is also a good approach.

      To more clearly depict gradient masking in unstable cases, we plot the data loss landscapes in Fig-
204   ure 1 from the corresponding checkpoints. Typically, more robust models tend to have smoother
205   loss surfaces, whereas less robust models exhibit more jagged landscapes (Chen et al., 2021). We
206   find that checkpoints with an overestimated robust accuracy tend to correspond to these jagged loss
207   landscapes, which supports that unstable cases obtain less robust representations, thereby leading to
208   gradient masking.
209
      We support our perspective by utilizing FOSC (Wang et al., 2019) and SGCS (Lee et al., 2021)
210   (described in Section 3.2). These metrics represent values that measure the degree of adversar-
211   ial convergence and can be used to assess the ruggedness of the loss landscape. As depicted in
212   Figure 2, one can observe that the difference in PGD-10 accuracy between the two cases is not
213   significant. However, both FOSC and SGCS in the unstable case “simultaneously” show a sharp
214   change, indicating a substantial loss of robustness and the occurrence of gradient masking, which
215   results in the PGD attacker struggling to find adversarial examples. We also provide a theoretical
      explanation of the relationship between FOSC and SGCS in Appendix E.

      5.1   TPGD C AUSES OVER - FITTING TO G RADIENT-BASED ATTACKS
292
293   In Figure 3, by monitoring the gap metric, defined as the difference between the clean training ac-
294   curacy and the adversarial training accuracy, insights can be gained into the dynamics of robustness
295   overestimation. In instances where the model becomes unstable, as indicated by an increase in the
296   FOSC values, we observe a sudden drop in the gap metric. In some epochs, this gap even tem-
297   porarily becomes negative, implying that the adversarial training accuracy has surpassed the clean
298   training accuracy. This counterintuitive outcome suggests that the addition of adversarial pertur-
299   bations through TRADES’ PGD (TPGD) somehow aids the model in better classifying the cases,
300   which is clearly abnormal.
301   As indicated in Equation 1, the inner-maximization step seeks to maximize the adversarial loss by
302   increasing the KL divergence between the clean and adversarial logits. Conversely, the outer min-
303   imization step aims to reduce this divergence. This push-and-pull dynamic in logit-logit relations,
304   rather than the typical logit-label relations used in robust methods like PGD-AT (Madry et al., 2018),
305   suggests the model may overfit to the unique characteristics of TPGD perturbations. Further analysis
306   of these dynamics can be found in Appendix F.
307   The above hypothesis falls in line with the findings from the prior work by Kurakin et al. (2017),
308   which discusses the learnable patterns of simpler attacks like Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM)
309   (Goodfellow et al., 2015). However, in this case, the model is not learning any specific patterns;
310   instead, it overfits the perturbations and converges at a point where most gradient-based attackers
311   struggle to cause large logit differences due to obfuscated gradients.

      5.3   S ELF -H EALING IN TRADES
352
353   During our experiments, we observed a phenomenon in some instances of TRADES training that we
354   term “self-healing”. This occurs when a model initially shows signs of robustness overestimation but
355   eventually stabilizes, correcting the overestimation without external intervention. Figure 5 illustrates
356   such a case, showing the dynamics of FOSC values, weight gradient norms, and clean training
357   accuracy over the training epochs. As mentioned earlier, instability is marked by spikes in FOSC
358   values, indicating a rugged loss landscape. However, in this example, these spikes are followed by
359
      a decline, suggesting a return to a smoother loss landscape and subsequent stability.
360   A distinctive feature of self-healing instances is a slight decline in clean training accuracy, accom-
361   panied by a drop in FOSC to nearly zero within the same epoch. In the following epoch, the weight
362   gradient norm decreases as well, suggesting that the model encountered a challenging batch, trigger-
363   ing the self-healing process and thus leading to a large optimization step, which effectively escapes
364   the problematic local loss landscape that causes obfuscated gradients. Although this step temporarily
365
      worsens the model’s predictions, it effectively resets the training process to a more stable state.
366

374
      training methods, which focused on overall performance, are inadequate for addressing such spe-
      cific challenges. Consequently, we must leverage the characteristics identified earlier to develop an

      appropriate solution.

      levels, signifying restored stability. It is worth noting that this solution might still result in a FOSC

      spike for one epoch, but as long as this checkpoint is not selected (as lines 19-20 of Algorithm 1),
445   our method ensures a good optimization process in all other epochs, similar to the regular case.

      not exhibit significant robustness overestimation, unlike the original unstable instance. Notably,

454   tasks without the risk of severe robustness overestimation.

      7   L IMITATIONS
457
458
      This paper focuses primarily on analyzing TRADES, a well-established adversarial training method.
459   Although proper hyperparameter tuning may mitigate some of TRADES’ potential issues, we be-
460   lieve these challenges are linked to sampling probability and gradient masking—an issue that should
461   not occur in an adversarial defense method—can still happen. Therefore, its existence should not be
462   overlooked.
463
      Additionally, while many modern defense methods empirically outperform TRADES, it remains a
464
      foundational baseline for numerous adversarial training approaches. Researchers continue to build
465   upon TRADES to validate the effectiveness of their own methods. In this sense, TRADES must still
466   maintain a degree of reliability, and this is exactly what we aim to challenge. Moreover, the logit
467   pairing technique used in TRADES raises the question of whether it genuinely enhances robustness
468   or if this is merely a misconception. This issue provides an opportunity for future researchers to
469   further investigate, building on the analysis and solutions we have proposed.

      8   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
472
473
      In conclusion, we have identified the issue of probabilistic robustness overestimation in TRADES,
474   analyzed its root causes, and proposed a potential solution. Hence, we believe that vanilla TRADES
475   should not be fully trusted as a baseline for multi-class classification tasks without applying our so-
476   lution techniques. Given that TRADES incorporates techniques similar to logit pairing—previously
477   abandoned due to unreliable robustness and gradient masking—we found that TRADES exhibits
478   similar issues. Future research can build upon this correlation by consolidating all these methods to
479   provide a more unified analysis and explanation. Our findings offer a valuable reference for future
480   research in adversarial training methods.
