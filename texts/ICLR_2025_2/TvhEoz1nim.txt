009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Generative model inversion attacks (MIAs) have garnered increasing attention for
012            their ability to reconstruct synthetic samples that closely resemble private training
013            data, exposing significant privacy risks in machine learning models. The suc-
014            cess of generative MIAs is primarily attributed to image priors learned by gen-
015            erative adversarial networks (GANs) on public auxiliary data, which help con-
016            strain the optimization space during the inversion process. However, GAN-based
017            generative MIAs still face limitations, particularly regarding the instability dur-
018
               ing model inversion optimization and the fidelity of reconstructed samples, indi-
               cating substantial room for improvement. In this paper, we address these chal-
019
               lenges by exploring generative MIAs based on diffusion models, which offer su-
020
               perior generative performance compared to GANs. Specifically, we replace the
021            GAN generator in existing generative MIAs with a single-step generator distilled
022            from pretrained diffusion models, constraining the search space to the manifold
023            of the generator during the inversion process. In addition, we leverage genera-
024            tive model inversion techniques to investigate privacy leakage issues in widely
025            used large-scale multimodal models, particularly CLIP, highlighting the inher-
026            ent privacy risks in these models. Our extensive experiments demonstrate that
027            single-step diffusion models-based MIAs significantly outperform their GAN-
028            based counterparts, achieving substantial improvements in traditional metrics and
029
               greatly enhancing the visual fidelity of reconstructed samples. This research un-
               covers privacy vulnerabilities in CLIP models and opens new research directions
030
               in generative MIAs. Our source code is available at this anonymous repository:
031
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/DDMI-F967/.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   As machine learning (ML) models advance and become increasingly integrated into critical do-
037   mains such as healthcare diagnostics (Richens et al., 2020), intelligent finance (Rundo et al., 2019),
038   and biometric authentication (Jain & Nandakumar, 2012), concerns about data privacy have grown.
039   These models, particularly deep neural networks (DNNs), rely on sensitive datasets for training,
040   making them appealing targets for adversarial attacks. One emerging threat is model inversion at-
041   tacks (MIAs) (Fredrikson et al., 2014), a category of privacy attack, in which adversaries reconstruct
042   samples to expose sensitive information from the private training data (e.g., personally identifiable
043   images) by analyzing the model’s outputs. This poses a significant risk to user privacy and security.
044   Earlier studies (Fredrikson et al., 2015) framed MIAs as an optimization problem in the raw input
045   space, aiming to reconstruct private data by adjusting synthetic inputs to maximize the likelihood of
046   a specific class. This method was based on the assumption that strong correlations were established
047   between inputs and model outputs during the training process. While this approach proved effective
048   for simpler ML models, it was insufficient for DNNs trained on high-dimensional data, such as facial
049   recognition models. In such cases, optimizing in the input space becomes highly complex and often
050
      ill-posed, leading to the generation of unrealistic features that lack semantic relevance.
051   To address this challenge, Zhang et al. (2020) proposed a novel approach called generative model
052   inversion attacks (GMI), which enhances the inversion process by learning a meaningful image prior
053   from public auxiliary data using generative adversarial networks (GANs) (panel (a) of Fig. 2). This
      ensures that the synthetic data generated during the attack resides on a realistic image manifold

      While GAN-based MIAs outperform traditional methods, they still suffer from instability during
      model inversion (cf. Fig.1(a)) and lower fidelity in reconstructed samples (cf. Fig.1(b)), resulting
082
      in suboptimal performance. These issues stem from inherent flaws in GANs. Specifically, the ad-
083
      versarial training framework is difficult to optimize and prone to mode collapse without precise hy-
084   perparameter tuning and regularization (Brock, 2019). Moreover, GANs capture less data diversity
085   than advanced likelihood-based models (Razavi et al., 2019; Song et al., 2021). Hence, generative
086   models with more stable training and better mode coverage are better suited for MIA tasks.
087
      To overcome these challenges, we propose exploring generative MIAs based on diffusion models
088
      (DMs) (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021; Karras
089
      et al., 2022), which offer superior generative performance over GANs. However, our analysis reveals
090   that traditional multi-step diffusion models are not directly applicable to MIAs (Sec. 3.2). This is
091   because the iterative refinement process in reverse diffusion introduces significant computational
092   and memory overhead, and additionally leads to the accumulation of numerical errors, resulting in
093   inaccurate latent codes that ultimately reduce the effectiveness of the attack.
094
      Building on recent advances in distilling diffusion models (Salimans & Ho, 2022; Luo et al., 2024;
095
      Yin et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024), which enable the distillation of a pretrained diffusion model
096   into a single-step generator, we propose a novel framework for generative MIAs, termed diffusion
097   distillation MIAs (DDMI). DDMI, like traditional GAN-based generative MIAs, includes two stages:
098   ⃝1 We pretrain a multi-step diffusion model on public auxiliary datasets and apply Score identity
099   Distillation (SiD) (Zhou et al., 2024) to create a high-performance single-step generator (cf. panel
100   (b) of Fig. 2). ⃝2 This single-step generator is utilized to guide the model inversion optimization
101   process, ensuring it remains within a meaningful image manifold (cf. panel (c) of Fig. 2).
102   Moreover, we extend generative MIA techniques, traditionally applied to classification models, to
103   explore privacy risks in Contrastive Language–Image Pre-training (CLIP) models (Radford et al.,
104   2021), a widely adopted multimodal model (cf. panel (d) of Fig. 2). Specifically, by crafting prompts
105   like “A photo of <NAME>.” and using model inversion optimization to maximize cosine similarity
106   between text and image features, we are able to generate images that align closely with the given
107   text prompt. This adaptation reveals the privacy vulnerabilities of multimodal models and provides
      insights into the extent of potential privacy leakage in such models.

      Our contributions and findings are summarized as follows:
109
110          • We identify key limitations in GAN-based generative MIAs (Zhang et al., 2020; Chen et al.,
111            2021; Nguyen et al., 2023) (Sec. 3.1) and propose a novel model inversion framework,
112            termed the diffusion distillation MIAs (DDMI) (Sec. 3.3), which addresses these challenges
113            and lays the foundation for future advancements in generative MIAs.
114          • To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to leverage generative MIAs to explore privacy
115            leakage in CLIP models (Sec. 2.1), expanding the scope of MIAs to large-scale multimodal
116            models and revealing serious privacy vulnerabilities within these models.
117          • Extensive experiments demonstrate that DDMI significantly outperforms SOTA GAN-
118            based MIAs in both white-box and black-box settings (Sec. 4). These results underscore
119            the urgent need for robust defense mechanisms to protect sensitive information in both
120            traditional classification models and complex multimodal models like CLIP.

126   Problem Setup. Let the input space be X ⊂ RdX and the private label space be Ypri = {1, . . . , C}.
127                                        C
      The target model, Mc : X → [0, 1] , is a classifier trained on a private dataset Dpri , sampled from the
128   private data distribution ppri (x, y). In MIAs, for a specific class y ∈ Ypri , the goal is to reconstruct
129   synthetic samples that reveal sensitive information about the private training data for that class,
130   exploiting only access to the target model Mc . In this setting, the adversary can query Mc and has
131   general knowledge of the private data domain, but no direct access to specific details of Dpri .
132   MIAs are typically framed as an optimization problem (Fredrikson et al., 2015), where for a given
133   class y, the objective is to find a sample x that maximizes the likelihood of Mc for y. However,
134   inverting DNNs trained on high-dimensional data, such as facial recognition models, is complex
135   and often ill-posed, which can generate unrealistic features without semantic meaning. To address
136   this challenge, Zhang et al. (2020) proposed using GANs to learn image priors that regularize the
137   inversion process. They introduced GAN-based generative MIAs, consisting of two stages:
138   ⃝1 Learning Image Priors with Generative Models. In this stage, a GAN is employed to learn
139   image priors from public auxiliary datasets Dpub , which share the same data domain as Dpri , but
140   have disjoint label spaces, i.e., Ypub ∩ Ypri = ∅. For brevity, we focus on approaches involving only
141   the GAN model, following the original GAN formulation (Goodfellow et al., 2014).1 Specifically,
142   this process uses the generator’s implicit distribution, pg , to estimate the public auxiliary data dis-
143   tribution, ppub . The generator, G : Z → X , maps input noise z ∈ Z to an generated image xg . The
144   discriminator, D : X → R, outputs the probability that an image x comes from ppub rather than pg .
145   The goal of D is accurately distinguish G(z) from xreal , while the goal of G is to fool D into making
146   mistakes. This is framed as a two-player minimax game:
147
148                min max LGAN (G, D) = Ex∼ppub [log D(x)] + Ez∼p(z) [log (1 − D(G(z))].                    (1)
                    G    D

151   in the well-trained generator’s latent space to obtain an optimal synthetic sample, x∗ = G(z∗ ), that
152   resembles private data for a specific class y. This is formulated as:
153
154                               z∗ = arg min Lid (z; y, Mc , G) + λLprior (z).                             (2)

179
180   Since we employ single-step diffusion-based generators as a superior alternative to GANs for gen-
181   erative MIAs, this section introduces Score identity Distillation (SiD) (Zhou et al., 2024), a state-of-
182   the-art, data-free distillation method. Unlike approaches that require access to training data, SiD re-
183   lies solely on a pretrained diffusion model, making it ideal for MIAs by leveraging readily available
184
      models. SiD distills a student model pθ (xg ) from a pretrained diffusion model, enabling single-step
      sample generation. The generator, denoted as G(·; θ) : Z → X , is a DNN parameterized by θ that
185
      maps noise z ∼ p(z) to the generated data xg . Let pdata (x0 ) represent the real data distribution,
186
      and pθ (xg ) the generated data distribution. Their marginals under the forward diffusion process are
187   semi-implicit distributions (Yin & Zhou, 2018), which are intractable and expressed as follows:
188                              R                                        R

191
      the initial data x0 and σt controlling the noise level added at each time step t.
192   MESM loss. A pretrained diffusion model provides a score network Sϕ , parameterized by ϕ, which
193   estimates the true data score at any time point t of the forward diffusion process as:
194                   ∇xt ln pdata (xt ) ≈ −Sϕ (xt ) := σt−2 (xt − at fϕ (xt , t)) = σt−1 ϵϕ (xt ),

      This section presents our novel model inversion framework, i.e., diffusion distillation MIAs (DDMI).
242   First, we present and discuss the motivation behind our approach (Sec. 3.1). Next, we explain in
243   detail why multi-step diffusion models are unsuitable for generative MIAs (Sec. 3.1). Then, we
244   introduce the general framework of DDMI (Sec. 3.3). Finally, we apply DDMI to two types of ML
245   models: traditional classification models and multimodal CLIP models (Sec. 3.4).
246
247   3.1   M OTIVATION : L IMITATIONS OF GAN- BASED G ENERATIVE MIA S
248
249   While SOTA generative MIAs (Chen et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2023) have
250   demonstrated impressive performance in reconstructing samples that closely resemble private train-
251   ing data, thereby potentially exposing sensitive information, our empirical observations reveal sev-
252   eral key limitations in these methods. The primary issues are the instability of the optimization
253
      process during model inversion (cf. Fig. 1(a)) and the low fidelity of the reconstructed samples (cf.
      Fig. 1(b)). This instability often prevents convergence to a desirable local optimum, resulting in
254
      suboptimal inversion performance. Additionally, the low fidelity of the reconstructed samples limits
255
      their ability to accurately capture fine-grained sensitive features from the private training data.
256
257   We attribute these limitations to inherent flaws in GANs. Specifically, the two-player minimax game
258   outlined in Eq. (1) is notoriously difficult to optimize and prone to collapse without careful selection
259
      of hyperparameters and regularization techniques (Miyato et al., 2018; Brock, 2019). Moreover,
      GANs struggle to capture data diversity as effectively as SOTA likelihood-based models (Razavi
260
      et al., 2019; Song et al., 2021), making them less suitable for generative MIAs. Consequently, gen-
261
      erative models with more stable training procedures and better mode coverage are more appropriate
262   for MIA tasks. To address these issues, we propose exploring generative MIAs based on diffusion
263   models (Ho et al., 2020; Karras et al., 2022), which offer superior generative performance compared
264   to GANs. In particular, diffusion models offer more stable training and better capture data diversity.
265

291   Building on recent advancements in the distillation of diffusion models (Salimans & Ho, 2022; Luo

294   for generative MIAs. This framework, termed diffusion distillation MIAs (DDMI), follows a similar
295   structure to traditional GAN-based generative MIAs, with two primary stages:
296   ⃝
      1 Learning Image Priors with Diffusion Models.
297
      This stage consists of two critical steps:

306

307

351   We compare single-step diffusion model (SDM)-based MIAs (i.e., DDMI) with SOTA GAN-based
352   methods for classifier inversion on real-world facial recognition tasks, specifically targeting low-

354
      suffer from poor visual fidelity in the reconstructions. Our evaluation includes GMI (Zhang et al.,
      2020), LOMMA (Nguyen et al., 2023), and PLG-MI (Yuan et al., 2023) in the white-box setting, as

      well as BREP-MI (Kahla et al., 2022) in the black-box setting. For CLIP inversion, the objective
356
      is to infer and reconstruct facial images in large-scale training data. In this initial exploration of
357   generative CLIP inversion, we use SDM and pretrained StyleGAN (Karras et al., 2020) as image
358   priors, compared to the input space optimization baseline, CLIPInversion (Kazemi et al., 2024).

      4.3   A BLATION S TUDY
512
513
      In this section, we present ablation studies to further investigate DDMI’s performance in both clas-
514
      sifier inversion and CLIP inversion tasks. Further discussions are available in Appx. E.
515
516   Prior loss. We examine the effect of applying or omitting the prior loss on DDMI’s inversion
517   performance. The prior loss is designed to constrain the reconstructed samples within the learned
518
      image manifold. As shown in the left panel of Fig. 4, adding the prior loss increases the KNN
      feature distance. This occurs because the label sets of the public and private datasets do not overlap,
519
      meaning the private data is likely located in low-density regions of the public data distribution. The
520
      prior loss tends to constrain the reconstructed samples to the high-density regions of the public data
521   distribution to improve visual quality, which in turn affects the model inversion performance.
522
523   More detailed templates. The prompts used in CLIP inversion are critical, as detailed and struc-
524
      tured text prompts enable the text encoder to generate more accurate features of the target. These
      text features directly influence the image optimization process. We used a more complex prompt: “A
525
      close-up portrait of <Name> with <hair>, <eye>, <lip>, and <face shape>, emphasizing
526
      their <facial characteristic> and <appearance>.” We averaged the KNN distance for
527   40 celebrities from the FaceScrub dataset, and the results are presented in the right panel of Fig. 4,
528   indicating that more detailed prompts can improve inversion performance for MIAs on CLIP.

531   5     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this research, we identified key limitations in GAN-based generative MIAs and addressed them
534   by proposing a novel framework based on single-step diffusion models, which we term diffusion
535   distillation MIAs (DDMI). Notably, to the best of our knowledge, this is also the first study to
536   investigate generative model inversion attacks on CLIP models, a previously uninvestigated area.
537   Through extensive experimentation, we demonstrate that our approach not only improves traditional
538   performance metrics but also significantly enhances the visual fidelity of reconstructed samples. Our
539   findings further highlight the urgent need for robust and effective privacy-preserving mechanisms
      during model training, particularly for CLIP models, a largely underexplored area.

      A     R ELATED W ORK
757
758
759   Model Inversion Attacks on Classification Models. Zhang et al. (2020) introduced GAN-based
760   generative MIAs, significantly advancing the field. Recent innovations build upon this framework,
761
      with some focusing on GAN training. For instance, Chen et al. (2021) developed an inversion-
      specific GAN, while Yuan et al. (2023) proposed pseudo label-guided MIAs using conditional GANs
762
      to more effectively extract knowledge from public datasets. Other works have focused on improving
763
      optimization techniques, such as Wang et al. (2021a); Struppek et al. (2022); Kahla et al. (2022),
764   which developed advanced inversion loss functions, and Han et al. (2023); An et al. (2022); Kahla
765   et al. (2022), which explored alternative gradient-based strategies for black-box settings.
766
767
      Inference Attacks on CLIP Models. The CLIP model advances multimodal learning by integrat-
      ing image and text encoders into a shared embedding space for semantic similarity (Radford et al.,
768
      2021). Hintersdorf et al. (2024) explored personal membership leakage by querying the model with
769
      both images and text. Ko et al. (2023) used cosine similarity between visual and textual features
770   for membership inference under a weak supervision framework, while Li et al. (2024) extend mem-
771   bership inference attack using only text queries. Kazemi et al. (2024) examined CLIP’s ability to
772   blend concepts and detect potential gender biases. In this paper, In this paper, we leverage generative
773   MIAs to investigate privacy leakage issues of CLIP models.
774
      Distilled Diffusion Models. Diffusion models have received significant attention for generating
775
      high-quality images through iterative noise refinement, though their multi-step process can be com-
776   putationally inefficient. To address this limitation, researchers have explored methods to distill the
777   reverse diffusion chain into more efficient processes. Salimans & Ho (2022) pioneered the concept
778   of progressive distillation, aiming to reduce the number of steps without sacrificing generation qual-
779   ity. Song et al. (2023) introduced consistency models, improving output consistency along the ODE
780   trajectory. Recent advancements (Luo et al., 2024; Yin et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024) have success-
781   fully distilled single-step generators, significantly reducing generation costs while often approaching
782   or even surpassing the performance of the original pretrained diffusion models.

1016   Limitation. One key limitation we found is the much higher dimensionality of the latent space in
1017   diffusion models compared to traditional GANs. In contrast to GANs, where the latent space is
1018   typically low-dimensional, the latent space in diffusion models matches the input space dimensions,
1019   creating challenges in extending DDMI to black-box attack methods like RLB-MI (Han et al., 2023)

1023   optimization within the Markov decision process (MDP). However, when DCGAN is replaced with
1024   a diffusion model, the latent space expands to much higher dimensions (e.g., 64 × 64 × 3), making it
1025   far more challenging for the agent to navigate and optimize within the MDP framework. Similarly,

       What are the potential future research directions?
1063
1064          • Extending DDMI to Black-Box Settings: Addressing the limitation we identified, an
1065            important future research direction would be to extend DDMI to black-box settings. This
1066            would enable the method to work in more restricted environments where access to the
1067            internal parameters of models is limited.
1068          • Inversion-Specific Distillation: A key challenge during diffusion distillation is effectively
1069            utilizing information from the target classifier. Exploring ways to more fully integrate this
1070            information into the distillation process remains an important area of research.
1071          • Generative CLIP Inversion: As the first work to extend generative MIAs to CLIP models,
1072            several open questions remain. A key challenge is the evaluation, where collecting images
1073            of celebrities who are highly represented in the training data of open-source CLIP models,
1074            such as the LAION-400M dataset, is essential for accurately assessing the privacy leakage
1075            risks in CLIP. Moreover, future work should investigate how to more effectively utilize text
1076            data, model structure, and intermediate outputs during the inversion process.
1077          • Robust Privacy-Preserving Mechanisms for CLIP Models: Our findings emphasize the
1078            pressing need for robust and effective privacy-preserving techniques during model train-
1079            ing, especially for CLIP models, which remain largely underexplored in terms of privacy
                vulnerabilities. Addressing this gap would be crucial in developing safer models.

1080
             • Improving Evaluation Metrics for Model Inversion Attacks: We observed that the pri-
1081           mary evaluation metric for MIAs, attack accuracy, does not always accurately reflect in-
1082           version success. Hence, developing more reliable metrics that capture the discriminative
1083           semantic information present in the samples is another important direction for future work.
