011            α, β-CROWN has won the last 4 VNNcomp(etitions), as the DNN verifier with the
012            best trade-off between accuracy and runtime. VNNcomp however is focusing on
013            relatively easy verification instances. In this paper, we consider harder verification
014            instances, on which α, β-Crown displays a large number (20 − 58%) of undecided
015            instances, that is, instances that can neither be verified, nor an explicit attack can
016            be found. Enabling larger time-outs for α, β-Crown only improves verification
017            rate by few percents, leaving a large gap of undecided instances while already
018            taking a considerable amount of time. Resorting to slow complete verifiers, does
019
               not fare better even with very large time-outs: They would theoretically be able to
               close the gap, but with an untractable runtime on all but small hard instances.
020
021
               In this paper, we propose a novel Utility function that selects few neurons to be
               encoded with accurate but costly integer variables in a partial MILP problem. The
022
               novelty resides in the use of the solution of one (efficient LP) solver to accurately
023
               compute a selection ε-optimal for a given input. Compared with previous at-
024            tempts, we can reduce the number of integer variables by around 4 times while
025            maintaining the same level of accuracy. Implemented in Hybrid MILP, calling
026            first α, β-Crown with a short time-out to solve easier instances, and then partial
027            MILP for those for which α, β-Crown fails, produces a very accurate yet efficient
028            verifier, reducing tremendously the number of undecided instances (8 − 15%),
029            while keeping a reasonable runtime (46s − 417s on average per instance).

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Deep neural networks (DNNs for short) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities, achieving
035   human-like or even superior performance across a wide range of tasks. However, their robustness is
036   often compromised by their susceptibility to input perturbations Szegedy et al. (2014). This vulnera-
037   bility has catalyzed the verification community to develop various methodologies, each presenting a
038   unique balance between completeness and computational efficiency Katz et al. (2019; 2017); Singh
039
      et al. (2019b). This surge in innovation has also led to the inception of competitions such as VN-
      NComp Brix et al. (2023b), which aim to systematically evaluate the performance of neural network
040
      verification tools. While the verification engines are generic, the benchmarks usually focus on lo-
041
      cal robustness, i.e. given a DNN, an image and a small neighbourhood around this image, is it the
042   case that all the images in the neighbourhood are classified in the same way. For the past 5 years,
043   VNNcomp has focused on rather easy instances, that can be solved within tens of seconds (the typ-
044   ical hard time-out is 300s). For this reason, DNN verifiers in the past years have mainly focused
045   on optimizing for such easy instances. Among them, NNenum Bak (2021), Marabou Katz et al.
046   (2019); Wu et al. (2024), and PyRAT Durand et al. (2022), respectively 4th, 3rd and 2sd of the last
047   VNNcomp’24 Brix et al. (2024) and 5th, 2sd and 3rd of the VNNcomp’23 Brix et al. (2023a); Mn-
048   BAB Ferrari et al. (2022), 2sd in VNNcomp’22 Müller et al. (2022), built upon ERAN Singh et al.
049   (2019b) and PRIMA Müller et al. (2022); and importantly, α, β-Crown Wang et al. (2021); Xu et al.
050
      (2021), the winner of the last 4 VNNcomp, benefiting from branch-and-bound based methodology
      Zhang et al. (2022); Bunel et al. (2020). We will thus focus in the following mostly on α, β-Crown.

      Our main contributions address the challenges of verifying hard DNNs efficiently:
109
110         1. We designed a novel Utility function to choose few neurons to encode with the exact MILP
111            encoding, while others are treated with the efficient LP relaxation, giving rise to partial
112            MILP (pMILP). Specifically, the novelty of Utility resides in the use of the solution to an
113            (efficient LP) solver on the node z we want to bound. Utility can then precisely evaluate
114            how much accuracy is gained by switching neuron a from LP (solution of the LP call) to the
115
               exact MILP encoding of ReLU (exact computation from the solution, which can be made
               thanks to Proposition 1), with a proved bound on the precision (Proposition 2). Because
116
               pMILP focuses on the improvement (binary - linear), it is much more efficient (≈ 4 times
117
               less integer variables for same accuracy (Table 6)) than previous attempts, which consider
118            the generic sensitivity to this neuron. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first time
119            such a solution of an (LP) call is used to evaluate the contribution of each neuron, including
120            heuristics for BaB, e.g. Bunel et al. (2020); De Palma et al. (2021).
121
            2. We then propose a new verifier, called Hybrid MILP, invoking first α, β-Crown with short
122            time-out to settle the easy instances. On those (hard) instances which are neither certified
123            nor falsified, we call pMILP with few neurons encoded as integer variables. Experimental
124            evaluation reveals that Hybrid MILP achieves a beneficial balance between accuracy and
125            completeness compared to prevailing methods. It reduces the proportion of undecided
126            inputs from 20 − 58% (α, β-Crown with 2000s TO) to 8 − 15%, while taking a reasonable
127            average time per instance (46 − 420s), Table 3. It scales to fairly large networks such as
128            CIFAR-10 CNN-B-Adv Dathathri et al. (2020), with more than 20 000 neurons.

      1.1   R ELATED W ORK
133
134   We compare Hybrid MILP with major verification tools for DNNs to clarify our methodology and
135   its distinction from the existing state-of-the-art. It scales while preserving good accuracy, through
136   targeting a limited number of binary variables, stricking a good balance between exact encoding
137   of a DNN using MILP Tjeng et al. (2019) (too slow) and LP relaxation (too inaccurate). MIP-
138   planet Ehlers (2017b) opts for a different selection of binary variables, and execute one large MILP
139   encoding instead of Hybrid MILP’s many small encodings, which significantly reduce the number
140   of binary variables necessary for each encoding. In Huang et al. (2020), small encodings are also
141
      considered, however with a straightforward choice of binary nodes based on the weight of outgoing
      edges, which need much more integer variables (thus runtime) to reach the same accuracy.
142
143   Hybrid MILP can be seen as a refinement of α, β-Crown Wang et al. (2021), though its refined
144   accurate path is vastly different than the base Branch and Bound technique used in α, β CROWN,
145   BaBSR Bunel et al. (2020) and MN-BaB Ferrari et al. (2022), which call BaB once per output
146   neuron. In the worst case, this involves considering all possible ReLU configurations, though branch
147
      and bound typically circumvents most possibilities. In simple networks, like those trained robustly,
      branch and bound is highly efficient, focusing on branches crucial for verifying the actual property.
148
      However, branch and bound hits a complexity barrier when verifying harder instances, due to an
149
      overwhelming number of branches, as displayed in Table 1. This is not the case of Hybrid MILP,
150   see Table 3, which is much more accurate than α, β-Crown. That shortcoming for hard instances
151   was witnessed in Wang et al. (2021), and a very specific solution using the full MILP encoding for
152   the first few layers of a DNN was drafted, following similar proposal Singh et al. (2019c). The main
153   issue is that it is slow and it cannot scale to DNNs with many neurons, as every neurons are encoded
154   using an integer variable, making it not that accurate for intermediate networks (e.g. 9×100, 9×200,
155   Table 3), and not usable for larger DNNs (6 × 500, CNN-B-Adv), whereas Hybrid MILP does scale.
156   Last, ERAN-DeepPoly Singh et al. (2019b) computes bounds on values very quickly, by abstracting
157   the weight of every node using two functions: an upper function and a lower function. While the
158   upper function is fixed, the lower function offers two choices. It relates to the LP encoding through
159   the following new (to our knowledge) insight: Proposition 1 state that the LP relaxation precisely
160   matches the intersection of these two choices. Consequently, LP is more accurate (but slower) than
161   DeepPoly, and Hybrid MILP is considerably more precise. Regarding PRIMA Müller et al. (2022),
      the approach involves explicitly maintaining dependencies between neurons.

      3     VALUE A BSTRACTION FOR DNN VERIFICATION
199
      In this section, we describe different value (over-)abstractions on z that are used by efficient algo-
200
      rithms to certify robustness around an input x. Over-abstractions of values include all values for z
201
      in the neighbourhood of x, and thus a certificate for safety in the over-abstraction is a proof of safety
202   for the original input x.
203
204
      3.1   T HE B OX A BSTRACTIONS
205
206   The concept of value abstraction involves calculating upper and lower bounds for the values of
207   certain neurons in a Deep Neural Network (DNN) when inputs fall within a specified range. This
208   approach aims to assess the network’s robustness without precisely computing the values for every
209   input within that range.
210   Firstly, it’s important to note that weighted sums represent a linear function, which can be explic-
211   itly expressed with relative ease. However, the ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit) function presents a
212   challenge in terms of accurate representation. Although ReLU is a relatively straightforward piece-
213   wise linear function with two modes (one for x < 0 and another for x ≥ 0), it is not linear. The
214   complexity arises when considering the compounded effects of the ReLU function across the vari-
215   ous layers of a ReLU DNN. It’s worth noting that representing ReLU(x) precisely is feasible when
      x is ”stable”, meaning it’s consistently positive or consistently negative, as there’s only one linear

249   3.2   MILP, LP AND PARTIAL MILP ENCODINGS FOR DNN S
250
251   At the other end of the spectrum, we find the Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) value
252   abstraction, which is a complete (but inefficient) method. Consider an unstable neuron n, whose
253   value x ∈ [LB(n), UB(n)] with LB(n) < 0 < UB(n). The value x̂ of ReLU(x) can be encoded
254
      exactly in an MILP formula with one integer (actually even binary) variable a valued in {0, 1}, using
      constants UB(n), LB(n) with 4 constraints Tjeng et al. (2019):
255
256                x̂ ≥ x    ∧     x̂ ≥ 0,       ∧    x̂ ≤ LB(n) · a     ∧    x̂ ≤ x − UB(n) · (1 − a)        (2)
257
258   For all x ∈ [LB(n), UB(n)] \ 0, there exists a unique solution (a, x̂) that meets these constraints,
259   with x̂ = ReLU(x) Tjeng et al. (2019). The value of a is 0 if x < 0, and 1 if x > 0, and can be either
260
      if x = 0. This encoding approach can be applied to every (unstable) ReLU node, and optimizing
      its value can help getting more accurate bounds. However, for networks with hundreds of unstable
261
      nodes, the resulting MILP formulation will contain numerous integer variables and generally bounds
262
      obtained will not be accurate, even using powerful commercial solvers such as Gurobi.
263
264   MILP instances can be linearly relaxed into LP over-abstraction, where variables originally restricted
265   to integers in {0, 1} (binary) are relaxed to real numbers in the interval [0, 1], while maintaining the
266
      same encoding. As solving LP instances is polynomial time, this optimization is significantly more
      efficient. However, this efficiency comes at the cost of precision, often resulting in less stringent
267
      bounds. This approach is termed the LP abstraction.
268
269   In this paper, we propose to use partial MILP, to get interesting trade-offs between accuracy and
      runtime: for a set of unstable neurons X, we denote by MILPX the MILP encoding where variables

297

298   variable. For X a set of open ReLUs, we denote by MX the MILP model where variables from X

300
301
      Usually, heuristics to choose X are based on evaluating the sensitivity of a neuron z wrt the ReLU
      nodes, that is how much a ReLU value impacts the value of z, and rank the ReLU nodes accordingly.
302
      This is the case of Huang et al. (2020), but also more generally of heuristics for BaB, such as SR
303
      Bunel et al. (2020) and FSB De Palma et al. (2021). Instead, Utility considers the improvement from
304   opening a neuron n, that is the difference for the value of z between considering ReLU(n) exactly
305   or using its LP relaxation LP(n). Indeed, it is not rare that z is sensitive to ReLU node n, and yet
306   LP(n) already provides an accurate approximation of ReLU(n). In this case, usual heuristics would
307   open n, while it would only improve the value of z in a limited way.

      6     C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In this paper, we developed a novel Utility function to select few ReLU nodes to consider with binary
      variables to compute accurately bounds on neurons of DNNs. The novelties are that it focuses on
526
      improvement wrt a given input, rather than on generic sensitivity of a neuron wrt to a ReLU node, and
527
      it uses the solution of one call to an (efficient LP) solver to evaluate this improvement. This makes
528   the choice particularly efficient, necessitating ≈ 4x less integer variables than previous proposals
529   Huang et al. (2020) for the same accuracy. Our empirical studies revealed that this can yield highly
530   accurate results, verifying up to 40% more images than the SOTA (α, β-Crown, winner of the 4
531   last VNNComp), with the same runtime, for DNNs with up to 20 000 neurons. The reason is that
532   α, β-Crown hits a complexity barrier, similarly as other competing solutions, when considering hard
533   (even small) DNNs. This opens a lot of perspectives, among which: verifying efficiently other hard
534   instances; certifying ϵ-robustness of images for ϵ as large as possible; verifying global rather than
535   local properties Wang et al. (2022).
536   Reproducibility Statement: We tested twice outlier results to confirm them, making sure of repro-
537   ducibility on the given hardware. Precise details on the settings used are provided in the appendix.
538   Additional results (e.g. ablation studies) are also provided in the appendix. Tested DNNs as well
539   as MNIST and CIFAR10 DataSet are freely available. The source code of Hybrid MILP will be
      provided on GitHub after acceptance (needing Gurobi as well as α, β-Crown).

666   additional package, namely IBM CPLEX solver, we do not have access to. From Zhang et al.
667   (2022), the number of undecided inputs of GCP-CROWN is ≤ 2% better than α, β-Crown on the
668   DNNs we experimented with, far from the 10 − 40% improvement seen from Hybrid MILP. The
669   conclusion are thus unchanged.

671   S ETTING FOR H YBRID MILP
672
673   Hybird MILP first call α, β-Crown with short time-out (TO), then call partial MILP on those inputs
674   which was neither certified nor falsified by this run of α, β-Crown. We are using two settings of TO,
675   for smaller DNNs we use T0= 10s, and for the two larger ones, we use TO= 30s.
676   The setting for partial MILP for fully-connected DNNs is about how many neurons need to be
677   opened (once set, the selection is automatic). The runtime depending crucially upon the number of
678   open ReLU neurons, we set it quite tightly, only allowing few neuron deviation to accommodate to a
679   particularly accurate/inaccurate bound computation (measure by the weight of the remaining Utility
680   function). As complexity increases with the layer considered, as the size of the MILP model grows,
681   we lower this number with the depth, only committing to an intermediate number for the output
682   neuron (the number of output neurons is smaller than hidden layer, and this is the most important
683   computation). We experimentally set this number so that each computing the bounds in each hidden
684
      layer takes around the same time. Remember that in layer 1, partial MILP is not necessary and
      propagating bounds using interval arithmetic is already exact. We open [48,48] to compute bounds
685
      for hidden layer 2, [21,24] for layer 3, [11,14] for layer 4, [6,9] for layer 5, [3,6] for layer 6, [2,5]
686
      for layer 7, [1,4] for hidden layer 8 (if any), and we open [14,17] for the output layer. The exact
687   number of open nodes in the range [a,a+3] is decided automatically for each neuron being computed
688   : ReLUs are ranked according to their value by Utility, and the a top ReLUs are open. Then, ReLUs
689   ranked a+1,a+2, a+3 are opened if their Utility value is larger than a small threshold. We set the
690   threshold at 0.01. It should be seen as a way to save runtime when Utility knows that the next node
691   by ranking (a+i) will not impact accuracy much (thanks to the upper bound from Proposition 2).

725     B    P SEUDOCODE AND C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
726
727
728     Algorithm 1: pMILP(K)
729     Input: Bounds [αn , βn ] for input nodes n at layer 0 (input neighbourhood)
730     Output: Bounds [αn , βn ] for every node n
731   1 for layer k = 1 · · · ℓ do
732   2     for neuron n in layer k do
733   3         Compute X a set of K nodes with the highest Utility for target neuron n.
734   4         Run MILPX to obtain [αn , βn ] from bounds of neurons in layers < k
735
736
737
       We provide the pseudo code for pMILP in Algorithm 1. pMILP(K) has a worst case complexity
738    bounded by O(N · MILP(N, K)), where N is the number of nodes of the DNN, and MILP(N, K)
739    is the complexity of solving a MILP program with K integer variables and N linear variables. We
740    have MILP(N, K) ≤ 2K LP(N ) where LP(N ) is the Polynomial time to solve a Linear Program
741    with N variables, 2K being an upper bound. Solvers like Gurobi are quite adept and usually do
742    not need to evaluate all 2K ReLU configurations to deduce the bounds. It is worth mentioning that
743    the ”for” loop iterating over neurons n in layer k (line 2) can be executed in parallel, because the
744    computation only depends on bounds from preceding layers, not the current layer k.
745    If K is sufficiently small, this approach is expected to be efficient. The crucial part is thus to find few
746    neurons which are particularly important when computing neuron n. This is where our novel Utility
747    function outperforms previous solutions, by using an call to an (LP) solver to obtain a solution,
748    which is novel as far as we know. This solutions allows to optimizing the choice of open nodes for
749    the particular input considers, and appears significantly better than previous attempts, explaining the
750    efficiency of the method.
751    For comparison, refined β-Crown and refined Prima used all the nodes up to a certain layer as binary
752    variables, which is particularly inefficient, see Table 8 and Fig. 7. Hence, it can only be applied to
753    small DNNs (it is implemented only up to MNNIST 8x200), while pMILP scales to at least 20.000
754    neurons (CNN-B-Adv). Huang et al. (2020) is the closest to pMILP, also implementing a choice of
755    nodes as binary variables. However, their choice is particularly inefficient, as revealed by Table 6,
       needing 4 times the number of open nodes for the same accuracy as our Utility function.
