010                                              A BSTRACT
011
012            In the fields of applied mathematics, statistics, and machine learning, particularly
013            deep learning, manifold optimization assumes a prominent role. By leveraging
014
               the intrinsic geometric properties of manifolds, the endeavor to solve constrained
               optimization problems can be equivalently transformed into the pursuit of un-
015
               constrained optimization problems over manifold structures. However, manifold
016
               optimization is slow to converge and unstable. To address these issues, an innova-
017            tive method is introduced, named Reset method that combines the other methods
018            (SGD,Adam and AdamW), aiming to enhance both the improvement of precision
019            and the reduction of convergence loss. Subsequently, our approaches have yielded
020            notable outcomes in terms of the improvement of precision and enhanced stability
021            of the model. The efficacy of our proposed methods is corroborated by the results
022            of deep learning experiments, which provide compelling evidence in support of
023            our initial hypothesis.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Fast and stable optimization methods have long been pursued by numerous researchers across different
028
      generations. Stochastic gradient-based optimization methods, exemplified by stochastic gradient
029
      descent (SGD), have achieved notable success in various domains. However, their convergence rate
030   remains relatively slow. In recent years, there has been a proliferation of innovative approaches
031   aimed at accelerating optimization through the application of adaptive learning rates. Notably, Adam
032   Kingma & Ba (2014) and AdamW Loshchilov & Hutter (2017) have gained widespread adoption due
033   to their ability to facilitate rapid convergence.
034
      Over the past few years, research into the manifold optimization has started to gain increasing
035
      attention. Several methods for manifold optimization have been proposed by leading scientists, such
036   as trust domain method Absil et al. (2008), adaptively regularized Newton’s method Wu et al. (2017)
037   , quasi-Newton-type method Huang et al. (2015), Broyden–Fletcher–Goldfarb–Shanno (BFGS)
038   method Huang et al. (2018) and Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG ) method Zhang et al.
039   (2016), and have established plenty of performance evaluation and analysis methods. In addition,
040   some scholars have provided vast open source software packages, such as the Convex Optimization
041   Simulation System CVX, the First-order Conic Curve Solver Paradigm TFOCS software, etc. There
042   are numerous problems that are large in size themselves, but the data itself may fall on a low-
043   dimensional manifold in a high-dimensional space, and thus can be transformed into an optimization
044
      problem on the manifold. Manifold optimization has been the subject of considerable attention and
      has been employed extensively in a number of fields, such as Douik & Hassibi (2022) and Hu et al.
045
      (2020), including computational mathematics, applied mathematics, statistics and machine learning.
046
      Of these, particular emphasis has been placed on the fields of deep learning.
047
048   However, by leveraging the inherent geometric structure of the manifold, a significant constrained
049   optimization problems, such as Ergen et al. (2022) and Ergen & Pilanci (2021), can be transformed into
050
      unconstrained optimization Hu et al. (2020) problems on the manifold itself. Extensive investigations
      have been conducted to explore the algebraic and topological structure, optimization conditions, and
051
      numerical analysis associated with manifold optimization Hu et al. (2020).
052
053   Building upon the existing theoretical foundation and analysing the advantages and disadvantages of
      the above optimization methods, this paper incorporates principles from machine learning, particularly

094
      While optimization methods in Euclidean space have been well-established, manifold optimization
      Hu et al. (2020) methods are different because not all the properties that hold in Euclidean space
095
      can be directly applied to Riemannian manifolds. Therefore, it becomes necessary to redefine some
096
      properties to suit the specific characteristics of the manifold.
097
098   In this section, motivated by the restart technique introduced by O’donoghue & Candes (2015) and a
099   novel Reset method is proposed, i.e., the manifold optimization methods are ran for a few iterations
100
      and then reset it with other methods (SGD, Adam Kingma & Ba (2014) and AdamW Loshchilov &
      Hutter (2017)). Our methods in algorithm 1 are described, and the convergences of our methods are
101
      proved in theorem 4.1. In addition to its practicality, the advantage of our Reset method is that our
102
      methods can improve the convergence trajectory and increase the stability of the model.

185   Adaptive Moment Estimation (Adam) Adaptive Moment Estimation (Adam) Kingma & Ba
186   (2014) stands out as an efficient optimization algorithm. It aims to adjust the learning rate of each
187   parameter. The Adam Kingma & Ba (2014) optimizer works by dynamically adjusting the step size,
188   which is larger in simpler regions and smaller in more complex regions, to ensure that the minimum
189   point, which represents the minimum loss in machine learning, is reached more efficiently and faster.
190   By adaptively adjusting the step size, the Adam Kingma & Ba (2014) optimizer is better able to adapt
191   to gradient variations in different parameters and thus converge to the optimal solution faster. This
192   strategy of dynamically adjusting the learning rate makes Adam Kingma & Ba (2014) more robust

1047   D.1    DATASETS
1048
1049   The CIFAR-100 Krizhevsky et al. (2009) dataset has 100 classes containing 600 images each, and
1050   each of size is 32 × 32. There are 500 training images and 100 testing images per class. The 100
1051   classes in the CIFAR-100 Krizhevsky et al. (2009) dataset are grouped into 20 superclasses. Each
1052
       image comes with a "fine" label (the class to which it belongs) and a "coarse" label (the superclass to
       which it belongs).
1053
1054   In the STL-10 dataset, there are 500 training images and 800 test images for each category. The
1055   unlabelled dataset comprises 100,000 unlabelled images, including animals and vehicles in categories
1056   other than the 10 categories. All images are sourced from the ImageNet.
1057   The SVHN (Street View House Numbers) dataset is a real-world dataset for numerical recognition of
1058   street house numbers, which contains two formats: full numbers and cropped digit. The Cropped
1059   Digit format is a color image cropped to 32 × 32, the training set contains 73257 images, the test set
1060   contains 26,032 images, and there is an extra training set containing 531131 images. an extra training
1061   set containing 531131 images. SVHN dataset is door number digits extracted from Google Street
1062   View images for developing machine learning and object recognition algorithms.
1063   The CIFAR-10 Krizhevsky et al. (2009) dataset contains 10 RGB colour image categories, each of
1064   size 32 × 32, with 6,000 images in each category, and 5,000 training images and 10,000 test images
1065   in the dataset.
1066
       The Market-1501 Zheng et al. (2015) dataset is collected from Tsinghua University campus, including
1067
       1501 persons, 32668 detected person rectangular frames. This dataset has 12,936 training images
1068   and 19,732 test images. The catalogue structure of the Market-1501 Zheng et al. (2015) dataset
1069   consists of a training set, a test set, and a query set, in which the training set contains 751 images of
1070   pedestrians, the test set contains 750 images of persons, and the query set contains 3,368 manually
1071   drawn pedestrian detection rectangles, the test set contains 750 person images, and the query set
1072   contains 3368 manually drawn rectangular box images for person detection. This dataset is collected
1073   to evaluate the performance of the person re-identification algorithm.
1074   The DukeMTMC-reID Zheng et al. (2017) dataset is a large-scale person re-identification image
1075   dataset, which is collected by Duke University specifically for person re-identification (ReID)
1076   research. The dataset consists of 16,522 training images, 2,228 query images and 17,661 gallery
1077   images involving 702 persons. In addition, the DukeMTMC-reID Zheng et al. (2017) dataset
1078   provides manually labelled bounding boxes, which are useful for training and testing person detection
1079   algorithms. Its wide application and recognition proves its significant value and influence in the field
       of person re-ID.
