009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            With the increasing ubiquity of large language models it has become crucial to
012            ensure guarantees for models trained to be aligned with human values to avoid
013            leaking information on the human judgements that have been provided to the algo-
014            rithm. To target this issue we focus on the problem of alignment via reinforcement
015            learning from human preference rankings, subject to the constraint of not revealing
016
               any information on the human data used to align the model. To achieve this, we
               analyze (ϵ, δ)-DP for both the Bradley-Terry-Luce (BTL) model and the Plackett-
017
               Luce (PL) model. We introduce a theoretically founded algorithm for learning
018
               rewards from human rankings that achieves this objective without leaking the hu-
019            man rankings. We further demonstrate that the privately learned rewards can be
020            used to train policies achieving statistical performance guarantees that asymptot-
021            ically match the best known algorithms in the non-private setting, which are in
022            some cases minimax optimal. Strikingly, our analysis and our results reveal that it
023            is possible to obtain the same model performance without any trade-off on the pro-
024            tection of the human judgments, and our paper provides the first algorithms that
025            can achieve provable privacy of human judgements, while still producing aligned
026            models with optimal performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      With the rise of large pretrained machine learning models that flexibly interact with humans, there is
031   an increasing need to ensure that the models do not exhibit harmful behaviour or ethical violations
032   that can cause unsafe circumstances for humans. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
033   (RLHF) is currently the standard method targeting this problem (OpenAI, 2023; Google Gemini,
034   2023), and has achieved significant success introducing several behavioral skills to language models
035   (i.e. probability distributions over sequences of tokens (Shannon, 1948)) from refusing to act on
036   improper requests to simply interacting with humans by responding to questions (Ziegler et al.,
037   2019; Wu et al., 2021; Nakano et al., 2021; Stiennon et al., 2020; Abramson et al., 2022; Glaese
038   et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Ganguli et al., 2022; Menick et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022; Gao
039
      et al., 2023; Ramamurthy et al., 2023). Yet there are still problems with large language models
      where recent work demonstrates the unethical behaviour that they can exhibit when they interact
040
      with humans (Ganguli et al., 2022; Perez et al., 2022).
041
042   While improving the safety and harmlessness of LLMs remains an active area of research, the use
043   of RLHF introduces an orthogonal set of problems relating to human interactions. In particular, the
044   input data used for RLHF training is human ratings of model responses to prompts. Furthermore,
045
      current language models record data when interacting with humans via chat interfaces, and this data
      can be used for future training (OpenAI, 2023). As a result, there are numerous reasons to worry
046
      about privacy when building a reward function from human-feedback, a few of which we now enu-
047
      merate. First, even when human raters are paid, they may be giving preference ratings that need
048   to be kept private. Honest preference ratings on sensitive topics can be very revealing of private
049   information e.g. political preference, gender identity etc. In some jurisdictions there is a legal man-
050   date for an employer of paid raters to avoid leaking such private information. Moreover, orthogonal
051   to this, several other issues would arise for an LLM trained to give basic medical advice based on
052   responses from raters who have real medical issues, in which case the preference data clearly must
053   be kept private. Furthermore, a glance at the terms of use of the major LLM providers indicates
      that user feedback on LLM outputs is being stored, with the possibility of later training on this data.

054
      Thus, the current paradigm of only using paid raters may change in the foreseeable future. From
055   another perspective, human preference data on specialized topics, e.g. legal or scientific questions,
056   may be very expensive to obtain, and thus could be viewed as trade secrets that must be kept private.
057   In this case, privacy is economically incentivized for providers of LLMs. Furthermore, one of the
058   major LLM providers has issued a statement indicating that maintaining privacy is a core principle
059   of responsible AI development DeepMind (2023). Finally, the ability to violate privacy with only ac-
060   cess to anonymous preference rankings was conclusively demonstrated in Narayanan and Shmatikov
061   (2008), for the case of the Netflix prize dataset consisting of anonymous movie ratings by Netflix
062   users. This paper shows that it is possible to deanonymize a target user with just a small amount of
063
      publicly available information about the target, and then subsequently to learn potentially sensitive
      information about the user e.g. political preferences. Therefore, it seems quite plausible that access
064
      to an LLM fine-tuned on human preference data, combined with the above well-established methods
065
      for deanonymization from preference data alone, can lead directly to privacy violations.
066
067   Thus, as large language models continue to scale to interact with millions of people in more complex
068   ways, the necessity of maintaining the privacy of individual interactions becomes even more signif-
069
      icant. To mitigate the privacy risks associated with machine learning, the framework of differential
      privacy is the primary approach to the design of algorithms with rigorous privacy guarantees (Dwork
070
      et al., 2006; Dwork and Roth, 2014).
071
072   The standard approach to RLHF starts with a pretrained language model and fixed dataset of
073   prompts. A prompt is sampled from the dataset, and K outputs from the language model are sam-
074   pled conditioned on the prompt. A human rater then gives a preference ranking of the K outputs.
075
      This process is repeated until a dataset D containing n human preference rankings over model re-
      sponses is collected. Following this a reward model rθ is trained to match the human preference
076
      rankings in D. Finally, the original pretrained language model is further trained via reinforcement
077
      learning to maximize the learned rewards rθ . Both the human ratings and prompts in the dataset D
078   are generated by humans interacting with the model, and thus may contain information that should
079   be kept private even when the final trained model is released to the public.
080
081
      Recent work of Zhu et al. (2023) studies the sample complexity of RLHF, and gives an algorithm
      achieving minimax optimal rates for RLHF in the setting where rewards are linearly parametrized
082
      in some feature space. In this paper we will prove that, in the same setting, it is possible to achieve
083
      minimax optimal sample complexity and differential privacy simultaneously. In particular, our dif-
084   ferential privacy guarantees imply that even if n − 1 of the human ratings in the dataset are revealed,
085   it will be statistically infeasible to learn the private information of the one remaining rating, when
086   given access to the final trained model.

      2.1   D IFFERENTIAL P RIVACY
217
218   Our results are stated in terms of the rigorous notion of differential privacy. Let D be a dataset
219   containing n items. In our case each item is a tuple (s, a1 , . . . , aK , σ) representing human preference
220   rankings. For another dataset D′ we use the notation ∥D − D′ ∥1 = 1 to indicate that D and D′ differ
221   in exactly one item, and are otherwise identical. The formal definition of differential privacy is then,
222   Definition 2.2. ((ϵ, δ)-differential privacy (Dwork and Roth, 2014)) A randomized algorithm A is
223   (ϵ, δ)-differentially private if for all O ⊆ Range(A) such that ∥D − D′ ∥1 ≤ 1:
224                                     P[A(D) ∈ O] ≤ eϵ P[A(D′ ) ∈ O] + δ                                    (1)
225
      where the probability space is over the coin flips of the mechanism A. When δ = 0, we say that A
226   satisfies ϵ-differential privacy.
227
228   Intuitively, differential privacy ensures that if one of the items in D contains private data for some
229   person, even if all the other items in D are revealed, the output of the algorithm A leaks a negligible
230   amount of information about the user. In particular, the distribution of the output is approximately
231   equal to what it would be if that user’s item were not present at all.

233   3     R ELATED W ORK
234
235   Learning from Ranking in Bandits and Reinforcement Learning: The most closely related work
236   is the paper of Zhu et al. (2023), which recently gave minimax optimal bounds for the suboptimality
237   of policies trained via RLHF when the rewards are assumed to be linearly parametrized. We con-
238   sider the same setting in our paper, but additionally achieve differential privacy for RLHF, while
239   asymptotically maintaining the same bounds on the suboptimality of the learned policy.
240   Privacy in Bandits and Reinforcement Learning: Differential privacy has been explored in linear
241   contextual bandits (Shariff and Sheffet, 2018; Neel and Roth, 2018; Huang et al., 2023), in stochastic
242   bandits with a central trust model1 (Mishra and Thakurta, 2015; Tossou and Dimitrakakis, 2016;
243   Sajed and Sheffet, 2019; Azize and Basu, 2022; Charisopoulos et al., 2023), with the local model
244   of trust (Kasiviswanathan et al., 2011; Tenenbaum et al., 2021; Chowdhury and Zhou, 2023), in
245   adversarial bandits (Tossou and Dimitrakakis, 2017), and in tabular MDPs Vietri et al. (2020). Wang
246
      and Hegde (2019) uses reproducing kernel Hilbert space norm-bounded noise to ensure private value
      function approximation with respect to the number of states queried. The notion of joint differential
247
      privacy in tabular MDPs was later extended to the linear MDP setting where the transitions and the
248
      reward functions parameterized by linear functions (Luyo et al., 2021; Ngo et al., 2022). Garcelon
249   et al. (2021) provides a lower bound for regret minimization in finite-horizon MDPs with local
250   differential privacy (LDP) guarantees. However, in all of the aforementioned settings, the rewards
251   are assumed to be part of the private input, and do not need to be learned from data as is necessary
252   in the setting we consider.

513   6   C ONCLUSION AND O PEN P ROBLEMS
514
515   We have shown that it is possible to perform reinforcement learning from human feedback with
516   minimax optimal rates and differential privacy when rewards are linearly parametrized. The setting
517   of linear parametrization in a fixed feature space is often used as a theoretical model in order to give
518   qualitative insight into real-world machine learning algorithms. We view our results as qualitatively
519   suggesting that it may be possible to simultaneously align large language models using RLHF while
520
      simultaneously protecting the privacy of the humans whose preference rankings are used during
      training. The ability to provide rigorous privacy guarantees can provably prevent the types of leaks
521
      of personal data described in The New York Times (2024), where a personal email address was
522
      leaked by a popular chat bot built on a large language model. The problem of privacy leaks due to
523   LLMs is likely to only grow more serious as these models are utilized more widely, and differential
524   privacy can be an important part of the solution.
525
526
      A natural avenue for future work is to see if these theoretical results can be extended beyond linear
      parameterization. For instance, it would be interesting to study the setting where the rewards r lie in
527
      a general PAC-learnable function class, and then attempt to achieve statistical efficiency alongside
528
      differential privacy in such a setting.
