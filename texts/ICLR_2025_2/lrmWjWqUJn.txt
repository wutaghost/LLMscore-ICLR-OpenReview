009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Matrix factorization is a fundamental method in statistics and machine learning
012            for inferring and summarizing structure in multivariate data. Modern data sets of-
013            ten come with “side information” of various forms (images, text, graphs) that can
014            be leveraged to improve estimation of the underlying structure. However, existing
015            methods that leverage side information are limited in the types of data they can
016            incorporate, and they assume specific parametric models. Here, we introduce a
017            novel method for this problem, covariate-moderated empirical Bayes matrix fac-
018
               torization (cEBMF). cEBMF is a modular framework that accepts any type of
               side information that is processable by a probabilistic model or neural network.
019
               The cEBMF framework can accommodate different assumptions and constraints
020
               on the factors through the use of different priors, and it adapts these priors to the
021            data. We demonstrate the benefits of cEBMF in simulations and in analyses of
022            spatial transcriptomics and MovieLens data.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Matrix factorization methods, which include principal component analysis (PCA), factor analysis,
028   and non-negative matrix factorization (NMF) (Lee and Seung, 1999), are very widely used methods
029   for inferring latent structure from data, performing exploratory data analyses and visualizing large
030   data sets (e.g., Alexander et al. 2023; Novembre and Stephens 2008; Sainburg et al. 2020). Matrix
031   factorization methods are also instrumental in other statistical analyses such as adjusting for unob-
032   served confounding (Leek and Storey, 2007). Recent innovations in matrix factorization methods
033
      include the development of sparse matrix factorizations such as sparse PCA (Zou et al., 2006) and
      sparse factor analysis (Engelhardt and Stephens, 2010; Lan et al., 2014). In these works, sparsity is
034
      generally induced through a penalty or a prior distribution; however, as in many penalized regression
035
      problems (e.g., Tibshirani 1996), selecting the “right” amount of regularization, or the “best” prior,
036   is an unresolved question, or may be reliant on cross-validation techniques that are inconvenient and
037   computationally burdensome
038
039
      When factorizing a matrix, say Z, the matrix may be accompanied with additional row or column
      data—“side information”—that may be able to “guide” the matrix factorization algorithm toward
040
      a more accurate or interpretable factorization. A recent prominent example of this in genomics
041
      research is spatial transcriptomics data (Marx, 2021), which is expression profiled in many genes at
042   many spatial locations (“pixels”) (Vandereyken et al., 2023). For a variety of reasons, one typically
043   seeks to factorize Z, which is the matrix of gene expression profiles, but the 2-d coordinates of the
044   pixels also provide important information about the biological context of the cells; for example, we
045   might expect nearby pixels to belong to the same cell type or tissue region. Therefore, “spatially
046   aware” matrix factorization methods have recently been proposed for spatial transcriptomics data
047   (Shang and Zhou, 2022; Townes and Engelhardt, 2023; Velten et al., 2022).
048   In this paper, we describe a novel matrix factorization framework that allows high-dimensional row
049   and column data to guide the factorizations without having to make specific assumptions about
050   how these data inform the factorization. For example, although our framework can be applied to
051   data that exhibit spatial properties, it does not assume or require that the data be spatial. (Further,
052   there are sometimes benefits to not making strong assumptions about the spatial organization of the
053   data even when we know the data are spatial.) Our framework is also flexible in that it includes
      many existing approaches, including unconstrained matrix factorization (Wang and Stephens, 2021;

      2          R ELATED WORK
096
097
098
      The literature on matrix factorization methods that incorporate side information is quite extensive.
      The different methods make different modeling assumptions, and are typically motivated by certain
099
      types of data. Although it is infeasible to review all relevant literature here, we mention a few of the
100
      more important methods, and highlight the methods that are most closely related to cEBMF.
101
102   Several variants of the topic model—which can be viewed as a matrix factorizations with “sum-to-
103   one” constraints on L and F (Carbonetto et al., 2021)—incorporate side information in different
104
      ways; for example, the correlated topic model (Lafferty and Blei, 2005) and the structural topic
      model (Roberts et al., 2016) incorporate document-level side information into the priors on L.
105
106   Collective matrix factorization (CMF) (Bouchard et al., 2013; Lee and Choi, 2009; Singh and Gor-
107   don, 2008) has gained considerable interest, but is based on ideas that are quite different from
      cEBMF: like cEBMF, CMF assumes that the side information is the form of a matrix; but unlike


                                                                                    2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      cEBMF, CMF assumes that the side information factorizes in a similar way to Z. Clearly, the CMF
109   assumpion will not make sense for some applications.
110
111   Another prominent theme in matrix factorization with side information is incorporating group-level
112
      or categorical side information (including ontologies). Among the several methods in this area
      include CTPF Gopalan et al. (2014) and the method of Hu et al. (2016).
113
114   The method most closely related to cEBMF is MFAI (Wang et al., 2024) (see also Porteous et al.
115   2010 for related ideas). MFAI is in fact a special case of cEBMF in which the priors on F are
116   normal and the prior means are informed by the covariates. Similar to cEBMF, MFAI allows these
117   priors to be adapted separately for each factor k. However, it is not nearly as general as cEBMF;
118
      it implements only a single model, a single prior family of a specific parametric form, a specific
      procedure for fitting these priors (using gradient boosted tree methods; Friedman 2001), and it only
119
      accommodates row-wise side information.
120
121   For spatial transcriptomics data (which we consider in Sec. 4.3), Spatial PCA (Shang and Zhou,
122   2022) models the spatial similarity among the rows of Z using Gaussian process priors. (Spatial
123   PCA is closely related to GP-LVM; Lawrence and Moore 2007, see also Zhou et al. 2012.) An NMF
124
      version of this approach generates “parts-based representations” guided by the spatial context of
      the data points (Townes and Engelhardt, 2023). IRIS (Ma and Zhou, 2024), regularizes the factors
125
      through a penalty function that encodes the spatial information in a graph (see also Cai et al. 2011).

130   3.1   BACKGROUND : E MPIRICAL BAYES MATRIX FACTORIZATION
131
      Empirical Bayes matrix factorization (EBMF) (Wang and Stephens, 2021; Willwersheid, 2021) is a
132
      flexible framework for matrix factorization: it approximates a matrix Z ∈ Rn×p as the product of
133   two low-rank matrices,
134                                               Z ≈ LFT ,                                        (1)
135   where L ∈ R  n×K
                       ,F∈R    p×K
                                   , and K ≥ 1. (Typically, K ≪ n, p.) EBMF assumes a normal model
136   of the data,
                                                                     −1
137                                  Z = LFT + E, eij ∼ N (0, τij       ),                         (2)
138
      in which N (µ, σ 2 ) denotes the normal distribution with mean µ and variance σ 2 , and the residual
139              −1
      variances τij  may vary by row (i) or column (j) or both. (EBMF, and by extention cEBMF, also
140
      allows Z to contain missing values, which is important for some applications of matrix factorization,
141   including collaborative filtering; see Sec. 4.2.) EBMF assumes prior distributions for elements of L
142   and F, which are themselves estimated among prespecified prior families Gℓ,k and Gf,k :
143                                       (ℓ)     (ℓ)
144                               ℓik ∼ gk ,    gk ∈ Gℓ,k ,    k = 1, . . . , K
                                         (f )     (f )

145                               fjk ∼ gk ,     gk ∈ Gf,k ,    k = 1, . . . , K.
146
147   The flexibility of EBMF comes from the wide range of different possible prior families (including
148   non-parametric families). Different choices of prior family correspond to different existing matrix
149   factorization methods. For example, if all families Gℓ,k and Gℓ,k are the family of zero-mean nor-
150   mal priors, then LFT is similar to a truncated singular value decomposition (SVD) (Nakajima and
151   Sugiyama, 2011). When the prior families are all point-normal (mixtures of a point mass at zero
152
      and a zero-centered normal), one obtains empirical Bayes versions of sparse SVD or sparse factor
      analysis (Engelhardt and Stephens, 2010; Witten et al., 2009; Yang et al., 2014). The prior families
153
      can also constrain L and F; for example, families that only contain distributions with non-negative
154
      support result in empirical Bayes versions of non-negative matrix factorization (NMF) (Gillis, 2021;
155   Lee and Seung, 1999). In summary, EBMF (2–3) is a highly flexible modeling framework for matrix
156   factorization that includes important previous methods as special cases, but also many new combi-
157   nations (e.g., Liu et al. 2023).

160
161   In covariate-moderated EBMF (cEBMF), we assume that we have some “side information” (covari-
      ates) for rows and/or columns of Z (Adams et al., 2010; Virshup et al., 2024). Let xi denote the

163   column of Z. In principle, xi and yj can be any information processable by a neural net (text,
164   graph, image, or other structured data), but for simplicity we assume that this information is stored
165   in matrix form. Let X ∈ Rn×pX be a matrix containing information on the rows of Z, with xi cor-
166   responding to the i-th row of X (e.g., xi might contain the 2-d coordinate of the cell i). Similarly,
167   let Y ∈ Rp×pY contain information on the columns of Z, with yj corresponding to the j-th row of
168   Y. In cEBMF, we incorporate this side information into the model through parameterized priors:
169                                     (ℓ)         (ℓ)

      is a probability distribution within the family Gf,k parameterized by yi .
174
175   A key limitation of many existing approaches is that they integrate the side information using re-
176   strictive parametric models. Additionally, the chosen priors may make strong or perhaps unrealistic
177   assumptions about the structure underlying the data; for example, Gaussian process priors, which
178
      have been used in this setting (e.g., Lawrence 2005; Lawrence and Moore 2007; Shang and Zhou
      2022), typically assume that the factors vary smoothly in space, which makes it difficult to accurately
179
      capture sharp changes at boundaries (Hyoung-Moon Kim and Holmes, 2005). Existing methods also
180
      usually rely on hyperparameters that need to be tuned or selected (e.g., using cross-validation).
181
182   To address these issues, we propose cEBMF, a method that:
183
        1. Can leverage a large variety of models (e.g., multinomial regression, multilayer perceptron,
184
           graphical neural nets, convolutional neural nets) to integrate the side information into the prior.
185
186
        2. Can use families of priors that are flexible in form and thus do not make strong assumptions.
187     3. Allows automatic selection of the hyperparameters in (3) using an empirical Bayes approach.
188

193   3.2.1   A N ILLUSTRATION : C EBMF WITH SIDE INFORMATION ON FACTOR SPARSITY
194
      Here we illustrate the implementation of the cEBMF framework using a prior family that is simple
195   yet broadly applicable. This prior family assumes that the covariates X, Y only inform the pattern
196   of sparsity—that is, the placement of zeros—in L, F. This type of prior is of particular interest in
197   the matrix factorization setting because matrix factorizations typically have the hidden complication
198   that they are invariant to rescaling; therefore, priors that inform the magnitude of ℓik , fjk are difficult
199   to design. (By “invariant to rescaling,” we mean that the likelihood or objective does not change if
200   we replace LFT by L̃F̃T , where L̃ = LD, F̃ = FD−1 , and D is an invertible diagonal matrix.)
201

206   (5) is the family of “spike-and-slab” priors (Mitchell and Beauchamp, 1988), and cEBMF with
207   Gℓ,k = Gss , Gf,k = Gss implements a version of sparse factor analysis (Engelhardt and Stephens,

209
      covariates. (The “ss” subscript in Gss is short for “spike-and-slab.”) Alternatively, if g1 is a distri-

      3.3     T HE C EBMF LEARNING ALGORITHM
217
218   A key feature of the cEBMF modeling framework is that the algorithm for fitting the priors and
219   estimating the factors is simple to describe and often straightforward to implement. In brief, the
220   cEBMF learning algorithm reduces to a series of covariate-moderated empirical Bayes normal
221   means (cEBNM) problems (Willwerscheid et al., 2024) so that any methods that solves the cEBNM
222   problem can be “plugged in” to the generic cEBMF algorithm. Thus, the cEBMF framework natu-
223   rally lends itself to modular algorithm design and software implementation.
224

319   4.1     S IMULATIONS
320
321   First we compared cEBMF with other matrix factorization methods in simulated data sets. We com-
322   pared cEBMF to two methods that do not use the side information—EBMF (flashier R package;
323   Wang and Stephens 2021) and the penalized matrix decomposition, “PMD” (PMA R package; Wit-
      ten et al. 2009)—and three methods that use side information: MFAI (mfair R package; Wang et al.


                                                                6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      2024), collective matrix factorization, “CMF” (cfmrec R package; Singh and Gordon 2008), and
325   Spatial PCA (SpatialPCA R package; Shang and Zhou 2022). Spatial PCA accepts only a specific
326   type of side information, the 2-d coordinates of the data points. For all methods, we set the rank
327   of the matrix factorization to the K that was used to simulate the data. The penalty parameters in
328   PMD were tuned via cross-validation as recommended by the authors. For EBMF and cEBMF, we
329   assumed homogeneous noise, τij = τ , and we chose prior families appropriate for each simulation
330   setting (except for the “mis-specified prior” simulation; see below). These prior families were all
331   slightly more flexible versions of the “spike and slab” priors in Sec. 3.2.1. The parameterized priors
332   in cEBMF were either single-layer neural networks with softmax activation function (multinomial

      Sparsity-driven covariate. This simulation was intended to illustrate the behaviour of cEBMF
337   when provided with simple row- and column-covariates that inform only the sparsity of L and F
338   (and not the magnitudes of their elements). The side information was stored in 1,000 × 10 and
339   200 × 10 matrices X and Y, and the 1,000 × 200 matrix Z was simulated using a simple cEBMF
340   model with K = 2, τij = 0.25, and with spike-and-slab priors chosen to ensure that 90% of the
341   elements of the “denoised” matrix, LFT , were zero. For EBMF, we assigned scale mixtures of
342   normals with a fixed grid of scales (Stephens, 2017) as the prior family to all rows and columns of L
343   and F. For cEBMF, we used a prior family of the same form, except that the weights in the mixture

344   were determined by covariates, g(di , θ) = π0 (di , θ)δ0 + m=1 πm (di , θ)N (0, σm      ).

      Uninformative covariate. To verify that cEBMF was robust to situations in which the side infor-
346   mation was not helpful, we considered an “uninformative covariate” setting in which the covariates
347   were just noise. The data sets were simulated in the same way as the sparsity-driven covariate sim-
348   ulation, except that τij = 1 and the true factors were simulated as ℓik ∼ (1 − πℓ )δ0 + πℓ N (0, 1),
349   fjk ∼ (1−πf )δ0 +πf N (0, 1), with the weights πℓ , πf chosen to achieve a target sparsity of 90% ze-
350   ros in the “denoised” matrix. EBMF and cEBMF were run in the same way as in the sparsity-driven
351   covariate simulations.
352   Tiled-clustering model. In this setting, we simulated matrix factorizations in which L (but not
353   F) depended on the 2-d locations of the data points. Specifically, we generated a periodic tiling of
354   [0, 1]×[0, 1], randomly labeling each tile 1, 2 or 3. (One of these simulations is shown in Fig. 1.) For
355   each data point i, we set ℓik = 1 if i was in the tile with label k, otherwise ℓik = 0. The F matrix,
                                                                                            PM             2
356   by contrast, was simulated from a simple scale mixture of normals, fjk ∼ π0 δ0 + m=1 N (0, σm          ).
357   We simulated homoskedastic noise with τij = 0.1. Notice that L in this simulation was always
358   non-negative. Therefore, we chose the prior families in EBMF to produce semi-non-negative matrix
359   factorizations (Ding et al., 2010); specifically, point-exponential priors for L—flexible priors similar
360   to the spike-and-slab prior (Sec. 3.2.1) but with support for non-negative numbers only (Willwer-
361   scheid et al., 2024)—and scale mixtures of normal priors, the same as above, for F. Analogously,
362
      the prior families for columns of L in cEBMF were scale mixtures of exponentials modulated by the
                                                  PM

      tial distribution with scale parameter λ, and λm−1 < λm , m = 2, . . . , M . The prior families for F
      were the same as in EBMF.

476   4.3      S PATIAL TRANSCRIPTOMICS
477
478   Although cEBMF was not specifically designed with spatial data in mind, here we show that cEBMF
479   also yields compelling results from spatial transcriptomics data (Marx, 2021). We illustrate this
480   using a data set (Pardo et al., 2022) that has been annotated by domain experts (Maynard et al.,
481   2021) and has been used in several papers to benchmark methods for spatial transcriptomics (e.g.,
482
      Shang and Zhou 2022; Zhao et al. 2021; Zhu et al. 2023). The data were collected from 12 slices
      of the human dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) tissue. After data preprocessing, each slice
483
      contained about 4,000 pixels and expression measured in about 5,000 genes (n ≈ 4000, p ≈ 5,000).
484
485   Our aim was to generate a “parts-based” representation of the data, with the hopes that the “parts”
      would resolve to biologically meaningful units (e.g., cell types, tissue regions) (Townes and Engel-


                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      hardt, 2023). With this aim in mind, we ran cEBMF so as to produce non-negative matrix factoriza-
487   tions (“cEBNMF”) using the same priors as in the MovieLens data set. We compared cEBNMF to
488   CMF, MFAI and Spatial PCA, and two other non-negative matrix factorizations that did not exploit
489   the spatial data—NMF (implemented in the R package NNLM; Lin and Boutros 2020) and EBMF
490   with point-exponential priors (“EBNMF”). Although Spatial PCA cannot be directly compared to
491   NMF, the Spatial PCA software also clusters the data points after projection onto the principal com-
492   ponents (PCs), and this clustering can be compared to the non-negative matrix factorizations. Fol-
493   lowing Shang and Zhou (2022), we computed the top 20 PCs, then we ran the walk-trap clustering
494   algorithm (Pons and Latapy, 2005) on the PCs. Since MFAI also does not produce a non-negative
495
      matrix factorization, we clustered the MFAI output in a similar way to Spatial PCA. Additional
      details are given in the Appendix.
496
497   Figure 4 shows the results on two of the slices, with results on all 12 slices given in the Appendix.
498   Qualitatively, some of the factors from NMF and EBMF seem to correspond to the expert-labeled
499   regions, but several other factors appear to be capturing other substructures that have no obvious
500   spatial quality. Comparatively, the cEBNMF results in slices 4 and 9 capture the expert labeling
501
      much more closely, with most factors showing a clear spatial quality. The clusters obtained from
      the Spatial PCA and MFAI factorizations also capture spatial structure and expert labeling well,
502
      with some exceptions, e.g., Spatial PCA cluster 7 in slices 4 and 9. (The Spatial PCA software
503
      performed an additional post-processing step on the clusters which is why these clusters look less
504   noisy.) The CMF results were very poor, reflecting the inappropriateness of the CMF assumptions in
505   this setting (Fig. 9 in the Appendix). Note that the NMF methods can capture continuous variation
506   in expression within and across cell types or regions—as well as the expectation that some pixels
507   might be combinations of cell types—whereas the clustering cannot.

510   5   L IMITATIONS
511
512   Overfitting. Empirical Bayes methods are known to be prone to overfitting; this is of particular
513   concern for more complex priors with many free parameters. Following Tansey et al. (2020), we
514   found that adding a simple L2 -penalty term on the hyperparameters helped, but this is an area where
515   our methods can be improved.
516   Priors for more complex covariates. The demonstrations of cEBMF in this paper all incorporated
517   relatively simple types of side information, so it remains to be seen how well cEBMF can work with
518   more complex covariates. In the spatial transcriptomics application, we tried using the histologi-
519   cal images included in the DLPFC experiment as side information, but it seemed that the images
520   provided in the spatialLIBD format (Pardo et al., 2022) were of too poor quality to be useful for
521   inferring cell types or tissue regions.
522
523
524   6   C ONCLUSIONS
525
526   We have introduced cEBMF, a general framework for matrix factorization in which (i) side informa-
527   tion is incorporated via flexible priors, and (ii) the priors are learned from the data using empirical
528   Bayes ideas. We have put considerable effort into optimizing the software implementation, in part
529   by leveraging previous work in this area (Wang and Stephens, 2021; Willwerscheid et al., 2024;
530
      Willwersheid, 2021), so that we expect these methods to scale well to even larger data sets with, say,
      hundreds of thousands of rows and/or columns.
531
532   Our experiments underscored the importance of having a matrix factorization framework in which
533   the model assumptions are appropriate for the target data set or sufficiently flexible that they can
534   adapt to a variety of settings. The priors in cEBMF can be virtually any probabilistic model that can
535   be optimized via (10–11). Our framework may suggest new ways to integrate other more complex
536
      types of side information such as images or graphs. Further, we envision that cEBMF could be used
      in conjunction with pre-trained models (e.g., convolutional neural networks for cell classification;
537
      Zhang et al. 2017) to reduce training time and improve accuracy in complex data sets.

      ZIP file accompanying the manuscript.

      A.4    C HOICE OF K
893
894   A noteworthy aspect of empirical Bayes methods for matrix factorization, highlighted in Bishop
895   (1999), is their inherent ability to automatically determine K Bishop (1999); Stegle et al. (2012);
896   Wang and Stephens (2021). This feature comes from the fact that the maximum-likelihood estimates
            (ℓ)       (f )
897   for gk and gk can converge to a point-mass at zero (assuming that the families Gℓ,k and Gf,k
898   include a point-mass at zero). Thus, if K is initially set to a large value, certain row or column factors
899   will converge to zero, which effectively “zeroes out” the corresponding component k. Alternatively,
900   in a stepwise or “greedy” approach where factors are introduced sequentially, the process halts upon
901   introducing a factor that optimizes to zero. These two algorithms for fitting cEBMF with unknown
902   K (“backfitting” with K taken larger than necessary and “greedily” attempting to add additional
903
      factors until failure) are summarized in Algorithms 2 and 3.
