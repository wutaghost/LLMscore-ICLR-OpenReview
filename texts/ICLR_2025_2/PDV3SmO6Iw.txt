011                                                    A BSTRACT
012
013           In this paper, we propose Singular Values and Orthonormal Regularized Sin-
014           gular Vectors Adaptation, or SORSA, a novel PEFT method. Each SORSA
015           adapter consists of two main parts: trainable principal singular weights Wp =
016           Up diag(Sp )Vp⊤ , and frozen residual weights Wr = Ur diag(Sr )Vr⊤ . These parts
017           are initialized by performing singular value decomposition (SVD) on pre-trained
018           weights. Moreover, we implement and analyze an orthonormal regularizer, which
019           we prove could decrease the condition number of Wp and make the optimization
020           more efficient. SORSA adapters could be merged during inference, thus eliminat-
021           ing any inference latency. We also introduce a method to analyze the variation of
022
              the parameters by performing SVD and discuss and analyze SORSA’s superiority
              in minimizing the alteration in the SVD aspect. After all, SORSA shows a faster
023
              convergence than LoRA and PiSSA in our experiments. On the GSM-8K bench-
024
              mark, Llama 2 7B adapted using SORSA achieved 56.03% accuracy, surpassing
025           LoRA (42.30%), AdaLoRA (47.30%), Full FT (49.05%), and PiSSA (53.07%).
026           On the MATH benchmark, SORSA achieved 10.36% accuracy, outperforming
027           LoRA (5.50%), AdaLoRA (6.48%), Full FT (7.22%), and PiSSA (7.44%). We
028           conclude that SORSA offers a new perspective on parameter-efficient fine-tuning,
029           demonstrating remarkable performance.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Pre-trained large language models (LLMs) show remarkable generalization abilities, allowing them
057   to perform various kinds of natural language processing (NLP) tasks (Peng et al., 2024; Touvron
058   et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024; Radford et al., 2019; OpenAI, 2023). For specific downstream
059   tasks, full parameter fine-tuning, which continues training all parameters of LLMs on downstream
060   data, is widely used.
061
      However, as the number of parameters in LLMs rapidly increases, full parameter fine-tuning be-
062
      comes increasingly inefficient. For example, the estimated VRAM requirement for fully fine-tuning
063   Llama 2 7B using Float32 could approach approximately 100 GB, making it unlikely to fully fine-
064   tune the model on a single GPU with current technology. Additionally, the VRAM requirement for
065   fully fine-tuning Llama 2 70B using Float32 exceeds 1 TB (Touvron et al., 2023; Anthony et al.,
066   2023), thus rendering it unfeasible on a single GPU with current technology.
067
      To address these challenges, several parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods (Houlsby et al.,
068
      2019; Lester et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2021) have been proposed. These methods enable the training of
069   only a few parameters, significantly reducing VRAM requirements while achieving comparable or
070   even superior performance to full fine-tuning. For instance, tuning Llama 2 7B in Float32 by LoRA
071   (Hu et al., 2021) with a rank of 128 only takes approximately 60GB VRAM, which allows training
072   on 1 × NVIDIA A100 (80GB), or even 3 × NVIDIA RTX 4090 (24GB).
073
      Among those PEFT methods, LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) and its variants (Zhang et al., 2023; Meng
074
      et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Dettmers et al., 2024) had become increasingly popular due to their: 1.
075   Low training VRAM requirement 2. No inference latency 3. Versatility in different neuron network
076   architectures.
077
078
      This paper proposes a novel PEFT approach, Singular Values and Orthonormal Regularized Singular
      Vectors Adaptation, or SORSA. A SORSA adapter has two main parts: principal singular weights
079
      Wp = Up diag(Sp )Vp⊤ , and residual weights Wr = Ur diag(Sr )Vr⊤ . These two parts are initialized
080
      by performing singular value decomposition (SVD) on pre-trained weight. Residual singular values
081   and vectors will be merged into one matrix and frozen while training. We only train principal
082   singular values and vectors with an orthonormal regularizer implemented to keep the orthonormality
083   of Up and Vp⊤ . The architecture of a SORSA adapter is illustrated in Figure 1.
084
      Furthermore, we analyze the pattern of variation of singular values and vectors during parameter
085
      updating and discuss the different patterns of fine-tuning (FT), LoRA, SORSA without regularizer,
086
      and SORSA with regularizer concerning singular values and vectors’ updating.
087
088   We also provide a comprehensive gradient analysis with a mathematical foundation for SORSA.
089   This analysis demonstrates several crucial properties of our method, including the convexity of the
090
      regularizer, Lipschitz continuity of the gradient, and bounds on the hyperparameter γ. Moreover,
      we prove that SORSA improves the condition number of the optimization problem compared to
091
      unregularized approaches.
092
093   SORSA retains all the benefits of LoRA and its variants while demonstrating remarkable perfor-
094   mance compared to PiSSA, LoRA, and full parameter fine-tuning in our experiments.
095
096   2     R ELATED W ORKS
097
098
      Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods have been developed to address the inefficiency of
099   full parameter fine-tuning for large language models. These methods focus on adapting the model for
100   downstream tasks while updating only a few parameters and keeping most of the model’s weights
101   frozen. This approach significantly reduces the memory and computational requirements during
102   training, especially VRAM.
103
104   2.1   A DAPTER - BASED PEFT
105
106   Adapter-based PEFT methods are the first type of PEFT initially designed by Houlsby et al. (2019).
107   It introduces additional trainable non-linear blocks into the frozen pre-trained model, which could
      effectively tune the pre-trained model with a limited amount of trainable parameters. Its variants,


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      e.g., Lin et al. (2020), reduce the number of adapter layers per block, and He et al. (2022) focus
109   on adding adapter modules parallel to existing layers. However, all adapter-based PEFT methods
110   introduce inference latency due to their non-mergeable attribute.
111
112
      2.2   P ROMPT- BASED PEFT
113
114   Prompt-based PEFT is a well-known PEFT type first proposed in Lester et al. (2021). This work
115   has several variants, including Liu et al. (2022a); Razdaibiedina et al. (2023). However, they have
116   some inevitable shortcomings, such as potential performance limitations compared to full parameter
117   fine-tuned models, additional inference latency due to expanding the length of the total input to the
118   model, and the complexity of designing effective initialization.
119
120   2.3   L O RA AND ITS VARIANTS
121
122   LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) and its variants are the most popular type of PEFT methods. This type
123
      of PEFT is popular due to its on-par or better performance than full parameter fine-tuning without
      introducing any inference latency. LoRA could be represented by equation W = W0 + BA, where
124
      W0 ∈ Rm×n is the pre-trained weight, A ∈ Rm×r , using Gaussian initialization, and B ∈ Rr×n ,
125
      using zero initialization, are low-rank matrices.
126
127   Its variant, for example, AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023), introduces an SVD decomposition and
128   pruning for least significant singular values for more efficient parameter updating.
129   DoRA (Liu et al., 2024) proposed a novel way to decompose weight into direction and magnitude by
130              W0 +BA
      W = m ∥W    0 +BA∥c
                          , where m is initialized by m = ∥W0 + BA∥c , ∥ · ∥c denotes column-wise norm.
131   The results show that DoRA has a better learning capacity than LoRA. However, DoRA introduced
132   a calculation of norms in every training step, which makes it much more inefficient than LoRA.
133
      OLoRA (Büyükakyüz, 2024) uses QR decomposition to initialize the LoRA adapters A and B,
134
      which initializes B as an orthogonal matrix. They discuss the significance of orthonormality in neu-
135
      ral networks’ weight (See Section 5 for more details). In their experiments, OLoRA demonstrates
136   faster convergence than LoRA.
137
138   PiSSA (Meng et al., 2024) decomposes pre-trained weight W0 = U diag(S)V ⊤ by Singular Value
139
      Decomposition (SVD) and then splits W0 into Wpri and Wres : Wpri = AB which is trainable.

149   2.4   OTHER METHODS
150
151
      There are also a few efficient adapting methods with unique techniques. For example, GaLore (Zhao
      et al., 2024) is a memory-efficient PEFT method that reduces VRAM usage by leveraging gradient
152
      accumulation and low-rank approximation. LISA (Pan et al., 2024) uses a layer-wise importance
153
      sampling approach, prioritizing layers significantly impacting model performance and selectively
154   fine-tuning essential parameters.

160   Giving a matrix W ∈ Rm×n , let k = min(m, n), we could perform SVD to decompose W by
161   W = U diag(S)V ⊤ . Here, U ∈ Rm×k is a matrix of left singular vectors and has orthonormal
      columns, V ∈ Rn×k is a matrix of right singular vectors and has orthonormal columns, and S ∈ Rk

      are singular values σ 1 , σ 2 . . . σ k arranged in descending order. diag(S) is constructed by placing the
163   elements of S ∈ Rk along the main diagonal, with all other elements zero.
164
165   According to our SVD notations, given a rank r where r ≪ k, we could perform the low-rank
166
      approximation by selecting the first r items on the diagonal of Σ, which is the first r most significant
      singular values, and also select the first r columns of U and first r rows of V ⊤ , which correspond
167
      to the selected singular values. By performing SVD low-rank approximation, we could get a low-
168
      rank matrix that preserves the largest significant values and vectors, containing the matrix’s “most
169   essential” data.
170
171   Therefore, for a pre-trained weight W0 ∈ Rm×n , we could split it based on its singular value
172
      into principal weight Wp and residual weight Wr , where Wp contains the most important part of
      information of the matrix, and Wr contains the least significant part

      4.2   A NALYSIS R ESULT
250
251   Based on our collected data, this section analyzes the results of different training methods: fine-
252   tuning, LoRA, and SORSA. The analysis data is illustrated in Figure 2.
253
      Based on our collected data, we analyze how different training methods - partial fine-tuning, LoRA,
254
      and SORSA (with and without regularizer) - affect the pre-trained weights’ structure and information
255
      preservation.
256
257   The analysis reveals several key insights about how these methods interact with the pre-trained
258   knowledge:
259
            1. Partial fine-tuning and LoRA show substantial alterations in singular vectors (large ∆D),
260            indicating significant disruption to the fundamental structure of the pre-trained weights.
261            This extensive modification likely damages the model’s carefully learned generalizations
262            across multiple domains, leading to catastrophic forgetting. The parallel updating patterns
263            across different layers suggest these methods make broad, potentially destructive changes
264            throughout the model rather than targeted adaptations.
265         2. SORSA with regularizer demonstrates significantly smaller changes in both singular values
266            (∆Σ) and singular vectors (∆D) compared to other methods. This controlled modification
267            suggests that SORSA better preserves the pre-trained model’s underlying knowledge struc-
268            ture while making precise adjustments for the downstream task. The orthonormal regular-
269            izer appears to act as a constraint that helps maintain the original geometric relationships
               within the weight matrices that encode the model’s generalized capabilities.

      7            C ONCLUSION
474
475   In this paper, we introduced SORSA, a novel parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) method de-
476
      signed to enhance the adaptation of large language models (LLMs) for downstream tasks. SORSA
      utilizes singular value decomposition (SVD) to split pre-trained weights into principal and residual
477
      components, only training the principal singular values and vectors while freezing the residuals. We
478
      implemented an orthonormal regularizer to maintain the orthonormality of singular vectors during
479   training, ensuring efficient parameter updates and preserving the integrity of singular values.
480
481   Our experiments demonstrated that SORSA outperforms existing PEFT methods, such as LoRA
482
      and PiSSA, in both convergence speed and accuracy on the NLG tasks. Specifically, Llama 2 7B,
      tuned with SORSA, achieved significant improvements in the GSM-8K and MATH benchmarks,
483
      highlighting the effectiveness of our approach.
484
485   We adopted singular values and vector analysis, comparing SORSA with FT and LoRA. SORSA
      is superior in preserving the pre-trained weight’s singular values and vectors during training. This


                                                                                                               9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      suggests an explanation for SORSA’s supreme performance demonstrated in the experiment. We
487   also show the significance of the orthonormal regularizer through analysis.
488
489   Our gradient analysis provided a mathematical foundation for SORSA, demonstrating its convexity,
490
      Lipschitz continuity, and the crucial role of the regularizer in improving the optimization landscape.
      This theoretical framework explains SORSA’s empirical superior performance and offers valuable
491
      insights for future developments in adaptive learning algorithms.
492
493   SORSA retains the advantages of LoRA and variants, including low training VRAM requirements,
494   no inference latency, and versatility across different neural network architectures. By offering a
495   more efficient fine-tuning mechanism, SORSA presents a promising direction for future research
496
      and application in the field of LLMs.
497   Overall, SORSA gives a new perspective on parameter-efficient fine-tuning, showcasing exceptional
498   efficiency and robust performance. It outperforms existing methods like LoRA and PiSSA in sev-
499   eral downstream tasks and maintains the practical benefits of low VRAM requirements, no inference
500   latency, and ease of implementation. This innovative approach offers a promising direction of sin-
501   gular values and vector analysis for future research and practical applications in adapting pre-trained
502
      models, making it a pivotal development in the field.

      8   F UTURE W ORK
506
507   While SORSA demonstrates improvements over existing PEFT methods, several promising direc-
508   tions for future research exist to enhance its capabilities and broaden its impact.
509
      A crucial area for exploration is the application of SORSA beyond natural language processing.
510   While our current evaluation focuses on language models, SORSA’s theoretical foundation in sin-
511   gular value decomposition suggests it could be equally effective for computer vision models like Ho
512   et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2022b); Dosovitskiy et al. (2021); Rombach et al. (2022) and multi-modal
513   architectures such as (Radford et al., 2021). Future work should evaluate SORSA’s performance on
514   vision transformers, convolutional neural networks, and other architectures across diverse tasks like
515   image classification, object detection, and semantic segmentation. This extended evaluation across
516   different domains would provide valuable insights into SORSA’s versatility and potentially uncover
517   domain-specific optimizations.
518   Another compelling direction is the integration of quantization techniques with SORSA, similar
519   to approaches like QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2024) and QPiSSA (Meng et al., 2024). Quantiza-
520   tion could significantly reduce SORSA’s memory footprint and computational requirements while
521   maintaining its efficient adaptation capabilities. This would be particularly valuable for deploying
522   adapted models on edge devices and resource-constrained environments. By combining SORSA’s
523   precise parameter updates with the efficiency gains of quantization, we could enable high-quality
524
      model adaptation across a much broader range of hardware configurations. This democratization of
      fine-tuning capabilities could accelerate the adoption of AI technologies in real-world applications,
525
      from mobile devices to IoT systems.
526
527   By pursuing these research directions, we can build upon SORSA’s theoretical foundations to create
528   more versatile and accessible model adaptation techniques. Success in these areas would not only
529   advance the field of parameter-efficient fine-tuning but also help bridge the gap between state-of-the-
530
      art AI models and practical applications. This could ultimately lead to more widespread integration
      of adaptive AI systems across different sectors of society, making advanced machine learning capa-
531
      bilities more accessible and impactful in people’s daily lives.

538   computational efficiency and environmental sustainability. As we push the boundaries of what is
539   possible with Machine Learning, it is essential to consider the broader impacts of these advance-
      ments on the environment and ethical standards within the field.

      According to the definition of SORSA from Equation (3), because diag(Sp ) is always a diagonal
867   matrix, it is equivalent to:
868
869                                                                       SORSA(x) = xWr + x(Up ⊙ Sp )Vp⊤ ,                                                                                    (15)
870
      where ⊙ denotes element-wise multiplication.
871
872   This transformation allows us to reduce the computational complexity of SORSA adapters. In the
873   original form, we had to perform matrix multiplication twice. However, in the Equation (15), we
874
      only have one matrix multiplication and one element-wise multiplication. The time complexity of
      Up ⊙ Sp is O(m × n), much less than complexity of Up Sp , which is O(m × n2 ). Therefore, while
875
      limm,n→∞ , the computation speed of SORSA adapters will be the same as LoRA and PiSSA.
