009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Data distribution often changes when deploying a machine learning model into
012            a new environment, but not all shifts degrade model performance, making inter-
013            ventions like retraining unnecessary. This paper addresses model post-deployment
014            deterioration (PDD) monitoring in the context of unlabeled deployment distribu-
015            tions. We formalize unsupervised PDD monitoring within the model disagreement
016            framework where deterioration is detected if an auxiliary model, performing well
017            on training data, shows significant prediction disagreement with the deployed
018
               model on test data. We propose D-PDDM, a principled monitoring algorithm
               achieving low false positive rates under non-deteriorating shifts and provide sample
019
               complexity bounds for high true positive rates under deteriorating shifts. Empirical
020
               results on both standard benchmark and a real-world large-scale healthcare dataset
021            demonstrate the effectiveness of the framework in addition to its viability as an
022            alert mechanism for existing high-stakes ML pipelines.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Performance guarantees of conventional machine learning (ML) models hinge on the belief that
028   the distribution of data with which these models train is identical to the distribution on which they
029   are deployed (Rabanser et al., 2019; Recht et al., 2018; Santurkar et al., 2020). In many real-world
030   scenarios such as healthcare, however, this assumption fails due to distribution shift during model
031   deployment. Benchmarks such as WILDS (Koh et al., 2021) and WILD-Time (Yao et al., 2022) have
032   encouraged machine learning researchers to study and better understand how data shifts influence
033
      predictive systems. Yet, the number of tools at a practitioner’s disposal for the creation of predictive
      models far exceed those to monitor model failures. There is a need to create guardrails that self-detect
034
      and alert end-users to critical changes in the model when its performance drops below acceptable
035
      thresholds (Habib et al., 2021; Zadorozhny et al., 2022).
036
037   Post-deployment deterioration (PDD) monitoring presents a distinct set of systemic challenges stem-
038   ming from considerations over the feasibility of deployment in real-world ML pipelines. Predominant
039
      is the scarcity of labels during deployment: for many downstream tasks such as in healthcare, labels
      are expensive to obtain (Razavian et al., 2015) or require human intervention (Liu et al., 2022).
040
      Moreover, due to deployed models predicting events temporally extended in the future (Boodhun &
041
      Jayabalan, 2018; Zhang et al., 2019), labels might even be unavailable. Another systemic challenge
042   is the robustness of the monitoring system, that the latter should be flagging critical changes in
043   deployment efficiently as early as a few samples, and that it should remain robust to non-deteriorating
044   changes as to minimize unnecessary interruptions of service among other practical considerations.
045
      To address these challenges, we conceive a set of desiderata for any algorithm monitoring PDD,
046
      targeting their practicality and effectiveness as plug-ins to ML pipelines. To address the scarcity
047
      of labels, PDD monitoring algorithms should operate on unlabeled data from the test distribution
048   to ascertain potential deterioration of the deployed model. PDD monitoring algorithms should not
049   depend on training data during deployment. Continuous (even indefinite) access to sensitive or
050   personally identifiable training data might violate certain regulations protecting the privacy of data
051   subjects (Mühlhoff, 2023). An algorithm satisfying this desideratum is thus a scalable algorithm as it
052   functionally only audits its input stream during monitoring with minimum data storage and regulatory
053   concerns. Finally, PDD monitoring algorithms should be robust to flagging non-deteriorating changes
      and effective in few-shot settings.

      Insofar as designing monitoring protocols satisfying the above, recent related works only partially
055   attend to individual desiderata. The literature on distribution shifts while achieving strong empirical
056   performance on unlabeled deployment data (Liu et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2022), are not robust to
057   false positives when the distribution shift is non-deteriorating. The model disagreement framework
058   (Chuang et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2021; Ginsberg et al., 2023; Rosenfeld & Garg, 2023) emerges
059   as the natural setup for monitoring with downstream performance considerations via the tracking
060   of disagreement statistics, while foregoing explicit distribution shift computations. However, shift-
061   based and disagreement-based monitoring methods all depend on the presence of training data
062   post-deployment, and do not provide any guarantees on robustness against false positives in the
063
      monitoring of non-deteriorating shifts.

403

404   constraints of our analysis. To demonstrate that our test enjoys low FPR on non deteriorating shifts
405   and high TPR on deteriorating shifts, we compare it against several distribution divergence-based
406   detection methods from the literature: Deep Kernel MMD (MMD-D) (Liu et al., 2020), H-divergence
407   (Zhao et al., 2022), adapt several f -divergences (Acuna et al., 2021) into a hypothesis test via
408   permutation testing (Ernst, 2004), Black Box Shift Detection (BBSD) (Lipton et al., 2018), and
409   Relative Mahalanobis Distance (RMD) Ren et al. (2021). Details can be found in Appendix B.5.
410   Evaluations. Synthetic data. For non-deteriorating shifts, we report the FPR at level ↵ = 0.05 of our
411   method and the baselines. We run 500 permutations times 100 independent tests for each baseline
412   whereas for D-PDDM, we report 100 independent realizations to compute the TPR/FPR, each run
413   running 500 pre-training steps. Importantly, the baseline methods have oracle access to the generating
414   distributions Px and Qx and re-sample new sets of data from Px and Qx for each permutation,
415   ensuring a fair comparison with our theoretical algorithm and empowers the baselines as it de-biases
416   their test statistics away from one particular sampling of Px and Qx , as they would otherwise have
417   to sample with replacement. Real-world dataset evaluation. For CIFAR 10.1 where there is known
418
      post-deployment deterioration, we evaluate the baselines’ and D-PDDM’s ability to detect shift
      at level ↵ = 0.05 in {50, 100, 200}-shot scenarios. For the GEMINI health dataset, we study the
419
      detection rates of said models on temporally-split sub-datasets and mixtures of subpopulation splits
420
      incurring deteriorating changes. Additional information for the Gemini Dataset and splits is present
421   in Appendix. B.6.

      Performance monitoring of ML models & deteriorating shift. Evaluating a model’s reliability

478

485   & Ramdas (2021) studied deteriorating shift detection in the continuous monitoring setting using a

506   Distribution shift detection. Methods to detect distribution shift arise from different perspectives.
507   In covariate shift detection, (Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2016; Liu et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2022) treated
508
      detection as two-sample tests via classifier, Deep Kernel MMD, and H-divergence. For label shift
      on the other hand, (Lipton et al., 2018; Azizzadenesheli et al., 2019) formulated the problem as a
509
      convex optimization problem by solving the label distribution ratio ↵ = Q(y)/P (y). The problem
510
      of (out-of-distribution) OOD (Liang et al., 2017; Kamulete, 2022) detection seeks to detect if an
511   individual sample x comes from the training distribution x ⇠ P (x). Some previous works (Ren
512   et al., 2019; Morningstar et al., 2021) also adopted the methods in covariate shift detection and
513   generalization by estimating the density ratio for the identification of OOD samples. Whilst these
514   methods detect shifts, they are constrained by their requirement of training data post-deployment and
515   do not consider the extent to which shifts affect model performance.
516
      Estimating test error with unlabeled data. Another rich body of research is the estimation of
517   test error. This technique and its variants are often inspired by domain adaptation theories (Ben-
518   David et al., 2006; 2010; Acuna et al., 2021; Ganin et al., 2016), seeking guarantess in the form
519   of err(f ; Qg )  err(f ; Pg ) + (f, H), with (f, H) = suph2H | err(h; Pf ) err(h; Qf )|. This
520   objective can be alternatively viewed as searching for a critic function h 2 H to maximize the
521   performance gap (Rosenfeld & Garg, 2023; Jiang et al., 2021). One could thus provably estimate the
522   upper bound of the test distribution error. These theories, however, implicitly assume the availability
523   of training data. Further, they assume that the test error should be larger than the training error,
524   making them sensitive to non deteriorating shifts as well i.e., high FPR in detection. Our theory for
525
      D-PDDM encompasses this regime of change as well.

527   7   C ONCLUSION
528
529   We study the problem of post-deployment deterioration monitoring of machine learning models in
530   the setting where labels from test distribution are unavailable. We propose a two-stage disagreement-
531   based monitoring algorithm, D-PDDM, which monitors and detects deteriorating changes in the
532   deployment dataset while being resilient to flagging non-deteriorating changes. Importantly, our
533   method does not require any training data during monitoring, allowing for efficient out-of-the-box
534
      deployment in many machine learning pipelines across various domains. We provide statistical
      guarantees for low FPR in the case of non-deteriorating shifts and reliable TPR in the deteriorating
535
      shift. Empirically, we validate insights from our theory on various synthetic and real-world vision
536
      and healthcare datasets evidencing the effective use of D-PDDM. The empirical success of D-PDDM
537   signals a step toward the robust, scalable, and efficient deployment of mechanisms to audit and
538   monitor machine learning pipelines in the break of dawn of ubiquitous AI.
