                                                 A BSTRACT
013
               Cytochrome P450s (CYP450s) are key enzymes involved in human xenobiotics
014
               metabolism. So it is critical to make accurate CYP450s substrate predictions
015
               for drug discovery and chemical toxicology study. Recent deep learning-based
016            approaches indicated that directly leverage extensive information from proteins
017            and chemicals in biological and chemical databases to predict enzyme-substrate
018            interactions, have achieved remarkable performance. Here, we present CypST, a
019            deep learning-based model that enhances these methods by pre-trained ESM-2
020            Transformer model to extract detailed CYP450 protein representations and by
021            incorporating our fine-tuned graph attention networks (GATs) for more effective
022            learning on molecular graphs. GATs regard molecular graphs as sets of nodes or
023            edges, with connectivity enforced by masking the attention weight matrix, creating
024
               custom attention patterns for each graph. This approach captures key molecu-
               lar interactions, improving prediction ability for substrates. CypST effectively
025
               recognizes substructural interactions, constructing a comprehensive molecular rep-
026
               resentation through multi-substructural feature extraction. By pre-training on a
027            large-scale experimental enzyme-substrate pair database and fine-tuning on 51,753
028            CYP450s enzyme-substrate and 27,857 CYP450s enzyme-non-substrate pairs,
029            CypST focuses on five major human CYP450 isforms, achieving 0.861 accuracy
030            and 0.909 AUROC and demonstrating strong generalizability to novel compounds
031            for different CYP450 isoforms.

034   1     I NTRODUCTION
035
036   1.1   C YTOCHROME P450 S S UBSTRATES P REDICTION
037
038   Cytochrome P450s (CYP450s), a highly diverse superfamily of heme-thiolate proteins, are indispens-
039
      able components of the oxidative metabolic machinery found across various life forms. CYP450s play
      a pivotal role in the metabolism of a wide range of xenobiotics, mainly including pharmaceuticals,
040
      cosmetic ingredients, and environmental pollutants. In humans, a total of 57 distinct CYP450 iso-
041
      forms have been identified, and they are responsible for catalyzing over 90% of enzymatic reactions
042   associated with xenobiotic metabolism (Danielson (2002)). The use of computational approaches to
043   accurately predict the interactions between chemical compounds and proteins can assist in reducing
044   the economical and labor cost, environmental burdens, and better facilitate pre-select hit compounds
045   for further drug discovery and chemical toxicology studies, accelerating research results.
046
      Previous computational methods for predicting CYP450s substrates can be classified into two
047
      main categories: structure-based and ligand-based (Tyzack & Kirchmair (2019)). Structure-based
048   approaches rely on the three-dimensional (3D) structures of proteins and ligands to evaluate protein-
049   ligand interactions at the atomistic level. These methods often employ techniques such as molecular
050   docking, molecular dynamics simulations, and quantum mechanics. However, these techniques
051   tend to be computationally intensive and often require licensed software, making them challenging
052   to apply to large-scale biochemical datasets and limiting their accessibility to a broader user base.
053   In contrast, ligand-based approaches focus primarily on quantitative structure-activity relationship
      (QSAR) models. These methods utilize mathematical models to establish correlations between

054
      molecular numerical descriptors and biological activity. In recent years, machine learning algorithms
055   have emerged as key methodologies within ligand-based approaches for developing predictive models.
056   These machine learning algorithms encompass a variety of techniques, including support vector
057   machines (SVMs), random forest (RF) (Tian et al. (2018); Holmer et al. (2021)), deep neural
058   networks (DNN) (Fu et al. (2024)), and others. Generally, these machine learning models are less
059   computationally demanding and can be trained on large molecular datasets. Many of them have been
060   deployed as web servers or software tools, making them widely accessible to a wider range user to
061   investigate the chemical molecules of interests (Wei et al. (2024)).

066   1.2   R ELATED W ORK
067
068
069   Traditional machine learning models for predicting CYP450 substrates primarily focus on molecular
070   information, necessitating the development of separate prediction models for different CYP450
071   isoforms. Recently, there has been a growing trend in enzyme-substrate prediction models that
072   integrate both protein and molecular representations using deep learning techniques. Protein language
073   models (PLMs), such as the ESM Transformer and its variants, are primarily employed to generate
074   protein representations, while graph neural networks are mainly used for molecular representations.
075   DeepP450 (Chang et al. (2024)) is one such model that integrates information from both CYP450
076   proteins and the molecules they interact with. Researchers employed the ESM-2 Transformer
077   model to extract protein embeddings and used Uni-Mol, a general 3D molecular representation
078   framework, built on two similar SE(3) Transformer architectures, to derive molecular representations.
079   This approach allows for the creation of a single model capable of predicting substrates for nine
080   CYP450 isoforms. Some other general enzyme-substrate prediction models also used the similar
081   strategies. ESP (Kroll et al. (2023)) is a notable enzyme-substrate pairs prediction model, researchers
082
      curated a large dataset of enzyme-substrate pairs from the UniProt-GOA database, enhancing it with
      negative data to create enzyme-non-substrate pairs. They slightly modified ESM-1b Transformer to
083
      better encode enzymes representations, while a graph neural network was employed for molecular
084
      representations. This led to the creation of datasets for enzyme-substrate and enzyme-non-substrate
085   pairs to train on a gradient boosting model, resulting in a high-quality dataset that enables accurate
086   predictions of new enzyme-substrate pairs. Subsequently, Du et al. utilized the ESP datasets,
087   training them with the ESM-2 Transformer to obtain enzyme representations and a MolFormer for
088   extracting molecular representations. They developed CLR-ESP (Du et al. (2024)), a multimodal
089   classifier that integrates protein and molecular language models with a novel contrastive learning
090   strategy for predicting enzyme-substrate pairs. This model ensures that the embeddings of positive
091   enzyme-substrate pairs are closer together in high-dimensional space, whereas negative pairs show
092   the opposite trend, resulting in better model performance while requiring fewer computing resources.
093
      ALDELE (Wang et al. (2024)) is a deep learning toolkit designed for screening biocatalysts. It uses
      convolutional neural networks to learn global sequence representations of enzymes, an artificial
094
      neural network for substrate RDKit descriptors, and a graph neural network to learn molecular graph
095
      representations from substrate SMILE inputs. This innovative ALDELE toolkit effectively predicts
096   protein-compound interactions and selects newly designed protein sequences that meet industrial
097   needs. With these remarkable advancements in the field, it is evident that utilizing deep learning
098   models to generate both enzyme and molecular representations for further classification tasks can
099   significantly enhance the models’ predictive capabilities.
100
      Our main contributions to the existing prediction models are as follows: we created a large-scale
101
      dataset of CYP450s enzyme-substrate and enzyme-non-substrate pairs; the molecules have diverse
102   scaffolds. We then integrated both protein representations, which were generated by pre-trained
103   ESM-2 Transformer, and the molecular representations, which were encoded by our fine-tuned graph
104   attention networks (GATs). Our fine-tuned molecular GATs incorporated self-attention mechanism to
105   the nodes message passing layers; this allow to assign greater weights to more significant nodes during
106   neighborhood aggregation. This enhancement provides GATs with better control over information
107   flow within complex graph structures, making them more adaptable for processing intricate chemical
      data.

224   2.3   DATASET
225
226   Our model has been fine-tuned using an experimental dataset from the ESP model (Kroll et al. (2023)),
227   which comprises 18,351 enzyme-substrate pairs sourced from the Gene Ontology Annotation (GOA)
228   database. To address the data class imbalance, negative samples were generated by selecting three
229
      structurally similar molecules for each enzyme sequence that are not true substrates.
230   Our curated CYP450 dataset includes 51,753 enzyme-substrate and 27,857 enzyme-non-substrate
231   pairs for five human CYP450 isoforms (CYP1A2, CYP2C9, CYP2C19, CYP2D6, and CYP3A4).
232   These data were sourced from the studies by Chang et al. (Chang et al. (2024)), Fang et al. (Fang
233   et al. (2024)), and Ai et al. (Ai et al. (2023)). As shown in the scaffold diversity curve (Figure 3, left),
234   the curve has gradual slope, which indicates that the molecules in our dataset are diverse and even
235
      distributed across various chemical scaffolds.

293   protein representations. However, GNN and GAT also produced competitive results, especially when

      Beyond the molecular representations, the choice of protein representations and classifiers also
297   significantly impacted performance. Unlike ESM-1b, which uses absolute sinusoidal positional
298   encoding, ESM-2 employs Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE). RoPE allows the model to extrapolate
299   beyond its training context by applying relative position encoding, achieved by multiplying query
300   and key vectors with sinusoidal embeddings in the self-attention mechanism (Su et al. (2024)). The
301   ability to capture relative positional information likely makes ESM-2 protein representations more
302   compatible with GAT molecular encodings.
303   Regarding the classifiers, XGBoost leverages an ensemble of decision trees using Gradient Boosting
304   Decision Tree (GBDT) methodology (Chen & Guestrin (2016)). By iteratively optimizing residual
305   errors and introducing regularization to control tree complexity, XGBoost effectively prevents overfit-
306   ting and achieves strong classification performance, particularly on molecular graph representations.
307   This makes XGBoost a better choice compared to the MLP for our classification tasks.

346   4   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
347
348   Our results indicate that all the tested setups during the prediction model development exhibit
349   reasonable performance. Notably, any modifications to the model pipeline can significantly influence
350   its predictive capability. Therefore, future work will focus on fine-tuning the ESM Transformers to
351   enhance their ability to generate more accurate CYP450s protein representations. It is important to
352   introduce a multimodal fusion strategy to CypST, which will better integrate protein representation
353   extraction, molecular representation encoding, and classification modules. This approach will enhance
354
      the model’s predictive performance and improve its robustness.
355   Our work highlights the potential to predict substrates based solely on the primary structures of
356   enzymes and the topological features of chemicals. However, deep learning models are highly
357   sensitive to the quality and quantity of training data used. Therefore, it is also essential to consider
358   protein and molecular conformation information during the model training. Moreover, our application
359   is limited to the most relevant human CYP450 isoforms, which are primarily involved in the pharmaco-
360
      toxicological outcomes. Future research could incorporate additional species or isoforms of CYP450s
      to extend the predictions to risk assessment procedures for chemicals, particularly pesticides and
361
      other xenobiotics. This expansion would broaden the use of our prediction model.

364   5   C ONCLUSION
365
366   Here we present CypST, a novel deep learning-based CYP450s substrates prediction model for
367   predicting CYP450 substrates. CypST integrates both protein and molecular information to predict
368   substrates of five key human CYP450 isoforms, and was trained on a dataset containing 51,753
369
      enzyme-substrate and 27,857 enzyme-non-substrate pairs, featuring molecules with diverse chemical
      scaffolds. We employ the ESM-2 Transformer protein language model for protein feature extraction,
370
      and fine-tune a molecular Graph Attention Network (GAT), which introduces self-attention to node
371
      message passing layers, enabling to more comprehensive learn the molecular information to generate
372   the molecular representations. Through comparative analysis of various combinations of protein and
373   molecular representation methods and classification techniques, we identified that the combination
374   of ESM-2 (for protein representations), GAT (for molecular representations), and XGBoost (for
375   classifications) demonstrates the best results. This combination achieves a prediction accuracy
376   of 0.861 and an AUROC of 0.909 on our dataset. The results suggest that the fine-tuned GAT
377   generated molecular representations are well compatible with ESM-2 extracted CYP450s protein
      representations. CypST also outperforms for predicting individual CYP450 isoforms’ substrates

      to traditional relative prediction model, such as CypReact and ADMETlab3.0, that rely only on
379   molecular information. We can see the effectiveness of incorporating both protein and molecular
380   representations for CYP450s substrate prediction. Our future work will focus on further fine-tuning
381   the ESM Transformers, introducing a multimodal fusion strategy to better incorporate each module
382   of CypST, and expanding the CYP450s and molecular data. These enhancements aim to improve
383   CypST’s accuracy, robustness, and generalizability in CYP450s substrate prediction. We hope our
384   work can facilitate the advancements in the pharmaceutical and toxicological fields.
