009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                Logical reasoning is a critical component of Large Language Models (LLMs),
012                and substantial research efforts in recent years have aimed to enhance their de-
013                ductive capabilities. However, existing deductive reasoning benchmarks, which
014                are crucial for evaluating and advancing LLMs, are inadequate due to their lack
015                of task complexity, presence of prior knowledge as a confounder, and superficial
016                error analysis. To address these deficiencies, we introduce JustLogic, a syntheti-
017
                   cally generated deductive reasoning benchmark designed for rigorous evaluation
                   of LLMs. JustLogic is (i) highly complex, capable of generating a diverse range of
018
                   linguistic patterns, vocabulary, and argument structures; (ii) prior knowledge in-
019
                   dependent, eliminating the advantage of models possessing prior knowledge and
020                ensuring that only deductive reasoning is used to answer questions; and (iii) ca-
021                pable of in-depth error analysis on the heterogeneous effects of reasoning depth
022                and argument form on model accuracy. Our experimental results on JustLogic
023                reveal that the performance of most state-of-the-art (SOTA) LLMs, specifically
024                Llama3-8B (57.8%), Llama3-70B (64.6%), and GPT-4o (65.6%), is significantly
025                worse than the average human performance (73.0%). A recently released reason-
026                ing model, OpenAI o1-preview, performed substantially better, with an accuracy
027                of 81.0%. However, it still lags behind the human ceiling of 100.0%. These re-
028
                   sults demonstrate that the JustLogic benchmark is realistic and achievable for both
                   humans and models and that there is still substantial room for improvement in the
029
                   deductive reasoning capabilities of LLMs. We posit that the use of prior knowl-
030
                   edge dependent and relatively simplistic benchmarks has misrepresented the rea-
031                soning abilities of many SOTA models. We release our open-source dataset to
032                provide accurate evaluations of model performance in deductive reasoning and to
033                facilitate LLM advancement through in-depth error analysis.1

036   1       I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Deductive reasoning is a crucial capability for large language models (LLMs). It refers to the pro-
039   cess of creating logically valid arguments, where conclusions necessarily follow from the premises.
040   In other words, if an argument’s premises are true, the conclusion must also be true. Recent state-
041   of-the-art (SOTA) LLMs (Achiam et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023) have exhib-
042
      ited outstanding performance and consistent improvement across various reasoning benchmarks,
      including HelloSwag (Zellers et al., 2019), ARC Challenge (Clark et al., 2018) and WinoGrande
043
      (Sakaguchi et al., 2021). However, we argue that the existing benchmarks are insufficient and often
044
      ineffective for evaluating LLMs’ true deductive reasoning capabilities.
045
046   We identify three major problems with the existing benchmarks. First, they lack complexity, which
047   is measured on two dimensions: natural language complexity, which refers to how arguments are
048
      linguistically expressed, and argument complexity, which pertains to the structure of the argument
      itself. Manually curated datasets, such as FOLIO (Han et al., 2022) and LogiQA 2.0 (Liu et al.,
049
      2020; 2023a) exhibit high natural language complexity but low argument complexity, while syn-
050
      thetic datasets like CLUTRR (Sinha et al., 2019) and ProofWriter (Tafjord et al., 2020) show the
051   opposite. Simplicity in either dimension makes these benchmarks prone to overfitting and memo-
052   rization, thus allowing models to perform well despite underlying weaknesses in logical reasoning.

135   2.1   E XISTING REASONING DATASETS FOR L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
136
137   Reasoning benchmarks are a vital part of LLM evaluation. Some benchmarks measure deductive
138   reasoning in conjunction with natural language inference (NLI), inductive reasoning, and common-
139   sense knowledge: HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019) tasks machines to select the most likely follow-
140   up of an event description, WinoGrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2021) is a pronoun resolution task, and
141
      MuSR (Sprague et al., 2023) tasks machines to solve fictional problems, such as murder myster-
      ies. Other benchmarks measure reasoning on domain knowledge: AI2 Reasoning Challenge (ARC)
142
      (Yadav et al., 2019) contains grade-school science questions, while Massive Multitask Language
143
      Understanding (MMLU) (Hendrycks et al., 2020) contains questions across 57 subjects in STEM,
144   humanities, and more. Finally, math-specific benchmarks include GSM-8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and
145   DROP (Dua et al., 2019).
146
147
      The aforementioned benchmarks explicitly evaluate skills beyond reasoning and do not specifically
      define the type of reasoning involved, e.g. inductive, deductive, and analogical. As such, bench-
148
      marks that solely test for deductive reasoning have seen a considerable increase in interest. They
149
      can be classified into two broad categories: synthetic and manually curated. Synthetic datasets in-
150   clude (i) CLUTRR (Sinha et al., 2019), where a machine must infer the relationship of two family
151   members based on stories, (ii) ProofWriter (Tafjord et al., 2020), where a machine must deduce a
152   statement’s truth value based on a set of facts and rules, and (iii) ProntoQA-OOD (Saparov et al.,
153   2024), where a machine must prove a statement based on a set of facts. Manually curated datasets
154   include (i) LogiQA 2.0 (Liu et al., 2023a), containing manually-translated logical reasoning ques-
155   tions from the Chinese Civil Service Exam, (ii) FOLIO (Han et al., 2022), containing questions with
156   manually-annotated content using Wikipedia pages, and (iii) ReClor (Yu et al., 2020), containing
157   reading comprehension questions from GMAT and LSAT.
158   As discussed earlier, synthetic datasets are prior knowledge independent and exhibit high argument
159   and low natural language complexity; manually curated datasets are the opposite. JustLogic, being
160   synthetic, contains all its advantages while offering the natural language complexity of manually
161   curated datasets. Further discussion on JustLogic’s complexity and prior knowledge independence
      can be found in Section 3.4 and 5.1 respectively.

180   2.2    R EASONING IN L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
181
182   As LLMs continue to increase in size, their performance on various reasoning-related benchmarks
183
      has improved dramatically. For example, in 2024, Gemini Ultra (Team et al., 2023) achieved 90.0%
      on MMLU when the SOTA model in 2020, UnifiedQA 11B (Khashabi et al., 2020), achieved a mere
184
      48.9%. In 2023, GPT-4 achieved 96.4% on ARC when the SOTA model in 2020, GPT-3 (Brown,
185
      2020), achieved 53.2%.
186
187   The advent of prompting techniques played an important role in developing LLMs’ reasoning abil-
188   ities. In-context learning (Dong et al., 2022) provides LLMs with instructions and examples in the
189
      input prompt to guide its response. Chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022) prompts LLMs to
      generate a series of intermediate reasoning steps before arriving at the final answer. Self-consistency
190
      decoding (Wang et al., 2022) chooses the most consistent answer after sampling multiple chain-of-
191
      thought outputs. Least-to-most prompting (Zhou et al., 2022) decomposes a complex problem into
192   simpler subproblems, which are then solved sequentially.
193
194   As mentioned above, LLMs are conventionally tested on datasets that combine reasoning with other
195
      skills. Moreover, existing logical reasoning-specific datasets possess major limitations that call into
      question the reliability of their evaluations. In response, JustLogic aims to robustly and accurately
196
      evaluate the deductive reasoning abilities of LLMs.

199   3     DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
200
201
      JustLogic is a programmatically generated dataset designed to evaluate a model’s ability of deductive
202   reasoning, specifically its capability to form logically valid arguments. A logically valid argument is
203   one where the conclusion necessarily follows from the premise(s); in other words, given the premises
204   are true, the conclusion must also be true.
205
      In order to test this, JustLogic presents a model with a paragraph consisting of premises, followed
206
      by a query statement. Based solely on the premises and assuming they are all true, the model needs
207
      to determine whether the query statement is true, false, or uncertain. In line with the open-world
208   assumption, the ”Uncertain” answer refers to cases where the premises neither support nor contradict
209   the query statement.
210
211
      The following outlines the process for generating each instance in the dataset:
212          1. Step 1: Generate an argument structure
213          2. Step 2: Add natural language statements to the argument structure
214          3. Step 3: Generate a query statement
215
      Figure 2 provides an example of this process, which we reference throughout the rest of this section.

254
      logism in Figure 2 respectively. If the intended depth has not been reached, one or more premises

289   3.3   S TEP 3: G ENERATE QUERY STATEMENT
290
291   The LLM’s task is to determine whether the given query statement is true, false, or uncertain based
292   on the premises provided. Using Figure 2 as an example, if we assign the query statement to be
293   the negation of the conclusion, i.e. ”It is not true that Japan is in Asia”, then the answer is false. If
294   the query statement is the same as the conclusion, then the answer is true. If the query statement is
295
      unrelated to the premises, then the answer is uncertain.

      Argument complexity. Argument complexity refers to the diversity of argument structures used
330
      in the dataset. A sufficiently high argument complexity is important because humans use a range
331   of argument forms to reason, beyond just conditionals and modus ponens. Moreover, a real-world
332   argument is typically composed of multiple argument forms, due to the inherent complexity of real-
333   life scenarios.
334
      We evaluate a dataset’s argument complexity based on two metrics: (i) the range of reasoning depth,
335
      and (ii) the number of unique argument structures. The upper limit of both metrics is calculated
336
      based on the theoretical maximum without any additional human input, rather than the highest depth
337   used in experiments in existing works. For example, CLUTRR’s dataset construction program can
338   generate any number of depths (referred to as relation length in the original paper), despite its ex-
339   periments only utilizing questions of up to a depth of 10. Thus, its upper limit of depth is infinite.
340

341
      area, as there is no upper limit to their reasoning depth and number of argument structures. Manually
342
      curated datasets, in contrast, either lack an explicit concept of reasoning depth and argument struc-
343   tures (e.g. LogiQA 2.0), or have a limited selection of both (e.g. FOLIO). While manual datasets
344   require significant human efforts and investment to expand their complexity, synthetic ones can scale
345   trivially.
346
      In summary, JustLogic combines the best aspects of both dataset construction methods, incorporat-
347
      ing the argument complexity of synthetic datasets and the natural language complexity of manually
348
      curated ones.

350   3.5   F UTURE - PROOFING J UST L OGIC
351
352   As the reasoning abilities of LLMs continue to improve, we expect LLMs to solve the existing
353
      JustLogic dataset eventually. As such, its difficulty level must be adjusted to remain relevant as a
      benchmark for deductive reasoning. We leverage JustLogic’s synthetic nature to increase complexity
354
      with minimal human input.
355
356   Argument complexity can be adjusted by (i) increasing the range of argument depth and (ii) increas-
357   ing the number of distinct argument forms to >7. Natural language complexity can be adjusted by
358   (i) increasing the number of expressions for each logical form and (ii) integrating a more complex
359
      knowledge base than GenericsKB. Importantly, these changes are programmatically achievable with
      minimal man-hours.

363
      We will first experimentally investigate the influence of prior knowledge on evaluating deductive
364   reasoning with existing benchmarks, using our test for prior knowledge independence. This validates
365   JustLogic’s ability to measure deductive reasoning without prior knowledge as a confounder. Next,
366   several SOTA LLMs of various parameter sizes are evaluated using JustLogic. Finally, an in-depth
367   error analysis of the LLMs’ results is conducted.
368
      JustLogic contains 7000 instances, with reasoning depths ranging from 1 to 7; each depth has
369
      1000 instances. It is then split into train/validation/test sets, with proportions of 70%/15%/15%
370   or 4900/1050/1050 instances. Train and validation sets facilitate in-context learning and model fine-
371   tuning if required, while the test set is used for evaluation. Note that the number of instances and
372   range of reasoning depths can be easily adjusted using JustLogic’s open-source dataset generation
373   program.

398   We evaluated various models of different sizes, including Llama3-8B (Dubey et al., 2024), Llama3-

      70B, GPT-4, GPT-4o, and OpenAI o1-preview (OpenAI, 2024b). Given that prompt quality signifi-
      cantly impacts LLM accuracy, a range of prompting techniques are tested: zero-shot, few-shot, and
400
      chain-of-thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022). OpenAI o1-preview had strict rate limits at the time of
401
      writing since it was released less than a month prior to manuscript submission. As such, 42 random
402   samples in the test set are used for OpenAI o1-preview. To ensure fairness, the selected subset has
403   the same proportion of reasoning depth and classes (True, False, and Uncertain) as the entire test
404   set. Further implementation details are provided in Appendix B.
405
      We also measured human performance. 18 anonymous participants, recruited from Amazon Me-
406
      chanical Turk 2 , are given a random subset of questions. This is because deductive reasoning ques-
407
      tions, especially those at high reasoning depths, are cognitively demanding and time-consuming; it
408   is impractical to expect humans to complete 1050 questions. To ensure fairness, both models and
409   participants are provided similar prompts and are given the same proportion of each reasoning depth.
410

      cally examining the heterogeneous effects of argument form and reasoning depth on model accuracy.

507
      Figure 3 illustrates the model accuracy by argument form (left) and by reasoning depth (right). It
508   shows the statistics for (i) Llama3-8B with CoT prompting, chosen for its superior performance

513

514   syllogism and constructive dilemma achieve considerably higher performance than modus tollens
515   and reductio ad absurdum. We hypothesize that these forms appear less frequently in the models’
516   training data. With less exposure to them, models may overlook these argument forms in favor of
517   more common ones during deductive reasoning, owing to the probabilistic nature of neural networks
518   (Fahlman & Hinton, 1987).
519

520   expectations that accuracy declines as the complexity of questions increases. Interestingly, Llama3-

522   sees a sharp decline in performance once depth is increased, while OpenAI o1-preview only sees
523   a moderate decline; OpenAI o1-previews’ superior performance is a result of better reasoning at
524   higher reasoning depths. This seems to suggest OpenAI o1-preview’s CoT prompting supports
525   deeper and longer lines of reasoning, which is crucial for deductive reasoning. Fluctuations on all
526   three trendlines are likely due to small sample sizes: OpenAI o1-preview has 6 samples per depth,
527   while the other 2 have 150. We expect the trend to be more explicit with a larger number of samples.

      6     C ONCLUSION
530   Deductive reasoning is one of the key challenges in LLM research. In response to the lack of reliable
531   benchmarks, we present JustLogic, a natural language deductive reasoning dataset that is (i) highly
532   complex, (ii) prior knowledge independent, and (iii) capable of in-depth error analysis. These qual-
533   ities are enabled by JustLogic’s dataset construction method: argument structures are synthetically
534   generated, and natural language is programmatically incorporated via expression templates and a
535   knowledge base. We empirically justify JustLogic’s merits. Moreover, most LLMs underperform
536
      the human average and all LLMs significantly underperform the human ceiling. We demonstrate
      that JustLogic is a highly challenging, future-proof benchmark that is reliable and insightful for
537
      evaluating logical reasoning in LLMs.

      C    I MPACT OF FACTUAL ACCURACY ON M ODEL P ERFORMANCE
703
704
      Given that JustLogic randomly chooses sentences from GenericsKB to add to each instance’s argu-
705
      ment structure, the final conclusion may be factually accurate or inaccurate in the real world. For
706   example, if the conclusion is “It is not true that Japan is in Asia.”, then the conclusion is factually
707   inaccurate. There is therefore a concern that models underperform due to confusion arising from
708   factually inaccurate conclusions. Moreover, since some conclusions are factually accurate, such
709   instances may exhibit artificially high performance.
710
      To study these concerns, we conduct the following empirical study. If the above concerns are true,
711
      we expect factually inaccurate conclusions to perform worse than factually accurate ones. Because
712   all GenericsKB sentences are factually accurate, we can straightforwardly deduce each conclusion’s
713   factual accuracy. For example, x ∨ y is factually accurate while ¬x is not.
714
