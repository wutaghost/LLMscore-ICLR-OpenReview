027                                               A BSTRACT
028
029            Current datasets for long-form video understanding often fall short of providing
030            genuine long-form comprehension challenges, as many tasks derived from these
031            datasets can be successfully tackled by analyzing just one or a few random frames
032            from a video. To address this issue, we present a novel dataset and benchmark,
033            CinePile, specifically designed for authentic long-form video understanding. This
034            paper details our innovative approach for creating a question-answer dataset, utiliz-
035            ing advanced LLMs with human-in-the-loop and building upon human-generated
036            raw data. Our comprehensive dataset comprises 305,000 multiple-choice questions
037
               (MCQs), covering various visual and multimodal aspects, including temporal com-
               prehension, understanding human-object interactions, and reasoning about events
038
               or actions within a scene. Additionally, we fine-tuned open-source Video-LLMs
039
               on the training split and evaluated both open-source and proprietary video-centric
040            LLMs on the test split of our dataset. The findings indicate that although current
041            models underperform compared to humans, fine-tuning these models can lead to
042            significant improvements in their performance.
043

045   1    I NTRODUCTION
046
047   Large multi-modal models offer the potential to analyze and understand long, complex videos.
048   However, training and evaluating models on video data offers difficult challenges. Most videos
049   contain dialogue and pixel data and complete scene understanding requires both. Furthermore, most
050
      existing vision-language models are pre-trained primarily on still frames, while understanding long
      videos requires the ability to identify interactions and plot progressions in the temporal dimension.
051
052   In this paper, we introduce CinePile, a large-scale dataset consisting of ∼ 305k question-answer
053   pairs from 9396 videos, split into train and test sets. Our dataset emphasizes question diversity,
      and topics span temporal understanding, perceptual analysis, complex reasoning, and more. It also


                                                        1

054
      emphasizes question difficulty, with humans exceeding the best commercial vision/omni models by
055   approximately 25%, and exceeding open source video understanding models by 37%.
056
057   We present a scene and a few question-answer pairs from our dataset in Fig. 1. Consider the
058
      first question, How does Gru’s emotional state transition throughout the
      scene? For a model to answer this correctly, it needs to understand both the visual
059
      and temporal aspects, and even reason about the plot progression of the scene. To an-
060
      swer the second question, What are the objects poking out of the book cover
061   and what is their purpose, the model must localize an object in time and space, and use
062   its world knowledge to reason about their purpose.
063
064
      CinePile addresses several weaknesses of existing video understanding datasets. First, the large size
      of CinePile enables it to serve as both an instruction-tuning dataset and an evaluation benchmark. We
065
      believe the ability to do instruction tuning for video at a large scale can bridge the gap between the
066
      open-source and commercial video understanding models. Also, the question diversity in CinePile
067   makes it a more comprehensive measure of model performance than existing benchmarks. Unlike
068   existing datasets, CinePile does not over-emphasize on purely visual questions (e.g., What color
069   is the car?), or on classification questions (e.g., What genre is the video?) that do
070   not require temporal understanding. Rather, CinePile is comprehensive with diverse questions about
071   vision, temporal, and narrative reasoning with a breakdown of question types to help developers
072   identify blind spots in their models.
073   The large size of CinePile is made possible by our novel pipeline for automated question generation
074   and verification using large language models. Our method leverages large existing sets of audio
075   descriptions that have been created to assist the vision impaired. We transcribe these audio descriptions
076   and align them with publicly available movie video clips from YouTube. Using this detailed human
077   description of scenes, powerful LLMs are able to create complex and difficult questions about the
078   whole video without using explicit video input. At test time, video-centric models must answer
079   these questions from only the dialogue and raw video, and will not have access to the hand-written
080   descriptions used to build the questions. We release the prompts for generating the question answers,
081
      the code for model evaluation, and the dataset splits in the Appendix.

083   2       C REATING A LONG VIDEO UNDERSTANDING BENCHMARK
084
085   Our dataset curation process has four primary components 1) Collection of raw video and related data.
086   2) Generation of question templates. 3) Automated construction of the Q&A dataset using video and
087   templates, and 4) Application of a refinement pipeline to improve or discard malformed Q&A pairs.

089   2.1      DATA COLLECTION AND CONSOLIDATION
090
      We obtain clips from English-language films from the YouTube channel MovieClips1 . This channel
091
      hosts self-contained clips, each encapsulating a major plot point, facilitating the creation of a dataset
092   focused on understanding and reasoning. Next, we collected Audio Descriptions from AudioVault2 .
093   Getting visual descriptions of video for free. Audio descriptions (ADs) are audio tracks for movies
094   that feature a narrator who explains the visual elements crucial to the story during pauses in dialogue.
095   They have been created for many movies to assist the vision impaired. The key distinction between
096   conventional video caption datasets and ADs lies in the contextual nature of the latter. In ADs,
097   humans emphasize the important visual elements in their narrations, unlike other video caption
098   datasets, which tend to be overly descriptive. We use the audio descriptions as a proxy for visual
099   annotation in the videos for our dataset creation.

      During early experiments, we found that giving a range of templates and scene-text-annotation
      to an LLM helped create more detailed, diverse, and well-formed questions. Thus, we adopted a
181
      template-based approach for question generation. Instead of limiting questions to a few hand-curated

183
184   Our starting point is approximately 30,000 human-curated questions from the MovieQA (Tapaswi
185   et al., 2016), TVQA (Lei et al., 2018), and Perception Test (Patraucean et al., 2024) datasets. We
186
      cluster these questions, select a few representatives per cluster, and then use GPT-4 to discern the
      underlying themes and write a prompt. First, we preprocess the questions by replacing first names
187
      and entities with pronouns, as BERT (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) embeddings over-index on proper
188
      nouns, hence the resultant clusters end up with shared names rather than themes. For instance, ‘Why
189   is Rachel hiding in the bedroom?’ is altered to ‘Why is she hiding in the bedroom?’. We used GPT-3.5
190   to do this replacement, as it handled noun replacement better than many open-source and commercial
191   alternatives. The modified questions are then embedded using WhereIsAI/UAE-Large-V1, a
192   semantic textual similarity model which is a top performer on the MTEB leaderboard3 . When the
193   first names were replaced, we observed significant repetition among questions, which prompted
194   us to duplicate them, ultimately resulting in 17,575 unique questions. We then perform k-means
195   clustering to categorize the questions into distinct clusters. We experimented with different values
196   of k = 10, 50, 100. Qualitatively, we found k = 50 to be an optimal number of clusters where
197
      the clusters are diverse and at the same time clusters are not too specific. For example, we see a
      ‘high-school dance’ cluster when k = 100, and these questions are merged into an ‘event’ cluster
198
      when we reduce k to 50. The Perception Test questions are less diverse as human annotators were
199
      restricted to creating questions based on a small number of themes, so we used k = 20 for this set.
200   The number of questions in each cluster ranges from 60 to 450. We selected 10 random questions from
201   each, and used them to prompt GPT-4 to create relevant question templates, as illustrated in Fig. 3.
202   We did ablations by selecting the closest 10 questions to the cluster center, however qualitatively
203   observed that random questions produced more general/higher quality templates.
204
      We generate four templates for each question cluster, resulting in around 300 templates across
205
      three datasets. We then manually reviewed all 300 templates, eliminating those that were
206   overly specific and merging similar ones. Overly specific templates and their proto-questions
207   looked like “Pre-wedding Dilemmas: What complicates character Z’s plans to
208   propose marriage to their partner?” and “Crime and Consequence: What is
209   the consequence of the character’s criminal actions?”. The authors also
210   added a many templates that were complimentary to the auto-generated ones. This process resulted
211   in 86 unique templates. Following that, we manually binned these into five high-level categories:
212   Character and Relationship Dynamics, Narrative and Plot Analysis, Thematic Exploration, Temporal,
213   and Setting and Technical Analysis. For a detailed discussion on the category definitions, examples
214
      of templates, and prototypical questions from each category, please refer to the Appendix C & D.

232   2.3     AUTOMATED QA GENERATION WITH LLM S
233   The pipeline for generating questions is shown in Fig. 4. While the question templates are general,
234   they might not be relevant to all the movie clips. Hence for a given scene, we choose a few relevant
235   question templates by providing Gemini with the scene-text-annotation of the scene, and asking to
236   shortlist the 20 most relevant templates to that scene, out of which we randomly select 5-6 templates.
237   We then provide a commercial language model with (i) the scene-text-annotation, which includes
238   both visual descriptions and dialogue, (ii) the selected question template names (e.g. ‘Physical
239   Possession’), (iii) the prototypical questions for the templates (e.g. “What is [Character Name]
240   holding”), and (iv) a system prompt asking it to write questions about the scene. Through rigorous
241
      experimentation, we devised a system prompt that makes the model attentive to the entire scene and
      is capable of generating deeper, longer-term questions as opposed to mere surface-level perceptual
242
      queries. We observed that providing the prototypical example prevents GPT-4 from hallucination, and
243
      also leads to more plausible multiple-choice question (MCQ) distractors. We also found that asking
244   the model to provide rationale for its answer enhances the quality of the questions. Additionally,
245   we found that including timestamps for the scene-text-annotation augments the quality of generated
246   temporal questions. Through this method, we were able to generate ≈ 32 questions per video.
247
      After experimenting with this pipeline, we analyzed the generated QA pairs and noticed a consistent
248
      trend: most questions are focused on reasoning or understanding. For diversity, we also wanted to
249
      include purely perceptual questions. To achieve this, we introduced additional hand-crafted prompt
250   templates for perceptual questions and also templates for temporal questions. While GPT-4 performs
251   well across all question templates, we found that Gemini excels particularly with perceptual templates.
252   Therefore, we utilized Gemini to generate a segment of perceptual questions in the dataset, while
253   using GPT-4 for reasoning templates. Our experiments with open-source models indicated subpar
254   question quality, despite extensive prompt tuning. We present example questions and a quantitative
255   investigation into the quality of the generations produced by GPT-4 and Gemini in Appendix E.
256   Moreover, we provide the prompt we use question-answer generation in Appendix L.

      2.4     DATASET QUALITY EVALUATION AND ADVERSARIAL REFINEMENT
258
259   While the process above consistently produces well-formed and answerable questions, we observed
260   that some questions are either trivial, with answers embedded within the question itself, or pertaining
261   to basic world concepts that do not require viewing the clip. To identify these, we evaluated our
262   dataset with the help of a few LLMs on the following axes and we improved the quality of those
263   whenever possible. In the few instances where this was not possible, we removed the questions from
264   the dataset or computed a metric that the users can use in the downstream tasks.
265   Degeneracy and educated guessing. A question is considered degenerate if the answer is im-
266   plicit in the question itself, e.g., What is the color of the pink house?. Similarly, an
267   educated guessing is the most probable answer to the question based on general knowledge, con-
268   text, or common sense, e.g. What is the bartender using the shaker for? a)
269   prepare a cocktail b) do groceries c) collect tips . Based on an investi-
      gation of a subset of the dataset, we found that such questions constituted only a small fraction.


                                                                                                  5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      However, since manually reviewing all the questions was impractical, we employed three distinct
271   language models (LMs) to identify weak Q&As: Gemini (Anil et al., 2023), GPT-3.5 (Achiam et al.,
272   2023), and Phi-1.5 (Li et al., 2023c). In order to do this, we presented only the questions and answer
273   choices to the models, omitting any context, and calculated the accuracy for each question across
274   multiple models. If multiple models with different pre-training or post-training setups all correctly
275   answer a question, it is likely that the answer was implicit, rather than due to biases of any one.
276
      Adversarial Refinement. After identifying weak Q&A pairs, wed an adversarial refinement process
277   to repair these Q&A pairs. The goal was to modify the questions and/or answer choices so that a
278   language model could no longer answer them correctly using only implicit clues within the question
279   and answer choices themselves. To achieve this, we used a large language model (LLM), referred
280   to as “deaf-blind LLM", to identify and explain why a question could be answered without extra
281   context. Specifically, when the LLM answered a question correctly, we asked it to provide a rationale
282   for its choice. This rationale helped us detect hidden hints or biases in the question. We then fed
283   this rationale into our question-generation model, instructing it to modify the question and/or answer
284   choices to eliminate these implicit clues. This process continued in a loop until the LLM could no
285
      longer answer the question correctly (after adjusting for chance performance), with a maximum of
      five attempts per question. Given the repetitive and computationally intensive nature of this process,
286
      we required a powerful yet accessible LLM that could run locally, avoiding issues with API limits,
287
      delays, and costs associated with cloud-based services. As a result, we selected LLaMA 3.1 70B
288   (Dubey et al., 2024), an open-source model that met these desiderata. Through this adversarial
289   refinement process, we successfully corrected approximately 90.94% of the weak Q&A pairs in the
290   training set and 90.24% of the weak Q&A pairs in the test set. Finally, we excluded the unfixable
291   Q&A pairs from the evaluation split (∼ 80 Q&A) of our dataset but retained them in the training set
292   (∼ 4500 Q&A). We share more details about adversarial refinement in Appendix Sec. N
293
      Vision Reliance. When generating the multiple-choice questions (MCQs), we considered the entire
294   scene without differentiating between visual text and dialogue. Consequently, some questions in the
295   dataset might be answerable solely based on dialogue, without the necessity of the video component.
296   For this analysis, we utilized the Gemini model. The model was provided with only the dialogue,
297   excluding any visual descriptions, to assess its performance. If the model correctly answers a question,
298   it is assigned a score of 0 for the visual dependence metric; if it fails, the score is set at 1. In later
299   sections, we present the distribution of the visual dependence scores across different MCQ categories.
300   Hardness. Hardness refers to the inability to answer questions, even when provided with full context
301   used to create the questions in the first place (i.e., the subtitles & visual descriptions). For this purpose,
302   we selected the Gemini model, given its status as one of the larger and more capable models. Unlike
303   accuracy evaluation, which uses only video frames and dialogues (subtitles), the hardness metric
304   includes visual descriptions as part of the context given to the model. After this, the authors reviewed
305   all the questions flagged as “hard" for verification and fixed any minor issues, if present.
306   In addition, the authors went through the question in the evaluation split across multiple iterations,
307   and fixed any systemic errors that arose in the pipeline. Furthermore, we conducted a human study to
308   identify potential weaknesses, and we discuss our findings in Appendix I.

356   4        M ODEL EVALUATION
357
358   In this section, we discuss the evaluations of various closed and open-source video LLMs on our
359   dataset, some challenges, and model performance trends. Given that our dataset consists of multiple-
360   choice question answers (MCQs), we assess a model’s performance by its ability to accurately select
361   the correct answer from a set of options containing one correct answer and four distractors. A key
362   challenge in this process is reliably parsing the model’s response to extract its chosen answer and
363
      map it to one of the predefined choices. Model responses may vary in format, including additional
      markers or a combination of the option letter and corresponding text. Such variations necessitate
364
      a robust post-processing step to accurately extract and match the model’s response to the correct
365
      option. To address these variations, we employ a two-stage evaluation method. First, a normalization
366   function parses the model’s response, extracting the option letter (A-E) and any accompanying text.
367   This handles various formats, ensuring accurate identification. The second stage involves comparing
368   the normalized response with the answer key, checking for both the option letter and text. If both
369   match, a score of one is awarded; However, if only the option letter or text appears, the comparison is
370   limited to the relevant part, and the score is assigned accordingly.

457   Why are (some) OSS models so far behind? We conducted further analyses to understand the poor
458   performance of some open-source models, focusing on qualitative evaluations of their raw responses
459   (Appendix H). Our findings indicate that a primary issue is their inability to follow instructions, often
460   generating irrelevant or repetitive content, which hinders accurate extraction of the intended answer.
461   To quantify these deviations, we introduced two alternative strategies for computing accuracy: a)
462   Embedding Similarity Matching: We compute the similarity between the model’s raw response and
463
      the various answer options within the embedding space of a sentence transformer (Zhang et al.,
      2019). The most similar option is selected as the predicted answer. b) GPT-4 as a judge: We use
464
      GPT-4 (Zheng et al., 2023) as an evaluator to extract the predicted answer key from the model’s raw
465
      response. The results from these strategies are illustrated in Figure 7a. We observe that although these
466   alternative evaluation strategies yield an improvement in the models’ performance, their accuracy
467   still falls significantly short compared to the best-performing open-source models. This suggests
468   that the underperformance cannot be solely attributed to an inability to follow instructions. Rather,
469   these models also exhibit fundamental limitations in video understanding capabilities. Notably, the
470   two alternative evaluation strategies—embedding similarity matching and the use of GPT-4 as a
471   judge—are highly consistent with each other, as well as largely aligning with the rankings obtained
472   from the original response extraction strategy. We provide further details and additional results
473   based on traditional video-caption evaluation metrics, such as BertScore (Zhang et al., 2019), CIDEr
474
      (Vedantam et al., 2015), and ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004), in Appendix H.
475   CinePile’s train-split helps improve performance In this section, we investigate the impact of
476   CinePile ’s training split in enhancing the performance of open-source video LLMs. We selected
477   Video-LLaVa as the baseline and fine-tuned it using CinePile ’s training data. For efficient training,
478   we load the model using 4-bit quantization. During fine-tuning, we freeze the base model, and
479   conduct training using Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021). We fine-tuned the model for
480
      5 epochs using the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017). We compare the performance
      of the fine-tuned Video-LLaVa against the base model, as shown in 7b. Our results indicate that
481
      fine-tuning led to an approximate 71% improvement in performance (increasing accuracy from
482
      25.72% to 44.16%), with gains observed consistently across all question subcategories. These results
483   demonstrate the significant utility of CinePile’s training split in enhancing model performance.

      5    R ELATED W ORK
487
488   LVU (Wu & Krähenbühl, 2021), despite being one of the early datasets proposed for long video
489   understanding, barely addresses the problem of video understanding as the main tasks addressed in
490
      this dataset are year, genre classification or predicting the like ratio for the video. A single frame
      might suffice to answer the questions and these tasks cannot be considered quite as “understanding”
491
      tasks. MovieQA (Tapaswi et al., 2016) is one of the first attempts to create a truly understanding QA
492
      dataset, where the questions are based on entire plot the movie but not localized to a single scene. On
493   closer examination, very few questions are vision focused and most of them can be answered just
494   based on dialogue. EgoSchema (Mangalam et al., 2024) is one of the recent benchmarks, focused
495   on video understanding which requires processing long enough segments in the video to be able to
496   answer the questions. However, the videos are based on egocentric videos and hence the questions
497   mostly require perceptual knowledge, rather than multimodal reasoning. Another recent benchmark,
498   Perception Test (Patraucean et al., 2024), focuses on core perception skills, such as memory and
499   abstraction, across various reasoning abilities (e.g., descriptive, predictive, etc) for short-form videos
500   that they collected by first preparing explicit video scripts. The MAD dataset introduced in (Soldan
501
      et al., 2022) and expanded in (Han et al., 2023) contains dialogue and visual descriptions for full-
      length movies and is typically used in scene captioning tasks rather than understanding. Another
502
      issue is this dataset does not provide raw visual data, they share only [CLS] token embeddings,
503
      which makes it hard to use. TVQA (Lei et al., 2018) is QA dataset based on short 1-min clips from
504   famous TV shows. The annotators are instructed to ask What/How/Why sort of questions combining
505   two or more events in the video. MoVQA (Zhang et al., 2023b) manually curates questions across
506   levels multiple levels—single scene, multiple scenes, full movie— by guiding annotators to develop
507   queries in predefined categories like Information Processing, Temporal Perception, etc. CMD (Bain
508   et al., 2020) proposes a text-to-video retrieval benchmark while VCR (Zellers et al., 2019) introduces
509   a commonsense reasoning benchmark on images taken from movies. Long video understanding
510   datasets, such as EpicKitchens (Damen et al., 2018), tend to concentrate heavily on tasks related
511   to the memory of visual representations, rather than on reasoning skills. More recently, multiple
512
      benchmarks focusing on long video understanding have been released, such as Video-MME (Fu
      et al., 2024), MVBench (Li et al., 2024), and LVBench (Wang et al., 2024), all having videos from
513
      multiple domains such as movies, sports, etc. Most of these datasets require significant human effort
514
      to generate questions, with costs increasing as you move toward longer video regimes. Hence, most
515   of them range on a scale of a few thousand question-answer pairs (while CinePile ranges 70-75 ×
516   more). We discuss works utilizing synthetic data for dataset creation in Appendix B.
517
518
      CinePile differs from all the above datasets, having longer videos and many questions to capture the
      perceptual, temporal, and reasoning aspects of a video. And it is truly multimodal where the person
519
      has to watch the video as well as dialogues to answer many questions. Unlike the previous datasets
520
      with fixed templates, we automated this process on previously human-generated questions, this let us
521   capture many more question categories compared to previous works. Lastly, our approach to dataset
522   generation is scalable, allowing us to fine-tune video models to improve performance. Moreover,
523   CinePile can easily be extended in the future with additional videos, question categories, and more.
524
525

      6    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
527   In this paper, we introduced CinePile, a unique long video understanding dataset and benchmark,
528   featuring ∼ 300k questions in the training set and ∼ 5000 in the test split. We detailed a novel
529   method for curating and filtering this dataset, which is both scalable and cost-effective. Additionally,
530   we benchmarked various recent commercial video-centric LLMs and conducted a human study to
531
      gauge the achievable performance on this dataset. To our knowledge, CinePile is the only large-
      scale dataset that focuses on multi-modal understanding, as opposed to the purely visual reasoning
532
      addressed in previous datasets. Our fine-tuning experiments demonstrate the quality of our training
533
      split. Additionally, we plan to set up a leaderboard for the test set, providing a platform for new video
534   LLMs to assess and benchmark their performance on CinePile.
535
536   Despite its strengths, there are still a few areas for improvement in our dataset, such as the incorpo-
537
      ration of character grounding in time. While we believe our dataset’s quality is comparable to or
      even better than that of a Mechanical Turk annotator, we acknowledge that a motivated human, given
538
      sufficient time, can create more challenging questions than those currently generated by an LLM.
539
      Our goal is to narrow this gap in future iterations of CinePile.

923   B       A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK
924
925   Synthetic data with human in the loop. Training models on synthetic data is a popular paradigm in
926   recent times. We have seen many advances in generation as well as usage on synthetic data in recent
927
      times, both in vision Wood et al. (2021); Bordes et al. (2024); Tian et al. (2023); Hemmat et al.
      (2023) and language Taori et al. (2023); Maini et al. (2024); Li et al. (2023c); Yuan et al. (2024); Wei
928
      et al. (2023). For instance, Self-Instruct Wang et al. (2022) proposes a pipeline to create an
929
      instruction dataset based on a few instruction examples and categories defined by humans. We
930   mainly derived inspiration and the fact that modern LLMs are quite good at understanding long text
931   and creating question-answer pairs. UltraChat Ding et al. (2023) is another synthetic language
932   dataset which is created by using separate LLMs to iteratively generate opening dialogue lines,
933   simulate user queries, and provide responses. This allows constructing large-scale multi-turn
934   dialogue data without directly using existing internet data as prompts. Additionally, Evol-Instruct Xu
935   et al. (2023), automatically generates a diverse corpus of open-domain instructions of varying
936   complexities by prompting an LLM and applying iterative evolution operations like in-depth
937   evolving (adding constraints, deepening, etc.) and in-breadth evolving (generating new instructions).
938
      To our knowledge, we are among the first to apply automated template generation and question
      synthesis techniques to vision and video modalities using LLMs.

941   C       A DDITIONAL QA G ENERATION D ETAILS
942
943   In addition to the hand-crafted perceptual templates, we also create long-form question and answers
944   based on a scene’s visual summary. To achieve this, we first generate a visual summary of a video
945   clip. Then, we prompt the model to create question-answers solely based on that summary.
946   We create a pure visual summary of the scene by using a vision LLM, similar to some of the recent
947   worksWang et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023a). First, we use a shot detection algorithm to pick the
948   important frames4 , then we annotate each of these frames with Gemini vision API
949   (gemini-pro-vision). We ablated many SOTA open-source vision LLMs such as Llava
950   1.5-13B Liu et al. (2023), OtterHD Li et al. (2023a), mPlug-Owl Ye et al. (2023b) and MinGPT-4 Zhu
951   et al. (2023), along with Gemini and GPT-4V (GPT-4-1106-vision-preview). While
952   GPT-4V has high fidelity in terms of image captioning, it is quite expensive. Most of the open-source
953   LLM captions are riddled with hallucinations. After qualitatively evaluating across many scenes, we
954
      found that Gemini’s frame descriptions are reliable and they do not suffer too much from
      hallucination. Once we have frame-level descriptions, we then pass the concatenated text to Gemini
955
      text model gemini-pro and prompt it to produce a short descriptive summary of the whole scene.
956
      Even though Gemini’s scene visual summary is less likely to have hallucinated elements, we however
957   spotted a few hallucinated sentences. Hence all the MCQs generated using this summary are added
958   only to the training split but not to the eval split.

979    Character and Relationship Dynamics: This category would include templates that focus on the

986    symbols, motifs, and subtext within the movie. It would also cover aspects such as moral dilemmas,

       Setting and Technical Analysis: This category would encompass templates that focus on the setting,

       environment, and technical aspects of the movie. It would include templates that analyze the location

       of characters and objects, the use of props, the impact of interactions on the environment, and the
991    description and function of objects.

1801   Adjusting for chance performance: While refining questions in our adversarial refinement pipeline,
1802   one concern was that the deaf-blind LLM might only get the right answer by chance. Since our
1803   problem involves a multiple-choice QA setup, there is a 25% chance that questions could be
1804
       answered correctly by a random baseline. Similarly, it was possible that the LLM got the wrong
       answer due to chance, even though it would be expected to answer correctly the majority of the time.
1805

1806
       different permutations of the choice order, rotating the options clockwise. We considered the
1807   refinement successful only if the LLM failed to answer the question correctly in the majority of cases,
1808   i.e., at least three out of five times. If the refinement failed, we repeated the process up to five times,
1809   although this is a hyperparameter that can be adjusted based on available computational resources.

1948   To ensure that the questions in our dataset capture a wide range of aspects, we take the following
1949   steps. Firstly, rather than applying fixed templates for every video, we automatically select relevant
1950   ones from a diverse bank of 86 templates tailored to various aspects, such as Character Reaction
1951
       Insight, Event Sequence Ordering, and Moral Dilemma Exploration. Thus, different videos receive
       different templates, ensuring diversity across the dataset. Secondly, the question generation process
1952
       is guided by detailed prompts that incorporate both the chosen template and the specific video clip
1953
       context. As a result, even when the same template is used, the questions vary significantly based on
1954   the unique characters, actions, and environments in each video. For example, the questions “How
1955   does the decision to buy the coffee machine and the Harry Potter collection lead to a significant
1956   consequence in the video?” and “What early tactical trait of Barcelona hinted at their ultimate
1957   attacking strategy?” both stem from the "Causal Chain Analysis" template but differ greatly in
1958   wording and focus due to the distinct video contexts. This approach contrasts with other datasets
1959   relying on human annotators, which often limit template categories (e.g., Perception Test uses four
1960   template areas) for human labeling feasibility.

2040   We had previously discussed one of the reasons for why are (some) OSS models so far behind in
2041   Sec. 4 of the main paper, where we found that, for extremely poorly performing models (sub 20%
2042   overall performance), it was partly due to their inability to follow instructions as we both qualitatively

2045
2046
2047   Does Scale (In Parameter Space) Alone Lead to Better Performance? There is a lot of focus on
2048   model scale these days, so we were curious whether scale alone can lead to better performance
2049   (ignoring the architecture, training data, etc). So we computed the Pearson-r correlation between the
2050   model scale and overall performance and found it to be weakly positively correlated i.e., 0.157.
2051   Obviously, there are alot of confounders across different models like different training data,
       architecture, etc, so this is not definitely saying that scale would not improve significantly

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2052
       performance, rather it alone is not enough. If we control for everything else by only analyzing one
2053   particular model family i.e., InternVL, we see a positive correlation of 0.72.
2054
2055
2056
2057   Poor ability to utilize visual information; and overdependence on LLM-priors Another
2058
       possible reason for the performance gap in open-source models could be their weaker reliance on
       visual information and over-reliance on language priors (Tong et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2023). In our
2059
       experiments (see Appendix Sec. K.1) examining the effect of model performance on the number of
2060
       sampled frames, we observe that while models improve with additional frames, the extent of this
2061   improvement correlates with the model’s overall performance. Specifically, better-performing
