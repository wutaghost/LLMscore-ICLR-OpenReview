009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Reconstructing controllable Gaussian splats from monocular video is a challeng-
012            ing task due to its inherently insufficient constraints. Widely adopted approaches
013            supervise complex interactions with additional masks and control signal annota-
014            tions, limiting their real-world applications. In this paper, we propose an anno-
015            tation guidance-free method, dubbed FreeGaussian, that mathematically derives
016            dynamic Gaussian motion from optical flow and camera motion using novel dy-
017            namic Gaussian constraints. By establishing a connection between 2D flows and
018            3D Gaussian dynamic control, our method enables annotation-free optimization
019            and continuity of dynamic Gaussian motions from flow priors. Furthermore, we
020
               introduce a 3D spherical vector controlling scheme, which represents the state
               with a 3D Gaussian trajectory, thereby eliminating the need for complex 1D con-
021
               trol signal calculations and simplifying controllable Gaussian modeling. Quanti-
022
               tative and qualitative evaluations on extensive experiments demonstrate the state-
023            of-the-art visual performance and control capability of our method. Project page:
024            https://freegaussian.github.io.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Controllable view synthesis (CVS) aims to recover the 3D structure and interactable motions of
030   a scene given a set of input views, which has garnered significant attention in various research
031   fields, including content creation (Liao et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024b), virtual
032   reality (Steuer, 1992; Kerbl et al., 2023a; Waisberg et al., 2023) and robotic simulator (Huang et al.,
033   2023; Qu et al., 2024; Lou et al., 2024). Mainstream methods Yu et al. (2023a); Fridovich-Keil et al.
034   (2023) have recently achieved high-quality real-time rendering via 3D Gaussian representation Kerbl
035   et al. (2023b) and extended to scene-level using large-scale annotated datasets (Qu et al., 2024).
036   Despite the impressive advances, a significant obstacle remains: the severe dependence on man-
037   ual annotations hinders the practical application of mainstream methods. Existing methods either
038   segment Gaussian ellipsoids in interactive regions via mask-based reprojection Yu et al. (2023a) or
039   input control signals to jointly model neural radiance fields Kania et al. (2022); Fridovich-Keil et al.
040   (2023); Qu et al. (2024). Without mask or control signal supervision in the training data, the model
041   collapses, failing to decode the feature to color and losing scene control capabilities. Manual annota-
042
      tion guidance such as mask and control signal has become an indispensable and stringent condition
      for existing methods and datasets.
043
044   To address this challenge, we propose FreeGaussian, a guidance-free but effective Gaussian splat-
045   ting method for controllable scene reconstruction, which automatically explores interactable struc-
046   tures and restores controllable scenes from successive frames, without any manual annotations. Our
047   novel insight is that dynamic Gaussian flow under instantaneous motion can be analytically de-
048
      rived from optical flow and camera motion via differential analysis. It enables us to track dynamic
      Gaussian motion solely relying on camera views in the training process, which allows for localizing
049
      controllable structures and providing continuous optimization constraints. This innovation stream-
050
      lines existing controllable view synthesis methods by introducing flow-based priors, eliminating the
051   need for annotations and improving their real-world applicability.
052
053   More specifically, in the training stage, FreeGaussian directly derive dynamic Gaussians flow from
      2D image optical flow and camera-induced camera flow, accumulated with Gaussian projection

069
070   • We propose FreeGaussian, a novel annotations guidance-free Gaussian Splatting method for

084   4D Novel View Synthesis. Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2020) has innovated
085   great progress in dynamic scene reconstruction. The existing methods can be categorized into three
086
      primary categories: time-varying, deformable-canonical, and hybrid representation methods. The
      time-varying methods (Du et al., 2021; Fang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2021; Park et al., 2021a; Pumarola
087
      et al., 2021; Tretschk et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2021) directly model the radiance field over time and
088
      enhance the temporal information with time embedding, scene flow and etc. While, the deformable-
089   canonical methods(Gao et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Park et al., 2021b; Xian et al., 2021) decouple
090   the 4D field into dynamic deformable fields and static canonical spaces, querying canonical features
091   by warped coondinates. In contrast, hybrid representation methods (Shao et al., 2023; Fridovich-
092   Keil et al., 2023; Cao & Johnson, 2023; Song et al., 2023) have achieved high-quality reconstruction
093   and fast rendering by exploiting time-space feature planes, dynamic voxels, and 4D hash encoding.
094
      In contrast to fitting complex dynamic scenes with MLPs, 3D Gaussians Splatting (Kerbl et al.,
095   2023b) has emerged as a popular choice recently, owing to the superior training efficiency and ultra-
096   high-quality rendering speeds. Related progress typically learn dense Gaussian movements (Yang
097   et al., 2023; Luiten et al., 2024) directly, leverage feature planes (Wu et al., 2023) or learnable motion
098   basis (Kratimenos et al., 2023) for better rendering quality, or introduce flow loss (Guo et al., 2024)
099   to enhancing different paradigms of dynamic 3DGS. More recently, S4D (He et al., 2024) introduced
100   a generalized streaming pipeline that leverages Gaussians and 3D control points to reconstruct 4D
101   real-world scenes.
102   Controllable Scene Representation. Decoupling color, occupancy, geometry from time provides
103   increased flexibility over 4D reconstruction, with significant implications for digital humans (Rivero
104   et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023) and simulators (Qu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). CoNeRF (Kania
105   et al., 2022) pioneered this effort by extending HyperNeRF (Park et al., 2021b) and regressing the
106   attribute and the mask to enable few-shot attribute control. CoNFies (Yu et al., 2023b) propose a
107   controllable representation for face self-portraits by utilizing AU intensities and facial landmarks.
      EditableNeRF (Zheng et al., 2023) introduces detection key points and joint weights optimization. In

242   3.3       S ELF - GUIDED C ONTROL WITH DYNAMIC 3DGS
243
      Based on the discussion in Section. 3.2, dynamic Gaussian flow constraint eq. (4) provides contin-
244
      uous Gaussian constraints and, critically, exposes the position of interactive areas, whose changing
245   topological structures in dynamic scenes are reflected in varying Gaussian. To overcome the severe
246   dependence on mask annotations in existing methods, we propose leveraging dynamic Gaussian flow
247   to explore dynamic Gaussians of interactive objects and extract their trajectories for joint training:
248
249
      Dynamic Gaussian clustering and tracking. With the formulations in eq. (4), we pretrain a de-
      formable 3DGS G↓ with a set of camera streams first. Then dynamic Gaussian flow uGS from eq. (4)
250
      can be extracted frame-by-frame and binaried to obtain flow maps. By back-projecting the flow maps
251
      to identify dynamic 3D Gaussians, we highlight Gaussians D = {gi | i = 1, 2, . . . , Q} with sharp
252   dynamics, as illustrated in Figure. 1. Next, we use unsupervised clustering algorithm DBSCAN
253   to group dynamic Gaussians into clusters C = {ci | i = 1, 2, . . . , K}, where K is the number of
254   interactive objects. The cluster centers evolve over time, generating continuous trajectories ε(t, k),
255   where k indexing which objects the trajectory belongs to.
256
      3D Spherical Vector Control. Conventional methods using a 1D state variable to describe object
257
      state changes are limited by the reliance on prior knowledge or Gaussian trajectory fitting, and their
258
      inability to accurately capture dynamic changes. We overcome these limitations by representing the
259   Gaussian states with 3D spherical vectors, which can be directly obtained from dynamic Gaussian
260   tracking trajectory. This technique eliminates the requirement of control signals and curve fitting
261   while increasing control flexibility.
262
      Specifically, in the training stage, we represent the Gaussian dynamics state using cluster trajectory
263
      coordinates vci = ε(t, k)↑ε(0, k), concatenated with Gaussian centers Xi . Then, we encode the co-

302   Implementation details. FreeGaussian is implemented based on nerfstudio (Tancik et al., 2023)
303   and gsplat (Ye et al., 2024). We use RAFT Teed & Deng (2020); Contributors (2021) for optical flow
304   prediction and perform DBSCAN clustering from dynamic Gaussian flow with Euclidean metric,
305   ϱ = 0.05 and minimal samples = 5. The cluster center corresponding to each Gaussian is encoded
306
      with hash grids and decoded with an 8-layer MLP with 256 neurons. The model is trained on an
      NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 GPU for 60k steps, using Adam optimizer with learning rate 1.6e↔4
307
      and batch size 1. The coarse-to-fine training process lasts 30 minutes and is divided into 3 stages,
308
      including 500 steps of canonical warmup, 30k steps 4D deformable training, and 30k steps of full
309   training. For all experiments, we set loss weights of LRGB , LD-SSIM , and LuGS as ϑ = 0.8, (1 ↑ ϑ) =
310   0.2, and ϖ = 0.5, respectively.

471   5      C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
472
473   In this work, we draw the mathematical connection among optical flow, camera motion, and dynamic
474   Gaussian flow with differential analysis, and introduce a guidance-free Gaussian Splatting method
475   for controllable view synthesis. By leveraging the flow constraints, we refine Gaussian optimization,
476   enabling accurate continuous Gaussian motion dynamic constraints. It not only guarantee smooth
477   motion and improves rendering quality but also highlights interactable Gaussians and eliminates the
478   severe dependence on manual annotations. We further introduce a 3D spherical vector controlling
479   scheme, simplifying and accelerating interactive Gaussian modeling by bypassing the 3D Gaussian
480   trajectory as a state representation. Extensive experiments demonstrate our superior performance
481
      in both view synthesis and scene controlling, enabling more accurate and efficient modeling of
      interactable content.
482
483   Limitations: FreeGaussian relies on optical flow estimators, and may compromise view synthe-
484   sis or control robustness in lighting variation interactive environments. Future work will focus on
485   improving the robustness of lighting variation scenes and extending the method to handle more
      challenging scenarios.

      Implementation Details. FreeGaussian is implemented based on nerfstudio (Tancik et al., 2023)
      and gsplat (Ye et al., 2024). We use RAFT Teed & Deng (2020); Contributors (2021) for optical flow
779
      prediction and perform DBSCAN clustering from dynamic Gaussian flow with Euclidean metric,
780
      ϱ = 0.05 and minimal samples = 5. The cluster center corresponding to each Gaussian is encoded
781   with hash grids and decoded with an 8-layer MLP with 256 neurons. The model is trained on an
782   NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 GPU for 60k steps, using Adam optimizer with learning rate 1.6e↔4
783   and batch size 1. The coarse-to-fine training process lasts 30 minutes and is divided into 3 stages,
784   including 500 steps of canonical warmup, 30k steps 4d deformable training, and 30k steps of full
785   training. For all experiments, we set loss weights of LRGB , LD-SSIM , and LuGS as ϑ = 0.8, (1 ↑ ϑ) =
786   0.2, and ϖ = 0.5, respectively.
787
      The CUDA implementation of the proposed Dynamic Gaussian Flow Constrain is based on
788   gsplat (Ye et al., 2024). We implemented a minimal modification to the source code, creating a
789   mapping from pixel coordinates to Gaussian sphere identifiers and their associated weights. Due to
790   the potential intersection of pixel coordinates with numerous Gaussian splats, we opted to store the
791   top 50 Gaussian ellipsoid indices per pixel and perform reweighting with wi = !50  Ti ωi
                                                                                                 as neces-
                                                                                         i Ti ωi
792   sary. Backpropagation only updates the gradients of associated weights, not the pixel coordinates to
793   Gaussian mapping.
794
795   Dynamic Gaussian Clustering. Gaussian clustering would impact the control ability of the model,
796
      which in turn is directly influenced by the quality of the back-projected flow map. We configure the
      frame interval to be 1 and establish correspondences between the optical flows of adjacent frames.
797
      By leveraging eq. (3), we compute the Gaussian interaction flow. Next, by randomly sampling 5% of
798
      the interaction flow map as keyframes, we perform back-projection and apply DBSCAN clustering
799   to obtain dynamic Gaussians. Small keyframe ratios lead to incomplete clustering, while a 5%
800   ratio is sufficient for achieving better clustering results. Conversely, higher ratios result in noisy
801   clustering, which hinders subsequent control.
