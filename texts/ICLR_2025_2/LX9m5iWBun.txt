009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we investigate the theoretical aspects of sampling from strongly
012            log-concave distributions defined on convex and compact supports. We propose
013            a general proximal framework that involves projecting onto the constrained set,
014            which is highly flexible and supports various projection options. Specifically, we
015            consider the cases of Euclidean and Gauge projections, with the latter having the
016            advantage of being performed efficiently using a membership oracle. This frame-
017
               work can be seamlessly integrated with multiple sampling methods. Our analysis
               focuses on Langevin-type sampling algorithms within the context of constrained
018
               sampling. We provide nonasymptotic upper bounds on the W1 and W2 errors, of-
019
               fering a detailed comparison of the performance of these methods in constrained
020            sampling.

037   Solving this constrained sampling problem is challenging and has garnered considerable interest
038   across various fields, including computer science and statistics. In the realm of computer science, a
039
      line of research initiated by Dyer et al. (1991) explored polynomial-time algorithms for uniformly
      sampling convex bodies. This has been followed by seminal studies on the convergence proper-
040
      ties of the Ball Walk and the Hit-and-Run algorithm toward uniform density on a convex body
041
      or, more broadly, to log-concave densities (Kannan et al., 1997; Kook et al., 2024; Lovász, 1999;
042   Lovász & Simonovits, 1993; Lovász & Vempala, 2007; Smith, 1984). Other Markov Chain Monte
043   Carlo (MCMC) methods, such as Gibbs sampling (Gelfand et al., 1992) and Hamiltonian Monte
044   Carlo (Brubaker et al., 2012; Gürbüzbalaban et al., 2022; Kook et al., 2022), have also been adapted
045   and enhanced to sample from distributions defined on convex and compact sets.
046
      In recent years, leveraging optimization techniques to facilitate sampling has become a prevalent ap-
047
      proach. By formulating the sampling challenge as an optimization problem, methods like projected
048   stochastic gradient descent (Bubeck et al., 2015; 2018; Lamperski, 2021; Lehec, 2023), proximal
049   approaches (Brosse et al., 2017; Durmus et al., 2018; Salim & Richtárik, 2020), particle-based al-
050   gorithms Li et al. (2022), and mirror descent (Ahn & Chewi, 2021; Chewi et al., 2020; Hsieh et al.,
051   2018; Zhang et al., 2020) have proven effective in navigating the target distribution to generate sam-
052   ples. Further innovations have emerged from the intersection of deep learning and neural networks,
053   leading to the development of novel sampling techniques via generative adversarial networks (Good-
      fellow et al., 2014) and variational autoencoders (Kingma & Welling, 2013). These advanced

      In this work, we tackle the challenge posed by a non-smooth target density ⌫ by employing a
      proximal method. This method leverages a regularization technique that involves projecting onto
059
      set K, effectively transforming the constrained sampling problem into an unconstrained one. Our
060
      framework is versatile, accommodating various projection options, such as the Euclidean projec-
061   tion—which corresponds to the Moreau envelope of the indicator function `K (Rockafellar & Wets,
062   2009; Durmus et al., 2018; Brosse et al., 2017; Pereyra, 2016)—and the Gauge projection (Lu et al.,
063   2022; Mhammedi, 2022), which can be efficiently performed using a membership oracle. Specif-
064   ically, we introduce a smooth and strongly convex surrogate distribution that closely mimics the
065   target density. By fine-tuning the regularization parameter, we can significantly reduce approxima-
066   tion errors. The advantageous properties of our surrogate distribution enable the effective application
067   of various sampling techniques. In this work, we specifically explore the vanilla Langevin Monte
068   Carlo (LMC) (Roberts & Tweedie, 1996; Dalalyan, 2017; Durmus & Moulines, 2017; Erdogdu &
069
      Hosseinzadeh, 2021; Mousavi-Hosseini et al., 2023; Raginsky et al., 2017; Erdogdu et al., 2018;
      Mou et al., 2022; Erdogdu et al., 2022), kinetic Langevin Monte Carlo (KLMC) (Cheng et al., 2018;
070
      Dalalyan & Riou-Durand, 2020; Shen & Lee, 2019; Ma et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023), and the
071
      parallelized versions of the midpoint randomization method for these algorithms (Shen & Lee, 2019;
072   Yu & Dalalyan, 2024; He et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2023) (referred to as pRLMC and pRKLMC, re-
073   spectively), within the context of constrained sampling. We derive the convergence rates for these
074   algorithms in Wasserstein-1 and Wasserstein-2 distances, and provide a detailed comparison of their
075   performance. To this end, we make the following contributions.
076
077          • We establish both general upper and lower bounds for the distance between the smooth
078            approximation of the target density and its original form in Wasserstein-q distance for any
079            q > 1. These bounds are detailed in Proposition 2.1 and Proposition 2.2, respectively.
080          • In Section 2, we demonstrate that our proposed framework can seamlessly incorporate
081            various projection options, including both Euclidean and Gauge projections.
082          • In Section 3, we incorporate several MCMC sampling methods, such as (kinetic) Langevin
083            Monte Carlo and the parallelized midpoint randomization method for these algorithms,
084            into our general framework. We present a detailed convergence analysis of these methods
085            in both Wasserstein-1 and Wasserstein-2 distances.
086
087
      In summary, we develop a comprehensive framework specifically designed for sampling from con-
      vex and compact sets, utilizing a regularization technique that involves projecting onto these con-
088
      strained sets. Our study notably presents an improved error bound for LMC in constrained sampling
089
      settings, as well as the first convergence analysis for KLMC, pRLMC, and pRKLMC algorithms in
090   this scenario. We emphasize that the convergence analysis for these three algorithms relies heavily
091   on the smooth properties of the target density—conditions that are not met in the constrained set-
092   ting we examine. Our proposed framework addresses these challenges and is exceptionally flexible
093   and adaptable, accommodating various projection operators and sampling methods. Additionally, it
094   enables clear comparisons of the behaviors of different sampling methods in constrained sampling
095   scenarios. Overall, this new framework provides valuable theoretical insights into the dynamics of
096   constrained sampling.

497   4    D ISCUSSION
498
499   Strongly convexity In this work, we focus on a strongly convex potential function f coupled
500   with specific choices of projection operators, resulting in a strongly log-concave surrogate density
501
      ⌫ . However, it is important to note that the approximation error bound between the target density
      ⌫ and the surrogate density ⌫ , as stated in Proposition 2.1, depends solely on the convexity of f .
502
      By relaxing the strong convexity requirement of f and considering alternative projection operators,
503
      one can generate a surrogate density ⌫ that is less restrictive than strongly log-concave. This
504   modification enables the extension of these findings to a broader spectrum of sampling methods.
505
506   Other metrics The Wasserstein distance we employ is a natural metric for measuring sampling
507
      errors due to its relevance to optimal transport theory. However, recent advancements in gradient-
      based sampling have investigated other metrics, including total variation distance, KL divergence,
508
      and 2 divergence. An intriguing avenue for future research would be to establish error guarantees
509
      for constrained sampling concerning these alternative metrics.
510
511   Limitation The primary focus of this work is to provide theoretical insights into the analysis of
512   constrained sampling. We hope that these insights pave the way for empirical evaluations of the
513
      performance of Langevin-type algorithms in various settings, as well as for implementations in real
      data applications, which offer promising directions for future research.
