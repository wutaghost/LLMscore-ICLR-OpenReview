009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Many alignment methods, including reinforcement learning from human feedback
012            (RLHF), rely on the Bradley-Terry reward assumption, which is insufficient to
013            capture the full range of general human preferences. To achieve robust alignment
014            with general preferences, we model the alignment problem as a two-player zero-
015            sum game, where the Nash equilibrium policy guarantees a 50% win rate against
016
               any competing policy. However, previous algorithms for finding the Nash policy
               either diverge or converge to a Nash policy in a modified game, even in a simple
017
               synthetic setting, thereby failing to maintain the 50% win rate guarantee against
018
               all other policies. We propose a meta-algorithm, Covergent Meta Alignment
019            Algorithm (COMAL), for language model alignment with general preferences,
020            inspired by convergent algorithms in game theory. Theoretically, we prove that
021            our meta-algorithm converges to an exact Nash policy. Additionally, our meta-
022            algorithm is simple and can be integrated with many existing methods designed for
023            RLHF and preference optimization with minimal changes. Experimental results
024            demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework when combined with
025            existing preference policy optimization methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2023; Dubey et al., 2024) have
030   fundamentally transformed the fields of natural language processing and artificial intelligence. They
031   excel in tasks ranging from text generation and translation to complex question answering and
032   interactive dialogue systems. As these models become more integrated into daily life, a key challenge
033   is ensuring they achieve high levels of alignment with human values and preferences.
034
      One of the most widely adopted approaches to addressing this challenge is Reinforcement Learning
035
      from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022). This framework
036   consists of two steps: first, learning a reward model from a dataset containing human preferences,
037   and second, optimizing the LLM using the proximal policy optimization (PPO) algorithm (Schulman
038   et al., 2017). Recently, Rafailov et al. (2024) observed that the first step can be bypassed, proposing
039   the direct preference optimization (DPO) algorithm, directly optimizing the LLM from the dataset.
040
      However, the aforementioned approaches crucially rely on the assumption that human preferences
041
      can be expressed using the Bradley-Terry (BT) model (Bradley and Terry, 1952). Unfortunately, the
042   BT model is too restrictive to capture the richness and complexity of human preferences. Specifically,
043   the BT model can only induce transitive preferences—i.e., if more people favor A over B, and B
044   over C, then more people must favor A over C. Such transitivity may not hold in the presence of
045   diverse populations and is also incompatible with evidence from human decision-making (May, 1954;
046   Tversky, 1969).
047
      To overcome this limitation, recent research has begun to explore alignment under general preferences.
048   Munos et al. (2024) formulate this alignment problem as a symmetric two-player zero-sum game,
049   where both players’ strategies are LLMs, and their payoffs are determined by the win rate against the
050   opponent’s LLM according to the preference model. The objective is to identify a Nash equilibrium
051   policy that guarantees at least a 50% win rate against any other policy (Azar et al., 2024; Munos
052   et al., 2024; Calandriello et al., 2024), a property we refer to as robust alignment. However, all the
053   proposed algorithms either diverge or converge to the Nash policy of a modified game, thereby failing
      to maintain the 50% win rate guarantee against all other policies.

      Our Contribution. We introduce a novel meta-algorithm, Covergent Meta Alignment Algorithm
055   (COMAL), inspired by the conceptual prox-method, a convergent algorithm for solving two-player
056   zero-sum games (Nemirovski, 2004). Our first observation is that many existing algorithms, including
057   PPO (Schulman et al., 2017), DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024), IPO (Azar et al., 2024), SPPO (Wu
058   et al., 2024), INPO (Zhang et al., 2024), etc., can be interpreted as implementations of the Prox
059   operator (Nemirovski, 2004). COMAL employs the Prox operator as its fundamental building block
060   and provably converges to the Nash equilibrium policy in the last iterate, assuming the Prox operator
061   can be computed exactly, thus achieving robust alignment. This approach allows us to leverage many
062   existing algorithms in a black-box manner. While several algorithms, e.g., IPO, SPPO, etc., in the
063
      literature demonstrate average-iterate convergence to the Nash equilibrium policy, they all diverge in
      the last iterate. Unfortunately, iterate averaging can be cumbersome, particularly when deep-learning
064
      components are involved, as it may not be feasible to average the outputs of LLMs.1 Compared to
065
      these algorithms, COMAL achieves the more desirable last-iterate convergence.
066
067   In addition to our theoretical analysis, we validate the effectiveness of COMAL through both synthetic
068   and LLM-based experiments.
069
070   Synthetic experiments. We construct a 3 × 3 two-player zero-sum preference game, and compare

212   Update Rule of COMAL In each iteration t, COMAL uses a regularized game solver (Algorithm 2)
213   to update the next-iteration policy π t+1 as the Nash equilibrium policy of a regularized game
214   Jτ (π1 , π2 , πref ) using the current policy as reference πref = π t . The rationale behind COMAL is
215   simple: update the reference policy when no further progress can be made, which occurs when the
      algorithm reaches the Nash equilibrium of the regularized game. Denote π ⋆ a Nash equilibrium of

      the original game. We show that KL divergence to π ⋆ is monotonically decreasing: KL(π ⋆ ||π t+1 ) ≤
217   KL(π ⋆ ||π t ). Since π t+1 is closer to the Nash equilibrium than π t , COMAL updates the reference
218   policy from π t to π t+1 for further optimization. We also note that in COMAL, KL(π ⋆ ||π t+1 ) ≤
219   KL(π ⋆ ||π t ) holds for any τ > 0, allowing us to choose the regularization parameter τt > 0 adaptively

278
279   We show how to implement COMAL in practical large-scale applications like LLM alignment by
280   computing the prox operator. Specifically, we observe that many existing algorithms designed for
281   RLHF and preference optimization with neural network parameters can be adapted to solve the prox
282   operator Prox(π, ηg) (η > 0 is the step size). These algorithms include RL algorithms like PPO and
283
      loss-minimization algorithms like DPO, IPO, SPPO, DRO, INPO, each of which may be preferred in

284
      methods to Appendix E. Our contribution here is not proposing new algorithms but unifying existing
285

286   opens the possibility of applying other algorithms from online learning and optimization to robust

416   5                      R EAL -W ORLD E XPERIMENTS
417
418   Apart from the controlled synthetic experiments, we conduct experiments with a pre-trained LLM,
419   Qwen2-1.5B (Yang et al., 2024), on a commonly used dataset UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2023) to
420   show the effectiveness of COMAL under the real-world preference optimization setting.
421
422   5.1                      E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
423
424   Datasets We use the UltraFeedback dataset, specifically its binarized version for preference fine-
425   tuning.4 It contains 64K data examples consisting of a user instruction and a positive-negative output
426
      pair annotated by GPT-4. The instructions in this dataset span a wide range of types, making it
      well-suited for studying preference optimization in real-world settings. Since we focus on online
427
      and iterative preference optimization, only the instructions are used because the output pairs will be
428
      generated and annotated online. In addition, to reduce the computational cost, the instructions are
429   randomly split into 6 equal-size subsets. Each subset therefore contains around 10K instructions and
430   is used in one training iteration.
431
                     4
                            https://huggingface.co/datasets/HuggingFaceH4/ultrafeedback_binarized.

432
      Preference Oracle The preference oracle we used is Llama-3-OffsetBias-8B (Park et al., 2024a),
433   which is a pairwise preference model that predicts which output is better given an instruction and
434   a pair of outputs. Fine-tuned from Meta-Llama-3-8B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024), it achieves
435   strong performance on various human preference alignment benchmarks in RewardBench (Lambert
436   et al., 2024). We selected it as the preference oracle for its balance of computational efficiency and
437   alignment with human preferences, making it suitable for iterative preference optimization.
438
      Online Preference Data Generation To construct the preference data, i.e., output pairs with a
439   preference annotation specifying which one is better, we adopt the setting of Zhang et al. (2024)
440   by sampling 5 candidate outputs for each instruction with a temperature of 0.8 and applying the
441   preference oracle to compare all the output pairs constructed. The best and the worst candidate
442   outputs, derived from the pairwise comparison results, are then selected to form a data point.
443
      Baselines We include the following baselines for comparisons with COMAL: (1) SFT, which fine-
444
      tunes the pre-trained Qwen2-1.5B on the UltraChat dataset, with the resulting checkpoint serving as
445   the starting point and/or reference policy for the other training algorithms; (2) vanilla DPO (Rafailov
446   et al., 2024) and (3) vanilla IPO (Azar et al., 2024), where one training iteration is performed over
447   the entire instruction set of UltraFeedback with output pairs sampled from the SFT policy; (4)
448   INPO (Zhang et al., 2024), where each iteration of training is performed on a single data split; (5)
449   iterative IPO, which follows a training setting similar to INPO but without the KL regularization with
450   respect to the reference policy.
451   Evaluations We use the instructions in a widely used benchmark, AlpacaEval (Li et al., 2023), to
452   construct the test set, since these instructions are diverse and cover various task scenarios. However,
453   instead of using GPT-4, the default evaluator for the AlpacaEval benchmark, we chose to use the
454   same preference oracle used during data generation, Llama-3-OffsetBias-8B, as the evaluator. This
455   decision was made to maintain a controlled experimental setting, ensuring that the preference oracle
456   the model learns to fit is also the one used to evaluate its performance.
457   Training Details We follow the training recipe proposed in Tunstall et al. (2023) for the experiments.
458   Specifically, at each training iteration, the models are fine-tuned for 3 epochs with a batch size of 32
459   and maximum learning rate of 5 × 10−7 , using a linear learning rate scheduler where 10% of the
460   steps are for warmup and the rest for linearly decreasing the rate. The checkpoints are selected based
461   on their validation loss on the UltraFeedback dataset. The training is performed on 8 NVIDIA A6000
462   Ada GPUs with 48GB memory, and one training iteration over the 10K instructions takes around
463   5 hours. Due to the relatively high computational requirements and the large number of training
464   iterations we tested (up to 18), we opted to use a moderately sized LLM and did not conduct an
465
      exhaustive hyper-parameter search, instead referencing settings from previous work when appropriate.
      To the best of our knowledge, multi-iteration training like ours has rarely been explored in previous
466
      work. For example, INPO (Zhang et al., 2024) only performed optimization for up to 3 iterations,
467
      which is equivalent to just one full round over UltraFeedback’s instructions.
468
469   Hyper-Parameters We conduct a grid search for the strength of the KL regularization, η −1 , in both
470   vanilla DPO and IPO. We found that DPO achieves the best performance when η −1 is set to 0.01,
471
      while IPO achieves the best performance when η −1 is set within the range of 0.002 - 0.01. We then
      choose the value of η −1 to be 0.002 to encourage larger learning steps.5 This value of η is also used
472
      for iterative IPO and INPO. INPO has another hyper-parameter τ which controls the strength of the
473
      KL regularization from the reference policy. We determine its value following the setting of Zhang
474   et al. (2024), where ητ is set to a fixed ratio, 1/3. Regarding COMAL, which is implemented based
475   on INPO as outlined in Algorithm 4, the reference policy is updated when the first optimization step
476   begins to converge or overfit, and η −1 is increased to 0.01 to improve training stability.

533   6              C ONCLUSION
534
535   We have proposed COMAL, a meta-algorithm for preference optimization that provably converges
536   to the Nash equilibrium policy in the last iterate. We have provided a theoretical analysis of the
537   properties of COMAL and have empirically demonstrated its effectiveness under both synthetic
538   and real-world experimental settings. We believe COMAL has significant potential to enhance the
539   performance of LLMs in the alignment fine-tuning setting, due to its theoretical guarantees and
      flexibility, as it can be integrated with existing learning algorithms while overcoming their limitations.

911   A    R ELATED W ORK
912
913   Alignment under Preference models Most existing approaches adopt the Bradley-Terry (BT)
914   preference model (Bradley and Terry, 1952; Christiano et al., 2017), which involves first learning a
915   preference model and then optimizing the objective function with a KL divergence penalty relative to
916   the original language model. For example, RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022) aims to ensure that LLMs
917   follow instructions by initially learning a BT model and subsequently fine-tuning the model based on
      the learned reward while regularizing it with the original LLM.


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      Building on this framework, Rafailov et al. (2024) introduces Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)
919   that maintains the assumption of the BT model for preferences but eliminates the preference learning
920   step by reformulating the objective and optimizing it directly. Additionally, Ethayarajh et al. (2024)
921   diverges from the traditional BT-based methods by deriving algorithms that bypass the preference
922   modeling step altogether. Instead, they model user preferences based on Kahneman and Tversky’s
923   utility theory.
924
925   Alignment Solution Concepts under General Preferences Azar et al. (2024) are the first to
926   consider general preferences and propose a family of optimization objectives that optimize a function
927   of the preferences probabilities regularized by the KL divergence with respect to the original model.
928   They propose the IPO algorithm, an offline algorithm that directly optimizes the win rate of the
929   model penalized by the KL divergence with respect to the original model. Munos et al. (2024)
930
      also consider general preferences and aim to find the von Neumann winner, which corresponds to
      the Nash equilibrium of a game played between the two LLMs over the win rate. They propose a
931
      variant of the Mirror Descent (MD) algorithm called Nash-MD and show last-iterate convergence
932
      in the KL-regularized game. Concurrently, Swamy et al. (2024) study the same solution concept
933   focusing more on sequential games. Calandriello et al. (2024) proved that the objective of the the
934   IPO algorithm coincides with the Nash policy under a proper choice of the parameter that controls
935   the regularization.
936
937   Iterative Self-Play Algorithms Apart from the aforementioned works, a line of recent work also
938   propose practical implementation of the Mirror Dscent (MD) algorithms, which can be used to learn
939   the Nash equilibrium via self-play.Rosset et al. (2024) propose Direct Nash Optimization (DNO),
940   where at each iteration, the model regresses the predicted preferences against the actual preferences
941   using cross-entropy loss. Similarly, Wu et al. (2024) introduce the Self-Play Preference Optimization
942   (SPPO) method, Gao et al. (2024) introduce Reinforcement Learning via Regressing Relative Rewards
943
      (REBEL), and Richemond et al. (2024) introduce the Direct Reward Optimization (DRO) which
      regresses the loss using the L2 distance at each iteration. Since these algorithms simulate the MD
944
      update, when applied in a (unregularized) zero-sum game, they only have average-iterate convergence
945
      but all diverge in last iterate. Moreover, all these methods require the estimation of the win rate,
946   which can be computationally intensive and may introduce estimation errors.
947
948   Most closely related to our work is Iterative Nash Policy Optimization (INPO) by Zhang et al. (2024),
949
      which continues to use L2 distance regression. However, by further reformulating and simplifying
      the objective similar to IPO, INPO eliminates the need to estimate the expected win rate. The primary
950
      distinction between our approach and INPO is that INPO is designed for the KL-regularized game
951
      and is equivalent to MD; while our algorithm COMAL is inspired by the Conceptual Prox algorithm
952   and guarantees last-iterate convergence in the unregularized game. This fundamental difference
953   allows COMAL to achieve more favourable convergence properties with robust alignment (i.e., 50%
954   against any other policy) for large language models.
955
956   Last-Iterate Convergence on Games It is well-established that Mirror Descent fails to converge
957   in simple zero-sum games, often resulting in cycling behavior (Mertikopoulos et al., 2018). In
958   contrast, several prominent algorithms have been shown to achieve last-iterate convergence including
959   the Proximal Point (PP) method (Rockafellar, 1976), Extra-Gradient (EG) (Korpelevich, 1976),
960   Optimistic Online Mirror Dscent (OOMD) (Popov, 1980; Rakhlin and Sridharan, 2013), and the
961   Conceptual Prox/Mirror Prox methods (Nemirovski, 2004), in the more general setting of monotone
962   variational inequality that covers zero-sum games as special cases. The asymptotic convergence
963
      properties of these algorithms have been extensively studied (Popov, 1980; Facchinei and Pang, 2003;
      Iusem et al., 2003; Nemirovski, 2004; Daskalakis and Panageas, 2018). In particular, variants of the
964
      Conceptual Prox algorithm (Nemirovski, 2004) instantiated with different regularizers have been
965
      shown to have last-iterate convergence in zero-sum matrix games (Abe et al., 2024) and zero-sum
966   imperfect-information games (Perolat et al., 2021). Recently, there has been a growing focus on
967   establishing finite-time convergence guarantees for these methods, addressing the practical necessity
968   of understanding their performance within a limited number of iterations (see e.g. (Mokhtari et al.,
969   2020b;a; Golowich et al., 2020b;a; Bauschke et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2021; Cai et al., 2022; Gorbunov
970   et al., 2022) and references therein). Although last-iterate convergent algorithms have been extensively
971   studied in optimization and game theory, only the divergent MD algorithm has been implemented
      for LLM alignment (see the "Iterative Self-Play Algorithms" paragraph above). Compared to the
