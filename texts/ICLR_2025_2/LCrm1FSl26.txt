009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Post-training pruning has gained increasing attention with the rapid growth of
012            large language models (LLMs). However, significant variations in weight dis-
013            tributions across different LLMs make a fixed pruning strategy inadequate for
014            multiple models. In this paper, we propose an efficient evolutionary optimiza-
015            tion framework, M ECON, for adaptive LLM pruning. In particular, we design
016            an effective search space built on our Meta pruning metric to mitigate diverse
017            weight distributions among LLMs. We then introduce model-wise reconstruction
018
               error, a lightweight search evaluation to speed up the evaluation of each search
               trial. We finally leverage Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm III (NSGA-
019
               III) as our search algorithm, handling both the single-objective problem of prun-
020
               ing metric search and the multi-objective problem of layerwise sparsity ratio
021            search in discovering the optimal pruning strategy. We extensively evaluate our
022            framework on LLaMA-1/2/3 and Mistral models across multiple benchmarks.
023            Our results demonstrate that our adaptive pruning metrics consistently outper-
024            form existing ones, and the layerwise sparsity ratios improve the effectiveness
025            of other pruning metrics. Furthermore, we validate the cross-task and cross-
026            model generalizability of our pruning metrics, offering a cost-effective solution to
027            streamline the search process. We release our code in the anonymous repository:
028            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/Mecon-5819.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large language models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a; Le Scao et al., 2023)
      have recently shown remarkable performance in a range of complex language benchmarks in the field
034
      of language understanding and generation (Bubeck et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2022a;b). Despite their
035
      impressive performance, their extensive model size causes significant computational demands, making
036   LLM inference and deployment a big challenge. One notable advancement in model compression
037   has centered on model pruning (LeCun et al., 1989; Hassibi et al., 1993; Han et al., 2015), which
038   shrinks model sizes by removing specific weights from the model – essentially setting them to zero.
039   Traditional model pruning methods, typically involve retraining (Liu et al., 2018; Blalock et al.,
040   2020) or iterative training to recover performance (Frankle & Carbin, 2018; Renda et al., 2019),
041   which are less feasible when scaling to large LLMs with billions of parameters. Recently, there has
042   been a growing effort in post-training pruning (PTP) due to its minimal resource demands. PTP
043   methods develop pruning metrics to evaluate the importance of weights, thus the weights with lower
044
      importance can be removed. (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Zhang et al.).
045   However, as shown in Figure 1, we observe a significant performance drop when applying recent well-
046   established pruning metrics (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Zhang et al.) to the LLaMA-3
047   (Meta, 2024) model. To analyze the reason for the performance drop, we demonstrate the distributions
048   of input activation norms and weight magnitudes, two main components considered by recent pruning
049   metrics. Despite the past success of SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023), Wanda (Sun et al., 2023),
050
      and RIA (Zhang et al.) on LLaMA-1 (Touvron et al., 2023a) and LLaMA-2 (Touvron et al., 2023b)
      models, the distinct weight distribution of LLaMA-3 (Meta, 2024) underscores the limitations of
051
      using a fixed pruning metric across LLMs with varying weight distributions.
052
053   In this paper, we study the essential adaption of pruning strategy across different LLMs, and propose
      an efficient evolutionary optimization framework, named M ECON, to automatically search for the

082   benchmarks. Our results demonstrate that, without any retraining or weight update, our M ECON-
083   derived pruning metrics consistently outperform all established pruning metrics. Additionally, our
084   M ECON-derived layerwise sparsity ratios could also boost the effectiveness of other pruning metrics
085   that consider both weight and activation, such as Wanda (Sun et al., 2023) and RIA (Zhang et al.).
086   Furthermore, we verify the generalizability of our M ECON-derived pruning metrics through cross-
087   task and cross-model evaluations, showing that metrics developed for complex arithmetic reasoning
088   tasks also perform well on simpler tasks like commonsense reasoning and language modeling, and
089   remain effective when applied to models of different configurations. Thus we provide a cost-effective
090
      alternative to streamline the adaptive search process.

095

096   2022; Wei et al., 2022c; Dettmers et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2024). The occurrence of these hidden state
097   features and input activations with large magnitudes is relatively rare, indicating the outlier patterns
098   within model internal representations. However, these outlier features are shown to have essential
099   importance in representing information, as zeroing out these outlier features during inference leads
100   to a significant degradation in model performance (Dettmers et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2024). Recent
101   development of quantization schemes (Lin et al., 2023; Dettmers et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2023)
102   and model pruning methods (Sun et al., 2023; Zhang et al.) for LLMs have been influenced by
103   the presence of these outlier features. Our research expands on this insight by demonstrating that
104
      the relationship between these weights and input activation outlier features should also act as key
      indicators for selecting which weights to prune in LLMs.

136   3     M ECON : A DAPTIVE P RUNING S TRATEGY S EARCH

138   3.1     M ETHOD OVERVIEW
139
      M ECON focuses on the essential adaption of pruning strategy across different LLMs. As shown
140   in Figure2, M ECON automatically searches for the adaptive pruning strategy through evolutionary
141   optimization, optimizing both the pruning metric and layerwise sparsity ratios. In each phase of the
142   search, M ECON iteratively samples pruning metrics or layerwise sparsity ratios from a predefined
143   search space. Each sample is then evaluated, producing a numerical measure of its quality. The
144   evaluation results are fed back into the search algorithm to improve future samplings. The details of
145   these two phases are as follows:
146
      • Pruning Metric Search - involves identifying the most effective metric for scoring the importance
147     of model weights. Similar to Wanda (Sun et al., 2023), we compare the weights on a per-output
148     basis, where weight importance is assessed locally within each output neuron.
149
      • Layerwise Sparsity Ratio Search - determines the optimal non-uniform sparsity ratios for different
150
        layers in the model. After assigning importance scores to the weights using the pruning metric, we
151     prune the weights with lower scores according to the specified sparsity ratio for each layer.
152
153   In Section 3.2, we outline the Search Space for each phase of M ECON, covering the range of pruning
154   metrics and sparsity ratios that can be explored. Section 3.3 describes the Evaluation Measurement,
155
      detailing how we efficiently assess the sampled pruning metrics and layerwise sparsity ratios. Lastly,
      in Section 3.4, we describe the search algorithm, highlighting how we leverage an evolutionary
156
      approach to identify the optimal pruning metric and layerwise sparsity ratios.

160   Meta Pruning Metric. Inspired by the discovery of emergent large weight magnitude (Puccetti
161   et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022c; Dettmers et al., 2022) and massive input activation (Sun et al., 2024)
      features in LLMs, recent pruning metrics (Sun et al., 2023; Zhang et al.) find that augmenting the

      standard weight magnitude pruning metric with the input activations shows great effectiveness in
163   evaluating the weight importance.
164
165   Building upon these concepts, we introduce a meta pruning metric to dynamically balance the
166
      relationship between weights and activations for LLMs with varying weight distributions. As shown
      in Equation 1, we score each weight Wij by applying a weighted transformation to the element-wise
167
      product between the weight magnitude |Wij | and the norm of input activations ∥Xj ∥2 . The weighted
168
      transformation is conducted by specific coefficients (α, β) and operations (F1 , F2 ).
169
170                                       Sij = αF1 (|Wij |) · βF2 (∥Xj ∥2 ).                             (1)

      Here, we use the absolute value of weights |Wij | to calculate weight magnitudes and ∥Xj ∥2 to
172
      measure the norm of input activations, which computes the l2 norm of the jth features aggregated
173   across different tokens. The predefined coefficients and operation candidates are listed in Table 1,
174   with further details and calculation equations provided in Appendix A.5.

191   Layerwise Sparsity Ratios. Within Transformer architectures, neurons across different layers
192   are observed to capture distinct types of information (Wang & Tu, 2020; Zhang et al., 2021), thus
193   exhibiting different priorities in maintaining original performance. To leverage this insight, we
194   prune LLMs in a non-uniform layerwise sparsity, for layers with more important neurons, we set a
195   lower pruning ratio, while layers with less important neurons are assigned a higher pruning ratio.
196
      Specifically, we identify the optimal sparsity ratio for each layer by selecting from a predefined
      sparsity ratio set, including target sparsity - sparsity step, target sparsity
197
      and target sparsity + sparsity step. Here, target sparsity is the pre-defined sparsity
198
      ratio for pruning the overall model. The sparsity step allows for adjustments to achieve slightly higher
199   or lower sparsity ratios, facilitating non-uniform sparsity across different layers. We empirically find
200   that for LLMs with more than 32 layers, using a discrete set of three sparsity ratios outperforms larger
201   sets when searching within a limited number of trials.

203   3.3   S EARCH E VALUATION
204
205   Search Evaluation measures each sampling from the search space, thus guiding the evolutionary
206   process toward finding the optimal pruning strategy (Bäck & Schwefel, 1993). The primary goal of
207   model pruning is to remove a subset of network weights while aiming to preserve performance (LeCun
208   et al., 1989; Han et al., 2015). Following this goal, M ECON leverages model-wise reconstruction
209
      error to evaluate each sampled pruning metric. Furthermore, we introduce a secondary measurement
      to assess the overall sparsity ratio of the pruned model to evaluate sampled layerwise sparsity ratios.
210
211   Model-wise Reconstruction Error. We propose a lightweight search evaluation, model-wise
212   reconstruction error, for the sampled pruning strategy. Existing automatic framework, like Pruner-
213   Zero (Dong et al., 2024), use perplexity as the evaluation measure. However, we demonstrate
214   that using perplexity requires more evaluation time and tends to generalize poorly across different
215   downstream tasks. Toward that end, the proposed model-wise reconstruction error admits a faster
      evaluation of each search trial while preserving generalizability.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Mathematically, the model-wise reconstruction error, denoted as frec , measures the discrepancy
217   norm in the final layer outputs between the dense model θ and the pruned model θ∗ . Specifically, we
218   denote the input activations for the final layer l as Xl , and weight with r output channels and c input
219   channels as Wl ∈ Rr×c . A layerwise sparsity mask Mi ∈ 0, 1r×c , i ∈ {0, ..., l} removes a certain
220   degree of model weights with lower importance scores, which are measured by the sampled pruning
221   metric. Therefore, the model-wise reconstruction error can be formally expressed as:
222
                                  frec (θ, θ∗ ) = ∥Wl Xl − (Ml ⊙ Wl ) · Xl ∥F rob ,                        (4)
223
224   where || · ||F rob is the Frobenius norm (Golub & Van Loan, 1996), ensuring the final output of pruned
225
      model θ∗ closely matches that of dense model θ.
226
227
      Sparsity Ratio Discrepancy. Layerwise sparsity search assigns each layer a sampled sparsity ratio
      which is slightly higher or lower than the pre-defined sparsity ratio. As a result, the overall sparsity
228
      of the pruned model may deviate from the pre-defined ratio to prune the dense model. Thus, we
229
      introduce a secondary measurement, sparsity ratio discrepancy, to evaluate the numerical difference
230   between the sparsity ratio of the pruned model and the pre-defined sparsity ratio. The sparsity ratio
231   discrepancy fratio is mathematically defined as:
232
                                                   parameters(θ) − parameters(θ∗ |R)
233                      fratio (θ, θ∗ ) = |Rd −                                     |.                    (5)
234                                                         parameters(θ)
235   Here, Rd denotes the pre-defined sparsity ratio for pruning the dense model, and R is the layerwise
236   sparsity ratios applied to the pruned model θ∗ . The function parameters(·) measures the total number
237   of parameters in the model. Therefore, the sparsity ratio of the pruned model is thus calculated by
238   comparing the number of removed parameters to the total number of parameters in the dense model.

291   4.1        S ETUP
292
293   Models and Evaluations. To demonstrate the effectiveness of M ECON, we adopt four prominent
294   open-sourcing large language models as our foundation model, including LLaMA-1 (Touvron et al.,
295   2023a) and LLaMA-2 (Touvron et al., 2023b) with sizes ranging from 7B to 70B, LLaMA-3 8B
296
      (Meta, 2024) and Mistral 7B (Jiang et al., 2023) with the base models and their instruction-tuned
      variants. Following previous works (Sun et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2023), we first evaluate on seven
297
      tasks from the EleutherAI LM Harness (Gao et al., 2023)1 , and the language modeling task based on
298
      the held-out WikiText (Merity et al., 2016) validation set. Furthermore, we also evaluate two more
299   challenging tasks, namely arithmetic reasoning on GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and the language
300   understanding benchmark MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020). For the comparison group settings, we
301   follow Wanda (Sun et al., 2023) to compare and remove weights on a per-output basis, where weight
302   importance scores are compared locally within each output neuron. We evaluate three sparsity types
303   as defined in previous research (Sun et al., 2023; Zhang et al.): unstructured sparsity, semi-structured
304   4:8 and 2:4 sparsity. We set the number of trails in the search process as 350, further details on
305   hyperparameter analysis are provided in Appendix A.4.
306   Baselines. We compare M ECON with five existing outstanding baselines. Magnitude pruning
307   (Han et al., 2015) is a straightforward but effective solution which discards weights based on their
308   magnitudes. SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023) solves the layer-wise reconstruction problem
309   to identify redundant weights and prune them accordingly. Wanda (Sun et al., 2023) utilizes large-
310   magnitude features and input activation to induce sparsity. RIA (Zhang et al.) further improves
311
      Wanda pruning by introducing the relative importance and channel permutation. Pruner-Zero (Dong
      et al., 2024) automatically searches for the optimal pruning metric based on weights and gradients,
312
      using perplexity on WikiText as the evaluation measure.
313
314
      Calibration Data. Calibration data is used to estimate input statistics from a small set of samples.
      For a fair comparison, we use the exact same calibration data as Wanda and SparseGPT when
315
      evaluating on LM Harness and WikiText, which includes 128 sequences sampled from the C4 training
316
      set (Raffel et al., 2020). For evaluations on GSM8K and MMLU, we randomly select 10 samples
317   from the training dataset, each truncated to a sequence length of 512, as our calibration samples.

400   Comparison to Pruner-Zero Pruner-Zero (Dong et al., 2024) is also an adaptation-based pruning
401
      method, which searches symbolic pruning metrics using genetic programming. Notably, M ECON
      differs from Pruner-Zero in two key aspects: 1) Search Space: Pruner-Zero’s search space involves
402
      weights, activations, and gradients, while M ECON deliberately omits gradient computations. Despite
403
      this omission, as shown in Tables 2 and 4, Pruner-Zero even underperforms when compared to
404   the baseline methods like Wanda and RIA, which rely on static metrics derived from weights and
405   activations. Moreover, the calculation of gradients also introduces additional computational overhead.
406   2) Search Evaluation: Pruner-Zero uses perplexity on WikiText as search evaluation, whereas M ECON
407   relies on model-wise reconstruction error, thus substantially decreasing the evaluation duration. For
408   instance, pruning LLaMA2-7B takes less than 10 seconds per trial with M ECON, compared to over
409   70 seconds with Pruner-Zero.
410   N:M Semi-Structured Pruning While M ECON is designed for unstructured sparsity, it can be
411   easily extended to semi-structured N:M sparsity (Mishra et al., 2021), which can leverage NVIDIA’s
412   sparse tensor cores to accelerate matrix multiplication in practice. In Table 5, we report the perfor-
413   mance of 4:8 and 2:4 sparsity constraints on the WikiText and GMS8K datasets. We find that M ECON
414   consistently achieves superior performance than baselines, except LLaMA-3 models. We think this is
415
      because the LLaMA-3 model, trained on a larger amount of data, exhibits higher knowledge density
      (Meta, 2024). Thus, pruning a continuous block of parameters in semi-structured pruning leads to a
416
      significant performance drop, necessitating weight updates in SparseGPT for recovery.

438   5.1    G ENERALIZABILITY OF THE S EARCHED P RUNING M ETRICS
439
      A potential limitation of M ECON might be the relatively high cost of the search process, which is
440
      conventionally necessitated for every model across all datasets. To alleviate this problem, we explore
441   the generalizability of our searched pruning metrics. We also outline the search costs for finding
442   optimal metrics and layerwise sparsity ratios for LLaMA-1/2/3 and Mistral models in Appendix A.3.
443
      Cross-task Generalization. We evaluate the generalizability of the pruning metrics identified from
444   the complex arithmetic reasoning task (e.g. GSM8K) to the easier tasks, such as language modeling
445   (e.g. WikiText) and zero-shot reasoning (e.g. LM Harness). This evaluation is partially inspired
446   by the work (Fu et al., 2022) of multi-step reasoning which finds that complex demonstrations
447   provide more valuable information. For instance, on the evaluation of LLaMA-1 7B on WikiText, we
448   directly leverage the metrics searched with LLaMA-1 7B on GSM8K. As shown in Table 8, GSM8K
449   metric, derived from the arithmetic reasoning task, consistently achieves the better performance than
450   SparseGPT and comparable scores against the task-specific pruning metrics across LLaMA-1/2/3
451   and Mistral models on the WikiText and LM Harness benchmarks. We also conduct the counter
452
      experiment – performing the metrics derived from WikiText on the harder tasks, like LM Harness and
      GSM8K. We regrettably observe consistent performance declines on the target tasks. These findings
453
      suggest that the generalization works from the complex tasks to those easier.
454
455
      Cross-model Generalization. For cross-model evaluation, we select models notable for their
      superior performance on arithmetic reasoning, specifically the LLaMA-2 7B and LLaMA-3 8B
456
      models. Metrics derived from these models, termed the LLaMA-2 and LLaMA-3 metrics, are applied
457
      across different model families to assess their effectiveness. The LLaMA-3 metric is tested on
458   Mistral models, while the LLaMA-2 metric is evaluated with the LLaMA-1 models. We also tried
459   the cross-model evaluations of transferring the metrics of LLaMA-3 to LLaMA-1/2 models. But we
460   found the results are insignificant and we think the reason is that their weight distributions largely
461   differ. As shown in Table 8, we find that the metrics from the superior model can consistently surpass
462   the established baselines across the board. More excitingly, it even exceeds the original M ECON.
463   Further details of the optimal metrics found for each LLM, using both C4 and GSM8K calibration
464   data, are provided in Appendix A.5.

534   6      C ONCLUSION
535   In this work, we introduce M ECON, an adaptive pruning framework that automatically determines
536   optimal pruning metrics and layerwise ratios for LLMs with diverse weight distributions. Inspired
537   by the discovery of significant weight and activation features in LLMs, we create a meta pruning
538   metric to balance these magnitudes. M ECON identifies effective sparse networks in pretrained LLMs
539   without retraining. Our evaluation shows that the metric from the best model for arithmetic reasoning
      also excels in simpler tasks with similar weight distributions.

      A.1     A BLATION S TUDY ON S EARCH A LGORITHMS AND ROBUSTNESS A NALYSIS
759
760   We conduct a robustness analysis using five search algorithms, including random search (Bergstra
761   & Bengio, 2012), which randomly samples hyperparameter values from a predefined search space
762   and does not take into account any information about the performance of previous trials, the Tree-
763   structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) (Bergstra et al., 2011; 2013; Ozaki et al., 2022), a Bayesian
764   optimization algorithm that uses a tree structure to model the relationship between hyperparameters
765   and the objective function, and Quasi-Monte Carlo (QMC) (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012) sampler,
766
      which generates a sequence of points that cover the search space more evenly compared to random
      sampling, for more efficient exploration. Additionally, we utilize the state-of-the-art multi-objective
767
      optimization algorithms Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA-II) (Deb et al., 2002)
768
      and NSGA-III (Deb & Jain, 2013; Jain & Deb, 2013), which are based on genetic algorithms and
769   optimize multiple conflicting objectives simultaneously. NSGA-II uses a non-dominated sorting
770   approach to rank solutions based on their dominance relationship, while NSGA-III extends NSGA-II
771   by incorporating reference points to guide the search toward the Pareto front.

      A.6    R ELATIONSHIP BETWEEN T RANSFORMED W IGHTS AND ACTIVATIONS
942
      In our analysis of the optimal searched pruning metrics, We find that the differences between trans-
943
      formed weights and transformed activations may affect the effectiveness of different pruning metrics.
944   Specifically, we analyze each pruning metric, such as Wanda, RIA, and Mecon, by decomposing
945   them into two distinct components: the transformed weights and the transformed activations, each
946   defined by specific coefficients or operations. As the SparseGPT metric combines weights and the
947   Hessian matrix, and the Wanda metric serves as a simpler approximation of the SparseGPT metric.
948   Due to this relationship, we omit the weight and activation analysis for SparseGPT.
949
      We measure the difference between transformed weights and activations as the layer-wise absolute
950   difference, which is calculated by summing the average absolute differences across all linear sub-
951   modules in each layer. We report the average layer-wise differences between the operated weights

953   detailed layer-wise difference curves available in Figures 6, 7, 8, 9.
