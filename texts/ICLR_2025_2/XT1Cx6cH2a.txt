009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The attention mechanism is a fundamental component of the Transformer model,
012            contributing to interactions among distinct tokens. In general, the attention scores
013            are determined simply by the key-query products. However, this work’s occa-
014            sional trial (combining DAPE and NoPE) of including additional MLPs on atten-
015            tion scores without position encoding indicates that the classical key-query multi-
016
               plication may limit the performance of Transformers. In this work, we conceptual-
               ize attention as a feature map and apply the convolution operator (for neighboring
017
               attention scores across different heads) to mimic the processing methods in com-
018
               puter vision. Specifically, the main contribution of this paper is identifying
019            and interpreting the Transformer length extrapolation problem as a result of
020            the limited expressiveness of the naive query and key dot product, and we
021            successfully translate the length extrapolation issue into a well-understood
022            feature map processing problem. The novel insight, which can be adapted to
023            various attention-related models, reveals that the current Transformer architec-
024            ture has the potential for further evolution. Extensive experiments demonstrate
025            that treating attention as a feature map and applying convolution as a processing
026            method significantly enhances Transformer performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Transformer-based models (Vaswani et al., 2017) have delivered exceptional performances across
031   widespread applications, including language processing (Zhang et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2022; Ainslie
032   et al., 2023), computer vision (Alexey, 2020; Touvron et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021a; Chen et al.,
033   2024; Peebles & Xie, 2023), quantitative research (Zhou et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2021b; Wu et al.,
034   2023), and scientific machine learning (Taylor et al., 2022; Geneva & Zabaras, 2022). However,
035   the quadratic cost of the key-query multiplication for processing a sequence raised much concern
036   about the modern architecture of Transformers especially for long context inputs. To address the
037   issue of storage and computation efficiency, recent research delves into developing more efficient
038   architectures, such as sparse structural attention (Xiao et al., 2024d; Zhu et al., 2024), adaptive key
039
      selection (Xiao et al., 2024a; Fountas et al., 2024), and hybrid models (Lieber et al., 2024). While
      these adaptations enhance efficiency, they often involve tradeoffs with model effectiveness.
040
041   At the same time, there is another voice advocating for refining the model design for tackling com-
042   plex tasks, rather than prioritizing efficiency. Positional encoding is one of the key components of the
043   attention mechanism. Although the widely recognized decoder-based Transformer can implicitly in-
044   corporate the positional information of tokens, growing evidence both theoretically and empirically
045
      shows that the well-designed explicit positional encoding significantly enhances the model perfor-
      mances, especially in long-context tasks (Su et al., 2024b; Press et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2023).
046
      In practice, Transformers depend on positional encoding to explicitly incorporate positional infor-
047
      mation, enabling the model to make meaningful token predictions. Without these encodings, token
048   generation would lack the necessary contextual order. The well-recognized RoPE (Su et al., 2024b),
049   which is adopted in LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023), distinguishes the token order by rotating with
050   different angles depending on the token position. However, it demonstrated a notable performance
051   degradation, failing entirely when the input length is double that of the training length (Peng et al.,
052   2023b; Chen et al., 2023a; Ding et al., 2024b). The undesirable performance degradation is also
053   observed for other positional encoding methods, e.g., ALiBi (Press et al., 2021) and Kerple (Chi
      et al., 2022) . FIRE (Li et al., 2023c) alleviates the long-context extrapolation by learnable posi-

054
      tional encodings, trying to capture the suitable positional representation by MLPs. Recently, the
055   data-adaptive positional encoding method, namely DAPE (Zheng et al., 2024), which adjusts dy-
056   namically with context, enhances the length generalization by incorporating the attention scores and
057   positional information with a more complex mechanism.
058
      In this paper, we propose that precise attention scores are crucial for improving Transformer length
059
      extrapolation, and we introduce a new perspective on the attention mechanisms. Traditionally, at-
060
      tention scores are computed through the dot product of the query and key vectors. As illustrated
061   in Figure 1, further processing these attention scores using a neural network—a general case of
062   DAPE (Zheng et al., 2024)—can significantly enhance the length generalization of Transformers,
063   even in the absence of positional encoding (NoPE). Therefore, we suggest treating attention scores as
064   feature maps. By conceptualizing attention as an image feature map (with dimensions [B, C, W, H]
065   for batch size, channel size, width, and height), we can achieve more accurate attention scores by ap-
066   plying techniques used in image processing. In this work, we employ different kernel sizes (such as
067   1×3) to process attention, finding that the perplexity (ppl) of attention decreases significantly—from
068   over 600 to just above 100—when trained on a sequence length of 128 and evaluated on a length of
069
      8192.
070   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
071
072         1. We highlight that the coarse attention mechanism, which is the direct result of the query
073            and key dot product, limits the Transformer’s ability to extrapolate to longer sequences.
074            However, Transformers can achieve good length extrapolation performance with careful
075            processing of attention scores.
076         2. Besides developing better position encoding (Vaswani et al., 2017) or position interpola-
077            tion (Chen et al., 2023b) for length extrapolation, we propose the thid direction: by treating
078            attention scores as feature maps and refining them using image processing techniques like
079            convolution, we can enhance the Transformer’s extrapolation capabilities.
080
            3. We conducted extensive experiments on language tasks to support our claims and believe
081            that these insights can significantly improve the Transformer’s performance in length ex-
082            trapolation.

085   2   R ELATED W ORKS
086
087   Absolute Positional Encoding Absolute positional encoding (APE), introduced by Vaswani et al.
088   (2017), enables Transformers to incorporate positional information. Specifically, at the first layer,
089   each position i is assigned a real-valued encoding ei ∈ Rd , which can be either learnable or a fixed
090   sinusoidal encoding (Vaswani et al., 2017; Kiyono et al., 2021; Likhomanenko et al., 2021; Wang
091   et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020), and this encoding is then added to the input sequence. Although
092   this approach is straightforward, Transformers relying on APE tend to struggle with generalizing to
093
      longer sequences (Press et al., 2021).
094
095   Relative Positional Encoding Relative positional encoding (RPE) offers an alternative for em-
096   bedding positional information (Shaw et al., 2018; Raffel et al., 2020; Press et al., 2021). A widely
097   used RPE method in large language models is rotary positional encoding (RoPE)(Su et al., 2024b;
098
      Chowdhery et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023). To address length extrapolation challenges(Press
      et al., 2021; Kazemnejad et al., 2024), positional interpolation (PI) has been introduced (Chen et al.,
099
      2023b) to extend the context window. Building on this approach, models like LongLora (Chen et al.,
100
      2023c), LongRope (Ding et al., 2024b), YaRN (Peng et al., 2023b), and CLEX (Chen et al., 2023a)
101   have emerged. Another notable direction involves additive positional encoding. For most additive
102   RPE techniques, the computation of pre-softmax attention logits can be expressed using the for-
103   mula: ARPE (X) = XWQ (XWK )⊤ + B, where the bias matrix B ∈ Rn×n is derived from the
104   positional encoding function b : N2 → R, with the (i, j)-th entry of B defined as b(i, j). Different
105   parameterizations of b give rise to various RPE variants. Methods supporting arbitrary sequence
106   lengths include T5’s RPE (Raffel et al., 2020), ALiBi (Press et al., 2021), Kerple (Chi et al., 2022),
107   Sandwich (Chi et al., 2023a), and FIRE (Li et al., 2023c). Recently, DAPE (Zheng et al., 2024) has
      been introduced, employing MLPs to dynamically adjust bias values based on the input data.


                                                        2

108
      Data-Adaptive Related Positional Encoding. Transformer-XL (Dai et al., 2019) introduced the
109   use of learnable query and key biases for adaptive positional encodings. Data-Adaptive Positional
110   Encoding (DAPE)(Zheng et al., 2024) extends this idea by leveraging MLPs to adjust positional
111   encodings based on attention over the head dimension for length extrapolation, ensuring different
112   input data receive unique positional encodings. Contextual Positional Encoding(Golovneva et al.,
113   2024) further refines this by conditioning position increments on specific tokens, as determined by
114   the model, allowing positions to adapt based on context.”

118   In this section, we first review the previously developed Data-Adaptive Positional Encoding method
119   (DAPE), which incorporates attention scores and positional information through MLPs. As a proof-
120   of-concept, our occasional trial on DAPE without the positional information (as shown in Figure 1)
121   suggests that regarding attention as a feature map and processing it with classical operators (e.g.,
122   convolution) can enhance the Transformers’ behavior. As discussed in some previous works the per-
123   plexity scores come mostly from the associative recall (i.e., copy) tasks. In addition, we theoretically
124
      show by construction that the proposed method can explicitly realize the associative recall task, in
      contrast to the implicit conduct through positional encoding in standard Transformers. The two key
125
      differences between DAPE (Zheng et al., 2024) and this work are: 1) Insight: DAPE attributes
126
      length extrapolation performance gains to adaptive position encoding, while this work finds DAPE
127   could still improve performance without position encoding so that we take a broader view, explain-
128   ing that the Transformer’s length extrapolation ability is limited by the expressiveness of the naive
129   query-key dot product, which can be enhanced using image processing techniques; 2) Performance:
130   As shown in Figure 1, DAPE is designed for additive RPE and may underperform with non-additive
131   RPE (e.g., RoPE), whereas this work suggests that increasing kernel size (e.g., with DAPE1×3 ) may
132   improve RoPE’s performance. The DAPE1×3 implementation is shown in Appendix L.

184   The result of DAPE-NoPE Compared with the standard Transformer architecture, DAPE-NoPE

190

191
      studies aim to enhance efficiency by reducing computation and storage in standard Transformers,
192   these often come at the cost of effectiveness, potentially hindering the evolution of next-generation
193   Transformer models. Motivated by these insights and observations, we enhance the Transformer’s
194   expressiveness and behavior by regarding attention as a feature map and applying convolutional
195   operations, akin to those used in computer vision.

      3.3                       DAPE V2: P ROCESS ATTENTION S CORES AS F EATURE M APS
209
210   As discussed above, improving Transformer performance necessitates refining the processing of at-
211   tention score computation beyond the conventional key-query multiplication. We propose regarding
212   the pre-softmax attention scores as feature maps (4-dimensional tensors) and applying convolutional
213   operators, which may could additionally involve position information with zero padding and higher
214   expressiveness (Kayhan & Gemert, 2020) but MLP does not involve additional position information
215   because there is no zero padding. This approach facilitates enhanced communication across neigh-
      boring tokens and heads, drawing parallels to popular techniques used in computer vision. This


                                                                                                   4

216
      novel method aims to leverage the spatial relationships within tokens, potentially unlocking new
217   aspects of model capabilities.
218
219   Rethink the DAPE formulation. In DAPE (Zheng et al., 2024), MLPs are utilized to process
220   and integrate attention and biases. Notably, these MLP operations can be equated to convolution
221   operations with 1 × 1 kernel (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014; He et al.,
222   2016), a stride of one, and no padding. Consequently, we can reformulate the DAPE in Equation 3
223   as the following:
224
225
                 ADAPE (X) = XWQ (XWK )⊤ + B + Conv(tril((XWQ (XWK )⊤ , B)).                                     (5)
226   where X is the input embedding, XWQ gives the query embedding and the XWK gives the kery
227   embedding. Under such formulation, DAPE employs convolution operation to process the pre-
228   softmax attention scores of key-query multiplication. The tril(·) returns the lower triangular part
229   of the matrix and the other elements of the result tensor out are set to 0. The resulting attention
230   tensor has a shape of [B, H, T, T ], where the four dimensions correspond to the batch size, number
231   of heads, and the context length for both the query and key. This mirrors the structure of an image
232
      feature tensor with shape [B, C, H, W ], where the dimensions represent the batch size, number
      of channels, image height, and image width, respectively. This structural similarity underscores
233
      the feasibility of considering attention scores as a tensor of feature mappings, where popular and
234
      effective convolution operations can be leveraged for refined processing.
235
236
      Process attention with more powerful convolution operation. In computer vision, the limita-
237   tions of 1 × 1 kernels for processing image features are well-recognized. To improve upon the atten-
238   tion scores processed by these kernels (e.g., DAPE), we introduce 1×k kernels with a stride of 1 and
239   padding of k − 1. This approach allows for wider and deeper convolution across key dimensions and
240   heads without information leakage, as we ensure the attention scores remain lower-triangular. This
241   mechanism is visualized in Appendix K. The use of 1 × k kernels suggests a targeted convolution
242   along the key dimensions across heads. In general, while extending this to include the query dimen-
243   sions as a standard kernel is theoretically possible, it would significantly increase computational
244   demands. Our forthcoming analysis demonstrates that Transformers modified with 1 × k convolu-
245
      tion are adept at associative recall tasks (i.e., the copy task), validating the benefits of integrating
      convolution in attention calculation. We left as a future work investigating the performances and
246
      the soundness of general convolution kernels, such as square sizes. The key contribution of this
247
      work is providing a novel insight that suggests applying convolution operations and processing
248   attention as feature maps to improve Transformers’ performances.
249
250
      Realizing associate recall tasks through convolution. As pointed out in some previous works
251   (Arora et al., 2024), the perplexity scores of Transformers mostly result from the performances
252   on associate recall tasks (i.e., the copy tasks). Numerous studies have explored the mechanism
253   of associative recall within Transformers, both from theoretical perspectives and experimental val-
254   idations (Arora et al., 2024; Bietti et al., 2024; Golovneva et al., 2024). Here, we theoretically
255   prove that the proposed model can realize the associative recall tasks. Notably, this capability is
256   achieved independently of positional encodings, marking a significant advancement in the flexi-
257   bility and applicability of the proposed architecture. By integrating convolutional operations, we
258   enable the model to handle associative tasks more effectively, leveraging spatial relationships in-
259
      herent in the data, similar to methods used in image processing. To explain the associative recall
      mechanism, (Bietti et al., 2024) proved that the first layer of the Transformer is responsible for the
260
      previous token mechanism through the positional encoding. More specifically, given a sequence
261
      of input tokens X = [x1 , x2 , · · · , xN ] with corresponding orthogonal positional encoding vectors
262   [p1 , p2 , · · · , pN ], the first layer primarily facilitates the copying of the previous token to the current
263   token (e.g., xi + WV1 xi−1 , where WV1 is the value matrix at the first layer of the Transformer).
264   The input tokens are combined with positional encodings xi + pi and the key-query weight ma-
                                  1⊤

267   the previous token. In contrast to this implicit mechanism in standard Transformers, our proposed

285   Comparisons with hybrid models of convolution and Transformers. Recent developments in
286   hybrid architectures have seen the integration of convolutional and Transformer models to capital-
287
      ize on the strengths of both. For instance, Fu et al. (2022) introduced the FlashConv layer, which
      combines the efficiency of State Space Models (SSMs) with the capabilities of attention-based mod-
288
      els. Similarly, Arora et al. (2024) developed a gated convolution layer, noted for its effectiveness
289
      in addressing associative recall tasks. These models typically stack convolution layers directly with
290   standard Transformer layers, resulting in modifications to the token values through convolution. In
291   contrast, our model adopts a distinctive approach by applying convolution along the key dimension
292   during the computation of attention scores. This method preserves the original token values while
293   still leveraging the convolution’s benefits for processing attention.

534   In this paper, we point out that the key of Transformer length extrapolation is the better and more
535   accurate attention score. Therefore, we develop and analyze DAPE1×3 by processing the attention
536   score as feature maps via convolution operation. Theoretically, we show that the associative recall
537   tasks, which account for the most perplexity scores, can be realized by the proposed Transformer
538   with convolution, in contrast to the vanilla Transformer. We conducted comprehensive experiments
539   on Arxiv, Books3, and CHE to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method, where the proposed
      method exhibits significant superiority.

990    In this discussion, we explore how to effectively use perplexity as a metric, incorporating concepts of
991    information gain and entropy. Let L(·) represent the process for calculating loss, and M (x) denote
992    the logit output generated by the model after processing an input sequence x. For evaluating model
993    performance, we define P (M (x), K) as follows:
994
995          1. Process the entire sequence x using M (x).
996          2. Compute the perplexity on the last K tokens of the sequence.
997
998    To interpret information gain, we consider the training sequence length Ttrain . Given an input x, we
999
       calculate the change in loss/perplexity, ∆P , as:
1000                                ∆P = P (M (x[−Ttrain :]), Ttrain ) − P (M (x), Ttrain )                      (6)
1001
1002
1003   The term ∆P provides insights into the model’s information gain relative to local and global context,
1004   allowing us to quantify entropy in terms of model uncertainty reduction. We interpret ∆L as follows:
1005
1006          • When ∆P = 0: The model’s information gain from the full sequence is negligible, in-
1007            dicating an entropy level comparable to local attention (e.g., models like ALiBi when the
1008
                evaluation length is 1024). This suggests the model does not leverage context beyond a
                limited range.
1009
1010          • When ∆P < 0: Processing the entire sequence increases entropy, resulting in worse per-
1011            formance than focusing only on the last Ttrain tokens. This implies negative information
1012
                gain and limited extrapolation capability (e.g. such as RoPE), as the model may overfit to
                recent tokens without capturing broader context effectively.
1013
1014          • When ∆P > 0: The model benefits from the information within x[: Ttrain ], achieving
1015            a reduction in entropy that reflects positive information gain. This suggests the model
1016
                leverages contextual information beyond the training sequence, indicating extrapolation
                capability.
1017
1018          • Our suggestion of bias matrix. The Kerple is a good choice for almost all settings, the
1019            FIRE may need longer training length/tokens to present its ability, and do not use ALiBi
1020
                unless necessary . It is easy to train Kerple, as Kerple usually has few trainable parameters
                compared to FIRE. If you do not know which one to use, directly use Kerple. FIRE may
1021
                have better performance, but may need longer training length     (diverges at 128 but works
1022                                                                 ψ(i−j)
1023            well at 512, with DAPE). FIRE b(i, j) = fθ ψ(max{L,i}) so that we may need longer
1024            training length or more training tokens to well-train the neural network fθ . Do not use
1025            ALiBi unless necessary. The ALiBi will quickly become local attention as the sequence
                length increases.
