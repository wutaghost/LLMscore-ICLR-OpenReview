009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Critique ability, a meta-cognitive capability of humans, presents significant chal-
012            lenges for LLMs to improve. Recent works primarily rely on supervised fine-
013            tuning (SFT) using critiques generated by a single LLM like GPT-4. However,
014            these model-generated critiques often exhibit flaws due to the inherent complexity
015            of the critique. Consequently, fine-tuning LLMs on such flawed critiques typi-
016            cally limits the model’s performance and propagates these flaws into the learned
017            model. To overcome these challenges, this paper proposes a novel data genera-
018
               tion pipeline, named MultiCritique, that improves the critique ability of LLMs by
               utilizing multi-agent feedback in both the SFT and reinforcement learning (RL)
019
               stages. First, our data generation pipeline aggregates high-quality critiques from
020
               multiple agents instead of a single model, with crucial information as input for
021            simplifying the critique. Furthermore, our pipeline improves the preference accu-
022            racy of critique quality through multi-agent feedback, facilitating the effectiveness
023            of RL in improving the critique ability of LLMs. Based on our proposed MultiCri-
024            tique data generation pipeline, we construct the MultiCritiqueDataset for the SFT
025            and RL fine-tuning stages. Extensive experimental results on two benchmarks
026            demonstrate: 1) the superior quality of our constructed SFT dataset compared
027            to existing critique datasets; 2) additional improvements to the critique ability of
028            LLMs brought by the RL stage. Notably, our fine-tuned 7B model significantly
029
               surpasses other advanced 7B-13B open-source models, approaching the perfor-
               mance of advanced 70B LLMs and GPT-4. Codes, datasets and model weights
030
               will be publicly available.

      The critique ability, i.e., the capability to identify and refine flaws in responses, has been widely
036   used to facilitate reliable automatic evaluation and self-improvement of LLMs (Lan et al., 2024; Wu
037   et al., 2024). As a meta-cognitive capability (Toy et al., 2024; Wang & Zhao, 2024), critique ability
038   requires LLMs to possess a deep understanding of user queries and evaluated responses beyond mere
039   criticism (Kim et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023). Therefore, it is challenging to improve the critique
040   ability of LLMs (Lan et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024).
041
      Recent researches indicate that open-source LLMs usually demonstrate weaker critique abilities
042   compared to advanced closed-source models (Lan et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024). Therefore, numer-
043   ous works have been proposed to improve the critique ability of LLMs, primarily through Supervised
044   Fine-Tuning (SFT) using critiques generated by one single LLM, typically GPT-4 (Li et al., 2024b;
045   Cui et al., 2023). However, these model-generated critiques often exhibit flaws like inaccurate de-
046   scriptions or suggestions about flaws in responses due to the complexity of the critique (Verga et al.,
047   2024; Lan et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024d). Consequently, the fine-tuned LLMs on these datasets are
048   constrained by these flawed critiques, and these flaws in critiques are propagated into the learned
049   models during the SFT stage, leading to the potential amplification of these issues.
050   To address these challenges, in this paper, we develop a novel data generation pipeline named Mul-
051   tiCritique. This approach utilizes multi-agent feedback to construct the high-quality dataset for the
052   SFT and RL fine-tuning stages. First of all, to mitigate the effects of flawed critiques generated
053   by one single LLM, we develop the MultiCritique-SFT pipeline, as shown in Figure 1 (Step 2).
      Specifically, it first collects the multi-agent analytical critiques from four advanced LLMs rather

124   2   R ELATED W ORK
125
126   Critique Ability of LLMs The critique ability of LLMs has been applied in three key areas: (1)
127   Reliable Automatic Evaluation: LLMs can achieve high correlation with human annotators in re-
128   sponse evaluation (Liu et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023; Saunders et al., 2022); (2) Self-Improvement
129   of LLMs: This ability facilitates the self-improvement of LLMs during inference and training
130
      stages (Yuan et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024); (3) Robust Reward Modeling: Textual critiques con-
      tribute to robust reward modeling by providing detailed feedback (Ye et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
131
      2024b). Recently, to bridge the gap between the weak open-source LLMs and advanced closed-
132
      source models, various datasets have been developed to enhance the critique capability of LLMs
133   by mimicking GPT-4 behavior (Lan et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024). For example, datasets such as
134   UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2023), Auto-J (Li et al., 2024b) and Prometheus (Kim et al., 2024), col-
135   lect critiques from GPT-4 to fine-tune open-source LLMs like Llama2-13B. Despite their potential,
136   these datasets often suffer from quality issues due to the inherent complexity of critiques (Verga
137   et al., 2024). Compared with these existing works, we first propose a novel data generation pipeline,
138   MultiCritique, to collect high-quality critiques from multi-agents. Then, reinforcement learning is
139   used to improve the critique ability of LLMs, moving beyond the mere behavior cloning on the
140   supervised critique dataset.
141   Preference-based Reinforcement Learning (RL) Reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms, like
142   PPO (Schulman et al., 2017), are widely utilized to guide LLMs to generate responses that are
143   more preferred by humans (Yang et al., 2024b; Xu et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024). RL algo-
144   rithms typically employ a reward model as a proxy for human judgment, learning through human-
145   annotated pairwise comparison of responses. This is often called Reinforcement Learning from
146   Human Feedback (RLHF) (Stiennon et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022). Recently, some works have
147
      been proposed to improve the critique ability through reinforcement learning. For example, Crit-
      icGPT (McAleese et al., 2024) improves the critique ability of ChatGPT through RLHF. Similarly,
148
      Themis (Hu et al., 2024), Self-Taught Evaluator (Wang et al., 2024b), SFR (Wang et al., 2024a)
149
      collect the preference critique pairs by examining consistency between the LLM’s judgment scores
150   and the human-annotated judgment scores. However, their solution is limited due to the high cost
151   of human annotations. In contrast, our proposed MultiCritique pipeline automatically collects pref-
152   erence critique datasets via a multi-agent framework without human supervision. Our concurrent
153   work, Meta-Rewarding (Wu et al., 2024), collects preference critique pairs using optimized LLM
154   as the meta-judge model. Compared with Meta-Rewarding, we propose the Multi-Agent-Revision-
155   Scoring (MARS) to filter high-quality critique pairs by using the quality of their revisions as an
156   indicator of critique quality, thereby facilitating a more robust RL.
157   Multi-agent Framework Current multi-agent frameworks are widely used in two applications: (1)
158   multi-agent framework for LLM alignment; and (2) multi-agent framework for LLM-based evalu-
159   ation. In the domain of LLM alignment, extensive research, including multi-agent debate frame-
160   works (Du et al., 2023; Khan et al., 2024), have proven the effectiveness of multi-agents in en-
161   hancing LLM’s alignment and response quality through fostering more divergent thinking and ag-
      gregating the diverse opinions of multiple LLMs (Liang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a; Ji et al.,


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      2024). Notable examples include Arena Learning (Luo et al., 2024), which leverages a Llama-3-
163   70B model (Meta, 2024) as a judge to evaluate battle results between multiple models for target
164   LLM improvement. Similarly, Stable Alignment (Liu et al., 2024a) employs multi-agent critiques
165   for alignment refinement. While these works primarily address LLM alignment, our work proposes
166   a unified multi-agent feedback framework that improves the critique capabilities of LLMs from the
167   SFT and RL phases.
168
      In the context of critique, recent multi-agent frameworks improve the reliability of LLM-based eval-
169   uation during the inference stage. For example, ChatEval (Chan et al., 2023) enhances the reliability
170   of LLM-based automatic evaluations via multi-agent debate. PoLL (Verga et al., 2024) addresses
171   the model-specific biases by pooling judgment scores of a panel of small models. Unlike these
172   works, our work proposes a multi-agent feedback framework to improve the critique ability of LLMs
173   through the SFT and RL fine-tuning stages.

      3.1   DATA P REPARATION
179
      As shown in Figure 1 (Step 1), we collect diverse queries and evaluated responses before running our
180
      proposed MultiCritique data generation pipeline. Besides, we also elicit crucial information from
181   the queries to simplify the critique. Details are elaborated as follows.
182
183   Diverse Queries Collection We first compile queries from several well-established datasets. These
184
      include alignment datasets such as OpenHermes-2.5, DEITA (Liu et al., 2024b) and OpenAssis-
      tant (Köpf et al., 2023); mathematical and coding reasoning datasets like MetaMathQA (Yu et al.,
185
      2024) and CodeFeedback (Zheng et al., 2024); as well as the critique dataset Auto-J (Li et al.,
186
      2024b). In total, we sample 10.7K queries covering 123 diverse task scenarios. The details of these
187   123 tasks are listed in Table 12 in Appendix D.
188
189   Diverse Evaluated Responses Collection Once the queries are collected, we use eleven LLMs
190
      with different capabilities to generate responses with varying response qualities. These responses
      are then evaluated using the robust reward model—InternLM2-20B-reward (Cai et al., 2024), which
191
      performs well in RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024). We select three low-, medium- and high-
192
      quality responses for each query, ensuring a significant performance gap. In total, 10.7K×3=32.1K
193   query-response pairs are collected. Due to the page limitation, the implementation details and sta-
194   tistical of these response quality are placed in Appendix C.1.
195
196
      Crucial Information Collection Once the query-response pairs are collected, we elicit three crucial
      information sequentially using GPT-4 to simplify the critique: (1) Task Description: Our prelimi-
197
      nary findings indicate that LLMs may misunderstand the objectives of queries. By prompting GPT-4
198
      to describe the task, we can mitigate this issue to some extent; (2) Customized Evaluation Criteria:
199   Once the task description is obtained, we propose generating customized two-tier structure evalua-
200   tion criteria tailored to each query to guide effective critiques. The first tier outlines the fundamental
201   evaluation dimensions for the task, while the second tier offers more customized criteria. Each crite-
202   rion is structured with a name, description and level of importance (Li et al., 2024b); (3) Reference
203   Response: Finally, we generate reference responses that satisfy all customized evaluation criteria.
204   Since reference responses tend to produce critiques that lack diversity for mathematical and coding
205   questions, we set reference answers as empty for these two tasks.
206   Following these three steps, we collect N=32.1K samples {(qi , ri , CI i )}N
                                                                                 i=1 , where qi , ri , CI i repre-
207   sents the i-th query, evaluated response and corresponding crucial information in the dataset. Please
208   refer to Appendix C and Appendix D for more details and statistical information about the collection
209   of queries and evaluated responses.

211   3.2   M ULTI C RITIQUE -SFT DATA G ENERATION P IPELINE
212
213   After collecting query-response and crucial information, we introduce our proposed MultiCritique-
214   SFT data generation pipeline to construct the high-quality SFT dataset. The overview of
215   MultiCritique-SFT pipeline is shown in Figure 1 (Step 2). This pipeline gathers multi-agent cri-
      tiques to mitigate flawed critiques generated by one single LLM.


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      To achieve this goal, we aim to aggregate the high-quality content in multi-agent critiques into a
217   comprehensive and accurate critique. Therefore, we first collect detailed analytical critiques si-
218   multaneously from multiple LLMs (Multi-Agent Analytical Critique). Each analytical critiques
219   consist of a list of Analytical Critique Units (ACUs). Then, the GPT-4 classifies each ACU into one
220   of seven quality categories (Meta-Critique Classification). Finally, a final critique is aggregated
221   by summarizing the accurate ACU while discarding the flawed ones (Critique Summarization).
222   Unlike previous works allowing multi-agent discussions or debates (Chan et al., 2023), our prelim-
223   inary study observes that LLM’s critiques could be easily influenced by other critiques, potentially
224   reducing the diversity and comprehensiveness of critiques. Thus, we do not allow the inner-model
225
      discussions. Instead, we utilize GPT-4 as a summarizer to evaluate each critique content given all
      LLMs critiques as context, since previous works (Lan et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024) have proven that
226
      only GPT-4 could effectively conduct the meta-critique.
227
228   Multi-agent Analytical Critiques We employ four LLMs to simultaneously critique the evaluated
229   responses: GPT-4, Claude-1-instant, Qwen-1.5-72B-Chat and InternLM2-20B-Chat, which exhibits
230   strong performance on C RITIC E VAL benchmark (Lan et al., 2024). Each LLM performs sentence-
231
      by-sentence and cross-sentence critique and generates a structured analytical critique, which is a list
      of Analytical Critique Units (ACUs). An ACU is a structured unit for identifying and addressing a
232
      specific flaw in the evaluated response, consisting of five values about the flaw: (1) the location1 ;
233
      (2) the description; (3) suggestions for revision; (4) the criteria type; and (5) the severity. The
234   structured ACUs not only benefits the robust subsequent meta-critique process (Sun et al., 2024) but
235   also exhibit great interprebability.
236
237
      Meta-Critique Classification Since LLMs often produce flawed critiques due to the limited model
      capability (Lan et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023), it is crucial to identify these flaws before aggregating
238
      them into a final comprehensive and accurate analytical critique. To achieve this goal, we employ
239
      GPT-4 to judge the quality of these model-generated analytical critiques, a concept known as meta-
240   critique (Sun et al., 2024; Lan et al., 2024). Specifically, GPT-4 classifies each ACU into one of seven
241   quality categories by considering all the multi-agent analytical critiques in the context. These quality
242   categories are determined by human annotators and are associated with human-annotated severity
243   scores ranging from 1 to 5. Each quality category reflects one specific flaw in an ACU. Please
244   refer to Appendix J.5 for more details. Importantly, for one model-generated analytical critique, the
245   accumulated severity scores of its ACUs could indicate its quality, whereas a higher accumulated
246   severity score indicates lower analytical critique quality.
247   Critique Summarization After the previous steps, GPT-4 summarizes these ACUs into a compre-
248   hensive analytical critique. This process involves retaining and merging accurate ACUs generated
249   by multiple LLMs while modifying or excluding those identified as flawed. Except for the final
250   summarized analytical critique, we also prompt GPT-4 to generate an overall description and judg-
251   ment score for the evaluated response. Finally, the final analytical critique, description and judgment
252   score are concatenated as the final critique for the evaluated response, denoted as C.
253   By following previous steps of MultiCritique-SFT, we construct a supervised fine-tuning dataset
254   MultiCritiqueDataset-SFT, consisting of N=32.1K samples: {(qi , ri , CI i , Ci )}N
                                                                                       i=1 .

      3.3      M ULTI C RITIQUE -RL DATA G ENERATION P IPELINE
257
258   Beyond the behavior cloning on the supervised dataset, we also conduct the MultiCritique-RL data
259   generation pipeline to construct the preference critique dataset for training reward models. As shown
260   in Figure 1 (Step 3), the MultiCritique-RL pipeline involves two steps.
261
      Preference Pairs Collection Four model-generated analytical critiques could be naturally collected
262
      from our proposed MultiCritique-SFT data generation pipeline. As described above, the quality of
263   ACUs in each analytical critique is classified by meta-critique, and each model-generated analytical
264   critique is associated with an accumulated severity score. Except for four model-generated analytical
265   critiques, we also supply the summarized final analytical critique with its accumulated severity score
266   of 0. For i-th sample in the SFT dataset, the chosen and rejected critiques, denoted as Ci,j+ , Ci,j− ,
267   are paired when there exists a significant performance gap between them, i.e., the difference in their
268   accumulated severity scores is greater than the threshold, which we determined to be 5. It should

      Multi-Agent-Revision-Scoring (MARS) Filtering The meta-critique analyses might be inaccurate
      due to the complex meta-critiques and limited GPT-4 capability (Lan et al., 2024), leading to the
275
      noise in the preference dataset. To address this issue, we leverage the quality of revision as an indi-
276
      cator of critique quality, and propose the Multi-Agent-Revision-Scoring (MARS) filtering to refine
277   the preference dataset. Specifically, four 7B LLMs first revise the evaluated response based on each
278   critique, each performing eight revisions, resulting in a total of 4×8=32 revisions. The reason for
279   using multiple LLMs rather than one single model for revisions is to ensure the reliability and ro-
280   bustness of the evaluation. These revisions are then evaluated using the InternLM2-20B-reward (Cai
281   et al., 2024). Finally, critique pairs in the preference dataset are reserved if the chosen critique’s av-
282   erage reward score is higher than the rejected critique’s score. For mathematical problems, we
283   compute the exact answer matching rather than reward model scores.

311
312   C RITIC E VAL evaluates critique ability across nine tasks, covering alignment, common NLP and
313   reasoning capabilities. We first evaluate the critique quality: (1) The objective feedback evaluation
314
      (Fobj. ) calculates the Spearman correlation between LLM and human judgments on response qual-
      ity; (2) The subjective feedback evaluation (Fsub. ) involves GPT-4 assessing the textual critiques
315
      quality. These scores range from 1 to 10.
316
317   Furthermore, we evaluate the quality of revisions generated by critiques as the indicator of cri-
318   tique quality: (1) The objective revision evaluation (Robj. ) measures the average Pass Rate of
319   five LLMs’ revisions for mathematical and coding questions. C RITIC E VAL evaluates the chain-
320
      of-thought (CoT) and program-of-thought (PoT) approaches for mathematics. For coding tasks, it
      compares two settings: with execution (CodeExec) and without execution results (CodeNE); (2)
321
      The subjective revision evaluation (Rsub. ) is assessed by GPT-4, with scores ranging from 1 to 10.
322
323   Importantly, C RITIC E VAL has proven a strong correlation between GPT-4 and humans in subjective
      evaluation, given the human-annotated critiques and revisions as references.

532   7      C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
533
534   In this paper, we propose a novel data generation pipeline, MultiCritique, to automatically construct
535   the dataset to improve the critique ability of LLMs through SFT and RL fine-tuning stages. Extensive
536   experiments demonstrate that MultiCritique significantly surpasses existing datasets. Additionally,
537   the RL fine-tuning stage on MultiCritique further improves the critique abilities of LLMs. In the
538   future, we plan to expand MultiCritique to the pairwise response comparison (Lan et al., 2024),
539   enhancing further LLMs’ ability to evaluate paired responses. Moreover, we plan to enhance the
      quality of MultiCritique further and tackle challenging mathematical and coding questions.

      8   E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      Our paper proposes a novel automatic data generation pipeline associated with a critique dataset for
543   the SFT and RL fine-tuning stages, named MultiCritiqueDataset. As described in Appendix C, all
544   the leveraged datasets in our work are from the publicly available datasets, which have been well
545   processed to protect user privacy. To ensure the diversity of our datasets, we source over 32.1K user
546   queries from 123 diverse task scenarios, and each user query consists of three evaluated responses
547   with varying qualities, like low-, medium, and high-quality. We have proposed numerous com-
548   ponents to ensure the quality of collected critiques, such as multi-agent revision scoring (MARS)
549   filtering. In the future, we will continue to improve the quality and enlarge the scale of our con-
550   structed MultiCritiqueDataset. For example, we plan to extend our MultiCritique data generation
551
      pipeline to the pairwise response comparison evaluation protocol. Besides, we also plan to collect
      more diverse mathematical and coding queries to enhance the critique ability of fine-tuned models
552
      on these challenging reasoning tasks.

      A     L IMITATIONS

866
      A.1   L IMITATIONS IN C RITIQUE P ROTOCOL
867
868   So far, there are two kinds of protocols for critiques (Li et al., 2024b; Lan et al., 2024): (1) single-
869   response evaluation; (2) pairwise response comparison. Our proposed MultiCritiqueDataset only
870   critiques the quality of one response given the conversation history or user query, while the pairwise
871   response comparison is not considered. Our proposed data generation pipeline could be easily mod-
872   ified to collect high-quality datasets for pairwise response comparison. In the future, we will extend
873   our proposed MultiCritiqueDataset to pairwise response comparison protocols and evaluate the mod-
874   els under some preference benchmarks, like RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024), PandaLM (Wang
875
      et al., 2024c) and Preference Collection (Kim et al., 2024).
876
877   A.2   L IMITATIONS IN M ULTI C RITIQUE -SFT P IPELINE
878
      This work uses four LLMs to critique the evaluated responses in our proposed MultiCritique-SFT
879
      pipeline. The capability of these four models may be sub-optimal at present. Our work began in
880
      April 2024, and these four models exhibited strong critique capabilities at that time (Lan et al.,
881   2024). In the future, we will continue to supplement more powerful models in the MultiCritique-
882   SFT pipeline to improve the quality of MultiCritiqueDataset, such as Llama-3.1 and OpenAI o1
883   series. Besides, our work only leverages four models, and more models in the MultiCritique-SFT
884   pipeline would introduce more perspectives for critiques. Since the cost of critique and meta-critique
885   continue to increase given more models, we only selected four models due to the limited budget. In
886   the future, we will explore how the number of used models affects the quality of critiques in the
887   MultiCritique-SFT pipeline.
888   Unlike the existing multi-agent framework Chan et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024c), our proposed
889   MultiCritique-SFT does not allow the inner-model debate and discussions. The reasons are as fol-
890   lows: (1) The inference efficiency of inner-model debate is much lower. It not only costs a huge
891   amount of API quota for evaluating each response but also slows down the data generation speed;
892   (2) Our preliminary study observes that other models easily influence LLM opinions during critique
893   generation. This may cause the multiple LLM critiques to degrade into the opinions of one or two
894   LLMs, and the inaccurate opinions could influence other LLMs, thereby reducing the comprehen-
895
      siveness and diversity of the critiques.
896   In contrast, our preliminary study observes that GPT-4-based meta-critique could effectively cal-
897   ibrate the flawed critiques with correct critiques of other LLMs. In future work, we will further
898   address this issue to unlock the potential of multi-agent frameworks in the critique task.
899
900   A.3   L IMITATIONS IN M ULTI C RITIQUE -RL P IPELINE
901
902   In MultiCritique-RL data generation pipeline, we introduce the Multi-Agent-Revision-Scoring
903   (MARS) filtering to refine preference critique pairs obtained from the MultiCritique-SFT pipeline,
904   using revision quality as an indicator of critique quality. Currently, revision quality is assessed by
905
      the InternLM2-20B-reward model (Cai et al., 2024), which, despite its strong performance on the
      RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024) benchmarks, may not consistently reflect the true quality of
906
      responses across all task scenarios. The most reliable method would be human annotation, but it
907
      is costly and not scalable. Therefore, we adopt this trade-off approach to balance effectiveness and
908   efficiency in measuring revision quality. In future work, we aim to enhance the accuracy of reward
909   modeling, facilitating better MARS filtering.
910
911
      A.4   L IMITATIONS IN E VALUATION E FFICIENCY
912
913   Compared to existing critique-tuned LLMs, like Auto-J and UltraFeedback, the models fine-tuned
914   on our proposed MultiCritiqueDataset will generate longer sequences, consisting of the task descrip-
915   tion, two-tier criteria, reference response and critiques. Therefore, the inference efficiency of our
916   fine-tuned models may be worse than that of existing works. However, our ablation study in Sec-
917   tion 6 reveals that crucial information is essential for more robust and accurate critiques. In future
      work, we will explore how to compress the crucial information and improve the inference efficiency.

      A.5     I NSUFFICIENT I NVESTIGATION ON A DVANCED 70B M ODELS
919
920   While our experiments on smaller models (7B-8B) are comprehensive (Table 1 and Table 16), our
921   investigation of larger models (≥ 70B) like Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct (Team, 2024) remains insuffi-
922   cient. Our preliminary experiments demonstrate the improvements brought by MultiCritique on
923   advanced 70B models are not very significant. Our analysis suggests two primary reasons for this
924   phenomenon: (1) These instruction-tuned 70B models have been sufficiently optimized, exhibiting
925   very strong critique ability. For example, Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct achieves 83.62% F1 on C RIT-
      IC B ENCH , surpassing GPT-4’s 78.75%. Besides, its objective critique performance in C RIT-
926
      IC E VAL is also superior to GPT-4 (78.72% > 76.09%). This high level of critique performance
927
      makes further improvements challenging; (2) The current experiments utilize only MultiCritique
928
      data for optimization. We hypothesize that integrating our dataset with other high-quality instruc-
929   tion data might yield more promising results for these larger 70B models. Due to constraints in
930   computational resources and scheduling, our studies on the data mixing strategy for these advanced
931   70B models are insufficient. We plan to address this limitation in future work.

      B       D IFFERENCES BETWEEN R ECENT W ORKS
934
935
      This section will discuss the primary differences between our designed data generation pipeline and
936
      existing works, like Prometheus (Kim et al., 2024).
937
938   Difference in Data Preparation Although Prometheus collects five responses with quality scores
939   ranging from 1 to 5 (Kim et al., 2024), these responses are synthesized using a GPT-4 reference
940   response, leading to responses that are very similar to the reference, which is a significant deviation
941   from real-world scenarios.
942
943   Difference in Crucial Information Although Prometheus also employs customized criteria for
944   better critiques (Kim et al., 2024), our work differs significantly. Our evaluation criteria are orga-
945   nized into a hierarchical two-tier structure, providing clear definitions for diverse evaluation dimen-
946   sions—a method proven effective in automatic evaluation (Lee et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024e). In
947
      contrast, Prometheus synthesizes one criterion using GPT-4, lacking sufficient guidelines for high-
      quality reference response and critique generation.

952   C.1     M ULTI C RITIQUE DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
953
954
      Query Preparation All the queries in Auto-J (Li et al., 2024b) and DEITA (Liu et al., 2024b)
      are collected. For OpenHermes-2.54 , we sample 1K queries for its 28 categories, leading to 28K
955
      queries. Following previous work (Yuan et al., 2024), we use 3.2K examples from the OpenAssistant
956
      dataset by sampling only the first conversation turns in the English language that achieves the highest
957   human-annotated scores. Besides, we also sample 2K mathematical and coding questions from
958   MetaMathQA (Yu et al., 2024) and CodeFeedback (Zheng et al., 2024) datasets to collect critiques
959   for reasoning tasks. Only the first conversation utterance (the coding question) in CodeFeedback is
960   used. None of the training samples are from the test set in C RITIC E VAL and C RITIC B ENCH
961   benchmarks.
962
963   Collect Evaluated Responses To collect diverse evaluated responses for queries, we use eleven
964   widely-used LLMs with varying scales and capabilities in this work: (1) Qwen-1.5-72B-Chat; (2)

       Existing benchmarks for evaluating the critique ability of LLMs could be classified into two cat-
1204
       egories: (1) single-response evaluation; (2) pairwise response comparison (Li et al., 2024b; Kim
1205
       et al., 2024). Single-response evaluation aims to evaluate the quality of a single response given the
1206   context of the conversation or user query. For example, C RITIC E VAL and C RITIC B ENCH evaluate
1207   whether LLMs could accurately score the quality of responses. Pairwise response comparison se-
1208   lects the better response from a pair of responses. For example, RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024),
1209   Feedback-Bench (Kim et al., 2024) and PandaLM test set (Wang et al., 2024c) consist of numerous
1210   pairs of responses with clear performance gap. Since pairwise response comparison is much simpler
1211   than comparing the scores corresponding to the two responses, it is unfair for our models under the
1212   pairwise response comparison benchmarks. Therefore, these benchmarks are not used in our paper.
1213   We will extend our proposed MultiCritiqueDataset from single-response evaluation to the pairwise
1214
       response comparison (Li et al., 2024b).
