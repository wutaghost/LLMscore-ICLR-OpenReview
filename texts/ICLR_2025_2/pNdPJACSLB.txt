009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Training deep models for time series forecasting is a critical task with an inherent
012            challenge of time complexity. While current methods generally ensure linear
013            time complexity, our observations on temporal redundancy show that high-level
014            features are learned 98.44% slower than low-level features. To address this issue,
015            we introduce a new exponentially weighted stochastic gradient descent algorithm
016            designed to achieve constant time complexity in deep learning models. We prove
017            that the theoretical complexity of this learning method is constant. Evaluation of
018            this method on Kernel U-Net (K-U-Net) on synthetic datasets shows a significant
019            reduction in complexity while improving the accuracy of the test set.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   The task of training deep models for time series forecasting is pivotal across a broad spectrum of
025   applications, ranging from meteorology to smart city management(Nie et al., 2023). These models
026   play a crucial role in learning complex patterns and making accurate predictions about future events
027   based on historical data. However, a significant challenge that arises in the development of such
028   models is managing the inherent time complexity in training. Traditional approaches generally
029
      operate within a framework that ensures linear time complexity. Yet, through meticulous analysis of
      training on U-Net, we have observed that up to 98% of data involved in low-level training processes
030
      can be considered redundant. This redundancy not only strains computational resources but also
031
      extends training durations unnecessarily, impacting the efficiency of model development.
032
033   To combat this inefficiency, our research introduces a novel algorithm called exponentially weighted
034   stochastic gradient descent with momentum (EW-SGDM), specifically designed to reduce the time
035
      complexity from linear to constant. This innovative approach optimally focuses computational power
      by ignoring redundant data during the learning process, thereby enhancing efficiency. We have
036
      empirically demonstrated that the lower bound of this method’s time complexity can reach constant
037
      levels, showing a potential theoretical limit to how much computational overhead can be reduced
038   while still maintaining robust model training.
039
040   We examined its application on Time series forecasting tasks with Kernel U-Net(You et al., 2024).
041
      This architecture is engineered to accept custom kernels, by separating the patch manipulation and
      kernel operation. Therefore providing conveniences for applying complexity reduction on various
042
      types of U-Net. The effectiveness of the application of algorithm EW-SGDM on Kernel U-Net was
043
      evaluated through a series of tests across diverse time series forecasting datasets. These evaluations
044   have demonstrated that Kernel U-Net achieves a significant reduction in computational complexity
045   while maintaining comparable accuracy levels.
046
047
      We summarize the contributions of this work as follows:
048
049
            1. Introduction of EW-SGDM for Complexity Reduction: We propose a novel exponentially
               weighted stochastic gradient descent with momentum (EW-SGDM) algorithm that reduces
050
               the time complexity of training deep models from linear to constant.
051
052         2. Integration with Kernel U-Net Architecture: We adapt EW-SGDM to the Kernel U-Net,
053            an architecture that separates patch manipulation and kernel operations, making it flexible
               for various U-Net variants.

      2.4   E XPONENTIALLY W EIGHTED SGD WITH M OMENT (EW-SGDM)
183
      In this work, we propose a novel approach to address the redundancy and imbalance in the gradient
184
      updates across different layers of U-Net architectures. Traditional Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)
185   tends to apply uniform updates across layers, which can lead to suboptimal learning dynamics. Layers
186   responsible for capturing low-level features often receive gradient updates of a similar magnitude as
187   those capturing high-level abstract features, despite the difference in the nature of the information
188   being processed at different stages of the network. This uniformity can slow down convergence,
189   particularly in deep networks where the interaction between low- and high-level features is crucial
190   for performance.
191   Our approach mitigates this issue by introducing an exponentially weighted gradient update mech-
192   anism. Specifically, we compute a weight for each layer, denoted as W (l) , where l refers to the
193   depth of the layer within the network. This weight W (l) is applied to the gradient update of the
194   corresponding layer, effectively scaling the contribution of gradients for low-level and high-level
195   features differently. The weights are computed as a function of the layer depth, such that:
196
197
198
                                                  W (l) = S l−1                                           (2)
199
200
201   where S is a hyperparameter that controls the rate at which the weights decay (or grow) as we move
202   through the layers. By adjusting the weightW (l) , we can prioritize updates to the deeper layers,
203
      which capture more abstract, high-level representations, or alternatively, emphasize updates to the
      shallow layers responsible for low-level details.
204
205   This exponentially weighted SGD method adjusts the imbalance in update ratios between low- and
206   high-level features by allowing more fine-grained control over the learning dynamics. The deeper
207   layers, which generally have smaller gradients due to vanishing gradient effects, benefit from larger
                             (l)
208   weight factors W = L1 , thereby accelerating their learning. Conversely, the earlier layers, which
209   deal with more fine-grained, local features, can have their updates scaled down, preventing the
210   dominance of lower-level features in the learning process.
211   The proposed weighting mechanism not only balances the learning across layers but also helps the
212   model converge faster and more efficiently. It reduces the risk of overfitting to specific layers or
213   features, as it ensures that both high-level and low-level representations are adjusted appropriately
214   during training. Empirical evaluations demonstrate that this method results in more stable training
215   and improved performance in various time series forecasting tasks, particularly when applied to deep
      U-shape architectures like K-U-Net.

      3     R ELATED W ORKS

      Stochastic Gradient Descent Methods Optimization plays a critical role in training machine learning
273
      models, especially deep neural networks. Various optimization algorithms have been proposed to
274
      minimize loss functions efficiently. This section discusses three optimization methods: Stochastic
275   Gradient Descent (SGD), Stochastic Gradient Descent with Momentum (SGDM), and Adaptive
276   Momentum Estimation (Adam).
277
278
      Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) is one of the earliest and most fundamental optimization tech-
      niques used in machine learning (Robbins & Monro, 1951). In contrast to the traditional gradient
279
      descent, which calculates the gradient of the loss function using the entire dataset, SGD approximates
280
      this gradient using a small, randomly selected batch of data samples. This approach not only reduces
281   the computational cost per iteration but also introduces noise, which can help the optimization
282   escape shallow local minima (Bottou, 2010). However, vanilla SGD suffers from slow convergence,
283   particularly in cases where the loss surface is not smooth or contains sharp curvatures.
284
      To address some of these limitations, the introduction of momentum into the gradient updates led
285
      to Stochastic Gradient Descent with Momentum (SGDM) (Qian, 1999). Momentum incorporates
286
      a moving average of past gradients to smooth out the oscillations that can arise when training with
287   high learning rates. As a result, SGDM accelerates convergence, especially in scenarios with narrow
288   valleys and plateaus in the loss landscape (Sutskever et al., 2013). By adding a momentum term,
289   SGDM not only reduces the variance in the gradient updates but also helps traverse the loss surface
290   more effectively.
291
      Adam (Adaptive Momentum Estimation) further builds on the ideas of SGD by adapting the learning
292
      rate for each parameter based on first and second SGDMs of the gradient (Kingma & Ba, 2015). Adam
293   computes individual adaptive learning rates for different parameters, which makes it particularly
294   useful for problems with sparse gradients or nonstationary objectives. The combination of momentum-
295   like behavior and learning rate adaptation has made Adam a popular choice in many deep-learning
296   applications. However, some studies have raised concerns about the generalization properties of
297   Adam compared to SGD in certain settings (Wilson et al., 2017).
298
      Despite the effectiveness of these methods, there remains ongoing research to improve convergence
299   rates, stability, and generalization capabilities. Recent works have explored adaptive variants of
300   SGD and methods that combine the benefits of multiple optimizers (Reddi et al., 2018). Moreover,
301   understanding the theoretical foundations behind the success and limitations of these methods
302   continues to be a critical area of investigation.

      3.1   E VOLUTION OF U-S HAPE A RCHITECTURES FOR T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING
305
306   Time series forecasting has seen notable advancements in recent years, with deep learning models
307   becoming increasingly effective at capturing temporal dependencies and complex data patterns.
308   Among these, U-shape architectures, which originate from the U-Net model developed for image
309   segmentation (Ronneberger et al., 2015), have gained significant traction. The core of the U-shape
310   design lies in its symmetric encoder-decoder framework. In this framework, the encoder progressively
311   compresses the input data, capturing high-level features, while the decoder restores the data’s original
312   resolution, aided by skip connections that link matching layers in the encoder and decoder paths.
313   The use of U-shape architectures in time series forecasting began with the adaptation of the U-Net
314   structure for one-dimensional signals (Madhusudhanan et al., 2023)Wang et al. (2024). These models
315   are particularly well-suited for capturing both short- and long-term dependencies, which are critical
316   in tasks such as multivariate time series forecasting. A significant advantage of the U-shape design in
317   time series forecasting is its ability to retain intricate temporal details, facilitated by skip connections,
318   which help preserve essential information throughout the down-sampling process (Weninger et al.,
319   2014).
320   Recent developments have introduced various U-shape architecture enhancements, aiming to improve
321   their efficacy in forecasting. These include hybrid approaches that integrate U-shape networks with
322   attention mechanisms (You et al., 2024) Madhusudhanan et al. (2023), as well as architectures that
323   embed recurrent layers, such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) units (You et al., 2024). These
      modifications enhance the model’s ability to selectively focus on important temporal features while
