                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Machine learning force fields show great promise in enabling more accurate force
012            fields than manually derived ones for molecular dynamics simulations. State-of-
013            the-art approaches for ML force fields stack many equivariant graph neural network
014            layers, resulting in long inference times and high memory costs. This work aims
015            to improve these two aspects while simultaneously reaching higher accuracy. Our
016            key observation is that successive states in molecular dynamics simulations are
017            extremely similar, but typical architectures treat each step independently, disregard-
018            ing this information. We show how deep equilibrium models (DEQs) can exploit
019            this temporal continuity by recycling intermediate neural network features from
020
               previous time steps. Specifically, we turn a state-of-the-art force field architecture
               into a DEQ, enabling us to improve both accuracy and speed by 10% − 20% on the
021
               MD17, MD22, and OC20 200k datasets. Compared to conventional approaches,
022
               DEQs are also naturally more memory efficient, facilitating the training of more
023            expressive models on larger systems given limited GPU memory resources.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   With increasingly more available compute, molecular dynamics (MD) simulations emerged as
029   an integral tool for studying the behaviour of molecules to develop a mechanistic understanding
030   of a large class of processes in drug discovery and molecular biology Lin & MacKerell (2019);
031   Hollingsworth & Dror (2018); Sinha et al. (2022); Durrant & McCammon (2011). The backbone
032   of an MD simulation is a force field, which predicts the forces acting on each of the atoms in a
033   molecule, given the current atom positions. These forces are then used to integrate the equations
034
      of motion numerically by multiplying the forces with a small time step dt to obtain velocities,
      which in turn is used to update the atom’s positions. Traditionally, force fields were designed by
035
      hand to capture known physical effects such as covalent bonds, electrostatics, and van der Waals
036
      forces Weiner & Kollman (1981); Pearlman et al. (1995). These hand-crafted force fields are
037   compact and fast but lack the expressivity to capture more complex quantum mechanical many-body
038   interactions. Alternatively, force fields can be calculated from highly accurate but costly quantum
039   mechanical calculations, so-called ab-initio methods. Therefore, a new approach has gained traction
040   over recent years: Training an expressive machine learning model on data from expensive ab-
041   initio methods. This results in models at near-quantum chemical accuracy at only a fraction of the cost.
042
043   Some early works on machine learning force fields used local atom environment descrip-
044   tors in combination with linear regression Thompson et al. (2015); Shapeev (2016), Gaussian
045
      processes Bartók et al. (2010), and feed-forward neural networks Behler & Parrinello (2007). The
      pioneering work SchNet Schütt et al. (2017) used a rotation invariant graph neural network to
046
      predict energies, forces and other properties. This was later improved by the use of equivariant
047
      neural networks that model angular dependencies more directly, such as Cormorant Anderson
048   et al. (2019), DimeNet Gasteiger et al. (2020), PaiNN Schütt et al. (2021), GemNet Gasteiger et al.
049   (2021), SphereNet Liu et al. (2022), and NequIP Batzner et al. (2022). Recent models have further
050   improved the expressivity and scalability of equivariant models. Equiformer introduces an attention
051   mechanism Liao & Smidt (2023), Allegro focuses on edge features with non-growing receptive
052   fields Musaelian et al. (2023), MACE introduces an efficient mechanism to calculate many-body
053   interactions with high-order tensor polynomials Batatia et al. (2022), eSCN improves the scaling of
      Clebsch-Gordan products involved in equivariant convolutions Passaro & Zitnick (2023), and ViSNet

054
      Wang et al. (2022) and QuinNet Wang et al. (2024) derive ways to incorporate four and five body
055   terms much more efficiently. CHGNet Deng et al. (2023) incorporates magmoms for extra physical
056   supervision. VisNet-LSRM Li et al. (2023) and 4G-HDNNP Ko et al. (2021) focus on modelling
057   long-range and non-local effects. The latter is the most related to our current works, as it employs a
058   physically motivated charge equilibration scheme to construct input features to their neural network.
059   All the works mentioned above are neural networks built on message-passing schemes that respect
060   permutation, translation, rotation and optionally inversion symmetries, leading to much-improved
061   sample efficiency.
062
063
      Despite these advancements, the computational cost of accurate predictions remains a sig-
      nificant challenge. Even though machine learning force fields are much faster than quantum chemical
064
      calculations, simulating systems with these models is still expensive. This is because, over the course
065
      of a full MD simulation, the force field has to be called millions to billions of times Hollingsworth
066   & Dror (2018). Even for relatively cheap machine-learning force fields, this incurs a high cost.
067   Additionally, equivariant networks are memory-consuming due to the nature of the equivariant
068   message-passing operation Passaro & Zitnick (2023), complicating the training of expressive models
069   on large systems. Thus, compute-efficient models are of great interest.
070
071   This work addresses the challenge of fast inference speed, low memory footprint, and high
072   accuracy by introducing the Deep Equilibrium Networks (DEQ) formalism Bai et al. (2019) to
073   equivariant architectures. DEQs replace the typical deep stack of layers with fewer layers and a
074
      fixed-point solver (see section 2.3), which results in more expressive models given a fixed parameter
      count. Memory efficient gradient computation is enabled by the implicit function theorem, with
075
      constant cost independent of number of function calls in the solver. Crucially, we exploit the temporal
076
      correlation between successive states in MD simulations to reduce the number of solver steps to find
077   the fixed-point, gaining a speedup. By reusing the fixed-point from the previous MD state as an
078   initial guess to warm start the following fixed-point solver iteration, we effectively ”share compute”
079   between time steps (see figure 1).
080
      We implement our method by adapting the EquiformerV2 architecture Liao & Smidt (2023); Liao
081
      et al. (2024) since at the time of writing it holds the top spots on the Open Catalyst Project leaderboard.
082   1
        In principle however, the methodology is compatible with other similar force field architectures
083   Gasteiger et al. (2021).
084
085   Our results show that, compared to the original EquiformerV2, DEQuiformer achieves (1) 10-20%
086
      faster inference and equally or higher accuracy on the MD17/MD22 dataset, (2) significantly improved
      accuracy for the OC20 200k dataset, (3) all at reduced training memory cost and (4) with 2-5x fewer
087
      model parameters.
088
089   We summarize our contributions as follows:
090
091             1. This is the first work to propose the use of DEQ with equivariant networks, and its application
092
                   to ML force fields
093             2. We demonstrate that it is possible to exploit the temporal continuity in molecular dynamics,
094                by reusing sequential fixed-points in DEQs, on common datasets and practical simulations
095
                3. Show that implicit models for ML force fields can improve upon speed, accuracy, training
096
                   memory, and parameter efficiency compared to their explicit counterparts

101   State-of-the-art ML force fields like EquiformerV2 belong to the class of equivariant graph neural
102   networks (GNN) Batatia et al. (2022); Musaelian et al. (2023); Liao et al. (2024); Batzner et al. (2022).
103   The central shared feature is the stacking of equivariant message passing layers, typically between
104   five Batzner et al. (2022) and twenty Liao et al. (2024). 3D rotational and translational equivariance
105   is achieved by building on irreducible representations and spherical harmonics, improving data
106   efficiency Batzner et al. (2022). We briefly introduce the idea of these equivariant GNNs.

110   Molecules can be seen as graphs living in Euclidean 3-dimensional space. As such, they adhere to
111   a set of known symmetries that a equivariant models exploit. Such symmetry exploiting networks
112   have emerged as the SOTA for molecular data Passaro & Zitnick (2023); Musaelian et al. (2023);

114   does not change if we rotate it or if we permute the index of two atoms; therefore, we call the energy
115   rotation and permutation invariant. Forces, on the other hand, rotate along with the molecule, which
116

194   2.3    D EEP E QUILIBRIUM N ETWORKS

196   Implicit models Most machine learning models, like EquiformerV2, are explicit. Explicit models
197   are defined by mapping the input to an output via a fixed computational graph. For example, most
198   traditional deep learning models stack a fixed number L of layers such that the depth of the model does
199   not change during runtime. In contrast, implicit models, which have recently surged in popularity,
200
      define their output as the solution to a learned dynamical system. Two prominent examples of implicit
      models are Neural ODEs and Deep Equilibrium Models (DEQ).
201
202   DEQs compute their output as the fixed-point of an input-conditioned mapping. In contrast to explicit
203   models, this cannot be expressed as a fixed computational graph. We can use different root solvers,
204   each yielding different performance characteristics. If we roll out the solver trajectory, the iterations
205   take the form of weight-tied layers. Therefore, one often says these models have ”continuous layers”
206
      or ”infinite depth”. One of the main selling points of implicit models is that they require constant
      memory with respect to their ”depth” during training, independent of the solver used. This starkly
207
      contrasts explicit models, where the memory complexity grows linearly with each layer.
208
209
      Implicit layer Deep Equilibrium models drastically reduce the model size by replacing the deep
210
      stack of layers with just one or two layers and a fixed-point solver. In early work Bai et al. (2018),
211
      it was first shown that a stack of L residual layers yields very competitive results even if all layers
212   are weight-tied. In Bai et al. (2019), the authors showed empirically that the network converges to a
213   fixed-point in the limit of infinite depth L → ∞. To formalize this, consider a function fθ , usually a
214   small neural network. Given some input x, repeated passes through fθ updates the features hs
215
                                                         hi+1 = fθ (hs , x)                                                       (8)

217

218
      after l layers with a fixed-point estimate of the features hs . Notice that the neural network layer fθ
219   has to take in the input x, in addition to the current features hs , at every pass, which is called the

221   through the NN layer fθ many times renders this approach slow, though. Instead, we search for the
222   fixed-point directly by using a root-solving algorithm like Anderson acceleration Anderson (1965) or
223   Broyden’s method Broyden (1965), which computes more sophisticated updates of h to reduce the
224   number of passes until the fixed-point is reached.

251
      solver over LDEQ ≪ L Equiformer layers, as shown figure 1. We now go through the steps necessary
252   to make this work in practice.
253
254   Embedding block for input injection Equiformer initializes the node features via an embedding
255   block emb = Embed (x). Using the embedding to initialize the initial fixed-point estimate h0 however
256   would stop gradients to flow to the encoder, since the gradient calculated via IFT is independent of
257   the solver trajectory. Instead, we use the embedding block’s output as the input injection, by adding
258   the embedding to the fixed-point estimate hs at every solver step before passing it through the layer

      Recurrent dropout Dropout is a widely used regularization that tends to hurt DEQ performance.
271   This is because dropout samples a new mask for each pass through the implicit layer, which hinders
272   finding a fixed-point Bai et al. (2019). EquiformerV2 uses two types of dropout, alpha dropout (acting
273   on nodes) and path dropout, also known as stochastic depth (acting on edges). For DEQuiformer we
274   instead use recurrent dropout, which applies the same mask at each step of the fixed-point solver, but
275   a different mask for each sample Bai et al. (2019); Gal & Ghahramani (2016). While recurrent path
276   dropout improved the generalisation of DEQuiformer, adding alpha dropout, either the recurrent or
277   regular version, reduced the accuracy in DEQuiformer. We hypothesise this is because dropout is
278   designed to reduce overfitting in large models, to which DEQs are less prone to due to their fewer
279
      parameters. We subsequently remove alpha dropout from DEQuiformer but keep alpha dropout in
      EquiformerV2.
280
281
282
      Training stability with fixed-point correction loss Without further regularization, DEQs may
      become unstable over the course of training, noticeable by increasing number of root solver steps Bai
283
      et al. (2022; 2021); Geng & Kolter (2023). A simple yet effective remedy is the sparse fixed-point cor-
284
      rection regularization loss Bai et al. (2022). Given a fixed-point solver trajectory h0 , · · · , hs , · · · , h∗
285   we pick some fixed-point estimates hs , s ∈ I and add their gradient as if they were the final
286   fixed-point estimate. We follow Bai et al. (2022) and uniformly pick three hs along the solver
287   trajectory.
288
289   Fast inference via fixed-point reuse Our main observation is that consecutive time steps in a
290   molecular dynamics simulation are highly similar; thus, their fixed-points should also be similar. At
291   inference time, the number of solver steps can therefore be significantly reduced by initializing the
292   fixed-point estimate not from all zeros but the fixed-point of the previous time step: h0t+1 = h∗t . The
293   same idea was successfully demonstrated before in the context of optical flow prediction from videos
294   Bai et al. (2022).
295
296   Accuracy-compute tradeoff in the root solver To quickly reach a fixed-point we use Anderson
297   acceleration as a root solver, since it is faster than naive fixed-point iteration while being more stable
298   than Broyden’s method. During training, we require low fixed-point errors to ensure that gradients
299   can be calculated with the IFT. However, we can trade off performance and time during inference
300
      by relaxing the error threshold for the root solver Bai et al. (2022). With the right threshold, this
      significantly speeds up inference while only marginally affecting performance. For simplicity, we
301
      adhere to the settings of Bai et al. (2022). During training we stop after the absolute fixed-point error
302
      falls below a relative threshold |fθ (hs ) − hs |/∥hs ∥ < ϵtrain = 10−2 or a maximum of 40 steps is
303   reached. During inference, we compute the first fixed-point at the same tight tolerance ϵtest = ϵtrain ,
304   but then relax the threshold for the following time steps to ϵF    P reuse
                                                                       test      = 10−1 . Relaxing the tolerance
305   further reduces the number of forward steps and thus inference time, without sacrificing accuracy, as
306   we show later.

325   with 9 to 21 atoms. For each molecule, there are between 100,000 to 1,000,000 data points. Following

329
      von Lilienfeld (2020), which contains more accurate forces. The data points in rMD17 are not from

333

338   of the strengths of DEQs, as it allows us to train expressive models even on large systems.
339

343
      speedup with fixed-point reuse cannot be tested, since the samples are not temporally ordered and no
      time information is available.

363   Training dynamics Our first question is whether or not our DEQuiformer converges to a fixed-point.
364   Since no prior work has combined DEQs with a rotation equivariant architecture, this is not at all
365   obvious. To answer this question, we look at the relative fixed-point error on the Aspirin molecule as
366   a function of the fixed-point solver steps at different steps in training; see figure 2a. We see that the
367

504   5     L IMITATIONS
505
506
      The training time for DEQuiformer can be about twice as long as for EquiformerV2. This has been
507   an issue for DEQs in general and is caused by the second fixed-point problem in the implicit function
508   theorem. Since DEQuiformer require less memory, longer training times can be offset to a certain
509   extent by increasing the batch size,. We did not increase the batch size in our experiments, though, as
510   it could have obscured the direct comparison to EquiformerV2.
511   DEQs are fastest if the data has a temporal structure like that of molecular dynamics simulations,
512   such that fixed-points can be reused between time steps. This is usually the case in simulations,
513   since modeling faster changing events requires proportionally smaller time steps. If only individual
514   configurations are evaluated, DEQs are slower, but still might offer higher accuracy and smaller
515
      model sizes, as on the OC20 benchmark.
      While converting EquiformerV2 into a DEQ worked remarkably well, it remains to be tested if every
516
      machine learning force field can be ”DEQuified”. Specifically, EquiformerV2 predicts forces via a
517
      separate output head, while some models compute forces as gradients of the energy. EquiformerV2
518   and others demonstrated that predicting the forces directly reaches state-of-the-art accuracy. In some
519   cases, predicting forces through gradients might be preferable since it ensures energy conservation,
520   which can improve MD simulation stability. Predicting forces via gradients in DEQ-style models
521   could simply be done by backpropagating through the solver; however, this would sacrifice the
522   training memory savings that we obtain in the current work. To save memory, one could use the
523   implicit function theorem and potentially similarly warm-starting the gradient fixed-point solver from
524   the previous time step. We leave this to future work.
525

      6     C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work, we explored the integration of Deep Equilibrium models and machine learning force
530   fields to enhance the efficiency of molecular dynamics (MD) simulations. We ”DEQuify” the state-
531
      of-the-art model EquiformerV2 with minimal changes by replacing its deep stack of layers with a
      more compact fixed-point layer. This approach allows us to leverage the temporal similarity between
532
      successive MD simulation states by reusing fixed-points, as well as the ability to trade off accuracy and
533
      speed. On the MD17 and MD22 datasets, our DEQuiformer model achieves substantial improvements
534   in parameter efficiency and inference speed at similar or better accuracy compared to the original
535   EquiformerV2. On the much larger OC20 200k dataset, DEQuiformer reaches significantly higher
536   accuracy compared to the base model. This suggests a promising new research direction for machine
537   learning force fields, focusing on exploiting the temporal nature of MD simulations to enhance
538   computational efficiency. Since DEQs are in principal orthogonal to the base model, we expect that
539   any improvements in the base architectures or DEQs in the future should complement each other and
      propel the performance of DEQ force fields even further.

964
      well as for the original DEQ Bai et al. (2019) in algorithm 1.
965
      The original DEQ paper (algorithm 1) is based on a transformer acting on a sequence of language
966   tokens. x1:T denote the input sequence and y1:T the output sequence of tokens. fθ is a (weight tied)
967   transformer layer.
968   DEQuiformer (algorithm 2) acts on an cloud of atom positions and atom types. We drop the token
969   indices ·1:T and omit the atom indices for readability. The BackwardDEQ procedure remains the
970   same. The the predicted and ground truth labels y each consist of forces and the energy instead of
971   sequences. We made a couple of changes to the original DEQ. The original DEQ paper Bai et al.
      (2019) used a linear initialization of the input injection, whereas we use EquiformerV2’s encoder. We


                                                              18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       also added a decoder (EquiformerV2’s force and energy prediction heads). The solver is similar, but
973    we use Anderson acceleration instead of Broyden’s method, where β is the mixing parameter, cj are
974    coefficients determined by minimizing the residuals, and m is the number of previous iterations used
975    in the mix Anderson (1965); Geng & Kolter (2023). We also add a normalization after each input
976    injection. The original DEQ initialized fixed-points as zeros, whereas we took inspiration from Bai
977    et al. (2022) and initialized with the previous fixed-point during inference. From [Bai et al. (2022)
978    we also take the fixed-point correction loss and the relaxed solver tolerance ϵ. The main change we
979    made to EquiformerV2 was to remove alpha dropout as it hurt performance and replace path dropout
980    with a recurrent path dropout (not shown in the algorithm).
