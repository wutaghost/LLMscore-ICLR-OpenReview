023                                                                              A BSTRACT
024
025                   Recent advances in motion diffusion models have enabled spatially controllable
026                   text-to-motion generation. However, despite achieving acceptable control pre-
027                   cision, these models suffer from generation speed and fidelity limitations. To
028                   address these challenges, we propose ControlMM, a novel approach incorporat-
029                   ing spatial control signals into the generative masked motion model. ControlMM
030                   achieves real-time, high-fidelity, and high-precision controllable motion gener-
031                   ation simultaneously. Our approach introduces two key innovations. First, we
032
                      propose masked consistency modeling, which ensures high-fidelity motion gener-
                      ation via random masking and reconstruction, while minimizing the inconsistency
033
                      between the input control signals and the extracted control signals from the gen-
034
                      erated motion. To further enhance control precision, we introduce inference-time
035                   logit editing, which manipulates the predicted conditional motion distribution so
036                   that the generated motion, sampled from the adjusted distribution, closely ad-
037                   heres to the input control signals. During inference, ControlMM enables parallel
038                   and iterative decoding of multiple motion tokens, allowing for high-speed mo-
039                   tion generation. Extensive experiments show that, compared to the state of the
040                   art, ControlMM delivers superior results in motion quality, with better FID scores
041                   (0.061 vs 0.271), and higher control precision (average error 0.0091 vs 0.0108).
042                   ControlMM generates motions 20 times faster than diffusion-based methods. Ad-
043
                      ditionally, ControlMM unlocks diverse applications such as any joint any frame
                      control, body part timeline control, and obstacle avoidance. Video visualization
044
                      can be found at https://anonymous-ai-agent.github.io/CAM

093   2   R ELATED W ORK

      Text-driven Motion Generation. Early methods for text-to-motion generation primarily focus on
096   aligning the latent distributions of motion and language, typically by employing loss functions such
097   as Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence and contrastive losses. Representative works in this domain
098   include Language2Pose (Ahuja & Morency, 2019), TEMOS (Petrovich et al., 2022), T2M (Guo
099   et al., 2022b), MotionCLIP (Tevet et al., 2022a), and DropTriple (Yan et al., 2023). However,
100   the inherent discrepancy between the distribution of text and motion often results in suboptimal
101   generation quality when using these latent space alignment techniques.
102   Recently, diffusion models have become a widespread choice for text-to-motion generation, operat-
103   ing directly in the motion space (Tevet et al., 2022b; Zhang et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2022), VAE latent
104   space (Chen et al., 2022), or quantized space (Lou et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023). In these works,
105   the model gradually denoises the whole motion sequence to generate the output in the reverse diffu-
106   sion process. Another line of work explores the token-based models in the human motion domain,
107   for example, autoregressive GPTs (Guo et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2023a; Jiang et al., 2023; Zhong
      et al., 2023) and masked motion modeling (Pinyoanuntapong et al., 2024b;a; Guo et al., 2023). These


                                                         2

108
      methods learn to generate discrete motion token sequences that are obtained from a pretrained mo-
109   tion VQVAE (Esser et al., 2020; Williams et al., 2020). While GPT models usually predict the next
110   token from history tokens, masked motion models utilize the bidirectional context to decode the
111   masked motion tokens. By predicting multiple tokens at once, the masked modeling methods can
112   generate motion sequences in as few as 15 steps, achieving state-of-the-art performance on gener-
113   ation quality and efficiency. Despite the performance gains of masked motion models, supporting
114   spatial controllability in these models remains unexploited. This paper is the first work that proposes
115   controllable masked motion model to simultaneously achieve high-quality motion generation with
116   high-precision spatial control.
117   Controllable Motion Synthesis. In addition to text prompts, synthesizing motion based on other
118   control signals has also been a topic of interest. Example control modalities include music (Li
119   et al., 2021b;a; Lee et al., 2019; Siyao et al., 2022; 2023; Tseng et al., 2022), interacting object
120   (Kulkarni et al., 2024; Diller & Dai, 2024; Li et al., 2023; Cha et al., 2024), tracking sensors (Du
121   et al., 2023), scene (Huang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024) programmable motion (Liu et al., 2024),
122   style (Zhong et al., 2024), goal-reaching task (Diomataris et al., 2024), and multi-Track timeline
123
      Control (Petrovich et al., 2024). Peng et al. (2021; 2022); Xie et al. (2021); Yuan et al. (2022);
      Luo et al. (2023a;b); Tessler et al. (2024) incorporate physics to motion generation. To control
124
      the trajectory, PriorMDM (Shafir et al., 2023) finetunes MDM to enable control over the locations
125
      of end effectors. CondMDI (Cohan et al., 2024) generates motion in-betweening from arbitrarily
126   placed dense or sparse keyframes. GMD (Karunratanakul et al., 2023) and Trace and Pace (Rempe
127   et al., 2023) incorporates spatial control into the diffusion process by guiding the root joint location.
128   OmniControl (Xie et al., 2023) extends the control framework to any joint, while MotionLCM (Dai
129   et al., 2024) applies this control in the latent space, both leveraging ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023b).
130   DNO (Karunratanakul et al., 2024) introduces an optimization process on the diffusion noise to
131   generate motion that minimizes a differentiable objective function. Recent approaches (Wan et al.,
132   2023; Huang et al., 2024) model each body part separately to achieve fine-grained control but are
133   limited to dense trajectory objectives.

      3   C ONTROL MM
137
138   The objective of ControlMM is to enable controllable text-to-motion generation based on a masked
139   motion model that simultaneously delivers high precision, high speed, and high fidelity. In particular,
140
      given a text prompt and an additional spatial control signal, our goal is to generate a physically
      plausible human motion sequence that closely aligns with the textual descriptions, while following
141
      the spatial control conditions, i.e., (x, y, z) positions of each human joint at each frame in the motion
142
      sequence. Towards this goal, in Section 3, we first introduce the background of conditional motion
143   synthesis based on the generative masked motion model. We then describe two key components of
144   ControlMM, including masked consistency training in Section 3.2 and inference-time logits editing
145   in Section 3.3. The first component aims to learn the categorical distribution of motion tokens,
146   conditioned on spatial control during training time. The second component aims to improve control
147   precision by optimally modifying learned motion distribution via logits editing during inference
148   time.

193   3.2   M OTION C ONTROL M ODEL
194
195
      ControlMM aims to generate a human motion sequence based on the text prompt (W) and spatial
      control signal (S). Towards this goal, we introduce a masked consistency modelling approach, which
196
      aims to learn the motion token distribution jointly conditioned on W and S by exploiting conditional
197
      token masking with consistency feedback.
198
199   Conditioned Masked Transformer with Motion Control Model. We design a masked transformer
200   architecture to learn the conditional motion token distribution. This is the first attempt to incorporate
201
      the ControlNet design principle (Zhang et al., 2023b) from diffusion models into generative masked
      models, such as BERT-like models for image, video, language, and motion generation (Devlin et al.,
202
      2019; Chang et al., 2022; 2023; Villegas et al., 2022). Our architecture consists of a pre-trained text-
203
      conditioned masked motion model and a motion control model. The pre-trained model provides a
204   strong motion prior based on text prompts, while the motion control model introduces additional
205   spatial control signals. Specifically, the motion control model is a trainable replica of the pre-trained
206   masked motion model, as shown in Fig 3. Each Transformer layer in the original model is paired
207   with a corresponding layer in the trainable copy, connected via a zero-initialized linear layer. This
208   initialization ensures that the layers have no effect at the start of training. Unlike the original masked
209   motion model, the motion control model incorporates two conditions: the text prompt W from the
210   pre-trained CLIP model (Radford et al., 2021) and the spatial control signal S. The text prompt W
211   influences the motion tokens through attention, while the spatial signal S is directly added to the
212
      motion token sequence via a projection layer.
213   Generative Masking Training with Consistency Feedback. The  conditioned masked transformer
214   is trained to learn the conditional distribution pθ xi | XM , W, S by reconstructing the masked
215   motion tokens, conditioned on the unmasked tokens XM , text prompt (W), and spatial control signal
      (S). The spatial control condition is a sequence of joint control signals S = [s1 , s2 , ..., sF ] with


                                                            4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      si ∈ Rj×3 . Each control signal si specifies the targeted 3D coordinates of the joints to be controlled,
217   among the total j joints, while joints that are not controlled are zeroed out. Since the semantics of the
218   generated motion are primarily influenced by the textual description, to guarantee the controllability
219   of spatial signals, we extract the spatial control signals from the generated motion sequence and
220   directly optimize the consistency loss between input control signals and those extracted from the
221   output. This consistency training not only enhances controllability but also addresses a unique
222   challenge in controllable motion generation. In the image domain, spatial control signals can be
223   directly applied, and uncontrolled regions are simply zeroed out. However, for motion control, zero-
224   valued 3D joint coordinates are ambiguous: they may indicate that a joint is controlled with its target
225
      position at the origin in Euclidean space, or that the joint is uncontrolled. To resolve this ambiguity,
      we concatenate the spatial control signal with the relative difference between the control signal and
226
      the generated motion, forming the final spatial control guidance s. Please refer to Section A.9 for
227
      more details.
228

231

233

234   the forward pass and approximates the categorical distribution with differentiable sampling using
235   the continuous Gumbel-Softmax distribution during the backward pass, i.e.,

301   4    A PPLICATIONS
302
303   Any Joints Any Frame Control . To control specific joints at particular frames, the spatial control
304   signal can be directly applied to the desired joint and frame in the global position, as the loss function
305   during training is specifically designed for this task.
306
      Obstacle Avoidance. Since inference-time logits and codebook editing is versatile, it can be com-
307
      patible with arbitrary loss function. The Signed Distance Function (SDF) can serve as a loss function
308   for obstacle avoidance, where the gradient field dictates the direction to repel from obstacles. This
309   loss function incorporates a safe distance threshold d, beyond which the gradient diminishes to zero,
310   and is defined as:                         X
311                                  Lobs (x) :=    − min [SDF (ĉi,n (x)) , d] ,                       (9)
312                                                                        i,n
313   where SDFn denotes the Signed Distance Function for obstacle i in frame n, which can change
314   across frames in the case of moving obstacles. While this application is similar to the one proposed
315   by GMD (Karunratanakul et al., 2023), ControlMM offers enhanced functionality by enabling ob-
316   stacle avoidance for any joint at any frame, rather than being limited to the root trajectory (pelvis)
317   as proposed in GMD.
318   Body Part Timeline Control. ControlMM supports motion generation conditioned on multiple
319   joints, enabling control over body parts. To support multiple prompts corresponding to various
320   body parts and timelines, ControlMM processes each prompt sequentially. Initially, it generates
321   motion without any body part control, then iteratively refines the motion by incorporating prompts
322   conditioned on the specified body parts and timeline constraints from the prior generation. Since
323   ControlMM allows spatial control signals to target any joint and frame, partial body or temporal
      frame control is applicable within this framework. The detail of this process is described in A.11.

327   Datasets. We conduct comprehensive experiments on the HumanML3D dataset (Guo et al., 2022b)
328
      HumanML3D covers a wide variety motions. It includes 14,616 motion sequences accompanied by
      44,970 text descriptions. The textual data contains 5,371 unique words. The motion sequences are
329
      sourced from AMASS (Mahmood et al., 2019) and HumanAct12 (Guo et al., 2020).
330
331   Evaluation. We follow the evaluation protocol from OmniControl (Xie et al., 2023) which com-
332   bines evaluation of quality from HumanML3D(Guo et al., 2022b) and trajectory error from GMD
333   (Karunratanakul et al., 2023). The Frechet Inception Distance (FID) is used to assess the natural-
334
      ness of the generated motion. R-Precision measures how well the generated motion aligns with its
      corresponding text prompt, while Diversity captures the variability within the generated motion. To
335
      assess control performance, we use the foot skating ratio, following Karunratanakul et al. (2023), as
336
      an indicator of coherence between the motion trajectory and the physical plausibility of the human
337   motion. We also report Trajectory error, Location error, and Average error of the controlled joint
338   positions in keyframes to evaluate control accuracy. All models are trained to generate 196 frames
339   for evaluation, using 5 levels of sparsity in the control signal: 1, 2, 5, 49 (25% density), and 196
340   keyframes (100% density). Keyframes are sampled randomly, and we report the average perfor-
341   mance across all density levels. During both training and evaluation, models receive ground-truth
342   trajectories as spatial control signals.

      7       C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this work, we present ControlMM, a new method that incorporates spatial control signals into the
532
      Masked Motion Model. ControlMM is the first model that enables precise control over quantized
533
      motion tokens while maintaining high-quality motion generation at faster speeds, consistently out-
534   performing diffusion-based controllable frameworks. ControlMM introduces two key innovations:
535   Masked Consistency Modeling uses random masking and reconstruction to ensure that the generated
536   motions are of high fidelity, while also reducing inconsistencies between the input control signals
537   and the motions produced. Inference-Time Logit and Codebook Editing fine-tunes the predicted
538   motion distribution to better match the input control signals, enhancing precision and making Con-
539   trolMM adaptable for various tasks. ControlMM has a wide range of applications, including any
      joint any frame control, obstacle avoidance, and body part timeline control.

851   A.3    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
852
853   We modified the MoMask (Guo et al., 2023) model by retraining it with a cross-entropy loss applied
854   to all tokens, instead of just the masked positions. This retrained model serves as our pretrained base
855
      model, and we kept the default hyperparameter settings unchanged. To improve robustness to text
      variation, we randomly drop 10% of the text conditioning, which also allows the model to be used
856
      for Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG). The weight for Eq. 5 is set to α = 0.1. We use a codebook
857
      of size 512, with embeddings of size 512 and 6 residual layers. The Transformer embedding size
858   is set to 384, with 6 attention heads, each with an embedding size of 64, distributed across 8 lay-
859   ers. This configuration demonstrates the feasibility of converting between two different embedding
860   sizes and spaces using the Dual-Space Categorical Straight-Through Estimator. The encoder and de-
861   coder downsample the motion sequence length by a factor of 4 when mapping to token space. The
862   learning rate follows a linear warm-up schedule, reaching 2e-4 after 2000 iterations, using AdamW
863   optimization. The mini-batch size is set to 512 for training RVQ-VAE and 64 for training the Trans-
      formers. During inference, the CFG scale is set to cf g = 4 for the base layer and cf g = 5 for the


                                                         16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      6 layers of residual, with 10 steps for generation. We use pretrained CLIP model (Radford et al.,
865   2021) to generate text embeddings, which have a size of 512. These embeddings are then projected
866   down to a size of 384 to match the token size used by the Transformer. Motion Control Model is
867   a trainable copy of Masked Transformer with the zero linear layer connect to the output each layer
868   of the Masked Transformer. During inference, Logits and Codebook Editing applies L2 loss with a
869   learning rate of 0.06 for 100 iterations in Codebook Editing for each of the 10 generation steps and
870   600 iterations in Logits Editing. We apply temperature of 1 for all 10 steps and 1e-8 for residual
871   layers. We follow the implementation from Karunratanakul et al. (2023); Xie et al. (2023); Wan
872   et al. (2023), applying the spatial control signal only to joint positions and omitting rotations.

       Ambiguity of Motion Control Signal
1078

1102   Approximated Mask Embedding for Decoder
1103
       As discussed above, motion Control Model requires the spatial signal difference as model input to
1104   avoid control signal ambiguity. To obtain the spatial signal difference, the model needs to decode
1105   [Mask] tokens for an initial motion token generation. The generated motion is compared with the
1106   control signal to obtain the spatial signal difference. However, [Mask] tokens are only used for
1107   the Masked Transformer, and there is no [Mask] token in the codebook, making it impossible to
1108   reconstruct motion from Masked Transformer embeddings. To address this issue, we approximate
1109   the [Mask] token for the codebook space by the average of all codebook. We visualize the embed-
1110   ding of the [Mask] token (black) compared to all Transformer tokens (red), as shown in Fig. 13.
1111   The visualization indicates that the [Mask] embedding is approximately the average of all embed-
1112
       dings. By using the average of all embeddings for the [Mask] position, we can utilize the relative
       differences between the generated motion and the control motion for Motion Control Model.

1133   In diffusion models, guided diffusion (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) applies classifier guidance on dif-

1135   that differ from the codebook space which requires for decoder of the Motion Tokenizer to recon-

1137   the logits which can approximate both the codebook space and Masked Transformers learnable token
1138   space.

1140

1142   generated motion through the gradient backpropagation.

1207   Generating multiple body parts based on their respective text prompts is not straightforward, as the
1208   HumanML3D dataset provides only a single prompt for each motion without specific descriptions

1212   new prompt related to the next body part, using the previously generated body parts as a condi-
1213   tion. This process can be repeated multiple times to create motion for each body part based on its
1214   corresponding text prompt, as illustrated in Fig. 15.
1215   It is important to note that this approach may lead to out-of-distribution generation since the model
1216   has not been trained on combinations of multiple body parts with their associated text prompts.
1217   However, our model handles out-of-distribution generation effectively due to the use of Logits Edit-
