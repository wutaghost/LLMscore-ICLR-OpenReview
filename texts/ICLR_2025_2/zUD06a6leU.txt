009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Linearization of attention using various kernel approximation and kernel learning
012            techniques has shown promise. Past methods use a subset of combinations of
013            component functions and weight matrices within the random features paradigm.
014            We identify the need for a systematic comparison of different combinations of
015            weight matrices and component functions for attention learning in Transformer.
016
               In this work, we introduce Spectraformer, a unified framework for approximating
               and learning the kernel function in linearized attention of the Transformer. We
017
               experiment with broad classes of component functions and weight matrices for
018
               three textual tasks in the LRA benchmark. Our empirical findings indicate that
019            different kernels are good at different tasks and that kernel choice is fundamental to
020            performant models. Our code is available at: https://anonymous.4open.
021            science/r/spectraformer-8A97.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) has revolutionized the landscape of natural language processing
027   (NLP) and forms the basis of almost all state-of-the-art language models. Its influence has reached
028
      beyond NLP into computer vision (Han et al., 2023), speech processing (Lin et al., 2022) and other
      fields. Compared to its precursors like the LSTMs, Transformer leverages parallelism since it is fully
029
      based on the attention mechanism. Therefore, the performance of Transformer is tied to the effective
030
      use of attention. Attention in the Original Transformer (referred to as ‘OT’ hereafter) uses the softmax
031   function which is quadratic in time complexity. However, softmax is not fundamental to attention.
032   The softmax serves the role of a compatibility function, which captures the degree of compatibility
033   between two tokens (see Equations 1 and 2). Extensive experiments with other compatibility functions
034   show that the softmax is not the only possibility, and that the ‘best’ compatibility function depends
035   on the task (Tsai et al., 2019).
036
      Replacing the softmax formulation with alternatives may allow us to reduce the time complexity
037   of attention computation. In this paper, we focus on the subset of alternatives that employs kernel
038   functions. Kernel functions have been historically used in machine learning algorithms to simplify
039   the estimation of parameters (Hofmann et al., 2008). Tsai et al. (2019) show that attention can be
040   formulated using kernels and then be linearized (Katharopoulos et al., 2020), given the feature map
041   of the respective attention kernel. Random feature-based algorithms (Liu et al., 2022) are a family of
042   algorithms, inspired by spectral analysis, which provides the feature map associated with a kernel. A
043   random feature consists of three components: component function, weight matrix, and component
044   weight (see Section 3). Due to their robustness and flexibility, random features give rise to ‘random
045
      feature Transformers’, the family of Transformers with linearized attention via random features
      (hereby abbreviated as RF), first with the work by Choromanski et al. (2021). This is seen to lead to
046
      three strands of research: (A) Optimization of weight matrices either by making the matrices have
047
      lower time or space complexity, or by incorporating beneficial properties like orthogonality to reduce
048   variance (Yu et al., 2016; Choromanski et al., 2021; 2022; Reid et al., 2023); (B) Enhancement of
049   component functions by either: (i) finding a function with a tighter variance or approximation bound,
050   or (ii) be engineered to output with numerical stability and be bounded (Choromanski et al., 2021;
051   Likhosherstov et al., 2022; 2023); (C) Parameterization of weight matrices to perform kernel learning
052   instead of kernel approximation (Chowdhury et al., 2022). (A) and (B) seek to improve approximation
053   quality via approximation error and variance reduction. (C) dispenses with the approximation of
      the attention kernel (typically the softmax), and makes the kernel itself learnable, to attain a better

054
      performance. However, the three have been explored separately, creating a situation where there
055   are different ways to construct the random features. Additionally, past works only explore certain
056   combinations of component functions and weight matrices, creating disjoint overlaps and gaps in the
057   literature. Finally, the weight matrices are often those explicitly designed in the context of kernelized
058   attention in the Transformer (Yu et al., 2016; Choromanski et al., 2021; 2022; Reid et al., 2023),
059   ignoring those which are popular in the kernel methods literature (e.g., Liu et al. (2022)). These
060   limitations necessitate a unified framework that allows the comparison of different combinations in
061   search of the best-performing one.
062   We introduce Spectraformer, a unified framework of random feature-based attention in Transformer.
063   It utilizes spectral analysis-inspired random features to construct linearized attention in Transform-
064   ers. Spectraformer allows for the experimentation of various combinations of weight matrices and
065   component functions. We will open-source our codebase upon acceptance. Through our benchmark-
066   ing experiments on three textual Long Range Arena (Tay et al., 2021) tasks and 18 combinations
067   of weight matrices and component functions, Spectraformer enables novel combinations of com-
068   ponent functions and weight matrices from the kernel literature to be experimented easily in the
069
      Transformer setting. This process makes possible the discovery of combinations that outperform
      existing random feature-based Transformers in terms of performance, training time, and peak mem-
070
      ory consumption. Various novel Spectraformer combinations are more accurate than the previous
071
      SOTA models, SADERF-ORF (a.k.a., FAVOR# (Likhosherstov et al., 2023)), OPRF-ORF (a.k.a.,
072   FAVOR++ (Likhosherstov et al., 2022)), PosRF-ORF (a.k.a., FAVOR+ (Choromanski et al., 2021)),
073   whilst significantly reducing training time and memory consumption over both. We hope that Spec-
074   traformer will enable the mechanism to benchmark more random feature-based Transformers for
075   these and other tasks. Our contributions are:
076
077         1. Our novel framework, Spectraformer, which generalizes over all past works in linearized
078            attention with kernel approximation and kernel learning.
079
            2. We demonstrate how Spectraformer can discover novel SOTA models in the random feature
080
               Transformer family for the LRA benchmark and that these models are more performant than
081            existing random feature Transformers.
082
083         3. We empirically identify the relationship between performant combinations and task charac-
084            teristics, aiding the right kernel choice when tackling a task.
085
            4. Our code is currently available for review, but they will be publicly released upon acceptance.
086
               We detail guide on how to incorporate future kernel works into Spectraformer. With
087            this work, every future kernel work that is introduced can easily be incorporated into the
088            transformer.

      2.4   R ANDOM F EATURES FOR K ERNEL L EARNING
194
195   Previous random feature techniques are robust in approximating kernels for specific learning problems.
196   However, kernels may be chosen using heuristics and by convention from a popular subset. The
197   kernel is very much a hyperparameter. This could pose a problem since there is no free lunch in
198   learning as much as in kernel choice (Schölkopf & Smola, 2018). Tsai et al. (2019) has shown
199   experimental results that this is also true in the context of attention in Transformer. Hence, instead
200   of picking a kernel for the attention, we could learn the kernel via kernel learning an established
201
      kernel methods technique. Kernel learning has shown to be effective in general (Tompkins et al.,
      2019; Wilson & Adams, 2013; Oliva et al., 2016) and in the case of attention via Gaussian Mixture
202
      Model (GMM), learnable weight matrices (FastFood), deep generative model (DGM) (Chowdhury
203
      et al., 2022). Kernel learning is achieved by making the weight matrix W learnable where ωi is the
204   output of a function parameterizing p(ω).
205
206   The kernelized attention via random features discussed here is a popular approach, however it is
207
      by no means the only one. A more detailed discussion is offered in related work in Section A.2.
      Specifically, Section A.2.1 provides alternative kernelized attention formulations and Section A.2.2
208
      provides alternative kernel approximations to random features.

297   3.2   C OMPONENT FUNCTIONS
298
      Spectraformer incorporates component functions f such as PosRF, OPRF, SADERF. Component
299   functions in Spectraformer combine each weight matrix row ωi with the input x. The base case of
300   the component function is TrigRF. Component function is given in Definition 3.2
301
      Definition 3.2. A function of x with respect to ω is a valid component function if and only if
302
      K ≈ ϕω (x)T ϕω (y) given that ωi ∼ p(.) in Equation 10.
303
304   It follows that all component functions have provided theoretical guarantees given that W is a random
305   matrix. In addition, PosRF, OPRF, and SADERF have also been proven theoretically to approximate
306   KRBF given W being the ORF.
307
      While TrigRF(Rahimi & Recht, 2007) is the base component function and guarantees K(δ) to be
308   close to ϕT rigRF (x) · ϕT rigRF (y) due to uniform convergence, it is typically evaluated using only
309   the real component of exp(−iωjT x). This leads to cos and sin functions which can produce unstable
310
      behavior, especially when K(δ) is close to 0, where variance is infinite (Choromanski et al., 2021;
311   Likhosherstov et al., 2022). Therefore, TrigRF is avoided in Spectraformer.
312
313   • PosRF (Choromanski et al., 2021) is introduced in Spectraformer as a simple modification over
314     TrigRF to counter this instability. PosRF guarantees positive function output and its variance
315     approaching 0 as K(δ) approaches 0. Likhosherstov et al. (2022) generalize TrigRF and PosRF to
316     GERF.
317   • OPRF (Likhosherstov et al., 2022) is one of the solution to the reduction of the variance equa-
318     tion of GERF. OPRF is both positive and bounded (whereas PosRF is positive and unbounded).
319     Likhosherstov et al. (2023) generalize GERF to dense matrix instead of scalar parameters leading
320     to DERF.
321   • SADERF (Likhosherstov et al., 2023) is one of the solutions to the reduction of the variance equa-
322     tion of DERF. Whilst DERF has many solutions, they all rely on operations which are incompatible
323     with many deep learning libraries on GPU and TPU like SVD and eigen decompositions. SADERF
        is an extension of GERF with a tighter variance bound on DERF.

      3.3     U TILITY
347
348   There has been an unsystematic attempt at combining component functions and weight matrices. On
349   one hand, most component functions have had theoretical and experimental results in combining with
350   Base and ORF. On the other hand, most weight matrices have had results in combining with TrigRF
351   or PosRF. This means that a lot of potential combinations have not been studied previously. Table
352   2 shows the wide research gap across 14 combinations which have never been studied in either the
353   kernel or Transformer setting. Spectraformer highlights this research gap. Excluding TrigRF due to
354
      its instability and Base due to its under-performance, we conduct experiments on 18 combinations.
355   Spectraformer is a generic random feature framework that allows for the combination of any weight
356   matrix with any component function provided that the weight matrix satisfies Definition 3.1 and the
357   component function satisfies Definition 3.2. We note that Spectraformer is an experimental rather
358   than a theoretical framework. Due to the large number of weight matrices and component functions, it
359   is infeasible for the time being to derive a general mathematical proof for the bounds and convergence
360
      of Spectraformer. Therefore, we seek to validate its feasibility and show its ability to discover novel
      and performant combination via experimental results.

      4.2    TASK C HARACTERISTICS
433
434   We first analyze the characteristics of the three textual classification LRA (Tay et al., 2021) tasks:
435   ListOps (Nangia & Bowman, 2018), Text (Byte-Level Text Classification, using the IMDb review
436   dataset) (Howard & Ruder, 2018), and Retrieval (Byte-Level Document Retrieval, using the ACL An-
437   thology Network dataset) (Radev et al., 2013). We take into consideration three main characteristics:
438
439
               • Number of output classes. ListOps is a task which inputs a sequence containing brackets
                 and simple mathematical operators, with the output being a integer between 1 and 10, hence
440
                 it has 10 classes. Text is a sentiment classification task on an IMDb review dataset with the
441
                 class being either negative or positive, hence it has 2 classes. Retrieval inputs two sequences
442              and the output is either False (meaning the sequences are not of the same document) or True
443              (meaning the sequences are of the same document).
444
               • Maximum sequence size. ListOps is 2K; Text is 4K; whilst Retrieval requires the processing
445
                 of two sequences each of 4K length making the total maximum sequence size being 8K.
446
447            • Required attention span. This is the ‘mean distance between the query token and the
448              attended tokens, scaled by attention weights’. The average values for required attention span
449
                 calculated by Tay et al. (2021) is: ListOps being 0.8, Text being 0.3, and Retrieval being 1.3.

532   5    C ONCLUSION
533
534   We present Spectraformer, a framework for approximating and learning the attention kernel in the
535   Transformer. Our paper generalizes past works and presents empirical findings on different component
536   function and weight matrix combinations. We experiment with 18 combinations, of which 14 are
537   novel. Novel combinations perform competitively against, if not more performant than the previous
538   SOTA in the family for LRA textual tasks. We show empirically that the choice of performant weight
539   matrix depends on the required attention span of the task. Our results and the extensibility of our
      work shows the viability of Spectraformer as a unifying random feature framework of Transformer.

914             approximation error. Geometric methods include: ROM (ORF, SORF) and SimRF. Among
915             these, we have explored the main ROM variants - ORF and SORF.

1069   A.4    T ECHNICAL D ETAILS OF W EIGHT L EARNERS
1070
1071   We give a non-mathematical definition of weight matrix learner in Section 3.1: weight matrix
1072   learner is any function which parameterize distributions. This allows for the discovery and future
1073   experimentation with other potential weight matrix learners in Spectraformer and random feature
1074   method in general. Weight matrix learner comes from a long line of work in kernel methods, namely,
1075   kernel learning via random features. Typically, a component function f is chosen and a weight
1076
       matrix W is produced via kernel approximation, providing us with the feature map ϕ. ϕ can then be
       combined with input x and weight v to fit the learning objective. Kernel learning, however, imposes
1077
       an additional objective: learning the weight matrix W .
1078
1079   The approach to the dual objectives separates the kernel learning methods into two-stage and one-
       stage methods (Liu et al., 2022). Two-stage methods (e.g., Yu et al. (2015); Wilson & Adams (2013);


                                                                20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       Bullins et al. (2018)) solves the dual objective separately: W is typically first learned via a kernel
1081   alignment scheme (Wang et al., 2015), then we solve for v. One-stage methods (e.g., Chowdhury
1082   et al. (2022); Tompkins et al. (2019)), on the other hand, solves the dual objective in parallel: p(.)
1083   corresponding to W is parameterized then v is solved typically. It is not plausible to apply kernel
1084   alignment (Wang et al., 2015) on Transformer architectures. Therefore, only one-stage methods
1085   can be considered in the context of weight matrix learner. Two kernel learning methods, GMM and
1086   FastFoodL , have been experimented in the non-Transformer setting (Yang et al., 2015), then adapted
1087   into the Transformer setting (Chowdhury et al., 2022). DGM (Deep Generative Model) has also been
1088   effective in modeling distributions (Kingma & Welling, 2013). It too has been adapted by Chowdhury
1089
       et al. (2022) for the Transformer setting. Due to the superior performance of FastFoodL with this
       technique, we have only experimented with this technique. We now explore them.
1090
1091
1092   FastFoodL (see Equation 17) is proposed to be learnable by Yang et al. (2015) with two variations:
1093   FSARD (scaling matrix S previously sampled from chi-squared is made learnable) and FSGBARD

       adapted by Chowdhury et al. (2022) to make S or S, G, B learnable parameters. In our experiments,
