009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Learning a robust policy that is performant across the state space, in a sample ef-
012            ficient manner, is a long-standing problem in online reinforcement learning (RL).
013            This challenge arises from the inability of algorithms to explore the environment
014            efficiently. Most attempts at efficient exploration tackle this problem in a set-
015            ting where learning begins from scratch, without prior information available to
016
               bootstrap learning. However, such approaches often fail to fully leverage ex-
               pert demonstrations and simulators that can reset to arbitrary states. These affor-
017
               dances are valuable resources that offer enormous potential to guide exploration
018
               and speed up learning. In this paper, we explore how a small number of expert
019            demonstrations and a simulator allowing arbitrary resets can accelerate learning
020            during online RL. We show that by leveraging expert state information to form
021            an auxiliary start state distribution, we significantly improve sample efficiency.
022            Specifically, we show that using a notion of safety to inform the choice of auxil-
023            iary distribution significantly accelerates learning. We highlight the effectiveness
024            of our approach by matching or exceeding state-of-the-art performance in sparse
025            reward and dense reward setups, including with images states spaces, even when
026            competing with algorithms with access to expert actions and rewards. Moreover,
027
               we find that the improved exploration ability facilitates learning more robust poli-
               cies in sparse reward, hard exploration environments.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Online reinforcement learning algorithms learn general behaviors without inductive biases and do-
033   main expertise through trial and error. By learning from environmental interaction, such methods
034   can potentially exceed the performance of supervised learning alternatives, reaching superhuman
035   performance levels on tasks such as Atari (Mnih et al., 2015) and Go (Silver et al., 2016). Despite
036   such successes, exploring environments efficiently remains challenging, resulting in long training
037   times (Pathak et al., 2017; Ecoffet et al., 2021; Song et al., 2023).
038   There has been a considerable amount of work on making online RL more efficient by promot-
039   ing exploratory behaviors that are novelty-seeking (Pathak et al., 2017) and state space-covering
040   (Haarnoja et al., 2018; Jain et al., 2023a; Seo et al., 2021). Although such approaches have the
041   potential to learn robust policies, the lack of task-directed exploratory cues (Mehta et al., 2022) and
042   a tendency to forget how to revisit promising exploration frontiers (Ecoffet et al., 2021) make them
043   inefficient at learning to solve hard-exploration tasks. Moreover, these methods have been designed
044
      to improve exploration efficiency without prior information. Consequently, when expert data or a
      simulator with arbitrary reset conditions are available, these approaches fail to adequately leverage
045
      these additional resources to accelerate exploration.
046
047   In contrast to conventional RL, imitation learning (Ho & Ermon, 2016) and offline RL (Kostrikov
048   et al., 2022; Kumar et al., 2020) can learn task-specific behavior purely from offline data. These
049   methods perform well within the distribution of the training data but fail to be robust in an out-of-
050
      distribution (OOD) setting, making them unsuitable for real-world applications where a sim2real
      gap is present. Moreover, they require access to expert actions and rewards, which is not always
051
      available in practice.
052
053   Hybrid RL approaches mix offline data with online interactions to bridge this gap and learn ro-
      bust policies efficiently. Bootstrapping online training with offline data is not straightforward, and

096   2   R ELATED W ORK
097
098   We explore related literature in this space through three broad category of methods: i) purely online
099   RL, ii) purely offline learning and iii) hybrid RL methods.
100
      Exploration in purely online RL: Exploration is an age-old problem in reinforcement learning
101
      that has received significant attention in the online RL context. Several methods inject additive
102   noise to the actions (Schulman et al., 2017) or network parameters (Burda et al., 2019) to perform
103   exploration. Such exploration is incidental to the primary objective of reward maximization and
104   not very efficient at exploring the state space (Song et al., 2023). Many approaches incentivize
105   exploratory behaviour through exploration bonuses such as surprise-maximizing intrinsic motivation
106   (Pathak et al., 2017), surprise-minimizing intrinsic motivation (Berseth et al., 2021), and action
107   (Haarnoja et al., 2018), state (Seo et al., 2021) and trajectory (Jain et al., 2023b) entropy maximizing
      rewards. Entropy maximization approaches fail to distinguish exploration in unseen regions from


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      exploration in regions of the state space where the policy is already proficient. This makes them
109   inefficient. While intrinsic motivation based methods are guided by a notion of surprise, they too
110   struggle in hard-exploration sparse-reward environments (Ecoffet et al., 2021). Moreover, these
111   methods are unable to leverage affordances like offline data and resetable simulators when available.
112
      Go-explore (Ecoffet et al., 2021) is a conceptual framework that disentangles the question of where
113
      to explore from how to get there. It is reminiscent of classical planners that first choose an explo-
114
      ration frontier, navigate to it quickly without exploring (or by resetting the simulator to that frontier
115   state) and then initiate exploration after arriving at the frontier. Go-explore maintains an archive of
116   visited states and chooses an exploration frontier from this archive either uniformly at random or
117   using a domain specific heuristic. Our work follows a similar theme but extends this framework by
118   investigating what is a good way of picking an exploration frontier. We present generic properties
119   that are desirable to have in this selection procedure and present a mechanism to select exploration
120   frontiers that will be broadly applicable across a range of tasks.
121   BARL (Mehta et al., 2022) is an information theoretic exploration method that uses a classical plan-
122   ner and a learnt posterior model to sample transitions that are maximally informative for the policy
123   to learn a given task. This enables it to solve tasks very efficiently. The setting used by its authors
124   bares close resembles to ours since they assume access to a simulator that supports arbitrary resets.
125   Moreover, their use of a Gaussian process (GP) to model the posterior is amenable to utilizing ex-
126   pert demonstrations during training. While very effective on small scale problems with dense reward
127   functions, BARL unfortunately does not scale to higher dimensions and sparse-reward settings. This
128   is confirmed by us in our experiments.
129   Learning policies efficiently offline: Another way to efficiently learn policies is by training on a
130   purely offline dataset of experiences. This sidesteps the issue of online exploration and efficiently
131   recovers a policy based on the offline dataset. Methods such as behaviour cloning and GAIL (Ho &
132   Ermon, 2016) fall under the broad class of imitation learning algorithms that model policy learning
133   as a supervised learning problem and learn a mapping from states to actions. A key issue with
134   imitation learning methods is that they are highly brittle and require access to large amounts of high
135
      quality expert data to succeed (Rashidinejad et al., 2021).
136   Offline reinforcement learning (Levine et al., 2020) is another offline learning paradigm capable of
137   efficiently learning policies from demonstration data of mixed quality while requiring good state
138   coverage in the offline dataset (Kumar et al., 2022; Rashidinejad et al., 2021). Consequently, a key
139   challenge with this approach is that the lack of online interactions leaves offline RL susceptible
140   to distribution shift. Wrongly estimating values for actions beyond the support of the dataset can
141
      hamper training (Levine et al., 2020). Conservative Q-learning (CQL) (Kumar et al., 2020) is a
      recent offline RL method that attempts to tackle this problem by maintaining pessimism within
142
      the Q-value function towards actions that are absent from the offline dataset. Implicit Q-learning
143
      (IQL) (Kostrikov et al., 2022) completely avoids predicting value estimates for unseen actions by
144   learning a distributional state-value function and computing an upper expectile over it to obtain the
145   value estimate of the best action in that state. These algorithms invariably encounter OOD states
146   and actions in-the-wild, quantities that such algorithm are not robust to by design, making online
147   finetuning a necessity for their real world deployment.
148
      Hybrid Reinforcement Learning: Hybrid reinforcement learning leverages a combination of of-
149
      fline data with online interaction to learn policies. The main challenge in hybrid reinforcement
150   learning is to devise methods that effectively bootstrap online learning from offline data. Several
151   approaches (Rajeswaran et al., 2018; Hester et al., 2018) do this by using imitation to learn a policy
152   from offline demonstrations before finetuning it with RL. However, most modern state-of-the-art
153   online RL algorithms are value based (Haarnoja et al., 2018; Schulman et al., 2017). Naively fine-
154   tuning an offline acquired policy with value based RL can cause significant performance degradation
155   as a value function of similar quality to the pretrained policy is not available at the start of online
156   finetuning (Uchendu et al., 2023). Though Monte Carlo return estimate based algorithms exist, their
157   online finetuning is known to be less efficient (Nair et al., 2020). Offline RL presents a transferable
158
      paradigm to train a policy and value function with identical objectives in both offline and online
      setups. However, not all offline RL methods are well suited for online finetuning due to their inher-
159
      ent pessimism towards distribution shift (Nair et al., 2020). Even better suited offline RL methods
160
      like IQL (Kostrikov et al., 2022) result in weaker policies after finetuning especially when limited
161   offline data is available (Uchendu et al., 2023). An alternative line of work (Song et al., 2023; Nair


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      et al., 2018; Vecerik et al., 2017) avoids finetuning a pretrained policy all together by pre-filling re-
163   play buffers at the start of training with transitions from the offline dataset. These transitions persist
164   through training and policy learning happens from scratch. JSRL (Uchendu et al., 2023) presents an
165   alternative approach to the finetuning-free idea of hybrid RL. It learns a guide policy from offline
166   data and uses it to roll out a part of the online episode before handing over control to a freshly ini-
167   tialized policy for completing the roll-out. The handover point is altered over the course of training
168   and all the captured experience is used to train the freshly initialized policy.
169   The proposed work also lies in this finetuning-free hybrid setting and shares similarities with JSRL.
170   Both the proposed work and JSRL conceptually belong to the Go-explore (Ecoffet et al., 2021)
171   family of algorithms. The two key differences between JSRL and the proposed work are: i) both
172   works encapsulate the idea of a reset distribution or equivalently a frontier state to explore from.
173   While ours is reached through environmental resets, JSRL uses a guide policy to reach it. For a
174   fair comparison, we ignore the time it takes JSRL’s guide policy to reach the handover point and
175   perform comparisons for the rollout beyond the handover point. ii) More importantly, JSRL induces
176   a specific kind of reset distribution through its variation of the handover point over the course of
177
      training. This differs from our reset distribution and we will show through our experiments that this
      is an important choice that influences the sample-efficiency and performance of the learnt policy.

191   Kakade & Langford (2002) have shown that training on p0 can down-weight the influence of unlikely
192   but important states during policy improvement by visiting them infrequently. Informally, these
193   states, which we refer to as task-critical states (C), are a set of states that would be a part of each
194   trajectory from a start state distribution p0 under an optimal policy π ∗ but have a low likelihood
195   under the state visitation distribution of an arbitrary policy π that is active during training (yellow

197   of C during training. As a result, the policy can quickly learn good actions for these states and utilize
198
      its training budget in propagating policy improvements to other more easily explorable parts of the

204   distribution (OOD) with respect to p0 (full equation in Appendix 7.2). A robust sample-efficient
205   policy would not only quickly learn to maximize Jp0 but would also improve robust performance
206   as measured by JµOOD . For policies designed to operate in the real-world, it is important for them
207   to generalize beyond the training distribution, making evaluation from µOOD a useful benchmark to
208   consider while training Rajeswaran et al. (2017).

      Overview: We first demonstrate state-of-the-art sample-efficiency and robustness of AuxSS on con-
297   tinuous sparse-reward hard-exploration tasks - one low dimensional 2D maze environment and two
298   variants of a high dimensional 3D Navigation task in Miniworld Chevalier-Boisvert et al. (2023)
299   with an image state space. Subsequently, we use a dense reward, easy exploration problem to show
300   that in the absence of strong safety cues, the presence of task critical states C enables AuxSS to
301   match algorithms with access to greater affordances. We then show that in hard exploration prob-
302   lems AuxSS is better suited for assimilating information from limited amounts of expert offline data
303   by demonstrating better sample-efficiency with 15× less offline expert data available to it. Finally,
304   we empirically demonstrate that approximating a more uniform visitation distribution over C through
305
      Ω facilitates accelerated learning. We showcase how AuxSS is a good way to approximate Ω while
      other distributions not motivated by state safety are not.

307   Setup: We conduct our experiments on three testbeds - a low dimensional sparse reward continuous
308   maze, two sparse reward high dimensional continuous 3D navigation tasks with image observations
309   (the first is an easier exploration instantiation and the second a harder exploration variant) and a
310   suite of three continuous control tasks in MuJoCo (Todorov et al., 2012) having dense rewards. For
311
      each task we assume access to one or two trajectories of demonstration data. For the maze tasks

      constitute hard exploration problems. They consist of large sections of untraversable regions that

316   Walker2D-v4. It constitutes an easier exploration problem where algorithms can quickly learn to
317   avoid early episode termination (see Appendix 7.9).

467   5.5   S TATE SAFETY INSPIRED START STATE SAMPLING FOR SAMPLE EFFICIENCY
468
469   In Section 4, we connect the notion of state safety Ω with task critical states (C) and discuss how
470   this can influence sample-efficiency. In this section, we empirically validate our claims. We modify
471   AuxSS by constructing a static distribution (Ω-SS) that samples start states with respect to a random
472   policy. Concretely, we sample start states inversely proportional to Ωπrand (s) where πrand (.|s) ∼
473   U |A| . In practice, we use Monte Carlo sampling of actions for a fixed time horizon (= 4 time
474   steps) to approximate this quantity for each state. Since the policy at the start of online training is
475   initialized randomly, this mimics the state safety distribution with respect to the policy at the start of
476
      training. Therefore if our claims hold we expect to see matching sample-efficiency trends to AuxSS
      in the early stages of training.
477
478   Figure 7 presents the findings of this study. We see that as expected, Ω-SS demonstrates matching
479   sample-efficiency and robustness trends as AuxSS early in training. In fact, since Ω-SS is the correct
480   state safety distribution with respect to the initialized policy from the start of training it learns even
481   faster than AuxSS, since AuxSS must gradually approximate this state safety distribution over the
482
      course of multiple training episodes.
483   We note here the divergence in robustness trends seen later in training. This is caused by the static
484   nature of Ω-SS which fails to adapt to the morphing C induced by the policy as it trains. This causes
485   the resulting loss of robustness. The dynamic nature of AuxSS helps prevent this degradation as its
      able to adapt its start state distribution based on changes in the policy.

502   5.6   D O START STATE DISTRIBUTIONS NOT DERIVING FROM STATE SAFETY FAIL TO BE
503         SAMPLE EFFICIENT ?
504
505   To study this inverse logical question, we construct two start state distributions, U-SS and GoalDist-
506   SS, that do not try to incentivize visitation of task critical states. U-SS is a static distribution that
507
      uniformly samples states from the provided demonstrations. GoalDist-SS is a dynamic distribution
      that exponentially weights states based on their distance from the task goal. States closer to the goal
508
      are assigned a higher probability to be sampled. The time varying component of this distribution
509
      arises from temperature scaling of the distribution with the temperature gradually rising over the
510   course of training. This promotes sampling near goal states early on in training and sampling more
511   uniformly from the demonstration data later on in training. More details about these distributions
512   can be found in Appendix 7.3.
513
      Figure 7 contains the findings of this study. It can be seen that both U-SS and GoalDist-SS are far
514
      slower to train than state safety inspired distribution demonstrating that not all start state distributions
515
      will accelerate learning. As a consequence of the poor choice of their state visitation, these methods
516   fail to learn good policies in the stipulated training budget and thus also have much lower robust
517   performance than AuxSS and Ω-SS (before its static nature causes robustness to degrade).

      6     D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
521
      In this work, we explore the use of commonly available affordances in RL tasks to guide online ex-
522
      ploration. We highlight the importance of auxiliary start state distributions, constructed by utilizing
523
      small quantities of expert demonstration comprising only state information, in facilitating sample-
524   efficient learning of robust policies. We find that in environments that allow arbitrary state resetting,
525   this is a very crucial design choice and we observe that deriving start state distributions from notions
526   of state safety can dramatically accelerate policy learning online. In terms of the Go-Explore philos-
527   ophy of disentangling the choice of exploration frontier and how to get there, this work sheds new
528   light on how the choice of exploration frontier can greatly influence sample-efficiency particularly
529   in hard exploration tasks. While the need for a simulator that supports arbitrary state resets can
530   be viewed as limiting, it is important to observe that most notable breakthroughs in RL have come
531   on the back of powerful simulators that enable such resetting. Consequently, understanding how to
532
      effectively utilize such an affordance is a pertinent question that this work seeks to address.
