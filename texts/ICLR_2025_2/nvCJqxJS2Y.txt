009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               We present SpectroMotion, a novel approach that combines 3D Gaussian Splat-
012
               ting (3DGS) with physically-based rendering (PBR) and deformation fields to
013            reconstruct dynamic specular scenes. Previous methods extending 3DGS to model
014            dynamic scenes have struggled to accurately represent specular surfaces. Our
015            method addresses this limitation by introducing a residual correction technique
016            for accurate surface normal computation during deformation, complemented by
017            a deformable environment map that adapts to time-varying lighting conditions.
018            We implement a coarse-to-fine training strategy that significantly enhances both
019            scene geometry and specular color prediction. We demonstrate that our model
020            outperforms prior methods for view synthesis of scenes containing dynamic specu-
021
               lar objects and that it is the only existing 3DGS method capable of synthesizing
               photorealistic real-world dynamic specular scenes, outperforming state-of-the-art
022
               methods in rendering complex, dynamic, and specular scenes.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023) has emerged as a groundbreaking technique in 3D
029   scene reconstruction, offering fast training and real-time rendering capabilities. By representing 3D
030   space using a collection of 3D Gaussians and employing a point-based rendering approach, 3DGS has
031   significantly improved efficiency in novel-view synthesis. However, extending 3DGS to accurately
032   model dynamic scenes, especially those containing specular surfaces, has remained a significant
033   challenge.
034   Existing extensions of 3DGS have made progress in either dynamic scene reconstruction or specular
035   object rendering, but none have successfully combined both aspects. Methods tackling dynamic
036   scenes often struggle with accurate representation of specular surfaces, while those focusing on
037   specular rendering are limited to static scenes. This gap in capabilities has hindered the application
038   of 3DGS to real-world scenarios where both motion and specular reflections are present.

      We present SpectroMotion, a novel approach that addresses these limitations by combining 3D Gaus-
055   sian Splatting with physically-based rendering (PBR) and deformation fields. Our method introduces
056   three key innovations: a residual correction technique for accurate surface normal computation during
057   deformation, a deformable environment map that adapts to time-varying lighting conditions, and a
058   coarse-to-fine training strategy that significantly enhances both scene geometry and specular color
059   prediction.
060
      Our evaluations demonstrate that SpectroMotion outperforms prior methods in view synthesis of
061   scenes containing dynamic specular objects, as illustrated in Figure 1. It is the only existing 3DGS
062   method capable of synthesizing photorealistic real-world dynamic specular scenes, surpassing state-of-
063   the-art techniques in rendering complex, dynamic, and specular content. This advancement represents
064   a significant leap in 3D scene reconstruction, particularly for challenging scenarios involving moving
065   specular objects.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
080
081
      Dynamic Scene Reconstruction. Recent works have leveraged NeRF representations to jointly
082
      solve for canonical space and deformation fields in dynamic scenes using RGB supervision (Guo
083
      et al., 2023; Li et al., 2021; Park et al., 2021a;b; Pumarola et al., 2020; Tretschk et al., 2021; Xian
084   et al., 2021). Further advancements in dynamic neural rendering include object segmentation (Song
085   et al., 2023), incorporation of depth information (Attal et al., 2021), utilization of 2D CNNs for
086   scene priors (Lin et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2023), and multi-view video compression (Li et al., 2022).
087   However, these NeRF-based methods are computationally intensive. To address this, recent research
088   has adapted 3D Gaussians for dynamic scenes (Yang et al., 2023c; Wu et al., 2023; Huang et al.,
089   2024; Liang et al., 2023c; Wang et al., 2024; Mihajlovic et al., 2024; Stearns et al., 2024), primarily
090   focusing on deforming spatial coordinates through deformation fields. Nevertheless, these approaches
091   do not explicitly account for changes in object surface during the deformation process. Our work
092
      extends this line of research by combining specular object rendering based on normal estimation with
      a deformation field, enabling each 3D Gaussian to effectively model dynamic specular scenes.
093
094
095   Reflective Object Rendering. While significant progress has been made in rendering reflective
096   objects, challenges arising from complex light interactions persist. Recent years have seen numerous
097   studies addressing these issues, primarily by decomposing appearance into lighting and material
098   properties (Bi et al., 2020; Boss et al., 2021; Li & Li, 2022; Srinivasan et al., 2020; Zhang et al.,
099
      2021b; Munkberg et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2021a; Verbin et al., 2024a; Zhao et al., 2024). Building
      on this foundation, some research has focused on improving the capture and reproduction of specular
100
      reflections (Verbin et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2023; Verbin et al., 2024b), while others have leveraged
101
      signed distance functions (SDFs) to enhance normal estimation (Ge et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023a;b;
102   Liu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023). The emergence of 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) has sparked a
103   new wave of techniques (Jiang et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023d; Yang et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2024;
104   Zhu et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2023) that integrate Gaussian splatting with physically-based rendering.
105   Nevertheless, accurately modeling dynamic environments and time-varying specular reflections
106   remains a significant challenge. To address this limitation, our work introduces a novel approach
107   incorporating a deformable environment map and additional explicit Gaussian attributes, specifically
      designed to capture specular color changes over time.

      3.2     S PECULAR R ENDERING
163
164   Limitations of existing methods. The current Dynamic 3DGS-based methods (Wu et al., 2023;
165   Yang et al., 2023c;b) encounter limitations in accurately modeling environments that include specular
166   objects. This issue arises from the inherent low-frequency characteristics of low-order spherical
167
      harmonics (SH), which are inadequate for capturing complex visual effects such as specular highlights.
      In contrast, other specialized 3DGS-based methods for static specular object scenes (Jiang et al.,
168
      2023; Liang et al., 2023d) often incorporate environment maps to model lighting, which is then
169
      combined with BRDF to simulate the entire scene. However, vanilla environment maps are not
170   suitable for modeling lighting scenarios that involve time-variant elements. This results in the existing
171   3DGS-based methods being insufficient for effectively modeling dynamic specular object scenes.
172
173   Proposed solution overview. To address these challenges, we introduce physical normal estimation
174   (Section 3.3) and deformable environment maps to model the specular color of real-world dynamic
175   scenes. However, this approach alone is insufficient, as precise scene geometry is crucial for accurate
176   reflections. Therefore, we introduce our coarse-to-fine training strategy, which helps stabilize scene
177
      geometry while simultaneously predicting accurate specular color. Our coarse-to-fine training strategy
      is divided into three stages: the static stage, the dynamic stage, and the specular stage. In the following
178
      paragraphs, we will introduce each of these stages in detail.
179

240   We decompose the final color ctfinal into diffuse and specular components to better distinguish
241   between high and low-frequency information:
242                                           ctfinal = cd + cts ⊙ stint ,                                 (11)
243   where cd is the diffuse color (using zero-order spherical harmonics as view-independent color),
244   stint ∈ [0, 1]3 is the learnable specular tint stored in each 3D Gaussian, and cs t is the view-dependent
245   color component. To manage the transition from spherical harmonics to ctfinal and mitigate potential
246   geometry disruptions, in the early specular stage, we fix the deformable Gaussian MLP and most 3D
247   Gaussian attributes, optimizing only zero-order SH, specular tint, and roughness. We temporarily
248   suspend densification during this phase. As ctfinal becomes more complete, we gradually resume
249
      optimization of all parameters and reinstate the densification process.
250   We further split the specular stage into two parts, applying a coarse-to-fine strategy to the environment
251   map. In the first part, we focus on optimizing the canonical environment map for time-invariant
252   lighting. This establishes a stable foundation for the overall lighting structure. In the second part, we
253   proceed to optimize the deformable reflection MLP for dynamic elements. This approach ensures a
254   more robust learning process, allowing us to capture the static lighting conditions before introducing
255
      the complexities of dynamic components.

      3.3     P HYSICAL N ORMAL E STIMATION
257
258   Challenges in normal estimation for 3D Gaussians. Normal estimation is crucial for modeling
259   specular objects, as it directly affects surface reflections. However, the discrete nature of 3D Gaussians
260   makes this process challenging, as it typically requires a continuous surface. GaussianShader (Jiang
261
      et al., 2023) observed that 3D Gaussians tend to flatten during training, leading to the use of the
      shortest axis as an initial approximation of the surface normal. To improve accuracy, they introduced
262
      a residual normal ∆n for each 3D Gaussian to compensate for errors in this approximation. However,
263
      this method alone is insufficient for deformed 3D Gaussians, as the residual should vary at each
264   time step. A straightforward approach of rotating the residual based on the quaternion difference
265   between canonical and deformed Gaussians proves inadequate, as it fails to account for shape changes
266   during deformation. If the shortest axis of the canonical 3D Gaussian is no longer the shortest after
267   deformation, this method results in incorrect rotation. Consequently, a more sophisticated approach
268   is needed to accurately model the normals of deformed 3D Gaussians. This approach must consider
269   both the rotation and the change in shape during the deformation process, ensuring accurate normal
      estimation for dynamic specular objects.

464
465   Different coarse to fine training strategy stages. As shown in the Tab. 4 and Fig. 8 , each stage
466   contributes effectively to the model’s performance. The Dynamic stage enhances object stability
467   compared to the Static stage alone, while the Specular stage improves reflection clarity beyond the
468
      combined Static and Dynamic stages. This coarse-to-fine approach establishes a stable geometric
      foundation before addressing complex specular effects. Note that the total iterations for each row in
469
      the Tab. 4 are 40,000.
470
471   Ablation study on coarse-to-fine, and loss function. The model’s performance was evaluated
472   without key components: the coarse-to-fine training strategy, normal loss Lnormal , normal regulariza-
473   tion Lreg , and γ k . Fig. 9 and Tab. 5 illustrate the effects of these omissions. Without the coarse-to-fine
474   approach, the model, trained directly at the specular stage, produces incomplete scene geometry,
475   affecting environment map queries for specular color. Omitting normal loss Lnormal removes direct
476   supervision on normals, impeding geometric refinement and reducing rendering quality. This also
477   leads to inaccurate reflection directions and less precise specular colors. Removing normal regular-
478   ization Lreg allows the normal residual to dominate normal optimization, resulting in insufficient
479
      optimization of the 3D Gaussians’ shortest axis towards the correct normal , which in turn reduces
      the rendering quality. Without γ k in normal regularization, the normal residual decreases for both
480
      non-flattened and flat Gaussians. This particularly affects less flat 3D Gaussians whose shortest axis
481
      significantly deviates from the surface normal. The insufficient normal residual correction causes
482   these 3D Gaussians’ shortest axes to deviate greatly from their original direction in an attempt to
483   align with the surface normal, ultimately reducing rendering quality.

      Tab. 6 demonstrate the superiority of the deformable environment map over the static environment

      SpectroMotion enhances 3D Gaussian Splatting for dynamic specular scenes by combining specular
      rendering with deformation fields. Using normal residual correction, coarse-to-fine training, and
536
      deformable environment map, it achieves superior accuracy and visual quality in novel view synthesis,
537
      outperforming existing methods while maintaining geometric consistency.

749
      We use deformable Gaussian MLP to predict each coordinate of 3D Gaussians and time to their
750   corresponding deviations in position, rotation, and scaling. As shown in Fig. 11, the MLP initially
751   processes the input through eight fully connected layers that employ ReLU activations, featuring
752   256-dimensional hidden layers, and outputs a 256-dimensional feature vector. This vector is then
753   passed through three additional fully connected layers combined with ReLU activation to separately
754   output the offsets over time for position, rotation, and scaling. Notably, similar to NeRF, the feature
755   vector and the input are concatenated in the fourth layer. For the deformable reflection MLP, we
      utilize the same network architecture, as shown in Fig. 12.
