009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding alignment techniques begins with comprehending zero-shot gen-
012            eralization brought by instruction tuning, but little of the mechanism has been
013            understood. Existing work has largely been confined to the task level, without
014            considering that tasks are artificially defined and, to LLMs, merely consist of
015
               tokens and representations. To bridge this gap, we investigate zero-shot general-
               ization from the perspective of the data itself. We first demonstrate that zero-shot
016
               generalization happens very early during instruction tuning, with loss serving as a
017
               stable indicator. Next, we investigate the facilitation of zero-shot generalization by
018            data arrangement through similarity and granularity perspectives, confirming that
019            encountering highly similar and fine-grained training data earlier during instruction
020            tuning, without the constraints of defined “tasks”, enables better generalization.
021            Finally, we propose a more grounded training data arrangement method, Test-
022            centric Multi-turn Arrangement, and show its effectiveness in promoting continual
023            learning and further loss reduction. For the first time, we show that zero-shot
024            generalization during instruction tuning is a form of similarity-based generalization
025            between training and test data at the instance level. We hope our analysis will
026
               advance the understanding of zero-shot generalization during instruction tuning
               and contribute to the development of more aligned LLMs.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The extraordinariness of large language models (LLMs) was originally brought by the zero-shot
032   generalization of instruction tuning (Brown et al., 2020). Early studies have found that when diverse
033   prompts are added to the inputs of traditional natural language processing (NLP) tasks and fed into the
034   model for instruction tuning, the model can generalize to tasks it has never encountered before (Chung
035   et al., 2024; Longpre et al., 2023; Sanh et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2021). To date,
036
      instruction tuning (Chung et al., 2024; Sanh et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2021) has become a crucial phase
      in LLM training, often preceding methods that incorporate preference data. In the meantime, the
037
      concept of “task” is also becoming increasingly blurred. Researchers are no longer constructing
038
      instruction data in the ways traditional NLP tasks dictate, but rather, they hope these tasks will be as
039   close to reality and as diverse as possible (Chiang et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2023; Rajani et al., 2023;
040   Taori et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024b).
041
042
      Although nearly all LLMs benefit from the zero-shot generalization brought about by instruction
      tuning, the in-depth and fine-grained research on this phenomenon is still insufficient. Particularly,
043
      there are few accurate and comprehensive conclusions about when and in what form it occurs, and
044
      how it would be influenced at an instance level. A line of existing research focuses on understanding
045   the relationships in task-pair transfer (Song et al., 2019; Vu et al., 2020; Zamir et al., 2018), suggesting
046   that not all tasks contribute positively to zero-shot generalization, with some may even result in
047   negative transfer effects (Kim et al., 2023; Muennighoff et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2022). These
048   works tend to train on one task and then evaluate on another (Kim et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2022), or
049   simply calculate intermediate task (Vu et al., 2020) or instruction (Lee et al., 2024) transfer scores.
050   However, all these efforts to select the most informative tasks to improve zero-shot generalization
051   are restricted to the task-based framework. This approach assumes that human-defined “tasks” and
052   even “categories” are sufficiently reasonable, but this is often not the case, as gaps exist regarding
053
      how humans and LLMs perceive the instruction tuning data. To this end, we strive to break free from
      the task-level framework and explore “generalization” from a more granular temporal perspective.

085   To further investigate why zero-shot generalization occurs early, we examine the generalization
086
      performance of each individual test task (a total of 225 tasks) by analyzing the changes in loss, rather
      than assessing overall generalization. We find that under different training data arrangements, the
087
      same test task can exhibit varying generalization behaviors. It may generalize rapidly in the early
088
      stages, while at other points, it can show slower or delayed generalization, or even fail to generalize
089   altogether, despite an overall tendency for early generalization. To understand the role of data in this
090   process, we identify two perspectives: similarity and granularity.
091
092
      From a similarity perspective, we discover that the model’s generalization is not truly “zero-shot”,
      as a high resemblance between the training and test data distributions could significantly impact
093
      generalization. To quantify similarity in experiments, we perform a linear combination of the average
094
      and minimum cosine similarity between the training and test data, revealing that encountering training
095   data with high similarity early during instruction tuning improves zero-shot generalization. From a
096   granularity perspective, we disclose that artificially defined “tasks” are not suitable for measuring
097   generalization. Instead, the similarity measure at the instance level serves as a better and more
098   essential indicator. We reveal through experiments that, by treating all data points equally without
099   the constraints of pre-defined tasks, we can better improve zero-shot generalization. As shown in
100   Figure 1, encountering highly similar and fine-grained training data earlier during instruction tuning,
101   without the constraints of defined “tasks”, enables better generalization. (Section 3)
102   Though the weighted similarity measure we adopt is effective in improving zero-shot generalization,
103   it still suffers from inherent limitations in that the individual role of each test data point cannot be
104   distinguished, as the whole test set is considered holistically during the calculation of similarity.
105   To this end, we propose the Test-centric Multi-turn Arrangement (TMA). TMA arranges training
106   data according to each individual test data instance and seeks to improve zero-shot generalization
107   regardless of the presence of predefined tasks. We demonstrate through experiments the effectiveness

111   We summarize our contributions as follows:
112   • We show that zero-shot generalization occurs at the very early stage during instruction tuning,
113     while loss serves as a more stable and fair metric compared to traditional ones.
114   • We identify similarity and granularity as two perspectives to gain a deeper understanding of
115     zero-shot generalization, revealing that encountering highly similar and fine-grained training data
116     earlier during instruction tuning enables better generalization.
117   • We propose the Test-centric Multi-turn Arrangement, a more grounded training data arrangement
118     method, and show that accessing high-similarity data during instruction tuning can facilitate
119     continual learning and further loss reduction.

121   2       P OSITIONING Z ERO -S HOT G ENERALIZATION
122
123   Early research shows that instruction tuning, which applies to various NLP tasks formatted with
124   instructions, can generalize to various unseen tasks. However, most studies (Chung et al., 2024; Iyer
125   et al., 2022; Longpre et al., 2023) focus on integrating diverse tasks or instruction templates, using
126   human-generated or synthetic data, or exploring different fine-tuning strategies, while few studies
127   address the timing of zero-shot generalization. To bridge this gap, we first seek to identify when
128   zero-shot generalization actually occurs during instruction tuning, and then justify that loss
129   serves as a more stable and fair metric to measure zero-shot generalization compared to traditional
130   ones including ROUGE series, Exact-Match, Reward Model scores, etc. We begin by giving a
131
      formalization of zero-shot generalization.
132   Formalization. In multi-task scenarios, zero-shot generalization refers to the ability to perform
133   effectively on unseen tasks (TUnseen ), while only trained on a subset of tasks (TSeen ). For each task
134   T ∈ TSeen ∪ TUnseen , there exists an instructional description IT , as well as several instances, where
135   each instance is composed of an input xT,i and an output yT,i . We define a model M as capable
136   of generalization on unseen tasks if, after training on every task in TSeen , given an unseen task
137
      T ∈ TUnseen , and for any (xT,i , yT,i ), the model’s output ŷ = M (IT , xT,i ) and the label yT,i achieve
      a score surpassing a certain threshold, with regard to the selected metrics, indicating successful
138
      generalization on the task T .

      3     FACILITATING Z ERO -S HOT G ENERALIZATION
245
246
      Acknowledging the importance of metrics in measuring the positioning of zero-shot generalization,
247
      we next seek to investigate why generalization occurs at an early stage and what role training
248   data plays during this phase. Our initial focus lies in the analysis of how various simple training
249   data arrangements affect zero-shot generalization in a fine-grained manner. Then, we investigate the
250   facilitation of zero-shot generalization from both data similarity and granularity perspectives.
251
252   3.1   E FFECT OF T RAINING DATA A RRANGEMENTS
253
254   The model receives only a limited amount of data at the early stage of instruction tuning. Therefore,
255   despite the scarcity, these data ought to play a significant role in facilitating generalization. Guided
256   by this intuition, we explore the impact of exposure to different training data arrangements from a
257   temporal perspective.
258   Data and Settings. We apply 1600 Flan-mini training tasks to get a series of instruction-tuned
259   checkpoints and evaluate them on various unseen test tasks in Flan-mini. As shown in Figure 4, we
260   examine the following three training data arrangements:
261
      • Round Robin: We select one data point from each task to form a data batch, ensuring that training
262
        tasks are as evenly distributed as possible.
263
      • Cluster: We arrange all data from each task together, resulting in task-level clusters throughout
264
        the entire training dataset.
265
266
      • Random: We randomly shuffle all training data as a baseline for comparison.
267   Results. Different training data arrangements entail different loss curve patterns. As shown in Fig-
268   ure 3, the pattern of random and round-robin scheduling is similar due to round-robin being an
269   extreme form of data shuffling. However, cluster scheduling differs largely from both. At certain
      steps during the instruction tuning, there exists a sudden decrease in average loss across different

      3.2     Z ERO -S HOT G ENERALIZATION THROUGH DATA S IMILARITY AND G RANULARITY
275
276   We have observed that training data arrangements could lead to significant changes in the loss curve
277   and that the timing of presence for certain data may greatly facilitate generalization on unseen
278   tasks. With these findings, we naturally ask what is the best arrangement that facilitates zero-shot
279   generalization the most, and how to arrange these “certain data” that improve early generalization. In
280   the following subsections, we seek to address these questions through two perspectives: similarity
281   and granularity.
282
283   3.2.1     I MPROVING Z ERO -S HOT G ENERALIZATION THROUGH H IGH -S IMILARITY DATA
284
      Previous research (Dai et al., 2019; Yauney et al., 2023) has consistently demonstrated that the
285
      performance of downstream tasks improves when the similarity between the pre-training data and the
286   downstream task data increases. This finding aligns with our intuitive understanding that data points
287   with higher similarity can better facilitate generalization. Based on these insights, we propose and
288   subsequently validate that:
289
290           Takeaway 3: Encountering data with high similarity early during instruction tuning will
291          greatly improve zero-shot generalization.
292
293
294   Selection of Similarity Measures. To validate our hypothesis, we first define what similarity
295
      measures to apply. Based on our setting and previous works, we investigate two main categories of
      similarity measures:
296
297    • N-gram similarity: We respectively measure the similarity distance by calculating the KL
298      divergence between the bigram word distributions of the training set data and test set data.
299    • Embedding similarity: We utilize all-MiniLM-L6-v2 1 from Sentence Transformer (Reimers
300      & Gurevych, 2019) to compute embeddings for each training and test data and then calculate
301      the Cosine and Euclidean similarity distances, as detailed in Appendix B.3. Next, we refer to
302      four classical distance calculation methods 2 , namely “max” (maximal distance), “min” (minimal
303      distance), “avg” (unweighted average distance), and “centroid” (centroid distance), to represent
304
         the distance from the training set data to test set data.
305   We analyze a series of instruction-tuned checkpoints on Flan-mini and calculate the similarity measure
306   score between the training data seen by the k th checkpoint and all the test data. This entails in total
307   nine similarity calculation methods ({N-gram} + {Cosine, Euclidean} × {max, min, avg, centroid}).
308   Please refer to Appendix B.3 for calculation details. Through analyzing Figure 15, we find that i)
309   Cosine and Euclidean measures show little difference; ii) the drop in minimum distance calculation
310
      coincides with the drop in loss curve; iii) average distance calculation promotes fairness by involving
      all test data in the actual calculation. Therefore, we consider using a simple linear combination of
311
      Cosine Average (Cosine-Avg) and Cosine Minimum (Cosine-Min) for similarity calculation in later
312
      experiments to raise the stability of computation and leverage the strengths from both Cosine-Avg
313   and Cosine-Min. We also prove they both satisfy optimal substructure, ensuring that the effect of
314   training set arrangement can accumulate over time as more data point is presented to the model with
315   good scalability, detailed in Appendix B.5.

      3.2.2   I MPROVING Z ERO -S HOT G ENERALIZATION THROUGH F INE -G RAINED DATA
359
360   Traditional methods to improve zero-shot generalization are mostly confined to the task level, focusing
361   on task-pair transfer. However, the so-called “tasks” or “categories” are artificially defined and, from
362   the perspective of LLMs, they are merely a collection of tokens or embedding representations.
363   Therefore, different “tasks” or “categories” may still appear relatively similar to LLMs, while
364
      instances from the same task may exhibit profound differences. Thus, we propose and validate that:
365
366
            Takeaway 4: Treating all data points equally in finer granularity without the concept of
           “task” as constraints better improves zero-shot generalization.
367
368
369   Data and Settings. We use the Flan-mini dataset and randomly sample up to 20 instances for each
370   training task. We employ two approaches to arrange the training data: i) coarse-grained setting,
371   where all instances under each training task are clustered. We define the embedding of a task as the
372   average embedding of all instances under that task and arrange the clusters based on NFT, as shown
373   in Figure 1. ii) fine-grained setting, where all data instances are directly arranged basen on NFT
374
      instead of being clustered first.
375   Results. Compared to the coarse-grained setting, the fine-grained setting is more beneficial for
376   improving zero-shot generalization. As shown in the right plot of Figure 6, the loss curve for the
377   fine-grained setting decreases more quickly and effectively, indicating that removing task framework
      constraints can further improve zero-shot generalization.

      4     U NDERSTANDING Z ERO -S HOT G ENERALIZATION
379
380
      In previous experiments, we empirically apply the linear combination of Cosine-Avg and Cosine-Min.
381   However, these measures inherently possess shortcomings: i) Cosine-Avg cannot distinguish the
382   variance within the test set. When the test set collapses to the point where all data points are the same,
383   the distribution becomes a spike; when the test set data is sufficiently spread out, the distribution
384   becomes uniform. In both cases, the Cosine-Avg distance from all test data points to a certain training
385   data point remains consistent, indicating that it could not distinguish the role of each test data point.
386   ii) Cosine-Min cannot holistically represent all test set data points. If a certain test data point is closer
387   to all training data points than all other test data points, Cosine-Min only considers the distance to
388   that test data point and does not take into account others, even if they are relatively far away.
389   Therefore, we seek an approach that can better distinguish and arrange the training data for enhanced
390   zero-shot generalization. To this end, we present the Test-centric Multi-turn Arrangement (TMA).

469   5     R ELATED W ORK
470
471
      In the course of deep learning’s development, several intriguing training dynamics have gradually
      been discovered, such as grokking (Power et al., 2022), double descent (Belkin et al., 2019), emergent
472
      abilities (Wei et al., 2022), and zero-shot generalization on unseen tasks. The first three phenomena
473
      have already received substantial explanations. For grokking, it happens from algorithm tasks (Power
474   et al., 2022) to a broader spectrum of realistic tasks (Liu et al., 2022), explained through the
475   Slingshot Effects (Thilak et al., 2022) or the competition between memorization and generalization
476   circuits (Varma et al., 2023). For double descent, Nakkiran et al. (2021) proposed that double descent
477   occurs both model-wise and epoch-wise, while Davies et al. (2023) unified grokking and double
478   descent through the learning speed and generalization ability of patterns. Regarding emergent abilities,
479   Schaeffer et al. (2024) suggested that the occurrence of emergent abilities is due to researchers using
480   non-smooth metrics. Hu et al. (2023) extended this idea by proposing an infinite resolution evaluation
481   to predict the emergence of abilities, and Du et al. (2024) attempted to understand emergent abilities
482
      from the perspective of loss. Research on these dynamics offers valuable insights for investigating
      zero-shot generalization, especially the work on emergent abilities. We suggest that model-wise
483
      emergent abilities may be analogous to step-wise zero-shot generalization.
484
485   LLMs have been proven capable of zero-shot generalization across a variety of downstream
      tasks (Brown et al., 2020), and instruction tuning has emerged as the most effective method to


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      achieve this (Chung et al., 2024; Sanh et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2021). The zero-shot generalization
487   phenomenon resulting from instruction tuning is crucial for building general LLMs. This means that
488   models trained on certain tasks can generalize well to unseen tasks. Multi-task datasets designed for
489   this purpose have continuously iterated in quality, quantity, and diversity, and numerous studies have
490   explored how zero-shot generalization occurs during the instruction tuning process. Sanh et al. (2021)
491   constructed P3 using explicit multitask learning, demonstrating that explicit task prompt templates
492   can promote zero-shot generalization. Wang et al. (2022) created the Super Natural Instructions V2
493   (NIV2) dataset, which comprises over 1600 task types, and empirically showed that more observed
494   tasks, an adequate number of training instances, and larger models improve generalization. Meta
495
      introduced OPT-IML (Iyer et al., 2022), investigating the impacts of dataset scale and diversity, dif-
      ferent task sampling strategies, and the presence of demonstrations on generalization. Subsequently,
496
      Longpre et al. (2023) proposed the Flan Collection, which encompasses up to 1836 tasks, and pointed
497
      out that scaling the number of tasks and model size, as well as incorporating chain-of-thought data,
498   can dramatically improve performance on unseen tasks.
499
500   In addition, a line of research focuses on understanding the relationships in task-pair transfer (Song
501
      et al., 2019; Vu et al., 2020; Zamir et al., 2018), suggesting that not all tasks contribute positively to
      zero-shot generalization; some tasks may even result in negative transfer effects (Kim et al., 2023;
502
      Muennighoff et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2022). However, a significant limitation of the aforementioned
503
      works is that, whether training on one task and then evaluating on another (Kim et al., 2023; Zhou
504   et al., 2022), or simply calculating intermediate task (Vu et al., 2020) or instruction (Lee et al.,
505   2024) transfer scores, all these efforts to select the most informative tasks to promote zero-shot
506   generalization are confined within the “task” framework. This approach is based on a premise: the
507   human-defined “tasks” and even “categories” are sufficiently reasonable. This is precisely the issue
508   our study strives to address: breaking free from the task-level framework to explore “generalization”
509   at a more fundamental level.
510
511
      6    C ONCLUSION
512
513   Our research sheds light on the mechanism underlying zero-shot generalization during instruction
514   tuning, moving beyond the conventional task-level analysis to a more data-centric and fine-grained
515   perspective. By demonstrating that zero-shot generalization occurs early during instruction tuning
516   and is significantly influenced by data similarity and granularity, we provide a new understanding
517   of how instruction tuning brings up zero-shot generalization. Our proposed Test-centric Multi-turn
518
      Arrangement further illustrates the importance of accessing high-similarity data early in the training
      process to facilitate continual learning and loss reduction. For future work, we suggest exploring the
519
      quantitative relationship between similarity distance and loss. Specifically, investigating whether
520
      similarity distance can predict a model’s generalization performance on new data could further help
521   the optimization of instruction tuning. We hope our findings will pave the way for developing more
522   aligned and robust LLMs, enhancing their ability to generalize effectively in diverse applications.

895   A.5    D ISCUSSION FOR M ETRICS
896
897
      In previous experiments, we have discovered that zero-shot generalization might occur early in the
      instruction tuning process based on the ROUGE series, Exact-Match, and RM score. However,
898
      these metrics may not be suitable for measuring generalization effectively. First, for the ROUGE
899
      series, ROUGE-1 refers to the overlap of unigrams, and ROUGE-L is based on the longest common
900   subsequence. Both metrics are limited to surface-level matching, primarily relying on lexical overlap
901   between model outputs and labels, to the extent that capturing semantic similarity or a deeper
902   understanding of the content conveyed in the sentences becomes challenging Ganesan (2018); Grusky
903   (2023). Outputs and labels with different wordings but similar meanings may receive low ROUGE
904   scores.
905
      While the reliability of ROUGE series scores is questionable, metrics like Exact-Match are nonlinear,
906   and previous research Schaeffer et al. (2024) has shown that nonlinear metrics are prone to observing
907   emergent abilities. Although emergence is a model-wise phenomenon, if we adopt such nonlinear
908   metrics step-wise, i.e., along the training timeline, we might also observe step-wise “emergence” so-
909   called generalization phenomena. This might lead to misjudgments regarding the timing of zero-shot
910   generalization. Therefore, we need to address this issue.
911
      Acknowledging that the Reward Model (RM) is trained on preference data, it is inevitable that there
912   will be a certain loss of ability when generalizing to out-of-distribution (OOD) datasets Singhal et al.
913   (2024). Consequently, scoring on different datasets may not be precise. As shown in Figure 2, RM
914   scores for NIV2 are notably higher than those for the other two datasets, indicating a bias. Further, we
915   compare the reward distribution with respect to the correctness of the model response respectively on
916   the three datasets. The large overlap under both curves in Figure 9 indicates that RM could not well
917   distinguish between responses of lower or higher quality. This further highlights the inappropriateness
      of using RM score to reflect zero-shot generalization.

1395
1396
       Although our research has made significant progress by discovering that zero-shot generalization
1397
       occurs in the early stage of instruction tuning and proposing various similarity distance measures
1398   to explore their impact on zero-shot generalization, we acknowledge that our study is far from
1399   perfect. Firstly, conducting a single experiment can be costly due to storage space requirements
1400   and computational resource limitations, so we only conducted limited explorations on a subset of
1401   datasets (NIV2, P3, flan-mini) using LLaMA-2-7B with a few runs, which may introduce biases
1402   in our conclusions. Secondly, the similarity distance measures we proposed may not have a strong
1403   theoretical foundation and can only serve as supplements to existing measures. Lastly, we chose loss
       as the metric for zero-shot generalization instead of traditional task-level evaluations often seen in


                                                                26
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1404
       benchmarks like MT-Bench. This is because we believe that traditional task-level generalization has
1405   certain limitations, as different tasks or categories may still appear relatively similar to LLMs, while
1406   instances from the same task may exhibit profound differences. However, this viewpoint still requires
1407   further validation. We hope future works can address these limitations.

1424   tuning and proposing several methods to improve zero-shot generalization. This contributes to
