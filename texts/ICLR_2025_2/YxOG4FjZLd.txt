011                                          Abstract
012
013           Vision and Language are two major modalities in Artificial Intelligence re-
014           search. Bridging the gap between these modalities has long been a key fo-
015           cus in the multimodal community. Inspired by human cognition, we believe
016           that if a model can see an image and directly associate it with its linguis-
017           tic meaning, the model possesses high-level intelligence that spans vision
018           and language. In our work, we focus on emojis in images, a widely-used
019           “cryptic symbol”, with a data form of both visual and linguistic features,
020
              i.e. emojis have the specific textual semantics while human understand
              the meaning from their visual information. Specifically, we first propose
              the novel task of translating emojis in images to corresponding idioms,
021
022
              thereby challenging Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) to (1)
023           understand the semantic correlation between language and emojis, and (2)
024           reason the intricate linguistic meaning from the emojis in images. To fa-
025           cilitate the advancement of this task, we construct a high-quality bench-
026           mark (Emoji2Idiom) following the process of automatic model generation
027           and human manual filtering. Based on our constructed Emoji2Idiom, we
028           employ multiple advanced MLLMs to conduct extensive experiments and
029           detailed analyses, demonstrating that existing MLLMs do not yet have
030           enough capability to understand and reason the linguistic information from
031
              visual data. We believe our proposed benchmark and interesting discover-
              ies will encourage the community to attach importance to the intelligence
              of MLLMs directly associating language from vision, to give MLLMs more
032
033
              comprehensive vision-language understanding ability 1 .

036   1   Introduction
037
038   Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have made remarkable progress and achieve-
039   ments in recent years Yin et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2023); Cui et al. (2024), especially
040   their visual-language understanding capabilities, which have laid a solid foundation for the
041   widespread development of multimodal applications Chen et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024a).
042   For how to improve the visual-language understanding capabilities of MLLMs, a core chal-
043
      lenge is how to bridge the gap between vision and language Koh et al. (2024); Peng
      et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024).
044
045   Naturally, we want to know to what extent MLLMs should understand vision and language

048   tremely ideal level. We notice that when a person sees an image, he or she can often directly
049
      associate it with the linguistic meaning behind the image. For example, when humans see
      special symbols, they can directly know the words represented by these symbols. Since pre-
050
      vious VQA-based benchmarks treat the vision and language separately, we try to transfer
051
      human analogy to MLLMs, when an image is fed to MLLMs, if MLLMs can directly

083   relatively advanced visual-language understanding intelligence. Images inputted
084   to MLLMs here have clear and discrete semantic meanings, manifested as concrete concepts,
085   specific symbols or logos, etc. The semantic information of these images can be accurately
086   interpreted as linguistic concepts or tokens.
087   Inspired by the above thinking, our work aims to explore the ability of MLLMs to directly
088   understand the linguistic meaning behind images. It is exciting that many graphic symbol
089   codes in cryptography exist as special indicators in an image so that when people see
090   the image, they can decode the textual meaning of the image. We have noticed that emoji
091   are increasingly becoming a kind of “cryptic symbol” widely used by people from
092   all over the world and from all cultural backgrounds Mostafavi & Porter (2021). People not
093
      only use emoji to enrich their expressions and show their moods, but also directly use emoji
      to replace the corresponding text Fischer & Herbert (2021). Emojis have specific textual
      semantics, while human understand the meaning from their visual representations instead of
094
095
      directly treating emojis as characters. Thus, emojis in images are strongly coupled with their
096   corresponding linguistic meanings, contributing to becoming the basis of our benchmarks
097   and serving as a bridge between visual and linguistic understanding. The understanding of
098   emoji not only requires MLLMs to comprehend the image information of individual emoji
099   but also to combine its textual indications with the related contexts so that the model can
100   further explore the deeper meanings of emoji. Therefore, understanding emojis in images is
101   a challenging vision-language task.
102   To promote the research on cryptic symbol emoji understanding of MLLMs, we propose a
103   novel task that requires MLLMs to receive input image information of emoji sequences and
104   generate their corresponding text information, shown in Figure 1. We introduce the visual
105   modality of emoji and require the MLLMs not only to identify the linguistic meaning of
106   individual emoji but also to understand the special utterances of the emoji and its associated
107   context, contributing to generating text with special semantic and format, e.g., a word or
      an idiom. Specifically, our task aims to challenge the following capabilities of MLLMs:

      (1) Harmonized Word Reasoning: Translating emojis into texts usually harmonizes
109   with a sound-like word. Therefore, MLLMs need to have rich language knowledge, to reason
110   about the harmonic words.
111
112
      (2) Abstract visual understanding of image: Emoji symbols often have strong indica-
      tive meanings, which requires MLLMs to deeply understand the abstract visual character-
113
      istics of emoji, rather than just the visual shape.
114
115   (3) Many-to-one or one-to-many mapping generation problem: According to our
116   observation, it is common for multiple emojis to correspond to one word, or one emoji to
117   correspond to multiple words. This requires the MLLMs to make correct predictions based
118
      on the origin emoji understanding and to realize the complex reasoning via context.
119   Furthermore, we construct the Emoji2Idiom benchmark to support the task of translating
120   cryptic symbol emojis in images to corresponding texts. To enrich the diversity of the bench-
121   mark, we set up emoji-to-Chinese idiom, emoji-to-English word, and emoji-to-English idiom
122   tasks, taking into account the language and semantic diversity. After automatic filtering and
123   manual filtering by human experts from raw data, we obtain a high-quality dataset. From
124
      the above challenges of understanding and the design of diverse benchmark tasks, we hope
      that MLLM can not only realize the complex understanding of real-world text substitution
125
      expressions using emoji, but also generalize to other cryptographic symbols understanding.
126
      We hope to realize a generalized unified visual-verbal understanding benchmark instead of
127   the traditional VQA-based benchmarks, which treat visual and verbal information sepa-
128   rately. Based on our constructed Emoji2Idiom, we employ multiple advanced MLLMs to
129   conduct extensive experiments and detailed analyses, demonstrating that existing MLLMs
130   do not yet have enough capability to understand and reason the linguistic information from
131   visual data. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
132
133        1. We first propose the task of translating a sequence of emojis in images to cor-
134
              responding texts, aiming to guide MLLMs to perform high-level vision-language
              understanding like humans.
135
136        2. We build the high-quality Emoji2Idiom benchmark, which is a new data resource
137           that can facilitate MLLMs to better understand cryptic symbol in images.
138        3. We conduct experiment of advanced MLLMs on Emoji2Idiom and provide some
139           detailed analysis, interesting discoveries , and valuable insights for the community
140           to further improve the visual-language understanding capabilities of MLLMs.

182   3.1   Task Definition
183
184   Given an image of a sequence of emoji Iiemoji = {emoji1 , emoji2 , · · · , emojin }, Emoji2Idiom
185   task aims to translate emojis in images to corresponding idiom text by model F :
                                                        Text = F (Iiemoji ).
186
                                                                                                                          (1)
187
188
      These emoji sequences correspond to texts with specific formats and semantics, represent-
189
      ing a Chinese idiom word, an English word, or an English idiom sentence. It requires not
190
      only understanding the direct corresponding text of a single emoji, but also inferring com-
191   plex linguistic meaning based on the surrounding emoji context, containing some harmonic
192   characters, multiple emoji mapping to a single character, or one emoji mapping to multiple
193   words. These more complex emoji understanding problems are prevalent in our dataset. We
194   further discuss the specific properties and challenges in Section 3.3 and Appendix C.
195
196   3.2   Benchmark Construction
197
198   Raw Data Collection As shown in the Figure 2, we collect raw data through two au-
199   tomatic generation methods: Retrieve from the Internet. There are a large number of
200   databases for guessing the corresponding words and idioms based on emojis on the internet,
201
      which can be obtained freely without commercial usage. We retrieve the relevant web pages
      of the game, web databases, and the video to get the original emoji images and the corre-
202
      sponding text answers. Generate Emoji Based on Text. The quality of internet retrieval is
203
      not high, due to 1) recurring emoji-text pairs, 2) a relatively higher number of four-word
204   idioms compared with a few multi-word idioms, and 3) a relatively low number of English
205   idioms. We select the texts of common English words, English idioms, and Chinese idioms,
206   and generate the corresponding emoji sequences by the text-to-emoji translation.
207
208   Data Annotation and Filtering Automatic filtering. The machine is utilized to auto-
209   matically perform data cleaning, including deletion of duplicate values and ethical checking.
210
211         • Deletion of duplicate values and missing values, etc. Notably, the machine au-
212           tomatically removes duplicate combinations of the same emoji inputs but retains
213
              combinations of the same text result with different emoji inputs.
214         • Perform ethical checking. Some of the emoji may contain expressions of violence,
215           pornography, or other safety violations, and we utilize GPT-4o to check all the
              images and remove those that are not ethically safe.

216
      Human Filtering. In this phase, we engage human experts to refine the semantics of emoji-
217   text pairs, focusing on the following aspects:
218
219         • Removal of Non-standard Idioms: Phrases like “蓝天白云 (blue sky and white
220           clouds)” lack historical context and cultural significance, leading to their exclusion.
            • Elimination of Low Consistency Pairs: Annotators assess the alignment be-
221
222
              tween emoji and text. Pairs with weak correlations are discarded to make the
223           translation process overly difficult and reducing the image’s indicative meaning.
224
225
            • Exclusion of Unclear Images: Images that are unclear to recognize, such as
              those from low-resolution video screenshots, are scored on their clarity. Images that
226
              score poorly are removed to ensure legibility.
227
228         • Mitigation of Repetitive Mappings: Frequent mappings, such as “ ” to “结
229           (jie)”-“节 (jie)”, can introduce data bias. To address this, we employ diverse emoji
230           databases, and manually adjust or remove repetitive mappings beyond ten times.
231         • Filtering of Unethical Content: We rigorously filter for emoji-text pairs linked
232           to violence, discrimination, or other inappropriate themes. A wide range of emoji
233           including multiple skin tones and gender categories is utilized to promote expression.
234
235   To eliminate subjectivity in manual filtering, we provide annotators with detailed guidelines
236   as shown in Appendix A. And additional information about our human filtering can be
237   found in Appendix A.

247   3.3   Data Statistic Analysis and Other Features
248
249
      We give the statistics of our proposed Emoji2Idiom in Table 1. In the Chinese idioms task,
      we collect 1,876 and 334 emoji-text pairs of four-character idioms and multi-character idioms,
      respectively. Among them, since the combinations of four-character idioms are naturally
250
251
      much larger than those of multi-character idioms in dictionaries, such a difference in the
252   distribution is similarly reflected in our dataset. For English words, we set the tasks of emoji
253   to word and idiom, with 842 and 783 sets of image-text pairs, respectively. There are some
254   additional details about Emoji2Idiom in the Appendix B. In our Emoji2Idiom, we observe
255   several interesting linguistic phenomena, and present some examples, with additional details
256   provided in Appendix C. The linguistic phenomena raise great challenges of Emoji2Idiom,
257   and also encourage the exploration of vision-language capabilities of MLLM.
258
259   Word Split In the English word, it is common for multiple emoji to represent one word.
260   For instance, the word “Panda” can be split into “Pan-” and “-da,” where “Pan-” corresponds
261   to . Beyond understanding the meaning of individual emojis, the MLLM must also remove
262   unnecessary letters and combine the parts to infer a completely new word.
263
264   Harmonic Characters Since it is sometimes difficult to find directly related emoji to
265   represent, harmonic characters with similar pronunciations are often chosen to replace them.
266   For example, “To be loaded”, “To” harmonizes with “Two”          , and “be” harmonizes with
267   “bee”     . In the Chinese idiom “难舍难离”, “舍” harmonizes with “蛇 (snake)” of emoji
268      , “离” harmonizes with “梨 (pear)” of emoji        . The understanding of these harmonics
269   usually requires the model to synthesize the relevant context of the emoji, to reason out the
      correct expression of the harmonized words.

288   Cross-cultural Issue Discussion Emoji, as a simple and universally recognized symbol,
289   is widely used across many countries, especially on global social platforms. While cultural
290   nuances are inevitable, emojis generally facilitate cross-cultural understanding. Our dataset
291   tries to minimize ambiguities and emotional shifts caused by complex linguistic contexts,
292   focusing on a sequence instead of a single emoji. In addition, Our dataset is constructed
293   with careful consideration of emoji diversity, covering categories such as smiley faces and
294   emotions, humans and bodies, animals and nature, food and drinks, travel and places,
295
      activities, objects, symbols, and flags.

355   always suffer from hallucination problems. When they catch a linguistic meaning of a single
356   emoji, they quickly focus on the word or idiom related to this emoji from the inner knowl-
357   edge they have, and ignore the relevant context of the emojis. Based on our Emoji2Idiom,
358   the community can explore the hallucination problem and improve the inference ability.
359
360   Evaluation of the semantic similarity of the response We further experiment the
361   semantic similarity between the responses and the ground truth. We input the model output
362   answers and ground truth into LLM and let LLM score the semantic similarity from 1 to
363   5. As shown in the Table 4.2, we observe that the average scores of the model on the

      English task are significantly higher than those on the Chinese task. In addition, when we
      carefully observe the distribution, we find that 1)for the Chinese task, most of the scores are
      concentrated in 1 and 2, which indicates that the MLLM can almost barely guess; 2)while
365

469   Harmonization Problem MLLMs often fail on harmonization problems. As shown in
470   Figure 6,     - “蛇 (snake, sound like “she”)” homonym to “舍 (leave, sound like “she”)”

      but the MLLM fails to recognize. In the English idiom,       - “whale” homonym to “well”.
472   Our Emoji2Idiom includes many harmonic character phenomena. Current MLLMs are not
473   yet capable of effectively capturing emoji context and reasoning with harmonic words, and
474   struggle with our challenging Emoji2Idiom.
475
476   Hallucination Problem In Figure 6, the model recognizes          and immediately outputs
477   “horsing around”, without considering other emojis. Another example shows GPT-4v and
478   GPT-4o recognizing the number       and associating it with the idioms “朝三暮四 (change
479   one’s mind often)” and “颠三倒四 (disorderly)”, both containing the number 3, without
480   considering the surrounding emojis. That is due to the hallucination problem. MLLMs
481   often think narrowly, focusing only on words or idioms directly related to a single emoji.
482   Our Emoji2Idiom is concerned about this issue, and look forward to further exploration of
483   the poor performance of MLLMs that we have discovered.

      this mapping issue, where MLLMs fail to perform a multi-to-one or one-to-multi mapping.

531   5    Conclusion
532
533
      We propose the Emoji2Idiom benchmark, containing emoji to Chinese idioms, English
534   words, and English idioms. It provides a way to measure the ability of MLLM to un-
535   derstand complex emoji symbol sequences on images. We design a measurement framework
536   containing harmonic characters, abstract visual understanding, and many-to-one mapping
537   problems, to validate the ability of the MLLM to synthesize the understanding of emoji
538   contexts with emoji-to-text coupled reasoning and generation. We evaluate advanced open-
539   source and closed-source MLLMs with our dataset, analyze the results, and highlight future
      research directions with case studies.

      A.1   Automatic Filtering
705
706   In this phase, we mainly utilize machines and large language models to filter large-scale
707   data, which includes the following steps in total:
708
709         1. Deletion of default values. We utilize a machine to automatically remove incomplete
710
               emoji-idiom pairs, including those with missing corresponding emoji images and
               those with missing corresponding idioms. It is guaranteed that each emoji image
711
               corresponds to the standard idiom answer one by one.
712
713         2. Removing Duplicate Values. We utilize the machine to automatically remove dupli-
714
               cate emoji-idiom pairs. Here, we only need to remove the emoji-idiom pairs corre-
               sponding to identical emoji sequences while retaining the pairs with the same idiom
               text but corresponding to different emoji representations, which helps to enhance
715
716
               the diversity of the dataset. Note that we will first filter the pairs corresponding
717            to the same idiom text by the machine with additional labels, and make a manual
718            decision on whether to perform the deletion in the next stage of manual filtering.
719
            3. Image Quality Check. We utilize LLM (specifically GPT-4o is used), to perform
720            image quality checking, which entails marking and removing: images that are too
721            blurry and those that do not meet the ethical norms (images that contain elements
722            of violence, abusive language, discrimination, etc.) along with their corresponding
723            idioms.
724         4. Text Ethics Checking. We utilize LLM (specifically GPT-4o) to perform text ethics
725            checking, which involves tagging and deleting idiom with elements of violence, dis-
726            crimination, abuse, etc. For example, “红颜祸水” is a sexist idiom, and we will
727            delete its corresponding emoji-idiom pair.
728
729   A.2   Human Filtering
730
731   In this phase, we invited human experts in Chinese and English languages to perform manual
732   data filtering, which included the following steps in total:
733
            1. Duplicate value checking: for the automatic filtering phase, the machine flags a
734
               portion of emoji-idiom pairs where the text is the same but the corresponding
735            images are not the same. the human expert needs to further check whether the
736            emoji expressions here are really different. For the pairs with identical emoji images,
737            the human expert will delete them.
738
            2. Image quality check: Human experts further check whether the emoji images are
739            unclear and illegible, and remove the illegible images.
740
            3. Idiom standardization check: Human experts need to check whether the idiom text
741
               expression is standardized, including the format of the idiom, whether it has a
742            specific linguistic meaning, and whether it is in line with common human usage,
743            etc., to ensure that our dataset meets the real-world usability. For example, for the
744            idiom “blue sky and white clouds”, although it is a four-word idiom that conforms to
745            the norms of human usage, it does not have a specific allusion, mythological story,
746            traditional story background, or special semantic meaning, and does not belong
747            to the standard idioms. For example, although “流水高山” is a four-letter word
748            with a specific historical background, people more often use the expression “high
749            mountains and flowing water”. Therefore, “流水高山” is not an expression that
750
               conforms to human language usage and will be deleted.
751         4. Emoji and Idiom Relevance Check: Since in emoji to idiom expression, many times
752            the representation of harmonic characters will be utilized, which will increase the
753            difficulty of emoji comprehension and the difficulty of generating the final idioms.
754
               Human experts will evaluate the relevance of emoji to idioms:
755              • If too many or too complex harmonic characters are used with the emoji rep-
                     resentation, at this time the task will be too difficult for not only MLLM but


                                                     14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
                   also humans to understand. At this point, the human expert will consider the
757                relevance of this emoji sequence to the idiom to be too low and delete the
758                emoji-idiom pair.
759             • It is noteworthy that we conducted an evaluation of human ability on this
760                benchmark in Sec. 4.4 and found that humans achieved an average score of
761                66.5 on the word-level accuracy of Chinese idioms. This score demonstrates
762                both that our dataset is challenging and that the task is accomplishable, and
763                that there is still much room for improvement in the performance of the current
764                MLLM on this task.
765        5. Repeated harmonic word mapping check: due to the limited expression of emoji,
766           when using emoji to replace textual expressions, harmonic words are often used to
767           find the corresponding emoji for expression. emoji2idiom also has a large number
768           of harmonic words. However, we found that if the mapping of the same emoji
769           corresponding to a certain harmonic word occurs too many times, it may cause data
770           bias to LLM in subsequent training, i.e., when LLM sees this emoji it automatically
771
              thinks of this harmonic word that occurs multiple times. To mitigate the bias caused
              by this harmonic word mapping, we performed:
772
773             • Count the repeated emoji-character harmonic word mappings, and when there
774                are more than ten occurrences, we manually replace the expression of the emoji
775
                   (find other harmonic word counterparts to replace the original repeated emoji),
                   or just delete the redundant emoji-idiom pair.
776
                • In addition, we also considered this issue during the original data collection.
777
                   Our retrieval and collection in different sources of the original emoji database
778
                   can reduce this duplicate mapping. We also take different generation methods
779                when manually constructing text-to-emoji data, which also helps to increase
780                the diversity of harmonic word mappings.
781
           6. Safety and Ethics Check: Based on the automatic detection, the human experts
782
              further conducted a safety and ethics check of the emoji images and idiom text,
783           checking whether there are any issues such as violent gore, abusive language, sexism,
784           racial discrimination, stereotyping, and so on, in the data.

789   safety checks. We also provide at least three examples for each item. For ethical safety
790   checks, we distinguish between subcategories such as violence, abusive language, gender
791
      discrimination, stereotyping, and racial discrimination. We provide examples at both the
      emoji and text levels to guide judgments.

1005   C.3   Chinese Idiom Format
1006
1007   Chinese idioms are a special kind of words, which often have specific formats and seman-
1008   tic information, so they cannot directly translate the meaning of a single emoji and con-
1009   catenate words into sentences. The most common format is four-character idioms, which
1010
       often come from ancient Chinese myths, historical stories, classics, etc., consisting of four
       Chinese characters, with a Chinese literary style, and often a symmetrical structure. In
1011
       addition, multi-character idioms, although far fewer in number than four-character idioms,
1012
       are equally important components. Some of them have less than four words (e.g., three-
1013   character idioms) and some have more than four words. Generally speaking, whether it
1014   is a four-character idiom or a multi-character idiom, it follows the one-to-one relationship
1015   between emoji and characters, but there are special cases.
1016
1017
       Chinese character mapping Usually, idioms follow a one-to-one relationship between
1018   emojis and characters, but there are special cases. First of all, there will be multiple emo-
1019   jis corresponding to one character. Often, many numbers will have this correspondence,
1020   especially those with large digits. For instance, “                       ” denotes the “万”
1021   (ten thousand). In addition, there is a mapping relationship between multiple characters in
1022   an emoji. This kind of correspondence is relatively rare, usually in multi-character idioms,
1023   and this one-to-many mapping relationship occurs when two or more characters can form a
1024   new word represented by an emoji. The above two mapping relationships require MLLM to
1025   further complete the understanding and reasoning of multiple emoji contexts on the basis
       of recognizing the meaning of a single emoji.

1146   D.2     Details of Evaluation Metrics for different tasks
1147
       D.2.1    Chinese Idiom Task
1148
1149   In the task of Chinese idioms, we evaluate them separately at the word level and at the
1150   character level. At the word level, we first calculate the Word level accuracy, which is the
1151   ratio of the number of words that exactly match the ground truth to the total number
1152   of words. In order to further validate the image-to-language comprehension and reasoning
1153   ability of MLLM, we further propose the Chr-1 and Chr-2 indicators at the word level, which
1154   represent the ratio of the number of words with one or more characters correctly and two
1155
       or more characters correctly compared to ground truth, to the total number of words. At
       the character level, we compare the difference between each character in the predicted word
       and each character in the ground truth to calculate the Precision, Recall, and F-1 values.
1156
1157
1158
       D.2.2    English Word Task
1159
1160   In the task of English words, we evaluate them separately at the word level and at the char-
1161   acter level. At both levels, we compare the difference between the predicted word/character
1162   and each word/character in the ground truth, calculating the Precision, Recall, and F-1
1163   values.
1164
1165   D.2.3    English Idiom Task
1166
       In the task of English idioms, we evaluate them separately at the sentence level and at the
       word level. At both levels, we compare the difference between the predicted sentence/word
1167
1168
       and each sentence/word in the ground truth, calculating the Precision, Recall, and F-1
1169   values. In addition, to further measure the similarity of the generated sentences to ground
1170   truth, we further calculated BLEU-1 and BLEU-2 values.

1172   D.3     Disccusion of Metrics
1173
1174   Since our primary goal is to propose the emoji-to-idiom task and assess MLLM’s ability
1175   to understand and reason about the textual semantics corresponding to abstract
1176   visual information, our work primarily focuses on task formulation, data construction,
1177   and the underlying assessment approach. We believe this task fills a crucial gap in evaluating
1178   MLLM’s visual capabilities in representing abstract symbols and bridging the visual-verbal
1179
       divide. Therefore, our current assessment metrics compare predicted answers with standard-
       ized answers that have undergone rigorous automated and manual filtering across multiple
       granularities, and we have not yet explored further metrics in our evaluation.
1180
1181
1182
       D.3.1    Discussion about Ground Truth
1183
1184   It is worth noting that an emoji has different meanings in different cultures and contexts,
1185   which is one of the key challenges in emoji-to-idiom task. Therefore, instead of focusing on
1186   understanding the direct meaning of a single emoji (in fact, the current MLLM can directly
1187   give multiple possible meanings for a single emoji), we provide a specific contextual context
       (a sequence of multiple emojis with a specific semantic meaning) to limit the semantic of

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
       single emoji. In addition，the correct answer of emoji sequence needs to meet the meaning
1189   of each emoji in the sequence and the structural information of the sequence, which largely
1190   avoids the generation of multiple possible answers.
1191
1192
       Certainly, in the process of data collection, we did encounter a very small number of scenarios
1193   where other answers were barely acceptable. For example, “         =    ”, the standard answer
1194
       is “Health is wealth”, while the other possible answer is “Money is power”.But there are
       two problems here: 1) the predicted answer does not fully satisfy the structural information
1195
       of the sequence, i.e., translating the idiom from left to right.2）The length of this emoji
       sequence is very short, which makes the possible prediction results more variable. As the
1196
1197
       length of the sequence becomes longer, the less likely it is that other matching answers
1198   will appear.In our data, the average length of the series is 4.11, 4.23, 7.48, 5.32 in Chinese
1199   four-character idioms, English words, Chinese multi-character idioms, and English idioms.
1200   Therefore, we believe that it is feasible to provide a standard answer to predict the outcome
1201   for evaluation, and to measure the consistency of emoji and text.

1203   D.3.2    Discussion about Furthur Metrics
1204
1205
       In future work, we plan to develop additional evaluation metrics to better assess MLLM’s
       ability to bridge the multimodal divide between vision and language. Our goals include:
1206
1207
             • Adding semantic similarity metrics: Our current metrics primarily quantify
1208           the direct correspondence between the standard answer and the generated result,
1209           with a relative lack of semantic similarity calculation. Incorporating semantic sim-
1210           ilarity into our evaluation, particularly in human assessments, will provide a more
1211           comprehensive measure of performance.
1212         • Measuring the similarity between the emoji’s original visual information
1213           and the final prediction: By annotating emojis with a standardized language
1214           base, we can compare results to predictions more effectively. For example, the emoji
1215           “ ” might correspond to the textual interpretation “sun, 阳 (read as ’yang’)” and
1216           relate to the harmonic word “养 (read as ’yang’)” in the final ground truth. While
1217           a predicted result like “日 (sun)” might not match the direct character level, it
1218           captures the initial visual information of the emoji and should be scored
1219           accordingly. This step will help identify specific bottlenecks MLLM faces in this
1220           task, whether in visual understanding, harmonic character mapping, or textual
1221           reasoning.
1222         • Including GENERATION metrics: In addition to common generative met-
1223           rics (e.g., ROUGE, METEOR, diversity, complexity), we will consider task-specific
1224           metrics, such as adherence to idiomatic format specifications, like meeting the four-
1225           character idiom requirement.

1630   Based on these results and error case studies, we propose potential training methods and
1631
       frameworks that could significantly improve MLLM performance in visual-linguistic tasks,
       drawing inspiration from human approaches to joint visual-semantic reasoning:
1632
1633
            • Direct fine-tuning: We can incrementally pre-train MLLMs on an emoji-rich corpus
1634          to build a basic understanding of emoji. Our initial tests indicate that MLLMs
1635          already demonstrate a foundational grasp of emoji, performing well in many cases.
1636          Following pre-training, we suggest a 4:1 division of the fine-tuning dataset and
1637          test set, with direct fine-tuning on the pre-trained MLLM. This method mirrors
1638          human learning, where repeated practice after initial knowledge acquisition leads
1639          to mastery in a specific domain.
1640        • Incorporating Chain of Thought (CoT) design: When translating emoji to idioms,
1641          we can model the process after human reasoning. This CoT design references the
1642          process of human thinking and reasoning, which can assist MLLM to think about
1643          idiom generation in a structured way, and is better able to further analyze where
1644          exactly MLLM goes wrong and provide inspiration for subsequent research work.
1645          We hope that such reasoning can be further generalized to more general symbol
1646
              understanding, and our emoji2idiom data can also be used as part of general symbol
              understanding to evaluate the general symbol understanding capability of the large
              language model.
1647
1648
1649        • Adding a symbol mapping set as external knowledge: A single emoji may correspond
1650
              to multiple characters. By constructing an emoji-to-character mapping set, we can
              enable MLLM to learn possible alignments. This approach is similar to how humans
              use external knowledge to accomplish tasks that might be challenging without it.
1651
1652
1653        • Multi-agent invocation: Referring to the CoT process, we can utilize multiple intel-
1654
              ligences for tasks like emoji comprehension, harmonic word association, and emoji
              combination, allowing for integrated task planning, memory iteration, and refined
              reasoning.

1657   Finally, our work significantly contributes to enhancing the visual comprehension and rea-
1658   soning capabilities of MLLMs. Most current unified evaluation metrics focus on MLLM’s
1659   understanding of natural images, often overlooking abstract visual information and symbolic
1660   representations—areas that receive less attention during training. Additionally, MLLMs
1661   struggle with recognizing complex textual information in images, particularly handwritten
1662   text or intricate symbols. We believe our emoji2idiom task not only complements existing
1663   evaluations of abstract symbolic representations but also offers a solution for deeper visual
1664   reasoning, thus promoting the development of visual-textual alignment and multimodal uni-

       fication architecture.
