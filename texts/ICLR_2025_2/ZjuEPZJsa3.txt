                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Readers can have different goals with respect to the text they are reading. Can
010
               these goals be decoded from the pattern of their eye movements over the text?
011
               In this work, we examine for the first time whether it is possible to decode two
012            types of reading goals that are common in daily life: information seeking and or-
013            dinary reading. Using large scale eye-tracking data, we apply to this task a wide
014            range of state-of-the-art models for eye movements and text that cover different
015            architectural and data representation strategies, and further introduce a new model
016            ensemble. We systematically evaluate these models at three levels of generaliza-
017            tion: new textual item, new participant, and the combination of both. We find that
018            eye movements contain highly valuable signals for this task. We further perform
019            an error analysis which builds on prior empirical findings on differences between
020
               ordinary reading and information seeking and leverages rich textual annotations.
               This analysis reveals key properties of textual items and participant eye move-
021
               ments that contribute to the difficulty of the task.1

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026
      Reading is a ubiquitously practiced skill that is indispensable for successful participation in modern
027   society. When reading, our eyes move over the text in a saccadic fashion, where there are periods of
028   time in which the gaze is stable at a specific location, called fixations, and rapid transitions between
029   fixations called saccades (Rayner, 1998). This sequence of fixations and saccades is generally hy-
030   pothesized to contain rich information about how readers interact with text. Automatic decoding of
031   such information is currently a growing area of research (e.g. Reich et al., 2022a; Shubi et al., 2024).
032
      In daily life, a reader may have one or several goals that they pursue with respect to the text. For
033   example, they may read the text closely or skim it to obtain the gist of the text’s content, they may
034   proofread it, or they may be seeking specific information of interest. Each such goal can have a
035   profound impact on online linguistic processing and on the corresponding eye movement behavior
036   while reading. Despite the many reading goals readers pursue in everyday life, research on eye
037   movements in cognitive science as well as work that integrated eye movements data in Natural
038   Language Processing (NLP) and machine learning (ML) have primarily focused on one reading
039   regime, which can be referred to as ordinary reading. In this regime, the reader’s goal is typically
040   general comprehension of the text. Nearly all broad coverage datasets used in research on eye
041
      movements in reading, such as Dundee (Kennedy et al., 2003), MECO (Siegelman et al., 2022) and
      CELER (Berzak et al., 2022) were collected in this regime. Other forms of reading, although widely
042
      acknowledged (Radach & Kennedy, 2004), received much less attention and remain understudied.
043
044   In this work, we go beyond ordinary reading and ask whether broad reading goals can be reliably
045   decoded from the pattern of the reader’s eye movements over the text. We focus on the distinction
046   between ordinary reading and information seeking, a highly common reading regime in everyday
047
      life, where the reader is interested in obtaining specific information from the text. Prior work sug-
      gests that on average across participants and texts, there are substantial differences in eye movement
048
      patterns between these two reading regimes (Hahn & Keller, 2023; Shubi & Berzak, 2023). How-
049
      ever, it is currently unknown whether there is sufficient signal in eye movements for automatic
050   decoding of the reading goal given eye movements of a single participant over a single textual item.
051   Furthermore, little is known about the factors that contribute to the difficulty of this task.

067   In this work, we address this gap by conducting a series of experiments on reading goal decoding.

               insights on the nature of the task.

086   2    TASK
087
088   We address the task of predicting whether a reader is engaged in ordinary reading for comprehension
089   or in seeking specific information, based on their eye movements over the text. Let S be a participant,
090   P a textual passage, and EPS the recording of the participant’s eye movements over the passage.
091
      Given a ground-truth mapping C(S, P ) → {Information Seeking, Ordinary Reading}, we aim to
      approximate C with a classifier h:
092
093                           h : (ESP , P ) → {Information Seeking, Ordinary Reading}
094
095   Where the passage P is an optional input, such that the classifier can be provided only with the eye
096
      movement data ESP or with both the eye movements and the underlying text P . We assume that the
      participant has not read the paragraph previously.
097
098   The information seeking regime is operationalized by presenting the participant with a question Q
099   prior to reading the passage. This question prompts the participant to seek specific information in
100   the text. We assume that the classifier does not receive the question nor any information on the
101   participant. Figure 1 presents the task schematically.

103   3    M ODELING
104
105   Eye movements during reading present a highly challenging case of temporally and spatially aligned
106   multimodal data, where fixations are temporal and correspond to specific words in the text. We use
107   a wide range of state-of-the-art architectures for processing this data. While the models were origi-
      nally applied to other prediction tasks, such as predicting participant’s reading comprehension and


                                                             2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      various participant properties, they offer general purpose mechanisms for processing eye movement
109   data during reading that can be applied to other tasks, and cover different architectural and data rep-
110   resentation strategies. The models can be broadly divided along three primary axes, the modalities
111   used (eye movements-only, or eye movements and text), the granularity at which eye movement
112   information is represented (global averages across the text, single word, or a single fixation), and for
113   the multimodal approaches, the nature of the text representations and strategy for combining them
114   with eye movements. These models are directly applicable, in some cases with small adaptations, to
115   the task of reading goal prediction. See Figures 5 and 6 in Appendix A for model diagrams.
116
117   3.1   E YE M OVEMENTS - ONLY M ODELS
118
      These models use only eye movement information, without taking into account the text. Such mod-
119
      els are valuable in common scenarios where the underlying text for the eye movement recording
120
      is not available. It is also the go-to approach when the eye-tracking calibration is of low qual-
121   ity, leading to imprecise information on the location of fixations with respect to the text. This is
122   a highly common situation, especially with web-based eye-tracking and lower grade eye-tracking
123   devices. Beyond practical considerations, the eye movements-only approach allows assessing the
124   added value of textual information for our task.
125
126   G LOBAL R EPRESENTATION
127
      Logistic Regression A logistic regression model with global eye movement measures from Mézière
128
      et al. (2023). The measures include averages of word reading times, single fixation duration, forward
129
      saccade length, the rate of regressions (saccades that go backwards), and skips (words that were not
130   fixated) during first pass reading (i.e. before proceeding to the right of the word). All the features
131   are standard measures from the psycholinguistic literature.
132
133   F IXATION -BASED R EPRESENTATION
134
135   BEyeLSTM - No Text is our adaptation of the BEyeLSTM model (Reich et al., 2022a) described
136
      below, which uses only eye movements input without the underlying text. This model is more
      expressive compared to the logistic regression model, in that rather than averaging eye movement
137
      measures for the entire trial, it explicitly represents each fixation in the eye movement trajectory.
138
      The final hidden state of an LSTM (see below), combined with global eye movement features, is
139   then used for classification.
140
141
      3.2   E YE M OVEMENTS AND T EXT M ODELS
142
143   We examine a number of recent multimodal models that combine eye movements with textual in-
144   formation. The models encode textual information in two ways. The first is using contextual word
145   embedding representations commonly used in NLP. The second is via linguistic word property fea-
146   tures, including word length, word frequency and surprisal (Hale, 2001; Levy, 2008), which are
147   motivated by their ubiquitous effects on reading times (Rayner et al., 2004; Kliegl et al., 2004;
148
      Rayner et al., 2011, among others).
149   The models implement three primary strategies for combining the two modalities at progressively
150   later stages of processing: (i) in the model input, (ii) merging them within intermediate model repre-
151   sentations, or (iii) with architectures that fuse the modalities using cross attention mechanisms after
152   each modality has been processed separately. Furthermore, since eye movements in reading are both
153   temporally and spatially aligned with the underlying text, the models can be categorized based on
154
      how they capture this alignment: (i) by aggregating eye movements for each word, thereby focusing
      on spatial alignment; or (ii) by aggregating eye movement information for each individual fixation,
155
      which explicitly encodes both spatial and temporal correspondences between eye movements and
156
      text.
157
158   W ORD - BASED R EPRESENTATIONS
159
160   RoBERTa-Eye-W(ords) (Shubi et al., 2024) is a RoBERTa transformer model (Liu et al., 2019)
161   augmented with eye movements. This model concatenates word embeddings and word-level eye
      movement features in the model input.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      MAG-Eye (Shubi et al., 2024) Integrates word-level eye movement features into a transformer-
163   based language model by injecting them into intermediate word representations using a Multimodal
164   Adaptation Gate (MAG) architecture (Rahman et al., 2020). The text is aligned with eye movements
165   by duplicating each word-level eye movement feature for every sub-word token.
166
167
      F IXATION - BASED R EPRESENTATIONS
168
169   PLM-AS (Yang & Hollenstein, 2023) This model represents the eye movements sequence by re-
170   ordering contextual word embeddings according to the order of the fixations over the text. This
171   reordered sequence is processed through a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN), whose final hidden
172   layer is used for classification.
173
      Haller RNN (Haller et al., 2022) This model is similar to PLM-AS in that it receives word em-
174   beddings in the order of the fixations. Differently from PLM-AS, each word embedding is further
175   concatenated with eye movement features.
176
177
      RoBERTa-Eye-F(ixations) (Shubi et al., 2024) uses the same architecture as RoBERTa-Eye-W, but
      represents fixations rather than words. Each fixation input consists of a concatenation of the word
178
      embedding and eye movement features associated with the fixation.
179
180   BEyeLSTM (Reich et al., 2022a) represents both the fixation sequence and textual features, com-
181   bining LSTMs (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and global features through a linear layer.
182   Eyettention (Deng et al., 2023) is a model that consists of a RoBERTa word sequence encoder and
183   an LSTM-based fixation sequence encoder. It uses a cross-attention mechanism to align the input
184   sequences. We use the adaptation of this model by Shubi et al. (2024) for binary classification.
185
      PostFusion-Eye (Shubi et al., 2024) is a model that consists of a RoBERTa word sequence en-
186
      coder and a 1-D convolution-based fixation sequence encoder. It then uses cross-attention to query
187   the word representations using the eye-movement representations, followed by concatenation and
188   projection into a shared latent space.
189

      3.3   BASELINES
191
192
      When examining the utility of eye movements for a prediction task, it is important to benchmark
193
      models against simpler approaches that do not require eye movement information (Shubi et al.,
194   2024). Here, we introduce two such baselines:
195
196   Majority Class Assigns the label of the majority class in the training set to all the trials in the test
197
      set. Since our data is balanced (see below), this baseline is equivalent to random guessing.
198   Reading Time (per word) Total reading time per word, computed by dividing the total reading time
199   of the paragraph by the number of words in the paragraph. This behavioral baseline does not require
200   eye-tracking and is motivated by the analyses of Malmaud et al. (2020), Hahn & Keller (2023) and
201   Shubi & Berzak (2023), which indicate that on average, reading is faster in information seeking
202   compared to ordinary reading.

      4.1   DATA
207
208
      Addressing the proposed task is made possible by OneStop Eye Movements (Malmaud et al., 2020),
209
      the first dataset that contains broad coverage eye-tracking data in both ordinary reading and infor-
210   mation seeking regimes. The textual materials of OneStop are taken from OneStopQA (Berzak
211   et al., 2020), a multiple-choice reading comprehension dataset that comprises 30 Guardian articles
212   from the OneStopEnglish corpus (Vajjala & Lučić, 2018). Each article is available in the original
213   (Advanced) and simplified (Elementary) versions. Each paragraph has three multiple choice read-
214   ing comprehension questions that can be answered based on any of the two paragraph difficulty
215   level versions. Each question is paired with a manually annotated textual span, called the critical
      span, which contains the vital information for answering the question. The mean paragraph length


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      is 109 words (min: 50; max: 165; std: 28). An example of a OneStopQA paragraph along with one
217   question and its critical span annotation is provided in Table 2 in Appendix B.
218
219   Eye movements data for OneStopQA were collected in-lab from 360 adult native English speakers
220
      using an EyeLink 1000+ eye tracker (SR Research), a state-of-the-art eyetracking device for reading
      research. Each participant read a batch of 10 articles (54 paragraphs) paragraph by paragraph and an-
221
      swered a comprehension question about each paragraph. Each paragraph was shown to a participant
222
      either in the Advanced or Elementary version, along with one of the three possible questions. The
223   experiment has two between-subjects reading goal tasks: information seeking and ordinary reading.
224   In the information seeking task, participants were presented with the question (without the answers)
225   prior to reading the paragraph. In the ordinary reading task, they did not receive the question prior
226   to reading the paragraph. In both tasks, after having read the paragraph, participants proceeded to
227   answer the question on a new screen, without the ability to return to the paragraph.
228
      In sum, each participant read 54 paragraphs, all of which are either in information seeking or in
229   ordinary reading. Each paragraph was read by 120 participants: 60 in ordinary reading and 60 in
230   information seeking (split equally between the Advanced and Elementary versions of the paragraph).
231   Overall, the data consists of 19,438 trials, where a trial is a recording of eye movements from a single
232   participant over a single paragraph. The data is balanced, with 9,718 trials in ordinary reading and
233   9,720 in information seeking. Figure 7 in Appendix B shows example trials for each of the two
234   reading regimes. Additional data statistics are described in Appendix B.

345   5.1     L OGISTIC E NSEMBLE
346
347   Ensemble models combine predictions from multiple individual models, and often yield better re-
348   sults than single models, especially when models have diverse predictive behaviors (Sagi & Rokach,
349   2018). To examine the appropriateness of this approach for our case, we compute the agreement
350
      between models’ predictions, quantified with Cohen’s Kappa (Cohen, 1960). Figure 3a depicts the
      pairwise agreement rates across models in the validation data, where we observe mostly moderate
351
      agreement rates. Figure 9 in Appendix E further suggests consistency across the three evaluation
352
      regimes. These outcomes indicate that while the examined models do not always differ substantially
353   in accuracy, they likely capture different aspects of the eye movement data and the task.
354
355   Motivated by the results of this analysis, we introduce a simple Logistic Ensemble: a 10-feature lo-
356
      gistic regression model that predicts the reading goal from the probability outputs of our 10 models.
      The model is trained for each fold on the validation set and tested on the corresponding three test
357
      sets. In Figure 3b we present the performance of the Logistic Ensemble, against the best perform-
358
      ing model, RoBERTa-Eye-F, and the Reading Time baseline. We find that the Logistic Ensemble
359   improves over the accuracy of RoBERTa-Eye-F in all the regimes, with an accuracy of 77.3 for
360   New Items, 64.6 for New Participants, 64.3 for New Items & Participants, and 70.5 overall. The
361   improvements over RoBERTa-Eye-F are statistically significant in all but the New Item & Partici-
362   pant regime, in which it is the only model that is statistically better than the Reading Time baseline.
363   These performance improvements suggest that the information encoded by the different models is
364   to some extent complementary.

      6       W HAT M AKES THE TASK E ASY OR H ARD ?
368
369   Having established that the prediction task at hand can be performed with a considerable degree of
370   success, we now leverage the best performing model to obtain insights about the task itself. We
371
      do so by examining which trial features, which were not given to the model explicitly, affect the
      ability of the model to classify trials correctly. This analysis takes advantage of the rich structure
372
      and auxiliary annotations of the OneStop dataset.
373
374   We define 10 features that capture various aspects of the trial. These include the following reader

      The vast majority of the literature on the psychology of reading and its interfaces with computational
481
      modeling is concerned with ordinary reading. However, several studies did address goal-oriented
482   (also referred to as task-based) reading. Most prior work focused on a small number of canonical
483   tasks: skimming, speed reading and proofreading. Several studies found different eye movement
484   patterns in these tasks as compared to ordinary reading (Just et al., 1982; Kaakinen & Hyönä, 2010;
485

              Random effects structure is simplified not to include slopes due to model convergence issues.

      Schotter et al., 2014; Strukelj & Niehorster, 2018; Chen et al., 2023). Rayner & Raney (1996) ex-
487   amined differences between ordinary reading and searching through the text for a target word. Prior
488   work also analyzed eye movements during human linguistic annotation, often used for generating
489   training data for NLP tools, such as annotation of named entities (Tomanek et al., 2010; Tokunaga
490   et al., 2017). Differences in reading patterns were also found when readers were asked to take dif-
491   ferent perspectives on a given text (Kaakinen et al., 2002) or given different sets of learning goals
492   (Rothkopf & Billington, 1979). We further note that information seeking has also been previously
493   examined in visual search (Jang et al., 2014a;b; Sharma et al., 2023).
494   Our work is closest to Malmaud et al. (2020), Hahn & Keller (2023) and Shubi & Berzak (2023) who
495   analyzed eye movement differences between ordinary reading and information seeking, where the
496   information seeking goal is formulated using a reading comprehension question. All three studies
497   found substantial differences in fixation and saccade patterns in information seeking as compared
498   to ordinary reading, in particular before, within and after the text portions that are critical for the
499   information seeking task. Here, we build on these findings, and examine whether these differences
500   can be leveraged for automatic discrimination between these two reading regimes.
501   While the above studies focus primarily on descriptive data analysis, Hollenstein et al. (2023) took
502   a predictive approach and attempted to automatically classify the reading task from eye movement
503   features. In this study, participants read single sentences from the ZuCo corpus (Hollenstein et al.,
504   2020), and engaged either in ordinary reading or in an annotation task where they had to determine
505   the presence of one of seven semantic relations in the sentence. While this benchmark is conceptu-
506   ally related to the current work, it is restricted in scope and ecological validity. First, the benchmark
507
      contains only 18 participants, which makes generalization and reliable evaluations highly challeng-
      ing. More fundamentally, the framework is limited by the nature of the tasks, which focus on highly
508
      specialized linguistic annotations that are not performed by readers in everyday life. If viewed
509
      through the lens of question answering, the setup comprises only seven possible questions that con-
510   cern the presence of a semantic relation in the sentence. In the current study we take a different
511   and more general stance on task based reading, with unrestricted questions that are more represen-
512   tative of the tasks commonly pursued by readers. Finally, additional related lines of work used eye
513   movements in reading for prediction of other types of reader cognitive state, such as reading com-
514   prehension (Reich et al., 2022b; Shubi et al., 2024), as well as inferring properties of the text such
515   document type (Kunze et al., 2013) and readability level (González-Garduño & Søgaard, 2017).

      8   S UMMARY AND D ISCUSSION
518
519
      Is it possible to decode reader goals from eye movements? We address this question by examining
520
      the possibility to automatically discriminate between ordinary reading and information seeking at
521
      the challenging granularity level of a single paragraph. We find that it is indeed possible to perform
522   this task with considerable success, and show that eye movements provide valuable information for
523   this task, above and beyond reading time. We further find that different models capture different
524   aspects of the signal and the task, and can be successfully combined with ensembling, which yields
525   the strongest results for our task. Our error analysis leverages the models to further reveal new
526   insights on the factors that determine task difficulty. Our evaluation setup which addresses different
527   aspects of model generalization provides an infrastructure for future work on the proposed task.
528   Our study has a number of important limitations. The information seeking tasks are over individual
529   paragraphs that span 3-10 lines of text. This leaves out shorter texts (e.g. single sentences) as well
530   as longer texts. It is also restricted to newswire texts, and does not include texts from other genres.
531   Finally, new datasets for the information seeking task, other types of tasks, additional populations
532   (e.g. second language readers, younger and older participants), and datasets in languages other than
533   English are all needed in order to study goal decoding more broadly.
534   While the current work takes a first step in addressing the proposed task, ample room for per-
535   formance improvements remains for future work, especially in the new participant regimes. New
536   strategies for modeling eye movements with text are likely needed to fully exploit the potential of eye
537   movements for this task. Furthermore, the addressed task is fundamentally limited in that it does not
538   distinguish between different information seeking tasks. A natural direction for future work could
539   address decoding of the specific question that was presented to the participant in the information
      seeking regime.

       C    M ODEL T RAINING AND H YPERPARAMETERS
973
974
       Since the models we use were developed for different tasks and datasets, we conducted a hyperpa-
975    rameter search for each model. The search space for each model is described below. In all cases, it
976    includes the optimal parameters reported in the work that introduced the model, extended to provide
977    a fair comparison between models.
978
       For all neural models we train with learning rates of {0.00001, 0.00003, 0.0001} and dropout of
979
       {0.1, 0.3, 0.5} following Shubi et al. (2024). Additionally, for all models that make use of word
980
       embeddings, we include both frozen and unfrozen language model variants in the search space.
981
982          • For Logistic Regression, the search space for the regularization parameter C is
983            {0.1, 5, 10, 50, 100}, with and without an L2 penalty.
984
             • Following (Reich et al., 2022a), for BEyeLSTM and BEyeLSTM-No Text, the search
985            space consists of learning rates {0.001, 0.003, 0.01}, embedding dimensions {4, 8} and
986            hidden dimensions {64, 128}.
987
             • For MAG-Eye the search space for injection layer index is: {0, 11, 23}.
988
989          • Following Yang & Hollenstein (2023), we train PLM-AS and Haller RNN with dropout
990
               rate search space of 0.1, and for PLM-AS, we use LSTM layer counts of 1, 2. Additionally,
               as in (Haller et al., 2022), we search over LSTM hidden layer sizes of 10, 40, 70. For PLM-
991
               AS, the LSTM hidden layer size is fixed at 1024 to match the LM’s dimensionality in Yang
992
               & Hollenstein (2023).
993
994
             • For Eyettention, we also train with a learning rate of 0.001 and dropout of 0.2, as done in
               (Deng et al., 2023)
995
996          • For PostFusion-Eye, the 1D convolution layers have a kernel size of three, stride 1 and
997            padding 1.
998
       We train the deep-learning based models for a maximum of 40 epochs, with early stopping after 8
999
       epochs if no improvement in the validation error is observed. Following Liu et al. (2019); Mosbach
1000
       et al. (2021); Shubi et al. (2024) we use the AdamW optimizer Loshchilov & Hutter (2018) with a
1001   batch size of 16. MAG-Eye, RoBERTa-Eye and PostFusion-Eye use a linear warm-up ratio of 0.06,
1002   and a weight decay of 0.1. We standardize each eye movements feature using statistics computed
1003   on the training set, to zero mean unit variance. Models are implemented based on code from (Shubi
1004   et al., 2024).
