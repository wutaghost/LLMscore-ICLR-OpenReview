029                                                    A BSTRACT
030
031            This work addresses the challenge of quantifying originality in text-to-image (T2I)
032            generative diffusion models, with a focus on copyright originality. We begin
033            by evaluating T2I models’ ability to innovate and generalize through controlled
034            experiments, revealing that stable diffusion models can effectively recreate unseen
035
               elements with sufficiently diverse training data. Then, our key insight is that
               concepts and combinations of image elements the model is familiar with, and saw
036
               more during training, are more concisly represented in the model’s latent space. We
037
               hence propose a method that leverages textual inversion to measure the originality
038            of an image based on the number of tokens required for its reconstruction. We
039            demonstrate our method using both a pre-trained stable diffusion model and one
040            trained on a synthetic dataset, showing a correlation between the number of tokens
041            and image originality. Our approach is inspired by legal definitions of originality
042            and aims to assess the trained model, without relying on specific prompts or having
043            access to the training data. This work contributes to the understanding of originality
044            in generative models and has implications for copyright infringement cases.

047   1   I NTRODUCTION
048
049   Large-scale Text-to-Image (T2I) Generative Diffusion-based models have revolutionized our ability
050   to produce visual content. T2I models, as their name suggests, are designed to produce images given a
051   textual prompt. Distinctively from a search engine, these models are not meant to retrieve an existing
052   image, but rather generate novel content that fits the description of the text. Hence, as the outcomes
053   of the model generally did not exist before, quantifying generated originality remains a formidable
      challenge in practice and theory.

054
      This challenge is not solely scholastic, and arises in the context of legal concerns surrounding
055   copyright laws, where T2I models, trained on expansive datasets (Schuhmann et al., 2022) that include
056   copyrighted materials, are often at the center of infringement accusations. Here too, quantifying
057   originality poses a challenge as copyright law only protects the aspects of expressive works deemed
058   original by the judiciary (Harper & Row, Publishers, Inc. v. Nation Enterprises, 1985; Feist
059   Publications, 1991; U.S.C, 1990), where originality necessitates a minimal degree of creativity and
060   authorship (Feist Publications, 1991).
061   In turn, methodologically sound methods for demonstrating creativity and originality in a T2I model
062   become a pressing matter. Traditional strategies often formalize the problem of not-copying as a
063   form of memorization constraint that inhibits overfitting of the data (Carlini et al., 2023; Bousquet
064   et al., 2020; Vyas et al., 2023). This is also highlighted in the recently implemented EU AI Act,
065   which mandates the disclosure of training data (Institute for Information Law (IViR), 2023), imposing
066   difficult transparency in the operation and training of these models. However, regulating memorization
067   is not necessarily aligned with the purpose of copyright law (Elkin-Koren et al., 2023), can be
068   overly restrictive, and also poses computational, practical, and statistical challenges (Feldman, 2020;
069
      Feldman & Zhang, 2020; Attias et al., 2024; Livni, 2024; Zhang et al., 2016).
070   In this paper we consider an alternative viewpoint. Instead of looking at the training data and what
071   information it holds, we analyze the model itself and what it had actually learned from the information
072   the data has to offer. Specifically, We investigate whether T2I models can themselves be utilized to
073   discriminate between generic and original content, according to their understanding of the world.
074   Toward this goal, we propose a quantitative framework to evaluate image originality based on the
075   model’s familiarity with training data. Our framework, tested on synthetic and real-world data,
076   demonstrates T2I models’ potential in identifying originality, helping develop metrics for auditing
077   generative models and analyzing image originality.
078
      We begin by assessing how well T2I models can innovate and generalize in a controlled set of
079   experiments using synthetic data. Experiments to assess the generalization capabilities of generative
080   models have been conducted in previous work (Zhao et al., 2018; Okawa et al., 2024), but the effects
081   of textual conditioning have yet to be explored. As we demonstrate through a series of experiments,
082   textual conditioning can help deepen our understanding of generalization. Our experiments reveal that
083   stable diffusion models are particularly adept at recreating unseen elements when sufficiently diversi-
084   fied data is used. Overall, our findings underscore demonstrate prompted generations can creatively
085   combine seen elements alongside unimaginative reconstruction, and underscore the importance of
086   diversity in datasets (Lemley & Casey, 2020).
087   Next, we introduce our conceptual framework to quantitatively measure originality or genericity of
088   images, followed by a practical implementation of it. Inspired by the theoretical work of Scheffler
089   et al. (2022), we look at the complexity of description as a measure of originality. The working
090   hypothesis is that common concepts are easier to describe in the machine’s language (i.e., the latent
091   space) than original concepts. Scheffler et al. (2022) build on the notion of Kolmogorov complexity.
092   Similarly, we observe that the latent representation’s length is also a great evaluator for complexity.
093
      We accordingly search for the shortest latent representation, using recent literature (Gal et al., 2022).
      By applying textual inversion techniques, we evaluate the extent to which a concept is familiar to the
094
      model, and thus, potentially unoriginal.
095
096   Finally, we validate our framework with empirical experiments utilizing both a widely used pre-
097   trained stable diffusion model and a custom-trained model designed specifically for this study. The
098   latter processes synthetic data composed of various shapes, colors, sizes, and infills. The experiments
099
      employ both textual inversion and DreamSim (Fu et al., 2023) to analyze the correlation between the
      ease of concept recreation — measured by the number of tokens needed — and the originality of the
100
      images relative to the training dataset . Our experiments reaffirm that embracing rather than avoiding
101
      memorization might enable generative models to produce more innovative and diverse content.
102
103   Overall we contribute to the study of originality and copyright in generative models by suggesting a
104   new technique to identify genericity without imposing transparency, as well as offering an analysis in
105
      lab conditions that further our understanding of these models and how they behave.

      Before presenting our general framework, we first conduct preliminary experiments to establish
132   T2I models ability to generalize and generate original content. Such experiments are prerequisite
133   to any attempt to quantify such originality. The generalization abilities of generative models have
134   been explored prior to the rise in T2I models popularity (Zhao et al., 2018), and in a simpler setting
135   on diffusion models as well (Okawa et al., 2024). However, the impact of textual conditioning
136   through prompts and the influence of training data diversity remain unexplored. In this section, we
137   demonstrate, using the synthetic setup, that while diffusion T2I models obviously can memorize
138   details from the training data and generate copied versions of it in the output, they can also generalize
139   to new concepts and content through the composition of seen properties, surprisingly well. We
140
      then explore the ability of these models to generalize, considering the influence of the training data
      distribution. Specifically, we examine how the diversity of the data and the guidance provided by
141
      prompts contribute to the models’ generalization capabilities.
142
143   We introduce a generalization assessment setup, as depicted in fig. 2, and present experimental
144   findings in fig. 3 and expand on those in the supplementary. Our quantitative analysis reveals that
145   generalization improves with increased training data diversity and textual conditioning. Additionally,
146
      we observe an enhancement in the quality of generated images with greater training data diversity.
147

149   specific elements during training and assessing their generation post-training. Each element in the

151   from minimal, with two shape types (square and circle) and two colors (red and blue), to maximal,
152
      with five shape types (square, circle, triangle, hexagon, and star), three colors (red, green, and blue),
      two sizes (big and small), and two textures (full and empty), creating 60 unique elements. The degree
153
      of generalization is quantified by the frequency of the missing element’s occurrence in the generated
154
      set.

      elements. An illustration of the experiment is provided in fig. 2, and additional details on the synthetic

201   Before presenting our method, we provide a background overview for some key-ingredient methods
202
      that we use, namely Stable diffusion Rombach et al. (2022) as our T2I models architecture, and a
      textual inversion Gal et al. (2022) as the process for measuring complexity.
203
204
      Stable Diffusion As this model has gained high familiarity in the community, we provide a
205
      short description of its components. The Stable Diffusion model generates images conditioned
206
      on textual input. The image generation process involves encoding the input image into a latent
207   representation using a Variational Autoencoder (VAE), and then refining this representation using a
208   U-Net, conditioned by a text embedding from the Text Encoder. Finally, the refined representation is
209   decoded back into an image using the VAE decoder, resulting in an output image that is both realistic
210   and semantically aligned with the input text.
211
212   Textual Inversion Textual inversion is a method employed in T2I latent diffusion models, such
213   as Stable Diffusion, to adapt the model for generating images that are specific to a particular visual
214   concept or object, which may not have been present in the original training data. This is achieved
215   by fine-tuning a pre-trained T2I model on a selected set of images x1 , x2 , . . . , xn that represents
      the target concept. The fine-tuning process results in the creation of a distinctive token S ∗ , which

239   3.1      M ETHOD
240
241   The overview of our approach can be seen in fig. 4, essentially performing image reconstruction and
242   quality assessment. Our approach builds on the textual inversion technique (Gal et al., 2022) for
243   reconstruction, originally designed for personalization and editing tasks by representing concepts
244   with a textual embedding. Unlike the original purpose of the method, our research aims to enhance
245
      the interpretability of the manifold of text-to-image (T2I) models, focusing on the originality of
      images rather than objects. For this purpose, we extend the method to employ multiple tokens instead
246
      of just one, building on the fact that a single-token representation may not sufficiently capture a
247
      complex, original, image. Then, we assess the generation quality in terms of reconstruction and
248   editability. Overall, we find and demonstrate that the number of tokens required for reconstruction is
249   correlated with the originality of an image.
250
251   Single Token vs. Multi-Token: Let T be a set of tokens representing a concept, where T =
252   t1 , t2 , . . . , tm is a multi-token representation with m tokens. In the original textual inversion method,
253   a single token (m = 1) is used to represent a concept. In contrast, our extension allows for multiple
254   tokens (m > 1) to represent the concept in a more detailed manner. The representation of the
                                                                                                                   ∗
255   concept in the latent space can be expressed as a sequence of the embeddings of the tokens: Sm                 =

257   of the tokens representing the query image. This multi-token approach enables a more granular
258   exploration of the T2I model’s manifold, facilitating a deeper understanding of the relationships
259
      between text and image representations, especially in the context of interpreting the originality of

      In-Distribution Assessment We employ our method in two experimental setups, one synthetic
      playground for controlled occurrence distributions and their induced behavior and the other for the
277
      real-world scenario. For Real-world Settings, we use editability as the criterion to maintain in-domain
278                                                                                     ∗
      generation Gal et al. (2022; 2023). Specifically, we use prompts like ”cat in Sm    ” to generate images
279   that are both representative of the concept in the query image and editable within the domain of
280   the model. For the synthetic settings, due to the model’s simplicity of the data it was trained on,
281   editability is not necessarily the right measure of in-domain generation. However, The underlying
282   advantage of the synthetic data is that the distribution of the data is fully known. This allows us
283   to measure in-domain generation by a more informed measure which we can validate. Instead of
284   editability, we check that for every seed, the shapes are in different positions, as the data distribution
285   positioned the shapes randomly (by design). This validation ensures that the model has not reached a
286   point of overfitting. We provide an ablation study to justify this decision in the supplementary.
287   Finally, we assess the originality of the query images by combining the reconstruction and in-
288   distribution validation. The combination of multi-token textual inversion and these evaluation
289   criteria enables a more detailed and original content generation, contributing to the assessment of the
290   originality of imagery and Interpretability of T2I diffusion models.

      Privacy and Copyright Infringements The intersection of privacy and copyright infringements in
      generative models has garnered significant attention. This approach assumes that to avoid copyright
478
      infringement, the output of a model shouldn’t be too sensitive to any of its individual training samples.
479
      Bousquet et al. (2020) suggest the use of differential privacy Dwork et al. (2006) to stabilize the
480   algorithm and avoid such sensitivity. Vyas et al. (2023) introduces a slightly less stringent notion
481   (Near-Access Freenes) but relies on a similar benchmark, a safe model that doesn’t have access
482   to the copyrighted data. Nasr et al. (2023), Carlini et al. (2023) and Haim et al. (2022) further
483   explored this area by investigating the extraction of training data from models, highlighting the
484   risks of memorization. Elkin-Koren et al. (2023), however, investigated the gap between privacy
485   and copyright infringement from the perspective of the law, and showed that requiring such notions
      of stability may be too strong, and are not always aligned with the original intention of the law.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Other work, including Lee et al. (2023) and Hacohen et al. (2024), try to highlight the challenges
487   in copyright assessment in generative AI and how to use methods such as in-painting to discover
488   biases of the models. Closer to our approach, Scheffler et al. (2022) suggests a framework to quantify
489   originality by measuring the description length of a content with and without access to the allegedly
490   copyrighted material. Our approach of textual inversion also looks for a succinct description of
491   the content but, distinctively, our definition depends on the distribution of the data, and measures
492   originality with respect to the whole data to be trained. This may lead to different outcomes, for
493   example, when the allegedly copyrighted material contains a distinctive trait that is not necessarily
494   original.
495
496   Attribution in Generative Models Attribution in generative models is a crucial area of research,
497
      focusing on identifying the sources of data that contribute to the generation of specific outputs. Park
      et al. introduced the TRAK method to address data attribution in large-scale models (Park, 2022),
498
      and recently, Wang et al. (2023) proposed a method for evaluating the attribution in Stable Diffusion
499
      models of data points in the generation process, which is closely related to assessing the originality
500   of generated images. However, such a method requires full access and knowledge of the training set
501   on which the model was trained.
502
503   Generalization and Memorization The interrelation between generalization and memorization is
504   a key challenge for Machine Learning. Classically, memorization and generalization are considered to
505   be in tension. Ideal learning would seem to extract relevant information but avoid memorizing irrele-
506   vant concepts. While limiting memorization does lead to generalization (Russo & Zou, 2019; Bassily
507   et al., 2018; Xu & Raginsky, 2017; Arora et al., 2018), recent studies suggest that memorization may
508   be critical, and unavoidable in certain tasks (Feldman, 2020; Feldman & Zhang, 2020; Livni, 2024).
509
      Most recently, (Attias et al., 2024) demonstrated how even in simple tasks such as mean estimation,
      memorization of the data is a prerequisite. On a practical level, (Zhang et al., 2016) explored the
510
      relationship between these two aspects, emphasizing their importance in the effectiveness of deep
511
      learning models.
512
513
514   7    D ISCUSSION
515
516   In this work, we introduced a novel approach to assess the originality of images with Text-to-Image
517   (T2I) Generative Diffusion models, and have investigated its behavior in this aspect under a controlled
518
      environment. Our methodology leverages the concept of familiarity within the model’s training
      data to quantify the originality of tested images. By employing textual inversion techniques, we
519
      demonstrated that the number of tokens required to represent and reconstruct an image is a measure
520
      of its originality, without requiring access to the training data, nor a specific prompt that potentially
521   poses copyright complications. Our analysis confirmed that T2I models can produce new original
522   content, highlighting the importance of training models on diverse and comprehensive datasets. These
523   findings also challenge the traditional view of avoiding memorization in models. Instead, we propose
524   that models familiarize themselves with a broad spectrum of data, respecting copyright constraints, to
525   enhance their ability to generate new content. In the supplementary we provide additional discussion
526   on the impact and ethical aspects of the work. In summary, our study introduces a novel approach to
527   evaluating originality in generative models, offering insights that inform copyright analysis and legal
528   protection. By quantifying concept familiarity, we address issues of copyright eligibility, infringement,
529
      and licensing while also opening new research avenues in originality assessment.
530
531
      Limitations One of the primary constraints for the method is the reliance on textual inversion,
      which may not capture all aspects of originality in complex images. Additionally, our method’s
532
      effectiveness is contingent on the quality and diversity of the training data, which might not always be
533
      optimal. Furthermore, the correlation between token count and originality, although significant, may
534   not be universally applicable across different model architectures or datasets. Future research should
535   explore alternative measures of originality and test the robustness of our approach across a broader
536   range of models and data, making it readily available for deployment. Finally, our work demonstrates
537   that T2I models can be utilized to discriminate original and non-original work. That being said, an
538   important motivation of our work is to assess originality of T2I content. Designing a framework
539   that exploits generative model’s ability to discriminate original content in order to audit genAI and
      safeguard content leads to several open problems and challenges which we leave to future work.
