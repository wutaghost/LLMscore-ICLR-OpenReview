009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Rank-adaptive low-rank adaptation (LoRA), a parameter-efficient fine-tuning
012            (PEFT) technology, has achieved state-of-the-art performance in fine-tuning foun-
013            dation models (FM). Directly transplanting the rank-adaptive LoRA methods from
014            centralized learning to federated learning raises two critical issues: client drift and
015            rank drift. This paper presents a Riemannian LoRA algorithm with adaptive rank
016
               for federated fine-tuning of foundation models (FFT-FM), RAFFT, which solves
               the client-drift and rank-drift issues, and significantly improves the computational
017
               cost. First, by utilizing Riemannian Procrustes analysis, we propose a Rieman-
018
               nian parameter matching method to avoid the client-drift issue for ensuring the
019            effectiveness of FFT-FM with rank-adaptive LoRA, and to reduce the cost of ma-
020            trix decomposition by transforming the singular value decomposition (SVD) of
021            high-dimensional full parameter matrices into the SVD of low-dimensional r × r
022            matrices, where r is the rank parameter in the LoRA. We theoretically derive the
023            equivalence between our RAFFT algorithm with rank-adaptive LoRA for the FFT-
024            FM and the standard FFT-FM on the full parameter matrices based on FedAvg
025            and verify the bounded error introduced by approximation. Second, by leveraging
026            Riemannian manifold theory, we develop a Riemannian gradient descent (RGD)
027
               method to guarantee the local full parameter matrices on clients in the form of
               low-rank ones with fixed rank optimized by the server in each FFT-FM round,
028
               for alleviating the rank-drift issue to speed up the convergence of RAFFT. We
029
               theoretically demonstrate that the RGD optimization on the Riemannian manifold
030            ensures the rank invariance during the local update process and the RGD opti-
031            mization can converge in the FFT-FM context.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) for fine-tuning of foundation models (FT-FM) has attracted
037   active research in recent years, such as adapter tuning (Houlsby et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2020; Pfeiffer
038   et al., 2021; Rücklé et al., 2021; He et al., 2022), prefix tuning (Li & Liang, 2021), P-Tuning (Liu
039
      et al., 2021b), P-Tuning V2 (Liu et al., 2021a), low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022),
      and prompt tuning (Lester et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021). These methods freeze the backbone
040
      parameters (i.e., original weights of pre-trained FMs) and adjust only a small portion of parameters
041
      (i.e., adapter weights during the FT phase), to improve the efficiency. Among the above PEFT
042   approaches, LoRA has achieved the state-of-the-art FT performance (Wu et al., 2024). The idea of
043   the LoRA method can be illustrated as follows: W = W0 +∆W = W0 +BA, where W ∈ Rm×n
044   is the complete parameter of a FM. W0 and ∆W are the backbone (pre-trained FM parameters)
045   and adapter parameters respectively. A low-rank decomposition ∆W = BA where B ∈ Rm×r ,
046   A ∈ Rr×n , and rank r << min(m, n). During the FT phase, only B and A are trained while
047   freezing W0 on the clients. Notice that in the LoRA-style methods, rank r is often user-defined and
048   keeps unchanged during the entire FT process.
049   In order to further enhance the performance of the LoRA-style methods, various rank-adaptive
050   LoRA methods have been recently proposed to dynamically allocate the ranks among parameter ma-
051   trices based on their importance: assigning more/less trainable parameters with higher/lower ranks to
052   the critical/insignificant parameter matrices for better model performance/efficiency. These methods
053   can be broadly classified into two categories: (1) SVD-based approaches in centralized fine-tuning
      (CFT) dynamically adjust the ranks by truncating singular values in the form of SVD (Zhang et al.,


                                                         1

      2023b; Ding et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a); (2) Rank sampling-based algorithms in both CFT
055   and FFT search for the best rank by sampling a range of ranks and sorting the representations learnt
056   by the model at different ranks during training (Valipour et al., 2023; Rajabzadeh et al., 2024; Xu
057   et al., 2024). They need to fine-tune the model multiple times at different ranks, raising non-trivial
058   cost. Several pioneering rank-adaptive LoRA algorithms for FFT-FM iteratively aggregate local full
059   parameter metrics into a global one and perform the SVD on the latter to find the optimal rank (Chai
060   et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2021; Niu et al., 2023). However, iterative SVD on the high-dimensional full
061   parameter matrices is prohibitive.

125   To our best knowledge, this work is the first to offer a rank-adaptive low-rank adaptation solution
126   for FFT-FM with Riemannian theory. Our RAFFT method exhibits three compelling advantages:
127   (1) The proposed Riemannian parameter matching technique based on Riemannian Procrustes anal-
128   ysis is able to solve the client-drift issue when transplanting the centralized LoRA methods to the
129
      federated setting. Existing rank-adaptive LoRA methods in the CFT can be plugged into our frame-

130
      parameter matrices, without the need of aggregating the global full parameter matrices; (2) Trans-
131

132   r × r matrices can significantly improve the efficiency of FFT-FM with low-rank adaption. In ad-
133   dition, this design avoids the expensive cost of training FFT multiple times at different ranks by
134   rank sampling-based algorithms; and (3) The combination of Riemannian gradient descent and Rie-
135   mannian manifold theory guarantees the local parameter matrices with fixed rank optimized by the
136   server in each FFT-FM round, for alleviating the rank-drift issue to speed up the convergence of
137   RAFFT.
138   Empirical evaluation on real datasets demonstrates the superior performance of our RAFFT model

140   adaptive LoRA methods. More experiments, implementation details, and hyperparameter setting
141   are presented in Appendices 7.5-7.6.

      As discussed in Section 1, directly using FedAvg McMahan et al. (2017) to aggregate the clients’
181
      local matrices Uk , Σk , Vk on the server side leads to client-drift issues, as shown in Eq.(1), which
182
      negatively impact the convergence and performance of federated learning. Among three categories
183   of rank-adaptive low-rank approximation techniques, SVD-based methods in centralized learn-
184   ing Zhang et al. (2023b); Ding et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023a) encounter the dilemma of the client
185   drift issue (Sun et al., 2024), rank sampling-based algorithms Valipour et al. (2023); Rajabzadeh
186   et al. (2024); Xu et al. (2024) and federated rank-adaptive LoRA algorithms Chai et al. (2022); Wu
187   et al. (2021); Niu et al. (2023) raise extremely expensive cost. To address these challenges, we pro-
188   pose a Riemannian parameter matching method to match the local parameter matrices Uk and Vk
189   from other clients with the pivot matrices U1 and V1 on client 1, i.e., Ũk ≈ U1 and Ṽk ≈ V1 ,
190   preserving both their lengths and directions. Correspondingly, we adjust the diagonal matrix Σk to
191   ensure consistency in the low-rank parameter measurements before and after Riemannian parameter
192   matching, i.e., Uk Σk Vk = Ũk Σ̃k Ṽk ≈ U1 Σ̃k V1 . This approach allows the server to directly
193   aggregate the aligned local low-rank matrices Uk , Σ̃k , and Vk while avoiding client-drift issues.
194   Moreover, the global diagonal matrix enables the server to easily identify the optimal rank for the
195
      global parameter matrix, enhancing both efficiency and performance.
196   Since both Uk and Vk are orthogonal matrices, they belong to the Riemann manifold, which
197   is defined as the set of standard orthogonal matrices S = {Uk ∈ Rm×r : (UK )T (UK ) =
198   I} (Chakraborty & Vemuri, 2019; Atiyah & Todd, 1960). Therefore, we can performing the Rieman-
199   nian parameter matching to ensure that the length and direction of the parameter matrices between
200   clients are as aligned as possible. We adopt the Procrustes distance metric proposed in Kendall et al.
201
      (2003) to efficiently compute the parameter matching on the manifold. The procrustes representa-
      tion Sp on the Riemann manifold S can be defined as follows: an m × r matrix Uk on Sr,m is

      The data heterogeneity issue in the FFT-FM may bring the rank-drift challenges. When the server
293
      dispatches the low-rank parameter matrix with the appropriate rank r to the clients, the rank of
294
      its local full parameter matrix ∆Wk = Uk Σk Vk on each client may significantly change after
295   local training epoch, and so does the appropriate rank of the global parameter matrix. This may
296   slow down the convergence of FFT-FM and degrade the model performance too. We develop a
297   Riemannian gradient descent (RGD) method to guarantee the local full parameter matrices ∆Wk in
298   the form of low-rank ones Uk , Σk , and Vk with fixed appropriate rank determined by the server in
299   each iteration of FFT-FM, to speed up the convergence of RAFFT.

302   full parameter metrics. We introduce a regularizer into the loss function, defined as R(Uk , Vk ) to
303   enforce the orthogonality of Uk and Vk .
304                            R(Uk , Vk ) = ∥(Uk )T Uk − I∥2F + ∥Vk (Vk )T − I∥2F                                           (17)

533   6                 C ONCLUSIONS
534
535   In this work, we propose rank-adaptive LoRA-based federated fine-tuning algorithm for foundation
536   models. First, we design a novel Riemannian parameter matching method with the SVD on a low-
537   rank matrix to avoid the client-drift issue to enhance the effectiveness of FFT-FM, while further
538   improving its efficiency. Second, we develop a Riemannian gradient descent method on Riemannian
539   manifold to guarantee the local parameter matrices with fixed optimal rank in each iteration, to speed
      up the convergence.

       Parameter Efficient Fine Tuning (PEFT). As the scale of large language models (LLMs) contin-
1001
       ues to grow, the cost associated with the standard full fine-tuning paradigm has become extremely
1002
       high. To address this issue, parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods Houlsby et al. (2019)
1003   have been proposed. These methods introduce a minimal set of additional trainable parameters to
1004   enhance model performance while keeping the majority of the pre-trained parameters frozen. Gen-
1005   erally, PEFT approaches can be classified into two main categories based on how they manipulate
1006   model parameters. The first category, additive PEFT, involves the incorporation of new trainable
1007   modules or parameters to adjust the model architecture, minimizing the parameter count for down-
1008   stream tasks. This includes Adapter Tuning (Houlsby et al., 2019), where small, trainable adapter
1009   modules are inserted within the model architecture to allow for task-specific adaptation without
1010   altering the original model weights, and methods such as prefix-tuning (Devlin et al., 2019), P-
1011
       tuning (Liu et al., 2022), P-tuning v2 (Liu et al., 2021a), and prompt tuning (Lester et al., 2021),
       which append learnable vectors at the beginning of input sequences. The second category involves
1012
       reparameterized PEFT techniques that build low-rank representations of original model parameters,
1013
       incorporating additional trainable parameters during training. This category includes Low-Rank
1014   Decomposition, techniques like LoRA Hu et al. (2022) and KronA (Edalati et al., 2022), which
1015   decompose original model parameters into low-rank and residual matrices, and LoRA derivatives
1016   such as AdaLoRA Zhang et al. (2023b) and SoRA (Liu et al., 2024b), which dynamically select the
1017   rank of LoRA to construct adaptable low-rank representations. Collectively, these methods represent
1018   the cutting-edge in efficient model training techniques, enabling the deployment of large language
1019   models in resource-constrained environments without sacrificing performance.
1020   Additive PEFT Methods in Federated Learning. These methods are specifically designed to
1021   minimize computational demands and associated communication overheads. The additive meth-
1022   ods integrate lightweight trainable blocks into the frozen foundational models (FMs) and fine-tune
1023   additional parameters for localized model adaptation. This approach not only enhances computa-
1024   tional and communication efficiency but also allows for the customization of heterogeneous models
1025   based on specific local data characteristics or user preferences. Recent work has introduced Fed-

2554   Rank-drift issue: The data heterogeneity is a common challenging problem in the federated learn-
2555   ing. The collected data on different clients are Non-Independent Identically Distributed (Non-IID).
2556
       Thus, the parameters of local models trained on the non-IID data are quite different from each other
       as well as the parameters of global model. Each local model may oscillate back and forth in dif-
2557
       ferent training rounds, leading to unstable and slow convergence and causing suboptimal model
2558
       performance.
2559
2560   At the same time, due to the heterogeneity of local models, the optimal ranks of local models are
2561   quite different from each other as well as the one of global model, raising the rank-drift issue. Sim-
2562
       ilarly, the oscillation of the optimal rank of each local model can slow down the model convergence
       and degrade the model performance. For example, given two clients 1 and 2, in round 1, the local
2563
       model on client 1/2 has the optimal rank 5/20. The server aggregates local models from clients to
2564
       generate a global model based on FedAvg. The global model by model aggregation has the optimal
2565   rank 12. In round 2, after the global model are sent back to the clients, the clients begin the training
2566   with the global model with the optimal rank 12. Thus, the optimal rank of local models oscillate
2567   between 5/20 and 12. The rank oscillation may repeat in each training round, slowing down the
2568   model convergence.
2569
       The following table 36 presents a case study regarding the rank-drift in federated learning. In round
2570
       1, the optimal rank of the global model is 16. In round 2, the optimal rank is changed to 11. In
2571   subsequent rounds, the optimal rank continue to oscillate back and forth. The ranks of local models
2572   fluctuate around values such as 9.75, 10.17, and 10.53, while the rank of global modelgradually de-
2573   creases and stabilizes at 8. These results confirm that rank-drift introduces instability into the train-
2574   ing process, slowing convergence and degrading performance, which emphasizes the importance
2575   of our Riemannian gradient descent optimization approach to stabilize ranks and ensure efficient
2576   training.
