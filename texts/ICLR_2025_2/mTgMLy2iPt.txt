                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009            Policy gradient methods are notorious for having a large variance and high sample
010            complexity. To mitigate this, we introduce SoftTreeMax—a generalization of
011            softmax that employs planning. In SoftTreeMax, we extend the traditional logits
012            with the multi-step discounted cumulative reward, topped with the logits of future
013            states. We analyze SoftTreeMax and explain how tree expansion helps to reduce
014            its gradient variance. We prove that the variance depends on the chosen tree-
015            expansion policy. Specifically, we show that the closer the induced transitions are
016            to being state-independent, the stronger the variance decay. With approximate
017
               forward models, we prove that the resulting gradient bias diminishes with the
               approximation error while retaining the same variance reduction. Ours is the
018
               first result to bound the gradient bias for an approximate model. In a practical
019
               implementation of SoftTreeMax, we utilize a parallel GPU-based simulator for
020            fast and efficient tree expansion. Using this implementation in Atari, we show
021            that SoftTreeMax reduces the gradient variance by three orders of magnitude.
022            This leads to better sample complexity and improved performance compared to
023            distributed PPO.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      Policy Gradient (PG) methods (Sutton et al., 1999) for Reinforcement Learning (RL) are often the
028
      first choice for environments that allow numerous interactions at a fast pace (Schulman et al., 2017).
029
      Their success is attributed to several factors: they are easy to distribute to multiple workers, require
030   no assumptions on the underlying value function, and have both on-policy and off-policy variants.
031
032   Despite these positive features, PG algorithms are also notoriously unstable due to the high variance
033
      of the gradients computed over entire trajectories (Liu et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2020). As a result, PG
      algorithms tend to be highly inefficient in terms of sample complexity. Several solutions have been
034
      proposed to mitigate the high variance issue, including baseline subtraction (Greensmith et al., 2004;
035
      Thomas & Brunskill, 2017; Wu et al., 2018), anchor-point averaging (Papini et al., 2018), and other
036   variance reduction techniques (Zhang et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2019; Pham et al., 2020).
037
038   A second family of algorithms that achieved state-of-the-art results in several domains is based on
039
      planning. Planning is exercised primarily in the context of value-based RL and is usually implemented
      using a Tree Search (TS) (Silver et al., 2016; Schrittwieser et al., 2020). In this work, we combine
040
      PG with TS by introducing a parameterized differentiable policy that incorporates tree expansion.
041
      Namely, our SoftTreeMax policy replaces the standard policy logits of a state and action, with the
042   expected value of trajectories that originate from these state and action. We consider two variants of
043   SoftTreeMax, one for cumulative reward and one for exponentiated reward.
044
045
      Combining TS and PG should be done with care given the biggest downside of PG—its high gradient
      variance. This raises questions that were ignored until this work: (i) How to design a PG method based
046
      on tree-expansion that is stable and performs well in practice? and (ii) How does the tree-expansion
047
      policy affect the PG variance? Here, we analyze SoftTreeMax, and provide a practical methodology
048   to choose the expansion policy to minimize the resulting variance. Our main result shows that a
049   desirable expansion policy is one, under which the induced transition probabilities are similar for
050   each starting state. More generally, we show that the gradient variance of SoftTreeMax decays at
051   a rate of |λ2 |d , where d is the depth of the tree and λ2 is the second eigenvalue of the transition
052   matrix induced by the tree expansion policy. This work is the first to prove such a relation between
053   PG variance and tree expansion policy. In addition, we prove that the with an approximate forward
      model, the bias of the gradient is bounded proportionally to the approximation error of the model.

054
      To verify our results, we implemented a practical version of SoftTreeMax that exhaustively searches
055   the entire tree and applies a neural network on its leaves. We test our algorithm on a parallelized
056   Atari GPU simulator (Dalton et al., 2020). To enable a tractable deep search, up to depth eight, we
057   also introduce a pruning technique that limits the width of the tree. We do so by sampling only the
058   most promising nodes at each level. We integrate our SoftTreeMax GPU implementation into the
059   popular PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) and compare it to the flat distributed variant of PPO. This allows
060   us to demonstrate the potential benefit of utilizing learned models while isolating the fundamental
061   properties of TS without added noise. In all tested Atari games, our results outperform the baseline
062   and obtain up to 5x more reward. We further show in Section 6 that the associated gradient variance
063
      is smaller by three orders of magnitude in all games, demonstrating the relation between low gradient
      variance and high reward.
064
065   We summarize our key contributions. (i) We show how to combine two families of SoTA approaches:
066   PG and TS by introducing SoftTreeMax: a novel parametric policy that generalizes softmax to
067   planning. Specifically, we propose two variants based on cumulative and exponentiated rewards. (ii)
068   We prove that the gradient variance of SoftTreeMax in its two variants decays with its tree
069
      depth. Our analysis sheds new light on the choice of tree expansion policy. It raises the question
      of optimality in terms of variance versus the traditional regret; e.g., in UCT (Kocsis & Szepesvári,
070
      2006). (iii) We prove that with an approximate forward model, the gradient bias is proportional to
071
      the approximation error, while retaining the variance decay. This quantifies the accuracy required
072   from a learned forward model. (iv) We implement a differentiable deep version of SoftTreeMax
073   that employs a parallelized GPU tree expansion. We demonstrate how its gradient variance is reduced
074   by three orders of magnitude over PPO while obtaining up to 5x reward.

      We introduce a new family of policies called SoftTreeMax, which are a model-based generalization
115
      of the popular softmax. We propose two variants: Cumulative (C-SoftTreeMax) and Exponenti-

      In this section, we first bound the variance of PG when using the SoftTreeMax policy. Later, we
      discuss how the gradient bias resulting due to approximate forward models diminishes as a function
170
      of the approximation error, while retaining the same variance decay.
171
172   We show that the variance decreases with the tree depth, and the rate is determined by the second
173   eigenvalue of the transition kernel induced by πb . Specifically, we bound the same expression for
174   variance as appears in (Greensmith et al., 2004, Sec. 3.5) and (Wu et al., 2018, Sec. A, Eq. (21)).
175
      Other types of analysis could instead have focused on the estimation aspect in the context of sampling
      (Zhang et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2019; Pham et al., 2020). Indeed, in our implementation in Section 5,
176
      we manage to avoid sampling and directly compute the expectations in Eqs. (3) and (4). As we
177
      show later, we do so by leveraging efficient parallel simulation on the GPU in feasible run-time. In
178   our application, due to the nature of the finite action space and quasi-deterministic Atari dynamics
179   (Bellemare et al., 2013), our expectation estimator is noiseless. We encourage future work to account
180   for the finite-sample variance component. We defer all the proofs to Appendix A.
181

           5      S OFT T REE M AX : D EEP PARALLEL I MPLEMENTATION
362
363
           Following impressive successes of deep RL (Mnih et al., 2015; Silver et al., 2016), using deep NNs
364
           in RL is standard practice. Depending on the RL algorithm, a loss function is defined and gradients
365
           on the network weights can be calculated. In PG methods, the scoring function used in the softmax is
366        commonly replaced by a neural network Wθ : πθ (a|s) ∝ exp (Wθ (s, a)) . Similarly, we implement
367        SoftTreeMax by replacing θ(s) in (2) with a neural network Wθ (s). Although both variants of
368        SoftTreeMax from Section 3 involve computing an expectation, this can be hard in general. One
369        approach to handle it is with sampling, though these introduce estimation variance into the process.
370        We leave the question of sample-based theory and algorithmic implementations for future work.
371
           Instead, in finite action space environments such as Atari, we compute the exact expectation in
372        SoftTreeMax with an exhaustive TS of depth d. Despite the exponential computational cost of
373        spanning the entire tree, recent advancements in parallel GPU-based simulation allow efficient
374        expansion of all nodes at the same depth simultaneously (Dalal et al., 2021; Rosenberg et al., 2022).
375        This is possible when a simulator is implemented on GPU (Dalton et al., 2020; Makoviychuk et al.,
376        2021; Freeman et al., 2021), or when a forward model is learned (Kim et al., 2020; Ha & Schmidhuber,
377        2018). To reduce the complexity to be linear in depth, we apply tree pruning to a limited width in all
           levels. We do so by sub-sampling only the most promising branches at each level. Limiting the width


                                                                  7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      drastically improves runtime, and enables respecting GPU memory limits, with only a small sacrifice
379   in performance.
380
381   To summarize, in the practical SoftTreeMax algorithm we perform an exhaustive tree expansion with
382
      pruning to obtain trajectories up to depth d. We expand the tree with equal weight to all actions, which
      corresponds to a uniform tree expansion policy πb . We apply a neural network on the leaf states, and
383
      accumulate the result with the rewards along each trajectory to obtain the logits in (2). Finally, we
384
      aggregate the results using C-SoftTreeMax. We leave experiments E-SoftTreeMax for future work
385   on risk-averse RL. During training, the gradient propagates to the NN weights of Wθ . When the
386   gradient ∇θ log πd,θ is calculated at each time step, it updates Wθ for all leaf states, similarly to
387   Siamese networks (Bertinetto et al., 2016). An illustration of the policy is given in Figure 2.

401   The dashed lines are the results for PPO. Each value is taken after convergence, i.e., the average

405   the reward and variance are negatively correlated and mirror each other in almost all games. This
406
      phenomenon demonstrates the necessity of reducing the variance of PG for improving performance.

407
      run-time in all experiments to one week The deeper the tree, the slower each step and the run consists

      of less steps. This explains the non-monotone behavior as a function of depth. For a more thorough

      Softmax Operator. The softmax policy became a canonical part of PG to the point where theoretical
435   results of PG focus specifically on it (Zhang et al., 2021; Mei et al., 2020b; Li et al., 2021; Ding et al.,
436   2022). Even though we focus on a tree extension to the softmax policy, our methodology is general
437   and can be easily applied to other discrete or continuous parameterized policies as in (Mei et al.,
438   2020a; Miahi et al., 2021; Silva et al., 2019). Tree Search. One famous TS algorithm is Monte-Carlo
439   TS (MCTS; (Browne et al., 2012)) used in AlphaGo (Silver et al., 2016) and MuZero (Schrittwieser
440   et al., 2020). Other algorithms such as Value Iteration, Policy Iteration and DQN were also shown to
441   give an improved performance with a tree search extensions (Efroni et al., 2019; Dalal et al., 2021).
442   Parallel Environments. In this work we used accurate parallel models that are becoming more
443
      common with the increasing popularity of GPU-based simulation (Makoviychuk et al., 2021; Dalton
      et al., 2020; Freeman et al., 2021). Alternatively, in relation to Theorem 4.8, one can rely on recent
444
      works that learn the underlying model (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018; Schrittwieser et al., 2020) and
445
      use an approximation of the true dynamics. Risk Aversion. Previous work considered exponential
446   utility functions for risk aversion (Chen et al., 2007; Garcıa & Fernández, 2015; Fei et al., 2021).
447   This utility function is the same as E-SoftTreeMax formulation from (4), but we have it directly
448   in the policy instead of the objective. Reward-free RL. We showed that the gradient variance is
449   minimized when the transitions induced by the behavior policy πb are uniform. This is expressed by
450   the second eigenvalue of the transition matrix P πb . This notion of uniform exploration is common to
451   the reward-free RL setup (Jin et al., 2020). Several such works also considered the second eigenvalue
452   in their analysis (Liu & Brunskill, 2018; Tarbouriech & Lazaric, 2019).
453
454   8    D ISCUSSION
455
456   In this work, we introduced for the first time a differentiable parametric policy that combines TS with
457   PG. We proved that SoftTreeMax is essentially a variance reduction technique and explained how to
458   choose the expansion policy to minimize the gradient variance. It is an open question whether optimal
459   variance reduction corresponds to the appealing regret properties the were put forward by UCT
460   (Kocsis & Szepesvári, 2006). We believe that this can be answered by analyzing the convergence rate
461   of SoftTreeMax, relying on the bias and variance results we obtained here.
462   As the learning process continues, the norm of the gradient and the variance both become smaller.
463   On the face of it, one can ask if the gradient becomes small as fast as the variance or even faster can
464   there be any meaningful learning? As we showed in the experiments, learning happens because the
465   variance reduces fast enough (a variance of 0 represents deterministic learning, which is fastest).
466
      Finally, our work can be extended to infinite action spaces. The analysis can be extended to infinite-
467   dimension kernels that retain the same key properties used in our proofs. In the implementation, the
468   tree of continuous actions can be expanded by maintaining a parametric distribution over actions that
469   depend on θ. This approach can be seen as a tree adaptation of MPPI (Williams et al., 2017).

       We note that not for all games we see monotonicity. Our explanation for this phenomenon relates to
1320   how immediate reward contributes to performance compared to the value. Different games benefit
1321   differently from long-term as opposed to short-term planning. Games that require longer-term
1322   planning need a better value estimate. A good value estimate takes longer to obtain with larger depths,
1323   in which we apply the network to states that are very different from the ones observed so far in the
1324   buffer (recall that as in any deep RL algorithm, we train the model only on states in the buffer). If
1325   the model hasn’t learned a good enough value function yet, and there is no guiding dense reward
1326   along the trajectory, the policy becomes noisier, and can take more steps to converge – even more
1327   than those we run in our week-long experiment.
1328   For a concrete example, let us compare Breakout to Gopher. Inspecting Fig. 5, we observe that
1329   Breakout quickly (and monotonically) gains from large depths since it relies on the short term goal
1330   of simply keeping the paddle below the moving ball. In Gopher, however, for large depths (>=5),
1331   learning barely started even by the end of the training run. Presumably, this is because the task in
1332   Gopher involves multiple considerations and steps: the agent needs to move to the right spot and
1333   then hit the mallet the right amount of times, while balancing different locations. This task requires
1334
       long-term planning and thus depends more strongly on the accuracy of the value function estimate.
       In that case, for depth 5 or more, we would require more train steps for the value to “kick in” and
1335
       become beneficial beyond the gain from the reward in the tree.
1336
1337   The figures above convey two key observations that occur for at least some non-zero depth: (1) The
1338   final performance with the tree is better than PPO (Fig. 3); and (2) the intermediate step-based results
1339   with the tree are better than PPO (Fig. 5). This leads to our main takeaway from this work — there

1347   When P πb is rank one, it is not only its variance that becomes 0, but also the norm of the gradient

1349   MDPs. This is a local minimum for SoftTreeMax and it would cause the PG iteration to get stuck,
       and to the optimum in the (desired but impractical) case where πb is the optimal policy. However,

1351   θ(s, a) → ∞ for one a per s. PG with softmax would suffer very slow convergence near these
