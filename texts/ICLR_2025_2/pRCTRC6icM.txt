009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            While contrastive pre-training is widely employed, its data efficiency problem
012            has remained relatively under-explored thus far. Existing methods often rely
013            on static coreset selection algorithms to pre-identify important data for training.
014            However, this static nature renders them unable to dynamically track the data
015            usefulness throughout pre-training, leading to subpar pre-trained models. To
016            address this challenge, our paper introduces a novel dynamic bootstrapping dataset
017            pruning method. It involves pruning data preparation followed by dataset mutation
018            operations, both of which undergo iterative and dynamic updates. We apply this
019
               method to two prevalent contrastive pre-training frameworks: CLIP and MoCo,
               representing vision-language and vision-centric domains, respectively. In particular,
020
               we individually pre-train seven CLIP models on two large-scale image-text pair
021
               datasets, and two MoCo models on the ImageNet dataset, resulting in a total of 16
022            pre-trained models. With a data pruning rate of 30-35% across all 16 models, our
023            method exhibits only marginal performance degradation (less than 1% on average)
024            compared to corresponding models trained on the full dataset counterparts across
025            various downstream datasets, and also surpasses several baselines with a large
026            performance margin. Additionally, the byproduct from our method, i.e., coresets
027            derived from the original datasets after pre-training, also demonstrates significant
028            superiority in terms of downstream performance over other static coreset selection
029            approaches. We include the code in the supplementary material to facilitate the
030
               reproduction of our experimental results.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Large models are heavily data-driven, particularly in the realm of pre-training (Chen et al., 2020c;
036   2021; Radford et al., 2021). This paradigm has been widely underpinned by the scaling law (Hoffmann
037   et al., 2022; Kaplan et al., 2020), which suggests that more data often lead to reduced generalization
038   errors. However, using large quantities of data frequently results in a notable increase in carbon
039
      footprints. Addressing this pressing issue requires substantial efforts to optimize the data efficiency.
040   This paper delves into the data efficiency problem for contrastive pre-training. Despite the per-
041   vasiveness of contrastive pre-training across both vision-centric (Chen et al., 2020c; 2021) and
042   vision-language (Jia et al., 2021; Radford et al., 2021) domains, nevertheless, the data efficiency
043   issue has received scant attention in the existing literature. We attribute the reason for this fact to
044   two challenges. I- Absence of reliable labels for self-supervised learning objectives. Unlike in super-
045
      vised learning, where explicit labels aid in class prediction, self-supervised learning in contrastive
      pre-training operates without such guidance, making it unable to estimate the class probability of data
046
      samples such as EL2N (Paul et al., 2021). II- Extensive data scale due to easy accessibility, e.g., the
047
      interleaved image-text data from the web (Radford et al., 2021). Current datasets usually comprise
048   millions (Changpinyo et al., 2021; Sharma et al., 2018) or even billions of samples (Schuhmann et al.,
049   2022). It is thus intractable in time for methods employing gradients (Paul et al., 2021) or second
050   derivative (Influence Functions) (Koh & Liang, 2017) to evaluate data usefulness individually. Recent
051   approaches in the vision-language area have resorted to coreset selection algorithms (Mirzasoleiman
052   et al., 2020) for a reduced pre-training dataset beforehand (Abbas et al., 2023; Maharana et al., 2024;
053   Mahmoud et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Webster et al., 2023). The crux of these methods lies
      in the semantic match/duplication that is quantified by some proxy metrics like CLIP matching


                                                         1
054
      score (Radford et al., 2021). Consequently, a subset, namely a coreset, of the original dataset is
055   filtered for pre-training from scratch.
056
057   Our motivation for this work is inspired by the advancement in dynamic sparse training (DST) (Nowak
058
      et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2024), which dynamically prunes less influential learnable
      weights from models. Compared to its static sparse training counterparts (Lee et al., 2019; Tanaka
059
      et al., 2020), DST demonstrates notable strengths in performance, robustness, and model compression
060
      without the need for over-parameterization (Liu et al., 2021a; Nowak et al., 2023). Intriguingly, we
061   recognize that recent coreset selection algorithms predominantly adhere to the static approach, akin
062   to the fixed weight masks employed in static sparse networks (Lee et al., 2019). As a result, we argue
063   that these coreset-based dataset pruning methods are subject to similar limitations as previous static
064   sparse training ones, albeit with a shift in application scope from learnable weights to individual data
065   samples. Given this context, approaching the dataset pruning challenge can be easily decomposed
066   into two sub-problems: 1)- metric identification and 2)- pruning strategy design. Specifically,
067   the proxy metric should meet several conditions: dynamic adaptability, quick obtainability (with
068   minimal additional cost), and reflecting the learning status of each sample. Regarding the pruning
069
      strategy, we introduce a novel data bootstrapping algorithm named SCAN. Instead of employing a
      consistent pruning ratio throughout training, our SCAN approach identifies and eliminates data from
070
      less important subsets in a bootstrapping manner. These two operations from our SCAN method are
071
      performed iteratively for stable pre-training.

      Dataset Pruning and Distillation represent two common approaches to enhancing dataset efficiency
111
      during training. The former aims to synthesize a smaller dataset that achieves test performance
112
      similar to a full dataset when using the same model (Chen et al., 2024; Du et al., 2023; Shang et al.,
113   2023; Sun et al., 2024). Recent advancements in this area have leveraged techniques such as mutual
114   information (Shang et al., 2023), frequency domain transformation (Shin et al., 2023), and multi-stage
115   generation (Chen et al., 2024) to craft datasets that exhibit enhanced performance. Two notable
116   limitations are inherent in these methods: I) The generalization capability is significantly constrained
117   due to the reliance on distillation from a specific dataset and model, e.g., the use of ResNet (He
118   et al., 2016). II) The dataset sizes utilized for comparison are frequently confined to small-scale
119   datasets such as CIFAR (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) and Tiny-ImageNet (Le & Yang, 2015). In contrast,
120   dataset pruning is employed to directly filter a smaller subset from the original dataset (Li et al., 2024;
121
      Sorscher et al., 2022). Typically, previous methods involve initially learning an indication score,
      which serves as a basis for identifying and subsequently removing data samples falling below or above
122
      a certain threshold. For image classification tasks, prevailing methods often utilize gradient (Paul
123
      et al., 2021), loss value (Qin et al., 2024), and second derivative (Koh & Liang, 2017) as the indicator.
124   More recently, efforts have emerged focusing on pruning vision-language pre-trained datasets (Li
125   et al., 2023a;b; Wang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; 2024). The key idea is to construct a coreset by
126   identifying the semantic mismatch/duplication, a process facilitated by often using a pre-trained CLIP
127   model (Beaumont, 2022; Radford et al., 2021).
128
      Contrastive Pre-Training has garnered wide attention as a technique for pre-training versatile models
129
      applicable to a range of downstream tasks (Gao et al., 2021). Its fundamental principle involves
130   bringing the embeddings of positive pairs closer while simultaneously pushing away negative ones.
131   Traditional approaches within the vision-centric domain often build positive samples by leveraging
132   alternative views of the anchor sample, as exemplified by approaches like SimCLR (Chen et al.,
133   2020a;b) and MoCo (Chen et al., 2020c; 2021). Benefiting from the advancement of transformer
134   architectures (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Vaswani et al., 2017), recent endeavors have shifted towards
135   patch masking followed by subsequent recovery (Bao et al., 2022; Caron et al., 2021; He et al., 2022).
136   Contrastive learning has achieved notable success in vision-language per-training as well (Jia et al.,
137   2021; Radford et al., 2021). The alignment of modalities has propelled significant advancements in
138
      downstream multi-modal tasks, including visual question answering (Antol et al., 2015; Zhou et al.,
      2022) and cross-modal retrieval (Bowyer & Flynn, 2000; Lin et al., 2014). Our study specifically
139
      targets the data efficiency challenge within CLIP and MoCo, which serve as prominent representatives
140
      of vision-language and vision-centric doamins, respectively.
141
142   Dynamic Sparse Training (DST). Unlike earlier static sparse training methodologies (Lee et al.,
143   2019; Tanaka et al., 2020), DST learns a sparse neural network by pruning weights and growing
144
      them back throughout the training process. The weight importance is typically quantified using
      metrics such as magnitude (Evci et al., 2020; Mocanu et al., 2018), gradients (Yuan et al., 2021),
145
      or sensitivity (Mozer & Smolensky, 1989). The demonstrated superiority of DST over its static
146
      counterpart inspires us to design a dynamic approach for dataset pruning, especially considering that
147   recent coreset-based methods predominantly adhere to fixed pruning strategies. Furthermore, the
148   well-developed DST methods provide additional hints for devising our dataset pruning strategy.

192      3.2   SCAN OVERVIEW
193
194     Our proposed SCAN method involves a two-step operation. Specifically, our first step entails
195     identifying a proxy metric that is dynamically adaptable, easily obtainable, and capable of tracing the
196
        learning status of each sample. We abandon the use of gradients as done in related domains (Paul
        et al., 2021), given the substantially increased compute overhead incurred for individual samples2 .
197
        Instead, we opt for the loss value as the reliable indicator as it meets the above conditions. Under this

        context, we disentangle the loss in Eqn. 1 to obtain a loss set L̄f →g = {L̄f →g , L̄f →g , ..., L̄f →gt },

        with each element corresponding to the loss value of one data sample.
201     The second core step is to determine the pruning strategy. Addressing sparsity within the dataset
202     pruning study presents a substantial challenge. To approach this, we propose a pruned data preparation
203     and then a dataset mutation approach. Specifically, in the pruned data preparation stage, candidate
204     data samples that are potentially less important are selected. Thereafter, in the dataset mutation stage,
205     samples are gradually bootstrapped for pruning across epochs. These two stages iterate through

        several rounds until the completion of pre-training.

      3.3.1   P RUNING DATA P REPARATION
217
218   We opt to utilize the loss values of in-batch samples rather than those from the entire dataset for
219   candidate selection. This decision is based on two facts: 1) Comparing InfoNCE losses across batches
220   holds less significance compared to supervised learning, as the instance loss varies drastically with
221   respect to the randomly selected batched samples. 2) Saving the losses for the entire dataset incurs
222
      more computational storage compared to in-batch ones.
223   Additionally, existing coreset selection approaches (Hessel et al., 2021; Schuhmann et al., 2021)
224   from the vision-language domain typically focus only on pruning ill-matched samples. These are
225   characterized by image-text pairs indicating less semantic alignment, where, for example, the text
226   inadequately describes the content of the paired image. However, we posit that beyond these ill-
227   matched samples, there also exist samples that are redundant. These redundant samples are effectively
228
      memorized during the early stages of training and are less likely to be forgotten with further training
      iterations (Feldman & Zhang, 2020). In view of this, we can safely eliminate these redundant data as
229
      training progresses.
230

304   3.4   T IME -E FFICIENCY OF SCAN
305
306   In fact, our proposed SCAN method introduces negligible additional pre-training time in comparison
307   to each base model. The potentially increased time involves three major steps: metric selection,
308   pruned data preparation, and pruned data retrieval. Since we directly utilize individual loss values, the
309   metric selection step does not impose an additional time overhead. Second, we obtain the candidate
310   pruned data from in-batch samples, typically containing only a few thousand data points, thereby
311
      enabling a fast sorting process. Last, retrieving from millions of pruned data sets also leads to
      negligible costs, as evidenced by our empirical observations.
312
313   More importantly, we maintain consistency in the number of training epochs for our SCAN model,
314   aligning it with the epochs utilized by each respective base model. This approach ensures time
315   efficiency within our proposed method, as demonstrated in Sect. 4.5.

518      5                         C ONCLUSION
519
520
        Limitations. We acknowledge two potential limitations of this work. Firstly, akin to other dataset
521
        pruning methodologies, our method necessitates the storage of the original large-scale dataset. This
522     may pose storage challenges for researchers with limited computing resources. Secondly, our method
523     may not seamlessly transfer to the recent popular large language model pre-training. Apart from
524     differences in pre-training objectives, large language models (LLMs) often require only a few training
525     epochs, typically one to three. In contrast, our iterative bootstrapping strategy requires more epochs
526     to converge, the same as each corresponding contrastive pre-training model.
527
        Summary. This work sets an initial effort to comprehensively address the data efficiency challenge
528     in contrastive pre-training. We propose a novel dataset bootstrapping approach, applying it to a
529     range of contrastive pre-training model architectures and evaluating it using various protocols. Our
530     experiments demonstrate that, across all experimental settings, the proposed method achieves a
531     superior balance between downstream model performance and data efficiency compared to both the
532     base models and several existing data efficiency approaches. Additionally, it also helps yield an
533     effective coreset dataset that significantly outperforms other coreset-based baselines, thereby further
534     reducing time costs and training overhead.
535     Future Work. In the future, we plan to validate the effectiveness of our method 1) in more domains
536     such as language-centric contrastive pre-training, 2) with larger pre-training datasets for vision-
537     language like LAION-400M (Schuhmann et al., 2021). Moreover, our method stands orthogonal to
538     other efficiency studies, such as model compression. As such, by integrating strategies from these
539     related domains, we aim to build a more efficient training pipeline framework, thus contributing to
        substantial reductions in carbon footprints.

      B.2     C OMPARED BASELINES
819
820   We compared with the following four baselines in this work:
821
822       • Random prunes ρ samples with randomness for each epoch. Notably, it falls under dynamic
823         pruning methods as the pruned samples vary across epochs.
824       • SemDeDup (Abbas et al., 2023) identifies the semantic duplicates based on embedding simi-
825         larities. We used one public implementation4 . This method is applicable only to multi-modal
826         models such as CLIP.
827       • D-Pruning (Yang et al., 2023) estimate the parameter influence of a training example through
828         the removal of it. We utilized the official implementation5 for CLIP models only. We abandoned
829         the use of MoCo due to its hard-to-configure running environment.
830       • Info-Batch (Qin et al., 2024) is a recent robust dataset pruning baseline. It prunes a portion of
831         less informative samples and then rescales the gradients of the remaining samples to approximate
832         the original gradients. We followed the original code6 to re-implement it for our experiments.
