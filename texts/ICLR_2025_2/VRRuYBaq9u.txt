009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement Learning (RL) in partially observable environments poses sig-
012            nificant challenges due to the complexity of learning under uncertainty. While
013            additional information, such as that available in simulations, can enhance training,
014            effectively leveraging it remains an open problem. To address this, we introduce
015            Guided Policy Optimization (GPO), a framework that co-trains a guider and a
016            learner. The guider takes advantage of supplementary information while ensur-
017            ing alignment with the learner’s policy, which is primarily trained via Imitation
018
               Learning (IL). We theoretically demonstrate that this learning scheme achieves
               optimality comparable to direct RL, thereby overcoming key limitations inherent
019
               in IL approaches. Our approach includes two practical variants, GPO-penalty
020
               and GPO-clip, and empirical evaluations show strong performance across vari-
021            ous tasks, including continuous control with partial observability and noise, and
022            memory-based challenges, significantly outperforming existing methods.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Many real-world tasks can be formulated as sequential decision-making problems where agents must
028   take actions in an environment to achieve specific goals over time (Puterman, 2014). Reinforcement
029   Learning (RL) has emerged as a powerful tool for solving such tasks, leveraging trial-and-error
030   learning to optimize long-term rewards (Sutton & Barto, 2018). Despite its success, RL encounters
031   significant hurdles in complex and partially observable environments, where agents often operate with
032   limited or noisy information (Madani et al., 1999). However, during training, we often have access to
033
      supplementary information that could significantly enhance learning efficiency and performance (Lee
      et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2022). For instance, in robotics, while real-world sensor data may be noisy
034
      or incomplete, simulation environments typically provide full state observability.
035
036   Although this extra information offers the potential to accelerate learning, effectively leveraging it in
037   practice remains a major challenge. By introducing a teacher with access to additional information,
038   Imitation Learning (IL) (Hussein et al., 2017) offers a promising approach to address this challenge,
039
      as it is often more sample-efficient than traditional RL by enabling agents to learn directly from a
      teacher’s actions. Yet, this approach presents new difficulties: a suboptimal teacher may propagate
040
      flawed strategies (Rajeswaran et al., 2017), while a teacher with extra information may set an
041
      unrealistically high standard, making it difficult for the agent to imitate effectively. The latter issue,
042   known as an "impossibly good" teacher (Walsman et al., 2023) or imitation gap (Weihs et al., 2024),
043   can impede learning and degrade performance. Prior efforts to address these issues have integrated
044   RL with IL (Weihs et al., 2024; Shenfeld et al., 2023a; Nguyen et al., 2023), but typically assume
045   access to a pre-trained teacher, which may not always be feasible. While one could train a teacher
046   using additional information before training the agent, this two-step process is often inefficient and
047   computationally expensive.
048   To better utilize available information, we consider a more integrated approach: training a "possibly
049   good" teacher that the agent can consistently follow. Drawing inspiration from Guided Policy
050   Search (GPS) (Levine & Koltun, 2013; Montgomery & Levine, 2016), we introduce Guided Policy
051   Optimization (GPO), a framework that alternates between RL for the teacher and IL for the agent,
052   ensuring the teacher remains aligned with the agent’s policy. The key insight is that by leveraging
053   the additional information during training, the teacher can be more easily trained, while maintaining
      a level of performance that is "possibly good" rather than perfect, being more straightforward for


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the agent to follow. Theoretically, we show that the agent can achieve optimality akin to direct RL
055   training, thus mitigating suboptimality and imitation gaps often faced by purely supervised agents.
056   Building on this framework, we present a practical implementation of GPO using Proximal Policy
057   Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017), with two variants: GPO-penalty and GPO-clip. These
058   methods introduce minimal modifications, making them efficient and straightforward to apply.
059
      We empirically validate our algorithm across various tasks. In tasks where traditional guidance
060
      methods fail to produce optimal policies, our approach proves highly effective. We further validate
061   our algorithm on challenging continuous control tasks in partially observable, noisy environments
062   within the MuJoCo (Todorov et al., 2012) domain, where GPO outperforms baseline methods.
063   Additionally, in memory-based tasks from the POPGym (Morad et al., 2023) benchmark, GPO shows
064   significant improvements, underscoring its ability to exploit extra information and deliver robust
065   performance across diverse domains.

      2     BACKGROUND
068
069   We consider Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) (Kaelbling et al., 1998), which
070   is characterized by the tuple ⟨S, A, r, P, O, γ⟩. S represents the set of states, A the set of actions, r
071   the reward function, P the transition probability function, O the partial observation function and γ
072   the discount factor. At each time step t, agent receives a partial observation ot ∼ O(·|st ) for current
073   state st ∈ S. The agent then selects an action at ∈ A according to ot or its action-observation history
074   τt : {o0 , a0 , o1 , a1 ..., ot }. The state transitions to the next state st+1 according to P(st+1 |st , at ),
075   and agent receives a reward rt . The goal for the agent is to find the optimal policy π ∗P     : τ → ∆(A)
                                                                                                       ∞
076   that maximizes the policy value, expressed as π ∗ = arg maxπ Vπ , where Vπ = E[ t=0 γ t rt |π]
077
      represents cumulative rewards.
078   Assuming that the state s is available during training, we can train a policy µ : s → ∆(A) based on
079   this state information. For clarity, we denote µ as the guider and π as the learner throughout the
080   paper. Unlike IL, we do not assume access to any additional policy; thus, the guider must be trained
081   from scratch. For convenience, while the observations available to the guider could be any form of
082   privileged information, we directly refer to the state s in the remainder of this paper.
083

      3     M ETHOD
134
135
      We present our Guided Policy Optimization (GPO) framework, which co-trains two entities: the
136
      guider and the learner. Inspired by Guided Policy Search, GPO iteratively updates both policies to
137   ensure alignment. We then explore both the theoretical properties and practical implementation of
138   GPO, introducing two variants: GPO-penalty and GPO-clip.
139
140   3.1   G UIDED P OLICY O PTIMIZATION
141
142   The GPO framework operates through an iterative process comprising four key steps:
143
144         1. Data Collection: Collect trajectories by executing the guider’s policy, denoted as µ(k) .
145         2. Guider Training: Update the guider µ(k) to µ̂(k) according to RL objective Vµ(k) .
146
            3. Learner Training: Update the learner to π (k+1) by minimizing the distance D(π, µ̂(k) ).
147
148         4. Guider Backtrack: Set µ(k+1) (·|s) = π (k+1) (·|o) for all state s before the next iteration.
149
150
      In step 3, D(π, µ) can be any Bregman divergence. For this work, we utilize the KL divergence
      weighted by the state distribution dµ . GPO iterates these steps until convergence, applying standard
151
      RL to train the guider, while the learner seeks to mimic the guider’s behavior. If the learner struggles
152
      due to discrepancies in observation spaces, the backtrack step adjusts the guider’s policy to mitigate
153   the imitation gap.
154
155   A key feature of GPO is that only the guider’s policy interacts with the environment, ensuring that
156
      data is always generated from the distribution induced by µ. Importantly, despite the learner not
      directly interacting with the environment, we demonstrate that GPO achieves the same convergence
157
      and optimality guarantees as direct RL training. For simplicity, we assume the guider µ has access to
158
      an unlimited policy class, while the learner π is limited to a constrained policy class Π.
159
      Proposition 1. If the guider’s policy is updated using policy mirror descent in each GPO iteration:

169   Policy Mirror Descent (PMD) (Tomar et al., 2020; Xiao, 2022) is a general family of algorithms
170   that covers a wide range of fundamental methods in RL, particularly trust-region algorithms like
171   TRPO (Schulman et al., 2015a) and PPO. This proposition demonstrates that if we use an algorithm
172   belonging to the PMD family for updating the guider’s policy, the iterative process of GPO can be
173
      viewed as applying the same algorithm directly to the learner. In other words, the update of the
      learner’s policy can inherit the properties such as monotonic policy improvement (Schulman et al.,
174
      2015a) from trust-region algorithms. This suggests that GPO can effectively address challenges in IL,
175
      such as dealing with a suboptimal teacher or the imitation gap, while still framing the learner’s policy
176   as being supervised by the guider. In Appendix ??, we provide an intuitive example to show how
177   GPO can achieve optimal in TigherDoor-alt problem.
178
179
      Given that GPO effectively mirrors direct RL for the learner, one may ask: What are the key
      advantages of GPO? The primary benefit is that GPO simplifies the learning process by leveraging
180
      additional information. The guider’s training is generally easier than the learner’s, particularly since
181
      policy gradients for the learner suffer from high variance, worsened by partial observability. By
182   dividing the learning process, GPO handles this challenge more effectively. The guider is updated
183   using policy gradients, while the learner is trained through supervised learning, thereby assigning
184   more complex tasks to the guider and simplifying the learner’s objective. For example, when training
185   an agent to be robust to noise, we may deliberately add noise to the observations. However, this will
186   complicate training due to the noise in both observations and policy gradients. GPO addresses this by
187   training the guider without noise and supervising the learner with noisy observations, making the
188   process more manageable and robust.

243   3.3   R EFINEMENTS OF GPO
244
      In this section, we introduce several refinements to the GPO framework. The key principle guiding
245
      these refinements is that an effective guider should remain at the boundary of the learner’s "possibly
246   good" region: if the guider is too far ahead, the learner struggles to follow; if too close, the guider’s
247   ability to provide effective supervision and better trajectory diminishes. To achieve this balance, the
248   guider should halt updates when it moves too far ahead and avoid backtracking when it is already
249   sufficiently close.
250

                         4.2         C ONTINUOUS C ONTROL TASKS IN M U J O C O
379
380                      In this subsection, we present the results of our algorithms and baselines on several continuous control
381                      tasks in the MuJoCo domain. We adopt the implementation of PPO and MuJoCo environments in Brax
382                      (Freeman et al., 2021). To transform the MuJoCo tasks into a POMDP setting, we follow a similar
383                      approach to that used in POPGym: the velocity information of all joints is removed, and varying
384                      levels of noise are added to the observations. The guider has access to full, noiseless information,
385                      while the learner operates with partial and noisy inputs. For more details, please refer to Appendix E.
386                      The results are shown in Fig. 2, where the performance hierarchy is generally GPO-clip > GPO-
387                      penalty > PPO-V > GPO-naive > other baselines. We have the following key observations:
388
                        1. GPO-based methods consistently outperform PPO, demonstrating that GPO effectively utilizes
389
                        additional information during training, thereby improving the learning efficiency of the agent.
390
391                     2. As a popular approach for utilizing additional information (Pinto et al., 2018; Andrychowicz
392                     et al., 2020), especially in MARL (Yu et al., 2022), PPO-V performs relatively well due to its more
393
                        accurate value function, which reduces the variance in policy gradients thanks to its access to noiseless
                        observations. Since PPO-V is theoretically equivalent to GPO with exact backtracking (Proposition
394
                        1), we provide further experiments and discussions in subsequent sections.
395
396                      3. Comparing GPO-naive to GPO-penalty and GPO-clip, we see that introducing RL training for
397                      the learner significantly improves performance. It compensates for policy improvement when the
398                      guider and learner are not closely aligned, and helps the learner itself make progress when the guider
399
                         struggles to optimize while staying close to the learner.
400                      4. Comparing PPO-BC and GPO-naive highlights the necessity of backtracking. If we train the guider
401                      without considering the learner’s progress, the imitation gap becomes significant, and the guider’s the
402                      supervision becomes ineffective, leading to suboptimal performance.
403                      5. ADVISOR-co performs similarly to PPO due to the absence of effective backtracking. The guider
404                      quickly outpaces the learner, causing the weight coefficient in ADVISOR to diminish, effectively
405                      reducing it to pure PPO training.
406
                         6. A2D performs poorly across all tasks. Although it also allows the co-training of the guider and
407
                         learner, it fails to maintain a good guider policy. Since its behavioral policy comes from the learner,
408
                         without proper backtracking, the guider soon drifts outside the "possibly good" region, rendering its
409                      RL training ineffective as the behavioral policy diverges from the current update policy.
410
411                      In summary, our method consistently outperforms the baselines across almost all tasks, highlighting
412
                         its effectiveness in solving noisy and partially observable continuous control tasks.

      4.3   M EMORY- BASED TASKS IN POPG YM
433
434   In this subsection, we evaluate GPO on several memory-based tasks from POPGym, using the JAX
435   (Bradbury et al., 2018) version from Lu et al. (2023), along with its PPO-GRU implementation. These
436   tasks include card and board games where agents must recall previous observations to extract useful
437   information for decision-making. For these tasks, the guider’s observation is designed to include
438   the critical information needed to remember, theoretically minimizing the imitation gap as long as
439   the GRU can store the necessary information. Although in practice, GRU models struggle to retain
440   all information, especially in complex tasks, this setup allows us to use a larger KL-threshold or
441
      clipping parameter, enabling the guider to explore further and provide more valuable supervision. For
      GPO-clip, due to the asymmetry with large δ, we replace the clip( πµ , 1 − δ, 1 + δ) with clip( πµ , 1r , r).
442
      Further details on the experimental settings are provided in Appendix E.
443
444   Fig. 3 shows the results on 15 POPGym tasks, where we compare GPO-penalty and GPO-clip
445   to PPO-V and PPO. The general conclusion mirrors the results from previous subsection, where
446   GPO-clip typically outperforms GPO-penalty, followed by PPO-V and PPO. Key insights include:
447   1. The superior performance of GPO-penalty indicate that the ability of the guider to explore further
448   without diverging too much from the learner proves valuable in these memory-based tasks.
449
      2. While PPO-V outperforms PPO, its performance improvement is less pronounced in memory-
450
      based tasks than in the MuJoCo domain. This suggests that using additional information in the value
451   function benefits noisy tasks but provides less of an advantage in tasks requiring memory.
452
453   3. In tasks like BattleshipMedium and CountRecallHard, neither GPO-penalty nor GPO-clip exhibit
454
      superior performance. We attribute this to the fixed hyperparameters across all tasks, which might
      not be optimal for these specific challenges. Further verification is presented in the next subsection.
455
456   Overall, our methods demonstrate strong performance across the majority of tasks, providing an
457   effective solution for memory-based problems.

514   When does GPO fail? One straightforward failure mode occurs when the guider learns slower
515   than directly training the learner via RL. This typically happens when the guider is provided with
516   inadequate information, which slows training instead of accelerating it. Another failure mode is
517   using inappropriate KL-threshold (clip parameters). For instance, in the CountRecallHard task
518
      in POPGym, both GPO variants underperform compared to PPO and PPO-V. To explore this, we
      conducted additional experiments (Fig. 6). In simpler tasks like CountRecallEasy and BattleshipEasy,
519
      larger KL-thresholds (clip parameters) improve performance. However, for more difficult tasks
520
      like CountRecallHard and BattleshipMedium, larger parameters lead to worse performance. This is
521   because harder tasks challenge memory models like the GRU used here. If the GRU cannot adequately
522   retain necessary information, the learner cannot follow the guider. In such cases, a large KL-threshold
523   (clip parameter) exceeds the "possibly good" region for learner, leading to an irrecoverable imitation
524   gap.
525
      How to set the KL-threshold/clip parameter? As seen in the previous experiments, the configura-
526
      tion of these hyperparameters depends on how large the "possibly good" region is. More specifically,
527   it depends on how well the learner’s observation ol can infer the guider’s observation og . In noisy
528   tasks, where og cannot be easily inferred from noisy ol , a smaller KL-threshold (clip parameter)
529   works best. In memory tasks, where og can be predicted by ol given a memory model, a larger
530   KL-threshold (clip parameter) depending on the ability of the model is preferable.

533   5                 C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE WORK
534
535   In this paper, we introduced GPO, a method designed to leverage additional information in POMDPs
536   during training. Our experimental results demonstrate that the proposed algorithm effectively ad-
537   dresses noisy and memory-based partially observable tasks, offering a novel approach to utilizing
538   auxiliary information for more efficient learning. Future work could explore extending guided policy
539   optimization to the multi-agent setting, where agents often have access to global information during
      training but are constrained to local observations during execution.

      A    R ELATED W ORKS
757
758   Leveraging additional information to accelerate learning in POMDPs has been explored across various
759   frameworks and application domains (Vapnik & Vashist, 2009; Lambert et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2023).
760   A prominent line of research focuses on Imitation Learning (IL), where expert knowledge, often
761   equipped with extra information, significantly enhances performance in practical domains like
762   autonomous driving (Bansal et al., 2018; De Haan et al., 2019) and robot navigation and planning
763   (Choudhury et al., 2017; Bhardwaj et al., 2017). However, traditional IL methods such as Behavioral
764   Cloning (BC) (Pomerleau, 1991; Torabi et al., 2018) and DAgger (Ross et al., 2011) often lead to
765   sub-optimal solutions in scenarios requiring active information gathering by the agent (Pinto et al.,
766
      2018; Warrington et al., 2020). To overcome these limitations, recent research has focused on hybrid
      approaches that integrate RL with IL, often in the context of policy distillation (Czarnecki et al.,
767
      2019). For instance, Nguyen et al. (2022)modifies Soft Actor Critic (SAC) (Haarnoja et al., 2018)
768
      by replacing the entropy term with a divergence measure between agent and expert policies at each
769   visited state. Similarly, Weihs et al. (2024) introduces a balancing mechanism between BC and RL
770   training, adjusting based on the agent’s ability to mimic the expert. Additionally, Walsman et al.
771   (2023) applies potential-based reward shaping (Ng et al., 1999) using the expert’s value function to
772   guide the agent’s policy gradient, while Shenfeld et al. (2023b) augments entropy in SAC to blend
773   task reward with expert guidance, where the balance is based on the agent’s performance relative to
774   a reward-only learner. Despite these advances, expert-driven approaches often assume access to a
775   reliable expert, which may not be feasible when only supplementary information is available. This
776   has led to a growing body of work on co-training approaches where the expert and agent are learned
777
      jointly, with the expert conditioned on additional information. For example, Salter et al. (2021)
      proposes training separate policies for the agent and expert using spatial attention for image-based
778
      RL, aligning attention mechanisms through shared experiences. Song et al. (2020) co-trains two
779
      policies, each conditioned on different information, and selects the most successful rollouts from both
780   policies to guide subsequent learning via RL or IL. Warrington et al. (2020) further develops this idea
781   in adaptive asymmetric DAgger (A2D), where the expert is continuously refined through RL while
782   supervising the agent. Beyond expert-based methods, a complementary approach involves embedding
783   supplementary information directly into the value function within the actor-critic framework (Pinto
784   et al., 2018; Andrychowicz et al., 2020; Baisero & Amato, 2021). This approach is particularly useful
785   in multi-agent settings where global information is naturally accessible (Foerster et al., 2018; Lowe
786   et al., 2017; Yu et al., 2022). In our experiments, we benchmark against several algorithms inspired
787   by these lines of work, with detailed descriptions of the baselines provided in Appendix E.1.

1050   Autoencode: During the WATCH phase, a deck of cards is shuffled and played in sequence to the
1051   agent with the watch indicator set. The watch indicator is unset at the last card in the sequence, where

1053   card to be output at each timestep.
1054   Battleship: A partially observable version of Battleship game, where the agent has no access to the
1055   board and must derive its own internal representation. Observations contain either HIT or MISS and
1056   the position of the last salvo fired. The player receives a positive reward for striking a ship, zero
1057   reward for hitting water, and negative reward for firing on a specific tile more than once. The guider
1058   has access to a recorder that tracks all previous actions taken by the agent.
1059   Count Recall: Each turn, the agent receives a next value and query value. The agent must answer
1060   the query with the number of occurrences of a specific value. In other words, the agent must store
1061   running counts of each unique observed value, and report a specific count back, based on the query
1062   value. The guider directly observes the running counts at each timestep.

       Repeat Previous: At the first timestep, the agent receives one of four values and a remember indicator.
1064
       Then it randomly receives one of the four values at each successive timestep without the remember
1065   indicator. The agent is rewarded for outputting the observation from some constant k timesteps ago,
1066   i.e. observation ot−k at time t. The guider has direct access to the value ot−k at time t.

       This section presents results for SOTA teacher-student learning methods, including ADVISOR and
1137   TGRL, using a pretrained teacher in the Brax environment. The Ant task serves as an example, where
1138   the teacher is trained on the fully observable task using PPO. This pretrained teacher is then employed
1139   to train various algorithms on both the original fully observable task (OriginalAnt) and a partially
1140   observable version (Ant), consistent with the setup in Section 4.2.
1141
       The results are illustrated in Figure 9. In Figures 9(a) and (b), we can observe that in the OriginalAnt
1142
       task (where the teacher was trained), teacher-student learning algorithms such as ADVISOR and
1143   TGRL significantly improve sample efficiency compared to baseline algorithms like PPO and SAC.
1144   However, Figures 9(c) and (d) reveal a contrasting outcome in the partially observable Ant task. Here,
1145   the teacher, being privileged, fails to provide meaningful supervision. As a result, ADVISOR and
1146   TGRL revert to their base algorithms, PPO and SAC. Additionally, PPO+BC does not degenerate
1147   into PPO due to the consistent BC loss, which adversely impacts its performance, making it worse
1148   than PPO.
1149   Figure 10 further examines the KL divergence of these methods relative to the teacher they learned
1150   from. The results indicate that teacher-student algorithms effectively minimize KL divergence when
1151   the teacher is not privileged. However, when the teacher is inimitable, the mechanisms in ADVISOR
1152   and TGRL adjust (e.g., changing weights or coefficients) to prioritize their base RL algorithms,
1153   effectively discarding the teacher’s influence.
