009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs), their ability to solve
012            complex reasoning problems remains limited by inconsistent planning and logical
013            flaws. We present a novel framework that significantly enhances LLMs’ problem-
014            solving capabilities by leveraging Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for plan gen-
015            eration. Unlike previous approaches that apply MCTS to solution search, our
016
               method uniquely integrates MCTS into the planning phase, guided by special-
               ized LLM-powered agents that evaluate plan quality. Experiments across diverse
017
               benchmark datasets demonstrate that our approach improves problem-solving ac-
018
               curacy by an average of 40.59% compared to zero-shot Chain-of-Thought prompt-
019            ing. Furthermore, we show that using smaller models for MCTS planning and
020            larger models for execution can maintain high performance while reducing com-
021            putational costs. This work opens new avenues for developing more robust and
022            efficient AI systems capable of tackling complex real-world problems, with po-
023            tential applications in fields requiring advanced logical reasoning and long-term
024            planning. Our code examples are publicly available at this Anonymous Github
025            Repository.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      In an era where artificial intelligence increasingly influences our daily lives, the quest for machines
030   capable of human-like reasoning remains a central challenge in computer science. Large Language
031   Models (LLMs) have emerged as powerful tools in natural language processing, demonstrating re-
032   markable capabilities across various tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2023). However,
033   when faced with complex, multi-step problems requiring logical consistency and long-term plan-
034   ning, even state-of-the-art models like GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024) often falter, revealing a critical
035   gap between machine and human problem-solving abilities.
036   The limitations of LLMs in complex reasoning tasks stem from their struggle to maintain logical
037   consistency and handle long-term dependencies throughout extended problem-solving processes.
038   Existing approaches, such as Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2022), have shown
039   promise in improving step-by-step reasoning. However, these methods often fail to address the fun-
040   damental challenge of generating and following a coherent, overarching plan (Wang et al., 2023b;
041   Yao et al., 2023). Similarly, current planning methods for LLMs, including task decomposition (Pa-
042   tel et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023) and explicit plan-and-solve approaches (Wang et al., 2023a; Yao
043   et al., 2023), are ultimately constrained by the LLM’s inherent reasoning abilities.
044   This research addresses a critical question: How can we enhance the planning capabilities of LLMs
045   to significantly improve their problem-solving performance in complex, multi-step tasks? We hy-
046   pothesize that by leveraging advanced search algorithms in the planning phase, we can generate
047   higher-quality plans that guide LLMs towards more effective and logically consistent solutions.
048   To this end, we propose a novel framework that integrates Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) into the
049   planning process for LLMs. MCTS, renowned for its success in complex game-playing AI (Silver
050   et al., 2016), offers a powerful method for exploring vast search spaces and identifying optimal
051   strategies. Our approach uniquely applies MCTS to the generation of problem-solving plans, rather
052   than to direct solution search. This is achieved through a two-step process: first, MCTS explores the
053   space of possible plans, guided by specialized LLM-powered agents that evaluate plan quality; then,
      the optimal plan is provided to the LLM for step-by-step execution.

139   P (Y |X, C). In zero-shot CoT prompting, the context C is typically the problem itself, and the LLM
140   attempts to generate the solution directly through a series of reasoning steps X = {X1 , X2 , ..., Xn }.
141   This direct approach often leads to suboptimal solutions due to the intertwined nature of planning
142   and reasoning.
143   To address this challenge, we employ a planning-based approach that decouples planning from rea-
144   soning. We introduce a plan π as an ordered sequence of actions, where π ∈ Π, and Π represents
145   the space of all possible plans. In this framework, the context C can be decomposed into two parts:
146   the problem description Cproblem and a plan π generated based on the problem, which we denote as
147   Cplan . Thus, we can rewrite the conditional probability as:
148
149                                                  P (Y |X, C) = P (Y |X, Cproblem , Cplan )                                                                            (1)
150
151   Assuming that the solution Y depends on the problem X and the plan Cplan , but not directly on
152   the problem description Cproblem once the plan is given, we can factorize this probability using the
153   chain rule:
154
155                                                P (Y |X, Cplan ) = P (Y |X, Cplan )P (X|C)                                                                             (2)
156
157   This factorization highlights the two distinct stages of our approach:
158
159          1. Planning (P (X|C)): Generating a sequence of reasoning steps (X) based on the initial
160
                context (C), which includes the problem description.
161          2. Reasoning (P (Y |X, Cplan )): Generating the solution (Y) given the problem (X) and the
                plan (Cplan ).

162
      By explicitly separating these stages, we aim to improve the logical consistency and coherence of
163   the problem-solving process. The planning stage focuses on finding an effective plan, while the
164   reasoning stage leverages the plan to guide the generation of the solution.
165
166
      Instead of relying on the LLM to implicitly generate a plan within its reasoning process, we leverage
      MCTS (Chaslot et al., 2008) to explicitly explore the plan space Π during the planning stage. MCTS
167
      is a powerful heuristic search algorithm that has achieved remarkable success in complex game
168
      AI (Silver et al., 2016). We adapt MCTS to the problem-solving domain by representing plans as
169   nodes in the search tree and using specialized LLM-powered agents to evaluate the quality of each
170   plan. These agents assess properties such as the logical consistency and feasibility of the plan,
171   providing feedback that guides the MCTS exploration.
172
      This approach differs significantly from previous work that has applied MCTS to LLMs. While some
173
      studies have used Breadth First Search (BFS) or Depth First Search (DFS) to guide the selection of
174
      reasoning steps in CoT (Yao et al., 2023), our framework focuses on using MCTS to optimize the
175   plan itself, providing a more structured and potentially more effective approach to complex problem-
176   solving.
177
178
      By decoupling planning and reasoning, and by leveraging the strengths of MCTS and LLMs, our
      proposed framework aims to enhance the problem-solving capabilities of LLMs, particularly in
179
      scenarios requiring complex reasoning and long-term planning.

      2.2   M ONTE C ARLO T REE S EARCH FOR P LANNING
182
183   To address the limitations of existing planning methods and improve the coherence and logical
184   consistency of LLM problem-solving, we propose leveraging MCTS for plan generation. Unlike
185   approaches that use MCTS to directly guide the reasoning process, our approach focuses on finding a
186   high-quality plan before the LLM begins to reason towards a solution. This corresponds to explicitly
187   searching for a good sequence of reasoning steps (X) in the probabilistic framework P (Y |X, Cplan )
188   discussed in Sec. 2.1.
189
190   Selection: Starting from the root node (which represents an initial plan, generated by prompting
191   the LLM with the problem description), we traverse the tree by selecting the child node with the
192   highest Upper Confidence Bound 1 (UCB1) value. UCB1 balances exploration and exploitation by
193
      considering both the average reward of simulations passing through a node and the number of times
      it has been visited:
194
195                                                            s
196                                                                ln(N (parent))
                               U CB1(node) = Q(node) + C                                                 (3)
197                                                                   N (node)
198
199   where Q(node) is the average reward of simulations passing through the node, N (node) is the
200   number of times the node has been visited, N (parent) is the number of times the parent node has
201   been visited, and C is an exploration constant that controls the balance between exploration and
202   exploitation.
203
204   Expansion: When a leaf node is reached, a new node is added to the tree. This new node represents
205   a modified version of the parent node’s plan.
206   Plans are represented as sequences of natural language instructions that guide the LLM’s reasoning.
207   For example, a plan to solve a math word problem might be: "1. Identify the given quantities. 2.
208   Determine the relationship between the quantities. 3. Formulate an equation. 4. Solve the equation."
209
210   Simulation and Reward: The newly generated plan is then subjected to a simulation to estimate
211   its effectiveness. Instead of real execution in traditional MCTS (Chaslot et al., 2008; Silver et al.,
212   2016), we employ multi-agents to assess the quality of the plan inspired by (Zhang et al., 2024b),
213   including:
214
             • Logical Consistency Agent: Checks for contradictions or inconsistencies in the plan.
215
             • Feasibility Agent: Determines whether the plan is executable.


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Each evaluation agent assigns a score (e.g., between 0 and 1) to the plan, and may also provide
217   textual feedback explaining its assessment. For example, the Logical Consistency Agent might give
218   a low score and feedback like: "Step 2 contradicts the information given in Step 1." This feedback
219   can be used to guide plan modification in subsequent expansion steps.
220
      The individual scores from the evaluation agents are combined by a reward function to produce an
221
      overall reward signal for the plan. The reward function can be a simple weighted average or a more
222
      complex function that takes into account the relative importance of different evaluation criteria.
223
224
225   Backpropagation: The reward signal obtained from the simulation and evaluation is backpropa-
226
      gated up the MCTS tree, updating the value estimates of all nodes along the path from the root to
      the newly expanded node.
227
228   By iteratively applying these steps, MCTS builds a search tree of potential plans, gradually focusing
229   on areas of the plan space that are likely to lead to high-quality solutions. Once a predefined number
230   of rollouts have been completed, or a time limit is reached, the MCTS algorithm selects the node
231   with the highest average reward as the optimal plan. We present an illustrative example of the
232
      MCTS planning process in Fig. 2 with counting “R”s in the word “Strawberry” and a more complex
      example in Sec. A.1.

238
239   In this research, we would like to investigatee the potential of MCTS to enhance the planning and
240   problem-solving capabilities of LLMs, addressing limitations of existing methods like zero-shot
241   CoT (Kojima et al., 2022) and Plan-and-Solve (Wang et al., 2023a) prompting in handling complex
242   multi-step problems. Specifically, we explore two core research questions:
243
      RQ1: Does MCTS-based planning improve LLM problem-solving performance? We will evaluate
244   this by comparing the accuracy of our proposed approach against baseline prompting and analyzing
245   the correlation between plan quality and LLM performance.
246
247
      RQ2: How to optimize the MCTS parameters for planning in LLMs? We will investigate the impact
      of different MCTS strategy, such as different LLM for planning, evaluating, and executing, on the
248
      problem-solving performance of our approach.
249
250   By answering these questions, we aim to provide insights into the interplay between planning and
251   reasoning in LLMs, contributing to the development of more robust and reliable AI systems capable
252   of tackling complex real-world problems.

285   3.3      RQ1: E NHANCED P ROBLEM -S OLVING THROUGH MCTS-G UIDED P LANNING
286
287   Existing methods for enhancing LLM problem-solving often face challenges in maintaining logical
288   consistency and handling long-term dependencies in complex multi-step problems. This is primar-
289   ily because these methods rely heavily on the LLM’s inherent reasoning capabilities, which can be
290
      limited in such scenarios. We hypothesize that applying MCTS to the planning process can ad-
      dress these limitations by generating higher-quality, more logically sound plans that guide the LLM
291
      towards more effective solutions. MCTS excels at exploring large search spaces and identifying
292
      optimal strategies through its balance of exploration and exploitation. By leveraging MCTS to gen-
293   erate plans, we aim to overcome the inherent limitations of relying solely on the LLM’s reasoning
294   for planning.
295
296
      To investigate the impact of MCTS-generated plans on LLM problem-solving, we conduct experi-
      ments comparing the performance of our MCTS-enhanced planning approach against two baselines:
297
      (1) standard CoT prompting (Kojima et al., 2022) and (2) a plan-and-solve approach (Wang et al.,
298
      2023a) where the LLM first generates a plan and then executes it. We evaluate these methods on a
299   diverse set of datasets introduced in Sec. 3.2.
300
301   We employ two state-of-the-art open-sourced LLMs: LLama 3.1 (8B parameters) (Dubey et al.,
302
      2024) and Qwen 2.5 (0.5B, 1.5B, and 7B parameters) (Yang et al., 2024). These models represent
      a strong baseline for current LLM capabilities and allow for a fair comparison between different
303
      planning approaches. Notably, we include smaller variants of Qwen 2.5 (0.5B and 1.5B) for MCTS
304
      to investigate the impact of model size on planning performance and computational efficiency, es-
305   pecially considering the potentially high computational cost of MCTS.
306
307   We adopt a zero-shot setting for both CoT and MCTS to assess the methods’ ability to generalize
308
      to new problems without task-specific fine-tuning. For the CoT baseline, we use code and data
      from (Kong et al., 2024) and (Kojima et al., 2022). We leverage the SGLang platform (Zheng et al.,
309
      2024) for hosting and interacting with the LLMs.

      MCTS in generating high-quality plans for LLMs. As expected, larger models generally perform

      rower for MCTS Planning compared to CoT, suggesting that our approach can partially compensate

      4.1   C HAIN - OF -T HOUGHT P ROMPTING
435
436   Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting has emerged as a prominent technique to improve the reasoning
437   abilities of LLMs by encouraging step-by-step problem-solving (Wei et al., 2022). By decomposing
438   complex tasks into smaller, manageable steps, CoT aims to enhance logical consistency and reduce
439   errors in multi-step reasoning. Subsequent studies have explored various enhancements to CoT,
440   such as self-consistency approaches (Wang et al., 2023b) and tree-of-thought methods (Yao et al.,
441   2023), which attempt to maintain logical coherence over extended reasoning paths. Despite these
442   advancements, CoT methods often struggle with maintaining overall logical flow in highly complex
443   scenarios, leading to inconsistencies and suboptimal solutions. How to maintain logical consistency
444
      and coherence in multi-step reasoning tasks is a research frontier in the field.
445
446   4.2   P LANNING T ECHNIQUES FOR LLM S
447
448   Beyond CoT, several planning-based approaches have been proposed to bolster the problem-solving
449
      capabilities of LLMs. Task decomposition techniques (Patel et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023) involve
      breaking down complex problems into simpler sub-tasks, which the LLM can solve sequentially.
450
      Explicit plan-and-solve frameworks (Wang et al., 2023a; Besta et al., 2024) require the LLM to
451
      generate a plan before executing it, aiming to structure the reasoning process more effectively. Ad-
452   vanced methods like Skeleton-of-Thoughts (Ning et al., 2024) and Graph-of-Thought (Besta et al.,
453   2024) introduce more sophisticated representations of plans to capture dependencies and improve
454   coherence. However, these methods remain constrained by the LLM’s inherent reasoning limita-
455   tions, often failing to produce optimal plans in the face of complex, multi-step problems. How to
456   generate high-quality plans for LLMs remains a challenging research question in the field.
457
458   4.3   M ONTE C ARLO T REE S EARCH
459
460   MCTS is a heuristic search algorithm renowned for its success in game-playing AI, particularly in
461   games with vast search spaces like Go and Chess (Silver et al., 2016). MCTS operates through four
462   main phases: selection, expansion, simulation, and backpropagation (Chaslot et al., 2008). Its ability
463   to balance exploration and exploitation makes it highly effective in navigating large decision trees
464   to identify optimal strategies. Recent research has begun to explore the application of MCTS in the
465
      context of LLMs, particularly for solution search in CoT processes (OpenAI, 2024) or self-training
      process (Zhang et al., 2024a). These studies have demonstrated that MCTS can enhance the search
466
      for high-quality solutions by efficiently exploring the space of possible reasoning paths. However,
467
      the direct application of MCTS to LLMs presents challenges, such as the computational expense of
468   evaluating generated content and the difficulty in effectively integrating heuristic evaluations within
469   the planning process. We investigate these challenges in our research and propose to use smaller
470   LLM for planning and larger LLM for evaluation and execution to address these challenges.
471
472
      4.4   R ESEARCH G AP AND O UR C ONTRIBUTION
473
474   While MCTS has shown promise in enhancing solution search for LLMs, its application to the plan-
475   ning process remains largely unexplored. Existing planning methods for LLMs, including Chain-
476   of-Thought prompting and plan-and-solve frameworks, predominantly rely on the LLM’s inherent
477   reasoning abilities to generate plans. However, these reasoning abilities can be inconsistent and
478   prone to errors, particularly when dealing with complex, multi-step problems requiring long-term
479   planning and logical consistency. For instance, in tasks involving multi-step mathematical reasoning
480   or intricate commonsense scenarios, LLMs often struggle to devise and maintain a coherent plan,
481   leading to suboptimal or incorrect solutions.
482   Our research addresses this gap by integrating MCTS into the planning phase. Unlike previous work
483   focusing on solution search, we leverage MCTS to explicitly generate high-quality plans before the
484   LLM starts reasoning. By systematically exploring the plan space and evaluating quality with spe-
485   cialized LLM agents, we aim to generate more effective and logically consistent plans, significantly
      enhancing LLM problem-solving accuracy and reliability in complex tasks.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
487
488
      This paper introduced a novel framework for enhancing the problem-solving capabilities of LLMs by
489   leveraging MCTS for plan generation. Our approach explicitly separates the planning and reasoning
490   stages of problem-solving, using MCTS to explore the space of possible plans before the LLM be-
491   gins to reason towards a solution. Experiments on a diverse set of benchmark datasets demonstrated
492   that MCTS-enhanced planning significantly improves LLM problem-solving accuracy compared to
493   standard Chain-of-Thought prompting and existing plan-and-solve methods.
494
      Our findings highlight the effectiveness of MCTS in generating high-quality, logically consistent
495   plans that guide the LLM towards more effective solutions, particularly in complex reasoning tasks.
496   We also observed that MCTS planning offers a stronger performance boost for smaller LLMs, sug-
497   gesting it can partially compensate for limited reasoning capabilities in resource-constrained set-
498   tings. Further analysis revealed that the performance of MCTS planning is influenced by factors
499   such as search depth and the number of rollouts, with deeper search and more rollouts generally
500   leading to better plans.
501   To address the computational cost of MCTS, we explored the use of smaller LLMs for planning
502   and larger LLMs for evaluation and execution. Our results demonstrated that this approach can
503   significantly enhance efficiency while maintaining high problem-solving performance, offering a
504   promising direction for optimizing MCTS planning in real-world applications.
505
      We believe that our research contributes to a deeper understanding of the interplay between planning
506
      and reasoning in LLMs and opens new avenues for improving AI problem-solving capabilities. The
507   integration of MCTS and LLMs holds significant promise for developing more robust and reliable
508   AI systems capable of tackling complex real-world problems.

      E THICAL C ONSIDERATIONS
512
513
      This research enhances LLM problem-solving via MCTS planning. While our benchmark datasets
      pose minimal direct ethical concerns, the potential impact of improved AI problem-solving necessi-
514
      tates broader ethical consideration.
515
516   Integrating MCTS introduces unique challenges. Unlike CoT methods, MCTS explores a wider
517   range of plans, some potentially ethical yet logically sound. For instance, directly plan and executing
518   a toxic action could be rejected by LLM with alignment to ethical principles. However, if the
519
      search objective is to find such a plan, MCTS may escape the LLM’s ethical constraints. In this
      context, Mitigation strategies warrant investigation. Incorporating fairness constraints into MCTS,
520
      adversarial training for agents, and human-in-the-loop plan review could enhance ethical outcomes.

523   L IMITATIONS
524
525   While our MCTS-enhanced planning approach demonstrates significant improvements in LLM
526   problem-solving capabilities, it is important to acknowledge several limitations of our current work.
527   Firstly, the computational cost of MCTS, particularly for larger language models, remains a signif-
528   icant challenge. Although we have shown that using smaller models for planning can mitigate this
529   issue to some extent, further research is needed to optimize the efficiency of MCTS in the context
530   of LLMs. Future work could explore pruning techniques or more sophisticated heuristics to reduce
531   the search space without compromising plan quality.
532   Secondly, our study primarily focused on a specific set of benchmark datasets. While these datasets
533   cover a range of problem types, they may not fully represent the diversity of real-world problems
534   that LLMs might encounter. Expanding our evaluation to a broader set of tasks and domains would
535   provide a more comprehensive assessment of our method’s generalizability and robustness.
536
      Despite these limitations, we believe that our work represents a significant step forward in enhancing
537   the problem-solving capabilities of LLMs. By explicitly addressing these challenges, we hope to
538   inspire further research that will lead to even more powerful and reliable AI systems capable of
539   tackling complex real-world problems.
