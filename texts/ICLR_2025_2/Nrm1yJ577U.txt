011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013             Textual entailment, or the ability to deduce whether a proposed hypothesis is logi-
014             cally supported by a given premise, has historically been applied to the evaluation
015
                of language modelling efficiency in tasks like question answering and text sum-
                marization. However, we hypothesize that these zero-shot entailment evaluations
016
                can be extended to the task of evaluating discourse within larger textual narratives.
017
                In this paper, we propose a simple but effective method that sequentially evaluates
018             changes in textual entailment between sentences within a larger text, in an ap-
019             proach we denote as “Entailment Progressions”. These entailment progressions
020             aim to capture the inference relations between sentences as an underlying com-
021             ponent capable of distinguishing texts generated from various models and proce-
022             dures. Our results suggest that entailment progressions can be used to effectively
023             distinguish between machine-generated and human-authored texts across multiple
024             established benchmark corpora and our own EP4MGT dataset. Additionally, our
025             method displays robustness in performance when evaluated on paraphrased texts
026
                a technique that has historically affected the performance of well-established met-
                rics when distinguishing between machine generated and human authored texts.

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031   As Large Language Models (LLMs) expand and evolve to accommodate more complex language
032   generation tasks (e.g., significant advances in machine translation (Lai et al., 2023), logical reason-
033   ing (Liu et al., 2023), summarization (Zhang et al., 2023), complex question answering (Tan et al.,
034   2023)), we are witnessing a growing number of machine-generated text (MGT) in both online and
035   offline environments.1 This, in turn, has raised concerns regarding authenticity and regulations,2, 3
036   drawing attention to MGT detection as both a safeguard and qualifier for authentic human author-
037   ship, which has become quite a hot topic in Natural Language Processing (NLP).4
038   Intuitively, machine-generated texts can display lexical, syntactic, and semantic properties that are
039   distinguishable from human authored texts, potentially guiding MGT detection implicitly, as a latent
040   property, or explicitly as a directly encoded feature (Georgiou, 2024). For example, MGT detection
041   methods like entropy and log-likelihood, which assess the probability of a text being machine gen-
042   erated based upon individual token probabilities encoded by a given LLM, take into account how
043   LLMs functionally operate as next word predictors (He et al., 2023). Thus, evaluating where LLMs
044
      situationally differ from human authorship in relation to both their observed behaviour and function-
      ality can expand the scope of feature selection within MGT detection to capture these differences
045
      more effectively and in a more interpretable manner.
046
047   Textual entailment, or the relationship between a given premise and its potentially inferred hypoth-
048   esis, has been previously used to evaluate how LLM text generation differs from human authorship
049   in regard to an LLM’s ability to generate text in accordance with prior informational constraints

074   2       R ELATED W ORK
075
076   The definition of recognizing textual entailment (RTE) as outlined by Dagan et al. (2005) and later
077   expanded upon by Korman et al. (2018) is as follows: “a text T textually entails a hypothesis H
078   relative to a group of end users G just in case, typically, a member of G reading T would be justified
079   in inferring the proposition expressed by H from the proposition expressed by T”. This definition
080   incorporates three key aspects of RTE. First, it does not require any knowledge beyond the justifiable
081   inference that can be made between a given text and its hypothesis Feldman (2003). Second, this
082   justifiable inference is subject to the characteristics exhibited by a group of end users G, in which
083
      users outside of this group may differ in their inferences due to personal factors that may influence
      how they interpret logical relationships Bos & Markert (2005). Third, the logical component of
084
      entailment is textually constrained, rendering it dependent on linguistic factors such as grammar,
085
      semantic, and syntactical choices Braun (2001).
086
087   Current RTE modelling approaches require two main steps. First, the features of premise T and
088   hypothesis H are extracted in order to represent the statements in accordance with relevant linguistic
089
      mechanisms associated with textual entailment. Second, the statements are fed into a supervised
      multi-class classification model which predicts whether a premise-hypothesis pair possesses posi-
090
      tive (the hypothesis can be inferred to be true if the premise is true), negative (the hypothesis can
091
      be inferred to be false if the premise is true), or neutral (the hypothesis’ truth is not sufficiently
092   conditional upon the premise being true) entailment. For an in-depth overview of RTE resources,
093   approaches, and applications, see Putra et al. (2024).

098   3.1       H YPOTHESIS
099
100   We incorporate Korman’s RTE approach into the task of detecting MGT under the premise that
101   determining inference relations between sentences in a text is a component of identifying authentic
102
      human authorship.
103   Take, for example, a short story written by ChatGPT. While the story may contain relevant content
104   pertaining to the subject matter and utilize vocabulary similar to its human counterpart, ChatGPT
105   may employ a more simplistic narrative structure without the stylistic nuance or variability typical
106   of human authors. While these LLMs are autoregressive models that generate the next token based

109   in exploring whether certain logical patterns are internally captured to some degree.

111   Regardless of the manner in which these evaluations are conducted, the structure of a textual narra-
112
      tive (like a short story) is an identifiable linguistic feature that can be used to distinguish between
      texts. We posit that in settings where texts must be logically structured to advance a given claim
113
      or narrative purpose, sentence-level evaluation can identify and distinguish structural differences
114
      between different generative processes. This process involves examining the inference relations be-
115   tween a new sentence and its overarching premise, as well as between sentences within the text.
116   RTE models can determine the probabilities of entailment, contradiction, and neutrality between a
117   sentence and its preceding text (to identify how the sentence logically corresponds to prior context).
118   These probabilities can be then assembled into “entailment progressions”, which are vectors com-
119   posed of sequentially calculated probabilities of inference relations between a given sentence and
120   the sentences preceding it.
121   The formal definition of the entailment progressions of a given text can be expressed as follows:

136   Motivated by observed discourse phenomena, such as the referential connection between (summa-
137   rizing) titles and the sentences in their corresponding texts, as well as between sentences in close
138   proximity (Mirkin et al., 2010), entailment progressions use entailment as a heuristic for identifying
139   logical relationships between key components of a text. Given this emphasis on the logical relation
140   between a chosen sentence and its overarching premise (i.e., a title), we also include the following

      where E represents the model used for calculating entailment between the general premise defining
      the full text p and a sentence or collection of sentences s.
147
148   Based on our analysis of existing RTE literature, we hypothesize that if the logical relationships
149   between components of a text are distinguishable linguistic features that underlie a set of texts pro-
150   duced by either models or humans, and if entailment progressions effectively represent this set of
151   relationships, then entailment progressions can be used to identify the source of a set of texts. Our
152
      hypothesis hinges upon two interconnected inquiries: Are entailment progressions a meaningful
      feature of a text? And, if so, is the governing structure of these logical relationships reproducible
153
      across texts produced by the same author? We suggest that our hypothesis can be validated by evalu-
154
      ating whether entailment progressions can serve as a feature for identifying and interpreting human
155   authorship. If we can identify MGTs using only their entailment progressions, this would exper-
156   imentally confirm that they are both meaningful and reproducible features across texts generated
157   through the same procedure.

      3.3    E XPERIMENTAL D ESIGN
194
195
      To ensure that our hypothesis is satisfied, we design an experimental setup that effectively accounts
196   for potential confounding limitations that may arise during analysis.
197
198   First, in order to establish a fair comparison between a set of human-authored and machine-generated
199
      texts, both sets must “further the same logical premise” and pertain to the same language genera-
      tion task. This effectively controls for style (e.g., news articles, social media discourse, persua-
200
      sive essays) that could otherwise overemphasize the differences in entailment progressions between
201
      human-authored and model-generated texts.
202
203   Second, the texts under examination must be preprocessed in a way that removes any textually con-
204   founding identifiers that can further accentuate comparative differences in entailment progressions.
205
      This process involves removing any elements within the text that are not relevant to the narrative at
      hand. These elements include, but are not limited to, the language in which the texts are written,
206
      identifiable markers from the media sources (e.g., platforms like Reddit include identifiable tags),
207
      and anomalies in sentence length. This helps ensure that the analysis focuses solely on the content
208   of the text.

      When controlling for these conditions, we design an experimental setting that is suitable for deter-
217   mining whether entailment progressions can be effectively used as a feature for assessing human
218   and model authorship. This setting involves calculating the entailment progressions for texts from
219   both human-authored and model-generated sets, and then training a classification algorithm to dis-
220   tinguish between the two sources. If the algorithm performs well on the classification task, then we
221   can assume that entailment progressions are a viable feature for differentiating between machine-
222   generated and human-authored texts.
223   Based on our hypothesis (cf. Section 3.1), we propose two key approaches for constructing the
224   entailment progressions. The first approach (denoted “Title-Sentence”) involves calculating the
225   entailment between the general premise of the text and the sentences within the text. This approach
226   assesses the logical relationship between each sentence and the premise it (is attempting to) support.
227   The second approach (denoted “Sentence-Sentence”) involves calculating the entailment between
228   a given sentence and its preceding context. This method uses a sliding context window, examining
229   a given number of sentences (based on the selected window size) directly prior to the evaluated
230   sentence.
231   In line with the experimental design previously outlined, we generated the Sentence-Sentence entail-
232   ment progressions using context window sizes of 1, 2, and 3 sentences for all datasets. Regarding
233   Title-Sentence entailment progressions, as we do not have the general premise for the MULTITuDE
234   and Ghostbuster datasets, we only generate it for the EP4MGT dataset. In this case, the general
235   premise is the title of the original human-authored CMV post, which we used to generate the LLM
236   responses addressing the argument conveyed by the title.
237   While most of the existing datasets (e.g., SNLI (Bowman et al., 2015), MNLI (Williams et al., 2017))
238   address the RTE task at sentence-level, logical connections can go beyond consecutive sentences. As
239   such, we rely on DeBERTa pretrained on eight RTE datasets, including DocNLI (Yin et al., 2021), a
240   dataset spanning various lengths for both premises and hypotheses. For performing the experiments,
241   we relied on the HuggingFace transformers library (Wolf et al., 2020).10 To test our hypothesis, we
242   trained multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) with a single hidden layer on these entailment progressions
243
      to classify texts within a dataset as either model-generated or human-authored. It is important to
      note that when assembling the training and testing datasets for the MLP models, we only selected
244
      entailment progressions that met the same conditions (e.g., Sentence-Sentence entailment progres-
245
      sions with a context window size of 2 sentences).11 Since the entailment progressions vary in length
246   and are sequential, we leveraged a Time Series MLP implementation available through tslearn,12 a
247   Python package dedicated to time series modelling and machine learning.

342   5    C ONCLUSION
343
344   In this paper, we introduce entailment progressions, a novel representation of the underlying log-
345   ical structures of textual narratives for identifying human and model authorship. We also present
346   EP4MGT, a dataset specifically designed to evaluate the logical approaches of humans and those
347   produced by a suite of state-of-the-art LLMs, highlighting new avenues for exploring the properties
348   and scope of entailment progressions as a latent descriptor of authorship.
349   Given that entailment progressions can be generated from any multi-sentence text, their potential
350   applications could extend to the broader area of text attribution, thus providing insights in their utility
351   as identifiers of authorship (be it human or model-based). This would also position our framework
352   alongside more traditional lexical, syntactic, and semantic descriptors of style.
353   In future work, we plan on examining the effectiveness of entailment progressions in other exper-
354   imental settings, across different languages, tasks, and genres. Although through our framework
355   we have successfully detected MGTs in several English corpora with fixed narrative structure (i.e.,
356   personal claims, news articles), testing entailment progressions on datasets in languages with dif-
357   ferent underlying logical conventions or genres without any explicit logical constraints could reveal
358   broader applicability.
