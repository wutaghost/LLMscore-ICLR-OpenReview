011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            This paper introduces the Convex Potential Mirror Langevin Algorithm (CPMLA),
014            a novel method designed to optimize sampling efficiency within Energy-Based
015            Models (EBMs). CPMLA employs mirror Langevin dynamics in conjunction
016
               with convex potential flow as a dynamic mirror map for sampling in EBMs. By
               leveraging this dynamic mirror map, CPMLA enables targeted geometric explo-
017
               ration on the data manifold, enhancing the convergence process towards the target
018
               distribution. Theoretical analysis proves that CPMLA achieves exponential con-
019            vergence with vanishing bias under relaxed log-concave conditions, supporting its
020            efficiency and effectiveness in adapting to complex data distributions. Experimental
021            results on established benchmarks like CIFAR-10, SVHN, and CelebA showcase
022            CPMLA’s enhanced sampling quality and inference efficiency compared to existing
023            techniques.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Energy-based generative models (EBMs) constitute a category of machine learning models employed
029   for data generation. In contrast to traditional generative models that directly model data distributions,
030   EBMs use an energy learning strategy that assigns low values to favorable samples and high values
031   to unfavorable ones (Xie et al., 2016; Nijkamp et al., 2019; Du & Mordatch, 2019). Known for
032   their simplicity and training stability, EBMs have found diverse applications ranging from 3D object
033
      recognition (Gustafsson et al., 2021) and analysis (Xie et al., 2018) to image segmentation (Kashyap
      & Gautam, 2017), super-resolution restoration (Zheng et al., 2021), machine translation (Tu et al.,
034
      2020), and protein folding (Tubiana et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2021).
035
036   EBMs encounter sampling challenges due to their dependence on computationally demanding
037   Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) techniques in high-dimensional spaces (Barbu et al., 2020;
038   Du & Mordatch, 2019; Kumar et al., 2019). The primary issue is the slow convergence that stems
039
      from the non-mixing phenomenon, i.e. the tendency to get trapped in local modes. These limitations
      make it difficult for MCMC-based approaches to efficiently approximate complex target distributions,
040
      especially when the data manifold is highly non-Euclidean (Zhang et al., 2020; Jiang, 2021).
041
042   Several recent methodologies have surfaced to address sampling inefficiencies within EBMs. While
043   some strategies concentrate on refining MCMC initialization (Hinton, 2002; Du & Mordatch, 2019),
044   others explore gradient approximation techniques (Kim & Bengio, 2016; Kumar et al., 2019). Despite
045
      these advancements, persistent challenges such as non-mixing issues remain unresolved (Xie et al.,
      2022b). Mirror Langevin algorithms have recently emerged as an alternative approach to alter
046
      sampling geometry via a fixed mirror map, i.e. a predefined function. Prior work (Ahn & Chewi,
047
      2021; Li et al., 2022) demonstrates that mirror Langevin algorithms, under certain assumptions,
048   exhibit vanishing bias, i.e. the bias approaches zero as the step size h → 0. This property ensures that
049   the samples reliably converge to the target distribution and improves sampling accuracy. Moreover,
050   mirror Langevin algorithms produce samples with a mixing time that can be rendered independent
051   of the condition number of the domain, enabling fast convergence (Hsieh et al., 2018; Zhang et al.,
052   2020). However, the limitation of the fixed mirror map in efficiently capturing the data manifold has
053   inhibited the exploration of this alternative sampling method for addressing large-scale problems
      especially those associated with deep neural networks.

      This paper introduces Convex Potential Mirror Langevin Algorithm (CPMLA), a novel approach for
055   sampling EBMs with enhanced efficiency. In contrast to conventional mirror Langevin algorithms
056   that function within Euclidean space, CPMLA facilitates an efficient and effective sampling process
057   via a dynamic mirror map, a parameterized function being optimized during the training process.
058   Inspired by Brenier’s theorem (Santambrogio, 2015), the dynamic mirror map is modeled via a
059   parameterized convex potential function, whose gradient w.r.t. the input corresponds to the unique
060   solution for the Monge problem between noise distribution and data distribution. While traditional
061   mirror Langevin algorithms are commonly utilized for constrained sampling tasks with fixed mirror
062   maps, CPMLA utilizes a dynamic mirror map to capture the intrinsic geometric complexities of the
063
      data manifold. This capability allows for adaptive adjustments in the sampling direction based on the
      data distribution, thereby augmenting the algorithm’s overall effectiveness.
064
065   We employ a cooperative learning strategy to optimize our model. Initially, CPMLA learns the
066   dynamic mirror map via optimizing a convex potential flow (CP-Flow) (Huang et al., 2020). Sup-
067   ported by Brenier’s theorem (Santambrogio, 2015), CP-Flow, derived from the gradient of a convex
068   energy function (Amos et al., 2017), can be optimized to capture the underlying data distribution.
069
      Subsequently, the EBM is trained by optimizing the energy difference between real data samples
      and those synthesized via CPMLA. Then, the synthesized samples by EBM are used to augment the
070
      training of the dynamic mirror map, i.e. CP-Flow. This iterative process fosters ongoing refinement
071
      and enhancement of the dynamic mirror map and the EBM.
072
073   We present a theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the proposed CPMLA. Following
074   the recent study (Jiang,
                               2021), we theoretically demonstrate our method can achieve a fast convergence
075   (Ω̃ M γ 2 d/β 2 δ mixing time) under relaxed log-concavity assumptions. In particular, we specialize
076   our proof for the dynamic mirror map modeled with deep neural networks, which broadens its
077
      applicability to a wider range of target distributions in various machine learning tasks. Additionally,
      we streamline the proof by taking advantage of the bounded gradients property exhibited by the
078
      mirror map. To the best of our knowledge, this work is the first to analyze mirror Langevin algorithms
079
      within the framework of deep neural networks, resulting in exponential convergence with vanishing
080   bias (Theorem 5.5).
081
082   We conducted a thorough evaluation of CPMLA across several standard benchmark datasets, including
083
      CIFAR-10, SVHN, and CelebA. The results demonstrate that CPMLA not only achieves superior
      sampling quality but also exhibits enhanced inference efficiency compared to existing cooperative
084
      algorithms. Specifically, CPMLA attained a FID score that is 27% of Flow+EBM’s (Gao et al., 2019;
085
      Nijkamp et al., 2020), indicating a substantial improvement in visual quality. Additionally, CPMLA
086   not only can achieve a FID score comparable to CoopFlow (Xie et al., 2022b) with fewer inference
087   iterations, but also operates with only 0.9% of the parameter count, underscoring its remarkable
088   efficiency in both sampling and inference. CPMLA has also demonstrated outstanding performance
089   in specialized tasks such as image reconstruction and inpainting, further emphasizing its effectiveness
090   in tackling complex image processing challenges.
091
      Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
092
           • We propose a novel Convex Potential Mirror Langevin Algorithm (CPMLA) for efficient
093           sampling of EBMs. The efficiency comes from the modification of the sampling geometry
094           through a dynamic mirror map modeled with a deep neural network.
095        • We provide a theoretical analysis of the convergence of the proposed CPMLA, focusing
096           specifically on deep neural networks under relaxed assumptions.
097        • We evaluate the efficacy of our proposed algorithm through comprehensive experimental
098           analyses on various benchmark datasets, including CIFAR-10, SVHN, and CelebA. Our
099           experiments demonstrate that our CPMLA achieves superior sampling efficiency compared
100           to existing methods. Furthermore, it surpasses alternative approaches in terms of sample
101           quality and the fidelity of image reconstruction and inpainting.

105   Langevin sampling Numerous discretizations of Langevin dynamics within Euclidean geometry
106   have been extensively studied in the literature, with non-asymptotic error bounds derived for various
107   sampling error metrics, such as Kullback-Leibler divergence, Total Variation, and Wasserstein
      distance. The most extensively studied scenarios include cases where the target distribution is m-


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      strongly log-concave (Durmus & Moulines, 2016a;b; Cheng et al., 2017; Dalalyan & Karagulyan,
109   2019; Mou et al., 2019) and those where it is relaxed log-concave (Wibisono, 2019; Ma et al., 2021;
110   Mou et al., 2022). Recently, mirror Langevin dynamics has garnered attention in the field of non-
111   Euclidean geometry sampling, due to its superior convergence properties in constrained optimization
112   problems. Originally introduced by Hsieh et al. (2018) as a measure transformation of the classical
113   Langevin dynamics, the convergence of mirror Langevin dynamics under relaxed log-concavity
114   was soon investigated in Zhang et al. (2020), where the authors demonstrated convergence to a
115   Wasserstein ball with non-vanishing bias. In Ahn & Chewi (2021), under similar relaxed log-concave
116   conditions, it was shown that the bias decreases to zero as the step size diminishes. Closely related to
117
      our work is Chewi et al. (2020), where the authors studied the convergence of the discretized process
      using functional inequalities similar to those we examine, though the practical applications were left
118
      for future exploration.
119
120   Cooperative learning The cooperative learning concept, first introduced in Xie et al. (2022a),
121   involves the joint maximum likelihood training of a ConvNet-EBM (Xie et al., 2016) and a top-down
122   generator (Han et al., 2022). In a similar vein, Xie et al. (2021) replaced the generator in the original
123
      CoopNets with a variational autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma & Welling, 2013) to improve inference
      efficiency. Our learning algorithm draws inspiration from the recent Coopflow approach (Xie et al.,
124
      2022b), which focuses on the collaborative training of a Langevin flow and a normalizing flow to
125
      elevate the quality of initial samples used in Langevin samplers. In contrast, our CPMLA excels
126   in two key aspects: firstly, our sampling strategy delves into the data manifold by leveraging a
127   convex potential; secondly, underpinned by mathematical principles, our CPMLA demonstrates
128   faster convergence in scenarios of relaxed log-concave sampling, especially tailored for deep neural
129   networks.

171   shown below.

249
      Our CPMLA employs MLAAFD as the algorithmic core and utilizes the aforementioned CP-Flow
250
      as a dynamic mirror map. CP-Flow adeptly transforms the geometry of data distribution with the
251
      underlying metric dictated by the Hessian of the convex potential. Consequently, the cooperation
252   between MLAAFD and CP-Flow guides sampling on the data manifold, rendering it well-suited for
253   fast convergence of the samples.
254

507   7     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE WORKS
508
509   In our experiments, estimating the Hessian can introduce bias to the optimal point and incur additional
510   computational cost. However, compared to the exact evaluation of the inverse Hessian, this is a
511   trade-off we must make. While our experimental results demonstrate effectiveness for diverse
512   sampling tasks, the assumption of the log-Sobolev inequality (Assumption 5.1) is rather general,
513   as we cannot ensure that the target distributions of different sampling tasks satisfy this assumption,
514   particularly in EBMs where the target distribution is highly complex. We must point out that this
515   paper provides improvement in the quality of generative models could be used to generate deepfakes
516   for disinformation. For future work, we plan to explore the deeper connection between sampling
517
      and optimization. For instance, are various optimization tricks, such as Trust-Region, RMSProp
      and Adam, justified for either accelerating sampling or correcting for bias? Additionally, would
518
      higher-order discretization schemes, such as Runge-Kutta methods, yield better convergence rates?
519
      We aim to investigate these questions and further advance the field of sampling and optimization.

      A    O PTIMAL T RANSPORT
757
758
      In recent years, there has been increasing interest in applying optimal transport theory to generative
759   modeling, which considers the training process as a task of minimizing the distance between two
760   probability distributions. More specifically, the objective is to transform a random distribution into
761   a target distribution that closely approximates the underlying data distribution, with the distance
762   between these two distributions often quantified using the Wasserstein distance in the context of
763   optimal transport. The Wasserstein p-distance between two probability measures µ and ν on a metric
764   space M with finite p-moments is
765                                                                           1/p
766                               Wp (µ, ν) =       inf E(x,y)∼γ d(x, y)p                               (14)
                                                  γ∈Γ(µ,ν)
767
768   where Γ(µ, ν) is the set of all couplings of µ and ν. Jordan et al. (1998) shows that the Langevin
769   dynamics manifests as the gradient flow of the Kullback-Leibler divergence within the probability
770   measure space, characterized by the Wasserstein metric, as elucidated through the Fokker-Planck
771   equation. This observation establishes a more substantial linkage between the realms of sampling
772
      and optimization; see also by the paper Otto & Villani (2000).
773
774   B    M IRROR L ANGEVIN A LGORITHM FOR C ONSTRAINED S AMPLING
775
776   The mirror Langevin algorithm is a powerful technique for sampling from complex distributions,
777   particularly those with constraints or intricate geometries. It leverages the concept of mirror maps,
778   which can adapt to the geometric structure of the target distribution, enabling efficient and accurate
779   sampling. One notable application of the mirror Langevin algorithm is constrained sampling, where
780
      the goal is to draw samples from a population while adhering to specific conditions or constraints.
781   Constrained sampling involves drawing a set of samples S from a population U = u1 , u2 , ..., un
782   while satisfying predefined constraint conditions expressed as inequalities. The general form of these
783   constraints can be written as:
784                                     C(x) : gi (x) ≤ 0,    i = 1, 2, ..., m                             (15)
785
      Here, gi (x) represents the constraint functions, and the goal is to ensure that gi (x) ≤ 0 for all i.
786
787   The mirror Langevin algorithm addresses constrained sampling by leveraging mirror maps that can
788   adapt to the geometry of the constraints. Specifically, we employ CP-Flow as our dynamic mirror
789   map, which utilizes Implicit Convex Neural Networks (ICNNs) to approximate arbitrary convex
790   functions effectively. Given that the derivative of a convex function is monotonic, we can ensure that
791
      the convergence of potential functions implies the convergence of the associated gradient fields, as
      stated in the following theorem:
792
793   Theorem B.1 (Optimality (Theorem 4 in Huang et al. (2020))). Let F be the Brenier potential of
794
      X ∼ µ and Y ∼ ν, and let Gn be a convergent sequence of differentiable, convex potentials, such
      that ∇Gn ◦ X → Y in distribution. Then, ∇Gn converges almost surely to ∇F .
795
796   In our context, where µ represents the unconstrained space and ν serves as the convex constraint,
797   Theorem B.1 guarantees the existence of a convex potential whose derivative maps µ to ν. By
798   leveraging the expressive nature of ICNNs through CP-Flow, we can adapt to the intrinsic geometry
799   of the constraints, resulting in accelerated convergence during constrained sampling. The mirror
800   Langevin algorithm’s ability to handle complex constraints makes it a valuable tool for various
801   applications beyond constrained sampling, such as sampling from EBMs, Bayesian inference, and
802   more (Ahn & Chewi, 2021).
