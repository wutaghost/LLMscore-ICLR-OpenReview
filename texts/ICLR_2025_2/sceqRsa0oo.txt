009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Inference latency and trustworthiness of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) are the
012            bottlenecks in deploying them in critical applications like autonomous driving.
013            Early Exit (EE) DDNs overcome the latency issues by allowing samples to exit
014            from intermediary layers if they attain ‘high’ confidence scores on the predicted
015            class. However, the DNNs are known to exhibit overconfidence, which can lead
016            to many samples exiting early and render EE strategies untrustworthy. We use
017            Selective Prediction (SP) to overcome this issue by checking the ‘hardness’ of
018            the samples rather than just relying on the confidence score alone. We propose
019            SPEED, a novel approach that uses Deferral Classifiers (DCs) at each layer to
020
               check the hardness of samples before performing EEs. The DCs at each layer
               identify if a sample is hard and either differ its inference to the next layer or
021
               directly send it to an expert. Early detection of hard samples and using an
022
               expert for inference prevents the wastage of computational resources and im-
023            proves trust. We also investigate the generalization capability of DCs trained
024            on one domain when applied to other domains where target domain data is not
025            readily available. We observe that EE aided with SP improves both accuracy
026            and latency. Our method minimizes the risk by 50% with a speedup of 2.05×
027            as compared to the final layer. The anonymized source code is available at
028            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/SPEED-35DC/README.md

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The demand for Artificial Intelligence (AI) systems to automate decision-making is growing. How-
034
      ever, their high memory, computational resource requirements, and the associated inference latency
035
      are bottlenecks in the deployment. Also, in socially sensitive or mission-critical machine learn-
036   ing applications, their trustworthiness is a concern (Kaur et al., 2022). For instance, the ability to
037   know what you do not know and not get overconfident about it is essential. However, the issue of
038   overconfidence in DNNs makes them vulnerable to wrong decisions and needs to be addressed.
039
      To address the first issue, various adaptive inference methods have been developed, including Early
040
      Exit DNNs (EEDNNs) (Kaya et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2020). EEDNNs use classifiers attached to
041
      the intermediary layers to perform adaptive inference, allowing samples attaining good confidence
042   scores on the predicted class to exit at shallow layers with a label, thus reducing the average inference
043   latency. To address the second issue, Selective Prediction (SP) (Chow, 1970) can be used, which
044   allows a model to abstain from predictions when uncertain about its hardness. SP helps models
045   better understand what they may not know, preventing them from being wrongly overconfident.
046   We leverage SP to detect if a sample is hard for the shallow layers and decide either to defer the
047   prediction to deeper layers or send it to an expert. This increases the trust in the model as incorrect
048   decisions with high confidence are avoided.
049   The use of traditional SP methods Pugnana et al. (2024) for EEDNNs is challenging due to two
050   major issues: 1) Existing methods use the confidence score output by the model to decide whether
051   to abstain or not on each sample. However, the overconfidence issue hinders the purpose of the SP
052   methods in effectively abstaining from the hard samples. This is illustrated in Figure 2a for the SST-
053   2 dataset where we plot the average confidence of the samples in the true class across the layers using
      the trained EEDNN backbone. The experiment shows that multiple samples gain fake confidence

      are applied directly, they can get fooled by the model’s fake confidence in these intermediary layers.
073
074   2) Existing methods make the decision to abstain after the entire model has processed the sample,
075   which negates the speed benefits of EEDNNs. For example, in autonomous driving, if a decision to
076
      hand control over to a human driver is delayed until the entire model has been computed, critical time
      is lost. Thus, a more efficient approach is required to identify hard samples earlier in the process.
077
078   To overcome the issues, SP methods need to distinguish whether the model’s confidence in a sample
079   can be ‘fake’ or ‘true’ at the shallow layers and refer it to an expert instead of passing through higher
080   layers. If a sample attains high fake confidence at the shallow layer, it is unlikely that the model’s
081   predictions would be better at deeper layers (see Appendix A.3). Thus, early referral of samples to
082
      experts saves crucial time and computation resources.
083   To address these issues, we propose a new framework named Selective Prediction for Early Exit
084   DNNs (SPEED). This framework introduces deferral classifiers (DCs) at each layer of an EEDNN
085   to detect if a sample is hard and the model can get overconfident, thus helping EEDNNs maintain
086   the speed advantage while avoiding making inferences on samples that it does not understand. To
087   train the DCs, we use samples that are labeled as ‘easy’ or ‘hard’ based on the average confidence
088
      they attain across the layer of EEDNN (see Subsection 3.4 for more details).
089   During inference, at each layer, the attached DC classifier detects if a sample is easy or hard. If hard,
090   it exits the sample from DNN and send it to an expert. If found easy, it is sent to the EE classifier
091   attached to the same layer. If the EE classifier completes the inference, the sample exits with a label,
092   else it is sent to the next layer where the process repeats. Figure 1 shows the flowchart of SPEED.
093   Our method is also robust to the domain changes of the input samples. The reason for its better per-
094   formance across diverse domains with similar tasks is the domain shifts do not change the semantic
095   structure of the sentence. For instance, consider an IMDB review (source domain): “The movie
096   would have been good if there was a better climax!,” which has negative sentiment, but the model
097   can say it is positive with high confidence due to the occurrence of the word ‘good.’ A similar review
098   from an hotel review “The place would have been good if the food quality was better.” also achieves
099   fake confidence. Our framework, once trained on the source domain, can also handle similar fake
100
      confidence issues on the target domain. This reduces the need for another round of training for the
      same task but with a domain shift. In summary, our main contributions can are as follows:
101
102          • We introduce a new framework, SPEED, that uses Deferral Classifiers (DCs) at each layer
103            of EEDNNs to make the inference fast and efficient.
104
             • We develop a new strategy to train DCs. Each training sample is labeled as easy and hard
105
               utilizing its training dynamics across the layers of DNN.
106
107          • Our method generalizes well to various domains with minimal loss in performance making
               it robust to domain shifts of incoming samples during inference.

      2     R ELATED WORKS
109
110
      Early Exit (EE) techniques allow models to make early predictions based on input complexity,
111   have been widely applied across various domains, including image classification Teerapittayanon
112   et al. (2016); Huang et al. (2017); Kaya et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2019b); Wołczyk et al. (2021),
113   natural language processing (NLP) Xin et al. (2020); Zhu (2021); Zhou et al. (2020); Bajpai &
114   Hanawal (2024), and image captioning Fei et al. (2022); Tang et al. (2023). These methods are
115   known for their strong generalization capabilities, as demonstrated by models like CeeBERT (Bajpai
116   & Hanawal, 2024). A key differentiator among existing approaches lies in the choice of confidence
117   measures, such as prediction consistency Zhou et al. (2020), ensemble methods Sun et al. (2021),
118   and output entropy Liu et al. (2021). Additionally, different training strategies, including separate
119
      training Xin et al. (2020) and joint training Zhou et al. (2020), have been explored to enhance the
      efficiency of EE methods. EE techniques have also been applied in practical settings like distributed
120
      inference Bajpai et al. (2024), where models are deployed across devices with varying computational
121
      capacities, such as mobile, edge, and cloud environments.
122
123   Selective Prediction (SP), also referred to as selective classification or the reject option, has been
124   extensively explored across various domains. Chow (1970) introduced a cost-based rejection model,
125
      analyzing the trade-off between errors and rejections. This concept has also been widely studied in
      the context of Support Vector Machines (SVMs) Brinkrolf & Hammer (2018); Hendrickx et al.
126
      (2024), nearest neighbors Hellman (1970), and boosting Cortes et al. (2016), demonstrating its ver-
127
      satility across different classification paradigms. In the domain of neural networks, LeCun et al.
128   (1989) introduced a rejection strategy based on output logits, comparing the highest and second-
129   highest activated logits to guide rejection decisions. Geifman & El-Yaniv (2017) proposed a selec-
130   tive classification technique to achieve a target risk with a specified confidence-rate function, laying
131   the groundwork for risk-controlled predictions.
132
      Recent advancements in SP have focused on architectural innovations Cortes et al. (2024) Geifman
133
      & El-Yaniv (2019). Other approaches, such as Deep Gamblers Liu et al. (2019) and Self-Adaptive
134   Training Huang et al. (2020), incorporate an additional class for abstention. Feng et al. (2022) crit-
135   ically examined the selection mechanisms of these models and highlighted their limitations. Their
136   findings suggest that the improved performance of these models is largely due to the optimization
137   process leading to a more generalizable model, which in turn enhances SP performance.
138
      Furthermore, several works have proposed deferral-based approaches, where the decision to predict
139
      or defer is determined by a cost function. These methods either assign prediction costs equal to
140   model loss or defer predictions at a user-defined cost Okati et al. (2021); Mozannar et al. (2023);
141   Verma et al. (2023).
142
143
      Our method differs from existing approaches in three main ways: (1) To the best of our knowledge,
      we are the first to apply SP to EEDNNs. (2) We accelerate the deferral process by eliminating the
144
      need for every sample to pass through the entire backbone. (3) Our method of deferral decision is
145
      more accurate due to the integration of multiple DCs. (4) The separate training of these DCs does
146   not affect the optimality of the backbone and enables better generalization across different domains,
147   making our approach more robust to minor domain shifts during inference.

      3.6   I NFERENCE
304
      For a test input x ∼ D̃ where D̃ = D when the source and target domain are the same, and for the
305   given thresholds α and β, let the sample is processed till the ith layer. At the ith layer, its Si value
306   is calculated using DC. If Si ≥ β, the sample is deferred and sent to an expert. If Si < β, then the
307   sample is referred to EC in the same layer, and its confidence score Ci is checked. If Ci ≥ α, then
308   the sample exits with a label completing the inference, else the sample is taken into the next layer,
309   and the process continues till the sample reaches the last layer.
310
      If the sample reaches the final layer, then the decision to infer or defer depends only on the confi-
311   dence score of EC. If Ci ≥ α, then the sample is assigned a label. Otherwise, it defers the sample
312   irrespective of the confidence of the DC. Hence, at every layer (except the final layer) of the back-
313   bone, every sample is either inferred, deferred, or passed on to the next layer.

355
356
      We have three parts in our experimental setup that are as follows:
357   i) Training the EE backbone on the source dataset: Initially, we train the backbone on the source
358   dataset. We add a linear output layer after each intermediate layer of the BERT/RoBERTa model
359   whose task is to map the hidden representation to class probabilities. We run the model for 5 epochs.
360   We perform a grid search over batch size of {8, 16, 32} and learning rates of {1e-5, 2e-5, 3e-5, 4e-
361   5 5e-5} with Adam Kingma & Ba (2014) optimizer. We apply an early stopping mechanism and
362
      select the model with the best performance on the development set. The experiments are conducted
      on NVIDIA RTX 2070 GPU with an average runtime of ∼ 3 hours and a maximum run time of
363
      ∼ 10 hours for the MNLI dataset.
364
365   ii) Creating the dataset for DC training: After training the EE backbone, we freeze the parameters
366   of the backbone and then calculate the average confidence and variance of confidence across all the
367   exit points. The dataset is then sorted in ascending order of confidence. The top-33% samples are
368
      provided the hard label and the remaining samples are treated as the easy ones. After creating the
      dataset, we attach the DCs i.e., a single linear layer mapping the hidden representations to hardness
369
      score. A hard sample will have a score closer to 1 and an easier sample will have a score close to 0.
370
371   The training for DCs is performed for additional 3 epochs. After training the DCs, the model can
372   classify or defer early at each layer. For training the hyperparameters α we perform a grid search
373   over the set {0.75, 0.8, 0.85, 0.9, 0.95} and for β, we choose the set as {0.55, 0.6, 0.65, 0.7, 0.75}.
374
      The thresholds could be chosen based on the user requirements of risk and coverage. We set the
      thresholds as the ones that show similar trade-offs for comparison with existing baselines. We also
375
      plot the risk-coverage trade-off and discuss in the Appendix A.1 (see Figure 3a, 3b).
376
377   iii) Inference: We perform the inference on the test split of the source dataset and to show the
      generalization capabilities of our model, we also perform inference on a target domain dataset.

      5    C ONCLUSION
477
478
      In this work, we introduced SPEED, a selective prediction tailored to improve inference latency and
      overconfidence issues in EEDNNs. We introduce new classifiers named deferral classifiers (DCs) to
479
      identify if a sample is hard for a given layer and if the model can get overconfident about its false
480
      class. Thus, it helps the model to understand what it does not know, which is crucial to prevent the
481   model from making a false prediction with high confidence! We develop a method to train the DCs
482   to effectively identify if a sample is hard or easy to infer at a given layer.
483
484
      Our experiments demonstrate that SPEED consistently outperforms existing baselines in both risk
      reduction and speedup. Further, SPEED generalizes well across different domains, minimizing the
485
      need for retraining and making it robust to domain shifts.
