009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
                 As LLMs continuously evolve, there is an urgent need for a reliable evaluation
012              method that delivers trustworthy results promptly. Currently, static benchmarks
013              suffer from inflexibility and unreliability, leading users to prefer human voting plat-
014              forms like Chatbot Arena. However, human evaluations require significant manual
015              effort. To address this, we propose the Auto-Arena, an innovative framework
016              that automates the entire evaluation process using LLM-powered agents. Firstly,
017              an LLM examiner generates questions. Then, two LLM candidates engage in a
018              multi-round peer battle based on individual questions, aiming at revealing their
019              true performance differences. Finally, a committee of LLM judges collaboratively
020
                 discusses and decides the winner, reducing bias and enhancing fairness. During the
                 peer battles, we observe intriguing scenarios where the LLM candidates display
021
                 competitive behaviors and even learn from the opponents. In our extensive experi-
022
                 ments involving 15 recent LLMs, Auto-Arena shows a 92.14% correlation with
023              human preferences, surpassing all previous expert-annotated benchmarks without
024              any manual efforts. As a result, Auto-Arena offers a promising alternative to
025              current human evaluation platforms for evaluating LLMs automatically. 1

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Since ChatGPT and GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024) gained popularity, Large Language Models (LLMs)
031   have risen to the forefront of technological innovation, capturing broad industry and social inter-
032   ests (Wu et al., 2023b). This enthusiasm has spurred numerous organizations to release their own
033   LLMs (Touvron et al., 2023; Team et al., 2024b). However, the rapid pace at which these models are
034
      released and updated poses a significant challenge for users attempting to understand their capabilities
      and monitor their evolution. Consequently, there has been a pressing demand for comprehensively
035
      evaluating LLMs recently (Chang et al., 2024a).
036
037   The most popular existing method is automatic evaluation with static datasets. Among these, static
038   datasets with predefined metrics, such as GSM8k (Cobbe et al., 2021) and MMLU (Hendrycks et al.,
039   2021a), are constructed with aspect-specific input-output pairs, such as human exam-type questions
040
      and their corresponding answers. Given the questions, the LLM-produced answers are compared to
      ground-truth answers using metrics such as accuracy. This approach could suffer from inflexibility,
041
      contamination, and high human annotation costs. Firstly, the closed-form ground-truth answers limit
042
      their utility in assessing models’ performances on general or open-ended questions, which are the
043   main use cases of LLMs. As the questions are static, they also risk contamination (Ravaut et al.,
044   2024), where models may have been inadvertently exposed to elements of the test datasets during
045   training, thereby skewing the evaluation results. The manual dataset construction also incurs high
046   costs, creating barriers for extending to other domains or languages. As an alternative, static datasets
047   with model-based evaluation, such as MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023) and AlpacaEval (Dubois et al.,
048   2024a), evaluates LLMs on open-ended generations. These methods typically ask two models to
049   generate responses to the same open-ended question and then employ a strong judge model (e.g.,
050   GPT-4) to choose the better response. However, the static question sets still bear contamination
051
      risks. Additionally, the assumption of the existence of a strong judge model makes the evaluation
      framework less generalizable and introduces model-specific bias.

067   Aside from automated evaluations, human assessment, although requiring significant manual efforts,
068
      remains the gold standard for users. A notable example is Chatbot Arena (Zheng et al., 2023), a
      crowdsourcing platform that gathers anonymous votes on LLM performances and calculates Elo
069
      scores (Elo & Sloan, 1978) to rank these models. The resulting leaderboard2 is widely considered as
070
      a trustworthy indicator of LLMs’ general capabilities. However, a reliable model evaluation on this
071   platform must be supported by a large number of human votes, which requires considerable time and
072   effort. Consequently, when newly developed models enter the scene, they often struggle to quickly
073   amass a large number of votes. Moreover, this strong reliance on human votes limits its application in
074   various scenarios. For example, the performance of non-English languages is difficult to estimate, as
075   most queries on the platform are in English. Moreover, the queries are mostly one-round and simple.
076   The completely open participation may also result in uneven evaluation quality.
077   To enable the evaluation of LLMs that is both automated and reliable while aligning with human
078   preferences, we introduce Auto-Arena, a framework that automates the entire LLM evaluation
079   process with LLM-powered agents. The framework consists of three stages: Firstly, an LLM examiner
080   agent is tasked with generating questions, mimicking real-life users posting queries. Secondly, two
081   LLM candidates interact with each other and engage in a multi-round peer battle by answering the
082   seed question individually, criticizing the opponent’s weaknesses, and raising targeted follow-up
083   queries to challenge the opponent further. During the multi-round battle process, the LLM’s true
084   capabilities are drawn out and performance gaps become more visible. Lastly, a committee of LLM
085
      judges collectively discusses and evaluates the ability of the two candidates, mimicking the human
      voting process. As shown in Table 1, Auto-Arena has several key advantages compared to previous
086
      evaluation methods: First and foremost, instead of the simple and one-round question-answering
087
      scheme, Auto-Arena introduces a dynamic multi-round peer battle, which displays deeper abilities
088   of LLMs, such as reasoning, interacting, and strategizing. The dynamic nature of peer battles also
089   reduces contamination risks. Secondly, by expanding a single LLM judge into a committee of LLM
090   judges, Auto-Arena alleviates potential model-specific evaluation bias. Finally, since the process
091   of generating questions and judgments is fully automated in an end-to-end way, Auto-Arena can
092   provide timely evaluations for new models and can easily extend to various domains and languages.
093
      To verify the reliability and alignment of the evaluation framework, we run an extensive experiment
094   with 15 LLMs. Compared to static and model-based benchmarks, Auto-Arena results in the
095   state-of-the-art alignment by achieving a 92.14% Spearman correlation with human preferences,
096   surpassing all previous benchmarks. Although no manual efforts is involved, the high alignment with
097   human preferences could originate from the human-like evaluation process, which is simulated using
098   LLM agents. The extensive ablation experiments also demonstrate the reliability of the framework:
099   Before and after peer battles, the Spearman correlation with human preferences increases by 5%,
100   verifying our hypothesis that the peer battles can better display performance gaps. Before and after
101   committee discussions, committee agreement increases by 11%, showing human-level agreement and
102
      verifying the effectiveness of the committee discussion mechanism. By studying the peer battles, we
      also discover intriguing LLM agent behaviors such as competitive and self-improvement actions. As
103
      the entire process is automatic, the evaluation can be easily adapted to other languages or domains by
104
      altering the prompts. We provide Chinese as a case study for extending to other languages.

136   2     T HE AUTO -A RENA F RAMEWORK
137
138   As illustrated in Figure 1, the Auto-Arena framework consists of three stages: Question Generation,
139   Multi-round Peer Battles, and Committee Discussions. These three stages are run sequentially and
140   fully simulated with LLM-powered agents. All prompts are included in Appendix A.
141
142   2.1   Q UESTION G ENERATION
143
144   For debate questions, as using a static dataset could incur data contamination concerns and result in
145   unfair evaluations, we ask an LLM examiner agent to dynamically generate questions. The examiner
146
      agent could be any capable LLM. Similar to MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023), the generated questions
      cover 8 common categories in real-life conversations: writing, roleplay, extraction, reasoning, math,
147
      coding, STEM knowledge, and humanities/social science knowledge. The examiner is provided with
148
      a sample question and encouraged to generate diverse and difficult questions to ensure the depth and
149   width of the evaluated debates. Examples of the generated questions are shown in Appendix B.
150
151   Specifically, as the examiner agent will also participate in the following debates, we try to alleviate
152
      self-enhancement bias with two designs: 1. We do not disclose to the examiner that it will participate
      in this tournament. 2. Previous methods (Bai et al., 2024) could incur self-enhancement bias as they
153
      ask the examiner agents to only devise questions that they are confident about. In comparison, we
154
      do not ask the examiner to only generate questions that it can solve. To further show that limited
155   self-enhancement bias is present, we include an ablation study in Appendix E.
156
157   2.2   P EER D EBATE
158
      After question generation, we conduct peer battles around these questions among the LLM candidates.
159
      In one peer battle, two LLM candidates (A and B) debate around the given question, point out the
160
      opponent’s weaknesses, and devise follow-up questions to further probe the opponent’s weaknesses.
161
      In the peer battle, each candidate LLM has four available types of actions:

179
180   The workflow of a peer battle takes the form of the Lincoln-Douglas debate format3 , the most
181   widely used one-on-one debate style in competitions such as those held by the National Speech and
182
      Debate Association. The peer battle consists of three rounds in which two candidate models alternate
      speaking. Both candidates can see the complete dialogue history. This process is depicted in Figure
183
      2. In the first round, model A RESPONDS to the examiner’s initial question; model B CRITICIZES
184
      the flaws in A’s response and RAISES a specific follow-up question; model A then RESPONDS to
185   B’s follow-up question. The second round follows the same format, with A and B switching roles.
186   In the third round, A and B cross-examine each other, starting with A CRITICIZING the loopholes
187   in B’s earlier responses and RAISING follow-up questions. After responding, model B CRITICIZES
188   A’s weaknesses and RAISES additional questions. Model A wraps up by RESPONDING once more.
189   Throughout this process, both A and B perform an equal number of actions to maintain fairness. To
190   minimize positional bias, the order of A and B is randomized at the start of each debate.
191   During the debate process, enhancement bias and contamination concerns are further reduced: The
192   process of candidates raising follow-up questions to each other essentially decentralizes the question-
193   generation process, reducing enhancement bias in the generated initial questions. Moreover, debating
194   ensures that candidates are evaluated not only on their response to the initial question, but also in
195   more comprehensive and deeper abilities, such as strategizing, criticizing the opponent, and drafting
196   questions. In other words, answering the initial question well does not necessarily win the whole
197   debate, which further reduce contamination concerns.
198   Depending on which turn it is, we provide an action guide to the candidate, specifying the objectives
199   and corresponding actions for this turn. Similar to human debate competitions, we time the candidates
200   by imposing a maximum length constraint, which is also specified in the prompts. Any responses
201   beyond the required length will be cut off. This design mitigates verbosity bias in LLM-as-a-
202   judge (Zheng et al., 2023), where LLM judges prefer longer and more verbose responses.
203
204   2.3   C OMMITTEE D ISCUSSIONS
205
206   After the peer battle takes place, a committee of LLM judges collectively determines the winner. The
207   committee is always selected as the five best LLMs according to the current ranking. To reduce bias,
208   we exclude the participants themselves and models from the same family as the participants from
209
      the committee. For example, GPT-4 will not serve as a judge in evaluating a debate participated by
      GPT-3.5. In the first round, the committee is initialized with MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021a) scores
210
      to approximate LLM performances. Each judge is individually asked to read the entire peer battle
211
      history, elaborate judgment reasons, and give a decision on whether A is better, or B is better, or if
212   there is a tie based on factors such as helpfulness, relevance, and accuracy.
213

216
      After the initial judgments are formed, the committee engages in a discussion. In a discussion
217   round, each judge reads the other judge’s verdicts in the previous rounds, elaborates its own thoughts
218   for judgments, and drafts a discussed verdict. During the process, the judge may decide to adjust
219   or maintain the previous judgments. Compared to the peer battles that exemplify multi-agent
220   competitions, this committee discussion component synthesizes a multi-agent collaboration scheme.
221   By enabling interactions among the judge agents and exchanges of different viewpoints, the discussion
222   allows the committee to form a collective intelligence. As a result, it improves the judgment quality,
223   boosts inter-judge agreement, and mitigates single-model bias. Finally, the winning candidate is
224   decided by majority voting of the discussed judgments.

      3.1    E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
229
230   Model Selection:       For the main experiment, we first select 9 best or latest models that are
231   representative of each popular model family on the top 30 list on the Chatbot Arena platform with
232   more than 10k votes each at the time of experiments: GPT-4-0409-Turbo, GPT-3.5-Turbo-0125,
233   Claude-3-Haiku, Qwen1.5-72B-Chat, Command-R+, Llama-2-70B-Chat, Mixtral-8x7b-Instruct-v0.1,
234   Yi-34B-Chat, and Deepseek-LLM-67B. To construct a leaderboard, we further add 6 models that
235   are newly released: GPT-4o-2024-05-13, Claude-3.5-Sonnet, Qwen2-72B-Instruct, Llama-3-70B,
236
      Gemma-2-27B, and Gemini-1.5-Flash. Appendix H provides a detailed list of the selected models.
237   Baselines:   For the baselines, we consider popular evaluation benchmarks, including fixed metrics
238   and model-based metrics. A comparison table is shown in Appendix I.
239
      1. Static datasets with fixed metrics: (1) OpenLLM Leaderboard (Beeching et al., 2023), a popular
240   benchmark for open-source models averaging performance metrics on 6 key benchmarks, covering a
241   large number of different evaluation tasks; (2) GPQA (Rein et al., 2023), a graduate-level google-proof
242   Q&A benchmark consisting of 448 domain-expert-written questions written in scientific subjects;
243   (3) MMLU (Massive Multitask Language Understanding) (Hendrycks et al., 2021a), an extensive
244   benchmark that covers 57 subjects and tests both world knowledge and problem-solving ability;
245
      2. Static datasets with model-based metrics: (1) MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023), a set of 80 multi-turn
246   questions. Model responses are graded by GPT-4; (2) Arena Hard (Li* et al., 2024), a benchmark
247   dataset with 1,000 challenging user queries collected on Chatbot Arena. Model responses are graded
248   by GPT-4-Turbo; (3) Length-Controlled AlpacaEval (Dubois et al., 2024a), a benchmark based on
249   AlpacaFarm evaluation set (Dubois et al., 2024b), which tests models’ abilities to follow general user
250   instructions. Models are evaluated by their win rates against GPT-4-Turbo, graded by GPT-4-Turbo.
251
      Setup:     Among the 9 participants, we conduct a swiss-style tournament: For n participants, instead
252   of pairing each participant with (n − 1) others, a swiss-tournament pairs each player with ⌈log2 (n)⌉
253   players of similar rankings without repeats. This design effectively reduces computational costs of
254   ranking n models from O(n2 ) to O(nlog2 (n)). A cost analysis is included in Appendix I.
255
      Each candidate pair engages in 40 peer battles, with 5 questions from each of the 8 task categories
256
      that are specified in Section 2.1. We provide studies showing that the generated questions can reduce
257
      contamination concerns in Appendix C and are generalizable to real-world scenarios in Appendix
258   D. As each battle consists of 3 rounds (each candidate speaks for 4 times), the competition scale is
259   approximately the same as MT-Bench (80 questions, each candidate speaks twice). In the tournament,
260   the rating scores are calculated with the Elo rating system (Bai et al., 2022; Boubdir et al., 2023),
261   which has become the standard practice in competitive games such as chess (Elo & Sloan, 1978).
262   Similar to the Chatbot Arena score calculation procedure (Chiang et al., 2024), we compute the
263   Bradley-Terry (BT) coefficients (Bradley & Terry, 1952) for better statistical estimation. Following
264   the Reference-Guided judge in Zheng et al. (2023), we ask the best-performing judge to give a
265   reference answer for evaluating logical-reasoning questions (math, coding, reasoning).
266   We initialize the Swiss tournament rankings according to MMLU scores, which is a static approx-
267   imation of model performances. At the end of each pairing, we re-calculate Elo scores of current
268   models. The committee is selected as the best 5 LLMs based on current Elo rankings at each round.
269   After forming initial judgments, the committee members engage in one round of discussion. The
      final result is decided by majority voting of the discussed judgments.

481   6    R ELATED W ORK
482
483   As LLMs evolve quickly, deriving trustworthy evaluations of their capabilities has become a challenge.
484   Current evaluation methods can be divided into automatic evaluations and manual evaluations, such
485   as Chatbot Arena (Chiang et al., 2024). We primarily focus on automatic evaluations as they deliver
      more timely feedback. Automatic evaluations mainly consist of static datasets with predefined metrics


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      and model-based metrics. Static datasets with predefined metrics, such as MMLU (Hendrycks et al.,
487   2021a), GPQA (Rein et al., 2023), and Open-LLM-Leaderboard (Beeching et al., 2023) consist
488   of expert-annotated question-answer pairs. Then, the models are evaluated based on performance
489   metrics such as accuracy. However, as they only evaluate closed-form answers, they are inflexible in
490   evaluating open-ended responses. Moreover, the static datasets may eventually become exposed to
491   the internet and could lead to contamination concerns (Ravaut et al., 2024).
492
      On the contrary, static datasets with model-based metrics offer a flexible, low-cost and fast evaluation
493   paradigm (Chang et al., 2024b). Studies have verified that LLMs can provide unbiased (Ning
494   et al., 2024; Chu et al., 2024), high-quality (Lin & Chen, 2023) metrics comparable to human
495   evaluations (Dubois et al., 2024a; Zheng et al., 2023). Among them, MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023)
496   and AlpacaEval (Dubois et al., 2024a) use LLM-as-a-judge to ask GPT-4 to compare model responses
497   to a static dataset of questions. The model’s judgments achieve over 80% agreement with human
498   preferences, proving the usability of using LLMs to evaluate response quality. Language-Model-as-
499   an-Examiner (Bai et al., 2024) asks an LM examiner to construct knowledge-intensive questions
500   within its memory, interact with the candidate in a series of follow-up queries, and rate the responses
501
      on dimensions including accuracy and factuality. KIEval (Yu et al., 2024) also incorporates an
      LLM-powered “interactor” role to examine deep comprehension of knowledge, which is shown to
502
      mitigate contamination issues on static datasets. However, such single-judge evaluations require the
503
      examiner to interact with each candidate parallelly, creating computational overheads and limiting
504   the scope of queries. They also suffer from single-model bias, including bias towards LLM-generated
505   summaries (Liu et al., 2023), inflated scores in multilingual evaluation (Hada et al., 2023), verbosity
506   bias (Dubois et al., 2024a), and difficulties when evaluating candidates with close performance (Shen
507   et al., 2023). Therefore, there have been studies on employing multi-agent evaluation to mitigate
508   single-model bias. For example, DRPE (Wu et al., 2023a) uses multi-roleplayer prompting to mimic
509   different roles with the same LLM and integrate outputs as votes for the final results. ChatEval (Chan
510   et al., 2023) simulates different personas with the same base model to engage in debates, reaching a
511   final evaluation result. PRD (Li et al., 2023a) allows two LLMs to discuss an evaluation and assigns
512
      higher voting weights to the LLM reviewers with stronger capabilities. Peer-review-in-LLMs (Ning
      et al., 2024) optimizes voting weights as a learnable parameter. They show that the multi-agent
513
      approach effectively mitigates single-model bias. This line of work is similar to our “LLM judge
514
      committee” component. However, they are still limited to static datasets and specific domains.
515
516   Outside the domain of LLM evaluations, some works study competitive behaviors in multi-agent
517   LLM systems, which is relevant to the peer battles in Auto-Arena. LM vs LM (Cohen et al.,
518
      2023) shows that LLM cross-examinations can effectively discover factual errors. Debate (Du et al.,
      2023) shows that multi-agent debate can improve factuality and reasoning. In MAD (Liang et al.,
519
      2023), LLM-debate can encourage divergent thinking, which helps tasks that require deep levels
520
      of contemplation. Khan et al. (2024) shows that even non-expert weak LLMs can supervise expert
521   LLMs if we allow the two LLM experts to engage in debates. Moreover, Zhao et al. (2023) and
522   Gu et al. (2024) show interesting case studies where LLMs are engaged in simulated competitive
523   environments and demonstrate human-like strategies.
524
525
526   7    C ONCLUSIONS
527
528
529   In this paper, we innovatively design a completely automatic evaluation framework: Auto-Arena.
530   By using LLM agents to generate questions, employing LLM candidates in peer battles, and evalu-
531
      ating responses using LLM committee discussions, Auto-Arena delivers timely and trustworthy
      evaluations and automates the evaluation process in an end-to-end way. In the extensive experi-
532
      ments, Auto-Arena achieves the highest correlation with human preferences, despite requiring
533
      zero human efforts. It is easily adaptable to other domains and resources, promoting the inclusiveness
534   of AI system evaluations. The peer battles also demonstrate several interesting LLM behaviors in
535   competitive environments, including attacking and learning from the opponents. Moreover, there are
536   still limitations to the current approach: The distribution of question domains is artificially designed,
537   which may deviate from real-life distributions. Currently, Auto-Arena focuses on 1-to-1 peer
538   battles, which limits its usage in multi-player scenarios. As shown in Chen et al. (2024), LLM-as-a-
539   judge can lead to biases such as Misinformation Oversight Bias, Gender Bias, Authority Bias, and
      Beauty Bias, which can cause Auto-Arena’s judgments to deviate from real human users.

       The design in the question-generation and peer-debate process ensures that contamination is mini-
1114   mized. Data contamination refers to the possibility of test instances showing up in pre-training or
1115   Supervised Fine-tuning data.
1116
1117
       Question-generation: As we generate the questions automatically, we reduce the risk of test
1118   instances being eventually exposed to the open web, which can happen in static datasets. Alleviation
1119   of data contamination is often shown to be an advantage of such dynamic and frequently updated
1120   evaluation frameworks (Li et al., 2023b).
1121
1122   Peer Debate: Peer debate ensures that we evaluate the entire debate instead of simple question-
1123   answers, which further reduces contamination. During debates, the models are evaluated on compre-
1124   hensive and deep abilities, such as planning the strategies, pointing out flaws of the opponents, and
1125   drafting further questions. Such interactive evaluation frameworks are shown to reduce contamina-
1126   tion (Yu et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2024).
1127
       Besides the design choices, we conduct a contamination analysis to compare the contamination
1128   percentage of Auto-Arena debate questions and test questions in popular benchmarks. Specifically,
1129   we use two types of contamination detection metrics:
1130
1131        1. The string match metric as in GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024), where a match is identified if
1132           any of three 50-character randomly sampled substrings from the evaluation data point (or
1133           the entire string if it is shorter than this) is a substring of the training set. If so, we mark the
               point as contaminated.


                                                          21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
             2. The sentence embedding similarity metric as in Platypus (Lee et al., 2024), where a question
1135            is deemed contaminated if it has a cosine similarity (using Sentence Transformer (Reimers
1136            & Gurevych, 2019) embeddings) greater than 80% against any training item. This detection
1137            method is more robust to rephrases, which ensures that we can detect cases where the LLMs
1138            are simply rephrasing existing questions on the web.
1139
1140   Although we do not have access to the training data, LLMs mostly use public web data for pre-
1141   training (Raffel et al., 2020; Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023). Therefore, we approximate
1142   it with the Bing search API: If verbatim test examples appear online, it likely indicates inclusion
1143
       or exposure to the training data. This procedure is also followed by Li et al. (2024) for detecting
       contamination.
1144

1146   As baselines, we use 3 popular evaluation benchmarks: MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021a), ARC

       detection method (mentioned above) against any of the 10 snippets, it is marked as contaminated.

       Table 12 shows a comparison between benchmark evaluation methods and Auto-Arena. Compared
1332   to previous methods, the main advantage of Auto-Arena is the zero need for human dataset
1333   construction or intervention and the freshness of queries. Another innovation compared to previous
1334   model-based systematic benchmarking procedures is using a committee of LLMs to discuss and
1335   vote for a final winner, which introduces diverse viewpoints. The most important innovation of
1336   Auto-Arena is the peer-battle mechanism, which asks LLM agents to compete and debate with
1337   each other. The resulting evaluation on the multi-turn debate then becomes more in-depth, interactive,
1338   and comprehensive.
1339   For the evaluation cost, the costs of Auto-Arena are on the same scale as other benchmarks: We
1340   note that the primary experiment among 9 models costs around $45 USD. Therefore, the estimated
1341   cost is $5 per model. As models on the ranking board increase, the costs of conducting debates should
1342   grow slowly in log scale, which comes from conducting nlog2 (n) pairings when adding 1 model
1343   to a ranking of (n − 1) models. The evaluation costs, however, shall remain the same as we use a
1344   committee of 5 LLMs at all times.
1345   To help better understand the computational cost breakdown for each component, we estimate the
1346   computational resources for each component based on input/output tokens in Table 13. For example,
1347   if all agents (candidates and judges) have costs and inference times that are on par with GPT-4o, the
1348   API costs would be USD 0.22 per evaluation question. Evaluating our set of 40 questions would
1349   cost USD 8.8. In the tournament, however, cheaper and non-proprietary models are engaged as well,
       which drives down the costs.
