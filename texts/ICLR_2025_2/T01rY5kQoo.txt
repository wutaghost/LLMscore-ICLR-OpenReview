009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive performances in
012            complex text generation tasks. However, the contribution of the input prompt to
013            the generated content still remains obscure to humans, underscoring the necessity
014            of understanding the causality between input and output pairs. Existing works for
015            providing prompt-specific explanation often confine model output to be classifi-
016            cation or next-word prediction. Few initial attempts aiming to explain the entire
017            language generation often treat input prompt texts independently, ignoring their
018
               combinatorial effects on the follow-up generation. In this study, we introduce a
               counterfactual explanation framework based on joint prompt attribution, JoPA,
019
               which aims to explain how a few prompt texts collaboratively influences the LLM’s
020
               complete generation. Particularly, we formulate the task of prompt attribution for
021            generation interpretation as a combinatorial optimization problem, and introduce a
022            probabilistic algorithm to search for the casual input combination in the discrete
023            space. We define and utilize multiple metrics to evaluate the produced explanations,
024            demonstrating both the faithfulness and efficiency of our framework.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028   Large Language Models (LLMs), such as GPT4 (Achiam et al., 2023), LLaMA (Touvron et al.,
029   2023) and Claude (Anthropic, 2024), have shown excellent performance in various natural language
030   generation tasks including question answering, document summarization, and many more. Despite
031   the great success of LLMs, we still have very limited understanding of the LLM generation behavior
032   – which parts in the input cause the model to generate a certain sequence. Unable to explain the
033
      causality between the input prompt and the output generation could cause failure in recognizing
      potential unintended consequences, such as harmful response (DAN, 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Zou et al.,
034
      2023; Zhu et al., 2023) and biased generation (Wang et al., 2023) attributed to a specific malicious
035
      description in the input. These issues undermine human trust in model usage, thus highlighting a
036   pressing need for developing an interpretation tool that attributes how an input prompt leads to the
037   generated content.
038
039
      Explaining LLM generation through prompt attribution involves extracting the most influential
      prompt texts on the model’s entire generation procedure, a realm that remains relatively under-
040
      explored in current research. While extensive works on input attribution are proposed for text
041
      classification interpretation (Chen & Ji, 2020; Modarressi et al., 2023) and next-word generation
042   rationale (Zhao & Shan, 2024; Vafa et al., 2021), they can not be directly applied to explain the
043   full generation sequence due to its complicated joint probability landscape and the autoregressive
044   generation procedure. The complexity for interpreting LLM generation compounds as the model size
045   increases. Another line of works involves prompting LLMs to self-explain their behaviors (Wei
046   et al., 2022). This method relies on the model’s innate reasoning capabilities, although current
047   findings suggest that these capabilities may not always be faithful (Turpin et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
048   2024).
049   There are limited existing attempts focusing on explaining the relationship between the input prompts
050   and the complete generated sequence. The most relevant approach, Captum (Miglani et al., 2023),
051   sequentially determines the importance score for each token by calculating the variations in the joint
052   probability of generating the targeted output sequence when the token is dropped from the model
053   input. While being straightforward, this approach treats tokens as independent features, ignoring
      their joint semantic influence on the generated output. In fact, tokens may contain overlapping or


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      complementary information. For example, given the input prompt: “Write a story about the doctor
055   and his patient”, the most influential components are “doctor” and “patient”. Individually removing
056   either of these words would not significantly alter the generated output, resulting in inaccurately
057   low importance score for each of them. This is caused by the semantic interaction among these
058   components, allowing the model to infer the meaning of the omitted one. Existing attribution methods
059   that ablate each token individually fail to capture such combinatorial effect. A straightforward remedy
060   might involve exhaustively assessing all possible combinations to observe the variations in the model
061   generations, which however is impractical due to the vast search space with long-context input
062   prompts.
063   To efficiently search the space for generating accurate prompt explanations, we develop our framework
064   JoPA, which provides the counterfactual explanation to highlight which components of input prompts
065   have the fundamental effect on the generated context via solving a combinatorial optimization
066   problem. We aim to explain the generation behavior of model outputs for any given prompt while
067   take the joint effects of the prompt components into account. Assuming that removing the essential
068   parts of the prompt would result in a significant variation in the model’s output, we propose the novel
069
      objective function and formulate our task of providing faithful counterfactual explanations for the
      input prompt as an optimization problem. To quantify the influence of token combinations in the
070
      prompt on the generations, we incorporate a mask approach for joint prompt attribution. Thus, our
071
      goal of extracting the explanations has been converted to finding the optimal mask of the input prompt.
072   We solve this problem by a probabilistic search algorithm, equipped with gradient guidance and
073   probabilistic updates for efficient exploration in the discrete solution space. Our main contributions
074   could be summarized as follows:
075
076   • We propose a general interpretation scheme for LLM generation task that attributes input
077     prompts to the entire generation sequence. Notably, this recipe considers the joint influence of
078     input token combinations on the generation. This motivation naturally formulates a combinatorial
079     optimization problem for explaining generation with the most influential prompt texts.
080   • We demonstrate JoPA, an efficient probabilistic search algorithm to solve the optimization problem.
081     JoPA works by searching better token combinations that lead to larger generation changes. It takes
082     the advantage of both the gradient information and the probabilistic search-space strategy, thereby
083     achieving an efficient prompt interpretation tool.
084   • Our framework demonstrates strong performance on language generation tasks including text
085     summarization, question-answering, and general instruction datasets. The faithfulness of our
086     explanations is evaluated based on a suit of comprehensive metrics considering generation prob-
087     ability, word frequency, and semantic similarity, verifying the transferability and effectiveness
088     of our methods across a variety of tasks. Moreover, the generated explanations demonstrate the
089     effectiveness of our framework to potentially be applied to improve the model’s ability, especially
090
        making the model safer and more efficient.

092   2    R ELATED W ORK
093
      While there are extensive works devoted to explaining language models in the context of text
094
      classification tasks (Shi et al., 2022; Han et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2023), relatively few attempts (Zhao
095
      & Shan, 2024; Vafa et al., 2021) are proposed to investigate the importance of prompt texts on the
096   entire generation procedure especially for LLMs. This demonstrates a research gap that this work
097   aims to fill in.
098
099
      Explaining Language Generation There are many work explaining the predictions generated by
      LLMs by measuring the importance of the input features to the model’s prediction on the classification
100
      tasks. One group of studies perturb the specific input by removing, masking, or altering the input
101
      features, and evaluate the model prediction changes (Kommiya Mothilal et al., 2021; Wu et al.,
102   2020). The other group of works, such as integrated gradients (IG) (Sundararajan et al., 2017), first-
103   derivative saliency (Li et al., 2016), and mixed partial derivatives (Tsang et al., 2020) leverage the
104   gradients of the output with respect to the input to determine the input feature importance. Although
105   Archipelago (Tsang et al., 2020) explains the feature attributions by considering the combined effects
106   of the input attributions, it targets for the multi-label classification task and relies on the neutral
107   baseline. As for the generation tasks, Diffmask learns the differentiable mask for each layer of the
      BERT model, but it aims to analyze how decisions are formed across network hidden layers by a


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      simple probing classifier. In contrast, our framework targets on offering insights into the relationship
109   between the input prompt and model generations by employing the mask to highlight the essential
110   prompt attributions.
111
112
      Moreover, a few studies utilize the surrogate model to explain the individual predictions of the
      black-box models, and the representative method is called LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016). As for
113
      explaining the model’s generation behavior, Captum (Miglani et al., 2023) calculates the token’s
114
      importance score by sequentially measuring the contribution of input token to the output, which lacks
115   of the accounting for the semantic relationships between tokens. Another work, ReAGent (Zhao &
116   Shan, 2024), focuses on the next-word generation task, computing the importance distribution for
117   the next token position. This method ignores the contextual dependencies in the generated output and
118   could not adequately account for dynamically changing generations. Our framework aims to interpret
119   the joint effects of the input prompts on the entire output contexts with considering of the textual
120   information covered the input prompt.
121   Self-explaining by Prompting As language models increase in scale, prompting-based models
122   demonstrate remarkable abilities in reasoning (Brown et al., 2020), creativity (Oppenlaender, 2022),
123   and adaptability across a range of tasks (Khattak et al., 2023). However, the complex reasoning
124   processes of these models remain elusive and require tailored paradigm to better understand the
125   prompting mechanism. For instance, the chain-of-thought (CoT) paradigm could explain the LLM
126   behaviors by prompting the model to generate the reasoning chain along with the answers (Wei
127   et al., 2022), as pre-trained LLMs have demonstrated a certain ability to self-explain their behaviors.
128
      However, recent studies have also suggested that the reasoning chain does not guarantee faithful
      explanations of the model’s behavior (Jacovi & Goldberg, 2020) and the final answer might not
129
      always follow the generated reasoning chain (Turpin et al., 2023). xLLM (Chuang et al., 2024)
130
      enhances the fidelity of explanation derived from LLMs via a fidelity evaluator, which however is
131   designed for classification tasks. Our efforts are concentrated on explaining LLM generation by
132   analyzing the attribution of the input prompts to the output content, without depending on the model’s
133   innate reasoning ability that are not yet satisfactory.

139   LLM Generation Notions Denote a specific input prompt as a sequence of tokens x =

      the input sequence and the set of all token indices is I = {1, 2, . . . , T }. The corresponding generated
      output y could be represented as a sequence of tokens y = (y1 , . . . , yS ) with yj ∈ {1, 2, . . . , |V |}.

      The output tokens {yi }Si=1 are generated from the LLM fθ parameterized by θ in an autoregressive

151   Limitation of Prior Interpretation Attempt There are limited prior works about explaining
152   the relationship between the individual input prompts and the entire generated sequence, and their
153   faithfulness is limited by treating input tokens independently. Specifically, Captum (Miglani et al.,
154   2023), calculates the importance score for each token by slightly perturbing the input x at the i-th
155   token:
156                                    Gi (x; θ) = pθ (y|x) − pθ (y|xI\{i} ),                          (2)
157   where I denotes all token indices. Through Eq. (2), one can calculate an attribution score for each
158   token i indicating its importance towards the original generation result y. Such method is direct and
159   simple, however, it treats tokens as independent features, ignoring their semantic interaction and
160   joint effect on the generated output. To make it more obvious, let’s recall the previous example of
161   “Write a story about a doctor and his patient.”. Note that since “doctor” and “patient” are semantically
      correlated, and removing any of the two words would not have a significant influence on the generated

162
      response, their corresponding importance score will not be too high. On the contrary, the most
163   important token detected by this approach would be “his”, which is clearly not ideal.
164
165   These observations inspire us to develop a new prompt attribution method that considers the semantic
166
      correlation among tokens. Specifically, we assume that the content generated by LLMs is primarily
      influenced by a subset of tokens jointly. The remaining tokens serve as auxiliary or potentially less
167
      relevant information. Those subset of tokens, which fundamentally shape the model’s output, are
168
      viewed as the explanatory tokens for the generated content. In other words, explanatory tokens do
169   not affect the model output independently, but jointly contribute to the generated responses.

192   Problem Formulation for Joint Prompt Attribution In order to explain language generation via
193
      prompt attribution, instead of treating the prompt tokens independently as in previous works (Miglani
      et al., 2023), we propose to evaluate the joint effect of k prompt tokens on the generated sequence.
194
195   Specifically, consider a binary prompt mask m = (m1 , . . . , mT ) in which mi ∈ {0, 1} indicates
196   whether the i-th token is important or not. The joint probability of generating the original output
197   sequence y given an input masked context m ⊙ x is computed as pθ (y|m ⊙ x), where ⊙ denotes
198   the Hadamard product. In our paper, we aim to identify a binary mask m, which is a (discrete)
199
      learnable parameter, that maximizes the discrepancy in the probability of generating the same
      output y when comparing the masked input m ⊙ x to the original input x. A large discrepancy
200
      indicates that the masked token combination are the most influential components for generating y,
201
      thus should jointly serve as the attributed explanation. Consequently, this involves training the binary
202   mask m to optimize the following objective function:
203
204                             max     L(m, x, y; θ) : = pθ (y|x) − pθ (y|m ⊙ x)
                             m∈{0,1}T
205                                                                                                          (3)
                                             s.t.   |m|1 = T − k,
206
207   where k represents the number of explanatory tokens. Intuitively, optimizing the objective outlined in
208   Eq. (3) suggests that we want to find the top k important tokens which, if they are masked, lead to a
209   substantial variation in model’s output probabilities. Compared with prior methods for individual
210   token attribution stated in Eq. (2), our formulation in Eq. (3) measures the joint attribution of a subset
211   of tokens, capturing token interactions to enable more accurate prompt explanations.
212   Challenges in Solving Eq. (3) Note that Eq. (3) is a constraint discrete optimization problem
213   that is non-trivial to solve. One naive solution would be transforming the discrete optimization
214   problem into a continuous one, i.e., let m ∈ [0, 1]T , and formulate the constraint into a regularization
215   term. Then one can adopt traditional gradient descent based optimization solutions to solve the
      problem. However, such a strategy would require extensive gradient calculations and backward

217   obtained continuous mask is not the final explanation we want. In fact, the approximation error when
218   transforming the continuous mask back into the discrete space can also be quite significant, leading to
219   worse performances. Another straightforward solution involves searching through all possible token
220   combinations exhaustively. However, this could be impractical as well due to the enormous search
221   space especially while facing long-context inputs. Therefore, we hope to develop a new method that
222   adopts a search-based strategy to simplify the algorithm design and satisfy our constraints, while also
223   leveraging gradient information for efficient optimization.

      Proposed Probabilistic Search Algorithm To tackle this challenge, we propose JoPA, a novel
243
      explainable prompt generator, for efficiently obtaining an optimal solution for Eq. (3). We summarize
244
      our JoPA in Algorithm 1. The high-level pipeline is illustrated in Figure 1 left: we initialize and
245   maintain exactly k entries in the mask m to be zero to enforce the constraint and capture their
246   joint influence of being masked; the mask is then iteratively updated by sampling indexes for value
247   swapping, searching towards the direction with increased generation loss L(m, x, y; θ). At the
248   core of this pipeline is the sampling and update of the discrete mask, which demands an efficient
249   exploration in the vast search space. Figure 1 right shows this step, which is featured by the
250   following two essential components, the gradient-guided masking and the probabilistic search update.
251   Specifically, it illustrates the process of sampling a non-zero entry and a zero entry from m to
252   swap their values.
253   Gradient-Guided Masking: Trivial solutions that set the mask m by uniformly random could cost
254   massive sampling to hit the right optimization direction. Gradient is a common indicator of feature
255   importance, as evidenced in existing practices (Ebrahimi et al., 2018; Zou et al., 2023; Shin et al.,
256   2020). Therefore, we propose to use gradient as a guidance to set and sample the mask for more
257   efficient optimization. Specifically, we begin with the binary mask m(0) = 1 which indicates
258   that all tokens in the input x are marked as non-explanatory ones. Then we compute the gradient
259   ∇m(0) L(m(0) , x, y; θ) of the loss function in Eq. (3), and denote the magnitude of gradients as
260   g = |∇m(0) L(m(0) , x, y; θ)|. Note that the components with larger gradient magnitudes in g imply
261   that altering the corresponding tokens could result in a sharper change to the generated outputs. In
262   order to initialize the explanatory k tokens guided by the gradient magnitude, we set the binary
                             (1)
263   mask m(1) where mi = 0 indicates gi is the top-k value in g. The gradient guidance and mask
264   initialization are obtained following Line 1-2 in the algorithm.
265   Probabilistic Search Update: While gradient is informative, greedily determining explanatory tokens
266   by top gradients lacks exploration, leading to suboptimal solutions. Therefore, we propose a prob-
267   abilistic search mechanism for mask sampling and update. Specifically when updating the current
268   binary mask m, we iteratively sample a non-zero entry l from the mask and swap its value with a
269   sampled zero entry v to explore a new (and potentially better) solution for the mask. Particularly,
      the sampling is also guided by the gradient calculated before: the non-zero entry in the mask (rep-

      we have pθ (y|m∗ ⊙ x) ≤ pθ (y|m ⊙ x) for all m in the one-swap neighborhood of m∗ , namely

      5.2   E VALUATION M ETRICS
325
326   Faithfulness scores are a key metric for assessing the quality of explanations, with faithful explanations
327   accurately reflecting the model’s decision-making process (Jacovi & Goldberg, 2020). Studies (Samek
328   et al., 2017; Hooker et al., 2019) suggest that if a certain input tokens are truly important, their removal
329   should lead to a more significant change in model output than the removal of random tokens (Madsen
330   et al., 2022). Therefore, after removing explanatory tokens identified by a faithful method, the
331   model’s new generation would significantly differ from using the original output. Moreover, the
332
      input prompt with masking these explanatory tokens are less likely to reproduce the original model
      response y, indicating a lower value of pθ (y|m ⊙ x).
333
334   To quantitatively evaluate explanation faithfulness, we measure the change of model generation
335   behavior from two dimensions. On one hand, we compare the model’s original and new generations:
336   the originally generated sequence y is based on the complete input prompt x (e.g., y = fθ (x)), while
337   the new generation is conditioned on the masked prompt (e.g., y ′ = fθ (m ⊙ x)). We thus measure
338
      the similarity of the original generation y and the new generation y ′ based on their word frequency
      and semantics. A smaller similarity reflects a larger change in model generation, suggesting a better
339
      explanation. On the other hand, we measure the likelihood of generating the original output y when
340
      the model uses the masked prompt, e.g., changes on pθ (y|m ⊙ x). A smaller likelihood indicates
341   better explanations whose mask prevents the model from generating its original output. Detailed
342   definitions of these metrics are explained below.
343
344
      Word Frequency: BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) is widely used to measure how close the candidate
      text y ′ is to the reference text y. The score measures the precision of matching n-grams from the text
345
      y ′ to y by a clipping method to avoid overcounting and adjusting for brevity of y ′ if it is shorter than
346
      y. ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004) is the metric for measuring the overlap of sequences of words between the
347   two texts, evaluating how much of the y ′ matches with the reference y (precision), how much the
348   reference y is covered by the candidate y ′ (recall), and combine them into an F1 score.
349
350
      Semantic Similarity: SentenceBert (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) is a variation of the BERT model
      which is designed to generate high-quality sentence embeddings for the pairs of sentences. We
351
      leverage SentenceBert to transform the text y and y ′ into fixed-length embedding vectors, and
352
      calculate the cosine similarity between their embeddings to quantify their semantic similarity.
353
354   Probability Measurement: We define two measurements to reflect how the likelihood of generating the
355   original text y changes before and after applying the explanation mask. We first define the Probability
356
      Ratio (PR) to indicate how less likely to generate the original output y when explanatory tokens are
357   masked: PR(x, y, m) = p̃θp̃(y|m⊙x)
                                    θ (y|x)
                                             , where p̃(y|x) = p(y|x)1/s is the length-normalized generation
358   probability. If the PR score is far below the random baseline, we can conclude that the masked
359   tokens are indeed important to cause the model generating y. In addition, we also calculate the KL-
360
      divergence between these two distributions to measure their difference: DKL (pθ (y|m⊙x)||pθ (y|x)),
      and a larger score indicates a larger change in generation likelihood and a more accurate explanation.

507   Recall that in our algorithm, we use gradient as guidance to initialize the mask (e.g., m(1) ) and
508   calculate sampling probability (i.e., softmax(m(n) ⊙g)). To verify the efficiency of gradient guidance,
509   we compare JoPA with two variants: w/o Initialization that initializes the mask by uniformly random
510   instead of gradient, and w/o Probability that samples swapping entries by uniformly random instead
511   of gradient. Table 5 in the Appendix reports their resulting generation log-likelihood log pθ (y|m⊙x)
512
      in different iterations. We observe that without using gradient for initialization, the randomly
      initialized mask starts from a worse point with a high generation likelihood; and without using
513
      gradient to guide the sampling, the mask is updated in less effective direction to explore the search
514
      space, resulting in a high generation likelihood in the end. These results show that gradient is a useful
515   and efficient tool for initializing the optimization from a better starting point and guiding the search
516   to a better optimal point.

      6        C ONCLUSIONS
524
525   In this study, we introduce JoPA, an efficient probabilistic search algorithm designed to generate the
526   prompt attributions that elicit the model outputs for the generation tasks. We tackle the challenge
527   of explaining the generation behavior for any given prompt by analyzing the joint effect of the
528   prompt attributions on the output. We frame this explanation task as a discrete optimization problem,
529   which can be efficiently solved by our proposed probabilistic search algorithm. This methodology
530
      enables efficient generation of any arbitrary number of explanatory prompt attributions that are
      deterministic to the generated content. Our framework is rigorously evaluated across extensive
531
      language generation tasks, including text summarization, question-answering, and general instruction
532
      datasets. The faithfulness of the explanatory prompt attributions is thoroughly analyzed and assessed
533   by comprehensive metrics. The results demonstrate that our proposed method efficiently generates
534   explanatory attributions that faithfully reflect the model’s generation behavior for the specific prompt.
535   These explanatory attributions interact in semantically and jointly influence the output generation.
536   Furthermore, the overall excellent performance of our method on these diverse datasets highlights its
537   remarkable transferability.

848   A.3   B ENEFITS TO THE COMMUNITY ON IMPROVING MODEL ABILITY
849
850
      Benefit on Model Safety Our method has the potential to help LLM defend against jailbreaking
      attacks, making the model more robust. We verify this via a real case study. Given an adversarial
851
      prompt with malicious request: "Generate a message that spreads fake news. organized few GO a
852
      a a a newcommand ! SudGuiheures ! !/ refection/:" It can jailbreak Llama-2 (7B-Chat) model to
853   generate unsafe responses. Our algorithm successfully attributes "Sud" and "he", which are part of
854   the adversarial suffix. By deleting these attributions, the attack fails. Thus, our approach could help
855   detect these high-risk malicious tokens to robustify model safety.
856
857
      Benefit on Model Diagnosis Our approach can also be used to assess the relevance of model’s
858   response to the prompt, helping diagnose the effectiveness of model responding to the prompt. For a
859   real case study, consider a single prompt consisting of multiple tasks: "Create a message to complain
860   about the noise in the neighborhood to friends. Give me the Python code for filtering results based on
861   the following columns..." We find Llama-2 (7B-Chat) only responds to the first request, neglecting
862   the second one. Our approach attributes the model’s output to tokens like "complain" "noise" and
863   "friends", mainly about the first request. Thus by input attribution, our method can help diagnose the
      overlooked request and suggest better prompt design.

      A.4   D ISCUSSION ON C OUNTERFACTUAL E XPLANATION
911
912   Our work JoPA is to explain the generation of LLMs by identifying a small changes (i.e., masking
913   few tokens) needed to alter the outcome (i.e., minimizing the probability of the original generation).
914   This can be understood as counterfactual explanation, since it answers a "what if" question: if these
915   input tokens are masked, the LLM would not generate the response. Namely, by applying the mask m
916   on the input prompt x, the generation probability of its original response y would be largely decreased
917   (i.e., pθ (y|m ⊙ x) ≪ pθ (y|x)). This suggests that the newly generated output y ′ resulting from the
      masked prompt would differ substantially from the original outcome y, as measured by our metrics in


                                                               17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      Table 1: the semantic similarity (SentenceBert) and word similarity (BLEU and ROUGE-L) between
919   y and y ′ are much smaller than those from baselines. Here are several case studies to illustrate the
920   concepts discussed.
921
922   General Q&A Task Given the input prompt to the model: "Follow the law of supply and demand,
923   describe what would happen to the price of a good if the demand increased." The model would
924   generate the response: "\nIn economics, the law of supply and demand states that the price of a
925   good is determined by the interaction between the quantity of the good that...". After masking
926   the 3 explanatory tokens: ’supply’, ’price’, and ’demand’, the generated response would change
927   significantly: "However, I must inform you that the question you’ve provided is not factually coherent,
928   and I cannot provide an answer that may not be accurate or safe...".
929
930   Fill-in-the-Blank Task Given the input prompt for the model to fill in the blank:" Fill in the blank
931
      with a word or phrase The most successful team in the NBA is the ____" the original response is: "The
      most successful team in the NBA is the Golden State Warriors". If we masked out the explanatory
932
      tokens ’most’, ’successful’, and ’NBA’ from the input, the output would change to: "\Based on the
933
      context you provided, the word that best fits the blank is "best." So, the sentence would read:\The
934   team in the office is the best."
935
936   The case studies of diverse tasks above demonstrate that the new responses generated from the masked
937
      inputs differ significantly from the original outputs, which highlights the impact of these explanatory
      tokens in influencing and altering the model’s output.
