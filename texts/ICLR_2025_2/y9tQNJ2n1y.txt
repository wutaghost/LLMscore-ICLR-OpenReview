009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                Aligning large language models (LLMs) with human values is essential for their
012                safe deployment and widespread adoption. Current LLM safety benchmarks
013                often focus solely on the refusal of individual problematic queries, which
014                overlooks the importance of the context where the query occurs and may cause
015                undesired refusal of queries under safe contexts that diminish user experience.
016                Addressing this gap, we introduce CASE-Bench, a Context-Aware Safety
017
                   Evaluation Benchmark that integrates context into safety assessments of LLMs.
                   CASE-Bench assigns distinct, formally described contexts to categorized queries
018
                   based on Contextual Integrity theory. Additionally, in contrast to previous
019
                   studies which mainly rely on majority voting from just a few annotators, we
020                recruited a sufficient number of annotators necessary to ensure the detection
021                of statistically significant differences among the experimental conditions based
022                on power analysis. Our extensive analysis using CASE-Bench on various
023                open-source and commercial LLMs reveals a substantial and significant influence
024                of context on human judgments (p <0.0001 from a z-test), underscoring the
025                necessity of context in safety evaluations. We also identify notable mismatches
026                between human judgments and LLM responses, particularly in commercial
027                models within safe contexts.1 Code and data used in the paper are available at
028
                   https://anonymous.4open.science/r/CASEBench-D5DB.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Aligning large language models (LLMs) with human values to ensure the safe use of LLMs is a
034   primary focus of current research in this field, and it is also a crucial prerequisite for their widespread
035   application (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Hendrycks et al., 2021a).
036   Various benchmarks have been proposed to evaluate the quality of alignment, focusing on different
037   aspects of LLM safety that are mostly triggered by a single harmful user query (Shaikh et al., 2023;
038   Wang et al., 2024; Parrish et al., 2022; Cui et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023; Röttger et al., 2023; Zou
039   et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Souly et al., 2024). Systematic and unified benchmarks have also been
040   proposed for comprehensive LLM safety evaluation (Vidgen et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2023; Mazeika
041   et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2024).
042   The aforementioned benchmarks, however, predominantly focus on the identification of refusal of
043   individual problematic queries. This leads to over-refusal behaviours in many LLMs, e.g., refusing
044   to answer questions that users may think should be answered, which may substantially impact user
045   experience. Although this issue has been acknowledged and highlighted in recent studies (Röttger
046   et al., 2023; Arditi et al., 2024), these studies still focus on judgments based on isolated queries
047   and overlook the contextual information in which queries occur (Weidinger et al., 2023; Leveson,
048
      2016). In fact, context plays a crucial role in determining whether it is safe to respond to a query,
      as demonstrated in Figure 1, thereby necessitating the incorporation of context in LLM safety eval-
049
      uations. Moreover, previous studies have confirmed that LLMs are capable of understanding and
050
      reasoning within social and moral contexts (Arora et al., 2022; Emelin et al., 2020; Hendrycks et al.,
051   2021b), aiming for human-centred AI assistant designs. Therefore, incorporating context is both
052   reasonable and promising for enhancing safety evaluations in LLM-related research.

076   To incorporate context in LLM safety evaluation, this paper proposes CASE-Bench, a Context-
077   Aware Safety Evaluation Benchmark. CASE-Bench is based on categorised queries from Xie et al.
078   (2024) and provides distinct contexts to each query with formalized context descriptions using the
079   modern conceptualization of contextual integrity (CI) theory established by Nissenbaum (2004).
080   Furthermore, in addition to providing binary outcomes, we provide safety ratings by collecting
081   2000+ participants from the crowd following power analysis methods (Cohen, 1992), thereby ob-
082
      taining a measure of uncertainty in human judgments. Extensive analyses were performed across
      a range of open-source and commercial LLMs, where a substantial and significant influence of the
083
      context to human judgments was found with p-values < 0.0001 with a z-test, showcasing the ne-
084
      cessity of incorporating context for safety evaluation. Moreover, clear mismatches between human
085   and LLM judgments were found when the context is safe for LLMs that are over-moderated (e.g.
086   OpenAI GPT-4o). The main contributions of this paper can be summarised as follows.
087
088          1. We propose the context-aware safety evaluation benchmark, CASE-Bench, for LLM safety
089             evaluation by associating 900 distinct contexts with queries from 45 different categories.
090
             2. We propose to use CI theory for formalized context descriptions in CASE-Bench, and
091             additionally collect non-binary safety ratings from over 2,000 human participants.
092
093
             3. Comprehensive analyses of LLM safety judgments and comparison across seven popular
                LLMs were conducted using CASE-Bench. Our results demonstrate the necessity and the
094
                substantial and significant influence of context to understand the (un)-safety of queries. Our
095
                results particularly showcase, among others, the over-refusal problem with safe contexts.

      2    P RELIMINARY: C ONTEXTUAL I NTEGRITY T HEORY
099
100
      Contextual Integrity (CI) theory, initially developed by Nissenbaum (2004), has been extensively
101
      adopted in the field of computer science to analyze the appropriateness of information flows within
102   different contexts (e.g. Apthorpe et al., 2018; Abdi et al., 2021; Mireshghallah et al., 2023; Kumar
103   et al., 2020). At its core, CI theory establishes norms that govern the appropriateness of information
104   sharing by addressing the critical question: ”Should this information be shared in this specific con-
105   text?” This principle has been fundamental in the development of personal privacy-enhancing tech-
106   nologies, such as intelligent agents and voice assistants, which assist users in making context-aware
107   decisions about information sharing, thereby strengthening their privacy protections (Ogunniye &
      Kokciyan, 2023; Ghalebikesabi et al., 2024).


                                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      CI theory provides a comprehensive framework for examining the parameters of information flows,
109   including actors (e.g., sender and recipients), information types, and transmission principles. These
110   parameters work together to form a coherent structure for understanding and evaluating the contexts
111   in which information flows occur. Unlike conceptualizing context in broad terms such as ”domains”
112   (e.g., health, work, family, civil and political spheres), CI theory offers a more nuanced and formal-
113   ized representation.
114
      Building on the successful application of CI theory in privacy settings, we extend this framework
115   to represent the context of information flows (i.e. conversations) between a user and a chatbot in
116   the general AI safety field. The CI definition of social norms, which govern the appropriateness of
117   information flows within a given context, aligns with our objective of determining whether or not a
118   chatbot should respond to user queries. Specifically, we focus on assessing the “appropriateness” of
119   the information (i.e., the potential response from the chatbot) that flows from the chatbot to the user.
120   With this understanding of the suitability of CI, we describe in detail in §3.1 how the CI parameters
121   are applied in this paper to represent context, marking the first instance of using CI theory to build a
122   foundation for real-world context representation.
123
124   3     CASE-B ENCH
125
126   CASE-Bench contains 900 queries+context pairs, that is 450 controversial and potentially harmful
127   queries with 2 distinct contexts per query that are automatically generated and then manually revised.
128   One of the contexts is intended to be safer than the other for each query, as detailed in §4.2. CASE-
129   Bench also contains human annotations on whether responding to each query is safe or unsafe given
130   each context from 2,000+ high-quality annotators. Each query-context pair as a task received 21
131   annotations which is determined by statistical power analysis, as described in §3.2. This process
132   resulted in a total of 47,000+ human annotations.2

134   3.1   A PPLYING CI T HEORY TO CASE-B ENCH
135
136   In this section, we present the CI parameters we adopted to represent the context in our study.
137   To tailor these parameters to our research needs, we made slight modifications to the original CI
138
      framework. While the original CI theory primarily includes parameters such as the sender and the
      recipient, we have specified these in greater detail by introducing sub-parameters. This refinement
139
      enhances their concreteness and facilitates the use of automated tools such as GPT-4o to generate
140
      more varied contexts. In the following descriptions, we define the chatbot as the sender and the user
141   as the recipient when evaluating the safety of the chatbot’s responses to user queries.
142
143
      Sender In this paper, the sender is the chatbot, as we are evaluating the appropriateness of its
144   response flows to the user. This parameter is further distinguished by two key sub-parameters, which
145   enable automated tools to generate more diverse and varied contexts: Nature of the Interaction
146   and Platform Type. The Nature of the Interaction determines whether the chatbot is intended for
147   general-purpose use or customized for a specific domain. In the case of customization, the domain
148   may encompass fields such as research, education, financial services, or role-playing. The second
149   sub-parameter, Platform Type, specifies the medium through which the chatbot operates, such as a
150   website, mobile application, social media platform, or dedicated support system.
151
152   Recipient In this paper, the recipient refers to the user interacting with the chatbot in this paper.
153   The Recipient is further categorized by two sub-parameters: Type and Background. The Type sub-
154   parameter identifies the nature of the recipient, which may include an existing user, a potential
155
      user, or an anonymous user, among other possibilities. The Background sub-parameter provides
      additional contextual information about the recipient, such as their previous interactions with the
156
      chatbot, the purpose of their query, and relevant demographic details. This background information
157
      helps to establish the recipient’s context more thoroughly, allowing for more accurate assessments
158   of the appropriateness of the chatbot’s responses.

      Transmission Principle This parameter governs the rules and norms that dictate how information
163   flows between the sender (the chatbot) and the recipient (the user). This parameter is defined by three
164   sub-parameters: Sender Purpose, Confidentiality, and Source Accountability. The Sender Purpose
165   sub-parameter specifies the intent behind the conversation from the sender’s perspective. This could
166   be a detailed, defined purpose (e.g., providing information, offering assistance) or indicate that no
167   specific purpose has been established. Confidentiality addresses whether the conversation is private
168   and restricted to the chatbot and user, or if it occurs in a public setting where others may have
169   access to the information exchanged. Lastly, the Source Accountability sub-parameter defines who is
170   responsible for the information flow—whether it is the chatbot, the platform, or another entity—and
171
      whether the information provided can be traced back to a source.

173   3.2       D ETERMINING N UMBER OF A NNOTATORS U SING P OWER A NALYSIS
174
      In this paper, we employed a between-subjects design (Charness et al., 2012), a well-established
175
      experimental research method commonly used in disciplines such as medicine, psychology and
176   human-computer interaction to assess user behaviour and judgments (Charness et al., 2012). Follow-
177   ing this approach, we recruited distinct groups of annotators, with each group assigned randomly to a
178   single experimental condition. This design enables a clean and clear comparison between conditions
179   while minimizing potential carryover, ordering or learning effects that could arise from exposure to
180   multiple conditions.
181
      Previous benchmarks (Xie et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2024; Ji et al., 2023) lack metrics or standards
182   to determine the sufficient number of annotators. To address this issue, we are the first to apply
183   statistical power analysis (Cohen, 1992) to derive the optimal sample size for reliably detecting the
184   effects of context on safety evaluation. This approach ensures a rigorous balance between statistical
185   power and resource allocation, improving the quality and representativeness of our crowd-sourced
186   dataset and making our findings more reliable and generalizable.
187
      Specifically, the power analysis was conducted using G*Power 3.1 (Erdfelder et al., 1996). We
188   selected ANOVA: Fixed effects, omnibus, one-way model (Girden, 1992) as it is commonly ap-
189   plied to compare means across multiple groups and determine if there are significant differences
190   between them. In our study, this approach was appropriate as we aimed to assess whether different
191   experimental conditions (e.g., with and without context) lead to meaningful changes in annotators’
192   responses and perceptions. We assumed an effect size of f = 0.4, which reflects a moderate to large
193   effect size, often deemed sufficient in social and behavioural research (Cohen, 2013). We set the
194   alpha level (Type I error rate) at α = 0.05, providing a 5% chance of falsely rejecting the null hy-
195   pothesis. Additionally, we aimed for a power of 0.8 (80%), ensuring an 80% probability of correctly
196
      rejecting the null hypothesis if a true effect exists. The experimental design involved five conditions:
      No context, Safe context (GPT generated), Unsafe context (GPT generated), Safe context (manually
197
      revised), and Unsafe context (manually revised).
198
199   Based on these parameters, the power analysis indicated that a total sample size of 80 annotators
200   would be needed across all conditions (results in Appendix E.1), translating to 16 annotators per task.
201   However, recognizing that ANOVA assumes normally distributed data, we accounted for potential
202
      deviations from normality by increasing the sample size by 10% to 20% (Box, 1953). To safeguard
      against low-quality responses or dropouts, as we apply high-quality data measures and filters as
203
      explained later on, we increased the sample size to 21 annotators3 per task. With 2,250 tasks in total,
204
      we recruited around 2,000 high-quality annotators, as detailed below, assigning each an average of
205   25 tasks to prevent task overload and promote data quality.

      4.2    C ONTEXT G ENERATION
236
      For each query, we generated both a safe context for which the chatbot should provide the response
237   and an unsafe context for which the chatbot should refuse to reply. To achieve this, a two-stage
238   approach was adopted where we first use GPT-4o to automatically generate the context, and then
239   manually revise and curate the contexts. This process highlights the CI framework’s key role in
240   guiding structured, consistent automatic context generation and enabling manual revisions to reduce
241   ambiguity, prevent over-moderation, and ensure clarity.
242
243   Automatic Context Generation For each given query, a template-based approach was employed
244   using GPT-4o to generate both safe and unsafe conversational contexts. This process was guided by
245   CI theory, with the parameters described in §3.1 forming the foundation for generating the structured
246   contexts. The prompt provided to GPT-4o followed a specific sequence: first, an overview was
247   given to explain the task of generating context for the query. Then, potential safety issues related to
248
      the query were disclosed, highlighting key risks. Afterwards, each CI parameter was described in
      detail, and GPT-4o was instructed to generate the context accordingly. The generated output strictly
249
      followed a predefined structured format, ensuring consistency and completeness across all contexts.
250
      The full details of the prompts used for this process can be found in Appendix C.1.
251
252
      Manual Revision While the GPT-4o-generated contexts provided a solid foundation, manual re-
253
      view and revision were essential to ensure accuracy, consistency, and the overall quality of the
254   contexts. GPT-4o, like other LLMs, employs strict self-safeguarding mechanisms—often referred
255   to as ”safety refusal behaviours” or ”content moderation filters”—to prevent the generation of harm-
256   ful or unethical content (Anwar et al., 2024; OpenAI). Consequently, the model often moderates
257   the unsafe query into a safe one before generating the safe context, which completely overwrites
258   the user intention. To address this, we employed two researchers to carefully review all generated
259   contexts, ensuring they align with the intended safe or unsafe conditions.
260   The revision process involved regular discussions between the researchers to review and improve
261   the contexts, ensuring accuracy and appropriateness for the study. In many cases, where the GPT-
262   4o output was too conservative or incorrect, the researchers replaced them with manually crafted
263   contexts. The full details of the revision process, including feedback and collaboration mechanisms,
264   are provided in Appendix D.2. Ultimately, all contexts were revised, with most replaced by author-
265   created content, ensuring the final dataset was reliable and suited for model evaluation.

      6     R ELATED W ORK
498
499
      6.1   S AFETY A LIGNMENT B ENCHMARKS
500
501
      Ensuring the safety of LLMs has become a pivotal focus in recent AI research, prompting the de-
502   velopment of various evaluation benchmarks with datasets (Ji et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2024; Gehman
503   et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2023; Vidgen et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023;
504   Zou et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Mazeika et al., 2024; Souly et al., 2024;
505   Shaikh et al., 2023). These papers investigated a range of issues concerning the generation of harm-
506   ful content by LLMs, each utilizing distinct criteria and safety taxonomies. One set of them focuses
507   on evaluating and enhancing the ability of LLMs to detect and appropriately respond to adversarial
508   “red-teaming” prompts (Ji et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2024; Gehman et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024;
509   Cui et al., 2023; Vidgen et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Mazeika et al., 2024). Another set explores
510
      methods to bypass model safety mechanisms, thereby contributing to improved security by identi-
      fying vulnerabilities and refining safeguards to prevent harmful or unethical uses (Qi et al., 2023;
511
      Zou et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Souly et al., 2024). However, this prior
512
      work predominantly focuses on binary classifications of prompts as safe or unsafe, often ignoring
513   contextual factors in safety decision-making. In contrast, our study is the first to evaluate the safety
514   performance of LLMs by incorporating context into the assessment.
515
516
      6.2   OVER - REFUSAL I SSUES
517
518
      Over-refusal, wherein LLMs incorrectly or excessively refuse to respond to user inputs, has been
519   identified in prior research (Bianchi et al., 2023; Röttger et al., 2023). This issue often arises when
520   LLMs are fine-tuned to comply with safety guidelines, leading to the erroneous classification of be-
521   nign prompts as harmful. A similar phenomenon has been observed in the field of computer vision,
522   where certain adversarial defence methods cause models to become “overly robust,” resulting in the
523   misclassification of safe inputs as adversarial attacks (Tramèr et al., 2020). To address this challenge,
524   researchers have developed large-scale datasets and benchmarks to evaluate and improve LLM re-
525   sponses to such inputs. Recent examples include the generation of auto-created pseudo-harmful
526   prompts by An et al. (2024) and the Or-Bench (Cui et al., 2024), which provides an automated
527
      pipeline for producing seemingly toxic prompts to assess model performance. In contrast, our work
      evaluates the over-refusal problem from a contextualization perspective.
528
529
530   7     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      This paper emphasizes the importance of context in LLM safety evaluation by proposing the
533
      Context-Aware Safety Evaluation Benchmark (CASE-Bench). CASE-Bench formalizes context de-
534   scriptions using CI theory and provides non-binary safety ratings reflecting the degree of uncertainty
535   in human judgments. Extensive analyses performed on CASE-Bench demonstrate substantial and
536   significant influence of context on human judgments. Notable mismatches between human and LLM
537   judgments due to over-refusal were also reflected, emphasizing the necessity and challenges in con-
538   sidering context in LLM safety judgments. A discussion of the limitations and potential directions
539   for future work is provided in Appendix A, including query selection, practical considerations of
      context acquisition and verification in the real world, and potential jailbreaking, etc.

      A    D ISCUSSION OF L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS

758
      In this section, we provide the detailed limitations of CASE-Bench and outline potential directions
759   for future work.
760
761   In the query selection process, we adapted queries from Sorry-Bench (Xie et al., 2024), leveraging its
762
      balanced taxonomy and other advantages, as detailed in Section 4.1. However, some queries remain
      inherently unsafe, particularly those that do not exhibit meaningful behavioural changes even when
763
      provided with safe contexts (as shown in Fig. 4). Attempting to ensure safety or create entirely

      safe contexts for such queries proves both impractical and inefficient. Future work may address this
765   limitation by incorporating datasets that include predominantly unsafe or controversial queries or by
766   curating new datasets specifically designed for this purpose.
767
768
      CASE-Bench assumes that the context provided is verified and reliable. Future research could ex-
      plore alternative methods for retrieving contextual information using the CI framework. For in-
769
      stance, verified recipient information and recipient background details could be sourced directly
770
      from administrators, or context could be extracted from multimodal inputs such as documents,
771   videos, or environmental recordings, considering the promising direction of LLMs continuing to
772   evolve and acquire robust multimodal capabilities.
773
774
      With such verified contextual information, it may also be possible to counteract jailbreaking or injec-
      tion attacks by identifying and rejecting fabricated information that contradicts the verified context.
775
      Enhanced context comprehension would allow LLMs to make more accurate safety judgments, even
776
      in adversarial settings.
777
778   Furthermore, the advanced contextual understanding exhibited by modern LLMs could prioritize
779   their use in domain-specific or task-specific agents, enabling more reliable decision-making aligned
780
      with safety-critical requirements. Future research should explore these possibilities to further en-
      hance the robustness and versatility of CASE-Bench.

794   or justifications, potentially reducing the perception of risk. This framing effect could lead some

       To ensure the quality and reliability of the generated contexts, a systematic revision process was
       carried out by two researchers. First, both researchers independently reviewed each context gener-
1046
       ated by GPT-4o, evaluating them for accuracy, coherence, and alignment with the intended safety
1047
       category (safe or unsafe). Regular discussions were held to compare revisions and resolve any dis-
1048   agreements. This collaborative process allowed for diverse insights and thorough cross-checking.
1049   During these discussions, multiple cycles of feedback and revisions were implemented. One re-
1050   searcher proposed changes, which were then reviewed and refined by the other, focusing on clarity,
1051   relevance, and the suitability of the context. In cases where GPT-4o generated overly conservative
1052   or inaccurate unsafe contexts — due to its built-in safety mechanisms — the researchers manually
1053   replaced the content with more appropriate and representative contexts.
1054   After completing the initial revisions, a third-party reviewer, a domain expert with extensive knowl-
1055   edge of ethics and AI safety, was enlisted to perform a final evaluation. This expert, with significant
1056   experience in the field, conducted a thorough review of all contexts to ensure they adhered to the
1057   study’s standards and accurately reflected the intended safe and unsafe scenarios. Following this
1058   expert review, both researchers conducted a final check to confirm consistency across all contexts
1059   and alignment with the predefined structure. The manual revision phase took place from June to
1060   August 2024.

1205   more annotators perceiving the chatbot’s responses as safe in some cases.
1206   For tasks included in this category, when the unsafe context was added, the impact of the manu-
1207   ally revised unsafe context was less detrimental than expected. In certain tasks, more participants
1208   actually rated the chatbot’s responses as safe under these contexts. This suggests that the harmful
1209   nature of some queries may lead participants to remain cautious and hesitant to change their opinion,
1210   regardless of the context provided.
1211   The analysis indicates that for inherently harmful queries, participants are reluctant to shift their
1212   perception and continue to be cautious in their judgments of whether the chatbot should respond.
1213   Additionally, for manually crafted unsafe contexts, the results provide valuable insights for future
1214   iterations. It highlights the need to refine and improve the query contexts in subsequent experiments
1215   to ensure a more accurate assessment of chatbot safety.
