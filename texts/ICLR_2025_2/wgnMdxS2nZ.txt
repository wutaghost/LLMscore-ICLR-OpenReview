011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The integration of fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) in federated learning (FL)
014            has led to significant advances in data privacy. However, during the aggregation
015
               phase, it often results in performance degradation of the aggregated model, hin-
               dering the development of robust representational generalization. In this work,
016
               we propose a novel multimodal quantum federated learning framework that uti-
017
               lizes quantum computing to counteract the performance drop resulting from FHE.
018            For the first time in FL, our framework combines a multimodal quantum mix-
019            ture of experts (MQMoE) model with FHE, incorporating multimodal datasets for
020            enriched representation and task-specific learning. Our MQMoE framework en-
021            hances performance on multimodal datasets and combined genomics and brain
022            MRI scans, especially for underrepresented categories. Our results also demon-
023            strate that the quantum-enhanced approach mitigates the performance degradation
024            associated with FHE and improves classification accuracy across diverse datasets,
025            validating the potential of quantum interventions in enhancing privacy in FL.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a powerful paradigm for collaboratively training machine
030
      learning models across decentralized data sources (McMahan et al. (2023)), allowing multiple
031   clients to enhance model performance while preserving data privacy and complying with regula-
032   tions such as the general data protection regulation (GDPR). FL alone is insufficient to fully protect
033   data privacy, as the global model and updates shared between clients and the central server remain
034   vulnerable to inference attacks. For instance, membership inference attacks use the model param-
035   eters or gradients to deduce whether a specific data sample was part of the training dataset (Shokri
036   et al. (2017)). Similarly, attribute inference attacks attempt to uncover sensitive attributes of in-
037   dividual data samples by exploiting correlations between model updates and data features (Melis
038   et al. (2019)). Fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) was proposed by Gentry (2009) to mitigate
039
      vulnerabilities by allowing computations on encrypted data without decryption, ensuring that sensi-
      tive information remains secure even during model training and aggregation within new gen FL. It
040
      works well against gradient leakage attacks, in which hackers try to recover private information by
041
      looking at the gradients that are sent back and forth during training (Phong et al. (2018)). Adding
042   FHE to FL adds a lot of extra work to computers, causing delays and less accurate models during
043   the aggregation phase (Zhang et al. (2023)). Consequently, while FHE protects data privacy, it can
044   hinder the overall performance of FL systems, which is crucial for applications requiring high accu-
045   racy or real-time predictions. Despite the growing interest in multimodal FL, which aims to leverage
046   various types of data from multiple clients, there exists a gap in research that addresses the unique
047   challenges posed by FHE in this context (Gong et al. (2024a)). Furthermore, the application of quan-
048   tum computing (QC) to enhance FL frameworks has been largely unexplored. QC has the potential
049   to accelerate specific computations, which could alleviate performance bottlenecks introduced by
050
      FHE (Dutta et al. (2024)).
051   To mitigate the performance degradation that occurs during model aggregation, we propose a novel
052   integration of QC with FHE in FL, taking into account the nature of the problem. Our method
053   employs QC to counteract the model degradation resulting from FHE, allowing for an improved
      collection of model updates while protecting privacy, and for the first time, we present a novel

      multimodal quantum mixture of experts (MQMoE) model within the FL framework that employs
055   FHE. We design this architecture to handle diverse multimodal data with MoE (Yu et al. (2023a))
056   from various clients, enhancing representational and task-specific learning performance.

      integrates quantum machine learning (QML) with QFL principles (Biamonte et al. (2017)). The

      experiments were conducted across genomics and healthcare datasets, laying the groundwork for in-
089
      vestigations into quantum-enhanced learning methods in privacy-sensitive domains. This has been
090   further advanced by efforts in QFL, which are creating new avenues in QML. In particular, studies
091   like Chen & Yoo (2021); Chehimi & Saad (2022) have emphasized using QFL in quantum systems
092   and decentralized data frameworks. Applications in healthcare and other domains, using both quan-
093   tum hardware and classical simulations, have shown promising results (Lusnig et al. (2024); Bhatia
094   & Bernal Neira (2024); Innan et al. (2024b); Javeed et al. (2024); Bhatia et al. (2023); Kais et al.

096   tion persist, as noted by Larasati et al. (2022); Ren et al. (2023); Gurung et al. (2023), highlighting
097   ongoing efforts to address these issues.
098   Privacy preservation in FL: Rahulamathavan et al. (2023) proposed FheFL, which incorporates a
099   multi-key additive homomorphic encryption scheme to mitigate privacy risks associated with gradi-
100   ent sharing in FL. The experimental results confirmed that FheFL maintains model accuracy while
101   providing robust security against potential data breaches, emphasizing the necessity of integrating
102   FHE techniques into federated learning frameworks to enhance data privacy. FedSHE was created
103   by Pan et al. (2024). It is a useful FL scheme that uses adaptive segmented CKKS homomorphic
104
      encryption to keep gradients safe from leakage attacks. Their analysis of security parameters under-
      scores the trade-offs between correctness, efficiency, and security in FL environments.
105
106   Multimodal data handling in FL: Yu et al. (2023b) developed CreamFL, a framework designed
107   for multimodal FL. This method enables clients with heterogeneous model architectures to con-
      tribute to a unified global model without sharing their private data. By implementing a contrastive

119   Consider a FL setup with K clients, each with local data Dk , where k ∈ {1, 2, . . . , K}. Each client
120   trains a local model with parameters wkt and shares the encrypted updates E(wkt ) with a central
                                                                                             1
                                                                                               PK
121   server. During the aggregation step, the global update is computed as E(wt+1 ) = K                 t
                                                                                                k=1 E(wk ),
122   ensuring data privacy by performing operations on encrypted weights without decryption.
123   FHE schemes, like Cheon-Kim-Kim-Song (CKKS), allow both homomorphic addition and multi-
124   plication with the help of cyclotomic polynomial ring topologies. For homomorphic addition, the
125   server computes the encrypted sum E(w1 +w2 ) = E(w1 )+E(w2 ). The decryption at the client side
126   yields the sum w1 + w2 ≈ Dec(E(w1 ) + E(w2 )). For homomorphic multiplication, the encrypted
127   product of two ciphertexts E(w1 · w2 ) expands into three components. To control ciphertext growth,
128   a re-linearization step reduces the result to two components, i.e., Relin(E(w1 ) · E(w2 )).
129   Despite the privacy benefits, the use of FHE leads to significant challenges like computational
130   overhead and model accuracy. The encryption and decryption processes, along with operations
131   on encrypted data, introduce noise and quantization errors which accumulate during operations
132   like multiplications, resulting in the degradation of the decrypted model’s accuracy wt+1 ≈
133   w1 + w2 + · · · + wK . Therefore, the primary challenge is to mitigate this accuracy degradation,
134   especially when multiple rounds of homomorphic operations are performed during model aggrega-
135
      tion in FL. To address this, we propose leveraging QC to reduce noise accumulation, thus preserving
      model accuracy while maintaining the privacy guarantees of FHE.

144
      with QFL, multimodal quantum federated learning (MQFL), and the novel multimodal quantum

      6    C ONCLUSION
517
518   Our study has shown that FL, despite its advantages for collaborative model training across de-
519   centralized data sources, faces significant challenges in data privacy and computational efficiency.
520   These challenges are particularly critical when high accuracy and quick processing are required,
521   such as in real-time applications. The introduction of FHE has proven effective in enhancing data
522   privacy by allowing computations on encrypted data. However, FHE increases the computational
523   load significantly, which can reduce the performance and speed of model training. To tackle these
524   issues, our research introduces an integration of QC with FL and FHE. This new approach has shown
525   potential in reducing FHE’s performance drawbacks and efficiently managing complex, multimodal
526
      datasets. Our MQMoE framework demonstrates how QC can improve the learning performance
      and privacy of federated learning systems. The results from various experimental setups, including
527
      classical and quantum models, indicate that while quantum solutions add complexity, they offer po-
528
      tential computational advantages. These findings suggest that quantum-enhanced federated learning
529   could be a viable solution for handling large-scale, privacy-sensitive applications. In summary, this
530   research points out the limitations of current FL systems and proposes a novel solution that uses QC
531   to enhance security and efficiency. While QC is not without its challenges, it represents a promis-
532   ing start in the search for more effective FL technologies. This could be particularly valuable in
533   fields where privacy and quick data processing are essential, marking a significant step forward in
534   developing advanced FL technologies.
