025                                                  A BSTRACT
026
027           In recent years, zero-shot and few-shot learning in visual grounding have garnered
028           considerable attention, largely due to the success of large-scale vision-language
029           pre-training on expansive datasets such as LAION-5B and DataComp-1B. How-
030           ever, the continuous expansion of these datasets presents significant challenges,
031           particularly with respect to data availability and computational overhead, thus cre-
032
              ating a bottleneck in the advancement of low-shot learning capabilities. In this
              paper, we propose a novel approach, Interpretative MAsking with Gaussian Ra-
033
              diation ModEling, aimed at enhancing vocabulary grounding in low-shot learning
034
              scenarios without necessitating an increase in dataset size. Drawing inspiration
035           from cognitive science and the recent success of masked autoencoders (MAE), our
036           method leverages adaptive masking on salient regions of the feature maps gener-
037           ated by the vision backbone. This enables the model to learn robust, generalized
038           representations through the reconstruction of occluded information, thereby facili-
039           tating effective attention to both local and global features. We evaluate the efficacy
040           of our approach on benchmark datasets, including COCO and ODinW, demon-
041           strating its superior performance in zero-shot and few-shot tasks. Experimental
042           results consistently show that IMAGE outperforms baseline models, achieving
043
              enhanced generalization and improved performance in low-shot scenarios. These
              findings highlight the potential of adaptive feature manipulation through attention
044
              mechanisms and Gaussian modeling as a promising alternative to approaches that
045
              rely on the continual scaling of dataset sizes for the advancement of zero-shot and
046           few-shot learning.

049   1   I NTRODUCTION
050
051   ”To see the world in a grain of sand,” – William Blake, Auguries of Innocence
052
053   When observing an object, humans naturally focus on key details to grasp its essence. Masking these
      key features in visual tasks may encourage models to learn more robust representations, potentially


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      enhancing performance. Low-shot object grounding has gained significant attention due to its abil-
055   ity to reduce reliance on large labeled datasets. The capacity to ground and recognize novel objects
056   with minimal examples is particularly valuable in applications like autonomous driving, where sys-
057   tems must handle rare or unseen situations with limited data Rezaei & Shahidi (2020). Additionally,
058   low-shot grounding aids embodied AI in associating new concepts or objects within interactive en-
059   vironments with few labeled examples Varley et al. (2024). Recent vision-language models, such
060   as CLIP Radford et al. (2021), have achieved notable success in bridging visual and textual modali-
061   ties by leveraging large-scale pre-training. However, despite their strong performance, these models
062   remain data-hungry, requiring substantial labeled data to adapt to new scenes. This reliance limits
063
      their utility in scenarios where data collection is challenging or impractical.
064   In visual grounding, recent efforts to enhance open-vocabulary detection have integrated textual
065   prompts and multimodal fusion into object detection frameworks. Models like GLIP Li et al. (2022),
066   YOLO-world Cheng et al. (2024), and Grounding DINO Liu et al. (2023) extend traditional detectors
067   by incorporating language understanding, enabling object detection based on textual descriptions.
068   While these approaches have advanced zero-shot grounding, they still demand extensive data to
069
      perform effectively. Furthermore, these models often struggle in complex scenes where visual cues
      are occluded or misaligned with textual descriptions.
070
071   These limitations highlight a critical issue: current multimodal models struggle to generalize from
072   seen to unseen categories without explicit training examples. This challenge is compounded by
073   their reliance on static visual cues and the lack of dynamic reasoning, as existing methods prioritize
074   dataset expansion over teaching models to effectively ”interpret” images. There are some methods
075
      such as Masked Autoencoder (MAE) He et al. (2022) and FLIP Li et al. (2023) attempt to improve
      the performance of a model by reconstructing the masked portion of the input data. However, this
076
      randomized masking approach suffers from poor interpretability, determinism and effect enhance-
077
      ment.
078
079   To address these limitations in a better way, we propose IMAGE, a novel method that introduces
080   an adaptive masking strategy on features within the framework. Inspired by the human ability to
081
      infer missing information and focus attention dynamically, IMAGE mirrors cognitive processes in
      human reasoning. By deploying an adaptive mask scheme, IMAGE enables the model to learn more
082
      robust representations and focus on discriminative features.(eg. it makes more sense to identify cats
083
      by focusing on silhouette features rather than colors). In a word, IMAGE allows the model to learn
084   how to “heed” objects rather than mechanically scanning and recognizing them.
085
086   We validate our method on datasets such as COCO Lin et al. (2014) and ODinW, and test it in
087
      both zero-Shot and few-Shot situations. Utilizing IMAGE’s adaptive masking strategy, we achieve
      measurable improvements in both few-shot and zero-shot detection accuracy without significant
088
      computational overhead. Extrinsically, our method reduces the dependence on ever-larger datasets.
089
      Intrinsically, it provides a theoretical based way to empower existing detection models with robust
090   learning and reasoning abilities. Our contributions are as follows:
091
092          • We introduce IMAGE, a novel adaptive masking framework that enhances low-shot visual
093            grounding by enabling models to focus on important object features and improve reasoning
094            capabilities, leading to more robust representations.
095          • We demonstrate theoretically and empirically that adaptive masking improves model ro-
096            bustness and generalization to unseen datasets, effectively addressing fundamental chal-
097            lenges in zero-shot and few-shot learning without relying on larger datasets.
098          • We provide empirical evidence on standard benchmarks showing that IMAGE outperforms
099            baseline models and random masking strategies in low-shot settings, enhancing both few-
100            shot and zero-shot performance with minimal computational overhead.

106   Zero-Shot and Few-Shot Learning in Visual Grounding Low-shot learning, especially Zero-
107   shot learning (ZSL), aims to recognize objects from unseen classes by leveraging knowledge transfer
      from seen classes Lampert et al. (2009); Farhadi et al. (2009); Socher et al. (2013). Early approaches


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      in ZSL for visual grounding focused on attribute-based methods and semantic embeddings to relate
109   seen and unseen classes Akata et al. (2015); Xian et al. (2018). With the advent of large-scale
110   vision-language models like CLIP Radford et al. (2021), recent works have utilized these pretrained
111   models for zero-shot grounding tasks Gu et al. (2021); Li et al. (2022). However, these methods often
112   rely on extensive datasets for pre-training and fine-tuning, limiting their scalability and practicality.
113   Few-shot learning, on the other hand, seeks to learn new concepts from a small number of labeled
114   examples Fei-Fei et al. (2006); Snell et al. (2017). In visual grounding, few-shot learning approaches
115   have been developed to enhance generalization to new classes with limited annotated data Kang et al.
116   (2019); Sun et al. (2021). Despite progress, many few-shot methods struggle with overfitting due
117
      to data scarcity and often require complex meta-learning frameworks Finn et al. (2017); Li et al.
      (2019).
118
119
120   Attention Mechanisms and Masking Strategies in Vision Models Attention mechanisms have
121   become integral in deep learning models for their ability to focus on relevant parts of the input
122   data Bahdanau et al. (2015); Vaswani et al. (2017). In vision transformers, self-attention enables the
123   modeling of global dependencies, enhancing feature representations Dosovitskiy et al. (2021); Liu
124   et al. (2021). In the self-supervised learning area, masking parts of the input data has been an effec-
125
      tive technique to improve feature representations. Methods like Masked Autoencoders (MAE) He
      et al. (2022) mask random patches of the input image and train the model to reconstruct them.
126
      BEiT Bao et al. (2021) extends this idea by using a tokenizer to create discrete tokens for masked
127
      patch prediction. However, these methods typically use random masking, which does not guide the
128   model to focus on important features.
129
130
131   Radiance Field Modeling and Gaussian Approaches Radiance fields have been employed in
132   computer vision and graphics to model the way light interacts with surfaces, enabling high-fidelity
133   scene reconstruction Mildenhall et al. (2020); Niemeyer et al. (2020). Neural Radiance Fields
134
      (NeRF) Mildenhall et al. (2020) represent scenes using continuous volumetric radiance fields pa-
      rameterized by neural networks. Gaussian modeling of radiance fields allows for smooth represen-
135
      tations and has been utilized in various applications Wang et al. (2021); Kim et al. (2022). Similarly,
136
      Zhou et al. Zhou et al. (2016) demonstrated that global average pooling enables CNNs to localize
137   discriminative regions without explicit localization training. In our work, we employ a dynamic
138   Gaussian modeling approach to represent the importance prior distribution of the feature map. This
139   approach allows us to flexibly apply an adaptive mask to the feature map, instead of using rigid
140   thresholding, thereby enhancing the model’s focus on salient regions.

143   3     M ETHOD
144
145   IMAGE aims to enhance zero-shot and few-shot visual grounding without relying on large-scale
146   datasets. Inspired by Masked Autoencoders (MAE), IMAGE leverages adaptive masking techniques
147   that emphasize salient regions within an image’s feature map, compelling the model to infer missing
148   information and learn robust, generalized representations. As is shown in Fig. 2. IMAGE consists
149   of two primary components: the Importance Prior Generation Block (θp ), which estimates the
150   importance of image patches based on their relationships within the feature map, and the Adaptive
151   Mask Generation Block (θm ), which creates adaptive masks guided by the importance prior to
152   direct the model’s attention during training. Given an input image, a pretrained Swin-Transformer
153
      backbone network processes it to produce hierarchical feature maps at multiple scales, denoted as
      {F1 , F2 , F3 , F4 }, where each feature map Fi has dimensions (B, Ci , Hi , Wi ), representing batch
154
      size B, number of channels Ci , and spatial dimensions Hi × Wi at scale i. IMAGE applies adaptive
155
      masking on these feature maps, focusing the model’s attention on the most relevant regions, thereby
156   improving its reasoning capabilities and generalization performance.

      Dataset We conduct experiments on the COCO and ODinW dataset. For training and evaluation
345   in a close-set setting, we use the COCO 2017 dataset. The training set (train2017) contains approx-
346   imately 118,000 images with 80 object categories, and the validation set (val2017) consists of about
347   5,000 images. To assess zero-shot detection capabilities, we utilize the ODinW datasets, specifically
348   the ODinW 13 and ODinW 35 subsets. These datasets comprise images from various domains and
349   contain object categories not present in the COCO training set, making them suitable for evaluating
350   zero-shot performance. For few-shot experiments, we create subsets of the COCO train2017 dataset
351   by randomly selecting 5%, 10%, 20%, and 30% of the data.
352
353   Evaluation Metrics We assess the performance of the proposed method using the following met-
354   rics: (1) Average Precision (AP): Following the standard COCO evaluation protocol, we report the
355   Average Precision at Intersection-over-Union (IoU) thresholds ranging from 0.5 to 0.95, denoted as
356
      AP@[0.5:0.95]. (2) Zero-Shot Detection: For the ODinW datasets, we use mean Average Precision
      (mAP) as the primary metric to evaluate the model’s zero-shot detection performance. (3) Few-Shot
357
      Performance: To assess generalization in few-shot settings, we report AP on the COCO val2017
358
      set, evaluating the model’s ability to learn from limited data.
359
360
      Implementation Details Our model is based on the Grounding DINO framework, incorporating
361
      a Swin-T backbone. The adaptive masking modules are integrated after the backbone’s feature
362   extraction stages, as described in Section 3. Different mask rates are applied to the four feature layers
363   from the Swin-T backbone, with initial mask rates set to 20%, 30%, 40%, and 50%, respectively. In
364   RF-GAM module, The parameter k0 in the Gaussian modeling starts at 0.5 and decays smoothly to
365   near zero over all epochs to facilitate progressive learning.

520   5   C ONCLUSION
521
522   In this paper, we introduced IMAGE (Interpretative MAsking with Gaussian Radiation ModEling),
523   a novel approach designed to enhance zero-shot and few-shot visual grounding without the need
524   for enlarging dataset sizes. Inspired by cognitive science and the success of Masked Autoencoders
525
      (MAE), our method employs adaptive masking on salient regions of the feature maps generated by
      the vision backbone, compelling the model to reconstruct occluded information and thereby learn ro-
526
      bust, generalized representations that effectively attend to both local and global features. Evaluated
527
      on benchmark datasets including COCO and ODinW, IMAGE consistently outperforms baseline
528   models, demonstrating superior performance in zero-shot and few-shot tasks. These findings un-
529   derscore the potential of adaptive feature manipulation through attention mechanisms and Gaussian
530   modeling as a promising alternative to methods relying on dataset scaling for advancing low-shot
531   learning capabilities.
532
      The challenges posed by complex real-world visual data, such as severe occlusions and missing
533
      key object features, present opportunities to further enhance our approach. Future work could fo-
534   cus on integrating more sophisticated data augmentation techniques or incorporating multimodal
535   data—such as depth information or temporal cues—to improve the model’s ability to generalize
536   from incomplete visual inputs. Additionally, applying our adaptive masking strategy to other ar-
537   eas like video understanding or 3D vision may extend its benefits. A deeper investigation into the
538   interplay between adaptive masking, attention mechanisms, and Gaussian modeling may provide
539   valuable insights, potentially leading to further advancements in zero-shot and few-shot learning
      across various domains.
