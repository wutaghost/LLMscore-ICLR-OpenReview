009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Prototype Learning methods provide an interpretable alternative to black-box deep
012            learning models. Approaches such as ProtoPNet learn, which part of a test image
013            “look like” known prototypical parts from training images, combining predictive
014            power with the inherent interpretability of case-based reasoning. However, exist-
015            ing approaches have two main drawbacks: A) They rely solely on deterministic
016            similarity scores without statistical confidence. B) The prototypes are learned in
017            a black-box manner without human input. This work introduces HyperPg, a new
018
               prototype representation leveraging Gaussian distributions on a hypersphere in la-
               tent space, with learnable mean and variance. HyperPg prototypes adapt to the
019
               spread of clusters in the latent space and output likelihood scores. The new archi-
020
               tecture, HyperPgNet, leverages HyperPg to learn prototypes aligned with human
021            concepts from pixel-level annotations. Consequently, each prototype represents
022            a specific concept such as color, image texture, or part of the image subject. A
023            concept extraction pipeline built on foundation models provides pixel-level anno-
024            tations, significantly reducing human labeling effort. Experiments on CUB-200-
025            2011 and Stanford Cars datasets demonstrate that HyperPgNet outperforms other
026            prototype learning architectures while using fewer parameters and training steps.
027            Additionally, the concept-aligned HyperPg prototypes are learned transparently,
028            enhancing model interpretability.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Deep Learning has achieved high accuracy in many computer vision tasks. However, the decision-
      making processes of these models lack transparency and interpretability, making deployment in
034
      safety-critical areas challenging. Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) seeks to develop inter-
035
      pretability methods to open the black-box reasoning processes of these models and increase trust in
036   their decisions.
037
038   XAI methods can be broadly divided into two categories: First, Post-Hoc Methods like LIME
039
      (Ribeiro et al., 2016), SHAP (Lundberg & Lee, 2017) or GradCAM Selvaraju et al. (2017) offer
      explanations for predictions without requiring retraining. While applicable in many scenarios, post-
040
      hoc methods may not actually align with the models’ decision making processes, potentially leading
041
      to interpretations that are not entirely faithful (Rudin, 2019). Second, inherently interpretable meth-
042   ods provide built-in, case-based reasoning processes. For instance, small decision trees are inher-
043   ently interpretable because their reasoning can be easily understood as a series of if-else statements
044   (Molnar, 2020). However, they are constrained in their representational power.
045
      Deep Prototype Learning Architectures such as ProtoPNet (Chen et al., 2019) and its derivatives
046
      (e.g., Rymarczyk et al., 2020; Donnelly et al., 2021; Sacha et al., 2023) integrate inherent inter-
047
      pretability into deep learning models through a prototype layer. Each neuron in this layer represents
048   a prototype, storing a latent feature vector. The model’s predictions are based on the distances be-
049   tween sample features and prototype parameters, for example by computing the L2 -distance. How-
050   ever, these deterministic similarity scores do not include statistical information like confidence. To
051   include such contextual information, prototypes could be modeled as probability distributions, like
052   Gaussian distributions. Yet, recent prototype architectures favor the cosine hypersphere as feature
053   space for its classification advantages (Mettes et al., 2019), where defining a Gaussian distribution
      analogue is challenging (Hillen et al., 2017).

054
      The prototypes in ProtoPNet and its successors are learned in an opaque manner via backpropaga-
055   tion. After training, the model “pushes” the learned prototypes on the embedding of the most similar
056   known training sample (Chen et al., 2019). However, these prototypes are learned without human
057   control and do not incorporate domain knowledge. A new training regime is needed to aligned
058   prototypes with human-defined concepts. The main contributions of this paper are as follows:
059
060          • HyperPg: A new prototype representation with learned parameters anchor α, mean µ and
061            standard deviation σ. This representation models a Gaussian distribution over cosine sim-
062
               ilarities, thereby projecting a Gaussian distribution on the surface of a hypersphere. Hy-
               perPg’s similarity score is based on the Gaussian’s probability density function and adapts
063
               its size through a learned standard deviation.
064
065          • HyperPgNet: A new prototype learning architecture built on HyperPg prototypes. Hyper-
066            PgNet learns prototypes aligned with human-defined concepts using pixel level annotations.
067
               These concepts describe features such as color and patterns of birds or car parts.
068          • Concept Extraction Pipeline: Based on Grounding DINO (Liu et al., 2023) and SAM2
069            (Ravi et al., 2024), this pipeline provides pixel-level concept annotations at scale.
070          • Classification Experiments: HyperPgNet is compared with other prototype learning archi-
071            tectures like ProtoPNet on the CUB-200-2011 (Wah et al., 2011) and Stanford Cars (Krause
072            et al., 2013) datasets. HyperPgNet outperforms the other models with fewer learned proto-
073            types, while infusing each prototype with more meaning.

075   2   R ELATED W ORK
076
077
      Prototype Learning. In image classification, prototype learning approaches using autoencoders
078   provide high interpretability by reconstructing learned prototypes from latent space back to the im-
079   age space (Li et al., 2018). However, these approaches are limited in their performance because each
080   prototype must represent the entire image. ProtoPNet (Chen et al., 2019) introduced the idea of pro-
081   totypical parts. In this setting, each prototype is a latent patch of the input image, commonly a 1 × 1
082   latent patch. The prototypes are each associated with a single class and learned via backpropagation
083   without additional information.
084   HyperPgNet builds on the idea of prototypical parts, but learns the prototypes in a transparent,
085   concept-aligned manner. The prototypes are not class exclusive, but class shared. Each prototype
086   corresponds to a human defined concept. To provide the required concept annotations, we propose
087   a labeling pipeline based on foundation models such as DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2024) and SAM2
088   (Ravi et al., 2024). HyperPgNet learns the concept-aligned prototypes by only adapting the loss
089   functions and providing additional annotations, without requiring changes to the feature encoder
090   like PIPNet (Nauta & Seifert, 2023) or Lucid-PPN (Pach et al., 2024), which uses a hybrid input
091   head to disentangle prototype color and shape.
092   Multiple successors build on the idea of ProtoPNet. ProtoPShare (Rymarczyk et al., 2020) and
093   ProtoPool (Rymarczyk et al., 2022) use additional optimizations to learn class-shared prototypical
094   parts. Deformable ProtoPNet (Donnelly et al., 2021) learns a mixture of prototypical parts with
095   dynamic spatial arrangement. Other work replace the linear output layer of ProtoPNet with other in-
096   terpretable models: ProtoKNN (Ukai et al., 2023) uses a k-nearest neighbor classifier and ProtoTree
097   (Nauta et al., 2021) employs a decision tree.
098   All these models change how the prototype activations are further processed. However, they all
099   rely on the point-based prototype formulation introduced by ProtoPNet based on the L2 or cosine
100   similarity. HyperPgNet proposes a novel prototype formulation based on Gaussian distributions on
101   the hypersphere, without changing the downstream processing. This probabilistic view with learned
102   mean and variance allows HyperPgNet to learn prototypes with different degrees of specialization.
103   Furthermore, the statistical confidence derived from the learned variance could be integrate in further
104
      downstream processing.
105   The view of prototype learning as clustering in latent space was introduced for image segmentation
106   (Zhou et al., 2022). The image patches are points in latent space with the prototypes as cluster
107   centers. ProtoGMM (Moradinasab et al., 2024) and MGProto (Wang et al., 2023) build on this idea
      to model the prototypes as Gaussian mixture models for image segmentation.

125   classification tasks (Mettes et al., 2019) and also been applied in recent prototype learning works

      Concept-Based Methods. Testing with Concept Activation Vectors (TCAV) (Kim et al., 2018) is
129   a post-hoc method that aligns model activations with human-understandable concepts. In image
130   classification, TCAV measures model activations on images representing a specific concept like
131   “striped” and compares them to activations on a random image set. Concept Activation Regions
132   (CAR) (Crabbé & van der Schaar, 2022) relax TCAV’s assumption of linear separability in latent
133   space by using support vector machines and the kernel trick. Another post-hoc approach, CRAFT
134   (Fel et al., 2023), generates concept attribution maps by identifying relevant concepts from the
135   training set via Non-Negative Matrix Factorization (NMF) and backpropagating with GradCAM
136   (Selvaraju et al., 2017).
137   Instead of analyzing the learned concepts post-hoc, some approaches integrate concept learning
138   into the classifier. MCPNet (Wang et al., 2024) extracts concepts from multiple layers by splitting
139   feature maps along the channel dimension, learning concepts during training and at multiple scales.
140   Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) (Koh et al., 2020) augment black-box models to predict pre-
141   defined concept labels in an intermediate step. In this manner, CBMs behave like two sequential
142   black box models and their internal reasoning remains opaque.
143   HyperPgNet combines the concept-aligned learning approach of CBM with interpretable prototype
144   learning. HyperPgNet learns multiple HyperPg prototypes per concept, instead of a fully connected
145   concept prediction layer. A novel concept extraction pipeline, built on foundation models, provides
146   pixel level annotations at scale. By using those annotations to learn concept prototypes, HyperPgNet
147   avoids the ambiguity of aligning post-hoc explanations with the model’s internal reasoning.

152   Prototype Learning is an inherently interpretable machine learning method. The reasoning process is
153   based on the similarity scores of the inputs to the prototypes, retained representations of the training
154   data. For example, a K-Nearest Neighbor (KNN) model is a prototype learning approach with the
155   identity function for representation and an unlimited number of prototypes. In contrast, a Gaussian
156   Mixture Model (GMM) uses a mean representation but restricts the number of prototypes to the
157   number of mixture components.
158   Prototype learning for deep neural networks involves finding structures in latent space representa-
159   tions. This section provides an overview of existing methods, which are also illustrated in Fig. 1.
160   Prior work uses point-based prototypes, computing similarity scores relative to a single point in la-
161   tent space. Aligning prototypes with human-labeled concepts requires a more powerful prototype
      representation, leading to the introduction of HyperPg - Prototypical Gaussians on the Hypersphere.

290   The original ProtoPNet implementation uses three loss functions: a task specific loss like crossen-
291   tropy for classification, a cluster loss to increase compactness within a class’s cluster, and a sepa-
292   ration loss to increase distances between different prototype clusters. However, the prototypes of
293   ProtoPNet and its successors are learned in a black-box manner.
294
      HyperPgNet is a new inherently interpretable deep learning approach built on HyperPg prototypes.
295   It introduces a “Right for the Right Concept” loss, inspired by “Right for the Right Reasons” (Ross
296   et al., 2017), to restrict the learned prototypes to human-defined concepts. This focus enhances the
297   interpretability and minimizes the influence of confounding factors. This section first provides an
298   overview of the used prototype learning losses, then introduces the Right for the Right Concept loss,
299   and finally discusses the overall network architecture and final multi-objective loss.

412   masks based on the bounding boxes. This automated pipeline labeled the 16k images of Stanford

      7     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      This work introduces HyperPg, a new prototype representation learning a probability distribution on
      the surface of a hypersphere in latent space. HyperPg prototypes adapt to the variance of clusters
532
      in latent space and improve training time and accuracy compared to other prototype formulations.
533
      HyperPgNet leverages HyperPg to learn human-defined concept prototypes instead of black-box
534   optimized prototypes. The combination of probabilistic prototypes on the hypersphere and concept-
535   aligned prototypes allows HyperPgNet to outperform other prototype learning approaches with re-
536   gards to accuracy and interpretability. One limitation HyperPgNet faces are slightly higher computa-
537   tional requirements due to the inclusion of concept annotations and LRRC during training. However,
538   this is offset by faster convergence. Coupled with the increased transparency and interpretability of
539   the model, this makes HyperPgNet a strong contender for scenarios with higher requirements for
      model trust and safety, like medical applications or human-robot interaction.

      C.1   L ATENT S PACE S TRUCTURE
936
937   Prototype learning is based on learning structures in the latent space. HyperPg specifically learns
938   Gaussian distributions on the surface of a hypersphere in a high-dimensional latent space. Dimen-
939   sionality reduction techniques like UMAP (McInnes et al., 2018) aim to preserve global and local
940   structures from a high dimensional space when projecting into a low dimensional one. UMAP sup-
941   ports multiple distance metrics, including hyperspheric manifold distances, thus retaining some of
942   the high dimensional structure when projecting onto a 3D sphere.
943   Fig. 13 illustrates UMAP projections of HyperPg concept-aligned prototypes trained on Stanford
944   Cars. This visualization indicates that HyperPgNet is able to disentangle the different concepts in
945   latent space, as the projection shows no overlap of the different clusters. When trained with LRRC ,
946   the prototypes are packed closer together. This could indicate, that the chosen hyper parameter of 20
947   prototypes per concept is higher than required as not all concept embeddings have the same diversity
948   in the latent space.
949   Fig. 14 illustrates one UMAP projection of HyperPg concept-aligned prototypes trained on CUB-
950   200-2011. In comparison to Stanford Cars, the latent space appears less structured. This could
951   explain the higher difficulty associated with this dataset.
