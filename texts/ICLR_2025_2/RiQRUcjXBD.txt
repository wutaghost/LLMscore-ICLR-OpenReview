009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                The exponential growth of knowledge and the increasing complexity of interdisci-
012                plinary research pose significant challenges for researchers, including information
013                overload and difficulties in exploring novel ideas. The advancements in large lan-
014                guage models (LLMs), such as GPT-4, have shown great potential in enhancing
015                idea proposals, but how to effectively utilize large models for reasonable idea
016                proposal has not been thoroughly explored. This paper proposes a scientific paper
017                idea proposer (SciPIP). Based on a user-provided research background, SciPIP re-
018                trieves helpful papers from a literature database while leveraging the capabilities
019
                   of LLMs to generate more novel and feasible ideas. To this end, 1) we construct
                   a literature retrieval database, extracting lots of papers’ multi-dimension informa-
020
                   tion for fast access. Then, a literature retrieval method based on semantics, entity,
021
                   and citation co-occurrences is proposed to search relevant literature from multiple
022                aspects based on the user-provided background. 2) After literature retrieval, we
023                introduce dual-path idea proposal strategies, where one path infers solutions from
024                the retrieved literature and the other path generates original ideas through model
025                brainstorming. We then combine the two to achieve a good balance between fea-
026                sibility and originality. Through extensive experiments on the natural language
027                processing (NLP) field, we demonstrate that SciPIP can retrieve citations similar
028                to those of existing top conference papers and generate many ideas consistent with
029                them. Additionally, we evaluate the originality of other ideas generated by SciPIP
030
                   using large language models, further validating the effectiveness of our proposed
                   method1 .

034   1       I NTRODUCTION
035
036   With the exponential growth of knowledge and the increasing complexity of interdisciplinary re-
037   search, machine learning researchers face significant challenges, including information overload
038
      and difficulties in exploring novel ideas. Against this backdrop, generating new ideas and inno-
      vative concepts efficiently has become a pressing need. Recent advancements in large language
039
      models (e.g., GPT-4 (Ouyang et al., 2022), LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a;b), Qwen (Bai et al.,
040
      2023; Yang et al., 2024), GLM-4 (Zeng et al., 2024), and etc), have demonstrated immense potential
041   in enhancing innovation generation. These models are not only capable of understanding and gen-
042   erating complex academic content but also excel in aligning multimodal information, constructing
043   implicit chains of thought, and uncovering non-obvious connections. Leveraging LLMs to assist
044   researchers in generating new ideas holds significant implications for improving research produc-
045   tivity and offers a theoretical foundation and practical guidance for the design of future intelligent
046   research assistants.
047   Large language model (LLM)-based idea proposers should have the ability to understand the user-
048   provided research background, autonomously retrieve relevant literature, and generate novel and
049   feasible ideas aimed at addressing problems within the given background. Some previous works
050   have proposed their methods (Wang et al., 2024; Baek et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024). However, exist-
051   ing LLM-based idea proposers still face two challenges: 1) Similar to human researchers, literature
052   retrieval is essential to inspire new ideas and avoid repetitive ideas. Nevertheless, online literature

055   mation or the existing literature, leading to incomplete and inaccurate retrieval results. 2) Scientific
056   paper ideas require both novelty and feasibility. However, it is still under-explored about how to
057   enable LLMs to generate entirely new ideas while ensuring their feasibility.
058
      To address the above challenges, we propose our Scientific Paper Idea Proposer (SciPIP). In terms
059
      of challenge 1), SciPIP first constructs a literature retrieval database. Specifically, we collect a
060
      large body of literature from the natural language processing (NLP) field and extract multiple dimen-
061   sions of information for each paper using techniques such as entity extraction, semantic encoding,
062   summarization, and citation analysis. The information is stored in the database, enabling rapid ac-
063   cess to various aspects of the literature during retrieval. Building on this database, we propose a
064   literature retrieval method based on semantics, entities, and citation co-occurrence (SEC-based
065   retrieval). In this framework, “semantics” captures the global information of a paper, “entities”
066   focus on local details, and “citation co-occurrence” reflects the hidden relationships uncovered by
067   previous researchers. By matching at these three different levels of granularity, SciPIP offers more
068   comprehensive literature retrieval.
069   To address the challenge 2), SciPIP introduces a new method for idea proposal. It first organizes
070   the retrieved literature and generates ideas inspired by the retrieved works. Subsequently, SciPIP
071   uses a brainstorming approach to generate new ideas without reference to the literature. Depending
072   on the combination of literature-based and brainstorming-based idea generation, we derive three
073   variants of SciPIP. The ideas generated by our method are further filtered and refined to enhance
074   both their novelty and feasibility.
075   Extensive experiments are conducted to evaluate both idea proposal and literature retrieval on the
076   NLP field. In the retrospective experiments, we use the backgrounds of ACL 2024 papers as in-
077   puts to test whether the models could generate the same ideas as those in the published papers, or
078   whether SciPIP could retrieve the same references as the actual citations. Additionally, we conduct
079   innovation experiments, in which the models are prompted to freely propose ideas based on a given
080   background, and the quality of the proposed ideas are assessed by an LLM in terms of novelty, fea-
081
      sibility, and etc. The experimental results demonstrate that, compared to existing methods, SciPIP
      can match more existing ideas and generate ideas with significantly greater novelty and potential.

084   2   R ELATED WORKS
085
086   Around 60 years ago, scientists began exploring scientific discoveries based on literature retrieval,
087   known as Literature-Based Discovery (LBD) (Swanson, 1986). This approach concentrated on a
088   specific, narrow type of hypothesis: the connections between pairs of concepts, often involving
089   drugs and diseases. LBD introduced the “ABC” model, positing that two concepts A and C are hy-
090   pothesized to be linked if they appear in conjunction with an intermediate concept B in the literature.
091   The advent of large language models (LLMs) has revolutionized various fields, and one of the most
092   intriguing applications is their ability to generate scientific hypotheses (Wang et al., 2024; Baek
093   et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024). LLMs, trained on extensive datasets encompassing a vast array of
094   scientific literature, possess an impressive capacity to recognize patterns and synthesize information
095   across disciplines. By leveraging their advanced natural language processing (NLP) capabilities,
096   these models can propose novel hypotheses that might not be immediately apparent to researchers.
097   The process begins with the model receiving a prompt, typically related to a specific scientific do-
098   main, which guides it to generate hypotheses grounded in existing knowledge while also incorporat-
099
      ing innovative perspectives. For example, SCIMON (Wang et al., 2024) uses retrieval of ”inspira-
      tions” from past scientific papers to generate ideas. It explicitly optimizes for novelty by iteratively
100
      comparing generated ideas with prior papers and updating them until sufficient novelty is achieved.
101
      In contrast, Research Agent (Baek et al., 2024) starts with a core paper as the primary focus and
102   expands its knowledge by connecting information over an academic graph and retrieving entities
103   from an entity-centric knowledge store based on their underlying concepts. It also leverages mul-
104   tiple Reviewing Agents to provide iterative reviews and feedback for refining the generated ideas.
105   AI Scientist leverages large language models (LLMs) to autonomously generate research ideas, im-
106   plement and execute experiments, search for related works, and produce comprehensive research
107   papers in machine learning. The AI Scientist is designed to automate the entire scientific process,
      from ideation to experimentation and iterative refinement.

      3.2     L ITERATURE RETRIEVAL AND FILTERING
181
182   Literature retrieval is an essential process for idea proposal. It should follow the rule of comprehen-
183   siveness and low-redundancy. On the one hand, a comprehensive retrieval can provide researchers
184   with instructive inspirations and avoid repetitive idea proposal. On the other hand, more retrieved
185   papers are not necessarily better because redundant papers may also introduce noise and disperse a
186   researcher’s attention. To this end, we first propose a SEC-based (Semantics, Entities, and Citation
187   co-occurrence) literature retrieval. Then, we propose a clustering-based literature filtering to pick
188   out the most helpful papers. The process is shown in Figure 2.

253   After SEC-based literature retrieval, we may get over 500 papers, so further filtering is essential to
254   pick out the most significant ones. Since we have observed that the retrieved papers often present
255   similar ideas, we hope to retain only one paper among those with similar content during the gen-
256   eration of new ideas. To achieve this, we propose clustering the papers based on cosine similarity
257   measures. Specifically, we first define the embedding of a retrieved paper as:

261
      We choose ws = wi = 0.5 in practice. Then, we apply clustering to group papers according to their
262   cosine similarity. In practice, since the semantic embeddings of all papers are pre-recorded in a
263   database, we only need to perform the similarity comparison and clustering processes. Finally, we
264   select one paper from each cluster, respectively, and make up the retrieved papers.

300   3.3.1      D IRECT IDEA PROPOSAL METHOD
301
302   As depicted in Figure 3(a), in the direct proposal method, we first retrieve papers following the
303   pipeline described in Section 3.2. Then, the user-provided background along with the retrieved
304   papers are utilized to prompt the LLM to summarize the core problem we aim to address and provide
305
      justifications. The specific prompts can be found in the Appendix A.1.
306   With the summarized problem and justifications, the LLM is prompted to generate around 10 initial
307   ideas. In the prompt, both the problem, the justification and the retrieved papers are provided. The
308   LLM is encouraged to generate clear, innovative, valid, and comprehensive ideas. The specific
309   prompts for this step can be also found in the Appendix A.1.
310   Though the prompt has declared, the initially generated ideas may still have shortcomings in terms
311   of novelty or relevance to the problem. To address this, we filter the initial ideas using prompt engi-
312   neering (prompts are illustrated in the Appendix A.1), with the primary criterion being that the ideas
313   are generated in response to the given problem. Additionally, the ideas must exhibit a high degree
314   of novelty and feasibility. During this process, each generated idea is evaluated independently, and
315   about half of them will be filtered.
316   Then, the LLM is encouraged to further improve the filtered ideas by considering their inter-
317   relationships. That is, the LLM is tasked with considering the compatibility of the ideas, ensuring
318   that it does not generate conflicting or repetitive ideas. Moreover, the LLM is required to gen-
319   erate formulas or algorithms to better elaborate the ideas if needed. The prompt is shown in the
320   Appendix A.1. Finally, about 3 to 4 refined ideas will be proposed.

337   3.3.2    D UAL - PATH IDEA PROPOSAL METHODS
338
339   We find that the directly generated ideas often rely heavily on the retrieved literature, sometimes
340   closely resembling the methods presented in those papers. They frequently involve transferring
341   approaches from other fields or making minor improvements to existing methods within the same
342   field, resulting in relatively ordinary novelty and rarely yielding breakthrough thinking.
343   Therefore, we further propose idea proposers that incorporates brainstorming, encouraging the LLM
344   to produce more novel thoughts. Specifically, brainstorming can play a role in both processes of idea
345   generation. As shown in Figure 3(b), the SciPIP-B has two paths, where one path follows the direct
346   proposal approach, while the other path uses the LLM to brainstorm possible solutions based on the
347   user-input background, outputting these as ideas. Ultimately, these ideas will be merged with those
348   generated based on the retrieved papers, filtered and refined to produce the final ideas. In this model,
349
      the results of brainstorming are independent of the generation based on retrieved papers.
350   In another approach, as shown in Figure 3(c), brainstorming generates ideas independently while
351   also being utilized in literature retrieval. Specifically, we extract entities from the brainstorming
352   results and incorporate them as part of the entity set in the literature retrieval process. With this
353   method, some keywords arising from the brainstorming will also help enhance the effectiveness
354   of literature retrieval. The ideas generated through brainstorming will also be merged with those
355
      produced after literature retrieval.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
358   4.1     E VALUATION DATASET
359
360   We collect all papers accepted by ACL 2024, including long papers, short papers, findings, and
361   workshop papers. After excluding a few PDFs that could not be correctly parsed, 1,968 papers
362   are remained for analysis. The remaining papers are processed similarly to those in the literature
363
      database in Section 3.1, with their entities, backgrounds, summaries, main ideas, detailed ideas, and
      references extracted in advance.
364
365   The experiments in this study are divided into two parts: retrospective experiments and innovation
366   experiments. Retrospective experiments refer to testing whether different algorithms can generate
367   the same ideas and literature retrieval results as the original papers on the evaluation dataset (i.e.,
368   ACL 2024 papers) with providing the background of the papers as input. In contrast, innovation
369
      experiments allow the models to freely propose new ideas, which are then evaluated from multiple
      perspectives, including novelty and feasibility.
370
371   4.2     R ETROSPECTIVE EXPERIMENTS FOR IDEA PROPOSAL .
372
373   Compared algorithms. AI Scientist (Lu et al., 2024), when given an existing idea, iteratively
374   refines the idea through multiple rounds of LLM inference. Afterward, the AI Scientist will expand
375   the Idea into a full paper. Since our algorithm only focuses on proposing ideas, we only compare
376   the idea proposal part with AI Scientist. For this purpose, we make slight adjustments to the AI

413   by SciPIP, we employ the LLMs for evaluation. For each round of comparison, we sample one idea
414   from each of 5 similarity scores and ask the LLM to rank them based on their novelty and feasibility.
415   We then record the win rate (i.e., the probability of ranking first) of ideas across different similarity
416   scores in all rounds.

534   In this paper, we propose a method for generating scientific paper ideas and demonstrate its ef-

537   match papers published at recent academic conferences but also exhibit significant potential in terms
