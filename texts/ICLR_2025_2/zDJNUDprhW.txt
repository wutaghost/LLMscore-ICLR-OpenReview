009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We study the problem of no-regret learning algorithms for general monotone and
012            smooth games and their last-iterate convergence properties. Specifically, we in-
013            vestigate the problem under bandit feedback and strongly uncoupled dynamics,
014            which allows modular development of the multi-player system that applies to a
015            wide range of real applications. We propose a mirror-descent-based algorithm,
016            which converges in O(T 1/4 ) and is also no-regret. The result is achieved by a
017            dedicated use of two regularizations and the analysis of the fixed point thereof.
018            The convergence rate is further improved to O(T 1/2 ) in the case of strongly
019            monotone games. Motivated by practical tasks where the game evolves over time,
020
               the algorithm is extended to time-varying monotone games. We provide the first
               non-asymptotic result in converging monotone games and give improved results
021
               for equilibrium tracking games.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   We consider multi-player online learning in games. In this problem, the cost function for each player
027   is unknown to the player, and they need to learn to play the game through repeated interaction with
028   other players. We focus on a class of monotone and smooth games, which was first introduced
029   by Rosen (1965). This encapsulates a wide array of common games, such as two-player zero-sum
030   games, convex-concave games, and zero-sum polymatrix games (Bregman & Fokin, 1987). Our
031   goal is to find algorithms that solve the problem under bandit feedback and strongly uncoupled dy-
032   namics. Within this context, each player can only access information regarding the cost function
033   associated with their chosen actions without prior insight into their counterparts. This allows mod-
034
      ular development of the multi-player system in real applications and leverages existing single-agent
      learning algorithms for reuse.
035
036   Many works have focused on the time-average convergence to Nash equilibrium on learning in
037   monotone games (Even-Dar et al., 2009; Syrgkanis et al., 2015; Farina et al., 2022). However,
038   these works only guarantee the convergence of the time average of the joint action profile. Such
039   convergence properties are less appealing, because while the trajectories of the players converge in
040
      the time-average sense, it may still exhibit cycling (Mertikopoulos et al., 2018). This jeopardizes
      the practical use of such algorithms.
041
042   Popular no-regret algorithms such as mirror descent have demonstrated convergence in the last it-
043   erate within specific scenarios, such as two-player zero-sum games (Cai et al., 2023) and strongly
044   monotone games (Bravo et al., 2018; Drusvyatskiy et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2021). Yet convergence to
045   Nash equilibrium in monotone and smooth games is not available unless one assumes exact gradient
046
      feedback and coordination of players (Cai et al., 2022; Cai & Zheng, 2023). It remains open as to
      whether a no-regret algorithm can efficiently converge to a Nash equilibrium in monotone games
047
      with bandit feedback and strongly uncoupled dynamics. In this paper, we investigate the pivotal
048
      question:
049
050    How fast can no-regret algorithms converge (in the last iterate) to a Nash equilibrium in general
051        monotone and smooth games with bandit feedback and strongly uncoupled dynamics?
052
053   In this work, we present a mirror-descent-based algorithm designed to converge to the Nash equilib-
      rium in monotone and smooth games. Our algorithm is uncoupled and convergent and is applicable

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
082
083
      Monotone games The convergence of monotone games has been studied in a significant line of
      research. For a strongly monotone game under exact gradient feedback, the linear last-iterate conver-
084
      gence rate is known (Tseng, 1995; Liang & Stokes, 2019; Zhou et al., 2020). Under noisy gradient
085
      feedback, Jordan et al. (2023) showed a last-iterate convergence rate of O(T 1 ). Under bandit
086   feedback, Bervoets et al. (2020) proposed an algorithm that asymptotically converges to the equi-
087   librium if it is unique. Bravo et al. (2018) subsequently introduced an algorithm with a last-iterate
088   convergence rate of O(T 1/3 ), while also ensuring the no-regret property. Later works (Lin et al.,
089   2021) further improved the last-iterate convergence rate to O(T 1/2 ) under bandit feedback using
090   the self-concordant barrier function. Jordan et al. (2023) gave a result of the same rate, but with the
091   additional assumption that the Jacobian of each player’s gradient is Lipschitz continuous. In the case
092   of bandit but noisy feedback (with a zero-mean noise), Lin et al. (2021) showed that the convergence
093   rate is still O(T 1/2 ).
094
      For monotone but not strongly monotone games, Mertikopoulos & Zhou (2019) leveraged the dual
095
      averaging algorithm to demonstrate an asymptotic convergence rate under noisy gradient feedback.
096   With access to the exact gradient information, Cai & Zheng (2023) gave a last-iterate convergence
097   rate of O(T 1 ). In the context of bandit feedback, Tatarenko & Kamgarpour (2019) proposed an
098   algorithm that asymptotically converges to the Nash equilibrium. Table 1 provides a summary of the
099   recent results.
100
101   Time-varying monotone games Motivated by real-world applications such as Cournot competi-
102   tion, where multiple firms supply goods to the market and pricing is subject to fluctuations due to
103   factors like weather, holidays, and politics. Duvocelle et al. (2023) studied the strongly monotone
104   game under a time-varying cost function. When the game converges to a static state, they propose an
105   algorithm that achieves asymptotic convergence under bandit feedback. Assuming the cost function
106   varies O(T ) across a horizon T , Duvocelle et al. (2023) provided an algorithm that attains a con-
107   vergence rate of O(T /5 1/5 ) under bandit feedback. Subsequent work of Yan et al. (2023) further
      improved this rate to O(T /3 2/3 ) under exact gradient feedback.

195   3.2   BANDIT F EEDBACK AND S TRONGLY U NCOUPLED DYNAMIC
196
197   In this work, we focus on learning under bandit feedback and strongly uncoupled dynamics. The
198   bandit feedback setting restricts each player to only observe the cost function ci (xi , x i ) with re-
199   spect to the action taken xi . The strongly uncoupled learning dynamic (Daskalakis et al., 2011)
200
      means players do not have prior knowledge of cost function or the action space of other players
      and can only keep track of a constant amount of historical information. As the bandit feedback
201
      and strongly uncoupled dynamic only require each player to access information of its own, this al-
202
      lows for modular development of the multi-player system, by reusing existing single-agent learning
203   algorithms.

206   4     A LGORITHM

208   Our algorithm builds upon the renowned mirror-descent algorithm. The efficacy of online mirror-
209   descent in solving Nash equilibrium has been demonstrated under full information, and in both
210   linear or strongly monotone games, with extensive investigations into its last-iterate convergence
211   investigated in Cen et al. (2021); Lin et al. (2021); Cai et al. (2023); Duvocelle et al. (2023).
212   Our algorithm differs from classic online mirror descent approaches by making use of two regular-
213   izers: A self-concordant barrier regularizer h to build an efficient Ellipsoidal gradient estimator and
214   contest the bandit feedback; and a regularizer p to accommodate monotone (and not strongly mono-
215   tone) games. Similar use of two regularizers has also been investigated (Lin et al., 2021). However,
      their method used the Euclidean norm regularization, which cannot be extended to our setting.
