009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance on
012            various tasks. However, it remains an open question whether the default Euclidean
013            space is the most suitable choice for embedding tokens in LLMs. In this study, we
014            first investigate the non-Euclidean characteristics of LLMs. Our findings reveal
015            that token frequency follows a power-law distribution, with high-frequency tokens
016            clustering near the origin and low-frequency tokens positioned farther away. Ad-
017            ditionally, token embeddings exhibit a high degree of hyperbolicity, indicating a
018
               latent tree-like structure in the embedding space. Building on the observation, we
               propose to efficiently fine-tune LLMs in hyperbolic space to better exploit the un-
019
               derlying complex structures. However, we found that this fine-tuning in hyperbolic
020
               space cannot be achieved with naive application of exponential and logarithmic
021            maps, when the embedding and weight matrices both reside in Euclidean space. To
022            address this technique issue, we introduce a new method called hyperbolic low-rank
023            efficient fine-tuning, HypLoRA, that performs low-rank adaptation directly on the
024            hyperbolic manifold, avoiding the cancellation effect caused by the exponential and
025            logarithmic maps, thus preserving the hyperbolic modeling capabilities. Through
026            extensive experiments, we demonstrate that HypLoRA significantly enhances the
027            performance of LLMs on reasoning tasks, particularly for complex reasoning prob-
028            lems. In particular, HypLoRA improves the performance in the complex AQuA
029
               dataset by up to 13.0%, showcasing its effectiveness in handling complex reasoning
               challenges.

      Large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023),
035
      and Gemma (Gemma Team, 2024) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in understanding and
036   generating human-like text (Qin et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2024). Despite their impressive capabilities,
037   these models often rely on Euclidean geometry for learning text representations, which may not
038   always be the complex, hierarchical nature of real-world data structures (Bronstein et al., 2017;
039   Bachmann et al., 2020). For example, in language, words are often organized into categories that
040   reflect varying levels of abstraction. These relationships naturally form a tree-like structure, where
041   general or abstract concepts, such as "fruits," sit at the top of the hierarchy, while more specific or
042   concrete terms, like "apples" or "bananas," reside at the lower levels. Representing such structures
043   effectively is crucial for understanding the semantics of language in LLMs.
044   Recent advancements suggest that non-Euclidean geometries, particularly hyperbolic spaces, offer
045   promising alternatives for modeling hierarchical data. Hyperbolic space, distinguished by its negative
046   curvature, is especially well-suited for representing tree-like data due to its exponential volume
047   growth, enabling efficient embeddings of hierarchies (Nickel & Kiela, 2017; 2018; Ganea et al.,
048   2018a; Khrulkov et al., 2020; Cetin et al., 2022). However, a significant research gap remains: existing
049   works have not attempted to study LLM embeddings in the context of non-Euclidean geometry.
050   Proposed Analysis Framework In this work, we first delve deep into how LLMs interact with token
051   embeddings and explore to what extent these embeddings exhibit non-Euclidean characteristics. We
052   approach this from both a global and local perspective. At the global level, we analyze the overall
053   distribution of tokens by frequency, examining how these frequency maps are distributed across the
      embedding space. At the local level, we measure the hyperbolicity (Borassi et al., 2015; Kennedy

093   adaptation directly on the hyperbolic manifold without transformation to the tangent space, thus

102   capabilities. We show that HypLoRA better understands complex reasoning tasks by implicitly
103   incorporating high-order interactions and token norms, achieving improvements of up to 13% on the
104

      Hyperbolic Representation Learning and Deep Learning Hyperbolic geometry has been suc-
111
      cessfully applied to various neural network architectures and models (Yang et al., 2022b; Mettes
112   et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2021), including shallow hyperbolic neural networks (Ganea et al., 2018a;b;
113   Chen et al., 2021; Shimizu et al., 2020), hyperbolic CNNs (Bdeir et al., 2023; van Spengler et al.,
114   2023), and hyperbolic attention networks or Transformers (Gulcehre et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2021;
115   Shimizu et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2024). These models leverage the inductive biases of hyperbolic
116   geometry to achieve remarkable performance on various tasks and applications (Chami et al., 2019;
117   Yang et al., 2022a; Sun et al., 2021; Khrulkov et al., 2020; Cetin et al., 2022; Weng et al., 2021;
118   Xiong et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2021). However, training LLMs from scratch remains computationally
119   expensive (Kochurov et al., 2020; Smith, 2014). The computational complexity increases further
120
      when considering Riemannian optimization (Kochurov et al., 2020; Smith, 2014; Bécigneul & Ganea,
      2018) and additional hyperbolic operations, like Möbius addition.
121
122   Parameter Efficient Fine Tuning (PEFT) and LoRAs Fine-tuning LLMs (Foundation, 2022;
123   2023; Touvron et al., 2023) for downstream tasks poses significant challenges due to their massive
124   number of parameters. To address this issue, PEFT methods have been proposed, which aim to
125   train a small subset of parameters while achieving better performance compared to full fine-tuning.
126
      PEFT methods can be broadly categorized into prompt-based methods (Lester et al., 2021; Li &
      Liang, 2021; Qin et al., 2021), adapter-based methods (Houlsby et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2021),and
127
      reparameterization-based methods (Hu et al., 2021; Aghajanyan et al., 2020; Edalati et al., 2022).
128
      Among these, LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) as the reparameterization-based method, has gained significant
129   attention due to its simplicity, effectiveness, and compatibility with existing model architectures.
130   Variants of LoRA, such as LoRA+(Hayou et al., 2024), DoRA (Liu et al., 2024), AdaLoRA (Zhang
131   et al., 2023), have been proposed to improve its performance and efficiency. Recent research has also
132   investigated ensembles of multiple LoRAs (Wang et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2024), and quantization
133   techniques (Dettmers et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023). Despite these advances, existing
134   methods operate within Euclidean space, ignoring the underlying structure represented by LLMs. The
135   proposed method is as a foundational algorithm, potentially combined with various LoRA variants,
136   to exploit their complementary strengths and achieve superior performance.

141
      model of hyperbolic geometry, hyperbolic linear transformations, and the concept of hyperbolicity.

143   LoRA Adapter The LoRA adapter offers an efficient approach for modifying large LLMs with
144   minimal computational overhead. Instead of retraining the entire model, LoRA focuses on adjusting
145
      specific components within the model’s architecture to transform an input x into an output z. In
      practice, LoRA targets the weight matrices found in each Transformer layer of an LLM. Typically, the
146
      weight W of the Transformer, which resides in the dimensions Rd×k , is adapted through a low-rank
147
      approximation. This is achieved by introducing an additional term, ∆W , to the original weight
148   matrix:
149
                                z = WLoRA (x) = W x + ∆W x = W x + BAx.                                (1)
150
151   Here, B ∈ Rd×r and A ∈ Rr×k represent two smaller, learnable matrices where r—the rank of these
152   matrices—is significantly less than either d or k. This design choice ensures that r ≪ min(d, k),
153   thereby reducing the complexity of the model adaptation. During the fine-tuning process, only the
154   matrices A and B are adjusted, while the pre-existing weights W are kept frozen. This method
155   significantly decreases the number of parameters that need to be trained, from dk to (d + k)r,
156   enhancing the efficiency of the fine-tuning process. As a result, LoRA enables the targeted adaptation
157   of LLMs, allowing them to transform an input x into an output z while maintaining high performance
158
      and adapting to new tasks or datasets with a fraction of the computational resources typically required.

161   for our study due to its ability to effectively capture hierarchical structures and maintain numerical

      4.2     δ-H YPERBOLICITY OF T OKEN E MBEDDINGS
236
237   To rigorously quantify the hierarchical nature of token embeddings, we examine the δ-hyperbolicity
238   of space spanned by the token embedding. δ-Hyperbolicity, introduced by Gromov (Gromov, 1987),
239   is a measure that captures the degree to which a metric space deviates from an exact tree structure.
240   Lower values of δ imply a space more similar to a perfect tree, while higher values indicate deviation
241   from a tree-like structure. A brief explanation of δ-hyperbolicity can be found on Wikipedia2 .
242   We compute δ-hyperbolicity using the four-point condition, which compares the Gromov products
243   between any four points a, b, c, and w in the metric space. Specifically, the hyperbolicity is defined
244   as:
245                                    [a, c]w ≥ min([a, b]w , [b, c]w ) − δ,                             (4)
246   where the Gromov product [a, b]w is:
247
                                                1
248                                 [a, b]w =     (d(a, w) + d(b, w) − d(a, b)).                            (5)
                                                2
249
250   To measure the hyperbolicity of token embeddings, we apply this algorithm to various open-source
251   LLMs. Following the methodologies proposed by Khrulkov et al. (Khrulkov et al., 2020) and Cetin
252   et al. (Cetin et al., 2022), we estimate δ-hyperbolicity using the efficient algorithm introduced by
253   Fournier et al. (Fournier et al., 2015). To ensure scale invariance, we normalize δ by the diameter of
                                                                                2δ
254   the embedding space, diam(X), yielding a relative measure: δrel = diam(X)      . This relative measure
255   ranges from 0 to 1, with values closer to 0 indicating a highly hyperbolic (tree-like) structure, and
256   values near 1 indicating a non-hyperbolic, flat structure. Following previous works (Khrulkov et al.,
257   2020), we employ Euclidean distance as a measure of the shortest distance. To further validate the
258
      correctness of this approach, we generate a series of random graphs with predefined hyperbolicity,
      embed them using a graph neural network (GNN), and then compute the hyperbolicity in Euclidean
259
      space. Details of this process are provided in Appendix B. Our experiments reveal a positive
260
      correlation between the hyperbolicity of the embeddings and the original graphs. Consequently, we
261   utilize this method as a proxy for estimating the hyperbolicity of token embeddings.
262
263   In our analysis, we calculate hyperbolicity at the prompt level, treating each token within a prompt
264
      as a point in the metric space spanned by the embeddings. By averaging the hyperbolicity across
      all prompts, we assess the overall hyperbolic structure of token embeddings in each dataset. Our
265
      results, as shown in Table 2, reveal that token embeddings exhibit significant hyperbolicity, suggesting
266
      that the embedding space has a strong tree-like structure. This observation further corroborates our

339   exponential maps. These additional operations, nevertheless, can be completed within O(N ) where
340   the N is the number of input tokens.

362   Implementation Details The exponential map transforms the original input space using an ex-
363   ponential operator, as also noted in (Desai et al., 2023). To prevent numerical overflow, we first
364   apply L2 normalization to the input before using the exponential map in Equation (8), then rescale
365
      it with a learnable norm scaling factor. The curvature for our proposed HypLoRA is treated as a
      hyperparameter, with values searched from the set {0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0}. Following the procedure
366
      in (Chami et al., 2019), to correctly use the exponential map, we append a zero to the beginning of
367
      the input vector x, forming xE . After applying the logarithmic map, the output vector z will have an
368   additional dimension with a zero value. To maintain consistency with the original input space, we
369   remove this extra dimension from z. It is important to note that the final results are micro-averaged
370   across datasets, which contain varying numbers of questions, such as 1,319 in GSM8K and 238 in
371   MAWPS. In micro-averaging, each prompt is treated equally. For the LoRA implementation, we
372   insert it into both the Multi-head Attention and MLP layers of the base model. All experiments are
373   conducted on a single GPU, using either the A40 (40G) or A100 (80G).

515   6   C ONCLUSION
516
517   In this study, we investigated the non-Euclidean properties of token embeddings in LLMs. Our
518   analysis token embedding has strong hyperbolic characteristics. Building on these findings, we
519   proposed HypLoRA, a hyperbolic low-rank adaptation method that incorporates hyperbolic geometry
520   into the fine-tuning process of LLMs. Extensive experiments showed that HypLoRA significantly
521   improves LLM performance on arithmetic reasoning tasks, particularly on complex datasets. By
522   leveraging the hyperbolic structure of the data, HypLoRA enhances the model’s ability to capture
523   and utilize intricate relationships, leading to better reasoning capabilities.
524   Limitation and Future Work In this study, we employed a consistent curvature across all prompts
525   during fine-tuning, which simplified the implementation and enhanced efficiency. However, this
526   uniform approach may not be optimal when applied to different datasets simultaneously. Our future
527   work will explore more adaptive fine-tuning techniques that can better accommodate the unique
528   characteristics of different prompts. Additionally, due to the computational overhead introduced by
529   the exponential and logarithmic maps, this is inevitable when transitioning from the original Euclidean
530
      space to a hyperbolic space. We will explore more efficient methods to reduce this computational
      cost in future work.
531
532   Despite these challenges, our research provides a thorough examination of token embedding distri-
533   butions from a non-Euclidean perspective and offers valuable insights. The fine-tuning method we
534   proposed holds significant potential for advancing geometrically inspired models, contributing to the
535   ongoing development of more effective LLMs.
536   Reproducibility Statement Due to the inherent randomness in large model inference, answers on
537   individual prompts may slightly different, but overall results are consistent. We averaged results over
538   multiple runs. For LoRA, we rerun LoRA on LLaMA-7B/13B, though performance on SVAMP with
539   LLaMA-7B was lower than reported. All DoRA experiments were run on A100 GPUs due to long
      inference times on A40, with LLaMA-13B still exceeding 24 hours.

986
987    In this section, we present examples to demonstrate the advantages of incorporating HypLoRA.
988    As the examples show in Table 7 and 8, HypLoRA consistently provides more accurate reasoning
989    compared to LoRA. For instance, in Example 2, LoRA incorrectly calculates the sum of the test
990    scores as 380 instead of the correct total of 400, which leads to an incorrect average. This mistake
991
       highlights a limitation of large models like LoRA, which do not perform arithmetic operations as
       precisely as computers but rather approximate probability distributions. While the values 380 and 400
992
       may seem close in Euclidean space, the hyperbolic geometry utilized by HypLoRA more effectively
993
       handles such variations, particularly in "tail" distributions, allowing for greater separation between
994    close values and thus leading to more accurate reasoning. A similar issue arises in Example 3, where
995    LoRA incorrectly computes 11, 600 − 9, 800, resulting in a wrong conclusion about the load capacity
996    of Gary’s truck. HypLoRA, on the other hand, correctly performs the calculation and arrives at the
997    accurate answer. These examples demonstrate how the hyperbolic space used by HypLoRA enables
998    better handling of complex reasoning tasks, improving both accuracy and reliability in such scenarios.
