009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We study the performance of the Thompson Sampling algorithm for logistic ban-
012            dit problems, where the agent receives binary rewards with probabilities deter-
013            mined by a logistic function, exp(β⟨a, θ⟩)/(1 + exp(β⟨a, θ⟩)), with slope pa-
014            rameter β. We focus on the setting where both the action a and parameter θ lie
015            within the d-dimensional unit ball. Adopting the information-theoretic frame-
016            work introduced by (Russo & Van Roy, 2015), we analyze the information ratio,
017            a statistic that quantifies the trade-off between the information gained about the
018
               optimal action and the immediate regret incurred. We improve upon previous
               results by establishing that the information ratio is bounded by 29 dα−2 , where
019
               α is a minimax measure of the alignment between the action space and the pa-
020
               rameterpspace, independent of β. Notably, we derive a regret bound of order
021
               O(d/α T log(βT /d)), which scales only logarithmically with the logistic func-
022
               tion parameter β. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first regret bound for lo-
023            gistic bandits that achieves logarithmic dependence on β while being independent
024            of the number of actions. In particular, when the action space encompasses      √ the
025            parameter space, the expected regret of Thompson Sampling is of order Õ(d T ).

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   This paper studies the logistic bandit problem, where for T time steps, an agent selects an action and
031   receives binary rewards with probabilities determined by the logistic function exp(β⟨a, θ⟩)/(1 +
032   exp(β⟨a, θ⟩)) with slope parameter β > 0. In this setting, both the action a ∈ A and the parameter
033   vector θ ∈ O lie within the d-dimensional unit ball. Logistic bandits are used to model various sce-
034   narios, such as click-through rate prediction, spam email detection, and personalized advertisement
035   systems, where, in the latter case, content is suggested to users who provide binary feedback, such
036   as ”like” or ”dislike” (Chapelle & Li, 2011; McMahan & Streeter, 2012; Russo & Van Roy, 2017).
037   The performance, or regret, of algorithms for logistic bandits has been extensively studied, with sig-
038   nificant contributions including analyses of Upper-Confidence-Bound (UCB) algorithms by (Filippi
039   et al., 2010), (Li et al., 2017), and (Faury et al., 2020) as well as the study of Thompson Sampling
040   (TS) by (Russo & Van Roy, 2014a) and (Abeille & Lazaric, 2017). However, nearly all existing re-
041   gret bounds exhibit an exponential dependence on the parameter β (see our comparison in Table 1).
042   This dependence is unsatisfactory because, in practice, as β increases, it can be faster to identify the
043   optimal action as the distinction between near-optimal and sub-optimal actions is more pronounced.
044   In this work, we focus on the Thompson Sampling algorithm (Thompson, 1933), which, despite its
045   simplicity, has proven to be highly effective across a wide range of problems (Russo et al., 2018;
046   Chapelle & Li, 2011). To analyze the regret of Thompson Sampling, (Russo & Van Roy, 2015)
047   introduced the concept of the information ratio, defined as the ratio between the squared expected
048   difference between the optimal and actual rewards, and the information gained about the optimal
049   action. (Dong & Van Roy, 2018) conjectured that the Thompson Sampling information ratio for
050
      logistic bandits could be bounded solely by the problem’s dimension d, and several studies have
      since aimed to characterize this ratio.

053   gistic bandit problem, but their result relies on a worst-case information ratio bound scaling with
      the cardinality of the action space |A| and their regret bound becomes vacuous for problems with

110   We consider a logistic bandit problem, where at each time step t ∈ {1, . . . , T }, an agent selects an
111   action At and receives a binary reward Rt ∈ {0, 1} with probability following a logistic function:

115
      a ∈ A and the unknown parameter θ ∈ O. Throughout the paper, we denote the logistic function as
116
      ϕβ (⟨a, θ⟩). The probability of obtaining a reward is maximized when the inner product between the
117   action and environment parameters is maximized. In our setting, both the action a and the parameter
118   vector θ lie within the d-dimensional Euclidean unit ball, Bd (0, 1). Note that this is equivalent to the
119   problem considered by (Faury et al., 2020) using β as the maximal norm for the parameters θ ∈ O.
120
      For a given action space A and parameter space O, we define their minimax alignment constant
121
      α := minθ∈O maxa∈A ⟨a, θ⟩. For the rest of the paper, we assume that the action and parameters
122
      spaces are such that α ≥ 0. This assumption is relatively mild, as it suffices for the action space A to
123   contain two opposed actions a, a′ (i.e. a= −a′ ) to ensure α ≥ 0 for any parameter set O ⊆ Bd (0, 1).

      3    T HOMPSON S AMPLING AND I NFORMATION RATIO
148
      An elegant algorithm for solving bandit problems is the Thompson Sampling algorithm. It works by
149
      randomly selecting actions according to their posterior probability of being optimal. More specifi-
150
      cally, at each time step t ∈ {1, . . . , T }, the agent samples a parameter estimate Θ̂t from the posterior
151
      distribution conditioned on the history H t and selects the action that is optimal for the sampled pa-
152
      rameter estimate, At = π⋆ (Θ̂t ). The pseudocode for Thompson Sampling is given in Algorithm 1.
153
154   Studying the regret of Thompson Sampling in bandit problems, (Russo & Van Roy, 2015) introduced
155   a key quantity to the analysis, the information ratio defined as the following random variable:

180   trarily large with the number of actions or get infinite if the action space is continuous. We address

      6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
487
488
      In this work, we analyzed the performance of the Thompson Sampling algorithm for logistic ban-
489   dit problems, focusing on settings where both the action and parameter spaces lie within the d-
490   dimensional unit ball. Using an information-theoretic framework, we provided a refined analysis of
491   the information ratio, a key statistic that captures the trade-off between exploration and exploitation
492   in logistic bandits. Our main result established that the information ratio is bounded by 92 dα−2 ,
493   where α is a minimax alignment constant between the action and parameter spaces. Importantly,
494   this bound is independent of the logistic function’s slope parameter, β.
495                                                                p
      Building on this, we derived a regret bound of O(d/α T log(βT /d)), which scales only loga-
496   rithmically with β, representing a significant improvement over prior results. To the best of our
497   knowledge, this is the first regret bound for logistic bandits that achieves logarithmic dependence
498   on β while remaining independent of the action set’s cardinality. Importantly, our results do not
499   depend on the fragility dimension η, unlike those of Dong et al. (2019). This distinction is signifi-
500   cant because, except in cases where α = 1, the fragility dimension can grow exponentially with the
501   dimension d. Finally, we presented specific settings where the dependence on α can be controlled.
502   For instance, when the action
                               √     space fully encompasses the parameter space, the regret of Thompson
503   Sampling scales as Õ(d T ).
504   A promising direction for future work is to extend our analysis to the broader class of generalized
505   linear bandits. The key properties of the logistic function that we leverage could potentially extend
506   to other link functions, such as those from exponential family models or Poisson regression.
507
      Another exciting avenue is exploring whether our approach can be applied to the optimistic informa-
508
      tion directed sampling algorithm introduced by (Neu et al., 2024), with the goal of deriving frequen-
509
      tist regret bounds for logistic bandits that scale logarithmically with the parameter β. Extending our
510   analysis to the frequentist setting would represent a significant advancement in the field.

       D                     N UMERICAL SIMULATIONS
1027
1028   To illustrate the improvement of our regret analysis compared to previous work, we performed nu-
1029   merical experiments on a synthetic problem. We considered a logistic bandit problem in dimension
1030   d = 10, with time horizon T = 200, and with parameter β ranging between [0.25, 10]. For both
1031   action space and parameter space, we used the entire d-dimensional unit sphere, A = O = Sd (0, 1)
1032   and assumed a uniform prior distribution for the parameter Θ. We computed the expected regret
1033   of the Thompson Sampling algorithm using a Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method (see
1034   Remark 2) and compared it to three Bayesian regret bounds that hold for continuous spaces: (Russo
1035   & Van Roy, 2014b, Proposition 4), (Dong & Van Roy, 2018, Theorem 3), and our Corollary 1.
1036   The results are presented in Figure 7. The left subfigure shows the evolution of the expected regret
1037   and the regret bounds for the different time steps t ∈ {1, . . . , 200} and for two different values of
1038   β = {2, 4}. For both values of β, our bound is tighter throughout the entire time horizon and is
1039   less sensitive to increasing β compared to (Russo & Van Roy, 2014b, Proposition 4) and (Dong &
1040   Van Roy, 2018, Theorem 3). This behavior is further illustrated in the right subfigure, were the dif-
1041   ferent regret bounds at t = 200 are compared for values of β ranging between [0.25, 10]. Our bound
1042
       remains competitive across all values of β and is tighter than (Dong & Van Roy, 2018, Theorem 3)
       for values of β ≥ 2. Importantly, we observe that both (Russo & Van Roy, 2014b, Proposition 4) and
1043
       (Dong & Van Roy, 2018, Theorem 3) increase exponentially with β while our bound increases only
1044
       logarithmically. We note that the actual expected regret decreases for larger β. This was anticipated
1045   since, for large values of β, the distinction between near-optimal and suboptimal actions becomes
1046   more pronounced, facilitating the identification of near-optimal actions.

       E    R EGARDING THE GAPS IN PREVIOUS LITERATURE
1081
1082
       In Section 1, we mention that the main results of Dong et al. (2019) are incomplete because of two
1083   shortcomings. The first one concerns a gap in their analysis of the Thompson Sampling information
1084   ratio for values of β > 2. The second one regards a mistake in their regret analysis, which combines
1085   incompatible results. We elaborate on both arguments below.
1086
1087   Regarding the first shortcoming We identified an issue in the proof of (Dong et al., 2019, The-
1088   orem 5) at the end of the proof on page 20, where the inequality χ > ξ > 0.1λ is stated without
1089   justification. This inequality plays a crucial role in deriving their bound on the Thompson Sampling
1090   information ratio, but the only evidence provided is (Dong et al., 2019, Figure 3), which illustrates
1091   the functions χ(λ, β) and ξ(λ, β) for the specific case of β = 2. While this figure suggests that the
1092   inequality holds for β = 2, it cannot be used to conclude that the inequality holds in general for
1093
       β ≥ 2. Additionally, we note that the computation of χ(λ, β) and ξ(λ, β) for given values of λ and
       β is highly intricate, and despite our best efforts, we were unable to reproduce (Dong et al., 2019,
1094
       Figure 3).
1095
1096
       Regarding the second shortcoming As discussed earlier, the regret analysis in (Dong et al., 2019,
1097   Theorems 1 and 5) combines incompatible results. Specifically, the paper uses a uniform bound on
1098   the information ratio of the “standard” Thompson Sampling (provided in (Dong et al., 2019, Ap-
1099   pendix B, Eq. (18))) together with (Dong & Van Roy, 2018, Theorem 1), which requires a uniform
1100   bound on the information ratio of the one-step compressed Thompson Sampling. This inconsistency
1101   invalidates the regret bounds derived in (Dong et al., 2019, Theorems 1 and 5). We emphasize that
1102   the problem is “hidden” in (Dong et al., 2019, Proposition 9), which incorrectly restates (Dong &
1103   Van Roy, 2018, Theorem 4). It is important to note that there is no straightforward way to extend
1104   the results from (Dong & Van Roy, 2018, Theorem 4) to (Dong et al., 2019, Proposition 9), that
1105
       is, to remove the need to bound the one-step compressed Thompson Sampling, as this compressed
       Thompson Sampling is the cornerstone of (Dong & Van Roy, 2018, Theorem 4).
1106
1107   While it is possible to use (Dong & Van Roy, 2018, Proposition 1) directly with a uniform bound
1108   on the “standard” Thompson Sampling information ratio, this approach is limited because (Dong
1109   & Van Roy, 2018, Proposition 1) provides a loose bound. Specifically, this bound depends on the
1110   cardinality of the parameter space Θ through the entropy H(θ⋆ ) (or on the cardinality of the action
1111
       space through H(A⋆ ) in the original version (Russo & Van Roy, 2015, Proposition 1)). This issue is
       highlighted in Dong & Van Roy (2018) on page 3 at the end of Section 3, and serves as a motivation
1112
       for the introduction of the one-step-compressed Thompson Sampling regret analysis in the paper.
1113
       Combining (Dong & Van Roy, 2018, Proposition 1) with our bound on the       pThompson Sampling
1114
       information ratio, Proposition 1, results in a regret bound of the order O( dT log(|O|)), which
1115
       becomes vacuous for infinite or continuous parameter spaces.
1116
1117   We emphasize that the one-step compressed Thompson Sampling information ratio is a fundamen-
1118   tally different quantity and is significantly more challenging to analyze due to the intricate con-
1119
       struction of the one-step compressed Thompson Sampling (c.f. (Dong & Van Roy, 2018, Proof of
       Proposition 2)). Notably, the techniques used to analyze the information ratio in Dong et al. (2019),
1120
       even for the case where β ≤ 2, do not apply to the one-step compressed Thompson Sampling infor-
1121
       mation ratio. For instance, (Dong et al., 2019, Proof of Proposition 5) requires a one-to-one mapping
1122   between parameters and optimal action (see (Dong et al., 2019, Proof of Proposition 15, eq. (22), in-
1123   equalities (c) and (e))). However, by definition, the equivalent requirement for one-step compressed
1124   Thompson Sampling (a one-to-one mapping between the statistic ψ and the optimal action) cannot
1125   hold, as by construction the ψ is constructed to be less informative than the parameter θ.

1135
1136
       If the mapping π⋆ (θ) is not one-to-one, it could either mean that a particular parameter is optimal
1137   for several actions or that a particular action is optimal for several parameters.
1138
1139   In the first case, for example, if a1 , a2 ∈ A are optimal for the same parameter θ1 ∈ O, this implies
1140
       that E[R(a1 , θ1 )] = E[R(a2 , θ1 )] ≥ E[R(a, θ1 )] for all a ∈ A with a ̸= a1 and a ̸= a2 . In this
       scenario, we can arbitrarily set π⋆ (θ1 ) = a1 without affecting the regret of Thompson Sampling, as
1141
       E[R(a1 , θ1 )] = E[R(a2 , θ1 )].
1142
1143   In the second case, if an action a1 ∈ A is optimal for multiple parameters, say θ1 , θ2 ∈ O, we can
1144   artificially construct an action label set A′ such that two labels, a′1 , a′2 ∈ A′ , are associated with
1145   a1 . For all other actions in A \ {a1 }, there is a corresponding label in A′ . We denote the mapping
1146
       between action labels and their corresponding actions using the function ρ : A′ → A. We can
       construct a function π⋆ : O → A′ such that π⋆ is a one-to-one mapping between the parameters
1147
       O and the action labels A′ . We define the optimal action label as A⋆ ′ = π⋆ (Θ) and the Thompson
1148
       Sampling action label as At ′ = π⋆ (Θt ). This artificial construction, illustrated in Figure 8, is
1149   intended solely for the purposes of our regret analysis and has no impact on the regret of Thompson
1150   Sampling. The instant regret of Thompson Sampling at time t ∈ {1, . . . , T } remains R(A⋆ , Θ) −
1151   R(At , Θ), where A⋆ = ρ(A⋆ ′ ) is still the optimal action for Θ, and At = ρ(A′t ) is the action
1152   selected by Thompson Sampling for Θt . In this context, the Thompson Sampling information ratio
1153   would be adapted and defined as:
