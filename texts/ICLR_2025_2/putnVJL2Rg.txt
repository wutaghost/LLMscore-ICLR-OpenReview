009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) enhances the alignment between hu-
012            mans and large language models (LLMs), with Reward Models (RMs) playing a pivotal role.
013
               RLHF and sampling techniques, such as Best-of-N, require RMs to provide reliable rewards to
               guide policy training or sample selection. However, despite the advancement of LLMs, critical
014
               issues in RMs persist, such as overestimation on out-of-distribution (OOD) data (also known
015
               as reward hacking) and a preference for verbose outputs (length bias). These issues undermine
016            the reliability of RM-generated rewards. Training an unbiased RM requires addressing these
017            challenges, yet there is a lack of in-depth analysis on RMs. In this paper, we first decompose
018            the RM training pipeline and identify three key aspects critical for developing an unbiased
019            RM: 1) model architectures, 2) training paradigms, and 3) the influence of preference data. For
020            each aspect, we conduct thorough empirical studies, revealing several insightful design con-
021            siderations. Building on our findings, we develop an RM capable of mitigating the identified
022            issues. This study represents the first comprehensive examination of various challenges from
023            a holistic perspective in RM training, offering in-depth analyses of essential concerns and pro-
024
               viding guidance for training unbiased RMs that can accurately guide downstream policies. The
               relevant code and models will be made publicly available.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028    Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) is attracting increasing research interest with the rapid
029   advancement of large language models (LLMs), enabling better alignment of generated texts with human pref-
030   erences (Ouyang et al., 2022; Dubey et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Achiam et al., 2023). The standard RLHF
031   process can be divided into two stages. First, a reward model (RM) is trained using collected preference datasets.
032   Second, reinforcement learning (RL) policies are trained using the RM as a proxy to maximize rewards. In ad-
033   dition to RLHF, RMs can be employed in offline sampling techniques to select high-quality samples (Liu et al.,
034   2023a; Dong et al., 2023). Despite the critical role RMs play in the LLM era, their underlying mechanisms and
035
      existing issues remain relatively underexplored.
036   The RM in large language models (LLMs) typically follows the Bradley-Terry assumption Bradley & Terry
037   (1952), serving as a proxy to approximate oracle preferences. Conventionally, training an RM relies on pref-
038   erence data collected by humans or strong AI models Hu et al. (2024). However, previous studies Wang et al.
039   (2024); Ramé et al. (2024); Quan (2024) have revealed that ambiguous preferences frequently occur within the
040   data. For instance, Quan (2024) shows that agreement between pairwise preference data is only around 60%-
041   70%, which hampers the model’s ability to accurately learn preferences. Additionally, RMs struggle to generalize
042
      effectively to unseen scenarios. Even when sufficient offline preference datasets are available, a distribution gap
      between training and validation test sets persists. This gap compels RMs to assign higher rewards to previously
043
      unseen samples, leading to the reward-hacking problem (Skalse et al., 2022). Another critical issue with RMs is
044
      the length bias problem, stemming from Goodhart’s law (Karwowski et al., 2023). After RLHF, LLMs often suf-
045   fer from verbosity, as they tend to generate longer responses to achieve higher rewards (Singhal et al., 2023; Park
046   et al., 2024). In such cases, both standard RMs and advanced models like GPT-4 (LLM-as-Judge) (Zheng et al.,
047   2023) may be misled into assigning higher rewards due to their preference for length. These issues, whether orig-
048   inating from the inherent characteristics of RMs or the improper collection of preference data, pose significant
049   challenges to developing unbiased RMs.
050
      In this paper, we address the aforementioned issues of RMs and conduct the first comprehensive examination
051   of training an unbiased RM. To achieve this, we begin with a thorough review of related works on RMs and
052   revisit the RM training pipeline. From this analysis, we identify three critical influencing factors. First, model
053   architectures. Recent studies have shifted from standard single RM training to using multiple experts or coun-

      terparts (Quan, 2024; Eisenstein et al., 2023; Ramé et al., 2024; Coste et al., 2023). One notable approach is the
055   adoption of mixture-of-experts (MoEs) (Quan, 2024), which mitigates bias through the use of multiple experts.
056   Additionally, some studies employ multiple RMs by either ensembling their outputs (Eisenstein et al., 2023;
057   Coste et al., 2023) or recomposing the parameter space (Ramé et al., 2024). We reexamine RMs using these
058   architectures, offering insights on when to adopt them. Second, training paradigms. RM training generally uti-
059   lizes ranking functions to align the RM with preferred responses while diverging from rejected ones. However,
060   tuning LLMs with large datasets requires careful parameter engineering (Dubey et al., 2024), and the varying
061   preference scales among pairwise data can lead to unstable training. We identify that the pairwise ranking prob-
062   lem is analogous to a multi-objective contrastive learning problem (Chen et al., 2020), and we discuss the effects
063
      of different training paradigms. Third, the effect of preference data. While the scaling laws of LLMs have
      been widely studied (Ouyang et al., 2022; Isik et al., 2024), only a few works have preliminarily explored how
064
      increasing the volume of preference data can improve overall performance (Touvron et al., 2023), or examined
065
      the impact of data quality (Wang et al., 2024). No comprehensive study has been conducted on the specific im-
066   pact of preference data on RMs. We explore this influence from two perspectives: the noise within the training
067   set and the length of the preference data. These three aspects form the basis of our discussion on training an
068   unbiased RM.
069
070   Derived from the three-fold discussions, we propose a RM that achieves higher classification performance while
071   providing neutral rewards for downstream tasks. As an early study in identifying crucial issues, we deconstruct
072   the different components of RMs and offer solutions to address these challenges, laying the foundation for train-
073   ing an unbiased RM. In summary, our contributions are as follows:
074
075         • We conduct a comprehensive examination of the existing RM training pipeline, identifying two critical
076           issues: reward hacking and length bias.
077
078         • We perform a thorough decomposition of RM components, providing a fine-grained analysis of the
079           impact of model architectures, training paradigms, and the effect of preference data, along with several
080           constructive suggestions.
081
082         • Building on these insights, we develop an unbiased RM that yields promising performance, demonstrat-
083           ing effective mitigation of the identified issues.
084
085
      As an empirical study on RMs, we emphasize that our primary goal is to investigate the influence of diverse
086   factors rather than pursue the highest performance. Throughout the discussion, we provide guidance on when to
087   employ specific model architectures, how to select appropriate training paradigms, and how to organize prefer-
088   ence data effectively for RM training. Additionally, we show that the two critical problems—reward hacking and
089   length bias—are mitigated.

145   2.2 C HALLENGES
146   The surge in LLMs has inspired increased research and real-world applications (Ouyang et al., 2022; Dubey et al.,
147   2024; Yang et al., 2024). Over the past years, the research focusing on LLMs has shifted to post-training (Dubey
148   et al., 2024), which heavily relies not only on providing reward signals for on-policy algorithms, such as PPO,
149   but also on selecting refined responses in strategies like rejection sampling (Liu et al., 2023a). Therefore, several
150   key challenges remain in the effective deployment and utilization of RMs.
151
      Length Bias It is observed that the text generated after the RLHF stage tends to be longer (Singhal et al., 2023),
152
      while RMs are prone to assign higher rewards to longer sentences (Singhal et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2024). The
153   longer text may contain redundant information compared to shorter, more concise text, which is infeasible for
154   optimizing RL policies. As revealed in Singhal et al. (2023), simply using the length of training samples as a
155   reward has brought significant improvements in downstream PPO tasks. Therefore, mitigating length bias in
156   RMs is a huge challenge to overcome.
157
      Reward Hacking Building a RM that can offer unbiased, accurate reward signals is non-trivial. The standard
158
      RM is trained on limited offline collected datasets, which inevitably brings distribution shift, resulting in the
159   reward hacking problem (Skalse et al., 2022). In addition, as revealed in (Wang et al., 2024), the existing
160   preference datasets are often noisy, containing incorrect and ambiguous preferences. This low-quality data issue
161   further hinders the RM’s ability to generalize to out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios.

225   Ensemble methods. The RM ensemble aims to mitigate the bias of a single RM, thereby deriving a more
226   robust RM by combining different RMs without the need for further training Coste et al. (2023). Several studies
227
      have discussed RM ensembles Eisenstein et al. (2023); Coste et al. (2023); Ramé et al. (2024); Zhang et al.
      (2024b), demonstrating their effectiveness. To summarize, the ensemble methods can be categorized into two
228
      types: logits ensemble (LE) and parameter recompose (PR). LE is used to combine the logits output by various
229
      RMs and estimates the ultimate reward using mean, worst-case, or uncertainty-weighted optimization Coste
230   et al. (2023). Given k RMs, LE requires k times the inference. In contrast, PR is more lightweight. While
231   PR also requires multiple RMs for input, it ensembles different RMs by recomposing their parameter space.
232   Let ϕS = {ϕ1 , ϕ2 , . . . , ϕk } denote the parameter space of k RMs. PR aims to recombine these parameters by
233   averaging the parameter spaces, resulting in an ensembled parameter space, ϕavg . During the inference stage,
234   the model averaged by PR initializes with the averaged parameters, eliminating the overhead of loading multiple
235   RMs and thus reducing inference costs.
236   To explore the explicit impact of the two ensemble methods, we conduct a comprehensive study based on multiple
237   models. To ensure the diversity of the initial models and to investigate the influence of the number of ensembled

240   form a diverse model zoo with distinct alignment capabilities on AlpacaEval 2.0. The win rates of these six
241   aligned models satisfy the following order:
242               Llama3-SFT > Instruct-A > Instruct-B > Instruct-C > Instruct-D > Llama3-Instruct.

      4   R ELATED W ORKS
461   Reward models are essential components that leverage human feedback to fine-tune LLMs, thereby improving
462   alignment with human preferences. Currently, RMs face four main challenges: length bias (Singhal et al., 2023),
463   reward hacking (Skalse et al., 2022), distribution shifts, and inconsistent human preferences. Addressing these
464   challenges is a key focus of ongoing RM research. This section reviews related work on RMs in three areas:
465   model structures, training paradigms, and data composition.
466
      4.1 M ODEL S TRUCTURES
467
468
      To address the instability and potential biases in single reward model, two common architectures are employed:
      the MoE architecture (Quan, 2024) and model ensembling. These approaches also help mitigate issues such
469
      as reward hacking and misalignment with human intentions. DMoERM(Quan, 2024), leverages the MoE ar-
470
      chitecture to resolve issues related to multi-task interference and data labeling noise. This approach enhances
471   generalization ability of the RM. In addition, numerous studies have focused on utilizing model integration to
472   improving the robustness and reliability of RMs. For instance, Eisenstein et al. (2023) analyze the problem of
473   prediction inaccuracies in RMs due to limited training data and examines methods for achieving more robust
474   reward estimations through integrating multiple reward models. Coste et al. (2023) found that conservative op-
475   timization methods, such as Worst-Case Optimization (WCO) and Uncertainty-Weighted Optimization (UWO),
476   effectively reduce over-optimization. Furthermore, Ramé et al. (2024) proposed Weight Averaged Reward Mod-
477   els (WARM). By fine-tuning multiple RMs and averaging them in the weight space, the reliability and robustness
478   of the model can be enhanced under conditions of distributional shifts and preference inconsistencies. Zhang
479
      et al. (2024a) proposed two efficient reward model integration methods: linear layer integration and Low-Rank
      Adaptation (LoRA) basis integration, which aim to address prediction inaccuracies caused by limited training
480
      data.
481
482   4.2 T RAINING PARADIGMS
483   According to (Bai et al., 2022), the training of RMs can be broadly categorized into two paradigms: contrastive
484   training and regression training. In contrastive training, RMs typically rely on the Bradley-Terry model to pull
485   representations of positive pairs closer and push representations of negative pairs further apart. This allows


                                                                                               9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      the RM to learn representations that capture key features of the data without the need for external annotation.
487   Regression training refers to a supervised learning process utilizing regression models to predict continuous
488   values. The regression algorithm attempts to find the relationship between input variables (features) and output
489   variables (targets), focusing on minimizing the error between predicted and actual values. Additionally, due
490   to the underdetermined nature of the Bradley-Terry model, which lacks a unique solution, integrating multiple
491   reward models can present challenges. To address this, Eisenstein et al. (2023) added a regularization term
492   to the maximum likelihood objective function, which ensures that the sum of the reward predictions for each
493   preference pair trends towards zero Recently, Yuan et al. (2024) proposed a Self-Rewarding Language Model
494   (SRLM), which provides its own rewards during training through a "LLM-as-a-Judge" prompt. By iterative
495
      DPO training, the model improves not only its ability to follow instructions but also its capacity to generate
      high-quality rewards.
496
497   4.3 DATA C OMPOSITION
498
      Data composition has a significant impact on the training of RMs. Factors such as data distribution, quality,
499
      diversity, and balance all influence the effectiveness of RM training. Among these, the length of training texts
500   and the composition of positive and negative samples are particularly important. Singhal et al. (2023) found
501   that RMs inherently exhibit a strong preference for longer responses during training. Even when controlling for
502   similar output lengths, RMs tend to assign higher reward scores to longer responses. Addressing this, Wu et al.
503   (2024) proposed a fine-grained RM training approach, which provides rewards at each stage of text generation,
504   for example, at the sentence level, and incorporates various reward models related to different feedback types.
505   The composition of positive and negative samples significantly impacts RM training in three main ways: the
506   intensity of positive samples, the diversity of negative samples, and the balance between positive and negative
507   samples.
508
509
      5   C ONCLUSION
510   In this paper, we conduct research on developing an unbiased RM. We identify two critical problems in RM: the
511   reward-hacking phenomenon and the length bias problem. Based on these two problems, we then decompose
512   the RM training pipeline and identify three main aspects that impact RM performance: the model architecture,
513   training paradigm, and preference data. For each perspective, we conduct a thorough empirical study, revealing
514   intrinsic factors of RM and offering insights on developing a holistic, unbiased RM. We will continue in conduct
515   deeper studies in combining RM with RL policies.
