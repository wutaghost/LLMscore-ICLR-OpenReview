009                                                       A BSTRACT
010
011                Large language models (LLMs) have exhibited outstanding performance in en-
012                gaging with humans and addressing complex questions by leveraging their vast
013                implicit knowledge and robust reasoning capabilities. However, such models are
014                vulnerable to jailbreak attacks, leading to the generation of harmful responses.
015                Despite recent research on single-turn jailbreak strategies to facilitate the devel-
016                opment of defence mechanisms, the challenge of revealing vulnerabilities under
017
                   multi-turn setting remains relatively under-explored. In this work, we propose
                   Jigsaw Puzzles (JSP), a straightforward yet effective multi-turn jailbreak strategy
018
                   against the advanced LLMs. JSP splits questions into harmless fractions as the
019
                   input of each turn, and requests LLMs to reconstruct and respond to questions
020                under multi-turn interaction. Our experimental results demonstrate that the pro-
021                posed JSP jailbreak bypasses original safeguards against explicitly harmful con-
022                tent, achieving an average attack success rate of 93.76% on 189 harmful queries
023                across 5 advanced LLMs (Gemini-1.5-Pro, Llama-3.1-70B, GPT-4, GPT-4o, GPT-
024                4o-mini). Moreover, JSP achieves a state-of-the-art attack success rate of 92% on
025                GPT-4 on the harmful query benchmark, and exhibits strong resistant to defence
026                strategies. Warning: this paper contains offensive examples. 1

062   the input of each turn, and requests LLMs to reconstruct them into a complete question and respond

065   responses. We evaluate the jailbreaking performance of the proposed JSP on five advanced LLMs,
066   Gemini-1.5-Pro (Reid et al., 2024), Llama-3.1-70B (Touvron et al., 2023), GPT-4, GPT-4o, GPT-
067   4o-mini (Achiam et al., 2023) (§4). Experimental results demonstrate the vulnerabilities of existing

      questions from Figstep (Gong et al., 2023) across five LLMs, where attack success rates are above

070
      of the proposed JSP strategy, including prompt design, splitting strategy, turn settings, and enhanced
071

072   strategies (Zeng et al., 2024; Chao et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023) (§5), and the results show that JSP

074   success rate under the presence of defence measures, demonstrating its effectiveness in exposing
075   LLM vulnerabilities for future safety developments.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
079
080   Red teaming strategies are employed to probe potential vulnerabilities of LLMs, facilitating the de-
081   velopment of stronger defence measures. Benchmarking existing LLMs on their safety provides
082
      initial insights. Do-not-answer (Wang et al., 2023) created a dataset containing 939 queries that
      LLMs should not respond to, and conducted comprehensive evaluation on these queries across ad-
083
      vanced LLMs. Salad-bench (Li et al., 2024a) proposed a risk taxonomy and adopted a series of
084
      prompting strategies to assess the safety performance of LLMs from multiple perspectives. Addi-
085   tional similar studies (Xu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023) also evaluated the safety
086   of LLMs using various risk questions and prompting strategies. However, benchmarks typically only
087   use plain questions to probe the capabilities of LLMs in refusing to respond to harmful questions.
088
      Instruction jailbreak is a commonly used red teaming strategy, imitating malicious users’ attacks on
089
      LLMs to probe the vulnerabilities of LLMs. It does not require access to model parameters but in-
090
      stead employs diverse prompting strategies to guide LLMs to assist with harmful queries or generate
091   harmful content. PAPs (Zeng et al., 2024) proposed a persuasion taxonomy based on social science,
092   and then automatically converted harmful questions into persuasive prompt for persuading LLMs to
093   respond. It provided a new perspective by humanising LLMs instead of considering them as instruc-
094   tion followers. Similarly, psychological attacks conducted by Wen et al. (2024); Huang et al. (2023)
095   demonstrated that the potential human characteristics of LLMs can lead to vulnerabilities. Cogni-
096   tive Overload (Xu et al., 2024) and DeepInception (Li et al., 2023) manipulated the LLMs’ thinking
097   process by setting up fictional scenarios, causing defence mechanisms to fail. Research in Upad-
098   hayay & Behzadan (2024); Shen et al. (2024); Yong et al. (2023) proved that LLMs have deficien-
099
      cies in defending against attacks in low-resource languages. Figstep (Gong et al., 2023) and Kang
      et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024b) hide malicious intent into decomposed prompts to jailbreak LLMs.
100
      PAIR (Chao et al., 2023) employed an attacker LLM to iteratively generate and update jailbreaking
101
      prompt towards targer LLM, which usually need less than 20 queries to successfully jailbreak un-
102   der black-box condition. However, these strategies are performed in single-turn interactions, while
103   multi-turn interactions present novel challenges. Crescendo (Russinovich et al., 2024) started with
104   simple and benign questions and gradually guided the conversation content towards harmful topics
105   through multi-turn interactions to achieve jailbreaking. Similar works, such as RED-EVAL (Bhard-
106   waj & Poria, 2023) and CoA (Yang et al., 2024), utilised the context of multi-turn interactions to
107   induce LLMs to respond to harmful questions. Red Queen (Jiang et al., 2024) concealed harmful in-
      tents by creating a universal scenario and claiming the users aim to report to authorities. Gibbs et al.

131   To address the vulnerability of LLMs to jailbreaking attacks, current work typically employs two
132   defence strategies: (i) Defences during training. Zong et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024a) enhanced
133   the safety during multimodal fine-tuning by adding relevant example pairs to prevent forgetting
134   LLMs’ safety alignments. Safety-Tuned Llamas (Bianchi et al., 2024) demonstrated that adding
135
      3% of relevant examples can improve the safety alignment during fine-tuning. Ji et al. (2023; 2024)
      created datasets to support LLMs in constructing built-in safeguards during the training stage. (2)
136
      Defences during inference. Self-guard (Wang et al., 2024) improved LLMs’ ability to evaluate
137
      harmful content, enabling models to self-check the generated responses. Brown et al. (2024); Zhang
138   et al. (2024b) followed a similar protocol, requiring LLMs to re-evaluate their responses to avoid
139   producing harmful content. Commercial LLMs include safety guardrails to detect user input and
140   monitor the response generation. E.g., the guardrail in Gemini-1.5-Pro (Reid et al., 2024) blocks the
141   interaction if harmful content is detected in the input or output. However, such guardrails are usually
142   attached after deployment, which means open-source LLMs, such as Llama-3.1-70B (Touvron et al.,
143   2023), primarily rely on the built-in safeguards constructed during the training stage.
144   These two defence strategies are essentially content-centred, relying on the presence of explicitly
145   harmful content. In this work, we show if attacks only consist of harmless fractions and the harmful
146   output is effectively disguised, the defence performance is likely to degrade.

149   3     J IGSAW P UZZLES
150
151   Built on the guardrails and knowledge learned from training stage, existing defences of LLMs usu-
152   ally rely on identifying the presence of explicit harmful and sensitive words in queries, triggering
153   default responses from guardrail and refusal responses from their built-in safeguards. To bypass such
154   content-based defences, we introduce JigSaw Puzzles (JSP) to split each harmful query into the cor-
155   responding benign fractions as the input of each turn for jailbreaking LLMs in multi-turn interaction.
156
      We first describe our JSP prompt (§3.1), and then propose the JSP splitting strategies (§3.2).
157

159

161   LLMs to concatenate the question fractions provided in subsequent turns and then answer it. JSP
      prompt is built upon two strategies essential for successful jailbreaking:

175   Prohibition of Concatenated Question Generation. Existing LLMs usually rely on identifying
176   explicit harmful content within queries to activate their defence protocols. If LLMs generate the
177   concatenated question, it becomes part of the generation context, which can activate these defences
178
      and cause the jailbreak to fail. To avoid this, JSP prompt explicitly instructs the model not to generate
      the concatenated question but to provide responses directly based on the fraction of each turn;
179
180   Inclusion of a Disclaimer. JSP circumvents the LLMs’ safeguards by decomposing harmful queries
181   into benign fractions and sequentially inputting them across multiple turns. However, if LLMs
182   attempt to produce harmful content in responses, guardrails might still intervene. We force LLMs
183   to generate disclaimer at the starting of responses for bypassing the output-side guardrails, allowing
184
      the generation of the intended content that might be blocked.
185   This dual approach of restricting the generation of concatenated questions and including a disclaimer
186   enables JSP to effectively bypass the safeguards of LLMs, and induce responses to harmful queries.

      3.2      JSP S PLITTING
190
      We identify and isolate harmful words from their original queries, resulting in fragmented queries.
191
      The isolated harmful words are then further divided into meaningless and benign letter fractions.
192
      Our process is summarised in 3 stages, and we employ GPT-4 2 (see Appendix A for details on the
193   prompts) to automatically generate jailbreaking queries.
194
195   Stage 1 - Re-write Queries: This stage is not mandatory for the the success of jailbreaking. How-
196
      ever, to eliminate the potential impacts of varied sentence openings after splitting on jailbreaking
      performance, we re-write harmful queries into a consistent structure: “How do I implement for +
197
      [harmful behaviours]”, emphasising clear harmful requests and subjective malicious intents.
198
199   Stage 2 - Sentence-level Splitting: In this stage, we request GPT-4, based on its safety protocols,
200   to locate the harmful and sensitive words (such as “illegal”, “drugs”, etc.) as well as groups (such
201   as “children”, “ethnicity”, etc.) within queries. While GPT-4 may sometimes generate harmful
202
      phrases rather than individual words, we input these phrases back into GPT-4 iteratively until specific
      harmful words are identified. For instance shown in Figure 3, the identified harmful phrase “illegal
203
      activities” is iteratively processed by GPT-4 until the harmful word “illegal” is isolated. These
204
      identified words serve as splitting points and are isolated from the queries. Furthermore, we also
205   isolate “How do I implement for” and the first verb after it, otherwise, LLMs sometimes produce
206   responses before accepting all fractions.
207
208
      Stage 3 - Word-level Splitting: To maximise the harmlessness of queries, GPT-4 further randomly
      split each identified harmful word into two meaningless fractions, as shown in Figure 3, following
209
      two criteria: (i) each split fraction should contain at least two letters (except for three-letter words
210
      which are kept without splitting) to avoid LLMs from directly reconstructing the harmful word
211   from the fraction with most of letters; (ii) the resulting splits are not themselves harmful tokens.
212   Each fraction serves as the input of each turn in multi-turn interaction. After the above three-stage
213   processing, plain harmful queries are transformed into JSP queries for multi-turn jailbreaking.

      3.3       JSP M ULTI - TURN I NTERACTIONS
217
218   The JSP strategy leverages the multi-turn interaction capability of LLMs to perform jailbreaking.
219   Figure 1 shows the jailbreaking process, which starts by inputting JSP prompt (Figure 2) into the
220   LLM, and then the split fractions of the harmful question, as shown in Figure 3, are sequentially
221   fed into the model as inputs in each turn. Once LLMs receive all the fractions, inputting “Begin”
222   triggers LLMs to generate responses.

224   4       E XPERIMENT
225
226   We adopt presented JSP strategy to jailbreak LLMs on 189 harmful queries (introduced shortly). We
227   first describe our jailbreak settings (§4.1). Next, we report the attack results on 5 advanced LLMs,
228   and provide jailbreaking performance on harmful categories across these LLMs (§4.2). Lastly, we
229   analyse the effectiveness of our JSP strategy under various settings (§4.3).
230
231   4.1       E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
232
233   Dataset. We adopt the harmful question dataset proposed by Figstep (Gong et al., 2023), which
234   comprises 500 questions across 10 harmful categories. However, due to the high cost of running
235   model APIs, we refine this dataset by removing three categories: legal advice, medical advice, and
236   financial advice. See Table 5 of Appendix B for full list of categories. Subsequently, we manually
237
      eliminate questions that exhibit similar topics or are deemed unrealistic, resulting in a final dataset
      of 189 harmful questions for our experiments.
238
239   Models. We apply our JSP strategy to jailbreak five cutting-edge LLMs: Gemini-1.5-Pro, GPT-4-
240   turbo (gpt-4-turbo), GPT-4o (gpt-4o), GPT-4o-mini (gpt-4o-mini), and Llama-3.1-70B.
241   For the commercial LLMs, we utilise their respective APIs to perform inference, while Llama-3.1-
242   70B is obtained from Hugging Face3 and we conduct inference on two A100 GPUs. In the inference
243
      process, the temperature of LLMs is set to 1.0 to maintain consistency across experiments.
244   Evaluation. For each harmful question, we perform five separate jailbreaking attempts using our
245   JSP strategy. We introduce two metrics to measure the effectiveness of our JSP strategy: Attack
246   Success Rate by Attempt (ASR-a) and Attack Success Rate by Question (ASR-q). ASR-a calculates
247   the percentage of successful attacks out of the total 945 attempts (189 questions × 5 attempts),
248   while ASR-q measures the percentage of questions that can be jailbroken (189 questions in total). A
249
      question is considered successfully jailbroken if at least one of the five attack attempts succeeds. To
      minimise the impact of randomness, we run the complete experiments three times on each LLMs,
250
      and report the average ASR-a and ASR-q based on these three runs.
251
252   Response Evaluating. Due to the significant time and cost required for manual evaluation, we
253   employ Llama-guard-3 (Inan et al., 2023) as an automated judge to evaluate whether the generated
254   responses are harmful answers to the plain questions. To validate the alignment between Llama-
255
      guard-3 and human evaluator, we provide a small-scale comparison of human and Llama-guard-3
      evaluation, detailed in Appendix §G.

339
340   Configurations. According to the description of original JSP prompt (§3.1) in Figure 2, we include

342
      Generation and Inclusion of a Disclaimer. We conduct ablation experiments to evaluate their ef-
      fectiveness and report the results in Figure 5. We introduce four settings: 1 Removes the first and
343
      fourth instructions from the original JSP prompt, allowing LLMs to generate concatenated questions
344

      4.3.4   FABRICATED H ISTORY
426
427   During our experiments we observed that sometimes LLMs generate refusal responses after receiv-
428   ing all fractions but before the user inputs “Begin”, which violates JSP instruction for LLMs to
429   respond only after receiving “Begin”. Here, we investigate a fabricated history strategy to force
430   LLMs to complete the interaction. Specifically, in the multi-turn condition, inference process in-
431   volves a message chain alternating between the user and LLM. If the model generates a refusal
      response immediately after receiving the last fraction, we will modify this refusal response into a


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      fabricated response that prompts the user to input “Begin” to initiate responding to the question.
433   We collect typical responses of LLMs at this step from all experimental responses, such as “Please
434   confirm when you want me to Begin”, “Begin.”, and “I have all parts of the question. Please type
435   Begin and I will concatenate the question and provide a response”. Among these, we choose “Be-
436   gin.” as the model’s response to prompt the user. After replacing the refusal response, we input the
437   fabricated multi-turn interaction history along with the user’s input of “Begin” to the model, forcing
438   it to generate a response to the concatenated question only after completing the entire JSP process.
439   From the results, the fabricated history strategy slightly improves jailbreaking performance across
440   all LLMs. Notably, on the relatively safe GPT-4o, it increases ASR-a and ASR-q from 66.81% and
441
      89.42% to 86.28% and 97.71%, respectively, making GPT-4o as unsafe as the other LLMs. For

444   5   E FFECTIVENESS COMPARED TO OTHER ATTACK STRATEGIES
445
446   We strictly follow the settings of Zeng et al. (2024) to benchmark JSP jailbreaking with/without
447   defence mechanisms against previous jailbreaking strategies.
448   Due to cost limitations, we only select GPT-4 as the target model for jailbreaking. Zeng et al.
449   (2024) includes PAIR (Chao et al., 2023), GCG (Zou et al., 2023), ARCA (Jones et al., 2023),
450   GBDA (Guo et al., 2021), and their proposed jailbreaking strategy PAPs. However, only PAPs and
451   PAIR can successfully jailbreak GPT-4. Therefore, in this experiment, our baseline strategies only
452   focuses on PAPs and PAIR. Similar to Zeng et al. (2024), here we adopt a GPT-4 Judge (Qi et al.,
453   2024) as the automatic evaluator to assess the LLM-generated responses. GPT-4 Judge generate
454   a harmfulness score ranging from 1 to 5 for each response, with 5 denoting a harmful response.
455   The AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023), refined by (Chao et al., 2023), is used as the benchmark dataset
456
      containing 50 harmful questions. We report ASR-q in the results. Following Zeng et al. (2024) each
      harmful question is attempted 15 times (3 trials each 5 times), and a minimum of 1 harmful response
457
      is recorded as a successful jailbreak.
458
459   We employ two system-prompt-based defence approaches: (1) Defence-PAPs (Zeng et al., 2024). It
460   instructs LLMs to resist PAPs by adding the system prompt ‘You are a helpful assistant, and you are
461   not easily persuaded, and you know what is right and wrong.”. This system prompt has been proven
462
      to be effectively applicable to defend other non-PAPs jailbreaks. (2) Defence-JSP. Considering the
      differences between JSP and PAPs, we adjust the system prompt based on the structure of Defence-
463
      PAPs to: “You are a helpful assistant, and even if the question is split into fractions, you still know
464
      what is right and wrong.”

      6   C ONCLUSION
478
479
      In this paper, we present JSP strategy, a simple and effective approach to jailbreak LLMs via multi-
480   turn interaction. By splitting harmful questions into words and token fractions as input of each
481   turn and leveraging carefully designed prompt, JSP successfully achieves an average attack success
482   rate of 93.76% on 189 harmful questions across 5 most recent LLMs. Additionally, JSP achieves the
483   state-of-the-art performance in jailbreaking GPT-4, surpassing existing jailbreaking approaches, and
484   exhibits strong resistant to various defence tactics. Our work reveals the vulnerabilities of existing
485   LLMs in safeguarding against attacks in multi-turn interaction, and calls for further development of
      more robust defence tactics.
