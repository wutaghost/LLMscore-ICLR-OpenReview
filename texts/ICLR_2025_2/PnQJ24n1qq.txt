009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Various data modalities are common in real-world applications. In healthcare, for
012            example, electronic health records, medical images, and clinical notes provide
013            comprehensive information for diagnosis and treatment. Thus, it is essential to
014            develop multimodal learning methods that aggregate information from multiple
015            modalities to generate meaningful representations for downstream tasks. The key
016
               challenge here is how to appropriately align the representations of the respective
               modalities and fuse them into a joint distribution. Existing methods mainly focus
017
               on fusing the representations via concatenation or the Kronecker product, which
018
               oversimplifies the interaction structure between modalities, prompting the need
019            to model more complex interactions. Moreover, the notion of joint distribution
020            of the latent representation that incorporates higher-order interactions between
021            modalities is also underexplored. Copula is a powerful statistical structure in
022            modelling the interactions between variables, as it bridges the joint distribution
023            and marginal distributions of multiple variables. In this paper, we propose a
024            novel copula modelling-driven multimodal learning framework, which focuses
025            on learning the joint distribution of various modalities to capture the complex
026            interaction among them. The key idea is interpreting the copula model as a tool
027
               to align the marginal distributions of the modalities efficiently. By assuming a
               Gaussian mixture distribution for each modality and a copula model on the joint
028
               distribution, our model can also generate accurate representations for missing
029
               modalities. Extensive experiments on public MIMIC datasets demonstrate the
030            superior performance of our model over other competitors. Ablation studies also
031            validate the effectiveness of the copula alignment strategy and the robustness of our
032            model over different choices of the copula family. Code is anonymously available
033            at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/CM2-C1FD/README.md.

036   1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Multimodal learning aims to aggregate information from multiple modalities to generate meaningful
039
      representations for downstream tasks. It has been widely explored in the context of vision-language
      models (Fu et al., 2023; El Banani et al., 2023), audio-visual applications (Chen et al., 2023; Mo
040
      & Tian, 2023; Huang et al., 2023), image-video models (Girdhar et al., 2023; Gan et al., 2023) and
041
      healthcare applications (Wu et al., 2024a; Hayat et al., 2022). For example, multimodal learning
042   has been applied to various healthcare tasks such as clinical prediction tasks (Zhang et al., 2023;
043   Wu et al., 2024a), report generation (Song et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023), and clinical trial site
044   selection (Theodorou et al., 2024). The existing fusion strategies can be divided into early, joint, or
045   late fusion (Huang et al., 2020), where the joint fusion paradigm is the most popular strategy and its
046   core idea is to model the interactions between the representations of the input modalities (Hayat et al.,
047   2022). The resulting fused embedding encodes the structural interaction between the modalities,
048   enabling accurate prediction for each modality.
049   However, due to the heterogeneity of different modalities (e.g., medical images, medical reports,
050   EHRs), properly aligning the distributions of the various modalities remains a challenging problem.
051   The existing alignment strategies mainly rely on concatenation or Kronecker products which oversim-
052   plify the interaction between different modalities. A recent work Salzmann et al. (2022) emphasizes
053   simple probabilistic assumptions on the marginals and neglects to explore statistical assumptions
      about the joint distributions of the modalities. This approach may result in biased fused representa-

054
      tions, limiting the performance of downstream tasks and the generalizability and robustness of the
055   resulting multimodal models. Therefore, there is still a need for an approach that more appropriately
056   aligns the distributions of modalities and models the potentially complex interactions between them.
057
058
      Copula models have shown great success in modelling the interactions of variables as they construct
      a bridge between the joint distribution and their marginals (Cherubini, 2004). However, copula
059
      models are less explored in the deep learning field as most of the existing approaches heavily rely
060
      on sampling-based methods (e.g., MCMC (Silva & Gramacy, 2009)), which are relatively slow
061   and difficult to scale to modern deep learning settings (Smith & Loaiza-Maya, 2023). Although
062   some recent works are attempting to introduce copula to deep learning models through stochastic
063   variational inference (Smith & Loaiza-Maya, 2023), the potential of copula in multimodal learning is
064   still underexplored.
065
      Moreover, existing multimodal learning methods mostly assume the existence of all modalities. In
066
      reality, some modalities may be missing for some observations due to various reasons, e.g., missing
067   medical images or reports for some patients due to clinical and administrative factors in healthcare,
068   which pose a major challenge in multimodal learning. The existing solutions either discard these
069   observations or impute simple values (e.g., zeros or means from other observations) to address the
070   missing modality problem. However, these approaches ignore the marginal distributions of the
071   modality and often mislead the learning of the joint distribution. Therefore, properly learning the
072   marginal distribution is also necessary to generate unbiased representations for the observations with
073   missing modalities.
074   In light of the above challenges, we propose a novel copula modelling-driven multimodal learning
075   framework, namely CM2 (Cross-Modal alignment via variational Copula Modelling), to tackle the
076   joint fusion paradigm from a probabilistic perspective. Our contributions can be summarized as:
077   (1) We for the first time introduce copula modelling into multimodal learning, where we interpret
078   copula as an effective tool of distribution alignment, guaranteed by Sklar’s theorem. (2) We employ
079   a Gaussian mixture model on the marginal distribution of each modality to enable more flexible
080   modelling of the high-dimensional feature distribution of different modalities. (3) We adopt stochastic
081
      variational inference to optimize the copula model, which enables the scalability of our model
      to large-scale datasets. (4) We adopt the learned marginal distribution as the data generator to
082
      accurately impute the missing observations. (5) Empirical results on real multimodal MIMIC datasets
083
      demonstrate the good performance of our method and ablation analysis corroborates the effectiveness
084   of copula in modality alignments and robustness to potential variations.

088
089
      Multimodal Representation Learning. Multimodal representation learning aims to effectively
090
      integrate information from different modalities for accurate predictions on the downstream tasks.
091
      Early works (Hayat et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2022; Trong et al., 2020) focus on late fusion that
092   merges unimodal representations via, for instance, concatenation or Kronecker product. However,
093   such approaches oversimplify the interactions of the modalities and mostly lead to biased fused
094   representations. Therefore, the structural interactions of the modalities need to be encoded in the
095   fused representation for more effective multimodal learning. Recently, modelling the interaction
096   between modalities has received increasing attention. Liang et al. (2024) proposed an information
097   decomposition framework to define and quantify different types of interactions between modalities.
098   Transformer-based methods have greatly facilitated the progress by modelling the cross-model
099   tokens (Zhang et al., 2023; Theodorou et al., 2024). However, matching the correspondence with
100
      transformers introduces high computational complexity, which prompts a more efficient approach for
      representation alignment.
101
102   Copula Deep Learning. Copula is a promising tool in modelling the interactions or correlations
103   between variables and it constructs a bridge between the joint distribution and marginal distributions.
104   Copula has been widely applied in financial risk management (Hofert, 2021; Rodriguez, 2007),
105   signal processing, and healthcare (Zeng & Wang, 2022) due to its capability in modelling complex
106   interactions. Traditional copula models rely on closed-form solutions of the likelihood and estimate
107   the copula parameter with sampling-based approaches (e.g., MCMC (Silva & Gramacy, 2009)).
      However, these algorithms suffer from high time complexity, making them less applicable to high-

      Learning with Missing Data. Traditional multimodal learning assumes all modalities are available,
141
      but in reality, some observations may be missing, e.g., missing medical images or reports in clinical
142   data. Late fusion is a common strategy to address missing modalities by aggregating predictions (Yoo
143   et al., 2019) or latent space representations (Theodorou et al., 2024) from the available modalities.
144   Although it is effective, it treats each modality independently and lacks interaction between them.
145   Some researches focus on extracting shared information across modalities to perform downstream
146   tasks (Deldari et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2024). However, learning such shared representations can be
147   challenging, particularly when the modalities are highly heterogeneous, as in the case of EHRs and
148   CXRs. Many approaches attempt to preserve model performance via modelling the relationships
149   between them (Zhang et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2024b) or generating a global representation for the
150
      missing data (Hayat et al., 2022). Other methods assume that the missing modality follows a certain
      distribution, imputing the missing values using the mean or mode of that distribution (Ma et al., 2021).
151
      Despite their successes, these distributional assumptions may be inaccurate, potentially introducing
152
      bias into the model. Therefore a probabilistic assumption is needed to guarantee the unbiasedness of
153   learned marginal distributions.

      3.2   C OPULA M ULTIMODAL L EARNING

180   The overview of the proposed copula-driven multimodal learning framework is shown in Figure
181   1. Given multimodal data, we extract each modality-specific embedding and compute its Gaussian
182   mixture model (GMM). We then model the marginal densities and estimate the joint distribution using
183   a copula family C. If modality m is missing, we generate feature embeddings from its GMM. The
184   concatenated embeddings z are passed through a fusion module and an MLP classifier for prediction.
185   The ELBO combines the copula log-likelihood and task-specific loss.

      3.4   H ANDLING M ISSING M ODALITY
247
248   Thanks to the probabilistic design of our method, our framework can also generate pseudo obser-
249   vations for missing modalities. Without loss of generality, we consider missing modalities with
250   complete labels where only the observations are missing. The learned GMM for each modality can
251   be treated as a data generation model, and we can generate feature embeddings through sampling
                                                  (i)
252   from the GMM of each modality (i.e., zm ∼ Fm ). Then the generated feature embeddings can be
253   treated as the future input to the classification layer and predictions can be obtained.
254   By learning the copula parameter α, the marginal distribution on each modality contains information
255                                                                                                    (i)
      from other modalities and information of the interactions. The generated feature representation xm
256
      can therefore better reflect the characteristics of the joint distribution, which would improve the
257   quality of the representation and the downstream task performance as a result.

      3.5   T HEORETICAL G UARANTEE WITH S KLAR ’ S T HEOREM .
260
261   We make use of Sklar’s theorem to demonstrate the uniqueness of the joint distribution. Sklar’s
262   theorem is given as follows.
263   Theorem 1. (Sklar’s theorem) (Sklar, 1959) Let F (x1 , . . . , xM ) be an M -variate c.d.f. for
264   (X1 , . . . , XM ) with the marginal c.d.f. for the m-th variable given by Fm (xm ), m = 1, . . . , M .
265   Then:
266
267         1. There exists an M -dimensional copula such that
268                                     C(F1 (x1 ), . . . , FM (xM )) = F (x1 , . . . , xM )                (2)
269
               for all xm ∈ R.


                                                            5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
            2. Conversely, given any copula C and univariate c.d.f.s F1 , . . . , FM , C is a valid joint c.d.f.
271            for (X1 , . . . , XM ). Furthermore, if F is continuous, then C in Eq. (2) is unique.
272
273
274   The above theorem allows us to construct joint distributions with the same margin but different
275
      dependence structures, or conversely by fixing the dependence structure and variating the behaviour
      in individual modalities (Tagasovska et al., 2019). This allows us to update the marginal distributions
276
      and the copula parameter separately. Furthermore, since we assume a GMM for each modality
277
      and they are continuous by definition, the uniqueness of the copula C can be guaranteed and the
278   identifiability of the model can be enhanced.

346   We compare CM2 against the following baselines: (1) MMTM (Joze et al., 2020) is a flexible plugin
347   module that facilitates information exchange between modalities. Since the model assumes full
348   modality availability, we compensate for missing CXR and clinical notes during training and testing
349   by filling in all zeros. (2) DAFT (Pölsterl et al., 2021) is a module designed to exchange information
350   between tabular data and image modalities when integrated into CNN models. Similarly, we replace
351   missing CXR and clinical notes with zero matrices during training and testing. (3) Unified (Hayat
352   et al., 2021) is a dynamic approach for integrating auxiliary data modalities, learning modality-specific
353   representations, and combining them via a unified classifier. It handles missing data inherently and
354
      leverages all available modality-specific information. (4) MedFUSE (Hayat et al., 2022) employs
      LSTM-based fusion to combine features from image or language encoders with EHR encoders. It
355
      handles missing modalities by learning a global representation for absent CXR or clinical notes.
356
      (5) DrFuse (Yao et al., 2024) leverages disentangled representation learning to create a shared
357   representation between the EHR and image modalities, even when one modality is missing.

507   5   C ONCLUSION
508
509   In this work, we introduce copula modelling into multimodal representation learning. Using a copula
510   can effectively model the interaction between the modalities, and impute the missing modalities
511   through sampling from learned marginals. Empirical evaluation validates the predictive performance
512   on multimodal learning tasks, on both the fully and partially matched datasets. Ablation studies
513   showed that the proposed copula model can serve as a promising modality alignment tool and the
514   consistent satisfactory performance over different copula families. Our work can be potentially
515   extended to works that require effective fusion or distribution alignment, including domain adaptation,
516
      multi-feature and multi-view learning.
517
      Limitations and Future Works. Using a neural network to learn the copula parameter α may be
518   insufficient (since the joint log-likelihood may not be necessarily convex). Hence an alternative
519   updating algorithm (e.g., partial likelihood) is needed in future development of copula multimodal
520   learning to ensure that each loss is convex and we can apply gradient descent. In addition, we select
521   healthcare datasets to demonstrate the effectiveness of our model, while we will extend our method
522   to other multimodal datasets in future works.

713   A.1      DATASETS
714
715   Table 8 provides a summary of the datasets used in our experiments.
716
      MIMIC-III dataset This dataset contains 46,520 ICU stays, each with 17 clinical variables. We split
717
      the dataset into training, validation, and test sets in a 70%-15%-15% ratio, following the procedure in
718   (Harutyunyan et al., 2019).
719
720   MIMIC-IV dataset This dataset includes 21,139 ICU stays, also with 17 clinical variables. The data
721   is split into 70% training, 10% validation, and 20% test sets, following (Hayat et al., 2022).
722
      For both MIMIC-III and MIMIC-IV datasets, we extract 17 clinical variables commonly monitored
723   in the ICU, including 5 categorical and 12 continuous variables. Data are sampled every two hours
724   during the first 48 hours of ICU admission for both tasks, in accordance with (Hayat et al., 2022).
725   This results in a vector representation of size 76 at each time step of the clinical time-series data.
726
727   MIMIC-CXR dataset This dataset contains 377,110 chest X-ray images, of which 5,931 are
728   associated with MIMIC-IV ICU stays. We split the data into 4,287 training samples, 465 validation
729
      samples, and 1,179 test samples. Following (Hayat et al., 2022), we retrieve the last Anterior-Posterior
      (PA) projection chest X-ray and apply transformations to the images, resizing them to 224 × 224
730
      pixels.
731
732   This dataset also includes radiology reports, which are unstructured text data. We choose the radiology
733   reports of the MIMIC-CXR dataset as an auxiliary modality to investigate the effectiveness of CM2
734   on more modality alignment since the radiology reports do not contain death information and can
735
      avoid possible overfitting and shortcuts. We divide the unstructured radiology reports into 4 sections,
      including Impression, Findings, Last paragraph, and Comparison.
736
737   MIMIC-III NOTE dataset This dataset consists of 5,273 clinical notes associated with MIMIC-III
738   ICU stays. The data is divided into 3,652 training samples, 815 validation samples, and 806 test
739   samples. In line with (Zhang et al., 2023), we select the last five clinical notes before the prediction
740   time. If fewer than five notes are available, we treat the notes for that ICU stay as missing. The
741   original number of matched ICU stays is around 15,000. We randomly sample one-third of the
742   matched ICU stays to form the training, validation, and test sets, keeping the scale of the notes nearly
743   the same as the CXRs in the MIMIC-IV dataset.
744   Both radiology reports sections and clinical notes are capped at a maximum length of 512 words,
745   tokenized into words, and embedded into 312-dimensional vectors using the pre-trained TinyBERT
746   model (Jiao et al., 2019)1 .

       CM2 is implemented in Python 3.11 using PyTorch 1.9. Following MedFuse (Hayat et al., 2022), we
973    use ResNet34 (He et al., 2016) as the backbone encoder for CXR, a two-layer LSTM (Graves &
974    Graves, 2012) as the encoder for time-series data, and pre-trained TinyBERT (Jiao et al., 2019)2 as
975    the encoder for clinical notes. We include a projection layer to map modality embeddings into the
976    same latent space. A two-layer LSTM is used as the fusion module to combine modality embeddings,
977    and a multilayer perceptron (MLP) with one linear layer and a sigmoid activation function serves as
978    the classifier.

980    E.2     A DDITIONAL S ETTINGS OF BASELINE M ETHODS
981
982    We compare CM2 with the following baseline methods.
983
984
                • MMTM (Joze et al., 2020) is a module that can leverage the information between modalities
                  with flexible plugin architectures. Since the model assumes full modality, we compensate
985
                  for the missing modality CXR and clinical notes with all zeros during training and testing.
986
                  For clinical notes, we replace the ResNet34 encoder with TinyBERT to embed the clinical
987               notes.
988
                • DAFT (Pölsterl et al., 2021) is a module that can be plugged into CNN models to exchange
989
                  information between tabular data and image modality. Similarly, we replace the input
990
                  of CXR and clinical notes with matrices of all zeros during training and testing and use
991               TinyBERT to embed the clinical notes.
992
                • Unified (Hayat et al., 2021) is a dynamic approach towards integrating auxiliary data
993
                  modalities, learning the data representations for the individual modalities, and integrating
994
                  the representations via a unified classifier. It inherently handles missingness and leverages
995               all of the available modality-specific data. Also, we use TinyBERT to embed the clinical
996               notes.
997
                • MedFuse (Hayat et al., 2022) uses an LSTM-based fusion to combine features from the
998
                  image encoder (or language encoder) and EHR encoder. Missing modality is handled
999
                  by learning a global representation for the missing CXR or clinical notes. We randomly
1000              initialized encoders for the time-series data, clinical notes, and CXR images.
1001
                • DrFuse (Yao et al., 2024) uses disentangled representation learning to learn a shared
1002
                  representation between the EHR and image modality even when one modality is missing.
1003
                  Drfuse uses ResNet50 as the image encoder and Transformer as the EHR encoder. We
1004              replace the ResNet50 encoder with TinyBERT to embed the clinical notes.
1005
1006   The Implementation of DrFuse follows the original paper(Yao et al., 2024)3 , and we use the same
1007   hyperparameters as the original paper. We directly adopt the implementations of MMTM, DAFT,
1008   Unified, and MedFuse provided by (Hayat et al., 2022)4 , and all hyperparameters are set to the
1009   default values provided by Hayat et al. (2022). We adapt the implementations of MMTM, DAFT,
1010   Unified, MedFuse and DrFuse to tri-modal setting, including EHR time-series data, CXR images,
1011
       and radiology reports.
