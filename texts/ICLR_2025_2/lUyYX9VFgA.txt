010
011           Large Language Models (LLMs) have rapidly advanced in multiple capa-
012           bilities, such as text and code understanding, leading to their widespread
013           use in a wide range of applications, such as healthcare, education, and
014           search. Due to the critical nature of these applications, there has been a
015           heightened emphasis on aligning these models to human values and prefer-
016           ences to improve safety and reliability. In this paper, we demonstrate that
017           contemporary efforts fall severely short of the ultimate goal of AI safety
018           and fail to ensure safe, non-toxic outputs. We systematically evaluate the
019
              safety of LLMs through a novel model interaction paradigm dubbed Code
              of Thought (CoDoT) prompting that transforms natural language (NL)
              prompts into pseudo-code. CoDoT represents NL inputs in a precise,
020
021
              structured, and concise form, allowing us to utilize its programmatic in-
022           terface to test several facets of AI safety. Under the CoDoT prompting
023           paradigm, we show that a wide range of large language models emit highly
024           toxic outputs with the potential to cause great harm. CoDoT leads to
025           a staggering 16.5× increase in toxicity on GPT-4 Turbo and a massive
026           4.6× increase on average, across multiple models and languages. Notably,
027           we find that state-of-the-art mixture-of-experts (MoE) models are approx-
028           imately 3× more susceptible to toxicity than standard architectures. Our
029           findings raise a troubling concern that recent safety and alignment efforts
030
              have regressed LLMs and inadvertently introduced safety backdoors and
              blind spots. Our work calls for an urgent need to rigorously evaluate the
              design choices of safety efforts from first principles, given the rapid adoption
031

              of LLMs.

035   1   Introduction
036
037   Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have led to their widespread de-
038   ployment in critical domains where they interface with humans, such as healthcare, finance,
039   and education. The rapid adoption of LLMs has led to significant efforts to develop novel
040   methods that align with human values and preferences. These safety measures permeate
041   the entire AI pipeline: from improving training data quality and implementing preference-
042   based fine-tuning to employing safer prompting techniques and content filtering at inference
043   time (Lukas et al., 2023; Ouyang et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2023b). These efforts attempt to
044
      improve safety and reliability to deploy these models at an unprecedented scale.
045   In this paper, we reveal that recent research efforts largely fail to address the root cause
046   of AI safety and alignment concerns. We develop code-of-thought prompting (CoDoT), a
047   novel model interaction paradigm, and demonstrate that state-of-the-art models trained on
048   novel safety measures catastrophically fail, emitting harmful and toxic outputs. The toxicity
049   exhibited by LLMs can increase up to 1500%, rendering them useless and dangerous.
050   Code of Thought (CoDoT) prompting transforms standard natural language prompts into
051   structured pseudo-code and enables practitioners to probe LLMs through rich programmatic
052   abstractions (Figure 1). Hence, we can leverage CoDoT to transform a common natural
053   language prompt into sophisticated programs where sentence parameters and intent are
      clear. For instance, consider a simple natural language prompt such as, “Make the following

144   2     Methodology
145
146   This work investigates how CoDoT prompting bypasses LLM safety measures. We begin
147   by formalizing the problem statement.
148
149   2.1   Formalization of the Induction and Amplification tasks
150
151   Let X be the set of all possible inputs and Y be the set of all possible outputs for a Large
152   Language Model (LLM). Let φ be a particular characteristic or property of the input or
153   output, such as exhibiting toxicity or containing hateful content. We define fφ : X → Y to
154
      be a transformation function that generates the output y ∈ Y for the given input x ∈ X ,
      where the property φ is present in y. This work studies two types of transformations:
      Induction and Amplification.
155
156
157   Definition 2.1. Induction is a transformation that introduces the property φ in an input.
158   Mathematically, given the function fφ , induction occurs when the output fφ (x) exhibits a
159
      stronger presence of the property φ than the input x.
160   Definition 2.2. Amplification is a transformation that potentially increases the intensity
161   or prominence of φ in the input by iterative application of the induction transformation over
      intermediate outputs. Mathematically, given the function fφ and a positive integer n > 1,


                                                   3

      amplification occurs when fφ (x) tends to exhibit a stronger presence of the property φ
162                                (n)
163
      than fφ (x), where fφ (x) denotes the n-fold composition of fφ (i.e., fφ applied n times).
                          (n)
164
165
166   To quantify these concepts, we introduce an evaluation function ε : Y → R that measures
167
      the degree of presence of property φ in the output. Using this function, we can now define:
168
              Induction: For the transformation fφ , induction occurs when for x ∈ X :
169
              ε(fφ (x)) > ε(x).
170
              Amplification: For some positive integer n, amplification occurs when for
171

      We now turn our attention to LLMs and the concept of prompting. We represent an LLM
      as a function M : P → Y, where P is the space of all possible prompts or instructions. A
175
      prompting strategy p : X → P generates a prompt from a given input. The transformation
176
      function can then be represented as a composition of the prompting strategy and the LLM
177   inference i.e., fφ = (M ◦ p).
178
179   LLMs are typically designed with built-in safety measures to restrict certain aspects of their
180
      output. These safety mechanisms aim to prevent the generation of harmful, biased, or
      inappropriate content. They often involve filtering techniques, content warnings, or refusing
181
      to engage with certain requests. However, the effectiveness and robustness of these safety
182
      measures remain an essential area of study.
183
184   This work focuses on a particular aspect of safety in LLMs: the generation and amplification
185   of toxic content. Toxicity in language can manifest as hate speech, severe insults, profanity,
186
      or other forms of harmful language. By examining how different prompting strategies can
      influence the toxicity of LLM outputs, we aim to better understand these models’ capabilities
187
      and potential vulnerabilities.
188
189   We now adapt the above definitions specifically for the study of toxicity in language models.
190   Let T be the set of all possible strings constituted by the vocabulary of an LLM, such
191   that P = X = Y = T . We define a toxicity measurement function τ : T → [0, 1] as a
192
      specific instance of ε for measuring the toxicity level of a given string. Toxicity induction
      and amplification are defined as:
193
194
              Toxicity Induction: For a significantly large subset S ⊂ T , toxicity in-
              duction occurs when for s ∈ S: τ ((M ◦ p)(s))) > τ (s)
195
196
197
              Toxicity Amplification: For a positive integer n and a significantly
              large subset S ⊂ T , toxicity amplification occurs when for s ∈ S:
              τ ((M ◦ p)(n) (s)) > τ (M ◦ p(s)) where (M ◦ p)(n) denotes the n-fold appli-
198
199
              cation of M with the prompting strategy p.

      2.2   Code of Thought Prompting
202
203
      While other prompting methodologies, such as persona-based prompting (Deshpande et al.,
204   2023b), can shape the input distributions, we focus on CoDoT prompting for several rea-
205   sons. CoDoT leverages the vast quantities of code structures that language models have
206   encountered during training, making it a natural and familiar format for them to process.
207   Moreover, CoDoT offers an efficient way to semantically encode the user’s intent through
208   structures such as function names, parameters, etc. CoDoT strikes an optimal balance,
209   providing sufficient structure while allowing for maximum expressibility in conveying in-
210   structions. Additionally, code-based prompts are easy to construct and evaluate, facilitating
211   systematic analysis of model responses.
212   Similar to standard prompting, we follow a zero-shot strategy using the user role provision
213   (Brown et al., 2020). However, the template that is used differs. We construct the template
214   as pseudo-code by transforming the template in standard prompting minimally, with the
215   major change being that the instruction is conveyed through the function name instead of
      natural language. This CoDoT template is constructed by following the best practices for

      We also explore the potential of CoDoT prompting in toxicity amplification. This is done
230   in a recursive setting, where the output of the current cycle is passed as the input to the next
231   cycle. Let Ii be the input at amplification cycle step i. The output of this cycle, represented
232   by Oi , can be defined as Oi = fφ (Ii ), where fφ = (M ◦ p) applies the prompting strategy
233   p and the LLM M to the input Ii . The output of the cycle at step i + 1, represented by
234   Oi+1 , is obtained as follows: Oi+1 = fφ (Oi ). The base case of this recursive process occurs
235   at step 0, where the input I0 consists of text snippets sampled directly from the dataset.

237   3       Experimentation Setup
238
239   Data: To obtain these text snippets, we use the RealToxicityPrompts dataset (Gehman
240   et al., 2020); a dataset of 100K sentence snippets extracted from the web. Since it’s com-
241   putationally prohibitive to perform experiments on the entire dataset, we perform all our
242   experiments on a subset. Similar to Gehman et al. (2020); Deshpande et al. (2023b), we
243   create this subset by splitting the dataset into ten distinct buckets, each representing an
244   equal interval of 0.1 based on the prompt toxicity scores: [0.0, 0.1), [0.1, 0.2),…, [0.8, 0.9)
245
      and [0.9, 1.0]. We randomly sample 200 prompts from each bucket to get a representative
      subset of the dataset. Thus, with this stratified random sampling, we obtain 2000 unique
246
      prompts evenly distributed across the various toxicity intervals.
247
248
      Models: We explore the following models: Mixtral 8x7B (Jiang et al., 2024), Wiz-
249   ardLM 2 (Xu et al., 2024), Llama 3 8B (Dubey et al., 2024), GPT-3.5 Turbo (OpenAI,
250   2023a) and GPT-4 Turbo (OpenAI, 2024).
251
252
      For Mixtral, Llama 3 and WizardLM 2 we use the inference provided by TogetherAI
      (Together, 2024) 2 with a temperature of 1, a repetition penalty of 1, topk as 50, and topp
253
      as 0.7. For the GPT class of models, we use nucleus decoding with a temperature of 1, topp
254
      as 1, and a frequency penalty of 14. Similar to Deshpande et al. (2023b), we observe that
255   the LLMs can often refuse to generate outputs for some prompts, where it anticipates the
256   output to be toxic, and instead generates a refusal message such as “ I’m sorry, but I cannot
257   fulfill that request.”. However, the same LLM with the same parameters can generate a toxic

405   4.2    Are Mixture of Experts Especially Vulnerable?
406
      Advancements in utilizing the Mixture of Experts (MoE) architecture (Jacobs et al., 1991;
      Jordan & Jacobs, 1993; Shazeer et al., 2017) have led to powerful foundational models such
407
408
      as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), Gemini 1.5 (Reid et al., 2024) and LLaMA 3 (Chiang
409   et al., 2024). Unlike traditional transformer architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017), MoE-based
410   models enable conditional computation by dividing the parameters into distinct “experts”.
411   When processing, input data is dynamically routed to specific experts, so each input sample
412   interacts with only a subset of the parameters. This differs from conventional deep learning
413   models, which use all the parameters for each input.
414
      We observe that the MoE class of models is particularly vulnerable to the different prompting
415   mechanisms. We study three MoE models (GPT-4 Turbo, Mixtral, WizardLM 2).
416   Compared to non-MoE models, MoE models exhibited substantially higher toxicity scores
417   (135.8% vs. 51.9%) and the prevalence of toxic conversations (168.17% vs. 102.98%).
418
      We hypothesize this increased vulnerability is due to the dynamic routing mechanisms.
      The exact instructions presented in natural language vs those formed in CoDoT activate
419
420
      different experts within the model, leading to the differences in the toxicity levels in the gen-
421   erations. The safety mechanisms typically activated in standard prompting setups may not
422   activate in the same manner for the experts used in CoDoT prompting setup. Thus, while
423   this selective combination of experts can be a powerful tool in improving model capabilities,
424   it can also lead to unique safety and generalization challenges.
425
426   4.3    How does the toxicity of the input affect the toxicity of output?
427
428   We examined the relationship between input and output toxicity by categorizing input data
429   into 0.1-interval toxicity buckets and computing average output toxicity scores across models
430   for the toxic induction task using the CoDoT methodology. Our analysis illustrated in
431   Figure 4 reveals a non-linear relationship between input and output toxicity. Models amplify
      toxicity significantly for low-toxicity inputs (scores < 0.2), with output scores around 0.7.

      As input toxicity increases, the amplification effect decreases, reaching a crossover point at
433   approximately 0.8 input toxicity.
434
435   Interestingly, models slightly reduce output toxicity for highly toxic inputs (scores > 0.8),

      possibly due to built-in safety mechanisms. However, output toxicity remains high, even for
      the most toxic inputs.

438   These findings indicate that current language models are more prone to amplifying toxicity
439   for benign or ambiguous content while potentially having some capability to mitigate ex-
440   tremely toxic inputs. This non-linear behavior underscores the complexity of safety systems
441   and the need for more sophisticated approaches to ensure safe and appropriate outputs

      across the full spectrum of input toxicity.

501   Ziegler et al., 2019; Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022a; Chaudhari et al., 2024). The

503   reward models (Bai et al., 2022b; Achiam et al., 2023; Faal et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024).

506   effectively reduces harmful outputs. Welbl et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022) show that im-

508   end-user can significantly improve the safety of LLM-generated content.
509   Jailbreaking in LLMs involves using sophisticated prompting techniques to circumvent the
510   ethical, legal, and safety constraints implemented during the model’s training process. The
511   goal of jailbreaking is to ultimately manipulate the LLM into generating content that would
512   typically be restricted or considered inappropriate. (Yu et al., 2024). Wallace et al. (2019)
513   show that adding “trigger words” can cause the model to generate harmful and biased re-
514   sponses. (Deshpande et al., 2023b) found that incorporating personas in the system prompt
515
      of LLMs like ChatGPT can significantly increase the toxicity of the generated content. Kang
      et al. (2023) explore a similar approach as ours, where they use string concatenation and
516
      prompting to evade the safety filters in models such as ChatGPT, InstructGPT, and GPT3.
517
      Our approach differs from this in its simplicity. Unlike Kang et al. (2023), we do not intro-
518   duce additional personas or instructions to the model, nor do we attempt to obfuscate the
519   input text. Instead, we use the exact text as in the instruction setting and only present it
520   in a pseudo-code format to jailbreak the model. Mehrotra et al. (2024) use a tree of attacks
521   with a pruning approach to iteratively refine prompts until one of the generated prompts
522   leads to a jailbreak.

525   6   Conclusion
526
527   Our work presents strong evidence that current safety and alignment efforts in Large Lan-
528   guage Models (LLMs) are insufficient and may even be introducing unforeseen vulnerabili-
529   ties. Our novel Code of Thought (CoDoT) framework reveals a significant increase in toxic
530   outputs across a wide range of LLMs, particularly in state-of-the-art mixture-of-experts
531   (MoE) models. These findings underscore the urgent need for re-evaluating current safety
532
      practices and a renewed focus on design choices based on first principles. As LLMs continue
      to deeply integrate into our societal fabric, ensuring their safe deployment is paramount.
      Future research should focus on developing robust safety mechanisms that address the vul-
533
534
      nerabilities exposed by CoDoT and guarantee the alignment of LLMs with human values.

       B     Limitations and Ethical Considerations
1747
1748   This study, while comprehensive, has several limitations to consider. While our study encompasses a range of
       open-source and proprietary LLMs with diverse architectures, it represents only a fraction of the models currently
1749   available. A broader survey including a more comprehensive range of models would provide a more complete
1750   picture of the vulnerabilities across the field.
1751   While critical to AI safety, our focus on toxicity represents only one dimension among many in the AI alignment and
1752   risk landscape. This work did not consider other important factors, such as bias and factual accuracy. Furthermore,
       we relied primarily on the Perspective API for toxicity scoring. Using multiple toxicity measurement tools and
1753   incorporating human evaluation could provide more robust and nuanced results.
1754   The experiments in this study focused on relatively short text snippets rather than extended conversations or
1755   entire documents. It’s important to note that the dynamics of toxicity may differ significantly in longer-form
       content. Additionally, while we showcase results in other languages, such as Hindi and Indonesian, most of our
1756   study centered on English content, toxicity patterns may vary considerably across different languages and cultures,
1757   potentially limiting the generalizability of our findings.
1758   Our investigation centers solely on the Code of Thought (CoDoT) prompting structure. While CoDoT has proven
1759   effective in bypassing safety measures, it is likely just one of many potential techniques for probing AI vulnerabil-
       ities. A more comprehensive understanding of AI vulnerabilities would require exploring a diverse array of input
1760   structures and interaction patterns.
1761   While we intend to highlight vulnerabilities to improve AI safety, the CoDoT prompting technique we devel-
1762   oped could potentially be misused to generate harmful content. We do not endorse any misuse or inappropriate
       applications of our findings.
