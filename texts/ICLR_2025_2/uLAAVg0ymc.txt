011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique (SMOTE) is a common rebalancing
014            strategy for handling imbalanced tabular data sets. However, few works analyze
015            SMOTE theoretically. In this paper, we prove that SMOTE (with default param-
016
               eter) tends to copies the original minority samples asymptotically. We also prove
               that SMOTE exhibits boundary artifacts, thus justifying existing SMOTE vari-
017
               ants. Then we introduce two new SMOTE-related strategies, and compare them
018
               with state-of-the-art rebalancing procedures. Surprisingly, for most data sets, we
019            observe that applying no rebalancing strategy is competitive in terms of predictive
020            performances, with tuned random forests, logistic regression or LightGBM. For
021            highly imbalanced data sets, our new methods, named CV-SMOTE and Multivari-
022            ate Gaussian SMOTE, are competitive. Besides, our analysis sheds some lights
023            on the behavior of common rebalancing strategies, when used in conjunction with
024            random forests.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Imbalanced data sets for binary classification are encountered in various fields such as fraud detec-
030   tion (Hassan & Abraham, 2016), medical diagnosis (Khalilia et al., 2011) and even churn detection
031   (Nguyen & Duong, 2021). In our study, we focus on imbalanced data in the context of binary
032   classification on tabular data, for which most machine learning algorithms have a tendency to pre-
033
      dict the majority class. This leads to biased predictions, so that several rebalancing strategies have
      been developed in order to handle this issue, as explained by Krawczyk (2016) and Ramyachitra &
034
      Manikandan (2014). These procedures can be divided into two categories: model-level and data-
035
      level.
036
037   Model-level approaches modify existing classifiers in order to prevent predicting only the majority
038   class. Among such techniques, Class-Weight (CW) works by assigning higher weights to minority
039
      samples. Another related proposed by Zhu et al. (2018) assigns data-driven weights to each tree of
      a random forest, in order to improve aggregated metrics such as F1 score or ROC AUC. Another
040
      model-level technique is to modify the loss function of the classifier. For instance, Cao et al. (2019)
041
      and Lin et al. (2017) introduced two new losses, respectively LDAM and Focal losses, in order to
042   produce neural network classifiers that better handle imbalanced data sets. However, model-level
043   approaches are not model agnostic, and thus cannot be applied to a wide variety of machine learning
044   algorithms. Consequently, we focus in this paper on data-level approaches.
045
      Data-level approaches can be divided into two groups: synthetic and non-synthetic procedures. Non-
046
      synthetic procedures works by removing or copying original data points. Mani & Zhang (2003)
047
      explain that Random Under Sampling (RUS) is one of the most used resampling strategy and design
048   new adaptive versions called Nearmiss. RUS produces the prespecified balance between classes by
049   dropping uniformly at random majority class samples. The Nearmiss1 strategy (Mani & Zhang,
050   2003) includes a distinction between majority samples by ranking them with their mean distance to
051   their nearest neighbor from the minority class. Then, low-ranked majority samples are dropped until
052   a given balancing ratio is reached. In contrast, Random Over Sampling (ROS) duplicates original
053   minority samples. The main limitation of all these sampling strategies is the fact that they either
      remove information from the data or do not add new information.

054
      On the contrary, synthetic procedures generate new synthetic samples in the minority class. One of
055   the most famous strategies in this group is Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique (SMOTE,
056   see Chawla et al., 2002)1 . In SMOTE, new minority samples are generated via linear interpolation
057   between an original minority sample and one of its nearest neighbor in the minority class. Other
058   approaches are based on Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN Islam & Zhang, 2020), which
059   are computationally expensive and mostly designed for specific data structures, such as images.
060   Random Over Sampling Examples (see Menardi & Torelli, 2014) is a variant of ROS that produces
061   duplicated samples and then add a noise in order to get these samples slightly different from the
062   original ones. This leads to the generation of new samples on the neighborhood of original minority
063
      samples. The main difficulty of these strategies is to synthesize relevant new samples, which must
      not be outliers nor simple copies of original points.
064
065   Contributions We place ourselves in the setting of imbalanced classification on tabular data, which
066   is very common in real-world applications (see Shwartz-Ziv & Armon, 2022). In this paper:
067
068            • We prove that, without tuning the hyperparameter K (usually set to 5), SMOTE asymp-
069
                 totically copies the original minority samples, therefore lacking the intrinsic variability
                 required in any synthetic generative procedure. We provide numerical illustrations of this
070
                 limitation (Section 3).
071
072            • We also establish that SMOTE density vanishes near the boundary of the support of the
073
                 minority distribution, therefore justifying the introduction of SMOTE variants such as Bor-
                 derLine SMOTE (Section 3).
074
075            • Our theoretical analysis naturally leads us to introduce two SMOTE alternatives, CV-
076              SMOTE and Multivariate Gaussian SMOTE (MGS). In Section 4, we evaluate our new
077              strategies and state-of-the-art rebalancing strategies on several real-world data sets using
078
                 random forests/logistic regression/LightGBM. Through these experiments 2 we show that
                 applying no strategy is competitive for most data sets. For the remaining data sets, our pro-
079
                 posed strategies, CV-SMOTE and MGS, are among the best strategies in terms of predictive
080
                 performances. Our analysis also provides some explanations about the good behavior of
081              RUS, due to an implicit regularization in presence of random forests classifiers.

085
086   In this section, we focus on the literature that is the most relevant to our work: long-tail learning,
087   SMOTE variants and theoretical studies of rebalancing strategies.
088   Long-tailed learning (see, e.g., Zhang et al., 2023) is a relatively new field, originally designed to
089   handle image classification with numerous output classes. Most techniques in long-tailed learning
090   are based on neural networks or use the large number of classes to build or adapt aggregated predic-
091   tors. However, in most tabular classification data sets, the number of classes to predict is relatively
092   small, usually equal to two (Chawla et al., 2004; He & Garcia, 2009; Grinsztajn et al., 2022). There-
093   fore, long-tailed learning methods are not intended for our setting as (i) we only have two output
094   classes and (ii) state-of-the-art models for tabular data are not neural networks but tree-based meth-
095
      ods, such as random forests or gradient boosting (see Grinsztajn et al., 2022; Shwartz-Ziv & Armon,
      2022).
096
097   SMOTE has seen many variants proposed in the literrature. Several of them focus on generating
098   synthetic samples near the boundary of the minority class support, such as ADASYN (He et al.,
099   2008), SVM-SMOTE (Nguyen et al., 2011) or Borderline SMOTE (Han et al., 2005). Many other
100   variants exist such as SMOTEBoost (Chawla et al., 2003), Adaptive-SMOTE (Pan et al., 2020),
101
      Xie et al. (2020) or DBSMOTE (Bunkhumpornpat et al., 2012). From a computational perspective,
      several synthetic methods are available in the open-source package imb-learn (see Lemaı̂tre et al.,
102
      2017). Several papers study experimentally some specificities of the sampling strategies and the
103
      impact of hyperparameter tuning. For example, Kamalov et al. (2022) study the optimal sampling
104   ratio for imbalanced data sets when using synthetic approaches. Aguiar et al. (2023) realize a survey

      4.1   R EBALANCING STRATEGIES
325
326   Class-weight (CW) [Model-level strategy] The class weighting strategy assigns the same weight
327   (choosen as hyperparameter) to each minority samples. The default setting for this strategy is to
328   choose a weight ρ such that ρn = N − n, where n and N − n are respectively the number of
329   minority and majority samples in the data set.
330
      Over/Under Sampling strategies [Non-synthetic data-level strategies] Random Under Sampling
331   (RUS) acts on the majority class by selecting uniformly without replacement several samples in
332   order to obtain a prespecified size for the majority class. Similarly, Random Over Sampling (ROS)

356   The limitations of SMOTE highlighted in Section 3 drive us to two new rebalancing strategies.
357   CV SMOTE [Synthetic data-level strategy] We introduce a new algorithm, called CV
358   SMOTE, that finds the best hyperparameter K among a prespecified grid via a 5-fold cross-
359   validation procedure. The grid is composed of the set √      {1, 2, . . . , 15} extended with the values
360   ⌊0.01ntrain ⌋, ⌊0.1ntrain ⌋, ⌊0.5ntrain ⌋, ⌊0.7ntrain ⌋ and ⌊ ntrain ⌋, where ntrain is the number of
361   minority samples in the training set. Recall that through Theorem 3.5, we show that SMOTE pro-
362   cedure with the default value K = 5 asymptotically copies the original samples. The idea of CV
363
      SMOTE is then to test several (larger) values of K in order to avoid duplicating samples, therefore
      improving predictive performances. CV SMOTE is one of the simplest ideas to solve some SMOTE
364
      limitations, which were highlighted theoretically in Section 3.
365
366   Multivariate Gaussian SMOTE(K) (MGS) [Synthetic data-level strategy] We introduce a new
367   oversampling strategy in which new samples are generated from the distribution N (µ̂, Σ̂), where
368   the empirical mean µ̂ and covariance matrix Σ̂ are estimated using the K neighbors and the central
369   point (see Algorithm 2 for details). By default, we choose K = d + 1, so that estimated covariance
370   matrices can be of full rank. MGS produces more diverse synthetic observations than SMOTE as
371   they are spread in all directions (in case of full-rank covariance matrix) around the central point.
372   Besides, sampling from a normal distribution may generate points outside the convex hull of the
373
      nearest neighbors.

      Re-weighting strategies RUS, ROS and CW are similar strategies in that they are equivalent to
450
      applying weights to the original samples. When random forests with default parameters are applied,
451
      we see that ROS and CW have the same predictive performances (see Table 6). This was expected, as
452   ROS assigns random weights to minority samples, whose expectation is that of the weights produced
453   by CW. More importantly, RUS has better performances than both ROS and CW. This advocates for
454   the use of RUS among these three rebalancing methods, as RUS produces smaller data sets, thus
455   resulting in faster learning phases. We describe another benefit of RUS in the next paragraph.
456
      Implicit regularization The good performances of RUS, compared to ROS and CW, may result
457
      from the implicit regularization of the maximum tree depth. Indeed, fewer samples are available
458   after the undersampling step, which makes the resulting trees shallower, as by default, each leaf
459   contains at least one observation. When the maximum tree depth is fixed, RUS, ROS and CW
460   strategies have the same predictive performances (see Table 8 or Table 9). Similarly, when the tree
461   depth is tuned, the predictive performances of RUS, ROS and CW are smoothed out (see Table 2).
462   This highlights the importance of implicit regularization on RUS good performances.
463
      SMOTE and CV-SMOTE Default SMOTE (K = 5) has a tendency to duplicate original obser-
464   vations, as shown by Theorem 3.5. This behavior is illustrated through our experiments when the
465   tree depth is fixed. In this context, SMOTE (K = 5) has the same behavior as ROS, a method that
466   copies original samples (see Table 8 or Table 9). When the tree depth is tuned, SMOTE may exhibit
467   better performances compared to reweighting methods (ROS, RUS, CW), probably due to a higher
468   tree depth. Indeed, even if synthetic data are close to the original samples, they are distinct and thus
469   allow for more splits in the tree structure. However, as expected, CV SMOTE performances are
470   higher than default SMOTE (K = 5) on most data sets (see Table 2).
471   MGS Our second new publicly available 3 strategy exhibits good predictive performances (best
472   performance in 4 out of 9 data sets in Table 2). This could be explained by the Gaussian sampling
473   of synthetic observations that allows generating data points outside the convex hull of the minority
474   class, therefore limiting the border phenomenon, established in Theorem 3.7. Note that with MGS,
475   there is no need of tuning the tree depth: predictive performances of default RF are on par with
476   tuned RF. Thus, MGS is a promising new strategy.

513   5   C ONCLUSION AND PERSPECTIVES
514
515   In this paper, we analyzed the impact of rebalancing strategies on predictive performance for binary
516   classification tasks on tabular data. First, we prove that default SMOTE tends to copy the original
517   minority samples asymptotically, and exhibits boundary artifacts, thus justifying several SMOTE
518
      variants. From a computational perspective, we show that applying no rebalancing is competitive for
      most datasets, when used in conjunction with a tuned random forest/Logistic regression/LightGBM,
519
      whether considering the ROC AUC or PR AUC as metric. For highly imbalanced data sets, rebal-
520
      ancing strategies lead to improved predictive performances, with or without tuning the maximum
521   tree depth. The SMOTE variants we propose, CV-SMOTE and MGS, appear promising, with good
522   predictive performances regardless of the hyperparameter tuning of random forests. Besides, our
523   analysis sheds some lights on the performances of reweighting strategies (ROS, RUS, CW) and an
524   implicit regularization phenomenon occurring when such rebalancing methods are used with random
525   forests.
526
      More analyses need to be carried out in order to understand the influence of MGS parameters (reg-
527   ularization of the covariance matrices, number of nearest neighbors...). We also plan to extend our
528   new MGS method to handle categorical features, and compare the different rebalancing strategies in
529   presence of continuous and categorical input variables.

750   General comment on the protocol For each data set, the ratio hyperparameters of each rebalanc-
751   ing strategy are chosen so that the minority and majority class have the same weights in the training
752
      phase.The main purpose is to apply the strategies exactly on the same data points (Xtrain ), then
      train the chosen classifier and evaluate the strategies on the same Xtest . This objective is achieved
753
      by selecting each time 4 fold for the training, apply each of the strategies to these 4 exact same fold.

       B     M AIN PROOFS
1459
1460   This section contains the main proof of our theoretical results. The technicals lemmas used by
1461   several proofs are available on Appendix C.
1462
1463   B.1   P ROOF OF L EMMA 3.1
1464
1465   Proof of Lemma 3.1. Let X be the support of PX . SMOTE generates new points by linear inter-
1466   polation of the original minority sample. This means that for all x, y in the minority samples or
1467   generated by SMOTE procedure, we have (1 − t)x + ty ∈ Conv(X ) by definition of Conv(X ).
1468   This leads to the fact that precisely, all the new SMOTE samples are contained in Conv(X ). This
1469
       implies Supp(PZ ) ⊆ Conv(X ).
1470
1471
1472   B.2   P ROOF OF T HEOREM 3.2
1473
1474   Proof of Theorem 3.2. For any event A, B, we have
1475                           1 − P[A ∩ B] = P[Ac ∪ B c ] ≤ P[Ac ] + P[B c ],                        (8)
1476   which leads to
1477                                   P[A ∩ B] ≥ 1 − P[Ac ] − P[B c ]                               (9)
1478                                             = P[A] − P[B c ].                                  (10)
1479   By construction,
1480
                                      ∥Xc − Z∥ ≤ ∥Xc − X(K) (Xc )∥.                                 (11)
1481
       Let x ∈ X and η > 0. Let α, ε > 0. We have,
1482
                             P[Xc ∈ B(x, α − ε)] − P[∥Xc − X(K) (Xc )∥ > ε]                         (12)
1483
1484                           ≤ P[Xc ∈ B(x, α − ε), ∥Xc − X(K) (Xc )∥ ≤ ε]                         (13)
1485                           ≤ P[Xc ∈ B(x, α − ε), ∥Xc − Z∥ ≤ ε]                                  (14)
1486                           ≤ P[Z ∈ B(x, α)].                                                    (15)
1487
