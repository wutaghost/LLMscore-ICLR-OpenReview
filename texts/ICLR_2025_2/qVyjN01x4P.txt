011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Time series forecasting, which aims to predict future values based on historical
014            data, has garnered significant attention due to its broad range of applications.
015            However, real-world time series often exhibit complex non-uniform distribution
016
               with varying patterns across segments, such as season, operating condition, or se-
               mantic meaning, making accurate forecasting challenging. Existing approaches,
017
               which typically train a single model to capture all these diverse patterns, often
018
               struggle with the pattern drifts between patches and may lead to poor gener-
019            alization. To address these challenges, we propose TFPS, a novel architecture
020            that leverages pattern-specific experts for more accurate and adaptable time series
021            forecasting. TFPS employs a dual-domain encoder to capture both time-domain
022            and frequency-domain features, enabling a more comprehensive understanding
023            of temporal dynamics. It then uses subspace clustering to dynamically identify
024            distinct patterns across data patches. Finally, pattern-specific experts model these
025            unique patterns, delivering tailored predictions for each patch. By explicitly learn-
026            ing and adapting to evolving patterns, TFPS achieves significantly improved fore-
027
               casting accuracy. Extensive experiments on real-world datasets demonstrate that
               TFPS outperforms state-of-the-art methods, particularly in long-term forecasting,
028
               through its dynamic and pattern-aware learning approach. The data and codes are
029
               available: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/TFPS-D001.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Time series forecasting plays a critical role in various domains, such as finance (Huang et al., 2024),
035
      weather (Bi et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023b; Lam et al., 2023), traffic (Long et al., 2024; Kong et al.,
036   2024), and others (Wang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a), by modeling the relationship between his-
037   torical data and future outcomes. However, the inherent complexity of time series data, including
038   temporal dependencies and non-stationarity, poses significant challenges in achieving reliable fore-
039   casting results.
040
      Recent works have shown the effectiveness of Transformer-based models for time series forecasting,
041
      due to their ability to capture long-range dependencies (Zhou et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
042   2024a). In particular, models like PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023) operate by splitting the continuous
043   time series into discrete patches and processing them with Transformer blocks. However, closely
044   examining these patches shows that they often exhibit distribution shifts, which can be attributed
045   to various factors such as concept drift (Lu et al., 2018). These shifts can manifest as sudden or
046   gradual changes in the underlying patterns and distributions of the time series data. For example,
047   patches corresponding to different regimes, seasons, or operating modes may have distinct statistical
048   properties. Despite the remarkable success of time series forecasting models (Nie et al., 2023; Zeng
049   et al., 2023; Eldele et al., 2024), they adopt the Uniform Distribution Modeling (UDM) strategy,
050
      which fails to account for the drifts and discrepancies between patches. This problem may result in
      poor generalization, hindering the performance of time series forecasting.
051
052   To quantify these distributional shifts, we split the ETTh1 dataset into patches and examined sud-
053   den drift and gradual drift in the time and frequency domains as shown in Figure 1. Specifically,
      we compute the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) between patches and display the results as

080   To address the challenges posed by distribution shifts in time series data, we propose a novel Time-
081   Frequency Pattern-Specific (TFPS) architecture to effectively model the complex temporal patterns.
082   In particular, TFPS consist of the following three key components. First, TFPS employs a Dual-
083   Domain Encoder (DDE) to capture temporal dependencies in the data. DDE extracts features from
084   both time and frequency domains to provide a comprehensive representation of the time series data,
085   enabling the model to capture both short-term and long-term dependencies. Second, TFPS addresses
086   the issue of concept drift by incorporating a Pattern Identifier (PI), that utilizes a subspace clustering
087
      approach to dynamically identify the distinct patterns present in different patches. Therefore, it can
      effectively handle nonlinear cluster boundaries and accurately assign patches to their corresponding
088
      clusters. Finally, TFPS constructs a Mixture of Pattern Experts (MoPE), a set of specialized expert
089
      models, each tailored to a specific pattern identified by the PI. By dynamically assigning patches to
090   the appropriate expert based on their identified patterns, MoPE enables the model to effectively han-
091   dle patch sequences across varying patterns and densities. This pattern-specific modeling approach
092   allows our TFPS model to capture the unique characteristics and dynamics of each pattern, leading
093   to improved forecasting accuracy.
094

112   Time Series Forecasting Models. In recent years, deep models with elaborately designed architec-
113
      tures have achieved great progress in time series forecasting (Liu & Wang, 2024; Qiu et al., 2024).
      Approaches like TimesNet (Wu et al., 2023a) and ModernTCN (Luo & Wang, 2024) utilize con-
114
      volutional neural networks with time-series-specific modifications, making them better suited for
115
      forecasting tasks. Additionally, simpler architectures such as Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP)-based
116   models (Zeng et al., 2023; Ekambaram et al., 2023) have demonstrated competitive performance.
117   However, Transformer-based models have gained particular prominence due to their ability to model
118   long-term dependencies in time series (Zhou et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
119   2024a). Notably, PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023) has become a widely adopted Transformer variant, in-
120   troducing a channel-independent patching mechanism to enhance temporal representations. This
121   approach has been further extended by subsequent models (Liu et al., 2024a; Eldele et al., 2024).
122   While previous work has primarily focused on capturing nonlinear dependencies in time series
123   through enhanced model structures, our approach addresses the distribution shifts caused by evolv-
124   ing patterns within the data, which is a key limitation of existing methods. We emphasize tackling
125   these shifts to improve forecasting performance in complex real-world scenarios.
126
      The Combination of Time and Frequency Domains. Time-domain models excel at capturing
127
      sequential trends, while frequency-domain models are essential for identifying periodic and os-
128   cillatory patterns. Recent research has increasingly emphasized integrating information from both
129   domains to better interpret underlying patterns. For instance, ATFN (Yang et al., 2020) demonstrates
130   the advantage of frequency domain methods for forecasting strongly periodic time series through a
131   time–frequency adaptive network. TFDNet (Luo et al., 2023) adopts a branching structure to capture
132   long-term latent patterns and temporal periodicity from both domains. Similarly, JTFT (Chen et al.,
133   2024b) utilizes the frequency domain representation to extract multi-scale dependencies while en-
134   hancing local relationships modeling through time domain representation. Yan et al. (2024) propose
135   TFMRN, which expands data in both domains to capture finer details that may not be evident in the
136
      original data. Recently, TSLANet (Eldele et al., 2024) leverages Fourier analysis to enhance feature
      representation and capture both long-term and short-term interactions.
137
138   Building on these approaches, our proposed method, TFPS, introduces a novel Dual-Domain En-
139   coder that effectively combines time and frequency domain information to capture both trend and
140   periodic patterns. By integrating time-frequency features, TFPS significantly advances the field in
141   addressing the complexities inherent in time series forecasting.
142   Non-stationary Time Series Forecasting. Concept drift describes unforeseeable changes in the un-
143   derlying distribution of data over time, posing a significant challenge for time series forecasting (Lu
144   et al., 2018; Fan et al., 2024). These non-stationarities complicate predictive modeling, necessitating
145   effective solutions to handle shifting distributions. To address varying distributions, normalization
146   techniques have emerged as a focal point in recent research, aiming to mitigate non-stationary ele-
147   ments and align data to a consistent distribution.
148   For instance, adaptive norm (Ogasawara et al., 2010) applies z-score normalization using global
149   statistics and DAIN (Passalis et al., 2019) introduces a neural layer for adaptively normalizing each
150   input instance. Kim et al. (2021) propose a reversible instance normalization (RevIN) to alleviate
151   series shift. Furthermore, Non-stationary transformer (Liu et al., 2022) points that directly station-
152   arizing time series will damage the model’s capability to capture specific temporal dependencies.
153   This work addresses the problem by introducing an innovative de-stationary attention mechanism
154
      within self-attention frameworks. Recent advancement include (Fan et al., 2023), which identifies
      both intra- and inter-space distribution shifts in time series data, and SAN (Liu et al., 2023b), which
155
      applies normalization at the slice level, thus opening new avenues for handling non-stationary time
156
      series data. Lastly, SIN (Han et al., 2024a) introduces a novel method to selecting the statistics and
157   learning normalization transformations to capture local invariance in time series data.
158
159   However, over-reliance on normalization can lead to over-stationarization, where important patterns
160
      or variations in the data are smoothed out (Liu et al., 2023b). Additionally, different patterns follow
      distinct internal dynamics, making a unified modeling approach inefficient. Our approach brings the
161
      intrinsic non-stationarity of the original series back to latent representation, enabling better handling
      of distribution shifts by tailoring the experts to the evolving patterns and densities within the data.

      3.1   P RELIMINARIES
186
187   Time series forecasting aims to uncover relationships between historical time series data and future
188   data. Let X denote the time series, and xt represent the value at timestep t. Given the historical
189   time series data X = [xt−L+1 , · · · , xt ] ∈ RL×C , where L is the length of the look-back window
190   and C > 1 is the number of features in each timestep, the objective is to predict the future series
191   Y = [xt+1 , · · · , xt+H ] ∈ RH×C , where H is the forecast horizon.
192
193   3.2   OVERALL A RCHITECTURE
194
195   Our model introduces three novel components: the Dual-Domain Encoder (DDE), the Pattern Iden-
196   tifier (PI), and the Mixture of Pattern Experts (MoPE), as illustrated in Figure 2. The DDE goes
197   beyond traditional time-domain encoding by incorporating a frequency encoder that applies Fourier
198   analysis, transforming time series data into the frequency domain. This enables the model to capture
199   periodic patterns and frequency-specific features, providing a more comprehensive understanding of
200   the data. The PI is a clustering-based module that distinguishes patches with distinct patterns, ef-
201
      fectively addressing the variability in the data. MoPE then utilizes multiple MLP-based experts,
      each dedicated to modeling a specific pattern, thereby enhancing the model’s ability to adapt to the
202
      temporal dynamics of time series. Collectively, these components form a cohesive framework that
203
      effectively handles concept drift between patches, leading to more accurate time series forecasting.

262   3.5   PATTERN I DENTIFIER
263
264   To address the complex and evolving patterns in time series data, we introduce a novel Pattern
265   Identifier (PI) module, an essential innovation within our framework. Unlike traditional approaches
266
      that treat the entire time series uniformly, our PI module dynamically classifies patches based on
      their distributional characteristics, enabling a more precise and adaptive modeling strategy.
267
268   The core of our approach lies in leveraging subspace clustering to detect concept shifts across mul-
269   tiple subspaces, as illustrated in Figure 3. This enables the PI module to distinguish patches by
      identifying their underlying patterns in both the time and frequency domains.

270
      The PI module’s uniqueness lies in its ability to directly analyze the properties of each patch, clus-
271   tering them into distinct groups. In the time domain, PI allows our framework, TFPS, to detect
272   shifts related to temporal characteristics, such as seasonality and trends, which can influence fore-
273   casting accuracy. In the frequency domain, the PI captures shifts associated with frequency-specific
274   features, like periodic behaviors and spectral changes, offering a comprehensive view of pattern
275   evolution across the entire time series.
276
      This dual-domain capability represents a significant advancement in our framework, making it
277   highly sensitive to pattern shifts occurring across both temporal and frequency dimensions. By
278   focusing on both domains, our model overcomes the limitations of existing methods that rely solely
279   on one representation, ensuring robustness in diverse scenarios.
280

      3.6   M IXTURE OF PATTERN E XPERTS
325
326   Traditional time series forecasting methods often rely on a uniform distribution modeling (UDM)
327   approach, which struggles to adapt to the complexities of diverse and evolving patterns in real-world
328   data. To address this limitation, we introduce the Mixture of Pattern Experts module (MoPE), which
329   assigns specialized experts to patches based on their unique underlying patterns, enabling more
330   precise and adaptive forecasting.
331   Given the cluster assignments s obtained from the PI module, we apply the Patch-wise MoPE to the
332   feature tensor z ∈ RC×N ×D . The MoPE module consists of the following key components:
333
      Gating Network: The gating network G calculates the gating weights for each expert based on the
334
      cluster assignment s and selects the top k experts. The gating weights are computed as:
335
336                                       G(s) = Softmax(TopK(s)).                                    (10)
337   Here, the top k logits are selected and normalized using the Softmax function to produce the gating
338   weights.
339
340
      Expert Networks: The MoPE contains K expert networks, denoted as E1 , . . . , EK . Each expert
      network is modeled as an MLP consisting of two linear layers and a ReLU activation. Given a
341
      patch-wise feature z, each expert network Ek processes the input to generate its respective output.
342
343   Output Aggregation: The final output h of the MoPE module is a weighted sum of the outputs
344   from all the selected experts, with the weights provided by the gating network:
345                                               K
                                                  X
346                                          h=         G(s)Ek (z).                                   (11)
347                                               k=1
348
349   After the frequency branch is processed by the inverse Fast Fourier transform, the time-frequency
350   outputs ht and hf , are concatenated to form h = concat(ht , hf ) ∈ RC×N ×2D .
351   Finally, a linear transformation is applied to the concatenated output h to generate the prediction:
352   Ŷ = Linear(h) ∈ RH×C .
353
354
      This approach ensures that the MoPE dynamically assigns and aggregates contributions from various
      experts based on the evolving patterns, improving the model’s adaptability and accuracy.

      4.6   A NALYSIS OF EXPERTS
514
515   Qualitative Visualizations of Pattern Identifier. Through training, pattern experts in MoPE spon-
516   taneously specialize, and we present two examples in Figure 5. We visualize the expert with the
517   highest score as the routed expert for each instance pair. In the provided examples, we observe that
518   expert-0 specialize in downward-related concepts, while expert-4 focuses on parabolic trend. These
519   examples also demonstrate the interpretability of MoPE.
520   Number of Experts. In Figure 6, we set the learning rate to 0.0001 and conducted four sets of
521   experiments on the ETTh1 and ETTh2 datasets, Kt = 1, Kf = {1, 2, 4, 8}, to explore the effect of
522   the number of frequency experts on the results. For example, Kt 1Kf 4 means that the TFPS contains
523   1 time experts and 4 frequency experts. We observed that Kt 1Kf 2 outperformed Kt 1Kf 4 in both
524   cases, suggesting that increasing the number of experts does not always lead to better performance.
525   In addition, we conducted three experiments based on the optimal number of frequency experts to
526   verify the impact of varying the number of time experts on the results. As shown in Figure 6, the
527   best results for ETTh1 were obtained with Kt 4Kf 2, while for ETTh2, the optimal results were
528   achieved with Kt 2Kf 2. Combined with the average MMD in Table 5 (Appendix A), we attribute
529   this to the fact that, in cases where concept drift is more severe, such as ETTh1 in the time domain,
530   more experts are needed, whereas fewer experts are sufficient when the drift is less severe.

532   5     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we propose a novel pattern-aware time series forecasting framework, TFPS, which
535   incorporates a dual-domain mixture of pattern experts approach. Our TFPS framework aims to ad-
536   dress the distribution shift across time series patches and effectively assigns pattern-specific experts
537   to model them. Experimental results across eight diverse datasets demonstrate that TFPS surpasses
538   state-of-the-art methods in both quantitative metrics and visualizations. Future work will focus on
539   investigating evolving distribution shifts, particularly those introduced by the emergence of new
      patterns, such as unforeseen epidemics or outbreaks.

      The time series X is segmented into N patches, where each patch Pn = {xn1 , xn2 , . . . , xnP }
      consists of P consecutive timesteps for n = 1, 2, · · · , N . For the frequency domain, we apply
839
      a Fourier transform F to each patch Pn , obtaining its frequency-domain representation as P̂n =
840
      F(Pn ).
841
842   Each patch’s probability distribution in the time domain is denoted as pt (Pn ), representing the
843   statistical properties of Pn , while its frequency domain distribution, denoted as pf (P̂n ), captures its
844   spectral characteristics.
845   The distribution shifts between two patches Pi and Pj are characterized by the comparing their
846   probability distributions in both time and frequency domains. These shifts are defined as:
847
848                                    Dt (Pi , Pj ) = |d(pt (Pi ), pt (Pj ))| > θ,                            (20)
849                                   Df (P̂i , P̂j ) = |d(pf (P̂i ), pf (P̂j ))| > θ,                         (21)
850
      where d is a distance metric, such as MMD values or Kullback-Leibler divergence, and θ is a thresh-
851
      old indicating a significant distribution shift. If Dt (Pi , Pj ) or Df (P̂i , P̂j ) exceeds θ, this implies a
852
      significant distribution shift between the two patches in either domain.

       Though TFPS demonstrates promising performance on the benchmark dataset, there are still some
1020   limitations of this method. First, the patch length is primarily chosen heuristically, and the cur-

1025   future work will focus on developing a more flexible and automatic patch length selection mecha-
