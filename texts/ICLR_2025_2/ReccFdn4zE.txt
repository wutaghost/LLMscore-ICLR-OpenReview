009                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011            It is desirable to have models of many physical phenomena, yet often data for these
012            phenomena are oddly structured. These structures, such as ungridded and arbitrary
013            length data prevents the use of many types of machine learning techniques, such
014            as feed-forward neural networks. It is thus quite desirable to be able to move this
015            data into a fixed size and shape for easier data ingest. We propose a method of
016            using cross attention to do this. An example of oddly shaped data is Total Electron
017            Content (TEC), or the vertical integral of electron density in the atmosphere. TEC
018            data is calculated using both the position of a satellite and a position on the surface
019
               of Earth, giving a non-fixed location per sample. This leads to a splattering of
               points on the globe where measurements exist that change in shape and amount
020
               each time step. We apply our technique to TEC in an autoregressive approach.
021
               This allows us to both obtain an embedding describing the global TEC and create
022            completed TEC maps, filling in where measurements are not taken. The global
023            embedding can then be further used in other models.
024

028   1.1   O DDLY S HAPED DATA
029
030   Oddly shaped data is data that does not present itself in a gridded or fixed-length form. Such data
031   can vary in length from sample to sample and also can vary in shape (i.e., distribution) from sample
032   to sample. An excellent example of this is Total Electron Content (TEC) data, which, because of the
033   nature of computing it, does not have a set shape or structure (see Figure 1 for a visual on how TEC
034   data changes shape at different timesteps). Additionally, during a single time step, TEC data is not
035
      ordered (i.e., to not introduce bias, the order data is processed should not affect the output).

047   Because of the nature of oddly shaped data, it cannot be used in all applications. For example, such
048   data cannot be ingested into feed-forward neural network models. This has led to the lack of using
049   historical TEC data to drive electron density models, despite the advantages that would come with
050
      doing so (i.e., giving the model the integral of its output makes computing the output easier).
051   Despite the lack of using TEC data in electron density models, efforts have been made to “complete”
052   TEC maps, or fill in the missing areas (Pan et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2023). Such a completed TEC
053   map could, in theory, be used as an input to feed-forward neural network models. However, the act
      of completing data can introduce errors (Schafer & Graham, 2002), as the false data is biased by

055   architecture or technique.

      1.2     I ONOSPHERE M ODELING
058
059   The ionosphere impacts both ground and space assets and is strongly coupled with space weather.
060   Of particular importance is the direct impact on wireless communications (Goodman & Aarons,
061   1990), especially satellite communications. This is because the ionosphere, which is the charged
062   layer of the atmosphere, acts like a plasma and causes electromagnetic waves that pass through it to
063   bend or even reflect. Such a strong impact on communications creates a desire to have an accurate
064   model driven by space weather observations. Such a model would be important to GPS positioning
065
      (Dubey et al., 2006; Hoque & Jakowski, 2006), as well as other systems. Additionally, solar storms
      have can have impacts on power grids and electronics (Eastwood et al., 2017; Kappenman, 2010), as
066
      well as human health (Eastwood et al., 2017; Kiznys et al., 2020; Babayev & Allahverdiyeva, 2005).
067
      A representative measure of the impact of the ionosphere is the electron density, which directly
068   impacts the aforementioned signal reflection. The electron density of the ionosphere is measurable,
069   but such measurements are very localized, leaving us with a sparse measurement at any one point in
070   time.
071
      Electron density is measured in four main ways:
072
073           • GPS Radio Occultation (GPSRO), which analyzes signal paths that go through the iono-
074             sphere between GPSRO satellites and GPS satellites.
075
              • Ionosondes, which send a variety of frequencies and measure return time.
076
077
              • Incoherent Scattering Radar (ISR) (Gordon, 1958), which look at the intensity of the scat-
                tering of a return radar beam.
078
079           • In-situ measurements, such as Langmuir probes, which directly measure electron density
080             in-place.
081
      Electron density can also be indirectly measured by measuring the TEC, which is the integral of the
082
      electron density along the path between the satellite and receiver used. It is usually measured in the
083   units of T ECU = 1 × 1016 electrons   .
                                      m2
084
085   Additionally, there are many correlated or otherwise related measurements we can take of the Earth-
086
      Sun system. Many models use indices, which represent magnetic activity, and other common mea-
      surements.
087
088
      1.2.1    E XISTING A PPROACHES
089
090   Modeling the ionosphere is done in one of two main ways, either with closed-form physics-based
091   modeling (Anderson et al., 1987; Huba & Krall, 2013; Fuller-Rowell et al., 1996; Scherliess et al.,
092   2009) or empirical-based modeling (Bilitza, 2018; Themens et al., 2017), with both having advan-
093   tages and drawbacks.
094   Physics-based models use known laws of physics, but need functions that are defined everywhere to
095   predict specific outputs (i.e., they can only use directly modeled observations).
096
      On the other hand, empirical models can be adjusted to incorporate additional factors, but are not
097
      necessarily restrained by physicals requirements.
098
099   Recently, Machine Learning (ML) approaches have become popular (Dutta & Cohen, 2022;
100   Sai Gowtam & Tulasi Ram, 2017; Gowtam et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021; Chu et al., 2017; Habarulema
101   et al., 2021). These models perform well compared to existing approaches, but they do not go much
102
      further than shallow feed-forward networks.

107
      Our TEC data comes from the Madrigal database (W. Rideout & K. Cariglia).

      Our data is split into training, validation, and test sets temporally. Training data is from 15-Oct-
109   1998 to 31-Dec-2020, validation data is from 1-Jan-2021 to 31-Dec-2021, and testing data is from
110   1-Jan-2022 to 21-May-2023.

112
      We aim to embed TEC data by using an autoencoder. This serves two purposes. First, it allows
      us to train a model to embed TEC data into a lower, fixed dimension without requiring additional
113
      data, and second, it has the side-effect of performing TEC completion, which, while not necessarily
114
      desirable for a model input, is useful as a science tool. We will later discuss the TEC completion,
115   but it is not the primary goal of this TEC embedding.
116
117   We start our approach to embedding TEC data by following the structure for a transformer outlined
118
      by Vaswani et al. (2017). Our model is composed of two parts, the encoder and the decoder or
      reconstructor. For both we use the same architecture but change which inputs are the keys, values,
119
      and queries. For the encoder, we set the keys and values to be the input TEC observation sequences,
120
      which have been preprocessed to have their longitude represented by a sin/cos pair and latitude
121   scaled to be between -1 and 1. We also normalize TEC. Thus, the observation sequences are scaled
122   latitude, sine of scaled longitude, cosine of scaled longitude, and normalized TEC. This gives the
123   inputs a shape of (batch size, sequence length, 4). Importantly, we omit the time (year, day of year,
124   time of day) to force the model to learn the TEC values themselves instead of relying on using time.
125   The query is a parameter of the encoder that acts like a learnable sequence. The multihead attention
126   used is followed by a linear layer that changes from the internal embedding to a target size.
127   For the reconstructor, we treat the observation sequences as the queries and the embedding as the
128   keys and values. There is a linear layer that goes from the embedded dimension to the attention
129   embedding dimension. For both the embedder and reconstructor we use an internal attention em-
130   bedding size that is the same.
131
      We also look at chaining these embedding units together like transformer blocks, where the output
132
      sequence is used as the input query sequence instead at each step. We notice a lack of improvement
133   by doing this, likely due to the experimental setup, so we do not present such models, but we plan
134   to investigate the possibility of the multi-step embedding in future work.

      2.2   I ONOSPHERE M ODELING
163
164   We have previously developed ML models for predicting electron density in the ionosphere. We use
165   a simpler version of our model here to compare the benefits of using embedded TEC data to models
166   without such information. These models aim to demonstrate that there is meaningful data captured
167   in the TEC embeddings, and future work aims to improve upon them.
168
      To use the embedded TEC data, we train the TEC model to embed a single timestep’s set of TEC
169   information into a vector of size 16 (we used the smaller sized embedding to save on memory and
170   data storage). For each datapoint used in DINN, the most recent embedded TEC data is concatenated
171   with the standard input vector. Any data where the most recent TEC information is greater than 5
172   minutes (TEC data is every 5 minutes) is rejected.
173
      Our electron density models are trained on GPSRO data from the CHAMP (UCAR COSMIC Pro-
174
      gram, 2020a) and GRACE (UCAR COSMIC Program, 2020b) satellites and the COSMIC 1 (UCAR
175   COSMIC Program, 2022) and COSMIC 2 (UCAR COSMIC Program, 2019) satellite constellations.
176   Overall, this data spans approximately 100-600 km altitudes and 20+ years.
177
178
      We pre-process to reject profiles with peak electron density outside of the 200-450 km altitude range
      with negative electron densities above 100 km (sub-100km negative densities are instead ignored),
179
      an approached introduced by Habarulema et al. (2021) to reject poor-quality samples.
180
181   Our data is split into training, validation, and test sets in a similar manner as the TEC data. Training
182   data is from 11-Jun-2001 to 31-Dec-2020, validation data is from from 1-Jan-2021 to 31-Dec-2021,
183   and testing data is 1-Jan-2022 to 5-Oct-2022.
184   For supplemental input data, we use Dst, Kp, ap, and f10.7 from NASA’s OMNI hourly data (Papi-
185   tashvili & King, 2020). Kp, ap, and Dst help describe the state of Earth’s magnetic field, while f10.7
186   is the solar flux at a wavelength of 10.7cm.
187
      For the ionosphere models, all models consist of input adjustments (e.g., sin and cos of 2πt
                                                                                                 24 , where
188   t is the UT hour), similar to the autoencoder of the TEC model. These adjusted inputs are used as
189   the base input tensor. This base input tensor is used in a simple feed-forward neural network we call
190   Deeper Ionosphere Neural Network (DINN), as it has more layers (i.e., it is deeper) than previous
191   feed-forward ionosphere neural networks. This architecture is visualized in Figure 3.

206   Standard model inputs are latitude, longitude, altitude, year, day of year, hour, Kp index, ap index,
207   Dst index, and solar index f10.7. The inputs are adjusted before being used in the feed forward
208   layers as follows: the latitude is scaled between -1 and 1, the longitude is turned into a sine/cosine
209   pair of values. Altitude is scaled to be in megameters. The year is simply used to determine if there
210   is a leap year so that the day of year can be turned into a sine/cosine pair. The hour of the day is also
211   converted into a sine/cosine pair. The Kp index is divided by 10, the Dst and ap indices are divided
212
      by 20 and the f10.7 index is divided by 150.
213   The DINN architecture has an input layer size of 12 (28 when used with embedded TEC, as we have
214   trained it with an embedding size of 16 for the TEC data), with hidden layers of sizes 64, 128, 128,
215   256, 128, 128, 64, and an output layer of size 1. The feedforward layers use Sigmoid activation
      functions. The model outputs the log of electron density.

      For our models we use Mean Squared Error (MSE) on the log of electron density as our loss func-
217   tion. We train for a total of 100 epochs using a time-grouped batching approach and select the
218   model where validation loss stops decreasing as the trained model. We use a time-grouped batching
219   approach. We use a batch size of 16384 data points.
220
      We also have more complex models, which we use for comparison. We have RDINN, which uses
221
      historical Kp, ap, Dst, and f10.7 values as inputs to RNNs whose subsequent inputs are concatenated
222
      like the TEC data is concatenated in DINN. We also have RDINN XRS, which does the same but
223   also with X-ray flux data. For the latter, GOES X-ray data is used as input data (NOAA), using
224   all available satellites at the time of measurement and averaging satellites together when multiple
225   were available. The historical index values are unscaled. We use hourly data of the past 3 days
226   of observations for all four indices. We also include the past 81 days of daily-averaged f10.7 data.
227   X-ray flux data is used for the past 90 minutes every minute, using both short and long wavelengths.
228
      Each RNN section has a hidden size of 64 with 3 layers followed by a feedforward layer with an
229   input size equal to 64 times the number of types of histories and output size of 16.
230
231   RDINN and RDINN XRS are trained the same way as DINN, although because GOES XRS data
232
      is not always present, RDINN XRS has seen fewer samples (1.28 billion vs 1.36 billion in each
      epoch).

      4   C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
420
421   In this paper we proposed a technique to take arbitrarily shaped data and embed it to fixed-length
422
      vectors. In particular, we used TEC data, although there are other types of appropriate data that it
      could be used on, such as weather data. We demonstrated that our technique is able to encode essen-
423
      tial information about the sequences, as reconstruction is improved against the lack of an embedding
424
      step. Additionally, using larger embeddings increases performance.
425
426   Our embedding technique also has the extra benefit of being able to provide completed TEC maps.
427   We aim to further pursue this in future work, but highlight within this paper that the ability to produce
428
      reasonable looking TEC maps hints towards the technique’s ability at encoding information.
429   After demonstrating that information can be successfully encoded using our transformer-like tech-
430   nique, we then demonstrated an example use of embedded arbitrary sequences. By including TEC
431   information in electron density prediction models we see an increase in model performance, particu-
      larly in regards to the electron density itself, while we note drawbacks in increasing electron density
