009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in
012            performing tasks across various domains without needing explicit retraining. This
013            capability, known as In-Context Learning (ICL), while impressive, exposes LLMs
014            to a variety of adversarial prompts and jailbreaks that manipulate safety-trained
015            LLMs into generating undesired or harmful output. In this paper, we propose a
016
               novel interpretation of ICL in LLMs through the lens of cognitive neuroscience,
               by drawing parallels between learning in human cognition with ICL. We applied
017
               the principles of Cognitive Load Theory in LLMs and empirically validate that
018
               similar to human cognition, LLMs also suffer from cognitive overload—a state
019            where the demand on cognitive processing exceeds the available capacity of the
020            model, leading to potential errors. Furthermore, we demonstrated how an attacker
021            can exploit ICL to jailbreak LLMs through deliberately designed prompts that in-
022            duce cognitive overload on LLMs, thereby compromising the safety mechanisms
023            of LLMs. We empirically validate this threat model by crafting various cognitive
024            overload prompts and show that advanced models such as GPT-4, Claude-3.5 Son-
025            net, Claude-3 OPUS, Llama-3-70B-Instruct, Gemini-1.0-Pro, and Gemini-1.5-Pro
026            can be successfully jailbroken, with attack success rates of up to 99.99%. Our
027
               findings highlight critical vulnerabilities in LLMs and underscore the urgency of
               developing robust safeguards. We propose integrating insights from cognitive load
028
               theory into the design and evaluation of LLMs to better anticipate and mitigate the
029
               risks of adversarial attacks. By expanding our experiments to encompass a broader
030            range of models and by highlighting vulnerabilities in LLMs’ ICL, we aim to en-
031            sure the development of safer and more reliable AI systems. CAUTION: The
032            text in this paper contains offensive and harmful language.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   In-Context Learning (ICL) enables LLMs to learn and adapt to new tasks during inference without
038   updating their internal parameters (Brown et al., 2020). Recognized as an emergent capability (Wei
039
      et al., 2022a), ICL has been theoretically analyzed as implicit Bayesian inference, demonstrating
      abilities such as learning various functions, acting as complex classifiers, and implementing near-
040
      optimal algorithms for diverse problems (Xie et al., 2021; Garg et al., 2022; Hollmann et al., 2022;
041
      Li et al., 2023b). While research has explored connections between human cognition (HC) and
042   artificial neural networks (Nayebi et al., 2024; Schaeffer et al., 2024; Saxena et al., 2022), no prior
043   work, to our knowledge, has drawn direct parallels between learning in ICL and human cognitive
044   learning. Despite ICL advantages in task adaptation and few-shot learning, ICL can be exploited to
045   generate unsafe and harmful responses (Qiang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024;
046   Shen et al., 2023; Rao et al., 2023). Vulnerabilities such as prompt injection, data poisoning, privacy
047   leaks, adversarial examples, and jailbreaking pose significant risks to LLM system security and user
048   safety (Liu et al., 2023b; He et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2021; Abdali et al., 2024b; Chao et al., 2023;
049   Wei et al., 2024; Abdali et al., 2024a). As models’ capabilities and context windows expand, so
050
      does the risk of adversarial attacks exploiting ICL, underscoring the need to understand how ICL
      functions and how it can be manipulated.
051
052   In this paper, we explore learning in ICL through the lens of Cognitive Load Theory (CLT) from
053   neuroscience and demonstrate how exceeding cognitive load (CL) can be used to jailbreak LLMs.
      We hypothesize that, akin to limited working memory in HC (Sweller, 1988; Cowan, 2014), LLMs

054
      possess comparable constraints that can lead to cognitive overload when CL increases beyond ca-
055   pacity. By designing experiments with carefully crafted prompts to measure the impact of CL on
056   LLM performance, we found that increasing CL degrades task performance, supporting the similar-
057   ity between learning in HC and ICL. Building on this insight, we developed an attack that exploits
058   cognitive overload in ICL to bypass safety mechanisms in LLMs. By incrementally increasing CL
059   in prompts, we achieved high attack success rates across several state-of-the-art (SOTA) models, in-
060   cluding GPT-4 and Claude-3-Opus. Our study reveals an inherent vulnerability in ICL, highlighting
061   the need for robust defenses against such exploits.
062   Our findings contribute to a deeper understanding of ICL and its parallels with HC, emphasizing the
063   importance of addressing cognitive overload vulnerabilities in LLMs to ensure system security and
064   user safety. We summarize our overall contribution as follows:
065
066         1. We present a study, inspired by high-level parallels between in-context learning (ICL) in
067            LLMs and human cognition, to demonstrate the utility of CLT in capturing performance
068            and safety alignment issues in LLMs
069         2. We empirically demonstrate that increased CL leads to cognitive overload in LLMs, de-
070            grading performance on secondary tasks similarly to HC .
071         3. We introduce Cognitive Overload attacks—a novel attack method exploiting cognitive
072            overload to bypass LLM safety mechanisms—and validate its effectiveness with attack suc-
073            cess rates up to 99.99% and 97% on the Forbidden Question and JailbreakBench Datasets,
074            respectively.
075         4. We show that higher-capability models can craft cognitive overload prompts to attack other
076            LLMs, demonstrating the transferability and widespread impact of cognitive overload at-
077            tacks.
078

086   2   C OMPARING H UMAN C OGNITIVE L EARNING AND ICL
087
088   Learning in Human Cognition (HC) vs ICL: In HC, learning involves acquiring, processing, and
089   retaining information, knowledge, or skills (Clark & Harrelson, 2002). Human working memory has
090   a limited capacity for holding abstract information representing objects or thoughts (Baddeley et al.,
091   1975; Cowan, 2014). Information, whether visual, auditory, or combined, is initially stored in work-
092   ing memory before being transferred to long-term memory (Cotton & Ricker, 2022; Miller, 1956;
093
      Cowan, 2008). Similarly, LLMs acquire information from input tokens or prompts. The limited
      context window and LLMs inner mechanism restricts the amount of information they can process
094
      at once, analogous to human working memory capacity. Although multimodal LLMs can process
095
      various data types, we focus solely on text-based LLMs. Furthermore, LLMs process text by trans-
096   forming input tokens into distributed representations in the latent space, where semantic patterns
097   and relationships are mathematically captured. A similar analogy could be made with human cog-
098   nition, where concepts are not stored as raw sensory inputs but are instead abstracted into patterns
099   of biological neural activations (Taylor et al., 2015; Nelli et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2006).
100
      In HC, a partially formed idea may be stored in long-term memory, leading to false beliefs that are
101
      later rectified when inconsistencies arise and are addressed using working memory (Cowan, 2014).
102   Similarly, language models learn by minimizing errors over many examples during pretraining or
103   fine-tuning. Studies show similarities between human and neural network learning processes, in-
104   cluding evidence that biological and artificial neural networks learn similar representations (Nayebi
105   et al., 2024; Schaeffer et al., 2024; Saxena et al., 2022; Goh et al., 2021; Güçlü & Van Gerven, 2015;
106   Yamins et al., 2014). Based on the aforementioned literature, we can imply that HC and LLMs
107   share similarities in learning processes, including error correction and knowledge construction
      from multiple examples.

108
      In HC, prior knowledge is essential for linking new information to existing schemas, updating the
109   mental model (Gerjets et al., 2004). Prior knowledge reduces intrinsic cognitive load, facilitating
110   learning (Moreno & Park, 2010). Similarly, ICL requires prior knowledge to solve tasks accu-
111   rately; larger models with more extensive pretraining perform better in few-shot demonstrations
112   than smaller models (Brown et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2022a; Ostendorff & Rehm, 2023). ICL results
113   from pretraining large-scale models on vast datasets, enabling generalization to new tasks based on
114   input label distributions and formatting. Similarly, the pretraining and fine-tuning of language
115   models parallel the role of prior knowledge in HC .
116   The definition of learning from HC does not fully apply to ICL. In HC, learning involves updating
117
      mental models and storing new information in long-term memory, whereas ICL models do not up-
      date their weights during task execution. According to Min et al. (2022), while language models
118
      may not learn new tasks in the traditional sense, they adapt to input patterns to improve prediction
119
      accuracy. We adopt this definition, stating that a model learns in ICL if it accurately executes
120   tasks conditioned on the input prompt.
121
122   Cognitive Load Theory (CLT) in ICL: In HC, CL refers to the amount of working memory (WM)
123
      resources being used during a mental task or learning process (Sweller, 1988). CLT builds on the
      concept of WM as a bottleneck for learning and prescribes guidelines for instructional design that
124
      allow learners to manage working memory load to learn successfully (Sweller, 1988; Klepsch et al.,
125
      2017). CLT differentiates the sources of memory load into three types: Intrinsic Cognitive Load
126   (INT CL), Extraneous Cognitive Load (EXT CL), and Germane Cognitive Load (GER CL) (more
127   details in App. C.1).
128
129
      INT CL in ICL: Similar to human cognition, we argue that INT CL in ICL stems from the inherent
      complexity of the task, depending on the interactivity of elements and the LLM’s prior knowledge
130
      during pretraining or fine-tuning. Elements—basic units like information pieces, concepts, or pro-
131
      cedures—processed in working memory contribute to cognitive load, especially when complexity
132   arises from patterns or relationships among them. Assessing INT CL in LLMs is challenging be-
133   cause we cannot determine task complexity from the model’s perspective due to unknown prior
134   knowledge. Because LLMs are trained on extensive data, general knowledge tasks may not induce
135   significant INT CL. However, more challenging tasks requiring models to generalize beyond their
136   training can induce INT CL. Increasing task complexity can raise INT CL, but it’s difficult to know
137   if one task is more complex for an LLM due to unknown prior knowledge. Therefore, we pro-
138   pose modifying the task to increase complexity based on its performance at a deterministic setting
139   (temperature=0) to induce more INT CL.
140   Several methods have been suggested to reduce INT CL in HC. To reduce it in HC, the Segmenting
141   principle (Mayer & Moreno, 2010) presents information step-by-step, which is similar to the chain-
142   of-thought process (Wei et al., 2022b) in LLMs. Similarly, the Pretraining principle (Mayer, 2005)
143   involves providing detailed information about the task in the prompt. Few-shot demonstrations,
144   including multiple examples in the prompt, exemplify this approach to aid ICL.
145   EXT CL in ICL: It refers to any irrelevant information presented in the prompt or generated in the
146   response. For instance, if the model is instructed to translate from English to French, adding Python
147   code in the prompt introduces irrelevant information, increasing EXT CL. Any unnecessary infor-
148   mation not required for the task can increase EXT CL. Similarly, requesting the model to generate
149   irrelevant responses to the observation task, such as first writing the multiplication table of 1337
150   before performing a translation, increases EXT CL.
151   Poorly designed prompts with ambiguous tasks create unnecessary complexity for the model, akin
152
      to poor instructional design for humans. Inconsistent formatting across examples adds processing
      overhead. Balancing the number of examples is crucial; too few may be insufficient, while too many
153
      can overwhelm the model’s context window. For example, in many-shot jailbreaking (Anil et al.,
154
      2024), the model was overwhelmed by numerous adversarial examples.
155
156   GER CL in ICL: It relates to the model’s engagement and effort in learning the task, dependent on
157   working memory resources to handle element interactivity in INT CL. Models can allocate resources
158
      to GER CL only if EXT CL doesn’t exceed their working memory capacity, similar to human cogni-
      tion (Klepsch et al., 2017). To increase GER CL, EXT CL should be reduced. The Self-explanation
159
      effect can be applied by having models reiterate the observation task, breaking it into smaller parts,
160
      and explaining it while solving. This approach resembles the SELF-EXPLAIN process (Zhao et al.,
161   2023)

197   3   C OGNITIVE L OAD M EASUREMENT
198
199   We argue that in ICL, learning inherently involves cognitive load. For given tasks to LLM, we cannot
200   quantify the exact cognitive load because we lack information on the LLMs’ prior knowledge or the
201   task’s difficulty for them. Instead of quantifying cognitive load for a specific task, we assume it
202   is at a certain level and can be increased or decreased from that baseline. According to CLT
203   theory, successful learning requires reducing intrinsic and extraneous load. We hypothesize (H0)
204   that as intrinsic and extraneous cognitive load increase, the bandwidth of working memory
205   will be exceeded, resulting in cognitive overload in LLMs and a decrease in performance.. To
206
      induce cognitive overload in LLMs, we need to design prompts that will increase cognitive load.
207   Prompt Design with CL: Our goal was to design prompts that increase cognitive load in LLMs by
208   intentionally going against established methods for reducing it. Research shows that task switching
209   engages working memory in human cognition (Wang et al., 2022) and contributes to cognitive over-
210   load by sustaining high mental loads and increasing error rates (Ren et al., 2023). Similarly, task
211   switching degrades performance in LLMs (Gupta et al., 2024), including when switching between
212
      languages (Xu et al., 2023; Upadhayay & Behzadan, 2024).
213   In HC, self-reporting and dual-task measures assess cognitive load (more details in App. C.1.1). In
214   dual-task measures, participants perform two tasks simultaneously; performance on the second task
215   declines as the first task becomes more demanding (Brünken et al., 2004). Drawing inspiration from
      this, we developed multi-task measurements, including an observation task preceded by a cognitive


                                                                                                                          4

216
      load tasks (primary tasks). We evaluated cognitive load based on performance on the observation
217   task. Based on this, we created prompts with multiple tasks where LLMs needed to switch between
218   different tasks.
219
220
      Crafting tasks: To increase CL in high-capability LLMs, we categorized potential tasks into general
      tasks, custom tasks, and unconventional tasks.
221
222   General tasks are questions or instructions the model learned during pretraining or fine-tuning, such
223   as writing an essay on a known topic or answering domain-specific questions.
224   Custom tasks require models to integrate learned knowledge with new user-provided information.
225   For instance, a model might be asked to add a feature to existing user code, necessitating reference
226   to and constraints from that code, which increases the INT CL. Another example is when a user
227   first asks the model to generate code and then requests a revision that omits certain packages used
228   initially. At temperature equal to 0 (when the model is more deterministic), the model tends to
229   prioritize its pretraining knowledge (Renze & Guven, 2024; Hinton et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2020;
230   2023); thus, asking it to avoid certain packages forces it to apply its coding knowledge within spe-
231   cific limitations.
232
      Furthermore, ICL itself can be considered a custom query, as the model uses its prior knowledge
      and new user-provided context to generate outputs based on the input format.
233
234   Unconventional tasks refer to tasks that are rare and precisely custom-based on user requests, which
235   LLMs might not have learned during fine-tuning or pretraining. For example, asking the model to
236   write a poem where every last word rhymes with ”xx” only. Another example could be asking the
237   model to write an answer by swapping each vowel with look-alike numbers (A-4, E-3, I-1, O-0, and
238
      U-7). Here, it will likely increase the INT CL. Another example could be providing the model with
      questions where each letter is wrapped with custom tags, such as ’Write a poem on Bee’ would be
239
      ”[s]W[/s] [s]r[/s] [s]i[/s] [s]t[/s] [s]e[/s] ... [s]B[/s] [s]e[/s] [s]e[/s]”. Adding unnecessary tags like
240
      ([s], [/s]) increases both INT CL and EXT CL. Such unconventional tasks generally increase both
241   INT CL and EXT CL.
242
243   Crafting Observation Tasks: In order to increase both the INT CL and EXT CL, we design the
244
      observation task with the idea of unconventional tasks. The main idea is to hide the observation
      task within the given context such that the model has to infer the observation task from the context.
245
      We will use obfuscation tags to hide the observation task (App. Figure 2 depicts an example of
246
      observation task).
247
248   Crafting Primary Tasks: LLMs are based on transformers, which are auto-regressive models
249   where each word is generated based on the previously generated words. Hence, we develop an
250
      intuition that if the observation task is presented after the primary tasks, it should induce more CL
      than asking the observation task before primary tasks.
251
252   The objective here is to design each task to increase the CL, either intrinsic or extraneous. For these
253   tasks as well, we are going to use custom and unconventional tasks. As pointed out earlier, it is
254   difficult to understand the level of INT CL for a particular query, but comparatively easier
255   to understand the level of EXT CL, as it can be increased based on unnecessary information.
256
      Furthermore, the underlying mechanisms of LLMs are token-dependent, through which they build
      semantic meaning. We hypothesize that when these tokens are further altered or fragmented, they
257
      act as irrelevant information, which should significantly increase both EXT CL and INT CL. By
258
      focusing on the above measures, we are going to define different tasks and the CL associated with
259   them.
260
261   Remove Instruction (T1): The model is asked to rewrite the observation task in original order,
262
      separating each letter with \\n. This increases EXT CL by introducing irrelevant tokens and splitting
      information but may reduce INT CL compared to the reverse instruction.
263
264   Reverse Instruction (T2) : The model is asked to rewrite the decoded observation task in reverse
265   order, separating each letter with \\n, increasing the EXT CL through irrelevant tokens, and also
266   increasing INT CL by introducing an additional inference task.
267   user instruction (T3): The model is asked to rewrite the observation task exactly as it is, using
268   obfuscation tags as specified in the user’s prompt. This increases EXT CL.
269

      3.1     E XPERIMENTS TO MEASURE COGNITIVE LOAD
285
      We conducted preliminary experiments to measure the effect of each CL task using dual-task mea-
286
      sures and the LLMs’ self-reporting method. For each question tested on the model, we created six
287
      prompts with a combination of CL tasks (T1-T6) followed by the observation task (T7). We then
288   measured the score for each question under each CL task. We observed that each CL task decreased
289   the performance of the observation task, validating that these tasks indeed increase CL. Thus, pro-
290   viding support to our hypothesis H0.
291
      Similarly, we used the LLMs’ self-reporting approach, where we created a prompt detailing what
292
      constitutes CL and provided six different CL prompts, and asked two judge LLMs, namely C3.5-
293
      Sonnet and GPT-4-Turbo, to provide a score for each of the CL tasks. We observed that both judge
294   LLMs agreed closely in the INT CL; however, the scores differed in the EXT CL. Since EXT CL
295   depends on the number of irrelevant tokens generated, and LLMs do not have access to their inner
296   mechanisms, they cannot predict how their performance is going to be (more details in App. C.3).

299   3.2     C OGNITIVE OVERLOAD IN LLM S
300
301
      From the preliminary experiment, we observed that the performance on the observation task dete-
      riorated; however, our experiment was limited to a single CL task. We hypothesize (H1) that as
302
      the load increases to a state of cognitive overload, the LLMs’ performance on observation task will
303
      significantly deteriorate, or the LLMs may become so disoriented that they are unable to generate
304   the correct answer at all. To test this hypothesis, we stacked the CL tasks in a progressive order
305   along with the observation task. The CL task CL1 will consist of (T1, T7); CL2 will consist of (T1,
306   T2, T7); similarly, CL6 will consist of (T1, T2, T3, T4, T5, T6, T7).

      3.2.1    V ISUALIZING THE COGNITIVE OVERLOAD
310
      In order to visualize the state of the cognitive overload, we asked the models to write code to that
311
      when run will draw an animal. As the CL progress, we can visualize the quality of the code by
312   running it. We created six different prompts by using the combination of CL tasks. The observation
313   task in this experiment is a combination of a custom task and an unconventional task, where the
314   model is asked to write a program that, when run, will draw an image of an owl, and it must use
315   Python and the Turtle package. Our intuition here is that the model might have learned the code to
316   draw an image of an animal with Python, and we are customizing the task with the restriction of
317   the image being of a bird (an owl) and the use of the Python Turtle package for the code. This task
318   should increase the INT CL from the task of drawing animal.
319   For the experiment, we increased the cognitive load, going from CL0 (no CL) to CL5 with the
320   maximum CL. In CL0, the model is instructed to draw an image of an owl using the Python Turtle
321   package. The task consists only of the INT CL in CL0; hence, the INT CL of a certain level is already
322   present in the prompt. After CL0, we created INTcl, in which the model needs to infer the questions
323   from the input and write the code in the answer. There is no irrelevant token generation in the model
      response; hence, it is likely to increase the INT CL (Figure 7 shows an example prompt CL5).

342   3.2.2   C OGNITIVE LOAD MEASUREMENT WITH V ICUNA MT B ENCHMARK
343
344
      To further investigate cognitive overload, we conducted multi-task measurements using SOTA
      LLMs. We curated 100 questions from the Vicuna MT Benchmark (Zheng et al., 2024), obfus-
345
      cated each question with specific tags, and combined them with six different CL tasks. For each
346
      model response, we extracted only the answer, omitting CL components, and performed pairwise
347   comparisons between the answer at CL0 and that at each CL combination. To minimize evaluation
348   bias, we used three judge LLMs: L3-70B-Ins, Gem1.5Pro, and GPT-4. We averaged the scores for
349   each CL combination, as shown in Figure 1B.
350
      The scores for each CL combination varied across models, supporting our interpretation that differ-
351
      ent models experience distinct CL due to their unique mechanisms. We performed a paired t-test by
352
      comparing scores CLi (”before”) vs CLi+1 (”after”) from four models for 100 questions. The anal-
353   ysis showed a statistically significant decrease in scores from the ”before” condition to the ”after”
354   condition (t = 3.1248, p = 0.0048). These findings provide robust support for our hypotheses, H0
355   and H1, indicating that as the load increases to the cognitive overload state, LLM performance
356   deteriorates. Additionally, our CL assessment method, using multi-task measurements, shows
357   validity comparable to dual-task measurements in HC, further highlighting similarities with
358   human cognition and ICL learning.
359
360   3.2.3   Q UANTIFYING COGNITIVE LOAD WITH TASK IRRELEVANT TOKEN INCREMENT
361
      One of our measurements to quantify the increase in CL is the increase in irrelevant token generation
362
      by the model before the observation task. We developed this measure based on the intuition that as
363   irrelevant tokens increase, EXT CL will rise, consequently increasing the INT CL, as models need to
364   discard these tokens. For the dataset mentioned above, we used GPT-4 and L3-70B-Ins tokenizers
365   to count the number of tokens in the input prompts, the tokens contributing to CL during generation,
366   and the tokens for the response of observation tasks for each question. We performed a statistical
367   paired t-test by comparing the token counts contributing to CL in CLi (”before”) versus CLi+1
368   (”after”) for each question. We found a statistically significant increase in CL token counts as the
369   CL combination increased (p<0.05 for both models). This strongly supports our intuition that an
370   increase in irrelevant tokens contributes to a higher cognitive load in ICL (more details in App. C.6).

404   Methodology: We tested the cognitive overload attack on SOTA models with the subset of Forbid-
405
      den Question Set (Shen et al., 2023) with 232 harmful questions and JailbreakBench (Chao et al.,
      2024) dataset, consisting of 100 harmful questions. We first generated paraphrased derivative ques-
406

      Turbo. The first reason for paraphrasing was to avoid possible contamination of questions during

409   create a cake?’ as presented in the given context in the input prompt. Additionally, it has been shown
410   that paraphrasing can successfully evade detectors (Krishna et al., 2024; Sadasivan et al., 2023). We
411   created an automated attack algorithm to attack the LLMs (more details in App. C.7.1).
412

413
      ([INST],[/INST]) and combine them with CL (CL1 to CL6) to form cognitive overload prompts
414   for attacking the LLM. Starting with CL1, we send the response to a judge LLM to evaluate it as
415   SAFE, UNSAFE, or NEUTRAL. If the response is UNSAFE, we stop; otherwise, we proceed to the
416   next load (CL2, etc.). An attack is considered successful if any derivative question results in a harm-
417   ful response. If a derivative question doesn’t jailbreak the LLM, we move to the next question and
418   repeat the process. To demonstrate the flexibility of our algorithm, we employed two different judge
419   LLMs for the automated attack. Table 1 presents our successful automated attack results, providing
420   significant support for our hypothesis H3.
421   In order to test the efficacy of our attack and avoid bias from a single judge LLM, we further in-
422
      vestigated the responses flagged as harmful by passing them through additional judge LLMs (more
      details in App. C.7.2).

425   handles content filtering with input-output protection (Inan et al., 2023). During the cognitive over-
426   load attack, adversarial prompts are sent to Llama Guard, which classifies them as safe or unsafe
427   before forwarding them to the target LLM. Llama Guard also evaluates the output for safety. The
428
      guardrail is considered to have failed if it allows harmful prompts or responses to pass as safe. Llama

429
      from automated attack, we achieved up to 45% ASR (more details in App. C.8).

      5   D ISCUSSIONS
447
448
      In this paper, we first draw a parallel between learning in ICL and human cognitive learning, and
449
      hypothesize that the theory of CLT applies to ICL in LLMs as well. Motivated by the neuroscience
450
      theory of CLT, we provide a comprehensive comparison of different CL in ICL and discuss what can
451   be done to increase or decrease these CL. The design of prompts and tasks is crucial for inducing
452   cognitive load. Using custom and unconventional tasks that increase both INT CL and EXT CL
453   has been shown to be effective. Combining multiple CL tasks incrementally increases the overall
454   load, eventually leading to cognitive overload in LLMs. While we can estimate the increase in INT
455   CL, the increment in EXT CL can be inferred from the increase in irrelevant tokens in the LLMs’
456   response. Both types of CL contribute to exceeding the working memory capacity, resulting in
457   cognitive overload, as evidenced by our results. It’s important to note that cognitive overload doesn’t
458   imply a complete failure to perform the observation task, but rather a decrease in performance.
459   Why do cognitive overload attacks jailbreak LLMs? We investigated how increased CL impacted
460   LLM performance. The observation task, a secondary task with low CL, becomes challenging when
461   combined with prior cognitive tasks due to the LLM’s limited working memory, which must be di-
462   vided among CL processing and the observation task. An aligned model without CL should refuse to
463   answer harmful questions, indicating high performance. However, when the same harmful question
464   is presented alongside CL tasks, the model’s performance deteriorates. According to CLT literature,
465
      this affects the model’s response to the observation task, leading to a jailbreak where the model gen-
      erates an UNSAFE response. We hypothesize this occurs because most working memory is allocated
466
      to preceding CL tasks, leaving insufficient resources for the observation task. In this situation, the
467
      model has two options: refer to its post-training safety alignment (from RLHF and safety training)
468   or rely on its prior knowledge. With low working memory and operating deterministically (temper-
469   ature=0), the model is more likely to access prior knowledge, which requires less cognitive effort
470   than applying safety protocols. We further support this reasoning by two failure modes: mismatched
471   generalization and competing objectives (Wei et al., 2023b). The attack exploits mismatched gener-
472   alization by leveraging the model’s broader capabilities not fully covered by safety training. Under
473   CL, the model defaults to pretraining knowledge rather than safety constraints, suggesting that safety
474   training does not generalize well under cognitive overload. Additionally, our attack exploits com-
475   peting objectives, forcing the model to balance working memory between CL tasks and maintaining
476
      safety guardrails. After CL tasks, the model may prioritize its language modeling objective over
      enforcing safety constraints, resulting in a jailbreak.

479   6   R ELATED WORK
480
481   As the use of LLMs has proliferated, so too have attacks targeting them during both training and
482   inference phases. Jailbreaking attacks aim to bypass safety alignments to generate harmful or uneth-
483   ical content (Wei et al., 2023a), and studies have demonstrated that such attacks can be automated
484   with minimal human intervention (Li et al., 2023a; Taveekitworachai et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023;
485   Chao et al., 2023; Perez & Ribeiro, 2022; Mehrotra et al., 2023; Shah et al., 2023; Deng et al.,
      2024; Yu et al., 2023). Prompt injection attacks, a form of jailbreaking, manipulate model behav-


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      ior by inserting specific text or instructions into prompts (Greshake et al., 2023a; Wei et al., 2023a),
487   enabling attackers to compromise LLM-integrated systems and perform goal hijacking, prompt leak-
488   ing, reveal system vulnerabilities, and generate malicious content (Greshake et al., 2023b; Liu et al.,
489   2023b;a). Low-resource languages have been exploited to create malicious prompts (Upadhayay &
490   Behzadan, 2024; Deng et al., 2023; Yong et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Puttaparthi et al., 2023), and
491   techniques like token smuggling (Kat, 2023), Base64 encoding (Handa et al., 2024), and code in-
492   jection (Kang et al., 2024) obfuscate harmful questions to bypass safety mechanisms. These attacks
493   often exploit vulnerabilities in ICL, as shown in in-context attacks (Wei et al., 2023b), few-shot
494   hacking (Rao et al., 2023), distraction-based attacks (Xiao et al., 2024), and many-shot jailbreaking
495
      (Anil et al., 2024).
496   We acknowledge the work of Xu et al. (2023), where the authors draw a parallel between CLT and
497   LLMs, demonstrating the impact of cognitive overload by introducing three distinct jailbreak vari-
498   ants: multilingual, veiled expressions, and effect-to-cause reasoning. However, our work extends the
499   experimental scope of authors by presenting a more comprehensive and generalizable framework for
500   the analysis and exploitation of cognitive load in LLMs. Specifically, we introduce tasks as atomic
501
      units of cognitive load, and propose a methodology for quantifying the CL induced by irrelevant
      tasks based on the number of tokens generated by the LLM in response. Using this approach, we
502
      demonstrate how cognitive load increases with additional irrelevant tokens, enabling the creation
503
      of increasingly complex jailbreak attacks. Our methodology extends beyond the three variants ex-
504   plored by authors, generalizing CL jailbreaks across arbitrary scenarios. Furthermore, we show that
505   not only the incremental loading process can be algorithmically automated, but also the crafting of
506   tasks themselves can be automated using SOTA LLMs, making the attack vector highly scalable.
507
      Our concept of cognitive overload can also be applied to analyze other LLM attacks. For example,
508
      in many-shot jailbreaking, an overwhelming number of prompts increases INT CL and EXT CL, and
509
      in the DAN attack (Shen et al., 2023), the model is overloaded with instructions before the harmful
510   question is posed. Unlike these approaches, which exploit longer input prompts with CL, we focus
511   on exploiting longer contexts through response generation.

      7   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORKS
515
      This study has explored the higher level parallels between ICL in LLMs and human cognitive learn-
516
      ing, focusing on the application of CLT to understand and exploit LLM vulnerabilities. Our research
517
      has yielded several significant findings, including the first known study drawing direct parallels be-
518   tween ICL and human cognitive processes, demonstrating the applicability of CLT to LLMs, and
519   developing guidelines for inducing and measuring CL. Our empirical results provide strong statisti-
520   cal evidence that increasing CL in LLMs leads to cognitive overload and degraded performance on
521   secondary tasks, mirroring effects observed in human cognition. We have demonstrated how attack-
522   ers can exploit this vulnerability through our novel attack, achieving high ASR across multiple SOTA
523   models. Our research highlights the transferability of these attacks and presents a low-cost attack
524   framework with significant implications for LLM security. These findings underscore the inherent
525   vulnerabilities in ICL and the urgent need for robust safeguards against cognitive overload-based
526
      attacks. As LLM capabilities continue to expand, understanding these parallels with human cogni-
      tion becomes increasingly crucial for developing effective defense strategies and ensuring the safe
527
      deployment of AI systems. Future work should focus on developing countermeasures against cog-
528
      nitive overload attacks, exploring ways to enhance LLMs’ resilience to such exploits, and further
529   investigating the cognitive processes underlying ICL, while maintaining a strong focus on ethical
530   considerations and responsible AI development practices.

823
824   This work is solely intended for research purposes. In our study, we present a vulnerability in
825   LLMs that can be transferred to various SOTA LLMs, potentially causing them to generate harmful
826   and unsafe responses. The simplicity and ease of replicating the attack prompt make it possible to
827   modify the behavior of LLMs and integrated systems, leading to the generation of harmful content.
828
      However, exposing vulnerabilities in LLMs is beneficial, not because we wish to promote harm, but
      because proactively identifying these vulnerabilities allows us to work towards eliminating them.
829
      This process ultimately strengthens the systems, making them more secure and dependable. By
830
      revealing this vulnerability, we aim to assist model creators in conducting safety training through
831   red teaming and addressing the identified issues. Understanding how these vulnerabilities can be
832   exploited advances our collective knowledge in the field, allowing us to design systems that are
833   not only more resistant to malicious attacks but also foster safe and constructive user experiences.
834   As researchers, we recognize our ethical responsibility to ensure that such influential technology is
835   as secure and reliable as possible. Although we acknowledge the potential harm that could result
836   from this research, we believe that identifying the vulnerability first will ultimately lead to greater
837   benefits. By taking this proactive approach, we contribute to the development of safer and more
838   trustworthy AI systems that can positively impact society.

840   B    L IMITATIONS
841
842   We present various limitations of our work as follows:
843
844         1. To induce cognitive load, we focused on only a handful of tasks and followed a pattern
845            of breaking words into smaller tokens. Our pattern is based on observations from our
846            preliminary experiments, but different patterns can be explored. We believe there are other
847            patterns than ours that might yield high cognitive load.
848         2. We estimated the extraneous cognitive load in terms of the increment of irrelevant tokens.
849            However, the addition of this load will depend on different LLMs. For example, in many
850            models, we observed that writing words in reverse order and having sentences in reverse
851            order added cognitive load. However, in C3.5-Sonnet, the model showed high accuracy
852            when working with reverse text as well. Also, the model was able to decode harmful
853
               questions obfuscated in the [INST],[/INST] tags. Our work is limited to using these tags,
               but we believe that other similar tags can induce higher cognitive load.
854
855         3. Our experiment is limited by the order in which load tasks are added in the combination.
856            We do not explore the impact of changing the order of different tasks. For example, asking
857
               the model to answer before any of the cognitive load tasks or keeping the answer in the
               middle of multiple tasks. We simply followed the intuition of dual-task in human cognition
858
               with our multi-task assessment by keeping the answer (observation task) at the end.
859
860         4. Our preliminary experiment to measure the impact of the cognitive load of each task has
861
               been limited to a single model, Llama-3-70B-Instruct. This was done to test whether each
               cognitive load task would decrease the performance of the observation task.
862

               about cognitive load and what constitutes cognitive load in LLMs. Because of this, the

900   C.1    C OGNITIVE L OAD T HEORY (CLT) IN H UMAN C OGNITION
901
902   Intrinsic cognitive load arises from the complexity of tasks and is influenced by two main factors:
903
      Element Interactivity and Prior Knowledge (Moreno & Park, 2010; Sweller, 2010). Element, in
      the context of the CLT, is considered as the concept, procedure, or unit of information that can
904
      interact with each other, leading to element interactivity (Sweller, 2010). As per the author, Ele-
905
      ment Interactivity involves the inherent difficulty of processing multiple interacting elements, while
906   Prior Knowledge helps link new information to existing models, reducing cognitive load. Success-
907   ful learning and cognitive load management also depend on minimizing EXT CL and maximizing
908   GER CL, which contribute positively to processing and integrating new information into long-term
909   memory (Paas et al., 2003) (Sweller, 2010) (Klepsch et al., 2017). These CL can be managed for
910   successful learning.
911
      The Segmenting Principle, proposed by (Mayer & Moreno, 2010), and the Pretraining Principle,
912   proposed by (Mayer, 2005), both aim to reduce INT CL by respectively organizing information in
913   a step-by-step manner to reduce element interactivity and providing an introduction to the content
914   to help learners form connections with prior knowledge. The split attention effect, first introduced
915   by (Ayres & Sweller, 2005), occurs when learners must mentally integrate disparate information,
916   which can cause cognitive overload, a concept echoed by (Mayer, 2009) in the Spatial Contiguity
917   Principle highlighting improved learning when related words and images are placed adjacent to each
      other. Increasing the GER CL can enhance learning outcomes, as proposed by (VanLehn et al., 1992)


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      through the self-explanation effect, where learners actively link new information with their existing
919   mental model, with the understanding that intrinsic load is productive and extraneous load is unpro-
920   ductive. Thus, reducing EXT CL can help increase GER CL. In summary, the load that is intrinsic to
921   the tasks is productive, while that which is extraneous is unproductive, whereas decreasing the EXT
922   CL will help to increase the GER CL.
923
924   C.1.1     M EASURING C OGNITIVE L OAD IN H UMAN C OGNITION
925
      The measurement of cognitive load in human cognition itself is a challenging task. However, various
926
      methods have been designed for this purpose, including self-reporting, dual-task measures, and
927
      the measurement of physiological parameters (Mayer & Moreno, 2002; Klepsch et al., 2017; Paas,
928   1992). In self-reporting which is a subjective measurement, participants are asked to rate the task
929   using a 9-point Likert scale, ranging from very low to very high mental effort.
930
931
      In dual-task measures, a learner (participant) is required to perform two tasks simultaneously. It
      is assumed that the performance of the second task will decline as the first task becomes more
932
      demanding (Brünken et al., 2004). As per the paper, there are two ways to conduct this measure. In
933
      the first method, while performing the main learning task, the learner is also required to complete
934   an observational task. Cognitive load is assessed based on the accuracy and response times in this
935   observational task. In the second method, while learning the first task, the learner must concurrently
936   perform a separate task, such as tapping a rhythm with their feet. Here, an increase in cognitive load
937   in the first task is indicated by a degradation in the performance of the secondary task. Physiological
938   parameters are also used to measure cognitive load such as heart rate (Paas & Van Merriënboer,
939   1994), and eye blink (Goldstein et al., 1992). These measures help in creating a comprehensive
940   understanding of cognitive overload.

1620                          Applying the concepts of intrinsic and extraneous cognitive load to in-context learning in Large Language Models (LLMs) is an interesting way to think
                              about how these AI systems process and learn from information. While LLMs don't have human-like cognitive limitations, we sure can draw some
1621                          analogies to help understand their learning process:

                              ** Intrinsic Cognitive Load in LLMs ** :

1622                          Task Complexity: The inherent difficulty of the task or concept being learned. For LLMs, this could relate to the complexity of the patterns or relationships
                              they need to identify in the data.


1623                          Context Length: The amount of relevant context needed to understand and perform a task. Longer, more complex contexts could be seen as having
                              higher intrinsic load.

                              Domain Specificity: Specialized domains with unique vocabularies or concepts might represent higher intrinsic load for the model.
1624                          Abstraction Level: Tasks requiring higher levels of abstraction or reasoning might be considered to have higher intrinsic load.

                              Example: Learning to perform complex multi-step reasoning tasks or understanding nuanced cultural references would have high intrinsic load for an
1625                          LLM.

                              ** Extraneous Cognitive Load in LLMs ** :

1626                          Data Quality: Poor quality or noisy training data could be seen as creating extraneous load, as the model has to filter out irrelevant or incorrect
                              information.


1627                          Prompt Design: Poorly structured or ambiguous prompts might create unnecessary complexity for the model, similar to poor instructional design for
                              humans.

                              Irrelevant Information: Including unnecessary details in the context or prompt could be seen as creating extraneous load.
1628                          Inconsistent Formatting: Variations in how information is presented across different examples might create additional processing overhead for the model.

                              Example: A prompt that includes irrelevant information or is structured in a confusing way could create extraneous load for the LLM, making it harder to
1629                          focus on the core task.

                              In the context of LLMs:

1630                          1. Managing intrinsic load: This could involve breaking complex tasks into simpler sub-tasks, or providing relevant examples to leverage the model's pre-
                              training effectively.


1631                          2. Reducing extraneous load: This might include optimizing prompt engineering, removing irrelevant information, and presenting data in consistent,
                              familiar formats.

                              3. Balancing loads: The goal would be to minimize extraneous load to allow the model to focus its processing capacity on managing the intrinsic load of
1632                          the task.

                              4. Adapting to model capabilities: Understanding the model's strengths and limitations can help in designing tasks and prompts that optimize its
                              performance.
1633                          While these concepts don't translate directly from human cognition to AI systems, thinking about intrinsic and extraneous load in LLMs can provide a
                              useful framework for optimizing in-context learning and task performance in these models.
