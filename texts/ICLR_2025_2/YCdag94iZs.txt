009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            In Multiple Instance Learning (MIL), a bag is comprised of instances and the
012            label is prescribed to the whole bag, with no information on the labels of each
013            instance. The leading approaches for MIL are Embedded Space (ES) solutions,
014            where the full bag is embedded into a vector space. While very complex models
015            were constructed for MIL classification tasks, we show that often some features
016
               are associated with a class, and a simple counting/summing algorithm leads to
               similar or better accuracy than current solutions. This can be improved in some
017
               cases by weighting these selected features using a fully connected network to
018
               predict the coefficient of each feature. However, a simple relative contribution of
019            each feature, where the sum of the coefficients is normalized to 1, fails to count the
020            feature. Thus instead, we replace the softmax by a projection of the coefficients to
021            [-1,1] or [0,1] but do not limit their sum. This allows the model to count features.
022            The resulting algorithm - MILCA (Multiple Instance Learning using Counting and
023            Attention) is applied to multiple previous and new real-world MIL tasks, as well as
024            recovering the host disease history from sequenced T Cell Receptor Repertoires.
025            In most cases, MILCA is significantly better and way more efficient than currently
026            used MIL algorithms, with a 3 % higher accuracy than current SOTA on average.
027
               To summarize, in MIL classification tasks, where often the number of features is
               large compared to the number of bags, complex models are typically not better
028
               than a weighted sum of informative features. The code for MILCA is available at:
029
               github.com/submissionanonymous6/MILCA

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) is the classification of a bag composed of instances, where no
035   information on the class of each instance is available (Dietterich et al., 1997). While the original
036   focus of MIL has been on Tabular (Dietterich et al., 1997), text (Zhou et al., 2005), and image
037   classification (Andrews et al., 2002), recently more problems are treated as MIL, including sound
038
      event detection, tumors recognition (Wang et al., 2023b; Sudharshan et al., 2019), and even deep
      fake video detection (Li et al., 2020).
039
040   In this context, one can define the MIL classification problem as follows: A bag B = {x̄1 , · · · , x̄n }
041   consists of instances x̄i . Each instance is represented by a feature vector (x1i , · · · , xm
                                                                                                   i ) (that may
042   have originated from an image or any other data). The bag has a label yB . This label is often binary
043   but may be categorical or continuous (Romeo et al., 2019). The bag’s instances may also have
044
      unseen labels, yxi that are unavailable during the training phase.
045   Classically, in strict MIL problems, a bag B has a positive label if and only if at least one of its
046   instances has a positive label (Dietterich et al., 1997), i.e. :yB = 1 ⇐⇒ ∃x̄i ∈ B : yx̄i = 1.
047   However, often, a more lenient definition is used, where we only assume that the distribution of
048   instances differs between the positive and negative bags(Foulds & Frank, 2010). We here use this
049   more lenient definition. Three main types of solutions were proposed for MIL problems: Instance
050
      Space (IS), Bag Space (BS), and Embedded Space (ES) solutions (Amores, 2013).
051   IS solutions are based on the assumption that bag labels are connected to their instance labels (Di-
052   etterich et al., 1997; Andrews et al., 2002). Therefore, one first estimates the labels of the instances
053   for each bag, and only then evaluates the label of the bag itself using a classifier F (yˆx1 , · · · , yxˆn ),
      where yˆxi is the estimated class of the instance. For example, a book (a bag) can be labeled as good


                                                            1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      or bad. The book has multiple chapters (instances) and each chapter has words (features). In IS
055   solutions, one first labels the book chapters as good or bad, and only then evaluates the book label.
056
057   BS Solutions treat each bag as an entity. The classification is performed using the similarity between
058
      different bags using various distance functions on the bags D(Bi , Bj ) (Wang & Zucker, 2000). In
      BS, one generally cannot determine if an instance is associated with a class. For example, one can
059
      compare books by their length, word count, mean word length, or other parameter.
060
061   Finally, ES solutions rely on the projection of the entire bag into a vector space (in contrast with the
062   projection of each instance) before performing a classification (Wei et al., 2016). In ES, even if the
063   projection is at the instance level, the instance projections are combined into a bag projection(Ilse
064
      et al., 2018; Tarkhan et al., 2022). In the book classification problem above, the book is projected
      into a set of parameters of the book, either predefined or learned. ES solutions are argued to be more
065
      accurate than IS (Wang et al., 2018).
066
067
068   2    N OVELTY
069
070   The main limitation of conventional ES solutions lies in their inability to effectively integrate feature-
071   level and bag-level information. In most MIL datasets, the feature dimension is high (e.g., words in
072   a bag of words or pixels in an image), only a small subset of features is relevant to the class, and the
073
      number of bags is limited. Consequently, current ES classifiers are prone to overfitting, and simpler
      models outperform more complex ones.
074
075   In this work, we argue the following:
076
077
            1. Overfitting can be mitigated through straightforward feature selection by identifying fea-
               tures associated with one of the classes (there is typically an imbalance, where features are
078
               positively associated with only one of the classes, as will be further discussed). Counting
079
               or averaging the relevant features across instances creates a simple classifier.
080
081
            2. This approach leads to a classifier we denote as MILCA (Multiple Instance Learning using
               Counting and Attention). MILCA is both significantly faster and often more accurate than
082
               State-Of-The-Art (SOTA) models on a variety of real-world datasets, including a set of
083
               novel datasets derived from Wiki pages. With virtually no learnable parameters, MILCA
084            also offers a highly interpretable model structure. The enhanced accuracy of MILCA
085            demonstrates that, in many cases, complex models are unnecessary.
086
            3. One can combine MILCA with a simple feed-forward network (FFN). The last layer of
087
               the FFN can be improved by limiting each weight to be in the range of [0,1] or [-1,1]
088            (depending on the importance of negative weight), instead of setting their sum
089
                                                                                              P to be 1. In
               practice, we replace a softmax with a sum of TANH on each feature: y = σ( tanh(αi ) ∗
090            vi ), where αi are learned weights, and vi are the features. This change can allow the layer
091            to count events.
092
093   We also propose a new set of MIL classification tasks, based on the detection of keywords associated
094   with Wiki pages, and each instance is the bag of words of a section of the wikipage.
095
096   3    R ELATED W ORK
097
098   We here focus on ES solutions. Reviews are available for IS and BS solutions (Dietterich et al.,
099   1997; Andrews et al., 2002; Wang & Zucker, 2000). The leading models for MIL classification
100   (that are ES solutions) use tailored algorithms. miVLAD and miFV (Wei et al., 2016) are using the
101   VLAD (Jégou et al., 2010) and FV (Sánchez et al., 2013) computer-vision based encoders. These
102   encoders turn each bag B into a high-dimension vector (b1 , · · · , bn ). VLAD clusters the instances
103   using K-means and represents each instance as its distance from its centroid. FV represents each
104
      bag as its Fisher Vector representation. Both models classify the bag using an SVM.
105   Attention-based ES models, such as Instance Attention and Gated Attention (Ilse et al., 2018;
106   Tarkhan et al., 2022) have a high accuracy in multiple classification tasks. Both Instance Atten-
107   tion and Gated Attention are based on the concept that some instances have less information on
      the bag labels. Both models use an attention layer as the encoder, creating a single vector b¯E that


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      represents its corresponding bag. The models then classify the vectors using a unique MIL pooling
109   technique (Ilse et al., 2018), and a fully connected neural network (Tarkhan et al., 2022). Recently,
110   SMILES (Wang et al., 2023a) claim to outperform all recent models using a useful representation
111   of the bags through self-supervised learning. SMILES generates a multi-instance graph for each
112   bag and encodes the bag’s graph using message-passing layers and an ordered weighted averaging
113   operator. SMILES does not offer a code or details to allow for a comparison of their results.
114
      The concept of ’counting’ has been previously discussed in MIL problems. However, it represents
115   different ideas in different applications. For example, Hajimirsadeghi et al(Hajimirsadeghi et al.,
116   2013) focus on counting the labels of instances within a bag, emphasizing the proportion of positive
117   versus negative instances to guide classification, in contrast with MILCA that emphasizes counting
118   to detect informative features (not instances) across instances. Hajimirsadeghi et al(Hajimirsadeghi
119   et al., 2015) count instance labels to enrich kernel functions to define similarities between bags. This
120   process aligns more with traditional statistical approaches that abstractly handle data relationships
121   and can also be considered a Bag Space solution. The work most similar to MILCA is Raykar et al
122   (Raykar et al., 2008) which also performs feature selection. However, it uses this to produce a prior
123
      on a bag of words classification. Note that a similar terminology, yet a different approach was used
      in the context of instance classification(Foulds & Frank, 2010). However, it is a different approach,
124
      since MILCA does not classify instances, but counts to choose features.
125
126   In contrast, MILCA distinctively counts and sums important feature occurrences among the dif-
127   ferent instances directly, using these counts to construct a straightforward and interpretable vector
128   representation for each bag.

196
      All the presented results are the average over 10 training/validation/test divisions. Each division led
197
      to different informative features. In each division, We randomly divided each data set into training
198   (64%), validation (16%), and testing (20%), except for the C1 counter, where the validation and
199   training were merged. The significant features were determined using the training and validation
200   sets. We trained the C3 model using an Adam optimization and a binary cross-entropy loss function
201   and added two fully connected layers before the final classifier. The hyper-parameters were opti-
202   mized for each division and each dataset by itself, to reach the maximum AUC score on the internal
203   validation set. Following parameter optimization, the models were trained on the combined training
204   and validation sets and tested on the test set. Lastly, we evaluated the models performance on the
205   test set using Area Under ROC Curve (AUC) and accuracy. While the accuracy metric is not optimal
206
      for imbalanced datasets, we report it here to ensure consistency with previous publications.
207   The following algorithms were compared: mi-SVM and MI-SVM (Andrews et al., 2002), EM-
208   DD (Zhang & Goldman, 2001), MIVLAD and mi-FV(Wei et al., 2016), MI-Kernel (Gärtner et al.,
209   2002), mi-Graph (Zhou et al., 2009), mi-Net and MI-Net (Wang et al., 2018), Attention and Gated
210   Attention (Ilse et al., 2018), BGraph (Pal et al., 2022). SMILES (Wang et al., 2023a), BDR (Huang
211   et al., 2022) , and MI-SDB (Feng et al., 2021) were not used as we could not validate their results,

      since no functioning github was available.

      6.1   I NFORMATIVE FEATURES
219
220   We first argue using a simplified model that some features have an average difference between the
221   classes that can be easily detected by a two-class comparison (t-test or Mann Whitney or Fisher exact
222   test - here we used a t-test). We show that for a large enough number of bags (NB ), the fraction of
223   False Positive (F P ) can be arbitrarily small. Similarly, even for a small number of bags, for a strong
224   enough association, the False Negative (F N ) fraction is small. Combining these two arguments
225   leads to a straightforward feature selection and counting argument that can reach any required level
226
      of sensitivity and specificity for a high enough dimension and number of bags. Surprisingly, these
      conditions hold for most real-world datasets classically used in MIL classification. We analyzed a
227
      simple model where all features are Bernoulli variables with an average of δ, except for a subset
228
      of the features (chosen randomly with a probability η) with a different average in the positive and
229   negative bags (δ2 ∈ [δ ± γ] vs. δ). We denote these features as informative features (IF ). We
230   simulated NB bags (See methods). The number of type I errors (False Positives F P (NB , δ1 )) when
231   using a T-test with a cutoff of T on the average of each feature in each bag is equivalent to simply
232   treating each instance as independent, as such:
233                                                                     p
234                               F P (NF , NB ) ≈ NF (1 − η)ERF ( δ ∗ NB ).                                (4)
235   The missed informative features obey |δ − δ2 | < c(NB ) for some c(NB ), and the probability of F P
236   is inversely proportional to γ. The expected number of informative features detected (T P (NF , η, γ)
237   is the product of the total Informative features and the probability of not missing them:
238
239                                    T P (NF , γ) ≈ ηNF (1 − c(NB )/γ)                                   (5)

243   as long as the variance is limited.

247   supported in the real datasets, where we compared the informative features on the training set and
248   estimated them on a test set (Fig. 2 right plot).

      6.3   S OME ATTENTION MAY HELP
325
326   The C1 and C2 models treat all features equally. However, some features may be more informative
327   than others. A simple solution to that would be a logistic regression. However, surprisingly, logistic
328   regression has a lower accuracy than C2 and often of C1, even when a ridge component is added.
329   Similarly, Fully Connected networks have a lower accuracy than the current models tested (Fig. 3).
330   We thus propose an attention-like model that produces a weighted average (Ilse et al., 2018; Tarkhan
331   et al., 2022). However, a weighted average is not sensitive to the number of elements that you sum,
332   only to their relative contribution, which may fail when the number of features is important. To
333   address that, we also propose a generalization of C1 and C2 that adds a weighting function between
334   −1 and 1 to each feature independently. This is in contrast for example with a ridge loss, where
335   the sum of the weights is regularized, but not each weight by itself, or a logistic term that transfers
336   the output range to [0, 1], with no limit on each weight. We tested such a combination by first
337   applying the feature selection. On these features, we computed weights using a two-layer FCN.
338
      These weights were then combined with the features using Eq. 3. Indeed the results are better or
      equal to the current SOTA for all datasets (C3 in Table 2), and have 4 % more accuracy on average
339
      than the current best model, with much fewer parameters and a much shorter runtime (Table 2).

343   The MUSK1 and MUSK2 datasets (Dietterich et al., 1997) represent molecules identified as musks
344   or non-musks by human experts. There are 166 features in each instance that represent the shape, or
345   conformation, of the molecule.
346
      The Fox, Tiger, and Elephant datasets (Andrews et al., 2002) are subsets of the Corel image retrieval
347
      dataset, separated into images containing (or not) the corresponding animal. Each image was divided
348   into segments, grouped into bags, and represented as a 230-d feature vector of the segment’s color,
349   texture, and shape. If any image segments contain the animal, the bag is labeled positive.
350
351
      The Web datasets are based on web index recommendations generated for research purposes (Zhou
      et al., 2005). The datasets were generated mainly on web index pages from the ”Yahoo”, ”CNN”,
352
      and ”FoxNews” websites. Nine volunteers categorized 113 web index pages based on their interests,
353
      resulting in nine distinct datasets. A web index page is positive if at least one volunteer expressed
354   interest in any of its linked pages. Each web index page has 4 to 200 linked pages, grouped into
355   bags, and described by the 1st to 20th most frequent terms appearing in the corresponding linked
356   page, with the terms’ frequencies.
357
      Given the limited size and number of existing MIL datasets, we introduced here a novel dataset
358
      developed for this analysis. The Wiki dataset is structured as a traditional MIL, where each bag
359
      corresponds to the contents of a Wikipedia page representing a city, with a particular focus on pages
360   about European countries. In this dataset, each bag is formed by the different sections of a Wikipedia
361   article, treating each section as an instance within the bag. The features are a bag of words (BOW),
362   combining all the words from all the bags except for the non-relevant words (stop words, etc.). Then,
363   we counted all the words in the BOW for each instance. Each bag is assigned a binary label of its
364   country. The dataset and the appropriate scrapper are available at this paper’s GitHub.

366   6.5   D ISEASE CLASSIFICATION USING T CELLS REPERTOIRES
367
368   An interesting application of the current counting method is the prediction of diseases from immune
369
      cell repertoires. The immune memory repertoire encodes the history of present and past infections
      and immunological attributes of the individual. As such, multiple methods were proposed to use
370
      T-cell receptor (TCR) repertoires to detect disease history(Emerson et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2022;
371
      Katayama & Kobayashi, 2021; Akerman et al., 2023). Each repertoire contains 105 − 107 different
372   TCRs, and the overlap between any two repertoires is less than 1%. Each TCR can be treated as
373   a feature. Some TCRs are associated with target pathogens (CMV in the current case). Although
374   recent models were developed to predict TCR-Antigen binding(Springer et al., 2021; 2020), those
375   are not accurate enough to detect in advance such TCRs. Thus, there is currently no appropriate
376   method to determine biologically informative TCRs. Additionally, there is no known mechanism
377   for a negative association between a TCR and a disease. As such, the only applicable models are C1
      and C3.

450   We simulated a simplified dataset. The generator produces two sets of NB bags each labeled positive

      7     D ISCUSSION
459
460
461
      Summary. Very complex methods were proposed to integrate advanced ML approaches in MIL
462   problems. We have here shown that a simplistic approach is better than current models for most
463   datasets. The basic explanation is that MIL problems are typically rich in data, but with a limited
464   number of samples. As such, almost any learning performed is an over-fit. Moreover, there is a
465   very limited range of parameters where the simple counting arguments fail but other more complex
466   algorithms may succeed. Thus, for many realistic scenarios, either the counting works and more
467   complex models are an overfit, or there is no information and no algorithm works. This is not to say
468   that a complex adapted model cannot perform better than simple counting or weighted counting, but
469   that the difference is typically not large.
470   Beyond the counting we propose an adaptation that weights the different features, using an attention-
471   like mechanism. One could propose that a neural network should be able to learn weights. However,
472   a neural network is not inherently built to perform a weighted count. A softmax output would find
473   the relative importance of different features, but would not reproduce the counting algorithm above.
474   However, a sum of TANH can combine the advantages of a weighted average and counting. We
475   show that it indeed produces a better accuracy for some real-world sets. This is similar to a hard
476
      limit or a strong regularization parameter limiting each weight in a regression to a certain range.
      However, in contrast with the hard limit, the optimization will not push the results to the boundaries
477
      to reproduce the C1 or C2 models.
478
479   We have then shown using a complex clinical example that these methods are appropriate even in
480   complex situations involving hundreds of millions of potential features, and a very small number of
481   samples.
482   Limitations. The main limitation of MILCA is ignoring all the interactions between the features.
483   MILCA is a naive Bayesian estimator. As such any information available in the interaction between
484   features will be lost. This delineates the applicability of MILCA to cases with many features. In
485   contrast, much more advanced methods are required when the input dimension is low and the bags
      number is large.
