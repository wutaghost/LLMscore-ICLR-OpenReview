011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Adaptive gradient methods, such as AdaGrad, are among the most successful opti-
014            mization algorithms for neural network training. While these methods are known
015            to achieve better dimensional dependence than stochastic gradient descent (SGD)
016            under favorable geometry for stochastic convex optimization, the theoretical justi-
017            fication for their success in stochastic non-convex optimization remains elusive. In
018            fact, under standard assumptions of Lipschitz gradients and bounded noise vari-
019            ance, it is known that SGD is worst-case optimal (up to absolute constants) in
020
               terms of finding a near-stationary point with respect to the ℓ2 -norm, making fur-
               ther improvements impossible. Motivated by this limitation, we introduce refined
021
               assumptions on the smoothness structure of the objective and the gradient noise
022
               variance, which better suit the coordinate-wise nature of adaptive gradient meth-
023            ods. Moreover, we adopt the ℓ1 -norm of the gradient as the stationarity measure,
024            as opposed to the standard ℓ2 -norm, to align with the coordinate-wise analysis
025            and obtain tighter convergence guarantees for AdaGrad. Under these new as-
026            sumptions and the ℓ1 -norm stationarity measure, we establish an upper bound on
027            the convergence rate of AdaGrad and a corresponding lower bound for SGD. In
028            particular, for certain configurations of problem parameters, we show that the it-
029            eration complexity of AdaGrad outperforms SGD by a factor of d. To the best of
030            our knowledge, this is the first result to demonstrate a provable gain of adaptive
031
               gradient methods over SGD in a non-convex setting. We also present supporting
               lower bounds, including one specific to AdaGrad and one applicable to general
032
               deterministic first-order methods, showing that our upper bound for AdaGrad is
033
               tight and unimprovable up to a logarithmic factor under certain conditions.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Adaptive gradient methods, including variants like AdaGrad (McMahan & Streeter, 2010; Duchi
040   et al., 2011) and Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015), have become essential for training large-scale
041   neural networks and language models. Their popularity over classic stochastic gradient descent
042   (SGD) (Robbins & Monro, 1951) stems from two key features: (i) adaptive step sizes based on past
043   gradients, eliminating the need for problem-specific parameters like the gradient’s Lipschitz con-
044   stant or stochastic gradient variance, and (ii) the use of coordinate-wise step sizes, allowing better
045   exploitation of the objective’s geometry compared to SGD’s uniform step size.
046   Their empirical success has motivated exploring theoretical guarantees that show a provable gain
047   for this class of methods over the traditional SGD method. To pursue this goal, adaptive gradient
048   methods were initially examined in the context of online convex optimization. In particular, it was
049   shown by Duchi et al. (2011) that depending on the geometry of the feasible set and the sparsity
050   of the gradients,
                 √       AdaGrad’s regret bound could be either better or worse than that of SGD by a
051   factor of d, where d represents the problem’s dimension. For further details, we refer readers
052   to (Hazan, 2016; Orabona, 2019). Moreover, using the classical online-to-batch conversion (Cesa-
053   Bianchi et al., 2004; Shalev-Shwartz, 2012), these regret bounds directly translate into convergence
      rate guarantees in stochastic convex optimization.

      In the non-convex setting, although significant work has been done to characterize the convergence
055   of adaptive methods under various assumptions (more details in the related work section), no prov-
056   able gain has been established for adaptive methods over SGD, and demonstrating such a gain for
057   AdaGrad in the non-convex setting remains an open problem, see (Chen & Hazan, 2024).
058
      Note that when the objective function is smooth and the stochastic gradients are unbiased with
059
      bounded variance, SGD can, after T iterations, find a point where the expected gradient ℓ2 -norm is
060
      bounded by O( T 11/4 ) (Ghadimi & Lan, 2013; Bottou et al., 2018). This convergence rate is known to
061
      be optimal for any method relying on first-order oracles under the discussed assumptions (Arjevani
062
      et al., 2023). Consequently, to demonstrate a provable gain for adaptive methods over SGD in the
063   non-convex setting, we must move beyond the classic setup. In particular, as we will discuss in
064   detail, we argue that modifying both the assumptions and the measure of stationarity is necessary to
065   better account for the coordinate-wise nature of adaptive methods.

096   1.1   R ELATED WORK
097
098   AdaGrad-Norm. Several prior works have established that AdaGrad-Norm achieves a convergence
099
      rate similar to that of SGD, but under stronger assumptions, such as bounded gradients (Ward et al.,
      2020; Kavis et al., 2022; Gadat & Gavra, 2022), the step-size being (conditionally) independent of
100
      the stochastic gradient (Li & Orabona, 2019; 2020), or sub-Gaussian noise (Li & Orabona, 2020;
101
      Kavis et al., 2022). Faw et al. (2022) addressed this issue and showed that under standard assump-
102   tions—Lipschitz gradients and bounded variance—AdaGrad-Norm achieves the same complexity
103   as SGD in terms of gradient’s ℓ2 -norm (up to a logarithmic factor). They further explored the set-
104   ting where the stochastic gradient has affine variance. In addition, several works (Attia & Koren,
105   2023; Liu et al., 2023) provided high-probability convergence guarantees for AdaGrad-Norm under
106   sub-Gaussian noise assumptions. The extension to the generalized smoothness setting (Zhang et al.,
107   2020) was developed in Faw et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023). However, as mentioned earlier, these
      results do not demonstrate any improvement over SGD in terms of convergence rate.

126

127

131   point x with E[∥∇f (x)∥2 ] ≤ ϵ. In addition to the use of ℓ2 -norm, these works focus on establishing
132   dimensional-free lower bounds and the constructed worst-case instance has a dimension that grows
133   with 1/ϵ. As a result, their techniques are unfit for studying lower bounds in a given dimension,
134   which is our focus here. Along a different line of work, people have studied the complexity of find-
135   ing ϵ-stationary points of a function in a small dimension (Vavasis, 1993; Cartis et al., 2010; Chewi
136
      et al., 2023). In particular, Chewi et al. (2023) showed that any deterministic first-order method
      would require Ω( ϵ12 ) to find the ϵ-stationary point of a one-dimensional smooth non-convex func-
137
      tion. To the best of our knowledge, our result is the first to establish a lower bound in terms of the
138
      ℓ1 -norm and highlight the dimensional dependence in the convergence rate.
139
      Concurrent work. The concurrent work by Liu et al. (2024), which appeared online two weeks
140
      after our initial paper, also examined AdaGrad’s convergence under anisotropic smoothness and
141
      noise assumptions, similar to our refined Assumptions 2.3b and 2.4b. They proved an upper bound
142   on AdaGrad’s convergence rate in terms of the gradient’s ℓ1 -norm, comparable to our result in
143   Theorem 3.1, and compared it with the classical upper bound for SGD in terms of the ℓ2 -norm.
144   In contrast, our approach focuses on establishing a lower bound for SGD, allowing us to directly
145   compare AdaGrad’s upper bound with SGD’s lower bound to demonstrate a clear advantage for
146   AdaGrad. Moreover, we further validate the tightness of our AdaGrad upper bound through two
147   lower bounds, one specific to AdaGrad and another for deterministic first-order methods.

526   6    C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we provided a theoretical justification for the advantage of AdaGrad over SGD in
529   stochastic non-convex optimization. We first discussed the impossibility of showing any con-
530   vergence rate improvement over SGD under the standard assumptions of Lipschitz gradients and
531
      bounded variance, as well as using the gradient’s ℓ2 -norm as the stationarity measure. Motivated
      by this observation, we introduced two refined assumptions on the Lipschitz constants and gradient
532
      noise of the objective (Assumptions 2.3b and 2.4b) and proposed using the gradient ℓ1 -norm as the
533
      stationarity measure, which better suit the coordinate-wise nature of adaptive gradient methods. Un-
534   der these refined assumptions, We established a convergence rate for AdaGrad (Theorem 3.1) and
535   a complexity lower bound for SGD (Theorem 4.1) in terms of the gradient’s ℓ1 -norm. Notably, by
536   comparing AdaGrad’s upper bound with SGD’s lower bound, we demonstrated that the complexity
537   of AdaGrad can be better than that of SGD by a factor of d. To our knowledge, this is the first result
538   showing a provable advantage of adaptive gradient methods over SGD in non-convex optimization.
539   In addition, by presenting two lower bounds, we established that the noiseless term in our upper
      bound for AdaGrad is unimprovable up to a logarithmic factor (Theorems 3.2 and 3.3).
