                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               As datasets grow in size, vector-based search becomes increasingly challenging
010            in terms of both storage and computational efficiency. Traditional solutions such
011            as quantization techniques involve trade-offs between retrieval speed and accu-
012            racy, while hashing methods often require further optimization for binarization.
013            In this work, we propose leveraging the compact nature of hyperbolic space for
014            efficient search. Specifically, we introduce Binary Hyperbolic Embeddings, which
015            transform complex hyperbolic similarity calculations into binary operations. We
016            prove that these binary hyperbolic embeddings are retrieval-equivalent to their
017            real-valued counterparts, ensuring minimal loss in retrieval quality. Our approach
018
               can be seamlessly integrated into FAISS to achieve improved memory efficiency
               and running speed while maintaining performance comparable to full-precision
019
               Euclidean embeddings. Notably, binary hyperbolic embeddings can also be com-
020
               bined with product quantization. We demonstrate significant improvements in
021            storage efficiency, with a natural byproduct of speeding up, with scaling potential
022            to larger datasets. A portion of the code is included in the supplementary materi-
023            als, and the full implementation will be made publicly available.

076   Since search typically needs to occur on-the-fly (Yuan et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2018) or on huge
077   collections (Jang & Cho, 2021; Chen et al., 2023), it is imperative to efficiently embed queries and
078   data collections. The efficiency of an embedding can be expressed in bits, where fewer bits can
079   ultimately only be obtained in two ways: using fewer embedding dimensions (Cao et al., 2020;
080   Hausler et al., 2021) and/or using fewer bits per dimension (Choukroun et al., 2019; Yao et al.,
081
      2022; Bai et al., 2022).
082
083
      Hyperbolic low-dimensional representations differ from the Euclidean representations for their
      ability to embed hierarchical structures with minimal distortion Ganea et al. (2018b); Tseng et al.
084
      (2023) due to the curved nature of the space Cannon et al. (1997). Most related, a variety of
085
      works find that hyperbolic space is naturally low-dimensional (Tifrea et al., 2019; Long et al., 2020;
086   Shimizu et al., 2021; Ghadimi Atigh et al., 2021; Desai et al., 2023; Tseng et al., 2023). Such po-
087   tential enables dimensionality reduction (Chami et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2022), but at the cost of
088   computational overhead (Shimizu et al., 2021; Peng et al., 2021). In this paper, we exploit the low-
089   dimensional nature, pushing it to fewer bits and turning the computational cost into acceleration.
090
091   Low-bit embeddings. For using fewer bits per embedding dimension, classical solutions are given
092   by quantization techniques (Jégou et al., 2011; Jacob et al., 2018), benefiting quantization models
093   designed for large-scale settings (Liu et al., 2021b; Yao et al., 2022). Inspired by these developments,
094   we seek to bring the advantages of low-bit binarization to hyperbolic embeddings.
095   Binary hashing embeddings (Wang et al., 2017) learn compressed representations into compact
096   binary codes (Shen et al., 2018b; Fan et al., 2020; Hoe et al., 2021b; Shen et al., 2020; Wei et al.,
097   2024) while preserving the semantic similarity (Yuan et al., 2020) or structure (Li & van Gemert,
098   2021) of the original data, thereby reducing storage and computational costs. Unlike our proposed
099   binarization approach, hashing requires NP-hard optimization and thus demands either complex
100   optimization (Shen et al., 2018a) or substantial relaxation (Wei et al., 2024).
101   Hyperbolic isometry. There are five isometric models for hyperbolic space (Cannon et al., 1997).
102   We focus on the Poincaré disk model as it is highly suited for binarization; the coordinates on the
103   Poincaré ball are finite, and each axis is symmetric, allowing us to use a simple binarization strategy.
104   Further discussion on the other hyperbolic models can be found in Appendix A.2.
105   Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN) search significantly reduces query time for large-scale
106   retrieval (Muja & Lowe, 2014; Douze et al., 2024), where the exact nearest neighbor search is com-

167   hyperbolic embeddings) and the hyperbolic distance (over real-valued hyperbolic embeddings).

411   4.4   E FFECT OF HIERARCHICAL KNOWLEDGE
412
413   A key benefit of hyperbolic space is the capability to embed hierarchical knowledge with minimal
414   distortion for hierarchical embeddings (Ganea et al., 2018b; Sala et al., 2018). Such property enables
415   a hyperbolic network to retrieve semantically similar items of adjacent classes (Long et al., 2020),
416   Following which setting, in Appendix D.2 we report results with hierarchical knowledge-aware
417   retrieval. We can maintain retrieval performance in both standard and hierarchy-aware settings.
418
      Even with the lowest number of bit lengths.
419
420
      Qualitative analysis. In Figure 5 we compare a non-hierarchical spherical space with our hierar-
      chical hyperbolic space. All classes are connected to classes with pairwise cosine similarity greater
421
      than 0.5. To measure the similarity between classes we average the embeddings for all instances of
422
      a class, reducing it to pair-wise relationships. The figure shows that we are better at organizing con-
423   cepts hierarchically, which as a consequence means that inputs with hierarchically similar concepts
424   are more likely to fall in the same quantization bin. This enables better hierarchical performance
425   even at low bit length. We suspect this is because spherical embeddings are learned by forcing
426   classes to be equally dissimilar, whereas in hyperbolic space we can enforce a margin between
427   classes while keeping track of siblings due to its infinite boundary nature.

522   5      C ONCLUSION
523
524   Hyperbolic deep learning has a wide range of applications, from images to videos. However, its
525   application in large-scale search has been hampered by slow distance calculations. In this work,
526   we overcome this limitation by proving the retrieval equivalence between hyperbolic and Hamming
527   distances, which allows binarization of the hyperbolic space and significantly accelerates distance
528   calculations. We experimentally verify this acceleration, across the video and image domain, ob-
529   taining significant accerlation at roughly equal performance. Our hyperbolic binary embeddings
530
      demonstrate the viability of hyperbolic space for large-scale retrieval and hierarchical retrieval.
531   Broader impact. The proposed model, through binarization of hyperbolic space, substantially de-
532   creases memory consumption and computational costs for retrieval, which contributes to reducing
533   energy costs and infrastructure expenses.
534   Limitations. A limitation of hyperbolic embeddings on a Poincaré disk is the issue of numerical
535   stability (Yu & De Sa, 2019; Mishne et al., 2023), as it relies on a finite numerical range to represent
536   an infinite volume. This constraint becomes particularly pronounced because the Poincaré disk
537   model compresses distances exponentially as they approach the boundary, leading to substantial
538   precision challenges. An induced limitation is that we binarize the embeddings uniformly in a
539   non-uniform space, which would be an inspiration for future work. Double-precision arithmetic is
      recommended to mitigate numerical instabilities near the disk’s edge.

789   A     H YPERBOLIC M ACHINE L EARNING P RELIMINARIES
790
791   Hyperbolic geometry is non-Euclidean geometry characterized by a constant negative curvature.
792
      Unlike Euclidean geometry, hyperbolic space exhibits unique properties, such as the exponential
      growth of volume (Ganea et al., 2018b) with respect to the radius and the divergence of parallel
793
      lines. It serves as a powerful mathematical framework for modeling hierarchical data, tree-like
794
      structures, and other settings where distances grow exponentially.
795
796   Hyperbolic spaces are equipped with several equivalent models Cannon et al. (1997) that offer
797   different perspectives on the same underlying geometry. These models provide flexibility for com-
798
      putation, visualization, and mathematical reasoning. Despite their different formulations, they are
      isometric, meaning their distance metrics can be transformed into one another through well-defined
799
      coordinate transformations.
800
801
      A.1   F IVE C OMMON M ODELS OF H YPERBOLIC G EOMETRY
802
803   Following the conventions in differential geometry, the five commonly used models of hyperbolic
804   geometry are isometric to each other:
805
806   1. Halfspace Model (H): Also known as the Poincaré halfspace model, it is defined as:
807
                                    H = {(1, x2 , . . . , xn+1 ) : xn+1 > 0} .
808
809   In this model, hyperbolic space is represented within a half-plane or half-space above a given axis,
      often used in complex analysis and conformal mapping.


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      2. Interior of the Disk Model (I): Also known as the Poincaré disk model, it is given by:
811
                                I = (x1 , . . . , xn , 0) : x21 + · · · + x2n < 1 .

812
813   Here, the hyperbolic space resides inside a Euclidean unit disk. This model is conformal, preserving
814   angles, and is commonly used in visualization.
815
816   3. Jemisphere Model (J): Pronounced with the ”J” as in Spanish, this model is defined as:
817
                     J = (x1 , . . . , xn+1 ) : x21 + · · · + x2n+1 = 1 and xn+1 > 0 .

818
819   The hyperbolic space is represented as a hemisphere of a unit sphere.
820
821   4. Klein Model (K): This model, known for its projective properties, is given by:
822
                             K = (x1 , . . . , xn , 1) : x21 + · · · + x2n < 1 .

823
824   The Klein model preserves straight-line geodesics, making it useful for certain computations, though
825   it does not preserve angles.
826
827   5. Loid Model (L): Short for the hyperboloid model, it is defined as:
                L = (x1 , . . . , xn , xn+1 ) : x21 + · · · + x2n − x2n+1 = −1 and xn+1 > 0 .

828
829
      This model is particularly important in physics and mathematics due to its direct connection to
830
      Lorentzian geometry and relativity.

1236   F     M ORE R ELATED W ORK
1237
1238   Hyperbolic representation differs from the Euclidean representation commonly used in deep
1239   learning. Early success was obtained by embedding the nodes of hierarchies as hyperbolic vec-
1240   tors, outperforming Euclidean embeddings. Nickel & Kiela (2017) introduce Poincaré Embeddings,
1241   where hierarchical nodes are positioned by pulling and pushing nodes based on parent-child rela-
       tions. Ganea et al. (2018b) extend this idea through hyperbolic entailment cones, where child nodes


                                                         23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       should strictly fall under the cone spanned by parent nodes. Other hyperbolic embeddings Sala et al.
1243   (2018); Balazevic et al. (2019); Tseng et al. (2023); Yu et al. (2024) further explore these ideas
1244   for incomplete information, graphs, and generalibility, respectively. To make the step towards deep
1245   learning in hyperbolic space, Ganea et al. (2018a) and Shimizu et al. (2021) introduce hyperbolic
1246   linear, recurrent, convolutional, and self-attention layers in the most commonly used model of hy-
1247   perbolic space: the Poincaré ball model. These works have served as a foundation for hyperbolic
1248   deep learning on graphs Pan & Wang (2021), dimensionality reduction Chami et al. (2021), com-
1249   plex networks Muscoloni et al. (2017), social media Sawhney et al. (2021), etc. For more details on
1250   hyperbolic layers, we refer to the survey of Peng et al. (2021).
1251   Hyperbolic learning has also been broadly investigated in the image and video domain Mettes et al.
1252   (2023). Hyperbolic geometry has been shown to aid in a variety of tasks, including image segmen-
1253   tation Atigh et al. (2022), object detection Lang et al. (2022), action recognition Long et al. (2020);
1254   Peng et al. (2020), image-text representation learning Desai et al. (2023), representing 3D point
1255   clouds Montanaro et al. (2022) and LiDAR pose regression Wang et al. (2023). These works have
1256   in common that they leverage hierarchical knowledge to maximize the benefits from the hyperbolic
1257
       space, embedding related concepts closer together, thereby allowing for more compact and powerful
       representations.
