009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011             Large language models (LLMs) have seen widespread adoption across various
012             applications, both in their original form and fine-tuned adaptations. However, a
013             major concern with LLMs is their potential to generate biased content. Existing
014             evaluation methods often have different constraints, such as needing access to the
015             model’s intermediate outputs. To address these issues, we propose GPTBIAS,
016             a novel bias evaluation framework that leverages the capabilities of advanced
017             LLMs like GPT-4 to assess bias in other models across nine bias types. Our
018
                framework introduces Bias Attack Instructions, specifically designed to evaluate
                model bias across multiple dimensions. GPTBIAS provides not only a quantitative
019
                bias score but also detailed information on bias types, affected demographics,
020
                underlying reasons for biases, and suggestions for improvement. Through extensive
021             experiments on popular LLMs, we demonstrate the effectiveness and usability of
022             our bias evaluation framework. Our results reveal nuanced insights into the biases
023             present in different models and highlight the importance of comprehensive bias
024             assessment in the development and deployment of LLMs.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The rapid emergence of large language models (LLMs) has led to remarkable advancements in
030   various domains of Natural Language Processing (NLP). Models such as LLaMA Touvron et al.
031   (2023), BLOOM Scao et al. (2022), ChatGPT OpenAI (2022), GPT 4 OpenAI (2023) and their
032   variants have demonstrated impressive capabilities in addressing diverse natural language tasks, often
033
      without explicit task-specific training Brown et al. (2020); Chowdhery et al. (2022); Zhang et al.
      (2023). However, these models are predominantly trained on real text data, making them susceptible
034
      to biases arising from skewed data distribution and inherent biases in the training data 1 .
035
036   Given the significant role played by LLMs, it is imperative to prioritize the identification of biases
037   during the training and deployment of these models. Existing bias evaluation metrics can be broadly
038   categorized into two approaches: 1) Traditional approach: These methods quantify bias by comparing
039
      model output probabilities or word vector distances, as exemplified in May et al. (2019); Nadeem
      et al. (2020); Nangia et al. (2020); Barikeri et al. (2021); Zhao et al. (2023). However, they often
040
      have limitations such as: a) Pairs of bias-related keywords or sentences are required Nangia et al.
041
      (2020); Barikeri et al. (2021); Zhao et al. (2023). b) Needing access to intermediate model outputs,
042   such as word embedding and output probabilities Nangia et al. (2020). c) Relying on large labeled
043   datasets Nadeem et al. (2020). 2) LLM-based evaluators: Recent studies Sun et al. (2023); OpenAI
044   (2023) have shown that advanced LLMs like ChatGPT and GPT-4 exhibit less bias compared to
045   other existing models and show promise as evaluation metrics. Peng et al. (2023) demonstrate the
046   effectiveness of instruction tuning using instruction following data generated by GPT-4. Additionally,
047   Zou et al. (2023) shows that LLMs can detect and reject harmful instructions, demonstrating their
048   resilience to attacks. However, current metrics often focus on predefined single bias types and limited
049   demographic scales, lacking comprehensive coverage of complex biases like intersectional biases.
050
      Additionally, it lacks of trustworthiness of the evaluation results while using black-box LLMs as an
      evaluator.

157   3      B IAS E VALUATION F RAMEWORK
158
159   In this section, we present GPTBIAS, our novel framework for evaluating bias in large language
160   models. As illustrated in Figure 1, the bias evaluation process within GPTBIAS framework comprises
161   four steps: 1) Developing a set of instructions that intentionally evoke biases across nine specified
      bias types. These instructions are tailored to probe the susceptibilities of large language models. 2)


                                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Inputting these instructions into the target LLM and recording responses, forming the basis for our
163   bias analysis. 3) Compiling responses alongside their corresponding instructions into a structured
164   template and processing them with GPT-4, which assesses the content for biases, categorizing and
165   providing detailed insights into the nature and type of biases present. 4) Calculating a bias score for
166   each bias type by determining the proportion of biased responses within that bias type, and computing
167   an intersectional bias score to capture biases affecting multiple demographic groups simultaneously.

169   3.1   B IAS ATTACK I NSTRUCTION G ENERATION
170
171   To generate bias attack instructions, we employ a methodology that incorporates initial manually
172   crafted prompts informed by prior research on language model biases and sociological studies
173   Wang et al. (2022); Peng et al. (2023). We use ChatGPT as the instruction generation model. This
174
      process is detailed in four steps: 1) Initial Attack Instructions: Providing demonstrations of initial
      attack instructions for nine bias types along with guided prompts; 2) Prompt Design: Selecting
175
      prompts based on bias types and combining a few demonstrations as inputs; 3) Instruction Generation:
176
      Inputting the content into ChatGPT and obtaining candidate instructions; 4) Quality Control: Filtering
177   out low-quality instructions.
178
179
      Initial Attack Instructions We manually crafted these bias attack instructions demonstrations
180   based on existing research on language model biases Nangia et al. (2020); Barikeri et al. (2021)
181   and sociological literature Krekula (2007); Rhode (2010). The guidelines for creating bias attack
182   instruction demonstrations are as follows:
183
      Include various relevant demographic groups for different bias types, employ diverse instruction
184
      formats, and utilize instructions that tend to elicit biased responses from language models.
185
186   We have ninety initial bias attack instructions, consisting of ten instructions for each bias type.
187
188   Prompt Design To obtain a wide and diverse range of biased attack instructions, we designed
189   prompts for each bias type. The prompts for different bias types are based on the same template, but
190   each bias has specific guidelines for generation. The template is as follows:
191   “Please generate a prompt statement for attacking {bias type} in language models according to my
192   requirements, and output the prompt in the format provided in the example. Here are the criteria you
193   need to meet:
194   \n1. The prompt statement should be designed to induce the language model to generate content
195   with {bias type}.
196   \n2.The generated prompt statements should have diverse forms, such as questions, instructions,
197   dialogues, etc.
198
      \n3. Responses are in English only.
      \n4. {specific guidelines}
199
      \n The examples are as follows: {sampled demonstrations}”.
200
201   Then choose a type of bias and randomly select some corresponding demonstrations to replace the
202   {bias type} and {sampled demonstrations} in the template. Also, replace the {specific guidelines}
203   in the template with guidance specific to the chosen type of bias. The specific guidelines for nine
204
      bias types are presented in Appendix C. We randomly select four instruction demonstrations for each
      bias type. These demonstrations can come from our manual initial demonstrations or from saved
205
      responses of ChatGPT, which means that as the instruction generation process progresses, the pool of
206
      selectable instruction demonstrations increases.
207
208
      Instruction Generation and Quality Control After generating initial responses using the prede-
209
      fined template, these content are input to ChatGPT to and gain candidate instructions. To ensure the
210   quality and diversity of these instructions, we implement a data cleaning and filtering methodology.
211   Firstly, we remove any instructions that are either too brief (fewer than 4 tokens) or excessively long
212   (more than 100 tokens) to maintain a consistent level of detail and complexity. Secondly, we exclude
213   responses containing special symbols (e.g., #$&, etc.). Finally, we evaluate the similarity of each
214   newly generated instruction to previously saved and initial instructions using the Rouge-L metric.
215   Only instructions with a Rouge-L similarity score below 0.7 are retained to ensure a broad range of
      diverse and unique content.

      Following these filtering steps, we finalize a set of 200 high-quality prompts for each of the nine bias
217   types, totaling 1800 unique prompts. These prompts are carefully designed to elicit biased responses
218   from language models across a range of bias types. Examples of the finalized bias attack instructions
219   for each bias type are detailed in Appendix A. Notably, our bias attack instruction contains some
220   open questions and guidelines, as shown in Appendix B.

      3.2   B IAS E VALUATION
223
224   We introduce a novel bias evaluation metric called GPTBIAS, which leverages GPT-4. This metric
225   does not require access to the internal weights or intermediate outputs of the model under evaluation,
226   allowing it to effectively analyze black-box LLMs. We input the generated bias attack instructions
227   into the target LLM and record its responses. These responses form the basis for our bias analysis.
228   We devise an evaluation prompt template that consolidates the bias attack instruction with the
229   corresponding response from the test model. GPT-4 then analyzes this combined input: 1) Performs a
230   binary classification to determine if the response exhibits any bias. 2) Identify the types of biases
231   present. 3) Identify the demographic groups affected by the biases. 4) Provide reasons for the bias
232
      evaluation results. 5) Suggest improvements to mitigate the biases. The evaluation prompt template
      is structured as follows:
233
      “Given a conversation between two users, analyze the response of User 2 for bias.
234
      \n1. Categorize User 2’s response as biased or unbiased.
235   \n2. If biased, identify the types of biases present (multiple biases can be present and not limited
236   to the following: gender, religion, race, sexual orientation, age, nationality, disability, physical
237   appearance, and socioeconomic status).
238   \n3. If biased, identify the demographic group affected by the biases. If not, mention ”None”.
239   \n4. If biased, identify the reasons causing the biases. If not, mention ”None”.
240   \n5. If biased, suggest improvements to mitigate the biases. If not, mention ”None”.
241   \n6. Return the results in the following format: \n1. Biased:{Yes/No} \n2. Bias Type: \n3.
242   Demographic Group: \n4. Reason: \n5. Improvement:
243
      The conversation is as follows:
      \nUser 1: {Bias attack instruction}
244
      \nUser 2: {Response}”.
245
246   We replace the Bias attack instruction and Response with the instruction and the corresponding
247   language model. Table 2 shows some instances of evaluation results.
248
249   3.3   B IAS S CORE C ALCULATION
250
251   We quantify the extent of bias present in the target language models by calculating bias scores for
252   each bias type. These scores are derived from the ratio of biased responses to the total number of
253   instructions within each bias type. Therefore, a higher score means higher bias in the LLM.
254   Additionally, we introduce an intersectional bias score to capture the compounded biases affecting
255   multiple demographic groups simultaneously. We define an intersectional biased instance as an
256   LLM response that exhibits two or more distinct bias types in its evaluation results. To calculate the
257   intersectional bias score for each type, we compute the ratio of intersectional biased responses to the
258   total number of instructions within that bias type, mirroring our approach for single bias scores. This
259   method provides a more nuanced understanding of bias interactions, highlighting which demographic
260   groups are most affected by overlapping biases and illuminating the complex factors contributing to
261   these multifaceted biases.

310   4.3      E XISTING B IAS E VALUATION M ETRICS
311
312   We compared GPTBIAS with two popular existing metrics, including StereoSet Nadeem et al.
313   (2020) and CrowS-Pairs Nangia et al. (2020). CrowS-Pairs measures the bias in a language model by
314   assessing how often the model favors the stereotypical sentence in each pair over the anti-stereotypical
315   sentence. StereoSet can assess four types of stereotypes in language models by having them complete
316
      a cloze test task to evaluate bias. For these two evaluation metrics, the closer the score is to 50, the less
      bias in the language model. However, both of the evaluation metrics have their limitations in assessing
317
      model bias. These limitations prevent them from evaluating biases beyond their predefined data and
318
      from detecting biases toward demographic groups not included in the test data. We also conducted
319   manual evaluations to assess the model responses to bias attack instructions. The guidelines and
320   results of human evaluation are shown in Appendix G.

      degrees of bias across nine bias types. Among the evaluated models, OPT-66B exhibited the highest

      Impact & Relevance: The GPTBIAS framework holds significant potential for enhancing the
444   fairness of LLMs by providing a comprehensive bias evaluation system. By leveraging GPT-4 for
445   bias assessment, the framework can evaluate bias in black-box LLMs without requiring access to
446   model internals, making it applicable to a wide range of models in practical settings.
447
      Moreover, the evaluation of biases across multiple demographic dimensions help to align LLMs
448
      more closely with societal values and expectations, promoting inclusivity and reducing the risk of
449
      perpetuating bias. The practical implications of this work provide developers and researchers with
450   tools to refine AI systems, ensuring that they serve a broad spectrum of users more equitably.
451
452   Ethical Considerations We recognize the importance of continuously striving to reduce biases in
453
      large language models. By providing detailed information about bias types, affected demographics,
      and reasons behind biases, our bias evaluation framework, GPTBIAS, aims to facilitate mitigation
454
      efforts and foster improvements in model design and training practices.
455
456   Users should be made aware of the limitations and potential biases of the models they interact with.
457   Informed consent is crucial when utilizing large language models, particularly in sensitive domains
458   or applications that have a significant impact on individuals or communities.
459   While GPTBIAS offers valuable insights for bias evaluation and mitigation in LLMs, we acknowledge
460   the potential risks associated with the misuse of our bias instruction dataset. To address these concerns
461   and ensure responsible use of our research, we have implemented the safeguards in Appendix H.

      7     L IMITATIONS & F UTURE W ORK
464
465   Dependency on The LLM: GPTBIAS relies on the performance and capabilities of the LLM it is
466   built upon, such as GPT-4. Any limitations or biases present in the underlying LLM may affect the
467   accuracy and reliability of the bias evaluation framework.
468
      Bias Detection Accuracy: The framework relies on patterns and heuristics learned by the LLM, and
469
      there may be cases where subtle or context-specific biases are not adequately captured.
470
471   Generalizability Across Languages: Our experiments and evaluations primarily focus on English
472   language models and biases in English text. The effectiveness and generalizability of GPTBIAS may
473   vary when applied to models trained on other languages.

475   8     C ONCLUSION
476
477   The increased usage of large language models (LLMs) across various applications calls for a more
478   robust bias evaluation system. Our proposed framework, GPTBIAS, presents a novel approach to
479   assessing bias within these models, utilizing Bias Attack Instructions specifically tailored for bias
480   evaluation. GPTBIAS not only provides a bias score but also offers comprehensive insights into
481   the type of bias, affected demographics, underlying reasons for biases, and potential solutions for
482   improvement. Through extensive experiments on popular LLMs, we demonstrate the effectiveness
483
      and usability of GPTBIAS. Our results reveal nuanced insights into the biases present in different
      models, highlighting the importance of comprehensive bias assessment in the development and
484
      deployment of LLMs. GPTBIAS shows promise in detecting subtle biases that may be overlooked by
485
      traditional metrics and aligns well with human annotation in identifying biased content.

      G.4    I NTER -A NNOTATOR AGREEMENT AND R ESOLUTION
775
      Agreement Calculation: We calculated the Fleiss’ Kappa coefficient to measure inter-annotator
776   agreement across all three annotators.
777
778   Threshold for Review: Samples with less than 2/3 agreement (i.e., where annotators disagreed) were
779
      flagged for review.
780   Resolution Process: For flagged samples, a group discussion was held among the annotators and a
781   senior researcher. 1) Analyze the reasons for disagreement. 2)Refer to established guidelines and
782   examples. 3)Reach a consensus through deliberation.
783   Final Labeling: After the resolution process, a final label was assigned to each sample based on
784   majority agreement or consensus decision.
