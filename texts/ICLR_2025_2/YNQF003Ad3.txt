011                                              A BSTRACT
012
               Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) is crucial for automotive design, requiring
013
               the analysis of large 3D point clouds to study how vehicle geometry affects pressure
014            fields and drag forces. However, existing deep learning approaches for CFD
015            struggle with the computational complexity of processing high-resolution 3D
016            data. We propose Factorized Implicit Global Convolution (FIGConv), a novel
017            architecture that efficiently solves CFD problems for very large 3D meshes with
018            arbitrary input and output geometries. FIGConv achieves quadratic complexity
019            O(N 2 ), a significant improvement over existing 3D neural CFD models that require
020            cubic complexity O(N 3 ). Our approach combines Factorized Implicit Grids to
021            approximate high-resolution domains, efficient global convolutions through 2D
022
               reparameterization, and a U-shaped architecture for effective information gathering
               and integration. We validate our approach on the industry-standard Ahmed body
023
               dataset and the large-scale DrivAerNet dataset. On DrivAerNet, our model achieves
024
               an R2 value of 0.95 for drag prediction, outperforming the previous state-of-the-art
025            by a significant margin. This represents a 40% improvement in relative mean
026            squared error and a 70% improvement in absolute mean squared error over prior
027            methods.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The automotive industry stands at the forefront of technological advancement, relying heavily on
032
      computational fluid dynamics (CFD) to optimize vehicle designs for enhanced aerodynamics and
      fuel efficiency. The accurate simulation of complex fluid dynamics around automotive geometries is
033
      crucial for achieving optimal performance. However, traditional numerical solvers, including finite
034
      difference and finite element methods, often prove computationally intensive and time-consuming, par-
035   ticularly when dealing with large-scale simulations, as encountered in CFD applications. The demand
036   for efficient solutions in the automotive sector necessitates the exploration of innovative approaches
037   to accelerate fluid dynamics simulations and overcome the limitations of current solvers.
038
      In recent years, deep learning methodologies have emerged as promising tools in scientific computing,
039
      advancing traditional simulation techniques, in bio-chemistry (Jumper et al., 2021), seismology (Yang
040
      et al., 2021), climate change mitigation (Wen et al., 2023), and weather (Pathak et al., 2022; Lam
041   et al., 2022) to name a few. In fluid dynamics, recent attempts develop domain specific deep learning
042   methods to emulate fluid flow evolution on 2D and 3D proof of concept settings (Jacob et al., 2021;
043   Li et al., 2020b; Pfaff et al., 2020a; Kossaifi et al., 2023). While most of these works focused on
044   solving problems using relatively low-resolution grids, industrial automotive CFD requires working
045   with detailed meshes containing millions of points.
046
      To address the time-consuming and compute-intensive nature of conventional CFD solvers on detailed
047
      meshes, recent studies (Jacob et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023) have explored replacing CFD simulations
048   with deep learning-based models to accelerate the process. In particular, Jacob et al. (2021) studies
049   DrivAer dataset (Heft et al., 2012), utilize Unet (Ronneberger et al., 2015) architecture and aim
050   to predict single number scalar car surface drag coefficients – the integration of surface pressure
051   and friction – directly by bypassing the integration. Furthermore, the architecture is applied on 3D
052   voxel grids that requires O(N 3 ) complexity, forcing the method to scale only to low-resolution 3D
053   grids. Li et al. (2023) propose a neural operator method for Ahmed body (Ahmed et al., 1984) car
      dataset and aims to predict the pressure function on the car surface. This approach utilizes graph

054
      embedding to a uniform grid and perform 3D global convolution through fast Fourier transform
055   (FFT). While this method, in principle, handles different girding, the FFT in the operator imposes
056   complexity of O(N 3 log N 3 ), which becomes computationally prohibitive as the size of the grid
057   increases. Both methods face scalability challenges due to their cubic complexity, which severely
058   limits their representational power for high-resolution simulations. Consequently, there is a pressing
059   need for a specialized, domain-inspired method capable of handling 3D fine-grained car geometries
060   with meshes comprising tens of millions of vertices (Jacob et al., 2021). Such massive datasets
061   demand a novel approach in both design and implementation.
062
063
      In this work, we propose a novel neural CFD approach with quadratic complexity O(N 2 ), signif-
      icantly improving scalability over existing 3D neural CFD models that require cubic complexity
064
      O(N 3 ). Our method outperforms the state-of-the-art by reducing absolute mean squared error by
065
      70%.
066
067   The key innovations of our approach include Factorized Implicit Grids and Factorized Implicit
068   Convolution. With Factorized Implicit Grids, we approximate high-resolution domains using a set of
069   implicit grids, each with one lower-resolution axis. For instance, a 1k × 1k × 1k domain containing
070   109 elements can be represented by three implicit grids with dimensions 5 × 1k × 1k, 1k × 4 × 1k, and
071   1k × 1k × 3. This reduces the total elements to just 5M + 4M + 3M = 12M , a significant reduction
072   from the original 109 . Our Factorized Implicit Convolution method approximates 3D convolutions
073
      using these implicit grids, employing reparametrization techniques to accelerate computations.
074
      We validate our approach on two large-scale CFD datasets: DrivAerNet (Heft et al., 2012; Elrefaie
075   et al., 2024) and Ahmed body dataset (Ahmed et al., 1984). Our experiments focus on surface pressure
076   and drag coefficient prediction. Results demonstrate that our network is an order of magnitude faster
077   than existing methods while achieving state-of-the-art performance in both drag coefficient prediction
078   and per-face pressure prediction.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
081
082   The integration of deep learning into CFD processes has seen significant research efforts. Graph
083   neural operator is among the first methods to explore neural operators on various geometries and
084   meshes (Li et al., 2020b). The architectures based on graph neural networks (Ummenhofer et al., 2019;
085   Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020; Pfaff et al., 2020a), follow message passing and encounter similar
086   computational challenges when dealing with realistic receptive fields. The u-shaped graph kernel,
087   inspired by multipole methods and UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015), offers an innovative approach
088
      to graph and operator learning (Li et al., 2020c). However, the core computational challenges in
      3D convolution remain nonetheless, even for FNO based architectures that are widely deployed (Li
089
      et al., 2022; Pathak et al., 2022; Wen et al., 2023). Deep learning models in computer vision, e.g.,
090
      UNet, have been used to predict the fluid average properties, such as final drag for the automotive
091   industry (Jacob et al., 2021; Trinh et al., 2024). Studies incorporating signed distance functions
092   (SDF) to represent geometry have gained attention where CNNs are used as predictive models in
093   CFD simulations (Guo et al., 2016; Bhatnagar et al., 2019). The 3D representation of SDF inflicts
094   significant computation costs on the 3D models, making them only scale to low-resolution SDF,
095   missing the details in the fine car geometries. Beyond partial differential equations (PDE) and
096   scientific computing, various deep learning models have been developed to deal with fine-detail
097   3D scenes and objects. In particular, for dense prediction tasks in 3D space, a network is tasked
098   to make predictions for all voxels or points, for which 3D UNets have been widely used for, e.g.,
099
      segmentation (Li et al., 2018; Atzmon et al., 2018; Hermosilla et al., 2018; Graham and van der
      Maaten, 2017; Choy et al., 2019). However, many of these networks exhibit poor scalability due to
100
      the cubic complexity of memory and compute O(N 3 ) or slow neighbor search.
101
102   Recently, decomposed representations for 3D – where multiple orthogonal 2D planes have been used
103   to reconstruct 3D representation – have gained popularity due to their efficient representation and
104   have been used in generation (Chan et al., 2022; Shue et al., 2023) and reconstruction (Chen et al.,
105   2022; Fridovich-Keil et al., 2023; Cao and Johnson, 2023). Such representation significantly reduces
106   the memory complexity of implicit neural networks on 3D continuous planes. Despite basing on
107   the decomposition of continuous planes and fitting a single neural network to a scene, this approach
      shares relevance with our factorized grid convolution approach.

      Prior works in the deep learning literature, focusing on large-scale point clouds, ranges from the use of
109   graph neural networks and point-nets to the u-shaped architectures along with advanced neighborhood
110   search (Qi et al., 2017a; Hamilton et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2019; Choy et al., 2019; Shi et al., 2020).
111   However, these methods make assumptions that may not be valid when applied to CFD problems.
112   For example, the sub-sampling approach is a prominent approach to deal with the social network,
113   classification, and segmentation to gain robustness and accuracy. However, in the automotive industry,
114   dropping points could lead to a loss of fine-details in the geometry, the vital component of fluid
115   dynamics evolution and car design. There is a need for a dedicated domain-inspired method able to
116   work directly on fine-grained car geometry with meshes composed of 100M vertices (Jacob et al.,
117
      2021), a massive size that requires a unique design and treatment.
118   2.1 FACTORIZATION
119
      Factorization of weights in neural networks has been studied to reduce the computational complexity
120
      of deep learning models Panagakis et al. (2021). It has been applied to various layers, including
121   full-connected Novikov et al. (2015), and most recently, the low-rank adaptation of transformers (Hu
122   et al., 2021), and the training of neural operators (Kossaifi et al., 2024). In the context of convolutions,
123   the use of factorization was first proposed by Rigamonti et al. (2013). This decomposition can either
124   be implicit Chollet (2017), using separable convolutions for instance (Jaderberg et al., 2014), or
125   explicit, e.g using CP (Astrid and Lee, 2017; Lebedev et al., 2015) or Tucker (Kim et al., 2016)
126   decompositions. These methods all fit within a more general framework of decomposition of the
127   kernels of the decomposition, where the full kernel is expressed in factorized form, and the full, dense
128   convolutional operation is replaced with a sequence of smaller convolutions with the factors of the
129
      decomposition (Kossaifi et al., 2020). Here, in contrast, we propose to factorize the domain, not the
      kernel, which allows us to perform parallel global convolution while remaining computationally
130
      tractable. The advantages include parallelism and better numerical stability, since we do not chain
131
      many operations. The factorization of the domain can lead to efficient computation, but the challenge
132   is to find an explicit representation of the domain (Sec. 3.2).

      4     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS AND T RAINING
325
326   We implement all baseline networks and FIG convnet using pytorch. In this section, we describe the
327   implementation details of the FIG convnet and the training procedure.
328
329   4.1   E FFICIENT R ADIUS S EARCH AND P OINT C ONVOLUTION
330   One of the most computationally intensive operation in our network is the radius search in Eq. 8 for
331   which we leverage a hash grid to accelerate the search. We first create a hash grid using Warp (Macklin,
332   2022) with the voxel size as the radius. Then, we query all 27 neighboring voxels for each point in
333   the point cloud and check if the point is within a unit sphere. For non spherical neighborhoods, we
334
      scale the point cloud by the inverse of the covariance matrix Σ and check if the point is within the
      unit cube.
335
336   We save the neighborhood indices, and number of neighbors per point in a compressed sparse row
337   matrix format (CSR) and use batched sparse matrix multiplication to perform the convolution in
338   Eq. 8. We provide a simple example of the radius search in the supplementary material.
339
340   4.2   FACTORIZED I MPLICIT G LOBAL C ONVOLUTION
341   To implement 3D global convolution using factorized representations, we use minimum three factor-
342   ized grids with one low resolution axis. We first define the maximum resolution of the voxel grid
343   that can represent the space explicitly e.g. 512 × 512 × 512. Then, we define the low resolution axis
344   as ri for each factorized grid. Note that ri can be different for each factorized grid. For example,
345   512 × 512 × 2, 512 × 3 × 512, and 4 × 512 × 512.

415   determination R2 of drag prediction. For the Ahmed body dataset, we follow the same setting as (Li

      6   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
520   In this work, we proposed a deep learning method for automotive drag coefficient prediction using a
521   network with factorized implicit global convolutions. This approach efficiently captures the global
522   context of the geometry, outperforming state-of-the-art methods on two automotive CFD datasets.
523   On the DrivAerNet dataset, our method achieved an R2 value of 0.95 for drag coefficient prediction,
524   while on the Ahmed body dataset, it attained a normalized pressure error of 0.89%.
525   However, our approach has some limitations. The FIG ConvNet directly regresses the drag coef-
526   ficient without incorporating physics-based constraints, which could lead to overfitting and poor
527   generalization to unseen data. Additionally, our method is currently limited to the automotive domain
528   with a restricted model design, potentially limiting its applicability to other fields. Looking ahead,
529   we plan to address these limitations and further improve our model. Future work will focus on
530   incorporating physics-based constraints such as Reynolds number and wall shear stress to enhance
531   generalization.

758   B    DATASETS
759
      The foundation of CFD in the automotive industry provides insight into design and engineering.
760
      Prior comprehensive texts provide a solid overview of computational methods in fluid dynamics and
761
      dedicate a comprehensive overview of traditional CFD techniques (Ferziger et al., 2019) along with
762   specification in automotive aerodynamics, also instrumental in understanding the principles (Katz,
763   2016). Solvers, such as OpenFOAM, a GPU-accelerated open-source solver, along with commercial-
764   ized licensed solvers are widely used for solving CFD equations in automotive simulations (Jasak
765   et al., 2007).
766
      Such simulations consist of two main components, i) the car designs, complex geometry often
767
      developed special software, and ii) running large scale computation to solving multivariate coupled
768   equations. Significant advancements have been enabled by the Ahmed body shape (Ahmed et al.,
769   1984), a generic car model simple enough to enable high-fidelity industry-standard simulations while
770   retaining the main features characterizing the flow of modern cars. Since, attempts have been made to
771   improve the realism of the shapes. Shape-net (Chang et al., 2015) in particular has provided a valuable
772   resource for simple car CFD simulations (Umetani and Bickel, 2018). Extending on Ahmed’s body
773   setting, the DrivAer data set introduces more complex and realistic car geometries (Heft et al., 2012),
774   with subsequent efforts, producing large-scale aerodynamics simulation on such geometries(Varney
775   et al., 2020). On such dataset, prior work attempts to predict car surface drag coefficients directly by
776
      bypassing the surface pressure prediction, pioneered by Jacob et al. (2021). However, this approach
      deploys an architecture applied to 3D voxel grids, forcing the method to scale only to low-resolution
777
      3D grids version of the data. The lack of resolution obfuscates the fine details of geometry, making
778
      the network predict the same results for cars with different information. This is in contrast to our
779   work that predicts pressure fields on large scales and detailed meshes.
780
781   Ahmed body consists of generic automotive geometries (Ahmed et al., 1984), simple enough to
782
      enable high-fidelity industry-standard simulations but retaining the main features characterizing the
      flow of modern cars. It was generated and used in the prior studies (Li et al., 2023) and contains
783
      simulations with various inlet velocities.
784
785   Ahmed body dataset, generated using vehicle aerodynamics simulation on the Ahmed body
786   shapes (Ahmed et al., 1984), consists of steady state simulation of OpenFOAM solver on 3D
787   meshes each with 10M vertices parameterized by height, width, length, ground clearance, slant angle
788
      and fillet radius. The dataset is generated and used in the prior studies (Li et al., 2023) and contains
      GPU accelerated simulations with surface mesh sizes of 100k on more than 500 car geometries, each
789
      taking 7-19 hours. We follow the same setting of this study using 10% of shapes testing. The dataset
790
      is proprietary from NVIDIA Corp. Following this work, both of the deployed datasets are in the
791   process of being made publicly available for further research.

       B.5 WARP - BASED R ADIUS S EARCH
1043
1044   Algorithm 1 describes how we efficiently find the input points within the radius of a query point
1045
       in parallel. It follows the common three step computational pattern in GPU computing when
       encountering dynamic number of results: Count, Exclusive Sum and Allocate, and Fill. We achieve
1046
       excellent performance by leveraging NVIDIA’s Warp Python framework, which compiles to native
1047
       CUDA and provides spatially efficient point queries with its hash grid primitive.
1048
1049   Procedure 1 GPU-accelerated points in a radius search
1050
1051
       Input: input points p, query points q, radius r
       Output: Results Array, Result Offset
1052
         procedure C OUNT R ADIUS R ESULTS(query points, input points, radius r)
1053
            Step 1: Count number of results
1054        for all query points q do
1055            while candidate p ← hash-grid query(q,r) do
1056                 if ∥q − p∥ < radius then count[q]++
1057                 end if
1058            end while
1059        end for
1060     end procedure
1061     procedure C OMPUTE O FFSET(count)
1062
            offset ← exclusive-sum(count)
            total number results ← offset[last]
1063
            results-array ← alloc(total number results)
1064
         end procedure
1065     procedure F ILL R ADIUS R ESULTS(query points, input points, radius r, offset)
1066        for all query points q do
1067            q-count ← 0
1068            while candidate p ← hash-grid query(q,r) do
1069                 if ∥q − p∥ < radius then
1070                     results-array[offset[q-count]] ← p
1071                     q-count++
1072                 end if
1073
                end while
            end for
1074
         end procedure
1075
         procedure P OINTS I N R ADIUS(input points, query points, radius)
1076        count ← C OUNT R ADIUS R ESULTS(query points, input points, radius)
1077        offset, allocated results array ← C OMPUTE O FFSET(count)
1078        results array ← F ILL R ADIUS R ESULTS(query points, input points, radius, offset, results array)
1079     end procedure
