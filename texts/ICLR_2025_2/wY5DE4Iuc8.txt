011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Single-cell spatial transcriptomics (scST) technologies have revolutionized our
014            understanding of the complex three-dimensional cellular landscapes of tissues.
015            However, the accuracy of spatial expression profiles is often compromised by
016            missing or distorted experimental data. To address this challenge, we introduce
017
               INRscrecon, a novel framework that leverages Implicit Neural Representations
               (INRs) known for their continuous signal encoding capabilities. INRscrecon ac-
018
               curately predicts and corrects spatial expressions, enhancing the clarity of 3D tis-
019
               sue reconstructions. Our study demonstrates the efficacy of INRscrecon across
020            various datasets and dimensions, highlighting its potential to restore spatial ex-
021            pression with high precision. The findings suggest broader applications for INR-
022            based methodologies in spatial transcriptomics, paving the way for more accurate
023            and detailed analysis of cellular interactions within tissues. Future research may
024            expand on the incorporation of INR techniques in spatial transcriptomics to further
025            enhance analytical capabilities.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Tissues consist of different cells arranged into specific functional areas, each with unique cell
031   types and diverse gene expression profiles(Wang et al., 2022a; Rao et al., 2021; Moses & Pachter,
032   2022). Single-cell spatial transcriptomics (scST), named ‘Method of the Year’ by Nature Methods
033   in 2020(Marx, 2021), enables gene expression measurement while preserving the spatial location in-
034
      formation at sites like spots, beads, or peaks. Figure 1 displays the various technologies available in
      scST. This technology enables the measurement of gene expression within the native tissue context,
035
      allowing researchers to characterize patterns of spatial gene expression, investigate cell-cell com-
036
      munication, and elucidate the spatiotemporal dynamics of cellular development(Tian et al., 2023;
037   Burgess, 2019; Bressan et al., 2023; Moffitt et al., 2022b).
038   However, accurate profile scST in 3D tissue faces numerous technical challenges. These include
039   cross-contamination of transcripts or data loss, which typically result in a significant number of out-
040   liers in the data(Zeira et al., 2022a; Walker & Nie, 2023; Atta & Fan, 2021). Additionally, each
041   spatial location may contain tens of thousands of dimensions of gene expression data. Coupled
042   with the extreme sparsity of measurement points, low spatial resolution, and overall poor data qual-
043   ity, these factors collectively obscure the continuous expression patterns of many genes in space.
044   This severely affects the accurate retrieval of expression values, thereby having a major impact on
045
      the construction and analysis of 3D tissue atlases(Velten et al., 2022; Townes & Engelhardt, 2023).
      Therefore, designing algorithms to restore the high-dimensional gene expression information in
046
      space and recover the spatial continuity of gene expression has become a crucial challenge in solv-
047
      ing problems in spatial transcriptomics.
048   Leveraging the potential of Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) to fit continuous functions known as
049   Implicit Neural Representations (INRs), combined with the theory of continuous gene expression,
050   we developed the INRscrecon specifically for reconstructing spatial transcriptomics data in 3D(Xie
051   et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2021; Darmon et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2020). This method encodes gene
052   expression in space through MLP-parameterized continuous functions, thereby decoupling the mem-
053   ory cost from the actual spatial resolution and allowing the reconstruction of expression at any de-
      sired resolution using a fixed number of parameters. The main features of this algorithm include: (i)

      2.1   3D MOLECULAR PROFILE METHODS OF SPATIAL TRANSCRIPTOMICS
089
090
      Spatial profiling in 3D presents a significant challenge for scST. Firstly, accurately aligning multiple
091   tissue slices is a significant challenge, often involving the use of thin tissue sections to generate spa-
092   tial molecular data. The insights derived from 2D spatial profiling are incomplete, offering merely a
093   snapshot that captures only a fragment of the complex spatial context of tissue structures. Secondly,
094   building sufficient resolution is constrained by the limited spatial resolution available, which hinders
095   our ability to deeply characterize intricate tissue architectures(Xiao et al., 2024; Moffitt et al., 2022a;
096   Coleman et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024).
097   To achieve precise alignment of high-resolution single-cell data across sequential slides, several
098   advanced methods have been employed: GPSA(Jones et al., 2023), a Bayesian model, aligns
099
      spatial genomic and histology samples, compensating for distortions or system differences with
      unique warping functions for each slice. STalign(Clifton et al., 2023) builds on advancements in
100
      Large Deformation Diffeomorphic Metric Mapping (LDDMM) using image varifolds to align ST
101
      datasets. PASTE(Zeira et al., 2022b) uses probabilistic pairwise alignments and fused Gromov-
102   Wasserstein optimal transport to enhance accuracy based on transcriptional and spatial similari-
103   ties. ST-GEARS(Xia et al., 2024) advances this approach by incorporating bi-sectional applica-
104   tions to improve predictive accuracy. Graph neural networks like STAGATE(Dong & Zhang, 2022),
105   STAIR(Yu & Xie, 2024), and Stitch3D(Wang et al., 2023) are increasingly used to enhance align-
106   ment and predictive accuracy in spatial transcriptomics. Additionally, integrating various multi-
107   omics data or multiple scST technology can enhance resolution and alignment efficiency for con-
      structing 3D tissue profiles. soScope(Li et al., 2024a) leverages a multimodal deep learning frame-

108
      work to integrate spot omics profiles, spatial relationships, and high-resolution morphological im-
109   ages, jointly inferring omics profiles at subspot resolution. This model effectively reduces variation
110   across datasets by selecting omics-specific distributions. SLAT(Xia et al., 2023) can align hetero-
111   geneous spatial data across different technologies and modalities. FuseMap(He et al., 2024) com-
112   bines various spatial transcriptomics technologies using a self-supervised learning approach to gen-
113   erate universal representations of genes, cells, and tissues, thereby eliminating the need for existing
114   cell annotations. SPACEL(Xu et al., 2023) is a deep-learning toolkit with three modules—Spoint,
115   Splane, and Scube—that collectively enable the stacking of multiple consecutive slices to construct
116   a comprehensive 3D architecture of tissue.
117
      In reconstructing 3D tissue structures, current methods typically use discrete representations and of-
      ten fail to predict missing sections between tissue samples. Most models overlook these gaps, except
118
      for the STAGE(Li et al., 2024b) model, which tries to predict these intermediate areas. However,
119
      INRs offer a promising solution due to their inherent continuity, enabling them to fill in missing parts
120   in spatial transcriptomics data effectively. This capability provides a novel and practical approach
121   for improving tissue reconstruction in research.
122   2.2 I MPLICIT N EURAL R EPRESENTATION IN BIOLOGY
123
124   Implicit Neural Representations (INRs), powered by deep learning algorithms, encode signals within
125   their weights, facilitating the transformation of discrete representations into a continuous spatial do-
126
      main. This capability greatly enhances their applicability across various biomedical tasks, thereby
      broadening researchers’ understanding of the significant functionalities and operations of these mod-
127
      els(Chen et al., 2023). Initially, Molaei et al. (2023a) offers a comprehensive summary of INR ap-
128
      plications in the medical field. For instance, the NeRP(Shen et al., 2022) framework proposes inte-
129   grating implicit neural networks to reconstruct sparsely sampled medical images across three stages
130   without requiring any training data. Another approach, DCTR(Fei et al., 2023), uses computed to-
131   mography (4D-CT) to reconstruct dynamic, time-varying scenes by employing INRs to estimate a
132   template reconstruction of the 3D volume’s linear attenuation coefficients (LACs), serving as a prior
133   model that captures the spatial distribution of LACs. The mirnf(Molaei et al., 2023b) model can
134   simulate both displacement vector fields and velocity vector fields, offering two distinct methods for
135   image registration: displacement vector fields for deformable registration and velocity vector fields
136   for diffeomorphic registration, both utilizing INRs to model transformations between the target and
137
      moving images. Additionally, TiAVox(Zhou et al., 2023) is a method for reconstructing 4D digital
      subtraction angiography (4D DSA) from sparse views based on time-aware attenuated voxels, reduc-
138
      ing radiation dosage while achieving high-quality 4D imaging, capable of generating both 2D and
139
      3D DSA images. These methods leverage the continuous implicit fields of INRs for high-definition
140   alignment and reconstruction. Furthermore, target-data-specific approaches like INR have shown
141   promise in effectively compressing diverse visual data. TINC(Yang, 2023), for instance, uses MLPs
142   to fit segmented local regions arranged in a tree structure to enable parameter sharing based on spa-
143   tial distance. Experiments on the HiP-CT dataset demonstrate TINC’s superiority over conventional
144   techniques, although it has slower compression speeds, its decompression speed is high.
145
      However, these approaches often overlook micro-scale biological elements such as protein structure
146   reconstruction. Recognizing the potential of INRs for both macro and micro-biological applications,
147   Cai et al. (2024) highlighted advancements with CryoDRGN(Zhong et al., 2021), which marks a
148   significant breakthrough in cryo-EM data processing and 3D structural reconstruction. CryoDRGN
149   employs a INR-based deep learning architecture to facilitate ab initio unsupervised reconstruction of
150   continuous distribution 3D volumes directly from unlabeled 2D images. Additionally, CryoDRGN-
151   ET(Rangan et al., 2024) was developed to visualize the structures of dynamic macromolecules in
152   native cellular environments. While cryo-ET can achieve molecular resolution, its image processing
153   algorithms are further enhanced by INR capabilities. Given the substantial demand for alignment
154
      and reconstruction in spatial transcriptomics, INRs possess significant potential to revolutionize this
      field.

158   We propose an method named INRscrecon (Figure 2), which consists of two main components: the
159   Alignment component and the Imputation component. The Alignment component can be regarded
160   as a data preprocessing step, where two sets of single-cell coordinates are aligned pairwise. The Im-
161   putation component focuses on learning the mapping between cell coordinates and gene expression
      profiles, subsequently imputing a missing slide based on the learned spatial patterns.

185   Thus, the transformation can be expressed as Φ(x) = Rx + t. Our objective is to find the optimal

304   To evaluate the effectiveness of INRscrecon in reconstructing spatial transcriptomic data, we con-
305   ducted experiments on multiple publicly available datasets, each representing diverse biological
306   contexts and technologies:

321   Drosophila Embryo (DE) Dataset. This dataset includes 12 slices of a Drosophila embryo at
322   the 16–18 hour developmental stage, captured with spatial transcriptomic techniques (Wang et al.,

      spatial continuity across developmental stages.

461   5    D ISSCUSION
462
463   Our study demonstrates the robust capabilities of the INRscrecon model within spatial transcrip-
464   tomics, revealing its significant potential for biological microscale applications. The superior per-
465   formance of INRscrecon across various datasets confirms its effectiveness in accurately reconstruct-
466   ing complex 3D gene profiles. This precision is critical not only for advancing our understanding of
467   cellular architectures but also for facilitating detailed exploration of spatial and genetic interactions
468   within tissues.
469   The transition from two-dimensional to three-dimensional spatial transcriptomics has been greatly
470   enhanced by computational techniques like Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2021)
471   and 3D Gaussian Splatting (GS)(Kerbl et al., 2023), which have proven essential in reconstructing
472   intricate biological structures at the microscale. These advancements contribute profoundly to our
473   capacity to visualize and analyze the spatial organization of cells and their gene expression in un-
474   precedented detail.
475   Looking forward, the integration of INRs with these advanced computational models opens new av-
476   enues for precision medicine. By improving the granularity and accuracy of spatial transcriptomics,
477   we can better align technological progress with clinical needs, particularly in the development and
478   optimization of personalized treatment strategies. The potential to tailor therapeutic approaches
479   based on detailed 3D mappings of tissue architecture promises a new era of treatment customiza-
480   tion, enhancing patient outcomes in complex diseases.
481   As research delves deeper into the microscopic aspects of biological phenomena, the ongoing refine-
482   ment of INRs and related technologies will play a crucial role in the future of biomedical research,
483   offering new insights and tools for scientists and clinicians alike. The application of these sophis-
484   ticated computational tools in routine clinical practice could revolutionize how we understand and
485   treat various diseases, marking a significant step forward in the integration of bioinformatics and
      healthcare.
