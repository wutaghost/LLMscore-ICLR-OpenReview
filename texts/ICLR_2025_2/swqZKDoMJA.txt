009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We focus on reducing communication overhead in multiplayer games, where fre-
012            quently exchanging strategies between players is not feasible and players have
013            noisy or outdated strategies of the other players. We propose Decoupled SGDA, an
014            extension of Stochastic Gradient Descent Ascent (SGDA), where players perform
015            independent updates using outdated strategies of opponents, with periodic strat-
016            egy synchronization. For Strongly-Convex-Strongly-Concave (SCSC) games, we
017
               demonstrate that Decoupled SGDA achieves near-optimal communication com-
               plexity comparable to the best-known GDA rates. For weakly coupled games
018
               where the interaction between players is lower relative to non-interactive part of
019
               the game, Decoupled SGDA significantly reduces communication costs compared
020            to standard SGDA. Our findings extend to multi-player games. To provide insights
021            into the effect of communication frequency and convergence, we extensively study
022            the convergence of Decoupled SGDA for quadratic minimax problems. Lastly, in
023            settings where the noise over the players is imbalanced, Decoupled SGDA signif-
024            icantly outperforms federated minimax methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Several real-world problems in diverse areas, such as economics and computer science, can fre-
      quently be described as N -player differentiable games (Von Neumann & Morgenstern, 2007). While
030
      players may have competing objectives, the aim is to identify an equilibrium, a strategy where no
031
      player benefits from deviating unilaterally. Examples of such games in machine learning include
032   Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs, Goodfellow et al., 2014), adversarial robustness (Madry
033   et al., 2017; Shafahi et al., 2019; Robey et al., 2023) and multi-agent reinforcement learning (e.g.,
034   Lowe et al., 2017; Li et al., 2019).
035
      Minimax optimization problems are a special case of N -player games, where the aim is to find a
036
      saddle point of an objective f pu, vq that minimizes f pu, ¨q over u and maximizes f p¨, vq over v.
037
038   Several gradient-based methods have been proposed for solving the above problem (Korpelevich,
039   1976; Popov, 1980; Balduzzi et al., 2018; Nouiehed et al., 2019; Chavdarova et al., 2020; Kovalev
040   & Gasnikov, 2022). One of the most widely used is the gradient descent method. In the context of
041
      zero-sum minimax games, this approach is referred to as Gradient Descent Ascent (GDA), where
      the minimizing player takes descent steps and the maximizing player takes ascent steps.
042
043   In some situations, however, players may not have direct access to their opponents’ exact strate-
044   gies. The u–player might only have a noisy estimate of v when updating its parameters, and vice
045   versa. In extreme cases, players might operate with outdated strategies from their opponents, with
046   limited opportunities to synchronize. We refer to this scenario as games with intermittent strategy
047
      communication (ISC-games). Here are a few illustrative examples:
048   • Corporate competitors. Companies frequently adjust their strategies based on individual objec-
049     tives and the strategies of their competitors. For instance, Netflix may need to lower its prices if
050     a competitor like Max reduces its subscription rates (Jagadeesan et al., 2022). Corporations may
051     occasionally release (noisy) general information about their strategies, giving each company an
052     imperfect understanding of its competitor’s actions. Alternatively, companies might hire experts
053     to estimate competitor strategies using publicly available data, although this process is expensive
        and infrequent.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      • N -agents with restricted communication. In control theory, applications involving drones or
055     robots are modeled with N -player games (see Spica et al., 2020; Laine et al., 2021; Zhou et al.,
056     2021, and references therein). However, due to factors like long distances or limited battery life
057     caused by weight constraints, communication between agents regarding learned strategies is costly
058     and can only occur intermittently.
059
060   In summary, this paper focuses on the following questions.
061
      • Can players learn “locally” when relying on noisy or outdated strategies from their opponents in
062     ISC-games?
063   • How do the convergence rate and communication costs of the proposed optimization method com-
064     pare to the baseline? Is acceleration achievable?
065
066   To address the former, we propose a simple extension of the gradient descent method where agents
067   perform local updates while using outdated strategies from their opponents. For minimax prob-
068
      lems, we refer to this method as Decoupled SGDA, and for N -player games Decoupled SGD. The
      second question is explored in detail by analyzing the convergence rate of Decoupled SGD(A) and
069
      identifying the problem class where acceleration is achieved, which we refer to as Weakly Coupled
070
      Games.
071
072   Contributions. Our contributions include:
073
074   • We introduce Decoupled SGD(A) for games with intermittent strategy communication, where
075     each player performs K updates based on outdated strategies from the other players, followed by
076     a synchronization step to exchange the updated strategies of all players.
077   • We analyze its convergence in both the strongly-convex strongly-concave (SCSC) setting and in
078     N -player games where each player’s utility is strongly convex. Additionally, we identify a specific
079     regime, termed Weakly Coupled Games, where Decoupled SGD(A) demonstrates communication
080     acceleration compared to the baseline GD(A), by removing the dependency on player condition-
081     ing. Moreover, under an additional assumption on the interactive part of the game, our method can
082
        outperform the optimal first-order method in terms of communication rounds for solving SCSC
        games.
083
084   • We study the convergence of Decoupled SGDA for quadratic minimax games with bilinear cou-
085
        pling between the players, providing in-depth insights into the algorithm’s convergence behavior.
        We provide a convergence guarantee for Federated Minimax games in the context of our method,
086
        matching the state-of-the-art results of Local SGDA.
087
088   • Through numerical experiments, we demonstrate the efficacy of Decoupled SGDA (i) in minimax
089
        optimization and non-convex GAN settings in weakly coupled games, and (ii) over federated
        learning in settings where opponents have gradients with imbalanced noise.
090
091   • We also propose a heuristic to accelerate the convergence of decoupled SGDA in Appendix G and
092
        present numerical evidence demonstrating its practical effectiveness.
093   To simplify the exposition, the main body of the paper focuses on the minimax setting, while the
094   extension to N -player games is presented in Appendix C.

470   Decoupled SGDA with gradient approximation Herein, we compare Decoupled SGDA with
471
      Federated Minimax, aka (local-SGDA). We focus on environments with gradient oracles with un-
      balanced noise. Each player has access to a gradient oracle that provides low-variance noise for
472
      their own gradients but high-variance noise for the remaining players. This reflects real-world chal-
473
      lenges where shared information can be unreliable. In such cases, it is more effective to wait for
474   synchronization for accurate updates, and between these periods, players should update only their
475   own parameters. In particular, we focus on a modified quadratic game (QG) we used earlier, with
476   each oracle adding zero-mean Gaussian noise to the full gradient. The variance of noise differs
477   between gradients related to a player’s own strategy (diagonal variance) and those related to other
478   players’ strategies (off-diagonal variance). Refer to Assumption 8 for a more rigorous definition.
479   In both experiments, diagonal variance was fixed at 0.1, while off-diagonal variance was linearly
480   increased from 1 to 10 in the second experiment.

      6   C ONCLUSION
524
525
      We proposed Decoupled SGDA as an effective optimization method for games with intermittent
526
      strategy communication, particularly in scenarios where interaction between players is weak, or
527
      noise levels are high. Through extensive theoretical and empirical analysis, we demonstrate that
528   Decoupled SGDA not only outperforms traditional methods like Local SGDA in terms of communi-
529   cation efficiency and robustness in weakly coupled games but also extends its benefits beyond SCSC
530   games to non-convex settings. The method’s ability to handle varying levels of interaction and noise
531   makes it highly adaptable, providing a valuable tool for federated and decentralized optimization
532   problems.
533
      Several future directions are possible. One can consider varying K per player, extensions of other
534   game optimization methods, such as extragradient, for classes beyond players having strongly con-
535   vex utilities, among others. In addition, the proposed approach has the potential to address privacy-
536   sensitive scenarios, as players can update their parameters independently without needing direct
537   access to others’ parameters, minimizing privacy risks posed by gradient sharing (see Zhu et al.,
538   2019; Zhao et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2020, and references therein). Future work could further ex-
539   plore this potential in privacy-preserving applications, making Decoupled SGDA a valuable tool for
      decentralized optimization under privacy constraints.

1749   E.1   D ECENTRALIZED OPTIMIZATION
1750
1751
       The key difference between decentralized and distributed minimax approaches is the presence of a
       central server. In the former, there is no central server, and nodes communicate directly with their
1752
       neighbors, whereas in the latter, a central server aggregates the parameters. Our method belongs
1753
       to the category of distributed methods. However, we will discuss later on that our approach is
1754   completely different from the general idea of distributed / federated optimization.
1755
1756   Decentralized optimization is widely studied for the case of minimization (Xiao & Boyd, 2004;
1757
       Tsitsiklis, 1984) with the goal of not relying on a central node or server. This idea is also applied
       to the case of minimax optimization problems. The paper Liu et al. (2020) is the first who studied
1758
       non-convex-non-concave decentralized minimax. They also used the idea of optimistic gradient
1759
       descent and achieved a rate of Opϵ´12 q. In Xian et al. (2021), authors proposed an algorithm called
1760   DM-HSGD for non-convex decentralized minimax by utilizing variance reduction and achieved a
1761   rate of Opκ3 ϵ´3 q. Recently, authors in Liu et al. (2023) proposed an algorithm named Precision for
1762   the non-convex-strongly-concave objectives which has a two-stage local updates and gives a rate of
1763   Op T1 q.

       Federated learning (FL) builds on the foundational work in distributed minimization, exploring var-
       ious settings. In the context of minimax optimization, methods like Local SGD have been extended
1843
       to achieve convergence rates for different classes of functions in both heterogeneous and homoge-
1844
       neous regimes. FL methods for games differ from the setting considered in this work. In FL, multiple
1845   copies of all strategies (parameters) are trained locally on different machines and datasets and pe-
1846   riodically aggregated. FL is suited for scenarios where a single local machine runs a multi-player
1847   algorithm and has access to all players’ loss functions, with ”collaboration” built into the design.
1848   In contrast, our method suits competitive distributed players (local machines) where each player
1849   has noisy or outdated strategies of the remaining players. For further discussion, see Appendix E.
1850   Additionally, federated learning assumes balanced noise across players, which is not required in our
1851   setting; revisited in § 2 and § 5. Finally, in § 4, we identify a class of games where our approach
1852   leads to faster convergence, even if fully centralized training is possible, which class similarly arises
1853
       in non-convex settings–§ 5.In the rest of this section, we study Federated Decoupled SGDA, which
       is a combination of Federated Minimax and Decoupled SGDA algorithms, and can benefit from the
1854
       advantages of both approaches. In the next section we propose this method with details.

       In this section, we introduce a new extension to the Decoupled SGDA algorithm called Ghost Se-
2259
       quence. The base Decoupled SGDA algorithm, explained earlier, is designed to take advantage
2260
       of problems with a dominant separable component. It minimizes communication complexity by
2261   reusing outdated strategies, which has already been analyzed theoretically in the prevous sections.
2262
2263   However, we can push this idea further by not just reusing old strategies but also predicting the
2264
       opponent’s next move. This smarter approach opens up a new line of research, where more advanced
       methods can be explored for estimating the opponent’s strategy, offering directions for future work.
2265
2266   To demonstrate the potential of this approach, we propose Decoupled SGDA with Ghost Sequence.
2267   The main idea is for each player to predict (or approximate) the next move of the opponent based
       on their previous actions and behaviour. This is achieved by computing the difference between


                                                                  42
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2268
       successive strategies during synchronization. Using this information, each player can update both
2269   their own and their opponent’s parameters, leading to improved performance. As shown in Figure
2270   8, Decoupled SGDA with Ghost Sequence can greatly improve the algorithm’s performance. It also
2271   achieves faster communication, even in highly interactive games, and does not require the problem
2272   to be weakly coupled.
