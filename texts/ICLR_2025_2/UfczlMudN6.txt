009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The reliable deployment of deep reinforcement learning in real-world settings
012            requires the ability to generalize across a variety of conditions, including both
013            in-distribution scenarios seen during training as well as novel out-of-distribution
014            scenarios. In this work, we present a framework for dynamics generalization in
015            deep reinforcement learning that unifies these two distinct types of generaliza-
016            tion within a single architecture. We introduce a robust adaptation module that
017            provides a mechanism for identifying and reacting to both in-distribution and out-
018
               of-distribution environment dynamics, along with a joint training pipeline that
               combines the goals of in-distribution adaptation and out-of-distribution robust-
019
               ness. Our algorithm GRAM achieves strong generalization performance across in-
020
               distribution and out-of-distribution scenarios upon deployment, which we demon-
021            strate on a variety of realistic simulated locomotion tasks with a quadruped robot.
022

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Due to the diverse and uncertain nature of real-world settings, generalization is an important ca-
027   pability for the reliable deployment of data-driven, learning-based frameworks such as deep rein-
028   forcement learning (RL). Policies trained with deep RL must be capable of generalizing to a variety
029   of different environment dynamics at deployment time, including both familiar training conditions
030   and novel unseen scenarios, as the complex nature of real-world environments makes it difficult to
031   capture all possible variations in the training process.
032   Existing approaches to zero-shot dynamics generalization in deep RL have focused on two com-
033   plementary concepts: adaptation and robustness. Contextual RL techniques (Hallak et al., 2015)
034   learn to identify and adapt to the current environment dynamics to achieve the best performance,
035   but this adaptation is only reliable for the range of in-distribution (ID) scenarios seen during train-
036   ing. Robust RL methods (Nilim & Ghaoui, 2005; Iyengar, 2005), on the other hand, maximize the
037   worst-case performance across a range of possible environment dynamics, providing generalization
038   to out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios at the cost of conservative performance in ID environments.
039   This work shows how to extract the benefits of these complementary approaches in a unified frame-
040   work called GRAM: Generalization in deep RL with a Robust Adaptation Module. GRAM achieves
041   generalization to both ID and OOD environment dynamics at deployment time within a single ar-
042   chitecture. Our main contributions are as follows:
043
044         1. We introduce a robust adaptation module in Section 4 that provides a mechanism for iden-
045            tifying both ID and OOD environment dynamics within the same architecture. We extend
046
               existing contextual RL approaches by using an epistemic neural network (Osband et al.,
               2023) to incorporate a measure of uncertainty about the environment at deployment time.
047
048         2. We propose a joint training pipeline in Section 5 that combines a teacher-student archi-
049            tecture for learning adaptive ID performance with adversarial RL training for robust OOD
050            performance, resulting in a single unified policy that can achieve both ID and OOD dynam-
051
               ics generalization.
052         3. We demonstrate the strong ID and OOD performance of GRAM in Section 7 through
053            comprehensive experiments on realistic simulated locomotion tasks with the Unitree Go2
               quadruped robot in Isaac Lab (Mittal et al., 2023).

      2   R ELATED WORK
055
056
      Zero-shot generalization in deep RL has received significant attention in recent years (see Kirk
057   et al. 2023 for a survey). The most common formulation of this problem is contextual RL (Hallak
058   et al., 2015), and many studies have demonstrated the importance of leveraging contextual infor-
059   mation in deep RL to improve generalization (e.g., Benjamins et al. 2023). For the case of known
060   contexts, both model-free (Beukman et al., 2023) and model-based (Prasanna et al., 2024) archi-
061   tectures have been proposed to effectively incorporate context into training. When the context is
062   unknown at deployment time, it must be inferred from past observations. Some domain random-
063   ization methods directly consider a policy conditioned on history (Peng et al., 2018; Tiboni et al.,
064   2024), while self-supervised approaches infer context from history through the use of variational
065
      inference (Yang et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2022; Ren et al., 2022), dynamics prediction (Lee et al.,
      2020b), or separation-based objectives (Luo et al., 2022). Similar techniques have also been ap-
066
      plied in the related area of meta RL (Nagabandi et al., 2019; Rakelly et al., 2019; Zintgraf et al.,
067
      2020), which require additional learning in the deployment environment for adaptation. Supervised
068   approaches, on the other hand, leverage privileged context information during training, and apply
069   a teacher-student architecture to train a policy that can be deployed using only the history of past
070   observations (Lee et al., 2020a; Kumar et al., 2021; Margolis et al., 2024). All of these methods
071   are designed to adapt across the range of ID contexts seen during training, but are not specifically
072   trained to handle OOD contexts with different environment dynamics. As a result, their ability to
073   generalize is sensitive to the distribution of ID training contexts, and they may not generalize well
074   to OOD contexts.
075   Robust RL focuses on generalizing to OOD environments at deployment time by maximizing worst-
076   case performance over a set of transition models (Nilim & Ghaoui, 2005; Iyengar, 2005). Deep RL
077   methods most commonly apply robustness during training through the use of parametric uncertainty
078   or adversarial training. Parametric uncertainty methods consider a range of simulation parameters
079   during training, and optimize for worst-case performance across these environments (Rajeswaran
080   et al., 2017; Mankowitz et al., 2020). Adversarial RL instead applies worst-case perturbations dur-
081   ing training to provide robustness to unknown dynamics or disturbances at deployment time. Various
082
      types of adversarial interventions have been considered, including external forces (Pinto et al. 2017;
      Reddi et al. 2024; Xiao et al. 2024) as well as perturbations to actions (Tessler et al., 2019), obser-
083
      vations (Zhang et al., 2020), and transitions (Queeney & Benosman, 2023; Queeney et al., 2024).
084
      Robust RL methods have also been applied to capture estimation uncertainty (Xie et al., 2022) and
085   context shifts (Lin et al., 2020; Ajay et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023) in contextual RL and meta RL.
086   These approaches provide robust generalization to environment dynamics that were not explicitly
087   seen during training, but often sacrifice ID performance to achieve robustness.
088
      In this work, we are interested in both ID and OOD generalization. The possibility of different modes
089
      at deployment is related to methods that train a collection of policies to select from at deployment
090
      time. Many learning-based approaches to safety switch between a task-based policy and a recovery
091   policy in order to guarantee safety at deployment time (Thananjeyan et al., 2021; Wagener et al.,
092   2021; Contreras et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Sinha et al., 2024). Other methods select between
093   a finite collection of different behaviors based on the deployment environment (Ajay et al., 2022;
094   Chen et al., 2023). Contextual RL can also be viewed as learning a collection of policies for ID
095   adaptation, and our robust adaptation module extends this approach to incorporate a mode for OOD
096   generalization as well.

      Contextual RL We model the problem of dynamics generalization in deep RL as a Contextual
101   Markov Decision Process (CMDP) (Hallak et al., 2015). A CMDP considers a set of contexts C
102   that define a collection of MDPs {Mc }c∈C . For each c ∈ C, we have an MDP given by the tuple
103   Mc = (S, A, pc , r, ρ0 , γ), where S is the set of states, A is the set of actions, pc : S ×A×C → P (S)
104   is the context-dependent transition model where P (S) represents the space of probability measures
105   over S, r : S × A → R is the reward function, ρ0 is the initial state distribution, and γ is the
106   discount rate. We focus on the setting where the transition model pc depends on the context c ∈ C
107   (i.e., varying dynamics), while the reward function r remains the same across contexts (i.e., same
      task). For a policy π and context c ∈ C, performance is given by the expected total discounted

115   Problem statement We assume that the context is available as privileged information during train-

      4    ROBUST ADAPTATION MODULE
132
      We build upon the teacher-student architecture for adaptation in deep RL, which has demonstrated
133
      strong performance in complex robotics applications (Lee et al., 2020a; Kumar et al., 2021; Margolis
134
      et al., 2024). However, because this approach focuses on adaptation across ID contexts observed
135   during training, its OOD generalization capabilities depend strongly on the relationship between
136   CID and COOD . For OOD contexts with environment dynamics that are not similar to ID training
137   contexts, we show in our experiments that the standard teacher-student architecture can perform
138   poorly. In order to generalize to both ID and OOD contexts at deployment time, we introduce a
139   robust adaptation module that explicitly incorporates a mechanism for identifying and reacting to
140   OOD contexts.
141
142   Teacher-student training The teacher-student approach to generalization in deep RL assumes

145   a context encoder f : C → Z that maps the context to a latent feature zt = f (ct ), which is then
146
      provided as an input to the policy π : S × Z → P (A) and critic V π : S × Z → R. In this work, we
      consider the latent feature space Z = Rd . The context encoding zt = f (ct ), policy π(at | st , zt ),

253   within the same architecture. The use of a single architecture also allows for implicit regularization
254   benefits of this robust training beyond zrob . In the next section, we provide details on the robust RL
255   techniques that we use during training to achieve OOD generalization.

      5   T RAINING FOR ROBUST ADAPTATION
258
259
      Our robust adaptation module provides an intuitive structure for achieving both ID and OOD dy-
260
      namics generalization within a single architecture. In order to accomplish this goal, we jointly train
261   our policy π(at | st , zt ) for adaptive performance in ID environments and robust performance in
262   OOD environments (i.e., when zt = zrob ). For a given iteration of RL training, we assign each train-
263   ing environment to either ID training or OOD training. This assignment determines how the latent
264   feature vector is calculated, as well as how data collection occurs in the environment. See Figure 2
265   for an overview.
266
267   Data collection Within each iteration, all data for a given training environment is collected ac-
268   cording to either standard ID data collection or adversarial OOD data collection, as described in
269   the following paragraphs. This provides temporal consistency when training the policy for adaptive
      or robust performance, respectively. However, we alternate these assignments between iterations,

271   iments, this mixed data collection design provides additional robustness benefits compared to using
272   the same training assignment for entire trajectories.

275   collection and training updates as the standard teacher-student architecture. We calculate the privi-

      provided to the policy as input.

313   There are several key factors that allow GRAM to achieve strong performance in both ID and OOD

343   7                 E XPERIMENTS
344
345   We evaluate the performance of GRAM on realistic simulated locomotion tasks with the Unitree
346   Go2 quadruped robot in Isaac Lab (Mittal et al., 2023). The goal of the robot is to track a velocity
347   command vtcmd = [vxcmd , vycmd , ωzcmd ] ∈ R3 provided as input, where vxcmd , vycmd represent target
348   forward and lateral linear velocities, respectively, and ωzcmd represents a target yaw angular velocity.
349   For each episode, we uniformly sample a forward linear velocity command between 0.5 and 1.0
350   meters per second (i.e., vxcmd ∼ U([0.5, 1.0]), vycmd = 0) and calculate the yaw angular velocity
351   command ωzcmd throughout the episode based on a target heading direction. The simulated quadruped
352   robot can be seen in Figure 2, with the velocity command represented by a green arrow.
353
      The policy has access to noisy proprioceptive observations available from standard onboard sensors
354
      at every timestep (joint angles, joint velocities, projected gravity, and base angular velocities), and
355
      outputs target joint angles at ∈ R12 for each of the robot’s 12 degrees of freedom that are converted
356   to torques by a PD controller with proportional gain Kp = 25 and derivative gain Kd = 0.5.
357   The maximum episode length is 20 seconds with target joint angles processed at 50 Hz, which
358   corresponds to 1,000 timesteps per episode. We consider the reward function used in Margolis et al.
359   (2024), which includes rewards for tracking the velocity command vtcmd and regularization terms to
360   promote smooth and stable gaits. See the Appendix for additional details.
361
      We conduct experiments to investigate several hypotheses related to our algorithm GRAM:
362
363                    H1. Existing contextual RL and robust RL methods demonstrate trade-offs between ID and
364                        OOD performance that depend critically on the set of contexts CID seen during training.
365
366
                       H2. GRAM can achieve strong ID and OOD generalization with a single unified policy.
367                    H3. GRAM identifies ID contexts from OOD contexts at deployment time in a way that auto-
368                        matically adjusts for different choices of CID .
369
                       H4. The unified architecture and joint training pipeline of GRAM outperforms other implemen-
370
                           tation choices for achieving ID and OOD generalization.
371
372   In order to test these hypotheses, we compare GRAM to contextual RL and robust RL on two

465   Analysis of GRAM Next, we perform additional analysis to better understand how our algorithm
466   GRAM is able to achieve strong ID and OOD generalization. GRAM automatically adjusts to both
467   ID and OOD contexts at deployment time through its robust adaptation module ϕGRAM in (8), which
468   incorporates a measure of uncertainty about the deployment environment through the coefficient
469   αt ∈ [0, 1] (αt → 1 for low uncertainty and αt → 0 for high uncertainty). We plot the average
470   GRAM coefficient αt in Figure 6 for every deployment environment considered in our experiments,
471
      where we see that GRAM automatically adjusts to both the deployment environment and the set of
      ID training contexts. This provides evidence to support our third hypothesis (H3). GRAM outputs
472
      low uncertainty (αt → 1) for ID contexts seen during training, and decreases αt as OOD scenarios
473
      become increasingly novel compared to the training set. This is evident in the Base ID setting,
474   where GRAM outputs high uncertainty (small αt ) in OOD scenarios because it was only exposed
475   to a limited set of contexts during training. When trained on a diverse range of contexts in the Base
476   ID + Frozen Joints setting, on the other hand, GRAM automatically adjusts to this training set by
477   applying larger values of αt that correspond to less uncertainty.
478
      Finally, we conduct an ablation study in Figure 7 to analyze the impact of GRAM’s unified archi-
479
      tecture and joint training pipeline. We compare GRAM to a variant that applies separate ID and
480   OOD data collection to train a unified policy (instead of alternating training assignments between
481   updates), as well as a modular approach that combines separate robust and adaptive policies at de-
482   ployment time using an uncertainty threshold for switching. We see that these approaches can lead to
483   comparable or slightly improved ID performance by isolating ID and OOD training, but this comes
484   at the cost of robustness in OOD scenarios. GRAM’s single unified policy and joint training pipeline
485   provide additional robustness benefits that are difficult to achieve with more modular approaches,
      providing support for our fourth and final hypothesis (H4).

517   8                 C ONCLUSION
518
519   In this work, we have presented a deep RL framework that achieves both ID and OOD dynamics
520   generalization at deployment time within a single architecture. Our algorithm GRAM leverages
521   a robust adaptation module that allows for adaptation in ID contexts, while also identifying OOD
522   environments with a special robust latent feature zrob . We presented a training pipeline that jointly
523   trains for adaptive ID performance and robust OOD performance, resulting in strong generalization
524   capabilities across a range of realistic simulated locomotion tasks on the Unitree Go2 quadruped
525   robot in Isaac Lab. The ability to achieve ID and OOD generalization within a unified framework
526   is critical for the reliable deployment of deep RL in real-world settings, and GRAM represents an
527
      important step towards this goal.
528
529   Limitations and future work Because OOD contexts are unknown during training by definition,
530   the OOD generalization of GRAM depends on how well the robust RL training pipeline captures
531
      worst-case OOD dynamics. We applied a single choice of adversary in our experiments that worked
      well in practice, but it would be interesting to extend GRAM to incorporate different levels of ro-
532
      bustness at deployment time. There are also opportunities to apply GRAM with different choices
533
      of contextual RL and robust RL techniques, extend our robust adaptation module to incorporate
534   other methods for uncertainty quantification (Gawlikowski et al., 2023), and address other forms of
535   generalization beyond dynamics related to modalities such as vision. Finally, we focused on simu-
536   lated quadruped locomotion experiments in this work to conduct a comprehensive empirical study
537   of GRAM across a variety of settings. We plan to deploy our GRAM framework in real-world hard-
538   ware experiments on the Unitree Go2 quadruped robot as part of future work. We are also interested
539   in applying GRAM to other applications where generalization is important, such as contact-rich
      manipulation tasks and agile quadrotor flight.
