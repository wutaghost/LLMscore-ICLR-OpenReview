009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Designing RNA sequences based on RNA tertiary structures is a crucial aspect of
012            future RNA design with significant potential to aid drug development. Recently,
013            deep learning-based methods have made progress in this area; however, these
014            methods are constrained by the limited availability of RNA structural data, making
015            it challenging to achieve optimal performance. In this paper, we propose EVO-
016            RDesign, which leverages the evolutionary priors embedded in extensive sequence
017            data to facilitate better RNA sequence design. Specifically, RNA language models
018            have recently been demonstrated to learn the evolutionary information of RNA.
019            Therefore, we consider RNA language models as repositories of evolutionary pri-
020
               ors and design a series of adaptors that enable EVO-RDesign to retrieve these
               priors conditioned on the input RNA structural information. To achieve better
021
               performance, the adaptor innovatively inputs RNA structural information and out-
022
               puts from existing RNA design methods into the language model. Experiments
023            demonstrate that EVO-RDesign outperforms RDesign, achieving a 3.5% increase
024            in sequence recovery on RNAsolo. It also exhibits zero-shot generalization, with
025            gains of 5.1% and 4.1% in sequence recovery on RNA-Puzzles and Rfam, respec-
026            tively. We also apply in-silico folding to validate whether the generated sequences
027            can fold into the specified 3D RNA backbones.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      RNA is a vital biological macromolecule that plays a crucial role in various biological processes.
033
      Since functional RNA molecules are essential for regulatory processes and transcription control,
034
      many RNA-based innovations are at the forefront of biotechnology, including mRNA vaccines and
035   CRISPR-based genomic medicine. In recent years, the design of RNA molecules has been attracting
036   widespread interest in fields such as therapeutics, synthetic biology, and bioinformatics.
037
038
      The RNA sequence consists of four types of nucleotides, which undergoes a hierarchical folding
      process, as shown in Fig.1. The secondary structure of RNA is formed through hydrogen-bond
039
      patterns between the nucleotides, and these interactions are then critical in driving the folding of
040
      RNA sequence into its 3D tertiary structure.
041
042   Since the intricate and diverse functions of RNA are determined by its 3D tertiary structure, one
043   fundamental challenge of RNA design is to generate RNA sequences that can fold into the desired
044
      structure, which is known as structure-based RNA design or RNA inverse folding.
045   Recently, some works (Tan et al., 2023; Joshi et al., 2024) have attempted to use geometric deep
046   learning to address tertiary structure-based RNA design, yielding some progress. Specifically, these
047   methods utilize RNA structure-sequence pair data as the training set. This training data is used to
048   train a Graph Neural Network (GNN) (Zhou et al., 2020) that encodes RNA 3D structures, enabling
049   the GNN to predict RNA sequences based on their structures.
050   However, these methods still face significant bottlenecks: the known RNA structure data is very
051   limited. The number of available RNA structures is less than 1% of the number of protein struc-
052   tures (Adamczyk et al., 2022), primarily due to RNA’s inherent instability and rapid degradation in
053   vitro (Rother et al., 2011). This results in insufficient training data, making it challenging for deep
      learning methods to achieve satisfactory performance.

072   To alleviate this bottleneck, we propose EVO-RDesign. The high-level insight is straightforward:
073   while RNA structure data is limited, RNA sequence data is abundant; moreover, the evolutionary
074
      priors encoded within these sequence data may be leveraged to enhance RNA design performance.
      Specifically, language models trained on large-scale RNA sequence data have been shown to encode
075
      evolutionary priors (Chen et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023). Therefore, we employ an RNA language
076
      model as an evolutionary prior knowledge base, and then use the input RNA structure to conditionally
077   retrieve the corresponding priors to improve the design results.
078
079   To enable retrieval of corresponding priors based on RNA structure, we propose a framework and
080
      corresponding adapters that allow structural information to be mapped into a format acceptable by the
      language model. Furthermore, we fine-tune the language model to predict RNA sequences directly
081
      based on the input structural information. Specifically, within this framework, an input structure is first
082
      fed into an existing RNA design model, RDesign (Tan et al., 2023). Subsequently, RDesign extracts
083   structural features of the RNA and outputs sequence design results. These structural features and
084   design results are subsequently fed into a module called the Structural Adapter, which translates them
085   into representations that the RNA language model can accept. These translated representations are
086   then input into the language model to retrieve the corresponding priors. In addition to the Structural
087   Adapter module, we map the hidden features from RDesign through a linear layer into a pairwise
088   matrix, which serves as an attention bias added to the RNA language model. This integration allows
089   the language model to receive RNA structural information via the attention bias.
090   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
091
092          • We propose, for the first time, the idea of using evolutionary priors in RNA sequence data to
093            assist structure-based RNA design, addressing the issue of insufficient structural data.
094          • We introduce the EVO-RDesign framework and a series of adaptors that enable language
095            models incorporating evolutionary priors to design and refine RNA sequence results based
096            on structural information.
097          • Extensive experiments demonstrate that EVO-RDesign significantly outperforms existing
098            methods in terms of sequence recovery rate, surpassing RDesign by 3.5% on RNAsolo. In
099            terms of zero-shot generalization, EVO-RDesign exceeds RDesign by 5.1% on RNA-Puzzles
100            and 4.1% on Rfam. Additionally, EVO-RDesign exhibits strong generalization capabilities
101            across different lengths and types of RNA.

105   2.1    S TRUCTURE - BASED RNA D ESIGN
106
107   Early research in computational RNA design focused solely on secondary structure constraints.
      Pioneering efforts like RNAinverse (Hofacker et al., 1994) and RNAiFold (Garcia-Martin et al.,


                                                                     2

108
      2013) utilized energy-based local search algorithms for compatibility with these secondary structures.
109   MCTS-RNA (Yang et al., 2017) employed Monte Carlo tree search to explore viable designs.
110   Additionally, aRNAque (Merleau & Smerlak, 2022) and eM2dRNAs (Rubio-Largo et al., 2023)
111   introduced evolutionary algorithms specifically tailored for secondary structure-based RNA design.
112   Recently, the field of RNA design has greatly benefited from the emergence of data-driven paradigms
113   and geometric deep learning. LEARNA (Runge et al., 2018) attempted to tackle the challenge of
114   secondary structure-based design using reinforcement learning. However, without considering 3D
115   structures of RNA, previous methods cannot meet accurate functional structure constraints, thus
116   restricting their practical application. In response, RDesign (Tan et al., 2023) and gRNAde (Joshi
117
      et al., 2024) have been developed as geometric RNA design pipelines that specifically address the
      challenges of tertiary structure-based RNA design.

      2.2   S TRUCTURE - BASED P ROTEIN D ESIGN
121
      Similar to RNA, structure-based protein design, also known as protein inverse folding, aims to
122   generate an amino acid sequence that will fold into a given protein backbone structure (Yue & Dill,
123   1992). Since the higher-order structures of RNA and proteins can be similarly described (Rother et al.,
124   2011), the field of protein design has exhibited a progression similar to that of RNA design. Originally,
125   structure-based protein design was tackled by physics-based energy minimization (Street & Mayo,
126   1999; Alford et al., 2017). Recently, it has benefited from the advantages of deep learning (Ingraham
127   et al., 2019a; Hsu et al., 2022), including the application of protein language model (Verkuil et al.,
128   2022; Mao et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023).
129
130   2.3   RNA L ANGUAGE M ODEL
131
      Self-supervised pre-trained language models have demonstrated their ability to extract knowledge
132
      from vast amounts of unannotated data, particularly in the realm of NLP (Devlin et al., 2019; Floridi
133
      & Chiriatti, 2020). This progress has paved the way for the application of language models to
134   the modeling of biomacromolecules, such as DNA, RNA, and proteins, given their inherent linear
135   sequence-based primary structure. The biological community’s proactive efforts in standardizing
136   high-quality biomolecular data (Watson, 1990; Suzek et al., 2007) contributes to the advancements
137   of language model in the fields of DNA (Nguyen et al., 2023; Schiff et al., 2024) and proteins (Lin
138   et al., 2022). Likewise, with the development of high-throughput RNA sequencing technologies,
139   the RNA field has witnessed the emergence of language models designed for specific types and
140   functions (Yang et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; Akiyama & Sakakibara, 2022), as
141   well as general-purpose, large-scale language models (Chen et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023). These
142
      models are now capable of performing sequence-to-structure downstream tasks, suggesting that
      structural information is embedded within sequence data. However, the potential of utilizing RNA
143
      language models for structure-to-sequence tasks remains an unexplored avenue.

725   A.5     L O RA
726
727   As language models grow in size and complexity, the resource demands for fine-tuning these models
728
      become increasingly prohibitive. To address this, LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) introduces a method
      for efficient adaptation by incorporating trainable low-rank matrices into the pre-trained model’s
729
      layers, while keeping the original model weights static (Hu et al., 2021).
730
731   LoRA modifies the weight matrix W ∈ Rd×k of the Transformer layers by adding a low-rank update.
732   The adaptation is formulated as follows:
733                                                   W ← W + AB,                                             (7)
734
                       d×r               r×k
735   where A ∈ R            and B ∈ R         are trainable matrices with r being much smaller than both d and k.
736   LoRA presents multiple advantages, including a drastic reduction in the number of parameters that
737   need to be trained, with reductions reaching up to 10,000 times less than what is required for full
738   model fine-tuning. It also substantially cuts down on GPU memory usage, requiring only a third
739   of the memory compared to traditional methods. Performance-wise, LoRA meets or exceeds the
740   results of full fine-tuning on various benchmarks such as RoBERTa, DeBERTa, GPT-2, and GPT-3.
741   Additionally, unlike methods that rely on adapters, LoRA does not introduce any extra latency during
742
      inference. In summary, LoRA offers an efficient and effective approach to adapting large pre-trained
      language models to new tasks. By utilizing low-rank adaptations, it significantly alleviates the
743
      computational and memory demands of fine-tuning while maintaining or even enhancing model
744
      performance.

748   A.6.1    ATTRIBUTES E NCODING
749
750   Node attributes V ∈ RN ×fn encompass fn -dimensional features for N nucleotides, describing the
751   local geometry of each nucleotide. These features involve: (1) dihedral angles, represented using sine
