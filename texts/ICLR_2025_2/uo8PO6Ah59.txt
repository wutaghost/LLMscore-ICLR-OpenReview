010
011
               Cell Painting, a high-content imaging-based profiling method, has emerged as a
012
               powerful tool for understanding cellular phenotypes and drug responses. How-
013            ever, batch effects severely constrain the integration and interpretation of data col-
014            lected across different laboratories and experimental conditions. To mitigate this
015            issue, here we introduce CellPainTR, a novel embedding approach through Tran-
016            former for unified batch correction and representation learning of Cell Painting
017            data, thereby addressing a critical challenge in the field of image-based profil-
018            ing. Our approach employs a Transformer-like architecture with Hyena operators,
019            positional encoding via morphological-feature-embedding, and a special source
020            context token for batch correction, combined with a multi-stage training process
021
               that incorporates masked token prediction and supervised contrastive learning.
               Experiments on the JUMP Cell Painting dataset demonstrate that CellPainTR sig-
022
               nificantly outperforms existing approaches such as Combat and Harmony across
023
               multiple evaluation metrics, while maintaining strong biological information re-
024            tention as evidenced by improved clustering metrics and qualitative UMAP vi-
025            sualizations. Moreover, our method effectively reduces the feature space from
026            thousands of dimensions to just 256, addressing the curse of dimensionality while
027            maintaining high performance. These advancements enable more robust integra-
028            tion of multi-source Cell Painting data, potentially accelerating progress in drug
029            discovery and cellular biology research.

035   Cell Painting, a powerful high-content imaging technique, has emerged as a promising tool for
036   biological research and drug discovery. This method generates rich, multidimensional data captur-
037   ing intricate cellular phenotypes and responses to perturbations. The potential applications of Cell
038   Painting are vast, from identifying novel drug candidates to understanding disease mechanisms at
039
      the cellular level (Bray et al., 2016; Gustafsdottir et al., 2013; Ljosa et al., 2013). Unfortunately,
      fully realizing the potential of Cell Painting is hindered by significant challenges in data integration
040
      and interpretation. While recent approaches explore direct learning from cellular images, most es-
041
      tablished pipelines rely on extracted Cell Painting features (morphological measurements extracted
042   from Cell Painting assays, in our case using CellProfiler software) Ando et al. (2017); Celik et al.
043   (2022); Borowa et al. (2024); Korsunsky et al. (2019) - Typical Cell Painting datasets consist of
044   thousands of such engineered features, leading to the “curse of dimensionality” and computation-
045   ally intensive analyses (Caicedo et al., 2017). Moreover, these datasets are prone to batch effects -
046   systematic variations unrelated to biology - due to differences in experimental conditions, imaging
047   equipment, and data processing across laboratories (Singh et al., 2017).
048   To address these critical limitations, we propose CellPainTR, a novel foundational model designed
049   to learn robust representations of Cell Painting features. By operating directly in the feature space,
050   our approach uniquely integrates batch correction and representation learning into a single model.
051   Through a three-stage training process, CellPainTR learns to distinguish batch-specific artifacts from
052   biologically meaningful features, enabling adaptive balancing of batch effect correction and preser-
053   vation of underlying biological signals. Leveraging a Transformer-like architecture (Vaswani et al.,
      2017) and Hyena operators (Poli et al., 2023), the model efficiently handles long-range dependencies

104   2   BACKGROUND
105
106   Classical Batch Correction Methods. The challenge of batch effects in high-dimensional biolog-
107   ical data, particularly in the field of image-based profiling like Cell Painting, has been a significant
      focus of research in recent years (Arevalo et al., 2024; Ando et al., 2017; Celik et al., 2022; Kraus


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2024; Borowa et al., 2024). Several classical algorithms have been developed to address batch
109   effects in biological data. One prominent example is Combat, which uses a parametric empirical
110   Bayes framework to adjust for batch effects (Johnson et al., 2007). The Combat method models the
111   batch effect as an additive and multiplicative effect on the data, and estimates these effects using an
112   empirical Bayes approach. This can be expressed mathematically as:
113
114                                    yij = αi + βi xj + γb + δb xj + ϵij                                 (1)
115
      where yij is the observed data, xj is the covariate of interest, αi and βi are the sample-specific
116   intercept and slope, γb and δb are the batch-specific intercept and slope, and ϵij is the residual error.
117
118   Another method, Harmony, uses iterative clustering and linear adjustment to correct batch effects
119
      in multi-modal single-cell data (Korsunsky et al., 2019). Harmony aims to identify a shared low-
      dimensional representation across batches by aligning cluster centroids in an iterative fashion. This
120
      can be expressed as:
121
122                                            zi = Wb hi + bb                                          (2)
123   where zi is the corrected representation for sample i, hi is the original high-dimensional represen-
124   tation, Wb and bb are the batch-specific linear transformation parameters.
125
      While these classical methods have demonstrated effectiveness in many scenarios, they may struggle
126
      to handle the high dimensionality and complex batch effects present in Cell Painting datasets (Singh
127
      et al., 2024).
128
129   Self-Attention and the Hyena Operator. The self-attention operator (Vaswani et al., 2017) is a
130   fundamental mechanism of Transformers. Specifically, given a sequence x ∈ RL×D with length L
131
      and D features, the self-attention operator A(x) is defined as:
132
                                         A(x) = σ(xWq )(xWk )T (xWv )                                      (3)
133
134   where Wq , Wk , Wv ∈ RD×D are learnable projection matrices, and σ is a softmax operation. This
135   allows the model to capture pairwise relationships between tokens in the sequence. However, one
136   limitation is that self-attention becomes computationally expensive for long sequences, with a com-
137   plexity of O(L2 ). To address the computational challenge of self-attention, the Hyena operator (Poli
138   et al., 2023) was introduced as a replacement for self-attention in Transformers. The Hyena oper-
139   ator is characterized by a structured self-attention mechanism that involves long convolutions and
140   element-wise gating:
                                                             L−1
141                                                          X
142                                       yt = (h ∗ u)t =           ht−τ uτ .                              (4)
143                                                          τ =0

144   In standard convolutional architectures, the filter length ℓ is typically constrained by ℓ ≪ L, where
145   L is the input sequence length. This constraint helps control computational costs. However, by
146   parameterizing the filter as a function of the temporal offset τ (i.e., hτ = γθ (τ )), we can design
147   extended convolution kernels without a proportional increase in parameters. This technique, known
148   as implicit convolution, enables efficient modeling of long-range dependencies. The implicit con-
149
      volution mechanism is exemplified by the Hyena operator, which employs a recursive framework
      incorporating extended convolutions and point-wise modulation:
150
151                          y = xN · (hN ∗ (xN −1 · (hN −1 ∗ (· · · x1 · (h1 ∗ v)))))                     (5)
152
153   Here, v denotes the initial input, {xi }N
                                              i=1 represent successive transformations, N indicates the
154   recursion depth, ∗ symbolizes convolution, and · denotes Hadamard (element-wise) product.
155
      Relevance to CellPainTR. The Hyena operator’s ability to efficiently model long-range dependen-
156   cies in high-dimensional data makes it particularly relevant to the CellPainTR model. By incorporat-
157   ing the Hyena operator, CellPainTR can effectively handle the complex feature interactions present
158   in Cell Painting data, which is critical for addressing batch effects while preserving biologically rel-
159   evant information. Furthermore, the Bidirectional Hyena (Oh et al., 2023) extension, which removes
160   the temporal causality, is particularly well-suited for the Cell Painting domain, where morphological
161   feature interactions are not constrained by sequential order. This non-causal, bidirectional mecha-
      nism aligns with the requirements of the CellPainTR model.

181   3     C ELL PAIN TR
182
183   This section describes our novel feature embedding approach to unified batch correction and repre-
184   sentation learning for Cell Painting data. We introduce a Transformer-like architecture that effec-
185
      tively handles the high dimensionality and complex relationships inherent in Cell Painting features
      while simultaneously addressing batch effects and preserving compound-specific molecular mecha-
186
      nisms of action (MoA) patterns (Arevalo et al., 2024; Moshkov et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023).
187
188   CellPainTR’s architecture and training process are specifically designed to address the unique chal-
189   lenges presented by Cell Painting data (see Fig. 2(a)). The high dimensionality of the data is tackled
190   through the use of Hyena operators, which efficiently capture long-range dependencies across the
191
      extensive feature space. The linear adaptor for morphological feature embedding ensures that the
      full spectrum of continuous features is preserved, addressing the challenge of information loss of-
192
      ten associated with discretization methods. The feature context embedding mechanism replaces
193
      traditional positional encoding, better capturing the intrinsic relationships between morphological
194   features that are critical in Cell Painting data. Finally, the source-specific token and multi-stage
195   training process directly address the batch effect problem by learning to distinguish between source-
196   specific variations and true biological signals. This comprehensive approach enables CellPainTR
197   to simultaneously correct for batch effects and learn biologically meaningful representations, a key
198   requirement for effective analysis of multi-source Cell Painting datasets. More details are as follows.
199
200   3.1    D ESIGN A RCHITECTURE
201
202   Linear Adapter for Morphological Feature Embedding. Cell Painting data consists of thousands
203
      of morphological features, each representing a specific aspect of cellular structure and organization.
      To effectively capture the rich information in this high-dimensional data, we introduce a linear adap-
204
      tor module to embed the continuous features without any loss of information (Weisbart et al., 2024).
205
      Unlike traditional approaches that rely on discrete token representations, our linear adaptor maps the
206   original feature space directly to the model’s input embeddings as shown in Figure 2(b). This allows
207   the CellPainTR model to operate on the full spectrum of the morphological features, preserving the
208   nuanced relationships between them. For each feature i, the embedding is computed as:
209
                                       Ei = Wi · Ci + bi where Ei ∈ Rdmodel                               (6)
210
                      dmodel                                                                             dmodel
211   where Wi ∈ R           is a learnable weight matrix, Ci ∈ R is the input feature value, and bi ∈ R
212
      is a learnable bias vector. This linear adaptor approach ensures that the CellPainTR model can
      effectively leverage the full contextual information present in the Cell Painting data, laying the
213
      foundation for robust batch correction and representation learning (Tromans-Coia & Jamali, 2023).
214
215   Feature Context Embedding. Traditional Transformer models use positional encoding to incorpo-
      rate the sequential nature of the input data. However, in the case of Cell Painting features, the order


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      of the features does not necessarily reflect any meaningful biological context. To better capture the
217   intrinsic relationships between the morphological features, we introduce a feature context embed-
218   ding mechanism (Seal et al., 2024), as shown in Figure 2(b). Specifically, the feature context em-
219   bedding replaces the standard positional encoding by learning an embedding of the feature context.
220   In this approach, each morphological feature is encoded with its own embedding (M1 , M2 , ..., ML )
221   with Mi ∈ R1×dmodel , which is then added to the expression embeddings. The complete feature
222   embedding matrix is then constructed by concatenating all feature embeddings:
223
224                                   E = [M1 ; M2 ; . . . ; ML ] ∈ RL×dmodel                            (7)
225
      where L is the number of morphological features and dmodel is the embedding dimension, and [·; ·]
226
      denotes concatenation along the row direction. This method allows us to provide the CellPainTR
227
      model with explicit feature context information. By using the feature context embedding, the Cell-
228   PainTR model can learn to associate the morphological features with their biological context, rather
229   than relying on their position in the input sequence. This enhances the model’s ability to capture the
230   complex interdependencies between the features, which is crucial for effective batch correction and
231   representation learning.
232
      Source Context Token. Cell Painting datasets often originate from multiple experimental sources,
233
      each with its own unique batch-related characteristics. To explicitly model these source-specific
234   variations, CellPainTR incorporates a special source context token (Weisbart et al., 2024). The
235   source context token S with dimension Sdim = Mdim is initialized as a learnable parameter, drawn
236   from its own embedding with a vocabulary size K with K the number of source in the dataset;
237   (S1 , S2 , ..., SK ) and is concatenated with the input feature embeddings, as shown in Figure 2(a).
238   We incorporate a learnable source embedding that is concatenated to the feature embeddings:
239
240          Sk = Embedding(k) where k ∈ {1, . . . , K}, H = [E; Sk ] where Sk ∈ R1×dmodel               (8)
241
242
      where K is the number of sources in the dataset. During training, the model learns to associate the
      source context token with the unique batch effects present in each data source. This allows the model
243
      to adaptively correct for batch-related biases while preserving the biologically relevant information
244
      in the learned representations. The inclusion of the source context token is a key innovation that
245   enables the CellPainTR model to handle the challenges of integrating Cell Painting data from di-
246   verse experimental sources, a critical requirement for advancing drug discovery and cellular biology
247   research (Tromans-Coia & Jamali, 2023).

      3.2   T RAINING
250
251
      The CellPainTR model is trained using a multi-stage process that combines unsupervised and su-
252   pervised learning objectives to achieve unified batch correction and representation learning. By
253   progressively exposing the model to increasingly diverse data contexts, we enable it to learn robust,
254   generalizable representations that preserve compound-specific MoA relationships while mitigating
255   batch-related confounders. More details are as follows.
256
      Channel-Wise Masked Morphology (CWMM). The initial stage of training utilizes Channel-Wise
257
      Masked Morphology (CWMM), a novel approach inspired by Masked Language Modeling (MLM)
258   in natural language processing and Masked Expression Modeling (MEM) in single-cell RNA se-
259   quencing analysis (Oh et al., 2023). CWMM is tailored to handle the continuous values of morpho-
260   logical features in Cell Painting data while respecting its channel-wise structure (Seal et al., 2024).
261   For additional details on CWMM refer to Appendix A.1
262
      As shown in Fig. 3(a), in the CWMM task, a subset of input morphological features is randomly
263
      masked, with the model tasked to predict these masked values based on the surrounding context.
264   Features are grouped based on both their channel origin and the cellular compartment they describe
265   (e.g., “DNA channel - Nucleus” features form one group, “Mito channel - Cytoplasm” features form
266   another). The masking probability for each training batch is chosen from a range of [0.05, 0.4]
267   and is applied uniformly across all feature sets, preserving the biological relationships within each
268   channel-compartment combination. Importantly, only non-zero values are masked and replaced with
269   a [MASK] token, as distinguishing between true and false zero values is not feasible in this context
      (Way et al., 2021).

      4   E XPERIMENTS
332
333
      Our experimental framework was designed to rigorously evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed
334
      method for batch correction and representation learning in Cell Painting data. We conducted a series
335
      of experiments using a large-scale dataset, applying a multi-stage training process and assessing
336   performance through both qualitative and quantitative measures. Qualitatively, we examined the
337   model’s ability to preserve clustering patterns that align with known compound Mechanisms of
338   Action (MoA). Quantitatively, we used established metrics from the Broad Institute benchmark
339   (Arevalo et al., 2024), to measure the preservation of compound-specific cellular responses.
340
      Dataset. For our experiments, we utilized the cpg-0016 (Chandrasekaran et al., 2023) Cell Painting
341
      dataset from the JUMP consortium. This extensive dataset encompasses a diverse array of biologi-
342   cal perturbations, including over 100,000 small molecule compounds and genetic perturbations such
343   as ORF overexpression and CRISPR knockouts targeting thousands of genes. The dataset captures
344   a wide spectrum of morphological features, typically numbering in the thousands per cell, which
345   provide detailed measurements of cell shape, size, texture, and intensity across multiple cellular
346   compartments (Bray et al., 2016). The dataset includes both negative and positive controls, which
347   are crucial for establishing baselines and validating assay performance. Negative controls, such
348   as non-targeting controls or DMSO treatments, provide a reference point for normal cellular mor-
349   phology (Caie et al., 2010). Positive controls, consisting of known bioactive compounds or genetic
350
      perturbations with well-characterized effects, serve to validate the assay’s sensitivity and specificity
      (Gustafsdottir et al., 2013).
351
352   Our preprocessing pipeline was designed to address common challenges in Cell Painting data. First,
353   we imputed all infinite and missing values with zero to ensure computational feasibility. Next, we
354   applied MAD (Median Absolute Deviation) normalization, using the negative control of each plate
355   as the baseline. This step helps to mitigate plate-to-plate variations and standardize feature scales.
356
      Finally, we employed a clipping strategy, constraining values between the 0.01 and 0.99 quantiles.
      This step was crucial in managing extreme outliers, which are common artifacts in Cell Painting
357
      data and can significantly impact model performance if left unaddressed.
358
359   To evaluate the robustness and generalizability of our model, we experimented with various train-
360   test split strategies. These included hiding certain data source generations from training, excluding
361   specific plates, and random partitioning of the data (Goodfellow et al., 2016). This approach allowed
362
      us to choose the best-performing model under different scenarios of data availability and batch
      effects.
363
364   Trainining. The pretraining phase of our model utilized all compound-related data from the dataset.
365   During this phase, the model learned to predict masked morphological features based on the sur-
366   rounding context, leveraging the bidirectional nature of the Hyena blocks. This approach enabled
367   the model to capture complex relationships within the data effectively, laying the groundwork for
368
      subsequent supervised learning stages (Devlin et al., 2018).
369   Following pretraining, we moved to a more focused training phase using a curated subset of the
370   data. This subset concentrated on compounds with rich metadata, including control compounds
371   and those with known Mechanisms of Action (MoA) (Schurer et al., 2011). This phase, which took
372   approximately one week on the same hardware, allowed the model to refine its representations based
373   on more specific biological contexts (Goodfellow et al., 2016). During this stage, we introduced the
374
      source-specific token, enabling the model to learn and account for source-specific variations in the
      data (Devlin et al., 2018). This approach was crucial in addressing batch effects while preserving
375
      biologically relevant information.
376

      contrastive learning. This phase also lasted approximately one week and used the same curated

      To assess the effectiveness of batch correction, we employed several complementary metrics. Graph
      Connectivity evaluates the preservation of biological relationships across batches by measuring the
444
      proportion of k-nearest neighbors that are maintained after batch correction, compared to the orig-
445
      inal data structure. This metric provides insights into how well the local structure of the data is
446   preserved while removing batch effects, with values closer to 1 indicating better preservation of bio-
447   logical relationships. The Silhouette Batch score quantifies the degree of separation between batches
448   by comparing the mean distance between samples from different batches to the mean distance within
449   batches, where values closer to 1 suggest more effective batch integration. Additionally, we intro-
450   duce a Batch Correction metric calculated as 1 - f1 score, where f1 represents the performance of
451   a classifier trained to detect batch effects in the corrected data. In this context, higher values indi-
452   cate superior batch correction as the classifier struggles to distinguish between batches, effectively
453   measuring the degree of batch effect removal.
454   The preservation of compound-specific patterns is crucial in ensuring that the batch correction pro-
455   cess does not compromise the underlying MoA-related information. We quantify this preservation
456   through multiple complementary approaches. Through the Leiden clustering algorithm, we com-
457   pute Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) and Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) scores by comparing
458   cluster assignments with known biological labels. These metrics provide different perspectives on
459   biological preservation: NMI values range from 0 to 1, indicating the degree of shared information
460
      between cluster assignments and biological labels, while ARI provides a chance-corrected mea-
      sure of agreement, with values above 0.3 indicating strong biological preservation. The Silhouette
461
      Label score further strengthens our evaluation by assessing how well samples cluster according to
462
      biological conditions rather than batch effects, with values above 0.5 suggesting robust biological
463   signal retention. To evaluate the impact on downstream analysis tasks, we employed mean Average
464   Precision (mAP) scores (see Appendix C.1)for compound retrieval performance in two contexts:
465   with controls (mAP control) and without replicate compounds (mAP no rep). These scores specifi-
466   cally assess the model’s ability to identify similar biological conditions across batches, providing a
467   practical measure of biological signal preservation where values above 0.5 indicate strong retention
468   of compound-specific patterns. Aggregate scores provide a holistic view of each method’s perfor-
469   mance. The Batch Correction score summarizes the overall effectiveness in removing batch effects,
470   while the Biological Metrics score encapsulates the method’s ability to preserve biological infor-
471
      mation. The Overall Score combines these aspects to give a comprehensive assessment of each
      method’s performance.
472
473   Our proposed CellPainTR method demonstrates strong performance across these metrics. Notably,
474   it achieves the highest batch correction scores both with and without controls, indicating superior
475   batch integration capabilities. The progressive improvement observed across the three steps of Cell-
476   PainTR (denoted as CellPainTR(1), CellPainTR(2), and CellPainTR) highlights the method’s ability
477

483   This balance is critical in ensuring that batch correction does not come at the cost of losing important
484   biological signals. In summary, the quantitative results presented in Table 1 provide strong evidence

      4.3   A BLATION S TUDY
490
491   To elucidate the contribution of each component in our CellPainTR method, we conducted an ab-
492   lation study by evaluating the model’s performance at different stages of training. The results are
493   presented in Table 1, where CellPainTR(1) and CellPainTR(2) represent intermediate stages, and
494   CellPainTR denotes the final model.
495
      The initial training stage, represented by CellPainTR(1), demonstrated a strong focus on batch effect
496   removal. This stage achieved the highest batch correction score without control (0.69), surpassing
497   both the baseline and traditional methods in this metric. However, this came at the cost of reduced
498   performance in biological metrics, particularly evident in the lower mAP scores (0.22 and 0.32) and
499   decreased graph connectivity (0.78) compared to the baseline (0.86). CellPainTR(2), the intermedi-
500   ate training stage, marked a significant shift towards preserving biological information. This stage
501   showed substantial improvements in biological metrics, most notably in Silhouette Label (0.70) and
502   mAP scores (0.54 and 0.63). Moreover, it achieved the highest Leiden NMI (0.43) among all meth-
503   ods, suggesting improved cluster separation. These gains in biological signal preservation were
504
      accompanied by the best overall biological metrics score (0.60). However, this stage also saw a de-

505
      connectivity (0.69). The final CellPainTR model emerged as a balanced solution, optimizing both
506
      batch correction and biological signal preservation. It achieved the best overall batch correction
507   score (0.76), effectively balancing correction with and without control. The model also recovered
508   graph connectivity (0.84) compared to the intermediate stages while maintaining strong performance
509   in biological information preservation (0.86 for control). Despite these improvements, there was a
510   slight decrease in some biological metrics compared to CellPainTR(2), particularly in Silhouette
511   Label and Leiden NMI.
512

513   vation throughout the training process. The initial stage prioritizes batch effect removal, potentially
514   at the expense of biological signal retention. The intermediate stage then shifts focus to preserving
515   biological information, enhancing clustering and representation quality. Finally, the complete model
516   achieves an optimal balance between these competing objectives. For qualitative comparison of the
517   steps, please refer to Appendix E

520
521
      This paper introduces CellPainTR, a novel approach to unified batch correction and representation
522
      learning for high-dimensional Cell Painting data. Leveraging a Transformer-like architecture with
523   Hyena operators, CellPainTR addresses the critical challenges of batch effects and dimensional-
524   ity reduction in image-based profiling. Extensive experimental results confirmed that CellPainTR
525   successfully addresses batch effects while preserving crucial biological information. However, we
526   acknowledge the limitations of our study, including the restricted comparison to only three other
527   batch correction methods due to difficulties in replicating results from benchmark papers (Arevalo
528   et al., 2024). Additionally, we observed a discrepancy between the qualitative improvements seen in
529   the UMAP visualizations and some of the quantitative metrics, highlighting the complexity of eval-
530   uating batch correction methods and the potential limitations of current evaluation approaches (for
531
      more details see Appendix D). Despite these challenges, CellPainTR represents a significant step for-
      ward in the analysis of Cell Painting data, offering a powerful approach to integrating multi-source
532
      datasets while maintaining compound-specific molecular signature preservation. The method’s abil-
533
      ity to handle high-dimensional data, correct for batch effects, and preserve biological information
534   positions it as a valuable tool for advancing drug discovery and cellular biology research.

666   A.1   C HANNEL -W ISE M ASKED M ORPHOLOGY (CWMM)
667
668   Channel-Wise Masked Morphology (CWMM) is a novel training objective designed for Cell Paint-
669   ing data analysis. Inspired by Masked Language Modeling (MLM) (Devlin et al., 2018) in natural
670
      language processing and Masked Expression Modeling (MEM) in single-cell RNA sequencing (Oh
      et al., 2023), CWMM adapts these concepts to address the continuous values and channel-wise struc-
671
      ture of morphological features. In CWMM, a subset of input morphological features is randomly
672
      and uniformaly masked with respect to each channel during training. The model then predicts these
673   masked values based on surrounding context. Key aspects of the implementation include:
674
675          • Masking probability: Randomly selected from a range of 0.05 to 0.4 for each training batch.
676
             • Uniform application: Masking is applied equally across all five channels in Cell Painting
677            data.
678
             • Non-zero value focus: Only non-zero values are masked and replaced with a [MASK]
679
               token.
680
681   The CellPainTR model, using bidirectional Hyena blocks, predicts the true values of masked fea-
682   tures. This process requires consideration of both preceding and following features, capturing com-
683   plex relationships within the data. The CWMM objective function is formulated as in Section 3.2
684   Eq. (9).
685
      CWMM offers several advantages:
686
687          • Encourages learning of generalizable features capturing biological meaning in morpholog-
688            ical data.
689
             • Facilitates understanding of contextual relationships and channel-wise dependencies.
690
691
             • Establishes a foundation for subsequent supervised learning, enhancing capabilities in
               batch correction and representation learning.
692
693
      By adapting proven techniques to Cell Painting data’s specific challenges, CWMM provides a pow-
694   erful tool for extracting insights from complex morphological datasets. This approach has the po-
695   tential to advance our understanding of cellular phenotypes and their relationships to underlying
696   biological processes, benefiting fields from drug discovery to basic cell biology research.

1014   D.6   P RACTICAL C ONSIDERATIONS FOR M ODEL S TAGES AND C OMPUTATIONAL E FFICIENCY
1015
1016
       The proposed three-stage approach—CellPainTR(1), CellPainTR(2), and CellPainTR—offers flex-
       ibility depending on the requirements of the analysis. Each stage reflects a trade-off between com-
1017
       putational demands, batch correction efficacy, and biological signal preservation.
1018
1019   CellPainTR(1): This stage serves as a foundational representation learning model, achieving a good
1020   starting point for biological signal extraction. However, it retains pronounced batch effects between
1021   data sources, making it more suitable for exploratory analyses or scenarios where inter-source batch
1022
       effects are less critical (Figure 7 6 5, Tab 1).
1023   CellPainTR(2): Optimized for single-source datasets, CellPainTR(2) effectively addresses intra-
1024   source batch effects while retaining biological signals with high fidelity. This model is particularly
1025   useful for studies focused on single-source data, such as analyzing cellular responses within a spe-
       cific experimental batch or environment (Figure (Figure 7 6 5, Tab 1).


                                                        19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       CellPainTR: The final stage represents the most balanced model for multi-source datasets, excelling
1027   in inter-source batch correction. While there is a minor loss in biological signal fidelity compared to
1028   CellPainTR(2), this trade-off enables robust integration and analysis across large-scale, multi-source
1029   data, making it well-suited for meta-analyses and cross-experiment studies (Figure 7 6 5, Tab 1).
1030
       These distinctions provide practical guidance for researchers on selecting the appropriate model
1031
       stage based on their experimental setup and goals. The additional visualizations E included in the
1032
       appendix further illustrate these trade-offs, emphasizing the nuanced improvements achieved by the
1033   final stage.
1034
1035   Computational Efficiency and Reproducibility: The training times reported in this study reflect
1036
       an unoptimized setup, involving a single Quadro 4000 GPU and no advanced strategies such as
       distributed training or mixed precision. The reported times are provided for transparency and to
1037
       guide users who may wish to replicate the experiments using the current public codebase.
1038
1039   To reduce computational time, several straightforward optimizations can be implemented:
1040
1041
              • Utilizing modern GPUs with higher processing power.
1042          • Incorporating distributed training techniques for parallel computation.
1043          • Leveraging mixed precision to reduce memory requirements and training time.
1044

1046   fectiveness in addressing batch correction and biological signal retention, rather than computational
1047   efficiency. Nonetheless, these optimizations can significantly lower the training cost without altering
1048
       the methodology.
