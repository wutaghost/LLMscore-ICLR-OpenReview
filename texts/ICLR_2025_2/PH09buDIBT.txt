009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            Gradient-based hyperparameter optimization methods update hyperparameters
012            using hypergradients, gradients of a meta criterion with respect to hyperparameters.
013            Previous research used two distinct update strategies: optimizing hyperparameters
014            using global hypergradients obtained after completing model training or local
015            hypergradients derived after every few model updates. While global hypergra-
016            dients offer reliability, their computational cost is significant; conversely, local
017
               hypergradients provide speed but are often suboptimal. In this paper, we propose
               glocal hypergradient estimation, blending “global” quality with “local” efficiency.
018
               To this end, we use the Koopman operator theory to linearize the dynamics of
019
               hypergradients so that the global hypergradients can be efficiently approximated
020            only by using a trajectory of local hypergradients. Consequently, we can optimize
021            hyperparameters greedily using estimated global hypergradients, achieving both
022            reliability and efficiency simultaneously. Through numerical experiments of hyper-
023            parameter optimization, including optimization of optimizers, we demonstrate the
024            effectiveness of the glocal hypergradient estimation.

035   Its inner-level problem (Equation (2)) is to minimize an inner objective ℓ(θ, ϕ; D) with respect
036
      to θ on data D. The outer-level or meta-level problem (Equation (1)) aims to minimize a meta
037
      objective ℓ̃(θ, ϕ; D̃) with respect to ϕ on data D̃, and usually D ∩ D̃ = ∅. A typical problem is
038   hyperparameter optimization (Hutter et al., 2019), where ℓ and ℓ̃ are training and validation objectives,
039   and D and D̃ are training and validation datasets. Another example is meta learning (Hospedales
040   et al., 2021), where ℓ and ℓ̃ correspond to meta-training and meta-testing objectives. In the deep
041   learning context, which is the main focus of this paper, θ corresponds to neural network parameters,
042
      which are optimized by gradient-based optimizers, such as SGD or Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015),
      using gradient ∇θ ℓ(θ, ϕ).
043
044   Similarly, gradient-based bi-level optimization aims to optimize the hyperparameters with gradient-
045   based optimizers by using hypergradient ∇ϕ ℓ̃(θ(ϕ)) (Bengio, 2000; Larsen et al., 1996). Although
046   this hypergradient is not always available, when it can be obtained or estimated, gradient-based
047   optimization is efficient and scalable to even millions of hyperparameters (Lorraine et al., 2020),
048   surpassing the black-box counterparts scaling up to few hundreds (Bergstra et al., 2011; 2013). As
049   a result, gradient-based approaches are applied in practical problems that demand efficiency (Choe
050
      et al., 2023), such as neural architecture search (Liu et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2021; Sakamoto et al.,
      2023), optimization of data augmentation (Hataya et al., 2022), and balancing several loss terms (Shu
051
      et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021).
052

083

085   verify its effectiveness in numerical experiments, namely, hyperparameter optimization of optimizers

      Our specific contributions are as follows:
088
089   1. We propose the glocal hypergradient estimation that combines the advantages of global and local
090      hypergradient techniques. Specifically, it leverages the Koopman operator theory to predict the
091      global hypergradients from local information, thereby retaining the efficiency of local methods
092      while capturing global information.
093   2. We provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the computational complexities involved
094      in the glocal approach, revealing its computational efficiency compared to the global method.
095      Additionally, we present an error bound quantifying the accuracy of the proposed glocal estimation
096
         relative to the actual global hypergradient.
097   3. We numerically demonstrate that the proposed glocal hypergradient estimation achieves perfor-
098      mance comparable to global approach while maintaining the efficiency of the local method.

      2.2   C OMPUTATION OF H YPERGRADIENTS
142
143
      To compute such global or local hypergradients, a straightforward approach is to differentiate through
144   the T - or τ - step optimization process (Finn et al., 2017; Grefenstette et al., 2019; Domke, 2012).
145   This unrolling approach is applicable to any differentiable hyperparameters. Yet, it suffers from large
146   memory requirements, O(T p) for the global one, when reverse-mode automatic differentiation is
147   used. This challenge may be alleviated using forward-mode automatic differentiation (Micaelli &
148   Storkey, 2021; Franceschi et al., 2017; Deleu et al., 2022) or truncated backpropagation (Shaban
149   et al., 2019). Moreover, differentiating through long unrolled computational graphs suffers from
150   gradient vanishing/explosion, limiting its applications.
151   Alternatively, implicit differentiation can also be used with an assumption that θT reaches close
152   enough to a local optimum. The main bottleneck of this approach is the computation of inverse
153   Hessian with respect to model parameters (Bengio, 2000), which can be bypassed by iterative linear
154   system solvers (Pedregosa, 2016; Rajeswaran et al., 2019; Blondel et al., 2021), the Neumann series
155   approximation (Lorraine et al., 2020), and the Nyström method (Hataya & Yamada, 2023) along with
156   matrix-vector products. Although this approach is efficient and used in large-scale problems (Choe
157   et al., 2023; Hataya et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2021), its application is limited to hyperparameters
158
      that directly change inner-level loss functions. In other words, the implicit-differentiation approach
      cannot be used for other hyperparameters, such as the learning rate of the inner-level optimizer Θ.
159
160   The proposed glocal hypergradient estimation can rely on the unrolling approach but differentiating
161   through only τ (≪ T ) iterations like the local unrolling approach; thus, this approach is applicable to
      various hyperparameters as the unrolling approach.

342   Implementation We implemented neural network models and algorithms, including DMD, using
343   JAX (v0.4.28) (Bradbury et al., 2018), optax (DeepMind et al., 2020), and equinox (Kidger
344   & Garcia, 2021). Reverse-mode automatic differentiation is used for model gradient computation.
345
      Forward-mode automatic differentiation is adopted for hypergradient computation.
      In the following experiments, we adopt Hankel DMD (Arbabi & Mezic, 2017), whose observation
346
      function g can be defined using time-delayed coordinates as g(ht ) = [ht ; ht+1 ; . . . ; ht+m−1 ] ∈
347
      Rmq and t = sτ, . . . , sτ − m + 1 for some positive integer m for each Is . Such a function g is
348   obviously left-invertible.
349
      Setup For DMD, we use the last σ hypergradients out of τ hypergradients in each Is because the
350   dynamics immediately after updating hyperparameters may be unstable. Throughout the experiments,
351   we optimize model parameters 10k times and hyperparameters every 100 model updates, i.e., τ = 100,
352   using Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.1 (Kingma & Ba, 2015). We set m = 10 and σ = 80,
353   except for the analysis in Section 4.3. When computing hypergradients, we use hold-out validation
354   data D̃ from the original training data, following Micaelli & Storkey (2021). To minimize the effect
355   of stochasticity, we use as large a minibatch size as possible for the validation. Average performance
356   on test data over three different random seeds is reported. Further experimental details can be found
357   in Appendix B.
358   Baselines We adopt local and global baselines that greedily update hyperparameters using global
359   and local hypergradients, respectively. As the global baseline is prone to diverge and computationally
360   expensive, we can only present its results on MNIST-sized datasets.

478   5                    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
479
480   This paper introduced glocal hypergradient estimation, which leverages the virtues of global and
481   local hypergradients simultaneously. To this end, we adopted the Koopman operator theory to approx-
482
      imate a global hypergradient from a trajectory of local hypergradients. The numerical experiments
      demonstrated the validity of gradient-based hyperparameter optimization using glocal hypergradients.
483
484   Limitations In this work, we have implicitly assumed that the meta criterion converges after enough
485   iterations, and so do hypergradients. This assumption may be strong when a certain minibatch
      drastically changes the value of the meta criterion, such as adversarial learning. Luckily, we did


                                                                                                                  9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      not suffer from such a problem, probably because we focused our experiments only on supervised
487   learning with sufficiently sized datasets and used full-batch validation data. Introducing stochasticity
488   by the Perron-Frobenius operator (Korda & Mezić, 2018; Hashimoto et al., 2020), an adjoint of the
489   Koopman operator, would alleviate this limitation, which we leave for future research.

690   Figures C.1 and C.2 present the test accuracy curves and the transition of SGD’s hyperparameters of
691   KMNIST and MNIST. As the results presented in the main text in Section 4.1, the proposed glocal
692   approach yields similar behaviors as the global one, while maintaining the efficiency of the local
693   approach. As can be seen from Figures C.2 and C.3, the global method often fails because of loss
694   explosion, as discussed in the literature (Micaelli & Storkey, 2021). Similarly, Figure C.4 shows the
695   results on the CIFAR-100 dataset and SVHN.
696   Figure C.7 shows test accuracy and the transition of SGD hyperparameters (learning rate, momentum,
697   weight decay) with different numbers of stacks of the Hankel DMD (m). When m = 1, equivalent to
698   the case when g is identity, the estimated hyperparameters yield much higher variances. This can be
699   explained as the error of approximating the nonlinear dynamical system of hypergradients with a
700   linear dynamical system in the space of hypergradients. Contrarily, the hyperparameters obtained
701   with Hankel DMD with m > 1 show limited variances, indicating that Hankel DMD could more
      appropriately capture the nonlinear dynamics with lower variances.
