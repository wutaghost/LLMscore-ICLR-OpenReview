009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            When data is generated by multiple sources, conventional training methods update
012            models assuming equal reliability for each source and do not consider their indi-
013            vidual data quality during training. However, in many applications, sources have
014            varied levels of reliability that can have negative effects on the performance of a
015            neural network. A key issue is that often the quality of data for individual sources is
016            not known during training. Focusing on supervised learning, we aim to train neural
017
               networks on each data source for a number of steps proportional to the source’s
               estimated relative reliability, by using a dynamic weighting. This way, we allow
018
               training on all sources during the warm-up, and reduce learning on less reliable
019
               sources during the final training stages, when it has been shown models overfit to
020            noise. We show through diverse experiments, this can significantly improve model
021            performance when trained on mixtures of reliable and unreliable data sources, and
022            maintain performance when models are trained on reliable sources only.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Data sources can have differing levels of noise but in many applications, are merged together to
028   form a single dataset. In healthcare, for example, data sources (such as different devices or sites) are
029   often combined together (Tomar & Agarwal, 2013; Baro et al., 2015), but may not provide the same
030   level of data quality and could contain noisy features, incorrect labelling, or missing values. These
031
      problems, if unaddressed, can have detrimental effects on the performance and robustness of machine
      learning models (Zhang et al., 2021; Arpit et al., 2017; Neyshabur et al., 2017).
032
033   In this work, we consider the supervised learning case, in which a neural network is trained on
034   data from multiple sources that are (ideally) producing unique features and labels from the same
035   distribution, but where some sources are producing noisy features or labels at an unknown rate1 .
036   Two possible solutions for this context naturally arise: Preprocessing can be used to remove out-
037   of-distribution observations from training (Gamberger et al., 2000; Thongkam et al., 2008; Delany
038   et al., 2012) but, this requires the user to define “out-of-distribution” for the features and labels, and
039   eliminating data assumes nothing can be learnt from noisy examples (Wang et al., 2018). Secondly,
040   techniques for training neural networks on noisy data can be applied, of which many exist (Han
041   et al., 2020; Song et al., 2022). In this case however, we are not utilising the information gained
042   from knowing an observation’s data source – which we show is useful, especially within settings of
043
      considerable noise.
044   We therefore propose Loss Adapted Plasticity (LAP); inspired by ideas from the literature, and loss
045   tempering, it is a general method for training neural networks on multi-source data with mixed
046   reliability. As we do not know the sources’ true noise level a priori, we maintain a history of the
047   empirical risk on data from each source that is used to temper the likelihood during training. This
048   is done such that the number of steps a model is trained on a source is proportional to its estimated
049
      reliability. Hence, a model trained with LAP will benefit from seeing examples from all sources
      during early training, and reduce learning on less reliable data later in training, when the model is
050
      prone to memorising noisy data points (Zhang et al., 2021; Arpit et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2020; Xia
051
      et al., 2021). To illustrate our method’s effectiveness, we present results on diverse settings and

      2    BACKGROUND
063
064
      Noisy labels can be introduced at any point during collection and when human experts are involved,
      are practically inevitable (Song et al., 2022; Frenay & Verleysen, 2014; McNicol, 2004; Frenay &
065
      Verleysen, 2014): An expert might have insufficient information (Hickey, 1996; Dawid & Skene,
066
      1979); expert labels are incorrect (Hickey, 1996); the labelling is subjective (Marcus et al., 1993);
067   or there are communication problems (Zhu & Wu, 2004). Further, noisy features can be introduced
068   through data processing, or faulty measurements (Li et al., 2021). With each of these issues affecting
069   data sources differently, we can observe varied data quality.
070
      The majority of the work on noisy data focuses on noisy labels where solutions usually target a
071
      combination of four aspects (Han et al., 2020; Song et al., 2022): sample selection, model architecture,
072
      regularisation, or training loss. IDPA (Wang et al., 2021) explores instance dependent noise, where
073   label noise depends on an observation’s features, in which they estimate the true label of confusing
074   instances using model confidence during training. Han et al. (2018) propose “Co-teaching”, a sample
075   selection method, in which two neural networks are trained simultaneously. Data for which one
076   model achieves a low training loss is selected to “teach” the other network, as they are assumed
077   to be more reliable. This work exploits the fact that neural networks learn clean data patterns and
078   filter noisy instances in early training (Zhang et al., 2021; Arpit et al., 2017), a fact that our work
079   will take advantage of. Noisy inputs are additionally studied in Li et al. (2021), where they present
080   RRL, employing two contrastive losses and a noise cleaning method using model confidence during
081
      training. This approach requires modifications to the model architecture and a k nearest neighbours
      search at each epoch. However, these methods do not consider multi-source data.
082
083   In federated learning, a server trains a global model using local updates on each source (Konečnỳ
084   et al., 2016), but when sources are noisy, these algorithms often fail (Li et al., 2020). To tackle
085   varied data quality, Li et al. (2022) propose ARFL, which learns global and local weight updates
086   simultaneously. The weighted sum of empirical risk of clients’ loss is minimised, where the weights
087
      are distributed by comparing each client’s empirical risk to the average empirical risk of the best k
      clients. The contribution to weight updates from the clients with higher losses are minimised with
088
      respect to the updates from other clients – forcing the global model to learn more from clients that
089
      achieve lower losses.
090
091   Further, Murphy (2022) discusses sensor fusion, where multiple observations from sensors with
092   differing (but known) noise are taken, with the goal to calculate the true values. This is connected to
093
      our setting, but in our case, sources generate multiple unique observations, and source reliability is
      unknown.
094
095   Therefore, we are exploring a context between many; federated learning, sensor fusion, and learning
096   with noisy data, but differing enough such that methods do not apply or under-perform compared
097   to our source-aware solution. Nevertheless, Co-teaching, IDPA, RRL, and ARFL will form our
098   baselines.

113   3    M ETHODS
114
115   Inspired by Co-teaching, ARFL, and loss tempering, we propose Loss Adapted Plasticity (LAP):
116   Briefly, we update a source reliability score as a function of the historical training empirical risk,
117   which is used to re-weight loss such that the more reliable a source is, the longer the model trains
118   on data from that source. Importantly, we use the fact that when training, neural networks learn
119   non-noisy patterns before noisy data (Zhang et al., 2021; Arpit et al., 2017; Han et al., 2018; Arazo
120
      et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2019; Shen & Sanghavi, 2019) and are observed fitting to noisy examples only
      after substantial progress is made in fitting to clean examples (Arazo et al., 2019). Therefore, in early
121
      training the empirical risk on clean data is lower than noisy data (Appendix A.2).

252   BASELINE METHODS
253
254   To contextualise our approach, we evaluate varied baseline methods: (1) ARFL, designed to tackle
255   label and input noise in a federated learning setting (Li et al., 2022); (2) IDPA, a probabilistic
256   method for instance dependent label noise (Wang et al., 2021) which modifies the training loss; (3)
257   “Co-teaching”, which trains two models simultaneously to perform sample selection based on loss
258   values during training (Han et al., 2018); (4) RRL, which uses contrastive learning and a k nearest
259   neighbours search to enforce a smoothness constraint on learnt representations (Li et al., 2021),
260
      modifying model architecture and training loss; and (4) an identical model but without any specific
      modifications for tackling noise. Baseline methods were selected based on the availability of code,
261
      their use as baselines in the literature, and for variety in the methods used to evaluate the performance
262
      of LAP. Although our setting assumes noise is independent of features, we felt it was still beneficial
263   to include IDPA, which is designed for instance dependent label noise. Note, that because ARFL is a
264   federated learning approach, data cannot be shuffled in the same way as the other models, since each
265   client trains on a single source.
266
      It is important to note that both IDPA and Co-teaching require the training of a model twice, making
267
      LAP significantly less computationally expensive.
268
269   All baselines were implemented using the available code and trained using the recommended parame-
      ters with the model and data we test. For further details, see Appendix A.7.

      E XPERIMENTAL DESIGN
271
272
273
274   To evaluate LAP, we employ various techniques to produce noisy data, extending the methods in
275   Li et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2021); Han et al. (2018); Li et al. (2021) and test on eleven datasets
276   from computer vision, healthcare time-series, natural language processing, and tabular regression for
277   diverse experiments.
278   Following Li et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2021); Han et al. (2018); Li et al. (2021), we test our proposed
279   method and baselines on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky, 2009), and F-MNIST (Xiao et al.,
280   2017) forming many comparisons with the literature. Along with Tiny-Imagenet and Imagenet (Deng
281   et al., 2009), these five datasets form well-studied computer vision tasks, easing reproducibility.
282   Additionally, we use a human labelled version of CIFAR-10, titled CIFAR-10N (Wei et al., 2022),
283   for which we use the “worst labels” allowing us flexibility in our experiments. We then study an
284   electrocardiograph (ECG) dataset, PTB-XL (Wagner et al., 2020); a time-series classification task
285
      with the goal to classify normal and abnormal cardiac rhythms and for which noise can be simulated
      following Wong et al. (2012) and random labelling to understand LAP applied to time-series data
286
      with multiple noise types. Additionally, a sentiment analysis natural language task allows us to
287
      compare LAP against the literature, for which we employ the IMDB dataset (Maas et al., 2011)
288   containing movie reviews and their sentiment. Next, we use the GoEmotions dataset (Demszky et al.,
289   2020), a natural language emotion prediction task, which contains real-world imbalanced source
290   sizes and class distributions, allowing us to test the robustness of LAP to varied source constructions.
291   Finally, to illustrate our method’s use for regression, we present results on the California Housing
292   dataset (Pace & Barry, 1997). For further information, see Appendix A.6 and A.13.
293
      To simulate data sources for CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, F-MNIST, Imagenet, IMDB, and California
294   Housing, data is uniformly split into 10 distinct groups, and for Tiny-Imagenet we use 100 groups to
295   study larger numbers of sources. For CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, 4 and 2 of these sources are chosen
296   to be noisy respectively; for F-MNIST, Imagenet, and Tiny-Imagenet 6, 5 and 40 are chosen; and
297   for IMDB and California Housing 4 are chosen. These are in line with the noise rates used in the
298   literature (often 20%, 40%, 50%). For CIFAR-10, in Appendix A.12 we also significantly increase
299   the number of sources. To generate noise for the vision datasets, we extended the methods in Li et al.
300   (2022): (1) Original Data: No noise is applied to the data; (2) Chunk Shuffle: Split features into
301   distinct chunks and shuffle. This is only done on the first and/or second axis of a given input; (3)
302
      Random Label: Randomly assign a new label from the same code; (4) Batch Label Shuffle: For a
      given batch of features, randomly shuffle the labels; (5) Batch Label Flip: For a given batch of data,
303
      assign all features in this batch a label randomly chosen from the same batch; (6) Added Noise: Add
304
      Gaussian noise to the features, with mean = 0 and standard deviation = 1; (7) Replace with Noise:
305   Replace features with Gaussian noise, with mean = 0 and standard deviation = 1.
306
307   For IMDB, GoEmotions, and California Housing we use random labelling.
308   PTB-XL was labelled by 12 nurses, naturally forming data sources. However, since this data is high
309   quality, synthetic noise is required. We add Gaussian noise to sources’ ECG recordings (simulating
310   electromagnetic interference as in Wong et al. (2012)) and label flipping to simulate human error in
311   labelling. This also allows us to test the setting with multiple noise types. Here, data from sources
312   are upsampled so that each source contains the same number of observations. For experiments with
313   PTB-XL, we linearly increase the number of noisy sources from 1 to 8 (out of 12 in total), and for
314
      each number of noisy sources we set the noise level for each source linearly from 0.25 to 1.0. For
      example, when training with 4 noisy sources, sources haves noise levels of 0.25, 0.50, 0.75, and 1.0.
315
316   Since GoEmotions is annotated by 82 raters, their indentification number is used to form the data
317   sources. Some raters contributed a handful of data points, whilst others labelled thousands; with
318   each rater providing a different distribution of class labels. This allowed us to explore the real-world
319   case of data with sources of imbalanced size and label distribution. A detailed discussion of the
320
      imbalanced sources is given in Appendix A.13.
321   Although CIFAR-10N contains real-world noise, we must still split the data into sources. For each
322   experiment, we assign sources as to evaluate varied levels of noise and numbers of noisy sources. As
323   is done for PTB-XL, we linearly increase the number of noisy sources from 1 to 7 (out of 10 in total),
      and for each set of noisy sources we linearly increase the noise level from 0.25 to 1.0.

      5    D ISCUSSION
494
495   This research reveals some interesting future research directions. In our experiments, we study models
496
      of varied capacity (for example: Table 1 and Appendix A.11, and Table 2 and Appendix A.16),
      however, it is interesting to further study the effects of ill-specified models on noisy data techniques,
497
      as most methods assume that models attain smaller losses on the non-noisy data points (for example:
498
      in our work and Co-teaching (Han et al., 2018)), or that logits are reliable (for example: in RRL (Li
499   et al., 2021) and IDPA (Wang et al., 2021)). Additionally, we focus on the setting in which knowing
500   the source of a data point provides extra information in learning from noisy data. However, when the
501   noise level is uniform across all sources, the source value does not provide additional information
502   about a data point’s likelihood of being noisy, and so in this case LAP performs as well as standard
503   training. Here, it would be beneficial to apply a noisy data technique in addition to our method, such
504   as with RRL + LAP, studied in the experiments (Table 2, Figure 3b, and Appendix A.10).
505   The additional compute required to apply LAP to a single source is O(S + B) and the extra memory
506   cost is O(S × H) (where S is the number of unique sources in the dataset, B is the batch size, and H
507   is the length of the loss history), as we calculate the source means and standard deviations online. For
508   multiple sources in a batch, the additional compute becomes O(S × Sb + B) (where Sb is the number
509   of unique sources in a batch). A measured time cost for a simple experiment is given in Appendix
510   A.18. The extra memory cost in this case is O(S × H + Sb × S). Although vectorised in the current
511   implementation, O(S × Sb ) could be performed with Sb parallel jobs of O(S), since each source
512   calculation is independent, significantly improving its speed for larger numbers of sources. However,
513
      this is already significantly faster than the baselines tested, in which Co-teaching trains two models,
      IDPA trains a model for twice as long, and RRL applies k-nearest neighbours at each epoch.
514
515   Importantly, as is the case with all methods designed for training models on noisy data, thought must
516   be given to the underlying cause of noise. For example, in some scenarios noise within a dataset
517   could be attributed to observations on minority groups during data collection, rather than as a result
518   of errors in measurements, labelling, or data transfer. This is why we began with the assumption that
519
      all data (if non-noisy) is expected to be generated from the same underlying probability distribution.
      If data from minority groups are not carefully considered, any technique for learning from noisy data,
520
      or standard training could lead to an unexpected predictive bias (Mehrabi et al., 2021).
521
522   Within this work we presented LAP, a method designed for training neural networks on data generated
523   by many data sources with unknown noise levels. In Section 4, we observed that using LAP during
524   training improves model performance when trained on data generated by a mixture of noisy and
525
      non-noisy sources, and does not hinder performance when all data sources are free of noise. The
      results also illustrated that applying the proposed method is beneficial at varied noise levels, when
526
      training on multiple sources with differing levels of noise, and whilst being robust to different types
527
      of noise. We additionally see through results in Table 1, 2, 3, 4 and Figure 3a, and 3b that our method
528   is applicable and robust across multiple domains with differing tasks. Moreover, within the Appendix
529   we present many further experiments, showing that: (1) Our method is robust to an extreme source
530   class distribution that tests the limits of the assumptions we made when proposing LAP (Appendix
531   A.14); (2) The improvements in results translate to large scale datasets (Imagenet), with multiple
532   noise types (Appendix A.16); (3) LAP is robust to overfitting of noisy data, and therefore achieves
533   significantly greater performance during late training (Appendix A.16.1); and (4) LAP continues
534   to outperform the baseline on a regression task (Appendix A.17). Further, our implementation
535   (Appendix A.1) allows this method to be easily applied to any neural network training where data is
536
      generated by multiple sources and our analysis in Section 3 and Appendix A.8 provides a detailed
      description of the introduced parameters and their intuitions.
537
538   This work shows that LAP provides improved model performance over the baselines in a variety of
539   noise settings and equal performance on non-noisy data, whilst being robust to a multitude of tasks
      and being cheaper to compute. We therefore imagine many scenarios where LAP is relevant.

732   A.2    L OSS ASSUMPTIONS
733
734   Within this work, and for many other methods designed for tackling noisy data, we make use of
735   the assumption that during training, neural networks learn non-noisy patterns before fitting to noisy
736   data, and therefore achieve a greater log-likelihood on non-noisy data during early training. This
737   is discussed within Zhang et al. (2021); Arpit et al. (2017); Han et al. (2018); Arazo et al. (2019);
738
      Yu et al. (2019); Shen & Sanghavi (2019); Li et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2021), which present both
      theoretical and empirical justifications. We also observe these training dynamics in Appendix A.16.1
739
      (specifically, Figure 7), in which the standard training method overfits to noisy data points significantly
740
      during the later training stages when compared to our method for learning from noisy data.

      A.3    F URTHER DETAILS ON DESIGN DECISIONS
743
744   W HY USE A WEIGHTED MEAN AND STANDARD DEVIATION ?
745
746   Firstly, we will discuss why the weighted mean and standard deviation is used for comparing sources
747
      with each other. This is done to allow for the identification of noisy sources by our method when
      different data sources might have significantly different noise levels. This is the case in Figure 2,
748
      where we have one source producing 100% noise and other sources with 5%, and 2.5% noise levels.
749
      Here, because the noise in the sources are so varied, the sources with lower noise levels would
750   likely never have an average loss more than the unweighted mean of the loss trajectory plus the
751   threshold created from the leniency multiplied by the unweighted standard deviation (Algorithm 1).
752   By weighting the mean and standard deviation with the calculated temperature, we are able to ensure
753   that all sources with noise level greater than 0% will be discovered in a number of model update steps
754   proportional to their noise level. This is shown in Figure 1, where the weighted mean and standard
755   deviation of the loss moves further to the left, as the weight reduces the influence of the unreliable
      source (on the right).


                                                          14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      W HY THE MEAN + STANDARD DEVIATION FOR THRESHOLDING ?
757
758   When calculating whether a source should be considered more or less unreliable, we need to be
759   able to calculate a reliability compared to the other sources. We therefore use a mean and standard
760   deviation over previous loss values, so that we can calculate a relative reliability. As described in
761   Section 3, it also provides an intuitive idea about the probability that a non-noisy source is incorrectly
762   identified as noisy on a single step (if the means of loss values are assumed to follow a Gaussian
763
      distribution, which is not unreasonable according to the central limit theorem).
764
765   W HY DO WE CLIP RELIABILITY AT 0?
766   In Algorithm 1, we clip Cs at 0 to ensure that when we have sources with noise levels of different
767   magnitudes, once the sources with larger noise levels have been heavily weighted and so have
768   insignificant effects on the weighted mean and standard deviation, we are able to start reducing the
769   reliability of other noisy sources immediately, rather than waiting for Cs to increase from some
770   negative value to 0. Also, since we use the tanh2 function to calculate loss (or gradient) scaling,
771   clipping Cs at 0 prevents us from introducing an error in which loss from reliable sources get scaled
772   with the same factor as unreliable sources.
773
774   E XPLANATION FOR USING tanh2
775
776
      Equation 3 describes the scaling of gradients (or equivalently, loss values) to reduce the contributions
      to model updates from less reliable data sources over time. Although any monotonically increasing
777
      function can be used for f (·) : [0, +∞) → [1, +∞), we find that 1/f (Cs ) = 1−tanh2 (0.005·δ ·Cs )
778
779
      provides some nice properties: y = tanh2 x has gradient of 0 at x = 0, and is asymptotic to y = 1,
      which are important qualities for calculating the model update scaling. The stationary point at x = 0
780
      means that small perturbations in the unreliability of each source around Cs = 0 have little effects on
781
      the scaling of model updates, which reduces the consequences of randomness in loss values from a
782   given source and ensures that source contributions are only significantly scaled once it is clear that
783   their loss values are consistently larger than those of the other sources. Secondly, since y = tanh2 x
784   is asymptotic to y = 1, the scale factor, 1 − ds (Equation 3) for sources that are calculated to be
785   unreliable eventually reduces to 0, allowing the model to “ignore” these data points as if they were
786   masked in late training.
787
788   E XPLANATION FOR SCALING WITH 0.005
789
790   The value of 0.005 used within the tanh2 function allows for the depression strength parameter δ to
791
      be on the scale of 1, as shown in Figure 2.

793   A.4    A LTERNATIVE INTERPRETATION
794
795
      Here, we present another interpretation of our proposed method which may provide inspiration for
      future research.
796
797   As discussed in Appendix A.2, the model evidence, p(D|M) (D: the dataset and M: the model)
798   is the probability that a dataset is generated by a given model (by marginalisation of the model
799   parameters). In our case, this could be used to calculate the probability that data from each source,
800   s is generated by the given model, namely: p(Ds |M) where Ds is the data generated by a source
801
      s. A model designed for non-noisy data should have a high p(Ds |M) when Ds has a low noise
      level, and a low p(Ds |M) when Ds has a high noise level. However, log(p(Ds |M)) is hard to
802
      compute for neural networks, and would require an approximation. It can be shown that p(D|M)
803
      can be approximated by the logsumexp of the log-likelihood over a training trajectory with noisy
804   gradients, which approximates the posterior samples with SGLD or SGHMC (Chen et al., 2014;
805   Welling & Teh, 2011). Within our work, when computing if a given source is noisy or non-noisy,
806   the mean loss for this source is calculated over a training trajectory of length equal to the history
807   length, H (given by µs in Algorithm 1). This is then compared to a weighted mean of the loss over a
808   training trajectory of length H of all sources excluding the source under scrutiny (given by µs′ in
809   Algorithm 1). This weighted mean is related to p(DR |M) where DR represents the data from all
      non-noisy sources, since the weights allow us to reduce the influence of noisy sources. Therefore, our


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      method is related to one in which the model evidence is calculated for each source at each step, and
811   where the sources with a relative model evidence higher than some threshold (defined in our work
812   through the leniency, λ) have larger influence on the model parameters when updating the weights.

1283   A.14                       S TRAINING THE ASSUMPTIONS OF OUR METHOD
1284
1285   To demonstrate the robustness of LAP, we now construct an experiment designed to strain the
1286
       assumptions we made when introducing our method.
1287   To work as intended, our proposed method assumes that all non-noisy sources contain similar data
1288   distributions. This allows us to say that the weighted likelihood ratio between a source under
1289   inspection and the other sources indicates whether to increase or decrease our reliability score
1290   (Equation 2). However, in real-world use cases, some sources might only produce a single class
1291   or a more challenging subset of classes. In such a case, we would like to understand whether our
1292
       method can correctly identify the noisy sources without mistakenly labelling the more challenging
       data source as noisy.
1293
1294   To construct this setting, we combined data from both MNIST (LeCun et al., 1998) and CIFAR-
1295   10 Krizhevsky (2009) (Appendix A.6). First, we evenly split the MNIST data into 99 sources, and
       applied random label noise to 95 of them. This created a setting with many noisy sources, which


                                                                                       24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       we hypothesised would be a challenging setting to learn in. We then randomly chose 2 classes from
1297   CIFAR-10 and assigned them to a single source, giving us 99 MNIST sources (with 95 of them noisy)
1298   and a single CIFAR-10 source (which remained non-noisy). We then kept the same non-noisy class
1299   labelling for MNIST from the original dataset (i.e: in the non-noisy data, a handwritten digit 0 was
1300   assigned class 0, etc) and mapped the two classes from CIFAR-10 to new classes: 10 and 11 – giving
1301   12 classes in total. Since CIFAR-10 is more challenging to classify than MNIST, this allowed us
1302   to construct a dataset in which almost all sources are easier to classify than the final source, which
1303   contains 2 classes of more difficult data to separate. We also ran the same experiment with no random
1304   label noise applied to the MNIST sources.
1305   To ensure the CIFAR-10 and MNIST images are the same size and have the same number of filters,
1306   we apply a grayscale and resizing transformation to the CIFAR-10 data. We then use a simple
1307   CNN consisting of two convolutional layers with 32 and 64 filters respectively, ReLU activation
1308   functions, max pooling layers (with kernel size 2), and two lineaer layers mapping representations to
1309   sizes 9216, 128, and finally 13. We use dropout, cross entropy loss, and the Adam optimiser with
1310   learning rate 0.001 and batches of size 128, trained for 25 epochs. The LAP parameters used were

1508   In practise using our implementation, with an MLP with hidden sizes 20 − 2000 − 2000 − 5, and

1510   backward pass through the model (without source reweighting) takes 15300 µ s ± 164 µ s and the
1511   reweighting of losses takes 469 µ s ± 10.8 µ s, increasing the time by 3%. With 50 and 100 sources,
       the reweighting of losses takes 1000 µ s ± 11.3 µ s and 1990 µ s ± 49.2 µ s respectively.
