009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose a new neural network based method for solving inverse problems for
012            partial differential equations (PDEs) by formulating the PDE inverse problem as
013            a bilevel optimization problem. At the upper level, we minimize the data loss
014            with respect to the PDE parameters. At the lower level, we train a neural network
015            to locally approximate the PDE solution operator in the neighborhood of a given
016            set of PDE parameters, which enables an accurate approximation of the descent
017            direction for the upper level optimization problem. The lower level loss function
018            includes the L2 norms of both the residual and its derivative with respect to the
019
               PDE parameters. We apply gradient descent simultaneously on both the upper and
               lower level optimization problems, leading to an effective and fast algorithm. The
020
               method, which we refer to as BiLO (Bilevel Local Operator learning), is also able
021
               to efficiently infer unknown functions in the PDEs through the introduction of an
022            auxiliary variable. Through extensive experiments over multiple PDE systems, we
023            demonstrate that our method enforces strong PDE constraints, is robust to sparse
024            and noisy data, and eliminates the need to balance the residual and the data loss,
025            which is inherent to the soft PDE constraints in many existing methods.

028   1     I NTRODUCTION
029
030   A fundamental task across various scientific and engineering fields is to infer the unknown pa-
031   rameters of a partial differential equation (PDE) from observed data. Applications include seismic
032   imaging (Deng et al., 2023; Martin et al., 2012; Yang et al., 2021b), electrical impedance tomog-
033   raphy (Uhlmann, 2009; Molinaro et al., 2023), personalized medicine (Lipková et al., 2019; Zhang
034   et al., 2024a; Schäfer et al., 2021; Subramanian et al., 2023), and climate modeling (Sen & Stoffa,
035
      2013). PDE inverse problems are commonly addressed within the frameworks of PDE-constrained
      optimization (Hinze et al., 2008) or Bayesian inference (Stuart, 2010). In the PDE constrained opti-
036
      mization framework, the objective is to minimize the difference between the observed data and the
037
      PDE solution, and the PDE is enforced as a constraint using adjoint or deep learning methods. In
038   the Bayesian inference framework, the inverse problem is formulated as a statistical inference prob-
039   lem, where the goal is to estimate the posterior distribution of the parameters given the data. This
040   requires sampling parameter space and solving the forward PDE multiple times. Here, we develop
041   a constrained optimization framework for solving PDE inverse problems using deep learning.
042
043   1.1   R ELATED WORK
044
045   The Adjoint Method is a widely used technique for computing the gradients of the objective func-
046   tion with respect to the PDE parameters using numerical PDE solvers in the PDE-constrained opti-
047   mization framework. This method provides accurate gradients and strongly satisfies the PDE con-
048   straint. However, the method requires explicitly deriving the adjoint equation and solving both
049   forward and adjoint equations at each iteration, which is complex and computationally expensive,
050
      especially for nonlinear or high-dimensional problems (Hinze et al., 2008; Plessix, 2006).
051   Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) have emerged as novel methods for solving inverse
052   problems in a PDE constrained optimization framework (Karniadakis et al., 2021; Raissi et al., 2019;
053   Jagtap et al., 2022b;a; Chen et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2024a; Yang et al., 2021a; Kapoor et al., 2024;
      Chen et al., 2020; Jagtap et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2024a). PINNs represent PDE solutions using


                                                         1
054
      neural networks and embed both the data and the PDE into the loss function through a mesh-free
055   approach. By minimizing the total loss, PINNs effectively solve the PDE, fit the data, and infer
056   the parameters simultaneously, showcasing integration of mathematical models with data-driven
057   learning processes. A related approach, Optimizing a Discrete Loss (ODIL), utilizes conventional
058   numerical discretizations of the PDEs and the loss is minimized over the parameters and the PDE
059   solutions at the grid points rather than the weights of a neural network (Karnakov et al., 2022;
060   Balcerak et al., 2024). However, in these methods, the PDE is enforced as a soft constraint, which
061   requires balancing the residual and the data loss, and can lead to a trade-off between fitting the data
062   and solving the PDE accurately.
063   Neural Operators (NOs) aim to approximate the PDE solution operator (parameter-to-solution
064   map) and can serve as surrogate models for the forward PDE solvers (Kovachki et al., 2022). Once
065   these surrogates are established, they can be integrated into a Bayesian inference framework or
066   other optimization algorithms to solve inverse problems, leveraging the speed of evaluating a neural
067   network (Zhou et al., 2024; Pathak et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2022b; Mao et al., 2023). Some examples
068   of operator learning frameworks include the Fourier Neural Operator (Li et al., 2021; 2024; White
069
      et al., 2023), DeepONet (Lu et al., 2021a; Wang et al., 2021b), In-context operator learning (Yang
      et al., 2023a), among others, e.g. (O’Leary-Roseberry et al., 2024; Molinaro et al., 2023). However,
070
      for solving the inverse problem, neural operators can encounter challenges when the ground truth is
071
      out of the distribution of the training dataset.
072
073   There are many other methods for PDE inverse problems using deep learning; see (Nganyu Tanyu
074   et al., 2023; Herrmann & Kollmannsberger, 2024; Brunton & Kutz, 2023) for more comprehensive
075
      reviews.

077   M AIN C ONTRIBUTIONS
078
079
      In this work, we focus on solving PDE inverse problems in the PDE-constrained optimization frame-
      work using deep learning methods. The contributions of this paper are as follows:
080
081   • We formulate the PDE inverse problem as a bilevel optimization problem, where the upper level
082     problem minimizes the data loss with respect to the PDE parameters, and the lower level problem
083     involves training a neural network to approximate the PDE solution operator locally at given PDE
084     parameters, enabling direct computationi of the descent direction for the upper level optimization
085     problem.
086   • At the lower level problem, we introduce the “residual-gradient” loss, which is the L2 norm of
087     derivative of the residual with respect to the PDE parameters. We show that this loss term com-
088     pels the neural network to approximate the PDE solution for a small neighborhood of the PDE
089     parameters, thus a “local operator”.
090
      • Extensive experiments over multiple PDE systems demonstrate that our novel formulation is both
091
        more accurate and more robust than other existing methods. It exhibits stronger PDE fidelity,
092     robustness to sparse and noisy data, and eliminates the need to balance the residual and the data
093     loss, a common issue in PDE-based soft constraints.
094
      • We solve the bilevel optimization problem using gradient descent simultaneously on both the
095
        upper and lower level optimization problems, leading to an effective and fast algorithm. The
096
        network architecture is simple and easy to implement.
097
098   • We extend our method to infer unknown functions that are also parameterized by neural networks
099
        through an auxiliary variable. This bypasses the need to learn a high-dimensional local operator.
100
      Our approach combines elements of PINN, operator learning, and the adjoint method. Our method
101
      is closely related to the PINN: both use neural network to represent the solution to the PDE, use
102   automatic differentiation to compute the PDE residual, and aim to solve the PDE and infer the pa-
103   rameters simultaneously. However, in the PINN, the PDE-constraint is enforced as a regularization
104   term (or soft constraint), leading to a trade-off between fitting the data and solving the PDE accu-
105   rately. Compared with operator learning, which solves the PDE for a wide range of parameters and
106   requires a large amount of synthetic data for training, our method only learns the operator local to
107   the PDE parameters at each step of the optimization process and does not require a synthetic dataset
      for training. Similar to the adjoint method, we aim to approximate the descent direction for the

                                                        2
108
      PDE parameters with respect to the data loss, but we do not require deriving and solving the adjoint
109   equation.

      We can also extend our method to learn an unknown function f (x) in the PDE, such as a variable
182
      diffusion coefficient in the Poisson equation or an initial condition in the heat equation. In these
183
      cases, the following PDE constrained optimization problem is solved:
184

                 f
186
187   where the constraint is a PDE that depends on the unknown function f . Given that these problems are
188   ill-posed, regularization of the unknown function is often necessary. A typical choice is the L2-norm
189   of the gradient of the unknown function, which penalizes non-smooth functions. While the selection
190   of an appropriate regularization form is critical and depends on the PDE problem, this paper assumes
191   such choices are predetermined, not an aspect of the method under direct consideration.
192   Suppose f is parameterized by a neural network f (x; V ) with weights V . A straightforward exten-
193   sion from the scalar parameter case is to learn the local operator of the form u(x, V ). However, this
194   would be computationally expensive, as the weights V can be very high dimensional. We propose
195   to introduce an auxiliary variable z = f (x), and find a local operator u(x, z) such that u(x, f (x))
196   solves the PDE locally at f . We define the following function a, which is the residual function with
197   an auxiliary variable z: a(x, z) := F (Dk u(x, z), ..., Du(x, z), u(x, z), z). If u is a local solution
198
      operator at f , then we should have: (1) a(x, f (x)) = 0, that the function u(x, f (x)) have zero
      residual, and (2) ∇z a(x, f (x)) = 0, that small variation of f should lead to small variation of the
199
      residual, which has the same interpretation as the parameter inference case (5). These two condi-
200
      tions translates to the corresponding residual loss and residual-gradient loss, similar to (4) and (5).
201   The definitions of the loss functions and the optimization problems are given in Appendix A.

205   The network architecture involves a simple modification at the input layer (embedding layer) of
206   the typical fully connected neural network: the embedding of the PDE parameters Θ is randomly
207   initialized and fixed during training, so that the residual-gradient loss can not be made 0 by setting
208   the embedding to 0. See Appendix B for more details.

      2.4    D IFFERENCE BETWEEN B I LO, PINN, AND NO FOR INVERSE PROBLEMS
246
247   Neural Operator Neural operators can serve as surrogate models for PDE solution operators, and
248   can be used in algorithms that require solving the forward PDE multiple times, such as Bayesian
249   inference or derivative-free optimization (Kaltenbach et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2022b), or gradient-
250   based optimization algorithms (Zhou et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2022b; Yang et al., 2023b). However,
251   if the objective is to estimate parameters from limited data, the considerable initial cost for data
252   generation and network training might seem excessive. The accuracy of specific PDE solutions
253   depends on the accuracy of the neural operator, and which may decrease if the true PDE parameters
254   fall outside the training data’s distribution (de Hoop et al., 2022). Thus, in the context of finding
255
      the best estimate of the parameters given the data in a PDE-constrained optimization framework, we
      mainly compare BiLO with PINNs.
256
257   PINN Within the PINN framework, the solution of the PDE is represented by a deep neural network
258   u(x; W ), where W denotes all the trainable weights of the neural network (Karniadakis et al., 2021;
259   Raissi et al., 2019; Lu et al., 2021b). Notice that the PDE parameters Θ are not part of the network
260   input. Therefore the data loss does not depend on the PDE parameters Θ directly, and we write the
261
      data loss as Ldat (W ).
262   Solving an inverse problem using PINN involves minimizing an unconstrained optimization prob-
263   lem, where the objective function is the weighted sum of the residual loss and the data loss
264                                      min Lres (W, Θ) + wdat Ldat (W )                             (13)
265                                      W,Θ

266    where wdat is the weight of the data loss. For simplicity of discussion, we assume the weight of
267   the residual loss is always 1. The key feature is that the PDE is enforced as a soft constraint, or
268   as a regularization term for fitting the data. The relationship between the PDE parameter and the
269   data loss is indirect: the descent directions of the PDE parameters are given by ∇Θ Lres , which are
      independent of the data loss.


                                                           5
270
      Challenges for PINNs Solving PDE inverse problems using PINNs can encounter challenges stem-
271   ming from the soft PDE constraint (13), especially when the data is sparse and noisy, or when the
272   PDE model does not fully explain the data (Zhang et al., 2024a). The soft PDE constraint can result
273   in a trade-off between fitting the data and solving the PDE accurately. In addition, since the PDE
274   parameters are updated in the descent direction of the residual loss, they can be biased toward pa-
275   rameters corresponding to very smooth solutions. It is important to recognize that PINNs can indeed
276   be effective for PDE inverse problems, if the weights are chosen properly or when data is abundant
277   and the noise is independent and identically distributed, as the the minimizer of the data loss still
278   gives a good approximation of the PDE solution.
279   There are many techniques to improve the performance of PINNs, such as adaptive sampling and
280   weighting of collocation points (Nabian et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2021b; Anagnos-
281   topoulos et al., 2024), new architectures (Jagtap & Karniadakis, 2020; Wang et al., 2024; 2021a;
282   Moseley et al., 2023), new optimization algorithms (Basir & Senocak, 2022b; Krishnapriyan et al.,
283   2021), new loss functions (Wang et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022; Son et al., 2021), adaptive weighting
284   of loss terms (Maddu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021a; McClenny & Braga-Neto, 2022; Wang et al.,
285
      2023). However, these techniques do not fundamentally change the soft PDE-constraints in the
      PINN framework. In our work, we propose a different optimization problem that does not involve
286
      a trade-off between the residual loss and the data loss, and our method can be used in conjunction
287
      with many of these techniques to improve the performance. Therefore, in the following numerical
288   experiments, we do not use any of these techniques, and we focus on comparing the two different
289   optimization formulations (BiLO and the soft PDE-constraints).
290
291
      The challenge of balancing trade-offs also motivated BPNHao et al. (2023), which applies a bilevel
      optimization framework to PDE inverse problems by representing the PDE solution with a neural
292
      network, using the residual loss for the lower-level problem, and approximating the upper-level
293
      hypergradient with Broyden’s method. In contrast, our approach incorporates the PDE parameter as
294   part of the network input, with the lower-level problem focused on approximating the local operator,
295   allowing more direct computation of the upper-level descent direction.

      4   C ONCLUSION
529
530
      In this work, we propose a Bi-level Local Operator (BiLO) learning framework for solving PDE
531   inverse problems: we minimize the data loss with respect to the PDE parameters at the upper level,
532   and learn the local solution operator of the PDE at the lower level. The bi-level optimization problem
533   is solved using simultaneous gradient descent, leading to an efficient algorithm. Empirical results
534   demonstrate more accurate parameter recovery and stronger fidelity to the underlying PDEs under
535   sparse and noisy data, compared with the soft PDE-constraint formulation, which faces the delicate
536   trade-off between adhering to the PDE constraints and accurately fitting the data. As limitations:
537   (1) the convergence results are mainly empirical with limited theoretical analysis, (2) the numerical
538   experiments are limited to low dimensional problems, and (3) the architecture of the neural network
539
      is simple. Future work includes theoretical analysis of the method, applying the method to more
      complex and higher dimensional problems, and improving the network architectures.

1609   the Fisher-KPP problem. The residual is evaluated on a 51×51 grid, while the data is evaluated on

1612   slightly easier and allowing for fewer fine-tuning steps to achieve convergence. Both BiLO and
1613   PINN are pretrained with an initial guess for 10,000 steps and fine-tuned for 20,000 steps.
1614   Figure 16 presents the performance metrics of both methods across different noise levels in the
1615   Fisher-KPP problem, with each noise level tested over five random trials. In terms of PDE parameter
1616   accuracy, BiLO consistently outperforms PINN across varying values of wdat and noise levels.
1617   Notably, the optimal wdat for PINN depends heavily on the noise level. For example, a relatively
1618   large wdat = 10 works well for low noise (σ 2 = 10−4 ) but performs poorly at higher noise levels
1619   (σ 2 = 10−2 ), suggesting that selecting the optimal wdat in practice may be challenging. In contrast,
       BiLO is more robust to noise and maintains consistent performance across all noise levels.
