009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Flow matching and diffusion generative models for tabular data face challenges in
012            modeling heterogeneous feature interrelationships, especially in data with contin-
013            uous and categorical input features. Capturing these interrelationships is crucial
014            as it allows these models to understand complex patterns and dependencies in
015            the underlying data. A promising option to address the challenge is to devise
016
               suitable encoding schemes for the input features before the generative modeling
               process. However, prior methods often rely on either suboptimal heuristics such as
017
               one-hot encoding of categorical features followed by separated modeling of cate-
018
               gorical/continuous features, or latent space diffusion models. Instead, our proposed
019            solution unifies the data space and jointly applies a single generative process across
020            all the encodings, efficiently capturing heterogeneous feature interrelationships.
021            Specifically, it employs encoding schemes such as PSK Encoding, Dictionary
022            Encoding, and Analog Bits that effectively convert categorical features into contin-
023            uous ones. Extensive experiments on datasets comprised of heterogeneous features
024            demonstrate that our encoding schemes, combined with Flow Matching or Diffu-
025            sion as our choice of generative model, significantly enhance model capabilities.
026            Our TabUnite models help address data heterogeneity, achieving superior perfor-
027
               mance across a broad suite of datasets, baselines, and benchmarks while generating
               accurate, robust, and diverse tabular data.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Tabular data are ubiquitous in data ecosystems in many sectors such as healthcare, finance, and
033   insurance (Clore et al., 2014; Moro et al., 2012; Datta, 2020). These industries utilize tabular data
034   generation for many practical purposes, including imputing missing values, reducing sparse data, and
035   better handling of imbalanced datasets (Jolicoeur-Martineau et al., 2024; Onishi & Meguro, 2023;
036   Sauber-Cole & Khoshgoftaar, 2022). However, a particular challenge inherent to tabular data that
037   generative models face is feature heterogeneity (Liu et al., 2023). Specifically, accounting for feature
038   heterogeneity is vital in flow matching and diffusion-based generative models, since they rely on
039
      continuous transformations of denoising score-matching, or invertible mappings between data, and
      latent spaces (Ho et al., 2020a; Lipman et al., 2022). Unlike homogeneous data modalities such
040
      as images or text, tabular data often contain mixed feature types, ranging from (dense) continuous
041
      features to (sparse) categorical features. More importantly, these tabular features, regardless of form,
042   are intertwined contextually (Borisov et al., 2023). For example, the numerical salary of a person is
043   correlated with their categorical age and education (Becker & Kohavi, 1996). Therefore capturing the
044   interrelationships between tabular heterogeneous features is crucial for flow and diffusion tabular
045   generative models to incorporate contextual knowledge for understanding complex patterns and
046   dependencies in the underlying data.
047
      A promising solution for the feature heterogeneity challenge is to devise suitable encoding schemes
048   for pre-processing the input features before applying the generative model. However, existing
049   methodologies often rely on (1) separate generative processes on discrete & continuous features
050   which do not model their correlations properly, (2) suboptimal encoding heuristics, or (3) learned
051   latent embedding which is parameter inefficient. For example, the one-hot encoding approach for
052   categorical variables leads to sparse representations in high dimensions, where generative models are
053   susceptible to underfitting (Krishnan et al., 2017; Poslavskaya & Korolev, 2023). On the other hand,
      creating a latent embedding space requires training an additional embedding model such as ResNet

      (He et al., 2015), or a Transformer-based β-VAE (Higgins et al., 2017; Kingma & Welling, 2013;
055   Zhang et al., 2023), and trained using e.g., self-supervised learning (Chen et al., 2020). Hence, the
056   quality of latent space generative models also depends on the embedding model’s capability to capture
057   the underlying dependency structure of the tabular data. To summarize, proper pre-processing of
058   heterogeneous features is crucial for high-quality tabular data generation, and poor encoding schemes
059   for the data features can lead to information loss that cannot be recovered from the generative model.
060
      Our goal is to generate high-quality synthetic tabular data using proficient categorical encoding
061   schemes to unify the data space. This enables a single flow or diffusion model to be applied, capturing
062   crucial heterogeneous feature interrelationships. In summary, our contributions are as follows:
063
064         1. We introduce two novel categorical encoding schemes, PSK Encoding, and Dictionary
065            Encoding, as well as leverage Analog Bits (Chen et al., 2022) from the discrete image domain
066            which seamlessly converts categorical variables into an efficient and compact continuous
067            representation. By facilitating the model to generate data in a unified continuous space, we
068
               can “unite” the mixed features to capture heterogeneous feature interrelationships based
               on a single flow/diffusion model on continuous inputs. Empirically, under our encoding
069
               schemes, the model learns to accommodate the heterogeneity of tabular features.
070
071         2. We conduct a comprehensive review between Flow Matching (Lipman et al., 2022) and
072
               DDPM (Ho et al., 2020b) as our generative model. Our results showcase that combining our
               categorical encoding schemes with DDPM attains state-of-the-art results on most settings.
073
               Conversely, Flow Matching speeds up the sampling speed dramatically, saving time and
074
               computation power, while yielding competitive results to DDPMs. Consequently, we
075            propose the following models: TabUnite(i2b)-Flow/DDPM, TabUnite(dic)-Flow/DDPM,
076            and TabUnite(psk)-Flow/DDPM. These models achieve superior performances across a wide
077            spectrum of tabular data generation baselines, datasets, and benchmarks. The architecture
078            of our models is illustrated in Table 1. Note that we also introduce TabFlow, a Flow
079            Matching/Discrete Flow Model (Campbell et al., 2024) that models heterogeneous features
080            separately, as a baseline.
081         3. We curate a large-scale heterogeneous tabular dataset from the Census dataset (Meek et al.,
082            2001) with over 80 mixed continuous/categorical features, and over 2.4 million samples.
083            This benchmark is significantly more challenging for tabular generative models than existing
084            benchmarks from public data repositories (Dua & Graff, 2017; Vanschoren et al., 2013)
085            which often have ≤ 100k datapoints and ≤ 30 features. It better reflects the scalability
086
               of tabular generative models, where our empirical results justify the importance of good
               encoding schemes for heterogeneous features.

089   2   R ELATED W ORKS
090
091   Generative Models in Tabular Data Generation. The latest tabular data generation methods have
092   made considerable progress compared to traditional methods such as Bayesian networks (Rabaey
093   et al., 2024) and SMOTE (Chawla et al., 2002). CTGAN and TVAE (Xu et al., 2019) were two
094   models based on the Generative Adversarial Network (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and Variational
095   Autoencoder (Kingma & Welling, 2013) architectures respectively. These models were applied along
096   with techniques such as conditional generation and mode-specific normalization to further learn
097   column-wise correlation. Other works such as GReaT (Borisov et al., 2023) and GOGGLE (Liu
098   et al., 2023) saw successes with the use of graph neural networks and autoregressive transformer
099
      architectures respectively in performing tabular data synthesis. Recently, Diffusion (Ho et al., 2020b)
      and Flow Matching (Lipman et al., 2022) provided new avenues for exploration within the tabular
100
      domain. This included STaSy (Kim et al., 2022), which employed a score-matching diffusion model
101
      paired with techniques such as self-paced learning and fine-tuning to stabilize the training process,
102   and CoDi (Lee et al., 2023), which used separate diffusion schemes for categorical and numerical
103   data along with interconditioning and contrastive learning to improve the synergy among different
104   features. TabDDPM (Kotelnikov et al., 2023) presented a similar diffusion scheme compared to
105   CoDi and showed that the simple concatenation of categorical and numerical data before and after
106   denoising led to improvements in performance. The most recent work in this domain was TabSYN
107   (Zhang et al., 2023), a latent diffusion model that transformed features into a unified embedding via a
      feature tokenizer before applying EDM diffusion (Karras et al., 2022) to generate synthetic data.

      Encoding Schemes. CoDi (Lee et al., 2023) and TabDDPM (Kotelnikov et al., 2023) utilized a
130
      separated data space, where Gaussian Diffusion (Ho et al., 2020b) was performed on numerical
131
      columns and Multinomial Diffusion (Hoogeboom et al., 2021) was performed on categorical columns,
132   with some additional techniques used to bind the two separate diffusion models. However, learning
133   the cross-correlation among various features through separate methods was often less effective than
134   conducting diffusion directly across a unified data space that included all features in the dataset. To
135   achieve this, various encoding schemes were employed to process both categorical and numerical
136   data so they occupy the same data space. One of the most widely used methods was one-hot encoding,
137   which was used in both STaSy (Kim et al., 2022) and TabSYN (Zhang et al., 2023) that encoded
138   categorical columns. One-hot encoding transformed categorical variables into a binary vector, where
139   each category was populated with 0’s with the exception of a single 1 that indicated the presence
140
      of a particular category. On top of one-hot encoding, TabSYN (Zhang et al., 2023) further used
      a column-wise feature tokenization technique that together transformed numerical and categorical
141
      features all into shared embeddings of the same length.
142
143   Flow and Diffusion Generative Models. Flow methods were introduced to the field of diffusion-
144   based deep generative models as Probability Flow ODEs (Song et al., 2021), which, originally based
145   on the concept of normalizing flows (Rezende & Mohamed, 2016), allowed for deterministic inference
146
      and exact likelihood evaluation. Compared to other diffusion-based methods such as score-matching
      (Song et al., 2021), DDPM (Ho et al., 2020b), and DDIM (Song et al., 2022a), flow-based models
147
      used continuous transformations defined by neural ODEs, to map samples from a simple distribution
148
      to a more complex target distribution. This allowed for efficient density estimation and generation of
149   high-dimensional data. In the context of tabular data, Flow Matching was applied to gradient-boosted
150   trees in place of neural networks to learn the vector field (Jolicoeur-Martineau et al., 2024).

      3.1   E NCODING S CHEMES
187
188   We explore PSK, Dictionary, (naming conventions inspired by (Carson, 1922; Mairal et al., 2008)),
189   and Analog Bits (Chen et al., 2022) to encode categorical features. An overview of our methods can
190   be found in Figure 2 and Table 1. One-hot encoding typically lead to high-dimensional sparse vectors
191   (Poslavskaya & Korolev, 2023) and causes underfitting when learning from it (Krishnan et al., 2017).
192   In contrast to traditional one-hot categorical encoding, our encoding methods offer more efficient and
193   dense representations while being able to incorporate crucial feature interrelationships.
194   Note that continuous features are encoded using the QuantileTransformer (Pedregosa et al., 2011) per
195   TabSYN’s and TabDDPM’s methodology (Zhang et al., 2023; Kotelnikov et al., 2023). Additionally,
196   the order in which the categories of a feature are assigned in our encoding schemes is based on
197   lexicographic ordering for simplicity purposes. In the following sections, we consider an element of a
198   categorical feature (single cell in a table), xcat                                  cat
                                                     i,j , that has K unique categories: xi,j ∈ {0, . . . , K − 1}.

200   PSK E NCODING – TAB U NITE ( PSK )
201

202   a unique phase angle in the complex plane, effectively transforming categorical variables into
203   continuous, circular representations. The K values will be evenly distributed as points on the unit
204   circle, such that the k-th category is positioned at phase θk = 2kπ

205   translated to cartesian coordinates in a complex domain, with a real component of cos(θk ), and an
206   imaginary component of sin(θk ) as per Euler’s Formula. The real and complex components are then
207   concatenated to create our PSK-encoding in the form of:

      Note that out-of-index (OOI) can occur due to the generative nature of models like Flow Matching
285
      and DDPM, which may produce continuous values that don’t directly correspond to the original
286   categorical encoding range. For PSK, OOI values are cast to the value of the 0-th index i.e., for 2
287   categories, we have {0, 1} → {[1, 0], [0, 1]}. If we encounter an OOI category like 4, it would be cast
288   to the value of the 0-th index. So: 4 → 0 → [1, 0]. For Dictionary, OOI values are cast to the closest
289   value in the embedding look-up table. For Analog Bits, OOI values are cast to the bit representation
290   of the numerically largest value. Although casting ensures that all generated categorical values fall
291   within the valid range, it introduces a bias. However, due to the generative capabilities of Flow
292   Matching and DDPM, out-of-index values rarely occur as shown by the performance of TabUnite.

294   3.2   F LOW M ATCHING AND D IFFUSION M ODEL
295
      After encoding our continuous and categorical      columns, we are presented with a unified and con-
296                                                PDcat
297   tinuous data space, Xi2b ∈ RN ×(Dcont + j=1 ⌈log2 (K:,j )⌉) , Xdic ∈ RN ×(Dcont +Dcat ) , and Xpsk ∈
298   RN ×(Dcont +2·Dcat ) . This enables us to directly model continuous state flow and diffusion models
299   without requiring a discrete state space (multinomial diffusion/discrete flow) or re-formulation of
300   the continuous flow/diffusion process (latent spaces). For convenience, we define Xunite to represent
301
      either Xi2b , Xdic or Xpsk , depending on the encoding method used.
302   Subsequently, we examine Flow Matching (FM) (Lipman et al., 2022) and Denoising Diffusion
303   Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) (Ho et al., 2020b) as our generative models where we apply our
304   encoding methods prior to the modeling process. FM is a simulation-free framework for training
305   continuous normalizing flow models (Chen et al., 2019) by replacing the stochastic diffusion process
306   with a predefined probability path constructed with theories from optimal transport (McCann, 1997).
307
      On the other hand, DDPM learns a reverse denoising process by gradually transforming Gaussian
      noises into data samples.
308
309   Our encoding schemes, combined with FM and DDPM, yield our TabUnite models which are referred
310   to as TabUnite(i2b)-Flow/DDPM, TabUnite(dic)-Flow/DDPM, and TabUnite(psk)-Flow/DDPM
311   respectively. Please find additional details regarding Flow Matching and DDPM in Appendix A, B,
312   Lipman et al. (2022), and Ho et al. (2020b). The following delineates the formulation for FM and
313
      DDPM in our setting.
314   Let x denote a sample from the dataset Xunite , i.e. x ∼ Xunite . For FM, We learn a vector field vt (x)
315   to approximate the true vector field ut (x|x1 ), yielding our objective function of the following:
316                              LFM (θ) = Eq(x1 ),pt (x|x1 ) ||vt (x) − ut (x|x1 )||2                    (4)
317
318   For DDPM, we learn a noise prediction network ϵθ (xt , t) to approximate the noise added during the
319   forward process, yielding our objective function of the following:
320
321                                                                             2
                                     LDDPM (θ) = Et,x0 ,ϵt ∥ϵt − ϵθ (xt , t)∥                             (5)

323   Relative to DDPM, FM synthesizes tabular data with a much higher sampling speed while also
      attaining a competitive generalization.

525   5      C ONCLUSION
526
527   We propose an efficient encoding framework for tabular flow and diffusion models that leverages
528   effective categorical encoding schemes to unify the data space. Since flow and diffusion models
529   rely on continuous transformations of denoising score-matching, or invertible mappings between the
530
      data and latent space, applying a single flow/diffusion model that captures heterogeneous feature
      interrelationships is crucial in improving generation quality. Our models are curated by employing
531
      PSK, Dictionary, and Analog Bits encoding that efficiently convert categorical variables into a dense
532
      and meaningful continuous representation, before applying Conditional Flow Matching to generate
533   the data. To further strengthen our findings on our categorical embedding schemes, we curate a
534   large-scale heterogeneous tabular dataset and benchmark TabUnite on it. Relative to the baselines,
535   our TabUnite models, notably TabUnite(psk)-Flow, outperform them across a wide range of datasets
536   while evaluated on a broad suite of benchmarks. Additionally, leveraging Flow Matching greatly
537   bolsters our sampling efficiency, saving computational cost and time while remaining competitive in
538   performance to DDPMs. Overall, we justify our claim of applying efficient encoding methods such
539   as TabUnite(psk) to a single efficient flow matching model on a coherent data space.

1084   developed baselines that would provide a direct and analogous comparison to justify flow-matching
1085   and our particular encoding methods. This includes the flow-matching-based one-hotFlow (oheFlow),
1086   TabFlow, and the DDPM-based i2bDDPM, dicDDPM, and one-hotDDPM (oheDDPM).
1087   Our DDPM-based baseline methods (i2bDDPM, dicDDPM, and oheDDPM) primarily inherit the
1088   design and implementation of TabDDPM (Kotelnikov et al., 2023). Whereas TabDDPM leverages
1089   two separate diffusion models, namely Gaussian diffusion and Multinomial diffusion, we devise
1090   i2bDDPM, dicDDPM, and oheDDPM to rely solely on Gaussian Diffusion. This is because their
1091   corresponding methods of Analog Bits, Dictionary Encoding, and One-Hot Encoding allow us to
1092   perform diffusion in a unified data space. Implementation of these methods is done by simply altering
1093   the data processing stage of the model. The DDPM architecture is largely kept the same.
1094   Our Flow-based baseline methods (oheFlow, TabFlow) are extended from the TabUnite architecture,
1095   which consists of i2bFlow and dicFlow. oheFlow, as the name suggests, utilizes One-Hot Encoding
1096   in its data processing stage. Tabflow, on the other hand, mirrors the idea of TabDDPM in that two
1097   separate models are used: one for learning categorical features and the other for learning numerical
1098   features. Here, the implementation combines ordinary Flow Matching (Lipman et al., 2022) with
1099   Discrete Flow Matching (Campbell et al., 2024). The respective results of these two models are
1100
       concatenated afterward to allow for the synthesis of mixed-type tabular data.
1101
1102   These methods all utilize the QuantileTransformer (Pedregosa et al., 2011) to process numerical data,
1103   which normalizes features to follow a uniform or normal distribution. This is done through sorting
1104   and ranking data points, and then mapping them to fit to the target distribution.

       specific points present in the dataset.

1437   TabDDPM, and TabDDIM (Song et al., 2022b) across various sampling steps. As early as 10
1438   steps, TabUnite(psk)-Flow converges, achieving high-quality structure and color in relation to the
1439   ideal “Ground Truth” (GT) visualization. However, there is no apparent “Olympic” structure for
1440   TabUnite(psk)-DDPM, TabDDPM, and TabDDIM. Although TabFlow presents an “Olympic” struc-
1441   ture, it is difficult to identify the colors. TabFlow requires approximately 100 steps to differentiate
1442   between the colors clearly. Even at 500 steps, TabDDIM is underperforming in terms of its color
1443   whereas TabDDPM is lacking in its structure where the rings are visually less precise when compared
1444
       to the GT. Therefore, the experiment highlights TabUnite(psk)-Flow’s faithfulness and integrity in
       generating high-quality samples that match the ground truth in a short period of sampling duration.

1449   from the numerical columns. We evaluate the synthesis of these categorical variables by taking the
1450
       absolute value of the difference between the real value and the synthesized value. Our result in
       Figure 5b depicts the accuracy of the generated categorical columns over the number of training
1451
       iterations. The models’ accuracy ranking from high to low at 100k training iteration is as follows:
