009                                             Abstract
010
011           Generative models unfairly penalize data belonging to minority classes, suf-
012           fer from model autophagy disorder (MADness), and learn biased estimates
013           of the underlying distribution parameters. Our theoretical and empirical
014           results show that training generative models with intentionally designed
015           hypernetworks leads to models that 1) are more fair when generating data-
016           points belonging to minority classes 2) are more stable in a self-consumed
017           (i.e., MAD) setting, and 3) learn parameters that are less statistically biased.
018           To further mitigate unfairness, MADness, and bias, we introduce a regu-
019
              larization term that penalizes discrepancies between a generative model’s
              estimated weights when trained on real data versus its own synthetic data.
020
              To facilitate training existing deep generative models within our framework,
021
              we offer a scalable implementation of hypernetworks that automatically
022           generates a hypernetwork architecture for any given generative model.

025   1     Introduction
026
027   Hypernetworks are neural networks that generate the weights of other neural networks (Ha
028   et al., 2017). Recent work has shown hypernetworks are useful for uncertainty quantification
029   (Rusu et al., 2019), few-shot learning (Sendera et al., 2022), continual learning (von Oswald
030   et al., 2022), among other tasks (Chauhan et al., 2023). To our knowledge, however, no work
031   has evaluated whether generative models trained with hypernetworks produce models that
032   are more fair in representing and generating data belonging to minority classes and more
033   robust to MAD collapse when their output is used as training data.
034
035   1.1   Motivation: Issues with Maximum Likelihood Estimation
036
037
      The inspiration for applying hypernetworks to improving fairness and mitigating MADness
      comes from a realization of the sub-optimiality of Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE),
038
      one of the most popular techniques for parameter estimation (Johnson, 2013). MLE is used
039
      to train most generative model architectures, including variational autoencoders (VAEs) (Pu
040   et al., 2016), normalizing flows (NFs) (Rezende and Mohamed, 2015), diffusion models (Ho
041   et al., 2020), and generative adversarial networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014)1 . Despite
042   MLE’s ubiquity, it often produces biased estimators of the underlying true parameters. The
043   most famous example was pointed out by Neyman and Scott (1948), showing that MLE can
044   produce inconsistent results when the number of parameters is large relative to the amount
045   of data (DasGupta, 2008a). In the Neyman-Scott problem, there is not enough data relative
046   to the number of parameters to mitigate the bias, leading to what Neyman called “false
047   estimations of the parameters”, or statistics where the stochastic limits are unequal to the
048
      values of the parameters to be estimated (Stigler, 2007). This overparameterized regime is
      precisely where most modern deep learning models are trained (Zhang et al., 2017; Belkin
049
      et al., 2019), leading to two problems resulting from this bias: unfairness (resulting from
050
      MLE overly prioritizing the generation of datapoints belonging to majority classes), and
051
052      1

053   the popular ELBO (Kingma and Welling, 2022). Any deep learning model that uses the negative

                                                       1
054
      MADness (where models trained on their own output generate poor data (Alemohammad
055   et al., 2023). For an illustration of how bias in MLE penalizes minority datapoints, see
056   Section 5.1, and for an illustration of how the bias in MLE significantly causes MADness,
057   see Section 5.3.
058
      We propose an alternative to MLE that ensures the statistics of parameters estimated from
059
      generated data match those estimated from observed data (See Equation 4). Our method,
060
      called Penalized Autophogy Estimation (PLE), differs from MLE in that it forces the learned
061   parameters to be recursively stable. Theoretical and emperical results show PLE constrains
062   MLE in a way that removes bias, mitigating the above problems and learning estimators that
063   are fairer and less susceptible to MADness. This recursive debiasing is easily translatable
064   to hypernetworks, where a forward pass maps real or synthetic data to the weights of a
065   downstream network. The difference in statistics between the weights estimated from real
066   versus synthetic data is effectively the bias, which can be penalized in an optimization routine
067   such as stochastic gradient descent. For more details on how this is implemented in a deep
068   learning context, see Section 3.2.

070   1.2   Fairness
071
      The term bias in parameter estimation is distinct from its colloquial usage2 , so to avoid
072
      ambiguity, we exclusively use the term “bias” to refer to the statistical bias of an estimator:
073
      b(θ̂) = EX|θ [θ̂] − θ. Recent work has shown that generative models carry and often amplify
074
      unbalances present in training data (Zhao et al., 2018). When MLE produces biased estimates
075
      of the parameters (as it often does), the parameterized distribution becomes even more
076   concentrated around existing high-probability events3 . Since probability distributions must
077   integrate to 1, increasing the frequency of some events comes at the expense of decreasing the
078   frequency of others. The other events in this case are those that are less frequent, or belong
079   to minority classes. As one can see in the first row in Figure H.3, biased maximum likelihood
080   estimates eventually collapse towards the mode(s) of the data and thus will underrepresent
081   data away from the mode. As a result, biased estimators will learn distributions where
082   majority-class data is overrpresented and minority-class data is underrepresented, while
083   unbiased estimators will learn distributions that more accurately represent the frequency of
084
      minority events.
085   While recent work has looked at improving fairness in generative models, our work differs
086   conceptually in its focus on removing statistical bias. By removing statistical bias, we avoid
087   over-representing data belonging to majority classes without needing to specify any protected
088   attributes or classes. Other approaches are either restricted to a single model type or require
089   data labeled with protected attributes. For instance, FairGAN (Xu et al., 2018) proposes a
090
      variant of GANs that requires labeled data with protected attributes, and can only be used
      for training GANs. Choi et al. (2020) proposes a method that uses two datasets in situations
091
      when a smaller dataset may better represent the population ratios, but does not address
092
      bias in the learning process itself.
093
094   The gradient clipping approach suggested by Kenfack et al. (2022) seeks to improve fairness
095   by biasing the dataset towards uniformity. They write that their goal is “to improve the
096
      ability of GAN models to uniformly generate samples from different groups, even when these
      groups are not equally represented in the training data.” This differs from our model in
097
      that 1) it actively biases the model to favor more uniform generation of points with different
098
      classes to achieve fairness, and 2) requires data labeled with protected attributes, which
099   may not be feasible to expect. Finally, Rajabi and Garibay (2022) suggests a method for
100   generating tabular data whose statistics match a reference dataset. This approach also relies
101   on explicit labels of the protected attribute in the training dataset and is restricted to GANs.
102   Our method can be used for any generative model and requires no labels or information
103   about the protected attribute.

      To evaluate the fairness of generative models, we look at the quality of generated samples
109   from models trained on unbalanced datasets containing a majority and minority class (CMaj
110   and CMin , respectively), where |CMaj | ≫ |CMin |. We define the imbalance ratio as the ratio
111   of datapoints belonging to the majority versus the minority class, RI = |CMaj |/|CMin |, and
112   is described by He and Garcia (2009) as the between-class imbalance4 .
113
      We compare this imbalance ratio to the ratio of representation quality from data generated
114
      from each class5 . Let S be a score function which evaluates the representation quality of
115
      samples generated from a generative model M (in our experiments in Section 5.1, S is
116   the inverse of the Frechet Inception Distance). S(M ) denotes the overall representation
117   quality, which can be broken up into two distinct components: S(M )Maj and S(M )Min ,
118   corresponding to the representation quality of samples from the majority class and minority
119   class, respectively. We introduce a quantity called the fairness ratio over a metric S, which
120   is defined as
121                                   RFair = S(M )Maj /S(M )Min .                              (1)
122
      Values of RFair closer to 1 correspond to models that do equally well representing majority
123   and minority datapoints, while values much larger than 1 refer to models that have better
124   performance representing datapoints from the majority class than the minority class. Virtually
125   all variants of empirical risk minimization (including MLE) weight each datapoint equally,
126   and we can thus expect that for a linear S(M ),
127
                                    |CMin |                      |CMax |
128                   S(M ) =                     S(M )Min +                   S(M )Max .                  (2)
129                             |CMin | + |CMax |            |CMin | + |CMax |
130   This implies that M will more accurately model the density around the majority class than
131   the minority class, and thus S(M )Max > S(M )Min . In other words, even if the training dataset
132   accurately represents the population frequencies of each classes, this relative imbalance often
133   harms performance when looking only at data from the minority class. This is empirically
134   observed with facial classifiers on unbalanced data (Buolamwini and Gebru, 2018) and is
135   seen for vanilla generative image models in Table 1.
136   In principle, weighing each datapoint equally corresponds to a process considered to be
137   procedurally fair (Tyler, 1996), despite the fact that the representation quality for samples
138   in the minority class may be far worse than those in the majority class. As a result, one can
139   argue that RFair = RI = |CMaj |/|CMin |, represents results from a procedurally fair training
140   process, where each datapoint is treated equally6 . However RFair ≫ RI is clearly unfair,
141   as it penalizes the generation of minority data far more than would be expected by its
142   underrepresentation in the training set. Comparing RI to RFair in practice shows standard
143   generative model training is far worse at representing data from minority classes than RI
144   would suggest. We observe that removing statistical bias when estimating parameters leads
145
      to increased performance representing data from minority classes. As a result, hypernetwork
      training and its bias-removal properties leads to more fair outcomes, as we show in Section
146
      5.1.

      1.3   Model Autophagy Disorder (MADness)
149
150   Recent work has shown that models trained on their own output, a process called a self-
151   consuming loop, progressively decrease in quality (precision) and diversity (recall) (Alemo-
152   hammad et al., 2023). Researchers call this phenomenon “going mad” or simply “mad cow,”
153   after bovine spongiform encephalopathy (BSE), the medical term for mad cow disease7 . This
154   phenomenon has become a growing concern for the machine learning community due to
155   the availability and ubiquity of synthetic data (Nikolenko, 2021). It often occurs with large

      language models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT: LLMs trained on their own output suffer in
163   diversity, eventually collapsing to a single point (Briesch et al., 2023).
164
165   Given the popularity and availability of these models, it is almost inevitable that future
166
      LLMs will be trained on a corpus containing at least some (if not much) synthetic data,
      implying that future versions of ChatGPT and similar LLMs may be subject to diversity
167
      collapse. We show that the presence of estimator bias worsens this phenomenon, and we
168
      propose a method of removing this bias for deep generative models in Section 4, effectively
169   slowing down this collapse. Biased maximum likelihood estimates of distribution parameters
170   also exhibit MADness, which is shown for several popular distributions in Figure 4.

      2     Background
174

      2.1   Unbiased Estimation
176   Bias correction literature is relevant to generative model training since MLE often produces
177   biased results (for a brief overview of generative models, see Section C). There are many
178   specific methods for reducing or eliminating bias in parameter estimation problems (Singh and
179   Singh, 1993), and bias correction methods have been proposed for generalized linear models
180   (Cordeiro and McCullagh, 1991), autoregressive-moving average (ARMA) models (Cordeiro
181   and Klein, 1994), convex regularized estimators (Bellec and Zhang, 2021), diffusion processes
182   (Tang and Chen, 2009) and specific distributions of interest (Singh et al., 2015; Cribari-Neto
183   and Vasconcellos, 2002). While bias correction is often done via bootstrapping (Efron,
184
      1979; Jiao and Han, 2020) or jackknifing (Quenouille, 1956; 1949), our work is most closely
      related to using parametric bootstrapping for bias correction (Kosmidis, 2014). Parametric
185
      bootstrapping uses synthetic or generated samples to estimate and remove empirical bias
186
      (Hall, 1992), and has been described by Efron (2012) as linking Bayesian and frequentist
187   perspectives. We explain how our estimation procedure also links Bayesian and frequentist
188   perspectives in Section A.
189
190
      Unlike much existing work, our method described in Equation 4 does not require assuming the
      bias is additive or multiplicative (Ferrari and Cribari-Neto, 1998). Furthermore, recent work
191
      has shown the penalizing the square of estimated bias produces asymptotic minimum-variance
192
      unbiased estimators (MVUEs) and asymptotically hits the Cramer-Rao bound (Diskin et al.,
193   2023). This is related to our relaxation of Equation 4 in Section 4.

212   3.1   Theoretical Formulation
213
214   PLE involves adding a constraint to the maximum likelihood estimator to force it to take
215   into account other possible models that could have generated the data. To illustrate the
      process, the conceptual model consists of several steps:

218            generative model parameterized by θ.

233   Equation 4 is essentially MLE with an equality constraint enforcing the statistics of synthetic
234   data, Y to match that of the observed data X. The key here is that the same estimation
235   procedure H (which will eventually be a hypernetwork in our experimental setup) that
236   produces θ from X can also be used to estimate parameters from synthetic data Y. When
237   this synthetic data is drawn from a distribution parameterized by θ itself, any change in
238   estimated parameters (in expectation) becomes a proxy for estimator bias.
239   The difference between Equation 4 and traditional debiasing techniques such as those
240   discussed in Section 2.1 is that PLE is recursive and adapts to the observed data X. As a
241   result, it does not depend on a specific choice of H and can be used in general to debias of
242   estimators when a closed form expression is available (See Sections E.4 and F.3) and, with a
243   few modifications, to debias the training generative models, as discussed in the next several
244   sections.

      4     Implementing H with Hypernetworks
271
272
      Solving the optimization problem in Equation 4 in real-world applications is intractable due to
273   two main computational bottlenecks. First, the operator H that produces the weights of the
274   generative model, P (X; θ), from training data, X, is not an explicit operator. Instead, it is
275   usually an optimization routine, (i.e., training of a generative model given data). As a result,
276   evaluating the PLE constraint in Equation 4 involves solving an inner optimization problem
277   that trains a secondary generative model on synthetic data. This is clearly intractable as it
278   requires training a new generative model at every iteration. Second, it is unclear how the
279   PLE constraint can be strictly imposed.
280   To address these challenges, we propose parameterizing H as a hypernetwork (Ha et al.,
281   2017) denoted by Hϕ , i.e., a neural network that is trained to predict the weights of another
282   neural network. In our case, we use Hϕ to learn the parameters of P (X; θ): Hϕ takes as
283   input training data and predicts the weights of a generative model that approximates the
284   distribution of the training data. This downstream generative model is never explicitly
285   trained via backpropagation; rather its weights are set via H. This allows the PLE constraint
286   to be tractably evaluated by a sample of data through Hϕ . To address the second challenge,
287
      we relax the optimization problem in Equation 4 so the constraint is turned into a penalty
      term.
288
289   Inspired by the form of H obtained analytically for some simple distributions in Appendix
290   E.1, and to impose permutation invariance — with respect to ordering of data points — we
291   propose the following functional form for the hypernetwork Hϕ following Radev et al. (2022):

428   To showcase the benefits of hypernetwork training when dealing with imbalanced datasets,
429   we estimate the weights of a one-dimensional Gaussian mixture model (GMM) with two
430   components that have considerable overlap. The means of the two Gaussian components
431   are 0.0 and 2.0 and the variances are both equal to 1.0. We vary the responsibility vector
      such that the contribution of one of the two Gaussian components is decreased. This makes


                                                                          8
432
      estimating the true GMM parameters challenging, especially in low-data regimes and as the
433   GMM becomes more imbalanced.
434
435   We estimate the GMM weights using expectation maximization (EM), the gold standard
436
      MLE approach, and compare the results to the estimated GMM via hypernetwork training.
      We rely on sklearn for estimating the GMM via the EM approach, choosing an optimization
437
      tolerance smaller than floating point precision, and setting the maximum number of iterations
438
      to 105 (we did not observe improvement by increasing this number). For hypernetwork
439   training, we define H to predict the means, variances, and the responsibility vector from the
440                                                     (1)
      training data in one step. The architecture of hϕ contains three fully-connected layers with
441                                                                        (2)
442   hidden dimensions of 8 and ReLU activation functions. We design hϕ in a similar manner,
443   with the only difference being the last layer, which contains three branches. Each branch
444   includes a linear layer aimed at predicting the mean, variance, and responsibility vector
445
      of the GMM. We ensure the predicted variance is positive by using a softplus activation
      function and ensure the predicted responsibilities are positive and sum to one by using a
446
      softmax function. The objective function in the PLE case is to maximize the likelihood
447
      of observing the training data under a GMM model whose weights are predicted via the
448   hypernetwork, while also including the PLE constraint as a penalty term with a weight of
449   0.1.

      divergence between the estimated and the ground truth GMMs using 105 samples. We repeat
452
      the GMM estimation for 100 different random seeds and average this quantity. Figure 3
453
      illustrates the difference between the KL divergence between PLE and the true GMM minus
454   the KL divergence between MLE and the true GMM, i.e., DKL (q MLE || p GMM )−DKL (q PLE ||
455   p GMM ). The negative values (shown in blue) correspond to settings where PLE outperforms

      Our experiments in this section show that models trained with PLE (either analytically
519   or via our hypernetwork approach) are less susceptible to MADness (Model Autophagy
520   Disorder (Alemohammad et al., 2023)) than models trained with MLE. In the following
521   experimental setups, parameters are estimated from fully synthetic data, generated either
522   from a model trained on the ground truth data, or the synthetic data from a previous
523   generation.
524
      An illustrative example involves estimating the parameter a of a one-sided uniform, U [0, a].
525   While the MLE and PLE of a are similar in their closed-form expression (See E.1 for more
526   details), the two quickly diverge and the MLE collapses to 0 as a new estimate is produced
527   from data generated from the previous estimate. MLE’s collapse is due to its bias: this bias
528   degrades the estimation quality after each generation, as seen in Figure 4. Section E.3 shows
529   the result of PLE when using a linear function class, while Section E.4 shows the result of
530   PLE when using a linear function of the nth order statistic. These two PLE results agree
531   in expectation and are both unbiased. Similar comparisons of MLE vs. PLE on various
532   distributions are shown in Figure 4.
533   We also trained BigGAN (Brock et al., 2018) on CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009)
534   and observe a similar result. Specifically, the BigGAN 11 data generation collapses after
535   3 iterations whereas our PLE method does not. We measure performance here using
536   FID (Heusel et al., 2017b), which does not change much for our method over the course of 3
537   generations yet completely explodes for the BigGAN baseline (see Figure 2). Additionally,
538   both the baseline and the PLE took about the same time, 14 hours on two GPUs.

      6   Conclusion
541
542
      Hypernetwork training is promising for the training of generative models due to its removal of
543   bias, its improvement of representation fairness, and its mitigation of MADness. Future work
544   can focus on additional applications of hypernetwork training, such as mitigating overfitting
545   due to the ability of hypernetworks to quantify uncertainty. Since hypernetwork training
546   involves sampling the generative model to evaluate the penalty, future work is needed to allow
547   tractable training of diffusion models, which are expensive to sample from12 . Furthermore,
548   future work can explore guidelines for setting and scheduling the PLE penalty λ during
549   training. By combining unbiased statistical estimation methods with deep learning, we
550   believe we can make artificial intelligence more fair and stable.

      A       PLE as Bayesian and Frequentest Estimation
829
830
      In the field of statistics, there is a divide between Bayesians and frequentists. The Bayesian
831   approach sees the fixed (and unknown) parameters as random variables (Wakefield, 2013).
832   In this case, the data is fixed while the parameters are random (Fornacon-Wood et al.,
833   2022). The benefit of the Bayesian approach is its ability to find optimal estimators: because
834   parameters are mapped to a probability measure, they can be compared and a maximum
835   conditioned on data can be found (when it exists). The drawback of Bayesian estimation is
836   that it requires an accurate prior; if the prior is inaccurate, the estimator may ignore the
837   data and produce a biased result (Dias et al., 2014). Additionally, detractors point out that
838   the choice of prior (and the form of the posterior itself) is often subjective Gelman (2008).
839   The frequentist approach, on the other hand, evaluates a hypothesis, which corresponds to
840   a specific choice of parameters, by calculating the probability of observed data under this
841   hypothesis. In this case, the hypothesis is fixed while the data is a random variable. As one
842   statistician writes:
843
                 As the name suggests, the frequentist approach is characterized by a fre-
844
                 quency view of probability, and the behavior of inferential procedures is
845
                 evaluated under hypothetical repeated sampling of the data (Wakefield,
846              2013).
847
848   Since the true parameters in an estimation problem are often assumed to be non-random,
849   the frequentist is correct to point out that uncertainty in estimation is usually epistemic, not
850   ontic. This observation, as true as it may be, comes at a cost: there becomes no obvious
851   way of choosing optimal parameters. For the Baysian, the a posteriori distribution serves as
852
      a goodness measure that tells us how well a given hypothesis fits the observed data. The
      frequentist, on the other hand, has no such measure: since the parameters are fixed, we
853
      are unable to make probability statements about the parameters given the observed data
854
      (Wagenmakers et al., 2008). This leads to the most fundamental limitation of frequentist
855   inference: it does not condition the observed data (Wagenmakers et al., 2008). Of course, a
856   frequentist can make choices based on the observed data (such as a particular choice of a
857   kernel function in Kernel Density Estimation), however the Bayesian will often point out
858   such assumptions are contrived (Hájek, 2007; Romeijn, 2022).
859
      The promise of a hybrid view comes from an acknowledgement of the benefits and short-
860
      comings of each approach. As Roderick Little suggests, “inferences under a particular model
861   should be Bayesian, but model assessment can and should involve frequentist ideas (Little,
862   2006)13 ” The Bayesian is correct to point out that we are estimating our parameters from
863
        13
             See also (Gelman, 2008; Rubin, 1984).


                                                     16
864
      observed data, so there will be uncertainty in the parameters themselves. The frequentist, on
865   the other hand, is correct to point out that the randomness in estimation results from the
866   data itself and not true parameters we wish to estimate. This is what causes the problem
867   in Section E.1, and with bias in MLE more generally: while the estimated parameters are
868   random under the data (which is true), the randomness of the data under the ground truth
869   model (which are used to estimate the parameters) is ignored.
870
      PLE bridges this gap: it treats the true parameters as nonrandom, while acknowledging
871   that the estimated parameters are random because the data is random. It incorporates the
872   uncertainty of the estimation process from the uncertainty of the data in a given model class.
873   As a result, in virtually all cases, the strength of estimation uncertainty is related to the
874   number of datapoints - for consistent models, as n → ∞, the mutual information between
875   the data and the true parameters increases.

      B       Sampling, Randomness, and Modal Logic
878
879   Probability theory is a way of saying how “likely” something is to happen. A probability
880   space is a 3−element tuple, S = (Ω, F, P ), consisting of
881
882            • A sample space Ω which is a (nonempty) set of all possible outcomes ω ∈ Ω
883            • An event space F , which is a set of events f which themselves are sets of outcomes.
884              More precisely, F is a σ-algebra over Ω (Stroock, 2010).
885            • A probability function P which assigns each f ∈ F to a probability, which is a
886              number between 0 and 1 inclusive.
887
888   This triple must satisfy a set of probability axioms to be considered a legitimate probability
889   space (Papoulis and Pillai, 2002). An event with probability 1 is said to happen almost surely
890   while an event with zero probability is said to happen almost never. Note that not all zero
891   probability events are logically impossible (The probability of any outcome on a continuous
892
      probability distribution is 0, even after one observes such an outcome). Impossible events
      are thus those not contained within F ; these are assigned probability of zero by definition.
893
894   This idea of probability is closely related to the idea possible worlds, other ways the world
895   could have been. We use the semantics of modal logic to write the different “modes” of truth,
896   including necessary propositions (□x), possible propositions (♢y) and impossible propositions
897   (¬♢z) (Garson, 2024). Consider a fair dice roll on a six-sided die, where each outcome is the
898
      corresponding number on top of the die (from 1-6). Let fn<10 be the event that one rolls
      a number less than 10. Since all possible outcomes have a value less than 10, we say it is
899
      necessary that one rolls a number less than 10, or □fn<10 . Let the f4 be the event that one
900
      rolls a 4. It is possible that one rolls a 4, so we say ♢f4 , however it is not necessary, because
901   one could have rolled a 5 instead. It is impossible to roll a 7, so we say ¬♢roll a seven.
902   Rolling a seven is not an event because it does not exist in Ω; 7 is not a legitimate outcome
903   as constructed14 .
904
      An outcome x ∼ P (θ) from a probability distribution P cannot feature all possibilities from
905
      the distribution except in the case of a trivial distribution (which has no randomness). For
906
      nontrivial distributions, or ones with infinite support, there will always be at least some
907   other outcome with nonzero probability x̃ that could have happened if our observed outcome
908   was different. Semantically, we say there is a possible world in which x̃ happens as long as
909   x̃ ∈ F (Menzel, 2023).

      C       Background on Generative Models
913
      Generative models use data to estimate an unknown probability distribution, generating
914
      “new” data by sampling from the estimated distribution. A good generative model generates
915   data whose statistics match that of the observed data used to train the model (Schwarz et al.,
916   2021). From a Bayesian perspective, training a generative model amounts to using observed
917
        14
             It is common to notate such events using the empty set.


                                                       17
918
      data to update the estimated parameters that are assumed to give rise to the data. The
919   architecture of a generative model can be viewed as a prior on the estimated distribution,
920   constraining the overall shape of the distribution itself (Ulyanov et al., 2018).
921
922
      Of course, the data are merely samples of (what is usually assumed to be) an underlying
      stationary stochastic process with fixed parameters. Generative models seek to find these
923
      fixed parameters so as to determine the shape and location of the distribution from which
924
      our data are assumed to have been drawn. Randomness is introduced in the estimation
925   process in two ways: 1) Each datapoint is random and 2) the sampling process itself contains
926   randomness based on the relationship between the given datapoints. More data implies
927   a given model can better discriminate between competing parameter choices, decreasing
928   estimation uncertainty and thus estimation randomness. In many cases, an infinite amount
929   of data would lead to an estimator that is completely deterministic, as there would be enough
930   data to remove any estimation uncertainty.
931
932   D     Maximum Likelihood Estimation
933
934   D.1    Model Estimation
935
936   Model estimation involves estimating an unknown probability function P (one of the elements
937   of a probability triple (Ω, F, P ) (Kolmogorov and Bharucha-Reid, 2018)) from samples, each
938   sample an outcome from its sample space (see B for more details). We notate this as
939
      X = [x1 , . . . xn ], xi ∈ Ω, i = 1, . . . , n. In most cases (and in all practical cases), the number
      of samples we observe is finite (|X| ∈ Z+ ). Estimating P from a finite number of samples is
940
      made difficult by the fact that we not only want to estimate the probability of the observed
941
      events (corresponding to the samples), but also estimate the probability of unobserved events
942   that could have happened. Since we do not directly observe all events or the probability
943   function itself, we must estimate the elements of a probability triple (Ω, F and P ) from the
944   samples we do observe.
945
      Estimating P is especially challenging when Ω is continuous. In these cases, we are estimating
946
      Ω from a set with zero measure in Ω. When |Ω| is finite, a sample of Ω may contain all
947
      possible outcomes, and thus the only unknown may be P . However, no finite sample can
948   come close to exhausting all outcomes in Ω when |Ω| is infinite.
949
950   To make the estimation problem more tractable, we often assume that P has a parametric
951
      form. Saying P is parameterized by θ means we can know everything there is to know about
      P from θ. To estimate P , we first estimate (or a priori assume) Ω, and then estimate θ.
952
953
954
      D.2    Maximum Likelihood Parameter Estimation
955   The maximum likelihood parameter estimation procedure chooses parameter values that
956   maximize the likelihood function: the conditional probability of the observed data on the
957   parameters, P (X; θ) (Murphy, 2012). The problem with maximum likelihood is that it is too
958   “greedy.” The maximum likelihood parameter estimation method does take into account the
959   randomness associated with the assumed parametric form of the distribution, but not the
960   randomness associated with choosing θ from n samples. Consequently, maximum likelihood
961   estimates are only guaranteed to be asymptotically unbiased and consistent (Johnson, 2013)
962   but not unbiased for any n. Our method, PLE, seeks to incorporate the randomness
963
      associated with sampling n times from a given parametrically defined probability function.
      This approach effectively de-biases the maximum likelihood estimate proportional to the
964
      uncertainty involved in the sampling process itself.

1292   H.1   Choosing H
1293
1294   As we show in E.1, as long as θ̂PLE (calculated from Equation 3) is in the domain of H,
1295   the choice of H itself does not matter. In other words, PLE is transformation invariant for
       any transformation that could produce θ̂PLE . In the absence of any prior information about


                                                                                       24
1296
       the form of H, the form of the MLE can be used as a reasonable prior for selecting H and
1297
       thus θ̂PLE . In cases when MLE is strictly unbiased (not just asymptotically unbiased, as it is
1298
       guaranteed to be (Johnson, 2013)), PLE will give the same result as MLE. At the very least,
1299   the MLE should be in the range of H, so when the MLE is unbiased, it is chosen.

       H.2   Implementation of Hypernetworks
1303
       In our implementation, we are able to automatically create a hypernetwork architecture given
1304
       a generative model architecture. As long as the given architecture is a torch.nn.Module, our
1305   hypernetwork implementation outputs a named dictionary containing the layer names and
1306   weights for the target generative model. In addition, to allow for seamless usage of our PLE
1307   formulation as a drop-in replacement for existing objective functions for generative models,
1308   we exploit the abstractions provided by PyTorch that allow functional calls to any PyTorch
1309   torch.nn.Module that uses the predicted weights from our hypernetwork architecture. This
1310   allows us train existing generative models with PLE in just a few additional lines of code.
1311   More details regarding our implementation and code to reproduce the results can be found
1312   on GitHub15 .

1366   I   Extending Fairness
1367
1368
       While our definition of fairness considers only two classes of data (a majority and a minority
1369   class), this idea can easily be extended multi-class data. Suppose we have a dataset X with n
1370   classes and a labeling of the data class(x) = y. Consider a partitioning of X into each of these
1371   classes, where Xi = {x ∈ X|class(x) = i} i = 1, . . . , n , where X = X1 ∪ X2 ∪ · · · ∪ Xn
1372   and |X| = |X1 | + |X2 | + · · · + |Xn |
1373
       This partitioning allows us to consider the imbalance ratios of data belonging to each class:
1374
       Let Ri:j = |Xi |/|Xj |. We also consider the ratio score ratio between generated data from
1375   each class, SRi:j = S(M )i /S(M )j (note that with two classes, this is simply the fairness
1376   ratio between the majority and minority class indices). With multiple classes, the ratio of
1377   representation scores for a metric M on generated data from two classes can be compared
1378   with the ratio of frequencies of data belonging to each class. This is a multi-class extension
1379   of the imbalance and fairness ratios described in Section 1.2.
1380
       Additionally, what is important to report is the generated freuqency of data belonging to
1381   each class. When the task is unconditional, an external classifier or oracle is needed to
1382   determine which class each generated datapoint belongs to. The frequency of generated
1383   data belonging to a given class should be compared to that class’s frequency in the training
1384   data; i.e. if X̂ refers to synthetic data from a generative model and X̂i i = 1, . . . , n is a
1385   partitioning of the data according to its classified value, one should compare
1386
1387                                         |X̂i |/|X̂| ⋚ |Xi |/|X|
1388
       to determine if the generative model generates biased data according to the given classification.

1391   J    Ablation Experiments for PLE Penalty
1392
1393   Our choice of λ = 0.1 was based on ablation experiments for the GMM example shown in
1394   Section 5.2. These experiments show that λ = 0.1 is the best choice; it performs better than
1395   MLE in the low-data regime and has less of a negative impact for larger samples than λ = 0.0
1396   and λ ≥ 10. Very large values of λ cause the training to ignore the maximum likelihood
1397   term altogether which leads to poor performance.
1398   Note that hyperparameter training provides an advantage over MLE even when the PLE
1399   penalty λ is zero, as shown in Figure 6). We believe some of this benefit comes from
1400   averaging the weights from different batches, which is part of hyperparameter training shown
1401   in Equation 6. Work by Izmailov et al. (2019) has shown that averaging in weight space (as
1402   is done with evaluating the hypernetwork on batches of data) leads to better generalization
1403   and wider optima. As shown in the following figures, this averaging accounts for some (but
       not all) of the benefit of using our hypernetwork approach for training generative models.
