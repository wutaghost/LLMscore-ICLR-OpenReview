                                                  Abstract
009
010
011           For learned image representations, basic autoencoders often produce blurry re-
012           sults. Reconstruction quality can be improved by incorporating additional penal-
013           ties such as adversarial (GAN) and perceptual losses. Arguably, these approaches
014           lack a principled interpretation. Concurrently, in generative settings diffusion has
015           demonstrated a remarkable ability to create crisp, high quality results and has
016           solid theoretical underpinnings (from variational inference to direct study as the
017
              Fisher Divergence). Our work combines autoencoder representation learning with
              diffusion and is, to our knowledge, the first to demonstrate jointly learning a contin-
018
              uous encoder and decoder under a diffusion-based loss and showing that it can lead
019
              to higher compression and better generation.. We demonstrate that this approach
020           yields better reconstruction quality as compared to GAN-based autoencoders while
021           being easier to tune. We also show that the resulting representation is easier to
022           model with a latent diffusion model as compared to the representation obtained
023           from a state-of-the-art GAN-based loss. Since our decoder is stochastic, it can
024           generate details not encoded in the otherwise deterministic latent representation;
025           we therefore name our approach “Sample what you can’t compress”, or SWYCC
026           for short.

032   1   Introduction
033
034
035
      Image autoencoders ultimately necessitate a pixel-level loss to measure and minimize distortion. A
036   common choice is to use mean squared error (MSE). This is a problem for image and video models
037   because MSE favors low frequencies over high frequencies (Rybkin, 2018). Although generalized
038   robust loss functions have been developed (Barron, 2019), they are insufficient on their own for
039   avoiding blurry reconstructions. A popular fix is to augment a pixel-level loss with additional
040   penalties. Typically, MSE is still used because it is easy to optimize due to its linear gradient.
041
      For example, Rombach et al. (2021) use a combination of MSE, perceptual loss, and adversarial
042   loss. Esser et al. (2021) noted that an adversarial loss helps them get high-quality images with
043   realistic textures. Unfortunately GANs remain challenging to train; which was most recently noted
044   by Kang et al. (2023), when they couldn’t naively scale up their architecture. The diversity of their
045   outputs is also limited, because modern GAN based decoders are deterministic, and thus lack the
046   capacity to sample multiple different possibilities.

065   As an alternative, this paper describes a technique for using a diffusion loss to learn an autoencoder.
066   The diffusion loss is sensible because it is a proper scoring rule with favorable theoretical properties
067   such as being formally connected to KL divergence (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015). It has proven itself
068   capable of generating crisp results with high perceptual quality as reflected by human evaluation
069   studies (Hoogeboom et al., 2023b; Karras et al., 2024b).
070   To demonstrate its simplicity, we take a popular encoder architecture Chang et al. (2022) and
071   marry it with a U-Net decoder, a popular denoising architecture for diffusion (Hoogeboom et al.,
072   2023b). With some additional details outlined in section 2, we show that this approach achieves
073   lower distortion at all compression levels as measured by the CMMD metric (Jayasumana et al.,
074   2024). Because our decoder is able to sample details at test-time that are not encoded in the latents,
075   we call our approach “Sample what you can’t compress” or SWYCC for short. This work will show
076
      that,
077                • SWYCC achieves lower reconstruction distortion at all tested compression levels vs SOTA
078                  GAN-based autoencoders (section 3).
079
                   • SWYCC representations enable qualitatively better latent diffusion generation results at
080                  higher compression levels vs SOTA GAN-based autoencoders (section 3.3).
081
                   • Splitting the decoder into two parts improves training dynamics (section 3.1 and 3.2).

      2.1    Architectural Details
157
158   Encoder: We use a fully convolutional encoder in all of our experiments, whose specifics we borrow
159   from MaskGIT(Chang et al., 2022). The encoder consists of multiple ResNet(He et al., 2016) blocks
160   stacked on top of each other, with GeLU(Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017) for its non-linearities and
161   GroupNorm(Wu & He, 2018) for training stability. The ResNet blocks are interspersed with strided
162   convolutions with stride 2 which achieves a 2× downsampling by itself. To get the 8 × 8 patch size,
163   we use 4 ResNet blocks with 3 downsampling blocks. The encoder architecture is common for all
164   of our experiments, and we only change the number of channels at the output layer to achieve the
165
      desired compression ratio.
166   Decoder: For the decoder in the GAN baseline and DInitial we use an architecture that is the reverse
167   of the encoder. For up-sampling, we use the depth-to-space operation. Just like the encoder, we
168   have 4 ResNet blocks interspersed with 3 depth-to-space operations. For DRefine we use a U-Net
169   as defined by Hoogeboom et al. (2023b). The U-Net has 4 ResNet blocks for downsampling and
170   corresponding 4 ResNet blocks for upsampling with residual connections between blocks of the same
171
      resolution. After 4 downsampling stages, when resolution is 16 × 16, we use a self-attention block
      to give the network additional capacity.

213
214   In this section we explore how the GAN-based loss compares to our approach. Without loss of
215
      generality, we define the relative compression ratio of 1 to be a network that maps 8×8 RGB patches
      to an 8 dimensional latent vector. Effectively, this means for our encoder E if and x ∈ R256×256×3 ,
216
      then E(x) ∈ R32×32×C where C = 8. In general, to achieve a relative compression ratio of K we set
217         8
      C=K     . The effect of increasing the compression ratio is plotted in Figure 1. Observe that distortion
218   degrades much more rapidly for the GAN based auto-encoder as measured by CMMD (Jayasumana
219   et al., 2024) which Imagen-3 (Imagen-Team-Google et al., 2024) showed better correlates with
220   human perception.
221
      Not only is our approach better at all compression levels; the gap between the GAN based autoen-
222
      coder and SWYCC widens as we increase the relative compression ratio. Using the much simpler
223
      diffusion formulation under Equation 3, we are able to reconstruct crisp looking images with de-
224   tailed textures (See Figure 3). Our method has the added benefit that we do not need to tune
225   any GAN related hyper-parameters, and can scale up effectively using the large body of diffusion
226   literature (Karras et al. (2022), Karras et al. (2024a)).

      3.5       Architecture and hyper-parameters
324   Autoencoder training hyper-parameters We train all of our models on the ImageNet dataset
325   resized at 256 × 256 resolution. During training, we resize the image such that the shorter side
326   measures 256 pixels and take a random crop in that image of size 256×256. For measuring reference
327   statistics on the validation set, we take the largest possible center square crop. All of our models
328   are trained at a batch size of 256 for 106 steps which roughly equals 200 epochs.

      GAN baseline: We use the popular convolutional encoder-decoder architecture popularized by
330
      MaskGIT (Chang et al., 2022) in our GAN-based baselines. This autoencoder design is used by
331   many image models, including FSQ (Mentzer et al., 2024) and GIVT (Tschannen et al., 2023), and
332   was extended to the video domain by MAGVIT-v2 (Yu et al., 2024), VideoPoet (Kondratyuk et al.,
333   2024), and WALT (Gupta et al., 2023). We take advantage of decoder improvements developed
334   by MAGVIT-v2 (see Section 3.2 in (Yu et al., 2024)) to improve reconstruction quality. Note that
335   while the video models enhance the base autoencoder architecture with 3D convolution to integrate

337   and thus are essentially unchanged in our model.

358   4   Related Work
359
360
361   Autoencoders for 2-stage generation: For discrete representation learning, van den Oord et al.
362
      (2017) showed the usefulness of the 2-stage modeling approach. In this broad category, the first
      stage fits an autoencoder to the training data with the goal of learning a compressed representation
363
      useful for reconstructing images. This is followed by a second stage where the encoder is frozen and
364
      a generative model is trained to predict the latent representation based on a conditioning signal.
365   This approach regained popularity when Ramesh et al. (2021) showed that it can be used for zero-
366   shot text generation, and is now the dominant approach for image and video generation (Chang
367   et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2024; Chang et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2023; Kondratyuk et al., 2024).
368
      Adversarial losses: Esser et al. (2021) extended the autoencoder from van den Oord et al. (2017)
369
      with two important new losses, the perceptual loss and the adversarial loss, taking inspiration
370
      from the works of Johnson et al. (2016) and Isola et al. (2017). The perceptual loss is usually
371   defined as the L2 loss between a latent representation of the original and reconstructed image.
372   The latent representation, for example, can be extract from the final layer activations of a ResNet
373   optimized to classify ImageNet images. The adversarial loss is a patch-based discriminator that
374   uses a discriminator network to predict at a patch-level whether it is real or fake. This encourages
375   the decoder to produce realistic looking textures.

376
      Latent Diffusion:       Rombach et al. (2021) popularized text-to-image generation using latent
377
      diffusion models. They kept the autoencoder from Esser et al. (2021) intact and simply removed
378   the quantization layer. This accelerated diffusion model research in the community owing to the
379   fact that the latent space was much smaller than the pixel space, which allows fast training and
380   inference compared to diffusion models like Imagen that sample pixels directly (Saharia et al., 2022).
381
      2-stage diffusion autoencoders: Pandey et al. (2022) and Preechakul et al. (2022) both train
382
      decoders with a diffusion loss. The crucial difference is that both these works train their autoencoder
383
      in 2-stages.
384
385   Compression and diffusion: Hoogeboom et al. (2023a) showed that diffusion models can be used
386   for compression. Crucially, compared to our approach they use a frozen autoencoder, and do not
387
      train their autoencoder end-to-end. They also use an objective based on modified flow matching. In
      contrast, we did not modify the loss or the sampling algorithm. Pandey et al. (2022) use a similar
388
      approach with a 2-stage autoencoder training process for their autoencoder.
389
390   In a similar context, Yang & Mandt (2024) developed an end-to-end optimized compression model
391   using a diffusion decoder. They show improved perceptual quality compared to earlier GAN-
392   based compression methods at the expense of higher distortion (pixel-level reconstruction accuracy).
393
      Different from our approach, they use a discrete latent space, which is required for state-of-the-
      art compression rates achieved via entropy coding. This limits the reconstruction quality but is
394
      required for a compression model that ultimately seeks to minimize a rate-distortion objective, not
395
      just a reconstruction and sampling quality objective.
396
397   Würstchen architecture (Pernias et al., 2024) has shown that training a cascade of diffusion models
398   improves training efficiency. Crucially Würstchen, does not apply a diffusion loss on pixels, instead
399
      resorting to a GAN loss. Whang et al. (2022) also pointed out that diffusion can fix a lot of pixel
      level artifacts, although they do not investigate training autoencoders.
400
401   Shi et al. (2022) learn an autoencoder using a diffusion loss for discrete vector quantized encodings.
402   We differ from them in 2 crucial ways; (i) we learn a continuous representation (ii) We show that
403   our architecture produces latents that are better for latent diffision by showing that we can achieve
404   higher compression and better generation performance (Figure 2) .

406   5   Conclusion
407
408   We have described a general autoencoder framework that uses a diffusion based decoder. Compared
409   to decoders that use GANs, our system is much more easier to tune and has the same theoretical
410   underpinnings as diffusion models. We showed our method produces sigificantly less distortions
411   as compared to GAN based autoencoders in Figure 1 and are better behaved as latent spaces for
412   diffusion in Figure 2. In Section 3.1 and 3.2 we studied the hyper-parameter settings on the 2 major
413   components of our decoder, DInitial and DRefine .
414   Possible extensions: The autoencoder technique we describe is fairly general and can be extended
415   to any other continuous modality like audio, video or 3D-point clouds. In addition, all improvements
416   to diffusion algorithms like those by Karras et al. (2024a) can be carried over.
417
      Limitations: The main limitation of our method is the increase in inference cost during decoding.
418   This can be partly mitigated by using fewer steps like in Figure 6. In addition, techniques used to
419   improve diffusion sampling time like Progressive distillation (Salimans & Ho, 2022) and Instaflow
420   (Liu et al., 2024) are also prudent. Because of DRefine , our training time compute cost is also higher.
421   Combining DInitial and DRefine in a clever way to reduce training time compute and memory could
422   be a promising research direction.
