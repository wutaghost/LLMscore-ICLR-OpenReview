                                                 A BSTRACT
011
012
               Sparse matrix computations are becoming increasingly significant in deep learn-
               ing and graph analytics, driving the development of specialized hardware systems
013
               known as accelerators to meet the growing need for optimized performance. Op-
014
               timizing these computations, however, presents significant challenges due to their
015            sensitivity to variations in input sparsity patterns and code optimizations. While
016            ML-based cost models and search techniques have shown promise in optimiz-
017            ing sparse matrix computations in general-purpose hardware like CPUs, these
018            cost models require large datasets for effective training. Collecting such ex-
019            tensive datasets is particularly impractical for emerging hardware platforms that
020            only have access to expensive simulators in the early design stages. To over-
021            come this, we propose DASH, which trains learned cost models using low-cost
022            data samples from widely accessible general-purpose hardware (such as CPUs),
023
               followed by few-shot fine-tuning to efficiently adapt to emerging hardware plat-
               forms. DASH introduces a novel approach that leverages the homogeneity of input
024
               features across different hardware platforms while effectively mitigating hetero-
025
               geneity. This enables DASH to achieve comparable accuracy using only 5% of
026            the data samples required by a cost model trained exclusively using data samples
027            from an accelerator. We evaluate DASH on two critical sparse operations—SpMM
028            and SDDMM—on an emerging sparse accelerator using 715 distinct sparsity pat-
029            terns. Our experimental results show that DASH outperforms existing techniques
030            that use transfer learning by 28.44%, achieving average speedups of 1.47× (up to
031            5.46×) for SpMM and 1.39× (up to 4.22×) for SDDMM.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
      Sparse matrix computations have gained increased significance with the recent advancements in
035
      sparse deep learning and graph analytics (Beltagy et al. (2020); Ye & Ji (2021); Child et al. (2019);
036   Dao et al. (2021)) workloads. As a result, many hand-crafted performance optimization techniques
037   have been suggested in the literature that improve the runtime performance of sparse matrix com-
038   putations (Kjolstad et al. (2017); Ye et al. (2023); Hong et al. (2019); Jiang et al. (2020)). These
039   computations use compressed sparse formats (e.g., compressed sparse row) to only compute on non-
040   zero values of matrices. Since the non-zero distribution of values in these matrices can vary, it has
041   been challenging to come up with performance optimizations that work well across diverse inputs.
042   To overcome this challenge, machine learning (ML)-based program optimization techniques have
043   been introduced to optimize sparse matrix computations on established hardware platforms such
044   as CPUs and GPUs (Won et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2023)). These techniques adaptively select a
045   program configuration based on the input sparse matrix features. For example, WACO (Won et al.
046   (2023)) introduced learned cost models to predict the runtime cost of programs under different sparse
047   matrices and program configurations. It then used search-based techniques to automatically find the
048   optimal program configuration based on the cost model output. Overall, these ML-based techniques
049   show superior performance and adaptability across a diverse range of inputs compared to manually
050
      crafted performance optimization techniques.
051   Recently, on the hardware front, new domain-specific machines specifically designed for sparse
052   operations are emerging (Aananthakrishnan et al. (2023); Gerogiannis et al. (2023); Hegde et al.
053   (2019); Li et al. (2023); Jin et al. (2024)). These machines, known as hardware accelerators, offer
      significant speedups over established hardware platforms. Similar to CPUs and GPUs, sparse accel-


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      erators offer different program configurations to accommodate diverse sparse inputs (Gerogiannis
055   et al. (2023); Jin et al. (2024); Li et al. (2023); Muñoz-Martı́nez et al. (2023); Gerogiannis et al.
056   (2024)) that should be configured by software performance optimizations. However, unlike CPUs
057   and GPUs, most emerging accelerators that are at early design stages, only have access to expensive
058   simulators. This poses a significant challenge for computer architects, as exhaustively evaluating all
059   potential program configurations to determine the best during the early stages is not feasible. Hence,
060   in the presence of any inefficiencies observed during early stage simulations, it has become difficult
061   to diagnose whether the inefficiencies stem from suboptimal hardware design choices or program
062   configurations. Further, when designing hardware accelerators, it would be ideal to avoid over-
063
      provisioning hardware resources (e.g., increasing cache size) if such inefficiencies can instead be
      mitigated via improved software strategies (e.g., adopting a different tiling strategy). Therefore, the
064
      need to automatically select the optimal program configuration during the design space exploration
065
      (DSE) phase of accelerator development is important, as it eliminates a dimension of complexity.
066
067   The learned cost models used in ML-based optimization techniques targeted at CPUs and GPUs are
068   trained with relatively large supervised datasets. Those datasets consist of program configurations
069
      and sparse matrices as inputs, and runtimes as labels. Usually, these datasets consist of hundreds
      of thousands of such labeled data items Won et al. (2023). Unfortunately, collecting datasets of
070
      comparable size for emerging hardware accelerators that have only simulators – which is the case
071
      for most – is prohibitively costly. The time needed for a simulation to complete is many orders of
072   magnitude longer than the real execution of the program on the actual chip. For example, it can take
073   up to two weeks to collect a single data point using the simulator of the state-of-the-art SPADE
074   sparse accelerator (Gerogiannis et al. (2023)). At the same time, the same program would take less
075   than a second to execute on the real chip when it is finally fabricated. Collecting large datasets would
076   require huge clusters running simulations for months or even years. Therefore, in order to bring the
077   same benefits of ML-based optimizations to accelerator platforms at their early stages, we need to
078   rethink learned cost model construction techniques that are data-frugal and highly sample-efficient.

128   DASH. In this paper, to overcome these challenges we present DASH, a novel framework for de-
129
      veloping learned cost models which enables effective knowledge transfer (Figure 1). DASH uses
      WACO’s (Won et al. (2023)) neural cost model architecture as the base model (WacoNet) but in-
130
      corporates key changes to make it amenable for transfer learning. DASH enables the discovery of
131
      better program configurations that are closer to the optimal (Figure 2), while requiring significantly
132   less accelerator data samples for fine-tuning.
133
134   DASH is centered around two key principles introduced in Neyshabur et al. (2020): feature reuse
135
      and the capture of low-level statistical information. We observe that, even though the program con-
      figurations between general-purpose hardware and accelerators are heterogeneous, there are certain
136
      feature spaces that can be mapped due to their similarities. Motivated by this observation, we pro-
137
      pose an approximate mapping of comparable code optimizations, effectively segregating the fea-
138   ture space generated by program configuration into homogeneous and heterogeneous components.
139   This allows feature reuse across the source and target platforms. The heterogeneous components
140   represent non-mappable hardware specific parameters that can be disparate across different plat-
141   forms. Such components can introduce challenges during transfer learning due to negative transfer.
142   To separately encode the heterogeneous feature spaces, we introduce a novel latent space repre-
143   sentation of the heterogeneous input feature space using an auto-encoder. This novel formulation
144   of the feature space allows us to effectively reuse features while minimizing the impact of negative
145   transfer. Further, we observe that certain layers of WacoNet do not contribute heavily to the final
146
      prediction and this over-parameterization can hinder transferability due to over-fitting. To mitigate
      this, DASH modifies WacoNet by reducing the number of layers and extracting features at various
147
      depths and scales, effectively allowing the model to capture low-level statistical information.
148
149   We evaluate DASH on two widely used sparse operations, Sparse Matrix-Matrix Multiplication
150   (SpMM) and Sampled Dense-Dense Matrix Multiplication (SDDMM), by transferring from a CPU
151   to an emerging sparse accelerator SPADE (Gerogiannis et al. (2023)). Our experimental results show
152
      that DASH outperforms existing techniques that leverage transfer learning by 28.44%, achieving an
      average speedup of 1.47× (up to 5.46×) for SpMM and 1.39× (up to 4.22×) for SDDMM. To further
153
      demonstrate the generalizability of our approach, we transferred from a CPU to a GPU, achieving
154
      an average speedup 1.17× (up to 1.61×) for SpMM and 1.15× (up to 1.49×) for SDDMM.

211   2.3    ML- BASED COST MODELS
212
213   Learned Cost Models. Cost models act as fast and cost-effective proxies for executing workloads
214   on real hardware. Their primary goal is to accurately estimate the execution time of workloads as
215   they would perform on real hardware. To achieve this, these cost models can be trained on data
      samples with various program configurations and then used to predict the program configuration


                                                       4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      that will deliver the optimal performance. Hence, generally, the training objective of cost models is
217   tied with minimizing |t∗CM − t∗ |, where t∗ is the runtime of the true optimal program configuration
218   and t∗CM is the runtime of the best program configuration suggested by the cost model (accuracy
219   objective)(detailed Appendix A). Finding the best configuration suggested by the cost model is usu-
220   ally done using auxiliary intelligent search techniques such as simulated annealing, Monte Carlo
221   tree search, and reinforcement learning.
222
      There have been numerous works on learned cost models to predict the runtime of different work-
223   loads targeting different hardware platforms Chen et al. (2018b); Adams et al. (2019). These tech-
224   niques range from simple XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin (2016)) based cost models Chen et al. (2018a)
225   to sophisticated deep neural network based models (Baghdadi et al. (2021); Kaufman et al. (2021);
226   Zhai et al. (2023); Zheng et al. (2020); Won et al. (2023)). WACO Won et al. (2023) is a learned
227   cost model specifically built for sparse matrix computations that we use as our base cost model.
228
      Transfer Learning. Transfer learning is a technique that leverages knowledge gained from a task
229   in a source domain to improve the performance of a related task in a target domain, where data
230   collection can often be challenging (Bozinovski (2020)). There have been many successful ex-
231   amples of transfer learning techniques in a wide range of fields (Weiss et al. (2016)). Transfer
232   learning can be categorized into two main types: homogeneous transfer learning (Zhuang et al.
233   (2020)), where the input and label spaces are the same, and heterogeneous transfer learning (Day &
234   Khoshgoftaar (2017)), where either one or both can be different. In program optimization, transfer
235   learning has been successfully used to transfer cost models learned from one hardware platform to
236   another, primarily in homogeneous settings, to minimize the target domain data requirements Zheng
237
      et al. (2021); Sasaki et al. (2022). In this work, we seek to minimize the target domain data re-
      quirement during fine-tuning (Shen et al. (2021)), by targeting heterogeneous input feature spaces
238
      present between general-purpose hardware and emerging sparse accelerators (data-collection objec-
239
      tive))(detailed in Appendix A).

      In this section, we present DASH, a novel framework to design data-efficient learned cost models to
259   accelerate the execution of sparse matrix computations on emerging hardware platforms. The fol-
260   lowing subsections outline our contributions toward achieving the objectives set forth in Section 2.3;
261   maximizing the accuracy while minimizing the data collection overhead. In Section 3.1, we review

267   3.1      WACO’ S C OST M ODEL
268
269   WACO (Figure 3(a)) (Won et al. (2023)) introduced the concept of directly feeding sparsity patterns
      as raw data into cost models. WACO’s cost model employs submanifold sparse convolution net-

      works (SCNN) (Graham & Van der Maaten (2017)) to extract features using the input featurizer. It
271   leverages a neural network-based program embedder to capture the impact of code optimizations on
272   sparse operations by encoding program configurations into embeddings. The program embeddings
273   are merged with the extracted sparsity pattern features produced by the input featurizer. The merged
274   inputs are then processed through a multi-layer perceptron predictor to estimate the execution cost.

276   3.2   OVERVIEW OF E NHANCEMENTS TO E NABLE T RANSFER L EARNING
277
278   We build upon WACO considering it as our base model by refining its architecture to better facilitate
279   transfer learning across diverse hardware platforms. Our improved cost model design (Figure 3(b)) is
280   structured around four key components: configuration mapper, input featurizer, latent encoder, and
281   predictor. The configuration mapper (Figure 3(b)(1)) and latent encoder (Figure 3(b)(2)) replace the
282
      program embedder in WACO, while the input featurizer (Figure 3(b)(3)) has been modified to more
      effectively capture low-level information from sparsity patterns. Both the configuration mapper and
283
      the input featurizer remain consistent across hardware platforms, serving as key components that
284
      enable efficient knowledge transfer.
285
286   Configuration Mapper (FM). The configuration mapper captures homogeneity across hardware
287   platforms by processing program configurations (cj ) and their parameters to identify similarities in
288
      code optimizations across various platforms. We designed it to approximately map similar configu-
      ration parameters across different hardware platforms (described in Section 3.3) to a unified feature
289
      space. This is achieved by using explicit mapping functions. The resulting parameters are subse-
290
      quently passed through a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) to produce the final configuration vector
291   pj . In this work, we approximate the code optimizations loop strip-mining and loop reordering as
292   pj = FM(ϕ(·), π(·), cj ). using the mapping functions ϕ and π, as detailed in Section 3.3.
293
294
      Input Featurizer (IFE). Matrices with identical dimensions and non-zero elements can exhibit
      vastly different sparsity patterns, making it difficult to extract meaningful features based only on
295
      statistical properties. Building on WACO’s input featurizer Won et al. (2023), we modify the net-
296
      work architecture (Figure 3) to more effectively capture low-level information from sparsity patterns.
297   Our network consists of 12 SCNN layers (compared to 14 layers in WACO), arranged in 4 blocks,
298   each containing 3 sparse convolution layers. At the end of each block, we apply max pooling to
299   condense spatial information. We increase the number of channels across blocks up to 256, whereas
300   WACO kept them fixed at 32. These additional channels enables our design to capture hierarchical
301   features more effectively throughout the network compared to WACO. For a given sparse matrix M ,
302   our input featurizer generates a sparse feature vector sM , expressed as sM = IFE(M ).
303   Latent Encoder (LE). We handle the heterogeneity of program configurations across hardware
304   platforms using per-target autoencoders that compress the heterogeneous components of the config-
305   urations into compact latent representations (described in Section 3.4). An autoencoder is trained
306   for each target–sparse primitive pair. During both training and inference, the latent encoder LE
307   processes a configuration (cj ), transforming it into a latent representation zj = LE(cj ), that encap-
308   sulates the unique characteristics of the program configuration.
309   Predictor (P). As the final component of the cost model, the predictor (Figure 3(b)(4)) integrates
310   the three feature vectors from the preceding components into a single unified vector, encapsulat-
311   ing all key information about the sparsity pattern and program configuration. This unified vector
312   (sM ∥pj ∥zj ) is passed through a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) to eventually output a scalar value
313   representing the predicted execution cost, which can be expressed as r̂M,cj = P(pj ∥sM ∥zj ).

315   3.3   E XPLOITING H OMOGENEITY: A PPROXIMATE M APPING OF C ODE O PTIMIZATIONS
316
      Different hardware platforms often use distinct programming systems, leading to variations in how
317
      code optimizations are parameterized (Figure 1). Further, an optimization available in one platform
318   may not be directly available on another, requiring the combination of multiple other code optimiza-
319   tions to replicate the same impact. For example, loop strip-mining code optimization on CPUs can
320   be closely approximated by collectively applying barrier and tiling optimizations in SPADE. By
321   mapping the effects of these code optimizations using their program configuration parameters, we
322   can expose patterns that facilitate effective knowledge transfer across hardware platforms. In this
323   section, we present our approaches for approximately merging loop strip-mining, barrier, and tiling
      optimizations between CPU and SPADE, and loop reordering optimization between CPU and GPU.


                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Loop strip-mining is a code optimization that decomposes large software loops into smaller seg-
325   ments to optimize computations for memory utilization and cache performance. In our context, it
326   is applied to loops iterating over the indices i, j, and k of matrices in SpMM and SDDMM sparse
327   operations (Section 2.1), where parameters I, J, and K are used to split these loops into outer and
328   inner segments, resulting in a loop nest of six decomposed loops. The resulting loop segments are
329   {i1 , i2 , j1 , j2 , k1 , k2 } and their execution order is denoted by ω. In SPADE, we approximate this us-
330   ing barrier and tiling optimizations. Tiling decomposes a matrix into smaller blocks to optimize data
331   reuse in the local memory, while barrier controls the execution order of tiles. For example, enabling
332   barrier optimization pauses the tiles scheduled by a control processing element until all previous
333
      tiles have been completed (Gerogiannis et al. (2023)). Similar to strip-mining parameters, the tiling
      parameters for i, j, and k indices of matrices are represented in SPADE as pcol , prow , dsplit , respec-
334
      tively, while barrier is represented by b, where b = 1 if barrier is enabled, and b = 0 otherwise.
335
      Intuitively, tiling divides computations into smaller blocks, while barriers control synchronization
336   during execution. By enabling and disabling barriers for various tiling configurations, we can dictate
337   the order of computation. This resembles loop strip-mining and reordering in CPUs, where optimiz-
338   ing loop execution enhances performance and cache utilization. We can approximately map tiling
339   and barrier parameters to the corresponding strip-mining parameters using the mapping function
340   ϕ : {pcol , prow , ssplit , b} → {I, J, K, ω} as follows:
341                                             ϕ(pcol , prow , ssplit , b) = (I, J, K, ω)
342
                                                                           [k2 , j2 , i2 , i1 , j1 , k1 ] if b = 1
343                     I ≈ pcol , J ≈ prow , K ≈ ssplit ; ω =
                                                                           [k2 , i2 , j2 , i1 , j1 , k1 ] if b = 0

345   Loop reordering is a code optimization that adjusts the execution order ω of loops to improve cache

      3.4    M ITIGATING H ETEROGENEITY: E NCODE H ARDWARE - SPECIFIC C ODE O PTIMIZATIONS
356
357   While we can use the strategies described in Section 3.3 to approximate code optimizations with ho-
358   mogeneity, such techniques are not applicable to hardware-specific code optimizations. An existing
359   approach for representing hardware-specific code optimizations across different hardware platforms
360   is to encode them using feature augmentation. However, this results in excessively sparse feature
361   vectors, as code optimizations that are not applicable to a selected hardware platform are zeroed out.
362   Training models on such sparse feature vectors often leads to sub-optimal performance (Figure 15).
363   To address this limitation, we propose indexing the parameters of the heterogeneous component
364   of the program configurations for each platform ai using low-dimensional latent representations.
365   Specifically, we train an autoencoder AE ai to learn a latent representation zj for each configuration
366   cj ∈ Cai . This is accomplished by determining the value ranges for all parameters of the hetero-
367   geneous component in the program configurations, followed by training an autoencoder to learn an
368   unsupervised embedding of this parameterization. Once trained, we use the encoder LE ai in AE ai ,
369
      which takes each configuration (cj ) as input and transforms it into its corresponding latent represen-
      tation zj , where zj is a fixed-size vector. By compressing configurations from different hardware
370
      platforms—each with varying parameters and ranges—into fixed-size vectors, we standardize the
371
      input for hardware-specific optimizations into the cost model. This compression significantly sim-
372   plifies the model compared to feature augmentation, as the cost model now processes fewer input
373   features, reducing its computational complexity. Since the hardware-specific optimizations from
374   different hardware platforms are now represented in a unified latent feature space, it becomes fea-
375   sible to capture any similarities in how they impact performance, which can then be leveraged in
376   fine-tuning. Finally, this approach facilitates the seamless integration of emerging hardware plat-
377   forms into DASH, as we can extend DASH to support new target hardware platforms by training
      new autoencoders and fine-tuning, eliminating the need to retrain the source model from scratch.

      A     P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
703
704
      In this work, our aim is to build accurate learned cost models for emerging hardware platforms
705
      to enable faster identification of optimal program configurations. A key challenge is the need to
706   maximize the accuracy of the cost model (accuracy objective) while using as few expensive (i.e.
707   collected through simulation) data samples as possible (data collection objective). We first formalize
708   the program optimization objective and then tie it with the cost model objectives.
709
710
      A.1   P ROGRAM O PTIMIZATION S ELECTION
711
712   The goal of program optimization in sparse matrix computations is to select the optimal program
713   configuration for a given hardware platform and input sparsity pattern from the total configuration
714   space. Let configuration space Ca be the set {c1 , c2 , . . . , cma } of all valid program configurations
715   for a given hardware platform a (ma ∈ Z+ ). For example, for CPU, a valid configuration from
716   CCP U is a tuple of program configuration parameters for loop strip-mining, loop reordering, and
717   format reordering (Table 1). The optimal program configuration minimizes the execution time of a
718
      sparse matrix computation. For an input sparse matrix (sparsity pattern) M , the optimal program
      configuration on platform a can be given as, c∗ = arg minci ∈Ca Ta (M, ci ), where Ta is the execu-
719
      tion time function for platform a (ground truth runtime). The execution time for the optimal program
720
      configuration is given by t∗ = Ta (Ml , c∗ ).
721
722
723
      A.2   C OST M ODEL P ERFORMANCE AND DATA E FFICIENCY O BJECTIVES
724
      We approximate the ground truth runtime Ta using learned cost models. Usually, these cost models
725   are trained with one objective: to achieve high accuracy. However, due to the high cost of simulation
726   in emerging hardware, we also want to minimize the amount of data samples required from these
727   platforms for model training. We formalize these two objectives as follows.
728
      Data Collection Objective (DCE). Let Da = {(Ml , ci ), ti | i ∈ ma , l ∈ Z+ } be the dataset
729
      collected from hardware platform a, and let βa represent the average cost of collecting a single data
730
      sample from the platform. Our objective is to minDa βa × |Da |.
731
732   Accuracy Objective. Let CMa (which approximates Ta ) be the learned cost model trained on
733   dataset Da . If the best program configuration returned by the cost model (c∗CMa ) has an actual
734   execution time t∗CMa , our objective is to min |t∗CMa − t∗ |, where t∗ is the execution time for the
735   optimal configuration. For a set of input sparse matrices {M1 , M2 , . . . , Mk }, our objective can be
736   extended to minimizing the Absolute Percentage Error (APE) across all matrices:

780   A.4   L EARNING O BJECTIVE
781
782   Our objective is to train a cost model that effectively learns to identify a program configuration
783
      that minimizes the runtime of a sparse operation for a given sparsity pattern. To achieve this, we
      begin by training our cost model to learn the relative rankings of program configurations during
784
      execution, enabling it to accurately identify optimal configurations based on their performance. This
785
      allows us to combine our cost model with a search technique to efficiently select the top-k (k-best)
786   program configurations from the configuration space. We use the pairwise ranking loss as our
787   learning objective (implemented using margin ranking loss) to rank program configurations based
788   on their true performance differences. For a given input matrixP M , the pairwise ranking loss (L)
789   across all program configuration pairs can be defined as L = (c1 ,c2 ) max(0, 1 − (r̂M,c1 − r̂M,c2 )) ·
790   δtrue ; δtrue = sign(tM,c1 −tM,c2 ) where r̂M,c1 and r̂M,c2 are the predicted scores for configurations
791   c1 and c2 , respectively; tM,c1 and tM,c2 represent their actual runtimes; and δtrue signifies the true
792   performance difference where sign(x) returns 1 if x > 0, -1 if x < 0, and 0 if x = 0. This
793   ensures that the model is penalized when the predicted ranking does not align with the true ranking.
794   By minimizing this loss (L), DASH improves its ability to accurately rank and identify the top-k
795   program configurations. This also contributes to achieving our accuracy objective (Section A.2).
