011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models have shown remarkable efficacy in generating streaming
014            data such as text and audio, thanks to their temporally uni-directional attention
015            mechanism, which models correlations between the current token and previous
016
               tokens. However, video streaming remains much less explored, despite a growing
               need for live video processing. State-of-the-art video diffusion models leverage bi-
017
               directional temporal attention to model the correlations between the current frame
018
               and all the surrounding (i.e. including future) frames, which hinders them from
019            processing streaming videos. To address this problem, we present L IVE 2D IFF, the
020            first attempt at designing a video diffusion model with uni-directional temporal
021            attention, specifically targeting live streaming video translation. Compared to
022            previous works, our approach ensures temporal consistency and smoothness by
023            correlating the current frame with its predecessors and a few initial warmup frames,
024            without any future frames. Additionally, we use a highly efficient denoising
025            scheme featuring a KV -cache mechanism and pipelining, to facilitate streaming
026            video translation at interactive framerates. Extensive experiments demonstrate the
027
               effectiveness of the proposed attention mechanism and pipeline, outperforming
               previous methods in terms of temporal smoothness and/or efficiency.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have recently been very successful in natural language processing
      and various other domains (Jiang et al., 2023; 2024; Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Vaswani et al., 2017). At
034
      the core of LLMs is the autoregressive next-token prediction, seamlessly enabling real-time streaming
035
      data generation. Such a mechanism processes data continuously as it streams in, bypassing the need
036   for batch storage and delayed processing. This token prediction mode has been widely used in many
037   applications such as dialog systems (Anthropic, 2024; OpenAI, 2024), text-to-speech (Wang et al.,
038   2023a; Zhang et al., 2023b), audio generation (Copet et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Kreuk et al.,
039   2022), etc. Despite the recent success of this streamed generation of sequential data like text and
040   audio, it has not been fully explored for another very common sequential data type: videos. However,
041   generating videos in a streaming manner is clearly worth investigating given the growing practical
042   demand, particularly in live video processing, where the original input frames need to be translated
043   into a target style on the fly.
044   Motivated by this, we study next-frame-prediction for producing streaming videos, with streaming
045   video-to-video translation as the target application. Most existing video diffusion models exploit tem-
046   poral self-attention modeling in a bi-directional manner, where the models are trained on sequences of
047   input frames to capture the pairwise correlations between all frames in a sequence (Blattmann et al.,
048   2023a;b; Geyer et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023). Despite some
049   promising results, these models have limitations for streaming video. Early frames in a sequence
050
      rely on information from later frames, and vice versa for those at the end, which impedes efficient
      real-time processing of each frame as it streams in.
051
052   To address this issue, we redesign the attention mechanism of video diffusion models for streaming
053   video translation, ensuring both high efficacy and temporal consistency. First, we make temporal self-
      attention uni-directional via an attention mask, akin to attention in LLMs (Jiang et al., 2023; Vaswani

084   results in artifacts in later frames. To tackle this, we introduce warmup area in the unidirectional
085

      limited context attention at the beginning of the stream. During inference, we include the attention
086
      from a few warmup frames at the start of the stream to the current frame. Such a tailored attention
087
      design ensures both stream processing efficacy and temporal consistency modeling.
088
089   Building upon our tailored attention mechanism, we present L IVE 2D IFF, a pipeline that processes
090   LIVE video streams by a uni-directional video DIFFUSION model while ensuring high efficacy and
091
      temporal consistency. First, our attention modeling mechanism removes the influence of later frames
      on previous frames, allowing for the reuse of K and V maps from previously generated frames. This
092
      eliminates the need for recomputation when processing subsequent frames. We carefully designed
093
      a KV -cache feature in the diffusion pipeline to cache and reuse K/V maps, resulting in significant
094   computation time savings. Second, we further include a lightweight depth prior in the input, ensuring
095   structural consistency with the conditioning stream. Finally, L IVE 2D IFF uses the batch denoising
096   strategy to further improve stream processing efficacy, achieving 16FPS for 512 × 512 videos on
097   an RTX 4090 GPU. We conduct extensive experiments to validate the superiority of L IVE 2D IFF in
098   terms of temporal smoothness and/or efficiency. We summarize our main contributions as follows,
099
100                            • To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to incorporate uni-directional temporal attention
101                              modeling into video diffusion models for video stream translation.
102
103                            • We introduce a new pipeline L IVE 2D IFF, which aims at achieving live stream video
104                              translation with both high efficacy (16FPS on an RTX 4090 GPU) and temporal consistency.
105
106                            • We conduct extensive experiments including both quantitative and qualitative evaluation to
107                              verify the effectiveness of L IVE 2D IFF.

111   Attention. LLMs (Jiang et al., 2023; 2024; Touvron et al., 2023a;b) owe their success largely to the
112   remarkable efficacy of the attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017). In order to support the auto-
113   regressive prediction of the next token, they use a uni-directional (or “masked”) attention mechanism,
114   restricting the model to learning the dependence of later tokens on earlier ones, but no dependence
115   of earlier tokens on later ones. However, for tasks with very long token sequences, relating the
116   current token to all previous tokens becomes intractable. To address this, S TREAMING LLM (Xiao
117
      et al., 2023) proposes to relate the current token to several initial tokens and a number of most
      recent tokens, which improves efficiency in handling long tokens. While such kind of uni-directional
118
      attention is widely used in generating text and audio, video generation has not yet followed this trend:
119
      Bi-directional attention without masks is commonly used in video diffusion models (Blattmann et al.,
120   2023a;b; Guo et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2023) to generate video chunks. In this work, we study the use
121   of uni-directional temporal attention in video diffusion models. While our method draws inspiration
122   from S TREAMING LLM, it is the first time that such a design is studied in the video domain.
123
      Video Diffusion Models. The multitude of possible conditioning modalities has made diffusion
124
      models the basis for image editing approaches (Meng et al., 2021; Kawar et al., 2023), as well as
125
      video generation models (Guo et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023; Kodaira et al., 2023). For example,
126   A NIMATE D IFF (Guo et al., 2023) extends S TABLE D IFFUSION by a so-called “motion module”,
127   enabling the denoising of entire video chunks based on temporal self-attention (Vaswani et al.,
128   2017). F REE N OISE (Qiu et al., 2023) is a method based on pretrained video diffusion models (e.g.
129   A NIMATE D IFF (Guo et al., 2023)) for long video generation. This method carefully selects and
130   schedules the latent noise for every time step in order to improve temporal smoothness. However,
131   F REE N OISE, according to their experiments section produces frames at under 3FPS on an NVIDIA
132   A100 GPU, which is not acceptable in the kinds of live streaming scenarios that we aim at (see
133   Section 1). F LOW V ID (Liang et al., 2023) and R ERENDER (Yang et al., 2023) produce frames at
134
      even lower rates, albeit with acceptably smooth results.
135   Accelerating Diffusion Models. Some recent diffusion-based methods (Luo et al., 2023a;b; Song
136   et al., 2023; Kodaira et al., 2023) have prioritized low latency and/or high throughput: (Latent)
137   consistency models (LCMs) (Song et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023a) have reduced the number of
138   denoising steps from 50 (the default in S TABLE D IFFUSION) to as low as 4, leading to large speed ups
139   without too much loss in quality. This principle has even been combined with the use of low-rank
140
      matrices for fine-tuning (Luo et al., 2023b), allowing further speedup. A work that very specifically
      targets the streaming frame-by-frame translation setting is S TREAM D IFFUSION (Kodaira et al., 2023):
141
      Not only is this technique utilizing the aforementioned low-rank-adapted LCMs, but also it denoises
142
      video frames in a “pipelined” manner for the streaming scenario, i.e. the batch of images to be
143   denoised can contain different levels of remaining noise, allowing new frames to be added to the
144   batch before previous frames in the batch have been completely denoised, which makes optimal use
145   of GPU parallelization. However, S TREAM D IFFUSION renders videos frame-by-frame without any
146   temporal modeling, leading to significant temporal discontinuity, which our method avoids due to the
147   temporal correlations learned during training.

151   3    M ETHOD
152
153
      Our method, L IVE 2D IFF, takes as input a stream of video frames, along with a matching text prompt.
154
      It produces a stream of output video frames, the spatial structure of which is similar to that of the
155
      input frames, but the appearance/style of which conforms to a specified target style, captured by
156   D REAM B OOTH (Ruiz et al., 2023). To achieve this, we replace the bidirectional temporal attention
157   used in previous approaches by uni-directional attention (Section 3.2). This allows us to cache K
158   and V maps from previous frames, leading to increased throughput (Section 3.3). Furthermore we
159   accelerate generation by pipelined denoising, i.e. multiple time steps with different levels of residual
160   noise are denoised in parallel. By employing LCM-L O RA(Luo et al., 2023b) we can drastically
161   reduce the number of necessary denoising steps, which also helps meet framerate criteria. We stabilize
      the spatial structure of frames with lightweight depth injection.

      3.1     P RELIMINARIES
181
182   Diffusion Models. Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) aim at undoing the
183   so-called “forward process”, that iteratively adds Gaussian noise to the representation of a sample
184   from a distribution. To achieve this, S TABLE D IFFUION (Rombach et al., 2022) trains a U-N ET
185   (Ronneberger et al., 2015) to estimate the noise component of a noisy latent representation of any
186   given image. By repeatedly estimating remaining noise and removing (some of) this noise from the
187   latent code, a purely Gaussian noise vector can iteratively be denoised to obtain a clean sample as
188
      follows: Given a noisy latent code zt , the U-Net parametrized by weights θ computes the estimated
      noise ϵθ (zt , t, T (c)), where T (c) is the CLIP encoding (Radford et al., 2021) of a conditioning text
189
      string c. The less noisy latent code zt−1 can then be approximated as
190
191                                               zt−1 ≈ λ · zt + µ · ϵθ (zt , t, T (c))                                                                                                  (1)
192
      where λ, µ ∈ R are constants derived from the noise schedule of the forward process (Song et al.,
193   2020). The U-Net is trained by sampling images x from the training distribution, mapping them to
194   latent codes z0 = E(x) and then adding varying amounts of Gaussian noise to obtain zt , such that
195   the U-Net output can be supervised by L1 distance to the known noise. Like in S TABLE D IFFUSION,
196   we use this as our main loss, but with x holding not single images, but chunks of consecutive video
197   frames.

301   3.3     H IGH E FFICIENCY I NFERENCE P IPELINE
302
303   In inference stage, similar to SDE DIT (Meng et al., 2021), we add a certain amount of Gaussian
304   noise to the input frame(s), and denoise the noisy input to clean one with target style. We introduce
305   KV -cache and pipelined denoising to establish a highly efficient inference pipeline in our method.
306   KV -cache. As described in Section 3.2 our warmup-based temporal self-attention makes sure that
307   when we compute the attention for a certain frame, the attention for all previous frames has already
308   been computed. This means that those parts of the matrices K and V in Eq. (2) that concern the
309   previous frames do not need to be computed again, but can be retrieved from a cache.
310
      Fig. 4 illustrates the behaviour of in our KV -cache for the simple case Lw = 4. In the first step,
311
      we feed Lw frames into the U-Net and denoise them completely, with bidirectional attention as
312   introduced in Section 3.2. This gives us the K and V matrices highlighted in orange in Fig. 4. They
313   are cached and used for all future frames for temporal consistency. In the second step, the Lw + 1
314   (blue) frame arrives. We calculate temporal attention with cached warmup tokens and add the K and
315   V of blue frame to KV -cache. The behavior in the third step is similar to that of the second step,
316   with the only difference being that we utilize both the cached K and V pairs from the second frame
317   and those from the warmup frames to compute the temporal attention. When video frames beyond
318   the training context window arrive, we discard the cached frames that are not part of the warmup
319   frames and are furthest from the current video frame. For instance, at Lw + 6 in Fig. 4, we discard
320
      the caches for Lw + 1 and Lw + 2, and perform temporal attention using the remaining cache with a
      length of context window L.
321
322   Note that the diffusion U-Net has multiple temporal attention layers, and that the U-Net needs to be
323   applied T times in order to fully denoise a frame. This means that for every combination of layer and
      denoising step, we keep a separate KV -cache.

      Pipelined denoising. Similarly to S TREAM D IFFUSION (Kodaira et al., 2023), we denoise frames in
325   a pipelined manner, i.e. as soon as the next input frame becomes available, we add it to the batch
326   of frames to be denoised, even though it may contain a much higher amount of noise than previous
327   frames in the batch that have already undergone multiple denoising steps. This way we utilize our
328   GPU capacity most efficiently, increasing throughput.

330   3.4   C ONDITIONAL M ODULE WITH S TRUCTURE P RIOR
331
332   To facilitate the preservation of spatial structure we use an additional depth input: We use M I DAS
333   (Ranftl et al., 2022; 2021) for frame-wise depth estimation. The depth frames are then encoded by
334   S TABLE D IFFUSION’s encoder E, with the results being fed into a lightweight convolutional module
335
      E cond (see Fig. 2). Finally, we add the output of the conditional module to the first convolution layer
      and pass it through the U-Net. We found this explicit structural prior to help the transferred video
336
      maintain better structural consistency with the source video. Evidence can be found in Fig. 3 and
337
      Section 4.

      3.5 T RAINING
340
341   To train our model we use data collected from Shutterstock (Shutterstock, 2024), resized to resolution
342
      256 × 256. We choose L = 16 and Lw = 8. We train our model as follows: We initialize the weights
      of our temporal self-attention modules with the weights from A NIMATE D IFF and fine-tune them for
343
      3000 iterations using our uni-directional attention (Section 3.2). Then we add E cond (Section 3.4),
344
      with the last layer initialized with zeros (Zhang et al., 2023a) and train all weights jointly for 6000
345   iterations. We use the Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) optimizer with a learning rate of 1e − 4 and train
346   on batches of 4 samples per GPU, on 8 GPUs. Accumulation of 32 gradients leads to an effective
347   batch size of 1024.

      4     R ESULTS

      4.1 E VALUATION S ETUP
352
353   Dataset. We evaluate on the DAVIS-2017 (Pont-Tuset et al., 2017) dataset, which contains 90
354   object-centric videos. We resize all frames to 512 × 768 via bilinear interpolation and use C OG VLM
355
      (Wang et al., 2023c) to caption the middle frame of each video clip. To specify the target style, we
      add the corresponding trigger words of DreamBooth and LoRA as suffix.
356
357   Metrics. We evaluate three aspects of the generated videos: structure consistency (Output frames
358   should have similar spatial structure as input frames), temporal smoothness (no sudden jumps in the
359   motion) and inference latency. We measure structure consistency as the mean squared difference
360   between the depth maps estimated (Ranftl et al., 2021) for the input and output frames. As in previous
361
      work (Wu et al., 2023; Khachatryan et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023) we measure temporal smoothness
      by CLIP score (Radford et al., 2021), i.e. by the cosine similarity of the CLIP embeddings of pairs
362
      of adjacent frames. In addition we compute the so-called “warp error” (Lai et al., 2018) for pairs
363
      of adjacent frames, i.e. we compute the optical flow (Teed & Deng, 2020) between the frames and
364   then warp the predecessor frame accordingly, to compute a weighted MSE between the warping
365   result and the successor. We also conduct a user study to evaluate structure consistency and temporal
366   smoothness: Each participant is given triplets of videos (original input video, result from our method,
367   result from a random different method) and is asked to identify the result with the best quality. Then
368   we calculate the rate of our method winning compared to other methods. A higher win-rate indicates
369   that users perceive our method to be better in the corresponding evaluation direction. Please refers to
370   supplementary for detailed information. We measure inference speed as the total amount of time it
371   takes each method to process an input stream of 100 frames at resolution 512 × 512, on a consumer
372
      GPU (NVIDIA RTX 4090).
373
374   4.2 C OMPARISONS
375   We compare our method to three previous works, all based on S TABLE D IFFUSION(Rombach
376   et al., 2022) and compatible with D REAM B OOTH (Ruiz et al., 2023) and L O RA (Hu et al., 2021):
377   S TREAM D IFFUSION (Kodaira et al., 2023) applies SDE DIT on a frame-by-frame basis. The same

403   achieves a better CLIP score and warp error for temporal smoothness. This is not surprising, as
404
      the way that time steps are correlated in F REE N OISE allows information to flow bidirectionally
      along the temporal axis, which, unlike for all other methods, required F REE N OISE to be given
405
      access to all frames at once (see first paragraph of Section 4.2). This is an unfair advantage to
406
      F REE N OISE, violating some assumptions of the streaming scenario, as it allows F REE N OISE to
407   correlate its output frames to input frames that would likely not be available at the time the output

412   the target style (see also Fig. 5). Table 1 also compares inference latencies, i.e. the average time
413   that goes by between receiving an input frame and producing the corresponding output frame. As
414   is to be expected for a method that first consumes all the frames before producing any output,

      F REE N OISE has by far the largest latency, which makes it unusable for the live streaming scenario.

416
      different tradeoff between performance and quality. This is confirmed by the MSE, CLIP scores,
417
      warp error, and the user study results, that consistently indicate higher output quality for our method.
