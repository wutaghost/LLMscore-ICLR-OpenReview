                                                     A BSTRACT
025
026
                 Various visual foundation models have distinct strengths and weaknesses, both of
                 which can be improved through heterogeneous multi-teacher knowledge distilla-
027
                 tion without labels, termed “agglomerative models.” We build upon this body of
028
                 work by studying the effect of the teachers’ activation statistics, particularly the
029              impact of the loss function on the resulting student model quality. We explore
030              a standard toolkit of statistical normalization techniques to better align the dif-
031              ferent distributions and assess their effects. Further, we examine the impact on
032              downstream teacher-matching metrics, which motivates the use of Hadamard ma-
033              trices. With these matrices, we demonstrate useful properties, showing how they
034              can be used for isotropic standardization, where each dimension of a multivari-
035              ate distribution is standardized using the same scale. We call this technique “PHI
036              Standardization” (PHI-S) and empirically demonstrate that it produces the best
037
                 student model across the suite of methods studied.
038

      1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041
      A body of work recently emerged on the topic of agglomerative models (Ranzinger et al. (2024)),
042   which is fusing multiple heterogeneous visual foundation models (Awais et al. (2023)) into a sin-
043   gle model via multi-teacher knowledge distillation (Hinton et al. (2015); Zuchniak (2023)) without
044   labels. Starting with AM-RADIO (Ranzinger et al. (2024)), and followed by Theia (Shang et al.
045   (2024)), and UNIC (Sariyildiz et al. (2024)). Theia and UNIC apply feature standardization to the
046   teacher output, and demonstrate the importance of the approach.
047
      While knowledge distillation has a large body of literature ( Buciluǎ et al. (2006); Ahn et al. (2019);
048   Heo et al. (2019); Huang & Wang (2017); Romero et al. (2014); Sun et al. (2021); Wei et al.
049   (2022a); Zagoruyko & Komodakis (2017)), agglomerative models - dealing with multiple teachers
050   coming from different modeling domains (e.g. vision-language contrastive Radford et al. (2021),
051   self-supervised learning Oquab et al. (2023); Zhou et al. (2022); Assran et al. (2023), and segmen-
052   tation Kirillov et al. (2023)) without ground truth labels - was new territory. In AM-RADIO, the
053   authors chose DFN CLIP (Fang et al. (2023)), DINOv2-g-reg (Darcet et al. (2023)), and SAM (Kir-
      illov et al. (2023)) as their teacher models. While the authors studied loss balancing between the

126   The goal of the agglomerative student model is to produce activations x(k) that match the teacher ac-
127   tivations y(k) ∈ Y(k) as closely as possible for each teacher k ∈ T , and the loss is (usually) linearly
128   aggregated using weights α(k) . Finding these α(k) is difficult due to the size of the design space,
129   so current methods typically default to α(k) = 1 and focus on conditioning Y(k) to handle distribu-
130   tional differences. For simplicity, we drop the ·(k) superscript as the same type of normalization is
131   applied for every teacher, and each teacher has independent normalization parameters. Throughout
132   this paper, we refer to Var [Z] as the diagonal of the covariance matrix Σ [Z] for distribution Z.

      2.2     N ORMALIZATION
175   Instead of balancing the different heads through loss weighting, we can alter the targets themselves.
176   In Wei et al. (2022a), the authors explore this to condition their single teacher’s distribution, however,
177   they use the non-invertible LayerNorm operator to rescale the teacher features. Because we want
178   to maintain compatibility for the student to replace the teacher in downstream tasks (by replacing
179   only the vision encoder part of the model), we require the student to still estimate the true teacher
180   distribution. To achieve this, during training, we use an invertible linear mapping fk (·) such that
181   Tk′ (x) = fk (Tk (x)) and Tk (x) = fk−1 (Tk′ (x)), where the student model learns to match teacher
182   (Tk′ (x)) for each of the k teachers.
183

      2.2.4   H ADAMARD W HITENING (HCA)
256
257   In PCA Whitening, each successive dimension explains the next-largest variance in the data. While
258   this can be a very useful form, we hypothesize that this sort of dimensional loading might not be
259   healthy for a model to learn to match, as effects such as regularization, step size, gradient clipping,
260   etc. may impact the ability of the model to learn each dimension. Instead of ranking the dimensions,
261   we’d like to do the opposite, and find a Q that explains exactly the same amount of variance irre-
262   spective of channel index. It follows that if we could construct an orthogonal basis where each axis
                                                                     1
263   captures an identical amount of energy from the diagonal Λ− 2 matrix, then we are able to achieve
264   this balance. First, this matrix R must be orthogonal for it to be a valid Q. Second, in order for
265
      the same proportion of the diagonal Λ to be captured by each row, then each cell must have the

461
462
463   Knowledge Distillation We base our work on Ranzinger et al. (2024) which considers a multi-
464   teacher distillation problem without ground-truth labels, and where the targets are the teacher fea-
465   tures themselves, instead of estimating e.g. a probability distribution for classification. They build
466
      upon extensive literature on knowledge distillation, popularized by Hinton et al. (2015), and then
      expanded with Kim et al. (2018); Ba & Caruana (2014); Mirzadeh et al. (2019); Beyer et al. (2022)
467
      for teacher logit estimation problems. For feature matching, Romero et al. (2014); Huang & Wang
468
      (2017); Ahn et al. (2019); Heo et al. (2019); Zagoruyko & Komodakis (2017); Sun et al. (2021);
469   Wei et al. (2022b) study this sub-problem. Specifically, Wei et al. (2022a) discuss the importance of
470   normalizing the teacher feature distribution, which is a notable omission in Ranzinger et al. (2024).
471   Further, in the knowledge distillation domain, the idea of distilling from multiple teachers at once
472   is heavily studied Hinton et al. (2015); Liu et al. (2020); Zuchniak (2023); Yuan et al. (2020); Zhao
473   et al. (2022); Yang et al. (2020); Park & Kwak (2020); You et al. (2017); Lan et al. (2018); Asif et al.
474   (2019); Fukuda et al. (2017). AM-RADIO Ranzinger et al. (2024) differentiates itself from those
475   largely through the lack of a unified target label or distribution, as the teachers aren’t even from
476   the same problem domain (e.g. CLIP (Radford et al. (2021)) versus SAM (Kirillov et al. (2023))),
477
      and thus will produce very different feature distributions for the same image. Similarly, much of the
      literature that covers balancing the multi-objective loss relies on having access to ground truth labels
478
      (Liu et al. (2020)). Generically, AdaLoss (Hu et al. (2019)) is capable of balancing losses without
479
      GT labels by setting the loss weight to be inversely proportional to the approximate expected loss
480   for each term, which AM-RADIO studied but found no significant effect. In Ruder et al. (2017),
481   the authors study domain adaptation where they have multiple classifier teachers from their own do-
482   main, and they seek to train a student on a new unlabeled domain, however their method relies on the
483   source and target domains being classification. Concurrently to our work, Shang et al. (2024) intro-
484   duced the “Theia” model which draws heavily from AM-RADIO including the loss formulation. In
485   their work, the authors chose to use the regular standardization method, a choice which this work ex-
      plores and demonstrates that it was both a great addition over AM-RADIO, but also not the optimal


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      choice compared against PHI-S which we propose here. We view the works as complementary, as
487   our study entirely revolves around the design choices in their section 3.2 and AM-RADIO’s section
488   3.4. Recently, UNIC (Sariyildiz et al. (2024)) is also based on AM-RADIO, and employs feature
489   standardization, showing strong positive effects, and UNIT (Zhu et al. (2024)) bases on AM-RADIO
490   employing feature standardization in addition to explicit supervised OCR learning.
491
492
493   Normalization The importance of normalization in distillation was identified in Heo et al. (2019),
494
      which used BatchNorm. More recently, Wei et al. (2022a) also considered normalized feature match-
      ing, however their choice of LayerNorm was non-invertible, and also doesn’t de-correlate the dif-
495
      ferent feature dimensions. We aim to preserve the ability of the student to estimate the teacher as
496
      in AM-RADIO, so we focus on invertible normalization techniques which allow us to estimate the
497   teacher’s true distribution. Liu et al. (2022) argue that normalizing the student and teacher features
498   improves distillation for semantic segmentation as the student otherwise spends most of its energy
499   matching the teacher magnitudes. Intuitively, we expand on this by also observing that control-
500   ling the relative magnitudes across teachers is critical. Kessy et al. (2018) provides an overview of
501   different whitening procedures, stressing the fact that there are infinitely many whitening matrices
502   for a given distribution, and focus their attention on the Q rotation matrix that relates them. Their
503   treatment covers many popular Q matrices, and we use their work as the foundation for our study.
504   There are also multiple works in the SSL vision domain that deal with distribution properties, such
505
      as Barlow Twins (Zbontar et al. (2021)) and VICReg (Bardes et al. (2022)). Their algorithms induce
      the model to produce regular features, where in contrast, we’re forced to deal with arbitrary models
506
      that didn’t undergo such regularization. In digital signal processing, using the Hadamard matrix to
507
      spread energy (to mitigate signal loss errors) is a common practice (Pratt et al. (1969); Kanj et al.
508   (2022)). We study the incorporation of this matrix both as a suitable Q matrix for rotation during the
509   whitening process, and also in a novel way to derive a scalar normalization factor that standardizes
510   any multivariate distribution with a known Hadamard matrix, which we call PHI Standardization.
511
512
513   6   C ONCLUSION
514
515   Through our experiments, we have conclusively demonstrated that using plain MSE without balanc-
516   ing has a large negative effect on the resulting quality of the student model. Among normalization
517
      methods, standardization worked better than whitening, which was an initially surprising result. We
      hypothesize that the major issue with whitening is that the teacher models aren’t producing full
518
      rank distributions (appendix, table 6), which makes the normalization factors unstable. Regular
519
      standardization is resistant to this because the principal components of the distribution are spread
520                                                                    1
      out across all of the dimensions, preventing degenerate Λ− 2 solutions. We found two novel ap-
521
      plications of Hadamard matrices with respect to distribution normalization: HCA and PHI-S. At
522   the ViT-B/16 model scale, we found that isotropic normalization methods (Global Standardize and
523   PHI-S) worked the best, and for ViT-L/16, PHI-S remained the best. On the topic of reconstruc-
524   tion errors, we found no significant result across the whitening methods with respect to downstream
525   metrics, and also found that the per-channel estimation errors were not uniform in general, unless
526   uniform is the optimal choice (HCA and PHI-S), implying that the student model is able to be robust
527   to the potentially high-distortion nature of the different transforms. Overall, PHI-S appears to be the
528   best normalization method studied, and it allowed us to produce ViT-B and ViT-L models that are
529   competitive with the original AM-RADIO (Ranzinger et al. (2024)) ViT-H model.
530   Future Work We’ve solely explored the use of PHI-S for agglomerative modeling, however it’s a
531   general standardization technique when certain assumptions about the data hold such as normality
532   and dimensionality of the distribution. PHI-S could additionally be used to post-hoc standardize the
533   output of existing models. Lastly, an opportunity arises when combining PHI-S with quantization
534   practices (similar to Ashkboos et al. (2024)) in the information retrieval domain as it balances the
535   information across all channels evenly, potentially unlocking higher fidelity quantizers.

816   Sylvester’s construction gives us a convenient way to construct a Hadamard matrix when C is a

819   divisible by 4. If true, then there are significantly more valid matrices, and in particular, common
820
      deep learning choices will be a multiple of 4. While not proven in general, the literature has found
      a way to construct many non-power-of-2 sized matrices using some of the following rules:
