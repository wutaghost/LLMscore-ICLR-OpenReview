029                                              A BSTRACT
030
031            The deployment of deep learning models in critical domains necessitates a bal-
032
               ance between high accuracy and interpretability. We introduce WASUP, an inher-
               ently interpretable neural network that provides local and global explanations of
033
               its decision-making process. We prove that these explanations are faithful by ful-
034
               filling established axioms for explanations. Leveraging the concept of case-based
035            reasoning, WASUP extracts class-representative support vectors from training im-
036            ages, ensuring they capture relevant features while suppressing irrelevant ones.
037            Classification decisions are made by calculating and aggregating similarity scores
038            between these support vectors and the input’s latent feature vector. We employ
039            B-Cos transformations, which align model weights with inputs to enable faithful
040            mappings of latent features back to the input space, facilitating local explanations
041            in addition to global explanations of case-based reasoning. We evaluate WASUP
042            on three tasks: fine-grained classification on Stanford Dogs, multi-label classifi-
043
               cation on Pascal VOC, and pathology detection on the RSNA dataset. Results in-
               dicate that WASUP not only achieves competitive accuracy compared to state-of-
044
               the-art black-box models but also offers insightful explanations verified through
045
               theoretical analysis. Our findings underscore WASUP’s potential for applications
046            where understanding model decisions is as critical as the decisions themselves.

049   1   I NTRODUCTION
050
051   Deep learning is increasingly becoming part of everyday life, e.g., targeted advertisements, gaming,
052   and high-stakes areas like banking and medicine. In the latter, identifying errors in the model’s
053   decision-making process is crucial to detect failure cases, which can be addressed by adding trans-
      parency to model prediction using explainable AI (XAI). Explanations are typically produced post-

054
      hoc for black-box models using approximation methods, which estimate the contribution of each
055   input feature to the prediction (Bach et al., 2015; Ribeiro et al., 2016; Selvaraju et al., 2017; Lund-
056   berg & Lee, 2017; Sundararajan et al., 2017; Petsiuk, 2018). While these methods can accurately
057   compute feature contributions for low-dimensional inputs Lundberg & Lee (2017), they introduce
058   approximation errors for high-dimensional inputs such as images (Adebayo et al., 2018).
059
      As an alternative, inherently interpretable models provide explanations by design. For instance, lo-
060
      gistic regression can be considered an inherently interpretable model since evaluating its coefficients
061   directly explains the effect of each input feature towards the prediction. Translating these properties
062   to complex functions implemented by neural networks was further fueled by the argument that if a
063   technique is able to summarize the decision-making of a black box without any error, the black box
064   could be replaced by an inherently interpretable model in the first place (Rudin, 2019). As a result,
065   a growing research community is dedicated to developing inherently interpretable models, e.g., for
066   decision-critical tasks like medicine Barnett et al. (2021); Kim et al. (2021); Wolf et al. (2023).
067   The explanations provided by these models can be categorized into local and global explanations,
068   and evaluated in terms of axioms to verify if they are faithful Sundararajan et al. (2017) 1 . Local
069   explanations focus on individual predictions, revealing how the model arrived at a specific outcome
070   for a particular instance. In contrast, global explanations provide insight into the model’s over-
071   all behavior. In logistic regression, global explanations are derived from the model’s coefficients,
072   which describe how each feature influences the prediction across all instances. To produce local
073   explanations, a sample’s specific feature values are multiplied by their corresponding coefficients,
074   illustrating how each feature contributed to the particular prediction.
075   As outlined in Tab. 1, locally interpretable deep learning models include the work by Wang &
076   Sabuncu (2022), who trained a non-parametric classification head with metric learning to classify
077   an image by summing the softmax-normalized similarity scores between training image latent fea-
078   ture vectors and the test image latent feature vector. They proposed to provide local explanations by
079   showing the most influential images alongside their corresponding scores for a given test sample,
080   similar to the k-nearest-neighbor algorithm. While intuitive, the approach lacks contribution maps
081
      in pixel space to identify important input features. To this end, BagNets (Brendel & Bethge, 2019)
      classify local patches of an image individually and sum the probabilities of all patches for the final
082
      classification. Providing the class-probability maps of the individual patches’ predictions as expla-
083
      nations shows which local structures showed evidence of a respective class. However, they lack
084   faithful fine-grained attributions computed on a pixel-level, which are provided by B-cos networks
085   (Böhle et al., 2022; Böhle et al., 2024). In these models, each forward pass is summarized as a
086   linear transformation, and its resulting weight matrix then serves to compute local explanations in
087   image space. More recently, they were used to guide knowledge distillation by Parchami-Araghi
088   et al. (2024), who showed that a student network trained with knowledge distillation detects the
089   same features as the teacher network, which explanation-guiding helped to overcome. However,
090   B-cos models lack global interpretability because the weight matrix is dynamically computed for
091   each input.

123   human comprehensible explanation (Nguyen et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2023; Jeyakumar et al., 2020),
124   the number of explanations to be evaluated in its current implementations grows with the number of
125   prototypical parts per class learned by the model.
126   We propose WASUP, an inherently interpretable neural network that fulfills all three properties:
127   local, global, and faithful explanations (see Tab. 1). We inject case-based reasoning into WASUP’s
128   decision-making to provide local and global explanations that reduce the complexity of explanations
129   to be evaluated. WASUP can implement any neural network architecture (e.g., ResNets He et al.
130   (2016), Transformers Vaswani (2017), Mamba Gu & Dao (2023)) in its feature extractor without
131   limiting their computational capacity. Specifically, the model extracts support vectors from training
132   images that capture only class-representative features by suppressing negative contributions from
133   other classes. Classification is performed by computing and summing similarity scores between
134
      these support vectors and the latent feature vector of the input image (see Fig. 1). Levering B-
      cos networks ensures that both the support vectors and their similarity to test images are directly
135
      and faithfully explainable in the input space, enabling an intuitive understanding of the model’s
136
      reasoning. In summary, the support samples serve two purposes: (i) they help the model capture the
137   high variability between different classes in the data, and (ii) they provide the user with examples
138   that illustrate which parts of the class-representative training images the feature extractor focuses
139   on. Our contributions are as follows:
140
141          • We propose WASUP, an inherently interpretable network that provides faithful local and
142            global explanations for image classification with either single or multiple labels.
143
             • We prove that explanations provided by WASUP fulfill the axioms required to be faithful.
144
145          • We empirically evaluate WASUP and its explanations on three tasks (fine-grained single-
146            label image classification on Stanford Dogs, pathology prediction on RSNA, multi-label
147            image classification on Pascal VOC), and three architectures with different numbers of
148
               parameters (DenseNet121: 8M, ResNet50: 26M, a hybrid vision transformer: 81M).
149          • We demonstrate how the explanations provided by WASUP enable model debugging.

170   Local explanations show the images of the most influential support samples in terms of softmax
171   probabilities (see Fig. 2). At first glance, these explanations seem intuitive, but the main limitations
172   are two-fold: (i) Computing probabilities from (inverted) Euclidean distances with softmax: Re-
173   gardless of the input (e.g., in an adversarial attack), the model explanations will suggest that at least
174   one support sample is similar with sufficient probability. Additionally, the softmax probabilities are
175
      inapplicable to multi-label classification tasks out of the box since they sum to one. Thus, the model
      cannot predict three classes simultaneously under a standard decision threshold of 0.5. (ii) No pixel-
176
      level explanations: While explanations using similar input images are intuitive for humans, the lack
177
      of attribution maps hinders a further understanding of the network’s mechanisms and the detection
178   of biased predictions.

265   We propose WASUP, which combines the strengths of B-cos networks and the Nadaraya-Watson
266   head, to obtain an inherently interpretable neural network that implements case-based reasoning
267   and provides faithful local and global explanations, thereby reducing the complexity of explanations
268   to evaluate. WASUP achieves this by extracting class-representative support vectors from training
269   images for similarity-based classification and by leveraging the B-cos transform to faithfully explain
      its decision, see Fig. 2.

310   3.3   E XPLAINABILITY
311
312   Global Explanations While global explanations provided by WASUP are ideal for a developer to
313
      debug a model, they also facilitate case-based reasoning, which is most intuitive to understand for
      humans (Nguyen et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2023; Jeyakumar et al., 2020).
314
315   We propose to first evaluate what is encoded in each support vector by computing the RGBA expla-
316   nations and contribution maps in terms of the similarity measure to its input image: B-cos(vic , v̂ic ) =
317   ∥vic ∥, since v̂ic = vic /∥vic ∥ = 1 and cos(vic , v̂ic )B = 1 for any B.
318   We observed negative attributions for the global features during initial experiments. While only vi-
319   sualizing features with positive class contributions is reasonable during the classification of an input
320   image, these negative attributions can serve as means of debugging a model if carefully evaluated.
321   The definition B-cos(vic , v̂ic ) = ∥vic ∥ implies that negative attributions are a means of the model to
322   downscale the range of the B-cos similarity (accordingly, we observed the negative attributions in
323   the corners of the images). This indicates the temperature scaling in Eq. 5 was chosen too small,
      and re-training with a more significant temperature helped to overcome this issue.

460   5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
461
462   Current inherently interpretable models either lack global explanations, fail to explain the inter-
463   nal mechanisms of the deep learning model, or cannot provide explanations that faithfully explain
464   their decision-making. To this end, we proposed WASUP for interpretable classification by pro-
465   viding faithful local and global explanations by leveraging case-based reasoning and weight-input
466   alignment achieved with B-cos neural networks. Our theoretical analysis shows that our proposed
467   method’s explanations satisfy the required axioms for faithfulness. We evaluated WASUPon three
468   tasks using three backbone architectures, demonstrating how the complex mechanisms learned by
469
      neural networks can be decomposed into intuitive explanations with limited complexity.
470   Additionally, we exemplified how its global and local explanation properties enable effective model
471   debugging from a developer perspective while giving intuitive explanations of its decision-making.
472   We focused on creating a model that yields comprehensible explanations, which can, if desired,
473   decompose each test contribution into individual components based on the similarity measure. This
474   generates additional contribution maps for each support sample, facilitating further validation in
475
      cases with high intra-class variability, albeit with an increase in complexity.
476   While k-means clustering is fast to optimize, WASUP introduces additional overhead by requiring
477   the computation of support vectors, which is a potential limitation in cases with many classes. How-
478   ever, as demonstrated with the Stanford Dogs dataset comprising 120 classes, WASUP is readily
479   applicable out of the box to datasets with hundreds of classes. Moreover, note that the number of
480   classes in decision-critical fields such as medicine is generally smaller. In future work, we are cu-
481
      rious to extend WASUP by conformal prediction to automatically show the explanations of a set of
      possible classes as opposed to the most likely one in single-label tasks.

706   B-cos modles are piece-wise linear models that allows to summarize each input as an input-
707   dependent linear transform f (x) = W1→L (x)x, derived in Eq. 3. Explanations are computed
708   in terms of the weight matrix W1→L (x) by multiplying each feature with its matrix weight
709   ϕ(x)i = [W1→L ]Tj ⊙ x](ch,i,j) . To show that computing explanations for a forward pass is faithful
710   (i.e., satisfies below six axioms introduced by Sundararajan et al. (2017)), we need to show that they
711
      hold considering the transformation matrix W1→L (x). While W1→L (x) is indeed input-dependent,

712

713

      A.2    O N THE FAITHFULNESS OF SUPPORT VECTORS
775
776   On a theoretical level, the global explanations are the explanations of an intermediate B-cos neuron.
777   Thus, they satisfy the above axioms as well and can be interpreted as the information encoded in the
778   respective support vector.
779
      When providing local explanations, we view support vectors as the weights of a B-cos linear trans-
780   form. Hence, computing the explanation of the prediction of the class in terms of the output yields
781   the input features compressed in any of the support vectors; and the log-probability scores of each
782   support vector indicate to which extend each was found.
783
784   Justification for ReLU in WASUP In contrast to other functions that could be used to implement
785   ⊕, the ReLU activation does not alter the range of the input to potentially huge or tiny numbers
786   like, e.g. the exponential. However, ReLU activations are commonly scrutinized for their role in
787   setting negative activations to zero, which can cause deep neural networks to interpret this suppres-
788   sion as the absence of a feature. Thus, many XAI methods (e.g. gradient-based) yield misleading
789   attribution maps, as they cannot account for this type of contribution. However, this phenomenon is
790   tightly controlled in WASUP, where the sole operation following the ReLU activation is a similarity
791
      computation between the latent vectors of two images. As a result, the absence of a feature does
      not, by design, affect the probability of class logits, rendering it valid to disregard any input and its
792
      explanation from neurons corresponding to such features.

      Optimization All models were optimized using the AdamW optimizer with fixed hyperparame-
797
      ters: weight decay set to 0.0, betas configured to (0.9, 0.999), and an epsilon value of 1e-08. We con-
798   ducted an extensive grid search over the hyperparameter space (see Tab. A.2) to identify the optimal
799   settings based on accuracy. The training employed a custom learning rate scheduling policy, which
800   began with a linear warm-up phase over the first two epochs, increasing the learning rate from 10% to
801   the initial learning rate. This was followed by maintaining the initial learning rate until 50% of the to-
