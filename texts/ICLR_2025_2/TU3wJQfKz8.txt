011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013
               Regret Matching+ (RM+ ) variants have been widely developed to superhuman
               Poker AIs, yet few studies investigate their last-iterate convergence. Their last-
014
               iterate convergence has been demonstrated only for games with strong monotonicity
015
               or two-player zero-sum matrix games. A primary obstacle in proving the last-iterate
016            convergence for these algorithms is that their feedback is not the loss gradient of
017            the vanilla games. This deviation results in the absence of crucial properties, e.g.,
018            monotonicity or the weak Minty variation inequality (MVI), which are pivotal for
019            establishing the last-iterate convergence. To address the absence of these properties,
020            we propose a remarkably succinct yet novel proof paradigm that consists of: (i)
021            recovering these key properties through the equivalence between RM+ and Online
022            Mirror Descent (OMD), and (ii) measuring the the distance to Nash equilibrium
023            (NE) via the tangent residual to show this distance is related to the distance between
024
               accumulated regrets. To show the practical applicability of our proof paradigm, we
               use it to prove the last-iterate convergence of two existing smooth RM+ variants,
025
               Smooth Extra-gradient RM+ (SExRM+ ) and Smooth Predictive RM+ (SPRM+ ).
026
               We show that they achieve last-iterate convergence in learning an NE of games
027            satisfying monotonicity, a weaker condition than the one used in existing proofs
028            for both variants. Then, inspired by our proof paradigm, we propose Smooth
029            Optimistic Gradient RM+ (SOGRM+ ). We show that SOGRM+ achieves last-
030            iterate convergence in learning an NE of games satisfying the weak MVI, the
031            weakest condition in all known proofs for RM+ variants. The experimental results
032            show that SOGRM+ significantly outperforms other algorithms.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Nash Equilibrium (NE) is a fundamental concept in the field of game theory. Recent advancements
037   in superhuman game AI, are largely attributed to NE learning (MoravcŐĆ√≠k et al., 2017; Brown &
038   Sandholm, 2018; 2019; P√©rolat et al., 2022). Despite these advancements, the most popular algo-
039
      rithms for learning an NE‚ÄĒno-regret algorithms, typically achieve only average-iterate convergence.
      Moreover, in two-player zero-sum matrix games, these algorithms are prone to divergence or cyclic
040
      behavior (Bailey & Piliouras, 2018; Mertikopoulos et al., 2018b; P√©rolat et al., 2021). Average-iterate
041
      convergence requires strategy averaging. This averaging poses significant challenges in large-scale
042   games where function approximation is used to represent the strategy since a new function has to be
043   trained to represent the average strategy (Liu et al., 2023).
044
045
      To address the challenges related to averaging, numerous studies consider the last-iterate conver-
      gence, ensuring iterates converge to NE (Mertikopoulos et al., 2018a; Daskalakis & Panageas, 2019;
046
      Tatarenko & Kamgarpour, 2020; Wei et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2021; Cen et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
047
      2023; Sokota et al., 2023; Abe et al., 2022a;b; 2023; P√©rolat et al., 2021; 2022; Cai & Zheng, 2023).
048   These algorithms are based on Online Mirror Descent (OMD) or Follow the Regularized Leader
049   (FTRL). Despite their theoretical appeal, Regret Matching+ (RM+ ) variants (Bowling et al., 2015;
050   Farina et al., 2021; 2023), are more commonly utilized in solving real-world games. Precisely, they
051   are widely used in superhuman Poker AIs (Bowling et al., 2015; MoravcŐĆ√≠k et al., 2017; Brown &
052   Sandholm, 2018). The key distinction between RM+ variants and FTRL/OMD based algorithms is
053   that RM+ variants update within the (subset of the) non-negative orthant, whereas FTRL/OMD based
      algorithms update within the original strategy space of the game.

069   Unfortunately, few studies investigate the last-iterate convergence of RM+ variants. To date, the only
070   known results on the last-iterate convergence of RM+ variants are confined to a specific game with
071   strong monotonicity (Meng et al., 2023) or two-player zero-sum matrix games (Cai et al., 2023).
072   In contrast, the OMD/FTRL based algorithms achieve last-iterate convergence in a broader class
073   of games, those that satisfy monotonicity. These games are also called as monotone games (Cai
074   et al., 2022b; Cai & Zheng, 2023; Abe et al., 2023; P√©rolat et al., 2021; 2022). They cover several
075   common game types, such as two-player zero-sum matrix games and convex-concave games, along
076   with significant applications like the training of Large Language Models (LLM) (Munos et al.,
077
      2023). Moreover, recent studies show that OMD/FTRL based algorithms even achieve last-iterate
      convergence in learning an NE of games satisfying the weak Minty variation inequality (MVI)) (Cai
078
      & Zheng, 2022; Diakonikolas, 2020; Pethick et al., 2023). Weak MVI is weaker and covers more
079
      games than monotonicity. It includes applications like Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN) (Cai
080   & Zheng, 2022). Therefore, a key question is:
081
082
                               Do RM+ variants achieve last-iterate convergence in
                   learning an NE of games satisfying monotonicity or even only the weak MVI?
083
084   Compared to traditional no-regret algorithms, e.g., FTRL/OMD based algorithms, the primary
085   challenge in proving the last-iterate convergence of RM+ variants is that their feedback is not the
086   loss gradients of the vanilla games. This deviation results in the absence of crucial properties, e.g.,
087
      monotonicity or weak MVI, which are pivotal for establishing the last-iterate convergence.
088   Contributions. (i) To address the absence of crucial properties, e.g., monotonicity or weak MVI,
089   we introduce a novel proof paradigm. Firstly, it recovers these properties by leveraging the equiv-
090   alence between RM+ and OMD in Liu et al. (2021). Secondly, it measures the distance of RM+
091   variants to NE via the tangent residual (Cai et al., 2022b) to show that this distance is related to the
092   distance between accumulated regrets. Specifically, in RM+ variants, the feedback does not exhibit
093
      monotonicity or weak MVI. However, in their OMD equivalents, the feedback is the loss gradient
      of the vanilla games, which satisfies monotonicity or weak MVI. Then, We establish the last-iterate
094
      convergence of RM+ variants by demonstrating that the distance between accumulated regrets, rather
095
      than the strategies in OMD based algorithms, converges to 0. This convergence occurs since, in RM+
096   variants, the tangent residual converges 0 as this distance converges to 0. (ii) To show the practical ap-
097   plicability of our proof paradigm, we utilize this paradigm to establish that two existing smooth RM+
098   variants (Farina et al., 2023), Smooth Extra-gradient RM+ (SExRM+ ) and Smooth Predictive RM+
099   (SPRM+ ), achieve last-iterate convergence in learning an NE of games satisfying monotonicity. (iii)
100   Inspired by our proof paradigm, we propose Smooth Optimistic Gradient RM+ (SOGRM+ ), which
101   combines Optimistic Gradient (OG) (Cai & Zheng, 2022) and smooth RM+ variants. SOGRM+
102   achieves last-iterate convergence in games satisfying the weak MVI. (iv) Experimental results show
103   that SOGRM+ significantly outperforms other algorithms. (v) Our proof paradigm yields explicit
104
      best-iterate convergence rates for SExRM+ , SPRM+ , and SOGRM+ without any modifications.

120   Technical Novelty. We develop a remarkably succinct yet novel proof paradigm via two techniques:
121   the equivalence between RM+ and OMD in Liu et al. (2021) and the tangent residual (Cai et al.,
122   2022b). These techniques have been overlooked in previous works about the last-iterate convergence
123
      of RM+ variants (Meng et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2023). To the best of our knowledge, neither of these
      techniques was used alone to prove the last-iterate convergence of RM+ variants. We combine and
124
      extend both techniques, but this process is not straightforward. For example, the proof for SOGRM+
125
      requires additional techniques, i.e., transforming variables via the definition of the inner product to
126   use the weak MVI and tangent residual, rather than directly using equalities as in OG.

      3      O UR P ROOF PARADIGM
250
251   We now introduce our proof paradigm that includes: (i) recovering monotonicity or the weak MVI by
252   leveraging the equivalence between RM+ and OMD proposed by Liu et al. (2021), and (ii) measuring
253   the distance of RM+ variants to NE via the tangent residual to show that this distance is related
254   to the distance between accumulated regrets rather than the strategies in OMD based algorithms.

515   7    C ONCLUSIONS
516
517   We study the last-iterate convergence of RM+ variants in learning an NE for games that satisfy
518   monotonicity or only the weak MVI. We introduce a novel proof paradigm to analyze the last-iterate
519
      convergence of RM+ variants. Using this paradigm, we show that two existing variants, SExRM+
      and SPRM+ , exhibit last-iterate convergence in games with monotonicity. Building on this, we
520
      propose a new variant, SOGRM+ , which achieves last-iterate convergence in games satisfying the
521
      weak MVI. To our knowledge, this is the first last-iterate convergence results for RM+ variants in
522   such games. Our paradigm stands out for its simplicity and innovation, and we believe this approach
523   can extend to proving last-iterate convergence for additional RM+ variants in broader game classes.

1718   For the reason why SOGRM+ allows large ő∑ compared to other RM+ variants, we hypothesize that
1719   it arises because our proposed algorithm, SOGRM+ , performs only a single prox-mapping operator
1720   per update step, unlike other smooth RM+ algorithms, which involve two prox-mapping operations
1721   at each iteration (the first occurrence of the prox-mapping operator is in the introduction of OMD,
1722   Section 2).
1723   Specifically, the prox-mapping operator in smooth RM+ variants (such as SExRM+ , SPRM+ , and
1724   SOGRM+ ) involves a projection onto the simplex at sometimes (Farina et al., 2023) (not always
1725   as in OMD algorithms), which may lead to significant changes in őł depending on the choice of
1726   ő∑. In contrast, the update rule of SOGRM+ (in the second line) omits this prox-mapping operator
1727   and instead relies solely on simple addition and subtraction operations. As a result, the initial
       parameter őł0 may become negligible compared to the term ő∑Fi (őł). Thus, the values of őłi in


                                                                                                              32
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1728
       SOGRM+ are likely to vary in direct proportion to ő∑, and the resulting strategy xi = őłi /‚ą•őłi ‚ą•1 will
1729   exhibit a more stable behavior with respect to changes in ő∑. Therefore, for different values of ő∑,the
1730   sequence of strategies generated by SOGRM+ exhibits small differences. Moreover, when ő∑ is small,
1731   Theorem 5.1 guarantees thatthe sequence of strategies produced by SOGRM+ converges to the set of
1732   NE. Consequently, SOGRM+ permits the use of larger ő∑ values compared to other algorithms.
