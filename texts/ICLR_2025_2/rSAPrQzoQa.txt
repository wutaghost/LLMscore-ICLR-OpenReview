011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Subject (e.g., cell or patient) clustering is an important problem in genetics and
014            genomics. Influential features PCA (IF-PCA) is a recent idea for clustering, where
015
               we first select a small fraction of measured features and then cluster subjects
               (e.g., cells or patients) into different groups using the classical PCA clustering
016
               approach. A challenge the method faces is that we may have complex signal and
017
               noise structures across features or subjects or both, which may make the IF-PCA
018            less effective. To deal with such a challenge, we propose a new approach, IF-PCA+,
019            which combines IF-PCA with the recent idea of manifold fitting. The latter was
020            shown to better support class separation. We compare our approach with the most
021            popular subject clustering approaches, including but not limited to DESC, SC3
022            and Seurat, using 10 gene microarray data sets and 8 single-cell data sets. We
023            show that with the new method, we have a significant improvement in feature
024            selection accuracy, and that on average, our method outperforms several of the
025            most competitive algorithms nowadays (including IF-PCA, DESC, Seurat) in terms
026
               of average rank and regret for clustering accuracy and adjusted Rand index. We
               also shed light on the insight underlying such improvements.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Subject clustering, or high-dimensional clustering, uses measured features to group subjects such
032   as patients or cells into multiple classes, and it is a problem of significant interest (e.g., [Eisen et al.
033   (1998); Xu and Wunsch (2005); Jain (2010); Kiselev et al. (2019)]). Consider a setting where we have
034   n subjects from K different classes (i.e., normal patients, diseased patients). For each 1 ⩽ i ⩽ n, we
035   have a class label Yi that takes values from {1, 2, . . . , K} and a p-dimensional measured feature Xi .
036   The class labels are unknown, and the goal is to use X1 , X2 , . . . , Xn to estimate them.
037   In many applications (e.g., genomics [Eisen et al. (1998); Butler et al. (2018); Kiselev et al. (2019)],
038   finance [Belloni and Chernozhukov (2011)], and astronomy [Takeuchi et al. (2024)]), an important
039   observation is that, out of all p features, only a small fraction of them are useful in deciding which
040   subject belongs to which class. In such a setting, a reasonable approach to subject clustering is a
041   two-step algorithm as follows. In the first step, we perform a feature selection and retain only a small
042   fraction of the features which we consider as important. In the second step, let Ŝ be the indices of all
043   retained features, and let XŜ be the sub-matrix of X with columns restricted to Ŝ. We cluster all n
044   subjects into K classes by applying the classical spectral clustering algorithm to XŜ .
045
      The influential feature PCA (IF-PCA) proposed in [Jin and Wang (2016)] is an algorithm of this line.
046
      This algorithm has demonstrated empirical success in both microarray and single-cell data sets and
047   has undergone rigorous theoretical evaluation (e.g., [Chen et al. (2023); Jin and Wang (2016)]). In
048   particular, the approach has been shown to be optimal in the rare/weak signal model (e.g., [Chen et al.
049   (2023)]).
050
      With that being said, for complicated data sets such as microarray data and single-cell data, IF-PCA
051
      has room for improvements.
052
053   First, in previous studies on IF-PCA (e.g., [Jin and Wang (2016)]), the authors usually assumed that
      samples are independent, an assumption that does not always hold in single-cell data. Second, in the


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      feature selection step of IF-PCA, we test the significance of each feature one at a time, overlooking
055   correlations among features. Third, IF-PCA is not designed for addressing high dropout noise.
056   High dropout noise is frequently found in single-cell data sets, posing a well-known challenge for
057   high-dimensional clustering. Last but not least, existing works on IF-PCA usually considered a
058   model which we may call a linear model. In this model, we assume there are K feature vectors
059   µ1 , µ2 , . . . , µK and E[Xi ] = µk if and only if sample i belongs to class k. However, for single-cell
060   data, such a model may not be appropriate, and the relationship between Yi and Xi may be more
061   complex and is likely to be significantly nonlinear.
062   To overcome these limitations, we propose IF-PCA+, an enhanced version of IF-PCA that integrates
063   manifold fitting into the clustering process. By projecting data onto an estimated smooth manifold,
064   manifold fitting can offer improvements in several directions.
065
      First, manifold fitting recovers missing or distorted feature signals by addressing high dropout noise.
066
      In Section 4 Figure 1, we compared two approaches: one using sample-wise manifold fitting (as
067   by [Yao et al. (2024)]), and the other using our new method, which combines sample-wise and
068   feature-wise manifold fitting. Although both methods are effective at denoising and recovering true
069   features, our approach, which utilizes correlations between samples and features, proves significantly
070   more powerful. This suggests that manifold fitting is not only useful for removing high dropout noise,
071   but also should be applied across both samples and features.
072
      Second, manifold fitting enhances PCA clustering by improving class separation, i.e., (a) reducing
073   intra-class distances and (b) increasing inter-class distances. For (a), the denoising effect of manifold
074   fitting brings points within the same class closer together. For (b), in data sets with significant dropout
075   noise (e.g., zero-inflated entries), points with many zeros are projected near the origin in spectral
076   embedding. By filling these zero-inflated locations with meaningful signals from neighboring points,
077   manifold fitting increases the separation between different classes.
078
      The manifold fitting component of our algorithm, diffusion-based manifold fitting (DMF), is an
079   enhanced version of previous manifold fitting approaches. By utilizing diffusion maps, DMF
080   improves robustness against high-dimensional noise during neighborhood selection and removes
081   the need for additional transformations required by earlier methods, as demonstrated by empirical
082   evidence in Section 2.1.
083
      To integrate DMF efficiently with IF-PCA, we introduce two key modifications to the original IF-PCA
084
      algorithm. First, we remove the feature-wise normalization step. Second, we replace the fixed number
085   of singular vectors used in the K-means clustering step with a more adaptive approach. These changes
086   lead to a modified IF-PCA component that leverages the manifold structure of high-dimensional data
087   more effectively.
088
      Additionally, IF-PCA+ integrates both sample-wise and feature-wise manifold fitting, followed by the
089
      modified IF-PCA component, to address correlations across samples and features. To our knowledge,
090
      IF-PCA+ is the first algorithm to combine multiple manifold fitting steps for clustering tasks.
091
092   We benchmarked our approach against popular subject clustering methods, including but not limited
093   to SC3 [Kiselev et al. (2017)], Seurat [Satija et al. (2015)] and DESC [Li et al. (2020)], using 10
094
      gene microarray data sets and 8 single-cell data sets. For single-cell data sets, IF-PCA+ is the
      top-performing method in terms of average ranks and regrets for clustering accuracy and ranks second
095
      for the adjusted Rand index (ARI). Similarly, for microarray data sets, our method achieves the
096
      second-highest performance in terms of average ranks and regrets for clustering accuracy. Compared
097   to IF-PCA, our method performs similarly on data sets where IF-PCA already achieves high accuracy
098   (e.g., Deng, Darmanis, and Patel) and outperforms it on data sets where it faces challenges (e.g., Grun
099   and Goolam).
100
      In summary, by integrating both sample-wise and feature-wise manifold fitting and leveraging the
101
      enhanced DMF method with diffusion maps, IF-PCA+ is designed to handle data with correlations
102
      among features and samples while providing robust noise handling, especially in high-dimensional
103   settings with complex noise structures such as zero inflation.
104
105   Content. In Section 2, we provide a detailed explanation of our main method. Section 3 presents
106
      the empirical results of IF-PCA+, comparing its performance against several recent methods using
      8 single-cell data sets and 10 microarray data sets. Supporting simulation results are included in
107
      Section 4, and Section 5 concludes with a discussion.

115   2.1   DMF: A N I MPROVED A PPROACH FOR ROBUST M ANIFOLD F ITTING
116
117   Manifold fitting is a long-standing problem that has been addressed theoretically in the past several
118   years by Fefferman et al. and Yao et al ([Fefferman et al. (2018; 0); Yao et al. (2023)]). Unlike popular
119   manifold embedding methods, which project data onto a low-dimensional space, manifold fitting
120
      methods fit a low-dimensional manifold within the original space. One of the latest advancements,
      ysl23, proposed in [Yao et al. (2023)], ensures that the estimated manifold is smooth (twice differen-
121
      tiable) by using Euclidean nearest neighbors to estimate projection directions and then projecting
122
      points onto these directions. Building on ysl23, a clustering framework called Single-cell Analysis
123   via Manifold Fitting (scAMF) was introduced in [Yao et al. (2024)]. The manifold fitting step of
124   scAMF, which we referred to as Yao2, is a modification of ysl23 that consists of four steps: (a)
125   correlation-based shared nearest neighbors (SNN) (b) value-to-rank transformation (c) estimation of
126   the projection direction and (d) calculation of projection. Compared to ysl23, Yao2 tackles the curse
127   of dimensionality with step (a), stabilizes variance through step (b), relaxes the smoothness condition
128   in step (c), and simplifies calculations in step (d).
129   However, Yao2 still has places to improve. For one, its value-to-rank transformation is not optimal
130   for dealing with heavy zero inflation. For another, its neighborhood selection relies solely on local
131   information. To address these limitations, we propose diffusion-based manifold fitting (DMF) as an
132   enhanced manifold fitting method utilizing diffusion maps. The DMF algorithm runs as follows.

194   2.2    M ODIFIED IF-PCA COMPONENT
195
196   The IF-PCA implemented in IF-PCA+ have two modifications to the orthodox IF-PCA proposed
197   in [Jin and Wang (2016)]. Firstly, we implemented a variant of orthodox IF-PCA, IF-PCA(X), that
198   applies the PCA step directly to the input matrix instead of a normalized matrix, as proposed in [Chen
199   et al. (2023)]. Since the input of the IF-PCA step in IF-PCA+ would be the manifold fitted result
200   M̂ , a feature-wise normalization from the original IF-PCA would distort the shape of the estimated
201   manifold, leading to loss of information and is thus unwanted.
202   Secondly, the PCA clustering step of the orthodox IF-PCA uses (K − 1) left singular vectors to run
203   the k-means algorithm. However, after the projection of noisy data to a low-dimensional manifold,
204   the (K − 1) left singular vectors may not be sufficient to capture useful signals of the manifold. This
205   problem is especially significant for data sets with a complex data model but a small K value (e.g.,
206   K = 2). To remedy this problem, we use K0 = max{4, K} top left singular vectors to run k-means.
207   For more discussion on the choice of K0 , see Appendix Section B.

247   2.3   IF-PCA+
248
249
      We now introduce our main method, IF-PCA+, which follows a pipeline consisting of data transfor-
      mation, sample-wise manifold fitting, feature-wise manifold fitting, feature selection, and clustering.
250
251   Note that two input parameters in DMF, Kdiff and ϵ, come from the diffusion map. In IF-PCA+,
252   we first let Kdiff = max{4, K} be the same with the K0 in modified IF-PCA, then implement
253   diffusion map using the ‘pyDiffMap’ python package, which automatically selects ϵ based on the
254   bgh algorithm by Berry, Harlim and Giannakis [Berry et al. (2015)]. When bgh does not converge
255
      or the automatically selected ϵ is insufficient for the convergence of diffusion map (commonly in
      feature-wise manifold fitting), we set ϵ to be 5 times the variance of the Euclidean distance matrix
256
      computed between the rows of the input matrix. Thus, in practice, the combination of sample-wise
257
      and feature-wise DMF gives rise to two tuning parameters knns and knnf , respectively.
258
259   Depending on the signal strength (average number of samples per cluster), applying feature-wise
260   manifold fitting may or may not be advisable, as the feature-wise signal strength in some data sets
261
      may be too low to reliably fit a manifold. For example, in the Goolam single-cell data set, 41% of
      features have fewer than 20 nonzero entries. Therefore, a tuning parameter n0 that determines when
262
      to apply feature-wise manifold fitting is necessary.
263

299   data sets properties, the full IF-PCA+ is recommended for general use.

      In summary, from the incorporation of diffusion maps to the development of modified IF-PCA and
      the integration of both sample-wise and feature-wise manifold fitting, each step in our algorithm
302
      is carefully designed to enhance its robustness, adaptability, and performance. This is evidenced
303
      by improved results in both simulations and empirical studies. Compared to the original IF-PCA,
304   IF-PCA+ is more effective at handling nonlinear data, capturing correlations between features and
305   samples, and providing robust noise management.

307   2.4   C OMPUTATIONAL COMPLEXITY AND OTHER METHODS
308
309   In addition to IF-PCA and IF-PCA+, we also considered a range of state-of-the-art clustering methods
310   including manifold learning techniques such as scAMF (Single-cell Analysis via Manifold Fitting)
311   [Yao et al. (2024)], deep-learning algorithms such as IF-VAE [Chen et al. (2023)] and DESC [Li et al.
312   (2020)], as well as established clustering methods like SC3 [Kiselev et al. (2017)] and Seurat [Satija
313   et al. (2015)]. For a brief introduction to these methods, see Appendix Section A.
314   A asymptotic computational complexity analysis of these methods is provided in Appendix Section
315   C. We find that, for large high-dimensional data sets, DMF has a lower computational complexity
316   than Yao2, primarily due to the omission of the value-to-rank transformation step, which significantly
317   increases the complexity in Yao2.  When dealing with weak signal data, IF-PCA+ achieves a
318   complexity of O n2 p + p log(p) , which is notably smaller than the manifold fitting method scAMF.
319   For stronger signal
                       data, IF-PCA+ incorporates feature-wise information, resulting in a complexity of
320
      O n2 p + p2 n .

322   p = 5547). The methods DESC, IF-VAE, IF-PCA, and IF-PCA+ are implemented in Python, with
323   the VAE steps of IF-VAE and the autoencoder step of DESC implemented through the Python ‘keras’
      package. SC3 and Seurat are implemented in R using the SC3 package from Bioconductor and the

527   5    D ISCUSSION
528
529   In this paper, we introduced IF-PCA+, an enhanced clustering algorithm designed to overcome the
530
      limitations of IF-PCA when applied to complex data sets such as microarray and single-cell data. By
      integrating both sample-wise and feature-wise manifold fitting, leveraging the enhanced DMF method
531
      with diffusion maps, IF-PCA+ demonstrates robust noise handling, especially in high-dimensional
532
      settings with challenging noise structures such as zero inflation.
533
534   Our two-step manifold fitting approach has more potentials. It can be combined with other feature
535   selection steps and clustering methods, both linear and nonlinear. Our approach can also be applied
536
      to finance, network analysis, astronomy, and many other fields.
537   One limitation of our method is the need for three tuning parameters. How should one optimally
538   combine two manifold fitting steps could be an interesting question to explore. Our method also
539   implicitly assumes the existence of a low-dimensional smooth manifold, which may not hold true for
      other data sets, and can impact the generalizability of our approach.

619   uses the ReLU activation, and the decoder uses the sigmoid activation. The only tuning parameter in
620   this algorithm is the dimension of the latent space in the VAE, denoted by d.

      Deep Embedding for Single-cell Clustering (DESC) is another autoencoder inspired clustering

      algorithm proposed in [Li et al. (2020)]. It uses a stacked autoencoder for data representation and

      B    A DDITIONAL COMMENTS ON MODIFIED IF-PCA
655
656   Let K0 denote the number of left singular vectors used in modified IF-PCA step. Here, we explain
657   the rationale for setting K0 = max{4, K}.
658
659
      Empirically, higher values of K0 often yield better performance. For instance, in the Grun data set
      with only two clusters (K = 2), using the top 6 left singular vectors during IF-PCA+ achieves an
660
      accuracy of 0.879, while using 7 left singular vectors improved accuracy further to 0.886. In contrast,
661
      using only 4 left singular vectors resulted in a lower accuracy of 0.853 . However, increasing K0
662   often comes with trade-offs, such as higher computational costs and a greater risk of overfitting,
663   particularly in data sets with a small number of clusters or with complex noise structures.
664
665
      Upon examining the scree plots of various single-cell data sets, including Camp1, Camp2, and Grun,
      we noticed a consistent pattern: the elbow point occured at 4-th singular values across these data sets.
666
      This observation strongly suggests that at least 4 dimensions are required to capture the core structure
667
      of the data. Additionally, to account for the loss of information during the fitting of a low-dimensional
668   manifold, we adjusted K − 1 to K for data sets with larger K values to ensure robustness.

671   C    A SYMPTOTIC COMPUTATIONAL COMPLEXITY OF METHODS
672
673
      In this section, we discuss the asymptotic computational complexity of the methods used in our
      study. Given that the complexity of deep-learning algorithms varies significantly depending on their
674
      parameters, we focus our analysis on non-deep-learning algorithms.
675
676   DMF: For the manifold fitting method DMF, there are three main steps: the diffusion neighborhood
677   step, the estimation of direction step, and the projection step. In the diffusion neighborhood step,
678   the computational complexity is primarily determined by three components: the computation of
679
      the Gaussian kernel matrix during diffusion map construction, the eigen decomposition required to
      extract diffusion coordinates, and the computation of k-nearest neighbors      in the resulting diffusion
680
      space. The Gaussian kernel computation has a complexity of O n2 p .  The eigen decomposition of
681
      the diffusion matrix contributes an additional complexity of O n2 Kdiff , where Kdiff is the number
682
      of retained diffusion dimensionality. Finally, finding the k-nearest neighbors in the diffusion space has
683

      a complexity of O n2 p in a naive implementation.       Together, the diffusion neighborhood step has a
684
      total computational cost dominated by O n2 p . Our implementation of the last two steps of DMF
685
      has a complexity of O(np · knn). With knn significantly smaller than p, DMF has a computation
686
      complexity of O(n2 p).
687
688   Yao2: Similar to DMF, Yao2 has a complexity of O(n2 p) from finding shared nearest neighborhoods,
689   a complexity of O(np log(p)) from the value-to-rank transformation, and a complexity of O(np·knn)
690
      from both the estimation of direction step and the projection step. Together Yao2 has a complexity of
      O(n2 p + np log(p)).
691
692   IF-PCA: IF-PCA consists of three main steps: a feature selection step using Kolmogorov-Smirnov
693   (KS) tests, an HCT step, and a PCA clustering step. The complexity of computing KS statistics
694   for a single feature is O(n log(n)), which results in a total complexity of O(np log(n)) for all p
695   features. The HCT step involves sorting feature scores and has a complexity of O(p log(p)). The PCA
696
      clustering step comprises two main components: singular value decomposition (SVD) and K-Means
      clustering, each with a complexity of O(npK), where K is the number of clusters. Therefore, the
697
      overall computational complexity of IF-PCA is O(np log(n) + p log(p)).
698
699   IF-PCA+: The IF-PCA+ algorithm integrates IF-PCA with both sample-wise
                                                                                 and feature-wise DMF,
700   resulting in a combined computational complexity of O n2 p + p2 n . However, for data sets with
701   weak signals, feature-wise DMF is not implemented, reducing the complexity to O n2 p + p log(p) ,
      which accounts for the sample-wise DMF and the PCA computation.


                                                        13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      scAMF: With Yao2 being the dominant step of scAMF, scAMF also has a complexity of O(n2 p +
703   np log(p)).
704
705   SC3: The five main steps of SC3 are: feature selection, pairwise distance calculations, PCA transfor-
706
      mation, k-means clustering, and consensus clustering. Their respective computational complexities
      are O(np), O(n2 p), O(npK), O(n2 p), and O(n2 log(n)), respectively. Overall, SC3 has a com-
707
      plexity of O(n2 p + n2 log(n)). For high-dimensional data, SC3’s complexity is dominated by
708
      O(n2 p).
709
710   Seurat: The clustering process in Seurat consists of the following steps: feature selection, data
711   scaling, PCA, and SNN modularity optimization-based clustering (using the Louvain algorithm).
712
      The computational complexities of these steps are, respectively, O(np + n log n), O(np), O(npK),
      and O(n2 ) (or O(k0 n log n) for sparse graphs, where k0 is the parameter for KNN ). Thus, the
713
      dominating complexity is O(npK + n2 ).
714
715   Summary: For large high-dimensional data sets, DMF has a lower computational complexity than
716   Yao2, primarily due to the omission of the value-to-rank transformation step, which significantly
717   increases the complexity in Yao2.
                                       When dealing with weak signal data, IF-PCA+ achieves a
718
      complexity of O n2 p + p log(p) , which is notably smaller than the manifold fitting method scAMF.
      For stronger signal
                       data, IF-PCA+ incorporates feature-wise information, resulting in a complexity of
719
      O n2 p + p2 n .

724   As discussed in Section 2.3, selecting optimal parameters is a difficult task. Without any prior
725   knowledge about the data set, we need to first set a reasonable value for the number of clusters K,
726   then proceed to select both n0 and knn parameters for DMF.
727   Selecting K: The selection of optimal K in high-dimensional clustering is an open question. Without
728   prior knowledge of the data set, a practical approach could involves initially estimating range of
729   K using methods like scree plots, then choosing a reasonable value using consensus matrices, as
730   implemented in SC3.

      in network clustering to high-dimensional clustering. This approach entails modeling the data as

734   matrix where the i th row is e′k (the k th standard basis vector of RK , 1 ≤ k ≤ K) if and only if
735   Sample i ∈ Class k, M ∈ RK,p is a matrix with each row corresponding to the mean signal of
736   a cluster, and Z representing the noise matrix. For each k = 1, 2, 3, . . ., one may then estimate
737
             P defining Xk = X − Ω̂k , one can define a test statistic using the idea of cycle-count,
      Ω̂k . By

      This theoretical framework would provide a more rigorous method for determining the optimal

742
743   After determining a reasonable K, one can proceed to select parameters such as n0 , knns and knnf .
744   Selecting knns and knnf : The parameters knns and knnf represent the number of nearest neighbors
745   considered in the sample-wise and feature-wise DMF step. Since both parameters function similarly,
746   we will focus on the selection of knns . Ideally, knns should be less than the smallest cluster size
747   to ensure meaningful local neighborhood √    structures. Without prior information on cluster sizes, a
748   common heuristic is to set knns close to n. However, as computational   √       demands increase with
749   larger knns values, it may be practical to threshold knns at max{ n, 100}.      √ When working with
750   multiple data sets of the same type, it is advisable to set knns to the smallest n across all data sets.
751   This strategy ensures consistency in parameter selection and facilitates comparative analyses.
752   Selecting n0 : The parameter n0 in DMF determines whether a feature-wise DMF step is implemented.
753   Serving as a lower bound, n0 ensures that a sufficient number of samples are available to accurately
754   recover the distribution of each feature. Without prior data set information, setting n0 = 50 is
755   advisable for noisy data. For data sets with particularly weak signals, increasing n0 to 100 may yield
      more reliable results.

791   F    S IMULATIONS FOR DMF
792
793   We are interested in studying the how neighborhoods of different metrics are affected under noise
794   and the curse of dimensionality. The simulation procedure consists of four main steps:
795
796          • Generate ground truth neighborhood: Randomly generate 777 points of a 3D sphere in
797            a 3D space. Use Euclidean distance in the original 3D space to define the ground truth
798            neighborhood, as Euclidean distance is naturally meaningful in this space. For each point,
799            find its top 15 nearest neighbors based on Euclidean distance in 3D space. This will act as
800
               the ground truth neighborhood.
801          • Embed data into 13111-Dimensional Space: The 777 points of 3D manifold give us a 777
802            by 3 matrix. Embed this matrix into a 13111-dimensional space by concatenating 13108
803            columns of entrywise Gaussian noise (with mean zero and variance σ 2 ) to the original
804
               3D data. Call the new 777 by 13111 data matrix A. To simulate dropout effect, we
               further multiply A entrywise with a Bernoulli random variable with probability q = 0.3,
805
               Xij = Aij Ber(q), with q = 0.3. So matrix X have a more complicated noise structure
806
               than A.
807
808          • Compute neighborhoods in 13111-dimensional space: Use the following four approaches to
809
               compute top 15 neighborhoods of each point:
               1. Euclidean Distance.

               2. Correlation Distance.
811
               3. Correlation-based Shared Nearest Neighbor (SNN) Neighborhood (Step (a) of Yao2).
812
               4. Diffusion-based Shared Nearest Neighbor (SNN) Neighborhood (Step (a) of DMF).
813
814          • For each approach, compare the neighborhoods obtained in the 13111-dimensional space to
815
               the ground truth neighborhoods from the original 3D space. Compute the average accuracy
               over 5 runs: fraction of correctly identified neighbors in the high-dimensional space that
816
               match the ground truth neighbors in 3D space.
