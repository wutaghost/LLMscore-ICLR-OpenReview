009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Interpreting machine learning models with high-level, human-understandable con-
012            cepts has gained increasing interest. The concept bottleneck model (CBM) is a
013            popular approach to providing interpretable models, relying on first predicting
014            the presence of concepts in a given input, and then using these concept scores
015            to predict a label of interest. Yet, CBMs suffer from lower accuracy compared
016            with standard black-box models, as they use a surrogate (and thus, interpretable)
017            predictor in lieu of the original model. In this work, we propose an approach
018
               that allows us to find a CBM in any standard black-box model via an invertible
               mapping from its latent space to an interpretable concept space. This method pre-
019
               serves the original black-box model’s prediction and thus has zero performance
020
               drop while providing human-understandable explanations. We evaluate the accu-
021            racy and interpretability of our method across various benchmarks, demonstrating
022            state-of-the-art explainability metrics while enjoying superior accuracy.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   As artificial intelligence (AI) demonstrates remarkable success in diverse domains, concerns regard-
028   ing interpretability (Holzinger et al. (2022); Dwivedi et al. (2023)), fairness (Chen et al. (2023);
029   Bharti et al. (2024)), and privacy (Oseni et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2024)) are also gaining increasing
030   attention. Particularly, while the complexity of deep learning models enables them to model com-
031   plex patterns, it also makes the decision-making process opaque. This “black-box” nature raises
032   concerns about deploying these models in high-stakes scenarios, and there is a growing demand for
033
      more transparent AI systems (Von Eschenbach (2021); Gryz & Rojszczak (2021)).
034   Numerous efforts have been made to enhance the interpretability of deep learning models. Many of
035   these works generate salience-map style explanations using gradient-based analysis (Simonyan et al.
036   (2013); Selvaraju et al. (2017)), game-theory approaches (Sundararajan & Najmi (2020); Teneggi
037   et al. (2022)), decomposition-based methods (Zhou et al. (2016); Shrikumar et al. (2017)), and
038   more. The salience map highlights important parts of the input (i.e., pixels in images; nodes in
039
      graphs; words in sentences, etc.), which provides valuable insights into where, or to what features,
      the model is looking at. However, salience maps might not always be sufficient, specifically when
040
      the prediction is based on global attributes, such as color, texture, or overall morphology, rather than
041
      specific input dimensions (Poeta et al. (2023)). This limitation is particularly evident in challenging
042   tasks like clinical diagnosis, where localizing a particular subregion in medical images may not fully
043   convey the reasoning of a model, and high-level, domain-relevant explanations are needed for more
044   meaningful explanations (Border & Sarder (2022); Venkatesh et al. (2024)).
045
      Concept-based explanations provide a compelling alternative, which explains classification models
046
      by high-level, human-understandable attributes such as color, shape, texture, and the presence of
047
      objects (Poeta et al. (2023)). The concept bottleneck model (CBM) (Losch et al. (2019); Koh et al.
048   (2020)) is one such method that consists of two interconnected predictors: a first concept predictor
049   that predicts the presence of specific abstract concepts in some embedded representation of the in-
050   put, and a subsequent (linear) model that outputs the probability of the class given the presence of
051   such concepts. Since this model explicitly constructs predictions that rely on the presence of con-
052   cepts, this layer is referred to as a concept bottleneck. More recently, Contrastive Language-Image
053   Pre-Training (CLIP) models are employed(Radford et al. (2021)) to replace the concept predictor
      and build the concept bottlenecks by calculating the alignment between certain images and textual

091   or CLIP models (which eliminates the need for dense concept annotations). Importantly, we impose
092   an invertibility constraint on the affine map to ensure the original black-box model’s prediction can
093   be recovered completely. In this way, the original black-box model is converted to a CBM without
094
      perturbing its prediction function (see Figure 1.c). Thus, we can explain that black-box model in a
      post-hoc manner without altering the original predictor, and retaining its performance. We evaluate
095
      our method on several image classification benchmarks, including general and fine-grained tasks.
096
      Unlike previous CBM-based works, which only show qualitative analysis or partial quantitative
097   results concerning the quality of interpretation, we quantitatively evaluate them by proposing precise
098   metrics with respect to human annotations (when possible), or to large-scale knowledge graphs.
099   Compared with other CBM-based works, our method consistently achieves the best accuracy and
100   offers comparable or better interpretations.

103   2   R ELATED WORKS
104
105   As alluded to above, our work is most closely related to CBM-based methods. More broadly, it is
106   also related to post-hoc conceptual explanation techniques, given our method aims to explain without
107   altering the original model. We review these prior works in both categories to put our contribution
      in context.

108
      Concept bottleneck model (CBM) The initial idea of CBM (Koh et al. (2020); Losch et al.
109   (2019)) relied on predicting concepts first and then using these predicted concept scores to make a fi-
110   nal classification, forming a concept bottleneck. Dense image-wise concept annotations are needed
111   to learn this concept bottleneck. Post-hoc CBM (PCBM) (Yuksekgonul et al. (2022)) proposed
112   learning concept activation vector (CAVs) in feature space as a concept bank (Kim et al. (2018)) and
113   project the image embeddings (extracted by a pre-trained image feature encoder) to CAVs to form
114   the concept bottleneck. Moreover, in cases where image-wise concept annotations are not available,
115   they show that the concept bottleneck can be obtained by aligning images with textual descriptions
116   of concepts through language-vision models such as CLIP (Radford et al. (2021)). Language in the
117
      Bottle (LaBo) (Yang et al. (2023)) and label-free CBM (Oikarinen et al. (2023)) follow a similar idea
      and further boost the accuracy by collecting concepts from a large language model and conducting
118
      the concept selection carefully. More recently, increasingly sophisticated methods have been dedi-
119
      cated to improving the concept bank quality from the aspect of completeness (Shang et al. (2024))
120   and flexibility Schrodi et al. (2024); Hu et al. (2024); Tan et al. (2024). All of these CBM methods
121   have the crucial limitation of performance drops compared to black-box models. While numerous
122   efforts have been made to minimize this performance gap, some prediction power is always lost in
123   all of these approaches. Our method, on the other hand, recovers the black-box model’s original
124   prediction exactly and therefore has zero performance drop by design.
125
126   Post-hoc concept explanations Post-hoc concept explanations typically need image-wise concept
127   annotations in the training set, or an auxiliary set equipped with such annotations. T-CAV (Kim et al.
128   (2018)) is a popular approach that learns a linear classifier in a black-box model’s feature space to
129   distinguish samples with and without certain concepts, parameterized by concept activation vec-
130
      tors (CAVs). The importance of the concept is then given by directional derivatives of prediction
      to CAVs. There are a bunch of extensions that generalize and enhance T-CAV. For instance, Auto-
131
      matic Concept-based Explanations (ACE) (Ghorbani et al. (2019)) consider superpixels in images as
132
      concepts and discover them automatically. ConceptSHAP (Yeh et al. (2020)) defines completeness
133   scores for CAVs, and uses Shapley values to quantify the individual importance. Concept activation
134   region (CAR) (Crabbé & van der Schaar (2022)) relaxes the linear separability assumption of CAV
135   and uses a region instead of a vector in latent space to represent a concept. Spatial CAV (Wang
136   & Lee (2022)) attributes CAVs to relevant spatial regions. Text2Concept (Moayeri et al. (2023))
137   derives CAV from texts. Casual Concept Effect (CaCE) (Goyal et al. (2019)), on the other hand, as-
138   sesses the causal effect of the presence of concepts by generating counterfactual samples on samples
139   annotated with concepts. More recently, Teneggi & Sulam (2024) uses conditional independence of
140   concepts and sequential kernelized testing (Shekhar & Ramdas (2023)) to assess concept impor-
141
      tance in predictions. The vast majority of these methods need costly concept annotations to define
      concepts in a black-box model’s hidden space.

      3.4   C OMPARATIVE METHODS
286
287   We compare our method with several CBM-based approaches, all of which employ CMBs coupled
288   with vision-language models like CLIP, but differ in their strategy for concept collection and in how
289   they predict labels.
290
291   Post-hoc CBM (PCBM) (Yuksekgonul et al. (2022)) This method has two versions depending
292   on whether dense concept annotations are available. In cases with annotations, PCBM trains a linear
293   classifier to learn concept activation vectors (CAV) (Kim et al. (2018)), and projects the image
294   embedding onto these CAVs to obtain concept scores. In cases without annotations, CLIP is used to
295
      encode the image and textual concepts, and the CLIP-derived image embedding vectors are projected
      onto text embeddings to get concept scores. In both versions a sparse linear classifier is trained on
296
      these concept scores to make final predictions. This method can also be extended by a hybrid PCBM
297
      (PCBM-h), which introduces an uninterpretable residual predictor to boost prediction accuracy, but
298   compromises interpretability.
299
300   Language in a Bottle (LaBo) (Yang et al. (2023)) This method assumes ground-truth concept
301   annotations are not available. The scheme is similar to the CLIP version of PCBM, but the concept
302   score is defined as the inner product of image embedding and concept embedding, instead of the
303   projection length. A linear predictor with softmax-normalized coefficients is then trained to predict
304   labels, with no sparsity regularization.
305
306   Label free CBM (LF-CBM) (Oikarinen et al. (2023)) LF-CBM first learns a linear mapping
307   from a black-box model’s hidden space to a concept space, and then trains a sparse linear predictor
308   on the mapped image features to predict labels. While the mapping from hidden space to concept
309   space shares a similar motivation to our method, it learns a new predictor to predict labels – thus, it
310
      cannot explain the original prediction, and typically results in a loss of predictive power. In contrast,
      our method learns an invertible mapping, ensuring the original model is preserved.
311
312   These methods also differ in the ways that their respective concept banks are constructed: PCBM
313   queries ConceptNet (Speer et al. (2017)), while LaBo and LF-CBM query large language models.
314   Our method is flexible, and we consider various concept sources for different types of datasets, as
315   expanded in Section 4.

358   (Radford et al. (2021)) as the backbone and attach a two-layer multi-layer perceptron (MLP) to it.
359   The hidden dimension of MLP is set to be 64 for CIFAR-10 and AwA2, and 256 for CIFAR-100,
360
      CUB-200, and Food-101. For ImageNet-1K, a linear layer replaces the MLP since the number of
      concepts is very large. During the training of black-box models, the image encoder is fixed across
361
      all datasets. Additionally, we use CLIP-ViT-L/14 as the CLIP model for interpretation, employing
362
      its image encoder as EI and text encoder as ES . Results of other versions of CLIP models are also
363   included in Appendix A.5, specifically in Figure A.4.

510   5   D ISCUSSION , L IMITATIONS , AND C ONCLUSION
511
      In this work, we introduce CBM-zero, which explains black-box models by constructing a CBM
512   via an invertible mapping between its latent space and an interpretable concept space. This con-
513   cept space can be derived from CLIP models, eliminating the need for dense concept annotations.
514   Unlike other CBMs, CBM-zero does not alter the original black-box model, preserving its perfor-
515   mance exactly. Experiments across various benchmarks demonstrate its superiority in maintaining
516   the model’s accuracy and providing high-quality interpretations compared with states-of-arts.
517
      This work also has several limitations. First, the affine mapping we use might not always be pow-
518   erful enough to map image features from hidden space to concept space accurately, which could
519   affect the faithfulness of the explanation (Margeloiu et al. (2021); Havasi et al. (2022); Huang et al.
520   (2024)). Leveraging more expressive yet still invertible models (e.g., normalizing flows Kobyzev
521   et al. (2020)) in the future might address this. Moreover, the reliance on CLIP models introduces
522   limitations when concepts are fine-grained features (e.g., CUB-200-2011 annotations) without fine-
523   tuning. CLIP scores measure correlation rather than directly indicating the presence of concepts.
524   Therefore, an image of a baby might get a high score for “stroller” even if there is no stroller
525   shown in the image. Exploring alternatives, such as querying large language and vision models
526
      (Chattopadhyay et al. (2024)) or human collaborators (Chauhan et al. (2023)) about the presence of
      certain concepts, might address this issue. Finally, the concept banks used are not perfect. Concept-
527
      Net, although large and evolving, is not sufficient to represent the numerous concepts and complex
528
      relationships in the real world. GPT-generated concepts are more flexible and diverse but might be
529   overly complex and sometimes these concepts are not visual descriptions. Both ConceptNet and
530   GPT-generated concepts can be inaccurate in describing the relationship between concepts, with
531   unclear levels of noise. Human annotations, while more accurate, are costly and labor-intensive,
532   hindering its board applicability. Incorporating recent advances in concept discovery (Shang et al.
533   (2024); Huang et al. (2024); Schrodi et al. (2024); Hu et al. (2024)), as well as incorporating notions
534   of uncertainty quantification to our results (Angelopoulos et al. (2020); Teneggi et al. (2023)), may
535   help generate more reasonable explanations.

      A.5     H YPERPARAMETER SENSITIVITY AND ABLATION
786
787   As described in Section 3.2, we apply an exponential transformation with power t on the CLIP scores
788   to emphasize concepts with higher correlation to images. In the optimization function (equation 3),
789   λ controls the regularization strength. In this section, we discuss the sensitivity of global explanation
790   quality to these two hyperparameters. Moreover, we explore the impact of using other versions of
791   CLIP models. Note that the classification accuracy remains unchanged given the same black-box
792   model, regardless of the choice of hyperparameters and CLIP models.
793
794   Exponential power t Figure A.2 shows X-factuality@k as a function of k with exponential trans-
795   formation power t of 1 (i.e., no exponential transformation), 3, and 5 across different datasets. In all
796
      cases, t = 5, gains the best results, particularly for Food-101.
797
798
      Regularization strength λ Figure A.3 shows X-factuality@k v.s. k of global explanation for
      different regularization strengths λ. The default choice, λ = d2 in general performs best across
799
      different sets, and the results are not sensitive to the choice of λ. Data-specific hyperparameter
800
      tuning is expected to boost the results further.
801
802   Other versions of CLIP models In the main text, we use CLIP-ViT-L/14 by OpenAI as the CLIP
803   model to construct the concept bottleneck models. Here, we explore the results of using other
804   versions of CLIP: RN50, RN101, ViT-B/32, and ViT-B/16. Figure A.4 shows the X-factuality for
805   different models, with CLIP-ViT-L/14 consistently obtaining the best results.
