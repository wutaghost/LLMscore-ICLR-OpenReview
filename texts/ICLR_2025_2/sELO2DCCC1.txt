009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Solar Radiation Management (SRM) is emerging as a potential geoengineering
012            strategy to address the climate change crisis, but its effective implementation re-
013            quires an iterative and large ensemble of highly accurate and efficient climate
014            projections. Traditional climate projections rely on executing computationally
015            demanding and time-consuming numerical climate models. Recent advances in
016            machine learning (ML) aim to enhance these approaches by emulating traditional
017            methods. In this work, we propose a novel framework for directly learning the
018            relationship between solar radiation flux at the top of the atmosphere and the cor-
019
               responding surface temperature response. To evaluate the feasibility of this direct
               ML-based projection, we developed a benchmark dataset using an intermediate
020
               complexity model, incorporating a comprehensive suite of different forcing pat-
021
               terns and evaluation metrics to rigorously assess the ML model’s performance. We
022            introduce a Conditional Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (cDDPM) for
023            this task, which demonstrates superior performance in representing climate statis-
024            tics under previously unseen forcing patterns. This approach provides a promising
025            pathway for direct climate projections by accurately learning the forcing-response
026            relationship, with a wide range of applications in climate change mitigation, emis-
027            sions policy design, and SRM strategies.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Reliable prediction of climate change response under external forcing and the uncertainty thereof
033   has emerged as a central focus of climate research, especially in the context of Solar Radiation Man-
034   agement (SRM) as a potential geoengineering strategy to mitigate climate change. Initial efforts
035   were limited to assessing the equilibrium climate sensitivity (ECS) that characterizes the long-term
036   mean global temperature response in response to anthropogenic forcing from a doubling of the at-
037   mospheric CO2 concentration (Roe (2009); Knutti et al. (2017)). More recent efforts have improved
038   our understanding of the regional heterogeneity of the climate response from a linear perspective
039
      (Dong et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2022)). However, understanding and accurately predicting the full
      nonlinear climate response has remained challenging. This is further compounded by the chaotic
040
      nature of the Earth’s climate system, its hierarchical structure, and the high dimensionality of the
041
      state space (Ghil & Lucarini (2020)). While running large ensemble Earth system models is the
042   most reliable technique for climate prediction, it is compute-intensive. This puts a strong constraint
043   on the number of climate scenarios and ensemble members that can be simulated and the number of
044   parameters that can be perturbed for uncertainty quantification.
045
      We have compiled data from a large suite of Green’s function solar perturbation experiments that
046
      systematically probe the quantitative forcing-response relationship in an intermediate complexity
047
      climate model. Here, we use a denoising diffusion model to learn the emergent dynamic response
048   function for global surface temperature conditioned on the applied solar forcing pattern. The tem-
049   perature responses generated by the diffusion model also capture the inter-annual variability in the
050   responses. Our approach aims to provide a cheap surrogate that can rapidly generate large ensem-
051   bles of climate projections under various forcing scenarios while accurately capturing the internal
052   variability in the climate response. Additionally, our trained model could be used for transfer learn-
053   ing by fine-tuning using minimal data to emulate the climate-forcing response in fully coupled Earth
      system models.

      1.1     R ELATED W ORK

056   1.1.1    L EARNING LINEAR FORCING - RESPONSE RELATIONSHIP IN CLIMATE
057
058   Our study falls into the broad category of climate emulators, specifically those that predict climate
059   responses to external forcing. Many of the conventional methods used for climate emulation are
060
      based on simple pattern-scaling (Tebaldi & Arblaster (2014)), 1D impulse response function (Mac-
      Martin & Kravitz (2016)), and linear regression (Liu et al. (2022)). More importantly, these methods
061
      rely on the strong assumption that the climate response is sufficiently linear and time-invariant.
062
063
      1.1.2    ML FOR ATMOSPHERIC MODELING
064
065   Recently, deep learning-based autoregressive models have made significant progress in achieving
066   efficient and accurate medium-range weather predictions (e.g., Keisler, 2022; Pathak et al., 2022;
067   Bi et al., 2023; Lam et al., 2023). However, transferring this skill to long-term climate projections
068   remains challenging due to error accumulation that emerges in forecast roll-outs beyond a two-week
069   timescale. Efforts have been made to extend stable forecast horizons to up to 10 years under normal
070   climate conditions (Weyn et al., 2021; Watt-Meyer et al., 2023; Guan et al., 2024; Cachay et al.,
071   2024), but the generalizability of these autoregressive climate emulators to different climate forcing
072
      backgrounds has yet to be demonstrated.
073   An alternative to autoregressive climate emulation focuses on directly learning the forcing-response
074   relationship. Recent efforts have employed machine learning techniques, such as random forests,
075   Gaussian processes, and neural networks, to predict the full nonlinear climate response to potential
076   future anthropogenic emission pathways (Watson-Parris et al., 2022). In this approach, the projected
077   time variations in the global mean of anthropogenic greenhouse gas (GHG) concentrations across
078
      different scenarios serve as the input. However, these methods overlook the spatial forcing patterns
      associated with anthropogenic GHG emissions, limiting their ability to capture the full complexity
079
      of climate responses.
080
081
082
      1.2     C ONTRIBUTIONS
083
      This study presents several novel contributions to climate modeling and projections. First, it utilizes
084
      a comprehensive range of forcing configurations generated from an intermediate-complexity climate
085   model, as opposed to the limited scenarios typically derived from more complex models (Watson-
086   Parris et al. (2022)). Second, it represents the first attempt to directly project the equilibrium state
087   of the climate system from a forcing field using a generative AI technique, enabling a distributional
088   learning of climate responses. This would allow for not only climate projection of mean but also
089   extreme states. Our approach also learns the spatial relationship between the forcing and response
090   patterns. Lastly, the model was validated with an additional test case using a realistic climate model
091   with CO2 forcing, where it successfully produced a consistent global mean temperature distribution,
092   demonstrating its potential applicability to more complex and realistic climate scenarios.
093
094   2     C LIMATE F ORCING AND R ESPONSE DATASET
095
096   The data used in our study is generated using a widely used intermediate complexity climate model
097   called Planet Simulator (PlaSim) (Fraedrich et al. (2005)). The dynamical core of PlaSim is a simpli-
098   fied global circulation model (GCM) of the atmosphere with parameterization schemes for modeling
099   physical processes such as diffusion, radiative processes, moist processes including cloud formation
100   and precipitation, and dry convective adjustment. It also includes linearized representations of im-
101   portant Earth system components such as a slab ocean with sea ice and a land surface with biosphere.
102   The grid resolution for the simulations is chosen to be 5.6◦ × 5.6◦ .
103   To probe the patterned forcing-response relationship in the climate system, a large number of
104   Green’s function experiments, in the vein of Hassanzadeh & Kuang (2016); Liu et al. (2022), are
105   conducted by perturbing the incoming solar radiation at the top of the atmosphere (TOA) over lo-
106   calized patches (16 × 16) distributed around the globe as shown in Fig 1. Note that applied forcing
107   is seasonally varying as shown in the schematic such that the annual mean of the forcing is roughly
      the same across all the patches irrespective of the location. We use six different forcing levels

138   We employ a conditional denoising diffusion probabilistic model (cDDPM) to generate an ensemble
139   of climate projections conditioned on the applied forcing pattern.

403   6   C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
404
405   In this study, we developed a conditional denoising diffusion model with a U-Net backbone to learn
406   the relationship between forcing perturbations and climate system responses. This proof-of-concept
407   study with a minimalist cDDPM demonstrates the potential for training a direct distributional pro-
408
      jection of global temperature responses based on the context of a forcing perturbation field as input.
      Notably, this work represents the first attempt to train a machine learning model for climate pro-
409
      jection using a comprehesive set of forcing configurations generated by an intermediate-complexity
410
      climate model. The generated samples were validated against independent test cases using various
411   temperature metrics, showing that the cDDPM is capable of producing samples that are represen-
412   tative of both the intermediate-complexity model and the even more realistic and computationally
413   intensive CMIP models.
414
      This endeavor will prove invaluable for a wide range of applications, including climate change
415
      mitigation efforts, the development of effective emissions policy designs, and the exploration of
416
      SRM strategies. By providing a more accurate and efficient means of projecting climate responses
417   to various forcing scenarios, this approach can inform policymakers and scientists in crafting data-
418   driven strategies to reduce greenhouse gas emissions, implement adaptation measures, and assess
419   the potential risks and benefits of geoengineering techniques.
420
      Future work will focus on exploring alternative backbone architectures to enhance performance
421
      further. We also aim to extend the model’s capabilities to predict precipitation, which poses an even
422
      greater challenge due to its larger nonlinearity compared to temperature. Moreover, one potential
423   challenge in directly projecting climate responses from forcing perturbations is the limited training
424   data available in terms of the variety of forcing configurations. However, we suggest that the dataset
425   used in this study could serve as a valuable resource for pre-training foundational models like the
426   cDDPM we developed, which could then be fine-tuned using outputs from more realistic climate
427   models.
