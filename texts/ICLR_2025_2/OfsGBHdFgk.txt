009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Distributed and federated learning (D/FL) is a powerful machine learning (ML)
012            paradigm in which clients collaborate to train a model under the coordination of
013            a central server. Depending on the nature of clients, data in each client might
014            have the same distribution (called the homogeneous setting) or different distri-
015            butions (the heterogeneous setting). The state-of-the-art D/FL algorithm SCAF-
016            FOLD addresses the critical issue of data heterogeneity through the use of control
017            variables. However, while theoretical analysis suggests that the convergence rate
018
               of SCAFFOLD is independent of data heterogeneity, the practical performance
               of SCAFFOLD is often inconsistent in homogeneous and heterogeneous settings.
019
               Motivated by the disagreement between theory and practice of SCAFFOLD, in
020
               this work, we propose a novel D/FL algorithm to bridge this experimental perfor-
021            mance gap while preserving similar theoretical guarantees as SCAFFOLD. The
022            proposed algorithm accommodates arbitrary data heterogeneity, partial participa-
023            tion, local updates, and supports unbiased communication compression. Theoreti-
024            cally, we prove that our algorithm is unaffected by data heterogeneity and achieves
025            state-of-the-art convergence rate as SCAFFOLD. Furthermore, numerical exper-
026            iments indicate that our algorithm achieves consistent (similar) test accuracy in
027            both homogeneous and heterogeneous settings while often converges faster than
028            existing baselines.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Distributed and federated learning (D/FL) has garnered significant attention due to its effectiveness
      in the large-scale training of machine learning models (Kairouz et al., 2021). Data used in large-
034
      scale training is typically dispersed across a wide variety of clients (or agents). In both settings,
035
      a central server is used to orchestrate the local data processing of clients and their collaboration.
036   Under this scheme, the privacy of the clients’ data can be maintained as no explicit data is sent from
037   a client to a server (McMahan et al., 2017).
038
039
      In practice, both paradigms face several key challenges that need to be addressed in algorithmic
      development. First, not all clients are active at each training step. So, partial participation of clients
040
      needs to be accounted for in these algorithms. In addition, the communication between clients and
041
      the server is often the computational bottleneck, so D/FL algorithms often implement techniques
042   such as local updates and compression to reduce the overall communication costs.
043
044   Beyond the above common challenges faced by both distributed training and federated learning,
045
      one key difference between these two scenarios is the source of clients (or agents). In distributed
      training of machine learning (ML) models, high-performance computing resources are abstracted
046
      as clients/agents. In this case, data are distributed mainly in order to facilitate parallel computing
047
      and to accelerate model training. Therefore, data are distributed in a uniformly random manner,
048   as there is really no difference among those HPC resources. In contrast, clients in federated learn-
049   ing are different in nature, e.g., smartphones and IoT devices (Kairouz et al., 2021). Thus, the
050   data between clients naturally have different distributions, and this data heterogeneity has been ob-
051   served to affect the overall performance of existing algorithms if not accounted for in algorithmic
052   development (Khaled et al., 2020). Therefore, a distributed optimization algorithm that performs
053   consistently well in both homogeneous and heterogeneous settings are in urgent need for distributed
      training and federated learning.

          FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017) (as well as distributed stochastic gradient (DSGD) methods) has
067
          emerged as one of the most popular classes of algorithms in distributed training and federated learn-
068       ing. The core idea of FedAvg (and DSGD) is to have clients perform several stochastic gradient
069       descent steps using their local data before a global aggregation period. Despite the simplicity of this
070       scheme, both theoretical and experimental findings show that the performance of FedAvg signifi-
071       cantly suffers when the data between clients is heterogeneous (Khaled et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020b).
072       To address this issue, the seminal algorithm SCAFFOLD (Karimireddy et al., 2020) employs local
073       control variables stored on each client alongside a global control variable determined by a central
074       server to correct the “client drift” that otherwise occurs when simply taking stochastic gradient de-
075       scent updates. Theoretical analysis reveals that the convergence rate of SCAFFOLD is irrespective
076
          of the amount of data heterogeneity.
077       The promising theoretical findings of SCAFFOLD motivate our close study of its practical perfor-
078       mance. Unexpectedly, experimental results reveal that the theoretical benefits of SCAFFOLD do
079       not necessarily translate to performance gains in practice. In particular, in the MNIST dataset (see
080       Figure 1), the practical performance of SCAFFOLD is severely inconsistent in homogeneous and
081       heterogeneous settings. This behavior is surprising as theoretically the performance of SCAFFOLD
082
          should be completely invariant to data heterogeneity. This observation motivates the following ques-
          tion:
083
084                 Can we design a distributed and federated learning (D/FL) algorithm of which both theoretical
085                               and practical performance are unaffected by data heterogeneity?
086
087       Our closer examination of SCAFFOLD allows us to provide a concrete answer to this question
088       and leads to a new algorithm ISCA. First, during one epoch of local updates, SCAFFOLD keeps
089       updating local model parameters while keeping the local control variable fixed. This is undesirable
090
          as the local control variable is supposed to track the evolvement of the local model. Second, in
          SCAFFOLD, the local control variable is calculated using an ancient local model rather than the
091
          newest one. In this work, the aforementioned two observations are leveraged to develop a novel
092
          D/FL algorithm that not only possesses the theoretical guarantees of SCAFFOLD but also exhibits
093       consistent practical performance in both homogeneous and heterogeneous settings. We believe this
094       bridges the performance gap between SCAFFOLD and FedAvg we observe in the experiments.
095

      1.2    R ELATED W ORK
136
137   The FedAvg algorithm was first introduced in the seminal work (McMahan et al., 2017) as an algo-
138   rithm that combines local stochastic gradient updates on clients with a server that performs model
139   averaging. Through extensive empirical studies, McMahan et al. (2017) establishes the effective-
140   ness of FedAvg as an FL algorithm that reduces the number of communication rounds needed to
141
      train on decentralized data by orders of magnitude. Inspired by the promising experimental results
      of FedAvg, extensive efforts have been made to analyze the convergence of FedAvg under various
142
      settings. The works (Stich, 2019; Yu et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2020; Wang & Joshi, 2021) analyze Fe-
143
      dAvg under the scenario in which the data between agents is homogeneous and all clients participate
144   during the global aggregation period. These analyses are also extended to the more practical setting
145   in which the data between clients are heterogeneous, and only part of the clients participate at each
146   iteration. It has been shown that the presence of data heterogeneity deteriorates the performance of
147   FedAvg because of the “client drift” phenomenon (Li et al., 2020b; Khaled et al., 2020; Karimireddy
148   et al., 2020).
149
      An extensive body of work has been dedicated to addressing the issue of data heterogeneity between
150   clients. FedProx (Li et al., 2020a) adds a proximal term to the objective in order to endow the server
151   with a principled way to account for data heterogeneity. FedNova (Wang et al., 2020) handles data
152   heterogeneity by correctly weighing local models during the global averaging period. FedPD (Zhang
153   et al., 2021) is a meta-algorithm that takes inspiration from primal–dual based algorithms to handle
154   data heterogeneity. FedGATE (Haddadpour et al., 2021) leverages gradient tracking (Xu et al.,
155   2015; Lorenzo & Scutari, 2016; Nedić et al., 2017) to account for the data heterogeneity between
156   clients. The work (Cheng et al., 2024) reveals that FedAvg with momentum can converge without
157   making any assumption that bounds the data heterogeneity even when using a constant stepsize.
158
      SCAFFOLD (Karimireddy et al., 2020) is among the most famous FL algorithms and leverages the
      use of control variables stored on both clients and the server to correct the “client drift” that occurs
159
      when clients naively take stochastic gradient updates.
160
161   Communication compression has been integrated into FL algorithms to further reduce communica-
      tion costs. Examples of compressed FL methods include FedPAQ (Reisizadeh et al., 2020), Fed-

237   3.2 D EVELOPMENT OF ISCA
238
      Although the theoretical convergence of SCAFFOLD is independent of data heterogeneity Karim-
239   ireddy et al. (2020), its numerical performance does not seem to be consistent with the theoretical
240   findings. As observed in Figure 1, SCAFFOLD performs much better in the heterogeneous setting
241   than in the homogeneous setting. To fill this gap between theory and practice, we examine the it-
242   erations in SCAFFOLD carefully and present our proposed FL algorithm ISCA in Algorithm 1. In
243   particular, ISCA improves upon SCAFFOLD in the following two aspects.
244   First, we observe that in SCAFFOLD, the updates of local control variables (2) rely on the increment
245   of the local model (i.e., xt − yit,K ). Also note that the most recent local model yit,K is computed
246
      using the stochastic gradient evaluated at the previous local iteration (i.e., ∇F (yit,K−1 )); see (1)
247
      with k ← K − 1. That being said, although Karimireddy et al. (2020) claim that the local control
248   variable in SCAFFOLD tracks the true local gradient (i.e., cti ≈ ∇fi (xt )), such an approximation is
249   computed using the stochastic gradient evaluated at the previous local model, rather than the most
250   up-to-date (stochastic) gradient. Therefore, each active client uploads the newest local model and an
251   ancient local control variable to the server. This inconsistency might deteriorate the performance of
252   SCAFFOLD. In comparison, the proposed algorithm ISCA performs an additional update of local
253   control variables, using the (stochastic) gradient evaluated at the newest local model; see Lines 12
254   and 13 in Algorithm 1. Modification regarding this aspect is highlighted in red in Algorithm 1, and
255   vit,k in ISCA plays a similar role to cti in SCAFFOLD.
256
      Second, SCAFFOLD updates the local model yit,k with a fixed momentum term −cti + ct . This is
257
      undesirable because the local model keeps updating in the inner loop while the local control variable
258   is fixed. Although it is often claimed that cti ≈ ∇f (xt ) in SCAFFOLD Karimireddy et al. (2020),
259   the accuracy of such an approximation should be improved if the local control variable is updated
260   appropriately in the inner loop. Motivated by this observation, modification regarding this aspect is
261   highlighted in blue in Algorithm 1, and {vit,k }K+1
                                                      k=0 are the local control variables in ISCA.

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      This paper presents ISCA, a new distributed and federated learning (D/FL) algorithm that admits
489   local updates, partial participation, and arbitrary data heterogeneity. Theoretically, under minimal
490   assumptions, ISCA matches the state-of-the-art convergence of D/FL methods. Empirically, ISCA
491   converges faster than other baselines and achieves consistent test accuracy in the data homogeneous
492   and heterogeneous settings.
493
      Moreover, to further reduce the communication overload, we incorporate unbiased communication
494
      compression into ISCA and propose a new compressed FL algorithm called ISCAM. Theoretical
495   convergence of ISCAM is established under standard compressibilities and matches (or improves)
496   the state-of-the-art result for compressed FL methods. Numerical experiments also support our
497   theoretical findings on ISCAM.
