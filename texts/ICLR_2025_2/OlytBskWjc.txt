011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Protein inverse folding—that is, predicting an amino acid sequence that will fold
014            into the desired 3D structure—is an important problem for structure-based protein
015            design. Machine learning based methods for inverse folding typically use recovery
016
               of the original sequence as the optimization objective. However, inverse folding
               is a one-to-many problem where several sequences can fold to the same structure.
017
               Moreover, for many practical applications, it is often desirable to have multiple,
018
               diverse sequences that fold into the target structure since it allows for more candi-
019            date sequences for downstream optimizations. Here, we demonstrate that although
020            recent inverse folding methods show increased sequence recovery, their “foldable
021            diversity”—i.e. their ability to generate multiple non-similar sequences that fold
022            into the structures consistent with the target—does not increase. To address this,
023            we present RL-DIF, a categorical diffusion model for inverse folding that is pre-
024            trained on sequence recovery and tuned via reinforcement learning on structural
025            consistency. We find that RL-DIF achieves comparable sequence recovery and
026            structural consistency to benchmark models but shows greater foldable diversity:
027
               experiments show RL-DIF can achieve an foldable diversity of 29% on CATH 4.2,
               compared to 23% from models trained on the same dataset. The PyTorch model
028
               weights and sampling code are available on GitHub.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The task of designing sequences of amino acids that fold into a desired protein structure, also known
034
      as protein “inverse folding” (IF) Yue & Dill (1992); Ingraham et al. (2019), is a critical step for many
035
      protein design applications in areas such as therapeutics, biomaterials, and synthetic biology Ferruz
036   et al. (2023); Cao et al. (2022); Dickopf et al. (2020). Deep learning-based IF models are typically
037   trained to recover the observed sequence S of a protein, when conditioned on the corresponding
038   backbone structure X. Models are then evaluated on their ability to recover the original sequence
039   (“sequence recovery”), consistency of the generated sequence’s (usually predicted) structure with
040   the target structure (“structural consistency”), and the diversity of generated sequences (“sequence
041   diversity”). Sequence diversity is of particular interest since the inverse folding problem is a one-to-
042   many mapping in which a single (input) structure could be formed by multiple (output) amino acid
043   sequences. For practical applications, it is often desirable to find these alternative sequences that
044
      can fold into the desired structure, since it enables greater breadth of options for critical downstream
      optimization steps such as improving stability, preventing aggregation, and reducing immunogenic-
045
      ity. However, methods of increasing sequence diversity (e.g. raising the sampling temperature of an
046
      autoregressive model) may degrade the structural consistency of candidates. Hence, a useful prop-
047   erty of a protein inverse folding model is its “foldable diversity”—i.e. the diversity of generated
048   sequences that fold into the target structure.
049
050
      In this paper, we first characterize the trade-off between sequence diversity and structural consis-
      tency across multiple IF methods. We use these observations to motivate foldable diversity as a new
051
      metric to measure performance of IF models.
052
053   We then report a new protein inverse folding model, RL-DIF (Figure S3). RL-DIF is a categorical
      diffusion model pre-trained on sequence recovery and fine-tuned with reinforcement learning (RL)


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      to optimize structural consistency. The categorical diffusion component of our method is inspired by
055   GradeIF Yi et al. (2023a) and PiFold Gao et al. (2022), and is trained on observed protein structures
056   and sequences from the CATH 4.2 dataset. We then fine-tune the diffusion model with denois-
057   ing diffusion policy optimization (DDPO) Black et al. (2023) to maximize the expected structural
058   consistency of designed sequences. We find that this two-phase training results in higher-quality
059   designs while simultaneously ameliorating the tension between sequence diversity and structural
060   consistency.
061   We evaluate state of the art IF models and RL-DIF on four benchmark datasets: CATH 4.2, TS50,
062   TS500, and CASP15, and show that our model is able to balance sequence diversity and structural
063   consistency resulting in an increase of foldable diversity (29% with RL-DIF compared to 23% with
064   the best prior model), whilst keeping structural consistency at competitive levels to SOTA inverse
065   folding methods.

      3.1   F OLDABLE DIVERSITY
141
142   In realistic protein design scenarios, it is necessary to have a diverse pool of candidates for synthesis
143   and experimental characterization. We are particularly interested in the sequence diversity of IF
144   models when restricted to faithful structures, and so we propose foldable diversity (FD):
145
146
147       F OLDABLE D IVERSITY({Ŝ 1 , . . . , Ŝ M }) =
148                  M Xj−1
               2                                    h    n                              o        i
                              dH (Ŝ j , Ŝ k ) × 1 min sc-TM(Ŝ j ; S), sc-TM(Ŝ k ; S) > TMmin
                    X
149                                                                                                             (8)
150       M (M − 1) j=1
                           k=1
151
152   Foldable diversity only considers the diversity between pairs of sequences that are both structurally
153   consistent with the input protein backbone. Unlike sequence diversity, it is not possible to increase
154   FD by sampling high-entropy sequences, without also ensuring they fold correctly. We therefore
155   argue that FD is a more grounded metric for evaluating IF methods than sequence recovery (which
156   penalizes high-quality, highly-diverse sequences) and sequence diversity (which does not consider
157   sequence quality).
158   Unless otherwise stated, we set TMmin = 0.7. Among observed protein structures, this threshold
159   typically guarantees two proteins share a topological classification Xu & Zhang (2010); Wu et al.
160   (2020), i.e. are structurally similar. In this regime, FD combines sequence diversity and structural
161   consistency into a single quality measure, if we assume that all sequences with sc-TM above TMmin
      are “equally good”.

      3.3   RL- REFINEMENT OF INVERSE FOLDING MODELS
199
200   As noted above, inverse folding is a one-to-many mapping problem, with potentially a large number
201   of sequences satisfying a conditioned structure. Furthermore, publicly-available protein structure
202   data represents a sparse and heterogeneous sampling of the desired distribution p(S0 = S|X).
203   This one-to-many mapping and data paucity are key challenges in the generalization of conditional
204   generative models Yi et al. (2023b); Dauparas et al. (2022a); Hsu et al. (2022). While collecting
205
      more data is possible, this is an expensive and slow process.
206   On the other hand, given a proposal sequence Ŝ, we may verify its suitability for the conditioned
207   structure X by evaluating the self-consistency TM score. We therefore propose a second phase of
208   training, in which the inverse folding diffusion model is optimized for J (θ) = EŜ∼pθ [sc-TM(Ŝ; S)].
209   In particular, we use the DDPO algorithm summarized in Section 2.2.
210
211   Training During the second phase of training, we use the same training dataset described in Sec-
212   tion 3.2. Each training step takes as input a batch of 32 protein backbone structures. First, we
213   sample 4 sequences per structure from the diffusion model. Raw rewards (sc-TM) are standardized
214   (µ = 0, σ = 1) separately for each structure: each set of 4 sequences are shifted and scaled by
215   their mean and standard deviation respectively. Then, we perform minibatch gradient descent on the
      DDPO objective over the sample sequences (batch size of 32).


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Unless otherwise specified, all RL models are trained for 1000 steps. We used the Adam optimizer, a
217   learning rate of 10−5 and an effective batch size of 32 (again over 4 Nvidia A10 GPUs). As in Black
218   et al. (2024), we used clipping to enforce a trust region for the policy update, with a clip value of
219   0.2.
220
221   Protein folding During this phase of training, we sampled hundreds of thousands of sequences
222   from the policy. Folding all of these sequences is computationally challenging, so we use ESM-
223   Fold Lin et al. (2023) instead of AlphaFold2 to strike a balance between speed and accuracy. We
224   leave it to future work to investigate more accurate folding algorithms Jumper et al. (2021); Wu et al.
225   (2022).
226
      We used the ESMFold implementation in the Huggingface Transformers library Research and
227   wrapped it in an MLflow v2.3.6 framework. This was deployed on a Kubernetes cluster containing
228   20 Nvidia A10 GPUs, with an application load balancer to distribute traffic evenly. This architec-
229   ture was critical to enable efficient on-policy training, as folding generated sequences is far more
230   computationally-intensive than updating the policy.

      4     R ELATED W ORKS
234
235
      Inverse folding models A majority of existing IF methods utilize transformers (GraphTrans In-
      graham et al. (2019) and ESM-IF Hsu et al. (2022)) or graph neural networks in which nodes are
236
      amino acids, edges are defined between amino acids close together in the protein structure, and node
237
      and edge features are constructed from the protein structure backbone (ProteinMPNN Dauparas
238   et al. (2022b), PiFold Gao et al. (2022), Grade-IF Yi et al. (2023a)). The learning objective of these
239   methods can be discriminative Gao et al. (2022) or autoregressive Dauparas et al. (2022b); Hsu et al.
240   (2022); Ingraham et al. (2019).
241
      More recently, GradeIF Yi et al. (2023a) introduced a diffusion-based IF method. We note that the
242
      GradeIF results are competitive, but use solvent accessible surface area (SASA) features, which are
243
      strongly correlated with amino acid identity. These additional features are not typically part of the
244   IF task specification and their effect is studied in Appendix A.1.
245
246   KWDesign Gao et al. (2024) fuses information from pre-trained protein structure and language
247
      models (GearNet Lopes & Costa (2013), ESM2Lin et al. (2022), and ESMIFHsu et al. (2022)) to
      improve amino acid representations and boost sequence recovery to 61%.
248
249   Discrete Flow Models Campbell et al. (2024) can also be applied to the IF setting, generalizing
250   the discrete diffusion approach used by this work and others. Building on this, Discrete Guid-
251   ance Nisonoff et al. (2024) guides the sampling trajectory towards high-quality samples. In the
252   context of IF, this has been demonstrated to improve protein stability. While Discrete Guidance has
253
      not been applied to improve structural consistency, we note that it is a complementary method to
      ours.
254
255
256
      RL for biological sequence diffusion SEIKO Uehara et al. (2024) proposes a framework for
      online tuning of a diffusion model given a reward model and compares their method to DDPO
257
      and DPOK Fan et al. (2024). Among the evaluated problems is the design of green fluorescence
258
      protein (GFP) sequences. We note that the IF task differs from GFP design in that the former is a
259   conditional generation task, and the primary goal is to generalize to conditions (i.e. structures) not
260   observed during training.

285   5.2   M ODEL SAMPLING STRATEGIES
286
287   To sample diverse sequences from IF models, different strategies can be employed, depending on
288   how the model parameterizes p(S|X):
289
            • Single-shot models: These models parameterize p(S|X) by factorizing the joint distribu-
290                    QN
291
              tion into i=1 p(S[i]|X). This distribution can be reshaped by a temperature parameter.
              A temperature of 0 always picks the highest-probability AA, and higher temperatures en-
292
              courage greater diversity.
293
294         • Autoregressive models: AR models return a probability distribution over possible amino
295           acids for a given residue, conditioned on already-sampled residues. As in the single-shot
296
              case, we can reshape the conditional distributions with temperature.
297           If an AR model is trained with randomly-permuted residues (instead of left-to-right), then
298
              we may also randomize the order in which we decode residues. This allows diverse sam-
              pling even at a temperature of 0.
299
300         • Diffusion models: Since a diffusion model iteratively maps a random vector to an amino
301           acid sequence, we can introduce diversity by repeatedly sampling from a stationary distri-
302           bution p(ST ). We do not reshape the model-predicted posterior pθ (St−1 |St ).
303
      For the specific IF models evaluated in our study, we use the following settings:
304
305         • ProteinMPNN: An AR model, so we use temperature sampling with temperatures of 0.1,
306           0.2, and 0.3. We also sample at a temperature of 0 with random decoding order.
307
            • PiFold: A single-shot model, so we use temperature sampling with values 0.1 and 0.2.
308
309         • KWDesign: Another single-shot model, so we use the same settings as PiFold.
310         • ESMIF: An AR model. We follow the authors’ recommendation and sample with a tem-
311           perature of 1.
312         • DIF-Only and RL-DIF: Diffusion models, so we sample from the uniform distribution
313           p(ST ) and iteratively denoise sequences using the model.

523   6   C ONCLUSION
524
525
      Although sequence recovery, sequence diversity, and structural consistency are commonly used to
526
      evaluate protein IF models, here we demonstrate that those metrics alone do not necessarily capture
527
      the ability of the model to generate multiple sequences that fold into the desired structure. For many
528   practical applications, foldable diversity is a useful metric since it gives users multiple “shots-on-
529   goal” for filtering or optimizing designs on criteria beyond structural consistency. We also present
530   new inverse folding models: DIF-Only and RL-DIF. We find that among evaluated methods, these
531   models achieves highest foldable diversity and sc-TM in the CATH4.2, CASP15, TS50, and TS500
532   datasets.
533
      Nonetheless, we note some limitations of RL-DIF. First is the exclusive use of ESMFold as the
534   folding model. Alternative models such as AlphaFold2 Jumper et al. (2021), OpenFold Ahdritz
535   et al. (2024), or OmegaFold Wu et al. (2022) have demonstrated superior folding accuracy and could
536   potentially enhance our results. An ensemble of these models could also be employed to leverage
537   the specific strengths and mitigate the weaknesses of each individual model. Additionally, instead of
538   limiting our sampling to four iterations, we could continuously sample until achieving four samples
539   with a sc-TM greater than 0.7. While this approach would likely improve model accuracy, it also
      significantly increases computational cost. Consequently, we imposed a limit of four samples to

      manage computational resources. Finally, we could leverage entropy bonus methods used in RL to
541   further increase sequence diversity and exploration of the policy.

      Our findings indicate that increasing sampling temperature leads to an expected trade-off: FdD rises
815
      at the expense of folding success. That is, diversity can be increased, but only by rejecting a large
816
      fraction of sampled sequences. While, DIF-Only and RL-DIF also demonstrate a tension between
817   FdD and folding success, they achieve competitive or improved FD scores.
818
819   Across most datasets, RL-DIF shows improved folding success, aligning with our expectations since
820
      sc-TM was used as the reward function during reinforcement learning. Table S4 reveals a significant
      increase in folding success within the first 1000 steps of RL-DIF training, followed by a decline
821
      and stabilization. This trend is also evident in models trained with more data (Table S3), where
822
      RL-DIF-100K consistently surpasses DIF-Only-100K in folding success.
823
824   A trade-off between folded diversity and folding success is documented between DIF-Only-100K
825   and RL-DIF-100K, with DIF-Only-100K achieving higher folded diversity, even surpassing ESMIF
826
      across tested datasets, but at the cost of reduced folding success. Ultimately, the choice of model
      depends on the specific application, so we report both to guide selection.
827
828
829
      A.4   T HE EFFECT OF EXTRA SAMPLING ON FOLDABLE DIVERSITY
830
      It is computationally infeasible to sample and fold hundreds of sequences for benchmarking pur-
831   poses. However, to investigate the impact of sampling on foldable diversity, we sampled 100 se-
832   quences from each of three PDB structures: 8JZ1, 8QOC, and 1ANF. We selected these three PDB
833   structures for their structural diversity and limited our choice to three due to the high computational
834   cost of folding hundreds of sequences. We first calculated the foldable diversity for the entire set of
835   100 sequences. Then, we randomly selected 100,000 groups, each consisting of n sequences (with
836   n being 2, 4, 8, 10, and 20) from these 100 sequences. For each group, we calculated its foldable
837   diversity and determined the mean absolute difference between these groups and the full set of 100
838   sequences. (Figure S6). We then compare these mean differences to the minimum margin by which
839
      RLDIF outperforms the next best model (7%).
840   The maximum mean difference sampling at 4 sequences was 3.6% less than the minimum 7% mar-
841   gin observed in our results. This implies that if we sampled more than 4 sequences our results would
842   not change, as we observe the mean difference only drops as the number of samples increases (Fig-
843   ure S6). However, it is important to note the margin for CATH-single and CATH-short in (Table 1)
844   is 0% and thus falls within this margin of error. This would imply more sampling would be nec-
845
      essary to effectively discern which model outperforms the other for these datasets, but for all other
      benchmarks our results remain valid.
