009                                             Abstract
010
011           Large language models must balance their weight-encoded knowledge with
012           in-context information from prompts to generate accurate responses. This
013           paper investigates this interplay by analyzing how models of varying capacities
014           within the same family handle intentionally misleading in-context information.
015           Our experiments demonstrate that larger models exhibit higher resilience to
016           deceptive prompts, showcasing an advanced ability to interpret and integrate
017           prompt information with their internal knowledge. Furthermore, we find that
018
              larger models outperform smaller ones in following legitimate instructions,
              indicating that their resilience is not due to disregarding in-context information.
019
              We also show that this phenomenon is likely not a result of memorization
020
              but stems from the models’ ability to better leverage implicit task-relevant
021           information from the prompt alongside their internally stored knowledge.

024   1   Introduction
025
026   Large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing, demonstrating
027   remarkable capabilities in understanding, generating, and interacting with human language.
028   These models leverage two primary sources of information during inference: the static, encoded
029   knowledge stored within their weights—referred to as their world model (LeCun, 2022)—and
030   the dynamic, in-context information presented in the prompt.
031   The internal world model of an LLM captures the extensive knowledge acquired from pretraining
032   on vast amounts of data and subsequent fine-tuning. This knowledge enables the model to
033   understand, reason, and generate contextually relevant responses. We hypothesize that larger
034   models, with more parameters, develop more robust world models, allowing them
035   to better integrate and validate new information. In contrast, in-context information
036   can include arbitrary content, ranging from legitimate user requests to unreliable or malicious
037   information intended to deceive the model and undermine its reasoning.
038   This work studies how LLMs of varying capacities within the same model family balance
039   in-context information against their internal world models during inference. We focus in particular
040   on the open-source models Llama (Meta, 2024), Gemma (Google, 2024), Mistral (Jiang et al.,
041   2024), and Phi (Microsoft, 2024) to isolate the impact of model size and architecture (a task
042   not feasible with proprietary models). By injecting intentionally misleading information into the
043   prompts, we observe how these models process and respond to deceptive inputs, measuring how it
044
      affects their performance on popular multiple-choice benchmarks. This methodology allows us to
      assess the resilience of the models’ world knowledge against misinformation and deceitful content.
045
046   Our main finding is that larger language models exhibit greater resilience to deception,
047   maintaining higher relative performance when faced with misleading information at inference time
048   compared to their smaller counterparts. This result prompts us to revisit our initial hypothesis
049   that larger models develop more robust world models. Conversely, smaller models tend to rely
050
      excessively on the provided in-context information and are more susceptible to misinformed
      and deceptive cues, even when these contradict their internal knowledge, making them more
051
      vulnerable to manipulation and malicious attacks.
052
053   To support this key finding, we conduct additional control experiments to rule out alternative
      explanations. First, we confirm that larger models do not simply ignore in-context information,

      as they still follow legitimate instructions and incorporate truthful cues. Second, we show that
055   this resilience to deception is unlikely due to memorization from data leakage, but rather stems
056   from their ability to better integrate conflicting in-context information with their world model
057   knowledge. In summary, the contributions of this work are:
058
059         1. Larger Models are more Resilient to Deception. Using our evaluation framework,
060            we show that larger language models consistently demonstrate a higher resilience to
061            misleading in-context cues. This finding highlights an enhanced ability to combine
062            in-context information with their internal knowledge.
063         2. Resilience is not a Result of Ignoring In-Context Information. Our evaluation
064            strategy further confirms that larger models follow legitimate instructions and truthful
065            hints, disregarding the hypothesis that they could simply ignore injected in-context
066            information.
067         3. Resilience is not a Result of Memorization. We demonstrate that the improved
068            resilience in larger models is not due to memorization by comparing the behavior of
069            a model overfitted on the test data with that of a model guaranteed to be free of test
070            data contamination in its training set.

      2    Background
074
075
      The concept of “stochastic parrots” was introduced by Bender et al. (2021) as a pessimistic
      view of the stored knowledge and reasoning capabilities of LLMs, suggesting that these models
076
      might merely regurgitate training data without true understanding. Similarly, Schaeffer et al.
077
      (2023) argue that emergent capabilities in LLMs may be a mirage caused by steadily increasing
078   model capacities. However, LLMs have demonstrated abilities in reasoning and planning (Hao
079   et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023), which can be considered evidence of a black-box world model in
080   a behaviorist sense, as elaborated in Appendix F. In this context, a world model (LeCun, 2022)
081   refers to an internal representation that holistically grasps concepts, akin to human understanding,
082   enabling more robust behavior. Additionally, Delétang et al. (2024) demonstrate that LLMs
083   act as effective compressors, indicating that their capabilities extend beyond mere memorization.
084   Research on world models in foundation models (Bommasani et al., 2022) often focuses on
085   multi-modal contexts (Assran et al., 2023; Bardes et al., 2024; Garrido et al., 2024). From a
086   benchmarking perspective, GQA (Ainslie et al., 2023) and OpenEQA (Majumdar et al., 2024)
087   assess models’ abilities to reason over complex environments in multi-modal settings. Notably,
088   the concept of a world model is less explored and more vaguely defined in language models
089   compared to model-based reinforcement learning, where the world model is a central component
090   (Sutton, 1990; Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018; Hafner et al., 2019).
091   In this work, we are interested in exploring the robustness of the world model in a purely
092   language-based context by altering the evaluation methodologies of existing benchmarks. The
093   impact of methodological changes on model performance has been highlighted by Alzahrani et al.
094   (2024), who demonstrate the vulnerability of LLM leaderboards. Several studies (Wang et al.,
095   2024b; Wei et al., 2024; Zong et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; Gupta et al., 2024) have shown that
096   minor changes in evaluation, such as reordering multiple-choice answers, can significantly affect
097   model performance. Additionally, Lyu et al. (2024) argue that the commonly used log-likelihood
098
      evaluation for multiple-choice tasks may not correlate well with human perceived performance.
099   We see these vulnerabilities in evaluation methodologies as indicators of incoherence or flaws
100   in LLMs’ world models. Therefore, our core idea is to characterize these incoherences through
101   methodological alterations. This approach differs from works like MMLU-Redux (Gema
102   et al., 2024) and MMLU-Pro (Wang et al., 2024c), which focus on methodological and data
103   improvements to the original MMLU benchmark (Hendrycks et al., 2021a).
104   Our methodology shares some similarities with studies on indirect prompt injection attacks (Rossi
105   et al., 2024), extensively studied by others (Yu et al., 2024; Chowdhury et al., 2024; Kumar et al.,
106   2024). However, unlike those works, our alterations are manual and not intended to jailbreak
107   models or cause harmful behavior. Instead, we aim to measure changes in performance via
      controlled ablations. Another related research area is adversarial robustness, with benchmarks like

      3        Evaluation Methodology
140
141
      To assess the sensitivity of language models to in-context cues, we evaluate how additional prompt
142
      information affects their performance on a collection of popular multiple-choice question-answering
143   benchmarks. Specifically, we measure and compare the performance of each model on both the
144   original and altered versions of each benchmark, utilizing carefully designed prompt modifications
145   such as misleading hints, truthful hints, or specific instructions intended to change the model’s
146   behavior. This approach allows us to reveal how models of different sizes within the same family
147   incorporate and process information that either supports or contradicts their internal knowledge.
148
149   3.1        Prompt Unification
150
151   To ensure consistency across different benchmarks and models, we standardize the structure
152
      of all prompts using the MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021a) format. This standardization, referred
      to as Prompt Unification, involves a uniform preprocessing step applied to each sample in the
153
      evaluation pipeline. Specifically, each prompt is formatted to include the question followed by the
154
      possible choices, each labeled with corresponding letters (e.g., A, B, C, D). This uniform structure
155   not only facilitates a fair comparison but also enables the model to employ cross-choice reasoning.
156
157   3.2        Prompt Alteration
158
159   Building upon the unified prompt structure, we implement various prompt alterations to investigate
160   how language models handle conflicting or supportive in-context information. Utilizing the Lan-
161   guage Model Evaluation Harness framework (Gao et al., 2024), we introduce specific modifications
      to the content of the prompts. These alterations, detailed on the following page, include:

            • Deception: Injecting incorrect, deceptive information, intended to contradict the
163           model’s internal knowledge.
164

              model knowledge.
166
167         • Directive Instructions: Adding explicit, legitimate instructions that push the model

169         • Context Removal: Omitting the original question from the prompt to evaluate if the
170           model, relying on memorization, can select the correct answer based on the choices.
171
      The Deception experiment is designed to test the core hypothesis of this paper regarding the
172
      resilience of larger models (Section 4.1). The Guidance, Directive Instructions (Section 4.2),
173   and Context Removal (Section 4.3) experiments aim to reject alternative explanations. Note that
174   per each sample in every benchmark, each type of ablation must be applied identically across
175   all model families and sizes. Each model’s response is then determined by selecting the candidate
176   label with the highest log-likelihood across the possible choices. An example of both the unified
177   and altered prompt structures is provided in the following example:

      3.3   Models
193
194   We evaluate eight open-source LLMs spanning different model families and sizes to gain insights into
195   resilience and parameter scaling. These models include Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct and Llama-3.1-
196   70B-Instruct (Meta, 2024), Gemma-2-2B-it and Gemma-2-9B-it (Google, 2024), Phi-3-mini-4k-

198   Mixtral-8x22B-Instruct-v0.1 (Jiang et al., 2024). By focusing on models within the same family
199   but with different parameter counts, we aim to isolate the effect of scale on model performance.
200
      Open-source LLMs provide transparency in model architecture and parameter sizes, enabling
      analysis of model behavior relative to capacity. We specifically choose instruction-tuned versions
201
      of each model to ensure they are optimized for following instructions and processing in-context
202
      information, which is particularly important for our experiments as discussed in Section 4.2.
203

206
      all models except Phi-3-medium-4k-instruct, which requires one A100 GPU (40GB); Mixtral-

207

231   3.5    Metrics
232
233   Our study involves comparing model performances across various ablation experiments. To
234   effectively quantify the change in performance of each model under different conditions and across
235
      multiple benchmarks, we require a metric that accurately reflects these variations. A natural
      candidate is the Accuracy Drop, defined as the difference between the original performance
236
      and the performance under ablation (Accuracy Drop = Original Accuracy − Altered Accuracy).
237
      However, this metric does not account for differences across model families, sizes, or benchmarks,
238   as it lacks standardization.
239
240   For example, consider a model A that experiences a 5% Accuracy Drop under a specific ablation,
241
      going from 80% to 75%. If another model, B, also exhibits a 5% Accuracy Drop but from a
      significantly lower original performance, say from 60% to 55%, the absolute Accuracy Drop does
242
      not capture the relative importance of the drop on each model and benchmark. The performance
243
      change should be perceived differently between A and B, but the absolute Accuracy Drop fails
244   to reflect this discrepancy.
245
246   To address this issue, we employ the Relative Accuracy Drop, calculated as the Accuracy Drop
247
      divided by the Original Accuracy. In our previous example, for the same Absolute Accuracy Drop
      of 5% for models A and B, their Relative Accuracy Drop would be 6.25% and 8.33% respectively.
248
      This normalization technique allows us to compare performance changes across different models,
249
      sizes, ablations, and benchmarks, facilitating meaningful aggregation and analysis.

252   4     Experiments: Model Resilience vs Scale
253
254
      In this section, we present our empirical findings from a series of experiments designed to
      evaluate how language models of varying sizes within the same families respond to different types
255
      of in-context information. Our results reveal a significant and consistent trend: larger models
256
      consistently outperform their smaller counterparts in terms of effective assimilation of in-context
257   information, using their weight-encoded knowledge, i.e., the world model.
258
259   4.1    How Resilient are LLMs to Deception?
260
261   To deceive LLMs, we augment each original prompt with an incorrect hint that falsely identifies
262   one of the incorrect answer choices as the correct one. For example, if the correct answer is
263   option B, the prompt will include a misleading hint like “(hint: the correct answer is A).”
264   Assuming the models can derive the correct answer from the original question, this manipulation
265   creates a conflict with their internal knowledge, forcing them to assess the reliability of the hint
266   against their world model. We hypothesize that while all models will exhibit some degree of
267   performance decline due to the misleading hint, the extent of this drop will vary with model
268   size. Specifically, smaller models are expected to follow the incorrect hint more often, resulting
269   in a larger Relative Accuracy Drop. In contrast, larger models are anticipated to more effectively
      (in)validate the in-context information against their more robust internal world models.

406   4.3   Is Resilience due to Memorization?
407
408   While our findings in Sections 4.1 and 4.2 thus far support the hypothesis that larger models
409   have developed more robust world models, an alternative explanation arises: could this resilience
410
      be attributed to memorization? Perhaps larger models have simply memorized portions of the
      evaluation set during training, especially if there was data contamination.
411
412   To investigate this possibility, we design a third control experiment using the MMLU dataset.
413   In this experiment, we remove the question from the prompt, leaving only the multiple-choice
414   answer options. If a model has memorized the association between answer options and questions,
415   it might still achieve high accuracy even without the question.
416   Remarkably, as depicted in Figure 4, the accuracy of almost all models remains well above
417   the chance level (25%) even in the absence of the question. At first glance, this suggests that
418   memorization could be influencing the results. Alternatively, it could be that many MMLU
419   samples can be answered correctly without the explicit question, for example, when the answer
420   choices themselves contain sufficient information (facts that are correct or incorrect by themselves).
421   To push our examination further, we perform an additional experiment with two models: (1)
422   DCLM-7B (Li et al., 2024a), a language model guaranteed to have had no prior exposure to MMLU;

469   5    Conclusion
470
471
      In this paper, we introduced a powerful and straightforward evaluation strategy that re-uses existing
472
      benchmarks with minimal changes, enabling us to empirically gain new perspectives on the behavior
473
      of LLMs. Our experiments revealed that larger models exhibit higher resilience to deceptive
474   prompts, demonstrating an advanced ability to integrate prompt information with their internal
475   knowledge. They not only better resist deceptive cues but also effectively utilize correct hints,
476   showing superior instruction-following capabilities. This suggests that as models scale, their world
477   models inherently becomes more robust, enabling them to better resist misleading information
478   without disregarding legitimate instructions. Furthermore, a control experiment demonstrated
479   that this observed resilience is unlikely due to memorization because of data contamination.
480   To our knowledge, this study provides the first empirical evidence linking LLM capacity to
481   resilience against misinformation. Despite these insights, our study has limitations. First, we
482   focused exclusively on multiple-choice datasets. While these allow systematic performance
483   measurement across tasks with clear, objective answers, they may not capture the nuances
484   of open-ended or generative tasks. Second, our experiments involved only open-source LLMs.
485   Although this ensures transparency and reproducibility, it limits the generalizability of our findings
      to proprietary models with different architectures, training regimes, or performance characteristics.

884    the heap until no elements remain.
885    1. Building the Heap: The heap is built in O(n) time. This is because to
886    build a heap from an array of n elements, each insertion into the heap takes

       E     Overfitting Details
1267
1268
       We performed an overfitting experiment on instructed models to assess their possible test
1269
       data contamination and memorization of test examples in benchmarks. For this experiment,
1270   we test the Meta-Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct model (Meta, 2024). We performed this overfitting
1271   using Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA; Hu et al. (2021)), which reduces the number of trainable
1272   parameters by introducing low-rank matrices into each layer. We set the LoRA rank to 8 and
1273   the scaling factor to 32. We used a learning rate of 0.00001, and a total batch size of 64, using
1274   4 80GB A100 GPUs. The model was overfitted on the test split of MMLU, and evaluations
1275   were also conducted on this test split to maximize the potential for memorization. The training
1276   loop was executed for 50 epochs, ensuring extensive exposure to the data.

       F                   Understanding World Models within LLMs
1297
1298
       The concept of a “world model” in large language models carries ambiguity and can lead to
1299   different interpretations. To clarify our use of this term, we outline two primary hypotheses
1300   regarding what LLMs have learned and how they process information:
1301
1302                        • LLMs are sophisticated pattern matchers. Some researchers (Bender & Koller,
1303                          2020; Bisk et al., 2020) posit that LLMs primarily learn an extensive collection
1304                          of statistical correlations from their training data without forming a coherent or
1305                          interpretable understanding of the data-generating processes. In this view, LLMs
1306                          function as sophisticated pattern matchers that excel at predicting the next word based
1307
                              on learned associations but lack deeper comprehension.
1308                        • LLMs form an internal world model. In contrast, other studies (Gurnee &
1309                          Tegmark, 2024; Li et al., 2024b; Nanda et al., 2023; Li et al., 2021; Patel & Pavlick, 2022;
1310                          LeCun, 2022) suggest that LLMs, through the compression of vast amounts of training
1311
                              data, develop compact, coherent, and interpretable models of the generative processes
                              underlying the data—essentially forming an internal world model. This model enables
1312
                              the agent to assess the probability of different elements and concepts, determining what
1313
                              is more likely, plausible, or less probable within a given context (LeCun, 2022).
1314
                              For instance, Gurnee & Tegmark (2024) demonstrated that LLMs can learn linear
1315
                              representations of spatial and temporal concepts, indicating that they encode structured
1316                          knowledge about space and time within their internal representations. Another study
1317                          (Li et al., 2024b) showed that transformers trained on next-token prediction for the game
1318                          Othello develop explicit internal representations of the game state. Furthermore, Nanda
1319                          et al. (2023) revealed that these representations are linear and interpretable, suggesting
1320                          that the models internally capture the game’s rules and state transitions.
1321
1322   This paper is grounded in the latter hypothesis: we propose that LLMs build internal world
1323   models that extend beyond surface-level statistical patterns.

       On the other hand, evaluating open-ended tasks presents significant challenges. The subjective
       nature of potential answers makes it difficult to establish objective evaluation metrics. Common
1371
       generative metrics like BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) or ROUGE (Lin, 2004) primarily assess
1372
       surface-level n-gram overlaps, which may not adequately capture the correctness or relevance
1373   of a response, especially in the context of resilience to deceptive information. For example, a
1374   model could generate a syntactically correct yet factually incorrect answer, and these metrics
1375   would fail to sufficiently penalize such outputs. While human evaluation is a potential alternative,
1376   it is resource-intensive and beyond the scope of this study.
1377
       Acknowledging the value of broader task settings, we have included two generative benchmarks
1378
       on assessing math-solving abilities: MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021b) and GSM8K (Cobbe et al.,
1379   2021), as shown in Figures 10 and 9.
1380
1381   To deceive the models, we could no longer mislead with incorrect labels. Instead, we consistently
1382
       pushed the model to generate double the correct answer in GSM8K and aimed for an answer
       of 0 in MATH. The metric used for both benchmarks is exact match.
1383
1384   Results in Figure 10 confirm the main findings on deception: larger models are more resilient than
1385   smaller ones. However, the difference in the Relative Drop (based on exact match) between smaller
1386   and larger models is less noticeable compared to what we observed in Figure 2. This was expected,
1387   as assessment here is more complex. As previously mentioned, determining the “correctness” of
1388
       answers and the semantic divergence from the original response is inherently harder to compute.
1389   These results are further supported by the Directive Instruction experiments. As shown in
1390   Figure 9, larger models generally exhibit greater relative drops, reinforcing the findings presented
1391   in Section 4.2.
