009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Periodic point sets model all solid crystalline materials (crystals) whose atoms
012            can be considered zero-sized points with or without atomic types. This paper ad-
013            dresses the fundamental problem of checking whether claimed crystals are novel,
014            not noisy perturbations of known materials obtained by unrealistic atomic replace-
015            ments. Such near-duplicates have already skewed ground truth because past com-
016            parisons relied on discontinuous cells and symmetries. The proposed Lipschitz
017            continuity under noise is a new essential requirement for machine learning on any
018
               data objects that have ambiguous representations and live in continuous spaces.
               For periodic point sets under isometry (any distance-preserving transformation),
019
               we designed the invariants that distinguish all known counter-examples to the
020
               completeness of past descriptors and detect thousands of (near-)duplicates in the
021            world’s five largest databases in a few minutes on a modest desktop computer.

024   1   M OTIVATIONS FOR CONTINUOUS INVARIANTS OF PERIODIC CRYSTALS
025
026   Real data such as periodic crystals often have ambiguous representations in the sense that experi-
027   mental databases contain many substantially different entries encoding near-duplicate crystals with
028   essentially the same properties Peplow (2023). Using descriptors or representations that discontin-
029   uously change under tiny perturbations of input data can lead to unjustified claims Krämer (2021)
030   and even to ‘paper mills’ Bimler (2022) reporting thousands of ‘new’ materials without proof. These
031   public investigations Francis (2023) already led to hundreds of retracted papers Chawla (2022). Ma-
032
      chine learning can avoid such embarrassment by embracing a new continuous approach to data.
033   Any discovery should be validated by proper measurements, which are formalized by the concept
034   of a distance metric d satisfying three axioms. The first axiom says that the distance d(S, Q) = 0
035   between any materials S, Q vanishes if and only if S, Q are the same. What materials should be
036   called ‘the same’ Sacchi et al. (2020)? The relation of being ‘the same’ is called an equivalence S ∼
037   Q if the following axioms hold: (1) any object is equivalent to itself S ∼ S, (2) symmetry: if S ∼ Q
038
      then Q ∼ S, (3) transitivity: if S ∼ Q and Q ∼ T then S ∼ T . The transitivity axiom is especially
      important by justifying a classification into disjoint equivalence classes [S] = {Q | Q ∼ S}. If two
039
      such classes [S] and [T ] share a common object Q, they should coincide by transitivity: [S] = [T ].
040
041   A scientific approach is to first define an equivalence and then look for properties that can distinguish
042   non-equivalent objects. For example, all crystals form disjoint classes by their chemical composi-
043   tion, though diamond and graphite composed of pure carbon have vastly different properties.
044   Because crystal structures are determined in a rigid form, the strongest equivalence (best separat-
045   ing all crystals) is rigid motion, which is a composition of translations and rotations in Rn from
046   the group SE(n). Because noise perturbs any rigid structure, all SE(n)-classes of crystals form a
047   continuous space. The slightly weaker isometry (denoted by S ≃ Q) is defined as any distance-
048   preserving transformation or, equivalently in Rn , any composition of a rigid motion and a reflection.
049   All isometries of Rn form the Euclidean group E(n). A classification under isometry suffices in
050
      practice because any mirror images can be distinguished by an extra bit (a sign of orientation).

065   A traditional representation of crystals is ambiguous in the sense that infinitely many pairs (cell,
066   motif) generate the same periodic set of points, see Fig. 1 (left). Much worse, Fig. 1 (right) shows
067   that any cell-based representation is inherently discontinuous because almost any perturbation of
068   atoms (due to ever-present thermal vibrations and measurement noise) can arbitrarily scale up a
069   minimal (by volume) cell. The past approach to ignore atomic perturbations up to a threshold ε
070   implies that sufficiently many tiny perturbations can transform any infinite set of points into any
071   other Zwart et al. (2008), which makes all sets equivalent by the transitivity axiom. Tiny differences
072   between crystals should be not ignored but quantified by a continuous metric as formalized below.
073   Problem 1.2. Find a descriptor I of all periodic point sets in Rn satisfying the conditions below.
074
      (a) Invariance: if S ≃ Q are isometric then I(S) = I(Q), i.e. I has no false negatives.
075
076   (b) Completeness: for any S, Q ⊂ Rn , if I(S) = I(Q) then S ≃ Q, i.e. I has no false positives.
077   (c) Metric axioms: there is a distance metrics d on invariant values satisfying three axioms (1)
078   d(a, b) = 0 if and only if a = b, (2) d(a, b) = d(b, c), (3) d(a, b) + d(b, c) ≥ d(a, c) for all a, b, c.
079
      (d) Continuity: if Q is obtained by perturbing every point of S up to ε, then d(I(S), I(Q)) ≤ λε.
080
081   (e) Reconstructability: any periodic set S can be reconstructed from I(S) up to isometry of Rn .
082
      (f) Computability: for a fixed dimension n, the invariant I(S), the metric d in (c), and a reconstruc-
083   tion of S ⊂ Rn from I(S) in (e) are computable in polynomial time of the motif sizes.               ■
084
085   The invariant I(S) can be a vector, matrix, or another object in a space where metric computations
086   should be easier than for isometry classes of S. Invariance condition 1.2(a) is stronger than equivari-
087   ance saying that a group action f (such as a rotation) changes I(S) to Tf (I(S)), where Tf is a map
088   depending on f . For example, any linear combination e(S) of point coordinates of S is equivariant
089   but can allow a false negative that is a pair S ≃ Q with e(S) ̸= e(Q). The invariance means that Tf
090   is the identity, hence different values I(S) ̸= I(Q) always guarantee that S ̸≃ Q are not isometric.
091   Completeness 1.2(b) is harder and is practically meaningful only with a Lipschitz continuous metric
092   in 1.2(d) because any noise makes all real objects at least slightly different as in Fig. 1 (right). This
093   unresolved discontinuity created a gigantic loophole that allows anyone to disguise known materials
094   as new by perturbing atomic positions, which scales up a minimal cell, and by changing atomic
095   types, which makes comparisons by symmetries, unit cells, and chemical compositions unreliable.
096   The metric axioms are essential for recognizing S ≃ Q by d(I(S), I(Q)) = 0. If the third (triangle)
097   axiom in 1.2(c) fails with any positive error, clustering may not be trustworthy Rass et al. (2022).
098
      Condition 1.2(e) asks for reconstructable invariants that can be inverted back to original objects and
099
      hence are more practical than a DNA code, which is used for identifying humans in practice (if we
100
      forget about identical twins) but a DNA code alone is insufficient yet to grow a living organism.
101
102   Problem 1.2 formalizes all verifiable conditions 1.2(a-d,f) for any discriminative problem (materials
103   identification) and the first goals 1.2(e-f) of the generative problem (designing new materials).
104   The contributions to notoriously hard Problem 1.2 are (1) new higher-order invariants, which dis-
105   tinguished all known counter-examples to the completeness of past descriptors, and (2) the ultra-fast
106   detection of (near-)duplicates in the world’s largest databases of experimental materials. The pre-
107   viously unrecognized (near-)duplicates skewed real data but can now be filtered out by continuous
      invariants for upholding scientific integrity and improving machine learning of materials properties.

      2    R EVIEW OF UNRESOLVED CHALLENGES IN CRYSTAL REPRESENTATIONS
109
110
      Problem 1.2 makes sense for any objects (finite clouds, graphs) under other practical equivalences
111
      (rigid motion excluding reflections) instead of crystals and isometry, respectively. The graph isomor-
112
      phism problem Grohe & Schweitzer (2020) considers only conditions 1.2(a,b,e,f) without continu-
113   ous metrics. Boutin & Kemper (2004) proved that pairwise distances distinguish all generic finite
114   clouds of unordered points in Rn . All singular examples within a subspace of measure 0 among all
115   point clouds were distinguished in Widdowson & Kurlin (2023) but we focus on periodic sets.
116
      In 1930, Pauling noticed the ambiguity of crystal structures obtained by diffraction Pauling & Shap-
117
      pell (1930), which called for stronger invariants. For n = 1, Theorem 4 in Grünbaum & Moore
118
      (1995) justified complete invariants for periodic sequences given by rational angles of the unit circle
119   (in the complex plane C) by using 6-factor products of complex numbers. Since the circle (a period)
120   was fixed, these invariants are discontinuous under perturbations. Indeed, the sequence Z of integers
121   can be infinitely close to S = {0, 1 + ε, . . . , m + ε} + (m + 1)Z ⊂ R for small ε > 0, though their
122   periods 1 and m + 1 are arbitrarily different. The much simpler complete invariant of a periodic
123   sequence S = {p1 , . . . , pm } + LZ ⊂ R with a period L, where 0 ≤ p1 < · · · < pm < L, is the list
124   of interpoint distances pi+1 − pi (up to cyclic permutations) for i = 1, . . . , m and pm+1 = p1 + L.
125   A continuous metric d(S, Q) on these cyclic classes of distance lists was introduced in Kurlin (2022)
126   but such a metric requires an expansion to the least common multiple of the sizes |S|, |Q| of motifs
127   and doesn’t come with a polynomial-time invariant. The brute force invariant for all periodic se-
128   quences S with motifs up to m points needs an expansion to at least 2m points, see Theorem 5(1)in
129   Farhi (2007), which violates condition 1.2(e). So Problem 1.2 remained open even for n = 1.
130
      A finite approach to measuring the similarity between periodic point sets is to compare their finite
131   subsets within a box or a ball of a large but fixed cut-off radius. However, any periodic point set has
132   many non-isometric finite subsets within differently positioned boxes or balls, see Fig. 1 (left).
133
134
      Considering local clouds centered at all points in a motif M gives invariants such as MACE Batatia
      et al. (2022), which achieved excellent results by training on large datasets. Perturbing a cut-off
135
      radius can discontinuously change these clouds by including new neighbors that were just outside
136
      a smaller cut-off. Even if this cut-off is smoothed out, any fixed size is insufficient Parsaeifard &
137   Goedecker (2022), Pozdnyakov et al. (2022): “indistinguishable configurations affect the expressive
138   power of models based on those features, which will be incapable of predicting distinct values for
139   the corresponding atom-centered properties, even if both structures are used during training.”
140
      Atomic vibrations are natural to measure by deviations of atoms from their initial positions but a sum
141
      of small deviations over infinitely many points can be infinite and also can give different values for
142
      different finite subsets. However, a maximum deviation of atoms is well-defined as the bottleneck
143   distance between any sets via bijections between atoms, which can be displaced but cannot vanish.
144

      Definition 2.2 (metrics and pseudo-metrics). A distance d between objects with an equivalence
154
      relation ∼ is called a metric if these axioms hold: (1) d(S, Q) = 0 if and only if S ∼ Q; (2)
155   d(S, Q) = d(Q, S); (3) d(S, Q)+d(Q, T ) ≥ d(S, T ). If axiom (1) is replaced with (1′ ) d(S, S) = 0
156   for any S, then non-equivalent S ̸∼ Q can have d(S, Q) = 0, and d is called a pseudo-metric. ■
157
158   Many descriptors are compared by distances (such as Euclidean) that satisfy metric axioms on in-
159   variant values but define only pseudo-metrics on isometry classes because of incompleteness of the
160   underlying invariants. If d(S, Q) > 0, then S ̸∼ Q by (1′ ), so a fast pseudo-metric can distin-
161   guish between some but not all objects. Pseudo-metrics are weaker than metrics, e.g. the difference
      ||S| − |Q|| of the set sizes is a pseudo-metric not distinguishing any sets S ̸≃ Q of the same size.

      Metrics (similar to complete invariants) are much more valuable than pseudo-metrics (similar to
163   non-invariants or incomplete invariants). Any algorithm using an incomplete invariant I cannot
164   predict different properties of a false positive pair of non-isometric sets S ̸≃ Q with I(S) = I(Q).
165
166
      Hence the discriminative problem should be solved first by (at least generically) complete and Lips-
      chitz continuous invariants before any generative attempts can succeed. Any non-complete invariant
167
      I is not invertible so that different S ̸≃ Q can be randomly chosen if I(S) = I(Q). We recall the
168
      recent invariants that satisfied almost all conditions 1.2(a-f) for finite and periodic point sets.

      Definition 2.3 (Pointwise Distance Distribution PDD). Let S ⊂ Rn be a periodic point set with a
      motif M of m points. For any integer k ≥ 1 and p ∈ M , let d1 (p) ≤ · · · ≤ dk (p) be the list of
171
      Euclidean distances from p to its k nearest neighbors within the whole set S. These lists become
172
      rows of the m × k matrix D(S; k). Any l > 1 identical rows are collapsed into a single row with the
173   weight l/m, which is written in the extra first column. The resulting matrix PDD(S; k) of unordered
174   rows with weights is the Pointwise Distance Distribution, see Widdowson & Kurlin (2022).            ■
175
176   If a unit cell of S is extended by a factor of l, then any point p in the original motif has l transla-
177   tionally equivalent copies in the extended motif. Then D(S; k) has l times more rows only because
178   each original row is expanded into l identical rows. The final PDD(S; k) is the same weighted dis-
179
      tribution of rows, independent of an initial cell of S. The equality between weighted distributions
      is interpreted as a bijection between unordered sets respecting all weights. This equality is best
180
      checked not by considering all bijections but by a metric that vanishes only on equal distributions by
181
      the first metric axiom. The PDD is Lipschitz continuous, computable in near-linear time (for a fixed
182   dimension) in both k and motif size m, and distinguishes all non-isometric sets in general position
183   (away form a measure 0 subspace), see Theorems 3.2, 4.3, 4.4, 5.1 in Widdowson & Kurlin (2022).

537
      the efficient barrier for homometric or noisy disguises of known crystals because the invariants can

      Some entries in the CSD and COD are incomplete or disordered (not periodic). After removing such
811   entries, we were left with 831,126 CSD structures and 344,127 COD structures.

813   Firstly, we computed PDM[10](S; 100) for all entries, taking 27 min 33 sec for the CSD and 12
814   mins 15 sec for COD (2 ms per structure on average). To find exact matches between databases by
815   PDM, we make use of the k-d tree data structure, designed for fast nearest neighbor lookup. A k-d
816   tree can be constructed from any collection of vectors, which can then be queried for a number of
817
      nearest neighbors of a new vector, using a binary tree style algorithm with logarithmic search time.
818   We flattened each PDM[10](S; 100) matrix to a vector with 1000 dimensions, constructed a k-d tree
819   for both CSD and COD, then queried the 10 nearest neighbors for each item in the other. If the most
820   distant neighbor for any entry is closer than the threshold 10−13 Å (within floating point error), we
821   extend the search and find more neighbors until all pairs within the threshold are found. We were
822   left with a total of 270,669 matches; an overlap between the databases of one third of the CSD and
823   almost 80% of COD.

1067
1068   Algorithm A.1 (adversarial generation). One can generate any number of ‘new’ structures as fol-
1069   lows.
1070   1. Take a Crystallographic Information File of a real periodic material from any public database.
1071
1072   2. Change a unit cell by applying any integer matrix with determinant 1 to a given basis.
1073   3. Arbitrarily extend a unit cell by a random integer factor in each direction of the basis.
1074
1075   4. Randomly perturb any cell parameters and atomic coordinates up to a small threshold.
1076   5. Replace some non-common chemical elements with similar ones in the periodic table.
1077
1078   Step 1 can choose a non-famous crystal with at least one non-organic element for a future substi-
1079   tution. Steps 2 and 3 are optional but include many choices to generate more structures. Step 4
       is essential because most comparisons miss near-duplicates as in Fig. 1 (right). Step 5 is the final

       disguise to avoid detection by chemical composition. One can also check if a new composition has
1081   not appeared in the main databases. After obtaining a new CIF (or millions of CIFs), can we deposit

       On the experimental side, some journals and databases now require extra data such as structure
       factors from diffraction, which can be perturbed similar to a CIF.

1086   On the computational side, most simulations claim that their materials are ‘stable’ meaning that their
1087   energy is below the convex hull over a spaces of compositions. Hohenberg and Kohn Hohenberg &
1088   Kohn (1964) proved the existence of a universal energy potential but there is no explicit formula.
1089   Since many algorithms calculate different energies, ‘stable’ materials are user-dependent.
1090   Even if we fix one easily computable energy such as the Lennard-Jones potential Jones (1924),
1091   numerical approximations can slightly deviate from a local energy minimum. Hence adding noise
1092   to a real structure might produce near-duplicates that have smaller energies, especially if millions
1093   of dollars can buy longer simulations. Geometrically, sampling many points around a vertex on the
1094   boundary of a convex hull will likely produce many new vertices on the boundary of a perturbed

       Algorithm A.2 (fast detection of near-duplicate periodic structures). We find all pairs of periodic
1099
       structures that differ by atomic displacements up to a given threshold ε.
1100
1101   1(a). Split a given database of CIFs into groups with equal (or ε-close) unit cell volumes.
1102
       1(b). Split each group into subgroups of CIFs with equal (or ε-close) unit cell parameters.
1103
1104   1(c). Split each subgroup into subgroups of CIFs whose motifs are ε-close as sets of unordered
1105   points. Exclude all the found (near-)duplicates from further comparisons.
1106   2. For remaining periodic point sets S, compute PDD(S; k) and AMD(S; k), say for k = 100.
1107
1108
       3. Find all pairs of structures with distances L∞ ≤ 2ε between their AMD vectors, which can be
       done in near-linear time by fast nearest neighbor search Elkin & Kurlin (2023). If L∞ > 2ε, the
1109
       structures cannot be obtained from each other by perturbing all atoms up to ε.
1110
1111   4. For any remaining pair, compute the Eearth Mover’s Distance (EMD) between PDDs, then
1112   between PDD{2} s. If EMD > 2ε, the structures cannot be obtained by perturbing all atoms up to
1113   ε due to Theorem 4.1.
1114   5. The EMD calculation finds an optimal matching between atoms, so we can check the displacement
1115   of any atom to estimate how much structures differ by atomic positions.
