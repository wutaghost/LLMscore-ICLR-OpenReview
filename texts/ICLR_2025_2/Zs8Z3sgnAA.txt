009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in
013
               processing long-context information. However, the quadratic complexity of atten-
               tion computation with respect to sequence length poses significant computational
014
               challenges, and I/O aware algorithms have been proposed. This paper presents a
015
               comprehensive analysis of the I/O complexity for attention mechanisms, focusing
016            on backward passes by categorizing into small and large cache scenarios. Using
017            the red-blue pebble game framework, we establish tight bounds on I/O complex-
018            ity across all cache sizes. We confirm that the de facto standard I/O aware algo-
019            rithm FlashAttention is optimal for both forward and backward passes for the large
020            cache size scenario. For small cache sizes, we provide an algorithm that improves
021            over existing methods and achieves the tight bounds. Additionally, we extend our
022            analysis to sparse attention, a mainstream speeding-up approach, deriving fine-
023            grained lower bounds for both forward and backward passes and both small and
024
               large caches. Our findings complete the theoretical foundation for I/O complex-
               ity in attention mechanisms, offering insights for designing efficient algorithms of
025
               LLM training and inference.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large Language Models (LLMs), such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), Claude (Anthropic, 2024),
033   Llama (Llama Team, 2024), and more recently o1 (OpenAI, 2024) from OpenAI, have demon-
034   strated immense potential to enhance various aspects of our daily lives, including conversational
035   AI (Liu et al., 2024), AI agents (Xi et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b), search AI (OpenAI, 2024),
036   AI assistants (Kuo et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024b), and many others. One of the most emergent
037   abilities of LLMs is dealing with long-context information, which is crucial for processing materials
038   such as academic papers, official reports, and legal documents. LLMs have proven adept at tack-
039
      ling long-context tasks, such as zero-shot summarization (Chhabra et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024)
      and maintaining very long-term conversations (Xu et al., 2022; Maharana et al., 2024). OpenAI’s
040
      o1 model (OpenAI, 2024) serves as a significant advancement in this area. It leverages Chain-of-
041
      Thought (CoT) reasoning (Wei et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022) and employs Retrieval Augmented
042   Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2023) to exhibit PhD-level abilities, where both
043   techniques require long context inputs for generation. This proficiency underscores the necessity for
044   developing long-context modeling capabilities within LLMs.
045
      LLMs are primarily based on the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017), whose core com-
046
      ponent is the self-attention mechanism. However, the quadratic complexity of attention computation
047
      with respect to sequence length dominates the computational FLOPs during long-context training
048   and inference. To address this issue, FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022; Dao, 2023; Shah et al., 2024)
049   accelerates attention computation and has become the de facto standard in the industry of LLM
050   training and inference deployment. The success of FlashAttention lies in its I/O awareness (Ag-
051   garwal & Vitter, 1988), accounting for reads and writes to different levels of fast cache (e.g., GPU
052   on-chip SRAM) and slow memory (e.g., GPU high-bandwidth memory) within the hardware hierar-
053   chy. Leveraging modern hardware design in GPUs, e.g., NVIDIA A100 and H100, efficiently allows
      FlashAttention to be integrated as a go-to method for LLM training and inference.

172   Attention Computation Acceleration. The quadratic time complexity of attention computation
173   with respect to the length of the input sequence (Vaswani et al., 2017) poses significant computa-
174   tional challenges, especially for long sequences. Consequently, accelerating attention computation
175   has become a crucial research area. From a theoretical standpoint, numerous works focus on ap-
176   proximating the attention matrix to accelerate computation (Han et al., 2024; Alman & Song, 2023;
177   2024a; Liang et al., 2024c; Alman & Song, 2024b; Liang et al., 2024f). Experimental approaches in-
178   volve modifying model architectures and optimizing implementations to accelerate inference. Meth-
179   ods such as Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024), Linearizing Transformers (Zhang et al.,
180
      2024b; Mercat et al., 2024), Hopfield Models (Hu et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024b; Hu et al., 2024c;
      Xu et al., 2024a; Wu et al., 2024a; Hu et al., 2024a;b) and PolySketchFormer (Zandieh et al., 2023;
181
      Kacham et al., 2023) aim to improve model performance and inference speed. System-level opti-
182
      mizations, such as FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022; Dao, 2023; Shah et al., 2024) and block-wise
183   parallel decoding (Stern et al., 2018), address bottlenecks in attention mechanisms and enhance
184   inference speed through efficient implementation strategies. Collectively, these advancements con-
185   tribute to making attention mechanisms more scalable and efficient, facilitating the deployment of
186   large-scale language models. Shi et al. (2024a) accelerates inference by compressing the input text.
187
188   Learning with Bounded Memory and I/O Complexity. A common memory model in compu-
189   tational systems is the two-level memory hierarchy. In this model, there are two layers of memory:
190   a small but fast layer called the cache, and a large but slower layer called the memory. The I/O
191   (input/output) complexity of an algorithm measures its efficiency based on the number of data trans-
192
      fer operations it performs between the cache and the memory. The early work of Hong & Kung
      (1981) formulated the I/O complexity mathematically using the language of graph theory. Learn-
193
      ing with bounded memory has been studied in various fields in machine learning such as online
194
      learning (Srinivas et al., 2022; Peng & Rubinstein, 2023; Peng & Zhang, 2023), convex optimiza-
195   tion (Marsden et al., 2022; Chen & Peng, 2023), active learning (Hopkins et al., 2021), attention
196   computation (Addanki et al., 2023), and continual learning (Chen et al., 2022; Ermis et al., 2022).
197
198   Sparse Attention. Over the past few years, there has been extensive research on sparse Trans-
199   former/Attention models with weights pruning and inputs pruning, aimed at accelerating computa-
200   tion and training (Ye et al., 2019; Sukhbaatar et al., 2019; Beltagy et al., 2020; Tay et al., 2020; Guo
201   et al., 2023; Shirzad et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
202   2024a). In practice, the attention matrix is sparse, significantly reducing computational costs. Theo-
203   retical studies, such as Yun et al. (2020), have demonstrated that sparse transformers are expressive
204   enough and can achieve universal approximation properties.

      3.3     I/O C OMPLEXITY
306
307   There are various ways to define the two-level memory hierarchy and the I/O complexity. We state
308   the definition in Hong & Kung (1981), which formulates the two-level memory hierarchy as a red-
309   blue pebble game played on a computational graph. Very recently, Saha & Ye (2024) proved that
310   the I/O complexity of forward computation of FlashAttention is optimal by analyzing the red-blue
311   pebble game on an attention forward computational graph.
312   Definition 3.4 (Red-blue pebble game (Hong & Kung, 1981)). Consider a game played on a di-
313   rected acyclic graph that has a limited number of red pebbles and an unlimited number of blue
314   pebbles. Initially, each input node (a node with no parents) is marked with a blue pebble, while all
315   other nodes have no pebbles. The player is allowed to perform the following operations:
316
317               • Input: Replace a blue pebble on a node with a red pebble.
318               • Output: Replace a red pebble on a node with a blue pebble.
319
                  • Compute: Place a red pebble on a node if all its parent nodes have red pebbles.
320
321               • Delete: Remove a pebble from a node.
322
323   The objective of the game is to place blue pebbles on all output nodes (i.e., nodes with no children)
      while minimizing the total number of input and output operations used throughout the process.


                                                                               6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      In the red-blue pebble game, each node represents a computational task. A red pebble denotes a
325   unit in the small but fast layer known as cache, while a blue pebble represents a unit in the large but
326   slower layer called memory. A task can only be computed once all its dependent tasks are completed.
327   All computations are assumed to occur within the cache. Hence, efficient use of cache plays a critical
328   role in reducing the I/O operations of an algorithm to minimize the cost associated with data transfer
329   between memory and cache. We can define the I/O complexity by using the red-blue pebble game.
330   Definition 3.5 (I/O complexity (Hong & Kung, 1981)). Consider the red-blue pebble game played
331   on a directed acyclic graph G. Let M be a positive integer. The I/O complexity, denoted as
332   Q(G, M ), is the minimum number of input and output operations to complete the objective of the
333   game with the restriction that no more than M red pebbles are present on the graph at any time. We
334   omit G when it is clear in the context.
335
336   The red-blue pebble game provides insight into cache management by modeling the limited cache
337   size through the number of red pebbles. The maximum number of red pebbles corresponds to the
338
      size of the cache, which means that there can be at most M items in the cache at any given time.

      This work provided a comprehensive analysis of the I/O complexity for attention mechanisms, fo-
489   cusing on backward passes. We established tight bounds on I/O complexity for both small and large
490   caches. Our results confirm that FlashAttention is optimal for both forward and backward on large
491   cache sizes. For small cache sizes, we provided improved upper and lower bounds compared to
492   existing methods. Additionally, we derived lower bounds for sparse attention for both forward and
493   backward and across cache sizes. Our findings complete the theoretical foundation for I/O com-

495   leave exploring practical implementations leveraging these theoretical insights and investigating I/O
496   complexity for other emerging attention variants as our future work.

931   Large Language Models. The exceptional success of generative large language models (LLMs),
932   such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), Claude 3 (Anthropic, 2024), Gemini 1.5 (Reid et al., 2024),
933   Llama 3.1 (Llama Team, 2024), Mistral Nemo (Jiang et al., 2023), Phi 3.5 (Abdin et al., 2024), is
934   fundamentally attributed to the transformer architecture introduced by Vaswani et al. (2017) and all
935
      support at least 128k input token length. The transformer architecture and its self-attention mech-
      anism have become indispensable in leading natural language processing (NLP) models (Chang
936
      et al., 2024), demonstrating remarkable capabilities across a diverse array of applications, including
937
      language translation (He et al., 2021), sentiment analysis (Usama et al., 2020), language model-
938   ing (Martin et al., 2019), the integration of differential privacy (Singh et al., 2024; Liang et al.,
939   2024e), and multi-modal tasks (Zhang et al., 2024a; Liang et al., 2024f; Wang et al., 2024). Trans-
940   formers’ emergent compositional abilities (Dziri et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024b) and proficiency in
941   in-context learning (Olsson et al., 2022; Min et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2024b) have led some to consider
942   them as early indicators of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) (Bubeck et al., 2023). As such, the
943   transformer architecture continues to play a pivotal role in advancing the field of AI.
944
945   More about Attention Computation Acceleration. The quadratic time complexity of attention
946   computation with respect to the length of the input sequence (Vaswani et al., 2017) poses significant
947   computational challenges, especially for long sequences. Consequently, accelerating attention com-
948   putation has become a crucial research area, with approaches broadly divided into two categories:
949   (1) theoretical optimization of computational complexity (Alman & Song, 2023; 2024a), and (2)
950   experimental improvements to model performance (Dao et al., 2022; Dao, 2023; Shah et al., 2024;
951
      Ge et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2024a).
952   From a theoretical standpoint, numerous works focus on approximating the attention matrix to ac-
953   celerate computation. For example, Alman & Song (2023; 2024a) utilize polynomial kernel approx-
954   imation techniques (Aggarwal & Alman, 2022) to speed up both training and inference of a single
955   attention layer, achieving almost linear time complexity, and extend this approach to multi-layer
956   transformer (Liang et al., 2024c) and tensor attention (Alman & Song, 2024b; Liang et al., 2024f).
957
      Other theoretical contributions include the conv-basis method introduced by Liang et al. (2024a)
      and a near-linear time algorithm proposed by Han et al. (2024) under the assumptions of uniform
958
      softmax column norms and sparsity.
959
960   Experimental approaches involve modifying model architectures and optimizing implementations
961   to accelerate inference. Methods such as Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024), Linearizing
962   Transformers (Zhang et al., 2024b; Mercat et al., 2024), PolySketchFormer (Zandieh et al., 2023;
963
      Kacham et al., 2023), and various implementations of the Hopfield Model (Hu et al., 2024b;a; Wu
      et al., 2024a; Xu et al., 2024a; Hu et al., 2024c; Wu et al., 2024b; Hu et al., 2023) aim to improve
964
      model performance and inference speed. Additionally, specific techniques like weight pruning Liang
965
      et al. (2024b); Li et al. (2024) have been developed to accelerate LLM generation. Some other tech-
966   niques are introduced for efficient adaptation, such as LoRA (Hu et al., 2022; Zeng & Lee, 2024;
967   Hu et al., 2024d) and prefix turning (Li & Liang, 2021; Liang et al., 2024d). System-level optimiza-
968   tions, such as Flash Attention (Dao et al., 2022; Dao, 2023; Shah et al., 2024) and block-wise parallel
969   decoding (Stern et al., 2018), address bottlenecks in attention mechanisms and enhance inference
970   speed through efficient implementation strategies. Collectively, these advancements contribute to
971   making attention mechanisms more scalable and efficient, facilitating the deployment of large-scale
      language models.

       More about Learning with Bounded Memory and I/O Complexity. Learning with bounded
973    memory has been studied in various fields in machine learning such as online learning (Maiti et al.,
974    2021; Srinivas et al., 2022; Peng & Rubinstein, 2023; Peng & Zhang, 2023), parity learning (Stein-
975    hardt et al., 2016; Raz, 2017; 2018; Garg et al., 2018), convex optimization (Woodworth & Srebro,
976    2019; Marsden et al., 2022; Chen & Peng, 2023), active learning (Hopkins et al., 2021), learning
977    linear classifiers (Brown et al., 2022), attention computation (Addanki et al., 2023), linear regres-
978    sion (Steinhardt & Duchi, 2015; Sharan et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2022), linear programming (Tau-
979    man Kalai et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2020), semi-definite programming (Song et al., 2023), principal
980    component analysis (Deng et al., 2023), continual learning (Chen et al., 2022; Ermis et al., 2022), en-
981
       tropy estimation (Acharya et al., 2019; Aliakbarpour et al., 2022) and others (Moshkovitz & Tishby,
       2017; Gonen et al., 2020).
982
983    A common memory model in computational systems is the two-level memory hierarchy. In this
984    model, there are two layers of memory: a small but fast layer called the cache, and a large but slower
985    layer called the memory. The I/O (input/output) complexity of an algorithm measures its efficiency
986    based on the number of data transfer operations it performs between the cache and the memory.
987
       In domains such as big data analytics and database management, these data transfers can become
       significant performance bottlenecks because massive datasets cannot be entirely accommodated in
988
       the cache, and thus optimizing I/O is essential for fast data retrieval and storage, directly impacting
989
       query performance and system scalability (Gropp et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2015). The early work
990    of Hong & Kung (1981) formulated the I/O complexity mathematically using the language of graph
991    theory. Vitter (2001) provides a comprehensive survey of the I/O complexity of various batched
992    and online problems. There exists a substantial body of work on the I/O complexity of numerous
993    problems, including sorting (Aggarwal & Vitter, 1988), graph algorithms (Cui et al., 2020; Jain
994    & Zaharia, 2020; Jiang et al., 2021; Deng & Tao, 2024), fine-grained I/O complexity (Demaine
995    et al., 2017), computational trade-off in data transfers (Demaine & Liu, 2018), computing prime
996    tables (Bender et al., 2016), attention computation (Saha & Ye, 2024), integer multiplication (Bilardi
997    & De Stefani, 2019; De Stefani, 2019b), and matrix multiplication (De Stefani, 2019a; Nissim &
998
       Schwartz, 2019).

1012   ⟨x ◦ y, 1n ⟩ = ⟨x, y⟩. For a vector x, we use x⊤ to denote the transpose of x. For a matrix A, we use
1013   A⊤ to denote the transpose of matrix A. For a matrix A, we use exp(A) to denote the matrix that
1014   (i, j)-th coordinate is exp(Ai,j ).
1015
       Given a matrix A ∈ Rn×m , we index an individual entry as A[i, j]. The i-th row is denoted A[i]
1016   while the j-th column is denoted A[∗, j]. A[i1 : i2 , j1 : j2 ] denotes a block of A consisting of
1017   entries (i, j) where i ∈ [i1 , i2 ] and j ∈ [j1 , j2 ]. Given a block size B, the block A[(i − 1) · B + 1 :
1018   i · B, (j − 1) · B + 1 : j · B] is denoted A(B) [i, j].

1021   the i-th block of size B as v (B) [i]. Let diag(v) denote the matrix D ∈ Rn×n with D[i, i] = v[i].

1583
1584   Hong & Kung (1981) introduces a method for analyzing I/O complexity using the concept of an
1585   M -partition on a graph. Before we define it, we first provide some definitions from graph theory.
1586   Definition E.1 (Dominator set). Let G = (V, E) be a directed acyclic graph and S ⊆ V . We define
1587   a set D ⊆ V as a dominator set of S if, for every path in G from a input node to any node in S,
1588   there exists at least one node in D on that path.
1589   Definition E.2 (Minimum set). Let G = (V, E) be a directed acyclic graph and S ⊆ V . We say
1590   that a set M ⊆ S is a minimum set of S if M contains all nodes in S that have no children in S.
1591   Definition E.3 (Vertex subset dependence). Let G = (V, E) be a directed acyclic graph. Let
1592   V1 , V2 ⊆ V be two disjoint subsets. We say that V2 depends on V1 if there is a directed edge from a
1593   node in V1 to a node in V2 .
1594   Definition E.4 (Cyclic dependence). Let G = (V, E) be a directed acyclic graph. Let V1 , . . . , Vh ⊆
1595   V be h disjoint subsets of V . We say that there is a cyclic dependence among {V1 , . . . , Vh } if there
1596   exists a permutation (i1 , . . . , ih ) of [h] such that Vi1 depends on Vih , and for every j ∈ {2, . . . , h},
1597   Vij depends on Vij−1 .
1598
       Now, we are ready to define M -partitons. In fact, the minimum number of sets in any M -partition
1599   provides a lower bound on the I/O complexity.
1600
       Definition E.5 (M -partition (Hong & Kung, 1981)). Let G = (V, E) be a directed acyclic graph.
1601
       Let V1 , . . . , Vh ⊆ V be h disjoint subsets of V . We say that {V1 , . . . , Vh } is a M -partition of G if
1602   the following conditions are satisfied
1603
                                                                                                    Sh
1604           • {V1 , . . . , Vh } is a partition of V , i.e., V1 , . . . , Vh are disjoint and V = i=1 Vi .
1605
1606
              • For each Vi , there exists a dominator set Di of Vi such that Di has at most M nodes.
1607          • For each Vi , there exists a minimum set Mi of Vi such that Mi has at most M nodes.
1608
1609          • There is no cyclic dependence among {V1 , . . . , Vh }.

       We use P (G, M ) to denote the minimum number of sets in any M -partition of G.
