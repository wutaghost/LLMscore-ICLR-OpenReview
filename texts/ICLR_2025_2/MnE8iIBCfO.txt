009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            To what extent are model predictions sensitive to modifications in training data?
012            Data attribution approaches have served to answer this question. These ap-
013            proaches can be used for estimating data brittleness i.e., identifying which subset
014            of training samples had the highest positive influence on a test sample. However,
015            these methods come at a high computational cost, are memory intensive, and are
016            hard to scale to large models or datasets. Current state-of-the-art approaches re-
017            quire an ensemble of as many as 300,000 models. In this work, we focus on a com-
018
               putationally efficient baseline centered on estimating two types of data brittleness
               metrics. Our baseline approach uses the image features from a single pretrained
019
               self-supervised backbone. In contrast to data attribution approaches, our approach
020
               does not explicitly utilize model information and focuses on the data. Our results
021            show this simple assumption works well, achieving competitive performance with
022            state-of-the-art attribution approaches on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, under limited
023            computational and memory requirements. Our work serves as a simple baseline,
024            showing that effective data brittleness estimates can be achieved based solely on
025            knowledge of the training data.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The effectiveness of a machine learning system’s performance hinges on the quality, diversity, and
031   relevance of the data it is trained on (Halevy et al., 2009; Sun et al., 2017). In various real-world
032   machine learning systems, e.g. in healthcare or finance, we often ask questions like, “Which training
033
      samples influenced this prediction?” or “How sensitive is this model’s prediction to changes in the
      training data?” Counterfactual insights enable us to assess the impact of hypothetical changes in the
034
      data distribution, which in turn helps us understand the basis of the model’s decisions and how to
035
      change the decision in the event of an error. These questions motivate research on data attribution
036   methods, focusing on understanding which data points most strongly influence a model’s outputs.
037
038   In principle, data attribution can be done perfectly by a brute-force leave-k-out strategy; simply train
039
      the model from scratch many times, removing k data points each time. The user can then examine
      the impact of each data point by examining how the corresponding ablated model differs from the
040
      original. Clearly, this procedure is intractable for any realistic problem as there are innumerable
041
      subsets, and training even a single machine learning model can be almost prohibitively expensive.
042   The goal of data attribution research therefore is to approximate this gold standard metric as closely
043   as possible while simultaneously using as little computation as possible. As such, the field of data
044   attribution is all about trade-offs between accuracy, runtime, and memory.
045
      We focus on estimating data brittleness metrics, a counterfactual estimation task for evaluating data
046
      attribution approaches. Concretely, for a test sample, we find the smallest subset of training data
047
      that when removed/mislabeled causes the model to misclassify. State-of-the-art data attribution ap-
048   proaches utilize logits (Ilyas et al., 2022), or gradient (Koh & Liang, 2017; Park et al., 2023) infor-
049   mation from an ensemble of models to evaluate these metrics. These techniques require retraining
050   models on different subsets of data and other compute or memory-intensive strategies for high ef-
051   ficacy (Ilyas et al., 2022; Feldman & Zhang, 2020; Koh & Liang, 2017; Park et al., 2023). Thus,
052   attribution approaches quickly become intractable as datasets become larger (Basu et al., 2021; Park
053   et al., 2023) and applications more realistic, such as attribution for large-language models (Grosse
      et al., 2023).

      Our baseline approach uses the feature space of a single self-supervised model to estimate data
      brittleness. We specifically focus on image classification. In contrast to data attribution approaches
080
      that focus on the specific way an algorithm/model behaves (Ilyas et al., 2022; Park et al., 2023),
081
      our approach does not explicitly utilize model information. Thus, our method cannot be directly
082   utilized to study model-specific behaviors, considered applications of data attribution methods, like
083   debugging model biases (Ilyas et al., 2022; Park et al., 2023; Shah et al., 2023), fairness assessment
084   (Black & Fredrikson, 2021) or identifying backdoor attacks (Khaddaj et al., 2023). However, based
085   on the simple intuition that different models leverage data similarly (Zhen et al., 2022; Kornblith
086   et al., 2019), our approach can provide accurate brittleness estimates.
087
      We show that our approach can outperform data brittleness estimates from state-of-the-art attribution
088   approaches under limited compute and storage requirements on the CIFAR-10 dataset, as shown in
089   fig. 1. Our method easily scales to much larger datasets such as ImageNet, while state-of-the-art
090   attribution approaches require significantly higher compute or storage (see section 4.3). In contrast
091   to claims in previous works, our results show that feature representations can serve as a simple,
092   compute, and storage-efficient baseline for data brittleness metrics (Ilyas et al., 2022; Park et al.,
093   2023). Our code is available as supplementary material.

      2.1   E VALUATING ATTRIBUTION M ETHODS
112
113   A core evaluation criterion for the performance of data attribution methods is their capacity to pro-
114   vide accurate counterfactual predictions (Park et al., 2023; Ilyas et al., 2022; Feldman & Zhang,
115   2020; Koh et al., 2019). While these metrics can be computationally demanding, they represent a
116   straightforward, yet valuable, proxy for assessing the efficacy of these approaches. In our work,
117   we focus on one of the approaches presented in Ilyas et al.(Ilyas et al., 2022), and Park et al.(Park
118   et al., 2023) and focus on data brittleness. Data brittleness metrics leverage attribution techniques
119
      to answer the following question: “To what extent are model predictions sensitive to modifications
      in the training data?” Hence, these metrics serve as a means of estimating counterfactual scenarios.
120
      To quantify data brittleness, we focus on two distinct types of data support for a test sample zt . We
121
      define these sets below:
122
123   Data Removal Support: The smallest subset Rr , that when removed from the training set S, causes
124   an average training run of the model to misclassify zt .
125   Data Mislabel Support: The smallest training subset Rm , whose mislabeling causes an average
126   training run of the model to misclassify zt . For each training sample in Rm , we change the labels to
127   the second-highest predicted class for zt .
128
      Intuitively, a better method should be able to find a smaller subset of training samples that can mis-
129   classify zt . We estimate these metrics over a set of test samples and plot the cumulative distribution
130   (CDF), which represents the probability that a sample’s label can be flipped as a function of the data
131   subset size. In fig. 1, we compare the Area Under Curve (AUC) of the CDF for the metrics described
132   above across our approach and other attribution methods.
133
      For a test sample zt and a data attribution approach τ (z, S), we rank the training samples based
134
      on decreasing order of positive influence on zt . Then, based on the ranking, we iteratively select
135   and modify a subset of training data. We perform this search, over different subsets to compute
136   the smallest training subset that can cause zt to be misclassified. Naively, checking all possible
137   subsets would be computationally expensive. (Ilyas et al., 2022) check only subsets with certain
138   discrete sizes to keep costs manageable. We instead propose to perform a bisection search to
139   approximate the search for the smallest subset, yielding more accurate results. The bisection search
140   approximation is supported by the observation that several data attribution approaches are additive
141   (Park et al., 2023). The exact algorithm and details are discussed in appendix A.6.
142   Linear Datamodeling Score (LDS) is another evaluation metric used for counterfactual evaluation
143   of data attribution approaches (Ilyas et al., 2022; Park et al., 2023). Let {S1 , ..., Sm |Si ⊂ S} be m
144   random subsets of the training set S, each of size α · n for some α ∈ (0, 1). The LDS metric is then
145   defined as:
146

147                  LDS(τ, z) = ρ f (z, θ∗ (Sj )) | j ∈ [m]}, {τ (z, S) · 1Sj | j ∈ [m]}
148
149   where ρ denotes Spearman rank correlation (Kokoska & Zwillinger, 2000), and 1Sj is the indicator
150   vector of the subset Sj . In other words, LDS compares the Spearman rank correlation on a sample
151   zt from m models trained on different subsets of training data b/w ground truth class margin and
152
      predicted margin from a data attribution approach τ . Note that while LDS metric accounts for both
      positive and negative influence in training samples, brittleness metrics only account for positive
153
      influence in training samples. Our baseline makes a simple assumption, that impacts performance
154
      on this metric.
155
156   Prior works focused on optimizing performance on LDS (Ilyas et al., 2022; Park et al., 2023), and
157   evaluating data brittleness metrics as a downstream task. However, our results in section 5.1 indicate
158
      that LDS scores do not always correlate with brittleness estimates. We also note that LDS focuses
      on counterfactual predictions for arbitrary changes in training data, while data brittleness metrics
159
      serve to quantify counterfactual predictions using targeted changes to training data.
160

      or leave-one-out influences (Koh & Liang, 2017; Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Jia et al., 2021). These

187   2.2                  BASELINES
188
189
      Datamodels (Ilyas et al., 2022): In the Datamodeling framework, the end-to-end training and eval-
      uation of deep neural networks is approximated with a parametric function. Surprisingly, the work
190
      shows optimizing a linear function is enough to predict model outputs reasonably well, given a train-
191
      ing data subset. By collecting a large dataset of training data subsets and model output pairs, (Ilyas
192   et al., 2022) demonstrate that such a linear mapping can accurately predict the correct class margin
193   for individual test samples. Among other use-cases, these Datamodels are shown to be effective at
194   counterfactual predictions and identifying visually similar train-test samples. But Datamodeling is
195   prohibitively expensive, requiring the training of hundreds of thousands of models (300,000 in the
196   original work) to accurately make counterfactual predictions. Unfortunately, this limitation makes
197   Datamodeling intractable for all but small toy problems.
198   TRAK (Park et al., 2023): By approximating models with a kernel machine, Tracing with the
199   Randomly-projected After Kernels (TRAK) makes progress toward reducing the computational cost
200   of data attribution. This work uses a randomly-projected gradient information from an ensemble
201   of models, to compute attribution scores. However, their method stores a high dimensional (from
202   4096 up to 20480 dimensional) projected gradient for each training and test sample, from a dozen or
203   more model checkpoints to use as a “feature”, leading to significant storage requirements. For our
204   experiments, the total storage cost of using TRAK surpassed 400 GBs when using 100 models on
205   CIFAR-10. Note that while our approach also stores feature embedding for each training sample, it
206
      only uses the penultimate layer of single self-supervised model, and thus our embeddings are much
      lower dimensional (128 dimensional for CIFAR-10).

209   3     O UR A PPROACH
210
211   Our approach utilizes the penultimate feature space representation of a network to extract features
212   from a test sample zt and each training sample in S. We approximate attribution scores by measuring
213   the distance in feature space between zt and each training sample in S. Datamodels and TRAK have
214   tried using features from an ensemble of supervised models and claimed them to be ineffective for
215   counterfactual estimation (Park et al., 2023; Ilyas et al., 2022). Next, we describe the details of our
      approach.


                                                                                  4
216
      Feature extractor. We find that the learning paradigm used for feature extraction heavily influ-
217   ences the estimation of data support. For example, embeddings from a ResNet-9 trained using a
218   self-supervised learning objective (MoCo, (He et al., 2020)) can be used to find smaller support sets
219   than the same model trained in a supervised manner (See ℓ2 MoCo vs ℓ2 Supervised in fig. 2). With
220   the exception of DINO (Caron et al., 2021) (whose test accuracy on CIFAR-10 was much lower), all
221   self-supervised feature extractors perform better than their supervised counterpart (see appendix A.3
222   for evaluation using multiple different feature extractors). We select MoCo as our preferred feature
223   extractor, as it outperforms other self-supervised approaches in both data removal support and mis-
224   labeling support scenarios. We find that the ResNet-18 backbone provides better support estimates
225
      than ResNet-9, and hence use it as default for all our experiments. Datamodels and TRAK, only
      compared against supervised feature extractors, leading to significantly worse counterfactual esti-
226
      mation.
227
228   Distance function When measuring the distance between two embeddings, Euclidean distance (ℓ2 )
229   is a common choice (Ilyas et al., 2022; Park et al., 2023). Cosine distance and Mahalanobis distance
230   have also been used to measure similarity, but these were found to perform similarly to Euclidean
231
      distances in previous work (Hanawa et al., 2021; Ilyas et al., 2022; Park et al., 2023).
232   However, we find that measuring distance as distance to the hyperplane of an Exemplar SVM
233   (ESVM) improves image similarity (Malisiewicz et al., 2011). To compute this, we train a linear
234   SVM using only the target sample embedding, as a positive sample and treating all other samples as
235   negative samples. We use the inverse of decision boundary distance, to measure the magnitude of
236   influence. Hence, samples closest to the decision boundary have a higher influence and vice-versa.
237
      In fig. 2, we show using hyperplane distance to ESVM yields better removal support estimates than
      ℓ2 distance.
238
239   Sign Estimation Directly using l2 or ESVM distance function, cannot identify training samples with
240   negative influence. Intuitively, to change the prediction of a test sample, we should only remove
241   samples from its vicinity that share the same class. While this assumption is simplistic, we show
242   that it works well for estimating data brittleness. For a test sample, zt = (x, y) we simply assign
243
      all training samples with label y, as a positive influence, and negative otherwise i.e. given a target
      image of an airplane, only airplane training images have a positive influence. See appendix A.4 for
244
      more discussion on this.

363   4.4   T RANSFER TO A DIFFERENT ARCHITECTURE ?
364
365   Datamodels and TRAK utilize information tied to the model architecture, such as gradients or logits
366   from an ensemble of models. Being data attribution methods, Datamodels and TRAK approximate
367   how training data influences a particular model’s output. Thus, it is not appropriate to use a Data-
368
      model for a ResNet-9 to attribute training data for a MobileNetV2. However, what could we learn
      if we tried to do so?
369

371   tions (Mania et al., 2019; Toneva et al., 2018). In order to better understand the role of architecture

373   architectures for estimating data brittleness.

470   6     OTHER R ELATED W ORKS
471
472   Data attribution methods should produce accurate counterfactual predictions about model outputs.
473   Although a counterfactual can be addressed by retraining the model, employing this straightforward
474   approach becomes impractical when dealing with large models and extensive datasets. To address
475   this problem, data attribution methods perform various approximations.
476   The seminal work on data attribution of Koh & Liang (2017) proposes attribution via approximate
477   influence functions. More specifically, Koh & Liang (2017) identify training samples most respon-
478   sible for a given prediction by estimating the effect of removing or slightly modifying a single
479   training sample. But being a first-order approximation, influence function estimates can vary wildly
480   with changes to network architecture and training regularization (Basu et al., 2021). Nevertheless,
481   approximations of influence functions have also been attempted for multi-billion parameter models
482
      (Grosse et al., 2023).
483   Measuring empirical influence has also been attempted through the construction of subsets of train-
484   ing data that include/exclude the target sample (Feldman & Zhang, 2020). In a related approach,
485   TracIn (Pruthi et al., 2020) and Gradient Aggregated Similarity (GAS) (Hammoudeh & Lowd,
      2022a;b) estimate the influence of each sample in training set S on the test example zt by mea-

508   has used Shapley values to ascribe value to data, but since Shapley values often require exponen-

510   In general, there seems to be a recurring tradeoff: computationally efficient methods tend to be
511   less reliable, whereas sampling-based approaches are more effective but require a large number of
512   models.

515   7   L IMITATIONS
516
517
      We emphasize that our approach is not meant to serve as a traditional data attribution method since
518
      it only uses information regarding the training data. Our approach relies on the intuition that models
519
      leverage data in similar ways (Zhen et al., 2022; Kornblith et al., 2019). It doesn’t explicitly utilize
520   information about model architecture, optimizer, or other aspects of training strategy. Since it relies
521   on visual similarity it may not directly perform well for certain downstream tasks such as debugging
522   biases specific to the training algorithm (Shah et al., 2023) or identifying backdoors (Khaddaj et al.,
523   2023). Also, unlike TRAK and Datamodels, it is unclear if utilizing a large ensemble of models
524   would benefit performance on our evaluation metrics.

527   8   C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   Data attribution approaches are computationally expensive, require an ensemble of models, and can
531
      be prone to inaccuracy. While these approaches exhibit promise and capability, their scalability to
      large-scale models remains uncertain. In this work, we present a simpler approach using features
532
      from a single self-supervised model, and evaluate it on two different counterfactual estimation sce-
533
      narios: data removal support and data mislabel support. Our approach scales easily and provides
534   tractable brittleness estimates on larger datasets such as ImageNet, outperforming other state-of-
535   the-art methods under manageable compute and storage requirements. By analyzing data support
536   estimates for each test sample, we find our approach can find smaller support sets in many cases.
537   By trasferring attribution scores from one model to another, we investigate the importance of ar-
538   chitecture in existing attribution methods. Additionally, our results highlight that the training data
539   itself plays an important role in estimating data brittleness, providing valuable insights into data
      attribution.

908   A.6               C OMPUTING DATA S UPPORT
909
910   We use bisection search to estimate data support. The use of bisection search is supported by the
911   observation that several data attribution approaches are additive Park et al. (2023), where the impor-
912   tance of a subset of training samples is defined as the sum of each of the samples in the subset. To
913   compute data removal support, we remove M samples (chosen using each attribution method) from
914
      the training data and log whether the resulting model misclassifies the target sample. For data mis-
      labeling support, we mislabel M samples (chosen using each attribution method) from the training
915
      data and assign a new label corresponding to the highest incorrect logit.
