047                                                                       A BSTRACT
048
049                 With the remarkable advancements in image generation and open-form text gener-
050                 ation, the creation of interleaved image-text content has become an increasingly
051                 intriguing field. Multimodal story generation, characterized by producing narra-
052                 tive texts and vivid images in an interleaved manner, has emerged as a valuable
053                 and practical task with broad applications. However, this task poses significant
                    challenges, as it necessitates the comprehension of the complex interplay be-

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
               tween texts and images, and the ability to generate long sequences of coherent,
055            contextually relevant texts and visuals. In this work, we propose SEED-Story,
056            a novel method that leverages a Multimodal Large Language Model (MLLM)
057            to generate extended multimodal stories. Our model, built upon the powerful
058            comprehension capability of MLLM, predicts text tokens as well as visual to-
059            kens, which are subsequently processed with an adapted visual de-tokenizer to
060            produce images with consistent characters and styles. We further propose multi-
061            modal attention sink mechanism to enable the generation of stories with up to 25
062            sequences (only 10 for training) in a highly efficient autoregressive manner. Addi-
063
               tionally, we present a large-scale and high-resolution dataset named StoryStream
               for training our model and quantitatively evaluating the task of multimodal story
064
               generation in various aspects. All models, training and inference codes are released
065
               in https://anonymous.4open.science/r/SEED-Story/.

068   1    I NTRODUCTION
069
070   Interleaved image-text data is ubiquitous on the internet, characterized by multiple images interspersed
071   with text. In recent years, there has been a surge of interest in generating interleaved image-text
072   content Tian et al. (2024); Ge et al. (2024); Aiello et al. (2023); Dong et al. (2023); Team (2024),
073   driven by the remarkable advancements in image generation Rombach et al. (2022); Lin et al. (2023);
074   Chen et al. (2023); Patashnik et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2023) and open-form text generation Touvron
075
      et al. (2023); Taori et al. (2023); Zheng et al. (2023). This has given rise to Multimodal Story
      Generation, an intriguing and valuable task that involves the generation of a sequence of consecutive
076
      storylines along with their corresponding vivid images in an interleaved manner, similar to that of a
077
      serialized comic.
078
079   Different from Personalized Story Visualization Ruiz et al. (2023); Avrahami et al. (2024); Tewel
080   et al. (2024), which aims to generate consistent images based on the provided captions following the
081
      pattern of text-to-image generation, multimodal story generation poses a more significant challenge
      due to the complexity of the inputs and the high demands of the outputs. Firstly, this task necessitates
082
      a thorough comprehension of interleaved data, where text is not only abstract and narrative in nature,
083
      but also deeply intertwined with complex images. The model must be adept at deciphering the
084   intricate relationships between images and texts to maintain a coherent narrative flow. Secondly, this
085   task requires the generation of not only a plausible text plot, but also visually captivating images that
086   are consistent in characters and styles. The model should be capable of achieving coherence in the
087   generation of both text and visuals, ensuring an engaging storytelling output.
088
      Recently, Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) Li et al. (2023); Zhu et al. (2023a); Peng
089
      et al. (2023a); Bai et al. (2023a); Liu et al. (2023c); Zhang et al. (2023); Lin et al. (2023); Laurençon
090   et al. (2024) have showcased powerful comprehension abilities in understanding multimodal data,
091   which makes them ideally suited for interleaved image-text content in multimodal stories. Conse-
092   quently, we introduce SEED-Story, as shown in Figure 1, a novel approach that builds upon the
093   MLLM to harness its comprehension strength, while further equipping it with the capability to
094   generate coherent images align with the narrative texts.
095
      Specifically, following previous work Sun et al. (2023a); Ge et al. (2024), we utilize a pre-trained
096   image tokenizer and de-tokenizer, which can decode realistic images with SD-XL Podell et al. (2023)
097   by taking the features of a pre-trained ViT as input. During training, given the interleaved visual
098   and textual data, we adopt the next-word prediction and image feature regression training objectives
099   to regularize multimodal generation. A fixed number of learnable queries are fed into the MLLM,
100   where the output hidden states are trained to reconstruct the ViT features of the target images. To
101   further enhance the consistency of characters and styles in generated images, we propose de-tokenizer
102   adaptation, where the regressed image features from the MLLM are fed into the de-tokenizer for
103   tuning SD-XL. This adaptation allows for better maintenance of coherence in low-level image details
104
      from the de-tokenizer, ensuring a more visually consistent storytelling output.
105   Furthermore, to enable the efficient generation of coherent long stories, we propose a multimodal
106   attention sink mechanism based on window attention Beltagy et al. (2020), which maintains a fixed-
107   size sliding window on the Key-Value (KV) states of the most recent tokens, as well as the beginning
      of text tokens, images tokens, and the end of image tokens. We empirically find that retaining these


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      tokens will largely mitigate the model’s failure with window attention when the token length surpasses
109   the cache size, allowing our model to generalize to longer sequences than the training sequence
110   length in an efficient manner. Our model with the proposed multimodal attention sink mechanism
111   can generate long stories with up to 30 multimodal sequences, featuring rich text plots and diverse
112   visual scenarios.
113
      Additionally, we introduce a dataset named StoryStream for training and evaluating multimodal
114
      story generation. We design an automatic pipeline that leverages MLLMs to obtain a large-scale and
115   high-resolution dataset featuring a sequence of narrative-rich texts and intriguing images, derived
116   from animated videos. StoryStream is four times larger in terms of data volume compared to the
117   existing largest story dataset Liu et al. (2023a), and it boasts higher image resolution, longer sequence
118   lengths, and more detailed story narratives. We further meticulously design evaluation metrics to
119   assess multimodal story generation, taking into account image style consistency, story engagement,
120   and image-text coherence. The evaluation results demonstrate that our model, SEED-Story, achieves
121   superior performance in these aspects.
122   In summary, Our contributions are three-fold. (1) We propose SEED-Story, a novel method that
123   leverages an MLLM to generate multimodal stories with rich narrative text and contextually relevant
124   images. (2) We propose multimodal attention sink to enable the efficient generation of long stories
125   with sequence lengths larger than those used during training. (3) We introduce StoryStream, a large-
126   scale dataset specifically designed for training and benchmarking multimodal story generation. All
127   models, training and inference codes are released in https://anonymous.4open.science/
128   r/SEED-Story/.

174   Multimodal Story Generation (Shen & Elhoseiny (2023)) aims to generate a sequence of consecutive
175   storylines along with their corresponding images, similar to that of a serialized comic. To
176   achieve this, a model must be capable of predicting reasonable story developments and generating
177   corresponding illustrations, by incorporating the previous results as context in an auto-regressive
178   manner. As shown in Figure 2, the generated story images exhibit rapidly changing backgrounds
179
      and characters, different from personalized story visualization where the main character consistently
      appears in the images.

181   The task of multimodal story generation presents a more substantial challenge, and we follow previous
182   research Shen & Elhoseiny (2023) to adopt a closed-set setting. We believe the ultimate goal of
183   multimodal story generation should be to generate highly diverse scenarios while also generalizing to
184   unseen characters, which will be explored in our future work.

187
      models (LLMs) Touvron et al. (2023); Brown et al. (2020); Chowdhery et al. (2022) and multimodal
      large language models (MLLMs) Li et al. (2023); Zhu et al. (2023a); Liu et al. (2023d); Peng et al.
188
      (2023b); Bai et al. (2023b); Liu et al. (2023b); Zhang et al. (2023); Lin et al. (2023); Sun et al. (2023c);

295   De-tokenizer Adaptation After instruction tuning, the SEED-Story MLLM effectively produces
296   story images with correct semantics but lacks style consistency and details. We attribute this issue to
297   the misalignment between the latent space of the MLLM output and the image features. To address
298   this, we perform de-tokenizer adaptation for style and texture alignment. In this stage, only the
299   SD-XL image de-tokenizer is trained. Conditioned on the MLLM output embeddings, SD-XL is
300   expected to generate images that are pixel-level aligned with the ground truth. The separate training
301
      of the de-tokenizer offers two key advantages. First, it avoids optimization conflicts between the
      LLM and the de-tokenizer. Second, it conserves memory, making the process executable on GPUs
302
      with limited memory. Please see more analysis in Section B of our appendix.

305   3.2    L ONG S TORY G ENERATION WITH M ULTIMODAL ATTENTION S INK
306
307   Generating long visual stories has substantial potential in various applications, including education
308
      and entertainment. However, creating these stories with MLLMs presents significant challenges.
      Datasets for extended, interleaved stories are not only rare but also impede the training process due
309
      to their complexity. To address this, we have to employ a train-short-test-long approach, training
310
      models on shorter narratives and extending to longer generations during inference.
311
312   Moreover, during inference, generating significantly longer stories than the training data often leads
313   to model degradation, producing lower-quality images, as illustrated in the first row of Figure 8.
314
      This process also requires extensive token usage to ensure continuity and coherence, which in turn
      increases memory and computational demands.
315
316   A simplistic solution for this is to use a sliding window technique, depicted in Figure 4 right (b).
317   However, this method disrupts the token relationships in the Key-Value (KV) cache, resulting in
318   subpar generative outcomes, as demonstrated by StreamingLLM Xiao et al. (2023). To overcome
319   this, StreamingLLM introduces an attention sink mechanism that preserves the initial tokens, thus
320
      allowing for efficient processing of lengthy generations without quality compromise. While effective
      in language models, its efficacy diminishes in multimodal contexts, as shown in Figure 4 right (c).
321
322   To enhance long multimodal generation, we revisit the attention maps of MLLMs. After conducting
323   numerous experiments (see Section D of the appendix for more details) across various models and
      cases, we analyze the attention maps across different layers and heads. Our analysis reveals that most


                                                               6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      queries predominantly focus on four types of tokens: (1) starting tokens, (2) punctuation tokens, (3)
325   beginning-of-image (BoI) tokens, and (4) end-of-image (EoI) tokens. Unlike language-only models,
326   MLLMs place considerable attention on specific image tokens, particularly those near the BoI and
327   EoI, as illustrated in Figure 4 left.
328
      Building on these insights, we propose a new mechanism for extended generation in MLLMs, termed
329
      the multimodal attention sink. During generation, we consistently retain the starting tokens and the
330
      image tokens adjacent to the BoI and EoI. Although punctuation tokens receive high attention values,
331   their latent value norms are minimal, contributing insignificantly to the final output, so we do not
332   keep them, as noted by Ge et al. (2023a). Our proposed mechanism enables our model to generate
333   high-quality images while maintaining a low computational footprint.

397   4.1   DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
398
      An ideal source for creating a multimodal story generation dataset is cartoon series, which inherently
399
      contain rich plots and consistent character portrayals. We selected three cartoon series to construct
400
      our dataset and we present the Curious George in the main body of our paper. The process begins with
401   collecting various series, from which we extract keyframes and their associated subtitles Kilian et al.
402   (2023). Each keyframe is then processed by GPT-4V OpenAI (2023) or Qwen-VL Bai et al. (2023b)
403   to generate a detailed image description. These elements—keyframe, subtitle, and description—are
404   compiled into a single group. We aggregate 30 such groups and input them into GPT-4, supplemented
405   with background information about the cartoon series. Following our instructions, GPT-4 generates
406   high-quality narrative texts suitable for training story generation models.
407   During dataset construction, we discovered that employing the above chain of thought approach
408   not only produces more accurate narrative text but also speeds up the construction process. Unlike
409   directly feeding all images directly to GPT-4, which is limited to 10 images due to API constrains, our
410   approach produces longer stories. We also significantly improve the model’s understanding of each
411   image by incorporating detailed descriptions. This enhancement in image comprehension enriches
412   the narrative details, providing a richer story generation reference.

      5.3                                        M ULTIMODAL ATTENTION S INK
521
522   To verify the effectiveness of multimodal attention sink in long story generation, we conduct an exper-
523   iment visualizing a long story using the SEED-Story model, but with varying attention mechanisms.
524   We chunk our data into stories of length of 10 considering the training efficiency. We set the window
525   size as the same as the training length. Qualitative results presented in Figure 8 demonstrate that
526   window attention quickly collapses when the inference length exceeds the training length. Both dense
527   attention and attention sink approaches fare better, yet still fail to produce meaningful images as the
528   inference sequence lengthens. In contrast, the multimodal attention sink consistently produces high-
529
      quality images. In terms of efficiency, the multimodal attention sink exhibits significant improvement
      over dense attention, with only a modest increase in time and memory costs compared to window
530
      attention and vanilla attention sink. These additional costs stem from retaining extra image tokens in
531
      the KV cache. Quantitative results presented in Table 2 substantiate the above conclusion.

536   This work introduces SEED-Story, a pioneering approach that leverages a Multimodal Large Language

538   We propose a multimodal attention sink mechanism to enable our model to generalize to generating
539   long sequences in an efficient manner. We further present a high-quality dataset named StoryStream
      for training and benchmarking the task of multimodal story generation effectively.

      For our language model (LLM), we utilize the LLAMA2-7B pre-trained model and finetune it
824
      using LoRA, supported by the peft library. The hyperparameter r is set to 6, and lora_alpha
825   is set to 32. The modules optimized include the q_projection_layer, v_projection_layer,
826   k_projection_layer, o_projection_layer, gate_projection_layer, down_projection_layer,
827   and up_projection_layer. We employ a learning rate of 1 × 10−4 to finetune this model on

830   C.3   D E - TOKENIZER A DAPTATION
831
832   In this stage we fully finetune the SD-XL model. The data format is as the same as instruction
833   tuning, but we fix all MLLM params and optimize only the SD-XL. It takes the MLLM output and
834   is asked to produce image correspond to the ground truth. The SD-XL model was trained using 4
835   NVIDIA-A800 GPUs. A learning rate of 1 × 10−4 was chosen to facilitate gradual weight updates,
836
      ensuring stable convergence, while a weight decay of 0.03 was applied for regularization to prevent
      overfitting. Training was performed using mixed precision (bf16), which significantly reduced
837
      memory usage and accelerated the training process without compromising the model’s accuracy.

1300   K    L IMITATIONS
1301
1302   Lack of Realistic Data Experimentation: This limitation points to a potential gap in the validation
1303
       of the SEED-Story model under practical, real-world conditions. Without experiments using realistic
       data, it’s difficult to ascertain how the model would perform in scenarios that are not perfectly
1304
       controlled or that deviate from the training conditions. This can be crucial, especially in applications
1305
       like storytelling where the context and variability of real-world data play significant roles. A possible
1306   solution would be to incorporate a broader range of test conditions, including noisy data or data from
1307   "in-the-wild" storytelling scenarios, to evaluate the robustness and adaptability of the model.
1308
1309   Training on a Non-Diverse Dataset: The second limitation is the restriction of the model’s training
1310   to animation datasets which does not cover a large scale or diverse styles. This can severely limit the
1311   model’s ability to generalize and produce outputs in styles that are not represented in the training
1312   data. This is particularly limiting in creative tasks such as storytelling where the ability to adapt to
1313   various artistic and narrative styles is crucial. To mitigate this, expanding the dataset to include a
1314   wider array of styles, genres, and visual aesthetics could be beneficial.
