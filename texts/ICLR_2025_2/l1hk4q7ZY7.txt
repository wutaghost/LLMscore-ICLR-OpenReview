009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In black-box optimization, when directly evaluating the function values of solu-
012            tions is very costly or infeasible, access to the objective function is often lim-
013            ited to comparing pairs of solutions, which yields dueling black-box optimization.
014            Dueling optimization is solely based on pairwise preferences, and thus notably
015            reduces cost compared with function value based methods such as Bayesian op-
016
               timization. However, an optimization performance gap obviously exists between
               dueling based and function value based methods. This is mainly due to that most
017
               existing dueling optimization methods do not make full use of the pairwise prefer-
018
               ences collected. To fill this gap, this paper proposes relation augmented preferen-
019            tial Bayesian optimization (RAPBO) via preference propagation. By considering
020            solution similarity, RAPBO aims to uncover the potential preferential relations
021            between solutions within different preferences through the proposed preferential
022            relation propagation technique. Specifically, RAPBO first clusters solutions using
023            a Gaussian mixture model. After obtaining the solution set with the highest intra-
024            cluster similarity, RAPBO utilizes a directed hypergraph to model the potential
025            relations between solutions, thereby realizing relation augmentation. Extensive
026            experiments are conducted on both synthetic functions and real-world tasks such
027
               as motion control and spacecraft trajectory optimization. The experimental results
               disclose the satisfactory accuracy of augmented preferences in RAPBO, and show
028
               the superiority of RAPBO compared with existing dueling optimization methods.
029
               Notably, it is verified that, under the same evaluation cost budget, RAPBO is
030            competitive with or even surpass the function value based Bayesian optimization
031            methods with respect to optimization performance. The codes can be found in
032            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/RAPBO-E15F.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Black-box optimization (Conn et al., 2009; Liu et al., 2022), also termed as derivative-free opti-
038   mization, is a class of optimization methods designed for situations where the objective function
039
      is unknown, complex, or expensive to evaluate. It enables global search for the optimal solution,
      with Bayesian optimization (BO) (Garnett, 2023; Mei et al., 2023; Shahriari et al., 2016) as a rep-
040
      resentative. Due to the significant advantages and progress of black-box optimization, it has been
041
      widely applied in fields such as chemical synthesis (Shields et al., 2021), machine learning (Freund
042   & Schapire, 1997; Elsken et al., 2019) and reinforcement learning (Qian & Yu, 2021).
043
044   In traditional black-box optimization, evaluating the numerical objective function values is typically
045
      necessary. However, in many real-world scenarios, acquiring the objective function values can be
      extremely costly or entirely infeasible (Brochu et al., 2010). It has been found that comparing
046
      two solutions by preferences is relatively cheaper than scoring solutions (Kahneman & Tversky,
047
      1979), such as in A/B tests (Siroker & Koomen, 2013). Thus, dueling or preferential optimization
048   has been developed as an easier and cheaper alternative, e.g., preferential Bayesian optimization
049   (PBO) (González et al., 2017). Instead of relying on function values, dueling optimization leverages
050   pairwise preferences (i.e., which solution is preferred) to guide the optimization process, making it
051   easier and cheaper in scenarios where evaluating objective function values is costly or infeasible.
052   Dueling optimization has been successfully applied in a wide range of fields, such as visual design
053   optimization (Koyama et al., 2020) and robotic gait optimization (Li et al., 2021), showcasing its
      adaptability and effectiveness across various domains.

054
      However, due to that most existing dueling optimization methods have typically made simple use of
055   pairwise preferences, a obvious optimization performance gap exists between dueling or preference
056   based methods such as PBO and function value based methods such as BO. It is obviously that
057   insufficient utilization of pairwise preferences may significantly impact the performance of dueling
058   optimization, while making a fuller use of the preferences can improve the optimization process.
059
      Problem. Although dueling optimization can optimize using only low-cost pairwise preferences, the
060
      insufficient exploitation of preferences of existing methods could significantly limit the optimization
061   performance of dueling optimization, e.g., preferential Bayesian optimization. This leads to an
062   obvious optimization performance gap between preference based and function value based methods.
063
064
      Contribution. This paper aims to fill the optimization performance gap between preference based
      and function value based methods, and answer whether the preference based methods can match
065
      or even surpass the performance of function value based methods. To this end, we propose the
066
      relation augmented preferential Bayesian optimization (RAPBO) method via preference propaga-
067   tion. RAPBO aims to uncover the potential preferential relations between different preferences
068   through the proposed preferential relation propagation technique based on solution similarity. The
069   experimental results reveal that the preferences augmented by the preference propagation technique
070   achieve satisfactory accuracy and verify its superiority over existing dueling optimization methods.
071   Notably, it is verified that, within the same evaluation cost budget, the performance of RAPBO can
072   match and even surpass that of function value based Bayesian optimization methods.
073   The following sections provide an overview of related work and essential preliminaries, detail the
074   proposed RAPBO method, present the experimental results, and conclude the paper.

082   2.1   P REFERENTIAL BAYESIAN O PTIMIZATION
083
      To extend BO to scenarios where direct access to the objective function is unavailable, but infor-
084
      mation about user preferences can be obtained, González et al. (2017) propose a framework called
085   preferential Bayesian optimization (PBO). PBO leverages pairwise preferences to fit a Gaussian pro-
086   cess (GP) (Rasmussen & Williams, 2006) within preference function domain. The PBO employs
087   the dueling-Thompson Sampling (DTS) to determine the potential optimal solution and the solution
088   with high uncertainty as candidates for the next duel. Benavoli et al. (2021) prove that the true
089   posterior distribution of the preference function is a skewed Gaussian process (SkewGP), and incor-
090   porate SkewGP to enhance the performance of PBO. Based on the work of Benavoli et al. (2021),
091   Takeno et al. (2023) propose a practical method, HB, which ensures high computational efficiency
092   and low sample complexity. Due to the lack of theoretical guarantees for most acquisition functions
093
      in PBO, Astudillo et al. (2023) introduce qEUBO, a promising acquisition function with a grounded
      decision-theoretic justification. Guided by the optimism principle, POP-BO (Xu et al., 2024) con-
094
      structs a confidence set from preferences and employs an optimistic strategy that ensures a bound
095
      on cumulative regret, enabling it to effectively report an estimated best solution with guaranteed
096   convergence. To address the dimensionality issue exacerbated by modeling the preference function,
097   PE-DBO (Zhang et al., 2023) extends the concept of intrinsic effective dimensionality to preference
098   function. Despite these advancements, these methods still do not fully utilize the available pairwise
099   preferences, which continues to impact the performance of dueling optimization.
100
      Instead of constructing a surrogate model to fit the preference function, Sui et al. (2017) and Xu
101
      et al. (2020) respectively propose kernel-self-sparring (KSS) and comp-GP-UCB (COMP-UCB).
102   KSS uses a GP to model the function, where the value represents the probability of one solution
103   beating the optimal solution, rather than modeling a preference function. COMP-UCB employs the
104   Borda function, inspired by the Borda score (Sui et al., 2018), to replace the preference function and
105   regards the average performance of all solutions as the basis for comparison. While these methods
106   simplify the dueling optimization problems compared to the methods that model the preference
107   function, they may still face challenges caused by the insufficient utilization of pairwise preferences,
      leading to performance that cannot match that of function value based methods.

      2.2   H YPERGRAPH R EPRESENTATION
109
110   Hypergraphs (Bretto, 2013) are mathematical models that extend the classical graph structure. In
111   a traditional graph, edges are binary relations connecting two vertices, while a hypergraph allows
112   edges to connect multiple vertices, and these edges are called hyperedges. This characteristic enables
113   hypergraphs to naturally represent more complex, higher-order relationships and interactions, par-
114   ticularly excelling in modeling multi-party interactions. Various algorithms, such as hypergraph par-
115   titioning (Papa & Markov, 2007) and hypergraph clustering (Zhou et al., 2006), have been developed
116
      to efficiently process hypergraph structures, further enhancing their applicability in large-scale data-
      driven tasks. Consequently, hypergraphs are widely used in fields such as machine learning (Gao
117
      et al., 2022), data mining (Ji et al., 2020), and social network analysis (Lin et al., 2009).

122   3.1   D UELING O PTIMIZATION
123
      Consider a black-box function f : X → R, where X ⊂ RD , which is costly to evaluate. The goal
124
      of global optimization is to find the optimal solution x∗ = argmaxx∈X f (x) in a D-dimensional
125   continuous solution space. Instead of directly evaluating numerical function values, the objective
126   function is evaluated by comparing pairs of solutions (x, x′ ), i.e., duels. An human oracle provides
127   feedback on which solution in a duel is better, yielding binary information (i.e., 0 for x′ and 1 for
128   x). This type of feedback is referred to as preference, and only these preferences will be used during
129   the optimization process. Throughout this paper, each duel is treated as a coloum vector, represented
130   by [x; x′ ] ∈ R2D , where the space with dimension 2D is called dueling solution space.
131   Preference Function. In dueling optimization, the feedback from a comparison between two so-
132   lutions [x; x′ ] is treated as a stochastic process. This feedback is sampled from a Bernoulli distri-
133   bution, where the probability reflects the likelihood that solution x is preferred over x′ . Under the
134   assumption that the probability of solution x being preferred over x′ is positively correlated with
135   the difference in their objective function values, i.e., P (x ≻ x′ ) ∝ f (x) − f (x′ ), and the logistic
136   function is commonly used to convert this difference into a probability. Therefore, the preference
137   function in the dueling solution space can be formulated as

158   3.2   D IFFERENT S AMPLING RULES IN P REFERENTIAL BAYESIAN O PTIMIZATION .
159
160   Rather than classifying dueling optimization methods based on the construction of surrogate models
161   (see Section 2.1), this paper categorizes them according to whether one solution in the next duel is
      fixed, specifically if the current best solution is used as one of the solution in the next duel.


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      For methods where one solution in the duel is fixed, such as HB (Takeno et al., 2023) and POP-
163   BO (Xu et al., 2024), the first solution is selected as the current best, while the second solution
164   is resampled based on a given acquisition function. In this case, the pairwise preferences are not
165   entirely independent, as there is a common solution in the duels of consecutive comparisons, which
166   allows a part of relations between different preferences to be inferred. However, this strategy limits
167   the ability of methods to explore the solution space. In contrast, in the second type of methods, both
168   solutions in a candidate duel are resampled through the acquisition functions, with PBO (González
169   et al., 2017) being a typical algorithm of this kind. The PBO uses DTS to choose the potential
170   optimal solution and the most uncertain one for the next duel, thereby balancing exploration and
171
      exploitation. However, these approaches lead to pairwise preferences being more isolated, making
      it challenging to obtain the relations between different preferences.
172
173   In this paper, we focus on the second type of methods and aim to uncover the potential preferential
174   relations through a preference propagation technique, thereby enhancing the performance of dueling
175   optimization to match that of function value based methods.
176
177   3.3   D IRECTED H YPERGRAPH
178
179   Directed hypergraphs are extension of traditional graphs in which edges, called directed hyperedges,
180
      can connect multiple vertices from a source set to a target set, unlike traditional graphs where edges
      only link pairs of vertices. Formally, a directed hypergraph is defined as G = (V, E), where V rep-
181
      resents the set of vertices and E represents the set of directed hyperedges. Each directed hyperedge
182
      ε ∈ E is an ordered pair of vertex subsets (Vs , Vt ), where Vs ⊆ V is the source set, and Vt ⊆ V is
183   the target set, with Vs ∩ Vt = ∅. The directed hyperedge ε ∈ E represents a relationship in which all
184   vertices in the source set Vs direct to all vertices in the target set Vt . Directed hypergraphs provide
185   a flexible way to model complex interactions between groups of vertices, avoiding the individual
186   connections between each pair, as would be necessary in traditional graphs.

      4     T HE P ROPOSED M ETHOD

191
      Although dueling optimization, e.g., preferential Bayesian optimization, adapts well to scenarios
      where the objective function can only be evaluated through comparing a pair of solutions, the op-
192
      timization performance gap still exists between preference based and function value based meth-
193
      ods due to the insufficient utilization of pairwise preferences (i.e., which solution is preferred).
194   This section introduces the proposed method, relation augmented preferential Bayesian optimiza-
195   tion (RAPBO), which aims to make fuller use of pairwise preferences and enhance the performance
196   of dueling optimization through a preference propagation technique, thereby achieving performance
197   comparable with function value based methods such as Bayesian optimization. To clarify the expla-
198   nation of the proposed method, we have included a notation section in Appendix E.

289   4.3    C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
290
291   In this section, we analyze the improvements in time and space complexity achieved by using hy-
292
      pergraphs to model the relations between solutions in the preference propagation technique.
293   The introduction of hypergraphs avoids the full connection that occurs when traditional graphs are
294   used in the preference propagation technique. To establish the connections between the three so-
295   lution sets, a traditional graph requires full connections from the bad solution set to the similar
296   solution set, and from the similar solution set to the good solution set. We denote the quantities of
297   bad solutions, similar solutions, and good solutions as n1 , n2 and n3 , respectively. Specifically, in
298
      the case of using a traditional graph, the time complexity of modeling the relations between solu-
      tions is O(n1 ∗ n2 + n2 ∗ n3 ), and the space complexity of the preference propagation technique is
299
      also O(n1 ∗ n2 + n2 ∗ n3 ). However, when employing a hypergraph instead of a traditional graph,
300
      the two solution sets can be directly connected through a single hyperedge, resulting in the time
301   complexity of modeling the relations reducing to O(m), where m is the number of hyperedges and
302   m = 2 in the preference propagation technique. Thus, the time complexity can also be expressed
303   as O(2). Additionally, the space complexity of the preference propagation technique also decreases
304   to O(m + n1 + n2 + n3 ) with m = 2. The reduction in complexity brought about by the directed
305   hypergraphs makes the preference propagation technique more efficient and practical.

530   This paper aims to fill the optimization performance gap between preference based and function
531
      value based methods, and verify that the preference based methods can match or even surpass the
      performance of the function valued based methods. We propose the method, relation augmented
532
      preferential Bayesian optimization (RAPBO), which enhances the performance of dueling optimiza-
533
      tion by capturing potential preferential relations through the proposed preference propagation tech-
534   nique. Extensive experiments on synthetic functions and real-world tasks disclose the satisfactory

538   the same cost budget. In future work, we plan to utilize the pairwise preferences more fully through
539   more efficient methods, and further improve the accuracy of the augmented preferences to enhance
      the performance of dueling optimization.

707   Algorithm 2 Preference Propagation Technique
708
709
      Input: Current dataset Dj = {[xi ; x′i ], pi }ji=1 , and preference propagation parameter k.
      Procedure:
710
       1: Fit a special GP D to Dj and compute the covariance between any two solutions in the dataset
711
          Dj to assess their similarity.
712    2: Compute distance between any two solutions by 1 − similarity, and cluster all solutions into
713       k sets.
714    3: Obtain the set of similar solutions Vs with the highest intra-cluster similarity, the set of bad
715       solutions Vbad and the set of good solutions Vgood .
716    4: Construct the directed hyperedges ε1 and ε2 to model the potential preferential relations.
717    5: Combine the augmented preferential relations with the dataset Dj and obtain the augmented
718       dataset Dj+ .
719    6: return the augmented dataset Dj+ .
720
721
722   The preference propagation technique, as shown in Algorithm 2, is designed to make fuller utiliza-
723   tion of the existing pairwise preferences by modeling potential preferential relations among solu-
724   tions. Initially, it requires the current dataset Dj and a preference propagation parameter k. The
725   preference propagation technique begins by fitting a specific Gaussian process model GP D to the
726   dataset Dj and computing the covariance between solutions to assess their similarity (line 1). Next,
727   the technique calculates distances based on the complement of similarity and clusters the solutions
728   into k sets (line 2). From these clusters, it identifies a set of similar solutions Vs with the highest
729   intra-cluster similarity, as well as the set of bad solutions Vbad and the set of good solutions Vgood
730   (line 3). Directed hyperedges are constructed to model the potential preferential relations among
731
      these solution sets (line 4). Finally, the augmented preferential relations are combined with the orig-
      inal dataset Dj to create an augmented dataset Dj+ (line 5), which is then returned as output. This
732
      technique aims to better uncover and utilize the potential preferential relations between preferences,
733
      thereby make fuller utilization of the existing pairwise preferences.
