009                                                   Abstract
010
011              In numerous machine learning contexts, the relationship between input variables
012              and predicted outputs is not only statistically significant but also strictly monotonic.
013              Conventional approaches to ensuring monotonicity focus primarily on construc-
014              tion or regularization methods. This paper establishes that the problem of strict
015              monotonic probability can be interpreted as a comparison between an observable
016              revenue variable and a latent cost variable. This insight allows us to reformulate
017              the original monotonicity challenge into modeling the latent cost variable and es-
018
                 timating its distribution. To address this issue, we introduce a generative model
                 for the latent cost variable, called the Generative Cost Model (GCM), and derive
019
                 a corresponding loss function. We further enhance the estimation of latent vari-
020
                 ables using variational inference, which reformulate our loss function accordingly.
021              Lastly, we validate our approach through a numerical simulation of quantile regres-
022              sion and several experiments on public datasets, demonstrating that our method
023              significantly outperforms traditional techniques. The code of GCM is available in
024              https://github.com/iclr-2025-4464/GCM.

027   1     Introduction
028
029   Many machine learning problems exhibit a monotonic relationship between inputs and outputs.
030   Some of these relationships are statistical in nature, such as the correlation between a person’s height
031   and weight or the relationship between a company’s stock price and its annual income. However,
032   these monotonicities are often empirical and not strictly defined. In contrast, certain problems
033
      necessitate strict monotonicity, such as the relationship between equipment availability and its age,
      or the connection between auction winning rates and bidding prices. For these strict monotonic
034
      problems, we require a model capable of predicting strict monotonic probability based on specific
035
      input variables. We refer to these input variables as revenue variables, where higher revenue
036   correlates with an increased probability of a more positive response.
037
038   The most common deep learning methods for addressing the monotonicity problem can be broadly
039
      categorized into two types (Runje & Shankaranarayana (2023)): monotonic by construction and
      by regularization. The construction approach maintains strict monotonicity through customized
040
      structures in deep neural networks, such as monotonic activation functions, positive weight matrices,
041
      and min-max structures (Sill (1997)). In contrast, the regularization approach promotes monotonicity
042   by designing specific loss functions (Sill & Abu-Mostafa (1996)).
043
044   Unlike traditional approaches, we propose a novel method to tackle the monotonicity problem using
045
      a generative framework. In the estimation of 𝑝(𝑦|x, r), where 𝑦 ∈ R is a response that maintains
      monotonicity with respect to the revenue variable r but is not necessarily monotonic with respect
046
      to x, we employ a two-step process. (i) We simplify the multivariate problem into a Bernoulli case
047
      via variable substitution trick, so that 𝑦 is reduced to binary values (0 or 1). (ii) We reformulate the
048   monotonicity problem by defining a latent cost variable c, such that 𝑦 = I(c ≺ r) ∈ {0, 1}. This
049   ensures that the monotonicity between 𝑦 and r is preserved, as we have 𝑃𝑟 (𝑦 = 1|x, r) = 𝑃𝑟 (c ≺
050   r|x, r). Here, ≺ denotes the partial order in the vector space and I represents the indicator function.
051   Through this transformation, we can bypass the need to design a strictly monotonic function and
052   instead focus on the latent cost variable c. Consequently, we can use any structure to model c with the
053   monotonicity constraints being ignored, as the monotonicity is inherently satisfied by the definition
      of c.

081
082   The main contributions of our paper are summarized as follows:
083
084          • We introduce a universal technique that reformulates the problem of monotonic probability
085            into a modeling problem for latent cost variables, avoiding restrictions in conventional
086
               monotonic neural networks.
087          • We address the modeling of the cost variable using a generative approach called the Gen-
088            erative Cost Model (GCM), and we present two loss functions derived from log-likelihood
089            and the variational lower bound.
090          • We evaluate our method for classification tasks using a simulated quantile regression and
091            tasks on four public datasets, demonstrating that our model consistently outperforms tradi-
092            tional monotonic models.

      Monotonic Modeling. In many machine learning tasks, we have the prior knowledge that the output
145
      should be monotonic with respect to certain input variables. A straightforward idea is to identify a
146   monotonic function and optimize its parameters to approximate the desired monotonic output. It can
147   be summarized as the following form:
148
149                                           minimize L (𝑦, 𝐹𝜃 (x, r))
150                                                      𝜕𝐹𝜃 (x, r)                                            (1)
151                                           subject to            ≻ 0.
                                                            𝜕r
152
153   The Min-Max architecture (Sill (1997)) is a pioneering work in monotonic neural networks, utilizing
154   a piecewise linear model to approximate monotonic target functions. Its monotonicity is ensured
155   through (i) positive weighting matrices, (ii) monotonic activation functions, and (iii) a Min-Max
156   structure.
157   Along the direction of monotonic by construction, Nolte et al. (2022) introduced the Lipschitz
158   monotonic network, which enhances robustness through weight constraint. Igel (2023) proposed
159   the smoothed min-max monotonic network, which replaces the traditional min-max structure with a
160   smoothed log-sum-exp function, preventing the network from becoming silent. Additionally, Runje
161   & Shankaranarayana (2023) developed the constrained monotonic neural network, which improves
      the approximation of non-convex functions by modifying activation functions.

      Another popular direction for improving monotonicity involves the use of regularization techniques,
163   which can be formulated as:
164
165                                  minimize L (𝑦, 𝐹𝜃 (x, r)) + R (𝐹𝜃 ),                                (2)
166
      where the regularization R (𝐹𝜃 ) > 0 if 𝐹𝜃 is not monotonic at some points. This direction includes
167   monotonicity hints proposed by Sill & Abu-Mostafa (1996), which use hint samples and pairwise
168   loss to guide model learning. The certified monotonic neural networks proposed by Liu et al. (2020)
169   certify monotonicity by verifying the lower bound of the partial derivative of monotonic features.
170   Furthermore, Gupta et al. (2019) proposed a pointwise penalization method for negative gradients,
171   while counter example guided methods were introduced by Sivaraman et al. (2020).
172
      In addition, the lattice networks (Garcia & Gupta (2009)) can solve the monotonic problem by either
173   a construction or regularization approach; extensive works have been conducted in this area by
174   Milani Fard et al. (2016), You et al. (2017), Gupta et al. (2019) and Yanagisawa et al. (2022), etc.

      Monotonicity also plays an important role in many areas of machine learning. Ben-David (1995);
      Lee et al. (2003); van de Kamp et al. (2009); Chen & Guestrin (2016) bring monotonicity into
177
      tree models; Rashid et al. (2020) propose the QMIX method using monotonic value functions in
178
      multi-agent reinforcement learning; Lam et al. (2023) propose a multi-class loss function using
179   monotonicity of gradients of convex functions; Haldar et al. (2020) and Xu et al. (2024) bring
180   monotonicity into online business, etc.
181
182
      Variational Inference and Generative models. Variational inference (VI) (Peterson (1987); Parisi
      & Shankar (1988); Saul & Jordan (1995)) is a powerful technique for working with generative
183
      models, and recent years have seen significant advances based on this approach (Kingma (2013);
184
      Rezende et al. (2014); Ozair & Bengio (2014); Burda et al. (2015); Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015);
185   Ho et al. (2020); Song et al. (2020)). VI transforms the complex task of Bayesian inference into a
186   computationally manageable optimization problem by approximating the latent variables within a
187   specified family of distributions. This is achieved by optimizing the evidence lower bound (ELB)
188   rather than the original evidence.
189
      Recent studies have highlighted the rapid growth of conditional generative models. In the realm of
190
      text-to-image generation, notable works include Ramesh et al. (2021), Ramesh et al. (2022), Saharia
191   et al. (2022), and Rombach et al. (2022). For text-to-video generation, key contributions come
192   from Esser et al. (2023) and Brooks et al. (2024). Unlike variational autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma
193   (2013)), which initiate generation from a latent variable, these conditional generative models begin
194   with a pair comprising a given condition (such as text, image, or video) and a latent variable. This
195   is typically expressed through the decomposition: 𝑝(𝑥, 𝑧) = 𝑝(𝑥) 𝑝(𝑧|𝑥), where 𝑥 is the condition
196   and 𝑧 is the latent variable. Consequently, these models primarily focus on conditional probability
197   𝑝(𝑧|𝑥). In this paper, we adopt this paradigm to construct our cost generation model.
198   Moreover, the normalizing flow is an important subject of generative models, it not only transforms
199   a simple distribution to a complicated distribution, but also requires these transformations to be
200   invertible, which is sufficient when the transformations are continuous and monotonic. There have
201   been studies that involve monotonicity in normalizing flows: Ziegler & Rush (2019); Ho et al. (2019);
202   Wehenkel & Louppe (2019); Müller et al. (2019); Jaini et al. (2019); Dinh et al. (2019); Ahn et al.
203   (2022).

506   6   Conclusion
507
508   This paper presents an innovative generative method for monotonic modeling by reformulating the
509   monotonicity problem through the incorporation of a latent cost variable c. We have developed a
510   robust generation process for this cost variable that accurately approximates the latent costs. Our
511   experimental results demonstrate that the proposed Generative Cost Model (GCM and GCM-VI)
512
      effectively addresses the monotonicity challenge, significantly outperforming traditional approaches
      across various tasks.

658   A    A Gamble Simulation
659
660   We design a card gamble and the rules are listed as follows:
661
662          • There are 𝑛 cards, each labeled with a number from 1 to 𝑛. The cards are shuffled and then
663
               the backsides are also labeled with numbers from 1 to 𝑛.
664          • In each round, the dealer shuffles the cards and then the player picks 𝑙 cards from the top of
665            the deck. The player sees the front sides of the selected cards and places a bet of 𝑟 chips,
666            where 𝑟 < 𝑛.
667          • The dealer rolls a dice to select one card from the 𝑙 selected cards. If the backside number
668            of this card is less than 𝑟, the player wins and receives 𝑛 chips as a prize, producing a net
669            profit of 𝑛 − 𝑟, otherwise the player loses, resulting in a profit of −𝑟.
670          • In the whole game, a player can only see the front side, but not the backside of all cards.
671
672   In our gambling model, the rules state that the more chips a player bets, the higher the likelihood of
673   winning, but correspondingly the prize of winning shrinks. We denote the winning event as 𝑦 = 1
674
      and the losing as 𝑦 = 0. The selected cards of the player are represented as x = [x1 , · · · , x𝑙 ], where
      each x𝑖 corresponds to an embedding of the 𝑖 th card. Consequently, the probability 𝑝(𝑦|x, 𝑟) is
675
      strictly monotonic with respect to the bet 𝑟. We train our generative cost model on a simulated
676
      dataset and evaluate the performance of our model 𝑝 𝜃 (𝑦|x, 𝑟) using the same strategy. To assess
677   the prize-winning capability of the models, we determine the optimal bet of model 𝑝 𝜃 is: 𝑟 ∗ =
678   argmax𝑟 {𝑝 𝜃 (𝑦|x, 𝑟)𝑛 − 𝑟.} The real profit generated by the choice 𝑟 ∗ is I(𝑟 ∗ > 𝑐)𝑛 − 𝑟 ∗ . To
679   maximize the total profit, a model has to learn the probability 𝑝 𝜃 (𝑦|x, 𝑟) accurately for every
680   combinations of x and 𝑟.
681
      The cost variable 𝑐 corresponds to a random choice of the unobservable values on the backsides
682
      of the picked cards x1 , · · · , x𝑙 , and we note these backside values as 𝑏 1 , · · · , 𝑏 𝑙 . As a result, the
683   model should infer the probabilities of the backside value of each x𝑖 . This inference is particularly
684   challenging, as the models can only deduce these probabilities from training samples consisting
685   of (x, 𝑟, 𝑦). In particular, the optimal solution for the generative cost model is to learn a precise
686   mapping from x to 𝑝(𝑐|x), which is given by:
687
                                                      I(𝑐 ∈ {𝑏 1 , · · · , 𝑏 𝑙 })

       One of the key advantages of our GCM model is its efficiency during the inference stage. For each
975    given x, the model can easily calculate 𝑝 𝜃 (𝑦|x, r𝑖 ) for multiple r𝑖 values. This efficiency arises
976    because the GCM model predicts the latent variables z and c based solely on x, allowing it to
977    subsequently predict 𝑦 using c and r𝑖 . As a result, we avoid the computation of inputting each pair
978    of (x, r𝑖 ) into a deep neural network as methods. We evaluated the inference efficiency for various
979    numbers of r while keeping x stable, and the results are presented in Table 12. As demonstrated,
980    the GCM becomes the fastest method when the number of r exceeds 64, validating its inference
981    efficiency in multi-revenue prediction scenarios. When the number of r reaches the extreme value
982    of 1024, GCM can save up to 72% time cost compared to the fastest baseline model.
