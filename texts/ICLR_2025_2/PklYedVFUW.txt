011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Skill-based reinforcement learning (Skill-based RL) is an efficient paradigm for
014            solving sparse-reward tasks by extracting skills from demonstration datasets and
015
               learning high-level policy which selects skills. Because each selected skill by
               high-level policy is executed for multiple consecutive timesteps, the high-level
016
               policy is essentially learned in a temporally abstract Markov decision process
017
               (TA-MDP) built on the skills, which shortens the task horizon and reduces the
018            exploration cost. However, these skills are usually sub-optimal because of the po-
019            tential low quality and low coverage of the datasets, which causes the sub-optimal
020            performance in the downstream task. It is intuitive to refine the skills. However,
021            it is a hard issue to refine the skills while ensuring performance improvement
022            and avoiding non-stationarity of transition dynamics caused by skill changes. To
023            address the dilemma of sub-optimality and ineffectiveness, we propose a unified
024            optimization objective for the entire hierarchical policy. We theoretically prove
025            that the unified optimization objective guarantees the performance improvement
026
               in TA-MDP, and that optimizing the performance in TA-MDP is equivalent to
               optimizing the performance lower bound of the entire hierarchical policy in orig-
027
               inal MDP. Furthermore, in order to overcome the phenomenon of skill space col-
028
               lapse, we propose the dynamical skill refinement (DSR) mechanism which names
029            our method. The experiment results empirically validate the effectiveness of our
030            method, and show the advantages over the state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Reinforcement learning has been widely used in complex application scenarios, such as robotics
036   manipulation Gupta et al. (2019); Chane-Sane et al. (2021); Kipf et al. (2019); Nair et al. (2020);
037   Pertsch et al. (2021); Shi et al. (2022); Huang et al. (2023); Pertsch et al. (2022). The tasks in
038   these domains are usually with sparse reward functions which give a positive feedback only when
039
      the task is completed. In order to reduce the extremely high exploration costs caused by sparse
      reward functions, prior works have proposed a variety of methods to incorporate prior knowledge
040
      into reinforcement learning Nair et al. (2020); Rajeswaran et al. (2017); Daoudi et al. (2023); Zhang
041
      et al. (2023); Wen et al. (2023); Guo et al. (2023); Pertsch et al. (2021; 2022). In these works,
042   skill-based reinforcement learning (Skill-based RL) has become an efficient approach to solve the
043   sparse-reward tasks Kipf et al. (2019); Pertsch et al. (2021; 2022). Skills are temporally extended
044   behaviors and usually manifest as the segments of consecutive actions in demonstration datasets.
045   These skills in the dataset are typically embedded into the latent space of the low-level policy which
046   serves as the action space of the high-level policy. The high-level policy selects the appropriate skills
047   according to the states, and lets each selected skill be executed for multiple consecutive timesteps
048   so as to shorten the task horizon and reduce the exploration cost.
049   These skills may be sub-optimal because of the potential low quality and low coverage of dataset Fu
050   et al. (2020), which causes the potential sub-optimal performance in downstream task. It is intuitive
051   to refine the extracted skills with online collected transitions while learning the high-level policy.
052   However, how to refine skills while achieving performance improvement is still an unresolved issue.
053   Furthermore, the change of skills will lead to the non-stationarity of the transition dynamics of the
      temporally abstract Markov decision process (TA-MDP), which we name temporal abstraction shift.


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Temporal abstraction shift hinders us from learning the high-level policy because the actual value of
055   any state-skill keeps changing, making it difficult for us to accurately estimate it.
056
057   Therefore, we usually have to face either the sub-optimal skills or the ineffective skill refinement,
058
      which constitutes a dilemma. Some prior methods assume that skills are nearly optimal and keep
      them fixed, but this assumption puts high requirements on datasets, which can hardly be met when
059
      the downstream task is different from all the tasks used to generate the datasets Kipf et al. (2019);
060
      Pertsch et al. (2021; 2022). ReSkill Rana et al. (2023) updates both the high-level policy and the
061   skills in an on-policy RL manner Schulman et al. (2015; 2017), which is an ingenious way to cir-
062   cumvent the temporal abstraction shift. However, in ReSkill, the high-level policy and the skills
063   are updated in the TA-MDP and the original MDP respectively. Inconsistent optimization objec-
064   tives can not theoretically guarantee the performance improvement. Skill-Critic Hao et al. (2024)
065   directly ignores the temporal abstraction shift and updates both the high-level policy and the skills
066   in the off-policy RL manner Haarnoja et al. (2018b). Skill-Critic achieves superior performance and
067   sample efficiency in some specific tasks. However, ignoring the temporal abstraction shift brings in
068   uncertainty, and Skill-Critic depends on SPiRL-based Pertsch et al. (2021) warm-up stage.
069   In order to address the dilemma, we propose an on-policy skill-based RL method named dynamical
070   skill refinement (DSR) which dynamically refines the skills under the optimization objective unified
071   with the high-level policy. We theoretically prove that this objective guarantees the performance im-
072   provement in TA-MDP. More importantly, we innovatively consider skill-based RL as the proxy of
073   the original RL task, and theoretically prove that optimizing the performance in TA-MDP is equiva-
074   lent to optimizing a lower bound of the performance in the original MDP. We simultaneously update
075
      high-level policy and skills in an on-policy RL manner, so as to circumvent the temporal abstraction
      shift. Furthermore, we point out that directly refining skills may lead to skill space collapse which
076
      brings in performance collapse. This is because all skills are embedded into the latent space of the
077
      same parametric low-level policy. Changing the behavior of one skill may cause the behaviors of
078   other skills to be changed as well. Therefore, we augment our method with the dynamical skill
079   refinement mechanism which ensures that the skills maintain their original behaviors before being
080   explored. We empirically validate the effectiveness of our method and its advantages over the state-
081   of-the-art (SOTA) methods in multiple sparse-reward tasks of the robotics manipulation domain, and
082   prove the necessity of dynamical skill refinement.
083
      We summarize the contributions of this paper as follows: (1) we propose a unified optimization
084   objective for both high-level policy and skills, and theoretically analyze its effectiveness, (2) we
085   devise an on-policy skill-based RL method and a dynamical skill refinement mechanism which
086   avoids skill space collapse, and (3) we empirically validate the effectiveness and superiority of our
087   method in multiple sparse-reward tasks.

091   2.1   H IERARCHICAL R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
092
093   In hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL), the policy stitches temporally extended behaviors
094   rather than primitive actions to be the behavior of solving tasks Pateria et al. (2021); Li et al. (2019;
095   2022); Kipf et al. (2019); Zhang & Whiteson (2019); Yang et al. (2023). The low-level policy is
096   usually conditioned on the latent space which serves as the action space of the high-level policy.
097   Both the high-level and the low-level policies can be learned from the experience obtained from the
098
      agent’s interacting with the environment Bacon et al. (2017); Haarnoja et al. (2018a); Levy et al.
      (2019). In practical applications, it is a mainstream approach to extract the low-level policy from
099
      the demonstration dataset and then learn to recompose the behavior modes of the low-level policy to
100
      solve the task Shankar et al. (2019); Krishnan et al. (2017); Kipf et al. (2019); Nachum et al. (2018).
101
102
      2.2   S KILL - BASED R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
103
104   Skill-based reinforcement learning (skill-based RL) extracts reusable behavior modes from the
105   demonstration dataset which are used to solve the downstream task Hausman et al. (2018); Pertsch
106   et al. (2021; 2022). These behavior modes are considered as skills and embedded into the latent
107   space, which is typically through a variational auto-encoder (VAE) Kingma & Welling (2013). Dur-
      ing online learning, the high-level policy learns to select the optimal skills to solve the downstream

108
      task. The high-level policy learns to choose temporally extended skills rather than single-timestep
109   actions, which shortens the task horizon and reduces the exploration cost. Prior works generally fo-
110   cus on extracting the skill prior from the demonstrations to improve the exploration efficiency Walke
111   et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2022); Nam et al. (2022); Pertsch et al. (2021; 2022). These works assume
112   that the skills can be approximately considered as optimal for the downstream task. However, this
113   assumption can hardly be met when the downstream task is different from the tasks used to generate
114   the demonstration dataset. It is intuitive and natural to refine the skills while learning the high-level
115   policy Rana et al. (2023); Hao et al. (2024), but this issue is still not well addressed.

139   3.1   TA-MDP
140
141   In skill-based RL, the high-level policy π h and the low-level policy π l whose latent space embeds
142
      the skills {z} constitute the entire hierarchical policy. The high-level policy selects the skills from
      the latent space Z which are the behavior modes of the low-level policy π l conditioned on the latent
143
      variables. Each skill manifests as a sequence of primitive actions {at , at+1 , ..., at+H−1 } over a fixed
144
      horizon H. Once a skill zt is selected from π h (·|st ) by the high-level policy, the action distributions
145   at the next H consecutive timesteps will be at+i ∼ π l (·|st+i , zt ), i ∈ [0, H − 1].
146
147   In reinforcement learning, the task is formulated in a Markov decision process (MDP) (S, A, p, r, γ)
148
      which consists of the state space S, the action space A, the transition dynamics p(s′ |s, a), the
      reward function r(s, a) and the discount factor γ. However, in skill-based RL, each skill z selected
149
      by the high-level policy π h is over the fixed horizon H, so π h is actually learning in a TA-MDP
150
      (S, A, pπl ,H , r̃, γ̃). The transition dynamics of TA-MDP pπl ,H (s′ |s, z) is the distribution over the
151   state st+H after H timesteps conditioned on the state st and the selected skill zt , p(st+H = s′ |st =
152                                                                 PH−1
      s, zt = z, π l ). The reward function of TA-MDP r̃(st , zt ) = i=0 rt+i is the sum of H consecutive
153   rewards obtained by executing skill zt from state st .

      3.2   E XTRACT S KILLS THROUGH VAE
156
157   In order to embed the skills into the latent space of low-level policy, it is common to sample state-
158   action segments from the demonstration dataset and encode them as latent variables through the
159   variational auto-encoder (VAE) Pertsch et al. (2021; 2022); Rana et al. (2023). The decoder condi-
160   tioned on state and latent variable will reconstruct action segments. The sum of the loss of recon-
161   structed action segment and the KL-divergence between the latent variable distribution and a prior
      distribution is the complete optimization objective. The trained decoder will serve as the low-level

176   H-timestep rewards in the TA-MDP is equivalent to optimizing a lower bound of the expected sum
177   of the discounted single-timestep rewards in the original MDP, which provides the effectiveness.

      5.2       DYNAMICAL S KILL R EFINEMENT M ECHANISM
325
326   We first explain why directly refining skills causes skill space collapse which manifests as perfor-
327   mance collapse. We then elaborate on the dynamical skill refinement mechanism.
328

329

      predicted by the low-level policy to form the practical action given by the skill. We assign a dynami-
357   cal weight to action increment, that is, when a skill has been sufficiently refined in a state, the action
358   increment for the state-skill will be given a high weight, otherwise, it should be given a low weight.
359   We name this measure dynamical skill refinement (DSR) mechanism.
360
      We use random network distillation (RND) Burda et al. (2019) to measure whether the behavior of
361
      a skill in a state has been sufficiently refined. It involves two randomly initialized neural networks,
362
      namely, fixed target network and variable predictor network. The target network takes a state-skill
363
      to an embedding f : S × Z → Rk . The predictor network fˆ : S × Z → Rk is trained to minimize
364
      the MSE loss ||fˆ((s, z); ξ) − f ((s, z))||2 with respect to its parameters ξfˆ. Every time we refine
365
      the behavior of skill z in state s, we optimize the prediction error of the predictor network on the
366
      state-skill (s, z). We expect that the prediction errors on those state-skills on which the predictor
367
      network has been trained for many times are obviously lower than the prediction errors on the novel
368   state-skills. We can map the prediction error on a state-skill to the weight of action increment. See
369   Appendix C for the complete algorithm incorporating the dynamical skill refinement.

523     7                       C ONCLUSION
524
525
        In this paper, we study the skill-based RL method of updating both high-level policy and skills. We
526     innovatively consider skill-based RL as the proxy task of RL, and theoretically prove that optimizing
527     the performance of the hierarchical policy in TA-MDP is equivalent to optimizing the performance
528     lower bound in original MDP. We devise the optimization objective of skills which is unified with
529     high-level policy and propose to update the entire hierarchical policy in an on-policy RL manner so
530     as to circumvent the temporal abstraction shift. We propose for the first time that direct skill refine-
531     ment leads to the skill space collapse phenomenon since all the skills are embedded into the latent
532     space of the same parametric policy. To address skill space collapse, we propose the dynamical skill
533     refinement mechanism which can be simply integrated into our algorithm. Dynamical skill refine-
534
        ment guarantees that the behavior of low-level policy on a given state-skill remains approximate to
        the extracted behavior before it is sufficiently refined, thus avoiding skill space collapse.

850   In the online learning stage, the high-level policy and the skills interact with the environment to
851   generate the trajectories which can be converted to the trajectories in the TA-MDP and divided into
852

856
857   During the interaction between the hierarchical policy and the environment, when a skill z predicts
858   an action in the current state s, the residual policy predicts the action increment of the skill in the
859   state. We convert the prediction error of the predictor network in this state-skill (s, z) into a weight in
860
      the interval [0, 1], assign this weight to the action increment, and add the weighted action increment
      to the predicted action to get the practical action. This conversion can be implemented by a scaled
861
      and shifted Sigmoid function.
