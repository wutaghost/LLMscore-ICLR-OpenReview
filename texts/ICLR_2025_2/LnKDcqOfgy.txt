009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The proliferation of large pre-trained neural networks has recently revived re-
012            search in both quantization of network weights (for faster inference), and in their
013            compression (to reduce file sizes). However, there has so far been little idea trans-
014            fer between the two lines of research. In this paper, we combine techniques from
015            quantization and compression to propose an efficient and highly effective post-
016            training compression method for large neural networks. Our method extends the
017            recently published quantization method OPTQ (Frantar et al., 2023) with a tun-
018
               able rate/distortion trade-off by introducing a cost per bit into OPTQ’s rounding
               operation. Crucially, we estimate the bit rate based on the predictive model used
019
               in the state-of-the-art neural network compression method NNCodec (Becking
020
               et al., 2023). In our experiments with several standard pre-trained networks from
021            the computer vision community, our method leads to significantly (up to 2.7x)
022            smaller file sizes than NNCodec at equal model performance, generally compress-
023            ing to less than half a bit per network weight and implicitly pruning insignificant
024            weights. Additionally, and in contrast to NNCodec, our method offers the same
025            opportunities for inference speed-ups as OPTQ. By proving that file size and in-
026            ference cost can be reduced simultaneously, we hope that our contribution shows a
027            path towards deploying large neural networks on end-user devices, alleviating pri-
028            vacy concerns, regulatory constraints, and dependency on large service providers.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Neural networks have achieved impressive performances in a large variety of tasks from different
      areas, from object recognition to language modeling. This performance usually comes at a price: in-
034
      creasing empirical evidence, summarized today under the term “neural scaling laws” (Kaplan et al.,
035
      2020), suggests that model size controls a fundamental bound on performance, and this insight has
036   recently driven a trend towards larger and larger neural networks. At the same time, neural networks
037   see adoption in more and more applications, creating the need for parameter sharing platforms such
038   as HuggingFace, where users can freely share parameters of their neural networks. Here, the large
039   sizes of neural networks directly translate to operating costs for server storage and network traffic.
040   Arguably even more importantly, the large file sizes of neural networks often makes it prohibitively
041   impractical to deploy them on end-user devices. As a result, it is today the norm that applications
042   relay any features that involve neural networks to a server, leading to increased latency and concerns
043   regarding privacy and regulatory constraints (Saravanan & Kouzani, 2023; Hohman et al., 2024).
044   With this development, there has been rising interest in the development of data compression tech-
045   niques for neural networks (Gholami et al., 2022; Marinó et al., 2023). Neural network compression
046   can help with two problems: improving the inference speed, allowing for faster and more energy
047   efficient neural networks (Chmiel et al., 2019), and decreasing the actual network size, reducing
048   memory bottlenecks and hardware costs caused by storage and transmission of the model parame-
049   ters. Most of the works we have surveyed focus on either one of these problems, but not both.
050   To improve the inference speed of neural networks, quantization (Jacob et al., 2017; Dettmers
051   et al., 2022) aims to represent the weights of a neural network in a lower bit precision, for example
052   4 bit integers, which can be processed much faster than normal 16 or 32 bit precision floating point
053   numbers on most GPUs. Pruning (LeCun et al., 1989; Blalock et al., 2020) tries to determine which
      of the weights in a neural network are the most important for the correct model output, and removes

      a certain number of non-relevant weights, which allows for the usage of fast kernels for sparse
055   matrix multiplication or even skipping computations completely when whole groups of neurons are
056   removed in the case of structured pruning (He & Xiao, 2024). Knowledge distillation (Hinton et al.,
057   2015; Gou et al., 2021) trains a small student network on the outputs of a larger teacher network,
058   aiming to replicate the behavior of the larger teacher model exactly in the smaller model.
059
      While these methods do reduce the required number or size of the parameters to store, their focus is
060
      not the size of the neural network in a compressed representation (as it would, e.g., be transmitted
061   over the internet or stored in an end-user application). An important step to achieve a data repre-
062   sentation of minimal size is entropy coding (Shannon, 1948; MacKay, 2003), where a probabilistic
063   model of the data source is built and then used to encode more probable symbols to shorter bit-
064   strings and less probable ones to longer bit-strings. Research on reducing the storage size of neural
065   networks (Choi et al., 2020; Becking et al., 2023) is more scarce, and some techniques, such as
066   quantizing to non-uniform grids using vector quantization (Baalen et al., 2024), prohibit inference
067   speed-ups, such as GPU kernels that can operate in low-bit integer arithmetic.
068   In this work, we propose a method that combines advantages from both quantization and entropy
069   coding, resulting in a practical and efficient algorithm. Our compression method
070
071         • achieves high compression strength (i.e., small compressed file sizes);
072         • is applicable to most neural networks without modifications;
073         • works in a post-training setting, i.e., no expensive re-training is required, and only a rela-
074           tively small calibration data set is needed to estimate Hessians;
075
            • allows for a smooth trade-off between compression strength and accuracy (and trying out
076           many points on this trade-off is very cheap as no new Hessians have to be estimated);
077
            • is compatible with existing methods for inference acceleration on GPUs through activation
078
              quantization, with a barely noticeable impact on model performance; and
079
080
            • has very high decoding speed and sufficient encoding speed for large neural networks.
081
      We term our method OPTQ-RD, as it generalizes the recent state-of-the-art quantization framework
082   OPTQ (Frantar et al., 2023) by introducing a rate-distortion trade-off into the optimization objec-
083   tive. To estimate bit rates in this trade-off, we use the entropy model used by the DeepCABAC
084   entropy coder (Wiedemann et al., 2020a), which is specialized to achieve high coding speeds and
085   a high compression performance for neural networks. However, our method is agnostic to the ex-
086   act entropy model used, which can be easily swapped out to fit the needs of a practitioner, who
087   for example might opt to use a simpler model to achieve even higher coding speeds on heavily
088   resource-constrained devices. We empirically verify the effectiveness of our algorithm on various
089   neural network architectures from the computer vision community.

091   2   R ELATED W ORK
092
093   We make an effort to distinguish between methods focused on inference speed (quantization, prun-
094   ing, knowledge distillation) and methods focused on storage size (entropy based methods, parameter
095   sharing), although the techniques are often combined in some form.
096   Methods focused on inference speed can be categorized into methods that require (re-)training the
097   neural network (such as knowledge distillation or quantization aware training (Baskin et al., 2021))
098   and post-training methods (such as post-training quantization and pruning), although the latter some-
099   times involve fine-tuning, a form of partial retraining. We focus on the post-training setting here,
100   under which our method also falls under. SynFlow (Tanaka et al., 2020) prevents layer collapse in
101   network pruning by using a data-independent score based on synaptic saliency. SparseML (Kurtz
102   et al., 2020; Singh & Alistarh, 2020) provides an easy-to-use tool for inference speed-up on ar-
103   bitrary networks using fisher information based pruning and structured sparsification. PENNI (Li
104
      et al., 2020) decomposes the filters of a convolutional neural network into a set of shared basis
      kernels, which are learned during a retraining process with a sparsity constraint.
105
106   On the frontier of storage-focused methods, efforts have been made to build very general compres-
107   sion algorithms and pipelines for neural networks. The Neural Network Compression and Represen-
      tation Standard (Kirchhoffer et al., 2022) defines a compression pipeline encompassing parameter

108
      reduction (pruning, sparsification, parameter sharing, et cetera), quantization and entropy coding.
109   Additionally, the standard defines interoperability with well-known neural network exchange for-
110   mats such as ONNX (Bai et al., 2019). Universal Neural Network Compression (Choi et al., 2020)
111   uses universal quantization (Ziv, 1985), i.e., it randomly perturbs the weights of a neural network be-
112   fore applying vector quantization, allowing their compression scheme to work independently of the

114   together with a sparsification process, resulting in parameter representations that allow for high
115   compression ratios and while keeping the inference speed-ups from general pruning techniques.
116   Additionally, a large body of work has recently appeared on the compression of very large language
117   models (Dettmers et al., 2022; Chee et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2024), but the proposed
118   methods address issues specific to LLMs (e.g., activation outliers (Xiao et al., 2023; Lin et al.,
119   2024)). By contrast, our proposed method is agnostic to the network architecture.

      3.2   E NTROPY C ODING : D EEP CABAC
163
164   In Wiedemann et al. (2020a), the authors propose the compression method DeepCABAC, which is
165   specifically designed to deal well with the common weight distributions of neural networks, which
166   the authors found to be mostly symmetric, centered around zero and with quickly vanishing tails.
167   The lossless entropy coder of DeepCABAC is based on the Context-based Adaptive Binary Arith-
168   metic Coder (CABAC), used in the video codecs H.264/AVC (Marpe et al., 2003) and H.265/HEVC
169   (Sze et al., 2014). CABAC contains a context model to adapt the coder on-the-fly to the statistics of
170
      the given data, making it universally usable. Additionally, stemming from its use in video coding,
      CABAC has a high throughput and allows very efficient encoding and decoding, making it suitable
171
      for encoding the large parameter sets from neural networks. In the rest of the paper, we use the term
172
      DeepCABAC to refer specifically to this entropy-coder.
173
174   In DeepCABAC, a quantized weight ŵ ∈ Z is binarized as follows: first, a series of flag bits are set,
175   called sigFlag (whether the weight is 0), signFlag (whether the weight is positive) and absGr(n)Flag
176
      (whether the absolute value of the weight is ≥ n ∈ {1, 2, 4, 8, . . . N }). If this this does not uniquely
      identify the weight, the remainder ŵ − N is transmitted directly. Additionally, if the absolute value
177
      is larger than N , the remainder is transmitted using an Exponential-Golomb code. To increase the
178
      compression strength of the encoding scheme, a context model is used to predict the value of the
179   flag bits. The context model is initialized to 0.5, and every time it encounters a value of the flag
180   bit, updates its probability model by a small step accordingly (increasing for 1, decreasing for 0).
181   The context model is used as the probability model of an arithmetic coder (Pasco, 1976; Rissanen
182   & Langdon, 1979; MacKay, 2003) and can thus shorten the expected length of the bit string even
183   further, encoding probable flag bits with less than 1 bit on average. Since the context model is
184   state-based and only depends on all previously seen values, it does not have to be transmitted to the
185   decoder side as the decoder can reconstruct it step by step while decoding.

417   5.3             D ETERMINING THE NECESSARY CALIBRATION SET SIZE
418
419   As we use a data-driven method to estimate the parameter sensitivity, we are naturally interested
420
      in how many samples are actually needed to achieve good results. In Figure 3, we have varied the
      amounts of samples used to estimate the Hessian of VGG16. We notice a saturation at around 40,000
421
      samples. As ImageNet consists of 1,281,167 images, this corresponds to seeing a mere 3.12% of
422
      the training data just once, which is much cheaper than methods that rely on performing multiple
423   full iterations over the training dataset during training or fine-tuning. Additionally, we also have to
424   perform this iteration only once for the whole rate-distortion curve, allowing us to cheaply obtain
425   versions of the network tuned for different performance levels (using different values for λ).
426

475    6                     C ONCLUSION AND FURTHER WORK
476
477    In this paper, we proposed OPTQ-RD, a compression method that creates highly compressible quan-
478    tized networks while keeping a simple and flexible uniform grid, suitable for accelerated inference.
479    There are still some promising research directions not fully mapped out yet, as our method has mul-
480    tiple building blocks that can be experimented on. For example, one might explore using differently
481    spaced grids to improve the compression performance even further (at the price of potentially slow-
482
       ing down inference), or use different entropy models than DeepCABAC. One might also try different
       methods of determining the compression strength λ for each layer, for example by leveraging global
483
       second order information such as the fisher information content of the weights.
484
485    A harder problem would be to investigate rate-constrained compression for (very) large language
       models, as these are known to be much more difficult to compress down to the very low bit-rates


                                                                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      we have reported in our results. While the computer vision networks tested in our experiments can
487   consistently be compressed to 0.5 bits-per-weight or lower with little performance drop, for LLMs,
488   sparsification of more than 50% of the weights has only recently been achieved (Frantar & Alistarh,
489   2023) and even the sub 1-bit barrier has just been broken this year (Dong et al., 2024). These
490   differences may indicate that language models may indeed encode more information per weight
491   than (convolutional) computer vision models.
492
      Additionally, we hope that this work inspires other research that combines more traditional ideas
493   from general compression with modern techniques from the model compression community. For
494   example, our observation that sparsification can result from an entropy constraint could provide
495   some interesting avenue on works that combine quantization with pruning into a single, unified
496   framework.
