009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            The integration of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) in Reinforcement Learning (RL)
012            systems has led to remarkable progress in solving complex tasks but also intro-
013            duced challenges like primacy bias and dead neurons. Primacy bias skews learning
014            towards early experiences, while dead neurons diminish the network’s capacity
015            to acquire new knowledge. Traditional reset mechanisms aimed at addressing
016
               these issues often involve maintaining large replay buffers to train new networks
               or selectively resetting subsets of neurons. However, These approaches either
017
               incur substantial computational costs or fail to effectively reset the entire network,
018
               resulting in underutilization of network plasticity and reduced learning efficiency.
019            In this work, we introduce the novel concept of neuron regeneration, which com-
020            bines reset mechanisms with knowledge recovery techniques. We also propose
021            a new framework called Sustainable Backup Propagation (SBP) that effectively
022            maintains plasticity in neural networks through this neuron regeneration process.
023            The SBP framework achieves whole network neuron regeneration through two key
024            procedures: cycle reset and inner distillation. Cycle reset involves a scheduled
025            renewal of neurons, while inner distillation functions as a knowledge recovery
026            mechanism at the neuron level. To validate our framework, we integrate SBP with
027
               Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) and propose a novel distillation function for
               inner distillation. This integration results in Plastic PPO (P3O), a new algorithm
028
               that enables efficient cyclic regeneration of all neurons in the actor network. This
029
               approach facilitates neuron regeneration while maintaining policy plasticity and
030            sample efficiency. Extensive experiments demonstrate that, with proper neuron
031            regeneration methods, the SBP framework can effectively maintain plasticity and
032            improve sample efficiency in reinforcement learning tasks.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Deep reinforcement learning has advanced significantly through the integration of deep neural
038   networks, resulting in notable achievements across various domains(Singh et al., 2022; Arulkumaran
039
      et al., 2017; Yu et al., 2021). Despite these advancements, a critical issue that has emerged is the loss
      of plasticity, as detailed in (Lyle et al., 2023; Abbas et al., 2023). This refers to the diminishing ability
040
      of a network to learn and adapt over time. As network neurons become saturated, they “become
041
      full”, losing the capacity to incorporate new information effectively. This reduction in plasticity
042   primarily affects the neurons in the network, leading to decreased effectiveness and eventually causing
043   neurons to become dead(Lu et al., 2019; Shin & Karniadakis, 2020) or dormant(Sokar et al., 2023).
044   Additionally, the problem of overfitting in deep learning, known as primacy bias (Nikishin et al.,
045   2022), further causes this loss of plasticity. Consequently, there is an urgent imperative to develop
046   mechanisms for the repair or revitalization of neurons affected by primacy bias or those that have
047   lapsed into dormancy, with the objective of reawakening their “hunger” for novel information.
048   Reset mechanisms have been proven to be effective measures for addressing the loss of plasticity in
049   neural networks. However, existing reset approaches have demonstrated various limitations. Early
050   studies (Nikishin et al., 2022; D’Oro et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2024) proposed resetting either the
051   final layer or all neurons to revitalize learning capabilities. However, these methods often led to
052   a performance-resource trade-off, requiring additional training to recover lost performance. More
053   targeted approaches, such as CBP (Dohare et al., 2021) and ReDo (Sokar et al., 2023), focused
      on selectively resetting non-contributing neurons. While this strategy reduced information loss,

      Reinforcement learning is formalized as a Markov decision process (MDP) (Puterman, 2014). An
116   MDP consists of a tuple ⟨S, A, R, P, γ⟩, where S denotes the set of states, A the set of actions,
117   R : X × S → R a reward function, P : S × A → P ( a possibly stochastic transition probability
118   function, γ ∈ [0, 1) the discount factor. In reinforcement learning, The goal is to seek an optimal
119   policy π ∗ : S → P (A) which maximizes the expected accumulated returns with discounted.
120
      In on-policy reinforcement learning, the Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) algorithm (Schulman
121
      et al., 2017) is utilized to update policies during interaction with the environment. The core objective
122   function of PPO, denoted as Lclip (θ), includes a clipping operation that sets gradients to zero
123   when the probability ratio rt (θ) falls outside [1 − ϵ, 1 + ϵ]. This prevents the policy from learning
124   from advantages that would push it further outside the trust region, thereby enforcing trust region
125   constraints in a computationally efficient manner. This operation ensures that the policy does not
126   deviate excessively from the previous policy. The objective function is expressed as:

136   2.2   P LASTICITY IN R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
137
138   Plasticity loss in neural networks, the gradual decline in learning capacity over time, poses a critical
139   challenge in deep learning by limiting adaptation to new tasks. This issue has prompted extensive
140   research(Lyle et al., 2023; Abbas et al., 2023; Nikishin et al., 2022; Nauman et al., 2024; Dohare
141
      et al., 2024) into methods for preserving neural network plasticity, with two main categories of
      approaches emerging to address this problem. The first involves various training techniques such as
142
      regularization, adjustments in activation functions, weight decay, and normalization strategies (Kumar
143
      et al., 2023; Delfosse et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2024; Lyle et al., 2024). These methods have proven
144   somewhat effective at alleviating the problem by reducing overfitting and preventing parameters from
145   becoming overly large, thereby delaying the onset of plasticity loss.
146
147
      The second category of approaches involves resetting the network (Nikishin et al., 2022; Schwarzer
      et al., 2023; Nikishin et al., 2024) to recover the plasticity. This reset-based methodology addresses
148
      both neuron dormancy and primacy bias. Typically implemented by reinitializing weights of specific
149
      layers or the entire network, resets have been shown to effectively scale replay ratios, contributing
150   to performance improvements (Kim et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023). However, while resets can revive
151   learning capabilities, they might lead to temporary performance degradation and require additional
152   training to restore previously learned information. To mitigate these drawbacks, methods like CBP
153   (Dohare et al., 2021) and ReDo (Sokar et al., 2023) selectively reinitialize neurons deemed less useful
154   based on certain metrics, minimizing the impact on overall performance. This approach highlights
155   the delicate balance between recovering plasticity and maintaining network efficiency. However,
156   there remains a need for more sophisticated methods that can effectively regenerate neurons while
157   preserving learned knowledge, which is the focus of our proposed approach.
158   The importance of addressing plasticity loss is further underscored by recent studies. These have
159   demonstrated that enhancing neural plasticity can lead to significant improvements in sample effi-
160   ciency (D’Oro et al., 2022; Schwarzer et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023; Nauman et al.,
161   2024), a particularly attractive property for reinforcement learning tasks where data efficiency is
      crucial.

188   3      N EURON R EGENERATION
189
190   Maximizing the utilization of a neural network’s capacity is a primary goal in training, yet several
191   challenges impede this objective. Plasticity in neural networks, often viewed as a consumable
192   resource, diminishes as the network learns and integrates knowledge. This depletion can result from
193   suboptimal configurations such as inappropriate activation functions (Abbas et al., 2023), poor data
194
      quality (Lee et al., 2024), or inherent limitations of backpropagation (Dohare et al., 2024), leading to
      biased or irrelevant information acquisition and suboptimal network performance.
195
196   While neuron reset techniques can restore plasticity, they risk performance degradation if not carefully
197   implemented(Nauman et al., 2024). Research by D’Oro et al. (2022) suggests that resetting all
198   neurons, including those affected by primacy bias, is necessary to maximize network capacity.
199   Building upon the idea of neuron reset while addressing its limitations, we introduce a new concept:
200
      neuron regeneration.
201
            Definition 3.1: Neuron Regeneration
202
203         Given a neural network parameterized by θ, let N R denote a regeneration operation that
204         resets arbitrary neurons to their initial plastic states, resulting in a new parameter set θ′ .
205         Neuron regeneration maintains two key properties:
206         1. Neuron-level Plasticity Recovery: Plasticity(N R(θ′ )) > Plasticity(θ)
207         2. Performance Guarantee: P (N R(θ′ )) ≥ P (θ)
208         where Plasticity denotes a measure of the network’s adaptability and P represents the net-
209         work’s performance.
210
211   Neuron regeneration aims to achieve sustainable plasticity without performance degradation, enabling
212   long-term learning and exploration of the network’s potential. This approach addresses the limitations
213   of traditional training methods and reset techniques, potentially maximizing the utilization of the
214   entire network’s capacity. By implementing neuron regeneration, we propose a framework for
215   maintaining learning ability and efficiency, pushing the boundaries of neural network capabilities
      beyond current limitations.

233   4     M ETHODOLOGY
234
235   To maximize the utilization of neural network capabilities, we propose the Sustainable Backprop-
236   agation (SBP) method. SBP achieves sustainable plasticity through a novel neuron regeneration
237
      mechanism, which combines two key components: Cycle Reset and Inner Distillation.
238
239   4.1   S USTAINABLE BACKPROPAGATION
240
      Our neuron regeneration mechanism consists of two complementary processes: Reset and Distillation.
241
      The Reset process discards outdated knowledge and rejuvenates inactive neurons by resetting their
242
      parameters to initial states. Complementing this, the Distillation process preserves valuable knowl-
243   edge by transferring information from the pre-reset state to the post-reset network. This combination
244   allows neurons to restore plasticity while retaining crucial learned information.
245
246
      To implement network-wide neuron regeneration, we introduce the Cycle Reset mechanism, governed
      by two key parameters: Reset frequency F and Reset ratio p. Every F training steps, p% of neurons in
247
      each layer undergo reset and regeneration. This cyclical process, illustrated in Figure 2, continuously
248
      refreshes neurons across all layers throughout training.
249
250   Inner Distillation completes the neuron regeneration process initiated by Reset. As shown in Figure 2,
251   before resetting, the current policy πθ is copied to a temporary policy πtem . Selected neurons then
252
      undergo reset, clearing outdated information. Subsequently, knowledge from πtem is selectively
      distilled back to the reset policy πθ′ at the neuron level, with reset neurons temporarily frozen
253
      to preserve their renewed plasticity. This neuron-specific distillation process complements the
254
      Reset operation, jointly achieving neuron regeneration. The integration of Cycle Reset and Inner
255   Distillation results in a cyclic neuron regeneration process. This approach ensures that all neurons in
256   the network undergo periodic regeneration, maintaining network-wide plasticity while preserving
257   valuable knowledge. Through this cyclic neuron regeneration, we achieve sustainable plasticity,
258   enabling the neural network to continually adapt to new information without compromising its learned
259   capabilities. This process maximizes the utilization of the neural network’s capacity, effectively
260   balancing ongoing learning with performance preservation.
261   The SBP algorithm, which incorporates reset operations and distillation, is outlined in Algorithm 1.

264

266   address this issue, we propose integrating the SBP framework into PPO, aiming to enhance its
267   capabilities. The Inner Distillation process presents a complex scenario where neurons may contain
268   either valuable knowledge or irrelevant information. To maximize generalization and plasticity, we
269   must carefully control this distillation process. In the context of reinforcement learning, we leverage

744   A.3     A LGORITHM
745
746   In this section, we present two novel algorithms designed to achieve sustainable plasticity in neural
747   networks: Sustainable Backup Propagation (SBP) and Plastic Proximal Policy Optimization (P3O).
748
      These algorithms address the challenge of maintaining neural network plasticity over extended
      periods.
749
750
      A.3.1    S USTAINABLE BACKUP P ROPAGATION (SBP)
751
752   SBP is a general framework designed to maintain the plasticity of neural networks over extended
753   periods. It incorporates a neuron regeneration mechanism into standard backpropagation, creating a
754   sustainable learning process. The key components of SBP are:
755
              • Cycle Reset: Periodically reinitializes a portion of neurons to prevent overspecialization.


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
              • Inner Distillation: Utilizes a temporary model as a teacher network to recover essential
757             knowledge after neuron reset.
758
759   This approach allows neural networks to maintain plasticity indefinitely, continually adapting to new
760   information without catastrophic forgetting.
761   The SBP algorithm implements the cycle reset and inner distillation mechanisms. The reset rate γ
762   determines the proportion of neurons reset in each cycle, while the distillation process ensures that
763   essential knowledge is retained after each reset.
764
765   A.3.2    P LASTIC P ROXIMAL P OLICY O PTIMIZATION (P3O)
766
767
      P3O is a concrete implementation of the SBP framework within the context of reinforcement learning,
      specifically tailored for the Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) algorithm. A specialized distillation
768
      function, DKL, is designed for PPO, ensuring effective knowledge transfer in policy space. P3O
769
      demonstrates how the general SBP framework can be applied to specific machine learning paradigms,
770   in this case, enabling sustainable plasticity in reinforcement learning policies.
771
772   Both algorithms represent a significant step towards creating AI systems that can learn continuously
773
      and adapt to changing environments without losing previously acquired knowledge. They offer a
      promising approach to overcoming the limitations of traditional neural network training methods,
774
      particularly in scenarios requiring long-term learning and adaptation.
775
