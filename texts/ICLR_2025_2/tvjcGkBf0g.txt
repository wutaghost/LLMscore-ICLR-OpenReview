009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in 3D deep learning have garnered significant attention,
012            given their superior performance in fields like AR/VR, autonomous driving, and
013            robotics. However, as the models and point cloud data continues to scale up,
014            managing computational and memory demands becomes a critical challenge, par-
015            ticularly for real-world applications with strict latency and energy requirements.
016
               Previous methods have primarily focused on reducing computational costs and
               memory usage by addressing spatial redundancy, i.e., filtering out irrelevant points
017
               or voxels. In contrast, this work presents a novel post-training weight pruning
018
               technique tailored specifically for 3D object detection. Our approach stands out
019            in two key ways: (1) it operates independently from existing point cloud sparsi-
020            fication methods, targeting redundant parameters in pre-trained models that mini-
021            mally affect both spatial accuracy and detection confidence (collectively referred
022            to as ŌĆØdetection distortionŌĆØ), and (2) it provides a flexible, plug-and-play frame-
023            work compatible with other sparsity schemes including spatial sparsity and with
024            any 3D detection model. Our method reduces detection distortion by employing
025            a second-order Taylor approximation to identify layer-wise sparsity, allowing for
026            a substantial reduction in model complexity without sacrificing detection accu-
027
               racy. To efficiently manage the necessary second-order information, we devised
               a lightweight algorithm to gather Hessian information, followed by dynamic pro-
028
               gramming to optimize layer-wise sparsity allocation. Extensive experiments on
029
               the KITTI, nuScenes, and ONCE datasets validate the effectiveness of our ap-
030            proach, where we not only preserve detection performance but also notice en-
031            hancement while significantly reducing computational overhead. Noticeably, we
032            achieve FLOPs reductions for Centerpoint model of as much as 3.89├Ś and 3.01├Ś
033            on ONCE and nuScenes datasets respectively, without noticeable loss in mean
034            Average Precision (mAP), and at most 1.65├Ś reduction losslessly for PVRCNN
035            model on the ONCE dataset, thus pushing the boundaries of state-of-the-art per-
036            formance.

      data with high computation and memory demand to make them more feasible in real applications
      becomes an urgent task.

078   sub-optimal on maintaining accuracy compared to typical magnitude-based and Taylor-based rank-

082   accuracy. He et al. (2022) addressed this problem by employing a greedy search on single-layer rate-

084
      real data collections, undermining the efficiency.
085
086   In this paper, we propose a generalized weight pruning framework for 3D object detection models.
087   Inspired by classic rate-distortion theory, our approach minimizes the detection distortion of 3D
088   detection models, including the detection locality and confidence, under the constraint of computa-
089
      tion complexity (FLOPs). The pruning of weights is performed in a layer-wise manner where the
      pruning ratio of each layer is decided according to a Hessian-based rate-distortion score, which can
090
      be calculated efficiently by pre-computed gradient values. We then develop an ultra-fast dynamic
091
      programming algorithm with polynomial time complexity to find the globally optimal pruning ratio
092   of each layer. Note that our proposed weight pruning and the previously proposed input pruning
093   approaches can compensate with each other. By incorporating both weight and input pruning, we
094   expect to obtain a maximal compression ratio. We compare with previous arts and perform extensive
095   experiments on various 3D detection models to demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach. As
096   shown in Fig. 1, we achieve remarkable speedups with negligible performance drops or even gains
097   on various datasets. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that systematically proposes a
098   weight pruning approach for 3D detection models in a distortion-minimized manner. We summarize
099   the main contribution of our paper as follows:

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
114
115
      Typically, neural networks for 3D point clouds primarily employ either Point-based Qi et al. (2017)
116   or Voxel-based approaches. Point-based methods directly process the raw 3D point cloud data using
117   neural networks, whereas Voxel-based techniques first voxelize the raw data and then operate on the
118   voxels rather than the original data. Numerous works Rukhovich et al. (2022); Yan et al. (2018); Shi
119   et al. (2020); Deng et al. (2021); Yin et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2023b) took voxel-based approach
120   for 3D object detection task.
121
122   2.1   S PATIAL S PARSITY
123
124   Sparse 3D Convolution. Sparse 3D Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are designed to ef-
125
      ficiently process sparse point cloud data, where a significant portion are background points with
      zero values. Therefore computing convolution on such data can be done by skipping empty po-
126
      sitions in 3D space. Engelcke et al. (2017); Riegler et al. (2017) built hierarchical structures for
127
      efficient point cloud data representation. Regular Sparse Convolution (RSC) Graham (2014) and
128   Submanifold Sparse Convolution (SSC) Graham & Van der Maaten (2017) were proposed to miti-
129   gate the increase of active sites in order to maintain spatial sparsity across Sparse Conv layers. Some
130   libraries Tang et al. (2022); Yan et al. (2018) have been developed to support the Sparse 3D convo-
131   lution for fast inference. Xu et al. (2020) applied different filters based on the input imageŌĆÖs spatial
132   location, addressing the inefficiencies of using standard convolutions on LiDAR data with spatially
133   varying distributions.
134   Spatial Redundancy Reduction. It is easy to observe that natural redundancy in points/voxels still
135   exist even in active regions of 3D data. Thereby, this approach aims to further identify redundant
136   spatial data points to reduce computational complexity. Graham (2014) was an early work pro-
137   posed that output points should be omitted when corresponding input points are absent, ensuring
138   computations are only performed for areas with relevant input data. Guo et al. (2022) simplified
139   set abstraction procedure for point-based 3D action recognition models to identify important points
140   and frames. Chen et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2022a) proposed to engage feature sparsity by predict-
141   ing importance maps to remove redundant regions, achieving computation and memory reductions.
142
      Multi Li et al. (2023) proposed a focal loss to predict voxel importance combined with voxel dis-
      tillation. Ada3D Zhao et al. (2023) further leveraged the spatial redundancy in 2D BEV Backbone
143
      by proposing Sparsity Preserving BatchNorm to perform 3D-to-BEV feature transform. Liu et al.
144
      (2022b); Chen et al. (2023a) exploited the fact that the differences (residuals) between consecutive
145   3D frames are typically sparse.
146
147
      2.2   S PARSITY IN M ODEL W EIGHTS
148
149   Apart from exploiting the intuitive spatial redundancy, much less attempts has been done to leverage
150   weight redundancy in 3D networks. Zhao et al. (2021) used a generator to propose pruning strategies
151   and an evaluator that employs Bayesian optimization to select out pruning strategies for 3D object
152   detection. They configured unique pruning schemes and rates for each layer. Not All NeighboursHe
153   et al. (2022) identified kernel neurons that are least frequently attended to compute output features
154   in 3D segmentation models. They also performed a layerwise sparsity allocation by greedy search
155
      on two-layer grouping strategy. CP3 Huang et al. (2023) leveraged structured pruning (channel-wise
      pruning) for point-based 3D networks. None of existing weight pruning approaches is established
156
      on minimizing the impact of pruning on the detection performance as we proposed in this paper.

362   Datasets and Models. We evaluate three 3D object detection tasks to prove the effectiveness of
363
      our proposed method, namely KITTI, nuScenes and ONCE. KITTI dataset includes 3712 training
      examples, 3769 validation examples and 7518 test examples. Detection targets are categorized into
364
      three classes: Car, Pedestrian, and Cyclist, with ground truth bounding boxes divided into ŌĆ£EasyŌĆØ,
365
      ŌĆ£ModerateŌĆØ, and ŌĆ£HardŌĆØ difficulty levels. Evaluation of detection performance employs average
366   precision (AP) for each category, using an IoU threshold of 0.7 for cars and 0.5 for pedestrians and
367   cyclists. The nuScenes dataset is a comprehensive autonomous driving dataset containing 1,000
368   driving sequences with different modalities including LIDAR and cameras. We follow the default
369   split, where train split includes 700 training scenes and val set has 150 scenes. The ONCE dataset
370   is a large-scale LiDAR-based collection for autonomous driving, featuring 1 million scenes with
371   16k fully annotated for 3D object detection, utilizing mAP for performance evaluation. We perform
372   post-train pruning followed by one round of finetuning to fully recover the performance. We provide
373   implementation details in the Appendix A.2.1.

      6      C ONCLUSIONS
474
475   We have presented a weight pruning scheme for voxel-based 3D object detection models orthogonal
476   to prevailing spatial redundancy-based approaches. The pruning scheme is based on second-order
477   Taylor approximation on the detection distortion, which is able to minimize detection locality and
478   confidence degradation on pruned model. The scheme is extremely lightweight, with polynomial
479
      complexity for Hessian information acquisition and linear complexity for layerwise sparsity search.
      We show the superiority of the novel scheme on various 3D detection benchmarks over state-of-
480
      the-art approaches that exploits only spatial redundancy. In the future, we aim to develop a unified
481
      pruning scheme that leverages both weight and spatial redundancies for optimal results.

740   A.2.4   L AYER - WISE SPARSITY ALLOCATION RESULTS .
741
742
      Fig. 5 shows the detailed allocation results of the proposed DM3D. In Fig. 5, we display the layer-
      wise sparsity levels optimized by DM3D under different FLOPs constraint levels. We analyze the
743
      behavior on three different networks. Since our method leverages weight redundancies, we are able
744
      to optimize the layer-wise sparsity of 3D and 2D backbones together and automatically decide the
745   sparsity allocations. We observe that on PVRCNN, our scheme results in more sparsity in layers
746   in 2D backbone than in 3D part, while on SECOND, it is the opposite case where more weights in
747   2D backbone is preserved. This implies that the expressiveness of PVRCNN is mostly coming from
748   3D feature extraction than SECOND. Earlier layers in 2D backbone survive from most pruning
749   cases regardless of the networks, probably because these layers are essential to smoothly transfer
750   information from 3D domain to 2D. As FLOPs target decreases, the sparsity distribution remains
751   roughly the same, where most sensitive layers regarding to the detection distortion remain in less
752   sparsity rates.
