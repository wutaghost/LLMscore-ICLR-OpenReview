009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Direct Alignment Algorithms (DAAs), such as Direct Preference Optimisation
012            (DPO) and Identity Preference Optimisation (IPO), have emerged as alternatives
013            to online Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) algorithms
014            such as Proximal Policy Optimisation (PPO) for aligning language models to hu-
015            man preferences, without the need for explicit reward modelling. These methods
016
               generally aim to increase the likelihood of generating better (preferred) comple-
               tions while discouraging worse (non-preferred) ones, while staying close to the
017
               original model’s behaviour. In this work, we explore the relationship between
018
               completion likelihood and model performance in state-of-the-art DAAs, and iden-
019            tify a critical issue of likelihood over-optimisation. Contrary to expectations, we
020            find that higher likelihood of better completions and larger margins between better
021            and worse completion likelihoods do not necessarily lead to better performance,
022            and may even degrade it. Our analysis reveals that while higher likelihood cor-
023            relates with better memorisation of factual knowledge patterns, a slightly lower
024            completion likelihood tends to improve output diversity, thus leading to better
025            generalisation to unseen scenarios. Moreover, we identify two key indicators that
026            signal when over-optimised output diversity begins to harm performance: De-
027
               creasing Entropy over Top-k Tokens and Diminishing Top-k Probability Mass.
               Our experimental results validate that these indicators are reliable signs of de-
028
               clining performance under different regularisation schemes, helping prevent over-
029
               optimisation and improve alignment with human preferences.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023;
035   Roziere et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024; Land & Bartolo, 2024) have significantly expanded their
036   capabilities, enabling applications such as code generation, tool use, and interactive communication.
037   As LLMs become increasingly powerful, the challenge of aligning them with human preferences has
038   grown in importance. Direct Alignment Algorithms (DAAs), such as Direct Preference Optimisation
039
      (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) and Identity Preference Optimisation (IPO) (Azar et al., 2024), have
      emerged as alternatives to Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ziegler et al.,
040
      2019; Bai et al., 2022) for training LMs on human preference data. These methods aim to bypass
041
      the traditional RLHF pipeline by directly optimising the policy without explicit reward modelling.
042
043   DAAs are designed to increase the likelihood of better completions while reducing the likelihood of
044   worse ones, all while staying close to the original model’s behaviour. However, a known issue with
045
      standard DAAs is that they may decrease the likelihood of better completions as long as the relative
      probability between better and worse completions increases (Rafailov et al., 2023; Pal et al., 2024).
046
      Recent research has sought to address this by focusing on maintaining a high likelihood for better
047
      completions (Pal et al., 2024). For example, several works (Pang et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024),
048   including L LAMA -3.1 (Dubey et al., 2024) and N VIDIA N EMOTRON (Adler et al., 2024), introduce
049   a scaled negative log-likelihood (NLL) loss on better completions, aiming to stabilise DAA training
050   by preserving the desired formatting and preventing a drop in log probability for better completions.
051   Despite these efforts, key research questions remain: Is it truly necessary to maintain a higher
052   likelihood of better completions, and aim for a larger likelihood margin between better and worse
053   completions? And if not, How can we strike a balance for completion likelihood to maximise model
      performance in terms of alignment with human preferences?

070   In this work, we first explore the relationship between completion log-likelihood and model per-
071
      formance in state-of-the-art DAAs (§3). Specifically, we find that neither a higher likelihood of
      preferred completions nor larger margins between better and worse completion likelihoods neces-
072
      sarily lead to better performance (measured by win probability) and may even degrade it (§4.2), as
073
      shown in Figure 1. Furthermore, our experiments demonstrate that optimising both factors simulta-
074   neously also does not guarantee improvement. Our results reveal that while a higher likelihood of
075   better completion generally has better memorisation of factual knowledge patterns, an excessively
076   high likelihood can result in over-optimisation. In contrast, slightly lower completion likelihood
077   tends to improve output diversity, thus leading to better generalisation to unseen scenarios (§4.3).
078
      While avoiding an overly high completion likelihood tends to improve model diversity and gener-
079
      alisation, it is crucial to strike a balance between diversity and maintaining a high likelihood for
080   desired outputs preferred by humans. To this end, our study outlines two key indicators that signal
081   when overly generating diverse outputs begins to negatively impact model performance (§4.4): (1)
082   Decreasing Entropy over Top-k Tokens1 : As the likelihood of better completions decreases during
083   training, an increasing entropy suggests that tokens within better completions still have higher prob-
084   abilities relative to other tokens in the Top-k, though the gap is narrowing. However, a decreasing
085   entropy over the Top-k tokens is a warning sign that the model is assigning disproportionately low
086   probabilities to tokens within better completions, allowing other tokens to rise in probability, which
087   may lead to outputs that are not aligned with human preferences. Notably, a reversed entropy trend is
088
      a particularly strong indicator of over-optimised diversity; and (2) Diminishing Top-k Token Prob-
      ability Mass: This occurs when the probability mass concentrated on the top k most likely tokens
089
      declines, resulting in more random outputs and a higher likelihood of selecting tokens outside the top
090
      k. Such a flattening of the probability distribution can lead to phenomena such as code-switching
091   (Doğruöz et al., 2021; Marchisio et al., 2024), making the model more prone to confusion. Our
092   experimental results validate that these two indicators are strong predictors of declining model per-
093   formance, providing critical markers to help avoid over-optimization while balancing diversity.

096   2        R ELATED W ORK
097   Preference learning. Recent years have seen significant progress in aligning LLMs with human
098   preferences (Hosking et al., 2024; Kirk et al., 2024a). RLHF, pioneered by Christiano et al. (2017);
099   Ziegler et al. (2019) and developed in subsequent works (Stiennon et al., 2020; Bai et al., 2022;
100   Ouyang et al., 2022b), typically consists of three stages: supervised fine-tuning (SFT), reward mod-
101   elling, and RL fine-tuning (Schulman et al., 2017; Mnih, 2016; Aryabumi et al., 2024; Ahmadian
102   et al., 2024). The reward model is trained to predict human preferences between pairs of model
103   outputs, while the RL phase optimises the model to maximise the reward (Ye et al., 2024; Lambert
104   et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2024b). More recently, researchers have proposed Di-
105
      rect Alignment Algorithms (Rafailov et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Azar et al., 2024) that aim to
      simplify RLHF by directly optimising the policy without a reward modelling or RL phase.
106

108
      Over-optimisation for preference learning. Over-optimisation occurs when a model’s perfor-
109   mance on a proxy measure improves while its true performance declines. Gao et al. (2023) was the
110   first to extensively characterise this issue for RLHF, where optimisation against a learned reward
111   model leads to increased proxy rewards, while actual task performance plateaus or worsens, a phe-
112   nomenon termed “reward over-optimisation”. Subsequent studies have observed similar patterns
113   (Eisenstein et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Dubois et al., 2023). To mitigate this, researchers
114   have proposed various approaches, such as using ensembles or data smoothing for reward mod-
115   elling (Eisenstein et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Coste et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024; Yang et al.,
116   2024b), and leveraging uncertainty signals (Yang et al., 2023; Zhai et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024b;
117
      Yang et al., 2024a). Rafailov et al. (2024) extended this analysis to DAAs, showing that even
      without an explicit reward model, DAAs exhibit similar over-optimisation patterns at higher KL-
118
      divergence budgets, where KL divergence as a primary metric. In contrast, we explore the DAAs’
119
      over-optimisation in the context of completion likelihood, which does not directly correlate with
120   KL-divergence. Both increases and decreases in completion likelihood can result in higher KL di-
121   vergence from the reference model. KL divergence is more about how far the model should move,
122   while our likelihood analysis is more about which direction the model should move.
123
124   Generalisation and diversity. Generalisation and diversity in LM outputs has been a growing
125   concern in the field of NLP, particularly regarding the impact of fine-tuning methods (Hendrycks
126
      et al., 2020). Several studies have explored how RLHF influences output diversity and generalisa-
      tion. Khalifa et al. (2021); Perez et al. (2022) suggests that RLHF tends to produce models with
127
      reduced output diversity. Kirk et al. (2024b) highlights a trade-off between generalisation and di-
128
      versity in current LLM fine-tuning, with RLHF showing better out-of-distribution generalisation but
129   substantially decreased output diversity compared to SFT. This trade-off between alignment, perfor-
130   mance, and diversity relates to the broader concept of “alignment tax” in LM fine-tuning. Bai et al.
131   (2022); Ouyang et al. (2022a); Bai et al. (2023); Kotha et al. (2023) observed that aligning models
132   with human preferences, through RLHF, can sometimes degrade performance on specific tasks, es-
133   pecially for smaller models. Various approaches have been proposed to mitigate the alignment tax
134   (Noukhovitch et al., 2023; Shi & Lipani, 2024; Qi et al., 2024). For example, Ouyang et al. (2022a)
135   suggested incorporating pretraining data into RLHF fine-tuning to minimise performance regres-
136   sions on standard NLP datasets. However, these studies have not explored how the optimisation of
137
      completion likelihood correlates with model performance, including diversity and generalisation.

145

182
      Standard DAAs do not guarantee an increase in the absolute probability of better completions. This
183
      can lead to scenarios where the model assigns very low probabilities to both better and worse com-
184   pletions, as long as the better completion has a higher relative probability.

192   where yw represents the better completion for a given input x. This loss term is typically combined

202   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
203
204   Model and Datasets. In our experiments, we utilise two instruction-tuned models: Cohere Com-
205
      mand R (7B) and Cohere Command R (35B) (Cohere For AI, 2024). We train and evaluate them on
      two datasets: (1) A binarised version of U LTRA F EEDBACK (Tunstall et al., 2024), which is collected
206
      based on Cui et al. (2024), containing 62,600 training examples and 647 examples for evaluation.
207
      (2) A Binarised preference dataset B INARIZED P REF, which comprises over 100,000 examples (see
208   details in Appendix §A). These include annotated conversational data across multiple languages,
209   synthetic code generation, and specialised tasks such as length control, safety, tool use, and natural
210   language-to-SQL generation.
211
212   Training and Evaluation Details. For each method (Hinge, DPO, and IPO), we test six different
213   values for its hyper-parameter (i.e., α, β, or τ ), respectively. We use a batch size of 32 for both
214   training and evaluation, with a maximum sequence length of 8192. The model is trained with a peak
215   learning rate of either 5 × 10−6 or 1 × 10−5 and an end learning rate ratio of 0.1. Following recent
      studies (Ouyang et al., 2022a; Howard & Whitaker, 2023; Shi et al., 2024), we train all models


                                                           4
216
      within a single epoch. The learning rate warms up over 128 steps. We monitor the model training
217   every 50 steps to apply early stopping. We use the Adam optimiser (Kingma, 2014) with β1 = 0.9,
218   β2 = 0.95, ϵ = 1 × 10−8 , an additive weight decay of 0.1, and a gradient clipping norm of 1.0. The
219   model training is conducted on TPU v5-128 for the 7B model and TPU v5-256 for the 35B model,
220   utilising the flash attention (Dao et al., 2022) to improve training efficiency. For both DPO and
221   IPO, we use the sum of the token log-likelihoods as the completion log-likelihood during training.
222   For the Hinge method, we compute the average token log-likelihood instead for better performance.
223   During evaluation, we calculate the log-likelihood for both the better and worse completions from
224   the validation set. For all methods, we report the average of token log-likelihoods for better and
225
      worse completions respectively, without normalising against the reference model. Additionally, we
      monitor the difference in log-likelihood between better and worse completions.
226
227
228   Generalisation Evaluation. Following the previous work (Kirk et al., 2024b), we evaluate the
229
      model in open-ended text generation tasks to assess generalisation ability. Specifically, we employ
      the LLM-as-a-Judge framework (Zheng et al., 2023; Taori et al., 2023) with a reward model to
230
      compare our models’ outputs against leading models, including GPT-3.5-Turbo, GPT-4o (Achiam
231
      et al., 2023), Claude-3-Sonnet (Claude, 2024), Llama-3 8B and 70B Chat (Dubey et al., 2024). The
232   evaluation uses a closed-source reward model, which ranked the top position on R EWARD B ENCH
233   (Lambert et al., 2024), validating that the evaluation provides a reliable proxy for human preferences.
234   We use win probability, denoted as Pwin , as the primary evaluation metric. It is computed as:
235
236                                            Pwin = σ(rv − rc ),                                       (7)
237
      where σ(·) is the sigmoid function, rv is the reward assigned to the policy model’s output, and rc is
238   the reward assigned to the competitor model’s output by the same reward model. We prompt models
239   with 433 diverse prompts, including code generation, chain-of-reasoning questions, closed QA, and
240   length control (see Appendix A for examples and details). During the decoding, we use a top-p
241   probability threshold of p = 0.75, a temperature of 0.5, and a maximum limit of 2048 tokens.
242
243   Diversity Evaluation. To assess output diversity, we also measure Per-Input Diversity, defined
244   as the average diversity of the output sets over inputs, and Cross-Input Diversity, which captures
245   the diversity of outputs across different inputs, similar to previous works (Kirk et al., 2024b; Hong
246   et al., 2024). However, instead of generating a set of K outputs from the model, we take a more
247   efficient way to measure Per-Input Diversity. Specifically, we compute the entropy over the top k
248   tokens with the highest probability in the model’s next token distribution (Kuhn et al., 2023). Let pk
249   represent the probability distribution over the top k tokens, and H(pk ) represent the entropy of the
250
      distribution. The entropy is calculated using the following formula:
251                                                     n

253                                                     i=1

      where b is the logarithm base. Here we set b = 2 and k = 10. This formula quantifies the un-
255
      certainty within the top k token predictions as a proxy for Per-Input Diversity. This entropy is
256   highest when the output is minimally informative: predicting the same probability for all possible
257   tokens, indicating more diverse outputs. To evaluate Cross-Input Diversity, we use distinct N-grams
258   (Li et al., 2016), which counts the unique N-grams across model outputs and averages them over
259   n = 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. Following Kirk et al. (2024b), we use the expectation-adjusted distinct N-grams
260   (EAD) formula to remove the bias towards shorter outputs.
261
262   Factuality Evaluation. We also evaluate model factuality performance on open-domain question-
263   answering tasks using NATURAL Q UESTIONS O PEN (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019) and T RIVIAQA
264   (Joshi et al., 2017) validation sets, with 3610 and 7993 examples respectively. Greedy decoding
265   is used to ensure deterministic outputs, and the word-level F1 score is reported.

459   4.3                   G ENERALISATION AND D IVERSITY
460
461   In this section, we explore the impact of model likelihood on generalisation and diversity.
462
463   1) Lower Completion likelihood improves the models’ Cross-Input Diversity. Figure 2
464   presents Cross-Input Diversity (measured by distinct N-grams) of the model outputs throughout
465   training. Specifically, within each DAA, models with lower likelihood tend to produce more diverse
466   outputs. For example, the pink lines for DAAs indicate that models with lower completion likeli-
467   hood typically show the highest level of Cross-Input Diversity scores throughout training. Better
468   output diversity tends to improve their generalisation to unseen scenarios, as reflected in increased
469   win probability at the early stage of the training phase. Figure 4 further demonstrates that output
470   diversity follows a similar trend under the different regularisation (i.e., Negative Log-Likelihood
471
      Loss), suggesting a strong correlation between likelihood and model diversity. However, it is worth
      noting that the relationship between diversity and win probability is not linear. While some diversity
472
      is beneficial for generalisation, excessive diversity can lead to performance degradation, similar to
473
      our previous discussion in §4.2. We will explore this phenomenon further in §4.4.
474
475
476   2) Higher Likelihood tends to have better memorisation of factual patterns. Figure 5 show-
477
      cases the relationship between model performance on NATURAL Q UESTIONS O PEN and T RIVIAQA
      and the log-likelihood of better completions. Our findings reveal a clear trend: higher mean log-
478
      likelihood values are associated with improved F1 scores. A higher F1 reflects better memorisation
479
      for some specific patterns, which can come at the expense of diversity. This can create a trade-off
480   between the ability to recall facts and the capacity to generate diverse, adaptive outputs in more
481   creative or open-ended tasks. To understand the potential issue of stylistic variations in answers,
482   we provide a further analysis with case studies and LLM-as-a-Judge as evaluation in Appendix §C.
483   Specifically, instead of relying on exact string matching, which can be overly rigid, we employ an
484   LLM-as-a-Judge using the GPT-4o model. Our analysis reveals that while the model performance
485   from LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation consistently yields higher performance metrics, it demonstrates a
      trend similar to the F1 score.

486
      4.4   S IGNALS FOR L IKELIHOOD OVER - OPTIMISATION
487
488   We have shown that completion likelihood correlates with model performance due to increased
489   output diversity. However, the key question remains: when should we stop reducing completion
490   likelihood? Here, we outline two indicators of over-optimising likelihood.
491
492
      1) Decreasing Entropy over Top-k tokens (Per-Input Diversity). Figure 2 and 4 presents
      Per-Input Diversity (measured by the entropy) of the model outputs throughout training. For DPO
493
      and IPO curves, at the beginning of the training, the Per-Input Diversity increases, signifying a
494
      broader distribution of selected tokens and a more uniform output distribution for the next token
495   prediction. Considering that the better completion likelihood is decreasing across the training, the
496   increase of entropy at the beginning phase indicates that those tokens from better completion have
497   a higher probability at the initial policy model over other tokens in the top k (here k = 10). The
498   decrease better completion likelihood gives the model a better chance to select other tokens, which
499   increases diversity and enhances generalisation, as reflected in the win probability. However, at
500   a certain point in training, this trend reverses. As Per-Input Diversity (entropy) starts decreasing,
501   the model begins to over-prioritise certain tokens. This suggests that those tokens in the better
502   completion now have an overly low likelihood, lower than other tokens in the top k. Despite this,
503
      Cross-Input Diversity keeps increasing, which indicates that the model is still generating diverse
      outputs, but now it includes tokens that are less relevant or nonsensical, i.e., tokens that humans do
504
      not prefer. Notably, the turning points of entropy often coincide with those of win probability for
505
      DPO and IPO, as the model’s outputs become less aligned with desirable outcomes.
506
507   2) Decreasing in Probability Mass in Top k Tokens. In another scenario, the entropy of the
508   top 10 tokens continues to increase, suggesting a progressively broader and more uniform output
509   distribution (refer to the hinge curves in Figure 2). This suggests that even as the likelihood of
510   better completions decreases, the model does not tend to over-prioritise any specific tokens during
511   training. However, this can result in degraded model performance. As depicted in the bottom row
512
      of the figure, the probability mass of all top-10 tokens diminishes, leading to more random outputs,
      with an increased likelihood of selecting tokens outside the top 10. This can introduce issues such
513
      as code-switching, where the model becomes prone to world-level language confusion when the
514
      number of tokens in the sampling nucleus is high and the distribution becomes too flat (Doğruöz
515   et al., 2021; Marchisio et al., 2024). Interestingly, hinge loss models do not exhibit the same patterns
516   observed with DPO and IPO. This could be attributed to the fact that DPO and IPO apply different
517   forms of regularisation compared to hinge loss.
518
      To demonstrate the generalisability of our findings, we provide additional experimental on different
519
      datasets with different model sizes in Figure 8, 9, and 10 of Appendix §B.
520

522   5     E PILOGUE
523
      Limitations. This study primarily focuses on two models (7B and 35B), which may not fully
524
      represent the broader spectrum of LLMs available. However, most LLMs are very standard trans-
525   formers (Vaswani et al., 2017), and we would not expect other LLMs to behave differently. While
526   we acknowledge the reviewer’s concern about testing additional methods such as KTO Ethayarajh
527   et al. (2024) or ORPO (Hong et al., 2024), our experiments with major DAA families (e.g., , DPO,
528   IPO, SLiC) provide strong evidence for the generalisability of our findings, which we leave for
529   future work to validate further.
530
531
      Implications for Practical Applications. The findings of this study have several implications for
      enhancing offline preference learning methods in practical applications: (1) Early stopping signal.
532
      In practice, we can integrate entropy/probability mass monitoring into the training loop. Training
533
      can employ adaptive methods like early stopping once entropy falls below a specific threshold. (2)
534   Adaptive regularisation for over-optimisation. Rather than using a fixed coefficient for the NLL
535   loss (Dubey et al., 2024), we could implement an adaptive regularisation based on the entropy and
536   probability mass, i.e., adding dropout or noise to prevent over-prioritisation of tokens or adding an
537   explicit regularisation term that maintains a certain degree of entropy and the probability mass of
538   the top-k tokens. While maintaining a certain degree of entropy and probability mass of the top-k
539   tokens is important, care should be taken not to overly constrain the model, as some tasks inherently
      require a broader token distribution (e.g., give me a random number between 0 and 10).

936   B INARIZED P REF Dataset. The B INARIZED P REF collection process used a robust multi-source
937   approach combining professional annotators, multiple independent annotation pipelines, and var-
938   ious validation methods. The foundation comes from professional annotation services ( 70% of
939   data), with rigorous quality control through multi-annotator consensus, adversarial validation sets,
940   and specialized verification datasets for issues like hallucination and repetition. We’ve ensured broad
941   domain coverage, incorporating specialised modules for code generation, RAG interactions, STEM,
942
      and medical domains while maintaining strong multilingual capabilities across French, Spanish,
      Korean, Japanese, German, and Italian - including dedicated datasets for handling code-mixing and
943
      language transition cases. Quality control is implemented through multiple layers: consensus-based
944
      annotation (1-3 annotators depending on complexity), dedicated adversarial validation sets, and spe-
945   cific datasets targeting quality aspects like anti-repetition, length control, and format adherence. The
946   data is predominantly recent (2024), with carefully weighted components and explicit test sets for
947   key capabilities. We use strategic copy multipliers (up to 5x) for crucial capabilities, and the entire
948   dataset is organised into functional groups (multilingual, code, RAG) to ensure balanced training
949   across all target capabilities.
