                                                 A BSTRACT
012            Talking head video generation aims to generate a realistic talking head video that
013            preserves the person’s identity from a source image and the motion from a driving
014            video. Despite the promising progress made in the field, it remains a challenging
015            and critical problem to generate videos with accurate poses and fine-grained facial
016            details simultaneously. Essentially, facial motion is often highly complex to model
017            precisely, and the one-shot source face image cannot provide sufficient appearance
               guidance during generation due to dynamic pose changes. To tackle the prob-
018
               lem, we propose to jointly learn motion and appearance codebooks and perform
019
               multi-scale codebook compensation to effectively refine both the facial motion
020            conditions and appearance features for talking face image decoding. Specifically,
021            the designed multi-scale motion and appearance codebooks are learned simulta-
022            neously in a unified framework to store representative global facial motion flow
023            and appearance patterns. Then, we present a novel multi-scale motion and appear-
024            ance compensation module, which utilizes a transformer-based codebook retrieval
025            strategy to query complementary information from the two codebooks for joint
026
               motion and appearance compensation. The entire process produces motion flows
               of greater flexibility and appearance features with fewer distortions across differ-
027
               ent scales, resulting in a high-quality talking head video generation framework.
028
               Extensive experiments on various benchmarks validate the effectiveness of our
029            approach and demonstrate superior generation results from both qualitative and
030            quantitative perspectives when compared to state-of-the-art competitors.
031
032   1 I NTRODUCTION
033
      Given a source image and a driving video, talking head video generation (Hong et al., 2022; Tao
034   et al., 2024) aims to animate the person in the source image using the pose and expression from the
035   driving video. Due to its widespread applications, such as video conferencing, the film industry, and
036   virtual reality, it has attracted growing interest in the community.
037   Significant progress has been made on this task in terms of both quality and robustness in recent
038   years. Existing works primarily focus on learning more accurate motion estimation and represen-
039   tation in 2D or 3D to enhance generation quality. Along the direction, unsupervised methods target
040   predicting local motion flows around unsupervised keypoints without relying on facial priors (Siaro-
041   hin et al., 2019b; Zhao & Zhang, 2022; Wang et al., 2024), and methods based on predefined models
042   (e.g., 3DMM) (Zakharov et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2023; Ha et al., 2020) focus on learning robust
043
      decoding features to generate high-quality face outputs. Despite the promising achievements, criti-
      cal challenges persist: 1) Some motion patterns cannot be inferred from a single image pair solely
044
      relying on unsupervised keypoints or predefined models for motion estimation, as such models often
045
      have limited power of motion representation and may fail to capture certain dynamic aspects of the
046   facial motion from single image pairs. 2) Even with accurate motion estimation, highly dynamic and
047   complex motions in driving videos can create ambiguity during generation, as a still source image
048   lacks sufficient appearance information to handle occluded regions or subtle expression changes.
049   This results in noticeable artifacts and a significant drop in the quality of the generated output.
050   Therefore, generating realistic-looking facial images not only requires inferring accurate motion
051   flow between given two facial images but also needs to compensate for the intermediate appearance
052
      decoding feature from the one-shot source image for the final generation of face images.
053   In this work, we aim to synergize motion and appearance by simultaneously learning accurate mo-
      tion flows for facial warping and robust facial appearance features for face image decoding, to ad-

055   both motion and appearance codebooks with multi-scale compensation. Specifically, to estimate the
056   motion flow between two facial images (i.e., source and driving), we design a multi-scale motion

064   both codebooks, we propose a joint training strategy in which the motion and appearance codebooks
065   are learned simultaneously with the entire framework. This approach allows both codebooks to be
066   optimized together, utilizing gradients from the refined warped features to strengthen their mutual
067   influence and improve overall performance. By learning both multi-scale motion and appearance
068   codebooks, our framework refines the motion flow to accurately warp the source facial features,
069   which are further compensated with additional details from the appearance codebook. This process
070
      yields robust intermediate facial decoding features, resulting in improved generation.
071   We conduct extensive ablation studies to verify the effectiveness of the learned multi-scale motion
072   and appearance codebooks. Experimental results demonstrate that both codebooks effectively en-
073   hance the motion flow and intermediate warped features, resulting in more accurate and detailed fa-
      cial motion flows and feature textures. Furthermore, results on two challenging datasets indicate that
074
      our method surpasses state-of-the-art approaches, producing realistic-looking talking head videos.
075
      In summary, our contributions are threefold:
076
077   • We propose a novel framework that jointly learns multi-scale motion and appearance codebooks.
078     The motion codebook captures motion patterns at varying levels of granularity, while the ap-
079     pearance codebook stores representative facial structure and texture features. This joint learning
080
        enables the model to effectively compensate both motion and appearance for advanced generation.
081   • We develop an effective multi-scale compensation mechanism that utilizes the learned motion and
082     appearance codebooks to progressively refine both motion and appearance representations. The
083     mechanism can couple the compensation of both aspects at each level, achieving higher consis-
084
        tency of appearance and motion, thus leading to high visual quality in generated videos.
085   • Extensive experiments including on challenging datasets demonstrate that our method not only
086     effectively compensates for facial motions and appearances but also significantly outperforms
087
        state-of-the-art approaches, producing more realistic and visually convincing talking head videos.

090   Talking Head Video Generation. Existing works on talking head video generation generally sep-
091   arate the motion estimation module and image generation module to disentangle appearance and
092
      motion. To transfer the motion, some works require facial priors provided by a pre-trained model
      during generation. For example, landmark-based approaches (Ha et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2021;
093
      Wu et al., 2018; Zakharov et al., 2020) detect pre-defined facial landmarks to transfer the facial
094   pose and expression from a driving frame to the source image. Some other methods (Ren et al.,
095   2021; Zeng et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2020) use parameters from 3D face models (Blanz & Vetter,
096   2023; Feng et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2017) as motion descriptors to disentangle identity and pose.
097   However, they normally cannot describe non-facial parts such as hair and neck, and their generation
098   quality is limited by the pre-trained model performance. To address the issue, several methods that
099   do not require any prior knowledge from pre-trained models are proposed. Monkey-Net (Siarohin
100   et al., 2019a) learns sparse motion-related keypoints in an unsupervised manner to describe object
      movements. FOMM (Siarohin et al., 2019b) extends it with local affine transformation assumption
101
      around the keypoints to model complex motion. Subsequent works introduce more flexible math-
102
      ematical models such as thin-plane spline transformation (Zhao & Zhang, 2022) and continuous
103   piecewise-affine-based transformation (Wang et al., 2024) to increase motion estimation accuracy.
104   Despite the expressiveness of the motion models, they cannot fully describe large head poses and
105   delicate expression changes. MRFA (Tao et al., 2024) tackles the problem by building a correlation
106   volume for each image pair and using it to refine the coarse motion flow iteratively. However, it
107   only uses the warped image feature and a plain image generator for image generation, which may
      fail when facing extreme pose change as the appearance information from the one-shot source im-

135   Codebook Learning. Codebook learning aims to learn useful discrete representations with a fixed
136   size. The learned codebook contains rich and compact information, which can facilitate various
137   tasks such as image classification (Cai et al., 2010; Zhang et al., 2009), image synthesis (Esser et al.,
138   2021; Chang et al., 2022), blind face restoration (Zhou et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2022) and audio-driven
139
      talking head video generation (Wang et al., 2023). Traditional methods often learn a codebook with
      unsupervised clustering such as k-means (Csurka et al., 2004). VQ-VAE (Van Den Oord et al., 2017)
140
      first incorporates vector quantization in a Variational Autoencoder to learn a codebook containing
141   discrete representative input features. To build a context-rich codebook for images, VQGAN (Esser
142   et al., 2021) further increases the compression rate and adds a discriminator and a perceptual loss
143   on images reconstructed with the codes from the codebook. A transformer is later used to model the
144   composition of the codes for high-resolution image synthesis. CodeFormer (Zhou et al., 2022) uses
145   a learned codebook of compressed high-quality face image features as discrete prior and predicts
146   the code sequence based on the low-quality facial input for blind face restoration. LipFormer (Wang
147   et al., 2023) learns two codebooks of the upper half face and the bottom half face respectively
148
      and predicts the lip codes from the input audio to generate a face video from the audio. We also
      adopt the idea of codebook learning, but we simultaneously learn multi-scale motion and appearance
149
      codebooks that store diverse motion and appearance patterns from the entire dataset during training
150   to facilitate high-quality talking head video generation. The codebooks and the entire framework
151   are trained together so that patterns useful for talking head video generation can be stored in and
152   retrieved from the codebooks.

339   We use the multi-scale compensated appearance features {Fic }N    i=1 and the image decoder DI for

341   upsamples it through a series of upsampling layers and ResNet blocks (He et al., 2016) until it

346   While we introduced multi-scale motion and appearance codebooks separately in Sec. 3.2 and
347
      Sec. 3.3, unlike traditional codebook-based methods (Esser et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Xing
      et al., 2023) where codebooks are pre-trained, we train both codebooks and the full framework end-
348
      to-end. This allows the network to effectively store and retrieve useful patterns at different scales,
349   leading to high-quality talking head video generation.
350
      We follow the unsupervised training pipeline from (Siarohin et al., 2019b), where the source and
351
      driving frames are extracted from the same video, and our framework learns to reconstruct the
352   driving frame. The training combines the codebook losses from Sec. 3.2 and Sec. 3.3 with common
353   losses for talking head video generation (Siarohin et al., 2019b; Hong et al., 2022). Specifically, we
354   use the equivariance loss Leq and keypoint distance loss Lkpd to guide keypoint prediction in the
355   motion flow estimator, and an image reconstruction loss Lrecon and an adversarial loss Ladv on the
356   output Ig . To avoid the image decoder relying too much on high-resolution appearance features, we
357   also generate an image I1g using only F1c and apply Lrecon to minimize the difference between I1g
358   and Id . The overall training objective is:

532   5     C ONCLUSION
533
      In this paper, we present a novel framework that jointly learns multi-scale motion and appearance
534
      compensatory codebooks to enhance the motion flows and appearance features for talking head
535   video generation. The jointly learned motion and appearance codebooks store local motion and
536   appearance patterns learned from the entire dataset at different scales, and our multi-scale motion
537   and appearance codebook compensation module retrieves useful codes from the codebooks with a
538   transformer-based strategy at different scales to gradually refine the motion flows and appearance
539   features during generation. Extensive results demonstrate the effectiveness of our codebook learning
      and compensation, synergizing motion and appearance to produce higher quality videos.
