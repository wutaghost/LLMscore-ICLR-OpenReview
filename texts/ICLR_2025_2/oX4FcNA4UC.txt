008                                                A BSTRACT
009
010            We propose a simulation-free algorithm for the solution of generic problems in
011            stochastic optimal control (SOC). Unlike existing methods, our approach does
012            not require the solution of an adjoint problem, but rather leverages Girsanov
013            theorem to directly calculate the gradient of the SOC objective on-policy. This
014            allows us to speed up the optimization of control policies parameterized by neural
015
               networks since it completely avoids the expensive back-propagation step through
               stochastic differential equations (SDEs) used in the Neural SDE framework. In
016
               particular, it enables us to solve SOC problems in high dimension and on long
017
               time horizons. We demonstrate the efficiency of our approach in various domains
018            of applications, including standard stochastic optimal control problems, sampling
019            from unnormalized distributions via construction of a Schrödinger-Föllmer process,
020            and fine-tuning of pre-trained diffusion models. In all cases our method is shown to
021            outperform the existing methods in both the computing time and memory efficiency.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Stochastic Optimal Control (SOC) problems (Mortensen, 1989; Fleming & Rishel, 2012) arise in a
027   wide variety of applications in sciences and engineering (Pham, 2009; Fleming & Stein, 2004; Zhang
028   & Chen, 2022; Holdijk et al., 2023; Hartmann et al., 2013; 2017). Their general aim is to add an
029   adjustable drift (the control) in a reference stochastic differential equation (SDE) so that the solutions
030   of the controlled SDE minimize some cost.
031   In low dimensions, SOC problems can be tackled by standard numerical methods for partial differ-
032   ential equations since the optimal control can be obtained from the solution of a Hamilton-Jacobi-
033   Bellman equation. In high dimensions, these methods are not applicable, and there has been a lot
034   of recent effort to solve SOC problems via deep learning (DL), see e.g. (Han & E, 2016; Han et al.,
035   2018; Huré et al., 2020; Domingo-Enrich et al., 2023; Germain et al., 2021; Hu & Lauriere, 2024).
036   The most direct way to leverage DL in the context of SOC is to parameterize the control by a deep
037   neural network, view the SOC objective as a loss for the network parameters, evaluate this loss
038   empirically using solutions of the controlled SDE, and use stochastic gradient descent to evolve the
039   parameters until convergence. This fits into the framework of Neural SDE (Tzen & Raginsky, 2019;
040   Li et al., 2020). While straightforward in principle, this approach faces a practical difficulty: since
041   the SOC objective involves expectation over the solutions of an SDE that depend on the control,
042   calculating the gradient of this objective requires differentiating through these solutions. This makes
043   the training costly both in terms of computing time and memory requirement, and has prevented
044
      scaling up deep learning calculations of SOC policies.
045   A way to avoid this difficulty is to use Girsanov theorem to compute the SOC objective via an
046   expectation over a reference process with a control that is independent from the control being
047   optimized. Unfortunately, this requires to introduce an exponential weighing factor under the
048   expectation to remove the bias incurred, and the variance of this factor is typically large if the control
049   in the reference process is far from the actual control. This again limits the scalability of the approach.
050   In this paper, we propose a different procedure based on the observation that the gradient of the SOC
051   objective over parameters in the control can be expressed exactly in terms of an expectation over the
052   controlled process (which is referred to as on-policy evaluation) without differentiating through the
053   solution of this process. We refer to this approach as simulation-free. This result, first derived in Yang
      & Kushner (1991), is obtained using Girsanov theorem, but it gives an expression for the gradient

054
      that does not involve any exponential weighing factor. We also show that it is possible to calculate
055   this gradient via automatic differentiation of an alternative objective, provided that we detach some
056   of its parameters via stopgrad.

058
      Specifically, our work makes the following main contributions:
059
      • We propose a simulation-free, on-policy algorithm for solving generic SOC problems by deep
060
        learning and scaling up these calculations to situations where methods based on the Neural SDE
061
        framework is too costly to be applied.
062
063
      • We discuss how to apply the method to construct Föllmer processes between a point mass and an
        unnormalized target distribution. Our approach can be used to sample this target and compute its
064
        normalization constant.
065
066   • We also discuss how to apply the method to fine-tune generative models based on diffusions: that
067     is, assuming that we have at our disposal an SDE that samples a given distribution, we show how
068
        to find, via solution of a SOC problem, a new SDE that samples the distribution obtained by tilting
        the first one by a given reward function.
069
070   • We illustrate these results with numerical experiments showing that our method is much less
071     expensive in terms of computing time and memory requirement than vanilla methods requiring
072
        differentiation through the solution of the SDE (such as in the Neural SDE framework).

074   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
075
      Besides the traditional applications of SOC, such as finance and economics (Pham, 2009; Fleming
076
      & Stein, 2004; Aghion & Howitt, 1992), and robotics (Theodorou et al., 2011; Pavlov et al., 2018),
077
      this framework has recently has also found a variety of new applications, for example, sampling of
078   complex non-log-concave probability distributions or simulation rare events (Zhang & Chen, 2022;
079   Holdijk et al., 2023; Hartmann et al., 2013; 2017; Ribera Borrell et al., 2024). Our approach is aimed
080   at solving problems of this sort by deep learning.
081
      Deep learning for SOC has attracted a growing interest in the recent years. Notably, Han & E (2016)
082
      have proposed a deep learning method for high-dimensional SOC problems by learning the feedback
083
      control function. This method then inspired other algorithms to solve backward stochastic differential
084   equations and partial differential equations (Han et al., 2018). Further developments can be found e.g.
085   in (Ji et al., 2020), which studied three different algorithms. Another line of research proposed to
086   use dynamic programming instead of learning the optimal control by simulating whole trajectories:
087   see (Huré et al., 2021) for applications to SOC problems, and (Huré et al., 2020) for an extension to
088   solving PDEs. Another recent approach is stochastic optimal control matching (Domingo-Enrich
089   et al., 2023), based on iterative diffusion optimization (IDO) (Nüsken & Richter, 2023). Compared to
090   the approach we propose, these methods either avoid differentiating through the SDE solution by
091   using a independent reference process (off-policy learning), but this typically leads to estimators
092
      with high variance; or they use on-policy objectives, but it then requires to differentiate through the
      solution of the SDE. Our approach is on-policy but avoids this costly differentiation step.
093
094   A variant of formula for the gradient of the SOC objective that we used first appeared in (Yang &
095   Kushner, 1991) in the context of sensitivity analysis with applications to finance and economics (Pham,
096   2009; Fleming & Stein, 2004; Aghion & Howitt, 1992). This formula is also discussed in Gobet &
097   Munos (2005), who generalized it to problems in which the volatility also depends on the control. It is
098
      also mentioned in several works interested in solving SOC problem with DL (Mohamed et al., 2020;
      Li et al., 2020; Lie, 2021; Domingo-Enrich et al., 2023; Ribera Borrell et al., 2024; Domingo-Enrich,
099
      2024), but to the best of our knowledge it has not been exploited systematically to develop the type of
100
      algorithm we propose here.
101
102   SOC also has deep connections with Reinforcement Learning (RL), see e.g. Quer & Borrell (2024);
103   Domingo-Enrich et al. (2024); Domingo-Enrich (2024) for a discussion. The algorithm we propose
104
      can be viewed as a continuous-time variant of the well-known REINFORCE method (Williams, 1992;
      1988; Sutton et al., 1999).
105
106   One application of SOC is the construction of a Föllmer process (Föllmer, 1986), i.e. a Schrödinger
107   bridge between a Dirac delta distribution and a target distribution with unknown normalization
      coefficient. The Path Integral Sampler (PIS) proposed in Zhang & Chen (2022) is a way to solve this


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      problem via DL. PIS offers an alternative to other deep learning methods that have been introduced
109   to calculate Schrödinger-Föllmer process (Huang et al., 2021; Jiao et al., 2021; Vargas et al., 2023) .
110   PIS performs the minimization the SOC objective on-policy, and requires to differentiate through
111   the solution of the controlled process. In contrast, our simulation-free approach performs on-policy
112   minimization of the same objective, but it avoids this differentiation step which, as we will show
113   below, results in significant gains in terms of memory requirement and computing time.
114
      Another context in which SOC problems have found applications is the fine-tuning of generative
115   models (Fan et al., 2024; Clark et al., 2024; Uehara et al., 2024). The aim here is to start from a
116   diffusion model trained to sample a given data distribution and adjust the drift in this model so that it
117   samples the probability distribution obtained by tilting the first by a reward function. Assuming that
118   this reward function is differentiable, a method termed Adjoint Matching was recently proposed in
119   Domingo-Enrich et al. (2024) to perform this fine-tuning: this method uses a specific scheduling of
120   the noise in the diffusion and uses an adjoint method to compute the gradient of a tailored-built SOC
121   objective. This second step avoids explicit differentiation through the solution of the controlled SDE
122   by solving instead an ODE for an adjoint variable backward in time. Our simulation-free approach is
123
      an alternative to Adjoint Matching which uses the original SOC objective and avoid solving the ODE
      for the adjoint. It also works for any noise schedule, but requires that the base distribution used in the
124
      generative model be a point mass distribution.

      3.2   S AMPLING FROM AN UNNORMALIZED DISTRIBUTION
451
      Next, we consider an example of SOC problem relevant to the setup of Sec. 2.5. Specifically we
452   sample Neal’s funnel distribution in d = 10 dimension, for which x0 ∼ N (0, σ0 ) and x1:9 |x0 ∼
453   N (0, ex0 Id). This distribution was also used by Zhang & Chen (2022) to test their Path Integral
454   Sampler (PIS): it becomes exponentially challenging to sample as σ0 increases, as the spread x1:9 is
455   exponentially small for negative values of x0 and exponentially large for positive values of x0 (hence
456   the funnel shape of the distribution), and the spread of x0 around 0 increases with σ0 . We study this
457   example with σ0 = 1 and σ0 = 3.
458   We use the same neural network parameterization as Zhang & Chen (2022), where the control is
459   parameterized as follows
460
                                   uθt (x) = NNθ1 (t, x) + NNθ2 (t) × ∇ log ρ(x).                      (24)
461
462
      Here ρ(x) is the probability density function of the funnel distribution and ∇ log ρ(x) is its score.
      Note that the score function is the exact control we need if the time horizon T → ∞. As what is
463
      reported in Zhang & Chen (2022), for one pass NNθ1 and NNθ2 , we lift the scalar t to 128 dimensions
464
      with Fourier positional encoding, followed by two fully connected layers with 64 hidden units
465   to extract 64-dimensional signal features. We use an independent two-layer MLP to extract 64-
466   dimensional features for x. Then, we concatenate the features of the two MLPs for x, t and use it
467   as the input to a three-layer neural network to obtain the output. To make the problem challenging,
468   we initialize at zero the weights of the last linear layers of NNθ1 and NNθ2 , so that uθt (x) = 0 at
469   initialization. We use the Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014) with a learning rate of 5 · 10−3 for all the
470   experiments on the funnel distribution example.
471
      To measure the quality of the learned samplers, we estimate the log normalization constants log Z of
472   the funnel distribution with σ0 = 1.0 and σ0 = 3.0 with the unbiased estimator given in (16). We
473   report the results as well as the computational costs in Table 2: our method reaches the same level
474   of accuracy as the vanilla PIS but with much smaller memory cost and computing time, which is a
475   big advantage for problems in high dimensions. The generated samples themselves are shown in
476   Figure 3.

      4     C ONCLUSION
479
480   We have introduced a simulation-free on-policy approach to SOC problems: we simulate trajectories
481   using an actual control but detach this control for the computational graph when computing the
482   gradient of the objective. This leads to a new way of training a deep neural network to learn the
483   feedback control, which is more efficient and scalable than the vanilla method commonly used thus
484   far. We have illustrated the efficacy of our method on several examples from the SOC literature
485   and the sampling literature, with applications to sampling via Föllmer processes and fine-tuning of
      diffusion-based generative models .
