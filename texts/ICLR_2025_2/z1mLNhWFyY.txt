009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural networks are trained primarily based on their inputs and outputs, with-
012            out regard for their internal mechanisms. These neglected mechanisms determine
013            properties that are critical for safety, like (i) transparency; (ii) the absence of sen-
014            sitive information or harmful capabilities; and (iii) reliable generalization of goals
015            beyond the training distribution. To address this shortcoming, we introduce gra-
016            dient routing, a training method that isolates capabilities to specific subregions
017
               of a neural network. Gradient routing applies data-dependent, weighted masks to
               gradients during backpropagation. These masks are supplied by the user in order
018
               to configure which parameters are updated by which data points. We show that
019
               gradient routing can be used to (1) learn representations which are partitioned in
020            an interpretable way; (2) enable robust unlearning via ablation of a pre-specified
021            network subregion; and (3) achieve scalable oversight of a reinforcement learner
022            by localizing modules responsible for different behaviors. Throughout, we find
023            that gradient routing localizes capabilities even when applied to a limited, ad-hoc
024            subset of the data. We conclude that the approach holds promise for challenging,
025            real-world applications where quality data are scarce.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   As AI systems become more powerful and more prevalent, there is an increasing need to explain and
031
      control the inner mechanisms governing their behavior. To address this challenge, some researchers
      aim to fully understand AI systems, either by reverse engineering the operations of conventionally
032
      trained models (Olah et al., 2020; Olsson et al., 2022) or with inherently interpretable architectures
033
      (Koh et al., 2020; Hewitt et al., 2023; Xin et al., 2022). This is not necessary. If we could control the
034   mechanisms underlying a neural network’s computation with respect to a limited set of safety-critical
035   properties, such as hazardous information or the capacity for deception, that might be sufficient
036   to make significant safety guarantees. Since manual specification of network internals is likely
037   infeasible, there is a need for mechanistic supervision: the use of data to exert targeted influence
038   over neural network internals.
039
      To achieve mechanistic supervision, we propose gradient routing, a modification of backpropaga-
040   tion that uses data-dependent, weighted masks to control which network subregions are updated by
041   which data points. By appropriately specifying these masks, a user can configure which parts of
042   the network (parameters, activations, or modules) are updated by which data points (e.g. specific
043   tokens, documents, or based on data labels). In this work, we apply gradient routing to a variety of
044   problems:
045
      Section 4.1 We use gradient routing to split the encoding learned by an MNIST autoencoder into
046        two halves, with each half representing different digits. We do the same for a CIFAR classifier
047        in appendix B.1. In this way, we demonstrate supervised control of learned representations.
048   Section 4.2 We apply gradient routing to localize features in language models. First, we train a
049        model that can be steered by a single scalar value, showing that feature localization is possi-
050        ble, even with narrowly-scoped labels. Next, we present Expand, Route, Ablate, an application
051        of gradient routing that enables robust removal of capabilities via ablation of a pre-specified
052        network subregion. When data is partially labeled, the method outperforms all baselines, in-
053        cluding data filtering, a gold standard of unlearning. Finally, we show that this unlearning
           method scales to a much larger (0.7B) model.

054
      Section 4.3 We apply gradient routing to the problem of scalable oversight (Amodei et al., 2016),
055        where the aim is to train a performant policy despite limited access to reliable labels. We train a
056        policy network by reinforcement learning to navigate to two kinds of grid squares in a toy envi-
057        ronment, D IAMOND and G HOST. Using gradient routing, we localize modules responsible for
058        these two behaviors. We show that we can steer the policy towards D IAMOND by ablating the
059        G HOST module. Gradient routing trains steerable networks even when the amount of labeled
060        training data is small (1%), and even when the policy is able to condition on the existence of
061        labels. As a result, our method outperforms baselines based on behavioral supervision alone.
062   Throughout, we find evidence of an absorption effect, where gradient routing applied to narrow data
063   localizes capabilities relevant to a broader superset of data. Absorption answers the question “if one
064   has labels that are suitable for localizing undesirable computation, why not use those labels to filter
065   the data?” When labels do not encompass all training data from which harmful capabilities arise
066   (Zhu et al., 2009), filtering may be inadequate (Welbl et al., 2021), whereas absorption means that
067   localization can still occur. Furthermore, localization influences model internals without modifying
068   the loss function. This can enable scalable oversight when perfect supervision is not feasible.
069   We conclude by noting that black-box training techniques may be insufficient for high-stakes ma-
070   chine learning applications. Localization techniques, like gradient routing, may provide a solution.

      2   BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK
074
075
      Training to localize pre-specified capabilities. Akin to gradient routing, work in modular machine
      learning trains modules to contain concepts or abilities determined in advance of training. Typically,
076
      modular architectures involve a routing function that selects modules to apply on a forward pass
077
      (Pfeiffer et al., 2023). Routing functions are often unsupervised, but some rely on metadata, inducing
078   modules with known specializations (Waibel & II, 1992). For example, routing has been based on
079   (i) the modality of data in multi-modal models (Pfeiffer et al., 2021), (ii) language (Pfeiffer et al.,
080   2020; 2022; Fan et al., 2021), and (iii) low- vs. high-level control or task type in robotics (Heess
081   et al., 2016; Devin et al., 2017). Gururangan et al. (2021) separate the training data of a language
082   model by domain and assign one expert in each layer to a single domain. By disabling the expert for
083   a domain, they are able to approximate a model that was not trained on the domain.
084   Other methods freeze the weights of a pre-trained model and train a new module, with the aim
085   of localizing the task to the new module (Rebuffi et al., 2017; 2018; Houlsby et al., 2019; Bapna &
086   Firat, 2019). Zhang et al. (2024) locate capabilities in models by learning a weight mask, transfer the
087   identified sub-network to a randomly initialized model, then train as if from scratch. By choosing a
088   suitable sub-network, they can, e.g., induce a vision model to identify ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009)
089   classes by shape, not texture. Appendix J contains extended comparisons to select methods.
090   Adversarial representation learning and concept erasure. In order to control the information
091   in learned representations, some have proposed to train feature extraction networks adversarially
092   against discriminator networks that predict this information (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Schmidhuber,
093   1992; Ganin & Lempitsky, 2015; Ganin et al., 2016; Edwards & Storkey, 2015). Other methods
094   attempt to remove concepts by modifying activations at inference time (Ravfogel et al., 2020; Bel-
095   rose et al., 2023; Elazar et al., 2020; Bolukbasi et al., 2016). In contrast, gradient routing localizes
096   capabilities during training, with the option of ablation afterward.
097   Robust unlearning. Machine unlearning seeks to remove undesired knowledge or abilities from
098   a pre-trained neural network (Cao & Yang, 2015; Li et al., 2024). Typical unlearning methods are
099   brittle in the sense that the unlearned abilities of the model can be recovered by fine-tuning on a tiny
100   number of data points (Henderson et al., 2023; Sheshadri et al., 2024; Lynch et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
101   2024; Shi et al., 2024; Patil et al., 2023; Lo et al., 2024; Lermen et al., 2023). Lee et al. (2024);
102   Łucki et al. (2024) suggest that undesired concepts are more easily “bypassed” than thoroughly re-
103   moved from model weights. In this paper, we pre-train models with gradient routing. Consequently,
104
      localized capabilities can be robustly removed via ablation. Tampering Attack Resistance (TAR)
      (Tamirisa et al., 2024) also targets robust unlearning in LLMs, but does so via fine-tuning.
105
106   Like gradient routing, some robust unlearning approaches prune or mask parts of the network most
107   important for the target behavior. SISA (Bourtoule et al., 2021) trains multiple independent models
      based on a partition of the dataset and ensembles them at inference time. Similar to ablating a

140   Gradient routing applies data-dependent, weighted masks to gradients during backpropagation to
141   configure what data (whether it be defined in terms of tokens, documents, or based on other labels)
142
      is learned where in the network (e.g. at the level of parameters, activations, or modules). The result
      is a model with a partially-understandable internal structure, where particular regions correspond
143
      to known capabilities. Throughout this paper, we will use “route X to Y ” to mean “use gradient
144
      routing to limit learning updates for data points X to region Y of the neural network.”

186   4.1   ROUTING GRADIENTS TO PARTITION MNIST REPRESENTATIONS
187
188
      As a first example of feature localization via gradient routing, we train a simple MLP autoencoder
      on the MNIST handwritten digit dataset (LeCun et al., 1998) and use label-dependent stop-gradients
189
      to control where features for different digits are encoded. The goal is to obtain an autoencoder that
190
      reconstructs all digits (0–9) via an encoding that is made up of non-overlapping subcomponents
191   corresponding to distinct subsets of digits. We choose subsets {0, 1, 2, 3, 4} and {5, 6, 7, 8, 9}. To
192   hint at the potential difficulty of this task, we note the encodings learned by an autoencoder trained
193   on one of these sets admit low-error reconstructions on the other set, despite never being trained on
194   it (details in appendix B).
195
      We use a simple architecture of three-layer MLP modules with ReLU activations: an Encoder, a
196
      Decoder, and two “certificate” decoders. The Encoder processes a 28×28 image into a vector in
197   R32 , and the Decoder processes that vector into a 28×28 reconstruction. Each certificate is trained
198   on half of the encoding, which takes values in R16 . Certificate updates do not affect the encoding.
199   If the Decoder can reconstruct a digit that a certificate cannot, this “certifies” that robust feature
200   localization occurred (away from the half of the encoding the certificate was trained on).
201
      We use gradient routing to train an encoding split such that the top half encodes digits 0–4 and the
202
      bottom half encodes digits 5–9. While training on all digits, we route digits 0–4 to the top half of the
203   encoding and route digits 5–9 to the bottom half of the encoding. To induce specialization in the two
204   halves of the encoding, we add the L1 norm of the encoding as a penalty term to the loss. The setup
205   is shown in fig. 2a. The results, shown in fig. 2b and fig. 2c, are stark: while using the entire encoding
206   allows the Decoder to reproduce all digits with low loss, the Certificate is only able to reproduce 5–9
207   from the bottom half of the encoding, as desired. Furthermore, the certificate’s learned predictions
208   for digits 0–4 are approximately constant. This suggests that we have successfully eliminated most
209   information relevant to digits 0–4 from the encoding. Appendix B contains experiment details,
210   ablations, and an extension to a ResNet (He et al., 2016) trained for CIFAR image classification
211
      (Krizhevsky et al., 2009).

254   central communication channel of a Transformer, with all layers “reading from” and “writing into”
255   it. Usually, the standard basis of the residual stream is indecipherable, with the axes not correspond-
256   ing to interpretable concepts. We pre-train a 20-layer, 303M parameter Transformer on the FineWeb-
257
      Edu dataset (Penedo et al., 2024) while routing the gradients for all California1 tokens to the 0th
      entry of the residual stream on layers 6–18. On token positions predicting California, we mask
258
      gradients (to zero) on every residual stream dimension except the 0th in layers 6–18. This masking
259
      causes the learning updates for those token positions to be localized to the weights that write into the
260   0th dimension of the residual stream. After training, we look at which tokens’ unembedding vectors
261   have the highest cosine similarity with the one hot vector for the 0th entry of the residual stream. We
262   find that California has the highest cosine similarity, followed by California, Californ,
263    Oregon, Colorado, Texas, Florida, Arizona, Sacramento, and Los; see ap-
264   pendix D for the top 300. These tokens all have semantic similarity to California, but gradient
265   routing was not applied to them. This shows that gradient routing localizes broader semantic con-
266   cepts, rather than the narrow set of explicitly-routed tokens.

      4.2.2   G RADIENT ROUTING ENABLES ROBUST UNLEARNING VIA ABLATION
294   Robust unlearning (Sheshadri et al., 2024) means training models that lack the internal mechanisms
295   or “knowledge” required for certain tasks, as opposed to merely performing poorly on those tasks.
296   To address this open problem, we show that gradient routing can be used to localize capabilities to
297   a known region of the network. Then, that region can be deleted to remove those capabilities. We
298   find that gradient routing excels in situations where data is only partially labeled.
299
      To enable comprehensive comparisons, our initial study on robust unlearning applies gradient rout-
300   ing to a small (28M parameter) Transformer. This model is trained on an LLM-generated dataset of
301   simple children’s stories based on the TinyStories dataset (Eldan & Li, 2023; Janiak et al., 2024). We
302   partition the data into a forget set made up of any story containing one of the keywords “forest(s)”,
303   “tree(s)”, or “woodland(s)”, and a retain set made up of all other stories; the forget set constitutes
304   20% of the training data. An example story is given in appendix C. The goal is to train a model that
305   performs well on the retain set but poorly on the forget set, and whose forget set performance is not
306   easily recoverable by fine-tuning.
307   To do this, we route specific forget tokens to designated MLP neurons using a three-step process
308   termed Expand, Route, Ablate (ERA): 1. Expand: Increase the dimensionality of the model by
309   adding randomly-initialized neurons to particular target layers. 2. Route: train the model from
310   scratch by supervised learning on next-token prediction. On select tokens in forget stories, reduce
311   the learning rate (possibly below 0) in the original dimensions of the model at the target layers.
312   Figure 3 illustrates the routing step. 3. Ablate: delete the additional neurons. Post-ablation, apply a
313   very small number of steps of fine-tuning on retain data to correct for degradation caused by ablation.
314   Experiments. We compare ERA against three unlearning methods. (a) Data filtering discards a
315   model trained on all data, then re-trains from scratch on retain data only. By not training on forget
316   data, it serves as a gold standard for unlearning. (b) Representation misdirection for unlearning
317   (RMU) (Li et al., 2024) fine-tunes a model trained on all data to corrupt its internal representations
318   of forget data. It is a conventional post-hoc unlearning method. (c) DEMix plus ablation replaces all
319   MLPs with domain expert mixture layers (Gururangan et al., 2021) comprised of an MLP that oper-
320
      ates only on retain data and an MLP that only operates on forget data; after training the whole model
      on all data, the forget expert is ablated. DEMix plus ablation serves as an alternative localization-
321
      based approach.
322

363     4.2.3                          S CALING ROBUST UNLEARNING TO LARGER LANGUAGE MODELS
364
365    Gradient routing can localize capabilities in larger models. Motivated by the dual-use nature of AI

400   4.3     L OCALIZING BEHAVIORAL MODULES ENABLES SCALABLE OVERSIGHT IN
401           REINFORCEMENT LEARNING
402
403   In complex settings, reliable data labels are often scarce, especially when labeling requires human
404   input (Zhu et al., 2009; Stiennon et al., 2020; Bai et al., 2022; Baker et al., 2022). The aim of scalable
405
      oversight (Amodei et al., 2016; Finn et al., 2016) methods is to leverage limited supervision in order
      to effectively learn from unlabeled data. A challenge to scalable oversight is that label quality or
406
      availability may be correlated with task difficulty, as in weak-to-strong generalization (Burns et al.,
407
      2024). The correlation poses a key challenge to scalable oversight of a reinforcement learner: the
408   learner may indirectly condition its behavior on the extent to which we can oversee it, exploiting
409   limitations of the labels (Krakovna et al., 2020; Karwowski et al., 2024). In this section, we show
410   that gradient routing can be used to achieve scalable oversight even in this challenging setting: by
411   routing labeled data to submodules, we leverage unlabeled data to obtain a performant policy that
412   does not exploit the limits of our labels.
413
      We use gradient routing to train a policy to reach two types of squares in a gridworld, D IAMOND
414   and G HOST. The aim is to produce a policy that reliably navigates to D IAMOND and not G HOST.
415   However, access to the type of square reached, or oversight, is available only for some episodes.
416   Furthermore, we allow the policy to condition on the presence or absence of oversight, modeling an
417   agent that observes features that are not accessible to the supervisor. Because of this information
418   asymmetry, the desired agent behavior cannot be specified by any reward function. Despite this
419   challenging setup, gradient routing induces efficient learning by causing learning updates from the
420   two behaviors to accumulate in different parts of the network.
421   Partial oversight environment. The environment is based on a 5 × 5 gridworld Markov Decision
422   Process (Puterman, 1990) with two randomly placed terminal squares: D IAMOND and G HOST. At
423   each step, the agent can move north, west, south, or east. Episodes end upon reaching a terminal
424   square or after 32 steps. The environment state includes a boolean variable for every grid square
425   that indicates terminal squares that are under oversight. These indicators are sampled randomly
426   and independently at the beginning of each episode according to the oversight level p ∈ [0, 1]. If
427   an episode ends with the agent reaching a terminal grid square with the oversight indicator set to
428
      FALSE, then the reward function does not have access to the square type labels (D IAMOND, G HOST)
      for that entire episode. The policy takes the entire state as input, including oversight indicators.
429
430   Architecture and training. The policy network is a Mixture of Experts (MoE) network Eigen et al.
431   (2013) with two MLP experts (a D IAMOND expert and a G HOST expert) and a MLP gating network,
      each of which takes the environment state as input. The expert outputs are combined via a convex

469    Results. Gradient routing successfully localizes expert modules responsible for navigating to D IA -
       MOND and G HOST (fig. 5b), even when evaluated at states where oversight is not present. Utilizing
470
       the D IAMOND expert outperforms baselines and achieves nontrivial performance at oversight as low
471
       as 1% (fig. 5a). At 5% oversight, the D IAMOND expert reaches performance comparable to that of
472
       the other methods trained with 6x greater oversight. Importantly, we note that improved baseline
473    performance at high oversight levels is largely a consequence of a greater proportion of evaluation
474    environments having oversight, rather than good baseline performance when oversight is lacking.
475
476
       Further discussion, learning curves at 10% oversight, experiment details, and ablations are given in
       appendix F. We find that naive training exploits the limits of oversight, learning to avoid G HOST
477
       with oversight and seek nearby G HOST without oversight. We also find that data filtering fails to
478
       learn even in the limit of train time. In contrast, an oracle filtering baseline, which is able to observe
479    all end states but downsamples the training data based on the oversight level, eventually achieves
480    convergence. In summary, gradient routing is strictly better than feasible baselines at low oversight.

483    5                     D ISCUSSION
484
485    Gradient routing induces absorption. Routing a subset of the data related to some knowledge or
       capability appears to localize that knowledge or capability more generally. This held for an i.i.d.


                                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      subset of the data (TinyStories unlearning in section 4.2.2), and for semantically limited data (steer-
487   ing scalar in section 4.2.1, virology unlearning in section 4.2.3, scalable oversight in section 4.3).
488   Notably, this effect did not hold for DEMix, a modularity method in which localized modules are
489   sequestered so that only one (per layer) participates in each forward pass. To explain these obser-
490   vations, we posit absorption: (i) routing limited data to a region creates units of computation or
491   features that are relevant to a broader task; (ii) these units then participate in the model’s predictions
492   on related, non-routed data, reducing prediction errors on these data, so that (iii) the features are
493   not learned elsewhere. Absorption may also amplify the features causing it. When data labels are
494   semantically or quantitatively limited, absorption means that gradient routing can be useful even in
495
      cases where conventional training or data filtering methods are inadequate.
496   Mechanistic supervision avoids Goodharting. When the ability to label (or score) outcomes is
497   imperfect, attempting to suppress undesirable behavior via behavioral training is fraught (Good-
498   hart, 1984; Karwowski et al., 2024). In contrast, gradient routing provides mechanistic supervision,
499   influencing training without modifying the behavioral objective. We showed this empirically in sec-
500   tion 4.3, where an agent trained naively based on partially observed outcomes learned to pursue the
501
      user-desired outcome when observed but not otherwise. On the other hand, gradient routing utilized
      the same observations to induce the desired behavior mechanistically.
502
503   Entangled capabilities motivate gradient routing. In many machine learning problems, capabili-
504   ties are entangled in the sense that there are connections or dependencies between the computation
505   learned to perform different tasks (Arora & Goyal, 2023; de Chiusole & Stefanutti, 2013). Entan-
506   glement might occur because certain capabilities or behaviors are reinforced by a broad range of
507
      training objectives (Omohundro, 2008; Turner et al., 2021; Krakovna et al., 2020). More simply,
      capabilities required to perform desired tasks may overlap with those required to perform unde-
508
      sired tasks. For example, biological knowledge entails much of the knowledge required to construct
509
      biological weapons. For this reason, filtering or training against bioweapon-specific data might
510   not prevent a network from learning enough to create bioweapons from general biology sources or
511   would require such broad filtering so as to render the model useless at biology in general. In princi-
512   ple, gradient routing can avoid this by localizing a more limited subset of capabilities, then ablating
513   them.3 Alternatively, gradient routing could be employed to robustly detect when a given capability
514   is being used by the model (when a localized module strongly activates). This kind of monitoring
515   would provide an avenue for the application of access controls (Sandhu & Samarati, 1994; Samarati
516   & de Vimercati, 2001) to high-stakes AI deployment, as explored in appendix L.
517   Limitations and future work. (a) Gradient routing’s performance is sensitive to its hyperparame-
518   ters: what data to route on, what regions to localize to, and what mask weights to use. This makes it
519   hard to balance retain set performance vs. unlearning, for example. We suspect that methodological
520   improvements will reduce this sensitivity. (b) In our experiments with language models, we route
521   gradients on a token-by-token basis, ignoring neighboring tokens. This naive strategy is surprisingly
522   effective. However, it is plausible that contextual information will be critical in some problems,
523   necessitating routing strategies that depend on entire sequences. Finding practical ways of choosing
524
      what data to route in order to localize broad capabilities is an intriguing open problem. (c) Our
      empirical results for scalable oversight pertain to a simplistic, narrow setting. Furthermore, our
525
      method for scalable oversight requires that the ablated policy produce coherent behavior. This does
526
      not hold in general, so scaling oversight via localization may require new ideas. (d) We elaborate on
527   application-specific limitations in appendix A.
528
529
530   6    C ONCLUSION
531
532   Gradient routing enables data-driven supervision of the internal mechanisms learned by neural net-
533   works. Even when this supervision is based on simple or limited data labels, it can achieve robust
534   unlearning of pre-specified capabilities and scalable oversight. Consequently, gradient routing may
535   facilitate the safe deployment of AI systems, particularly in high-stakes scenarios where black-box
536   methods are insufficiently robust.
537
538       3
            Entangled capabilities present fundamental tradeoffs: the removal or attenuation of a capability may nec-
539   essarily harm capabilities entangled with it. The claim is not that gradient routing avoids this tradeoff, but that
      it plausibly enables more efficient tradeoffs.

1139        FUTURE WORK
1140
1141   MNIST autoencoders. The cleanly separated MNIST autoencoder representations depicted in
1142   fig. 2c depend on the problem setup (e.g. the choice to not use data augmentation, like rotations)
1143   and use of heavy L1 regularization on the encoding vector. L1 regularization is required because,
1144   by default, a regular MLP autoencoder trained on a subset of MNIST digits retains information
1145   necessary to decode other digits.
1146   For a wide range of hyperparameters, we find that gradient routing achieves quantitative representa-
1147   tion splitting: the Certicate’s reconstruction of digits 0–4 has higher average loss than its reconstruc-
1148   tions of digits 5–9 for a wide range of settings, including different partitions of the digits. However,
1149   outside the specific hyperparameters chosen for the results in the main body of the paper, the qual-
1150   itative results are poorer: the visual difference in reconstruction quality between the different digit
1151   subsets is less stark than in fig. 2c. We take this to highlight the problem-dependent characteristics
1152   of feature localization. In the case of autoencoding handwritten digits, separation of features for
1153
       encoding different digits is “unnatural,” so achieving it requires a specific setup and heavy regular-
       ization.
1154
1155   Language models. We speculate that gradient routing on particular tokens introduces an “internal
1156   tug of war” between the expanded and original dimensions of the model (these dimensions depicted
1157   in fig. 3), where parameter updates in the original dimensions consistently decrease the logits for
1158   routed tokens and parameter updates in the expanded dimensions increase logits for routed tokens.
1159
       This effect can be understood as a consequence of the mismatch between the implicit estimands
       (learning targets) for the original and expanded dimensions. We were concerned that this effect,
1160
       rather than localization of capabilities, explained the post-ablation increase in forget loss. However,
1161
       preliminary measurements suggest that this is not the case. For example, we find that the loss of
1162   ERA models is higher on average on non-routed forget tokens than a pure model, whereas it is lower
1163   on average on routed tokens. In general, the learning dynamics of gradient routing remain an open
1164   question.
1165
       If routing one token to a dimension of the residual stream creates an interpretable, axis-aligned
1166
       feature as discussed in section 4.2.1, then routing many tokens to many neurons could produce
1167
       a neural network with transparent internal representations. These representations might be made
1168   up of “individual neurons. . . [that] corresponded to cleanly interpretable features of the input,” as
1169   imagined in Elhage et al. (2022), or they could be organized in different ways. In principle, gradient
1170   routing provides a straightforward means of achieving this. However, we suspect that naive attempts
1171   to localize large numbers of concepts to unique regions will lead to high training loss.
1172
       Scalable oversight. Our reinforcement learning results demonstrate the promise of a localization-
1173
       based strategy for scalable oversight, but further empirical and conceptual work is needed. The
1174   toy environment we use is simple, lacking the complexity and asymmetries of real-world problems.
1175   Additionally, our proposed solution relies on the fact that ablating an otherwise-active module of a
1176   policy network produces a policy with coherent behavior, which may not be true in practice (and
1177   isn’t true in general, in principle). We discuss these considerations in appendix G.

2071   G     I MPACTS OF LOCALIZING CAPABILITIES VS . DISPOSITIONS FOR
2072         SCALABLE OVERSIGHT
2073
2074
       To achieve scalable oversight, our proposed strategy for preventing bad behavior (for example) is to
2075   (1) localize a submodule responsible for bad behavior, then (2) ablate the submodule. In this section,
2076   we one factor that may complicate this strategy in real-world applications.
2077
2078
       We distinguish between two types of processing that might occur within a neural network to cause
       some behavior, like navigating to a red tile in a gridworld. With respect to a particular behavior, we
2079
       define:
2080
2081   Capability. Processing that is necessary for engaging in the behavior; for example, feature extrac-
2082     tion and computation to detect a red tile and compute the shortest path to reach it.
2083   Disposition. Processing that is not a capability but that determines behavior (as a probability dis-
2084     tribution over network outputs). For example, a submodule that processes features representing
2085     the shortest path to a red tile and a blue tile and then returns action probabilities corresponding to
2086
         the red tile.
2087   These definitions are informal. Note: Similar terms have been used in the context of AI evaluations
2088   (Beverley et al., 2024), but, to the best of our knowledge, have not been formalized. See Beverley
2089   et al. (2024) for a philosophical treatment of related terms.
2090   Depending on whether capabilities or dispositions are to be localized, the application of gradient
2091   routing to scalable oversight faces different challenges, as summarized in table 4.
2092
2093   Table 4: An overview of the challenges to localizing capabilities vs. dispositions as a means of
2094   achieving scalable oversight. A checkmark (✓) indicates a step that we speculate is easy to achieve;
2095   a challenge indicates a fundamental difficulty.
2096
2097                                                        Localization during training        After ablating the target region
2098
           Localizing capabilities                        Challenge: entangled capabilities                  ✓
2099       Localizing dispositions                                       ✓                       Challenge: distribution shift
2100
2101
2102   In the case of capabilities localization, obtaining a performant policy post-ablation is straightforward
2103   in principle: by localizing and ablating, one has created an encoding of the state which does not
2104   admit any postprocessing which will exhibit the capability (analogous to the MNIST split encoding,
2105   whose bottom half did not admit any learned decoding for digits 0–4 as shown in fig. 2). In that
       case, one can simply train freeze this feature encoder and train on top of it. However, there is a


                                                                              39
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2106
       fundamental challenge: in many problems, capabilities may not factor because they are entangled.
2107   For example, the skills required to be a cybersecurity researcher vs. a hacker overlap significantly.
2108
2109   On the other hand, we speculate that localizing dispositions is straightforward, and suitable for prob-
2110
       lems where capabilities are entangled. For example, even if cybersecurity and hacking involve the
       same capabilities, we expect to be able to localize the disposition for (harmful) hacking. However,
2111
       localizing dispositions for scalable oversight does not permit post-ablation training, because further
2112
       training could change the agent’s disposition. Instead, we must either zero-shot ablate, or find an-
2113   other manner of post-training that avoids this issue (e.g. fine-tuning on high-quality labeled data
2114   only). The fundamental difficulty to zero-shot ablation is distribution shift: suppose that during the
2115   training of a policy, an internal module is learned that governs the policy outputs in some regions
2116   of state space but not others. If, upon ablation, that module “becomes responsible” for regions that
2117   were previously governed by an ablated component, there is no reason to expect it to perform well
2118   in these states which are, with respect to its role in training, off-distribution.

2120   H    C OMPUTATIONAL COST OF GRADIENT ROUTING
2121
2122   Memory. Storing edge weights for every data point would incur a hefty cost of O(|B||E|) memory
2123   per batch. In practice, this cost is easily avoided by reducing dependence on the amount of data and
2124   the number of edges. First: instead of assigning unique gradient routes to each data point, we assign
2125   routes according to membership in parts of a partition P of data points, reducing the |B| term to |P|.
2126   For example, in a typical unlearning application, we would use P = {Pretain , Pforget } with a single
2127   gradient route assigned to each set. Second: we restrict the set of edges considered. For example,
2128   using only edges leaving parameters reduces the |E| factor to O(p) if the neural net parameters have
2129
       dimensionality p. This amounts to choosing elementwise learning rates for each parameter entry,
       for each data point.
2130
2131   Runtime. In the general case, gradient routing requires |B||E| floating point operations to apply a
2132   scalar multiplication to each edge in the computational graph. Since we apply gradient routing to
2133   a sparse set of edges, like the dmodel entries of a hidden activation of a Transformer, the number of
2134   operations is much lower: |B| · dmodel , for example. This is negligible compared to the number of
2135
       operations required for matrix multiplication.

2137   I   E XTENDED LITERATURE REVIEW
2138
2139   We start by reviewing further works that, like gradient routing, modify learning rates or backpropa-
2140   gation.
2141   Adjusting learning rates. Discriminative fine-tuning (Howard & Ruder, 2018) sets the learning
2142   rate for each layer independently to improve training efficiency. You et al. (2017) introduce Layer-
2143   wise Adaptive Rate Scaling (LARS), which dynamically adjusts learning rates for each layer during
2144   training.
2145
       Modifying backpropagation. Sun et al. (2017b)’s meProp uses only the top-k dimensions by mag-
2146
       nitude of the gradient when updating parameters during training, which improves the accuracy of
2147   MNIST classifiers. Panda et al. (2024b) and Sung et al. (2021) optimize only a sparse subnetwork
2148   of a model during fine-tuning, minimizing catastrophic forgetting and memory usage. Rosenfeld &
2149   Tsotsos (2019) go a step further by updating only a small subset of parameters during pre-training,
2150   demonstrating competitive performance compared to conventional methods.
2151
       The methods above can be framed as multiplying the gradient by a mask vector. Mohtashami et al.
2152
       (2022) prove the theoretical convergence properties of binary gradient masking methods using a
2153   similar notation to our definition of gradient routing in Section 3.
2154
2155   Geiger et al. (2022b) train models to respect certain causal structure by applying interventions to
2156
       the forward pass and minimizing the difference between the actual output and the expected output
       according to a user-supplied causal model. This method could be used to localize capabilities by
2157
       ensuring some modules are causally relevant to certain outputs.
2158
2159   Fine-tuning parameter subsets. Many popular fine-tuning methods update only a small subset
       of parameters with the goal of computational efficiency or minimizing catastrophic forgetting or


                                                         40
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2160
       catastrophic interference (Sun et al., 2017a; Sung et al., 2021; Rosenfeld & Tsotsos, 2018; Kaplun
2161   et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022; Mallya & Lazebnik, 2018; Panda et al., 2024a).
2162   In some sense this localizes the new capabilities to this small subset of the network (as gradient
2163   routing does), although these tuned parameters may be activating latent abilities already present in
2164   the network (Ben Zaken et al., 2022).
2165
       Safe LoRA (Hsu et al., 2024) projects fine-tuned weights into a “safety-aligned subspace’, while
2166
       subspace-oriented model fusion (SOMF) (Yi et al., 2024) masks task vectors (Ilharco et al., 2023)
2167   such that they do not interfere with the subspace identified as relevant for safe behavior, before
2168   merging them into the model using model fusion (Zhang et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2023).
2169
2170
       Hierarchical reinforcement learning. Early work in hierarchical reinforcement learning used hand
       designed sub-behaviors assigned to individual modules to divide and conquer more complex tasks
2171
       (Maes & Brooks, 1990; Singh, 1992; Mahadevan & Connell, 1992) although later works discard this
2172
       approach in favor of automatically learned sub-behaviors (Hutsebaut-Buysse et al., 2022).
2173
2174   Disentangled representations. While gradient routing partitions representations using supervised
2175   training, disentangled representation learning attempts to separate representations in an unsuper-
2176
       vised manner (Bengio et al., 2013; Wang et al., 2024) using methods such as VAEs (Kingma &
       Welling, 2013; Mathieu et al., 2019) and GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2016).

2179   J   E XTENDED COMPARISONS TO OTHER MODULARITY METHODS
2180
2181   Some modular training techniques have similar aims as gradient routing. Others are mechanistically
2182   similar but are suitable for different problems. In this section, we compare gradient routing to a select
2183   few of these methods, explaining similarities and highlighting key differences. These comparisons
2184   clarify the novel aspects of gradient routing that enable its unique applications. Table 5 provides a
2185
       summary.
2186   DEMix Layers. Gururangan et al. (2021) introduce DEMix Layers, which are modular collections
2187   of MLP experts trained on different domains. In Transformer language models, they are interleaved
2188   with standard attention blocks.

2194               – Gradient routing decouples the localization of learning from the localization of com-
2195                 putation. With gradient routing, two data points (or losses) can be assigned to two
2196                 different network subregions, while both subregions still participate in inference for

2198

2200

2203                 might mean that the model does not learn the feature elsewhere. But in DEMix, in-
2204                 ducing a feature in one expert does nothing to prevent another expert from learning
2205                 the same feature, because there is no way a different expert can utilize a feature that is
2206                 not available in its forward pass.

2208                 any level of computation, like individual neurons, parameters, or activations. As a
2209                 consequence, gradient routing enables new kinds of localization. For example, we
2210                 achieve unprecedented control of learned representations in MNIST autoencoders in

2298   In this section, we discuss how to choose gradient routes in practice.
2299   Choosing gradient routes is like choosing a neural net architecture. Much like choosing a neural
2300   architecture, the choice of gradient routes is guided by intuition about neural net learning dynamics,
2301   data characteristics, and the needs of a particular application. Possible considerations include:
2302
2303          • Does the target subregion have sufficient representational capacity to learn the task routed
2304
                to it? (What proportion of the training data is being routed?)
2305          • Is the intended localization consistent with the neural network’s inductive biases? If not,
2306
                strong regularization may be needed.
2307          • Will part of the model be ablated after training? If so, training should be configured such
2308
                that model performance is minimally harmed by ablation.
2309
       Ultimately, gradient routes are chosen based on empirical performance and ease of use, on a
2310   problem-by-problem basis. Small-scale preliminary experiments are helpful.

2388   Towards auditable AI specialists. Here, we consider the implications of localization for advanced
2389   AI systems of increasing capability.
2390   General-purpose AI systems may be difficult to control or validate. For example, a factory planning
2391   AI with broad knowledge of economics might optimize its objective by manipulating market prices,
2392   while a research assistant AI with deep understanding of human psychology might shape its outputs
2393   to maximize positive evaluations rather than accuracy. In general, powerful AI systems may pur-
2394   sue unintended strategies enabled by capabilities beyond what is necessary for them to fulfill their
2395   intended function.
2396   By tailoring otherwise-general AI systems to specific tasks through the removal of unnecessary
2397   capabilities, we could make their behavior more predictable and verifiable. This aligns with the
2398   established principle of least privilege from computer security (Saltzer & Schroeder, 1975), where
2399   each component receives only the permissions required for its intended function. For any AI de-
2400   ployment, we can systematically evaluate which potentially-dangerous capabilities are necessary
2401   and remove those that are not. This removal could be verified through systematic testing, for exam-
2402   ple, by attempting to elicit the supposedly-removed capabilities through fine-tuning or automated
2403   red-teaming efforts.
2404   Alternatively, instead of removing capabilities entirely, we could apply access controls to limit which
2405   parties are able to utilize sensitive capabilities of a general model (Sandhu & Samarati, 1994; Sama-
2406   rati & de Vimercati, 2001). Gradient routing could allow overseers to robustly detect when certain
2407   capabilities are active by monitoring neural net modules with known functions.
