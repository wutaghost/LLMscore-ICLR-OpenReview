009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper leverages 3D Gaussian Splatting to tackle the challenging task of gen-
012            erating novel views of humans from given single-view images. Existing methods
013            typically adopt an indirect supervision manner, i.e., splat-based rasterization for
014            differentiable rendering. However, the intricate coupling of various 3D Gaussian
015            attributes complicates precise error backpropagation during optimization, often
016            resulting in convergence to local optima. In contrast, we propose a novel direct
017            paradigm to train a conditional diffusion model directly supervised by proxy-
018            ground-truth 3D Gaussian attributes. Specifically, we propose a two-stage con-
019
               struction process to derive consistent and smoothly distributed proxy-ground-truth
               3D Gaussian attributes. Subsequently, we train a point-based conditional diffu-
020
               sion model customized to learn the data distribution of these proxy attributes. The
021
               resulting diffusion model can generate the 3D Gaussian attributes for the input
022            single-view image, which are further rendered into novel views. Extensive exper-
023            imental results showcase the significant performance advancement of our method
024            over state-of-the-art approaches. Source code will be made publicly available.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Image-driven 3D human digitization has gained significant attention due to its rich applications in
030   film-making, game production, AR/VR, immersive telepresence. In recent years, there has emerged
031   a series of works performing novel view synthesis (NVS) from observed images of human captures.
032
033
      Image-based approaches (Saito et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2023; Ho et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024)
      infer the geometric surface of the human body and then perform texture estimation, which typically
034
      suffers from relatively lower resolution. Video/multi-view-based approaches (Mildenhall et al.,
035
      2020; Gao et al., 2022; Weng et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2024) can produce more fine-grained visual
036   appearance, but require multi-view images or monocular videos as inputs. SHERF (Hu et al., 2023)
037   makes the first attempt to build a single-view generalizable NeRF learning framework, but still lacks
038   the ability to restore accurate geometric details. More recently, 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023) rapidly
039   evolves, serving as a more powerful neural rendering pipeline, suggesting another promising way
040   (Zheng et al., 2024) of achieving real-time human NVS.
041
      The most common way of building generalizable 3DGS (Zheng et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2024) is to
042   train a parameterized neural model for generating the desired Gaussian attribute set, and then im-
043   pose supervision by comparing pixel-level differences between the rendered and observed images
044   (see Fig. 1(a)). However, the supervision gap between the pixel and attribute domains, as well
045   as the many-to-one mapping relationship between the Gaussian attribute set and rendered
046   image cast doubt on the training effectiveness. To address these issues, we propose to directly
047   impose attribute-level supervision by pre-creating proxy-ground-truth Gaussian attribute sets by cus-
048   tomizing a two-stage workflow, including per-scene overfitting and distribution unification.
049   By leveraging proxy-ground-truth Gaussian attributes, we establish a direct paradigm utilizing an
050   attribute-wise loss function for supervision during training (see Fig. 1(b)). We adopt a diffusion-
051   based framework to tackle this challenge as a conditional generation task, focusing on modeling
052   the distribution of 3D Gaussian attributes conditioned on a monocular image. While existing point-
053   based diffusion models are tailored for tasks like point cloud generation, completion, or upsam-
      pling (Luo & Hu, 2021; Zhou et al., 2021; Qu et al., 2024), they are not directly applicable to our

089           2.1      H UMAN R ECONSTRUCTION
090
091           Geometry Reconstruction. There exist various 3D scene representations used in different appli-
092           cations, such as voxels, point clouds, meshes, and implicit functions. Currently, implicit functions
093
              have been widely applied to recovering the geometric surfaces of the human body. Saito et al. (2019;
              2020) pioneeringly employed implicit functions for monocular human body reconstruction by re-
094
              gressing the occupancy value of each point in space and then using the marching cubes algorithm
095
              to extract the human body surface. However, due to relying solely on image features and lacking
096           human body priors, they often struggled with occluded humans. To address this issue, PAMIR and
097           ICON (Zheng et al., 2021; Xiu et al., 2022) introduce the parametric human model SMPL to make
098           implicit functions aware of human body structure. SiFU (Zhang et al., 2024) employs a text-to-
099           image diffusion model to predict invisible information and generate realistic results. However, the
100           strong constraints of SMPL make implicit-based methods heavily reliant on the accuracy of SMPL
101           estimation, and they typically require numerous query points to extract 3D surfaces. In comparison,
102           HaP (Tang et al., 2023), a purely explicit-based method, offers greater flexibility in manipulating the
103           human body as a point cloud in 3D space, enabling unconstrained-topology modeling of arbitrarily-
104
              clothed human body shapes. Owing to its efficiency and effectiveness, we utilize HaP to generate
              the 3D Gaussian positions in this paper.
105
106           Novel View Synthesis. Recently, an implicit neural radiance representation (NeRF) (Mildenhall
107           et al., 2020) has achieved great success in synthesizing high-quality novel views. By equipping the
              parametric human model SMPL, NeRF-based (Jiang et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2021) methods do not re-


                                                                                      2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      quire ground truth 3D geometry information and are capable of rendering such high-quality views of
109   humans. NeuralBody (Peng et al., 2021) innovatively adopts NeRF for human novel view synthesis
110   by learning the structured latent code of the canonical SMPL model among different frames. Hu-
111   manNerf (Weng et al., 2022) decomposes the rigid skeleton motion and non-rigid clothes motion in
112   monocular videos to learn a better NeRF representation in the canonical space. More recently, some
113   3DGS-based methods (Hu et al., 2024; Kocabas et al., 2024) have attempted to learn 3D Gaussian
114   attributes in the canonical space for fast rendering speed. However, these methods typically require
115   monocular or multi-view videos as input for per-scene optimization and cannot be generalized to
116   other scenes.
117
118   2.2   G ENERALIZABLE N OVEL V IEW S YNTHESIS
119
120   Large language models have achieved remarkable success in text-to-image generation. While sev-
121   eral methods (Liu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Xue et al., 2024) attempted to exploit this strong
122   imaginative ability to generate novel views of a single image with text prompts, they face difficul-
123
      ties in maintaining consistency among different views due to the absence of 3D information for
      supervision. Zhao et al. (2022) and Gao et al. (2022) projected the SMPL models to multi-view
124
      images and combine the image features with the canonical SMPL features to implement generaliz-
125
      able human NeRFs. SHERF Hu et al. (2023) further proposed a hierarchical feature map to realize
126   a generalizable single-view human NeRF. More recently, several works attempt to train generaliz-
127   able 3DGS models (Liu et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; Wang
128   et al., 2024). (Zou et al., 2024) combines the triplane representation and the 3DGS representation,
129   following PIFu to prepare the pixel-aligned feature for training a generalizable 3DGS model.
130   Zheng et al. (2024) designed a generalizable multi-view human novel view synthesis framework
131   by composing an iterative depth estimation module and a Gaussian parameter regression module.
132   These generalizable 3DGS methods follow the indirect paradigm. In contrast, we introduce a direct
133   paradigm that supervises the training process with ground truth 3D Gaussian attributes to provide
134
      attribute-wise loss.

172   3.2      T RAINING PARADIGM OF G ENERALIZABLE F EED -F ORWARD 3DGS
173
174   In contrast to the conventional working mode of per-scene overfitting, many recent studies are de-
175   voted to constructing generalizable 3DGS frameworks (Liu et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024; Zou et al.,
176   2024; Zheng et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024) via shifting the actual optimization target from the Gaus-
177   sian attribute set to a separately parameterized learning model M(∗; Θ), where ∗ denotes network
178   inputs and Θ denotes network parameters. Generally, we can summarize that all such approaches
179
      uniformly share the same training paradigm, where the learning model M(·, ·) consumes its input
      to generate scene-specific Gaussian attributes at the output end. Through differentiable rendering
180
      R, these approaches impose pixel-level supervision in the image domain, as formulated below:
181
                                        XK
182
                                   min         ℓpmet (R(M(∗; Θ); V (k) ), I(k) ).                         (4)
183                                                                 Θ          k=1
184   Though such a training paradigm is reasonable and straightforward, the learning effectiveness can
185   be weakened due to the gap between the image domain where supervision is imposed and the actu-
186   ally desired Gaussian attribute domain. More importantly, different Gaussian attribute sets can
187   produce the same rendered image. Such a many-to-one mapping relationship can complicate
188   the solution space and cause certain degrees of confusion for model learning.
189   The above-analyzed drawbacks of the existing mainstream training paradigm naturally motivate us
190   to get rid of what we call the supervision gap. Accordingly, we propose a paradigm shift by directly
191   imposing attribute-level supervision. Under our targeted setting with single-view image Is as input,
192   the proposed training paradigm can be formulated as:
193

194                                                         Θ

197   Overall, our proposed single-view generalizable human 3DGS learning framework consists of two
198
      core processing phases: 1) creating proxy-ground-truth Gaussian attributes as supervision signals,
      and 2) training a conditional diffusion model for Gaussian attribute generation, as introduced in the
199
      following Sections 3.3 and 3.4.

265   3.4   D IRECT G AUSSIAN ATTRIBUTE D IFFUSION
266
267   Having created a collection of proxy-ground-truth Gaussian attributes {Âj }Jj=1 as supervision sig-
268   nals, we shift attention to modeling the target distribution q(A|Is ) conditioned on the input single-
269   view image Is to predict the desired Gaussian attribute set A. First, we tend to separately predict
      Gaussian positions, which is essentially a 3D point cloud. Second, we treat the obtained point cloud

270
      as geometric priors and extract human-centered features, then feed them into a conditional diffusion
271   pipeline for diffusing the rest types of Gaussian attributes.
272
      Generation of Gaussian Positions. In the training phase of the generalizable human 3DGS frame-
273                                                      (n)
      work, we directly use the Gaussian positions {p̂j }N   n=1 prepared in the proxy-ground-truth creation
274
      process. In the inference phase, we need to specifically estimate the Gaussian positions from the in-
275
      put image. In our implementation, we design a position generator with the rectification of the SMPL
276   parametric human model.
277
      The position generator begins with monocular depth estimation (Patni et al., 2024) to generate from
278   Is the corresponding depth map, which is converted into a partial 3D point cloud. In parallel, we
279   also estimate from Is the corresponding SMPL model, whose pose is further rectified by the partial
280   point cloud. Then, we feed the rectified SMPL model and the partial point cloud into a point cloud
281   generation network to output the desired set of 3D Gaussian positions. To promote the uniformity
282   of point density, we perform point cloud upsampling and then apply farthest point sampling. In this
283   way, we can stably obtain a set of accurate 3D Gaussian positions {p(n) }N   n=1 as geometric priors.
284   Extraction of Human-Centered Features. To supplement more informative conditioning signals
285   for the subsequent attribute diffusion, we further extract two aspects of human-centered features.
286   The first is pixel-aligned features for providing visual appearance information. To achieve this, we
287   project the 3D Gaussian positions onto the input image space. Then we utilize (Brooks et al., 2023)
288   to predict a back-view image Iback with respect to the view of Is . The visible and invisible partitions
289   of {p(n) }Nn=1 are respectively projected onto Is and Iback , and the feature maps corresponding to Is
290   and Iback are extracted via 2D CNNs. Finally, we concatenate the visible and invisible pixel-aligned
291   features to form the pixel-aligned feature β (n) .
292   The second is SMPL-semantic features for strengthening the awareness of human body structure.
293   To achieve this, we incorporate semantic labels defined on SMPL. We perform the nearest neighbor
294   searching to identify the nearest points of the 3D Gaussians on the SMPL surface. For each 3D
295
      Gaussian, we retrieve the corresponding nearest SMPL point index, the distance, and the semantic
      label, which are embedded into the latent space through MLPs. The resulting feature embeddings
296
      are concatenated to assign each point the SMPL-semantic feature γ (n) .
297

299   SMPL-semantic features γ (n) , we perform condition diffusion to generate the rest attributes in-

301

      separate the diffusion of SH coefficients and the other three types of attributes.

343   evaluated H UMAN -DAD on Thuman2 (20 humans), CityuHuman (20 humans) (Tang et al., 2023),
344   2K2K (25 humans) (Han et al., 2023) and CustomHuman (40 humans) (Ho et al., 2023). We adopted
345   the peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR), structural similarity index (SSIM), and Learned Perceptual
346   Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS) as evaluation metrics.
347   When constructing the proxy ground truth 3D Gaussian attributes, we rendered 360 views for each
348   human, and we uniformly sampled 20000 points from each human surface, which was the initial
349   3D Gaussian position. In the first stage, we utilized a Point Transformer as the backbone to predict
350   the 3D Gaussian attributes. For each human subject, we overfitted the Point Transformer for 4000
351   epochs, using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.0002. In the second stage, we employed
352   another Point Transformer as the backbone. The batch size was set to 4, the number of epochs was set
353   to 1300, and we continued to use the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.0002. Other settings
354   of 3DGS were following (Kerbl et al., 2023). To train H UMAN -DAD, we designed the attribute
355
      diffusion model using PointNet++ as the backbone1 . ResNet18 with the pre-trained weights was
      used to extract the image feature when preparing the pixel-aligned feature. During training, the
356
      batch size was set to 4, the number of epochs was set to 300, the optimizer was Adam, and the
357
      learning rate was 0.0002.

      5       C ONCLUSION
525
526
      We have introduced a novel direct paradigm for training a generalizable 3DGS model of the hu-
      man body. In crafting this direct paradigm, we implemented a two-stage process to acquire proxy
527
      ground truth 3D Gaussian attributes. Furthermore, we devise a diffusion model to capture the global
528
      distribution of 3D Gaussian. Extensive experimental results have demonstrated the significant supe-
529   riority of H UMAN -DAD over the current state-of-the-art methods. We will explore the potential of
530   H UMAN -DAD to support multi-view images (Kwon et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2023). At present, the
531   direct paradigm has only been applied to the monocular human rendering task. In future work, we
532   intend to explore and validate its effectiveness in a broader range of 3DGS application scenarios.

      Subsequently, a point transformer is employed to extract point-wise features from the human point
747
      cloud. These point-wise features are then concatenated with the human point cloud and input into
748   various MLPs to learn different 3D Gaussian attributes. In the second stage of overfitting, we also
749   incorporate spherical harmonics features into the point transformer to unify the distribution across
750   different scenes. For the MLPs responsible for Scale, Rotation, and Opacity, we further enhance
751   their geometric perception by inputting the KNN graph of the point cloud.
