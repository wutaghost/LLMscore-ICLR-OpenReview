009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Modern privacy regulations have spurred the evolution of machine unlearning, a
012            technique enabling a trained model to efficiently forget specific training data. In
013            prior unlearning methods, the concept of “data forgetting” is often interpreted and
014            implemented as achieving zero classification accuracy on such data. Neverthe-
015
               less, the authentic aim of machine unlearning is to achieve alignment between
               the unlearned model and the gold model, i.e., encouraging them to have identical
016
               classification accuracy. On the other hand, the gold model often exhibits non-zero
017
               classification accuracy due to its generalization ability. To achieve aligned data
018            forgetting, we propose a Twin Machine Unlearning (TMU) approach, where a
019            twin unlearning problem is defined corresponding to the original unlearning prob-
020            lem. Consequently, the generalization-label predictor trained on the twin problem
021            can be transferred to the original problem, facilitating aligned data forgetting.
022            Additionally, we introduce a noise-perturbed fine-tuning scheme to balance the
023            trade-off between retaining the model’s generalization ability and enhancing its
024            resilience to Membership Inference Attacks. Comprehensive empirical experi-
025            ments illustrate that our approach significantly enhances the alignment between
026
               the unlearned model and the gold model. The code is available here.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Machine learning model providers tend to collect extensive data from the Internet and utilize it to
031   train their machine learning models. The recent introduction of data privacy and protection regula-
032   tions (European Union’s GDPR Regulation (2018), and California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA)
033   Goldman (2020)) obligate these model providers to comply with the request-to-delete Dang (2021)
034   from the data owner. For example, a corporation offering facial recognition services might acquire
035   facial images from the Internet to train their facial recognition models. Subsequently, a user might
036
      discover that the company has utilized his/her facial images in model training. In such a scenario,
      the user reserves the right to petition the company to revise the facial recognition model to forget
037
      his/her facial image data.
038
039   The straightforward solution is to entirely discard the trained model, delete his/her facial images
040   from the training data, and re-train a new model from scratch (i.e., called gold model). Unfortunately,
041   re-training from scratch is expensive. Therefore, machine unlearning Garg et al. (2020); Ginart et al.
042
      (2019); Cao & Yang (2015); Gupta et al. (2021); Sekhari et al. (2021); Brophy & Lowd (2021);
      Ullah et al. (2021) has garnered considerable attention, aiming to efficiently revise a trained model
043
      to forget a cohort of training data, without affecting the performance on the remaining data.
044
045   In most prior unlearning methods Graves et al. (2021); Tarun et al. (2023), the “forgetting” of a
046   cohort data Df is commonly interpreted and implemented by decreasing the classification accuracy

069   In this paper, we denote the samples x ∈ Df that can be correctly classified by the gold model as
070
      the ‘easy’ samples. Conversely, the samples that cannot be correctly classified are referred to as the
      ‘hard’ samples. As these easy/hard labels for Df are defined based on the model’s generalization
071
      ability, we refer to them as generalization-labels. Therefore, a well-aligned unlearning algorithm
072
      should specifically decrease the classification accuracy on hard samples rather than all samples
073   in Df . In other words, it is essential to first identify the hard samples from Df , and subsequently
074   reduce the classification accuracy on such samples. However, since the gold model is unknown,
075   obtaining these generalization-labels becomes challenging. This is the reason why prior unlearning
076   methods have to adopt achieving zero classification accuracy as the objective of data forgetting.
077
      In this paper, we aim to achieve aligned data forgetting. To this end, we propose to train a binary
078
      classifier to predict the generalization-labels for Df . With these predicted labels, Df is partitioned
079
      into an easy subset Dfe and a hard subset Dfh . Subsequently, we specifically decrease the classifica-
080
      tion accuracy on Dfh , while retaining the classification accuracy on Dfe . The most challenging aspect
081
      of our approach lies in training such a binary classifier on a specific dataset, which involves two sub-
082
      challenges: (1) Build a specific labeled dataset for training the binary classifier; (2) Construct a
083
      discriminative representation for the binary classifier.
084
085   To address the first challenge, we formulate a twin unlearning problem corresponding to the original
086   one. Specifically, the original model Mo is fine-tuned with Dtest to produce the Twin Model Mt .
087
      As a result, in the context of Mt , the original model Mo can be seen as analogous to Mt ’s gold
      model (i.e., Mtg = Mo ), where Dtest can be seen as analogous to Mt ’s forgetting data, as shown in
088
      Fig.1. In another word, given a trained model Mt , the Twin Unlearning Problem is to forget Dtest
089
      from Mt . Note that in the twin unlearning problem, the gold model is known Mtg = Mo .
090
091   The reason for formulating such a twin unlearning problem is that we can easily obtain a labeled
092   dataset – the dataset Dtest . Since the gold model of the twin unlearning problem is known (i.e., Mo ),
093
      we can easily obtain the generalization-labels for Dtest , specifically by employing Mo to make class
      predications on Dtest .
094
095   Regarding the second challenge, we propose to integrate three complementary features, each pos-
096   sessing strong discriminability to distinguish between easy and hard samples. (1) The first feature
097   is the Distance Feature (DF). For each sample x in Df , we identify its nearest neighbor in Dr ,
098   and the distance between them is considered as a feature. Intuitively, if x is an easy sample, it
099
      typically has similar samples in Dr , resulting in a small distance. In contrast, the distance feature
      for a hard sample tends to be large. (2) The second feature is the Adversarial-attack Feature (AF).
100
      We construct this feature by employing adversarial attacksMadry et al. (2017); Goodfellow et al.
101
      (2014); Moosavi-Dezfooli et al. (2017), which aim to fool a trained model into making incorrect
102   predictions with small adversarial perturbations. Clearly, hard samples are more vulnerable to ad-
103   versarial attacks compared to easy samples due to their proximity to the decision boundary. Hence,
104   the results of adversarial attack can be considered a discriminative feature.(3) The third feature is
105   the Curriculum-learning-loss Feature (CF). Curriculum learning theory Kumar et al. (2010); Tullis
106   & Benjamin (2011); Bengio et al. (2009) suggests that easy samples are learned earlier than hard
107   samples. Therefore, the loss value of the first fine-tuning epoch is considered a feature to distinguish
      between easy and hard samples.

      At last, in addition to reducing the classification accuracy on the hard subset Dfh , it is equally crucial
109
      to maintain the model’s generalization ability on the easy subset Dfe , ensuring model can correctly
110
      classify those samples. A naive approach to achieving this is by directly minimizing their classifica-
111   tion loss during machine unlearning. However, such an approach would leave the model vulnerable
112   to Membership Inference Attack (MIA), which is commonly used to assess the success of data for-
113   getting. To address this, we propose a noise-perturbed fine-tuning scheme. This scheme minimizes
114   the classification loss on a noise-perturbed version of Dfe . By doing so, we could strike a balance
115   between preserving the model’s generalization ability and enhancing its resilience to MIA.
116
      Overall, we summarize our contributions as follows.
117
118
             • We emphasize alignments in data forgetting and introduce a Twin Machine Unlearning
119            (TMU) approach to enhance such alignments.
120
121          • We devise three discriminative features to distinguish easy and hard samples, employing
122            adversarial attack and curriculum learning strategies.
123          • To withstand the assessment of data forgetting from MIA, we propose a noise-perturbed
124            fine-tuning scheme to enhance model’s resilience to MIA.

127   2     P ROBLEM OF DATA F ORGETTING
128
129   Let D = {xi , yi }Ni=1 be a dataset of images xi , each with a label yi ∈ {1, . . . K} representing a
130   class. The original model Mo (θ) is trained with the D, which could be a DNN with parameters θ.
131
      Let Df be a subset of the D, whose information we want to forget from the trained model Mo (θ).
132   The Df is called the forgetting data, which could be all or some of the data with a given label k, i.e.,
133   corresponding to forgetting an entire class or a subset of a class, respectively. In this paper, we are
134   particularly interested in the case of forgetting a subset of a class, as the alignment problem becomes
135   more critical in this scenario. The remaining data is denoted by Dr , whose information is desired
136   to be kept unchanged in the model. Df and Dr together represent the entire training set D and are
137   mutually exclusive, i.e., Dr ∪ Df = D and Dr ∩ Df = ∅.
138   Since Df has been used to train the Mo (θ), the model parameters θ will contain information about
139   Df . The task of data forgetting aims to forget the information of Df from the trained model
140   Mo (θ). The ideal solution is to train a new model from scratch with Dr , which is denoted as the
141   gold model Mog (θr ). However, obtaining the gold model is time-consuming due to the expensive
142   re-training procedure. Thus, the practical data forgetting aims to efficiently revise the original model
143   θ with a ‘scrubbing/forgetting’ function s(), so that the revised model s(θ) is as close to the gold
144   model θr as possible. The s() is often implemented by a machine unlearning algorithm.
145

      3.2     D ISCRIMINATIVE F EATURES
249   Besides building a specific labeled dataset for training the binary classifier, another core of our
250   approach is to construct discriminative features for the classifier. Briefly, we propose to integrate
251   three complementary features by employing adversarial attack and curriculum learning strategies.
252
253   3.2.1    D ISTANCE F EATURE (DF)
254
255   In the case of forgetting a subset of a class k, the remaining data Dr will still contain samples
256
      belonging to the forgetting class k. Therefore, for each sample x ∈ Df , we can find some samples
      x′ ∈ Dr similar to x. The similarity can be measured by the distance l(x, x′ ) with respect to a
257
      feature extractor g(),
258
259                                           l(x, x′ ) = |g(x) − g(x′ )|                                   (4)
260
261   For each x ∈ Df , we can identify the top-N similar samples x′i ∈ Dr , (i = 0, . . . , N − 1).
262
      Intuitively, these distances l(x, x′i ) for an easy sample would be smaller than that for a hard sample.
      Thus, the concatenation of these top-N distances l(x, x′i ), (i = 0, . . . , N − 1) can be regarded as
263
      a discriminative feature to distinguish between easy and hard samples. In practice, we adopt the
264
      activation value of the penultimate layer (i.e., the layer just before the last fully-connected layer) as
265   the g().

267   3.2.2    A DVERSARIAL - ATTACKING F EATURE (AF)
268
269   Whether a sample can be correctly generalized or not (i.e., belong to a easy or hard sample) is closely
      related to the positional relation between it and the classification boundary. If it is near the boundary,

      it is hard to correctly classify it with high confidence. Thus, the easy samples often stay far from the
271   boundary, while the hard samples tend to stay near the boundary.
272
273   In order to leverage such positional relation knowledge to distinguish between easy and hard sam-
274
      ples, we employ the untargeted adversarial attack technique. As we know, given any sample x,
      we can generate a corresponding adversarial sample x̃ whose classification result is different from
275
      its ground-truth label. The adversarial sample x̃ is often generated by perturbing x with a certain
276
      perturbation r. The perturbation r is typically constrained by a certain attack budget ϵ, as follows,

      The essence of adversarial attack is to move x across the classification boundary, i.e., changing
      the classification result. Thus, given a sufficiently large budget ϵ, we can successfully conduct the
282
      adversarial attack on any sample, i.e., successfully generating x̃.
283
284   Intuitively, the attack budget for a sample is related to the positional relation between it and the
285   classification boundary. If the sample is near the boundary, a small budget is enough to conduct
286   a successful attack. Thus, we can distinguish between hard and easy samples like that: we use a
287
      relatively small attack budget to conduct adversarial attack, and we identify easy and hard samples
      based on whether the attack is successful or fails, i.e., hard samples can be successfully attacked
288
      with a small attack budget, since it is near the classification boundary.
289
290   Furthermore, the bipolar results about successful or failure attack cannot be regarded as a good
291   continuous discriminative feature. Instead, we compute the classification score/logits s(x̃) for the
292   adversarial sample x̃. On the other hand, we calculate the classification score s(x) for the clean
293
      sample x, and measure the cross-entropy between s(x̃) and s(x),
294                                         dist(x, x̃) = H(s(x̃), s(x))                                    (6)

299   3.2.3    C URRICULUM - LEARNING - LOSS F EATURE (CF)
300
301
      Curriculum learning theory suggests that easy samples are learned earlier than hard samples. Thus,
      we propose to employ a curriculum learning strategy to build our third feature. Curriculum learning
302
      has illustrated that neural networks tend to learn easy samples very quickly at the beginning of the
303
      training iterations. Conversely, the hard samples are learned at the later iterations. Specifically, we
304   train a randomly-initialized network Mr from scratch, where the architecture of Mr is the same as
305   the architecture of original model Mo . To distinguish between easy and hard samples, we just train
306   the model Mr for one or two epochs, instead of completing the full training procedure.
307
      Regarding whether a sample x has been well-learned by Mr , we use the loss loss(x) as a metric,
308
      i.e., a small loss value implies that x has been well learned (indicating it is a easy sample) while a
309
      large loss value implies that x has not been well learned (indicating it is a hard sample). Therefore,
310   the curriculum-learning-loss loss(x) is considered as the third feature to distinguish between easy
311   and hard samples.
312
313
      Note that the model Mr is trained with a part of Dr + Df and Dtest . In practice, we find that it is
      enough to train Mr by just utilizing 30% of all Dr + Df . Thus, the training of Mr is much cheaper
314
      than the training of Mo .

      3.3     B INARY C LASSIFIER
317
318   We have build a specific labeled dataset Dtest . On the other hand, we have devised three discrimi-
319   native features to distinguish between easy and hard samples. Next, we will train a binary classifier
320   to distinguish between easy and hard samples with these feature over the labeled dataset. Specifi-
321   cally, we employ an MLP network with two hidden layers as the binary classifier. We concatenate

      4.1     E XPERIMENTAL SETTING
327
328   Datasets & Models. We evaluate our approach using three public image datasets: CIFAR-10,
329   CIFAR-100 Krizhevsky et al. (2009) and VGGFaces2 Cao et al. (2018). CIFAR-10 comprises 10
330   classes, and we perform data forgetting evaluations for each class independently. For CIFAR-100,
331   we randomly select 17 out of 100 classes for evaluation due to space constraints. Since we emphasize
332   on forgetting only a subset of one class, we randomly select 100 images as Df . For VGGFaces2,
333   we randomly select 10 out of 100 celebrities as the forgetting class, and then randomly choose 50
334
      facial images to comprise Df for each forgetting class.
335   Three common image classification neural networks are employed for evaluation: ResNet-18 He
336   et al. (2016), AllCNN Springenberg et al. (2014), and Vision Transformer Lee et al. (2021). The
337   original models are trained for 200 epochs using Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) optimizer with
338   a momentum of 0.9, weight decay of 5e − 4, and an initial learning rate of 0.01. The learning rate is
339   divided by 10 after 100 and 150 epochs.
340   Baseline Methods. We compare our approach against four machine unlearning methods: (1) Neg-
341   ative Gradient Golatkar et al. (2020a): fine-tune the original model on D by increasing the loss for
342   samples in Df , which is the common surrogate objective adopted by most unlearning methods. (2)
343   Fine-tuning: fine-tune the model on Dr using a slightly large learning rate. This is analogous to
344   catastrophic forgetting, as fine-tuning without Df may cause the model to forget Df . (3) Random
345   Labeling Graves et al. (2021): fine-tune the model on D by assigning random labels to samples in
346
      Df , causing those samples to receive a random gradient. (4) Bad Teacher Chundawat et al. (2023):
      it explores the utility of competent and incompetent teachers in a student-teacher framework to in-
347
      duce forgetfulness. Note that this work is closely related to our approach since it also emphasizes
348
      alignments in data forgetting.
349
350   Evaluation Metrics. We assess the alignment quality of unlearning methods using three metrics: (1)
351   Accuracy on Df and Dtest : Ideally, the unlearned model is expected to achieve the same accuracy
                                                     g
352
      as the gold model. Let ACCDf and ACCD            f
                                                         denote the accuracy of the unlearned model and
                                                                                                  g
353   the gold model on Df . Thus, the differences between them, ∆ = |ACCDf − ACCD                  f
                                                                                                      |, can be
354   considered as the measure of alignment quality. Additionally, accuracy on Dtest is employed to
355   assess whether the model accuracy is compromised after data forgetting. (2) Activation Distance:
356
      This metric calculates the average L2-distance between the activation values of the unlearned model
      and the gold model on Df . A smaller activation distance indicates a better alignment between two
357
      models. (3) Membership Inference Attack (MIA): MIA aims to determine whether a given sample
358
      was included in a model’s training set. Consequently, if we have an ideal MIA, we can employ it to
359   evaluate the success of data forgetting. The lower the success rate of the MIA, the more effective the
360   data forgetting process. Thus, the attack success rate (ASR) is employed as a key evaluation metric
361   for data forgetting effectiveness. Although a perfect MIA is unattainable, we utilize a state-of-the-art
362   MIA approach Ye et al. (2022) in our evaluation.

532   5     C ONCLUSION
533
534   This paper aims at the task of data forgetting, emphasizing the alignment in data forgetting. We
535   introduce a Twin Machine Unlearning approach, where a twin unlearning problem is constructed
536   and leveraged to solve the original problem. Furthermore, three discriminative features are devised
537   by employing adversarial attack and curriculum learning strategies. Additionally, to withstand the
538   assessment of data forgetting from Membership Inference Attack, we propose a noise-perturbed
539   fine-tuning scheme to enhance model’s resilience to MIA. Extensive empirical experiments show
      that our approach significantly improve the alignment in data forgetting.
