009                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011                Statistical watermarking offers a theoretically-sound method for distinguishing
012                machine-generated texts. In this work, we first present a systematic theoretical
013                analysis of the statistical limits of watermarking, by framing it as a hypothesis test-
014                ing problem. We derive nearly matching upper and lower bounds for (i) the optimal
015                Type II error under a fixed Type I error, and (ii) the minimum number of tokens
016                required to watermark the output. Our rate of Θ(h−1 log(1/h)) for the minimum
017
                   number of required tokens, where h is the average entropy per token, reveals a
                   significant gap between the statistical limit and the O(h−2 ) rate achieved in prior
018
                   works. To our knowledge, this is the first comprehensive statistical analysis of
019
                   the watermarking problem. Building on our theory, we develop SEAL (Semantic-
020                awarE speculAtive sampLing), a novel watermarking algorithm for practical ap-
021                plications. SEAL introduces two key techniques: (i) designing semantic-aware
022                random seeds by leveraging a proposal language model, and (ii) constructing a
023                maximal coupling between the random seed and the next token through speculative
024                sampling. Experiments on open-source benchmarks demonstrate that our water-
025                marking scheme delivers superior efficiency and tamper resistance, particularly in
026                the face of paraphrase attacks.

050   1      I NTRODUCTION
051
052   Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language tasks (Brown et al., 2020;
053   Bubeck et al., 2023; Chowdhery et al., 2023). LLMs excel at generating human-like texts that can be
      difficult to distinguish from those written by human (OpenAI, 2023; Team et al., 2023; Anthropic,


                                                                  1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2024). This capability raises several societal concerns regarding the misuse of LLM outputs. For
055   instance, LLM-generated texts might contaminate training datasets for future language models (Shu-
056   mailov et al., 2023; Das et al., 2024), facilitate the spread of misleading information (Zellers et al.,
057   2019; Vincent, 2022), or be used in academic misconduct (Jarrah et al., 2023; Milano et al., 2023).
058   The widespread use of LLMs underscores the need for effective detection methods to identify whether
059   a human-like text is produced by an LLM system.
060
      To detect machine-generated content, recent research works (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023a; Kuditipudi
061   et al., 2023; Christ et al., 2023; Yoo et al., 2023; Fernandez et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
062   2023; Yang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023; Koike et al., 2023; Li et al.,
063   2024; Ren et al., 2024; Liu & Bu, 2024; Hou et al., 2024b;a) have proposed the use of statistical
064   watermarks. These are signals embedded within generated texts to reveal their source. Statistical
065   watermarking modifies the decoding mechanism of LLMs so that the text output X is sampled jointly
066   with a sequence of random seeds S. Consequently, outputs from a watermarked LLM are always
067   correlated with the accompanying random seeds, while texts generated from other sources (e.g.,
068   human writers) are not. Therefore, detecting whether X is generated by an LLM reduces to testing
069
      the independence of S and X. This procedure can be framed as a hypothesis test with two competing
      hypotheses:
070
071                                H0 : X is sampled independently from S,
072                                H1 : (X, S) is sampled from a joint distribution
073   Here, the null hypothesis H0 implies that X is not generated by the watermarked LLM, while
074   the alternative hypothesis H1 implies that X is sampled from the watermarked LLM with joint
075   distribution P.
076
      The major benefit of statistical watermarking is that it comes with formal statistical guarantees (Aaron-
077   son, 2022b; Kuditipudi et al., 2023; Christ et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024). Specifically,
078   these guarantees control the following three quantities:
079
080         1. Distortion (Bias): The distance between the watermarked LLM and the original LLM.
081         2. Type I error: The probability that an independently sampled output X is incorrectly rejected
082            as being generated by the watermarked LLM.
083         3. Type II error: The probability that an output from the watermarked LLM fails to be detected.
084
085   Despite these statistical guarantees, theoretical understanding of their statistical limits remains
086   unsatisfied. For example, one may wonder, à la Neyman-Pearson:
087           Q1: What is the best achievable Type II error under a fixed Type I error constraint?
088
089   In practice, users often modify text outputs from large language models (LLMs) or insert their
090   own human-written content. This complicates the task of identifying whether an article is entirely
091   generated by LLMs, while highlighting the need to detect specific sequences of words produced
092   by LLMs. For practitioners, a key consideration is to minimize the number of tokens used for
093
      watermarking and detection of a sequence. This leads to an important question:
094              Q2: What is the minimum number of tokens required to watermark the output?
095
096   To the best of our knowledge, these questions have not been addressed in prior research. As discussed
097   in Section 1.1, previous rates on the number of tokens consistently fails to surpass h−2 where h is the
098   average entropy per token, and it remains unknown whether this rate is optimal at all.
099   A key objective of theoretical analysis is to guide the development of practical algorithms. In light
100   of the aforementioned questions raised, it is essential to translate theoretical insights into improved
101   watermarking design. Therefore, we pose a final question:
102
103
           Q3: Can statistical theory be leveraged to design more effective and robust watermarking
                                                 algorithms?
104

131   1.1   R ELATED WORKS
132
133   Watermarking is a powerful white-box method for detecting LLM-generated texts (Tang et al., 2023).
134   Watermarks can be injected either into a pre-existing text (edit-based watermarks) or during the
135   text generation (generative watermarks), while our work falls in the latter category. Edit-based
136
      watermarking (Rizzo et al., 2019; Abdelnabi & Fritz, 2021; Yang et al., 2022; Kamaruddin et al.,
      2018) has been the focus of several studies in the past. The concept of generative watermarking
137
      dates back to the work of Venugopal et al. (2011), while our work is more relevant to the seminal
138
      works by Aaronson (2022a); Kirchenbauer et al. (2023a) that introduce statistical watermarking as
139   a provable method of embedding statistical signals into language model generations. To develop
140   formal guarantees, Kuditipudi et al. (2023) introduces the notion of distortion-free and inverse
141   transform sampling as a new watermarking method. Following Kirchenbauer et al. (2023a), several
142   works (Ren et al., 2024; Liu & Bu, 2024; Hou et al., 2024b;a; Fu et al., 2024) leverage the semantics
143   of preceding tokens to determine the green list and adjust the bias applied to green-list tokens.
144   Specifically, Hou et al. (2024b;a) use the partition of semantic space to serve as green&red lists
145   for sentence embeddings and perform rejection sampling to sample the sentences conditional on
146   certain green region in the semantic space; Fu et al. (2024) add some semantically-similar tokens
147
      into the green list; Ren et al. (2023) use a trained MLP to generate semantic values. However, these
      approaches lack formal guarantees for Type I error, with challenges to precisely control the probability
148
      associated with green-list membership due to their heuristic designs. In contrast, our work focuses
149
      on statistical watermarking with provable Type I error guarantees, therefore distinguishing us from
150   previous semantic-aware approaches. While the statistical watermarking commonly uses private keys
151   for generation and detection, watermarks can also be injected with private forgeability and public
152   verifiability (Fairoze et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023), hence functioning effectively as digital signatures.
153
      Several prior studies provide upper bounds on the minimum number of tokens required for watermark-
154
      ing. The theoretical challenge in statistical watermarking lies in the regime h ≪ 1, where h is the
155
      average entropy per token. Specifically, Aaronson (2022b) asserts that the Type II error will be small
156   when the number of tokens scales as n ≳ h−2 . Similarly, Christ√      et al. (2023) show watermarking

      entropy per token h is at least Ω(δ). Consequently, Zhao et al. (2023) also suggests the same rate of

178
      evaluating quality, efficiency, and tamper resistance of practical watermarking methods.

310
      Proposal model. We apply a proposal model to capture the semantic context of the preceding
311
      tokens. The proposal model should meet two criteria: (1) it is publicly accessible to detectors, and (2)
312

397   4     E XPERIMENTS
398
399   In this section, we present our experimental setup and results. We evaluate the performance of our
400   watermarking scheme by comparing it with several baselines.
401
402   4.1   S ETUP
403
404   We select Llama2-7B-chat (Touvron et al., 2023) as the model to be watermarked and Phi-3-mini-
405   128k-instruct (Abdin et al., 2024) as the proposal model. We evaluate our watermark using the
406   M ARK M Y W ORDS benchmark (Piet et al., 2023).
407   M ARK M Y W ORDS is an open-source benchmark designed to evaluate symmetric key watermarking
408   schemes. It measures efficiency (watermark strength), quality (impact on utility), and tamper-
409   resistance (ability to withstand simple perturbations without quality degradation). It has been used to
410   benchmark multiple prior schemes applied to Llama2-7B-chat (Touvron et al., 2023) and Mistral-
411   7b (Jiang et al., 2023). Mark My Words performs a grid search over watermarking parameters. It
412   selects the set of parameters with the best efficiency, defined as the number of tokens needed to detect
413   the watermark at a fixed p-value (0.02 in the original paper) while preserving the original model’s
414
      generation quality.
415
416
      Dataset. M ARK M Y W ORDS generates 300 outputs spanning three tasks — book summarization,
      creative writing, and news article generation — which mimic potential misuse scenarios. Outputs are
417
      truncated after 1000 tokens.
418
419
      Perturbations. Watermarked outputs undergo a set of transformations: (1) character level pertur-
420
      bations (contractions, expansions, misspellings and typos); (2) word level perturbations (random
421   removal, addition, and swap of words in each sentence, replacing words with synonyms); (3) text-level
422   perturbations (paraphrasing, translating the output to another language and back).
423
424   Baselines. The paper evaluates four watermarking schemes, coined “Distribution Shift” (Kirchen-
425   bauer et al., 2023a), “Exponential” (Aaronson, 2022b), “Binary” (Christ et al., 2023), and “Inverse
426   Transform” (Kuditipudi et al., 2023).

509   5   D ISCUSSIONS
510
511   In this paper, we advance the understanding of watermarking in large language models (LLMs)
512   through both theoretical analysis and practical algorithm designs. By deriving an explicit formula
513   for the optimal Type II error in Neyman-Pearson’s fashion, we demonstrate how statistical limits are
514
      shaped by the properties of model distributions. Our nearly tight bound on the number of tokens
      required to detect statistical watermarks, h−1 log(1/h), significantly improves upon the previous
515
      h−2 rate, revealing a fundamental gap in prior work. Building on these theoretical insights, we
516
      introduced SEAL (Semantic-aware Speculative Sampling), a novel watermarking algorithm that
517   achieves both high efficiency and robustness. SEAL leverages semantic-aware random seeds, making
518   it more resilient to paraphrase attacks compared to earlier methods. Additionally, SEAL’s use of
519   maximal coupling via speculative sampling allows it to achieve greater efficiency, particularly at
520   higher temperatures (e.g., temperature 1). These advantages make SEAL especially well-suited for
521   practical, real-world applications where the persistence of watermarks under adversarial conditions is
522   crucial. In future work, we aim to study embedding watermark in speculative decoding and explore
523   how advanced speculative decoding techniques might enhance SEAL’s performance.
524
525   Limitations and broader impacts. While our theory and method show promising results for
526   watermarking LLM outputs, there are some limitations. First, while we provide both upper and
527   lower bounds on the minimum number of tokens required in the theoretical i.i.d. setting, in the more
528   realistic non-i.i.d. setting, we only characterize the upper bound, and the lower bound remains an
529
      open and challenging problem. Second, SEAL’s watermarking approach involves inference on a
      smaller model, which can slow down the overall inference and detection speed.
530
531   In terms of broader societal impacts, our work has the potential to contribute positively by helping
532   to prevent and detect the misuse of LLMs. This includes mitigating issues such as misinformation,
533   academic misconduct, and data contamination. By providing a more robust watermarking solution,
534   we hope to promote responsible use of LLMs while minimizing potential harms.

717   B        WATERMARKING AS A H YPOTHESIS T ESTING P ROBLEM
718
719   In the problem of statistical watermarking, a service provider (e.g., a language model system), who
720   possesses a distribution ρ over a sample space Ω, aims to make the samples from the service provider
721
      distinguishable by a detector, without changing ρ. The service provider achieves this by sharing a
      watermark key (generated from a distribution that is coupled with ρ) with the detector, with the goal
722
      of controlling both the Type I error (an independent output is falsely detected as from ρ) and the Type
723
      II error (an output from ρ fails to be detected). This random key together with the detection rule
724   constitute a (random) rejection region. In the following, we formulate this problem as hypothesis
725   testing with random rejection regions.
726
      Problem B.1 (Watermarking). Fix ϵ ≥ 0. Given a probability measure ρ over sample space Ω1 , an
727
      ϵ-distorted watermarking scheme of ρ is a probability measure P (a joint probability of the output
728   X and the rejection region R) over the sample space Ω ⊗ 2Ω such that TV(P(·, 2Ω )∥ρ) ≤ ϵ, where
729   P(·, 2Ω ) is the marginal probability of X over Ω. In the generation phase, the service provider
730   samples (X, R) from P, provides the output X to the service user, and sends the rejection region R
731   to the detector.
732
      In the detection phase, a detector is given a tuple (X, R) ∈ Ω ⊗ 2Ω where X is sampled from an
733
      unknown distribution and R, given by the service provider, is sampled from the marginal probability
734   P(Ω, ·) over 2Ω .
735
736   The Type I error of P, defined as α(P) := supπ∈∆(Ω) PY ∼π,(X,R)∼P (Y ∈ R), is the maximum
737   probability that an independent sample Y is falsely rejected. The Type II error of P, defined as
738
      β(P) := P(X,R)∼P (X ∈   / R), is the probability that the sample (X, R) from the joint probability P
      is not detected.

753   Remark B.2 (Difference between classical hypothesis testing). In classical hypothesis testing,
754   the rejection region is often nonrandomized or independent from the test statistics. However, in

      Remark B.3 (Implementation). In fact, it is imperative for the detector to observe the rejection
      region that is coupled with the output: otherwise, the output from the service provider and another
762
      independent output from the same marginal distribution would be statistically indistinguishable.
763
764   In practice, the process of coupling and sending the rejection region can be implemented by cryp-
765   tographical techniques: the service provider could hash a secret key sk, and use pseudo-random
766   functions F1 , F2 to generate (X, R) = (F1 (sk), F2 (sk)). Now it suffices to send the secret key to
767
      the detector, who can then reproduce the reject region using the pseudo-random function F2 . This
      process is illustrated in Figure 3.
768
769   Thus, the difference between our theoretical framework and the practical implementation lies in our
770   usage of random oracles in place of cryptographic pseudorandom functions. With the coupled and
771   random rejection region, this allows us to focus solely on the statistical trade-offs.
772
773   For practical applications, it is additionally desirable for watermarking schemes to be model-agnostic,
774   i.e, the marginal distribution of the rejection region is irrelevant to the watermarked distribution.
775
      Recall from Remark B.3 that in practice, detectors usually adopt a pseudo-random function to
      generate the reject region from the shared secret keys. If the watermarking scheme P depends on
776
      the underlying distribution ρ, then the pseudo-random function, and effectively the detector, need
777
      to know ρ. On the other hand, model-agnostic watermarking enables the detector to use a fixed,
778   pre-determined pseudo-random function to generate the reject region, and hence perform hypothesis-
779   testing without the knowledge of the underlying model that generates the output. This is an important
780   property enjoyed by existing watermarks (Aaronson, 2022b; Kirchenbauer et al., 2023a; Christ et al.,
781   2023; Kuditipudi et al., 2023). Therefore in the following, we formulate model-agnostic within our
782   hypothesis testing framework.
783
      Problem B.4 (Model-Agnostic Watermarking). Given a sample space Ω and a set Q ⊂ ∆(Ω), a
784
      Q-watermarking scheme is a tuple (η, {Pρ }ρ∈Q ) where η is a probability measure over 2Ω , such
785   that for any probability measure ρ ∈ Q, Pρ is a distortion-free watermarking scheme of ρ and its
786   marginal distribution over 2Ω , Pρ (Ω, ·), equals η(·).
787
788   A model-agnostic watermarking scheme is a ∆(Ω)-watermarking scheme.
789
790   A Q-watermarking scheme can be interpreted as a way to watermark all distributions in the set Q
791
      while revealing no information of the model used to generate the output other than the membership
      inside Q (i.e., observing the rejection region, one is only able to infer that the output comes from a
792
      model in Q, but is unable to know which exactly the model is). By letting Q be ∆(Ω), model-agnostic
793
      watermarking thus reveals no information of the model.

1104   In the context of watermarking large language models, it’s crucial to acknowledge users’ capability
1105   to modify or manipulate model outputs. These modifications include cropping, paraphrasing, and
1106   translating the text, all of which may be employed to subvert watermark detection. Therefore, in this
1107   section, we introduce a graphical framework, modified from Problem B.1, to account for potential
1108   user perturbations and investigate the optimal watermarking schemes robust to these perturbations.
1109   The formulation here shares similarity with a concurrent work by Zhang et al. (2023).
1110
       Definition C.15 (Perturbation graph). A perturbation graph over the discrete sample space Ω is
1111
       a directed graph G = (V, E) where V equals Ω and (u, u) ∈ E for any u ∈ V . For any v ∈ V ,
1112   let in(v) = {w ∈ V : (w, v) ∈ E} denote the set of vertices with incoming edges to v, and let
1113   out(v) = {w ∈ V : (v, w) ∈ E} denote the set of vertices with outcoming edges from v.
1114
1115
       The perturbation graph specifies all the possible perturbations that could be made by the user: any
1116   u ∈ V can be perturbed into v ∈ V if and only if (u, v) ∈ E, i.e., there exists a directed edge from u
1117   to v.
