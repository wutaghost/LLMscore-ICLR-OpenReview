009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This study investigates the robustness of deep reinforcement learning agents
012            against targeted attacks that aim to manipulate the victim’s behavior through ad-
013            versarial interventions in state observations. While several methods for such tar-
014            geted manipulation attacks have been proposed, they all require white-box access
015            to the victim’s policy, and some rely on environment-specific heuristics. Further-
016            more, no defense method has been proposed to counter these attacks. To address
017            this, we propose a novel targeted attack method for manipulating the victim, which
018            does not depend on environmental heuristics and applies in black-box and no-box
019
               settings. Additionally, we introduce a defense strategy against these attacks. Our
               theoretical analysis proves that the sensitivity of a policy’s action output to state
020
               changes affects the defense performance and that the earlier in the trajectory, the
021
               greater the effect. Based on this insight, we introduce a time-discounted regu-
022            larization as a countermeasure for such behavior targeted attacks, which helps
023            to improve robustness against attacks while maintaining task performance in the
024            absence of attacks. Empirical evaluations demonstrate that our proposed attack
025            method outperforms baseline attack methods. Furthermore, our defense strategy
026            shows superior robustness against existing defense methods designed for untar-
027            geted attacks.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Applications of Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) have grown significantly in recent years
033   (Berner et al., 2019; Kiran et al., 2021; Ouyang et al., 2022). However, trained DRL agents re-
034   main vulnerable to adversarial attacks (Huang et al., 2017). While numerous studies have explored
035   adversarial attacks and defenses in DRL agents (Pattanaik et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2020b; Wu
036   et al., 2022), much of the attention has been pained to untargeted attacks, where the adversary aims
037   to minimize the cumulative reward the victim earns from the environment. In real-world scenarios,
038   the adversary’s goal can be more specific—manipulating the victim’s behavior to achieve malicious
039
      outcomes. For example, in autonomous driving, an adversary could control the route or speed to
      cause a collision or traffic jam under certain conditions. These behavior-manipulating attacks are
040
      more precise and sophisticated than untargeted ones, presenting a stealthier and more severe threat.
041
      In this work, we define such attacks as behavior-targeted attacks (Figure 1). One of the key objec-
042   tives of this study is to develop a methodology to attain behavior-targeted attacks, assuming that the
043   adversary can intervene in the victim’s observations and induce false observations of the environ-
044   ment. It is important to note that our focus is on attacking trained victim agents, which differs from
045   poisoning attacks that occur during the victim’s training process.
046
      Prior works proposing behavior-targeted attacks face several limitations. Hussenot et al. (2020)
047
      introduced an attack method that precomputes universal adversarial perturbations for each action
048   in advance, forcing the victim to choose actions consistent with the adversary’s specified policy at
049   each step. However, this approach becomes impractical when the action space is large or continuous,
050   limiting its applicability in more complex tasks. Boloor et al. (2020) developed an attack method
051   specifically for autonomous driving agents. This method, however, relies on a heuristic objective
052   function that requires detailed knowledge of the autonomous driving task, resulting in a lack of
053   generalizability across other tasks. A further limitation common to both methods is their dependence
      on white-box settings that assume full access to the victim’s policy. In many real-world scenarios,

068   In this work, we propose a novel behavior-targeted attack, Behavior Imitation Attack (BIA), which is
069
      applicable even under limited access to the victim’s policy (i.e., black-box and no-box settings) and
      does not require environment-specific heuristics. Specifically, we define the adversary’s objective as
070
      forcing the victim to imitate a policy specified by the adversary, referred to as the target policy. As
071
      demonstrated in Section 7, a naive approach that attempts to match the victim’s action with the target
072   policy’s action at each step is ineffective. This result is because when the adversary’s intervention
073   capability is limited, there are often no false states in which the victim’s behavior fully aligns with
074   the target policy. Instead, our approach aims to guide the victim to imitate the overall behavior
075   rather than matching individual actions at each step. Consequently, our attack can achieve overall
076   behavioral alignment even in situations where perfect action matching is not possible at each step.
077
      Another goal of this study is to develop a countermeasure for the behavior-targeted attack. We
078   present the first robust training framework, Time-Discounted Reglarization Training (TDRT), for
079   such attacks as a defense method. Our theoretical analysis reveals two important insights: (1) sup-
080   pressing the sensitivity of the policy’s action outputs to state changes enhances robustness against
081   attacks, and (2) achieving less sensitivity during the early stages of a trajectory significantly im-
082   proves overall robustness. Through this analysis, we introduce a time-discounted regularization
083   approach, where stronger regularization is applied during the critical early stages of the trajectory
084   and gradually weakened in the later, less critical stages.
085   This method is particularly effective against attacks that do not reduce rewards much but alter the re-
086   sulting behavior significantly. This is especially important for situations where a change in behavior
087   does not correlate to the reward.Since defense methods designed for untargeted attacks cannot deal
088   with this type of attack, this regularization strategy is effective for behavior-targeted attacks. Fur-
089   thermore, time-discounted regularization mitigates performance degradation without compromising
090   robustness compared to regularization without time discounting. Consequently, time discounting
091   improves the trade-off between robustness against attacks and performance under non-attack condi-
092
      tions. To the best of our knowledge, no defense mechanism has been considered for the behavior-
      targeted attack.
093
094   Our primary contributions can be summarized as follows:
095
096          • We formulate the concept of behavior-targeted attack in reinforcement learning and propose
097            the Behavior Imitation Attack (BIA), an attack algorithm in this setting. BIA does not
098
               require environment-specific heuristics and is applicable in both black-box/no-box settings.
099          • We propose a robust training framework, Time-Discounted Regularization Training
100            (TDRT). TDRT is robust against attacks that do not significantly reduce rewards but greatly
101            alter behavior. Existing defenses designed for untargeted attacks are ineffective against
102            such threats. Furthermore, time-discounted regularization mitigates performance degrada-
103            tion without compromising robustness, outperforming regularization methods without time
104
               discounting. To the best of our knowledge, TDRT is the first defense method specifically
               designed to counter behavior-targeted attacks.
105
106          • We demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed attack and defense methods using the
107            reinforcement learning benchmark Meta-World (Yu et al., 2020) and MuJoCo environment.
               Our attack method is more effective than baselines.

      2    R ELATED WORKS
109
110
      Untargeted attack and its defense Untargeted attacks aim to minimize the victim’s cumulative
111
      reward. Gradient-based methods, which require white-box access to the victim’s policy, have been
112
      proposed Huang et al. (2017); Pattanaik et al. (2018); Gleave et al. (2020). One common defense
113   method against untargeted attacks is policy smoothing. Shen et al. (2020) and Zhang et al. (2020b)
114   introduced regularization terms to smooth the policy, demonstrating increased robustness. However,
115   unlike our approach, these methods do not incorporate time discounting. Additionally, adversarial
116   training has been proposed Zhang et al. (2021); Oikarinen et al. (2021); Sun et al. (2022); Liang
117   et al. (2022). While these methods demonstrated high levels of robustness, Korkmaz (2021; 2023)
118   pointed out that agents trained to be adversarial robust are still vulnerable to attacks that were not
119   anticipated during training. Liang et al. (2024) introduced a new concept called temporally-coupled
120   perturbations, where consecutive perturbations are constrained. Belaire et al. (2024) proposed a
121
      novel defense method based on regret minimization. Liu et al. (2024) have proposed PROTECTED,
      which is effective not only against worst-case attacks but also against more general forms of attack.
122
      Another approach is certified defense (Wu et al., 2022; Kumar et al., 2022; Mu et al., 2024; Sun
123
      et al., 2024). These methods guarantee a lower bound on the reward obtained by that victim under
124   adversarial attacks.
125
126
      Targeted attack Targeted attacks manipulate the victim to follow a specific direction and can
127
      be categorized into two main types: enchanting attacks and behavior-targeted attacks. Enchanting
128
      attacks aim to lure the victim to a predetermined final state without specifying the entire behavior
129   trajectory. Lin et al. (2017) and Tretschk et al. (2018) proposed methods to guide the victim to a
130   target state through crafted perturbations. More recently, Ying et al. (2023) introduced an enchanting
131   attack using universal adversarial perturbations. Behavior-targeted attacks, on the other hand, aim
132   for more detailed control over the victim’s entire behavior. The introductory section has discussed
133   the works of (Hussenot et al., 2020) and (Boloor et al., 2020) in detail.
134
135   Poisoning attack Several studies have proposed poisoning attacks against RL agents that inter-
136   vene during the victim’s training phase (Sun et al., 2021; Rakhsha et al., 2020; Rangi et al., 2022;
137   Xu et al., 2023; Xu & Singh, 2023). These works explored various approaches, from theoretical
138   investigations to practical black-box attacks. However, as our research focuses on attacking trained
139   victims rather than interfering during training, our threat model differs fundamentally from these
140   poisoning attack studies. A comprehensive review of related work is provided in Appendix A.

185
      tation learning to train an adversarial policy aimed at manipulating the victim’s behavior following
186

      4     T HREAT MODEL
223
224
      In this section, we outline the threat models for the behavior-targeted attack. We assume that the
225   victim follows a fixed policy π while it observes false states generated through the adversary’s
226   intervention. We define this process as an SA-MDP M = (S, A, R, B, p, γ). The adversary’s
227   objective is to manipulate the victim into performing a specific behavior desired by the adversary,
228   which is denoted by target policy πtgt . To achieve this objective, the adversary trains an adversarial
229   policy ν that makes the victim observe a false state at each step. In this context, the adversary’s
230   influence against the victim is characterized as follows:
231
232   Access to the victim’s policy. We suppose the adversary does not have full access to the victim’s
233   policy π, including the neural network parameters and the training algorithm of the victim’s policy.
234   The adversary in the black-box setting can obtain the victim’s policy inputs and outputs at each
235
      time, specifically the victim’s states in the environment and the corresponding actions taken. In
      contrast, the adversary in the no-box setting can only obtain the victim’s state in the environment at
236
      each time. The information available to the adversary influences the type of imitation learning the
237
      adversary employs for its attack. In the black-box setting, the adversary can use Imitation Learning
238   from Demonstration (ILdD) to train the adversarial policy. In the no-box setting, the adversary needs
239   to use Imitation Learning from Observation (ILfO), which is generally less effective than ILfD in
240   terms of performance.
241
242   Intervention ability on victim’s state observations. We assume that the adversary can alter the
243   victim’s state observation from the true state s to the false state ŝ ∈ B(s). This assumption is
244   common in most adversarial attacks on RL.

358   6.1   F ORMULATION OF D EFENDER ’ S OBJECTIVE
359
360   Since the defender has no access to the target policy specified by the adversary, the defender cannot
361
      directly maximize the adversary’s objective function as given in equation 6 for defense. Instead, we
      redefine the defender’s objective as minimizing the change in the state-action distribution caused by

368   training. The distance between the state-action distributions of π and π ◦ ν indicates the extent to

372   adversarial policy.
373   Equation 12 is designed to be robust against behavior-targeted attacks that existing defense methods
374   designed for untargeted attacks cannot mitigate. Existing defenses against untargeted attacks com-
375   monly aim to minimize the reduction in cumulative reward caused by attacks. However, policies
376   trained under such objectives may remain vulnerable to attacks that, while not significantly affect-
377   ing cumulative rewards, change the agent’s behavior significantly. In contrast, policies trained with
      equation 12 can be robust against these behavior-targeted attacks.

527   8    C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work, we proposed the Behavior Imitation Attack (BIA), which manipulates victim behav-
530   ior through perturbed state observations using imitation learning and operates with limited victim
531   policy access. We also introduced Time-Discounted Regularization (TDR), the first defense method
532   specifically designed for behavior-targeted attacks, which achieved robustness without compromis-
533   ing normal performance.
534   Limitations and Future work. Behavior-targeted attacks cannot force victims to perform behav-
535   iors that they would never execute under any observation. When the target policy’s behavior sig-
536   nificantly deviates from the victim’s original patterns, appropriate adversarial states may not exist,
537   leading to attack failure. Additionally, while TDR is effective against behavior-targeted attacks, it
538   shows limited effectiveness against untargeted attacks in the later stages of the trajectory, making the
539   development of defense methods effective against both types of attacks an important future research
      direction.

      A     R ELATED WORKS
757
758
      In this section, we discuss prior research on attacks and defenses on DRL that mainly assume an
759   adversary’s perturbed the victim’s state observations.
760
761
      A.1   U NTARGETED ATTACK AND ITS D EFENSE
762
763   In untargeted attacks, the adversary’s goal is to minimize the cumulative reward received by the
764   victim. The most basic approach is gradient-based methods. Huang et al. (2017) proposed an attack
765   method that uses the Fast Gradient Signed Method (FGSM) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) to compute
766   adversarial perturbations that prevent the victim from choosing optimal actions. Pattanaik et al.
767   (2018) performed a more powerful attack by creating adversarial perturbations that minimize the
768   value estimated by the Q-function. (Gleave et al., 2020) proposed an attack that assumes a two-
769
      agent environment. This method creates an adversarial agent that maximally degrades the victim’s
      performance.
770
771   One approach to learning robust policies against untargeted attacks is to make the policy smooth.
772   Shen et al. (2020) introduced a regularization term to smooth the policy and demonstrated increased
773   sample efficiency and robustness. Zhang et al. (2020b) formulated the State-Adversarial Markov
774   Decision Process (SA-MDP) to represent situations where an adversary interferes with the victim’s
775
      state observations. Based on SA-MDP, they showed that regularization to smooth the policy is
      effective in resisting untargeted attacks.
776
777   Another approach for defense is adversarial training. Zhang et al. (2021) showed that the optimal
778   adversarial policy can be learned as a policy in MDP and proposed ATLA, an adversarial train-
779   ing framework that exploits this insight. Sun et al. (2022) proposed a theoretically optimal attack
780   method that finds the optimal direction of perturbation and proposed PA-ATLA as an extension of
781
      ATLA, which is efficient even in large state spaces. Oikarinen et al. (2021) proposed a framework
      for training a robust RL by incorporating an adversarial loss that accounts for the worst-case input
782
      perturbations. Additionally, they introduced a new metric to efficiently evaluate the robustness of the
783
      agent. Liang et al. (2022) efficiently estimated the lower bound of cumulative rewards under adver-
784   sarial attacks and performed adversarial learning with partial smoothness regularization. However,
785   Korkmaz (2021; 2023) pointed out that agents trained to be adversarially robust are still vulnerable
786   to attacks that were not anticipated during training.
787
      McMahan et al. (2024) proposed a comprehensive framework for computing optimal attacks and de-
788
      fenses, modeling the attack problem as a meta-MDP and the defense problem as a partially observ-
789
      able turn-based stochastic game. Bukharin et al. (2024) proposed a robust Multi-Agent RL (MARL)
790   framework that uses adversarial regularization to promote Lipschitz continuity of policies, thereby
791   enhancing robustness against environmental changes, observation noise, and malicious agent ac-
792   tions. Liang et al. (2024) introduced a new concept called temporally-coupled perturbations, where
793   consecutive perturbations are constrained. Their proposed method, GRAD, demonstrates strong ro-
794   bustness against standard and temporally-coupled perturbations. Belaire et al. (2024) proposed a
795   novel defense method based on regret minimization. Liu et al. (2024) have proposed a new frame-
796   work called ”PROTECTED.” This framework is characterized by its effectiveness against not only
797   worst-case attacks but also more general forms of attacks. Additionally, another approach in robust
798
      reinforcement learning is certified defense Wu et al. (2022); Kumar et al. (2022); Mu et al. (2024);
      Sun et al. (2024). These studies guarantee a lower bound on the rewards obtained by the victim
799
      under adversarial attacks.
800
801
802
      A.2   TARGETED ATTACK
803
      The adversary’s goal of the targeted attack is to manipulate the victim so that it follows some direc-
804   tion made by the adversary. One type of targeted attack is the enchanting attack. This attack aims
805   to lure the victim agent to reach a predetermined target state. Lin et al. (2017) first proposed this
806   type of attack. In their approach, the adversary predicts future states and generates a sequence of
807   actions that cause the victim to reach the target state. The adversary then crafts a sequence of per-
808   turbations to make the victim perform the sequence of actions. Tretschk et al. (2018) proposed an
809   enchanting attack where adversarial perturbations to maximize adversarial rewards are heuristically
      designed for the attack purpose, thereby transitioning to the specified target state. Buddareddygari


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      et al. (2022) proposed a different enchanting attack using visual patterns placed on physical objects
811   in the environment so that the victim agent is directed to the target state. Unlike (Lin et al., 2017)
812   and (Tretschk et al., 2018), which affect the victim’s state observations, this attack affects the envi-
813   ronmental dynamics, not the victim’s state observations. Ying et al. (2023) proposed an enchanting
814   attack with a universal adversarial perturbation. When any state observations are modified with this
815   perturbation, the victim agent is forced to be guided to the target state. All of these enchanting
816   attacks require white-box access to the victim’s policy.
817   The other type of targeted attack is the behavior-targeted attack, which aims to manipulate not only
818   the final destination but also the behavior in a more detailed direction by the adversary. Hussenot
819   et al. (2020) proposed a behavior-targeted method that forces the victim to select the same actions
820   as the policy specified by the adversary. More specifically, this attack precomputes a universal per-
821   turbation for each action so that the victim who observes the perturbed states takes the same action
822   as the adversarially specified policy. One limitation of this attack is that the computation cost to
823   precompute such universal perturbations can be large when the action space is large or continu-
824   ous. Since the cost required for pre-computing perturbations is significant, applying this attack is
825
      challenging. Boloor et al. (2020) proposed a heuristic attack specifically designed for autonomous
      vehicles. They formulated an objective function with a detailed knowledge of the target task, which
826
      is not applicable to behavior-targeted attacks for general tasks. We remark that all existing behavior-
827
      targeted attacks also require white-box access to the victim’s policy.
828
829
830
831   A.3   P OISONING ATTACK
832
833   Several studies have proposed poisoning attacks against RL agents, which intervene during the vic-
834
      tim’s training phase. Sun et al. (2021) proposed VA2C-P, a poisoning attack framework that can
      adapt without access to environment dynamics. They proposed both untargeted attacks and target
835
      attacks that aim to align the victim’s policy with a target policy.Rakhsha et al. (2020) theoretically
836
      investigated the attack problem in both offline planning and online RL settings with tabular MDPs.
837   Rangi et al. (2022) also provided theoretical insights into the fundamental limits of poisoning attacks
838   in episodic reinforcement learning. Xu et al. (2023); Xu & Singh (2023) proposed more practical
839   black-box attacks that do not require knowledge of environment dynamics or the victim’s learning
840   algorithm. As our research focuses on attacking trained victims, our threat model differs from those
841   of these studies.

1214     E.1                        E FFICIENCY OF BIA-IL F D/IL F O
1215
1216     We analyze the efficiency of BIA-ILfD/ILfO from two perspectives: training time of the adversarial
1217     policy and testing time. The training phase uses standard imitation learning without additional over-
1218     head, keeping learning costs low. Regarding demonstrations, the adversary’s effort to prepare target
1219     policy demonstrations is more practical than designing new target reward functions. For testing ef-
1220     ficiency, BIA only requires inference cost from the trained adversarial policy, giving it a significant
1221
         advantage over methods like the targeted-PGD attack that requires optimization at each step. Addi-
         tional experiments on attack performance are provided in Sections F.2 and F.3. Section F.2 inves-
1222
         tigates the relationship between demonstration quantity and attack performance, while Section F.3
1223
         analyzes attack efficiency across different attack budgets. These comprehensive experiments provide
1224     insights into both the data requirements and computational constraints of our proposed method.

1227     E.2                        E FFICIENCY OF TDRT-PPO
1228
         TDRT-PPO adds regularization terms to standard RL algorithms. Following SA-PPO Zhang et al.
1229
         (2020b), we applied common convex relaxation methods (Zhang et al., 2018; Wong & Kolter, 2018;
1230
         Salman et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020a) to obtain KL term bounds. We implemented this using the
1231     auto LiRPA toolkit(Xu et al., 2020), reducing regularization term computation costs and enabling
1232     efficient TDRT-PPO implementation. This regularization-based approach achieves shorter training
1233     times compared to adversarial training methods that require learning adversarial policies Liang et al.
1234     (2022).

1394   during which it updates states using FGSM in each iteration. Specifically, it uses the L2 distance
1395   between current victim actions and target actions as the loss function and updates states by scaling
1396
       in the direction of gradient signs by ϵstep . In all experiments, we set T = 30. We also implement

          G.3                 F ULL RESULTS IN DEFENSE PERFORMANCE EVALUATION
1440
1441     In our evaluation of defense methods in Section 7, we only provide the results of the best attack. In
1442     this section, we present the results of all attacks in 3. Targeted PGD is ineffective against robustly
1443     trained victims. We observed a trend where the Reward Maximization Attack tended to be slightly
1444     more effective than BIA when attacking highly robust victims. When the victim’s robustness is high,
1445     it becomes difficult to make the victim behave like the target policy, which may cause the learning
1446     process in BIA to fail or collapse. On the other hand, in the Reward Maximization Attack, the
1447     reward serves as a good guidepost, allowing learning to proceed even when the victim’s robustness
1448     is high.
