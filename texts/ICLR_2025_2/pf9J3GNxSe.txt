009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive performance. To
012            understand their behaviors, we need to consider the fact that LLMs sometimes
013            show qualitative changes. The natural world also presents such changes called
014            phase transitions, which are defined by singular, divergent statistical quantities.
015            Therefore, an intriguing question is whether qualitative changes in LLMs are
016            phase transitions. In this work, we have conducted extensive analysis on texts gen-
017            erated by LLMs and suggested that a phase transition occurs in LLMs when vary-
018
               ing the temperature parameter. Specifically, statistical quantities have divergent
               properties just at the point between the low-temperature regime, where LLMs gen-
019
               erate sentences with clear repetitive structures, and the high-temperature regime,
020
               where generated sentences are often incomprehensible. In addition, critical be-
021            haviors near the phase transition point, such as a power-law decay of correlation
022            and slow convergence toward the stationary state, are similar to those in natural
023            languages. Our results suggest a meaningful analogy between LLMs and natural
024            phenomena.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive performance in various tasks, in-
030   cluding machine translation and code generation. Uncovering the origin of the unexpectedly good
031   performance of LLMs is essential both from theoretical and practical perspectives, as it would allow
032   us to devise even better LLMs. A complete theory for LLMs is yet to be developed, but it definitely
033
      needs to take into account the fact that LLMs sometimes show qualitative changes when varying
      parameters. Indeed, various qualitative changes play a crucial role in LLMs. For example, it is
034
      empirically known that LLMs can exhibit emergent abilities: The performance of LLMs sometimes
035
      improves unexpectedly when increasing model scales (Brown et al., 2020; Srivastava et al., 2022;
036   Ganguli et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022), although some argues that the emergent ability may be an
037   artifact due to the arbitrary choice of metrics (Schaeffer et al., 2024). The grokking phenomenon
038   (Power et al., 2022), where, during the learning process, the generalization loss drastically decreases
039   long after the convergence of the training loss, is also an example of such qualitative changes that
040   are observed in language models (Liu et al., 2022b; Zhu et al., 2024a).
041
      Qualitative changes are ubiquitous in nature, often expressing themselves as phase transitions. For
042   instance, as temperature increases, ferromagnets undergo a phase transition from the ordered phase,
043   in which the magnetic spins are aligned, and the materials are magnetized even with an infinitesimal
044   external magnetic field, to the disordered phase with zero net magnetization. This phase transition
045   accompanies the divergence of the response of spins to the magnetic field, known as susceptibility.
046   Therefore, the phase transition point defined by the divergent susceptibility separates these two
047   phases unambiguously, as illustrated in Fig. 1 (A). This point does not depend on any subjective
048   factors, such as metrics and thresholds.
049   These raise an intriguing question: Are qualitative changes observed in LLMs phase transitions?
050   If so, it suggests a meaningful analogy between LLMs and natural phenomena. In this study, we
051   numerically investigate the possibility of phase transitions in LLMs. We shall first focus on the
052   change in the statistics of generated texts with the temperature parameter. This parameter is easy
053   to control and known to induce an evident change to generated texts: At low temperatures, clear
      repetitive structures appear in the texts, whereas, at very high temperatures, they are often incom-

073   decay of correlation in natural languages follows a power law (Li, 1989; Ebeling & Pöschel, 1994;

081   These suggest the existence of a phase transition in LLMs, as illustrated in Fig. 1 (B): The phase
082   transition occurs at a certain temperature Tc , at which the LLM exhibits critical behaviors similar to
083   those in natural languages.
084
      To examine the above speculation, we have conducted extensive statistical analysis of sequences
085
      generated by LLMs, to detect phase transitions. Our numerical results strongly suggest that a phase
086   transition occurs at temperature Tc ≈ 1. Below Tc , the correlation converges to a positive value,
087   and sequences exhibit repetitive structures. On the other hand, above Tc , the correlation converges
088   to zero, and repetitive structures disappear. We further demonstrate that sequences at the transition
089   point have critical properties, such as a power-law decay in a correlation and slow convergence to-
090   ward the stationary state. Additionally, the observed behaviors exhibit several intriguing features
091   that are not known in typical physical systems: At high temperatures, we find that the correlation
092   does not follow an exponential decay, which is observed in the disordered phases of typical physical
093   systems, and in the low-temperature phase, extremely numerous periodic structures coexist. These
094
      findings suggest that LLMs and natural languages are more complex than typical physical systems.
      Finally, we show natural language datasets have critical properties as well as the LLMs at the tran-
095
      sition temperature, implying that the scenario presented in Fig. 1 (B) indeed holds.
096
097   The presence of the phase transition in LLMs opens the possibility of understanding LLMs through
098   theories and methods for studying phase transitions. Importantly, our statistical analyses can apply
099   to changes induced by any parameter other than the temperature parameter. We propose a novel,
100
      physics-based approach to deepen the theoretical understanding of LLMs.
101   Our major contributions are the following:
102
103          • We propose to formulate qualitative changes in LLMs as phase transitions studied in sta-
104            tistical physics, providing the first strong evidence that practical LLMs exhibit a phase
105            transition.
106
107          • We conduct a numerical analysis of the statistical properties of natural language datasets
               and discuss connections with the criticality of LLMs.

113   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
114
115   Investigation of the effect of temperature on LLMs: Previous research has studied how the sam-
116   pling temperature affects the behavior of LLMs in inference. Renze & Guven (2024) studied the
117   effect of temperature on the performance on problem-solving tasks. Xu et al. (2022); Pursnani et al.
118   (2023); Zhu et al. (2024b), and Grandi et al. (2024) also evaluated the performance on tasks such
119   as code generation, engineering exams, and material selection under different temperature settings.
120   Fradkin et al. (2023) examined how narratives generated by GPT-2 change with temperature from a
121   psychiatric perspective. In contrast to these studies, our work does not focus on the task-specific per-
122   formance of GPT-2 at various temperatures but rather measures the statistical properties of generated
123
      texts to find the existence of phase transitions and critical behaviors.
124   Phase transitions in language models: Emergent abilities and grokking are discussed in connec-
125   tion with phase transitions (Wei et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022a; Thilak et al., 2022; Nanda et al.,
126   2023; Varma et al., 2023). In these studies, their formulation of phase transitions is not rigor-
127   ous. Indeed, Schaeffer et al. (2024) has argued that emergent abilities may not be actual qualitative
128   changes. Some theoretical studies formulate emergent abilities, grokking, and other phenomena in
129
      language models as phase transitions, yet they consider only mathematical models, not practical
130
      LLMs (Žunkovič & Ilievski, 2022; Chang, 2023; Rubin et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2024). Contrary to
      these earlier studies, we show that a phase transition occurs in practical LLMs. We also note that
131
      the earlier studies do not focus on the temperature-induced change in generated texts.
132
133   During the preparation of our manuscript, we noticed an independent work by Bahamondes (2023),
134   which measured statistical quantities of sequences of tokens or embedding vectors generated by
135   GPT-2 trained on OpenWebText dataset (Wolfram Research, 2019; Radford et al.). This study
136
      claimed that a phase transition occurs at T ≈ 0.1. However, they did not mention the dependence of
      correlation on time interval or the asymptotic behavior in the limit of infinite sequence length. We
137
      should also note that the phase transition discussed in Bahamondes (2023) would be distinct from
138
      the one we study in this work, as the temperature scale is very different.
139
140   Correlation in natural languages and generated texts: Power-law decays in correlations and mu-
141   tual information have been observed across different natural language datasets1 (Li, 1989; Ebeling &
142
      Pöschel, 1994; Ebeling & Neiman, 1995; Tanaka-Ishii & Bunde, 2016; Lin & Tegmark, 2017; Taka-
      hashi & Tanaka-Ishii, 2017; Shen, 2019; Takahashi & Tanaka-Ishii, 2019; Sainburg et al., 2019;
143
      Mikhaylovskiy & Churilov, 2023). These studies, however, did not measure the correlation or mu-
144
      tual information between part-of-speech (POS) tags, which is our focus. Futrell & Levy (2017)
145   and Futrell et al. (2019) measured mutual information between POS tags, yet they did not show
146   the power-law decay. The analysis of correlation and other statistical quantities in texts gener-
147   ated by language models have also been conducted numerically and theoretically in earlier studies
148   (Takahashi & Tanaka-Ishii, 2017; Shen, 2019; Takahashi & Tanaka-Ishii, 2019; Mikhaylovskiy &
149   Churilov, 2023), which concluded that the presence of power-law decays in correlations can depend
150   on models, methods, and setups. These studies have not examined how the correlations depend on
151   parameters such as temperature. Lippi et al. (2019) has studied a power-law decay in a correlation in
152   generated texts with varying temperatures in comparison with natural language datasets. However,
153
      the existence of phase transitions was not discussed in their works.
154   Criticality in biological systems: Critical behaviors have also been observed in various biological
155   systems, including neural activity (Munoz, 2018). Previous research has shown that Ising models
156   fitted to neural activity exhibit a phase transition near the temperature where the models are close to
157   the data (Tkacik et al., 2006; Mora & Bialek, 2011). It is noteworthy that their models and data are
158   quite small and simple compared to LLMs.

      2    P HASE TRANSITIONS AND CRITICAL PHENOMENA
163
164
      In statistical physics, phase transitions are defined by the singularity of a statistical quantity. To be
165   more precise, a phase transition exists at a parameter point if any of the quantities changes with
166   a singularity at the point, i.e., the quantity or its derivative diverges. We emphasize that a true
167   singularity can exist only in the large-size limit, where the degrees of freedom of the system are
168   infinitely large.
169
      Near the phase transition point, the system can exhibit critical phenomena. In this case, the phase
170
      transition point is also called the critical point. An essential characteristic of critical phenomena is
171   that the correlation decays in a power law. This decay is qualitatively slower than the exponential
172   decay typically observed in ordered or disordered phases. Moreover, because power-law functions
173   are invariant under scale transformations, the power-law decay means the divergence of the length
174   and time scales of the system. This divergence leads to intriguing phenomena: a local perturbation or
175   fluctuation can influence the entire system, the susceptibility or integrated correlation diverges, and
176   the dynamics slow down. Critical behaviors have been discussed in various scientific disciplines,
177   including computer science, biology, finance, and others (Bak et al., 1988; Langton, 1990; Munoz,
178   2018; Bouchaud, 2024).
179   In general, theoretically proving the existence of a phase transition is so challenging that it has only
180   been achieved in a few simple theoretical models because we need to consider the large system
181   size limit. However, by estimating statistical quantities with varying system sizes, we can follow
182   the dependence of the quantities on the size to discuss how the quantities diverge. For example,
183   in a ferromagnetic Ising model with a finite number of spins, the susceptibility increases smoothly
184   near the critical temperature. As the system size, i.e., the number of spins, increases, this change
185
      becomes sharper and, in the large size limit, it eventually becomes a strictly singular change at the
      critical point (Domb, 2000), as illustrated in Fig. 1 (A). This transition temperature unambiguously
186
      separates the parameter space into two phases, that is, the ordered and the disordered phases. Our
187
      approach in this study also follows this method.

190   3    S ETUP
191
192   We generate texts using pretrained LLMs with temperature sampling (Ackley et al., 1985). Each
193   sequence starts with the single beginning-of-sentence token and ends once the end-of-sentence token
194   appears or when its length reaches the default length limit. The t-th token xt is sampled according to
195
      the softmax distribution P (xt |x0 , · · · , xt−1 ) ∝ exp(−H(xt |x0 , · · · , xt−1 )/T ) without top-k (Fan
      et al., 2018) or top-p (Holtzman et al., 2020) sampling strategies. H(xt |x0 , · · · , xt−1 ) is the logit
196
      of xt , while T is the temperature parameter, although it does not necessarily coincide with physical
197
      temperature. We then tokenize each of the generated sequences.
198
199   To estimate statistical quantities precisely, it is necessary to map each generated text into a sequence
200   of variables that take a small number of states while preserving linguistic information. For this
201
      purpose, we map the text to the sequence of universal POS tags. This mapping is useful as it can
      be easily applied to different languages. Note that our analyses in the following part of this work
202
      are applicable to another mapping, such as one-to-semantic tags. In addition, the phase transition is
203
      observed with the mapping based on characters, as we will show in App. J. POS tagging transforms
204   the generated texts into POS sequences (y0 , · · · , yNPOS −1 ), where yt takes one of 18 different POS
205   tags, that is, 17 universal POS tags plus SPACE. To study the POS sequences of length N , we
206   choose POS sequences that are longer than N from all the samples, and analyze the first N tags
207   (y0 , · · · , yN −1 ) from the beginning for each sequence. For example, a POS sequence of NPOS =
208   300 is used for N = 256 but not for N = 512. We have confirmed that this sampling method does
209   not qualitatively affect the results presented below, as discussed in App. A. Throughout this paper,
210   we interpret index t as time and a POS sequence (y0 , · · · , yN −1 ) as a time series. In this case, the
211   length N of the time series corresponds to the system size 2 .
212       2
            In POS tagging, subsequent context (interpreted as future events) can sometimes influence the POS of
213   preceding words (interpreted as past events). Although this may affect our interpretation, the analysis remains
214   valid. Indeed, we have confirmed a similar phase transition is observable when text is mapped to a sequence by
215   replacing each character with a number, as presented in App. J. In this case, the mapping of each character is
      independent of the mappings of subsequent characters.

      4.3   T IME EVOLUTION OF POS SEQUENCES
367
368   Thus far, we have analyzed the statistical properties of the entire sequences of length N from the
369   beginning. The behavior of correlation C(t, t + ∆t) in Fig. 2 has suggested that, at small t, the
370   distribution of the POS tags strongly depends on t, and it gradually approaches its stationary state at
371   large t as the correlation with the initial time decreases. This observation motivates further analysis
372   of the sequences: How the POS tags evolve with time.
373   We calculate the probability of POS tag at time t being a, va (t) = E[δyt ,a ]. Figure 5 shows v(t) =

468   6     D ISCUSSION
469
470   In summary, we have studied the effect of the temperature parameter on the GPT-2 sequences and
471   shown that a critical phase transition occurs at Tc ≈ 1. We have further demonstrated that the criti-
472   cality in some statistical quantities appears in natural language corpora as well. It is reasonable that
473   LLMs exhibit critical behaviors similar to those of natural languages near the default temperature
474   T = 1, at which logits are not rescaled. However, the existence of the phase transition is nontrivial.
475   Moreover, the high and low temperature regimes far from T = 1 show the unexpected behaviors,
476
      implying the complex nature of LLMs. Our results also provide a possible picture that, near Tc , com-
      prehensible sentences emerge only during the transient time before reaching the stationary state. We
477
      now discuss some implications of our numerical results.
478
479   Phase transition in the large context window limit: Since we have analyzed the statistical proper-
480   ties of sequences shorter than the context window of the LLM, the phenomena discussed here should
481   be observed within its scale. Therefore, to increase the system size N , the context window must be
482
      longer than N . The true phase transition in the N → ∞ limit should exist in the limit where the size
      of models and the length of texts in the training data are infinitely large.
483
484   Robustness of the phase transition: The phase transition possibly occurs with varying other pa-
485   rameters because in crossing a phase boundary in a multi-dimensional parameter space, a phase
      transition is observed, irrespective of the direction. We also expect that the phase transition exists


                                                                   9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      with other mappings of texts to sequences, other languages, and other LLMs from the following
487   facts: the power-law decay of correlation is universally observed in different mappings and lan-
488   guages, as mentioned in Sec. 1; the behavior of generated texts with varying temperature is similar
489   with different LLMs, that is, generated texts have repetitions at low temperatures while they are in-
490   comprehensible at high temperatures; the effect of details of systems is weak near the critical point
491   due to the divergence of scale. Indeed, in Apps. I and J, we conduct the same analyses with a map-
492   ping based on characters, and for Japanese GPT-2 medium (361M) (Zhao & Sawada; Sawada et al.,
493   2024) and Pythia (70M and 1B) (Biderman et al., 2023), to obtain similar results. Specifically, the
494   integrated correlation saturates above Tc ≈ 1 and diverges below it, suggesting the presence of a
495
      phase transition. In particular, observing phase transitions in models of different sizes (70M, 124M,
      361M, and 1B) suggests that similar phase transitions may also occur in larger models.
496
497   Universality class of LLMs and natural languages: We have discussed the critical behaviors in
498   sequences generated by GPT-2 at around Tc and shown that GPT-2 and the natural language corpora
499   have common critical behaviors. In the literature of statistical physics, critical phase transitions are
500   classified into universality classes, each of which has a distinct set of exponents that describe the
501
      behavior of various quantities near the critical point, such as the exponent characterizing the transient
      time (This is roughly estimated in App. E). If two systems belong to the same universality class, it
502
      means they share fundamental characteristics. An interesting direction for future work is to precisely
503
      estimate the exponents for different LLMs to identify their universality classes and investigate the
504   relationship between these classes and performance.
505
506   Unique statistical properties at high and low temperatures: The statistical behavior at high tem-
507
      peratures has a characteristic behavior that is not common in conventional equilibrium disordered
      phases: Whereas the integrated correlation is convergent, the decay of correlation is not simply ex-
508
      ponential. This is due to the correlation decreasing with both ∆t and t. Precisely following the t
509
      dependence of C(t, t + ∆t) with fixed ∆t is needed to address this point. Whereas we have not fully
510   examined the potential effect of POS tagging at very high temperatures, where generated texts con-
511   tain many incomprehensible words yet the tagger automatically assigns POS tags, any such effect
512   should be insignificant at low temperatures and near the critical point, which are our main focus.
513
      The low-temperature phase also exhibits an intriguing phenomenon: This phase is definitely or-
514
      dered in the sense that the correlation converges to a nonzero value. However, unlike conventional
515
      ordered phases in equilibrium statistical mechanics, the dynamics of the low-temperature GPT-2
516   is complex: Sequences have repetitive structures with many peaks in the power spectrum. Up to
517   length N = 512, the number of peaks grows with N , suggesting that the power spectrum would
518   have continuous support of diverging components. If this observation is confirmed, it implies that
519   the low-temperature sequences are chaotic in nature. We have also noticed that, at T = 0, GPT-2
520   performs top-1 sampling and is deterministic, and the correlation must be strictly zero. Therefore,
521   another phase transition could exist at very low temperature between the repetitive and deterministic
522   phases. We cannot identify this temperature scale because fluctuations are too small to precisely
523   follow the N dependence at this temperature regime. Even more extensive calculations are neces-
524
      sary to study this possible transition. We cannot conclude on the connection between this potential
      transition and the one argued in Bahamondes (2023).
525
526   These peculiar phenomena should originate from the distinct features in LLMs: nonreciprocal and
527   infinite-range interactions implemented with attention mechanisms and highly non-uniform parame-
528   ters trained on large-scale corpora. Importantly, although mathematical models of machine learning
529   have been extensively studied in statistical physics (Carleo et al., 2019), existing models do not
530
      have critical phase transitions with the unique properties observed in this study. An important and
      promising future direction is to explore the development of mathematical models that exhibit phase
531
      transitions similar to those observed in this study.

       As discussed in Sec. 4.3, our results imply that generated texts are natural within the transient time,
1299   which diverges as the temperature parameter approaches Tc . From a statistical-physical viewpoint,
1300   the divergence is expected to follow a power law of the form ∼ |T −Tc |−a . In general, the exponents
1301   characterizing the behavior of statistical quantities near the critical point, such as the exponent a,
1302   have an essential relationship to the fundamental characteristics of the system. In this appendix, we
1303   estimate the transient time and the exponent a.
1304
       At high temperatures T > Tc , although the decay in the correlation presented in Fig. 2 does not
1305   exhibit the time scale clearly, we can estimate roughly the scale of the transient by the minimum
1306   time interval ∆t that satisfies C(0, ∆t)/C(0, 0) < θ with a threshold θ. Figure 18 shows the
1307   interval, denoted by ∆tthreshold , as a function of T − 1. This result implies that the transient time at
1308   high temperatures diverges with an exponent ≈ 1 when approaching the critical point.

       I    A NALYSIS OF JAPANESE GPT-2
1621
1622
       In the main text, we have investigated statistical properties of POS sequences generated by GPT-2
1623   with varying temperature and have found the phase transition between repetitive sequences and in-
1624   comprehensible ones. An interesting question is if this phase transition occurs with other languages
1625   and LLMs. Here, we analyze Japanese GPT-2 medium with 361M parameters (Zhao & Sawada;
1626   Sawada et al., 2024) using ja core news sm pipeline from spaCy library (Montani et al., 2023). For
1627   each T and N , 2.4 × 103 sequences were sampled. Error bars in the figures represent an 80%
1628   confidence interval estimated using the symmetric bootstrap-t method (Hall, 1988).
1629   As shown in Fig. 25, the integrated correlation diverges at low temperatures, while it seems to
1630   converge to a finite value at high temperatures. This result implies the phase transition point, such
1631   that the decay of correlation is slow below it and rapid above it. The power spectrum is also shown in
1632   Figs. 26–29. From these, we can observe that repetitive structures with many distinct Fourier modes
1633   emerge only at low temperatures. These results suggest that this LLM exhibits a phase transition
1634   similar to that in GPT-2.

       J    A NALYSIS OF P YTHIA
1891
1892
       In the main text, we have generated texts with GPT-2 and mapped them to POS sequences, to show
1893   the existence of a phase transition. In this appendix, to examine if the phase transition is observed
1894   with other models and mappings, we use Pythia with 70M and 1B parameters (Biderman et al.,
1895   2023) and a character-based mapping. First, we transformed texts to POS sequences in the same
1896   manner as in the main text. Second, we also transformed texts to sequences of numerals, where “A”
1897   or “a” is mapped to 0, “B” or “b” is mapped to 1, . . . , “Z” or “z” is mapped to 25, other characters
1898   to 26, and space, tab, etc. to 27. For each T and N , 104 sequences were sampled. Error bars in
1899   the figures represent an 80% confidence interval estimated using the symmetric bootstrap-t method
1900   (Hall, 1988).
1901   Figures 30 and 31 show the integrated correlations τPROPN,PROPN for POS sequences and τ26,26 for
1902   character-based sequences generated by Pythia 70M, respectively. Note that τPROPN,PROPN and τ26,26
1903   have the largest contributions for each setup. Similarly, Figs. 32 and 33 show the corresponding
1904   results for sequences generated by Pythia 1B. All these results suggest that the correlation diverges
1905   at low temperatures and converges at high temperatures. These results, similar to those in the main
1906   paper, support that the phase transition can occur with different mappings and LLMs, including
1907
       those of larger sizes.
