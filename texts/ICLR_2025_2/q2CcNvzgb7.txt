009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Nash equilibrium (NE) plays an important role in game theory. However, learn-
012            ing an NE in normal-form games (NFGs) is a complex, non-convex optimization
013            problem. Deep Learning (DL), the cornerstone of modern artificial intelligence,
014            has demonstrated remarkable empirical performance across various applications
015            involving non-convex optimization. However, applying DL to learn an NE poses
016            significant difficulties since most existing loss functions for using DL to learn
017            an NE introduce bias under sampled play. A recent work proposed an unbiased
018            loss function. Unfortunately, it suffers from high variance, which degrades the
019
               convergence rate. Moreover, learning an NE through this unbiased loss function
               entails finding a global minimum in a non-convex optimization problem, which
020
               is inherently difficult. To improve the convergence rate by mitigating the high
021
               variance associated with the existing unbiased loss function, we propose a novel
022            loss function, named Nash Advantage Loss (NAL). NAL is unbiased and exhibits
023            significantly lower variance than the existing unbiased loss function. In addition,
024            an NE is a stationary point of NAL rather than having to be a global minimum,
025            which improves the computational efficiency. Experimental results demonstrate
026            that the algorithm minimizing NAL achieves significantly faster empirical conver-
027            gence rates compared to previous algorithms, while also reducing the variance of
028            estimated loss value by several orders of magnitude.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Game theory is a powerful tool for modeling multi-agent interactions. A common goal to address
      games is Nash equilibrium (NE) where no player gains by unilaterally deviating from this equilibrium.
034
      However, learning an NE often involves a complex, non-convex optimization problem. Theoretically,
035
      learning an NE is PPAD-complete and thus computationally intractable (Daskalakis et al., 2009).
036
037   Deep Learning (DL) (LeCun et al., 2015) has risen as a predominant technology in contemporary
038   artificial intelligence, demonstrating remarkable performance in diverse real-world applications
039
      involving non-convex optimization problems such as image and speech recognition (Deng et al.,
      2014), natural language processing (Achiam et al., 2023), autonomous vehicles (Bojarski et al.,
040
      2016), and financial modeling (Heaton et al., 2017). Since NE learning is known as a non-convex
041
      optimization problem (Gemp et al., 2024), leveraging DL for NE learning presents a promising
042   research direction. However, the application of DL to NE learning remains largely unexplored.
043
044   A significant challenge for applying DL to learn an NE is the design of an appropriate loss function.
045
      Specifically, for an n-player, m-action, general-sum normal-form game (NFG), storing the payoff ma-
      trix requires nmn entries. As m and n increase, the storage complexity O(nmn ) grows exponentially,
046
      rendering it computationally prohibitive to load the entire payoff matrix into memory when solving
047
      large-scale NFGs. Thus, sampling a portion of the payoff matrix becomes necessary for solving
048   large-scale NFGs. However, most existing loss functions introduce bias (Nikaidô & Isoda, 1955;
049   Shoham & Leyton-Brown, 2008; Raghunathan et al., 2019; Gemp et al., 2022; Duan et al., 2023)
050   under sampled play (Gemp et al., 2024), making it infeasible to learn an NE in sampled settings. To
051   address this issue, Gemp et al. (2024) propose a novel loss function that can be unbiasedly estimated
052   under sampled play. However, this loss function suffers from high variance as its value is estimated
053   with the inner product of two independent and identically distributed random variables, which may
      degrade the convergence rate.

054
      To improve the convergence rate by mitigating the high variance associated with the existing unbiased
055   loss function, we propose a novel loss function called Nash Advantage Loss (NAL). Our key insight
056   is that: finding a way to obtain an unbiased estimate of the first-order gradient to eliminate the
057   need for calculating the inner product, which introduces high variance. Specifically, previous works
058   overlook the fact that optimizers commonly used in DL (Robbins & Monro, 1951; Bottou, 2010;
059   Kingma & Ba, 2014) require only unbiased estimates of the first-order gradient, rather than unbiased
060   estimates of the loss function. Consequently, NAL guarantees that obtaining an unbiased estimate of
061   its first-order gradient does not require the computation of the inner product between two random
062   variables, avoiding the high variance associated with inner products. In addition, inspired by the
063
      fact that learning a stationary point (e.g., a point where the first-order gradient is 0) is simpler than a
      global minimum since a global minimum is necessarily a stationary point while a stationary point is
064
      not always a global minimum (Jin et al., 2017), we ensure that an NE is a stationary point of NAL to
065
      improve the computational efficiency.
066
067   We conduct an empirical evaluation of the convergence rates and the variances of the estimated values
068   of the loss functions on eight NFGs from OpenSpiel (Lanctot et al., 2019) and GAMUT (Nudelman
069
      et al., 2004). Our results reveal that the algorithm that minimizes NAL significantly surpasses the
      convergence rates of previous algorithms, including algorithms that minimize existing unbiased
070
      or biased loss functions. Additionally, our algorithm exhibits significantly lower variance in the
071
      estimated values of its loss function compared to the algorithm minimizing the existing unbiased loss
072   function. Particularly, compared to the existing unbiased loss function, the variance of estimating
073   the value of NAL is typically reduced by two orders of magnitude. In some games, this variance
074   reduction can even reach six orders of magnitude. Moreover, we analyze the difference between the
075   estimated and true values across different loss functions. Our findings indicate that the difference
076   between the estimated and true values for our loss function is usually two orders of magnitude smaller
077   compared to that of other tested loss functions.
078   In conclusion, our contributions are as follows:
079
080          • We propose a novel loss function for using DL to learn an NE, named Nash Advantage Loss
081            (NAL). NAL can be estimated without bias under sampled play. More importantly, NAL will
082            incur significantly lower variance than the existing unbiased loss function as NAL avoids
083            the inner product of two random variables. In addition, learning an NE via minimizing NAL
084            implies only leaning a stationary point of NAL rather than learning a global minimum.
085          • We conduct a comprehensive empirical evaluation of the convergence rates and the variances
086            of estimating loss function values. The results demonstrate that our algorithm significantly
087            outperforms the algorithms minimizing previous loss functions, in terms of the empirical
088            convergence rate and the variance.

090   2    R ELATED W ORK
091
092   Our research aligns with studies that conceptualize the problem of learning an NE in NFGs as a
093   non-convex optimization problem and address it through DL methodologies, due to DL’s remarkable
094   empirical performance in solving such problems (Chen et al., 2019; Zou et al., 2019). Specifically,
095   we focus on studies that reduce NE computation to minimize a loss function via DL.
096   Sampling is critical for solving large-scale NFGs since the shape of the payoff matrix increases
097   exponentially as the action size increases linearly. However, most existing loss functions are unsuitable
098   for unbiased estimation under sampled play. These functions are biased under sampled play due to
099   either (i) the presence of a random variable as the argument of a complex, nonlinear function, or (ii)
100   unclear sampling methods (Gemp et al., 2024). For instance, duality gap based loss functions (Nikaidô
101   & Isoda, 1955; Shoham & Leyton-Brown, 2008; Duan et al., 2023; Gemp et al., 2022) introduce bias
102   through a max operator. Additionally, Gradient-based Nash Iteration (NI) (Raghunathan et al., 2019)
103   is biased due to the requirement of a projection operator that projects a random variable onto the
104
      simplex, which involves a max operator (Chen & Ye, 2011). Moreover, unconstrained optimization
      methods (Shoham & Leyton-Brown, 2008) that penalize deviation from the simplex lose the ability
105
      to sample from strategies when each iterate is no longer within the simplex.
106
107   To mitigate bias under sampled play, Gemp et al. (2024) propose a loss function that allows unbiased
      estimation under sampled play. Nevertheless, this loss function suffers from high variance due to that

      its value is estimated with the inner product of two independent and identically distributed random
109   variables. Specifically, the variance of this loss function, estimated using the inner product of two
110   random variables, is the square of the variance of estimating an individual random variable. This high
111   variance may degrade the convergence rate. To improve the convergence rate by mitigating the high
112   variance associated with the existing unbiased loss function, we propose a novel loss function, which
113   allows unbiased estimation under sampled play, while incurring lower variance.

      We do not consider algorithms that replicate tabular methods with DL, i.e., those that approximate
115   table-represented variables using deep neural networks without modifying update rules, such as
116   NFSP (Heinrich & Silver, 2016), PSRO (Lanctot et al., 2017), and Deep CFR (Brown et al., 2019).
117   More discussions about learning NE via DL can be found in Appendix A.

172   4.1   OVERVIEW OF NAL
173
174   Our key insight is that: finding a way to obtain an unbiased estimate of the first-order gradient to
175   eliminate the need for calculating the inner product, which introduces high variance. In particular,
176   the insight comes from a fact overlooked in previous work that optimizers (Robbins & Monro, 1951;
177
      Bottou, 2010; Kingma & Ba, 2014) commonly used in DL require only unbiased estimates of the
      first-order gradient.
178
179   Lemma 4.1. For any vector b ∈ Rd and any y in a (d − 1)-dimensional simplex, the equation
180   b − ⟨b, y⟩1 = 0 holds if and only if all coordinates of b are equal.
181
182   Specifically, NAL aims to ensure that (i) its first-order gradient can be estimated without bias using a
183
      single random variable to reduce the variance, and (ii) its first-order gradient with respect to x ∈ X ◦
      equals 0 if and only if x ∈ X ∗,◦ to improve the computational efficiency, inspired by the fact that
184
      finding a stationary point, i.e., a point where the first-order gradient is 0, is simpler than finding a
185
      global minimum (Jin et al., 2017). To achieve this, we build on the key insight from the loss function
186   defined in Eq. (1)—where for any x ∈ X ◦ and i ∈ N , ∇xi ui (x)(ai ) = ∇xi ui (x)(a′i ) ∀ai , a′i ∈ Ai
187   if and only if x ∈ X ∗,◦ —and recognize that ∇xi ui (x) can be estimated without bias using a single
188   random variable (e.g., via importance sampling). Then, inspired by Lemma 4.1, we define NAL’s
189   first-order gradient as the difference between the gradient of the utility function of the game and the
190   inner product of the utility function’s gradient with any arbitrary given strategy x̂. This difference is
191   the advantage of each action’s gradient for making the gradients of actions more uniform. Formally,
192   the first-order gradient can be

529   6     C ONCLUSIONS
530
      We introduce a novel loss function for using DL to compute an NE, named NAL. This loss function
531
      can be estimated without bias, and will incur extremely lower variance than the existing unbiased
532   loss function. In addition, an NE is only a stationary point of NAL rather than having to be a global
533   minimum. Experimental results show that the algorithm minimizing NAL significantly outperforms
534   other tested algorithms.
535
      Our approach offers a promising new direction for computing an NE, with the potential to address the
536
      challenges posed by large-scale games. One direction of our future works is to extend our approach
537
      to solve imperfect information extensive-form games.

      A     D ISCUSSION ON L EARNING NASH EQUILIBRIA VIA D EEP L EARNING
703
704
      Deep learning has emerged as a powerful framework for solving complex optimization and decision-
705   making problems across a wide range of domains. At its core, deep learning leverages neural networks
706   to approximate high-dimensional, non-linear functions, making it particularly suited for modeling
707   strategic interactions in multi-agent systems and solving NE (LeCun et al., 2015; Goodfellow, 2016).
708   Unlike traditional methods, which often rely on explicit mathematical formulations or exhaustive
709   enumeration of strategies, deep learning-based approaches can generalize across diverse scenarios by
710   learning directly from minimizing a loss function (Silver et al., 2017; Mnih et al., 2015).
711   One of the primary strengths of deep learning is its expressive power, allowing it to capture highly
712   complex patterns and relationships that are difficult to model with traditional methods. Neural
713   networks, especially deep architectures, can approximate arbitrary non-linear functions (Hornik,
714   1991), making them well-suited for representing the strategic decision-making process in game
715   theory. The ability of deep learning models to learn from raw data—without the need for hand-
716   crafted features—enables them to uncover intricate equilibrium strategies that might otherwise be
717   overlooked (Brown et al., 2019; Goktas et al., 2022; Marris et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022).
718   A series of deep learning DL-based methods have been developed to learn NE. To the best of
719   our knowledge, the first work on learning NE via DL is proposed by Duan et al. (2023), which
720   demonstrates the feasibility of using DL to learn NE. Subsequently, Marris et al. (2022); Goktas et al.
721   (2022); Liu et al. (2022) conduct extensive research on network architectures tailored for learning NE
722   through DL. However, these architectures are unsuitable for solving real-world games, as they assume
723   that the payoff matrix can be fully loaded into memory as input to the network. In real-world games,
724   the payoff matrix is often too large to fit into memory, necessitating solutions based on sampling a
725
      subset of the matrix, referred to as sampled play.
726   Under sampled play, designing appropriate loss functions specifically tailored for equilibrium com-
727   putation remains a significant challenge. Many existing loss functions rely on sampling to estimate
728   gradients or payoffs, which can introduce significant biases (Duan et al., 2023; Gemp et al., 2022) or
729   result in high variance (Gemp et al., 2024), making training unstable. This limitation underscores the
730   need for further research into designing unbiased, low-variance loss functions that are better aligned
731
      with the requirements of equilibrium computation. Nonetheless, the intersection of deep learning and
      game theory offers a promising avenue for solving complex, real-world problems, from economic
732
      markets to game theory.
