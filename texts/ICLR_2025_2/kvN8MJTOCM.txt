009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Model stealing poses a significant security risk in machine learning by enabling
012            attackers to replicate a black-box model without access to its training data, thus
013            jeopardizing intellectual property and exposing sensitive information. Recent
014            methods that use pre-trained diffusion models for data synthesis improve effi-
015            ciency and performance but rely heavily on manually crafted prompts, limiting
016            automation and scalability, especially for attackers with little expertise. To assess
017            the risks posed by open-source pre-trained models, we propose a more realistic
018
               threat model that eliminates the need for prompt design skills or knowledge of
               class names. In this context, we introduce Stealix, the first approach to perform
019
               model stealing without predefined prompts. Stealix uses two open-source pre-
020
               trained models to infer the victim model’s data distribution, and iteratively refines
021            prompts through a genetic algorithm based on a proxy metric, progressively im-
022            proving the precision and diversity of synthetic images. Our experimental results
023            demonstrate that Stealix significantly outperforms other methods, even those with
024            access to class names or fine-grained prompts, while operating under the same
025            query budget. These findings highlight the scalability of our approach and sug-
026            gest that the risks posed by pre-trained generative models in model stealing may
027            be greater than previously recognized.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Model stealing allows attackers to replicate the functionality of machine learning models without di-
033
      rect access to training data or model weights. By querying the victim model with hold-out datasets,
      the attacker can construct a proxy model that behaves similarly to the original by mimicking its pre-
034
      dictions. This type of attack compromises the model owner’s intellectual property and may expose
035
      sensitive information contained in the model, posing both security and privacy risks (Beetham et al.,
036   2022; Carlini et al., 2024).
037
038   Current model stealing methods for image classification can be categorized based on the source of
039
      the queried images: (1) using publicly available images (Orekondy et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2024),
      (2) generating images by training a generator within Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) from
040
      scratch (Truong et al., 2021; Sanyal et al., 2022), or (3) synthesizing images by prompting pre-
041
      trained open-source generative models (Shao et al., 2023; Hondru & Ionescu, 2023). The latter
042   uses models like Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) to achieve superior efficiency by reducing
043   the dependence on online data sources and by eliminating the high computational cost of training
044   new generators. Previous approaches use human-crafted prompts or class names to synthesize im-
045   ages with a text-to-image model, but they overlook scenarios where class names lack context or fail
046   to capture victim data features. Attackers may also lack sufficient knowledge of the victim’s data
047   distribution or may struggle to describe it accurately. Moreover, the dependence on human interven-
048   tion can greatly hinder scalability and automation, thus limiting the applicability of model stealing.
049   These issues are most prevalent in specialized fields, where the highest value models can be found
050
      (e.g., medical applications). Therefore, existing research under the current assumptions may over-
      simplify the problem and underestimate the threat posed by model stealing enhanced by pre-trained
051
      models.
052
053   To address these limitations and assess the risk of pre-trained models in model stealing, we propose
      a realistic threat model where the attacker lacks prior knowledge or expertise in designing prompts

072   In this work, we introduce Stealix, the first model stealing approach that removes the need for
073
      human-crafted prompts by leveraging two pre-trained open-source models, as depicted in Figure 1.
      Our method employs a text-to-image generative model and a vision-language model to iteratively
074
      generate multiple refined prompts for each class. We employ contrastive learning to optimize the
075
      prompt to describe the target class based on features extracted from the prompt itself and from
076   image triplets by the vision-language model. To further improve the precision and diversity of the
077   prompts, we propose a proxy metric as the fitness function to evaluate and evolve the prompts. In
078   practice, our approach requires only a single real image per class. We show that this is sufficient
079   to achieve superior performance without requiring manual prompt engineering. We emphasize that
080   this assumption is practical, as potential attackers, typically competitors, often have limited data
081   available, but fail to synthesize more. Overall, we summarize our contributions as follows.
082   Contributions. (i) We introduce a more practical threat model that eliminates the need for expertise
083   in prompt design and closely reflects scalability needs in real-world scenarios. (ii) We propose
084   Stealix, the first prompt-unknown approach that leverages a proxy metric to iteratively refine the
085   prompts. The statistical analysis demonstrates a high correlation between the proxy metric and the
086   feature distance to the victim data. (iii) Our method outperforms approaches that rely on class names
087   or human-designed prompts across multiple datasets, an assumption frequently not held in practice.
088   It achieves up to a 22.2% improvement in attacker model accuracy at a low query budget. (iv)
089   We expose significant risks associated with open-source models in model stealing, highlighting the
090
      urgent need for advanced defenses or strategies to prevent their harmful exploitation.

092   2   R ELATED W ORKS
093
094
      Knowledge distillation. Knowledge distillation (KD) is a model compression technique that trains
095
      a smaller student model to replicate the performance of a larger teacher model, thereby enabling
096   deployment on hardware with limited computational resources (Ba & Caruana, 2014; Hinton et al.,
097   2015). Traditional KD methods assume access to the teacher’s training data, allowing the student to
098   learn from the same data distribution. When this is impractical due to data size or sensitivity, alter-
099   natives such as surrogate datasets (Lopes et al., 2017) or data-free KD using data generators (Fang
100   et al., 2019; Micaelli & Storkey, 2019) are employed. These methods typically require white-box
101   access to the teacher model for back-propagation. In contrast, model stealing adopts an adversarial
102   approach, where neither training data nor internal model details are available to the attacker.
103   Model Stealing. Model stealing aims to replicate either the victim model’s properties, such as
104   hyperparameters or learned parameters (Wang & Gong, 2018; Tramèr et al., 2016), or its behavior,
105   known as functionality model stealing (Oliynyk et al., 2023). The latter involves training a proxy
106   model to mimic the victim’s behavior and is a threat across domains, including images (Truong
107   et al., 2021), language (Krishna et al., 2020), and robotics (Zhuang et al., 2024). Our work falls
      into the second category in the image domain. Prevalent methods train a generator from scratch

108
      to adversarially synthesize data for querying the victim model (Truong et al., 2021; Sanyal et al.,
109   2022; Beetham et al., 2022), but this demands millions of queries, making it costly. Recently, more
110   efficient methods using pre-trained diffusion models have been proposed, reducing query budget and
111   enhancing model stealing performance (Shao et al., 2023; Hondru & Ionescu, 2023). For instance,
112   Active Self-Paced Knowledge Distillation (ASPKD) (Hondru & Ionescu, 2023) uses a three-step
113   process: generating images with a diffusion model, querying the victim model with a limited set,
114   and pseudo-labeling the remaining samples using nearest neighbors for attacker model training.
115   However, these methods depend on class-name prompts, which are inadequate for generating images
116   that are similar to the victim data in complex scenarios. To address this, we propose a method that
117
      begins with a randomly initialized prompt and refines it iteratively, effectively removing the reliance
      on such prior knowledge.
118
119   Textual inversion. Textual inversion (Gal et al., 2023) is a method that learns a prompt corre-
120   sponding to a specific image or set of images, enabling pre-trained text-to-image models to generate
121   personalized and more targeted outputs. One notable application is DA-Fusion (Trabucco et al.,
122   2024), which employs textual inversion to learn prompts from a seed image and synthesize simi-
123
      lar images for data augmentation. This approach shows potential for our threat model, where the
      attacker has a seed image but lacks the prompt needed to generate relevant images. To assess the
124
      effectiveness of DA-Fusion in our threat model, we adopt it as a baseline by replacing the original
125
      labels with the victim model’s predictions for attacker model training.

128   3    T HREAT M ODEL
129
130   In this section, we formalize the proposed practical threat model for model stealing. We begin by
131   introducing the necessary notations and definitions. Next, we describe the behavior of the victim
132   model. Finally, we detail the attacker’s goals and available knowledge, highlighting the constraints
133   that make model stealing challenging.
134
135   Notations. Let D = {(xi , yi )} be the dataset used to train an image classification model, where
136   xi ∈ RH×W ×C represents input images with height H, width W , and C channels, and yi ∈
137   {1, 2, . . . , K} denotes the corresponding class labels, with K being the total number of classes.
138   Each class is indexed by c ∈ {1, 2, . . . , K}. The pre-trained generative model G generates an image
139   x ∼ G(p, ϵ) from a given prompt p by denoising noise ϵ drawn from a standard normal distribution
140   ϵ ∼ N (0, 1). For brevity, we denote this process as x ∼ G(p).
141
142   Victim model. The victim trains a classification model V with parameters θv on a dataset DV ,
143   where images are drawn from the victim data distribution x ∼ PV . Once deployed, it oper-
144   ates as a black-box accessible for queries. To mitigate model stealing risks, we consider the
145   victim model returns only the top-1 predicted class, effectively reducing the amount of infor-
146
      mation available to potential attackers (Sanyal et al., 2022). Thus, for a given input image x,
      y ∗ = V (x; θv ) ∈ {1, 2, . . . , K} is the predicted class label.
147
148
149   Goal and knowledge of the attacker. The attacker’s objective is to train a surrogate model
150
      A(x; θa ), parameterized by θa that replicates the behavior of the victim model V . The attacker
      has black-box access to V , allowing them to query the model with images and receive the predicted
151
      top-1 labels. The attacker is constrained by a query budget, representing the total number of queries
152
      available per class, denoted as B. The attacker lacks knowledge of (i) the architecture and parame-
153   ters of V , (ii) the dataset DV used to train V , and (iii) prompt design expertise. We also limit the use
154   of class names, as they may by chance serve as good prompts; using them would diverge from the
155   assumption that the attacker lacks prompt design expertise. The lack of the prompt design expertise
156   significantly limits the attacker to leverage the generative model for efficient model stealing.

183   can be expressed by minimizing the loss between the outputs of the victim and surrogate models
184   over the victim’s data distribution, for this task we consider cross-entropy loss:

239   4.3   P ROMPT C ONSISTENCY
240
241   To evaluate whether the optimized prompt effectively captures the features learned by the victim
242   model, we propose a proxy metric, prompt consistency (PC). Since direct access to the victim data
243   distribution is unavailable, this metric serves as an indicator of distribution similarity and is used
244   for prompt reproduction. We assume that if a prompt captures the latent features of the target class
245
      learned by the victim model, the synthetic images will be consistently classified as the target class by
      the victim model, implying a closer resemblance with the victim data. Based on this assumption, PC
246
      measures how well a prompt generates images that match the target class c (Figure 2 right). Given
247
      a prompt p, a batch of synthetic images {xi }M i=1 ∼ G(p) is generated, where M is the number of
248   images. PC is computed as:

306   Dataset. We train the victim model on four datasets: EuroSAT (Helber et al., 2019), PASCAL
307   VOC (Everingham et al., 2010), DomainNet (Peng et al., 2019), and CIFAR10 (Alex, 2009). Each
308   dataset is chosen for its specific challenges in evaluating model stealing attacks. EuroSAT requires
309   specialized prompts for satellite-based land use classification, as class names alone fail to generate
310   relevant images. In PASCAL VOC, images are labeled by the largest object, testing the ability to
311   identify the primary target from the victim model’s prediction. DomainNet evaluates transfer learn-
312   ing across six diverse domains: clipart, infograph, paintings, quickdraw, real images, and sketches.
313   A seed image is randomly chosen from one domain to test cross-domain generalization, using 10
314
      of 345 classes for manageability. In CIFAR10, class names can guide image synthesis, leading to
      strong baselines when used by other methods, compared to ours, which does not. See Appendix B
315
      fore more details. In Appendix J, we introduce results on two medical datasets, highlighting the
316
      challenges when the diffusion model has limited domain-specific knowledge.
317

319   ImageNet-pretrained weights. Victim models are trained with SGD, Nesterov with momentum 0.9,

      a 0.01 learning rate, 5 × 10−4 weight decay, and cosine annealing for 50 epochs.

322   refinement, with a learning rate of 0.1 and 500 optimization steps using the AdamW optimizer. We
323   employ Stable Diffusion-v2 (Rombach et al., 2022) as the generative model, with a guidance scale
      of 9 and 25 inference steps. PC evaluation uses M = 10 images. Stable Diffusion-v2 is used

      Limitations and future work. Our approach, unlike GAN-based methods, does not require back-
515
      propagation through the victim model to train the generator, which enhances generalization across
516
      victim model architectures (Appendix E). Although the attacker model architecture can still influ-
517   ence the performance (Appendix D), our method consistently outperforms the baselines. Further-
518   more, since image synthesis and attacker model training are decoupled, attackers can reuse synthetic
519   images for hyperparameter tuning and neural architecture search. This key advantage could be fur-
520   ther explored in future work to improve model accuracy. Finally, as open-source generative models
521   advance, integrating more powerful models into our framework offers significant potential for fur-
522   ther enhancements.

      7     C ONCLUSION
526
527   We demonstrate that attackers can leverage open-source generative models to steal proprietary mod-
528
      els, even without expertise in prompt design or access to class information. Without direct access to
      victim data, we show that prompt evolution as done by Stealix significantly improves model extrac-
529
      tion efficiency. Furthermore, we underscore the crucial role of matching the similarity between the
530
      generated data with the victim data, which enhances the effectiveness of the attack. This is the first
531   study to expose the risks posed by publicly available pre-trained generative models in model theft
532   under a realistic attack setting. We call for more attention toward developing defense mechanisms
533   to mitigate this emerging threat.

707   Prompt refinement. We implement the hard prompt optimization method proposed by Wen et al.
708
      (2024) to optimize the prompt to capture target class features learnt by the victim model (Algo-

709
      embedding set E. The soft prompt is iteratively mapped to its nearest neighbor embeddings using
710

877   G    L IMITATIONS OF DFME
878
879   We analyze the performance of DFME (Truong et al., 2021) under realistic attack scenarios. Fol-
880   lowing the original DFME setup, we attempted to extract our ResNet34 victim model trained on
881   CIFAR-10 using 2 million queries per class with soft-label access, achieving an attacker model ac-
882
      curacy of 87.4%, which is comparable to the results reported in the original work. However, DFME
      generates images with pixel values in the range (−1, 1) due to the use of Tanh activation, which is
883
      incompatible with real-world APIs that expect standard image formats (e.g., pixel values in [0, 255]).
884
      After quantizing these images to the standard format, the attacker model accuracy dropped to 76.4%,
885   despite using the same query budget. This performance degradation occurs because DFME relies
886   on adding small perturbations to the generated images to estimate gradients via forward differ-
887   ences (Wibisono et al., 2012). Quantization can negate these subtle perturbations. Furthermore,
888   when the victim model provides only hard-label outputs as a defense mechanism, the attacker model
889   accuracy further decreased to 23.7%. In this case, the output labels remain constant under small
890   input perturbations, rendering forward difference methods ineffective for gradient estimation and
891   significantly limiting the attacker’s ability to train the generator.
892   We present the results across all datasets in Table 11. In the case of PASCAL, we reduced the
893   batch size from 256 to 64 due to computational constraints imposed by the large image size (256 ×
894   256 pixels). Notably, DFME fails to extract the PASCAL victim model, likely due to this higher
895   image resolution. Furthermore, for the fine-grained EuroSAT dataset, even with soft-label access
896   and without quantization, the attacker model achieves only 19.0% accuracy.
