011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            For mental disorder diagnosis, most previous works are task-specific and focus
014            primarily on functional connectivity network (FCN) derived from functional MRI
015            (fMRI) data. However, the high cost of fMRI acquisition limits its practicality in
016            real-world clinical settings. Meanwhile, the more easily obtainable 3D T1-weighted
017            (T1w) MRI, which captures brain anatomy, is ofen overlooked in standard diagnos-
018
               tic processes of mental disorders. To address these two issues, we propose CINP
               (Contrastive Image-Network Pre-training), a framework that employs contrastive
019
               learning between 3D T1w MRI and FCNs. CINP aims to learn a joint latent
020
               semantic space that integrates complementary information from both functional
021            and structural perspective. During pre-training, we incorporate masked image
022            modeling loss and network-image matching loss to enhance visual representation
023            learning and modality alignment. Furthermore, thanks to contrastive pre-training
024            which facilitates knowledge transfer from FCN to T1w MRI, we introduce network
025            prompting. This protocol leverages 3D T1w MRI from suspected patients and
026            FCNs from confirmed patients for differential diagnosis of mental disorders. Ex-
027            tensive experiments across three mental disorder diagnosis tasks demonstrate the
028            competitive performance of CINP, using both linear probing and network prompt-
029
               ing, compared with FCN-based methods and self-supervised pre-training methods.
               These results highlight the potential of CINP to enhance diagnostic processes with
030
               the aid of 3D T1w MRI in real-world clinical scenario.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) is a modern neuroimaging technique, which can
037   non-invasively investigate brain function by detecting the blood-oxygen-level-dependent (BOLD)
038   responses to task-related neuronal activity (Logothetis, 2008). Over recent years, there has been
039
      growing evidence that mental disorders arise from dysfunction of interconnected patterns of regions-
      of-interest (ROIs) in the whole brain (Krishna et al., 2023), and fMRI-derived functional connectivity
040
      network (FCN), as a graph/network architecture with nodes being ROIs and each edge being functional
041
      connectivity (FC) between paired ROIs, has received considerable attention in diagnosis of mental
042   disorders (Yang et al., 2021; Bastos & Schoffelen, 2016), where FC is in general measured as
043   statistical dependence between BOLD time courses of paired ROIs.
044
045
      A significant amount of work has been dedicated to learning deep and differentiable representations
      for FCNs, with explorations based on deep learning models, such as graph neural networks (GNN) (Li
046
      et al., 2021b; Kan et al., 2022a; Kim et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024), convolutional neural networks
047
      (CNN) (Kawahara et al., 2017; Huang et al., 2017), and graph transformer (Kan et al., 2022b).
048   Despite significant progress, deep learning based FCN analysis for mental disorder diagnosis has
049   yet to be widely adopted in real-wrold clinical practice. There are two pervasive challenges, i.e., the
050   limited generalizability and adaptability caused by the small size of annotated fMRI dataset and the
051   lack of integration with anatomical information/features from the easily obtainable 3D T1w MRI.
052   Mental disorders can remodel functional neural circuitry as mentioned above, and yet the anatomical
053   knowledge of the brain necessarily constrains its function (Pang et al., 2023). This indicates that 3D
      T1w MRI, which assesses brain anatomy structures, holds potential for mental disorder diagnosis, and

054
      an efficient integration of structural and functional perspectives may provide a more comprehensive
055   view of neurobiological abnormalities in mental disorders, resulting in better diagnostic precision.
056
057   It is well known that supervised models usually require sufficient labeled training data, which are
058
      expensive and time-consuming. Therefore, self-supervised pre-training (SSP) has recently emerged
      for establishing a foundation model that can be transferred to downstream biomedical tasks including
059
      disease diagnosis and medical image segmentation. For example, considering that MAE (masked
060
      autoencoder)-based methods (He et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023b) have achieved
061   high performance in various natural image analysis tasks, masked image modeling (MIM) has been
062   applied to learning robust representations of 3D medical images (Zhou et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
063   2023c) through reconstructing 3D volumes of medical images from their highly-masked versions.
064
      In addition, motivated by visual-language pre-training (VLP) that has received tremendous success
065
      on downstream vision and language tasks by pre-training a visual language model on large-scale
066
      image-text pairs (Radford et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021a; 2022), efforts have been attempted to adapt
067   self-supervised VLP in the biomedical domain, which means pre-training a visual language model
068   using 3D medical images with natural language supervision from the corresponding radiology
069   reports (Boecking et al., 2022; Bannur et al., 2023). Beyond images and text, it is noteworthy that
070   various modalities of medical imaging can inherently offer contrasting perspectives, such as sMRI
071   and fMRI, which provide structural and functional information of the human brain, respectively.
072   A bidrectional mapping scheme Ye et al. (2023) was proposed to conduct ROI-level contrastive
073   learning between diffusion MRI (dMRI) derived strucutral connectivity network and BOLD signal.
074   However, the model architecture and the limited number of dMRI data limit the scalability.
075   In this regard, this paper focuses on self-supervised contrastive pre-training on paired 3D T1w
076   MRI image and FCN data for mental disorder diagnosis. We propose Contrastive Image-Network
077   Pre-training (CINP) to jointly learn 3D T1w MRI and FCN representations that can be fully leveraged
078   by downstream mental disorder diagnosis, as illustrated in Fig. 1. Specifically, paired 3D T1w
079   MRI image and FCN data are fed into a visual encoder and a network encoder to extract the
080   embeddings, respectively. Subsequently, the similarity matrix between image embeddings and FCN
081
      embeddings is computed by utilizing cosine similarity to facilitate image-network contrastive loss
      calculation. Masked image modeling loss and image-network matching loss are involved for better
082
      visual representation learning and modality alignment. In particular, we develop network prompting
083
      protocol which leverages 3D T1w MRI from suspected patients and FCNs from confirmed patients
084   for differential diagnosis of mental disorders. Networks of different subject classes are used as
085   prompt where the similarity between the embedding of 3D T1 MRI and the embedding of networks is
086   calculated and the image is assigned to the class where the corresponding networks hold the highest
087   similarity with the image. The effectiveness of CINP is finally demonstrated on three mental disorder
088   datasets by comparing CINP with FCN-based models and SSP-based models with linear probing,
089   and network prompting protocols.
090   To sum up, our contributions are as follows: (1) We proposed CINP which builds a joint latent
091   semantic space for 3D T1w MRI images and FCNs. It can combine the complimentary information
092   from multi-modal neuroimaging and benefit downstream biomedical tasks. (2) We introduced network
093   prompting protocol which can achieve differential diagnosis of mental disorders based on 3D T1w
094   MRI from suspected patients and FCNs from confirmed patients (3) Extensive experiments across
095   three mental disorder diagnosis tasks demonstrate the competitive performance of CINP.

141   2.2   P RE - TRAINING O BJECTIVES
142
143   Inspired by the insights of previous multi-modal learning methods (Radford et al., 2021; Li et al.,
144   2021a) between image and natural language, CINP is pre-trained with three objective functions
145   associated with image-network contrastive learning (INC), masked image modeling (MIM), and
146   image-network matching (INM), respectively.
147
148   Image-Network Contrastive Learning. Studies on vision-language pre-training have shown that
149   contrastive learning between image-text pairs can align the hidden semantic spaces of image and
150
      text modalities. With this hypothesis, we aim to enhance the representation of 3D medical images
      with semantic information from FCNs by conducting contrastive learning between image-network
151
      pairs. Specifically, Given a pair of image and network data (i.e., a 3D T1w MRI image and an FCN),
152
      we want to learn a similarity function s = vIT vN , such that the image-network pair from the same
153   individual have a higher similarity score.

      3.1     DATASETS AND P REPROCESSING
243
244
      Datasets. We downloaded several publicly available datasets where the subjects have both 3D
245
      T1w MRI images and resting-state fMRI (rs-fMRI) data. As a result, a healthy cohort used for
246   pre-training was composed of four datasets, i.e., HBN (Alexander et al., 2017), HCP (Van Essen
247   et al., 2013), QTIM (Strike et al., 2023), and CNP (Bilder et al., 2020), and three mental disorder
248   datasets (i.e., ABIDE (Di Martino et al., 2014), ADHD (consortium, 2012), and SRPBS (Tanaka et al.,
249   2021)) were used for evaluation. We listed these datasets with basic demographic information in
250   Table 1. Briefly, the pre-training cohort consisted of 4619 healthy subjects. For the evaluation datasets,
251   the ABIDE dataset included 460 health controls (HCs) and 395 patients diagnosed with Autism
252   Spectrum Disorder (ASD); the ADHD dataset included 547 HCs and 325 patients diagnosed with
253   Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD); and the SRPBS dataset included 783 HCs and 614
254
      patients individually diagnosed with nine distinct mental disorders, including ASD, Major Depression
      Disorder (MDD), Obsessive Compulsive Disorder (OCD), schizophrenia spectrum disorder (SSD),
255
      and so on.
256
257
258   Data Preprocessing. All MR imaging data were preprocessed by fMRIPrep (Esteban et al., 2019),
259   an easily accessible, state-of-the-art fMRI data preprocessing pipeline that is robust against variations
260   in scan acquisition protocols. For 3D T1w MRI images, intensity correction, skull-stripping, and
261
      spatial normalization to standard anatomical space (MNI152) were performed. For rs-fMRI data,
      slice timing correction, head-motion correction, skull-stripping, co-registration to T1 reference, and
262
      spatial normalization to MNI152 space were conducted. After such preprocessing procedures, both
263
      3D T1w MRI images and rs-fMRI data had a voxel size of 2mm3 . To derive FCNs from rs-fMRI on
264   the automated anatomical labelling (AAL) atlas (Tzourio-Mazoyer et al., 2002) that divide the whole
265   brain into 116 ROIs, we defined FC as Pearson’s correlation between BOLD time courses of paired
266   ROIs. To align the node feature dimensions of the graph-represented FCNs from datasets where the
267   scanning durations were different, we employed nodal connection profiles, i.e., the corresponding
268   row for each node in the FCN matrix, as the node features. The defined node features have been
269   demonstrated to achieve superior performance over other kinds of node features, such as node
      identities, degrees, and eigenvector-based embeddings (Cui et al., 2022; Kan et al., 2022a).

      3.2   BASELINES
271
272   FCN-based Methods. We deployed four types of FCN-based models based on their network archi-
273   tectures for comparison, which were GNN-based (GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016) and BrainGNN (Li
274   et al., 2021b)), hyperGNN based (HGCN (Wang et al., 2024) and MHAHGEL (Wang et al.,
275   2024)), CNN-based (BrainNetCNN (Kawahara et al., 2017)), and transformer-based (vanilla Trans-
276   former (Kan et al., 2022b) and BrainNetworkTransformer (Kan et al., 2022b)), respectively. It is
277   worth mentioning that we here used a single-atlas version of MHAHGEL. These methods all utilized
278
      FCNs as inputs, and were trained from scratch on the three mental disorder datasets separately in that
      they were task-specific methods. For fair comparison, we followed the hyper-parameter selection and
279
      implementation of these methods in the corresponding papers.
280
281
      Medical SSP based Methods. Three medical SSP based methods were used for comparison, which
282
      were MedicalNet (Chen et al., 2019), PRCLv2 (Zhou et al., 2023), and Swin-UNETR (Tang et al.,
283   2022), respectively. These baseline models for comparison applied 3D medical images (i.e., computed
284   tomograph (CT) and sMRI) as inputs and pre-training resources, and were executed following the
285   instructions and implementation in their respective papers as well. We employed both linear probe and
286   fine-tuning protocols on these methods. For linear probe, we used the pre-training models provided
287   in their papers. The feature maps of the last layer of these methods were flattened by an average
288   pooling layer and reshaped to feature vectors, which were then directly fed into an SVM classifier for
289   classification. On the other hand, considering the semantic gap and distribution shift between the
290   pre-training data and test data, we also fine-tuned the models that were initialized with pre-trained
291
      weights for 10 epochs on the evaluation datasets.
292
293   3.3   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
294
      Implementation Details. The CINP pre-training model was implemented with PyTorch 1.13.1 and
295   MONAI 1.2.0 (Cardoso et al., 2022). For pre-training, we utilized the Adam optimizer with an initial
296   learning rate of 10−5 and a weight decay of 10−5 . The cosine annealing schedule was applied for the
297   learning rate decreasing to 10−6 . The batch size was set to 256. The pre-training performed on a
298   server equipped with 8 NVIDIA A800-80G GPUs for 400 epochs cost about 100 hours. Since the
299   scales of the three component losses are similar, we set α = β = 1 in the formula 5.
300
      During the pre-training, the input 3D T1w MRI images were randomly augmented in order to learn
301   robust visual representations. Random augmentations included Gaussian noise addition, random
302   flipping, random intensity scaling, and random intensity shifting. After augmentation, the volumes of
303   3D T1 sMRI images were resized to 96 × 96 × 96.
304
305   Performance Evaluation. The diagnoses of ASD and ADHD were two binary classification tasks
306   on ABIDE and ADHD datasets, respectively, which were evaluated in terms of accuracy (ACC), area
307   under ROC curve (AUC), and Matthews correlation coefficient (MCC). For the SRPBS dataset, the
308   classification of ten classes among HC, ASD, MDD, OCD, SSD, pain, bipolar disorder, dysthymia,
309   and other mental disorders was conducted, and ACC and MCC were calculated for evaluation.
310   For linear probe and fine-tuning protocols, we divided the evaluation dataset into training (70%),
311
      validation (10%), and testing (20%) sets randomly, ensuring fair comparison of all models. Note that
      the network prompting protocol had the same data partition, where the FCNs were sampled from the
312
      training set and 3D T1w MRI images in the testing set were evaluated.

351   3.5      R ESULTS A NALYSIS
352
353   The supervision from FCNs can enhance the predictive power of 3D T1 MRI images for mental
354   disorder identification. Compared with the best results among SSP-based models, our CINP model
355
      using linear probe improved the accuracy performance by 5.55%, 1.26%, and 5.72% on the ABIDE,
      ADHD, and SRPBS datasets, respectively. The AUC performance on the ABIDE and ADHD datasets
356
      were respectively improved by 2.92% and 2.61%, while the MCC performance on the ABIDE dataset
357
      was increased by 4.69%. This indicates that by conducting contrastive learning between 3D T1
358   MRI images and FCNs, their mutually complementary information can be fully captured, benefitting
359   mental disorder identification. Moreover, by fine-tuning the model on downstream tasks, SSP-based
360   models achieved better performance than CINP using linear probe, which means that the performance
361   of CINP holds potential to be further improved.
362
363   The predictive power of various neuroimaging modalities differs across different mental dis-
364   orders. Although the CINP model with the linear probe protocol defeated all SSP-based models
365   and some FCN-based models, it failed to achieve the-state-of-art performance on the ABIDE dataset
366   while FCN-based models were weaker than all SSP-based models and the CINP model on the ADHD
367
      dataset. This suggests that 3D T1 MRI images can provide more effective information for the diag-
      nosis of ADHD while the ASD identification may require more involvement of FCNs, even though
368
      the imaging feature embeddings were enhanced by FCN supervision during contrastive pre-training.
369

370   of FCN-based methods and SSP-based methods were interlaced, and the CINP models which were
371   pre-trained on both 3D T1 MRI images and FCNs achieved the best performance. It again emphasizes
372   the importance of integration of 3D T1 MRI images and FCNs for mental disorder diagnosis.
373

427
428   4.1     S ELF - SUPERVISED P RE - TRAINING FOR 3D M EDICAL I MAGE A NALYSIS
429
430   Self-supervised pre-training for 3D medical image analysis primarily follows two main approaches:
431   contrastive learning and pretext task. Following the approaches on natural images, medical con-
      trastive learning continues to focus on the interplay between medical images and the descriptive


                                                                        8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      text. Gloria (Huang et al., 2021) was an attention-based framework that contrasted sub-regions of
433   2D medical images and words in the paired report to learn global and local representations. To
434   enlarge the size of 3D medical image-text pairs, several methods (Liu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b)
435   attempted to convert the 3D images into 2D slices and subsequently employ generative models to
436   generate captions for the 3D medical images. To create unified representations for diverse modalities
437   of medical images, UniMedI (He et al., 2023) which utilized diagnostic reports as common semantic
438   space was proposed. On the other hand, pretext tasks were designed to learn representation from inner
439   structure space of 3D medical images and have shown promise in dense prediction tasks, e.g., lesion
440   segmentation. MedicalNet (Chen et al., 2019) and PCRL (Zhou et al., 2023) were ResNet-based
441
      architectures, pre-trained with the segmentation task and the pixel restoration task on 3D medical
      images, respectively. Pre-training with MIM and ViT-based MED3D (Chen et al., 2023c) showed
442
      effectiveness under different practical scenarios. Swin-UNETR (Tang et al., 2022) was a 3D swin
443
      transformer with three pretext tasks including rotation prediction, masked volume inpainting, and
444   contrastive coding, while MIS-FM (Wang et al., 2023a) was pre-trained on the self-supervised
445   segmentation task with a large-scale unannotated dataset.
446
447   4.2   FCN- BASED M ENTAL D ISORDER D IAGNOSIS
448
449   Given that FCNs can be easily represented with graph structures, it is quite natural to apply GNNs,
450   which have the capacity to capture information from neighbor nodes in a neural circuit of a specific
451   brain node, for mental disease identification based on FCNs. BrainGNN (Li et al., 2021b) designed
452   ROI-aware GNNs to leverage the functional information in brain networks and used a special pooling
453   operator to select those crucial nodes. FBNetGen (Kan et al., 2022a) learned to generate the structure
454
      of FCNs and explored the explainability of the generated brain networks towards downstream tasks.
      Without focusing on static brain networks, STAGIN (Kim et al., 2021) utilized GNNs with spatio-
455
      temporal attention to model dynamic FCNs, and MHAHGEL (Wang et al., 2024) constructed FCNs
456
      as hypergraphs and applied hypergraph convolutional networks for capturing complex relations
457   among multiple brain nodes. Besides, CNN can also be used to facilitate mental disorder diagnosis
458   and brain network analysis, such as BrainNetCNN (Kawahara et al., 2017) and Deep Convolutional
459   Auto-Encoder (Huang et al., 2017). More recently, researchers have shown increasing interest in
460   graph transformer, due to its outstanding performance in graph representation learning while most
461   existing Transformer-based networks (Ying et al., 2021; Kreuzer et al., 2021) have achieved only
462   limited success in FCN analysis. To overcome this limitation, BNT (Kan et al., 2022b) was proposed
463   with orthonormal clustering readout to learn distinguishable cluster-aware node embeddings and
464   informative graph embeddings, which elevated the performance of graph transformer based models
465
      over GNN-based models. However, these FCN-based models are limited by the small number of
      annotated fMRIs and the absent of structural information, which may restrict their performance and
466
      generalization abilities.
467
468
469   5     C ONCLUSION
470
471   In this paper, we proposed CINP, a contrastive pre-training framework, on paired 3D T1w MRI image
472   and FCN data for mental disorder diagnosis. The image-network contrastive loss, masked image
473   modeling loss, and network-image matching loss are utilized for jointly learn 3D T1w MRI image
474
      and FCN representations that can be fully leveraged to the downstream mental disorder diagnosis
      task. Thanks to contrastive pre-training which facilitates knowledge transfer from FCN to 3D T1w
475
      MRI, we introduce network prompting. This protocol leverages 3D T1w MRI from suspected patients
476
      and FCNs from confirmed patients for differential diagnosis of mental disorders by calculating
477   the similarity between the sMRI embedding and the FCN embeddings of different subject classes.
478   Extensive experiment demonstrated the effectiveness of CINP on three downstream mental disorder
479   classification tasks. With pre-trained CINP, 3D T1 MRI which are easily accessible can be utilized
480   for mental disorder detection and eventually benefit clinical practices.
