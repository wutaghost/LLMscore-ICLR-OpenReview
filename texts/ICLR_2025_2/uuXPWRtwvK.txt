009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            One important approach to improving the reliability of large language models
012            (LLMs) is to provide accurate confidence estimations regarding the correctness
013            of their answers. However, developing a well-calibrated confidence estimation
014            model is challenging, as mistakes made by LLMs can be difficult to detect. We
015            propose a novel method combining the LLM’s self-consistency with labeled data
016            and training an auxiliary model to estimate the correctness of its responses to ques-
017            tions. This auxiliary model predicts the correctness of responses based solely on
018
               their consistent information. To set up the learning problem, we use a weighted
               graph to represent the consistency among the LLM’s multiple responses to a ques-
019
               tion. Correctness labels are assigned to these responses based on their similarity
020
               to the correct answer. We then train a graph neural network to estimate the prob-
021            ability of correct responses. Experiments demonstrate that the proposed approach
022            substantially outperforms several of the most recent methods in confidence cali-
023            bration across multiple widely adopted benchmark datasets. Furthermore, the pro-
024            posed approach significantly improves the generalization capability of confidence
025            calibration on out-of-domain (OOD) data.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across
031   various natural language processing tasks such as question answering (Wei et al., 2022; Shen et al.,
032   2023; Zheng et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2023; Singhal et al., 2023), text summarization (Tang et al.,
033
      2023; Deroy et al., 2023; Tam et al., 2023; Roit et al., 2023), and even creative writing (Gómez-
      Rodrı́guez & Williams, 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024). Despite their impressive per-
034
      formance, LLMs often give wrong answers in question-answering tasks. There is an urgent need
035
      to check the correctness of LLMs’ responses. One particularly interesting question is to calibrate
036   the confidence levels of the correctness of responses from LLMs (Kuhn et al., 2022; Ulmer et al.,
037   2022; Van Landeghem et al., 2022; Vazhentsev et al., 2023; Ulmer et al., 2024). Accurate confidence
038   estimation is vital for deploying LLMs in the real world, as it allows users to gauge the reliability
039   of the model’s predictions and make informed decisions based on these outputs. On the contrary,
040   miscalibrated confidence may lead to over-trust in incorrect responses or doubts about the correct
041   ones. For example, a misleading response may steer a patient in the wrong direction when making
042   health decisions; it may also lead an investor to impulsive investment decisions.
043   In this work, we consider calibrating the confidence with the correctness of LLMs’ responses. This
044   task is challenging in several aspects. First, due to LLMs’ superior ability to generate text, mistakes
045   in an LLM’s response are usually at the semantic level, making it hard to detect even for humans.
046   There are methods using an auxiliary Language Model (e.g., DeBERTa (He et al., 2020)) to check
047   whether the LLM’s response answers the question Ulmer et al. (2024). Since the LLM is supposed to
048   be much stronger than the LM, the LLM should be able to avoid most mistakes that can be detected
049   by an LM; this type of method may omit a significant fraction of wrong answers. Second, it is
050
      hard to detect mistakes from the LLM’s internal working mechanism. Because the LLM uses many
      hidden layers to process the information, it is hard to discern the signal from a small number of
051
      hidden units. Even if this is possible, it is not easy to apply this type of method to black-box LLMs.
052
053   Recently, there has been some progress in quantifying the model’s own confidence in a response
      through consistency among the model’s responses (Chen & Mueller, 2023; Lin et al., 2024). If the


                                                        1

054
      LLM always gives similar responses, then there is less uncertainty, and any one of these responses
055   tends to have a high probability of being correct. In particular, the results show that the model’s own
056   confidence in its response has a strong correlation with the correctness of the response. This new
057   development leads to an important research question: whether we can calibrate the confidence of
058   correctness from consistency among the LLM’s responses.
059
      In this work, we develop a new method to calibrate the confidence of correctness from the consis-
060
      tency of an LLM’s responses. To achieve this goal, we train a separate calibration model to predict
061   the correctness of the LLM’s responses. To get the input to the calibration model, we form a simi-
062   larity graph over the LLM’s multiple responses to the same question. The similarity graph encodes
063   information about the consistency between LLM’s responses. A response consistent with more an-
064   swers tends to have a higher likelihood of being correct, so the consistency graph is predictive of the
065   responses’ correctness. In our new approach, the calibration model only considers the consistency
066   among responses without processing any actual language information. Thus, we can use a relatively
067   simple and efficient model.
068   Our model achieves premium performance in the empirical study. Compared with previous calibra-
069   tion methods, our model has much better calibration performance because of the usage of consis-
070   tency graphs. Compared to prior methods based on consistency inputs, our method improves not
071   only the calibration performance but also the ranking performance. Furthermore, our method im-
072   proves the generalizability of confidence calibration for out-of-domain settings, demonstrating the
073   advantage of using a separate learning model. In summary, our main contributions are:
074
             • Graph-based confidence calibration method: We propose a novel graph-based confi-
075
               dence calibration approach to improve the reliability of LLMs.
076
077          • Enhanced calibration performance: Our evaluations demonstrate that the proposed
078            method substantially outperforms recent methods in confidence calibration across several
079
               widely used benchmark datasets.
080          • Improved OOD generalizability: Evaluations on OOD confidence calibration show that
081            our graph-based approach significantly improves generalizability in OOD settings.

083   2   R ELATED W ORK
084
085   Due to the urgent need to improve the reliability of LLMs, confidence estimation and calibration
086   for these models have become active areas of research. Existing research in LLM uncertainty quan-
087   tification can be summarized into two main categories: uncertainty quantification and confidence
088   calibration (Geng et al., 2023). Confidence estimation for short responses (e.g., for multi-choice or
089   yes-no questions) is generally less complicated than for long responses (Ye et al., 2024). For a brief
090   response, the LLM’s output logits are informative about its confidence; the easy comparisons of re-
091   sponses to the true answer facilitate both calibration and evaluation. Confidence estimation for long
092   responses cannot simply depend on LLM’s output logits (Duan et al., 2023; Bakman et al., 2024)
093
      because the logits indicate more about the probability of text and less about the semantics behind
      it. There are also methods using the internal state of an LLM (Ren et al., 2022), but it is not always
094
      available to have such information about the LLM interface.
095
096   Another approach is to check the LLM’s consistency in its responses. Kotelanski et al. (2023)
097   demonstrate that repeated sampling and consistency checks across multiple outputs can serve as re-
098   liable proxies for model confidence. Manakul et al. (2023) generates multiple responses from the
099
      LLM and checks the consistency between responses using various methods, including querying the
      LLM. Chen & Mueller (2023) combines the consistency between responses and the LLM’s self-
100
      reflection certainty to quantify the uncertainty. Kuhn et al. (2022) considers confidence from seman-
101
      tic equivalence and proposes a method based on clustering of responses. Lin et al. (2024) organize
102   responses in a graph with their pairwise semantic similarity and then extract graph statistics for con-
103   fidence estimation. Zhang et al. (2024) examines methods of comparing responses via entailment
104   and contradiction relationships. These studies highlight the importance of semantic consistency in
105   confidence estimation. These methods are evaluated by comparing their estimated confidence values
106   against the correctness of responses. The correct labels are often obtained by reference matching
107   (Huang et al., 2024), checking whether responses match true answers with a particular similarity
      measure.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      To better calibrate the confidence estimation, some methods directly use correctness labels in their
109   calibration procedures. Mielke et al. (2022) trains a calibrator to predict the correctness of a re-
110   sponse for a given question. With a similar idea, Ulmer et al. (2024) trains a language model (e.g.,
111   DeBERTa) based on question-response pairs to predict the probability of responses’ correctness.
112   Based on SelfCheckGPT (Manakul et al., 2023) and JAFC (Tian et al., 2023), Chen et al. (2024)
113   train supervised models to reduce grouping losses and improve the confidence estimation. The
114   method by Liu et al. (2024) uses an LLM’s latent representations to predict the correctness of re-
115   sponses. Detommaso et al. (2024) uses the “multicalibration” technique to calibrate the probability
116   of correctness. (Fadeeva et al., 2023) offers a detailed comparative study of various confidence es-
117
      timation methods, providing empirical evidence on their effectiveness across different tasks. How-
      ever, these studies have not sufficiently exploited response consistency to predict the probabilities of
118
      the responses being correct.

123   Our ultimate goal is to quantify the probability of the correctness of a response from an LLM. Since
124   the LLM can give a correct answer with different phrases, we need to consider the probability that
125   the response is semantically correct.
126   Background: The formulation of semantic equivalence (Kuhn et al., 2022) provides a framework
127   for our analysis. Let R be the space of all possible responses. Given a question q, the space R

130   considered as the same semantic response: if one is the correct answer, the other is correct as well,
131   and vice versa. Then, we can consider the quality of the LLM’s responses at the semantic level. In
132   particular, a semantic response C has probability

244   3.2   I MPROVE THE ESTIMATION THROUGH MULTIPLE PROMPTS
245
      It is well known that the syntactic form of a question influences responses and introduces additional
246
      variance. To reduce this variance and evaluate the LLM’s semantic consistency, we analyze the
247
      LLM’s responses to multiple prompts derived from the same question. These responses are treated
248   as answers to the same semantic question. We then apply the same method as before to predict the
249   correctness of each response.
250
251
      In particular, we rephrase the original question q into k different forms {q1 , ..., qk } while maintaining
      the original sentence’s semantic meaning. We employ a multiple rephrased questions strategy for
252
      answer sampling. Specifically, we prompt the GPT-4 to give k different but with the same meaning
253
      rephrased questions for the given question q. Then, we sample n/k responses from the LLM for
254   each rephrased question and still get a total of n responses, from which the confidence calibration is
255   the same as we have described above. For questions about which the LLM is less certain, the model
256   is more likely to produce diverse responses. In this scenario, confidence calibration is more accurate
257   because the model’s uncertainty becomes more apparent.

267
268   Dataset: We conduct experiments on two public benchmark datasets: (1) CoQA (Reddy et al.,
269   2019), an open-book conversational question answering (QA) task; (2) TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017),
      a commonsense QA task. and (3) TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022a), a comparably more challenging

271   both of equal size. For TruthfulQA, we use the 7:1:2 split for training, validation, and testing. We
272   provide more details about training in the Appendix A.
273
274
      Baselines: We compare our methods with the following baselines. Length-normalized sequence
      likelihoods (Seq. likelihood) (Malinin & Gales, 2021; Kuhn et al., 2022) is a standard measure for
275
      confidence. This method calculates the likelihood of each sequence and normalizes it by the length
276
      of the sequence to provide a fair comparison between different lengths of sequences. Platt scal-
277   ing (Platt, 1999), a variant of the sequence likelihood baseline, applies Platt scaling to the raw like-
278   lihoods. GraphSpectral (Chen et al., 2024) uses the graph theory to estimate the confidence. Then
279   we also include post-hoc uncertainty calibration, GraphSpectral+Iso and GraphSpectral+Platt
280   into the baseline methods. Self-check GPT(Manakul et al., 2023) checked the consistency between
281   responses querying the LLM. Verbalized Uncertainty (Lin et al., 2022b; Tian et al., 2023; Xiong
282   et al., 2024) generates verbal statements about the model’s confidence in its predictions. Verbalized
283   Qual maps the confidence percent (Verbalized %) into numerical values. APRICOT (Ulmer et al.,
284   2024), a supervised method, fine-tunes the Deberta language model to predict confidence scores for
285
      LLM outputs. Furthermore, we also include the baseline of applying two post-hoc uncertainty cal-
      ibration methods, APRICOT+Iso and APRICOT+Platt, to adjust the confidence scores obtained
286
      by Apricot. We performed all the baseline experiments utilizing the open-source codebase and used
287
      the default parameters.
288
289   Graph construction: For each question, we prompt the LLM to generate 30 answers. Each gener-
290   ated answer is then processed using the SentenceBert model Reimers & Gurevych (2019) to obtain
291
      the answer’s high-dimensional embeddings. To quantify the semantic similarity between the an-
      swers, we compute the cosine similarity between every pair of answer embeddings. These similarity
292
      scores are then utilized as edge weights in our similarity graph, where each node represents an
293
      individual answer, and the edges signify the degree of semantic relation between them.
294
295   Model hyper-parameters: To ensure our model can capture complex and abstract features at each
296   layer, our model comprises three Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) layers, with embedding
297
      dimensions of 256, 512, and 1024 for the first, second, and third layers, respectively. The initial
      learning rate was set to 10−4 . If the validation loss did not show improvement over ten consecutive
298
      epochs, the learning rate was reduced by a factor of 0.9. The optimization was performed using the
299
      Adam optimizer, configured with hyperparameters β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.98. The batch size was 16.
300
301   LLMs: We assess our confidence calibration method on two LLMs with excellent performance:
302   Llama3-8B (Llama3)(Meta, 2024), and Vicuna-7b-v1.5 (Vicuna) (Zheng et al., 2024).
303   Labeling the data: To obtain the correctness label for CoQA and TriviaQA datasets, we followed
304   previous work (Kuhn et al., 2022) and used the Rougel-L metric for labeling. For the TruthfulQA
305   dataset, given its focus on factual correctness and longer answers, we employed GPT4 Liu et al.
306   (2023) to generate the labels.
307   Evaluation metrics: The evaluation metrics include Expectation Calibration Error (ECE), Brier
308   Score, and AUROC. Specifically, (1) ECE quantifies the consistency between the prediction er-
309   ror and the uncertainty of the prediction. An ideal calibration curve should exhibit a lower ECE.
310   It measures the consistency between the prediction error and the confidence of the prediction.
311   Specifically, the confidence interval is grouped into fixed bins, and the average of the differ-
312   ence between the confidence and error in each bin is compared. Formally, ECE is calculated as
                PB
313   ECE = b=1 nNb |acc(b) − conf (b)|, where nb is the number of predictions in bin b, N is the total
314   number of data points and acc(b) and conf (b) are the accuracy and confidence of bin b, respectively.
315   (2) Brier Score (Brier, 1950), which is the mean squared difference between predicted probabili-
316   ties and the actual binary results. Lower Brier Scores indicate better performance. (3) AUROC to
317
      indicate the models’ discriminatory ability.

      4.3   O UT OF D OMAIN EVALUATION
433
434   Domain shift poses significant challenges for deploying machine learning models in real-world sce-
435   narios where data variability is expected. To comprehensively assess the robustness and general-
436   ization capabilities of our proposed model compared to baseline methods, we conducted a series of
437   out-of-domain (OOD) evaluations.
438   Experiment setup: We evaluate the confidence calibration of different approaches under out-of-
439   domain settings. We have two experiment configurations: out-of-domain dataset OODD, and out-
440   of-domain LLMs (OODL). For OODD, we train the confidence calibration model on TriviaQA
441   from Llama3 responses and test it on CoQA Llama3 and TruthfulQA Llama3 answers. For OODL,
442   we use the same training data from Llama3 but test the Vicuna model’s responses on the TriviaQA
443   and CoQA datasets. We compare our model with the Apricot and GraphSpectral (with Platt scaling)
444   methods.
445   Results and Analysis: Table. 3 shows the OOD performance of the baseline methods. The OOD
446   experiment results revealed that our model maintained a high level of performance across tested do-
447   mains. Specifically, the model demonstrated consistent calibration, as evidenced by low ECE values
448   and strong discriminative ability, reflected in high AUROC scores on in-domain and OOD datasets.
449   For example, while the model achieved an ECE of 0.016 and an AUROC of 0.82 on TriviaQA (in-
450   domain), it maintained an ECE of 0.077 and an AUROC of 0.77 on CoQA. Furthermore, the Brier
451   scores across domains remained within acceptable ranges, demonstrating reliable probabilistic pre-
452
      dictions even when faced with unfamiliar data distributions. The relatively small increase in ECE
      and a slight decrease in AUROC for OOD datasets suggest that while there is some degradation in
453
      performance, the model retains substantial robustness and accuracy. This is primarily because simi-
454
      larity graph patterns are highly invariant to the data distribution. Specifically, our model employs the
455   consistency graph and the clustering feature that does not alter with data distribution shifts, enabling
456   it to maintain stable performance across different datasets.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
528
529   In summary, in this work, we proposed one effective strategy of confidence calibration by combining
530   the LLM’s self-consistency with labeled data and training an auxiliary GNN model to estimate
531
      the correctness of its responses to questions. Experiments demonstrate that the proposed approach
      improves confidence calibration significantly across several datasets compared to baseline methods.
532
      Our calibration model enhances the reliability of LLMs by evaluating response accuracy, enabling
533
      them to abstain from uncertain queries and empowering users to determine trust levels, thereby
534   promoting responsible deployment in society. However, there are instances where an LLM might be
535   highly confident in an incorrect semantic response, resulting in a consistency graph similar to that
536   of a correct answer. In such cases, our calibration model may not provide an accurate confidence
537   estimation. Unfortunately, without a model stronger than the LLM itself, there is no straightforward
538   solution to this problem. We hope that advancements in LLMs will help mitigate this issue. In future
539   work, we aim to extend the framework to incorporate the data uncertainty coming from ambiguous
      questions and also explore the multi-step confidence calibration in the chain-of-thought framework.

      A    H YPERPARAMETERS AND M ODEL CONFIGURATIONS
738
739
      Model hyper-parameters:
740   Our model used three GCN layers; typically, the embedding dimension was 256, 512, and1024 for
741   three GCN layers. For the training process, we used the binary cross-entropy loss with a decaying
742   learning rate that reduced the learning rate by 0.9 if the validation loss did not improve 10 epochs
743   (with an initial learning rate of 10− 4 and a minimum learning rate of 10−7 ). The optimizer was
744   Adam with β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.98. The batch size was 32. For the rephrased prompts, we set
745
      k = 3, n = 30, so for each rephrased question, we sampled ten answers. While calculating the ECE,
      we divide the confidence into B = 10 bins.
746
747   Evaluation Setup:
748
      For each question, we evaluate the confidence prediction corresponding to the most likely answers
749   from the LLM response. The setup is consistent with the baseline methods.
750
751   Graph construction:
752   For each question, we prompt the LLM to give 30 answers, and the temperature for LLM is set to
753   be 0.6. For each answer, the SentenceBert model Reimers & Gurevych (2019) is used to get each
754   answer’s embedding. The cosine similarity between each answer’s embedding is taken as the edge
755   weight of the graph. We apply the K-Means clustering method to cluster similar semantic responses.
      The maximum cluster number is set as 3.
