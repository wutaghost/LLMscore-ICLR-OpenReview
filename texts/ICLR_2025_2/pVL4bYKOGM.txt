009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Uncertainty quantification of causal effects is crucial for safety-critical applica-
012            tions such as personalized medicine. A powerful approach for this is conformal
013            prediction, which has several practical benefits due to model-agnostic finite-sample
014            guarantees. Yet, existing methods for conformal prediction of causal effects are
015            limited to binary/discrete treatments and make highly restrictive assumptions such
016            as known propensity scores. In this work, we provide a novel conformal predic-
017            tion method for potential outcomes of continuous treatments. We account for
018            the additional uncertainty introduced through propensity estimation so that our
019
               conformal prediction intervals are valid even if the propensity score is unknown.
               Our contributions are three-fold: (1) We derive finite-sample validity guarantees
020
               for prediction intervals of potential outcomes of continuous treatments. (2) We
021
               provide an algorithm for calculating the derived intervals. (3) We demonstrate the
022            effectiveness of the conformal prediction intervals in experiments on synthetic and
023            real-world datasets. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to propose con-
024            formal prediction for continuous treatments when the propensity score is unknown
025            and must be estimated from data.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Machine learning (ML) for estimating causal quantities such as causal effects and the potential
031   outcomes of treatments is nowadays widely used in real-world applications such as personalized
032   medicine (Feuerriegel et al., 2024). However, existing methods from causal ML typically focus
033
      on point estimates (e.g., Nie et al., 2021; Patrick Schwab et al., 2020), which means that the
      uncertainty in the predictions is neglected and hinders the use of causal ML in safety-critical
034
      applications (Feuerriegel et al., 2024; Kneib et al., 2023). As the following example shows, uncertainty
035
      quantification (UQ) of causal quantities is crucial for reliable decision-making.
036
037   Motivating example: Let us consider a doctor who seeks to determine the dosage of chemotherapy in
038   cancer care. This requires estimating the tumor size in response to the dosage for a specific patient
039
      profile. A point estimate will predict the average size of the tumor post-treatment, but it will neglect
      that chemotherapy is ineffective for some patients. In contrast, UQ will give a range of the tumor
040
      size that is to be expected post-treatment, so that doctors can assess the probability that the patients
041
      will actually benefit from treatment. This helps to understand the risk of a treatment being ineffective
042   and can guide doctors to choose treatments that are effective with large probability.
043

057   Adapting CP to causal quantities is non-trivial for two main reasons. Challenge a : Intervening on the
058
      treatment induces a shift in the covariate distribution, specifically, in the propensity score. As a result,
      the so-called exchangeability assumption, which is inherent to CP (Vovk et al., 2005), is violated
059
      between the observational and interventional distribution, and because of this, standard CP intervals
060
      are not valid. Thus, we must later account for the distribution shift and derive treatment-conditional
061   guarantees. Challenge b : Assessing the aforementioned shift in the distribution requires information
062   about the propensity score; yet, the propensity score is typically unknown. Hence, estimating the
063   propensity score introduces additional uncertainty. However, incorporating the additional uncertainty
064   in the overall CP intervals cannot be done in a simple plug-in manner, and it is highly non-trivial.
065
      Unique to CP for effects of continuous treatments is a third challenge a : data points with the same
066
      treatment value are rarely observed. Thus, we later employ smoothing to model the propensity shift.
067
068   In this paper, we develop a CP method for causal quantities, such as potential outcomes of continuous
069   treatments. Our method is designed to account for the additional uncertainty introduced during
070
      propensity estimation and is thus applicable to settings where the propensity score is known or
      unknown.
071
072   Our contributions:1 (1) We propose a novel method for CP of causal quantities such as potential
073   outcomes or treatment effects of continuous treatments. For this, we mathematically derive finite-
074   sample prediction intervals for potential outcomes under known and unknown propensity functions.
075   (2) We provide an algorithm for efficiently calculating the derived intervals. (3) We demonstrate the
076
      effectiveness of the derived CP intervals in experiments on multiple datasets.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
079   UQ for causal effects: Existing methods for UQ of causal quantities are often based on Bayesian
080   methods (e.g., Alaa & van der Schaar, 2017; Hess et al., 2024; Hill, 2011; Jesson et al., 2020). How-
081   ever, Bayesian methods require the specification of a prior distribution based on domain knowledge
082   and are thus neither robust to model misspecification nor generalizable to model-agnostic machine
083   learning models. A common ad hoc method for computing uncertainty intervals is Monte Carlo (MC)
084
      dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016). However, MC dropout yields approximations of the posterior
      distribution, which are not faithful (Le Folgoc et al., 2021).
085
086   Conformal prediction: CP (Lei & Wasserman, 2014; Papadopoulos, 2002; Vovk et al., 2005) has
087   recently received large attention for finite-sample UQ. For a prediction model ϕ trained on dataset
088   DT = (Xi , Yi )i=1,...,m and a new test sample Xk , CP aims to construct a prediction interval C(Xk )
089   such that P (Yk ∈ C(Xk )) ≥ 1 − α for some significance level α. We refer to (Angelopoulos et al.,
090
      2024) for an in-depth overview. Due to its strong finite-sample validity guarantees, CP is widely used
      for traditional, predictive ML with widespread applications such as in medical settings (Zhan et al.,
091
      2020) or drug discovery (Alvarsson et al., 2021; Eklund et al., 2015).
092
093   Several extensions have been developed for CP. One literature stream focuses on CP with marginal
094   coverage under distribution shifts between training and test data (e.g., Cauchois et al., 2020; Fannjiang
095   et al., 2022; Gendler et al., 2022; Ghosh et al., 2023; Gibbs & Candès, 2021; Gibbs et al., 2023; Guan,
096
      2023; Lei & Candès, 2021; Podkopaev & Ramdas, 2021; Tibshirani et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2024).
      Our setting later also involves a distribution shift due to the intervention on the treatment but differs
097
      from the latter in that the true distribution shift is unknown. Another literature stream constructs
098
      intervals conditional on the variables following the shifted distribution. Since, in general, exact
099   conditional coverage has been proven impossible (Lei & Wasserman, 2014; Vovk, 2012), the works
100   in this literature stream have two key limitations: (1) they only guarantee approximate conditional
101   coverage (e.g., Barber et al., 2021; Cai et al., 2014; Lei & Wasserman, 2014; Romano et al., 2020);
102   or (2) they are restricted to specific data structures such as binary variables (e.g., Lei & Wasserman,
103   2014; Vovk, 2012). Because of that, none of the existing methods for marginal and conditional
104   coverage can be applied to derive prediction intervals with finite-sample validity guarantees for causal
105   quantities of continuous treatments.

      Throughout this work, we build upon the potential outcomes framework (Rubin, 2005). We denote
141   the potential outcomes after a hard intervention a∗ by Y (a∗ ) and after a soft intervention A∗ (x) ∼
142   π̃(a | x) = PA∗ |X=x by Y (A∗ (x)).2 We make three standard identifiability assumptions for causal
143   effect estimation: positivity, consistency, and unconfoundedness (e.g., Alaa et al., 2023; Jonkers
144   et al., 2024). Finally, we consider an arbitrary machine learning model ϕ to predict the potential
145   outcomes. Hence, we define the outcome prediction function as ϕ : X × A → R, ϕ(X, A) 7→ Y .
146   We assume the dose-response curve to be sufficiently smooth. This is common in causal inference
147   with continuous treatments (e.g., Patrick Schwab et al., 2020; Schweisthal et al., 2023).

150   intervention, ♢ = A∗ (Xn+1 ) ∼ π̃(a | Xn+1 ). The derived intervals are called valid for any new
151   exchangeable sample Xn+1 with non-exchangeable intervention ♢, i.e.,

165   Why is CP for causal quantities non-trivial? There are two main reasons. First, coverage guarantees
166
      of CP intervals essentially rely on the exchangeability of the non-conformity scores. However,
      intervening on treatment A shifts the propensity function and, therefore, induces a shift in the
167
      covariates (X, A) (→ Challenge a ). Formally, we have a propensity shift in which the intervention
168
      ♢ shifts the propensity function π(a | x) to either a Dirac-delta distribution of the hard intervention,
169   δa∗ (a), or to the distribution of the soft intervention, π̃(a | x), without affecting the outcome function
170   ϕ(x, a). As a result, the test data sample under ♢ does not follow the same distribution as the train
171   and calibration data, i.e., the exchangeability assumption is violated.
172
      Second, the propensity score π is commonly unknown in observational data and, therefore, must be
173
      estimated, which introduces additional uncertainty that one must account for when constructing CP
174
      intervals (→ Challenge b ). Crucially, existing coverage guarantees (e.g., Vovk, 2012; Tibshirani
175   et al., 2019) do not hold in our setting. Instead, we must derive new intervals with valid coverage
176   under propensity shift.
177
178
      In the following section, we address the above propensity shift by performing a calibration conditional
      on the propensity shift induced by the intervention, which allows us then to yield valid prediction
179
      intervals with significance level (1 − α) for potential outcomes of a specific hard or soft intervention.
180
      We derive the potential outcomes and emphasize that the extension to causal effects is straightforward.
181   For the latter, one combines the intervals for each potential outcome under a certain treatment and
182   without treatment, so that eventually arrives at CP intervals for the individual treatment effect (ITE).
183   Details are Supplement A.

388   5     E XPERIMENTS
389
      Baselines: As we have discussed above, there are no baselines that directly compute prediction
390   intervals with finite-sample validity guarantees for potential outcomes of continuous treatments.
391   Therefore, we compare our method against MC dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016) and deep
392   ensemble methods (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). Yet, we again emphasize that MC dropout is
393   an ad hoc method with poor approximations of the posterior, which is known to give unfaithful
394   intervals (Le Folgoc et al., 2021). Furthermore, we report the empirical coverage achieved by a
395   vanilla CP (V-CP) method, i.e., CP which does not account for the distribution shift, and intervals
396   from a Gaussian process regression (GP). By doing so, we (i) show the necessity of accounting for the
397   distribution shift induced by the intervention, and (ii) consider a method that assesses the underlying
398
      aleatoric uncertainty.
399   Implementation: All methods are implemented with ϕ as a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) and an
400   MC dropout regularization of rate 0.1. Crucially, we use the identical MLP for both our CP method
401   and MC dropout. Hence, all performance gains must be attributed to the coverage guarantees of
402   our conformal method. In the MC dropout baseline, the uncertainty intervals are computed via
403   Monte Carlo sampling. In scenario 2 with unknown propensity scores, we perform the conditional
404
      density estimation through conditional normalizing flows (Trippe & Turner, 2018). Details about our
      implementation and training are in Supplement F.
405
406   Performance metrics: We evaluate the methods in terms of whether the prediction intervals are
407   faithful (e.g., Hess et al., 2024). That is, we compute whether the empirical coverage of the prediction
408   intervals surpasses the threshold of 1 − α for different significance levels α ∈ {0.05, 0.1, 0.2}.
409   Additionally, we report the width of the resulting intervals in Supplement G.
410
      5.1   DATASETS
411
412   Synthetic datasets: We follow common practice in causal ML and evaluate our methods using
413   synthetic datasets (e.g., Alaa et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2023). The reason is the fundamental problem
414   of causal inference, because of which counterfactual outcomes are never observable in real-world
415   datasets. Synthetic datasets enable us to access counterfactual outcomes and, thus, to benchmark
416   methods in terms of whether the computed intervals are faithful. Additionally, we perform experi-
417   ments on the semi-synthic TCGA dataset in Supplement C. We hereby show the applicability of our
418   method to high-dimensional real-world data in a controlled environment.
419   We consider two synthetic datasets with different propensity scores and outcome functions. Dataset 1
420   uses a step-wise propensity function and a concave outcome function. Dataset 2 is more complex and
421   uses a Gaussian propensity function and oscillating outcome functions. Both datasets contain a single
422   discrete confounder, a continuous treatment, and a continuous outcome. By choosing low-dimensional
423   datasets, we later render it possible to plot the treatment–response curves so that one can inspect the
424   prediction intervals visually. (We later also show that our method scales to high-dimensional settings
425
      as part of the real-world dataset.) Details about the data-generating processes are in Supplement F.
426   Medical dataset: We demonstrate the applicability of our CP method to datasets from medicine
427   by leveraging the MIMIC-III dataset (Johnson et al., 2016). MIMIC-III contains de-identified
428   health records from patients admitted to critical care units at large tertiary care hospitals. Our goal
429   is to predict patient outcomes in terms of blood pressure when treated with a different duration
430   of mechanical ventilation. We use 8 confounders from medical practice (e.g., respiratory rate,
431
      hematocrit). Overall, we consider 14,719 patients, split into train (60%), validation (10%), calibration
      (20%), and test (10%) sets. Details are in Supplement F.

1353   E.1   E XTENDED LITERATURE REVIEW
1354
1355   Uncertainty quantification for causal quantities
1356
1357   There exist various methods for uncertainty quantification of causal quantities. These are often based
1358
       on Bayesian methods (e.g., Alaa & van der Schaar, 2017; Hess et al., 2024; Hill, 2011; Jesson et al.,
       2020). However, Bayesian methods require the specification of a prior distribution based on domain
1359
       knowledge and are thus neither robust to model misspecification nor generalizable to model-agnostic
1360
       machine learning models. Other methods only provide asymptotic guarantees (e.g., Jin et al., 2023;
1361   Jonkers et al., 2024). The strength of conformal prediction, however, is to provide finite-sample
1362   uncertainty guarantees.
1363
1364
       In the following, we present related work on CP for causal quantities in more detail.
1365   Recently, Alaa et al. (2023) provided predictive intervals for CATE meta-learners under the assump-
1366   tion of full knowledge of the propensity score. As an extension, Jonkers et al. (2024) proposed a
1367   Monte-Carlo sampling approach to receive less conservative intervals. Chen et al. (2024) provide
1368   prediction intervals for counterfactual outcomes. However, the proposed method requires access to
1369   additional interventional data and is thus not applicable to real-world applications on observational
1370
       data. All methods are restricted to binary treatments.
1371   Other works focus on prediction intervals for off-policy prediction (Taufiq et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
1372   2023) and conformal sensitivity analysis (Yin et al., 2022), thus neglecting estimation errors arising
1373   from propensity or weight estimation or for randomized control trials (Kivaranovic et al., 2020).
1374   Wang et al. (2024) constructed intervals with treatment-conditional coverage of discrete treatments.
1375   Aiming for group-conditional coverage, Wang et al. (2024) adapted CP to cluster randomized trials.
1376
       Nevertheless, the method only applies to a finite number of treatments and thus is not applicable to
       continuous treatments.
1377
1378   Lei & Candès (2021) consider the estimated propensity by incorporating the estimation error as
1379   a TV-distance term in the coverage guarantees. However, for large TV-distances (close to 1), the
1380   proposed method can only construct intervals with a very limited coverage α ∈ (0, 1 − T V ). Hence,
1381   the method is not suitable for applications in medical practice. Our method, however, can also
1382
       construct intervals with high coverage guarantees for high estimation errors. An increased error
       will widen the prediction intervals instead of reducing the coverage guarantee. We consider our
1383
       approach more suitable for medical practice, as one can visually inspect the intervals and decide on
1384
       the suitability of the task at hand.
1385
1386   Overall, no method can provide exact intervals for continuous treatments. Especially, no method
1387   considers the error arising from propensity estimation in the analysis.
1388   Conformal prediction under covariate shift
1389
       Multiple works on CP with marginal coverage under distribution shifts between training and test data
1390
       have been introduced in the literature (e.g., Cauchois et al., 2020; Fannjiang et al., 2022; Gendler
1391
       et al., 2022; Ghosh et al., 2023; Gibbs & Candès, 2021; Gibbs et al., 2023; Guan, 2023; Lei & Candès,
1392   2021; Podkopaev & Ramdas, 2021; Tibshirani et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2024). Our setting also
1393   involves a distribution shift due to the intervention on the treatment but differs from the latter in that
1394   the true distribution shift is unknown.
1395
       Gibbs et al. (2023) introduced an approach to derive CP intervals under unknown distribution shifts. It
1396
       proves valid finite-sample prediction intervals for all distribution shifts in a finite-dimensional function
1397
       class. However, the approach does not directly apply to causal inference settings. Nevertheless, our
1398   framework builds upon the work by Gibbs et al. (2023) in that we re-frame the proposed approach to
1399   apply to the distribution shift induced through the intervention in causal effect estimation. In this
1400   setting, the distribution shift is captured by the shift of the propensity function. Adapting Gibbs et al.
1401   (2023) to a causal inference setting requires carefully addressing the underlying challenges that come
1402   from computing CP intervals in a causal inference setting (e.g., propensity score estimation, hard/soft
1403   interventions), which we regard as our main novelty and which is of immediate practical relevance
       (e.g., in personalized medicine).

       E.2   A NOTE ON CHALLENGES AND DIFFICULTIES IN CP FOR CAUSAL EFFECTS OF
1405         CONTINUOUS TREATMENTS
1406
1407   Existing works on conformal prediction for binary or (low-dimensional) discrete treatment are
1408   commonly based on (a) weighted conformal prediction (Tibshirani et al., 2019) or (b) conformal
1409   prediction local coverage guarantees (Lei & Wasserman, 2014). The first approach provides marginal
1410   coverage under a distribution shift through reweighting. It requires computing the weights based on
1411   the probability of treatment A = a. However, for continuous treatments, this is always zero. Although
1412
       applicable to binary or low-dimensional discrete treatments (e.g., Lei & Candès, 2021), this weighting
       approach cannot be extended similarly to continuous treatments. Furthermore, the propensity of a
1413
       continuous treatment given by the Dirac delta function δa would require us to restrict the calibration to
1414
       data samples of the specific treatment, which are extremely rare or even might be missing. Therefore,
1415   the calibration step cannot be employed in our setting. The second approach provides treatment
1416   group-conditional coverage. Although again possible for binary or low-dimensional treatments, this
1417   approach does not apply to continuous treatments as no treatment groups can be defined. Instead,
1418   we propose a novel method for conformal predictions that circumvents the above problems and is
1419   carefully tailored to continuous treatments.
1420
1421   E.3   C AUSAL EFFECTS OF CONTINUOUS TREATMENTS & KERNEL SMOOTHING
1422
1423   Causal inference becomes challenging with continuous treatments primarily due to the infinite number
1424   of potential outcomes per sample, from which only one outcome is observed. Continuous treatments
1425   thus result in causal effects that are generally represented by curves (called dose-response curves) (?).
1426   This is unlike binary treatment, where the causal effects are represented by a single discrete value.
1427   For continuous treatments, the dose-response curves are typically assumed to fulfill some smoothness
1428   criterion (e.g., Patrick Schwab et al., 2020; Schweisthal et al., 2023). Hence, when estimating
1429   treatment effects, interpolation and kernel smoothing of the outcome function are commonly employed
1430   (e.g., Kennedy, 2019; Nagalapatti et al., 2024).
1431   Underlying causal estimation with continuous treatments is the generalized propensity score (Imbens,
1432   2000). It is defined as the conditional probability of receiving treatment a∗ given the covariates X
1433   under the following regularity conditions: (i) For each i, Yi (a), xi , Ai are defined on a common
1434   probability space; (ii) Ai is continuously distributed with respect to the Lebesgue measure; and
1435   (iii) Yi = Yi (Ai ) is a well-defined random variable.
1436
       Approximating the density δa∗ (a) of the hard intervention a∗ through a Gaussian kernel follows
1437   directly from the definition of δa∗ (a) as the limit of such kernel. This is also common in the literature
1438   (e.g., Kallus & Zhou, 2018). Importantly, we note that we do not directly approximate the potential
1439   outcome Y (a∗ ) (but only the propensity scores). Thus, we do not have a bias-variance trade-off of
1440   the estimated outcome. Due to the smoothness of the dose-response curve, it is now valid to employ
1441   observed samples within a treatment region of a∗ defined by σ to construct the intervals. We note that
1442   the importance of the samples is weighted by the inverse distance of the sample to a∗ in treatment
1443   space. We give further intuition on the relationship between σ, the importance of observational
1444   samples, and the prediction interval width in the following.

       E.5   I NTERPRETATION OF PARAMETER M
1466
       Our optimization requires the specification of a parameter M , denoting a bound on the propensity
1467   estimation error. One can view the parameter M as a type of sensitivity parameter. Therefore, we
1468   follow former work in causal inference and propose to incorporate domain knowledge to specify the
1469   parameter M (e.g., Frauen et al., 2024; Tan, 2006). Another way of making use of the parameter M
1470   is to observe how the intervals change for varying M . This indicates how much effect the propensity
1471   misspecification has on the prediction interval and can help in making reliable decisions. A third
1472   option to calibrate M is to employ measures for epistemic uncertainty on top of the propensity
1473   estimate when there is no domain knowledge for specifying M .

1475   E.6   A NOTE ON THE STABILITY OF OUR METHOD
1476
1477
       In our experiments, one can observe some instability for certain privacy budget and intervention
       combination. This is likely due to the fact that the CP coverage guarantees are only marginal.
1478
       Therefore, we might experience under- or over-coverage. However, we note that across all runs, our
1479
       method, on average, achieves the desired coverage. These instabilities only occur in single settings.
1480   Furthermore, the variance in coverage of our method is much lower than of the coverage variance of
1481   the baselines. A more in-depth analysis of the stability of the proposed method is left for future work.
