                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Deep learning models are vulnerable to privacy attacks due to their tendency to
011
               memorize individual training set examples. Theoretically-sound defenses such as
               differential privacy can defend against this threat, but model performance often
012
               suffers. Empirical defenses may thwart existing attacks while maintaining model
013
               performance but do not offer any robust theoretical guarantees.
014
015
               In this paper, we explore a new strategy based on the concept of plausible deniability.
               We introduce a training algorithm called Plausibly Deniable Stochastic Gradient
016
               Descent (PD-SGD), which aims to provide both strong privacy protection with
017
               theoretical justification and maintain high performance. The core of this approach
018            is a rejection sampling technique, which probabilistically prevents updating model
019            parameters whenever a mini-batch cannot be plausibly denied. This ensures that
020            no individual example has a disproportionate influence on the model parameters.
021            We provide a set of theoretical results showing that PD-SGD effectively mitigates
022            privacy leakage from individual data points. Experiments also demonstrate that
023            PD-SGD offers a favorable trade-off between privacy and utility compared to
024            differential privacy (i.e., DP-SGD) and empirical defense methods.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Deep learning models (LeCun et al., 2015) have become integral components of many contemporary
030   technological applications, ranging from image (Obaid et al., 2020) and speech recognition (Zhang
031   et al., 2018) to natural language processing (Deng & Liu, 2018). Their ability to uncover complex
032   patterns in data and provide high predictive accuracy has driven broad acceptance and deployment
033   across multiple industries. However, the pervasive usage of deep learning raises significant security
034   and privacy issues. Privacy attacks, such as membership inference attacks (Shokri et al., 2017; Ye
035   et al., 2022; Carlini et al., 2022), have been shown to exploit vulnerabilities, compromising the
036   confidentiality of the model’s training data.
037   Protecting privacy while maintaining model performance is a major challenge. Current defense
038   strategies are such that practitioners have to choose between strong privacy guarantees and high
039   model utility. Approaches based on differential privacy (DP) (Dwork, 2006) offer strong mathematical
040   privacy guarantees. When applied to machine learning, these approaches usually consist of clipping
041   and adding large amounts of noise to the gradients (Abadi et al., 2016) during training, but this often
042   results in drastic degradation of model performance. On the flip side, empirical defense strategies
043   such as Adversarial Regularization (Nasr et al., 2018), SELENA (Tang et al., 2022) often preserve
044
      performance but come without mathematical justification that privacy is protected and thus may
      ultimately prove to be highly vulnerable to future (yet-to-be-discovered) attacks.
045
046   In response to these challenges, this work aims to bridge the gap between robust theoretical privacy
047   guarantees and practical performance. We introduce a novel training algorithm called Plausibly
048   Deniable Stochastic Gradient Descent (PD-SGD), which takes inspiration from the principle of
049   plausible deniability (Bindschaedler et al., 2017). Unlike existing approaches, PD-SGD seeks to offer
050
      a novel method for private learning without compromising performance.
051   The innovation at the core of the proposed learning algorithm is an efficient privacy test, which
052   inspects potential gradients from mini-batches before they are used to update the model parameters.
053   This privacy test enforces that anomalous gradients — those that are not plausibly deniable — will be
      discarded, thereby eliminating the leakage that may otherwise result from such updates.

      The paper first discusses the theoretical foundations of the PD-SGD approach, including its design and
055   the privacy guarantees it offers. The proposed approach is then evaluated experimentally, comparing
056   its performance and trade-offs with those of existing methods such as DP-SGD and empirical defenses.
057   Results demonstrate that PD-SGD offers a superior privacy-utility trade-off compared to alternatives.

062   2.1   D EEP LEARNING
063
064   In this paper, we consider supervised models to predict a target label/value in a set Y for an example
065
      given features in a set X. The model is a function f : X → Y that is parameterized by a vector θ
      of trainable parameters. The model is trained using a dataset D of n data points (xi , yi ), i ∈ [1, n]
066
      where xi ∈ X and yi ∈ Y and solving for the vector θ that minimizes the loss function L(·) on D.
067
068   To (approximately) solve this optimization problem, we can use Stochastic Gradient Descent
069   (SGD) (Gower et al., 2019) or one of its many variants (Haji & Abdulazeez, 2021). We focus
070   on mini-batch SGD which we refer to as (vanilla) SGD. In each iteration, the algorithm partitions
071
      the training set into (roughly) equal-sized mini-batches, randomly picks a mini-batch, and updates
      the parameters according to the mini-batch’s gradient. Specifically given a mini batch Bj , we let
072
073
      gj = ∇θ L(θ, Bj ) ∈ Rk denote the gradient of the loss on Bj with respect to the model parameters
074
      θ ∈ Rk . The update at step t is therefore: θt = θt−1 − ηgj , where η is the chosen learning rate.
075
076   2.2   M EMBERSHIP I NFERENCE ATTACKS
077
078   Membership inference attacks (MIAs) have been extensively studied in recent years (Shokri et al.,
079   2017; Salem et al., 2018; Yeom et al., 2018; Sablayrolles et al., 2019; Long et al., 2020; Choquette-
080
      Choo et al., 2021; Carlini et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2022; Matsumoto et al., 2023; Bertran et al., 2023;
      Zarifzadeh et al., 2024). These are privacy attacks where the adversary aims to determine if a specific
081
      example was included in a target model’s training set. Specifically, given a specific target example
082
      (x, y), the adversary seeks to discern between two competing hypotheses:
083
084          • H0 (“non-member” or “out”): (x, y) ∈/ D, or
085          • H1 (“member” or “in” or ‘): (x, y) ∈ D.
086   Membership inference attacks were first introduced by Shokri et al. (2017), employing shadow
087   models trained on data similar to the target’s to emulate its behavior and generate attack data. Recent
088   works like Ye et al. (2022) propose different attack variants aim to reduce adversarial uncertainty to
089   improve attack effectiveness. Carlini et al. (2022) propose a Likelihood Ratio Attack while advocating
090   focusing on increasing true positive rates at low false positive rates.

135
      applying knowledge distillation to mitigate the attacks. While these empirical defense mechanisms
136
      can preserve the model utility and offer some level of privacy protection, they lack provable theoretical
137   guarantees. Consequently, it is unclear to what extent they truly eliminate sensitive information
138   leakage or the degree to which they will be effective against future attacks, especially adaptive attacks.
139
140
      To the best of our knowledge, no existing defense mechanism simultaneously offers a provable
      theoretical guarantee and maintains good model utility. Our proposed method, PD-SGD, is designed
141
      to help bridge this gap (see Table 1).

      2.4   P LAUSIBLE D ENIABILITY
145
      It is often said that differential privacy provides plausible deniability. This makes sense on the basis
146   that differential privacy ensures that the probabilities of any output on neighboring datasets (datasets
147   that differ in exactly one example) are tightly bounded in terms of the privacy budget ε.
148
149
      Plausible deniability as a formal privacy notion was proposed by Bindschaedler et al. (2017) in the
      context of synthesizing tabular microdata. In their setting, they repeatedly select a single row of a
150
      database as a “seed” and use it to probabilistically produce a new synthetic row similar to it. The
151
      problem is that this procedure may not preserve privacy since the process statistically ties the synthetic
152   to the seed. To get around this issue, the authors formalize the notion of plausibility deniability.
153
154   Informally, a synthetic is plausibly deniable if we can find that in the original database, more than T
155
      (integer parameter) alternative rows could have led to generating the synthetic with similar probability.
      This similarity in probability is determined by a ratio bounded by some α > 1, assuming those rows
156
      have been (as a counterfactual) selected as seed. To enforce this constraint, a privacy test using
157
      rejection sampling is defined. The test ensures that if a synthetic is ever produced that does not meet
158   the plausibility deniability constraint, it will be thrown away. With some additional randomization of
159   this test, this procedure can be made to yield (ε, δ)-differential privacy.

      3     P LAUSIBLE D ENIABILITY FOR D EEP L EARNING
163
164
      We propose a new formulation of plausible deniability that can be applied to SGD training at the
165   level of mini-batches. To enforce plausible deniability, we implement a privacy test on the potential
166   gradient updates from a mini-batch. If a mini-batch includes one or more examples that yield an
167   implausible gradient (with respect to other mini-batch’s gradients), we reject this gradient — we do
168   not use it to update the model parameters.
169
170   3.1   T HE A NATOMY OF P RIVACY L EAKAGE
171
172   A root cause of privacy leakage in deep learning — the kind that membership inference attacks exploit
173   — is the disproportionate impact of including a single example in the dataset onto the model. For
174
      instance, imagine an iteration of SGD where we have selected a batch B and computed its gradient
      vector g. We can consider the counterfactual of having selected a batch B ′ = B ∪ {(x, y)} that
175
      includes some example (x, y). The crucial observation is that the gradient vector g ′ for B ′ may be
176
      completely different than g, even if the batch B is large. For instance, g ′ may point in the opposite
177   direction, i.e., g ′ = −g, or g ′ ⊥ g, or even g ′ = 0. There is no guarantee that adding any example
178   to any batch will not arbitrarily distort the gradient. The consequence for data privacy is that if the
179   adversary observes this, directly or indirectly (through the model parameters), then they can infer
180   membership of (x, y).
181
      DP-SGD avoids this problem by using per-example gradient clipping. In this work, we take a
182
      different approach. Instead of trying to constrain the change in the gradient that would result from
183   adding/removing any example, we seek to detect those batches with gradients that are not plausibly
184   deniable. We can think of such batches are “anomalous” compared to other batches, and we can
185   simply discard any potential parameter updates based on them.
186
187   3.2   P RIVACY N OTION AND P RIVACY T EST
188
189   We propose to update the model parameters only if the gradient gi is plausibly deniable, i.e., if it
190   is not too dissimilar to the gradients of some other mini-batches. To formalize this, we first need
191   to add isotropic Gaussian noise to the gradient vector g as g̃ = g + Z, where Z ∼ N (0, σ 2 I).
192   Note that adding noise to the gradient in SGD is a well-known technique that has benefits for
193
      convergence (Neelakantan et al., 2015; Ziyin et al., 2022). In our case, this allows us to view each
      (noisy) mini-batch gradient g̃ as a random variable. Given this, we can define the probability that a
194
      given fixed gradient vector g̃ is plausibly obtained from any mini-batch gradient gi , and from there
195
      the concept of a plausibly deniable gradient update.
196
      Definition 1. Let B1 , . . . , Bm be disjoint mini-batches and g1 , . . . , gm be their associated gradient.
197
      Let Bs be the chosen “seed” batch with associated gradient gs . We say that batch Bs is (α, σ, T )-
198
      plausibly deniable if there are at least T > 1 distinct batches Bi with i ∈ [1, m] that satisfy:
199
200                                                    p(g̃s − gs )

203
204   Now let α = exp (γ) for some γ > 0 and p(·) denotes the probability density function (pdf) of
205   N (0, Iσ 2 ). We will often think of γ as the privacy parameter (instead of α).

247   Privacy-Utility Tradeoff. Rejections of the privacy test drive the privacy (and utility) of the model.
248   In particular, if the test never rejects any candidate gradient updates, then Algorithm 1 is equivalent to
249   (vanilla) SGD. Informally, we expect utility to be maximized when the rejection rate is near 0, and we
250   expect privacy to increase as rejection rates increase. Critically, the privacy test must reject precisely
251
      those gradients from batches that would leak private information (e.g., those that would increase the
      vulnerability to membership inference). We show theoretically why this is guaranteed to happen in
252
      the next section. We also demonstrate experimentally that this happens in practice in Section 5.
253
254   Algorithmic Complexity. Compared to SGD, Algorithm 1 only performs at most a single update
255   of model parameters in each step. This update only occurs if the privacy test passes and it requires
256   computing up to m batches’ gradients. Checking Eq. (2) is reasonably efficient in practice so the main
257   computational bottleneck is the gradients’ computations. However, observe that when the rejection
258   rate is expected to be low, the algorithm will often not need to compute all m batches’ gradients to
259   pass the test. In experiments (supplementary materials) we find that although PD-SGD is slower than
260
      SGD, it is often much faster than DP-SGD for a single training step, in large part because it does not
      require calculating per-example gradients.

368   CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 and Purchase-100. For the models, we fine-tune ViT-B-16 for CIFAR-10 and
369   CIFAR-100 following few-shot settings in (Tobaben et al., 2023) using 500 shots for CIFAR-10 and
370   1000 for CIFAR-100, linear model for Purchase-100, and Wide ResNet16-4 for CIFAR-10 training
371
      from scratch. We use the Privacy Meter toolbox 2 for the implementation of membership inference
372   attacks. From it, we use the Population Attack (P-Attack), Reference Attack (R-Attack), Shadow
373   model Attack (S-Attack) based on Ye et al. (2022) and Carlini et al. Attack (C-Attack) based on
374   Carlini et al. (2022). We employ these four widely used attacks to comprehensively evaluate empirical
375   privacy leakage and make fair comparisons between different methods. Note that our goal here is not
376   to use the most exotic or recent attack, but to establish a fair empirical comparison between different
377   defense methods, and thus we use a well-understood set of popular recent membership inference
      attacks. We provide more details in Appendix C.

      6.2   R EJECTION OF ANOMALOUS BATCHES
517
518   How do we know that PD-SGD rejects gradient updates from anomalous batches and only those from
519   anomalous batches? We intentionally generate anomalous batches to evaluate this by flipping the
520   labels of a subset of examples (“poisoned examples”) and grouping them into a single batch with
521   other normal samples. We ensure that throughout training the poisoned examples are in the same
522   “anomalous” batch. We then collect the rejection rates when the anomalous batch is the seed and
523   when other batches are the seed, for varying proportion of poisoned examples.
524   Results are shown in Fig. 3, where we observe that for normal batches remain consistently low,
525   as expected and desired. This means that the privacy test does not discard updates unnecessarily.
526   However, when the anomalous batch is selected as seed, the rejection rate increases significantly and
527   quickly plateaus near 100% as the proportion of poisoned examples increases. This indicates that
528   PD-SGD effectively identifies and rejects anomalous batches, preventing the model parameters from
529   being updated in such cases.

531   7     C ONCLUSIONS
532
533   We proposed PD-SGD, a new approach for private learning without compromising performance.
534   PD-SGD is based on a rejection sampling approach using a privacy test. Theoretical and experimental
535   results demonstrate that PD-SGD provides a superior privacy-utility trade-off compared to both
536   existing methods with provable privacy such as DP-SGD and empirical defenses. This makes PD-
537   SGD a promising solution for enhancing privacy protection in practical deep-learning applications.

867
868
      We use the three of the most commonly used datasets for evaluating membership inference attacks
869
      (Shokri et al., 2017; Ye et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2022) and DP-SGD (De et al., 2022; Bao et al., 2024).
870
871   CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) contains 60,000 images with 10 classes. We use 50,000 as the
872   full training set and 10,000 as the test set as most papers do. Each example has three RGB channels
873
      and size 32 × 32 pixels. For fine-tuning tasks, we only use 500 data samples for training and 30,000
      for training from scratch.
874
875   CIFAR-100 is a well-known benchmark in the field of computer vision, also collected by Krizhevsky
876   et al. (2009). CIFAR-100 contains 60,000 color images, each with a resolution of 32 × 32 pixels. It
877   is more complex than the CIFAR-10 dataset; the images are organized into 100 distinct classes. The
878   dataset allocation includes 50,000 images for training purposes and 10,000 for testing. For finetuning
879
      task, we only use 1000 data samples for training and the rest of training data examples are used
      for MIA evaluation. For training from scratch, we use 25,000 data samples as the same setting in
880
      Zarifzadeh et al. (2024).
881
882   Purchase-100 is based on Kaggle’s “acquire valued shoppers” challenge3 and processed and simpli-
883   fied as introduced in Shokri et al. (2017). The dataset contains shopping records for thousands of
884   individuals and includes 197,324 data entries. For training, we use 25,000 samples and the rest for
885   testing. For MIAs, we use 25,000 samples from test set as shadow dataset.

       D.3    U NDERSTANDING BATCH SIZE IN PD-SGD
1010
1011
       The batch size plays an important role in terms of privacy. There are extreme edge cases that are
1012
       unrealistic where the batch size is the entire training set or the batch size is a single example. For
1013
       more realistic batch sizes there are several tradeoffs and ultimately the behavior depends also on the
1014
       chosen privacy parameters.
1015
1016   We conduct experiments on Purchase-100 and CIFAR-10 to further understand the batch size in
1017   PD-SGD and report results in Table 10. Results indicate that as batch size increases, the rate of
1018   deniability typically decreases—larger batches more easily pass the privacy test (for fixed privacy
1019   parameters) due to the averaging effect you described across different datasets. However, this does
1020   not necessarily translate into better privacy protection, as the potential for individual sample contri-
1021   butions to still be inferred remains.
1022
1023   Moreover, we found that adjusting other parameters—e.g., σ, γ, and threshold can help mitigate
1024   these effects, maintaining a balance between utility and privacy across varying batch sizes. For ex-
1025   ample, for the batch size = 1024, if we double the γ, we can decrease the reject rate to 56.98% and
       achieve a test accuracy of 63.87% with Best Attack AUC of 0.68.

1102   To further investigate the impact of the clipping threshold in DP-SGD on privacy protection, we fixed
1103   all other parameters and varied the clipping threshold, as shown in Table 12. We can observe that
1104   even though the clip threshold changes, the model’s utility and privacy are almost the same. However,
1105   during these experiments, we do find that if the clip threshold is changed, the learning rate also needs
1106   to be tuned properly to get the optimal utility. It makes sense that the impact on privacy of the clipping
1107   threshold should not be substantial since in DP-SGD the noise added to the gradient is scaled by the
1108   clipping norm.

1114   There are two broad strategies for tuning the privacy (hyper)parameters: (1) leverage the theoretical
1115   insights from Section 3.1; or (2) rely on empirically successful heuristics.
1116   Theory-based strategy: As explained in Section 3.1, by tuning σ and γ, we can make q(d) arbitrarily
1117
       small. If we have a desired bound on d, then we can find combinations of σ and γ that achieve the
1118
       desired effects (e.g., see Fig. 1). This can for example be done through a grid search.
1119
1120   Empirical strategy: Alternatively, we found that the following two-steps strategy is easy to follow
1121   and yields good trade-offs. Step 1: tune the noise σ to achieve acceptable utility, ignoring the privacy
1122   test. This helps determine an upper limit for utility. Step 2: tune γ and the threshold T , which allows
1123   for fine-grained control over the privacy-utility trade-off. We used this two-step sequential tuning
1124   approach in our experiments.
1125
       A useful heuristic while tuning γ and T is to monitor the rejection rate. However, note that there
1126
       exists favorable trade-offs for a wide-range of rejection rates, and a useful rule of thumb is therefore
1127
       only to avoid extreme values (e.g., 0% — no privacy guarantee; 100% — no utility / full privacy).

1132   PD-SGD provides favorable privacy-utility tradeoffs compared to alternative methods both empirical
1133   and DP-SGD. It also provides a guarantee that anomalous batches are provably rejected with high
       probability. However, it is not meant as a direct replacement for DP-SGD, since the guarantees are

1137   Note that we intend our technique to mostly be used in a centralized learning environment where
1138   adversaries only observe the final model weights (or run inference with the trained model as a black
1139   box). We assume that the full training transcript (i.e., gradient updates, intermediate updates, whether
1140   the privacy test passes) is not accessible to the adversary. This assumption may prevent PD-SGD
1141   from being used in some settings such as federated learning.
1142   PD-SGD has the advantage of providing better utility. It also starts a promising direction of future
