011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            While originally designed for unidirectional generative modeling, decoder-only
014            large language models (LLMs) are increasingly being adapted for bidirectional
015            modeling. However, these unidirectional and bidirectional models are typically
016            trained independently with distinct objectives (generation or representation
017            learning) thereby missing the potential opportunity for one objective to enhance
018
               the other. In this work, we introduce M AGNET, an adaptation of decoder-only
               LLMs that enhances their capabilities in generating robust representations and
019
               infilling missing text spans, while maintaining coherent and non-repetitive text
020            generation. M AGNET employs three self-supervised training objectives and
021            introduces an attention mechanism that combines bidirectional and causal attention,
022            enabling unified training across all objectives. We show that LLMs adapted using
023            M AGNET can outperform powerful text encoders on token-level and sentence-level
024            representation learning tasks. We also demonstrate that M AGNET enhances the
025            base LLM’s ability to generate contextually appropriate text infillings by enabling
026            it to take future context into consideration. Lastly, we show that, unlike other
027
               bidirectional language models for representation learning, the LLMs adapted using
               M AGNET can still perform open-ended text generation.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Language models are computational models designed to understand and generate human language.
034   They have transformed natural language processing, powering applications such as text annotation,
035   machine translation, summarization, speech recognition, and dialogue systems. Traditionally,
036   language models are categorized into three main types: (1) Encoder-only models (Devlin et al.,
037   2019; Liu et al., 2019; Lan et al., 2019; Sanh et al., 2019), which focus on encoding the input
038   into fixed-dimensional representations and excel in tasks like sentiment analysis (sentence-level
039   classification) and named entity recognition (token-level classification). (2) Decoder-only models
040
      (Brown et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Scao et al., 2022; Penedo et al.,
      2023), which are adept at generating coherent text, thereby specializing in tasks like creative content
041
      generation and dialogue systems. (3) Encoder-decoder models (Raffel et al., 2019; Lewis et al., 2019),
042
      wherein an encoder understands the input and a decoder generates the corresponding output, making
043   this architecture suitable for tasks like machine translation and summarization.
044
045   Recently, the NLP community has increasingly embraced decoder-only architecture (Large Language
046
      Models or LLMs) due to their efficient training and scalability to larger datasets, resulting in enhanced
      performance across various tasks. However, since these models are trained using causal attention and
047
      lack bidirectional context, they are less suitable for tasks like (1) sentiment analysis and named entity
048   recognition, which require understanding contextual representations of sentences or words, and (2)
049   text infilling, where predicting missing text spans must maintain coherence with subsequent context.
050   With the goal of leveraging the scalable decoder-only LLMs, some recent efforts (BehnamGhader
051   et al., 2024; Li & Li, 2023; Li et al., 2023; Duki’c & vSnajder, 2024; Du et al., 2021; Donahue et al.,
052   2020) have aimed to adapt decoder-only LLMs for these tasks. However, as illustrated in Figure 1,
053   methods that enhance LLMs for text infilling do not enable them to function as effective text encoders,
      while approaches focused on representation learning hinder their generative capabilities.

075
      In this work, we propose M AGNET (Modified Attention for Generation and Encoding of Text),
076
      an adaptation of decoder-only LLMs for (1) generating robust sentence-level and token-level
077
      representations, (2) infilling missing spans of text while preserving coherence with bidirectional
078   context, and (3) performing open-ended text generation without excessive repetition of words or
079   phrases. To achieve this, we use three self-supervised training objectives: (1) a masked modeling
080   objective to learn token-level representations, (2) a contrastive objective to learn sentence-level
081   representations, and (3) a missing-span generation objective to infill text and retain generative
082   capabilities. To facilitate simultaneous training across all these objectives, we deploy a specially
083   crafted attention mask that is a combination of bidirectional and causal attention.
084   Without any model-specific design or loss of generality, we apply M AGNET to LLaMA-2-7B (Touvron
085   et al., 2023b). We demonstrate that the proposed method requires simple modification and fine-tuning
086   of an off-the-shelf LLM to augment it with representation learning and infilling capabilities. Our
087   results show that M AGNET-adapted LLaMA-2-7B outperforms other methods that adapt the same
088   model for token-level and sentence-level representation learning tasks1 . Further, we also show
089   that M AGNET significantly improves the infilling capability of the LLM by enabling it to consider
090
      bidirectional context. Lastly, we analyze the repetition problem in text generated by text encoders
      and demonstrate that M AGNET-adapted models are significantly better at open-ended text generation
091
      than other text encoders.

095
096   Representation Learning.
097   Text representation learning focuses on understanding contextual relationships within sentences.
098   Traditionally, encoder models dominated this field due to their bidirectional context modeling,
099   using masked language modeling for token-level representations (Devlin et al., 2019; Liu et al.,
100   2019; He et al., 2020; Clark et al., 2020; He et al., 2021) and special tokens with similarity-based
101   optimization for sentence-level understanding (Gunel et al., 2020; Reimers & Gurevych, 2019; Wu
102   et al., 2020; Carlsson et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2020). Recent work has explored
103
      adapting decoder-only LLMs for representation learning, either by using last-token or mean-pooled
      representations (Neelakantan et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023), or by fine-tuning with masked modeling
104
      (BehnamGhader et al., 2024) or label supervision (Li et al., 2023; Duki’c & vSnajder, 2024). While
105
      some approaches modify the decoder’s causal attention to be bidirectional (BehnamGhader et al.,
106

      2024; Muennighoff et al., 2024; Li & Li, 2023; Duki’c & vSnajder, 2024), this often compromises
109   the model’s text generation abilities. In contrast, M AGNET employs a hybrid attention mechanism
110   that combines causal and bidirectional attention, enabling both robust representation learning and
111   preserved generation capabilities.
112
      Text Infilling. Text infilling requires considering both left and right context when generating
113
      text in the middle of a sequence. Encoder-decoder models (Raffel et al., 2019; Lewis et al., 2019;
114   Kalinsky et al., 2023) can handle this task by encoding available context and decoding infilled
115   text. Other approaches have extended masked language modeling to perform span infilling (Joshi
116   et al., 2019; Shen et al., 2023; 2020). Decoder-only models have also been adapted for infilling
117   through various strategies: training models to directly fill marked blanks (Donahue et al., 2020;
118   Du et al., 2021), rearranging training examples to align with infilling objectives (Bavarian et al.,
119   2022; Yang et al., 2019; Aghajanyan et al., 2022; Fried et al., 2022), or using dual generation from
120   both ends of a sentence until convergence (Nguyen et al., 2023; Serdyuk et al., 2017). However,
121   while these approaches successfully enhance LLMs with infilling capabilities, none have attempted
122
      to simultaneously equip them with both infilling and representation learning abilities, as done by
      M AGNET.
123
124   Unifying Text Understanding and Generation. Prior works on unifying natural language
125   understanding and generation within a single framework usually focus on proposing pretraining
126   objectives and task formulations. These approaches typically extend traditional masked language
127
      modeling, with innovations like permutation-based objectives for bidirectional context modeling
      (Yang et al., 2019), autoregressive blank infilling (Du et al., 2021), multi-directional attention masks
128
      (Dong et al., 2019), and sequence-to-sequence pretraining (Song et al., 2019; Raffel et al., 2019).
129
      However, these approaches require pretraining new networks from scratch, despite decoder-only
130   models demonstrating exceptional scalability and effectiveness. Instead of starting from scratch,
131   we propose a parameter-efficient method that builds upon the rich representations already learned
132   by existing large language models, transforming them into a unified framework for representation
133   learning, text infilling, and text generation.

      Decoder-only models, based on the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017), process input
      sequences through successive blocks of multi-head self-attention, feed-forward networks, and layer
139
      normalization. The self-attention mechanism converts the input x ∈ Rl×d into queries Q, keys K,
140   and values V using a linear projections, and computes attention using the formula:
141
142
                                                                     QKT + M

143                            Attni (Q, K, V) = softmax               √           V                       (1)
144                                                                      dk
145
146   where Attni is the ith head of the multi-head self-attention, dk represents the dimensionality of the
147   keys/queries, and M represents the causal mask. This causal mask M for an autoregressive LLM is
148   an l × l strictly upper triangular matrix with the upper triangle set to −∞, as shown in Figure 2a. M
149
      ensures that the softmax operation assigns an attention weight of zero to the future positions in the
      sequence, which in turn ensures that each token i can only attend to itself and tokens that precede it
150
      in the sequence.
151
152   M AGNET seeks to update the causal attention mechanism of an LLM by incorporating elements of
153   bidirectionality and thereafter fine-tunes the model using unsupervised objectives. We look at the
154
      modifications to the attention mechanism in Section 3.1 and the training objectives in Section 3.2.
155

157
158

      within segments of the input sequence, as illustrated in Figure 2. After the input text is tokenized for
159
      the language model, we categorize each token as either context tokens or span tokens:

187   causal/unidirectional for open-ended text generation tasks, (2) fully bidirectional for tasks requiring
188   representation learning, or (3) a combination of causal and bidirectional for text infilling.

      3.2     T RAINING O BJECTIVES
191
192   M AGNET fine-tunes an off-the-shelf LLM using three self-supervised objectives aimed at enhancing
193   the model’s ability to learn contextually-rich representations and autoregressively fill in missing spans
194   of text. These objectives are illustrated in Figure 3 and discussed below.
195
196   3.2.1    M ASKED N EXT T OKEN P REDICTION (MNTP)
197
198   MNTP enables the model to realize its newly enabled bidirectional attention capability. The task is
199   defined as follows: Given an input sequence x = (x1 , x2 , ..., xL ), we select a fraction of the input
200   tokens for masking and train the model to predict these masked tokens. In our setup, we find that
201
      selecting 20% of the input tokens for masking works well. Further, following Devlin et al. (2019),
      we replace 80% of the selected tokens with a [MASK] token, 10% with a random token from the
202
      model’s vocabulary, and leave the remaining 10% unchanged. Since LLMs are trained to predict the
203   next token in a sequence, we use the token representations from position l to predict a masked token
204   at position l + 12 . MNTP is optimized using categorical cross-entropy loss:

240   3.2.2   S ELF -S UPERVISED C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING (SSCL)
241
242   Since LLMs are not explicitly trained to capture the entire input context and generate sentence-level
243   representations, we employ SSCL to transform them into text encoders. The training objective is
244   defined as follows: Given an input sequence x, generate its augmented view x+ and align their
245   encoded representations e = f (x) and e+ = f (x+ ) in the embedding space, while distancing them
246
      from the encodings e− = f (x− ) of other input sequences x− in a training batch. Specifically,
      we employ paraphrasing (Damodaran, 2021) to generate augmented views of an input, and add
247
      an instruction "Given the sentence, find its representation:" to the training examples (Jiang et al.,
248
      2023). Then, we use the representations corresponding to the last token ([EOS]) of the final hidden
249   states as the sentence encoding. Our choice of using the last token representation as the encoding
250   is guided by the fact that M AGNET optimizes simultaneously for token-level and sentence-level
251   representations. Since the last token’s representation is not used for token-level optimization (because
252   the representation of input token i is given by output token i − 1), this choice enables us to disentangle
253   the two representation learning tasks during joint training. We use InfoNCE (van den Oord et al.,
254   2018) with in-batch negatives as the loss function:

304

      Figure 4 provides an overview of M AGNET. Starting with a training example x, the process unfolds
      in two parallel streams – (1) One or more contiguous spans of M tokens in x are marked as span
306
      tokens, while a fraction of the remaining tokens (context tokens) is masked to form xm . (2) x is
307   augmented to get x+ . The input sequences x, xm and x+ are processed by the base decoder model to
308   produce hidden states h, hm and h+ . From hm , a language modeling head generates ym , which is
309   used to compute LMNTP and LMSG . Parallelly, h and h+ are processed using a projection head to get
310   e and e+ , which are used to compute LSSCL . The overall loss function is given as:
311
312
                                       L = λ1 LMNTP + λ2 LSSCL + λ3 LMSG                                      (5)
313
314   For processing x to get x+ , the decoder utilizes a bidirectional attention mask, illustrated in Figure 2b.
315   While processing xm , the decoder employs an attention mask similar to those depicted in Figures 2c
316   and 2d. In some cases, when all input tokens are marked as span tokens, the attention mask reduces
317   to causal attention, as shown in Figure 2a.

410
      To test infilling capabilities, we evaluate the perplexity (PPL) of LLaMA-2-7B and M AGNET-adapted

414   Donahue et al. (2020), we compute PPL only for the tokens comprising the original masked out
415   spans. The results are presented in Table 4, and they show that the base model (LLaMA-2-7B)
416   exhibits significantly higher perplexity for the masked spans compared to M AGNET, demonstrating
417   that M AGNET effectively augments the base model with text infilling capabilities. This improvement
418
      is attributed to M AGNET’s ability to incorporate all the surrounding contextual information when
      infilling text, which increases the likelihood of correctly predicting the original masked content.
419
420   We also conducted experiments using zero-shot and few-shot learning to enable LLaMA-2-7B to
421   incorporate all the surrounding context when infilling a missing span. We explored various prompting
422   strategies and found that while a zero-shot setup did not yield sensible infillings, a five-shot setup
423   with descriptive prompts resulted in more context-aware infillings (refer Appendix B for details).
424
      For a comprehensive analysis, we conducted a human evaluation to compare the quality of infillings
      generated by the base model, its zero-shot variant, its few-shot variant, and its M AGNET adaptation.
425
      In this evaluation, we randomly sampled 100 stories from the ROC Stories dataset, masked out
426   one of their middle sentences, and tasked the models with infilling the missing sentence. Two
427   human annotators on Amazon Mechanical Turk then independently assessed whether each generated
428   sentence was contextually appropriate and contributed to a coherent story. The results are presented
429   in Table 5, and they show that the infillings generated by M AGNET-adapted model are significantly
430   more coherent than those generated by the variants of the base model. We show some qualitative
431   examples in Table 10, demonstrating that M AGNET successfully delivers relevant and contextually
      appropriate infillings.

      The repetition problem in text generation refers to the issue when generative models repeatedly
      produce the same phrases or sentences. Prior studies have identified that this issue often results from
450
      biases in the training data, limitations in the model’s design, or standard likelihood training and
451   inference (Holtzman et al., 2019; Welleck et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2022). In our study,
452   we find that when generative decoder models are adapted into text encoders by enabling bidirectional
453   attention (BehnamGhader et al., 2024; Li & Li, 2023; Li et al., 2023), the issue of repetition is
454   significantly worsened. For example, Table 11 shows the texts generated (using greedy decoding) by
455   the original LLaMA-2-7B and its adapted version for text encoding via LLM2Vec (BehnamGhader
456   et al., 2024). We observe noticeable repetitions in the text generated by LLM2Vec-adapted-Llama,
457   although the fine-tuning data (Wikitext-103) had almost no sentence-level repetitions (0.02%).
458   For quantitative detection of repetitions in texts generated by an LLM and its bidirectional adaptations,
459   we compute Rep-Sen and Rep-4 (as done by prior works analyzing the repetition problem (Holtzman
460   et al., 2019; Welleck et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2022)):

473   We conjecture that LLM2Vec is significantly more prone to generating repetitive text because it

476   known to repeat words when used for text generation (Table 11). M AGNET solves this issue by
477
      having autoregressive text generation as one of the training objectives.

479   5     C ONCLUSION
480
481   In this work, we presented M AGNET, a method to transform causal LLMs into text encoders and
482   infilling language models with bidirectional context-capturing ability. Through extensive experiments,
483   we show that M AGNET uniquely equips LLMs with abilities that are beyond the scope of traditional
484   text encoders or decoders. Thus, M AGNET shows the potential to unify text generation and text
485   encoding within a single framework. Future research could explore scaling M AGNET to multimodal
      settings.

      A     T RAINING D ETAILS
757
758   M AGNET fine-tunes LLaMA-2-7B using LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) with r = 16 and α = 32. We use the
759   AdamW optimizer with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999 and ϵ = 1e − 8, apply bfloat16 quantization, and use
760   scaled-dot-product attention (SDPA). All experiments are performed on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU,
761   with the M AGNET adaptation of LLaMA-2-7B taking approximately 7 hours. The hyperparameters
762   for the different training objectives are as follows:
763
      MNTP. We train for 4200 iterations using the Wikitext-103 train set (Merity et al., 2016) with a
764   batch size of 32, a learning rate of 3e-5, and a max sequence length of 512. We select 20% of the
765   tokens for masking – 80% of the selected tokens are replaced with a [MASK] token, 10% tokens are
766   replaced with a random token from the model’s vocabulary, and 10% tokens are left unchanged. For
767   LLaMA-2-7B, we use "_" as the mask token.
768
      SSCL. We train for 800 iterations with a batch size of 64, a learning rate of 3e-5, and a max
769   sequence length of 128. To extract representations we use the prompt "Given the sentence, find its
770   representation:" and extract the representations corresponding to the last token. The training data is
771   created from Wikitext (Merity et al., 2016) by extracting lines longer than 20 words and paraphrasing
772   them for the positive examples. We set τ = 0.1 in equation 3.
773
      MSG. Similar to MNTP, we train for 4200 iterations using the Wikitext-103 train set (Merity et al.,
774   2016) with a batch size of 32, a learning rate of 3e-5, and a max sequence length of 512. A training
775   example can have up to 2 missing spans with span length ranging from 4 to 128 tokens.
776
777
      Overall Loss. For the first 3400 iterations, we optimize the loss (equation 5) with λ1 = 1, λ2 = 0,
      and λ3 = 1, and for the next 800 iterations λ1 = 1, λ2 = 9, and λ3 = 1. We initially train with only
778
      MNTP and MSG because these objectives help the model learn to capture future context, a capability
779
      the base model lacks.
780
781   Word-Level Tasks. Using the frozen representations from the last hidden layer of the base model, we
782   train a linear classifier for the three word-level tasks (Chunking, NER, and POS-tagging). Specifically,
      we train on the CoNLL-2003 train set for 4000 steps using a batch size of 8, a learning rate of 5e-4,
783
      and a dropout rate of 0.1.

941   F    L IMITATIONS
942
943   While M AGNET better preserves the open-ended generation capability of the base LLM compared to
944
      other bidirectional adaptation methods, it still reduces generation quality. For instance, fine-tuning
      LLaMA-2-7B with M AGNET increases the test set perplexity (PPL) on Wikitext-103 from 6.4 to 7.6.
945
      Although qualitative analysis shows no major artifacts in the generated text, the model’s confidence
946
      in predicting the next word seems to be somewhat diminished.
947
948   In the infilling task, we only focus on augmenting the base LLM with the ability to consider all
949   the surrounding information to produce contextually coherent infilling. We have observed that the
      quality of infilling decreases when using the M AGNET-adapted LLM to infill very long sequences
950
      in the middle of a text. We believe this observation is a result of the parameters set for the MSG
951
      objective, and it can be addressed by the following three ways: (1) Instead of attempting to infill
952   a long segment of say 128 tokens at once, divide it into smaller chunks, such as infill 32 tokens at
953   a time; (2) Adjusting the parameters of the MSG task; (3) Fine-tuning the model specifically for
954   infilling tasks.
955
      Since the pre-training data for LLaMA-2-7B is not publicly available, there is a potential risk of test
956
      set contamination. To this end, we compare test set performances with the original LLaMA-2-7B
957
      and other recent methods that use the same base model (LLM2Vec and Echo Embeddings). This
958   comparative analysis helps ensure that our results are not unduly influenced by data contamination.
959   We leave it to the future works to establish and test on benchmarks that are surely not a part of the
960   pre-training data of the LLMs.
