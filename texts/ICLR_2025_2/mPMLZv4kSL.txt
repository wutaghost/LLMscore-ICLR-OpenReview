009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Nature creates diverse proteins through a ‘divide and assembly’ strategy. Inspired
012            by this idea, we introduce ProteinWeaver, a two-stage framework for protein back-
013            bone design. Our method first generates individual protein domains and then em-
014            ploys an SE(3) diffusion model to flexibly assemble these domains. A key chal-
015            lenge lies in the assembling step, given the complex and rugged nature of the inter-
016
               domain interaction landscape. To address this challenge, we employ preference
               alignment to discern complex relationships between structure and interaction land-
017
               scapes through comparative analysis of generated samples. Comprehensive exper-
018
               iments demonstrate that ProteinWeaver: (1) generates high-quality, novel protein
019            backbones through versatile domain assembly; (2) outperforms RFdiffusion, the
020            current state-of-the-art in backbone design, by 13% and 39% for long-chain pro-
021            teins; (3) shows the potential for cooperative function design through illustrative
022            case studies. To sum up, by introducing a ‘divide-and-assembly’ paradigm, Pro-
023            teinWeaver advances protein engineering and opens new avenues for functional
024            protein design.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Nature employs a sophisticated ‘divide and assemble’ strategy to create large and intricate protein
029
      structures that meet diverse biological functional needs (Fig. 1A) (Pawson & Nash, 2003; Huddy
030   et al., 2024; P Bagowski et al., 2010). This process primarily involves the recombination of existing
031   structural blocks, particularly protein domains, which serve as the fundamental, recurring units in
032   protein structures. Remarkably, a limited number of protein domains (approximately 500 as clas-
033   sified in CATH) suffice to create more than hundreds of thousands of structures satisfying a wide
034   array of functions (Orengo et al., 1997). This strategy enables the creation of multi-domain protein
035   backbones, facilitating the emergence of cooperative functions.
036   Recent advances in protein backbone design have enabled the generation of novel and diverse struc-
037   tures with high designability (Watson et al., 2023; Ingraham et al., 2023; Yim et al., 2023; 2024;
038   Bose et al., 2023; Lin & AlQuraishi, 2023; Lee et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2024a). However, our
039   analysis reveals a significant limitation: designability decreases markedly as the backbone length
040   increases (Fig. 1E). This limitation may stems from inadequate inter-domain interaction, evidenced
041   by a dramatic decrease in domain numbers and interface scTM compared to native proteins (Fig.9
042   and Fig.1F). These findings highlight the need for an approach to address the intricacies of multi-
043   domain organization in backbone design, particularly for larger protein backbones.
044   In this study, inspired by nature’s strategies, we present ProteinWeaver (Fig.1B). Our method ad-
045   dresses the limitations of current approaches by breaking down the complex problem of backbone
046   design into manageable components, framing it as a divide and assembly problem. ProteinWeaver
047   operates in two stages (Fig. 1B): (1) Domain generation: We first divide the long sequence into
048   multiple domains, focusing on generating these local structures independently, allowing for stable
049   and accurate generation of individual domains; (2) Flexibly assembly: we employ a SE(3) diffusion
050
      model to learn the flexible assembly between these domains (Fig. 1C). This stage aims to capture the
      crucial inter-domain interactions. In short, this two-stage approach represents a brand-new paradigm
051
      for de novo backbone design.
052
053   The second assembly stage of our approach presents a significant challenge: designing optimal inter-
      domain interactions through precise weaving of independently generated protein domains. This

098   et al., 2021). Two primary factors pose difficulties: (1) The scarcity of domain interaction data,

105   first conducted extensive sampling to generate diverse multi-domain structures and quantitatively
106   evaluate their interaction quality using scTM metrics. This process captures the complex distribu-
107   tion of the structure-interaction energy landscape in a fine-grained manner (Fig. 1D). Then, we
      implemented preference alignment. Rather than learning mappings to predefined labels in conven-

      Our primary contributions can be summarized as follows:
113         (1) We propose the first ‘divide-and-assembly’ two-stage generation framework for protein
114             backbone design. ProteinWeaver enables flexible assembly of general protein domains,
115             allowing for the creation of sophisticated structures.
116
            (2) We adopt the preference alignment technique to effectively navigate the domain interaction
117             landscape and generate interaction-reasonable backbones in our diffusion model, which
118             may provide a general approach benefiting backbone design.
119
            (3) We present an extensive experimental evaluation of ProteinWeaver by comparing its per-
120
                formance with the state-of-the-art methods including RFdiffusion (Watson et al., 2023),
121             Chroma (Ingraham et al., 2023), FrameDiff (Yim et al., 2023), and FrameFlow (Yim et al.,
122             2024). ProteinWeaver significantly outperforms RFdiffusion by 13% to 39% in the quality
123             of long-chain backbones (Fig. 1E) and exhibits comprehensive advantages across various
124             metrics (Fig. 1F).
125         (4) We present ProteinWeaver’s potential applications for cooperative function design through
126             targeted domain assembly, as illustrated by case studies in substrate-directed enzyme de-
127             sign and bispecific antibody engineering.

      2.2   T RAINING
217
218   In this section, we provide the details of ProteinWeaver training, including datasets, pretraining, and
219   preference alignments. More details can be found in Appendix B.2.1.
220
      Dataset. To train ProteinWeaver, we constructed a set composed of (M̄, S) pairs. The structure of
221
      protein backbone S in this set is sourced from the Protein Data Bank (PDB). Following Yim et al.
222   (2023), we filter for single-chain monomers between length 60 and 512 with resolution < 5Å down-
223   loaded from PDB (Berman et al., 2000) on March 2, 2024. We further filtered the data with more
224   than 50% loops and left with 22728 proteins. Multi-domain PDB structures were further filtered,
225   resulting 5835 PDB structures for pretraining. For each PDB, we identify the domain number m and
226   domain index {D1 , D2 , ..., Dm } through Unidoc (Ribeiro et al., 2019). Finally, we convert them to
227   Cα distance maps and use Eq. (1) to get M̄.
228   Pretraining. In our pipeline, domain assembly generation involves the flexible assembling of do-
229   mains into integrated backbones. The intra-domain structures are alternated during the assembly for
230   optimal inter-domain interaction. To maintain consistency between the pretraining and inference
231   stages, we refolded each domain {SD1 , SD2 , ..., SDm } to {S̄D1 , S̄D2 , ..., S̄Dm } of multi-domain
232   PDB using ESMFold (Lin et al., 2023), mimicing their unassembled states for training.
233
      In the pretraining stage, we adopted the training loss from FrameDiff, comprising two main compo-
234   nents: diffusion score-matching loss for translation and rotation, along with auxiliary losses related
235   to the coordinate and pairwise distance loss on backbone atoms, as depicted in Eq. (3).

      bone design: This task focuses on the design of de novo proteins, a subject of significant interest in

      3.5           A BLATION STUDY
450
451   Training using refolded domains is crucial for ProteinWeaver to learn domain assembly. We
452   hypothesized that training using refolded domains, which mimics their isolated states, facilitates
453   ProteinWeaver’s learning of structural interactions during domain assembly. To evaluate this, we
454   compared ProteinWeaver trained on refolded domains (yellow bars) with a version trained on do-
455   mains directly split from PDBs without refolding (green bars). As shown in Fig.6 and Tab.1, the
456   model trained on directly split structures exhibited significantly impaired performance in domain
457
      assembly tasks. This phenomenon was consistently observed in both domain assembly and protein
      backbone design tasks (Fig.7). These results demonstrate the importance of using refolded domain
458
      structures in model training for effective domain assembly learning.
459
460   Preference alignment efficiently optimizes performance for domain assembly and backbone
461   design. To evaluate the effectiveness of preference alignment in optimizing domain assembly, we
462   compared ProteinWeaver with alignment (red bar) to a version without alignment (yellow bar). As
463
      shown in Fig.6 and Tab.3, alignment significantly improved performance across backbones assem-
      bled using various domains, including split domains from PDB, designed domains, and CATH do-
464
      mains. Inter-domain scTM evaluation further confirmed these results, demonstrating preference
465
      alignment’s high efficiency in optimizing domain interaction quality. We extended this ablation
466   study to general backbone design (Fig.7). ProteinWeaver with alignment (purple line) consistently
467   outperformed the version without alignment (blue line) in generating assembled domains of higher
468   quality. This suggests that alignment is effective in optimizing inter-domain interactions across
469   different design tasks.
470
      Preference alignment outperforms Supervised Fine-tuning in optimizing domain assembly. We
471
      conducted ablation studies to compare the effectiveness of preference alignment with Supervised
472   Fine-tuning (SFT), an alternative method for optimizing high-quality domain assembly. For SFT,
473   we utilized the “winner” dataset from the preference alignment process to fine-tune the model. As
474   shown in Fig.7, ProteinWeaver fine-tuned with preference alignment (red bar) significantly outper-
475   formed the version fine-tuned with SFT (purple bar). These results strongly suggest that preference
476   alignment is more effective than SFT for optimizing domain assembly in this task.

519   5   C ONCLUSIONS AND DISCUSSIONS
520
521
522   Conclusions. In this paper, we introduce ProteinWeaver, the first protein backbone design model
523   based on a ‘divide-and-assembly’ framework. This approach enables the creation of novel, high-
524   quality backbones through the flexible assembly of diverse domains. ProteinWeaver demonstrates
525
      superior performance in general backbone design, particularly excelling in the challenging task of
      designing long-chain backbones compared to existing state-of-the-art approaches. The assembly
526
      capability of ProteinWeaver not only advances current protein design methods but also lays the
527
      groundwork for cooperative function design, potentially opening new avenues for protein engineer-
528   ing.
529
530   Discussions. While our framework demonstrates a new paradigm for protein backbone design, we
531
      acknowledge several technical aspects that warrant further investigation: (1) Recent advancements
      have revealed that flow matching-based protein backbone generation methods outperform diffusion-
532
      based approaches. An exciting avenue for future research lies in exploring whether replacing our
533
      current diffusion-based framework with a flow matching-based approach could yield further ben-
534   efits; (2) Domain structure representation: Our current method explicitly represents local domain
535   structures as distance maps. However, the efficacy of alternative pairwise representation methods
536   remains an open question. (3) Integration of design stages: Currently, our approach employs two
537   separate steps for backbone design. A promising direction for future research is developing an end-
538   to-end unified framework that efficiently integrates these steps. Future research could further refine
539   and extend this framework by addressing these questions, potentially revolutionizing our ability to
      create functional protein backbones with unprecedented precision.

662   A     B RIEF I NTRODUCTION OF D OMAIN A SSEMBLY
663
664   Domain assembly is a well-established task in protein design that has been extensively researched
665   using traditional computational biology methods, such as RFdiffusion. To clarify, we provide the
666   following examples that highlight the significance of this task:
667
      [Domain-Assembly for Structural Design] A recent study demonstrated the use of helical protein
668
      blocks for designing extendable nanomaterials (Huddy et al., 2024). This work serves as a proof
669
      of concept for the domain-based design approach, but it focused solely on pure helical-bundle as-
670   semblies. In contrast, our study generalizes this idea to encompass a broader range of domains with
671   structural variations. We present the first application of deep learning methods for general domain
672   assembly, marking an important new paradigm in this field.
673
      [Domain assembly for functional design] Nearly two decades ago, research showed that well-
674
      assembled domains, through interface-directed evolution, could dramatically enhance both affinity
675
      and specificity—achieving over 500-fold and 2,000-fold increases, respectively (Huang et al., 2008).
676   More recently, another study revealed that assembling a substrate recruitment domain to an enzyme
677   significantly improved its functional specificity (Park et al., 2023). These studies underscore the
678   validity and importance of our motivation for pursuing domain-assembly-based protein structure
679   and function design, a concept we introduced in our manuscript. We have added more references in
680   the revised manuscript to emphasize this importance.

685
686   B.1.1    P RETRAINING DATA
687
688   To train ProteinWeaver, specifically the assembly generator, a series of spliced distance maps M̄ and
689   the corresponding structures S are required. In pretraining stage, our structures are sourced from
690   the PDB dataset. Following Yim et al. (2023), we filter for single-chain monomers between length
691   60 and 512 with resolution < 5Å downloaded from PDB (Berman et al., 2000) on March 2, 2024,
692
      resulting in 22,728 proteins.
693   For each protein S, we used Unidoc to identify the domain number m and domain indices
694   {D1 , D2 , ..., Dm }. For proteins where m = 1, , we directly converted their structures into the
695   corresponding Cα distance map M̄. If m > 1, we initially segmented the PDB structure into
696   domain structures {SD1 , SD2 , ...SDm } based on the domain indices identified by Unidoc. Subse-
697   quently, we refolded each domain structure using ESMFold to construct the refolded domain struc-
698
      tures {S̄D1 , S̄D2 , ...S̄Dm }. Refolding with ESMFold ensures the independence of domain structures,
      maintaining consistency between training and inference.
699
700   Finally, we converted the refolded domain structures {S̄D1 , S̄D2 , ...S̄Dm } into their corresponding
701   Cα distance map {M̄D1 , M̄D2 , ..., M̄Dm } and utilized Eq. (1) to transform them into spliced dis-
      tance map M̄. Among these 22,728 proteins, Unidoc identified 5,835 multi-domain PDB structures.

      B.1.2    A LIGNMENT DATA
703
704   In this paper, we utilized SPPO for preference alignment of the pretrained models. To construct the
705   alignment dataset, a series of pair datas consisting of winner data Sw and loser data Sl needed to
706   be obtained. To achieve this, the following steps were undertaken: (1) Construction of the distance
707   map M̄. (2) Generation of multiple structural data using M̄ as a condition. (3) Selection of metrics
708
      to evaluate the quality of data, leading to the construction of winner data and loser data.
709   To construct the distance map M̄, similar to building the pretraining dataset, we segmented the pro-
710   teins from the PDB into single-domain structures using Unidoc. Subsequently, we de-duplicated
711   these single-domain structures using TMalign (with a threshold set at 0.3). Following this, we
712   randomly selected 100 single domains and combined them randomly to create 10,000 domain com-
713   binations. Eq. (1) was then applied to derive the corresponding spliced distance map for each com-
714
      bination.
715   For each spliced distance map, ProteinWeaver was employed to generate three structures, with the
716   scTM score utilized as the metric for distinguishing between winner and loser data. Ultimately, we
717   established 10,000 pairs of pair datas for SPPO alignment.

721   B.2.1    M ODEL ARCHITECTURE
722   ProteinWeaver iteratively updates the structural frames of proteins through a sequence of L layers of
723   folding blocks. A folding block is composed of three modules: an IPA module, a backbone update
724   module, and a edge update module. Each layer of the folding block takes single representation, pair
725   represetation and frames as input.
726
      Following Yim et al. (2023), we add a skip connection and a 2-layers transformer encoder to original
727
      IPA (Jumper et al., 2021). The IPA module takes the single and pair representations and frames
728   as input. For edge update module, we use a linear layer operates on pair representation with a
729   single representation-gated structural bias. For backbone update module, we use a MLP to predict
730   translation and rotation updates for the frames of each residue, where the input to MLP is single
731   represetation.
732
      There are no weights shared among the L folding blocks. The sequence representation is initialized
733
      using the diffusion timestep and the edge representation is initialized using spliced distance map.
734

898   B.6   TASKS
899
900   Protein domain assembly. In this study, we evaluated the model’s capability for domain assem-
901   bly using domains from three distinct sources: (1) reassembling domains from native PDBs, (2)
902
      assembling randomly sampled native domains from CATH domains, and (3) assembling structures
      generated/synthesized by the backbone design models.
903
904   For the experiment involving the reassembly of domains of natural proteins, we curated a test set
905   of 500 proteins from the multi-domain dataset of the Protein Data Bank (PDB). Specifically, we
906   employed Unidoc to identify and partition the domains of these natural proteins, transforming them
907   into spliced distance maps. Following model training, we used these spliced distance maps as con-
908
      ditions to generate corresponding proteins and computed their TM score and scTM score against
      actual natural proteins. The results indicated that ProteinWeaver has learned the assembly patterns
909
      of natural proteins.
910
911   In the experiment involving the splicing of CATH domains, we selected 500 proteins with distinct
912   topological structures from the CATH dataset and constructed 10,000 novel splicing combinations
913   using a random assembly approach. Subsequently, we tested these combinations using Protein-
914
      Weaver and further use SPPO for preference alignment.
915   Finally, we also assessed the performance of ProteinWeaver in assembling structures generated by
916   different backbone design models. Notably, in this experiment, we did not filter the generated struc-
917   tures based on conditions. As a result, we observed that RFdiffusion, known for its stable generation
      quality, demonstrated superior performance across various lengths without conditional screening.
