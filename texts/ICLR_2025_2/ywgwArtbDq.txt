009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Modern CAPTCHAs often rely on vision tasks that are supposedly hard for com-
012            puters but easy for humans. Although image recognition models pose a signif-
013            icant threat to such CAPTCHAs, they can be fooled by hiding “random” noise
014            in images. However, these methods are model-specific and thus can not aid
015            CAPTCHAs in fooling all models. We show in this work that by allowing for
016            more significant changes to the images while preserving the semantic informa-
017            tion and keeping it solvable by humans, we can fool many state-of-the-art models.
018
               Specifically, we demonstrate that by adding masks of various intensities the Top
               1 Accuracy (Acc@1) drops by more than 50%-points for all models, and suppos-
019
               edly robust models such as vision transformers see an Acc@1 drop of 80%-points.
020
               These masks can therefore effectively fool modern image classifiers, thus showing
021            that machines have not caught up with humans – yet.
022

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Not surprisingly, CAPTCHAs are threatened by advanced image recognition models. Plesner
027   et al. (2024) has recently shown that the most popular CAPTCHA environment (reCAPTCHA by
028   Google (6sense, 2023)) can be solved equally well by machines and humans.1 If CAPTCHAs are to
029   have a future, a new approach is needed.
030   Adversarial machine learning is related to CAPTCHAs, as researchers try to build samples where the
031   machine fails to recognize the image while the human does not register any manipulation happening.
032   On the one hand, these imperceptible manipulations are more ambitious than CAPTCHAs since even
033   the earliest CAPTCHAs did not bother to hide the manipulation of the input. On the other hand,
034   adversarial image generation is not robust enough for automatic bot detection, as it often tailors the
035   attack to a specific model.
036   We want images that can effectively fool every machine learning model, but we do not mind having
037   a visible manipulation. However, the manipulation should be easy for humans to filter out. In
038   other words, we do not mind if many pixels are changed a lot, as long as the image is still easily
039   recognizable to humans. This can be achieved if the image manipulation is somehow predictable,
040   for instance by overlaying the original image with a periodic signal like a grid. A promising new
041   form of CAPTCHAs, known as hCaptcha, is doing exactly that, and in this work, we want to get a
042   clearer understanding of what this approach can and cannot do.
043   The signals, or masks, inspired by hCaptcha can be surprisingly simple. In addition, to fully assess
044   their capabilities and potential impact on vision models, we have established the following key
045   motivations for this study.
046
047         1. Exploration of significant adversarial perturbations: In contrast to traditional adversar-
048            ial attacks that aim for imperceptibility, our study focuses on the domain of CAPTCHAs
049            where visible perturbations are acceptable. In this context, we can allow aggressive pertur-
050
               bations, as the limit is not imperceptibility but rather semantic preservation for humans.

            2. Exploiting the human-machine vision gap: Our research aims to understand and leverage

            3. Accessibility of attacks: The simplicity and ease of execution of the proposed attacks

064   In summary, our work examines adversarial examples through the lens of CAPTCHA services. We
065   challenge the constraints of imperceptibility in adversarial attacks, proposing that any semantics-
066   preserving distortion that effectively differentiates human users from automated solvers is acceptable
067   within this domain. This approach allows for large perturbations, shifting our focus to metrics that
068   quantify semantic change rather than visual imperceptibility.
069   Although reCAPTCHA has been broken, hCaptcha remains undefeated in the ongoing attack-
070   defense arms race and has recently added multiple new challenges and layers of security mea-
071   sures (QIN2DIM, 2022; allerallegro, 2022).
072
073   Approach To investigate these issues, we focus on evaluating the performance of state-of-the-art
074   vision models against a range of image filters inspired by hCaptcha techniques. Our study aims to:
075
076         1. Quantify the drop in Acc@1 and Acc@5 accuracy when various filters are applied to input
077
               images.
078         2. Compare the resilience of different model architectures to these adversarial examples.
079         3. Assess whether models specifically designed for robustness offer significant advantages in
080            this context.
081
082   Our preliminary findings underscore the effectiveness of masks in challenging even the most ad-
083
      vanced vision models, motivating our deeper investigation of these adversarial techniques.
084   Through this research, we hope to contribute to the ongoing discussion on AI safety and reliability,
085   emphasizing the need for vision models that can maintain high performance in the face of real-
086   world image manipulations. Our findings have implications not only for the development of more
087   robust models but also for the broader challenge of creating computer vision systems that can match
088   human-level adaptability in visual perception tasks.

090   2   R ELATED W ORK
091
092   Deep learning models have achieved unprecedented performance in computer vision tasks, fre-
093   quently exceeding human-level accuracy on image classification benchmarks (He et al., 2015; 2016;
094   Russakovsky et al., 2015; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021). State-of-the-art architectures such as Vision
095   Transformers (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), ConvNeXt (Liu et al., 2022), and EVA-02 (Fang
096   et al., 2024) now form the foundation of numerous critical applications, ranging from autonomous
097   vehicles (Yurtsever et al., 2020) to medical imaging (Chen et al., 2022; Shamshad et al., 2023). How-
098
      ever, the robustness of these models against adversarial attacks remains a pressing concern for their
      deployment in real-world scenarios, which could compromise their reliability and security (Serban
099
      et al., 2020).
100
101   The field of adversarial examples in machine learning has seen significant advances in recent
102   years (Hendrycks et al., 2021). Our work on geometric masks for CAPTCHAs builds on the foun-
103   dational concept of robust and non-robust features in machine learning models, as proposed by
104
      (Ilyas et al., 2019). This perspective suggests that adversarial examples exploit non-robust features
      susceptible to imperceptible perturbations while preserving robust features crucial for human inter-
105
      pretation.
106
107   Expanding on this framework, recent studies have demonstrated the potential of geometric metrics to
      detect adversarial samples. Venkatesh & Steinbach (2022) showed promising results using density

108
      and coverage metrics to identify adversarial examples in datasets such as MNIST and biomedical
109   imagery. This approach aligns with our focus on geometric perturbations that disrupt machine learn-
110   ing models’ reliance on non-robust features while maintaining image semantic integrity for human
111   solvers.
112
      In the specific context of CAPTCHAs, researchers have explored various innovative approaches to
113
      enhance security against automated solvers. Sheikh & Banday (2022) proposed a novel animated
114
      CAPTCHA technique based on the persistence of vision, which displays text characters in multiple
115   layers within an animated image. This word-level adversarial attack demonstrates ongoing efforts
116   to develop more robust CAPTCHA systems that can effectively distinguish between human and ma-
117   chine solvers. Similarly, Hajjdiab (2017) introduced a random CAPTCHA system to match images
118   that eliminates the need for an image database while maintaining ease of use. Their approach gener-
119   ates random images and asks users to match feature points between two images, leveraging concepts
120   from computer vision research.
121   By synthesizing these diverse research directions, our work aims to contribute to the ongoing efforts
122   to enhance the robustness of machine learning models against adversarial attacks, particularly in
123   the context of CAPTCHA systems. We seek to leverage insights from geometric perturbations,
124   adversarial training, and innovative CAPTCHA designs to develop more effective and secure visual
125   challenges that maintain a clear distinction between human and machine solvers.

129
130   In this section, we will go over the data that we used for the analysis along with the model choices.
131   We have selected multiple models, which we will evaluate on the datasets to demonstrate the effec-
132   tiveness of the masks we have constructed.
133
134   Models We selected several models to evaluate the performance of, namely: “Con-
135   vNeXt XXLarge” (Liu et al., 2022), Open CLIP’s “EVA01-g-14-plus” (Fang et al., 2023b) and
136   “EVA02-L-14” (Fang et al., 2024), “DFN5B-CLIP-ViT-H” by Apple (Fang et al., 2023a), the origi-
137   nal “ViT-L-14-378” and “ViT-H-14-378-quickgelu” (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), “ResNet50x64” (He
138
      et al., 2015), and RoBERTa-B and RoBERTa-L (Conneau et al., 2020); the RoBERTa models are
      selected as they are supposed to be robust against adversarial attacks.2 Due to time constraints, we
139
      were not able to test the method presented recently by Fort & Lakshminarayanan (2024); we leave
140
      this for future work. The models were selected to represent landmark architectures in both convo-
141   lutional and transformer-based approaches. This selection allows us to evaluate the effectiveness of
142   our masks across different model paradigms.
143
144
      Data We conducted our experiments using both the enriched ImageNet dataset with 1,000 entries
145
      provided by “visual-layer” on HuggingFace and the reduced ImageNette dataset (Howard, 2019).
146
      The ImageNette dataset, consisting of approximately 10,000 images evenly distributed across 10
147   categories, was chosen to make the computations more feasible. To accommodate the need for
148   multiple iterations on each image, we created three smaller datasets: SubSet200, SubSet500,
149   and ResizedAll. SubSet200 and SubSet500 contain 2,000 and 5,000 images, respectively,
150   maintaining the full resolution of ImageNette. ResizedAll includes all ImageNette images scaled
151   down to 128x128 pixels, a standard size for CAPTCHAs, to speed up image processing. Note that
152   this resizing may result in a slight performance drop compared to full-resolution images. Table 1
153   shows results for the clean images, and the models generally achieve Acc@1 accuracy in the high
154   80% to low 90% range, with Acc@5 accuracy in the high 90% range.
155   We defined four masks – “Circle”, “Diamond”, “Square” and “Knit” – which we apply to the images
156   at various intensities. These masks were selected based on an experiment involving 1,600 web-
157   scraped and hand-labeled images from hCaptcha. The number and intensity of mask elements are
158   determined by the density and opacity values, with the density fixed to a constant value in our
159   subsequent experiments focusing on the effects of varying opacity; for details, see Appendix A.1.

195   Perceptual Quality and the Accuracy Metric Perceptual quality is a crucial aspect of our evalua-
196   tion, assessing the visual fidelity of adversarial examples. We used a weighted average metric to cap-
197   ture various aspects of image quality. This metric combines cosine similarity (15% weight) (Singhal
198   et al., 2001), Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR, 25% weight) (Faragallah et al., 2021), Structural
199   Similarity Index (SSIM, 35% weight) (Wang et al., 2004), and Learned Perceptual Image Patch
200   Similarity (LPIPS, 25% weight) (Zhang et al., 2018). The weights were chosen to balance the im-
201
      portance of each component in the overall quality assessment.
202   Moreover, we evaluate the models based on their accuracy. The models predict a likelihood for each
203   of their pre-trained classes so the classes can be sorted by likelihood in descending order from top
204   to bottom. We focus on the accuracy@k (with k = 1 and k = 5), denoted Acc@k, which measures
205   how often the ground truth label is in the top k classes.

      5        C ONCLUSION
325
326
      In this study, we have demonstrated the high effectiveness of geometric masks in fooling state-of-
327
      the-art vision models, and the experiments leverage the gaps between human and machine abilities.
328
      This suggests potential new directions for developing more robust vision models over the long term
329   while creating secure visual challenges in the short term. We show that there is a clear trade-off
330   in the perceptual quality of images for them to be effective against vision models. However, while
331   the perceptual quality decreases, the accuracy of the models also drops, often with more than 50%-
332   points. This highlights vulnerabilities in advanced vision systems and underscores the continued
333   capability of CAPTCHA-style challenges in differentiating humans from machines.
334
      Although our study focused on specific mask types and datasets, one could easily expand into other
335   masks or determine how effectively models can be fine-tuned on images with masks applied. Fur-
336   thermore, one could try the methods on the recently published DeepMind model which is supposed
337   to be very robust against adversarial examples (Fort & Lakshminarayanan, 2024). In addition, a
338   detailed human evaluation of the masks should be performed.
339
      Overall, this study contributes to the ongoing discussion on AI safety and reliability, highlighting the
340
      persistent challenge of creating truly robust vision systems that can match human-level adaptability
341   in visual perception tasks.
342
343   Finally, for thousands of years, humans have pondered what makes humans different from animals.
344
      A popular story from ancient Greece goes that after Plato gave the tongue-in-cheek definition of man
      as ”featherless bipeds”, Diogenes the Cynic plucked a chicken and brought it into Plato’s Academy,
345
      saying, ”Here is Plato’s man.”3
346
347   More recently, the question has been what makes humans different from machines – with the Turing
348   test being a famous example. Given recent advances in computer science and neuroscience, it can
349   be argued that machines will eventually surpass humans at every task – the so-called Technological
350
      Singularity event. But until that happens, there exist AI-hard tasks where humans surpass machines.
      And if the least intelligent human can surpass the most intelligent machine on a task, then the task
351
      could be a good CAPTCHA. The computer vision domain may be an area where AI-hard tasks can
352
      be found, but it needs to move beyond simple image classification. Even smart humans can struggle
353   to recognize different breeds of dogs or tell very similar colors apart. However, machines are very
354   good at this, as they can easily memorize all possibilities. Meanwhile, the machines struggle to
355   classify images with noise or other artifacts not seen during training, while humans excel at this.
356
      CAPTCHA generation might sound like a mundane and boring topic, but it is rooted in a question
357
      as old as philosophy: What makes a human “human”?

489   A.1   H YPERPARAMETER O PTIMIZATION
490
491   In our hyperparameter optimization phase, we focused on classification models because of their in-
492   terpretability advantages over segmentation models. Our initial dataset comprised 1600 scraped and
493   annotated hCaptcha samples, which we used to benchmark several state-of-the-art closed-vocabulary
494
      classification models. The “EVA01-g-14 model”, trained on “LAION-400M”, emerged as the top
      performer with Acc@1 of 94.39% and Acc@5 of 98.93%. Other models like “ConvNeXt-XXLarge”
495
      and “ViT-H-14” also showed strong performance, although none achieved 100% accuracy, a notable
496
      departure from the results typically seen with reCAPTCHAv2 (Plesner et al., 2024).
497
498   Upon analysis of the misclassified images, we observed a combination of imperceptible perturba-
499   tions and perceptible geometric masks. We identified four distinct geometric mask types for re-
500
      construction and added a novel “knit” mask, essentially a modified “diamond” mask allowing for
      overlapping shapes. We intentionally left out word-level adversarial attack masks, as they have been
501
      proven to be easy to mitigate (Zhang et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2023; Shayegani et al., 2023). For each
502
      mask, we parameterized three variables: “opacity” (alpha value of the overlay), “density” (shapes
503   per row/column and nesting, ranging from 0-100), and “epsilon” (for white-box FGSM attacks with
504   CLIP-ViT on ImageNet).
505
506
      We conducted a hyperparameter grid search using the visual-layer/imagenet-1k-vl-
      enriched dataset on HuggingFace, testing 5-20 examples per combination on the validation set.
507
      We chose the CLIP ViT model for this phase due to its superior adversarial robustness, as noted
508
      by Wang et al. (2024). Our optimization metric combined the difference in model accuracy pre-
509   and post-mask application with an average of three perceptual quality metrics. To identify optimal
510   parameters, we selected examples with the highest perceptual quality for each level of accuracy
511   difference and performed a linear regression. We then focused on samples above the regression
512   line in multidimensional space. This approach proved to be more tractable than our attempts with
513   multi-objective optimization with multiple variables.
514
      Our findings revealed that FGSM perturbations generally degraded the results when combined with
515   masks. We determined that the optimal density value was consistently 70, while the most effective
516   opacity range was 50-170 (equivalent to 19%-66% alpha). These insights allowed us to isolate the
517   best-performing masks for a comprehensive benchmark against the latest models.
518
      This rigorous optimization process, grounded in semantic computer vision research, enabled us to
519
      systematically explore the parameter space and identify the most effective adversarial techniques
520
      inspired by hCaptcha challenges. The results, visualized in Figure 1, provide a quantitative basis for
521   comparing the masks.
