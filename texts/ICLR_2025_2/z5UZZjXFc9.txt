011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Multi-task learning (MTL) can leverage shared knowledge across tasks to improve
014            data efficiency and generalization performance, and has been applied in various
015
               scenarios. However, task imbalance remains a major challenge for existing MTL
               methods. While the prior works have attempted to mitigate inter-task unfairness
016
               through loss-based and gradient-based strategies, they still exhibit imbalanced per-
017
               formance across tasks on common benchmarks. This key observation motivates us
018            to consider performance-level information as an explicit fairness indicator, which
019            can more accurately reflect the current optimization status of each task, and ac-
020            cordingly help to adjust the gradient aggregation process. Specifically, we utilize
021            the performance variance among tasks as the fairness indicator and introduce a
022            dynamic weighting strategy to gradually reduce the performance variance. Based
023            on this, we propose PIVRG, a novel performance-informed variance reduction
024            gradient aggregation approach. Extensive experiments show that PIVRG achieves
025            state-of-the-art performance across various benchmarks, spanning both supervised
026
               learning and reinforcement learning tasks with task numbers ranging from 2 to 40.
               Results from the ablation study also show that our approach can be integrated into
027
               existing methods, significantly enhancing their performance while reducing the
028
               variance in task performance, thus achieving fairer optimization.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Multi-task learning (MTL) (Caruana, 1997; Ruder, 2017; Zhang & Yang, 2021) is an approach
034   where a single model is trained to solve multiple tasks simultaneously. This paradigm allows tasks
035   to share information and representations, which can enhance the generalization capabilities of the
036   model and improve performance across tasks (Baxter, 2000; Standley et al., 2020; Navon et al.,
037   2020). MTL is especially beneficial in scenarios where computational resources are limited, as it
038   reduces the need for separate models for each task. Its applications span a variety of domains, such
039
      as computer vision (Achituve et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2019), natural language
      processing (Chen et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2017; Pilault et al., 2020), and robotics (Devin et al.,
040
      2017; Xiong et al., 2023). Despite its advantages, MTL faces one major significant challenge: task
041
      imbalance, which describes the phenomenon where some tasks dominate the learning process while
042   others are under-optimized, leading to degraded overall performance. Overcoming this challenge
043   requires careful design of optimization strategies to ensure that all tasks benefit equally from the
044   shared model.
045
      To address these issues, previous works have focused on two primary approaches: loss-based and
046
      gradient-based methods (Liu et al., 2021b; Senushkin et al., 2023). Loss-based methods aggregate
047
      the losses of different tasks by adjusting the loss scales, then backpropagate the total loss to compute
048   gradients for shared parameters. These methods attempt to reflect the optimization status of each task
049   through their respective losses. However, since tasks often use different loss functions (e.g., cross-
050   entropy for classification and L1 loss for regression), their scales can differ significantly, making
051   it hard to compare or balance them directly. Various techniques have been proposed to normalize
052   these losses, such as linear scaling, logarithmic scaling, and polynomial scaling, but the fundamental
053   issue of differing loss magnitudes remains. On the other hand, gradient-based methods compute the
      gradient for each task separately and then aggregate these gradients using various algorithms to

054
      produce a final update. Although these methods ensure that the gradient of each task is considered,
055   relying on the gradients to represent optimization fairness can be misleading at certain stages of
056   the optimization process. For instance, the gradient approaches zero at a local minimum, while the
057   optimization state may still be suboptimal. Additionally, these methods typically aim to equalize
058   the task gradients at the shared layers (Chen et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2021b), but can not guarantee
059   the balance in the training progress because the difficulty of tasks may differ. Easier tasks may
060   converge quickly, while difficult tasks require more time to optimize (Guo et al., 2018). As a result,
061   considering only gradients can overlook differences in task difficulty, making it insufficient to ensure
062   balanced optimization across all tasks.
063   A clear illustration of the issues above can be observed in the widely used NYUv2 benchmark (Sil-
064   berman et al., 2012), which involves three tasks: segmentation, depth estimation, and surface normal
065   prediction. While recent MTL methods have demonstrated improvements over single-task learning
066   (STL), as evidenced by negative average performance drops ∆m (Navon et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
067   2024; Ban & Ji, 2024), their experiments show that these methods outperform STL primarily on the
068   segmentation and depth tasks. However, their performance on the surface normal task consistently
069
      lags behind STL, leading to a substantial variance in the performance drop across tasks. This vari-
      ance contradicts the original goal of MTL, which aims to achieve balanced optimization across all
070
      tasks. This key observation prompts us to rethink: Is information from loss-level and gradient-level
071
      metrics sufficient to represent fairness in multi-task optimization? In our view, performance-level
072   information should also be considered, and ∆m is a good choice for this purpose. On one hand,
073   ∆m serves as the final performance metric we ultimately compare, providing a direct and definitive
074   reflection of each task’s optimization status. On the other hand, ∆m for each task reflects its relative
075   performance drop compared to its respective STL baseline, leading to an invariant scale across tasks.
076   This property allows for a direct comparison of optimization progress and provides a guideline for
077   promoting fairness in the optimization process. While this observation may seem intuitive, it is
078   precisely the aspect that has been overlooked by most previous MTL methods.
079   Building upon the above motivation that considers performance-level fairness, we employ the per-
080   formance variance across tasks as an indicator and implement a dynamic weighting approach aimed
081   at progressively decreasing this performance variance. This enhances the generalization and robust-
082   ness of the shared representations, reducing excessive performance discrepancies between tasks. In
083   summary, our contributions can be outlined as follows:
084   1). We rethink the fairness representation in MTL optimization and suggest incorporating
085   performance-level information as a prior. Based on the common task imbalance issues observed
086   in the NYUv2 benchmark, we argue that loss-level and gradient-level information is insufficient to
087   capture fairness in MTL. Instead, performance-level information should be considered to reflect the
088   varying difficulty levels across different tasks.
089
      2). We propose PIVRG, a novel performance-informed variance reduction gradient aggrega-
090   tion approach. Specifically, we utilize the performance variance among tasks as a fairness indicator
091   and introduce a dynamic weighting strategy, which serves as a regularization mechanism balancing
092   the performance drop across different tasks during MTL. Both theoretical analysis and experimen-
093   tal results demonstrate that our method not only converges to the Pareto stationary point but also
094   achieves superior performance.
095
      3). Extensive experiments demonstrate that PIVRG achieves state-of-the-art performance
096   across various benchmarks. Notably, on the NYUv2 benchmark, unlike previous methods that
097   consistently underperform compared to STL on the surface normal task, PIVRG surpasses STL
098   across all three tasks and achieves the best overall performance drop. Moreover, on the Cityscapes
099   and CelebA benchmarks, PIVRG is the first to achieve a negative performance drop, meaning it
100   surpasses STL in average performance for the first time. On the more challenging QM9 benchmark,
101   PIVRG reduces the average performance drop by over 20%.
102   4). The proposed performance-informed dynamic weighting strategy is orthogonal to existing
103   approaches, making it possible to integrate with these methods. Experimental results demon-
104   strate that incorporating our dynamic weighting strategy not only significantly improves the overall
105   performance of these methods but also substantially reduces the performance variance across tasks,
106   leading to a more balanced optimization. This further validates the potential of our approach.

110
      Loss-based MTL approaches. These methods reweight task-specific losses with loss-level infor-
111
      mation. A key advantage of loss-based methods is their efficiency, as they only require backprop-
112   agation on the aggregated loss, reducing computational overhead compared to handling each task
113   individually (Liu et al., 2024). These approaches include operations on the scale of the loss, such as
114   Linear Scalarization (LS), which minimizes the sum of task losses, and Scale-Invariant (SI), which
115   reduces the sum of logarithmic losses. Additionally, various approaches for handling task weights
116   have been proposed, including using homoscedastic uncertainty weighting (Kendall et al., 2018b),
117   task prioritization (Guo et al., 2018), dynamic weight averaging (Liu et al., 2019), self-paced learn-
118   ing (Murugesan & Carbonell, 2017), geometric loss (Chennupati et al., 2019), random loss weight-
119   ing (Lin et al., 2021b), impartial loss weighting (Liu et al., 2021b) and fast adaptive optimization
120
      (Liu et al., 2024). Although loss-oriented methods are more computationally efficient, they often
      underperform gradient-oriented ones in most multi-task benchmarks.
121
122   Gradient-based MTL approaches. These methods address the task-balancing problem by fully
123   leveraging the gradient information of the shared network across different tasks. Several studies
124   have reported notable performance improvements using techniques such as Pareto optimal solutions
125   (Sener & Koltun, 2018), gradient normalization (Chen et al., 2018), projecting gradient conflicts
126
      (Yu et al., 2020a), gradient sign dropout (Chen et al., 2020), impartial gradient weighting (Liu et al.,
      2021b), conflict-averse gradients (Liu et al., 2021a), independent gradient alignment (Senushkin
127
      et al., 2023), and Bayesian uncertainty gradients (Achituve et al., 2024). Recent works by Navon
128
      et al. (2022) and Ban & Ji (2024) employ the Nash bargaining solution and fair resource allocation
129   respectively to address the gradient aggregation problem. Their utility functions are primarily based
130   on the first-order Taylor expansion of the loss, thereby incorporating loss-level information. Follow-
131   ing Navon et al. (2022) and Ban & Ji (2024), our proposed PIVRG method is also a gradient-based
132   approach. However, it not only incorporates loss-level information but also introduces higher-order
133   insights from the performance-level.

139   Pareto Optimality. Optimization in MTL can be understood as a specific instance of multi-objective
140   optimization (MOO). For a set of objective functions ℓ1 , · · · , ℓk , the quality of a solution x is deter-
141   mined by the vector of its corresponding objective values, i.e., (ℓ1 (x), · · · , ℓk (x)). A key character-
142   istic of MOO is the absence of a natural linear ordering for such vectors, meaning that solutions are
143   not always directly comparable, and thus no single optimal solution exists.
144   We define a solution x as dominating another solution x′ if it is strictly better in at least one ob-
145   jective while being no worse in all others. A solution that is not dominated by any other solution
146   is termed Pareto optimal, and the set of all such solutions forms the Pareto front. In the case of
147   non-convex problems, a solution is considered locally Pareto optimal if it is Pareto optimal within
148   a neighborhood around it. Furthermore, a solution is called Pareto stationary when there exists a
149   convex combination of the gradients at that point that equals zero, which is a necessary condition
150   for Pareto optimality.
151   Multi-Task Optimization Objectives. One of the most crucial distinctions between different MTL
152   methods lies in their choice of optimization objectives. A traditional approach is to minimize the
153   average loss across all tasks:

      We evaluate the proposed PIVRG on a variety of multi-task learning (MTL) problems under both su-
341   pervised learning and reinforcement learning settings to demonstrate its effectiveness. For multi-task
342   supervised learning, we validate on the Scene Understanding benchmarks NYUv2 (Silberman et al.,

348   that for the QM9 and CelebA benchmarks, which already have predefined validation sets, we use
349
      ∆m from the validation set to update w. For the NYUv2 and Cityscapes benchmarks, which lack
      validation sets, we use ∆m from the training set to update w to maintain consistency with other
350
      methods on the dataset. Moreover, we visualize the optimization process of PIVRG on a 2-task toy
351
      example (Liu et al., 2021a) in Fig.2 in the appendix.

503   4.4      I NTEGRATING P ERFORMANCE -I NFORMED W EIGHTING INTO E XISTING M ETHODS
504
505   Previous loss-based and gradient-based methods have often overlooked performance-level informa-
506   tion, leading to a lack of clarity regarding task difficulty during the training process. We propose
507   to integrate our performance-informed weighting strategy into these methods to enhance fairness in
508   optimization. Specifically, for loss-based approaches, we adjust the initial loss L = (ℓ1 , ℓ2 , · · · , ℓk )
509   using weights ω to reflect the current optimization progress of different tasks, replacing L with
510
      L′ = ω ⊙ L.
511   For gradient-based methods, since the motivation behind the aggregation algorithms varies, it is
512   necessary to analyze each method individually to incorporate ω into the design of the aggregation
513   process. For instance, Nash-MTL maximizes the sum of log utilities, we thus replace the original
514   equal summation (1, 1, · · · , 1) with a weighted sum (ω1 , ω2 , · · · , ωk ).
515   We apply the performance-informed weighting strategy to a series of MTL methods, including LS,
516   RLW (Lin et al., 2021a), DWA (Liu et al., 2019), UW (Kendall et al., 2018a), MGDA (Sener &
517   Koltun, 2018), and Nash-MTL (Navon et al., 2022), and evaluate their performance on the NYUv2
518   benchmark. Table 5 shows that incorporating performance-level information and integrating dy-
519   namic weighting can bring significant performance improvements for these methods. Var[∆mi ] is
520   also reduced, which indicates a notable alleviation of task imbalance.

523   5     C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
524
525
      In this paper, we propose PIVRG, a novel performance-informed variance reduction gradient aggre-
526
      gation approach. Building on the observation that previous loss-based and gradient-based methods
527
      exhibit common task imbalance across standard benchmarks, we point out the necessity of incorpo-
528   rating performance-level information to better represent fairness across tasks during the optimization
529   process. Specifically, we use performance variance across tasks as a fairness indicator and introduce
530   a dynamic weighting strategy aimed at gradually reducing this variance. Extensive experiments
531   show that PIVRG achieves state-of-the-art performance across various benchmarks. The experi-
532   mental results also show that incorporating our dynamic weighting strategy into existing loss-based
533   and gradient-based methods not only significantly improves overall performance but also reduces
534   performance variance across tasks, leading to a more balanced optimization process.
535   Limitations and Future Work. In this work, we regard performance variance across tasks as a
536   fairness indicator and design a dynamic weighting strategy to progressively reduce this variance.
537   However, there are numerous ways to incorporate performance-level information, and we would
538   like to explore more effective fairness indicators in our future work. Additionally, our underlying
539   optimization objective is not fixed, and future work may explore alternative designs and approaches
      to further enhance fairness and efficiency in multi-task learning.

       C.3   A DVANTAGES OF O UR M ETHOD
1135
1136   Through the analysis of the aforementioned methods, we found that since loss-based methods cannot
1137   obtain the accurate gradient for each task, they primarily achieve fairness at the loss level through
1138   various scaling and weighted averaging of the loss. A major idea of gradient-based methods is to
1139   alleviate gradient conflict during the optimization process to achieve fairness at the gradient level.
1140   Additionally, some gradient-based methods use the first-order Taylor expansion to design utility
1141   functions, approximating the loss difference with gi⊤ d, thereby incorporating loss-level information.
1142   However, only our proposed PIVRG considers performance-level information and uses the vari-
1143   ance of performance drop as a fairness indicator to redefine fairness in the optimization process
1144   of MTL. Extensive experiments demonstrate that PIVRG not only achieves state-of-the-art per-
1145   formance but also realizes further fair optimization, mitigating the common task imbalance phe-
1146   nomenon observed in previous methods. Integrating our dynamically designed weighting strategy
1147   based on performance-level information into existing methods can significantly enhance their perfor-
1148
       mance and reduce the variance of performance drop, achieving more equitable results. This further
       confirms the potential of our method and its contribution to the MTL community.
