009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Clustering is a challenging NP-hard problem. Polynomial approximations are of
012            paramount importance for identifying intriguing hidden representations of data at
013            reasonable execution times. In this work, we propose a novel clustering algorithm
014            called Thetan Berserker (TB). TB is a centroid-based clustering method controlled
015
               by a single distance parameter. TB revitalizes an old family of sequential algorithms,
               which are adored for their speed but are known to be order-sensitive. In addition,
016
               TB enables widely used algorithms such as KMeans and DBSCAN by improving
017
               their initial conditions. Theoretical aspects are provided in detail, along with
018            extensive comparisons and benchmarks. Examples of real world applications
019            are provided using publicly available data of different dimensionalities. A wide
020            range of performance boosts in clustering accuracy, memory usage, and runtime
021            are reported. By dramatically reducing clustering ambiguities while staying at
022            incredibly low complexity, TB creates a new standard for clustering.
023
024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Clustering has been the epitome of AI research for more than half a century because, when achieved,
027   it can infer the underlying structure of the data without any annotations, substantially improving
028   automation. It has a range of applications across the fields of science and medicine. For example,
029   collaborative filtering (Ungar & Foster, 1998), trend analysis (Aghabozorgi et al., 2015), LLMs
030   (Tirumala et al., 2023), computer vision (Caron et al., 2018), social networks (Mishra et al., 2007),
031
      biological data analysis (Zhao & Karypis, 2005) and signal processing (Orhan et al., 2011). In
      addition, inference based on clustering is of cardinal importance as it is often applied as the first step
032
      in ML pipelines. Information retrieved from clustering is fed into subsequent learning algorithms
033
      in many applications such as recommendation systems (Lu et al., 2015), medical analytics (Xu &
034   Wunsch, 2010), and detection of unexpected patterns (Agrawal & Agrawal, 2015).
035
036   At the same time, clustering still stands as an extremely hard algorithmic problem (NP-hard). Its
037
      main challenges include: a) dealing with clustering ambiguity, e.g. clusters mixing; b) tackling order
      sensitivity i.e. incorrect outputs that depend on data ordering; c) estimating the correct number of
038
      clusters; d) long execution times, e) major memory needs, f) large number of hyper-parameters and f)
039
      handling of outliers.
040
041   Our primary interest in proposing a new method stems from the fact that, in nature, we often have
042   some prior knowledge of the clusters we intend to find, such as the physical dimensions of atoms,
043
      cells, animals, etc. In such cases, it is much more useful to infer the number of clusters rather
      than setting a fixed limit on the number of clusters in the data. For this reason, we propose a new
044
      approach, Thetan Berseker (TB), which uses a single hyperparameter. Nonetheless, TB outperforms
045
      the state-of-the-art in accuracy, speed, and robustness in more than 30 experiments across dimensions
046   and domains.
047
048   2    R ELATED W ORK
049
050   Given the wide range of applications, the problem of clustering has been tackled using different
051   approaches such as dimensionality reduction, density estimation, probabilistic methods, spectral
052   methods, and distance-based techniques. Among all these methods, distance-based techniques have
053   been used extensively. Distance-based methods can be separated in four categories. Those that
      cluster with assumptions concerning a) the maximum number of clusters ( KMeans (Lloyd, 1982),

      KMeans++ (Arthur & Vassilvitskii, 2007)), Bisecting KMeans (Steinbach & Karypis, 2000; Di &
055   Gou, 2018), b) a distance threshold (Hierarchical Clustering (HC) (Murtagh & Legendre, 2014)),
056   c) cluster density (MeanShift (Comaniciu & Meer, 2002), DBSCAN (Ester et al., 1996; Schubert
057   et al., 2017)) or d) any combination of the above (HDBSCAN (Campello et al., 2013; McInnes &
058   Healy, 2017)). Another separation can be due to the type of problems that they can try to solve:
059   a) Linearly-separable (KMeans), b) Nonlinearly-separable (DBSCAN), or c) both (Hierarchical
060   Clustering). A third divide can be due to the way they process the data: a) sequentially (process each
061   sample as they arrive) or b) offline (process the entire dataset).
062   Researchers have worked to figure out distance-based clustering for linearly separable problems
063   for more than 60 years. The idea that we may be able to approximate clustering solutions started
064   getting attention in the 1950s (due to KMeans) and HC in the 1960s. A few decades after sequential
065   algorithms such the Leader Algorithm (Rush & Russell, 1988), BIRCH (Zhang et al., 1996), BSAS
066   (Theodoridis & Koutroumbas, 2006), MBSAS, TTSAS (Real et al., 2014) and SL (Patra et al., 2011)
067   appeared. In addition, some of these methods, such as QuickBundles (Garyfallidis et al., 2012), were
068   successful in specialized domains but not used widely. The reason is that the results of these methods
069
      depend heavily on the order of sampling, and therefore results can change drastically from one run to
      the other. This is known as the ordering problem or order sensitivity. This is not an issue with only
070
      sequential algorithms. Many others, including KMeans, suffer from this problem.
071
072   Thetan Berserker (TB) is introduced here to dramatically reduce this problem while sustaining low
073   complexity at the expense of a single parameter θ. In contrast, BSAS has two parameters, KMeans,
074   and MeanShift has three, etc.
075   In this paper, the focus will be primarily on TB tackling linearly separable problems, but TBSCAN
076   will also be introduced, which can tackle nonlinear problems. TB will be challenged and compared
077   across 30 experiments and more than 20 methods.

079   3    T HETAN B ERSERKER
080
081   The name Thetan Berserker (TB) is derived from the way the algorithm works with some inspiration
082   from history. Thetan stems from the algorithm’s single distance threshold θ. Berserkers, in the
083   context of this algorithm, are cluster modes that compete for spatial territory in data space.
084   TB’s foundation stone is Thetan Sequential (TS), a straightforward sequential clustering approach.
085   See Alg. 1. TS is in simple terms a simplified version of a basic sequential scheme. In short, TS will
086   visit each data point only once and if a distance metric between a sample and the centroid is less than
087   a threshold θ will enter the same cluster otherwise it will create a new cluster.
088   TS has multiple advantages: a) single pass - examining each feature only once, b) low time com-
089   plexity, c) use of a single hyper-parameter, d) minimal memory footprint in contrast, for example,
090   to Hierarchical Clustering, e) online execution - ideal for asynchronous or streaming systems and f)
091   easy implementation. However, a major disadvantage of TS is the lack of stability when the order of
092   selection changes in datasets with underlying dense clusters (manifestation of the ordering problem).

      We postulate the following theorems assuming L2 is our norm of choice. First, let’s identify which
167
      datasets would be exactly satisfied (reach a unique and global solution) by Alg. 1.

169   Theorem 1. Given a clustering problem C with clusters c1 , c2 , . . . , cK . If there are no pairs of
170   samples xi , xj from ci to cj , i ̸= j where xi ∈ ci and xj ∈ cj , that have a distance < θ, then Alg. 1
171   will never mix clusters with a threshold parameter θ at exactly one pass. The order of the selection of
172
      the samples will have no effect on the final outcome as long as all features are used.
173   Proof. In Alg. 1. all the distances are computed either inside a cluster or between clusters. All the
174   inside cluster distances will be < θ, and all the distances between clusters will be > θ. Therefore,
175   there are no cases where clusters are created between the actual clusters given that there are no pairs
176   of samples xi , xj from cluster i to cluster j that have a distance < θ.                             ■
177   This brings us to an equivalence relationship between how the algorithm performs and how close the
178   hypothetical boundaries of the underlying clusters are.

192   Lemma 1 If there are no pairs of samples xi , xj from cluster ci to cj , i ̸= j where xi ∈ ci and
193   xj ∈ cj that have a distance l < θ, then due to Theorem 1 the order of selection will not affect the
194   result. However, this also tells us that if the L2 diameter of the circumscribed hypersphere is < θ
195   then also the shape of the clusters will not affect the results. In other words, if the contours of the
196   clusters are convex or non-convex, the outcome will be the same.
197   Proof Imagine two clusters c1 and c2 bounded (circumscribed) in hyperspheres of diameters d1 and
198   d2 . Assume that d2 > d1 (one hypersphere is larger than the other one). The clusters can contain any
199   shape of points x, convex or non-convex. The minimum distance between c1 and c2 is denoted with
200   l. The optimal result would be if Alg. 1 could exactly find the two clusters. Anything more or less
201   than two would be incorrect. Using proof by cases we can see that if l < d1 , d2 , and θ > d1 , θ > d2
202   and θ > l. Alg. 1 will not generate two clusters missing the global solution. The same would happen
203   if l > d1 , d2 and θ < l. However, if l > d1 , d2 , and θ = l then Alg. 1 is guaranteed to find the two
204
      clusters. This is because all distances between points will be less than threshold θ only in the same
      clusters. Similarly, we would reach a global solution if l > d1 , d2 and θ < l but θ > d1 , d2 . In short,
205
      we now have the following condition where l > θ > d2 > d1 . In this condition, there is no point x
206
      that can be assigned to the wrong cluster.                                                             ■.

      data as their centroids increases empty space (a proxy for sparsity).

225   Proof Given that the centroids are local averages in an ideal scenario, they would exactly approximate

237   Theorem 2 Starting with the centroids of Alg. 1 helps Alg. 2 reach an improved solution (less
238   incorrectly assigned samples).
239   Proof We will show this for two uniform distributions at distance l (see visual guide at Fig. 2B).
240   The sides of each distribution are equal to θ. Let’s assume that l < θ. A selection order where
241   the first points selected were x1 , x2 and x3 (close to the edges of the two clusters) is guaranteed
242   to create unnecessary extra clusters. This is because Alg. 1 will create a new centroid at µ =
243   (x1 + x2 + x3 )/3. However, if we start with c1 , c2 and c3 Alg. 1 is forced to create two enduring
244   centroids µ1 = (c1 + c2 )/2 or µ2 = (c2 + c3 )/2. Note that the order of selection of centroids (123,
245   213, or 312) does not change the outcome.                                                          ■
246   This generalizes for an arbitrary number of clusters. See details in sections A.10-A.12. Due to
247   Theorems 1-2 and Lemmas 1-2, TB is adding a new constraint that TS cannot satisfy. The constraint
248   is ∥µi − µi ∥ > θ. Centroids will be far from each other, providing better coverage and a reduced
249   number of clusters.
250   Complexity analysis. Alg. 1 has a worst-case time complexity (upper bound) O(N KD) that depends
251   on the number of samples N and the number of estimated clusters K and number of dimensions D.
252   We assume here that most of the computation is from the calculation of distances between samples
253   and centroids. The worst-case complexity takes place when every data point belongs to a different
254   cluster (all singleton clusters). In such an event, the worst time complexity is O(DN 2 ). The best time
255   complexity is O(N D) when only one cluster. Assuming most memory is spent on saving centroids
256   and labels, Alg. 1 has a best case (lower bound) space complexity of O(N ) and worst case (upper
257   bound) of O(N ). The highest bound is when all clusters are singleton clusters. However, in most
258
      cases K << N . Therefore, TS requires a remarkably small amount of memory. TB (Alg. 2) builds
      on top of TS (Alg. 1), but now the time complexity also depends on the number of iterations I.
259
      However, everywhere in this work, we fixed I = 2. Therefore, Alg. 2 is of the same complexity as
260
      Alg. 1. In addition, TB’s cleanup operations work in the space of centroids or small label updates.
261   Therefore, they do not change the order of complexity. In short, TB’s time and space complexity is
262   the same as that of TS. This is also experimentally shown in Tab. 1. Proofs are available at A.8 and

513   7     D ISCUSSION
514
515   In this work, a new algorithmic set around Thetan Berserker (TB) was introduced. TB demonstrates
516
      an important advantage in terms of both speed and memory use. In addition, TB shows an increase
      in accuracy across many evaluation metrics, simulations, benchmarks, and real data experiments.
517
      Another surprising fact about TB is that it is able to provide highly accurate low-level segmentation.
518
      This is indeed surprising because we simply provided the location and intensity of each pixel as input.
519   Usually, superpixel algorithms are specifically tailored for superpixel problems and highly optimized
520   for that specific purpose. However, TB does not have this requirement nor was it built for that task
521   in mind. Nonetheless, it generates useful superpixels at acceptable times. More importantly, the
522   results match well the underlying images simplifying the underlying representations in a meaningful
523   manner. Similarly, we achieve great overall segmentations for 3D imaging data. Both types of data
524   are remarkably hard to process. Nonetheless, TB identifies the relevant structures in the data without
525   compromising edges, which achieves an outstanding compression ratio (see Fig. 7). However, TB
526   is also anticipated to struggle with the curse of dimensionality as any other clustering method. We
527
      were happy to report results in grouping text embedding of up to 1024 dimensions. TB is not bound
      by density limits in the same way that MeanShift, HDBSCAN, and DBSCAN do, as distributions
528
      become sparser when dimensions increase. It is also not bound to be affected by outliers as KMeans.
529
      TB outperformed KMeans++ seeding. TB is still aimed for linearly separable clusters and it will not
530   perform well on nonlinearly separable clustering problems. In these cases, TBSCAN should be used
531   instead (see Tab. A3). The proposed approach is general purpose and can be used across data science.
532   More importantly, it provides some theoretical ground for the AI community to rethink and rework
533   unsupervised learning. The code will be available on GitHub upon acceptance for publication.
534
535   8     C ONCLUSION
536   Thetan Berserker (TB) is as uniquely accurate as a fast clustering algorithm. In addition, TB
537   enables widely used algorithms by improving their conditioning and initialization. Important real-
538   world applications were demonstrated, such as the segmentation of natural images, edge-preserving
539   compression of magnetic resonance data, and grouping of text embeddings. TB outperformed more
      than 20 methods across 30 experiments.

758   A.1    A PPARENT C ENTROID METRIC
759
760   In this work, Apparent Centroid distance (AC) was introduced as a strict criterion to evaluate
761
      clustering results using the predicted centroids.
762   The metric is calculated as follows. The complete distance matrix between all estimated and ground
763   truth centroids is created. We count the number of centroids close to the ground truth as being less
764   than a pre-specified threshold. AC is bounded between 0 and 1. The recommended threshold is at
765   10% of the circumscribed radius of size θ. 0.1 is used for all relevant experiments. AC drops quickly
766   when even a small number of incorrect centroids are found. This is not the case with other metrics
767
      that were reported. This allows a quantifiable identification of issues that are rather easy for humans
      but hard for other metrics to catch. See Fig. A4-A15 and corresponding Tab. 1.

       A.5    S IGNAL P ROCESSING OF TIME SERIES
1027
1028   The ability of TB to identify patterns in one-dimensional signals that change over time is demonstrated
1029   here. For this purpose, common signals such as sine, pulses, or combined sine and pulse signals are
1030   used. In addition, real publicly available PTB-XL Electrocardiography (ECG) datasets (Wagner et al.,
1031   2020) are used. Fig. A16 to A23, show results obtained using TB. For Figs. A16 to A18, a small
1032   sliding window of 3, 5, and 3 is used, respectively. This sliding window becomes TB’s feature space.
1033   The signal is normalized so that the two axes are relevant. Therefore, we move from a 1D signal to a
1034
       2D array X where the number of rows is as many as the samples of the signal and the number of
       columns is the size of the sliding window. TB is then applied to this 2D array with θ values of 90,
1035
       130, and 95, respectively. Note that TB is identifying accurately the patterns in the signal. The colors
1036
       correspond to different clustering labels.
1037
1038   For ECG Signals, two types of pre-processing were performed. In the first type of pre-processing, a
1039   Gaussian filter was used with σ = 9 (see Fig. A19), σ = 6 (see Fig. A20) and σ = 11 (see Fig. A21).
1040
       Sliding windows of sizes 3, 7, and 3 were used, respectively. In the second type of pre-processing,
       i.e., for Fig. A22 and Fig. A23, the entire dataset is used along with time as the feature space. Sliding
1041
       windows and Gaussian filter were not used in this method. The θ values that were used are 1, 6, 26,
1042
       90, and 50, respectively. The signal was normalized in both cases by scaling the data by 100. As
1043   shown in Fig. A16 to A23, TB with L2 is able to identify the patterns in the signal without needing
1044   to calculate the derivatives (Duan & Guo, 2022). In the case of the PTB-XL ECG dataset, TB is able
1045   to identify the QRS and T-wave together (Fig. A19) or as QR, RS, and T-wave separately (Fig. A20)
1046   or as QRS and T-wave separately (Fig. A21) (Maglaveras et al., 1998). Alternatively, the full cardiac
1047   cycles are also captured as discrete groups (see Fig. A22 and Fig. A23).

1734   An algorithm is said to be stochastic if it incorporates randomness in its process or decision-making.

1736   given the same input. More importantly, stochastic algorithms use random variables or probabilistic
1737   components as part of their logic. Thetan Berseker (TB) arrives at similar conclusions given the
1738   random orderings of the data samples. In addition, TB employs randomness to avoid repeating the
