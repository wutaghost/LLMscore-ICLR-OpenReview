                                                  A BSTRACT
009
               The k-means of a given set S ⊆ Rd of n segments is a set X ⊆ Rd of
010
               |X| = k centers which minimizes their sum of squared distances D(S, X) :=
011            P
                  S∈S minx∈X D(S, x). Here, the distance        D(S, x) between a segment S and a
012                                                    R
               point x is the integral of its distances s∈S ∥p − x∥ over each point on the segment.
013            More generally, the farthest m input points (outliers) may be ignored, other distance
014            functions may be used, such as M-estimator or non-squared, and each distance
015            may be multiplied by a function that depends on the size of its cluster, say, to
016            obtain balanced clustering. For a given ϵ > 0, an ϵ-coreset C ⊆ S for all these
017            problems is a weighted subset C ⊂ S, that approximates D(S, X) up to 1 ± ϵ
018            multiplicative factor for every set X ⊆ Rd of (possibly weighted) k centers. Such a
019            coreset enables handling streaming, big, distributed input in parallel using existing
020            techniques. We suggest the first coreset construction that, with high probability,
021
               returns an ϵ-coreset C for any input    set S of segments. For constant k, ϵ, the size
022
               of the coreset is |C| ∈ O log2 (n) and is computed in time O(nd). Experimental
               results and real-time video tracking application demonstrate the applicability of our
023
               algorithm, the latter demonstrates that our method supports vectorized segments.

026   1     I NTRODUCTION
027
028   This work aims to provide novel compression and approximation to segment clustering. This task is
029   of significant use for various applications as demonstrated in our experimental results at Section 3.
030
031   1.1   S EGMENT CLUSTERING
032
033   To define the problem considered we utilize the following definitions and notations.
034   Definition 1.1. Let u, v be vectors in Rd . A segment is a function ℓ : [0, 1] → Rd that is defined
035   by ℓ(x) = u + vx, for every x ∈ [0, 1]. For every pair (p, p′ ) ∈ Rd × Rd of points, let D(p, p′ ) :=
036   ∥p − p′ ∥2 denote the Euclidean distance between p and p′ .

048   1.2   C ORESETS
049
050   Informally, for an input set L that consists of segments, a coreset C is a data structure that approxi-
051   mates the loss ℓ L, (Q, w) for every weighted set (Q, w) of size k, up to a multiplicative factor of
052   1 ± ε, in time that depends only on |C|. Hence, ideally, C is also much smaller than the original
053   input L. A coreset C is thus efficient if C is much smaller than the original input P . For a detailed
      explanation on the motivation for coreset construction see "Why coresets?" in (Feldman, 2020).

068   Coreset for segments. Fortunately, the segment clustering problem stated in Section 2.1 has a small
069   coreset which is a weighted subset from the input points. Often the goal is to have a coreset that is
070
      a weighted subset of the input, with few exceptions, e.g., (Jubran et al., 2021) and (Rosman et al.,
      2014), for which no coreset is a weighted subset of the input, which is similar to our coreset that is a
071
      weighted set of points and not segments (that are the input).
072
073   However, since there is a significant amount of work on fitting weighted centers to weighted points,
074   for example, see (Feldman & Schulman, 2012; Arthur & Vassilvitskii, 2007), and on the other
075   hand we are not aware of similar works that consider the problem of fitting points to segments or
076
      even involving the non-discrete integrals, in our case a coreset that is a weighted set of points is a
      significantly more usable result that enables us to utilize the significant amount of work on fitting
077
      weighted centers to weighted points.
078
079   Coreset for convex shapes and hyper-plane fitting. Convex shapes can be considered as a union
080   (of infinite size) of segments. This enables us to compute a small coreset also for the case where
081   the input is n convex shapes, as done in Algorithm 3. Moreover, since we consider the problem of
082
      fitting weighted centers the generalization for hyper-plane fitting immediately follows, as previously
      mentioned at (Feldman & Schulman, 2012).

086   1.3   N OVELTY
087
088   Our main contribution is in the provable reduction (or intermediate coreset) of the problem of segment
089   clustering to the problem of point clustering, from which provable coreset construction is immediate
090   via utilizing previous work such as (Feldman & Schulman, 2012) or (Bachem et al., 2018).
091   Our result can be seen as a generalization of various previous works, whose subset is given as follows.

094

095

480   4    F UTURE WORK AND CONCLUSION
481
482   This paper provides a coreset construction that gets a set of segments in Rd and returns a small
483   weighted set of points (coreset) that approximates its sum of (fitting) distances to any weighted
484   k-centers, up to a factor of 1 ± ϵ. This was done by a reduction to the problem of problem of fitting
485   k-centers to segments to the problem of fitting k-centers to points. In the experimental section, this
      reduction allowed us to utilize the extensive literature on k-means to obtain an efficient approximation.


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      While we have not analyzed the variants of the problem where there is an additional constraint
487   that each segment should be assigned to a single center, we believe that this would follow from
488   incorporating our result with Section 15.2 of (Feldman & Langberg, 2011). We have not considered
489   this direction since our method is based on reducing the segments to a set of points and applying the
490   classic k-means clustering, which can be computed efficiently via (Arthur & Vassilvitskii, 2007), and
491   even after compression this variation on Problem 1 yields a complex set clustering problem.
492
      Our results support loss functions where the integral in equation 1 at Problem 1 is not necessarily
493   elementary, and as such direct minimization seems to us unfeasible.

1370
       G.1    P RELIMINARIES ON VIDEO TRACKING AND DETECTION
1371
1372   The problem of tracking objects in RGB videos is a well-studied problem for which numerous
1373   heuristics using various approaches were proposed. A meta-survey on such approaches (Zou et al.,
1374   2019) states that in recent years there were thousands of papers published on this subject. One of the
1375   very prominent approaches is utilizing neural networks. While neural networks yield unprecedented
1376   results, such improvements come at the price of training, which along with the labeling of data is
1377   rather costly. Another challenge is the cost of utilizing the results after the training, which frequently
1378
       requires at least mid-level GPUs ability to achieve 30-fps (frames per second) in real-time. Moreover,
       recently (Su et al., 2019) demonstrated the problem where even small changes in the data may “fool”
1379
       the network. We note that while there were works on addressing similar problems in recent years
1380
       using more sophisticated training, see for example (Chen et al., 2022), it is uncertain whether more
1381   sophisticated “attacks” could cause such or similar problems to what is shown in (Su et al., 2019).
1382   We note that given object detection, which is not required for our method, there are existing real-time
1383   methods, such as (Comaniciu et al., 2000).
1384
       Our proposed method for video tracking and detection utilizes motion vectors as detailed.
1385
1386
1387   G.2    M OTION VECTORS
1388
       Motion vectors are computed in real-time as part of existing encoders for videos, such as H.264
1389   ((Wiegand et al., 2003)), H.265 ((Pereira et al., 2002)), etc. In general, those are mapping from one
1390   frame to another, intending to usually minimize some loss function, such as Mean Squared Error
1391   (MSE) between the RGB values, to allow keeping only this mapping (as a vector) and the difference
1392   between the blocks.
1393
       We consider the rather simple case where the mapping is from a frame to its previous frame. For a
1394
       more detailed explanation on motion vectors and their computation, see (Wiegand et al., 2003).
1395
1396   We illustrate the motion vectors concept in Figure 6.
1397   Note that since the computation utilizes only the motion vectors, and not the RGB part of the image,
1398   this method also allows privacy preservation to some degree while providing real-time object tracking.
