009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Hiding data in deep neural networks (DNNs) has achieved remarkable successes,
012
               including both discriminative and generative models. Yet, the potential for hiding
013
               images in diffusion models remains underdeveloped. Existing approaches fall
014            short in extracting fidelity, secrecy, and efficiency. In particular, the intensive
015            computational demands of the hiding process, coupled with the slow extraction
016            due to multiple denoising stages, make these methods impractical for resource-
017            limited environments. To address these challenges, we propose hiding images
018            at a specific denoising stage in diffusion models by modifying the learned score
019            functions. We also introduce a parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) approach
020            that combines parameter selection with a variant of low-rank adaptation (LoRA)
021            to boost secrecy and hiding efficiency. Comprehensive experiments demonstrate
022
               the effectiveness of our proposed method.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
028
      Data hiding involves concealing secret messages within various forms of cover media, such as bit
      streams (Cox et al., 2007), texts (Jassim, 2013), audios (Li & Yu, 2000), images (Baluja, 2019),
029
      videos (Swanson et al., 1997), and neural networks (Adi et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2020). The goal
030
      is to minimize distortion to the cover media while ensuring the accurate retrieval of the hidden mes-
031   sages. Much like other areas in signal and image processing, data hiding has seen notable progress,
032   especially with the integration of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs). Typically, DNN-based meth-
033   ods employ an autoencoder architecture, where the encoding network embeds the secret message
034   into some cover media and the decoding network retrieves it. However, this scheme of hiding data
035   with DNNs presents several limitations. First, the need to transmit the decoding network through a
036   secure subliminal channel diminishes its practicality. Second, current DNN-based detection meth-
037   ods (Boroumand et al., 2018; You et al., 2020) have a good chance of detecting the presence of
038   hidden data, undermining its secrecy. Last, hiding multiple secret messages for different recipi-
039
      ents using the same encoding and decoding networks has proven challenging, thereby limiting its
      flexibility.
040
041   In stark contrast, the paradigm of hiding data in DNNs (Liu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021a;b;
042   Chen et al., 2022; Fei et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024) has immediately improved secrecy due to
043   the lack of detection methods taking DNN weights as input. Additionally, it has eliminated the
044   need for sharing a decoding network between the sender and receiver with enhanced practicality.
045
      Initially, the focus was primarily on hiding data in discriminative models (Liu et al., 2020; Wang
      et al., 2021a;b), but there has been a shift towards using generative models for this purpose as their
046
      utility has become more recognized. Certain existing approaches (Chen et al., 2022; Fei et al.,
047
      2022; Zhang et al., 2024) have effectively concealed images in GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014).
048   However, these techniques are insufficient and, in some cases, infeasible for diffusion models (Song
049   & Ermon, 2019; 2020; Song et al., 2021; Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Karras et al.,
050   2022), primarily due to two reasons: 1) The neural network in diffusion models learns the score
051   function (Song et al., 2020) of the data distribution, which differs from the neural network generator
052   in GANs that learns to generate data samples directly, 2) The generative process in diffusion models
053   entails multi-step denoising utilizing a shared neural network, leading to a distinct training scheme
      that is more intricate and time-consuming compared to training GANs.

054
      To address these challenges, recent approaches (Zhao et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023; Xiong et al.,
055   2023; Chou et al., 2024; Fernandez et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2024) have been proposed for hiding
056   data in diffusion models. However, these methods have their limitations. Some (Zhao et al., 2023;
057   Peng et al., 2023) require computationally intensive re-training of diffusion models, making them
058   unfeasible in resource-constrained environments. Others (Xiong et al., 2023; Chou et al., 2024;
059   Fernandez et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2024) use fine-tuning to reduce computational costs but still
060   suffer from limited hiding capacity and efficiency. Additionally, they are exclusively applicable to
061   latent diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022), due to the necessity of modifying text input for text-
062   to-image synthesis (Chou et al., 2024), latent representations produced by latent encoder(Feng et al.,
063
      2024), or latent decoder(Xiong et al., 2023; Fernandez et al., 2023).
064   This paper introduces a new approach for hiding images in diffusion models by modifying the
065   learned score function, distinct from previous methods, focusing on a secret time step in the genera-
066   tive process (refer to Fig. 1), which offers increased flexibility, security, and efficiency. We introduce
067   a secrecy loss that regulates performance degradation in the original generation task, employing a
068   distillation framework with a pre-trained diffusion model as a reference model, as shown in Fig. 2b.
069
      Furthermore, we construct a hybrid parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) method by combining
      selective PEFT techniques (Han et al., 2024) with parameterized PEFT techniques (Kalajdzievski,
070
      2023; Hayou et al., 2024), largely reducing the number of modified parameters and accelerating the
071
      hiding process. Experiments demonstrate the proposed method yields: 1) high fidelity, extracting
072   the secret images with minimal distortion, 2) high secrecy, as the modified diffusion model behaves
073   normally, 3) high efficiency, significantly reducing the time cost for hiding images in diffusion mod-
074   els, compared to existing methods, and 4) high flexibility, enabling the hiding multiple images for
075   different receivers.

      2    R ELATED W ORK

      In this section, we provide a review of the pertinent research fields related to this paper, encompass-
080
      ing neural network steganography, diffusion models, and parameter-efficient fine-tuning.
081
082
      Neural Network Steganography (NNS). Hiding data in neural networks can be applied in
083
      NNS (Song et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021a;b; Chen et al., 2022), which involves
084
      concealing a secret message within a neural network for covert communication, striking a balance
085   between extraction accuracy and secrecy. Various existing NNS methods employ diverse strategies
086   to embed confidential information in the neural network. These strategies include replacing the least
087   significant bits (LSB) of model parameters (Song et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2020), replacing selected
088   redundant parameters (Liu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021a), mapping the values of model parame-
089   ters to secret message (Song et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021b), mapping the signs
090   of model parameters to secret bits string (Song et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2020), memorizing arbitrarily
091   labeled synthetic data whose labels encode secret information (Song et al., 2017), and concealing
092   secret image in neural network-based probabilistic models (Chen et al., 2022). However, NNS with
093
      diffusion models has been relatively underexplored. This paper investigates the viability of diffusion
      model-based NNS by introducing an image-hiding method for diffusion models.
094
095
096
      Diffusion Models. Diffusion models employ a manageable forward corruption process that can
      be reversed to generate data. The reverse process is learned by diffusion models, eliminating the
097
      need for carefully designed network architectures. UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015) and Trans-
098
      former (Vaswani et al., 2017) are commonly employed architectures for diffusion models. Diffusion
099   models can be categorized into pixel-space diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021;
100   Karras et al., 2022) and latent-space diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Peebles & Xie, 2023;
101   Podell et al., 2024), depending on whether the diffusion process and its reverse apply to the pixels or
102   the latent representations of the image. This paper specifically concentrates on concealing images
103   in pixel-space diffusion models, particularly the Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM)
104   with UNet architecture, as an illustrative example.
105
106   Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT). Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods can
107   be divided into reparameterized and selective fine-tuning (Han et al., 2024). Reparameterized fine-
      tuning introduces additional trainable parameters into the frozen pre-trained backbone. Low-rank

      3.2     I MAGE H IDING S CENARIO
172
173   Three entities are involved in the image-hiding scenario:
174
175           • The sender, who hides images within a pre-trained diffusion model and transmits the stego
176             diffusion model.
177           • The receiver, who possesses the secret key to extract the hidden images from the received
178             stego diffusion model.
179
              • The inspector, who inspects the stego diffusion model before it reaches the receiver, using
180             commonly used metrics to determine if the diffusion model meets expectations. If the stego
181             diffusion model fails to meet the expectation, it will be considered suspicious and deleted.
182
183   In the given scenario, an image-hiding method should ensure: 1) The extracted secret image should
184   closely resemble the ground truth secret image. 2) The generation performance of stego diffusion
185   model should be similar to that of the original diffusion model.
186
187   3.3     P ROPOSED M ETHOD
188
189
      Previous studies (Zhao et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023) modify the diffusion model to enable a guided
      reverse process, where the secret image is generated step by step using a predefined secret key
190
      (e.g., a secret pattern blended with Gaussian noise), refer to Fig. 1a and Fig. 2a. However, such
191
      approaches result in inferior secret image reconstruction and notably reduce the original generation
192   performance of diffusion models, particularly when the secret image is a natural image rather than
193   a simple icon. Moreover, constructing a guided reverse process with a large number of steps results
194   in slow extraction and hiding processes, resembling the standard inference and training processes of
195   diffusion models.
196
      Motivated by recent studies (Liu et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023; Sauer et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023)
197
      that aim to accelerate the inference of diffusion models by distilling a one-step diffusion model from
198   a pre-trained diffusion model, we propose hiding/extracting images in/from one secret time step of
199   the denoising process. Our proposed hiding scheme allows the secret image to be concealed in an
200   arbitrarily chosen secret time step, which serves as the component of the secret key along with the
201   predefined fixed noise for secret image extraction, as shown in Fig 1b and Fig 2b.
202

      3.3.2   PEFT M ETHOD
275
276   To enhance the efficiency of the hiding process and ensure better preservation of secrecy by mod-
277   ifying fewer model parameters, we propose a hybrid PEFT algorithm that combines the strengths
278   of selective and reparameterized PEFT techniques. The proposed PEFT algorithm consists of three
279   steps: computing parameter-level sensitivity, selecting the sensitive layers, and applying reparame-
280   terized PEFT to the sensitive layers.
281
282   Parameter Sensitivity. The importance of parameters in a pre-trained neural network for a spe-
283   cific task can be indicated by the parameter sensitivity s, which is defined as the squared gradient of
284   the loss function ℓ with respect to its parameter (He et al., 2023):
285                                                  si = gi2 ,                                         (11)
286
287   where i is the parameter index, si is the i-th element of s, gi is the i-th element of gradients g =
288   ∂ℓ/∂θ. We focus on parameter sensitivity because the highest sensitivity indicates the direction of
289   the fastest convergence of the loss function, thereby aiding efficient gradient descent during fine-
290
      tuning. To obtain accurate parameter sensitivity, s is accumulated over M iterations before fine-
      tuning, to create the accumulated sensitivity S:
291
292                                                    M

296

297   is a hyperparameter indicating the sparsity of the sensitive parameters. Finally, a set of sensitive
298   parameters, denoted as θs , is obtained using the indices of the top-τ largest elements from S.

306   PEFT method does not eliminate the need to calculate the gradients of all parameters in θ, resulting
307   in computational costs identical to those of full fine-tuning. To improve the fine-tuning efficiency,
308   LoRA can be applied to selected layers of the neural network. By updating only the LoRA parame-

311   Sensitive Layers Selection. To determine the most appropriate layers to fine-tune, we select layers
312
      based on parameter sensitivity, referred to as sensitive layers. Specifically, given a set of sensitive
      parameters θs , we analyze the distribution of sensitive parameters across each layer of the pre-
313
      trained neural network. We define the sensitive layers as the top-n layers that contain the highest
314
      number of sensitive parameters, where n = δK, K is the total number of layers in the pre-trained
315   neural network, and δ ∈ [0, 1] is a hyperparameter indicating the sparsity of sensitive layers. Once
316   the sensitive layers are identified, we can proceed to apply LoRA to these selected layers.
317
318   LoRA-based PEFT. While numerous variants of LoRA techniques exist, offering improved effi-
319   ciency over the basic approach, our method utilizes two variants of LoRA, namely rsLoRA (Kala-
320   jdzievski, 2023) and LoRA+ (Hayou et al., 2024), to achieve faster convergence speed. It is impor-
321   tant to note that the sensitive layers in the UNet architecture of the diffusion models can encompass
322   both linear layers and convolutional layers. Since standard LoRA-based techniques are restricted to
323   linear layers, we employ the LoCon method from the LyCORIS library (Yeh et al., 2023), which
      extends the standard LoRA for convolutional layers.

353   4.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
354
355
      Model and Dataset. We conduct experiments using DDPM, although our approach can be easily
      applied to other categories of diffusion models such as EDM (Karras et al., 2022) and consistency
356
      models (Song et al., 2023). The architecture and hyperparameters of DDPM follow the specification
357
      of Ho et al. (2020). The default secret time step ts for hiding/extracting the secret image is 500. For
358   better demonstration, we not only hide low-resolution (32 × 32) images in the DDPM pre-trained
359   on CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2009) but also hide high-resolution images (256 × 256) in the

532   6     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In conclusion, this research addresses the limitations of current methods for hiding images in diffu-
535   sion models by introducing a new approach that hides images at a secret time step in the denoising
536   process, leveraging a hybrid PEFT method that combines the advantages of selective and repa-
537   rameterized PEFT techniques to improve hiding efficiency. Extensive analyses and experiments
538   demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach in terms of fidelity, secrecy, and hiding effi-
539   ciency. Future work could explore potential enhancements and applications of our approach in more
      advanced multi-modal diffusion models.
