009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011              Shape completion, a crucial task in 3D computer vision, involves predicting and
012              filling the missing regions of scanned or partially observed objects. Current meth-
013              ods often suffer from orientation-dependent inconsistencies, particularly under
014              varying rotations, limiting their real-world applicability. We introduce ESCAPE
015              (Equivariant Shape Completion via Anchor Point Encoding), a novel framework
016              designed to achieve rotation-equivariant shape completion. Our approach employs
017              a distinctive encoding strategy, representing objects by selecting anchor points and
018              utilizing them in a distance-based encoder akin to the D2 shape distribution. This
019
                 enables the model to capture a consistent, rotation-equivariant understanding of the
                 object’s geometry. ESCAPE leverages a transformer architecture to encode and
020
                 decode the distance transformations, ensuring that generated shape completions
021
                 remain accurate and equivariant under rotational transformations. Additionally, we
022              perform optimization to refine the predicted shapes from anchor point positions and
023              predicted encodings, Experimental evaluations demonstrate that ESCAPE achieves
024              robust, high-quality reconstructions across arbitrary rotations and translations,
025              showcasing its effectiveness in real-world applications.

047   1   I NTRODUCTION
048
049   3D perception has been dependent on canonical orientations, causing significant challenges in
050   dynamic environments, such as robotic manipulation or real-time object recognition, where objects
051   interact from varying viewpoints. Traditional methods, like voxel-based approaches and early
052   point cloud networks such as PointNet(21) and PointNet++(22), have laid a strong foundation but
053   cannot inherently handle rotational variance. Recent methods have introduced improvements by
      leveraging attention mechanisms and hierarchical processing capabilities but still rely on orientation


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      normalization or data augmentation techniques to manage rotations, which do not fully resolve the
055   inherent challenges of varying viewpoints.
056
057   Despite progress in the field, existing shape completion techniques struggle to maintain robustness
058
      and accuracy under arbitrary rotations. This notably limits their practicality in real-world applications
      where objects can appear in any orientation. Recent methods, including transformer-based models like
059
      SnowflakeNet(35) and SeedFormer(46), have introduced improvements by leveraging the attention
060
      mechanisms, progressively growing points, and hierarchical processing capabilities of transformers.
061   However, these models expect partial shapes in aligned orientation across a category and only learn
062   completion in canonical coordinates, which do not guarantee performance consistency in varying
063   viewpoints or unknown local coordinates.
064
      In addition, progress has been made in developing rotation-invariant descriptors, particularly in
065
      shape registration tasks, which require detecting points of interest in objects regardless of orientation.
066
      However, these advancements have yet to be fully extended to shape completion. These descriptors
067   are vital for applications such as 3D model retrieval and pose estimation but do not directly facilitate
068   the reconstruction of entire object geometry in a rotation-equivariant manner.
069
070
      To bridge these gaps, we introduce ESCAPE, a novel approach to ensure rotation-equivariant shape
      completion. ESCAPE employs a unique encoding strategy by selecting high-curvature anchor points
071
      around the input shape and transforming point coordinates into a set of distances from these anchor
072
      points. This method constructs a robust representation of objects equivariant to their orientation. The
073   representation is processed through a transformer architecture, which enables the model to reconstruct
074   the distance to the same anchor points on a complete object point set under varying conditions.
075   Subsequently, an optimization is utilized to find point coordinates of the completed geometry from
076   the predicted distances.
077
      We propose a complete pipeline to generate, encode, decode, and interpret rotation equivariant
078
      features for the first non-canonical 3D shape completion.
079
080   In summary, our contributions are threefold:
081
             • We introduce ESCAPE, a novel rotation-equivariant 3D encoding strategy using high-
082
               curvature anchor points, enabling robust shape description and reconstruction.
083
084          • We develop a transformer-based architecture that leverages our equivariant encoding to gen-
085            erate the completed point cloud, maintaining consistency across varying object orientations
086
               and partial inputs.
087          • We present the first end-to-end rotation-equivariant shape completion method, demonstrat-
088            ing robust performance under arbitrary rotations and in the absence of canonical object
089            coordinates.
090          • We establish and rigorously evaluate a challenging real-world shape completion benchmark
091            using the OmniObject dataset(34), featuring partial point clouds with diverse geometries
092            and arbitrary poses.

      2.1   P OINT C LOUD P ROCESSING
097
098   Point clouds, representing unordered and sparse 3D data, challenge conventional CNNs. Voxel-based
099   methods like VoxNet (17) and VoxelNet (47) convert point clouds into regular grids for 3D CNN
100   processing but are computationally intensive. PointNet (21) and PointNet++ (22) address unordered
101   sets with MLPs and hierarchical grouping. PointNet creates a permutation-invariant structure with
102   pointwise MLPs, while PointNet++ adds hierarchical layers and furthest point sampling, increasing
103   computational complexity. These methods are often combined with voxel-based approaches in
104
      large-scale applications (28; 47).
105   Graph neural networks (GNNs) like PointCNN (16) and EdgeConv (33) capture local geometric
106   features through spatial relationships and dynamic graph construction. Recent advancements integrate
107   edge-based attention for improved efficiency (31; 30). Transformers and attention mechanisms have
      also been applied to point clouds, with Graph Attention Networks (GATs) (31) using self-attentional


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      layers and the Transformer architecture (30) introducing multi-head attention for robust feature
109   aggregation. Set Transformer (15) and Perceiver (13) adapt attention mechanisms for 3D data. Point
110   cloud-specific transformers, such as PCT (10) and Point Transformer (45), apply self-attention to
111   neighborhood points, though they can be computationally expensive.

113   2.2   ROTATION -I NVARIANT E NCODINGS
114
115   Handcrafted rotation-invariant descriptors have been widely explored in 3D by researchers before the
116   popularity of deep neural networks. To guarantee invariance under rotations, many handcrafted local
117   descriptors (25; 24; 29; 11) rely on an estimated local reference frame (LRF), which is typically based
118   on the covariance analysis of the local surface, to transform local patches to a canonical representation.
119   The major drawback of LRF is its non-uniqueness, making the constructed rotational invariance
120
      fragile and sensitive to noise. Consequently, attention has shifted to LRF-free approaches (5). These
      methods focus on mining the rotation-invariant components of local surfaces to represent the local
121
      geometry. For instance, PPF (5), PFH (25), and FPFH (24) encode the geometry of the local surface
122
      using histograms of pairwise geometrical properties. Despite being rotation-invariant by design, these
123   handcrafted descriptors are often inadequate for complex geometry and noisy data.
124
125   Recently, many deep learning-based methods have aimed to learn rotation-invariant descriptors. PPF-
126
      FoldNet (4) encodes PPF patches into embeddings, using a FoldingNet (37) decoder to reconstruct
      the input, enabling correspondences from the rotation-invariant embeddings. SpinNet (1) and
127
      Graphite (26; 27) align local patches to defined axes before learning descriptors. However, these
128
      methods are limited by their locality, as descriptors are learned only from the local region, making
129   them less distinctive.
130
131   YOHO (36) introduces a rotation-equivariant approach by leveraging an icosahedral group to learn
132
      a group of rotation-equivariant descriptors for each point. Rotational invariance is achieved by
      max-pooling over the group, but this method struggles with efficiency and complete rotational
133
      coverage. Object-centric registration methods (38; 6) strengthen rotational invariance by combining
134
      rotation-invariant descriptors with rotation-variant inputs, though performance drops under large
135   rotations.
136
137   In point cloud classification, methods (44; 3) describe whole shapes as rotation-invariant descrip-
138
      tors but lack global awareness in node/point descriptors. Methods such as Transformer-based
      approaches (30) aim to incorporate global context, but robustness to rotational changes is often
139
      achieved through data augmentation, which is not optimal. Recent works such as Rotation-Invariant
140
      Transformer (40) and Riga (39) have proposed more robust rotation-invariant and globally-aware
141   descriptors. Despite these advancements, these approaches have not yet been thoroughly explored in
142   shape completion tasks, highlighting a significant area for future research.

      2.3   S HAPE C OMPLETION M ETHODS
145
146   Pre-deep learning point cloud completion methods rely on object symmetry (18), or a database of
147   complete shapes (20) to achieve effective results. However, these methods are constrained by the
148   need for specific preconditions to be met by the input data, limiting their applicability compared to
149   deep learning-based approaches.
150   Learning-based methods can be classified into two primary categories based on the type of rep-
151   resentation used for 3D data: methods utilizing point clouds and methods employing alternative
152   representations such as voxel grids, implicit functions, and others. Methods using alternative repre-
153   sentations often need higher memory consumption, making them less scalable for high-resolution
154   inputs. Although researchers have proposed more efficient representations such as Octrees (32),
155   sparse lattice networks (23), and sparse convolution operations (9), none have proven to be as efficient
156   and effective as directly processing 3D point coordinates.
157   PointNet (21) and its variant PointNet++ (22) introduced specialized operations that enable learning
158   directly from point cloud coordinates, revolutionizing point cloud processing tasks. PCN (43) was
159   built on top of PointNet and became the first deep learning-based method for point cloud completion,
160   utilizing an encoder-decoder architecture with a folding operation to complete given partial input
161   point clouds. Following PCN, many other methods with similar model architectures have been
      developed (43). In addition to these methods, some researchers formulate point cloud completion

      Another milestone for point cloud completion is the application of transformer-based methods. Due
      to their efficiency with unordered data, transformers are well-suited for point cloud processing
186
      tasks. PoinTr (41) formulates the completion task as set-to-set translation, utilizing transformers
187
      for the first time in this context. SnowflakeNet (35) employs an encoder-decoder architecture based
188   on transformers to complete point clouds in a coarse-to-fine fashion. Each level of the decoder
189   architecture inputs a prediction of a subset of the complete point cloud generated by the previous
190   level and splits the input into child points to generate a finer prediction. Similarly, Seedformer (46)
191   introduces a new representation called patch seeds, consisting of points and their features, to be
192   used in the decoding step. Unlike SnowflakeNet, Seedformer’s decoder levels utilize point features
193   while generating a new set of complete point clouds. AdaPointTr (42) builds on top of PoinTr by
194   incorporating a denoising task and an adaptive query generation mechanism. Finally, our method
195   show some similarities to a recent method named: AnchorFormer (2) which also utilizes points to aid
196
      shape completion. It employs points(anchors) around the shape to capture regional information of
      objects. These points are then used to reconstruct fine-grained object using a modulation scheme.

198   Although these models predict completion well, they rely on aligning objects into canonical coordi-
199   nates and fail when local coordinates are unknown, limiting their effectiveness in more generalized
200   and unstructured scenarios. This reliance on known local coordinates and failure to predict under
201   arbitrary rotations highlights a significant challenge that our work aims to address.

204   3    M ETHODOLOGY
205
206
      Our method addresses point cloud completion by leveraging a combination of rotation-invariant
207
      features and a specially designed transformer model. Initially, anchor points and rotation-invariant
208
      features are extracted from the partial input point cloud. These features are then passed into the
209   transformer to predict the complete geometry of the object relative to the anchor points.
210
211   It is important to note that the final point coordinate optimization is influenced by the input anchor
212
      points, which are affected by the rotation of the input. As a result, the final predictions are also
      rotated, ensuring that the entire pipeline remains rotation-equivariant. This means that the output is
213
      consistently aligned with the input rotation.
214
215   The overall architecture is shown in Figure 2. Below, we detail the different components of our
      ESCAPE model.

      3.2   T RANSFORMER A RCHITECTURE
244
245   Our point cloud transformer model is inspired by AdaPoinTr (42), but we revised it to meet the
246   specific requirements of rotation invariance.
247
248
      First, we modify the feature extraction process used to generate point proxies. In the original
      architecture, a DGCNN (33) model is employed to extract local neighbor features through hierarchical
249
      downsampling and processing of initial features. Instead of using absolute point coordinates, we
250
      input the distances dij to the anchor points into the DGCNN, with dimensionality handled through
251   linear layers of size k. This modification is applied consistently across the AdaPoinTr architecture,
252   ensuring encoding with the same anchor points throughout the network.
253
254
      The use of distances also affects the training objective. The original AdaPoinTr loss function focuses
      on predicting the correct coordinates for noisy points perturbed during training. Since our network
255
      predicts distances, we modified the loss function to account for the noisy distances between noisy
256
      input points and noise-free anchor points. This ensures that our network can still remove noise from
257   the inputs, even when operating in distance space.
258
259   Similarly, in the self-attention layer, we replaced the point coordinates with the distances to anchor
260
      points to better capture the geometric relationships in the point cloud while maintaining invariance to
      input rotation.
261
262   These changes rely on the intuition that distances can effectively serve as encoded point coordinates.
263   Specifically, two points will remain neighbors in Euclidean space when their distances to anchor points
264   are used as descriptors, effectively representing the points in a "distance space." Therefore, point
265   coordinates can be replaced by distances to anchor points without losing the geometric relationships
266
      between the points.
267   The final output distances are inherently unaffected by any rotations applied to the input, ensuring
268   both consistency and rotation invariance. To reconstruct the completed point cloud with accurate 3D
269   geometry, we solve an optimization problem to determine the final coordinates of the 3D shape from
      the predicted distances.

271
272   To finalize the point cloud completion, the predicted distances must be converted into point coordi-
273   nates. This step involves finding the coordinates of points whose distances to known anchor points
274   match the predicted values. More formally, the optimization problem is defined as:
275
276                             k  p
                                X                                                       2
277                     min            (x − xai )2 + (y − yai )2 + (z − zai )2 − Dc (i) ,                 (1)
                        x,y,z
278                             i=1
279
      where xai , yai , zai are the coordinates of the known anchor points, and x, y, z are the coordinates of
280
      the point to be determined, while Dc represents the distances predicted by the network. This optimiza-
281
      tion problem is solved independently for each point using the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm (19)
282   to recover the full object shape.
283
284   The final coordinates predicted by the pipeline retain the same orientation as the partial input cloud,
285
      ensuring that the method is rotation-equivariant. Moreover, due to the model’s rotation invariance, the
      predicted complete object coordinates remain consistent under varying transformations of the input.

505   In these experiments, we also tested an alternative algorithm to select anchor points based on the
506   curvature values of points. First, we use the FPS algorithm to select a anchor points, then use these
507   points as cluster centers, assigning all other points to their closest cluster. For each cluster, we select
508   the point with the maximum curvature as the anchor point, provided its curvature exceeds a predefined
509   threshold. If no point in the cluster meets the curvature threshold, we retain the original FPS-selected
510   point as the anchor. The results of this algorithm, referred to as "Clustering," are shown in Table 5.
511   For comparison, the final row of Table 5, labeled "FPS," shows the results of using the FPS algorithm
512   alone without further refinement. Both results demonstrate the importance of well-distributed anchor
513   points around the shape for the model’s success. Selecting anchor points from clusters provides
514   more flexibility for points to move away from their initial positions, thereby covering less area of the
515   object’s geometry.
516   Additionally, we limit the refinement of anchor points to a maximum distance (radius) from their
517   initial positions. The rows labeled "Ball Query" in Table 5 show the results for different radius
518   values. The results indicate that a larger radius does not improve performance, as it allows too much
519   flexibility for anchor points to shift around the shape, resulting in less coverage of critical regions.
520   This observation aligns with the conclusions from previous experiments.
521   The "Ball Query" results further show that selecting points with respect to their curvature is beneficial.
522   By allowing points to move a small distance, such as 0.075, from their initial positions, we were able
523   to choose better anchor points, resulting in improved point cloud completion performance.

526   5    C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this work, we presented Equivariant Shape Completion via Anchor Point Encoding (ESCAPE),
529   a novel method for achieving rotation-equivariant shape completion. Our approach tackles the
530
      key challenge of reconstructing object geometries from various orientations by leveraging rotation-
      equivariant keypoint detection and a distance-based feature encoding inspired by the D2 shape
531
      distribution.
532
533   Through comprehensive experiments on the PCN, KITTI, and OmniObject datasets, ESCAPE
534   consistently outperformed existing models, mainly when dealing with rotated input data. This
535   demonstrates the method’s robustness in scenarios where input orientation is not controlled or known
536
      in advance.
537   ESCAPE provides a practical solution for dynamic environments, such as robotic manipulation
538   and real-time object recognition, by enabling effective shape completion without requiring prior
539   knowledge of object orientation or additional pose estimation modules. The combination of rotation-
      equivariant keypoint detection and a transformer-based architecture enables ESCAPE to reconstruct
