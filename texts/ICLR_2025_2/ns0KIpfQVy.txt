009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Financial organizations collect a huge amount of data about clients that typi-
012            cally has a temporal (sequential) structure and is collected from multiple sources
013            (modalities). However, despite the urgent practical need, developing deep learn-
014            ing techniques suitable to handle such data is limited by the absence of large open-
015            source multi-source real-world datasets of event sequences. To fill this gap mainly
016            caused by security reasons, we present the industrial-scale publicly available mul-
017            timodal banking dataset, MBD, that contains more than 2M corporate clients with
018
               several data sources: 950M bank transactions, 1B geo position events, 5M em-
               beddings of dialogues with technical support and monthly aggregated purchases
019
               of four bank’s products. All entries are properly anonymized from real proprietary
020
               bank data. Moreover, we introduce a novel multimodal benchmark incorporating
021            our MBD and two open-source financial datasets. We provide numerical results
022            demonstrating the superiority of fusion baselines over single-modal techniques
023            for each task. Moreover, our anonymization techniques still save all significant
024            information for introduced downstream tasks.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The key tasks in the banking industry, such as campaigning, fraud detection, credit risk assess-
032   ment, customer segmentation, and personalized recommendations, heavily rely on various aspects
033
      of clients’ financial activities, e.g., product purchase history. This data, spanning extended periods,
      is typically annotated with temporal information, forming what is known as event sequences Babaev
034
      et al. (2022); Udovichenko et al. (2024); Yeshchenko & Mendling (2022); Kolosnjaji et al. (2016).
035
      An event is described by several heterogeneous fields, numerical and categorical. An essential prop-
036   erty of event sequences is that these data are often gathered from multiple sources or channels,
037   rendering multimodal.
038
039
      Thus, the success of financial organizations strongly depends on their availability to analyze such
      multi-source heterogeneous event sequences accurately. However, existing multimodal models Xu
040
      et al. (2023); Zhang & Yan (2023) cannot be directly applied to such event/tabular data due to
041
      their significant difference with audio, images, texts, and regular time-series. Unfortunately, despite
042   the urgent business needs, the progress in the development of multimodal techniques for multi-
043   source event sequences is limited by the absence of large-scale datasets Indeed, though several
044   datasets of event sequences are used in research, e.g., credit card transactions Padhi et al. (2021) or
045   MIMIC Johnson et al. (2023), they are either small or contain only one modality. Thus, tackling the
046   complexity of multimodal event sequence data is still very challenging.
047
      To bridge this gap, this paper introduces the Multimodal Banking Dataset (MBD), an unprecedented
048   open-source resource encompassing extensive multichannel event sequence data of banking cor-
049   porate clients. It is the largest of its kind, featuring detailed records of approximately 2 million
050   clients across four distinct modalities: money transfers (about 950 million events), geo position data
051   (around 1 billion events), technical support dialog embeddings (approximately 5 million entries),
052   and monthly aggregated bank product purchases categorized into four types. Each modality encom-
053   passes roughly one or two years of historical, time-annotated data, making it a rich resource for
      analyzing the dynamics of client behavior over time.

      The MBD dataset enables the research of several critical business problems in a multimodal context,
055   such as future purchase prediction (campaigning) and matching of different modalities for the same
056   clients. In addition, we provide benchmarks for these tasks using the MBD and other existing
057   financial datasets of much smaller size. Our possibility to publish the dataset is caused by properly
058   anonymizing all data to protect client privacy. Our experiments confirm that this procedure preserves
059   the consistency of model performance between original and anonymized data.

      2.1   F INANCIAL DATA
064
065
      The multitude of services and processes in banks generates a variety of data that can be considered
066   as modalities. Early works such as Moro et al. (2014); Mancisidor et al. (2021) use feature pro-
067   cessing techniques that remove multimodal complexity and present data in tabular form. The Amex
068   dataset ame improves information content and complexity. Here, a wide range of different finan-
069   cial aggregates are presented as a sequence of historical slices. The development of deep learning
070   methods has led to the ability to work with complexly structured data, such as sequences of events.
071   Quite a few datasets age; ros; alp, mostly unimodal, presented mainly at ML competitions. To work
072   with such data, both supervised Ala’raj et al. (2022); Babaev et al. (2019); Wang & Xiao (2022) and
073   unsupervised methods Padhi et al. (2021); Babaev et al. (2022); Skalski et al. (2023) are used. A
074
      multimodal financial sequential dataset was introduced in DataFusion 2022 competition dat. There
      are two sequential modalities, transaction and web clickstream, and two downstream tasks: match-
075
      ing and education level prediction. However, this is an extremely small dataset of 22K clients, and
076
      no accurate baseline model is available.
077
078
      2.2   OTHER EVENT SEQUENCE DOMAINS
079
080   Temporal point process Mei & Eisner (2017); Zhuzhel et al. (2023) model streams of discrete events
081   in continuous time by constructing a neurally multivariate point process. The authors use a large
082   collection of datasets from different types of modalities: Media (Retweets Zhao et al. (2015), Meme-
083   Track Leskovec & Krevl (2014), Amazon ama (2018), IPTV Luo et al. (2014)), Medical (MIMIC-II
084   Johnson et al. (2016)), Social(Stack Overflow Leskovec & Krevl (2014), Linkedin Xu & Zha (2017))
085   and Financial (Transaction Fursov et al. (2021)) data. All datasets are independent, and each one is
086   single-modal. EventStreamGPT McDermott et al. (2024) uses multimodal medical record datasets,
087   MIMIC-IV Johnson et al. (2023). The authors propose a GPT-like approach for continuous-time
088
      event sequences. The structure of this dataset is close to the MDB. However, financial data, unlike
      medical data, contains longer chains of events, more regular patterns, and individual transactions are
089
      less informative.

092   2.3   G EOSTREAM AND DIALOGUES
093
      Geodata is used for various tasks. One of the uses of geo is a visualization of analytics on a map Hao
094
      et al. (2011). However, geo is used here not as a separate modality but as additional tags to the main-
095   stream of tweets. Mobile marketers Baye et al. (2024) use geo-targeting for pricing and send per-
096   sonalized recommendations. In Verma et al. (2020), geo hashes are used for user mobility detection

098
      We encode our dialogue entries via a pretrained NLP model. Such models have already been used
099
      Hassan et al. (2019) for text anonymization tasks. Embeddings preserve the meaning of the text,
100
      which was shown in Vaswani et al. (2017). Pre-trained text embeddings can capture text sentiment
101   and improve text-to-speech models Hayashi et al. (2019).

104   3     P ROPOSED DATASET
105
106   Modern innovative banking institutions actively develop AI technologies for customizing their
107   human-oriented technologies and making everyday decisions. A superior level of technologies will
      lead to new cases of customer experience, which should form a competitive advantage of services


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      provided, including the speed, accuracy, and price of customer services, including personal credit
109   conditions, individual finance strategy, etc.
110
111   One of the main benefits of using AI is the ability to analyze large amounts of customer data. This
112
      helps banks better understand their customers’ needs and offer them the most suitable products and
      services. Additionally, AI can protect customers from fraud and prevent financial losses. More
113
      accurate forecasting of financial risks associated with lending or investing allows us to provide more
114
      favorable conditions to more reliable clients. This strategy is more effective if there is a lot of data,
115   so banks strive to accumulate as much information as possible.
116
117   Being a team of a bank that stores petabytes of data about bank processes and clients, we understand
118
      the urgent needs of fintech data scientists in large sets of publicly available temporal data from
      various sources (bank transactions, client locations, purchased products, etc.) to drive innovation and
119
      scientific discovery. Unfortunately, the number of appropriate datasets is limited because providing
120
      such data comes with certain risks. Typically, banks are wary of sharing their data due to potential
121   leaks of confidential information or violations of data protection regulations. In addition, the data is
122   considered to have commercial value, and companies do not want to disclose it. To mitigate these
123   risks, it is required to take all necessary steps to ensure data security and remove any identifying
124   information. This allows information to be shared without violating client confidentiality, but this
125   procedure requires significant effort from engineers, managers, and lawyers. Though there exist
126   a small number of properly anonymized banking datasets, such as credit card transactions Padhi
127   et al. (2021) or AlphaBattle alp, to the best of our knowledge, there are no publicly available large
128   multimodal temporal datasets for banks.
129   Thus, in this paper, we introduced the first large-scale multimodal banking dataset to support future
130   research on multimodal techniques for event sequences. In particular, we select several practically
131   important tasks, such as campaigning, i.e., prediction if a client would purchase some of four rather
132   popular products in the next month. Each client is described by sequences of typical bank data:
133   transactions, geo positions where the customer used the bank application, and dialogues with tech-
134   nical support. These data sources highlight the main difficulties in developing multimodal mod-
135
      els, namely, asynchronous events in different modalities, various intensities of events, rare/irregular
      events, and even the absence of some modalities for many clients. Based on our dataset, the re-
136
      searchers will be able to fully take into account cross-modal connections of sequences from multiple
137
      sources at the level of individual events.
138
139   Let us discuss the details of the dataset collection procedure. At first, we select a complete sample of
140   clients for two years (2021 and 2022) to cover all seasons. Among all customers who had the oppor-
141
      tunity to purchase at least one of four products during 2022, we randomly choose 2,186,230 clients,
      among which 1M customers are labeled by monthly aggregated purchases of each of four products
142
      in each month. For these clients, we collect 947,899,612 financial operations, 1,117,213,760 geo
143
      position events, and 5,080,781 dialogues with technical support. Our data raise typical practical
144   challenges for training multimodal models. For example, many clients do not have all three modali-
145   ties simultaneously because they can never make a transaction, call tech support, or leave their geo
146   trace while running the bank application. Next, all the data are properly anonymized to guarantee
147   the confidentiality and privacy of customer information. As a result, it is impossible to recover real
148   clients from our anonymous data. We will show in the experimental study that such anonymization
149   still allows us to extract valuable information about clients.
150   To demonstrate the complete transformation of data from various sources, Fig. 1 briefly shows ex-
151   ample data for each modality, and the temporal structure of the data for a campaigning model is
152   presented. Let us introduce further details for each modality in the following subsections.
153
      We present a comparative analysis of various event sequence datasets alongside our MBD dataset.
154
      As shown in Table 1, the MBD dataset is substantially more comprehensive, offering a greater
155
      number of modalities, events, clients, and downstream tasks compared to other datasets. MBD in-
156   corporates a diverse set of data modalities, including bank transactions, geo-locations, and technical
157   support dialogues, providing a richer and more realistic basis for analysis. Additionally, it supports
158   a broader range of downstream tasks, detailed in the following sections, allowing for more sophisti-
159   cated and flexible modeling approaches.

          3.1      M ODALITIES
194
         1. Bank transactional data are financial operations (events) carried out between different clients.
195      Collected over a two-year period (2021 and 2022), the sequence of financial operations can uniquely
196      characterize the client Babaev et al. (2022), so this data source plays one of the most significant
197      roles in planning and recommendations. Thus, the main component of our MBD dataset is each
198      client’s transactional history, represented by an event with a timestamp and various attributes of the
199      anonymized counterparty. Clients have 638 transactions on average.
200
         2. Dialogues. The dialogue data consists of transcriptions from customer calls to technical support
201      and negotiations between clients and their managers, collected over a two-year period (2021 and
202      2022). We incorporate dialogues from key communication channels, including sales and service
203      calls, which account for most interactions with bank customers. It is an extremely important source
204      of information about client needs and problems Bauman et al. (2024). The audio utterance is fed into
205      a commercial Speech-to-Text algorithm. Personal information, e.g., the client’s name, is detected
206      in the text and masked. To further anonymize the dialogue, we feed its text into a pre-trained NLP
207      model1 and save the resulting embeddings of size 768 in dialogue modality. Only 46% of customers
208      contact support and have records of conversations, 98% of them have no more than 10 dialogues.
209      3. Geostream data contains a sequence of geo-coordinates of a client obtained throughout 2022.
210      To anonymize this modality, the coordinates are encoded using geohashes2 , a geocoding system that
211      converts a geographic location into a short string. Each unique geohash corresponds to a region on
212      the Earth’s surface. It is possible to adjust the accuracy and size by removing characters from the end
213      of the code. In our dataset, the coordinates are encoded with a precision of 4, 5, and 6 characters,
214

219   4. Products purchases. Our dataset serves as a valuable resource for analyzing the needs of bank

      customers and optimizing the campaigning process, a critical task that directly impacts both the
      volume of products sold by the bank and its overall profitability. High-quality recommendations

222
      business outcomes but also for customer satisfaction. Specifically, MBD includes monthly data on
223   the purchases of four distinct banking products throughout 2022, providing a broader temporal scope
224   that captures patterns beyond the pandemic’s peak. We concentrate on the most popular of these
225   products, as internal analysis across various tasks using proprietary data consistently showed that
226   these products provide a robust foundation for model selection. The insights from this data enable
227   the development of models that demonstrate superior performance across a broad spectrum of related
228   tasks. To predict a purchase in a certain month, it is necessary to take events (transactions, geo,
229   dialogues) strictly before the beginning of this month. Therefore, the date range for the purchases
230   dataset is shifted by 1 month, i.e., information is available from February 1, 2022, to January 31,
231
      2023. The campaigning task is a multi-label classification problem, i.e., we store a binary label for
      each product that indicates whether it is purchased by a customer in a certain month. The peculiarity
232
      of this dataset is its imbalance, which is specific to this type of business task: 81% of clients have no
233
      purchases, 15% have one, and the remaining 4% have two or more purchases. A historical overview
234   over 12 months allows us to model the customer behavior dynamic and predict the date of purchase
235   more accurately.

240   3.2   DATA ANONYMIZATION
241
      Our dataset contains no personal or confidential information whatsoever. Nevertheless, the event
242
      sequences are detailed enough that it could be possible to compare individuals from the publicly
243
      accessible portion of the dataset with the original proprietary data. To mitigate this risk, noise is
244   introduced to the data, ensuring that such comparisons and identification are impossible. The noise
245   patterns were selected by our bank’s internal security department. These patterns are applied locally,
246   preserving the overall structure of the data. The specific noise parameters are not disclosed to prevent
247   potential attacks on the dataset.
248
      All ID fields are hashed with a random salt. All categorical field values are mapped to enumerated
249
      indexes. Random noise is added to numerical fields and dates, preserving the hour of the original
250   date, which may be the cause of the shuffle of the local sequence. The dialogue embedding space is
251   divided into regions, which are then shuffled.

254   4     B ENCHMARK
255
256   In this paper, we introduce a benchmark for widely used event sequence datasets, incorporating prac-
257
      tically important downstream tasks. This section provides a detailed description of the downstream
      tasks, baseline methods, and evaluation protocols.
258
259
260   4.1   DATASETS AND DOWNSTREAM TASKS
261
      For each downstream task in every dataset, we implement an out-of-fold validation protocol to con-
262
      duct our experiments. The client dataset is partitioned into five folds, with four folds used for train-
263
      ing and the remaining fold reserved for testing. The training and testing sets are publicly available
264   alongside the dataset, allowing future researchers to compare performance metrics. As each dataset
265   in our benchmark exhibits label imbalance, ROC AUC is the most robust and informative evaluation
266   metric due to its resilience to class imbalances. In real-world business, campaign effectiveness is
267   measured by revenue, but conducting A/B tests for every ML model is impractical. Instead, ROC
268   AUC is a reliable proxy metric for model comparison, with only the top performers advancing to
269   A/B testing. This method was validated through real-world A/B tests and is recognized as the core
      evaluation metric by leading institutions, including one of the largest global banks. The choice


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      of AUC for campaigning is supported by the reason that ranking models by their ROC curves are
271   similar to comparing their non-response ratio at all possible cutoff points simultaneously Liu et al.
272   (2012); Rosset et al. (2001). Let us discuss the details of downstream tasks for each dataset in our
273   benchmark.
274
      1. MBD. For our dataset, we introduce a campaigning downstream task. In this task, it is required to
275
      predict the customer’s propensity to purchase four different products in the next month (Fig. 1) given
276
      sequences of transactions, geo locations, and dialogues from the beginning of this month. Solutions
277   to this problem are used to plan marketing campaigns and prepare sales communications through
278   various communication channels with the client.
279
280
      The baseline methods outlined in the following section are applied as follows. First, we train our
      models using the training set. Considering the temporal structure of our target, we compute the
281
      embedding of each client’s history for up to one month, focusing on the presence of the target
282
      product (Fig. 1). We then evaluate the model using the multi-label classification metric ROC AUC
283   across the 12 months of 2022 and for four binary product labels.
284
285   2. Datafusion. In this dataset, the proposed downstream task is to predict the higher education
286
      attainment of bank clients. It involves analyzing two client modalities (transaction histories and
      clickstream data) to accurately infer their educational background. The task is formulated as a
287
      binary classification problem, with 75% of the labels corresponding to clients with higher education.
288
      The objective is to develop predictive models that leverage these multimodal data sources to extract
289   meaningful insights, which can be applied to further analysis.
290
291   3. Alphabattle. We incorporate the large unimodal Alphabattle dataset into our benchmark along-
292
      side the multimodal datasets. This inclusion of data from various financial institutions aims to
      support more robust and reliable conclusions. In this dataset, the downstream task estimates the
293
      probability of a customer defaulting based on their historical card transaction behavior. This task
294
      is framed as a binary classification problem, with 2.7% of the labels representing clients who have
295   defaulted. Although the dataset is unimodal, the downstream task remains highly relevant for finan-
296   cial institutions, offering critical insights into credit risk assessment in improving decision-making
297   processes related to customer management and financial strategies.
298
      Multimodal matching
299
300   For the multimodal datasets MBD and Datafusion, we propose a downstream task of multimodal
301   matching Zong et al. (2023). Multimodal matching involves aligning and comparing modalities to
302   identify meaningful relationships or connections. Frequently, data from multiple sources for the
303   same client are matched using predefined rules or heuristics, which may not always yield optimal
304
      results. To enhance the accuracy of this process, specialized identification algorithms are required
      to compare modalities more precisely.
305
306   For the matching task, we employ a framework analogous to CLIP Radford et al. (2021). We utilize
307   GRU encoders to embed pairs of samples from two input modalities, labeling them as either positive
308   (i.e., data from the same client) or negative matches (i.e., data from different clients). The model is
309   trained using the InfoNCE loss function Chen et al. (2020), which maximizes similarity for positive
310
      pairs while minimizing it for negative pairs. To assess the model’s performance, we use Recall@1,
      Recall@50 and Recall@100 metrics.

314
      To establish performance baselines, we implement several widely adopted architectures. Our ap-
315
      proach prioritizes unsupervised and semi-supervised methods Balestriero et al. (2023), enabling the
316
      training of a general-purpose encoder on unlabeled sequential data. Additionally, we incorporate su-
317   pervised methods that allow for the immediate training of the encoder in a fully supervised manner.

      4.2.1     U NIMODAL APPROACHES
320
321   The following techniques are implemented in our benchmark to extract features from data:
322
323           1. Aggregation Baseline that contains hand-crafted aggregation statistics Babaev et al.
                 (2022): Events are represented either numerically, such as transaction amount, or cate-


                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
                 gorically, like event types. For numerical attributes, we apply aggregation functions (e.g.,
325              sum, mean, std) across all events in a sequence. Categorical attributes are grouped by
326              unique values, aggregating numerical attributes using functions like count or mean.
327
              2. CoLES (Contrastive Learning for Event Sequences), a self-supervised contrastive
328
                 model Babaev et al. (2022) specially developed to obtain representations of such event
329              sequences as bank transactions. The sequence encoder is a GRU (Gated Recurrent Unit)
330              with a hidden size of 256.
331
              3. Two Tabular Transformers from IBM Padhi et al. (2021). The first model, TabBERT,
332              adapts BERT to event sequences such as bank transactions. The second model, TabGPT,
333              was initially proposed to generate synthetic tabular sequences. Both models extract 256-
334              dimensional embeddings of an input event sequence. After that, we pool output embed-
335              dings of the client in result embedding of size 1024, calculating min, max, mean, and std.
336
337   To obtain representation of a sequence of dialogues, we borrow several conventional techniques to
338
      aggregate the sequence of embeddings of each dialog of a client: 1) mean pooling of all embeddings;
      and 2) use only the most recent embedding for the date of interest.
339
340   For unimodal supervised methods (Supervised RNN), we utilize GRU architectures Babaev et al.
341   (2019) with a hidden size 32. The models are trained in a multi-label setting using binary cross-
342   entropy (BCE) loss, ensuring effective optimization for tasks with multiple targets.
343
344   4.2.2     M ULTIMODAL APPROACHES
345
      To explore the potential of multimodal processing for event sequence analysis, we compare several
346   fusion techniques:
347
348           1. Blending computes a weighted sum of class posterior probabilities from individual single-
349              modal classifiers, effectively combining the predictions from each modality.
350           2. Late Fusion: embeddings from all data sources are concatenated and fed into a classifier.
351              This technique allows the model to learn interactions between modalities after they have
352              been individually processed Huang et al. (2020). In supervised Late Fusion, we utilize
353              separate GRU encoders for each modality, concatenating their embeddings to form a unified
354              representation (Supervised RNN).
355           3. Early Fusion combines representations from multiple modalities at the initial stages of the
356              model, enabling joint processing of multimodal data. We employ the CrossTransformer
357              approach Zhang & Yan (2023), which utilizes a cross-attention mechanism to integrate
358              information across modalities efficiently. In our experiments, this method is applied within
359
                 a supervised learning framework.

      We employ the unsupervised baseline methods (CoLES, TabGPT, TabBERT, Aggregation) with de-

516   7     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
517
518
519
      In this paper, we present the first large-scale, publicly available multimodal banking dataset, MBD,
      which comprises anonymized sequential data, including bank transactions, geo-locations, and tech-
520
      nical support dialogues for more than 2 million bank clients. Our findings indicate that anonymiza-
521
      tion does not significantly affect algorithm performance, making the dataset ideal for selecting mod-
522   els suitable for deployment in real-world production environments. Furthermore, excluding sensi-
523   tive attributes such as gender, age, and race mitigates the potential for bias in the resulting models,
524   promoting the development of more ethical AI systems.
525
      Moreover, MBD, together with the Datafusion and Alphabattle datasets, serves as the foundation
526
      for a novel benchmark targeting key practical downstream tasks. This benchmark paves the way
527
      for the development of scalable algorithms, both multimodal and unimodal, with potential applica-
528   tions in event sequence prediction across various industries. Our experimental results demonstrate
529   that even basic multimodal fusion techniques surpass single-modal baselines in overall model qual-
530   ity (Table 3). Furthermore, based on our results of Late and Early Fusion (Table 5), it is possible
531   to develop more advanced models that can effectively capture interactions between modalities, to
532   achieve further improvements in overall performance. Given the scale of data and its real-world ap-
533   plicability, even moderate improvements in model performance metrics can translate into substantial
534   financial benefits when applied to a large customer base.
535   In the future, we are going to extend the dataset to its new versions. First, it is necessary to in-
536   corporate new data sources, which will predominantly be utilized as additional data sources (e.g.,
537   clickstreams or enriched dialogue features, e.g., discussed topics). Second, it is important to expand
538   a set of downstream tasks to campaigning for other financial products and analyzing customer be-
539   havior insights (customer churn, fraud detection, credit risk assessment). Finally, it is possible to
      extend the time range of the dataset, enabling longitudinal studies and trend analysis.
