                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Large amounts of missing data are becoming increasingly ubiquitous in modern
               high-dimensional datasets. High-rank matrix completion (HRMC) uses the power-
011
               ful union of subspace (UoS) model to handle these vast amounts of missing data.
012
               However, existing HRMC methods often fail when dealing with real data that does
013            not follow the UoS model exactly. Here we propose a new approach: instead of
014            finding a UoS that fits the observed data directly, we will find a UoS in a latent
015            space that can fit a non-linear embedding of the original data. Embeddings of this
016            sort are typically attained with deep architectures. However, the abundance of
017            missing data impedes the training process, as the coordinates of the observed sam-
018            ples rarely overlap. We overcome this difficulty with a novel pseudo-completion
019            layer (in charge of estimating the missing values) followed by an auto-encoder
020            (in charge of finding the embedding) coupled with a self-expressive layer (that
021
               clusters data according to a UoS in the latent space). Our design reduces the
               exponential memory requirements that are typically induced by uneven patterns of
022
               missing data. We give exact details of our architecture, model, loss functions, and
023
               training strategy. Our experiments on several real datasets show that our method
024            consistently outperforms the state-of-the-art accuracy by more than a staggering
025            40%.

      Motivation: missing data. Missing data is a widespread challenge across various fields, including
031   epidemiology, social sciences, finance, clinical research, computer vision and many more Enders
032   (2022); Baraldi & Enders (2010); Beaulieu-Jones et al. (2018); Gelman & Loken (2016); Little &
033   Rubin (2019); Garcia-Laencina et al. (2010). For example, missing data is observed in epidemiology
034   due to participant attrition and incomplete responses during health assessments Beaulieu-Jones et al.
035   (2018), while in social science research, non-response bias in survey-based studies compromises
036   representativeness Gelman & Loken (2016). Clinical trials also face missing data due to participant
037   dropout Little & Rubin (2019). In finance and economic analysis, missing data occurs frequently,
038   particularly in time-series data where gaps may arise due to reporting lags, data collection constraints,
039
      or economic events affecting data availability Garcia-Laencina et al. (2010). Additionally, computer
      vision encounters missing data when input image files are corrupted.
040
041   Prior work and limitations. Over the years, various methods have been developed to address miss-
042   ing data, but each has limitations Woods et al. (2021). For instance, (1) single imputation methods
043   Zhang (2016) can reduce the variability of the data and introduce bias due to the assumption that
044   one value can adequately replace the missing ones. (2) Deletion methods Baraldi & Enders (2010);
045
      Newman (2014) can lead to significant data loss and potential bias if the missing data are not missing
      completely at random. (3) Model based methods Enders (2022); Ma & Chen (2018) and (4) machine
046
      learning methods Khosravi et al. (2020) are computationally intensive and rely on the assumed
047
      underlying statistical model, which can lead to biased estimates and misleading conclusions. (5) Mul-
048   tiple imputation methods Schafer (1999); White et al. (2011) require complex statistical expertise to
049   implement and interpret results correctly. (6) Hybrid methods that include combinations of the afore-
050   mentioned methods can be difficult to analyze and implement, potentially requiring careful tuning to
051   balance the strengths and weaknesses of combined approaches, and some combinations can probably
052   yield incorrect solutions Elhamifar (2016). Moreover, none of these methods can handle the large
053   amounts of missing data that are present in modern datasets and that are information-theoretically
      feasible Pimentel-Alarcon & Nowak (2016).

075   would make it easier to apply LRMC to each cluster for data completion and subspace learning; on

      Architecture novelty. Another model that has shown success in the related problem of Subspace
      Clustering (SC) serves as the basis for our design, DeLUCA. Remember that when data is completely
132
      observed, SC can be thought of as the particular case of HRMC, where the objective is just to cluster
133
      the data based on a UoS. More specifically, we modify the DSC-net architecture Ji et al. (2017) by
134   introducing a unique pseudo-completion layer made up of two partially connected layers as a first
135   component. The main innovation is that the missing elements will be imputed from the normalized
136   entries of the observed data after the data has gone through the pseudo-completion layer. This makes
137   it possible for us to enter data that is incomplete into a clustering network that would not operate
138   otherwise. By including this pseudo-completion layer, our design can now smoothly handle clustering
139   and finishing at the same time.

145   This section contains a detailed presentation of our LUoS model, along with an explanation of the
146   difficulties a deep learning architecture faces in the event of missing data and how we overcome
147   them to create our DeLUCA network. Hereafter, we will use X to denote a full-data matrix of
148   size m × n, and XΩ to denote the incomplete version of X that is only observed in the entries of
149   Ω ⊂ {1, . . . , m} × {1, . . . , n}.
150   HRMC assumes that the rows of X lie near the union of K subspaces denoted by U1 , . . . , UK . Given
151   XΩ , the goals of HRMC is (a) to infer the underlying subspaces U1 , . . . , UK , (b) to cluster the
152   columns of XΩ according to their closest subspace, and (c) to complete the missing values in XΩ .
153   LUC. Unfortunately, many datasets do not lie near a UoS. However, any data is more likely to lie
154   near a non-linear structure that can be represented as a UoS in a latent space. That is because UoSs
155   are the special case of this latent model where the embedding is simply the identity map. Hence, we
156   will assume that there exists an embedding Z ∈ Rm×r of X where the rows of Z lie near the union
157   of K subspaces denoted by V1 , . . . , VK . We make no assumptions about the number of subspaces or
158   their dimensions. Given XΩ , the goals of LUC are (a) to find the embedding Z and infer the latent
159   subspaces V1 , . . . , VK , (b) to cluster the columns of Z (and by correspondence, the columns of XΩ )
160   according to their closest latent subspace, and (c) to complete the missing values in XΩ according
161   to the inverse embedding and the latent UoS. To achieve these goals, we will use a deep network
      architecture that is detailed below.

178   2.1   A RCHITECTURE
179
180   Three major parts make up our DeLUCA network (Figure 4): (i) a self-expressive layer, (ii) an
181   auto-encoder, and (iii) a pseudo-completion layer. The pseudo-completion layer, which enables the
182   handling of missing data, is where we mostly contribute. This is by no means a simple task, as each
183   sample contains uneven patterns of observed entries that disturb the data to the point where it can
184
      no longer be fed directly into the auto-encoder, as is usually the case with full-data methods such as
      DSC-net Ji et al. (2017) (for more information, see Section 2.3).
185
186   Pseudo-completion layer. This consists of two flattened, partially linked layers. In order to create a
187   completed-data matrix Xc with placeholder imputations, these layers normalize all of the observed
188   elements in XΩ . These normalized values then replace the missing entries. In this manner, the matrix
189   Xc can be smoothly fed into our architecture’s auto-encoder component. See Section 2.3 for more
190   information.
191   Auto-encoder. The auto-encoder, which performs the task of mapping the pseudo-completed data
192   into an embedding where the self-expressive layer can locate the latent UoS structure that clusters the
193   given data, is the second key element of our architecture. This auto-encoder, as usual, consists of two
194   parts. The encoder is the first component whose goals are to capture important features and patterns
195   in the input data and compress the data into a reduced dimensional space until it generates a compact
196   representation in the latent space. The second part being the decoder, which returns the data to its
197
      original space, is the second part of the auto-encoder. Completing the data in accordance with the
      latent UoS is its goal.
198
199   Self-expressive layer. The self-expressive layer is our model’s final essential element. The auto-
200   encoder contains this layer in the center, in between the encoder and the decoder. Its objective is to
201   use Sparse Subspace Clustering (SSC) to determine the UoS Elhamifar & Vidal (2013). Sparsity in
202   data representations is used in SSC, a technique for exposing UoS structures in high dimensional
203
      datasets. The method is to express each sample as a linear combination of the remaining data, so
      creating a sparse representation of each sample. Subspaces are then revealed by clustering the data
204
      according to the coefficients of these combinations.
205
206   These three layers achieve their respective tasks through a coupled loss function, detailed below.
207

      2.3   T RAINING S TRATEGY
238   The self-expressive layer requires receiving the entire dataset at once (rather than one sample at a
239   time). This is required because the layer must compute similarities between all samples to learn the
240   patterns in the observed data that will reveal the UoS. Since we are dealing with missing data, it is
241   not possible to pretrain the autoencoder, and hence, we do not pretrain our model. It also does not
242   require initialization of the model with a pre-processed dataset for performing subspace clustering
243   unlike other SC models. It is also to be noted that for training we do not have a set epoch value for
244   termination but rather the termination happens when the learning rate reaches a value of the original
245   learning rate/10. Furthermore, for the loss function, we determine the values of Θ and Φ by an
246
      iterative tuning where multiple configurations were explored, and the final values were selected based
      on their performance in minimizing the loss.
247
248   The additional challenge is that, contrary to what is frequently accomplished by other approaches
249   Yang et al. (2015), the missing entries cannot be naively supplied with zeros or means since this type
250   of imputation introduces bias and distorts the true underlying low-dimensional structure Elhamifar
251   (2016). In a similar vein, we are unable to truncate or sketch (keep only a few characteristics) due to
252
      the possibility of bias, information loss, and decreased generalization ability caused by missing data
      in every column Pimentel-Alarcón et al. (2016b).
253
254   It was decided to mask the missing entries so that their absence wouldn’t impact the weights assigned
255   to them by the network. But it needed to be done cautiously. The rationale is that every neuron
256   encodes a single complete feature. However, every feature contains a significant number of missing
257   entries under the high missing data regimes that we are interested in. Therefore, masking any neuron
258
      with missing values would mask all the neurons, meaning that there would be no active connections
      between the encoder and the input layer.
259
260   Creation of Pseudo Completion Layer. We solved the masking problem by flattening XΩ into a
261   1 × ℓ dimensional vector, where ℓ = m × n. Now each node contains one entry and all of the missing
262   entries were masked. An additional layer of 1 × ℓ dimension was introduced in the model before
263   encoder. For the earlier version of the architecture these layers were fully connected. These layers
264   also served the purpose of preserving the original shape after the masking procedure. The entries in
265   the second layer were then reshaped from 1 × ℓ into its original m × n. We termed this obtained
266
      matrix as Xc with xcj ∈ Rm representing the feature vectors of Xc . For activation, RELU activation
      function was implemented in the pseudo-completion layer.
267
268   Refining Pseudo Completion Layer. But fully connected layers created large requirements of com-
269   puting resources. To reduce this requirement, connections between initial layers were modified
      to be partially connected layers. Now each xΩ

      There have been numerous research performed in the field of high rank matrix completion. Long
282
      Short-Term Memory (LSTM) Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997) is one of the most commonly
283   used methods for this. Further there are multitude of methods which utilize autoencoders for data
284   imputation. Autoencoders are designed for learning efficient representations of data and are used for
285   imputing missing values by training the network to reconstruct the missing values as demonstrated
286   in McCoy et al. (2018). Autoencoders are also used in combination with other methods including
287   PCA, variational autoencoders, denoising autoencoders and, masked autoencoders. One of the
288   major contributions in in image reconstruction field was provided in Zheng et al. (2023) where
289   they had implemented masked autoencoders for preserving intrinsic dimension instead of pursuing
290   reconstruction in the traditional pixel level methods but this method was confined to Deep Neural
291
      Networks alone.
292   MIDAS, a neural network for data imputation was introduced recently which applies the dimensional
293   reduction strategy with an autoencoder to initially corrupt and then reconstruct the image by perform-
294   ing multiple imputations (MI) Lall & Robinson (2022). MIDAS suffers from the usual challenges
295   that MI suffer which include the inability to ignore bias and is also prone to performance loss when
296   subjected to unconventional data structures Lall & Robinson (2022).
297   Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) Goodfellow et al. (2014) are also used for matrix com-
298   pletion Zeng et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2020); Yu et al. (2019) but are majorly confined in the image
299   inpainting field. Then Yoon et al. (2018) introduced a new GAIN algorithm that outperformed all
300   the comparative models but also faced all the challenges of mode collapse where the model only
301   generates similar, instead of diverse values. Another research explored was where the structure of a
302   CNN model was inspired by autoencoders and GANs Altay & Velipasalar (2018).
303   Another new approach is the use of Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM) for data
304   imputation and image inpainting and has shown to be an alternate paradigm for GANs. The most
305   notable work presented in Lugmayr et al. (2022) in which the RePaint model solely leverages an
306   off-the-shelf unconditionally trained DDPM. Specifically, instead of learning a mask-conditional
307   generative model, they conditioned the generation process by sampling from the given pixels during
308   the reverse diffusion iterations. But, since this is an inpainting method that relies on an unconditional
309
      pretrained DDPM. Further a nre diffusion model based method was presented recently for handling
      missing data Zheng & Charoenphakdee (2023). This method could effectively handle categorical
310
      variables and numerical variables simultaneously but the model architecture is still inefficient and
311
      requires more optimization.
312
313   For subspace clustering, Sparse Subspace Clustering Elhamifar & Vidal (2013) is predominantly
314   implemented. Then Yang et al. (2015) have also implemented SSC while also presenting two
315
      new approaches labeled as SSC-MC in which they initially perform matrix completion which they
      then follow it up by implementing SSC. The second approach was termed SSC-EWZF where they
316
      implemented the zero fill method followed by SSC and these two variations of SSC performed best
317
      for high dimensional data. They have gotten good results for both synthetic and real data but face
318   the challenge of inaccurate clustering when the subspaces in the dataset are not properly separated
319   and also if the data within them are not well distributed. Another variation of SSC is provided in

321   combination with neural networks, it solely focuses on the clustering for a complete dataset. It can
322   handle non-linearity which traditional subspace clustering methods may struggle to represent. GANs
323   have also been used specifically for subspace clustering Yu et al. (2022). This paper introduced two
      new architectures for subspace clustering using GANs but focuses only on clustering.

      The subsequent sections will describe the baseline models that were used for comparison, how
342   the tests were set up, how the findings were analyzed, and the overall improvements noted in this
343   research. Interestingly, the results achieved for synthetic data are only equivalent to the baseline
344   models, even though our DeLUCA network has shown exceptional performance on real datasets. The
345   synthetic data experiments were carried out with consideration for several factors. These datasets
346   have been diligently constructed to include precisely separated clusters with almost orthogonal
347   subspaces of the same tiny dimensions. The number of subspaces, the even distribution of data
348   across subspaces, the isotropic distribution of data inside each subspace, and the known dimensions
349   were assumed. Furthermore, particular parameters were selected to maximize the performance of
350
      the baseline models—basically, customizing the experimental configuration to correspond to their
      strengths. Even under these idealized conditions, which favored the baseline models, our DeLUCA
351
      network produced competitive results. This demonstrates that even while it was tested in a setting
352
      designed to bring out the advantages of other approaches, it is flexible and resilient when dealing
353   with a wide range of datasets and circumstances.
354
355   Comparative Baselines. For the comparative analysis, synthetic and real data were provided to 10
356
      different methods to undergo data completion and data clustering. These methods can be categorized
      into two types: the first type includes methods exclusively for data completion, while the second type
357
      encompasses methods capable of both completion and clustering. The following are examples of
358
      completion-only algorithms: (1) SimpleFill: This method uses the most recent non-missing value to
359   fill in any missing values in a dataset. (2) K-Nearest Neighbors: This technique estimates missing
360   values by using the similarity between data points Troyanskaya et al. (2001). (3) Iterative Imputer:
361   Using a sophisticated imputation method, each feature with missing data is modeled as a function of
362   other characteristics, allowing for the iterative prediction of missing values Van Buuren & Groothuis-
363   Oudshoorn (2011). (4) SoftImpute: A penalty term is incorporated while the rank of the finished
364   matrix is minimized to recover missing points Mazumder et al. (2010). (5) MIDAS: To corrupt and
365   recover data, this method uses denoising autoencoders Lall & Robinson (2022).The remaining five
366   models, which are able to do both subspace clustering and reconstruction, belong to the second group.
367
      These models, which are covered in the related work section, include SSC-MC, SSC-EWFZ, GSSC,

471
      In this paper we introduced our novel DeLUCA architecture which finds a UoS in a latent space
472
      that can fit a non-linear embedding of the original data and performs sparse subspace clustering.
473
      We have presented a novel Pseudo-completion layer designed to effectively handle missing data,
474   complemented by an autoencoder that uses the inherent self-expressiveness of latent subspaces for
475   both clustering and data reconstruction tasks. Throughout our research, we encountered significant
476   challenges associated with missing data handling and observed that our model’s performance is
477   highly contingent upon the selection of hyperparameters, spanning network architecture and, learning
478   rates. And impressively our experimental findings showcase remarkable enhancements in data
479   reconstruction, surpassing existing models by margins ranging from 5% to 60%, while achieving
480   substantial improvements in clustering performance, with enhancements ranging from 40% to 80%.
