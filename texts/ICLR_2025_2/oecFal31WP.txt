009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The rapid evolution of large language models (LLMs) holds promise for reforming
012            the methodology of spatio-temporal data mining. However, current works for
013            evaluating the spatio-temporal understanding capability of LLMs are somewhat
014            limited and biased. These works either fail to incorporate the latest language
015            models or only focus on assessing the memorized spatio-temporal knowledge. To
016            address this gap, this paper dissects LLMs’ capability of spatio-temporal data into
017            four distinct dimensions: knowledge comprehension, spatio-temporal reasoning,
018            accurate computation, and downstream applications. We curate several natural
019            language question-answer tasks for each category and build the benchmark dataset,
020
               namely STBench, containing 15 distinct tasks and over 70,000 QA pairs. Moreover,
               we have assessed the capabilities of 13 LLMs, such as GPT-4o, Gemma and Mistral.
021
               Experimental results reveal that existing LLMs show remarkable performance on
022
               knowledge comprehension and spatio-temporal reasoning tasks, with potential for
023            further enhancement on other tasks through in-context learning, chain-of-though
024            prompting, and fine-tuning. The code and datasets of STBench are released on
025            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/STBench-14C2.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) has opened up new possibilities across
032   various domains (Wang et al., 2024; Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023). One promising
033
      direction is enhancing spatio-temporal data analysis with the ability of LLMs (Li et al., 2024b; 2023;
      Manvi et al., 2023). Spatio-temporal data, characterized by both spatial and temporal dimensions,
034
      encompasses a variety of datasets crucial for many fields such as geography, meteorology, trans-
035
      portation, and epidemiology. Despite LLMs’ remarkable proficiency in language-related tasks, their
036   applicability and effectiveness in handling spatio-temporal data remain relatively unexplored.
037
038   Existing evaluations of spatio-temporal data fall in two categorizes. The first category (Shi et al.,
039
      2022; Mirzaee & Kordjamshidi, 2022; Li et al., 2024a) focus on evaluating the spatial analysis
      capability of LLMs and design QA pairs of spatial reasoning such as asking “Is the yellow apple to
040
      the west of the yellow watermelon?”. The QA pairs are constructed in toy environments without
041
      temporal information, which is insufficient to assess the ability of LLM on real spatio-temporal
042   tasks. The second category (Gurnee & Tegmark, 2023; Yamada et al., 2023) aims to evaluate the
043   spatio-temporal analysis capability but only assesses the abilities of LLMs’ in one specific dimension.
044   For example, the most recent work (Gurnee & Tegmark, 2023) tends to evaluate the memory ability
045   of spatio-temporal knowledge. For a comprehensive evaluation, we argue that the abilities of LLMs
046   in spatio-temporal analysis should contain not only the memory ability but also other dimensions,
047   such as reasoning and knowledge comprehension.
048   To achieve this goal, we propose a framework, namely STBench, for evaluating the spatio-temporal
049   capabilities of LLMs. As shown in Figure 1, STBench dissects the LLMs’ capacity into four distinct
050   dimensions: knowledge comprehension, spatio-temporal reasoning, accurate computation, and
051   downstream applications. Knowledge Comprehension examines the model’s capacity to understand
052   and interpret the underlying meaning and context of spatio-temporal information. Spatio-Temporal
053   Reasoning evaluates the ability to understand and reason about the spatial and temporal relationships
      between entities and events. Accurate Computation handles the precise and complex calculations of

091   The contributions of this paper are summarized as following:
092
093            • This paper presents STBench, a comprehensive benchmarking framework designed to
094              evaluate the spatio-temporal analysis capabilities of LLMs. STBench is both user-friendly
095              and highly extensible, allowing users to effortlessly reproduce experimental results across 13
096              LLMs and 15 tasks with a single script. Its modular design facilitates the seamless addition

102              the potential of LLMs in spatio-temporal analysis.
103            • Extensive experiments are conducted and the results highlight the remarkable performance
104              of LLMs in knowledge comprehension and spatio-temporal reasoning tasks, while also
105              identifying areas for improvement in accurate computation and downstream applications. It
106              reveals the great potential of LLMs in spatio-temporal analysis.

111   The rapid development of large-scale language models has attracted widespread interest from various
112   communities (Zhang et al., 2024b; Jin et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a; Kasneci et al., 2023). Many
113   researchers studied the capabilities of LLMs (Chang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2021; 2024) and some
114   of them investigated the potential in spatio-temporal mining.
115   Spatial analysis capabilities. (Mirzaee et al., 2021) proposed a question-answering (QA) benchmark
116   for spatial reasoning with natural language texts. (Shi et al., 2022) presented a QA dataset to evaluate
117   language models’ capability of multi-hop spatial reasoning. (Mirzaee & Kordjamshidi, 2022)
118   provided two datasets about spatial question answering and spatial role labeling problems. (Li et al.,
119   2024a) further improved a previous benchmark to provide a more accurate assessment. However,
120   these works only focus on spatial reasoning in toy environments. They ignore the temporal dimension
121
      and are far from the real scenarios of spatio-temporal applications.
122   Spatio-temporal analysis capabilities. (Ji & Gao, 2023) evaluated the ability of LLMs to represent
123   geometric shapes and spatial relationships. (MOONEY et al., 2023) examines the performance of
124   ChatGPT in a geographic information systems exam to evaluate its spatial literacy. (Roberts et al.,
125   2023a) investigates the geographic capabilities of GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) through a series of qualita-
126   tive and quantitative experiments. (Gurnee & Tegmark, 2023) analyzes the learned representations
127
      of several spatial and temporal datasets by training linear regression probes. (Yamada et al., 2023)
      evaluates the ability of LLMs to represent and reason about spatial structures, such as squares and
128
      hexagons. (Hochmair et al., 2024) assesses four closed-source LLMs on a set of tasks, primarily
129
      focusing on coding capabilities, such as code interpretation and code generation. These works either
130   only analyze a specific model or only examine the capabilities of a specific aspect, failing to provide
131   a comprehensive evaluation of the latest closed-source and open-source LLMs. There are two most
132   relevant works and one of which is (Roberts et al., 2023b), which assesses the geographic and geospa-
133   tial capabilities of multimodal LLMs. Their tasks are completely designed for multimodal models
134   and are not applicable to single-modal large language models. The other one is (Feng et al., 2024),
135   which design 7 tasks in 2 categories of perception-understanding and decision-making to evaluate
136   the capability of LLMs. To comprehensively assess the spatio-temporal ability of LLMs, in this
137   paper, we categorize the spatial-temporal abilities into four dimensions: knowledge comprehension,
138
      spatio-temporal reasoning, accurate computation and downstream applications. Based on this, we
      propose a benchmark consisting of 15 tasks and over 70,000 QA pairs. We benchmarked 13 latest
139
      LLMs to assess their capabilities and to investigate their potential in spatio-temporal mining.

      In spatio-temporal data mining, concepts such as Point of Interest (POI) and trajectory play a funda-
      mental role in representing and analyzing spatio-temporal data. Before presenting the construction
147
      methodology of our benchmark, we formally define these concepts in this section.
148
149    D EFINITION 1 (Point of Interest): A point of interest (POI) is a specific geographic location
150   p =< ip , latp , lonp , cp , Mp >, where ip is the ID number, latp is the latitude, lonp is the longitude,
151   cp denotes the category of this POI and Mp = {m1 , m2 , · · · } is a set of comments about this POI.
152
153    D EFINITION 2 (Trajectory): Each trajectory t =< t1 , t2 , · · · > is a sequence of points, where each
154   point ti =< lati , loni , timei > is a triplet of latitude, longitude and timestamp.
155
156    D EFINITION 3 (Region): A region is a defined area that is distinct from its surroundings. Each
157   region r =< br , cr , Pr > is characterized by its boundary lines br and the region function category
158   cr . The set Pr = {p1 , p2 , · · · } denotes the POIs that fall in this region.
159
160    D EFINITION 4 (Inflow/Outflow): The inflow Iir and outflow Oir are defined as the number of
161   trajectories entering and leaving a specific region r within the i-th time interval, respectively.

      4       B ENCHMARK C ONSTRUCTION
173
174
      In this section, we propose a benchmark, STBench, to assess the ability of LLMs in spatio-temporal
175   analysis. We will begin by presenting the considerations that guide the design of STBench. Subse-
176   quently, we will delve into a detailed exposition of the construction of STBench.
177
178
      4.1       OVERVIEW
179
180   To construct a benchmark for assessing the ability of LLMs in spatio-temporal data, we should first
181   consider the evaluation tasks and the data format.
182
      Ability Categories. Choosing or designing appropriate tasks is crucial for assessing the ability of
183
      LLMs in spatio-temporal data mining. Real-world applications often require a mixture of multiple
184   abilities, e.g., the POI recommendation task requires both the knowledge comprehension ability to
185   understand the semantics of different POI categories and the spatio-temporal reasoning ability to infer
186   the mobility patterns. Thus it is difficult to separately evaluate each capability dimension of LLMs
187   and analyze their strengths and weaknesses solely based on real-world tasks. Therefore, to provide
188   a comprehensive evaluation, we categorize the requisite abilities into four dimensions: knowledge
189   comprehension, spatio-temporal reasoning, accurate computation, and downstream applications. For
190   each category, we design several tasks for assessment.
191   Data Format. Another important question is what data format we should adopt. There are some
192   problems if we directly ask the model through dialogue and allow open-ended answers. Firstly, the
193   response of LLMs is uncontrolled. For instance, models may only apologize for not being able to
194   provide an accurate answer, rather than directly responding to our question. Moreover, open-ended
195   answers make it difficult to identify the final answer of LLMs, e.g., LLMs may reply with a lot of
196   explanation or even some unrelated content. Therefore, we have LLMs complete the input texts,
197   rather than asking LLMs through dialogue. As shown in Table 1, each data sample in STBench
198
      consists of three parts: the question, the options and the guidance. The LLMs should continue the
      guidance text, i.e., they should generate an option number, thus the output is controllable. Note that
199
      some chat models do not support text completion, thus we instruct these models to complete the texts
200
      through system prompts. The details are in Appendix A in the supplementary material.
201

      4.2       K NOWLEDGE COMPREHENSION
203
204   The model’s capacity to understand and interpret the underlying meaning and context of spatio-
205   temporal information is important. This involves the ability to comprehend the semantic nuances
206   within the data and the knowledge of relevant spatio-temporal concepts and entities, e.g., under-
207   standing and distinguishing different POI categories. We provide valuable insights into LLMs’
208   spatio-temporal knowledge comprehension capabilities through four tasks: POI category recognition,
209   POI identification, urban region function recognition, and administrative region determination.
210   POI Category Recognition (PCR). The semantics of POI are crucial in various applications
211   such as POI recommendation, thus we design this task to evaluate LLM’s understanding of POI
212   semantics. Data samples of this task are generated based on the public Yelp dataset2 . Specifi-
213   cally, we randomly sample some POIs from the Yelp dataset for data construction. For each POI
214   p =< ip , latp , lonp , cp , Mp >, we randomly select two comments mi1 , mi2 from the comment

245   4.3      S PATIO - TEMPORAL REASONING
246
      Spatio-temporal reasoning encompasses the ability to understand and reason about the spatial and
247
      temporal relationships between entities and events. For example, given a POI and some regions, LLMs
248   should determine which region the POI falls in according to their coordinates and boundary lines.
249   We design four tasks to assess the spatio-temporal reasoning ability of large language models: point-
250   trajectory relationship detection, point-region relationship detection, trajectory-region relationship
251   detection and trajectory identification.
252
      Point-Trajectory Relationship Detection (PTRD). The task is to determine whether a trajectory
253
      passes through a point. To generate a data sample, we downsample the trajectory in the public
254   Xi’an dataset4 into a shorter trajectory t = {t1 , · · · , tn } and construct five points as options. We
255   take < (lati + lati+1 )/2, (loni + loni+1 )/2 > as the true option, where < lati , loni > and
256   < lati+1 , loni+1 > are two adjacent points in the trajectory. To construct an error option, we sample
257   a point tj =< latj , lonj , timej > from the trajectory and perturb its coordinates with Gaussian
258   noise, i.e., the error option is < latj + ϵ1 , lonj + ϵ2 >, where ϵ1 , ϵ2 ∼ N (0.01, 0.001).
259
      Point-Region Relationship Detection (PRRD). Given a point and several regions, this task aims
260   to infer which region the point falls in. To generate a data sample, we select i regions {r1 , · · · , ri }
261   located in the same city from the EULUC dataset Gong et al. (2020). Then, a region rj is chosen
262   from these i regions and we randomly selected a point p in region rj . The coordinates of point p and
263   the boundary lines of i regions are used to generate the question texts, and all i regions are provided
264   as options. We construct four sub-datasets by varying the value of i from 2 to 5.
265
      Trajectory-Region Relationship Detection (TRRD). Given a trajectory and some regions, this task
266   aims to determine which regions the trajectory has passed through chronologically. To construct a
267   data sample, we randomly select five regions {r1 , · · · , r5 } located in the same city from the EULUC

      Trajectory Identification (TI). In this task, we ask LLMs to determine if two point sequences
      t′ and t′′ are sampled from the same trajectory. We propose two strategies to construct positive

278   use t′ =< t1 , t2 , t3 , · · · > and t′′ =< t1 , t3 , t5 , · · · > to generate the question, while the staggered
279   sampling strategy use t′ =< t1 , t3 , t5 , · · · > and t′′ =< t2 , t4 , t6 , · · · > to generate the question. To
280   construct negative samples, we downsample a trajectory t into t′ and add temporal offsets or spatial
281   offsets to t′ to obtain t′′ .

      4.4   ACCURATE COMPUTATION
285
      In the context of handling spatial-temporal data, accurate computation plays a pivotal role. It focuses
286
      on the model’s capability to perform precise and complex calculations related to spatial-temporal
287
      data. We include three tasks that challenge the model’s accuracy in spatial-temporal computations for
288   assessment: direction determination, navigation and trajectory-trajectory relationship detection.
289
290   Direction Determination (DD). This task is to determine the direction between two geographical
291
      points. To create a data sample, two POIs are randomly chosen from the Yelp dataset, and the model
      is asked to calculate the corresponding azimuth and to determinate their relative direction based
292
      on the calculation result. Eight options are provided for all data samples: north, south, west, east,
293
      northeast, northwest, southeast and southwest.
294
295   Navigation (NAV). In this task, LLMs are asked to plan a shortest route from a source point to a
296   destination point based on a given road network. To construct a data sample, we randomly selected n
297
      points p1 , · · · , pn from a given region, interconnect them to form a complete graph, and subsequently
      apply Kruskal’s algorithm (Kruskal, 1956) to derive the minimum spanning tree G from this complete
298
      graph. We add edges to G so that we can obtain a connected graph G′ with 1.5n edges. We randomly
299
      sample two points ps and pd as the source point and destination point, and ask LLMs which edge
300   connecting to ps is on the shortest path. All edges connecting to ps are provided as options. There are
301   two sub-datasets, i.e., edges with weights and edges without weights, where LLMs need to minimize
302   the length or hop count of the route, respectively.
303
      Trajectory-Trajectory Relationship Analysis (TTRA). This task is to calculate the number of
304
      times two trajectories encounter each other. To construct a data sample, we generate two trajectories
305
      t =< t1 , · · · , tn > and t′ =< t′1 , · · · , t′n > through random walks within a certain area. We count
306   it as an encounter if ti ti+1 and t′j t′j+1 intersect in space and overlap in time, where 1 ≤ i, j ≤ n − 1.
307   We provided the ground truth and other four wrong answers as options.

310   4.5   D OWNSTREAM A PPLICATIONS
311
312   Downstream tasks require the model to not only understand the spatial-temporal context but also apply
313   this understanding to practical applications. We assess this aspect of LLMs through four downstream
314
      tasks: flow prediction, trajectory anomaly detection, trajectory classification and trajectory prediction.
315   Flow Prediction (FP). This task requests LLMs to predict the future inflows and outflows based on
316   the historical inflows and outflows. Specifically, to construct a data sample, we randomly select a
                                                                                             r         r
317   region r and a timestamp t from TaxiBJ (Zhang et al., 2017) and ask LLMs to predict It+i    and Ot+i
                                                                                    r                  r
318   (1 ≤ i ≤ 6) of the next 6 time intervals according to the historical inflows It−j and outflows Ot−j
319   (0 ≤ j ≤ 12) over the past 12 time intervals.
320   Trajectory Anomaly Detection (TAD). In order to detect anomalous trajectories, LLMs should
321   infer the underlying route and shape from trajectory data. We consider trajectories in Xi’an dataset
322   as normal and perform detours to generate anomalous samples. Specifically, given a trajectory
323   t =< t1 , · · · , tn >, we identify the direction perpendicular to the line connecting t1 and tn , and
      move the middle one-third of the trajectory along this direction to generate an anomalous sample.

      Trajectory Classification (TC). This task requires the model to comprehensively consider the
325   coordinates, length, speed and other relevant information to distinguish different trajectories. We
326   construct dataset for this task based on the Geolife dataset5 . Due to the input length limitation of
327   LLMs, we downsample each trajectory and ask LLMs to infer what generates the trajectory. Three
328   options are provided: bike, car and pedestrian.
329
      Trajectory Prediction (TP). This task is to predict the next point based on the historical points of
330
      a trajectory, which involves the ability to model the trajectory patterns and the moving speed. We
331   construct data samples for this task based on the trajectories in the Xi’an dataset. Specifically, we
332   first downsample the each trajectory with a time interval of 30 seconds. Then, for each trajectory
333   t =< t1 , t2 , · · · , tn >, we ask LLMs to predict the coordinates of tj according to the historical
334   points < t1 , · · · , tj−1 >, where 3 ≤ j ≤ n. Note that we do not provide options in this task.

338
339
      We conduct extensive experiments on STBench to evaluate the spatial-temporal ability of LLMs and
      to investigate if in-context learning, chain-of-thought and fine-tuning can improve the performance.

341
342   5.1       E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
343
      Evaluated models. We evaluate the performance of two closed-source model, i.e., ChatGPT and
344
      GPT-4o, and a set of open-source models: Llama-2 (Touvron et al., 2023), Vicuna6 , Gemma (Mes-
345   nard et al., 2024), Phi-2, ChatGLM2, ChatGLM3, (Du et al., 2022; Zeng et al., 2023), Mis-

347   et al., 2023) and Yi (Young et al., 2024). More introduction to these models can be found in Appendix
348   B.1 in the supplementary material.
349
      Metrics. We adopt accuracy for tasks other than trajectory prediction and flow prediction. For
350
      trajectory prediction, we report absolute error, i.e., the distance in meters between the predicted
351   coordinates and ground truth. For flow prediction, we adopt MAE and RMSE as the metrics.
352
353   Experimental details. In our experiments, we adopt the precision of FP32 for all LLMs. For all tasks
354
      except trajectory prediction, LLMs are expected to answer an option or “Yes”/“No”, thus we set the
      max new tokens to 15, i.e., the maximum length of the generated new tokens is 15. For trajectory
355
      prediction and flow prediction, we set the max new tokens to 50. For other hyperparameters, we
356
      adopt the default value of each model. All experiments of open source models are conducted on two
357   NVIDIA H100.

499   5.5   F INE - TUNING E VALUATION
500
501   While in-context learning and chain-of-thought is less effective for smaller models, we conduct
502   experiments to investigate if fine-tuning can significantly improve the performance on STBench.
503   Specifically, we select several tasks and follow the construction strategies in Section 4 to generate
504   1,2000 samples as the training dataset for each task. We adopt QLoRA Dettmers et al. (2023) to
505   fine-tune the model on the training dataset for each task, with the learning rate of 2e-4, the rank
506
      of 8 and NF4 quantization. Due to the very high computational cost and memory usage, we only
      fine-tune a 2B model for evaluation, i.e., Gemma-2B. To compare the fine-tuned LLM with existing
507
      supervised methods, we train two effective flow prediction method, i.e., STID (Shao et al., 2022) and
508
      PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023), on the same dataset. The results are shown in Fig. 2(c) and Table 4.
509
510   The performance on all tasks in Fig. 2 is significantly improved after fine-tuning. For instance, the
511   accuracy on administrative region determination and direction determination increased from 19.89%
512
      to 91.98%, and from 19.72% to 47.08%, respectively. For trajectory prediction, Gemma-2B achieves
      the absolute error of 147.8 meters, which is better than all 7B models in Table 3. This confirms LLMs’
513
      potential in spatial-temporal analysis and existing LLMs’ lack of training on relevant corpora.
514
515   As shown in Table 4, the zero-shot capability of LLMs is surprising that Phi-2 (without fine-tuning
516   and few-shot prompting) can surpass the supervised method STID. While Gemma-2B performs
517   poorer than both STID and PatchTST, it outperforms STID and achieved comparable performance to
518
      PatchTST after supervised fine-tuning. Overall, the experimental results reveal the bright prospects
      of LLMs in spatio-temporal data analysis.

521   6     C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this work, we propose STBench to assess LLMs’ ability in spatio-temporal analysis. STBench
524   consists of 15 tasks and over 70,000 QA pairs, systematically evaluating four dimensions: knowledge
525   comprehension, spatio-temporal reasoning, accurate computation, and downstream applications.
526   We benchmark 13 latest LLMs and the results show their remarkable performance on knowledge
527   comprehension and spatio-temporal reasoning tasks. Our further experiments with in-context learning,
528   chain-of-thought prompting and fine-tuning also prove the great potential of LLMs on other tasks.
