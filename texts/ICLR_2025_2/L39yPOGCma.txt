009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent studies have shown that vector representations of embeddings learned by
012            pre-trained large language models (LLMs) are effective in various downstream
013            tasks in numerical domains. Despite their significant benefits, the tendency of
014            LLMs to hallucinate in such domains can have severe consequences in applica-
015            tions like finance, energy, retail, climate science, wireless networks, synthetic tab-
016
               ular generation, among others. To guarantee prediction reliability and accuracy
               in numerical domains, it is necessary to have performance guarantees through ex-
017
               plainability. However, there is little theoretical understanding of when pre-trained
018
               language models help solve numeric downstream tasks. This paper seeks to bridge
019            this gap by understanding when the next-word prediction capability of LLMs can
020            be adapted to numerical domains through the lens of isotropy. Specifically, we first
021            provide a general numeric data generation process that captures the core charac-
022            teristics of numeric data across various numerical domains. Then, we consider
023            a log-linear model for LLMs in which numeric data can be predicted from its
024            context through a network with softmax as its last layer. We demonstrate that,
025            in order to achieve state-of-the-art performance in numerical domains, the hidden
026            representations of the LLM embeddings must possess a structure that accounts
027
               for the shift-invariance of the softmax function. We show how the isotropic prop-
               erty of LLM embeddings preserves the underlying structure of representations,
028
               thereby resolving the shift-invariance problem problem of softmax function. In
029
               other words, isotropy allows numeric downstream tasks to effectively leverage
030            pre-trained representations, thus providing performance guarantees in the numeri-
031            cal domain. Experiments show that different characteristics of numeric data could
032            have different impacts on isotropy.

037   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated broad success in adapting to various downstream

039

053   ding space Arora et al. (2016); Mu & Viswanath (2018). In particular, we first introduce a general

      1.1   R ELATED W ORKS
109
110   The most relevant works Arora et al. (2016; 2017); Brown et al. (2020); Cai et al. (2021); Gao et al.
111   (2019); Ethayarajh (2019); Rajaee & Pilehvar (2021) are primarily focused the natural language
112   processing (NLP) domain and depend on latent variable model. For instance, in Arora et al. (2016),
113   the latent variable model is employed to explain and unify diverse word embedding algorithms.
114   This theoretical framework is further extended to justify sentence embedding methods, either by
115   leveraging the latent variable model Arora et al. (2017) or through the lens of compressed sensing
116
      Arora et al. (2018). Language modeling can also be used to exploit hidden representations, as the
      probability of the next word is typically computed as the softmax of the product of the hidden
117
      representation and the dictionary matrix. Consequently, any zero-shot application of pre-trained
118
      autoregressive language models, such as GPT-3 Brown et al. (2020) and T5 Raffel et al. (2020), can
119   be used as a suitable method of exploiting the hidden representations.
120
121   Isotropy, on the other hand, often makes the embedding space more effectively utilized and more ro-
122
      bust to perturbations, i.e., no extreme directions that can cause numerical instability Ji et al. (2023).
      In Cai et al. (2021), isotropy is found withing the clusters in the contextual embedding space (i.e.,
123
      local assessment), as opposed to the previous study of anisotropy caused be the misleading isolated
124
      clusters (i.e., global assessment) in Gao et al. (2019); Ethayarajh (2019). Motivated by the local as-
125   sessment findings in Cai et al. (2021), a local cluster-based method is proposed in Rajaee & Pilehvar
126   (2021) to address the degeneration problem that makes the embedding space less isotropic. How-
127   ever, the prior art in Ji et al. (2023); Cai et al. (2021) relies on the isotropy assessment in the NLP
128   domain only and no assessment has been done so far in numerical domains. Our work is the first to
129   theoretically analyze the efficacy of pre-trained LLMs on numeric downstream through isotropy.

136   various numerical domains. For instance, the primary characteristics of the numeric data are typ-
137   ically dynamic, noisy, time-varying, and often subject to interference. Let xt be an observation
138   of some numeric data (e.g., stock price, financial index, or received signals from sensors) at time
139   instance t. We can express a general numeric numeric data generation process as follows:

142   where st is the true underlying signal (e.g., true asset price, financial index, or received sensor
143   signals) that we want to observe, ht represents the environmental or system dynamics that modify

145   (e.g., random fluctuations or market noise, environmental noise, measurement errors), and ◦ is the

      3.1   N EED OF STRUCTURE IN LEARNED REPRESENTATIONS
231
232   Observation 1: The hidden representations of LLM embeddings must exhibit structure to address
233   the shift-invariance problem of the softmax function. Without such structure, the model can shift the
234   logits while keeping the training loss unchanged and leaving the logits ineffective for the numeric
235   downstream tasks.
236

237   softmax layer and the numeric downstream task is determined by the function of the logits. The
238   underlying relation between the logits and softmax function determines the performance of the nu-
239   meric downstream tasks. However, the softmax function is shift-invariant, that is, the output of the

267
268   Observation 2: The isotropic property of LLM embeddings preserves the underlying structure of the
269   representations by approximating the partition function (in equation 3) with a constant for different

479   5    C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
480   Isotropy in embeddings as studied here can serve as a foundation for future research on the deeper
481   understanding of LLMs and their applications in various domains. Beyond isotropy, there could be
482   other methods to approximate the partition function with a constant and make the logits useful for
483   the numeric downstream tasks. Moreover, our isotropy study only ensured the existence of structure
484   in the LLMs’ hidden representations and provides a performance guarantee when the structure is
485   preserved by isotropy. Improving the numeric downstream task performance when structure is not
      preserved in the LLM representations is a topic of future work.
