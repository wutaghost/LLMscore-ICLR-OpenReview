009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Geodemographic analysis is essential for understanding population characteris-
012            tics and addressing socio-economic disparities across regions. However, limited
013            research has been conducted on modelling changes in demographic data over time
014            using Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). In this study, we address this gap by
015            leveraging GNNs to model correlations between the 2011 census data (England
016            & Wales), observing changes over time, and the Output Area Classification 2021,
017            which reflects socio-economic differences between Output Areas. We propose a
018
               novel framework that utilises Supervised Contrastive Learning on graphs to ob-
               tain robust OA embeddings, with a particular focus on improving the model’s
019
               performance for minority classes. To evaluate the effectiveness of our frame-
020
               work, we conducted two downstream tasks based on the 2021 OA embeddings.
021            Our results demonstrate that the proposed approach provides valuable insights
022            for geodemographic analysis and offers policymakers a useful tool for assessing
023            socio-economic transitions over time, and planning ahead on the basis of it.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Demographics is the study of the statistical characteristics of populations, including factors such
029   as age, gender, and income. These data are often utilised to understand population trends, fore-
030   cast changes Mielczarek & Zabawa (2021), and guide policy decisions. Geodemographics extends
031   demographic analysis by integrating geographical and sociological data, enabling the study of pop-
032   ulations within specific locations.
033   Geodemographic analysis is essential for understanding the spatial distribution of population char-
034   acteristics, providing a detailed view of socio-economic diversity and its geographic implications
035   (Birkin & Clarke, 2009; Singleton & Longley, 2009; Webber, 2007). By integrating demographic
036   data with geographic information, geodemographics uncovers patterns related to inequality, access
037   to services, and consumer behaviour in specific locations and across locations. These insights en-
038   able policymakers and businesses to make informed, location-based decisions—whether for public
039
      health initiatives (Abbas et al., 2009; Guan, 2020; Brown et al., 1991), urban planning (Batey, 2022),
      or market segmentation (Farr et al., 2008). The ability to analyse data at multiple geographic scales,
040
      from neighbourhoods to regions, enhances the effectiveness of targeting interventions and allocating
041
      resources to areas with diverse socio-economic profiles. Overall, spatial analysis through geodemo-
042   graphics is crucial for addressing regional disparities and improving service delivery by tailoring
043   resources to meet the specific needs of different communities.
044
045
      Geodemographic data fundamentally consists of two primary components: the spatial relationships
      between geographic areas and the demographic characteristics of those areas. Spatial relationships
046
      refer to the connections and proximities between locations, such as neighbourhoods or regions, while
047
      demographic characteristics encompass variables including income levels, age distributions, educa-
048   tional attainment, and housing attributes. Traditionally, the analysis of geodemographic data using
049   computational models necessitates a significant preprocessing phase. This phase often includes ex-
050   tensive feature engineering, where spatial relationships are carefully integrated into the attribute data
051   to enable models to capture the intricate correlations between geographic and demographic factors
052   effectively (De Sabbata & Liu, 2023; Wyszomierski et al., 2024). Although essential for developing
053   accurate and predictive models, this process is time-intensive and requires substantial expertise in
      both data science and domain-specific knowledge.

      The emergence of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) has introduced a transformative paradigm in
055   geodemographic studies, providing a novel methodological approach (De Sabbata & Liu, 2023).
056   GNNs are specifically designed to process structured data in an end-to-end manner, reducing the
057   need for extensive manual feature engineering. Since geodemographic data can naturally be rep-
058   resented as graphs—capturing the relationships between nodes, such as the connections between
059   geographic areas—GNNs allow for the development of more robust models capable of addressing
060   complex social challenges. This approach enables the detection of patterns that were previously
061   difficult to discern using traditional methods, representing a significant advancement in the field and
062   enabling more sophisticated and precise analyses of geodemographic data.
063   In this research, we construct graphs using datasets from the 2011 and 2021 Censuses of England
064   and Wales (ONS, 2011), along with geospatial data for Output Areas (OAs) from the same years.
065   An OA is the smallest census geographic unit, each comprising 40 to 250 households (ONS, 2011).
066   Each node in the graph represents an OA, with adjacency relationships determined using Queen’s
067   contiguity (Gwani & Sek, 2024). Node attributes are derived from census data, covering various
068   sectors such as employment, residence, living arrangements, ethnicity, and origins. These attributes
069
      are aligned with the data selection criteria of the 2021 UK Output Area Classification (OAC) (Wys-
      zomierski et al., 2024), produced through a collaboration between the Office for National Statistics
070
      and University College London.
071
072   As mentioned earlier, a key objective of geodemographics is to identify changes and observe regional
073   differences. However, limited research has focused on utilising GNNs to model the relationship be-
074   tween changes in census data and regional differences, while incorporating geographic information.
075
      In this study, we address this gap by using GNNs to model the correlations between the 2011 census
      data, observed changes over time, and the OAC, which reflects the differences between OAs in 2021.
076
077   However, GNNs often face two critical issues: oversmoothing (Rusch et al., 2023) and oversquash-
078   ing (Nguyen et al., 2023). These issues can degrade learning performance and lead to indistinguish-
079   able embeddings. Oversmoothing is especially problematic in imbalanced datasets (Yang et al.,
080   2023), where nodes from minority classes are overwhelmed by the majority, resulting in a loss of
081
      important information. In OAC 2011 and 2021, the minority classes represent more deprived groups
      (e.g., Hard-Pressed Living and Legacy Communities), and it is crucial that they are not ignored.
082
083   As noted in (Nguyen et al., 2023), graphs with positive curvature bring nodes closer together, ac-
084   celerating the mixing of information. This can lead to oversmoothing, where node representations
085   become overly similar, preventing the model from distinguishing between different nodes. Con-
086   versely, graphs with negative curvature or stretched structures tend to distribute information un-
087
      evenly, leading to oversquashing. In this case, information from distant nodes is compressed into
      a few intermediary nodes, limiting the model’s ability to represent all relationships. Figure 1 illus-
088
      trates the curvature of edges in the UK graph, highlighting its complex structure and the potential
089
      for oversmoothing and oversquashing during GNN training.
090
091   To address these challenges, we propose a framework using Supervised Contrastive Learning (SCL)
092   (Khosla et al., 2020) on graphs to obtain embeddings for OAs. The 2011 OA embeddings are
093
      learned through SCL, using 2011 OA graph as input and the 2011 OAC (Gale et al., 2016) as labels.
      The 2021 OA embeddings are then generated by incorporating both the 2011 OA embeddings and
094
      changes in OA attributes between 2011 and 2021, with the 2021 OAC as labels. We hypothesise
095
      that the 2011 OA embeddings implicitly capture the socio-economic conditions of the OAs at that
096   time, providing valuable insights into their status. By combining the 2011 OA embeddings with the
097   changes in OA attributes over the decade, we aim to infer the states of OAs in 2021, where the new
098   states are represented by the corresponding labels. While SCL was originally designed for image
099   classification tasks, we extend its application to graph data. Our motivation for using SCL is to
100   improve the model’s performance for minority classes by mapping those embeddings into a more
101   distinguishable feature space. By pulling together embeddings of the same classes while pushing
102   apart those of different classes, SCL helps ensure minority class embeddings are not overwhelmed
103   by majority classes.
104   Moreover, we conducted both supervised and unsupervised classification tasks using the 2021 OA
105   embeddings to demonstrate the advantages of our proposed framework for geodemographic analysis.
106   First, we trained and tested a fully-connected neural network using these embeddings to predict the
107   2021 labels (Wyszomierski et al., 2024). This approach provides policymakers and stakeholders

146   2.1   G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
147
      As research into deep learning methods for graph data continues to expand, more and more GNN
148
      solutions are being developed (Wu et al., 2020). Driven by advances in convolutional neural net-
149
      works(CNNs) (LeCun et al., 1995) and variational autoencoders(VAE) (Kingma & Welling, 2013),
150   researchers have created new methods for summarizing and manipulating graph data. Currently,
151   common GNN solutions include GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016a), VGAE (Kipf & Welling, 2016b)
152   inspired by VAE, Graph Transformer (Shi et al., 2020) which inspired by the Transformer model
153   (Vaswani, 2017), and GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017).
154
      Kipf & Welling (2016a) present a scalable approach for semi-supervised learning on graph-
155
      structured data using an efficient variant of convolutional neural networks that operate directly on
156   graphs. This model scales linearly in the number of graph edges, and the learned hidden layer rep-
157   resentations encode both local graph structure and node features. Variational Graph Auto-Encoders
158   (VGAEs) represent a robust methodology in graph representation learning by integrating variational
159   inference with GNNs to derive latent representations useful for node classification, link prediction,
160   and graph generation. First introduced by Kipf & Welling (2016b), VGAEs employ a Graph Con-
161   volutional Network (GCN) for the encoder, facilitating efficient unsupervised learning with graph-
      structured data. Concurrently, GraphSAGE Hamilton et al. (2017), another pivotal development in


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      graph representation learning, specialises in creating node embeddings for expansive and intricate
163   graphs. GraphSAGE’s innovation is its use of inductive learning, which, unlike transductive meth-
164   ods that learn specific embeddings for each node, learns a function to produce embeddings based on
165   the attributes of nodes and the characteristics of their surrounding local network.
166
      To address the challenges of combining feature and label propagation, the proposed Unified Message
167
      Passing Model (UniMP) Shi et al. (2020) introduces a Graph Transformer network that takes both
168
      node features and label embeddings as input, unifying feature and label propagation within a single
169   framework. To prevent overfitting from self-loop label information, UniMP incorporates a masked
170   label prediction strategy, where a portion of the input labels is randomly masked, and the model is
171   trained to predict these masked labels. By effectively combining the complementary strengths of
172   GNNs and Label Propagation Algorithms (LPAs), UniMP achieves state-of-the-art results on semi-
173   supervised classification benchmarks in the Open Graph Benchmark.

175   2.2   C LUSTERING A LGORITHMS
176
177   While it is useful for policy makers to classify areas according to features, there is no observable
178   objective ground truth classification and feature data is high-dimensional, reflecting the complexities
179
      present in society. In the UK, researchers have recently preferred classifying Output Areas (OA’s)
      using the well-known K-Means algorithm (Gale, 2014; Gale et al., 2016; De Sabbata & Liu, 2023;
180
      Wyszomierski et al., 2024). K-Means is straight-forward to use, efficient, works with hierarchical
181
      clustering, and produces understandable outputs (Wyszomierski et al., 2024); all important factors
182   when creating policy from results.
183
184   Some issues have been identified with K-Means clustering when applied to geodemographic data.
185
      Vickers & Rees (2011) found that manual classification by people in OAs did not match the K-means
      clustering with mean accuracy across classes of 0.839 with standard deviation 0.095. Potential issues
186
      relate to the assumptions of K-means (MacQueen, 1967): Firstly, it is not clear the feature space
187
      forms approximate hyperspherical clusters in Euclidean space, as required for accurate K-means
188   clustering of high-dimensional data. Secondly, it is uncertain clusters are separable into distinct
189   high-dimensional convex Voronoi cells which reflect a diverse UK population.
190
191
      To solve some of these issues, De Sabbata & Liu (2023) use an unsupervised Variational Graph
      Autoencoder (VGAE) to generate embeddings from features, forcing them onto a latent-space which
192
      approximates a hypersphere, before clustering with K-Means. By using a GNN, this method also
193
      ensures latent representations include the effect of geographic relationships between OAs. A differ-
194   ent approach to clustering was taken by Muscatiello (2023), who used Bayesian Profile Regression
195   (BPR) with respect to incidence of respiratory issues. Where a target variable is unknown, Bayesian
196   Gaussian Mixture Models (BGMM) (Roberts et al., 1998), with full covariance matrix and Dirichlet
197   Process with stick-breaking (Görür & Edward Rasmussen, 2010; Begehr & Panfilov, 2022), sup-
198   ports non-hypersphere clusters and does not require them to be linearly separable, overcoming some
199   of the identified issues (Chang et al., 2020).

303   4.1     DATA C LEANING AND P REPROCESSING
304
      Due to differences in the questions and response options between the 2011 and 2021 censuses, some
305   questions were merged, while others had their wording. To ensure data consistency and enable
306   comparison with the OAC results presented by Wyszomierski et al. (2024), all 60 variables from
307   their study were reviewed. Corresponding variables in both the 2011 and 2021 datasets were re-
308   identified. For variables that had changed, alternative questions and responses that closely matched
309   the originals were selected. As a result, a total of 61 variables were generated.
310
      Additionally, OAs serve as the smallest geographical units in the census. Due to population shifts
311   and household changes, there are some differences between the OA divisions in 2011 and 2021. The
312   2021 OAs consist of a mix of unchanged 2011 OAs and modified 2021 OAs. Therefore, using the
313   reference table4 , the 2011 OAs were merged and split to align with the 2021 OAs. As this merging
314   and splitting process affects the values corresponding to each area, specific strategies were adopted:
315   for merging, the average value of the regions being combined was used, and for splitting, the values
316   of the original region were copied into the new subdivided OAs. This ensured that all data from
317   2011 and 2021 were fully aligned. Before conducting the experiments, all the necessary data from
318   2011 and 2021 were aligned based on OAs. We used percentages for the variables, meaning the
319
      variables represent the percentage of responses within each OA.

345
346   According to the framework shown in Figure 2, the 2011 OA graph was used to train the Graph
347   Transformer and obtain the 2011 OA embeddings. Notably, using the full 2011 dataset for training
348   ensures that the embeddings capture the latent features of the 2011 data without significant informa-
349   tion loss, providing a relatively robust starting point for the subsequent work. The size of the 2011
350   embeddings was set to 61 to maintain alignment with the work of Wyszomierski et al. (2024).
351   After calculating the changes in OA attributes between the 2011 and 2021 data, these changes were
352   concatenated with the corresponding 2011 OA embeddings. The resulting dataset was then split
353   into training and testing sets at an 8:2 ratio. Fixed data masks were used to generate the two sets
354   and were preserved throughout the experiments to ensure that the test data remained unseen by
355   the model during training. The training data was then used to train both the Graph Transformer
356   and GraphSAGE models, generating the OA 2021 embeddings. This process ensured that no data
357   leakage occurred. Finally, a neural network was trained on the 2021 OA embeddings using the 2021
358   OAC as labels.

372   The experimental results in Table 1 show that our proposed framework does not achieve the highest
373   overall accuracy. However, considering that the framework has two objectives—OAC classification
374   and improving model performance on minority classes—we believe the performance is acceptable,

377   This may be because GraphSAGE is better suited to our framework, and the use of SCL significantly
      aids in capturing information and improving performance.
