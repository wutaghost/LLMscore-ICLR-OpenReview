009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning models can extract predictive and actionable information from com-
012            plex inputs. The richer the inputs, the better these models usually perform. How-
013            ever, models that leverage rich inputs (e.g., multi-modality) can be challenging to
014            deploy widely, because some inputs may be missing at inference. Current popular
015            solutions to this issue include marginalization, imputation, and training multiple
016            models. Marginalization can obtain calibrated predictions, but it is computation-
017            ally expensive and therefore only feasible for low dimensional inputs. Imputation
018
               may result in inaccurate predictions because it employs point estimates for miss-
               ing variables and does not work well for high dimensional inputs (e.g., images).
019
               Training multiple models, where each model is designed to handle different sub-
020
               sets of inputs, can work well but requires knowing prior knowledge of missing
021            input patterns. Furthermore, training and retaining multiple models can be costly.
022            We propose an efficient method to learn both the conditional distribution using full
023            inputs and the marginal distributions. Our method, Knockout, randomly replaces
024            input features with appropriate placeholder values during training. We provide
025            a theoretical justification for Knockout and show that it can be interpreted as an
026            implicit marginalization strategy. We evaluate Knockout across a wide range of
027            simulations and real-world datasets and show that it offers strong empirical per-
028            formance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In many real-world applications of machine learning and statistics, not all variables might be avail-
      able for every data point. This issue, as known as missingness, is well-studied in the literature (Little
034
      & Rubin, 2019) and common in fields like healthcare, social sciences, and environmental studies.
035
      From a Bayesian perspective, missingness can be viewed as a marginalization problem, where we
036   would like a model to marginalize out the missing variables from the conditioning set. However,
037   during training, we often do not know which features will be missing at inference time.
038
039
      In lieu of training multiple models for every missingness pattern, a common strategy is imputation,
      which uses a point estimate (usually the mean or mode or a constant) to impute the missing fea-
040
      ture (Le Morvan et al.). This can be seen as approximating the marginalization with a delta function.
041
      More sophisticated methods for handling missingness include using EM imputation (Josse et al.,
042   2019) or neural-based imputation (Mattei & Frellsen, 2019; Ipsen et al., 2022). Although many
043   prior methods may work well in some instances, they may not scale readily to high-dimensional
044   inputs like images (Kyono et al., 2021; You et al., 2020), require additional networks for genera-
045   tion of missing variables (Ipsen et al., 2022), only apply to continuous inputs (Le Morvan et al.,
046   2020; 2021), assume linearity of predictors (Le Morvan et al.), or make assumptions about the data
047   distribution (Hazan et al., 2015).
048   In this work, we propose a simple, effective, and theoretically-justified augmentation strategy, called
049   Knockout, for handling missing inputs. During training, features are augmented by randomly
050   “knocking out” and replacing them with constant “placeholder” values. At inference time, using
051   the placeholder value corresponds mathematically to estimation with the appropriate marginal dis-
052   tribution. In particular, we demonstrate how Knockout can be seen as implicitly maximizing the
053   likelihood of a weighted sum of the conditional estimators and all desired marginals in a single
      model.

      In a suite of experiments, we demonstrate the broad applicability of Knockout. We use both syn-
055   thetic and real-world data with image-based and tabular inputs. Real world experiments include
056   Alzheimer’s forecasting, noisy label learning, multi-modal MR image segmentation and detection,
057   and multi-view tree genus classification. We show the effectiveness of Knockout in handling low and
058   high-dimensional missing inputs, and compare it with appropriate baselines, including imputation
059   and ensemble-based methods.

      2.1   BACKGROUND
064
065   The goal of supervised machine learning (ML) is to learn the conditional distribution p(Y |X) where
066   Y is the output (predictive target) and X → RN are the vector of inputs or features. The prediction
067   for a new sample x is ŷ = arg maxY p(Y |X = x). However, in many practical applications, not
068   all features may be present for a given input. Consider the case when Xi is missing, and denote the
069   vector of non-missing features as X→i . In general, multiple features may be missing at a time, and
070   we can represent this with a missingness indicator set M and corresponding non-missing features
071
      as X→M . In this case, what we really want is p(Y |X→M ).
072   How can we account for missingness? A simple approach is to train a separate model for
073   p(Y |X→M ), i.e. a model that takes only the non-missing features X→M as inputs. However, this
074   is expensive because a separate model is needed for each missingness pattern. Furthermore, there is
075   no sharing of information between these separate models, even though they are theoretically related.
076   Another approach relies on rewriting p(Y |X→M ) in terms of the already available p(Y |X):
077                         !                               !
078         p(Y |X→M ) =          p(Y, XM |X→M )dXM =             p(Y |X)p(XM |X→M )dXM .                (1)
079                           XM                                XM
080
      The goal now is to obtain p(XM |X→M ) and perform the integration over all possible XM .
081
082   Imputation methods approximate Eq. (1) by replacing p(XM |X→M ) with a delta function. For
083   example, “mean imputation” uses the mean of the missing features XM , E[XM ], for XM itself.
084
      In Eq. 1, this corresponds to approximating p(XM |X→M ) ↑ ω(E[XM ]), a delta function. While
      convenient and commonly used, mean imputation ignores the dependency between XM and X→M ,
085
      and does not account for any uncertainty.
086
087   More sophisticated approaches to imputation capture the interdependencies between inputs (Troy-
088   anskaya et al., 2001; Stekhoven & Bühlmann, 2012), for example by explicitly modeling
089   p(XM |X→M ) by training a separate model. At inference time, the point estimate xM =
090
      arg maxXM p(XM |X→M ) can be used for the missing XM . While properly accounting for in-
      terdependencies between inputs, this approach requires fitting a separate model for p(XM |X→M ).
091
      In multiple imputation, multiple samples from p(XM |X→M ) are drawn and a Monte Carlo approx-
092
      imation is used to estimate the integral on the RHS of Eq. 1 Kyono et al. (2021). Although this is
093   more accurate than single imputation, it is not effective in high dimensional space.

      2.2   K NOCKOUT
096
097   We propose a simple augmentation strategy for neural network training called Knockout that en-
098   ables estimation of the conditional distribution p(Y |X) and all desired marginals p(Y |X→M ) in a
099   single, high capacity, nonlinear model, such as a deep neural network. During training, features are
100   augmented by randomly “knocking out” and replacing them with constant, “placeholder” values.
101   At inference time, using the placeholder value corresponds mathematically to estimation with the
102   suitable marginal distribution.
103   Specifically, let M = [M1 , M2 , . . . , MN ] → {0, 1}N denote a binary, induced missingness indicator
104   vector. Let x̄ := [x̄1 , x̄2 , . . . , x̄N ] → RN denote a vector of placeholder values. Then, define
105   X ↑ (M , X) = M ↓ x̄ + (1 ↔ M ) ↓ X as augmented Knockout inputs, where 1 is a vector of ones
106   and ↓ denotes element-wise multiplication. During one training iteration, a different Knockout input
107   is used corresponding to a different randomly sampled M for every data sample. The model weights
      are trained to minimize the loss function with respect to Y , as is done regularly.

231   The treatment above assumes complete training data, and inference-time missingness only. We now
232   consider the situation where training data has observed missing inputs. Let N be the binary mask
233   indicating the observed data missingness. N is different from M , which denotes the missingness
234   induced by Knockout during training. Thus, N is fixed for a data sample, while M is stochastic.
235   Observed missingness generally falls under the following scenarios (Little & Rubin, 2019).
236   Missing Completely at Random (MCAR): This implies that N ↘        ↘ X, Y . Let M ↑ := N ⇑ M
237   be the augmented masking indicator, where ⇑ denotes the logical OR operation. Since N ↘  ↘ X, Y
238   and M ↘  ↘ X, Y , so M ↑ ↘
                               ↘ X, Y . Therefore, we can obtain the same result in Section 2.2 when
239   using M ↑ instead of M as the masking indicator vector. This implies that Knockout can be applied
240   to MCAR training data simply by masking all the missing values using the same placeholders x̄.
241
      Missing at Random (MAR) and Missing not at Random (MNAR): This implies that N ↘                ↗↘ X, Y .
242   Thus, we cannot replace the missing values in training data using the same placeholders. However,

248   ẋi as the placeholder. Otherwise, if we know xi is missing at completely random, we use x̄i .

251   3      R ELATED W ORK
252
253   Knockout is similar to and inspired by other methods with unrelated aims. Dropout (Srivastava et al.,
254   2014; Gal & Ghahramani, 2016) prevents overfitting by randomly dropping units (hidden and visi-
255
      ble) during training and can be viewed as marginalizing over model parameters. During inference,
      marginalizing over parameters can be approximated by predicting once without dropout (Srivastava
256
      et al., 2014) or averaging multiple predictions with dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016). Blank-
257
      out (Maaten et al., 2013) and mDAE (Chen et al., 2014) learn to marginalize out the effects of
258   corruption over inputs. In contrast, Knockout learns different marginals to handle different missing
259   input patterns.
260
261
      Imputation techniques impute missing inputs explicitly, for example by imputing with the mean,
      median, or mode. In model-based imputation, a separate model or technique first predicts the
262
      missing inputs to impute. These models include k-nearest neighbors (Troyanskaya et al., 2001),
263
      chained equations (Van Buuren & Groothuis-Oudshoorn, 2011), random forests (Stekhoven &
264   Bühlmann, 2012), autoencoders (Gondara & Wang, 2018; Ivanov et al., 2019; Lall & Robinson,
265   2022), GANs (Yoon et al., 2018; Li et al., 2019; Belghazi et al., 2019), or normalizing flows (Li et al.,
266   2020). Although more accurate than simple mean/median imputation, model-based imputation in-
267   curs significant additional computation costs, especially when missing inputs are high-dimensional.
268   In contrast, Knockout makes predictions without having to impute missing inputs explicitly. For
269   example, some approaches (Ma et al., 2021; Peis et al., 2022) require additional training of multiple
      VAEs or sub-networks. Other approaches (Mattei & Frellsen, 2019; Ma et al., 2019) require training


                                                           5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      only one VAE but they are formulated for homogeneous data (all continuous variables or all binary
271   variables) and therefore not as flexible as Knockout. Besides, these approaches still require training
272   two models (VAE and classifier), whereas Knockout trains only a single model (the classifier).
273
274
      Another relevant line of work is causal discovery (Spirtes et al., 2000), which often involves fitting
      a model using different subsets of available inputs and multiple distributions simultaneously (Lippe
275
      et al., 2022; James et al., 2023). To reduce computational cost, it is common to train a single model
276
      that can handle different subsets of inputs using dropout (Ke et al., 2023; Brouillard et al., 2020;
277   Lippe et al., 2022).
278
279   Techniques like Knockout are often used in practice to train a single neural network that models
280
      multiple distributions, but are often justified empirically with little care taken in choosing place-
      holder values. Many works use zeros without theoretical justification (Belghazi et al., 2019; Ke
281
      et al., 2023; Brouillard et al., 2020; Lippe et al., 2022). GAIN (Yoon et al., 2018) and MisGAN (Li
282
      et al., 2019) impute using out-of-support values similar to Knockout. However, both are limited
283   in their treatment by assuming that the supports are bounded, and do not consider categorical vari-
284   ables. While the approach is similar to some prior work for structural inputs (Neverova et al., 2015;
285   Parthasarathy & Sundaram, 2020) or low-dimensional inputs (Bertsimas et al., 2024), Knockout’s
286   theoretical backing shows that it can handle multiple data types and multiple missingness types
287   (complete/MCAR/MAR/MNAR). Many self-supervised learning techniques can be interpreted as
288   training to reconstruct the inputs with Knockout. In addition, Knockout can be trained with standard
289   empirical risk minimization while some approaches need more complex optimization (Ma et al.,
290   2021; 2022). For example, masked language modeling (Devlin et al., 2019) randomly maps to-
291
      kens to an unseen “masked” token. Denoising autoencoders (Vincent et al., 2010) randomly replace
      image patches with black patches, which are arguably out of the support of natural images.

510   5    C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
511
512   We introduced Knockout, a novel, easy-to-implement strategy designed to handle missing inputs,
513   using a mathematically principled approach. By simulating missingness during training via random
514   “knock out” and substitution with appropriate placeholder values, our method allows a single model
515   to learn the conditional distribution and all desired marginals. Our extensive experimental evaluation
516   underscores the versatility and robustness of Knockout. Across diverse datasets, including both
517   synthetic and real-world scenarios, Knockout consistently achieves competitive performance levels
518   compared to conventional imputation and ensemble-based techniques across both low and high-
519   dimensional missing inputs. We also extend Knockout to handle observed missing values in the
520
      training set. Our results highlight the importance of choosing the appropriate placeholder values
      for induced and observed missingness in training and during inference. Furthermore, we present
521
      structured version of Knockout that is more effective when entire feature vectors or input modalities
522
      might be missing.
523
524   There are several future directions for further investigation. While our paper highlights the im-
525   portance of choosing an appropriate placeholder value, and there appears to be a practical tension
526
      between selecting an unlikely/infeasible value versus achieving numerical stability (e.g., avoiding
      exploding gradients), one can conduct a more detailed study of this to optimize the placeholder
527
      value. In our experiments, we did not compare Knockout with individual strong baseline models
528
      trained for specific missingness patterns. We considered this out of scope, as it became computa-
529   tionally infeasible for all the scenarios we considered. However, in practice, missingness patterns
530   may be limited, making such an approach feasible. It remains unclear how Knockout would per-
531   form against such a strong baseline, which requires further evaluation. Another promising direction
532   of future research is adapting Knockout to address distribution shifts in the presence of missingness.
533   Finally, Knockout’s theoretical treatment hinges on the use of a very high capacity, non-linear model
534   trained on very large data. In applications, where low capacity models are used and/or training data
535   are limited, Knockout might not be as effective.
