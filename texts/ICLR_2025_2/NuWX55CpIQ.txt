                                                       A BSTRACT
010
011             Quantum machine learning, an emerging field in the noisy intermediate-scale
012             quantum (NISQ) era, faces significant challenges in error mitigation during train-
013             ing and inference stages. Current noise-aware training (NAT) methods typically
014             assume static error rates in quantum neural networks (QNNs), often neglecting
015             the inherently dynamic nature of such noise. By addressing this oversight, our
016             work recognizes the dynamics of noise in the NISQ era, evidenced by fluctuating
017             error rates across different times and qubits. Moreover, QNN performance can
018
                vary markedly depending on the specific locations of errors, even under similar
                error rates. This variability underscores the limitations of static NAT strategies in
019
                addressing the dynamic nature of noisy environments. We propose a novel NAT
020
                strategy that adapts to both standard and fatal error conditions, cooperating with a
021             low-complexity search strategy to efficiently locate fatal errors during optimiza-
022             tion. Our approach marks a significant advancement over current NAT methods
023             by maintaining robust performance in fatal error scenarios. Evaluations validate
024             the efficacy of our strategy against fatal errors, while maintaining performance
025             comparable to state-of-the-art NAT approaches under various error rates.

      Different from classical circuits, the execution on PQCs faces a more rigorous situation with noise.
047
      As quantum computers move to the noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) era, errors in qubits
048   and quantum operations are inevitable due to the dynamic nature of the quantum system and its
049   environment Preskill (2018). Under the complexity of quantum systems and the fragility of qubits,
050   basis gates usually have error rates on the scale of 10−4 to 10−2 , as reported in IBM quantum
051   calibration data, while classical logic gates already achieve error rates lower than 10−6 decade
052   ago Mielke et al. (2008). Thus, error mitigation in PQCs is critical for robust execution on quantum
053   computers, where mitigation strategies have been proposed during QNN training Wang et al. (2022b)
      or execution Cai et al. (2023); Endo et al. (2021); Strikis et al. (2021).

      In this work, we focus on noise-aware training (NAT) for QNN models, aiming to enhance the
055   robustness of PQC against errors. The current NAT approach proposed by Wang et al. (2022b)
056   involves deliberately introducing simulated noise into quantum gates, based on a predefined error
057   rate. This technique is designed to improve the robustness of PQC to a specific error rate. Despite
058   the pronounced effectiveness, we highlight a critical limitation in the current NAT strategy and PQC
059   simulators, which rely on static error rates during noise analysis. In reality, the noise landscape is
060   dynamic across both time and qubits. Recent studies have demonstrated that noise levels in quantum
061   computers can fluctuate significantly over time, even within a transient time window of operation Ravi
062   et al. (2023). In our work, we also emphasize the dynamic nature of noise across different qubits. In
063
      extreme cases, the error rate for a quantum gate can vary dramatically — from as high as 8% on one
      qubit to as low as 0.1% on another — illustrated in Figure 2. Such a highly variable noise landscape
064
      renders the static error rate assumption in NAT insufficient for practical applications. For instance, a
065
      qubit in an optimized QNN model that was assumed to have a low error rate during simulation could
066   be mapped to a physical qubit with a significantly higher error rate or might experience increased
067   errors over extended runtime.
068
069
      Additionally, current approaches to evaluating errors in noisy QNN models predominantly focus
      on the probability of errors, rather than assessing the actual detrimental impact these errors have
070
      on QNN performance. Practically, even errors with identical probabilities can have vastly different
071
      consequences. Certain errors may have a negligible influence on the QNN’s inference performance,
072   while some fatal errors can degrade its performance to levels worse than random guessing. The
073   situation is more critical when qubits with possible fatal errors are mapped to high-error-rate physical
074   qubits, posing a significant risk to the robustness of QNN inference.
075
      In this paper, we characterize the influence of dynamic noise on PQCs, and demonstrate the scenarios
076
      where state-of-the-art (SOTA) NAT strategy is not competent. While it can mitigate errors occurring
077
      at predictable rates, it falls short in managing fatal errors that critically undermine QNN performance.
078   Our analysis underscores the need for a more comprehensive NAT strategy that transcends static
079   error rate consideration and effectively mitigates fatal errors. To tackle this challenge, we propose a
080   low-complexity search strategy. It can search for fatal errors along with QNN training, and evolve
081   with PQC parameters simultaneously. Rather than randomly sampling errors, we utilize the searched
082   fatal errors to boost the robust QNN training. We highlight our contributions as follows:
083
084          • We reveal the significant influence of dynamic noise landscape on QNN models. The
085
               uncertainty of error rates hinders the practice of current NAT strategies on static error rates.
086          • We quantify the negative influence of error cases on QNN models, and propose the analysis
087            methods for the fatal errors on a QNN model.
088          • To the best of our knowledge, this is the first NAT method that is independent of error rates,
089            but concentrates on fatal errors of PQCs. Our strategy aims to develop QNN models adaptive
090            to various noise distributions and with good tolerance to these fatal error cases.
091          • We thoroughly evaluate the effectiveness of our strategy, which greatly improves the ro-
092            bustness of QNN models against fatal errors. Also, our strategy can achieve performance
093            comparable to that of the SOTA NAT strategy under various error rates.

240   The most commonly used metric to evaluate
241   QNN performance is the inference accuracy at specific error rates, which we will refer to as Noisy
242   Accuracy. This metric determines QNN performance by introducing errors at predefined rates across
243   individual samples or batches, where the negative impact of fatal errors is diluted over multiple
244   samples. Consequently, this approach evaluates only the average performance of a noisy QNN, not
245   its variation. Besides, assessing accuracy at fixed error rates does not accurately reflect the real-time
246   performance of a QNN on a quantum computer due to the dynamic nature of quantum noise. A more
247   reliable measure of QNN robustness in noisy conditions is the performance under fatal error scenarios,
248
      which we call Fatal Accuracy. This metric, focusing on QNN behavior under worst-case conditions,
      provides a critical measure of robustness by evaluating variation in performance across different
249
      noisy environments. A QNN model that scores high in both noisy and fatal accuracies demonstrates
250
      not only strong average predictive performance under noise but also consistent robustness across
251   various noisy conditions.

254   3.3   W ORST C ASE A NALYSIS
255
256   Evaluating all possible error scenarios during QNN training is both time-consuming and impractical,
257   especially since many of these scenarios have negligible probabilities. Instead, it is more meaningful
258   to focus on error cases with relatively high probabilities, as these are the most common during
259
      quantum execution. For example, with an average error rate of 0.001 (such as in ibmq cusco)
      across a 100-gate PQC, the conditional probability that one or two errors occur given the error
260
      occurring, is P (NE ≤ 2|NE ≥ 1) ≈ 99.84%. This indicates that nearly all error events involve one
261
      or two gate errors. Therefore, we emphasize concentrating on these relatively “high-probability”
262   errors, e.g., considering up-to-two gate errors (denoted as M = 2), since they are the most likely
263   to affect the model’s performance when errors occur. The specific collection strategy of these error
264   cases is discussed in Appendix E. In our analysis, we locate fatal errors in the high-probability error
265   set and utilize the deterministic noise model described in Eq. 3.
266

267
      loss to the PQC measurement f (|x⟩ , θ), as the supremum of losses caused by all E ∈ Ωe ,
268

      Accordingly, the QNN fatal accuracy evaluates the worst-case performance of PQC measurement
271   against all noise cases in Ωe . That is, the “worst-case” performance refers to the maximum loss
272   that surpasses other losses with high probability. Our metric in Eq. 4 does not involve gate error
273   rates during evaluation; thus, the evaluation remains fair and robust against dynamic noise changes
274   in quantum computers. Please note that although we consider the general/average error rate when
275   collecting Ωe to narrow the scope of noise events under consideration, this approach does not affect
276   the dynamic noise landscape or compromise the error-rate independence of our fatal loss metric. This
277   is because large fluctuations in few qubits’ error rates can be effectively averaged out in the general
278   error rate without introducing significant bias; meanwhile, a simultaneous and sudden change in all
279
      qubits’ error rates is improbable during normal operation of a quantum computer.
280   To conclude, there are two challenges for noise-aware training on QNN models: 1 noisy accuracy
281   challenge—Since there is no obvious routine for the error rate change on qubits, it is necessary to
282   develop a general NAT strategy, so that the optimized PQC is supposed to perform well under various
283   error rates on average. 2 fatal accuracy challenge—The optimized PQC should have acceptable
284   performance even with high-probability fatal errors. A desired NAT strategy should be error-rate
285
      independent, i.e., equitable to all error rates without deliberate bias, so that the performance loss of
      QNN under a dynamic noise landscape can be alleviated.

      4.2   L OW-C OMPLEXITY S EARCH
305
306   To determine the supremum of losses caused by high-probability error cases, the most straightforward
307   method is to exhaustively traverse all error cases in Ωe . However, the cardinality of the error set |Ωe |
308   actually increases polynomially along the PQC scale, i.e., the gate number G. Since Lsup must be
309   evaluated for each data batch and at every iteration during training, the computational time quickly
310   becomes impractical as the PQC scales up. Therefore, it is essential to adopt low-complexity yet
311   effective search strategies to address this scalability challenge.
312   Scalable Evolution-Based Low-Complexity Search. We adopt the idea from genetic algorithm Mir-

532   6     C ONCLUSION
533
534   We highlight the critical impact of dynamic noise and fatal errors on QNNs. Existing training
535   and evaluation methods fall short facing a dynamically changing quantum noise environment. We
536   propose fatal error analysis, which is independent of error rates and assesses QNN robustness against
537   high-probability fatal errors. We also present the first noise-aware training strategy aimed at general
538   fatal error mitigation, rather than a specific error rate. Our evaluations confirm the efficacy of our
539   strategy for fatal errors; besides, QNNs optimized by our approach perform on par with SOTA NAT
      strategies under various error rates.

      Parameterized Quantum Circuit (PQC) involves a set of parameters θ to convert the input state |x⟩
      to a new state |ψ⟩ for measurement, denoted f (|x⟩ , θ) following Eq. 1. Optimization of θ aims to
706
      minimize the loss function between the ground truth and the expectation value of an observable,
707
      L(f (|x⟩ , θ), y). During iterative updating of the parameter θ, two commonly adopted strategies are
708   applied. The first scenario is for PQC optimization based on simulation, i.e., emulating the quantum
709   propagation on the classical computer, so that the gradient ∂L/∂θ is derived straightforwardly as
710   normal NN training, then the parameter is updated through θ ← θ − η∂L/∂θ.
711
      Another scenario is to keep the quantum propagation on real quantum computer, and treat it as a
712
      black-box execution (due to the noise existence). Then, the gradient of θ is estimated from the
713
      parameter-shift rule Simeone et al. (2022) as
714

718   where ϵ > 0 is a small positive number and θg is the unit vector for the g-th parameter. E is sampled
719   from the noise distribution D at the time of PQC execution on quantum computer. Afterwards, the
720   parameter is updated iteratively. The benefit of this strategy is considering the real noise distribution
721   E during PQC optimization, although it is unknown (as a black box). However, in practice, the noise
722   distribution is changing dynamically and significantly, making it difficult for optimized PQC under

      To effectively estimate the supremum of losses, i.e., supE∈Ωe {L(f (|x⟩ , θ|E), y)}, an efficient
870
      search strategy should be provided. Multiple in-training strategies are utilized in modern search tasks,
871
      such as differentiable search Liu et al. (2018), reinforcement learning Kaelbling et al. (1996), and
872   evolutionary search Mirjalili & Mirjalili (2019).
873
874
      F.1   S EARCH S TRATEGY S ELECTION
875
876   Deficiency of Differentiable Search and Reinforcement Learning. In the differentiable search
877   strategy, all the candiate operations, such as {I, X, Y, Z}, should be differentiable during model
878   execution. In this case, we use the probabilistic noise model (Eq. 2) to express each noisy gate
879   operation, i.e., employing the density matrix representation of qubit states. Although this format is
880   commonly used in theoretical analyses, such as noise channel modeling, it raises scalability concerns
881
      in classical computing. This is because the size of the density matrix is 2Q × 2Q for a Q-qubit system;
      the computational complexity increases exponentially with the QNN scale, making it inefficient
882
      for practical use. For the reinforcement learning search, integration with the QNN model training
883
      involves setting up an agent model to decide the next action (error case selection) given the current
884   error case and PQC parameters. However, the action space grows linearly with the number of QNN
885   gates, which makes agent model training harder and more time-consuming. On the other hand, we
886   utilize evolutionary algorithms, which avoid model training during the search, instead relying on
887   multiple model inferences. Furthermore, as long as the population size per generation is appropriate,
888   the search effort does not have to increase linearly with the QNN scale.
889
      Our Evolution-Based Low-Complexity Search Design. We employ an evolutionary search strategy
890   to minimize the online fatal loss in Ωe . Initially, we randomly generate Npop candidate error cases Se
891   for the first generation, evaluated against the deterministic noise model described in Eq. 3. For parent
892   selection during crossover and mutation, we apply elite tournament selection within a substantial
893   population. We then use uniform crossover and binary mutation techniques to generate the subsequent
894   generation. It is important to note that the evolution of generations occurs concurrently with model
895   training. Consequently, during each iteration of model training, only one generation is assessed based
896   on the current state of the QNN model. The algorithm is referred to Algorithm 1.
897   An Sequential Search Alternative. Reviewing the composition of Ωe , it only contains error cases
898   with certain error numbers, such as 1 and 2 when M = 2. Given this characteristic, we also propose
899   another search strategy that approximates the supremum of Ωe , which is efficient for a quick search
900   in small PQC scale. The algorithm of our sequential search strategy is based on the idea of greedy
901   search, where the algorithm is provided in Algorithm 3. We categorize the error cases in Ωe by the
                                    PQ
902   total number of errors m = q=1 mq . From the group with the least number of errors, we evaluate
903   all the cases, and find the error case with the largest loss. Then, this candidate is stored for further
904   search. If we locate the worst error case with m errors, then we boldly assume that these errors will
905   also occur in fatal error cases with (m + 1) errors. Therefore, we proceed with the search for (m + 1)
906   errors by fixing the m errors as the worst error case of the m errors, plus one error occurring in the rest
