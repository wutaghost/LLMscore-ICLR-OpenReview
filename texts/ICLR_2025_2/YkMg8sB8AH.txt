009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We consider explainability in equivariant graph neural networks for 3D geometric
012            graphs. While many XAI methods have been developed for analyzing graph neu-
013            ral networks, they predominantly target 2D graph structures. The complex nature
014            of 3D data and the sophisticated architectures of equivariant GNNs present unique
015            challenges. Current XAI techniques either struggle to adapt to equivariant GNNs
016            or fail to effectively handle positional data and evaluate the significance of geomet-
017            ric features adequately. To address these challenges, we introduce a novel method,
018
               known as EquiGX, which uses the Deep Taylor decomposition framework to ex-
               tend the layer-wise relevance propagation rules tailored for spherical equivariant
019
               GNNs. Our approach decomposes prediction scores and back-propagates the rel-
020
               evance scores through each layer to the input space. Our decomposition rules
021            provide a detailed explanation of each layer’s contribution to the network’s pre-
022            dictions, thereby enhancing our understanding of how geometric and positional
023            data influence the model’s outputs. Through experiments on both synthetic and
024            real-world datasets, our method demonstrates its capability to identify critical ge-
025            ometric structures and outperform alternative baselines. These results indicate that
026            our method provides significantly enhanced explanations for equivariant GNNs.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Equivariant graph neural networks have shown significant promise in addressing complex problems
032   across quantum physics, molecular science, materials science, and protein research (Thomas et al.,
033
      2018; Fuchs et al., 2020; Liao & Smidt, 2022; Liao et al., 2023; Batzner et al., 2022; Passaro &
      Zitnick, 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Du et al., 2024). Despite their potential, a critical
034
      challenge in assessing the scientific plausibility of these models’ outcomes is their interpretability.
035
      Most equivariant GNNs are treated as black boxes, which undermines their reliability and limits
036   their applicability in scientific domains. Therefore, developing explainable artificial intelligence
037   (XAI) techniques tailored for equivariant GNNs is highly desirable. These techniques can provide
038   insights into how equivariant GNNs make predictions, thereby increasing the trustworthiness of their
039   outcomes. Moreover, XAI techniques can not only diagnose and improve existing models but also
040   facilitate further scientific knowledge discovery.
041
      While many XAI methods have been proposed to study GNNs, they primarily focus on 2D
042   graphs (Yuan et al., 2023; 2020; Zheng et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2021). The
043   high dimensionality of 3D geometric data and the complexity of equivariant GNN models pose
044   unique challenges and opportunities in this domain. Current XAI techniques either struggle to adapt
045   to equivariant GNNs or fail to effectively handle positional data and evaluate the significance of geo-
046   metric features adequately. Specifically, many XAI methods (Huang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2021;
047   Vu & Thai, 2020) overlook the complex behavior of equivariant models, thus requiring additional ef-
048   fort before they can be applied to equivariant GNNs. On the other hand, some XAI methods, known
049   for their simplicity and adaptability, such as SA (Baldassarre & Azizpour, 2019), are insufficient to
050
      provide a comprehensive explanation for the importance of geometric features.
051   To fill this gap, we introduce a novel XAI method called EquiGX, which measures the importance
052   of input components by decomposing the model predictions. The primary challenge in decom-
053   posing the predictions of spherical equivariant GNNs lies in attributing the tensor product-based
      message-passing operations that are central to these networks. Our approach uses the Deep Taylor

      decomposition framework to extend layer-wise relevance propagation rules specifically for spherical
055   equivariant GNNs. By explicitly considering the tensor product (TP) operations, we derive new rel-

      2     BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
062
063   We denote a geometric graph with n nodes as G = {X, A, C}. Here, X = [X1 , · · · , Xn ]T ∈ Rn×d
064   is the node feature matrix, where each Xi ∈ Rd is the d-dimensional feature vector of node i.
065   C = [C1 , · · · , Cn ]T ∈ Rn×3 is the node coordinate matrix, where Ci is the coordinate of i-th
066   node. Nodes are generally connected by edges using a predetermined radial cutoff distance c ∈ R+ ,
067   so that the adjacency matrix A ∈ {0, 1}n×n is defined as Aij = 1 if and only if ∥Ci − Cj ∥2 ≤ c.
068   We use N (i) to denote the set of neighboring nodes of node i.

070   2.1   E QUIVARIANT G RAPH N ETWORKS
071
072   Equivariant graph neural networks are critical in the domain of AI for science, particularly for
073
      modeling geometric graphs derived from three-dimensional atomic systems. These networks are
      specifically designed to capitalize the physical symmetries and integrate these symmetries into the
074
      model architecture to ensure that the learned hidden representations are equivariant to any symme-
075
      try transformations applied to the input. Specifically, if the input geometric graph is transformed
076   under any operation in SE(3), which stands for the special Euclidean group in 3D space, the cor-
077   responding hidden representations at each layer are transformed correspondingly. Formally, a func-
078   tion f : Rn×3 → R2ℓ+1 mapping between 3D coordinates to a (2ℓ + 1)-dimensional vector is
079   SE(3) equivariant, if for any input coordinates C, we have f (RCT + t) = Dℓ (R)f (C), where
080   t ∈ R3 is a translation vector, R is a rotation matrix satisfying RT R = I and |R| = 1, and
081   Dℓ (R) ∈ R(2ℓ+1)×(2ℓ+1) represents the Wigner-D matrix of R (Gilmore, 2008). Here, function f
082   is invariant to translation, exemplifying a specific type of translation equivariance.
083   Among the various types of equivariant GNNs (Jing et al., 2020; Schütt et al., 2021; Satorras et al.,
084   2021), spherical equivariant GNNs (Thomas et al., 2018; Fuchs et al., 2020; Liao & Smidt, 2022)
085   are particularly prominent. In these approaches, spherical harmonics functions are used to first
086   encode 3D geometric information into higher dimensional SE(3) equivariant features. We denote
087   the order-ℓ1 SE(3) equivariant hidden features of node i as Hiℓ1 ∈ R2ℓ1 +1 . These features are used
088   in a tensor product operation to compute anPequivariant message from node i to node j, denoted as
089   Mj→i , and the aggregated message Mi = j∈N (i) Mj→i is used to update the equivariant hidden

      2.2   E XPLAINABILITY IN G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
109
110   Explainability in neural networks is vital for validating the trustworthiness and reliability of their
111   predictions, especially when applying these models to scientific domains. Current XAI methods pre-
112   dominantly focus on GNNs designed for 2D graphs. These approaches can be mainly categorized
113   into four classes, namely, gradients/feature-based methods, perturbation-based methods, decompo-
114   sition methods, and surrogate methods. Gradients/Feature-based methods, such as SA (Baldassarre
115   & Azizpour, 2019) and CAM (Pope et al., 2019), use gradient values to assess the importance of
116
      input components. Their popularity stems from their simplicity and direct approach. Perturbation-
      based methods (Ying et al., 2019; Yuan et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2020) evaluate changes in predictions
117
      by perturbing different input features to identify the most impactful ones. Surrogate-based methods
118
      (Huang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2021; Vu & Thai, 2020) involve fitting a simpler, interpretable
119   model, such as a decision tree, to mimic the behavior of the original model. The surrogate model’s
120   explanations are then used to understand the original predictions. Decomposition methods (Schnake
121   et al., 2021; Xiong et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023) decompose prediction scores and back-propagate
122   them layer-by-layer to the input space to compute importance scores and provide deeper insights
123   into each network layer. Despite significant advances in XAI for 2D GNNs, these methods primar-
124   ily focus on evaluating the importance of edges, nodes, and subgraphs, struggling to incorporate
125   positional information effectively and fully evaluate the importance of geometric features. Conse-
126   quently, the application of these techniques to 3D geometric graphs, especially within equivariant
127
      graph neural networks, poses significant challenges. Recently, Miao et al. (2023) introduces a learn-
      able interpreter model that applies random noise to each 3D point to generate importance scores.
128
      However, this method treats the models as black boxes and overlooks the equivariance of the model.
129
      It also requires training the interpreter alongside the prediction model. To sum up, the challenge of
130   explaining equivariant neural networks highlights a significant gap in the current landscape of XAI,
131   underscoring the need for innovative approaches that consider the complex behaviors of equivariant
132   neural networks.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
136
      Previous XAI methods on 2D graphs encounter limitations when adapting them on geometric graphs,
137
      particularly in effectively incorporating positional information and evaluating geometric features.
138   To address these challenges, we introduce a novel method, EquiGX, which recursively decomposes
139   network predictions back to the input elements. Our approach use the Deep Taylor decomposition
140   framework (Montavon et al., 2017), adapted to extend the layer-wise relevance propagation rules
141   specifically for TP based message passing process. This adaptation allows for a detailed explanation
142   of each layer’s contribution to the network’s predictions, thus enhancing our understanding of how
143   geometric and positional data influence the model’s outputs.
144

325
326   Synthetic Datasets. We create two kinds of geometric graph classification datasets, namely Shapes
327   and Spiral Noise. For the Shapes dataset, we begin by randomly selecting a 3D motif shape from
328   two options, including a cube or an icosahedron, the latter being a polyhedron with 20 triangular
329   faces. Subsequently, we choose a 3D base shape, either a pyramid or a star. A random translation
330   and rotation are performed on the base shape. The classification task is to predict whether the motif
331   shape in the geometric graph is a cube or not. In the Spiral Noise dataset, we randomly select a
332
      3D motif shape, either a tetrahedron, a polyhedron with four triangular faces, or a triangular prism.
      We then introduce a variable number of noise points to create a spiral pattern in 3D space. The
333
      classification task is to determine whether the motif shape is a tetrahedron or not.
334
335   Real-world Datasets. In addition to synthetic datasets containing perfect 3D geometric shapes, we
336   evaluate our method on three real-world datasets, including the Structural Classification of Proteins
337   (SCOP), BioLiP, and Actstrack. The SCOP database (Murzin et al., 1995; Andreeva et al., 2007;
338
      Chandonia et al., 2019) is a predominantly manually curated classification of protein structural do-
      mains, organized based on similarities in their structures and amino acid sequences. While using the
339
      same training and validation datasets as Hou et al. (2018); Hermosilla et al. (2020), our focus is on
340
      the fold classification task, which is to predict the broad types of protein tertiary structure topolo-
341   gies. Hence, we only use the Fold test set. There are seven categories in total, such as all-alpha
342   and all-beta proteins. Protein labels, provided by human experts, are based on the secondary struc-
343   ture, which reflects the local spatial conformation of proteins. Specifically, labeling for all-alpha
344   and all-beta proteins is determined by the presence of α-helices and β-sheets within their structures,
345   respectively. BioLiP (Yang et al., 2012; Zhang et al., 2024) is a semi-manually curated database ded-
346   icated to high-quality ligand-protein binding interactions. The 3D structural data primarily sourced
347   from the Protein Data Bank are complemented with biological information, such as binding affinity
348   scores, from literature and other databases. The task is to predict whether there is a tight binding
349
      between a protein-ligand pair. Like previous methods (Somnath et al., 2021; Öztürk et al., 2018;
      Townshend et al., 2020), we do not differentiate between the inhibition constant (Ki ) and dissoci-
350
      ation constant (Kd ), instead predicting whether a protein-ligand pair is of affinity of Kd /Ki ≤ 1
351
      nM. ActsTrack (Miao et al., 2023) is a particle tracking simulation dataset in high-energy physics.
352   The task is to predict whether a collision event contains a z → µµ decay based on a point cloud of
353   detector hits. Each point in the point cloud corresponds to a particle interaction with the detector.
354   Positive samples include hits from both the z → µµ decay and background interactions, thus the
355   particle hits left by the two muons (µs) are labeled as the ground truth for model explanations.
356
      Baselines. We compare our method with the following baseline methods, including (1) Grad (Bal-
357
      dassarre & Azizpour, 2019), which uses the norm of the gradient of the predictions with respect
358   to the 3D coordinates to evaluate node importance; (2) Grad-CAM (Pope et al., 2019), a gradient-
359   based method combining with activations from hidden node representations; (3) GNN-Explainer
360   (Ying et al., 2019), a perturbation-based method identifying important edges through optimization
361   of soft masks; (4) LRI-Bern (Miao et al., 2023), which learns a model to inject Bernoulli noise to
362   evaluate the significance of point existence; (5) LRI-Gaussian (Miao et al., 2023), which learns a
363   model to inject Gaussian noise to evaluate the significance of point positions; (6) PG-Explainer (Luo
364   et al., 2020), which generate explanations by learning parameterized masks that highlight the most
365   relevant subgraphs. For methods that assign importance scores to edges, we distribute the score to
366
      the connecting nodes to evaluate node-level explanations.

507   5    C ONCLUSIONS
508
509   In this work, we propose a method, known as EquiGX, to explain equivariant graph neural net-
510   works for geometric graphs. Our method recursively decomposes network predictions back to the
511   input elements. We adapts the Deep Taylor decomposition framework to TP based message pass-
512   ing process, leading to specifically designed layer-wise relevance propagation rules. Experimental
513   results demonstrate the capability of EquiGX to identify critical geometric structures and provide
514   significantly enhanced explanations for equivariant GNNs.

724
      randomly select a 3D motif shape from two options, namely √      a cube or an icosahedron. The cube
725
      has a side length of 2, and the icosahedron has a radius of 3. For the base shape, we choose
726   either a pyramid or a star. The pyramid has a base length and height of 1, while the star has an arm
727   length of 1. A random vector is then used to translate the base shape, ensuring that it remains a
728   certain distance from the motif shape without overlapping. Additionally, the motif shape undergoes
729   a random rotation. The classification task is to predict whether the motif shape in the geometric
730   graph is a cube or not. We use a radial cutoff of 5 to construct the geometric graph.
731
      In the Spiral Noise dataset, we randomly select a 3D motif shape, either a tetrahedron or a triangular
732   prism. The tetrahedron has a radius of 1, and the triangular prism has a length and height of 1. The
733   chosen motif shape is transformed using a randomly sampled translation vector and rotation matrix.
734   Next, we randomly sample 4 to 8 noise points, which form a spiral pattern with a radius of 1 in 3D
735   space. The classification task is to determine whether the motif shape is a tetrahedron. We use a
736   radial cutoff of 2 to construct the geometric graph.
737
      For the SCOP dataset, we extract the backbone atoms of the protein to construct the geometric graph.
738   Specifically, for each amino acid residue of the protein, the backbone atoms (i.e., nitrogen N, alpha
739   carbon CA, and carbon C) are extracted and used as the nodes of the geometric graph. The atom
740   type and residue index are used as features for each atom. We apply a radius cutoff of 5Å to create
741   the geometric graph.
742
      For the BioLip dataset, we extract the backbone atoms of the proteins and all atoms of the ligands to
743
      construct the geometric graph. Specifically, we use the alpha carbon CA of each amino acid residue
744   in the protein as the nodes of the geometric graph. Additionally, every atom of the ligand is also
745   used as a node in the graph. The atom type and residue type serve as node features. A radius cutoff
746   of 10 Å is applied to create the geometric graph.

      A.3   E VALUATION M ETRICS
773
774   In addition to common metrics, such as AUROC and AP, we also use Fidelity and Sparsity scores. In
775   this section, we provide detailed definitions of these scores. Given an input geometric graph G, XAI
776   methods compute an importance score for each node. Based on these scores, we compute a hard
777   node mask that contains only binary values. Using this mask, we can generate a masked graph G ′ ,
778   where important nodes are masked out. Let f denote a well-trained equivariant GNN. The Fidelity
779   score is computed as
780                                        Fidelity = f (G)y − f (G ′ )y ,                               (12)
781   where f (G)y and f (G ′ )y means the predicted probability of class y of graph G and G ′ , respectively.
782   Intuitively, Fidelity measures the change in predictions when important input elements are removed.
783
      In addition, we use Sparsity to measure the fraction of important nodes in the explanations as
784                                                             |G ′ |
                                               Sparsity = 1 −          ,                                 (13)
785                                                              |G|
786
      where |G ′ | and |G ′ | denote the number of nodes in G ′ and G ′ , respectively. The final Fidelity and
787
      Sparsity scores are averaged over the test dataset. Note that good explanations should exhibit high
788   Sparsity along with high Fidelity.

847   D    M ORE D ISCUSSIONS ABOUT E QUIVARIANT G RAPH N ETWORKS
848
849   As mentioned in Duval et al. (2023a), equivariant networks can be categorized into four main types:
850   Invariant GNNs: These networks, such as SchNet (Schütt et al., 2017), DimNet (Gasteiger et al.,
851   2020), SphereNet (Liu et al., 2022), and GemNet (Gasteiger et al., 2021), encode the invariant
852   geometric information like distances and directions directly into their model design to consider the
853   3D structures. Cartesian equivariant GNNs: Networks like GVP-GNN (Jing et al., 2020), PaiNN
854   (Schütt et al., 2021), and E(n)GNN (Satorras et al., 2021) further consider direction vector as input
855
      and use scalar-vector operations to consider their interactions within the architectures. Spherical
      Equivariant GNNs: These networks such as TFN (Thomas et al., 2018), SEGNN (Brandstetter et al.,
856
      2021), NequIP (Batzner et al., 2022), Equiformer (Liao & Smidt, 2022), Allegro (Musaelian et al.,
857
      2023), MACE (Batatia et al., 2022), usually use the spherical harmonics of the directions as the input
858   spherical tensors. Then they combine spherical tensors using equivariant operations like Tensor
859   Product (TP) and convert them into irreducible representations. These networks have more complex
860   interactions between equivariant irreducible representations, demonstrating superior performance
861   and widespread application in property prediction (Ramakrishnan et al., 2014), force field prediction
862   (Chmiela et al., 2017), and Hamiltonian matrix prediction (Schütt et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2024).
863   Given the widespread use of the powerful spherical equivariant GNNs, understanding their key
      components, especially Tensor Product (TP), is one of the most essential problems in studying the
