009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) has seen significant progress in
012            recent years, enabling multiple agents to coordinate and optimize their actions
013            in complex environments. However, integrating effective communication proto-
014            cols into MARL frameworks remains a challenge, as it introduces issues such as
015            increased state space dimensionality, lack of stationarity, and the need for inter-
016
               pretability. Inspired by human communication, which relies on prior knowledge,
               contextual awareness, and efficient information exchange, we propose a novel
017
               framework for incorporating human-like communication strategies to enhance the
018
               learning process. Motivated by recent advancements in natural language pro-
019            cessing (NLP), multi-modal AI and object detection, we use text-to-mask models
020            and human feedback to learn compact and informative communication strategies
021            that facilitate coordination among agents to improve the overall performance. We
022            demonstrate the efficiency of our approach on various multi-agent tasks and pro-
023            vide insights into emergent communication behaviors observed during training.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) is a widely studied subject, where multiple agents
      interact with a shared environment, learning to optimize their return. While typically, each agent
029
      operates according to its own experience (i.e., decentralized joint-policy), many potential real-life
030
      applications allow the agents to communicate. By enabling agents to exchange critical information
031   relevant to their shared task, communication can potentially enhance overall performance and co-
032   ordination. However, integrating communication into multi-agent learning-frameworks introduces
033   unique challenges; the communication increases the dimensionality of the state-space, and learning
034   how to both control and communicate results in an increased non-stationarity. Previous research
035   in the field can be divided to methods that utilize various aspects of centralized learning (Foerster
036   et al., 2016; Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Lowe et al., 2017; Jaques et al., 2019), use reward shaping to
037   encourage communication (Jaques et al., 2019; Eccles et al., 2019), or harness special architectures
038   to model the communication (Jiang & Lu, 2018; Lin et al., 2021; Lo et al., 2023). While these
039
      approaches benefit the learning process and show good empirical performance, the experiments are
      usually done in simple environments, in which either the optimal control or the optimal communica-
040
      tion policies are relatively simple. In complex settings, current methods still perform suboptimally,
041
      or require an infeasible amount of interactions with the environment.
042
043   We aim to address complex problems. To better grasp the difference between a complex and a
044   simple environment, consider the following examples. A navigation task in which one agent, the
045
      ‘navigator’, has to find a path to a control panel, which is always at the same location, from an
      arbitrary starting point, then press on either the blue button or the red button. Another agent, the
046
      ‘dispatcher’, knows on which button, red or blue, should the ‘navigator’ press. This is a simple task,
047
      for two reasons: (1) The information to communicate is stationary and (2) the communication affects
048   only a single decision, when choosing on which button to press. With or without communication,
049   the ‘navigator’ has to learn how to navigate to the control panel. If the location of the control panel
050   is initiated randomly and the ‘dispatcher’ knows it, there is an added layer of complexity, since
051   the entire navigation becomes communication-dependent. When dealing with a complex task, it
052   is accepted to decouple the control-policy from the communication-policy; in the context of the
053   example, the varying location of the control panel translates to a complex control task but a simple
      communication task, as the location of the control panel and the correct button stays fixed. If the


                                                        1
054
      location of the control panel may change during an episode, the communication task would become
055   even more challenging. We formulate a proper definition for such problems and provide further
056   insights at Section 3.
057
058
      When considering communication, it could be beneficial assimilating to human beings, as human
      communication is used daily to solve complex tasks. But while human-like communication proto-
059
      cols addressed for humans, it is not clear whether artificial agents that use RL would actually benefit
060
      from them. Nevertheless, the interpretability of such protocols may allow additional benefits, such
061   as teaming up with humans or learning from them. This concept has been previously studied (Lazari-
062   dou et al., 2016; Havrylov & Titov, 2017; Karten et al., 2023), although in most cases, the main focus
063   revolves around the emergence of language in rather simple control tasks, or involves a complex,
064   task-specific learning schemes. Moreover, it has been shown that a true human-like communication
065   is less likely to emerge naturally (Kottur et al., 2017). Another approach would be to learn from hu-
066   mans how to communicate, via behavioral cloning (BC) or RL from human feedback (RLHF), but
067   in the face of a complex task it requires a vast amount of human-feedback which is hard to collect.
068   Drawing inspiration from human communication, which relies on prior knowledge, contextual un-
069   derstanding, and efficient information exchange, there exists an opportunity to enhance multi-agent
070   learning by incorporating human-like communication strategies. The aim here is to learn what is
071   important to communicate, not to mimic human communication. With advancements in natural lan-
072   guage processing (NLP), multimodal AI, and object detection a wide set of tools is now available,
073   and we propose using them for leveraging human knowledge to mitigate the challenges induced by
074   communication. We propose a framework for a simple and efficient injection of human knowledge,
075
      which can greatly improve the performance by having a good-enough strategy to begin with, and
      reducing the inherent non-stationarity.
076
077   Our contributions:
078
079
             • We propose a comprehensive framework for incorporating human-like communication
               strategies to enhance multi-agent learning, making it possible to solve complex tasks.
080
081          • We demonstrate the effectiveness of our framework on two complex multi-agent tasks and
082
               provide insights into the emergent communication behaviors observed during training.
083          • We publish our code and environments for further research in the field.

085   2   R ELATED W ORK
086
087   The non-stationary nature of cooperative MARL problems is both challenging and interesting,
088   thus gained some attention recently. Many existing approaches embrace the centralized-learning
089   paradigm, which enables better performance. In Foerster et al. (2016), the authors present a method
090   for learning across agents by propagating gradients through a communication channel. The paper
091   Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) introduce a multi-agent communication model that uses a continuous vector
092   to transmit messages between agents. Lowe et al. (2017) utilize the policies of the other agents when
093
      choosing an action. Jaques et al. (2019) propose a reward shaping for promoting causal influence
      on other agents, which requires knowing all agents’ policies. Decentralized learning has also been
094
      explored in recent research, here the focus is on the communication protocols. In Lin et al. (2021),
095
      the agents use autoencoders to learn an encoding for their observation, while the encoding is com-
096   municated to the other agents. Lo et al. (2023) utilize similar concepts for encoding the joint state
097   of all agents. Eccles et al. (2019) utilize reward shaping for motivating the agents both to change
098   their behavior upon receiving different messages, and to send more diverse messages to represent
099   different experiences. While using centralized critic, Jiang & Lu (2018) propose an attention-based
100   communication model that allows agents to selectively attend to incoming messages, effectively fil-
101   tering out irrelevant information. Finally, Das et al. (2019) using targeted communication, to address
102   specific messages to specific agents.
103   Interpretability and the emergence of natural language has also been studied in the MARL setting.
104   Lazaridou et al. (2016) propose a method for communication using a discrete set of symbols, which
105   can be converted to natural language by matching emergent symbols with corresponding human
106   labels. Although this paper mainly focuses on referential games, which are rather simple in terms
107   of control. Havrylov & Titov (2017) use a sequence of symbols for encoding complex information,
      such as pictures, and use grounding to make the resulted encoder more similar to a natural language,


                                                        2
108
      which induce similarities but not necessarily preserve the meaning of words. Karten et al. (2023)
109   propose a three-phase learning, where agents first learn an emergent communication protocol, then,
110   uninformative messages are pruned, and the final phase involves teaming up with human players.
111   On the other hand, Kottur et al. (2017) show that emergent language of artificial agents is less likely
112   to assimilate natural language without additional constraints.
113
      Our work is built upon these previous approaches; by learning across agents (Foerster et al., 2016),
114
      use communication as a mapping of the observation (Lin et al., 2021), filter out irrelevant informa-
115   tion (Jiang & Lu, 2018), and shaping the reward (Eccles et al., 2019; Jaques et al., 2019). In addition,
116   our work introduces a novel framework that combines human-knowledge with RL and could be ap-
117   plied jointly with (almost) any other method for our setting, to enhance its performance and increase
118   its interpretability.

      3    BACKGROUND AND P ROBLEM S ETTING
123
124   Decentralized Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (DEC-POMDP), as introduced in
125   Bernstein et al. (2002), describes a framework in which multiple agents need to apply a decen-
126   tralized policy, based on each agent’s observation independently. Here, the reward function and the
127   transition kernel operate over the joint policy of all agents, and the partial observability may extend
128   to the centralized setting (i.e., a decentralized POMDP is a DEC-POMDP). A popular framework to
129   deal with the challenges arising from the decentralized approach is using a communication channel,
130
      where agents share information regarding their observations and future actions to result with a better
      overall policy.
131
132   Formally, consider a standard DEC-POMDP: I, S, {Ai }i∈I , P, R, {Ωi }i∈I , {Oi } , where I is the
133   set of agents, S is the state space, Ai is the action space of agent i ∈ I, P is the global transition
134   dynamics, R is the global reward function, Ωi , Oi are (respectively) the observation-space and the
135   conditional observation probabilities of agent i ∈ I. Additionally, Let n = |I| be the number of
136
      agent in the environment. Where a few formulations exist, adding communication to this setting
      can be reduced to an equivalent DEC-POMDP with increased observation-spaces (due to the com-
137
      munication signals) and additional action-spaces (for the communication-policy). In our setting,
138
      communication is allowed under the following condition: at time-step t where an agent observes ot
139   (the current observation) and Ct−1  i

140   needs to choose both at , the action for the environment, and Ct the communication signal that would
141   be available for the other agents at the next time-step t + 1. That means that Ct could only depend
142   on information the agent has at time-step t, hence the receiver obtains the information in delay of a
143   single time-step.

      While communication may help improve the joint policy by coordinating the agents actions and
145   allow mitigating the partial observability that originates from the decentralized setting, it poses a
146   major challenge – the decision-making problem of which messages should be communicated, and
147   how to use them. Many prior works utilize a discrete communication channel, which is similar to the
148   communication form of human beings, and can be used to decipher the message transaction. How-
149   ever, without additional constraints, communication may greatly differ from human-communication

      4.1   H UMAN - LIKE C OMMUNICATION S TRATEGIES
174
175   When facing a new task, humans often communicate with each other quite effectively. This happens
176   thanks to an already existing form of communication protocol (i.e., language), an agreed termi-
177   nology, and prior knowledge of the task, that allows the players to communicate well. Generally,
178   humans have an object-oriented perception, and a task’s terminology usually refer to a textual de-
179   scription of objects and their states. This allows to focus on a few relevant objects when communi-
180
      cating information, to avoid misunderstanding. While Humans determine the relevancy of an object
      from the task’s description, prior-knowledge and previous biases, artificial agents can not directly
181
      interpret it, and it is unclear how to generally embed them. Similarly to humans, artificial agents can
182
      benefit from more focused communication (i.e., less uninformative features), but learning a ‘human’
183   communication-policy from demonstrations or including feedback in the learning process (RLHF)
184   is likely to be unrealistic; the dimensions of MARL problems are relatively high, which would re-
185   quire either collecting a very large dataset of demonstrations or requesting human feedback over
186   huge amount of simulations. We propose a hybrid approach, that combines the ability of humans
187   to identify relevant objects with the ability of artificial agents to process the information with high
188   dimensions.
189   To ground the observations, we rely on a task-dependent component, text-to-mask, which maps
190   between a textual description of an object to a mask m ∈ {0, 1} in the same dimensions of the
191   (decentralized) observation-space. Potentially, the text-to-mask model may use both the textual
192   description and the current observation to calculate the mask, for example, in case of image obser-
193   vations, a mask can be a segmentation of the desired object. In the general vector case, it is natural
194   to view the observation as a collection of feature-sets, each one corresponding with a textual term
195   that represent an object. Formally, we define a text-to-mask model F : T × O → {0, 1}|O| , where
196   T is the textual input space, O is the observation space of each agent, and {0, 1}|O| is a binary
197   mask space with the same dimensionality as O. While most environments have a description of the
198   features (e.g., angle, velocity, position, etc.) that could be used for constructing T , complex settings
199   may require a tailored set of terms. For image observations, it is possible to use existing pretrained
200
      object detection models to extract the mask and additional features. In our implementation, we use a
      set of terms, each corresponding with a fixed set of elements of the observation space, which defines
201
      a mask, (i.e., F : T → {0, 1}|O| ). Learning from demonstrations can be fairly simple, as this model
202
      only need to construct a mask for the current observation and textual input, which does not involve
203
      control.

460   6   I NTERPRETABILITY AND E MERGENT B EHAVIOR
461
462   Our framework and model are designed for convenient transfer of human-knowledge to artificial
463   agents (Section 4.1). However, for interpretability, we need to transfer knowledge in the other
464   direction – from artificial agent to humans. As discussed earlier, while the resulted communication
465   is not directly grounded to natural language, the context is – what the agents are talking about,
466   instead of what are they saying. The context along with the agents’ behavior can provide a wider
467   understanding on their policy and what are they actually communicating.
468   In Fig. 8, we present a sample trajectory from the resulted ‘hybrid’ method, in the Coordinate sim-
469   ulation (Section 5.1). For every time-step, we show the state and the objects each agent chose to
470   transmit. Here, One agent has a private-goal, and the other have a shared-goal. Initially, the agent at
471   the top communicates its own position and the position of the shared-goal, while the other (bottom)
472   agent only communicates its own position. Over the next time-steps, the top agent moves towards
473   the shared-goal, then waits for the other agent. The bottom agent marks and claims its private-goal,
474   then moves on to mark the position of the top agent’s shared-goal. During this period, agents only
475   communicate their position. Once marked, the bottom agent communicates the mark’s position, and
476
      the ‘achieved’ status, then, the shared-goal is claimed and the simulation ends.
477   Analyzing the agents’ behavior, we can learn a few things: (1) Since the shared-goal position is
478   communicated once at the beginning, while the bottom agent knew exactly where to mark, we can
479   deduce that the policy indeed utilize its memory. (2) After the bottom agent marks the shared-goal
480   position, it immediately communicates both the mark’s location and his ‘achieved’ status. This can
481   be attributed to the bottom agent rushing the top agent to claim his goal – “I already claimed mine,
482
      go ahead and claim it”. (3) The agents communicate their location at all times, except the bottom
      agent, once marked the shared-goal. This information can be used to determine which goal to claim
483
      first, and once an agent finished their task, their location is irrelevant anymore.
484
485   Although it is a rather simple environment, this form of communication provides a specific context
      for a human “side-listener” that observes the agents’ behavior, and could be used in environments

507   7   D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
508
509   Communication can potentially improve the performance in any MARL task, but in practice it is
510   very hard to learn effective communication protocols, especially in dynamic settings that involve
511   partial observability, where efficient communication is critical. However, there is no free lunch, and
512   to learn efficient communication we turn to the masters – humans. Our abstraction of the object-
513   oriented perception derived an architecture that with a minimal engineering effort could be adapted
514   to many domains, extracting and utilizing human knowledge to enable effective communication.
515
      Moreover, as we demonstrate in Section 6, we are able to observe the context of a conversation,
      which helps to interpret and even communicate with the agents.
516
517   We believe that hybrid approaches, such as ours, that incorporate human-knowledge in RL pave the
518   path for practical applications in many domains that are perceived to be hard for artificial agents.
519   One major issue that arises when involving humans in the training is the data-collection process.
520   Especially in RL, collecting human demonstrations is not trivial and in many cases impractical. On
521
      the other hand, smart architectures and framework that consider this issue may be utilized, such as
      in this work.
522
523   Further research could be done in several directions. First, we use a simple text-to-mask model,
524   which could be replaced with a more sophisticated LLM-based model, in light of the recent devel-
525   opments in this field. Second, more complex textual descriptions may require an impractical action-
526   space for the communication policy, this could again be addressed with an LLM. Third, the concept
527
      of policies that mask other policies observations could be extended to the communication times (i.e.,
      policies that determine at what times to communicate if at all), or applied in a single-agent setting,
528
      where the observation-space is large or contains many distractions. Finally, our framework allows
529
      humans to intervene during the training or even through the inference by communicating various
530   observations over the communication channel, although it is possible, it is outside our scope.
