009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Quantized neural networks (QNNs) are often used in edge AI because they reduce
012            memory and computational demands. In practical applications such as control
013            systems, medical imaging, and robotics, controlling input noise is crucial for en-
014            hancing system robustness. Thus, improving the noise resilience of QNNs is an
015            important challenge in achieving effective edge AI applications. In this paper,
016            we investigate the impact of input noise on QNN performance and propose the
017            safe haven activation quantization (SHAQ) method. This approach leverages the
018
               characteristics of the quantization function to constrain outputs before quantiza-
               tion within a more noise-resilient ’safe’ range, effectively reducing the impact of
019
               noise across quantized layers. Our methods achieve state-of-the-art, 73.11% accu-
020
               racy with 2-bit activations under the fast gradient sign method (FGSM) adversarial
021            attacks with an epsilon of 8/255 on the CIFAR-10 dataset. Furthermore, we ex-
022            tend our methods into a plug-and-play solution we call quantized helmet (QH),
023            comprising a series of quantized layers that can be integrated into any unquan-
024            tized neural network to enhance its noise robustness. Our experimental code and
025            analysis are open-source and publicly accessible.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Quantized neural network (QNN) models use few bits to encode activations of their layers Hubara
031   et al. (2018). This will enable running larger models without requiring more capable hardware
032   and new machine learning capabilities on resource-constrained devices like wearables Yan et al.
033
      (2021); Yi et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024). Since resource efficiency is crucial in achieving better
      performance in neural networks, model quantization has been a highly active research area Polino
034
      et al. (2018); Fan et al. (2020); He et al. (2024). However, recent research Lin et al. (2023) indicates
035
      that basic quantization methods leave QNNs more susceptible to adversarial attacks compared to
036   unquantized models. Therefore, enhancing noise robustness in QNNs without the loss of accuracy
037   in noisy environments is an important challenge.
038
039
      QANS Lin et al. (2023) and DQ Lin et al. (2019) are previous efforts that incorporated regularization
      terms based on the Lipschitz constant of each layer’s weights to mitigate error amplification in
040
      deeper layers due to noisy input. In addition, employing adversarial training strategies, such as
041
      using Projected gradient descent (PGD) Shafahi et al. (2019); Wong et al. (2020) or adversarial
042   fast gradient sign method (FGSM) noise, has enhanced noise robustness Pan et al. (2024); Jia et al.
043   (2024). However, despite using these training strategies, QNN accuracy still suffers significantly
044   under adversarial noise. For instance, when testing a VGG16 model on the CIFAR-10 dataset, the
045   accuracy under clean input with QANS is 90.75%, but it drops to 58.12% under FGSM and 33.82%
046   under PGD-20 (ε = 8/255 ), reflecting a roughly 30-50% decrease. This drastic degradation in
047   model performance under noisy input highlights the potential for further improvement and is the
048   main motivation of our research.
049   In this paper, we propose the safe haven activation quantization (SHAQ) method. Our method mit-
050   igates noise-related performance degradations in QNNs by leveraging quantized activation values.
051   Since quantization functions map a range of input values to a single output value. The key insight
052   is that within this range, some values are more noise-resilient than others. For example, suppose in
053   [0, 1], we quantize it by a step of 0.5, giving rise to three quantized values, 0, 0.5, and 1. In the range
      of (0.5, 1], the value of 0.75 can tolerate a noisy input value within ±0.25 of the original value while


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      producing the same output value as the quantized activation. Our method aims to push the activation
055   values into such values, we call ‘safe havens’ where the greatest amount of noise can be tolerated.
056
057   With SHAQ, our quantized neural network achieves a 91.54% accuracy under clean input on the
058
      CIFAR-10 dataset using the VGG16 architecture. Under a white-box FGSM adversarial attack with
      an ε of 8/255, the model achieves state-of-the-art accuracy (73.11%). Additionally, our method
059
      provides superior performance for QNN models against a range of other perturbations, including
060
      variations of PGD, random noise, R+FGSM, CW2, and DDN2 Goodfellow et al. (2014); Tramèr
061   et al. (2017); Madry et al. (2017).
062
063   Furthermore, we extended our robust QNN training method to enhance the noise robustness of any
064
      neural network model. We propose a quantized helmet (QH) method where target neural networks
      are augmented with a learnable ‘helmet’ structure, which consists of clamped and quantized acti-
065
      vations placed at the head of the model. The augmented models are trained with SHAQ, which
066
      is designed to penalize input values near the decision boundaries of the clamped and quantized
067   activation functions, pushing these activations into a safer range and thereby minimizing error am-
068   plification. Our findings demonstrate substantial improvements in noise robustness across various
069   convolutional neural networks. For example, a non-quantized VGG16 network on the CIFAR-10
070   dataset, when subjected to an FGSM attack with ε of 8/255, achieves 21.95% higher accuracy with
071   the ‘helmet’ compared to without it.
072
      The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:
073
074          • We analyze the impact of noise on the performance of QNNs and provide an analytical
075            perspective to understand noise-related performance degradations in QNNs.
076          • We introduce SHAQ, a training method involving a quantization-aware loss function, to
077            enhance noise robustness by minimizing error propagation. We achieve SOTA accuracy
078            (73.11%) for QNNs under FGSM attacks with ε of 8/255.
079          • We propose a plug-and-play ‘helmet’ structure that consists of clamped and quantized lay-
080            ers. Our Quantized Helmet can be placed at the head of any neural network model, in-
081            cluding non-quantized networks. It leverages the same idea in SHAQ but offers a modular
082            solution for improving noise robustness of already existing models.
083
      This paper is organized as follows: Section 2 details our approach to enhancing the noise robust-
084
      ness of quantized models through SHAQ and their application of QH to improve the robustness of
085
      unquantized neural networks. In Section 3, we present results demonstrating the improved noise
086   resilience of quantized VGG and ResNet architectures applied to the CIFAR-10 and SVHN dataset,
087   alongside a comparison with existing methods. Finally, Section 4 explores the the combination of
088   our methods with adversarial purification, concluding with a summary of our findings.

      2.1   S AFE H AVEN ACTIVATION Q UANTIZATION (SHAQ)
093
094   Quantized activation functions map continuous values into discrete ranges, resulting in information
095   loss that may lead to residual errors. On the other hand, another characteristic of quantized activation
096   functions is that they are stable under perturbations of the input values to a certain extent. Then, a
097   research question arises: Can this natural quantization characteristic be exploited to eliminate the
098   adverse effects of the input noise? Although it is true that most values stay stable under noise,
099   the impact of perturbation is potentially amplified when the perturbed value is near quantization
100
      boundaries. Our method proposes a way to tune the network to produce only ‘safe’ input values that
      are away from quantization boundaries, eliminating the impact of noise.
101

103   distribution U[−ε, ε] where ε is the scale of the noise.

283   Random perturbation attack: This attack involves adding uniformly sampled noise within the

343
344   We present our results for both QNNs and non-QNNs under six different types of noise. The white-
345   box attack results for CIFAR-10 and SVHN using popular architectures such as VGG16 Simonyan
346   & Zisserman (2014) and ResNet-18 He et al. (2016) are shown in Table 1. In QNNs, to prevent
347   input noise errors from growing exponentially, a common strategy involves limiting the network’s
348   Lipschitz constant, which is the product of the Lipschitz constants of individual layers. A Lipschitz
349
      constant greater than 1, often observed during standard training, indicates that perturbations will be
      amplified. To mitigate this, a regularization term ∥W T W − βI∥ is applied, ensuring the orthog-
350
      onality of the weight matrix rows Lin et al. (2019; 2023). By controlling the Lipschitz constant
351
      during quantization, these works effectively limit error amplification. Notably, Lin et al. (2019)
352   and Lin et al. (2023) independently developed defensive quantization (DQ) and quantization ad-
353   versarial noise suppression (QANS) to improve model robustness against adversarial noise. For
354   instance, under FGSM attacks with ε = 8/255, DQ and QANS achieved maximum accuracies of
355   65.52% and 58.12% respectively on CIFAR-10 with VGG-16. In addition this, we also compare our
356   method to non-quantized networks using mainstream adversarial training and image preprocessing
357   methods, such as PGD-AT Madry et al. (2017), TRADES Zhang et al. (2019), and MART Wang
358   et al. (2019). Although these methods achieve higher accuracy under attacks like PGD-20, CW2,
359   and DDN2 compared to QNN approaches, this often comes at the cost of reduced clean accuracy.
360   In the context of QNNs, our SHAQ method with 2 bits activation setting consistently achieves high
361   accuracy against adversarial attacks while maintaining similar or even higher clean accuracy com-
362   pared to existing approaches. When comparing our QH method (2-bits 2-hidden-layer quantized
363   helmet) for enhancing noise robustness in unquantized models, it consistently outperforms, achiev-
364   ing 5-10% higher accuracy under clean conditions and against random noise, FGSM, FGSM+R,
365   and DDN2 attacks. For PGD-20 and CW2 attacks, our results are comparable or slightly better than
366
      other Non-QNN methods.

      In this section, we compare the performance of the VGG-16 model on the CIFAR-10 dataset with
441   and without the quantized helmet. We begin by presenting an ablation study on the number of hidden
442   layers and their impact on both clean and adversarial FGSM accuracy. We experimented with both
443   2-bit and 3-bit helmet settings and found that performance did not vary significantly. However,
444   the 2-bit helmet consistently achieved approximately 1% higher FGSM accuracy (ACCF ) than the
445   3-bit setting. Furthermore, it is more energy saving, leading to our choice of the 2-bit activation
446   quantization for the helmet.
447   As shown in Figure 5 (a) to (d) for the choice of hidden layers, deeper layers capture more robust
448   features against adversarial attacks, increasing FGSM accuracy with depth. However, adding more
449   layers results in a drop in clean accuracy, higher training costs, and increased energy consumption.
450   We found that nearly all robust features were captured with two hidden layers, achieving high ACCF .
451   Therefore, we selected the 2-hidden-layer, 2-bit helmet configuration for all subsequent experiments.
452   For instance, as shown in Table 4, only with QH, although the clean accuracy slightly decreases from
453   93.87% to 92.15% with the 2-bit, 2-hidden-layer helmet, there is an improvement in accuracy under
454   adversarial FGSM attack, from 42.39% to 64.34%, an improvement of over 20%.

479   cally, we adapt the TPAP framework Tang & Zhang (2024), which utilizes an FGSM-robust overfit-

      purification with ε = 8/255, and then tested on the purified noisy data to improve robustness.
483   As shown in Table 5, combining our algorithm with TPAP results in significant improvements in

485   lower when using our method alone, as seen in Table 1. Additionally, compared to using TPAP
      alone, our combined approach consistently achieves higher accuracy across all noise settings.

      5   C ONCLUSION
494
495
      In this paper, we analyze the root cause of quantization-related performance degradations in QNNs.
496   Using the insights that some values in the quantization range tolerate errors better than others, we
497   devised a way to exploit the characteristics of quantized activations to mitigate numerical errors
498   caused by noisy input values. We achieve this by pushing the input values of quantized activations
499   closer to ‘safe havens’ by fine-tuning the network using an augmented loss function. We introduced
500   our SHAQ method that achieved state-of-the-art accuracy for QNNs under various adversarial noise
501   models on the CIFAR-10 and SVHN dataset with different model architectures, with minimal impact
502   on clean accuracy. Based on the insights of SHAQ, we also introduced the ‘plug-and-play’ quantized
503   helmet structure that provides a solution to enhance the noise robustness of any neural network
504
      model, in particular, unquantized ones. We achieved 88.59% and 92.09% accuracy in the face of
      the harshest DDN2 and CW2 attacks using VGG16 on the CIFAR-10 dataset. We also showed
505
      that SHAQ and QH can complement existing state-of-the-art methods. Future direction of research
506
      beyond our work might include conducting a thorough analysis of the trade-offs between model size,
507   inference speed, and robustness when applying our methods to resource-constrained devices, such
508   as mobile phones and IoT devices.
