                                                   A BSTRACT
011
               For tabular datasets, the change in the relationship between the label and covariates
012
               (Y |X-shifts) is common due to missing variables. Since it is impossible to general-
013
               ize to a completely new and unknown domain, we study models that are easy to
014            adapt to the target domain even with few labeled examples. We focus on building
015            more informative representations of tabular data that can mitigate Y |X-shifts, and
016            propose to leverage the prior world knowledge in LLMs by serializing the tabular
017            data to encode it. We find LLM embeddings alone provide inconsistent improve-
018            ments in robustness, but models trained on them can be well adapted to the target
019            domain even using 32 labeled observations. Our finding is based on a systematic
020            study consisting of 7650 source-target pairs and benchmark against 261,000 model
021            configurations trained by 20 algorithms. Our observation holds when ablating the
022
               size of accessible target data and different adaptation strategies.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Predictive performance degrades when the distribution of target domain shifts from that of source
      (training) (Bandi et al., 2018; Wong et al., 2021; Hand, 2006; Ding et al., 2021a; Amorim et al., 2018).
028
      Distribution shifts can be categorized into shifts in the marginal distribution of covariates (X-shifts)
029
      or changes in the relationship between the label and covariates (Y |X-shifts). In computer vision,
030   X-shifts are prevalent since high-quality human labels are consistent across different images (Recht
031   et al., 2019; Miller et al., 2021; Shankar et al., 2019); in contrast, Y |X-shifts are prevalent in tabular
032   data due to missing variables and hidden confounders. There is a large body of work addressing
033   X-shifts due to its dominance in vision and language (Li et al., 2017; Zhuang et al., 2020; Zhou et al.,
034   2022), yet the work on Y |X-shifts remain relatively limited (Liu et al., 2023).
035
      The main challenge with addressing Y |X-shifts in tabular tasks is that the source data may provide
036   little insight on the target distribution. Since it is impossible to generalize to a completely new and
037   unknown domain (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Rosenfeld et al., 2021), we focus on leveraging few labeled
038   target examples (on the order of 10 to 100) to address small Y |X-shifts that negatively impact model
039   performance. Our goal is to build a feature representation ϕ(X) such that the difference between
040   Esource [Y |ϕ(X)] and Etarget [Y |ϕ(X)] are learnable even based on a few target data.
041
      Using the wealth of world knowledge learned during pre-training, LLMs have the potential to build
042   representations that mitigate the impact of confounders whose distribution changes across source and
043   target. Specifically, we use a LLM encoder (e5-Mistral-7B-Instruct) to featurize tabular
044   data—which we referred to as LLM embeddings—and fit a shallow neural network (NN) on these
045   embeddings for tabular prediction (Figure 1). In contrast to classical numerical encoding of tabular
046   data, our approach automatically incorporates the semantics of each covariate using off-the-shelf
047   LLMs, and can include additional contextual domain-level information that can help account for
048   missing variables whose distribution shifts from source to target.
049   In this work, we restrict attention to lightweight probing approaches instead of expensive end-to-end
050   updates (Hegselmann et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024) that require full weight access and weight
051   updates to the LLM (we find this infeasible across the thousands of source-target pairs we consider).
052   Throughout our investigation, we use the same LLM encoder to extract the LLM embeddings, and
053   we only “finetune” the shallow NNs we use as the main prediction model across target domains.
      Investigating how different LLM encoders affect tabular Y |X shifts is left as a future work.

156   Related work Tabular data is a common modality in electronic health records, finance, and social
157   and natural sciences. Unlike other modalities like images and text, gradient-boosted trees (GBDT;
158   GBM (Friedman, 2001; 2002), XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016), LGBM (Ke et al., 2017)) remain
159   the state-of-the-art (Gorishniy et al., 2021; Shwartz-Ziv & Armon, 2022; Gardner et al., 2022) even
160   when compared to neural networks specifically designed for tabular data (Arik & Pfister, 2021;
161   Huang et al., 2020; Kadra et al., 2021; Katzir et al., 2020). GBDTs have recently been observed
      to provide strong performance under distribution shifts, which forms the basis of its use as a main

      baseline (Gardner et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023). In addition, some recent works employ in-context
163   learning to address the few-shot classification problem (Hegselmann et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024).
164   However, they are significantly more resource-intensive to finetune the LLM itself. Given the huge
165   number of settings, we only compare with GPT4-mini for this line of research in this work. Besides,
166   while we use standard LLMs to generate embeddings, LLMs specialized for tabular data (Yan et al.)
167   can also be used as embedding extractors.
168
      A wide range of methods have been proposed to address distribution shifts, notably robust learning
169   methods, balancing methods, and invariant learning methods. Distributionally robust optimization
170   (DRO) construct an uncertainty set around the training distribution and optimizing for the worst-case
171   distribution within this set, thereby mitigating the impact of potential distribution shifts. Variants of
172   DRO methods have been developed using different distance metrics, such as χ2 -divergence (Duchi
173   & Namkoong, 2021; Duchi et al., 2021), KL-divergence (Hu & Hong, 2013), and Wasserstein
174   distance (Blanchet et al., 2017; 2018; 2019a; 2023a). However, these approaches have recently been
175   observed to be ineffective in addressing real-world tabular distribution shifts Liu et al. (2023). On
176   the other hand, invariant learning (Peters et al., 2016; Arjovsky et al., 2019; Koyama & Yamaguchi,
177
      2020) seeks to learn causally invariant relationships across multiple pre-defined environments. In
      this work, we include 5 typical DRO methods but do not consider invariant learning methods as
178
      they require multiple training environments, which is not the focus of this work. Some statistical
179
      works (Li et al., 2022; Tian & Feng, 2023) provide theoretical guarantees for simple linear models
180   in transfer learning, but these guarantees often do not extend to more complex models like decision
181   trees or neural networks used in real-world applications. This version improves clarity and flow while
182   retaining the original meaning.Additionally, many studies have explored domain adaptation (Iwasawa
183   & Matsuo, 2021; Liang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023). However, most of these focus on X-shifts in
184   image data, whereas our work addresses Y |X-shifts in tabular data.

      2     M ETHODS
187
188   In this section, we introduce a series of methods utilizing LLM embeddings for tabular prediction, as
189   well as different choices to incorporate additional domain information, different model architectures,
190   and target adaptation techniques using a small amount of labeled target samples. To the best of our
191   knowledge, this work is the first to comprehensively explore the impact of LLM embeddings on
192   tabular Y |X-shifts.
193
194   2.1   LLM E MBEDDINGS FOR TABULAR P REDICTION
195
196   We first introduce how we transform tabular data into LLM embeddings, where the key idea is to
197
      serialize each sample into a natural language format that the LLM can process. There is a substantial
      body of research on serialization, including using another LLM to rewrite tabular data into natural
198
      language (Hegselmann et al., 2023), adding descriptions of the classification task, training and test
199
      examples (Hegselmann et al., 2023; Slack & Singh, 2023), etc. Among these methods, Hegselmann
200   et al. (2023) demonstrate that using a straightforward text template with a task description consistently
201   achieves the best empirical performance.
202
203
      Using an income prediction problem to illustrate, consider a simple task description
      such as “Classify whether US working adults’ yearly income is above
204
      $50000 in 2018.” along with a simple serialization template that enumerates all features
205
      in the format “The [feature name] is [value]”. Adopting this serialization approach,
206   we employ the encoder model e5-Mistral-7B-Instruct to generate the LLM embedding.
207   Formally, the encoder takes the serialization Serialize(X) of sample X as input and outputs its
208   corresponding embedding Φ(X) as
209                           serialization             e5-Mistral-7B-Instruct
210                        X −−−−−−→ Serialize(X) −−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−→ Φ(X).
211   Since e5-mistral-7b-instruct requires input data to be formatted in the following template:
212
              Instruct:                description of the classification task \n
213
              Query:                   description of the data,
214
215   we provide task description in the “Instruct” part, and use the serilization template to format the
      tabular data in the “Query” part. An illustrative example is provided in Part A-D of Figure 1, with

      2.2   A DDITIONAL D OMAIN I NFORMATION
220
221   Another advantage of using LLM embeddings is their ability to incorporate additional domain
222   information or prior knowledge, denoted by C. As demonstrated in Section 1, incorporating domain-
223   specific information can help address Y |X-shifts and improve generalization performance in the
224   target domain.
225   In this work, we propose a simple yet effective approach for integrating domain knowledge into
226   tabular predictions. Rather than combining the domain information with serialized tabular data
227   and generating a single LLM embedding, we generate separate LLM embeddings for the domain
228   knowledge and the serialized tabular data, and then concatenate them together. The benefits of this
229   approach are twofold: (a) although the domain information may contain significantly more words than
230   the serialized tabular features, our concatenation method ensures a balanced 1:1 ratio between the
231   two, preventing a single embedding that disproportionately focuses on the longer domain information;
232
      (b) by separating the tabular features from the domain information, we can efficiently update the
      domain information without having to regenerate all the embeddings for the entire dataset.
233
234   We explore three sources of domain information: Wikipedia, GPT-4, and labeled target samples.
235   Given that our experiments (see Table 1 and Section 3) focus primarily on socioeconomic factors,
236   we collected “Economy” data for each U.S. state from Wikipedia as C. For GPT-4, we prompt it
237   to provide background knowledge relevant to each prediction task in each state as C. For labeled
238
      target samples, we serialize 32 labeled samples from the concerned domain as the prior knowledge
      C. Further details can be found at Appendix A.2. After obtaining domain information C, we use
239
      e5-mistral-7b-instruct to generate an LLM embedding for C. As illustrated in Parts E.2
240
      and F.1 of Figure 1, this embedding is then concatenated with the LLM embeddings of the tabular data,
241   which serve as input to the backend neural network models (NN). This approach allows us to generate
242   the LLM embedding for the dataset just once, and subsequently concatenate it with embeddings
243   from different prompts as needed. In Section 3, we study whether and how this additional domain
244   information can enhance generalization under Y |X-shifts.
245
      In addition, recent works on prompt engineering have focused on incorporating additional domain
246
      information to enhance prediction tasks, often through detailed instructions (Schick & Schütze,
247   2020; Shin et al., 2020). Our proposed framework introduces a novel approach to leveraging such
248   information and remains fully compatible with these existing methods.
249
250   2.3   M ODEL T RAINING AND TARGET A DAPTATION
251
252   Model architecture For the backend model, we use a vanilla neural network (NN) classifier on both
253   tabular features and LLM embeddings for tabular data classification. The NN is a simple feedforward
254   neural network with several hidden layers, dropout layer, and ReLU activation functions.
255   When adding additional domain information via an embedding layer, the same embedding is applied
256   to all samples from the same domain. Since the output of e5-mistral-7b-instruct is a
257   4096-dimensional vector, we simply concatenate the LLM embeddings with the embeddings of the
258   domain information. This concatenated vector is then passed through the hidden layers, dropout
259   layer, and ReLU activation functions. For all NNs, the final linear layer an output dimension of
260   2, followed by a softmax layer for binary classification. During training, we use cross-entropy as
261
      the loss function, batch size as 128, and use the Adam optimizer. Detailed model architecture and
      hyper-parameters are provided in the Appendix A.3 and A.4, with a discussion on hyperparameter
262
      selection provided in Section 3.1.
263
264   Target Adaptation Even with the incorporation of LLM embeddings and domain information, our
265   model may still experience Y |X-shifts. In practice, it is common to have a small set of samples from
266
      the target domain, which can be leveraged to better adapt the model to the target domain.
267   For each (source domain, target domain) pair, we begin by selecting the best training hyperparameter
268   based on a validation criterion, which will be discussed in the Section 3.1. Using this model trained
269   on the source domain, we explore four primary methods for target adaptation: in-context domain info,
      full-parameter fine-tuning, low-rank adaptation (LoRA), and prefix tuning for domain information.

289   For in-context domain info (F.1 of Figure 1), we keep the trained model frozen and only update the
290   domain information, switching it from natural language description of labeled sample from the source
291   domain during training to that of target domain during inference phase. For the other three methods,

293   neural network is fine-tuned using the target samples. For LoRA, we introduce a low-rank adaptation
294
      layer to each linear layer by incorporating two smaller matrices, A and B, both with a rank of 16.
      Specifically, matrix A has dimensions corresponding to the input size and the rank, while matrix B
295
      has dimensions corresponding to the rank and the output size. Matrix A is initialized with a mean
296
      of 0 and a standard deviation of 0.02, whereas matrix B is initialized with zeros. These matrices
297   are then multiplied together and added to the original weight matrix. We then fine-tune only these
298   LoRA parameters, while keeping the rest of the model unchanged. In prefix tuning (F.3 of Figure 1),
299   the initial domain information embedding serves as a starting point for further refinement. During
300   training, both the the NN and the domain information embedding of the source domain are trained.
301   For target adaptation, we switch the domain information embedding from the source to the target
302   domain. The NN is kept frozen, and only the domain information embedding of the target domain is
303   updated using these samples from the target domain. We refer to this process as prefix tuning.
304   As shown in Table 1, we use different hyperparameters for target adaptation. Detailed hyperparameters
305   are provided in Appendix A.4, and the hyperparameter selection process is discussed in Section 3.1.

316   Dataset In this work, we use the ACS dataset (Ding et al., 2021b) derived from the US-wide ACS

318         • ACS Income: The goal is to predict whether an individual’s income is above $50K based

320           with usual working hours of at least 1 hour per week in the past year, and an income of at

352
      Given the numerous training hyperparameters—learning rate, number of training epochs, hidden
      layer dimension, dropout ratio—we use a validation set of 32 randomly sampled labeled target
353
      domain samples to choose the optimal training hyperparameters, based on the highest F1 score in
354
      the validation dataset. Since our metric is Macro F1 score, the validation set is set as balanced
355   between positive and negative classes. Note that the hyperparameter selection is near-oracle, as it
356   leverages target samples, albeit in a limited quantity. When doing finetuning, we sample another
357   32 labeled target samples to finetune the model. And we use the same 32-sample validation dataset
358   (for training hyperparameter selection) to select the target adaptation hyperparameters that yield
359   the best Macro F1 score. Note that our testbed allows flexible sample sizes for training, validation,
360   testing, and finetuning. Additionally, we perform an ablation study on different allocations of the
361   overall target samples in validation and finetuning (see Figure 8). See details on hyperparameters in
362   the Appendix A.4.

            3.3                                       AUXILIARY F INDINGS
468
469        In addition to the primary findings, we have several other noteworthy observations.
470
471        “Right” domain information matters. From Figure 6, we observe that additional domain infor-
472        mation from either Wikipedia or 32 target samples (via in-context domain info, F.1 of Figure 1)
473        does not lead to significant improvements (comparing the shallow blue and yellow curves with
474
           the shallow red curve). However, when combined with finetuning, this extra domain information
           performs significantly better on ACS Pub.Cov. Since finetuning can be considered a method for
475
           incorporating domain-specific information, this suggests that identifying the right domain information
476
           is crucial.
477
478        Specific finetuning approaches are less crucial than expected. Given the large number of model
479        parameters and limited labeled target samples, one might expect parameter-efficient methods like
480        Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) and Prefix Tuning to offer a clear advantage. However, as shown in 7,
481        all methods significantly outperform the non-finetuned version when using LLM embeddings, and
482
           the choice of finetuning method appears less significant in our setting.
483        An exception is prefix tuning on the ACS Pub.Cov task, where performance was several percentage
484        points lower. While this requires further investigation, our key takeaway is that under Y |X shifts, 1)
485        finetuning models using LLM embeddings can greatly enhance classification performance; 2) popular
           finetuning methods yield comparable results.

      Allocation of labeled target samples matters. In Figure 8, we compare two allocation schemes of

762
763   As discussed in Section 2.1, serializing a row of tabular data, such as X, requires two components: a
764   task description and a description of the data.
765
766   Task description For the ACS Income, ACS Mobility, and ACS Public Coverage datasets, we
767   consider the same binary classification task as described in Ding et al. (2021a). We adopt concise
768   task descriptions as suggested by Hegselmann et al. (2023), as follows:
769
770          • ACS Income: Classify whether US working adults’ yearly income is above $50000 in
771
               2018.
772          • ACS Mobility: Classify whether a young adult moved addresses in the last year.
773          • ACS Public Coverage : Classify whether a low-income individual, not eligible for
774            Medicare, has coverage from public health insurance.
775
776   Description of the data For all three ACS datasets, we utilize features as shown in (Ding et al.,
777   2021a, Appendix B), along with the domain name (state) to characterize the data. Specifically, for
778   data from the source state, we apply the source domain name, and for data from the target state, we
779   use the target domain name.
780   As illustrated in Figure 1 and recommended by Hegselmann et al. (2023), we adopt a straightforward
781   text template: ”The feature name is value.” However, for certain features with less common
782   or more complex feature names, we opt for a template as “The person (appropriate verb) value” to
783   more clearly convey the data description. For example, for the feature “Gave birth to child within the
784   past 12 months” with the value “No”, the serialization would be “The person did not give birth to a
785   child within the past 12 months.” The features that use this alternative template are as follows:
786
787
             • ACS Income: class of worker;
788          • ACS Mobility: class of worker, disability, employment status of parents, citizenship,
789            military service, hearing difficulty, vision difficulty, cognitive difficulty, grandparent living
790            with grandchildren, employment status;
791          • ACS Public Coverage: disability, employment status of parents, citizenship, mobility,
792            military service, hearing difficulty, vision difficulty, cognitive difficulty, gave birth to child
793            within the past 12 months, employment status.

850   A.3                          M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
851
852
      We detail the baselines used in our paper.
853
854
      Fully-connected Neural Networks (NN) Given the varying input dimensions for Tabular features,
      LLM embeddings, and LLM embeddings with additional domain information, we employ three
855
      neural networks with similar architectures. We then train these networks and conduct target adaptation
856
      using Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM).
857
858   For all three datasets using Tabular features, we use a hidden layer with output dimension being
859   hidden layer dim as a hyperparameter, a dropuput layer with dropout ratio being a
860
      hyperparameter, ReLu activation. We then have another hidden layer with input and output dimension
      both being hidden layer dim, and then softmax layer with dimension 2 as the output.
861
862   For datasets using Tabular features, the network includes a hidden layer where the output dimension
863   is set by the hyperparameter hidden layer dim. It is followed by a dropout layer (dropout
      ratio as a hyperparameter), ReLU activation, another hidden layer the input and output dimensions


                                                                                                                    16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      are both equal to hidden layer dim. The network concludes with a softmax layer with an
865   output dimension of 2.
866
867   For datasets using only LLM embeddings, the input dimension is 4096, which is the output dimen-
868
      sion of e5-mistral-7b-instruct. The network consists of three hidden layers with fixed
      dimensions, where the input and output dimensions are (4096, 1024), (1024, 256), and (256, 128),
869
      respectively. Each layer uses ReLU activation. Next, there’s a hidden layer with an input dimension
870
      of 128 and an output dimension set by the hidden layer dim hyperparameter. This is followed
871   by a dropout layer (with dropout ratio as a hyperparameter), ReLU activation, and another
872   hidden layer where both the input and output dimensions are hidden layer dim. A ReLU
873   activation follows this final hidden layer, and the network concludes with a softmax layer that outputs
874   a dimension of 2.
875
      For datasets using LLM embeddings concatenated with additional domain information, the input
876
      dimension is 8192. We slightly update the first three hidden layers, with input and out dimensions
877   being (8192, 2048), (2048, 512), and (512, 128), respectively. All other neural network structure and
878   hyperparameters are the same as NNs with LLM embeddings only.
879
880
      When applying low-rank adaptation (LoRA) for target adaptation, we introduce a low-rank adaptation
      layer to each linear layer by incorporating two smaller matrices, A and B, both with a rank of 16.
881
      Specifically, matrix A has dimensions corresponding to the input size and the rank, while matrix B
882
      has dimensions corresponding to the rank and the output size. Matrix A is initialized with a mean of
883   0 and a standard deviation of 0.02, whereas matrix B is initialized with zeros. These matrices are
884   then multiplied together and added to the original weight matrix.
885
886
      Tree Ensemble Models Gardner et al. (2022) show that several tree-based methods are competitive
887
      on tabular datasets. And gradient-boosted trees (e.g., XGB (Chen & Guestrin, 2016), LGBM (Ke
888
      et al., 2017), GBM (Natekin & Knoll, 2013)) are widely considered as the state-of-the-art methods
889   on tabular data. Therefore, we compare XGB, LGBM, and GBM in this work. For GBM, we use
890   the standard implementations in scikit-learn (Pedregosa et al., 2011). For XGB and LGBM,
891   we use the standard implementations in the xgboost package2 and the lightgbm package3 . All
892   these methods are trained on CPUs.
