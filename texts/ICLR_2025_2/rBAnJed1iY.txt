009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Federated Learning (FL), which is a decentralized machine learning (ML) approach,
012            often incorporates differential privacy (DP) to enhance data privacy guarantees.
013            However, differentially private federated learning (DPFL) introduces performance
014            disparities across clients, particularly affecting minority groups. Some recent
015            works have attempted to address large data heterogeneity in vanilla FL settings
016            through clustering clients, but these methods remain sensitive and prone to errors
017            further exacerbated by the DP noise, making them inappropriate for DPFL settings.
018            We propose an algorithm for differentially private clustered FL, which is robust
019
               to the DP noise in the system and identifies clients’ clusters correctly. To this
               end, we propose to cluster clients based on both their model updates and training
020
               loss values. Furthermore, when clustering clients’ model updates, our proposed
021
               approach addresses the server’s uncertainties by employing large batch sizes as
022            well as Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) to reduce the impact of DP and stochastic
023            noise and avoid potential clustering errors. This idea is efficient especially in
024            privacy-sensitive scenarios with more DP noise. We provide theoretical analysis
025            justifying our approach, and evaluate it extensively across diverse data distributions
026            and privacy budgets. Our experimental results show its effectiveness in addressing
027            large data heterogeneity in DPFL systems with a small computational cost.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Federated learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017) is a collaborative ML paradigm, which allows
033   multiple clients to train a shared global model without sharing their data. However, in order for
034   FL algorithms to ensure rigorous privacy guarantees against data privacy attacks (Hitaj et al., 2017;
035   Rigaki & García, 2020; Wang et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2019; Geiping et al., 2020), they are reinforced
036   with DP (Dwork et al., 2006b;a; Dwork, 2011; Dwork & Roth, 2014). This is done in the presence
037   of a trusted server (McMahan et al., 2018; Geyer et al., 2017) and in its absence (Zhao et al., 2020;
038   Duchi et al., 2013; 2018). In the latter case and for record-level DP, each client adds noise to its
039
      stochastic gradients locally and shares its noisy model update with the server at the end of each round.
040   A key challenge in FL settings is ensuring a similar performance across clients under heterogeneous
041   data distributions, where several existing works focus on accuracy parity across clients with a single
042   common model (Mohri et al., 2019; Michieli & Ozay, 2021). However, a single global model often
043   fails to adapt to the data heterogeneity across clients (Chu et al., 2023), especially with extreme
044   covariate and label shifts. To address this, multiple methods were proposed to achieve performance
045
      parity in non-DP FL settings: agnostic federated learning (Mohri et al., 2019), client reweighting (Li
      et al., 2020b;a; Zhang et al., 2023), multi-task learning (Smith et al., 2017; Li et al., 2021; Marfoq
046
      et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2023), transfer learning (Li & Wang, 2019; Liu et al., 2020) and clustered
047
      FL (Ghosh et al., 2020; Mansour et al., 2020; Ruan & Joe-Wong, 2021; Sattler et al., 2019; Werner
048   et al., 2023; Briggs et al., 2020), where the latter is the focus of this work. On the other hand, when
049   augmenting FL with DP for getting rigorous privacy guarantees, DP can have disparate impacts
050   on the accuracy of different subgroups of clients - even with small imbalances and loose privacy
051   guarantees (Farrand et al., 2020; Fioretto et al., 2022; Bagdasaryan & Shmatikov, 2019). In fact,
052   groups with minority data experience a larger drop in model utility (larger privacy cost). Being due to
053   the inequitable gradient clipping in DPSGD (Abadi et al., 2016; Bagdasaryan & Shmatikov, 2019; Xu
      et al., 2021; Esipova et al., 2022), this behavior has become increasingly important to be addressed.

054
      As mentioned, clustered FL was proposed as an efficient personalization technique in vanilla FL for
055   performance parity under extreme data heterogeneity across clusters of clients: subsets of clients
056   are grouped together by the server based on their loss values (Ghosh et al., 2020; Mansour et al.,
057   2020; Ruan & Joe-Wong, 2021; Chu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022) or their gradients (model updates)
058   (Sattler et al., 2019; Werner et al., 2023; Briggs et al., 2020). As discussed in (Werner et al., 2023)
059   in details, the aforementioned two categories of clustered FL approaches are vulnerable to errors in
060   clustering due to their sensitivity to: 1. model initialization 2. randomness in clients’ model updates
061   due to stochastic noise. DP noise exacerbates this vulnerability, especially in the first few rounds of
062   FL training. To address this, we propose a clustered DPFL algorithm which uses both clients’ model
063
      updates and losses values to cluster them, making it more robust to DP/stochastic noise.
064   A correct clustering of clients results in equity of privacy cost between the client groups (Esipova
065   et al., 2022; Tran et al., 2020). Justified by our theoretical analysis, our proposed algorithm uses
066   a full batch size in the first round to reduce the noise in clients’ model updates at the end of this
067   round. Then, the server soft clusters clients based on these less noisy model updates using a Gaussian
068   Mixture Model (GMM). Depending on the “confidence" of the learned GMM, the server keeps using it
069
      to soft cluster clients during the next few rounds. Finally, the server switches the clustering strategy
      to local clustering of clients based on their loss values in the remaining rounds. These altogether
070
      make our method effective and robust. The highlights of our contributions are as follows:
071
072          • We propose a DP clustered FL algorithm, which combines information from both clients’
073            model updates and their loss values. The algorithm is robust and achieves high-quality
074            clustering of clients, even in the presence of DP noise in the system.
075          • We theoretically prove that increasing clients’ batch sizes, particularly in the initial commu-
076            nication round, consistently improves the server’s ability to cluster clients based on their
077            model updates at the end of the first round.
078          • We theoretically prove that using sufficiently large client batch sizes in the first round,
079            enables super-linear convergence rate for learning a GMM on clients’ model updates, which
080            leads to fast and accurate clustering of clients with low computational overhead.
081
             • Extensive evaluation across diverse and heterogeneous datasets and scenarios demonstrates
082            the effectiveness of our robust clustered DPFL (RC-DPFL) algorithm in detecting the
083            clustering structure of clients, which leads to a utility improvement for minority clusters.

      2    R ELATED WORK
087
      Performance parity in FL: Performance parity of the final trained model across clients is an
088
      important goal in FL. Addressing this goal, Mohri et al. (2019) proposed Agnostic FL (AFL) by
089
      using a min-max optimization approach. TERM (Li et al., 2020a) used tilted losses to up-weight
090   clients with large losses. Finally, Li et al. (2020b) and Zhang et al. (2023) proposed q-FFL and
091   PropFair, inspired by α-fairness (Lan et al., 2010) and proportional fairness (Bertsimas et al., 2011),
092   respectively. Generating one common model for all clients, these techniques do not perform well
093   when the data distribution across clients is highly heterogeneous, leading to low overall performance
094   in the system. This leads us to use stronger personalization techniques, e.g., client clustering.
095
      Clustered FL: Clustered FL has been originally proposed for personalization in vanilla non-DP FL
096   with highly heterogeneous data, where clients can be naturally partitioned into clusters. Existing
097   clustered FL algorithms cluster clients based on their loss values (Mansour et al., 2020; Ghosh et al.,
098   2020; Ruan & Joe-Wong, 2021) or their model updates (based on e.g., their euclidean distance
099   (Werner et al., 2023; Briggs et al., 2020) or cosine similarity (Sattler et al., 2019)). As studied in
100   (Werner et al., 2023), the algorithms are prone to clustering errors in the early rounds of FL training
101   (due to gradient stochasticity, model initialization or the form of loss functions far from their optima),
102   which can even propagate in the subsequent rounds. This vulnerability is exacerbated in DPFL
103   systems, due to the extra DP noise. Without addressing this vulnerability, Luo et al. (2024) proposed
104
      a clustered DPFL algorithm with a limited applicability, which clusters clients based on the labels
      that they do not have in their local data, and is inapplicable when clients have all possible labels.
105
106   Differential privacy, group fairness and performance parity: Gradient clipping and random
107   noise addition used in DPSGD disproportionately affect underrepresented groups. Some works tried
      to address the tension between group fairness and DP in centralized settings (Tran et al., 2020)


                                                          2

108
      (by using Lagrangian duality) and FL settings (Pentyala et al., 2022) (by using Secure Multiparty
109   Computation (MPC)). Another work tried to remove the disparate impact of DP on model performance
110   of minority groups in centralized settings (Esipova et al., 2022), by preventing gradient misalignment
111   across different groups of data. Unlike the previous works on group fairness, this work adopts
112   cross-model fairness, where the performance cost of adding privacy to a non-private model must
113   be fairly distributed between different groups. We adopt the same notion - which is also used in
114   (Chu et al., 2023). Considering a highly heterogeneous data split, the mentioned approaches are
115   not appropriate due to generating one single model for all groups. In contrast, we propose a robust
116   “clustered" DPFL algorithm, which identifies different groups of clients and learns a model for each.

179   We start with the shortcomings of the existing non-DP clustered FL algorithms. As discussed in
180   (Werner et al., 2023), algorithms clustering clients based on their loss values (Mansour et al., 2020;
181
      Ghosh et al., 2020; Ruan & Joe-Wong, 2021), i.e., assign client i to cluster Re (i) = arg minm fi (θm
                                                                                                          e
                                                                                                            )
      at the beginning of round e, are prone to clustering errors in the first few rounds, mainly due to
182                                             e M
      random initialization of cluster models {θm }m=1 . On the other hand, clustering clients based on their
183
      model updates (gradients) (Werner et al., 2023; Briggs et al., 2020; Sattler et al., 2019) makes sense
184   only when the updates are obtained on the same model initialization. Additionally, even if we assume
185   these algorithms can initially cluster clients perfectly in each round e, the clients’ model updates
186   (gradients) will approach zero as the clusters’ models converge to their optimum parameters. Hence,
187   clients from different clusters may appear to belong to the same cluster, which results in clustering
188   mistakes. To illustrate these shortcomings, we provide a more detailed example in Appendix C.
189
      For the above mentioned reasons, we next propose an algorithm which starts with clustering clients
190   based on their model updates for the first several rounds and then switches its strategy to cluster
191   clients based on their loss values. We also augment this idea with some other non-obvious techniques
192   to enhance the clustering accuracy in the first few rounds, when the most clustering uncertainty exists.

400   4.4   A PPLICABILITY OF RC-DPFL
401
402   Even when the number of the underlying clusters (M ) is not known beforehand, we can find it with
403   high accuracy based on the confidence metric MSS ∈ (0, +∞) defined above (line 9 of Algorithm 1).
404
      Intuitively, we choose the M which yields to the largest confidence level MSS for the resulting GMM.
      We have provided further details about how to find M in these scenarios in Appendix F.2.
405
406   The strategy switching time Ec can also be set using the uncertainty metric MPO ∈ [0, 1). Intuitively,
407   if the learned GMM is not certain about its clustering decisions, RC-DPFL should not rely on its
408   decisions for a large Ec , and vice versa. Hence, we can set Ec as a decreasing function of MPO. For
409   instance, Ec = (1 − MPO) E2 means that if a GMM is completely confident about its clusterings, e.g.,
410
      what happens in Figure 2 right, the server changes the clustering strategy to loss-based after the first
      half of the training time. This change happens earlier as the uncertainty increases (e.g., when ϵ is
411
      small), and RC-DPFL slowly gets close to the completely loss-based clustering.

414   first round, {b>1   n

      5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
425
      Datasets, models and baseline algorithms: We evaluate our proposed method on three benchamark
426   datasets, including: MNIST (Deng, 2012), FMNIST (Xiao et al., 2017) and CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky,
427   2009), with heterogeneous data distributions from covariate shift (rotation) (Kairouz et al., 2021;
428   Werner et al., 2023) and concept shift (label flip) (Werner et al., 2023), which are the commonly
429   used data splits in the literature (see Appendix B). We consider four clusters of clients indexed by
430   m ∈ {0, 1, 2, 3} with {3, 6, 6, 6} clients, where the smallest cluster is considered as the minority
431   group. To the best of our knowledge, there was no prior work on DP clustered FL, so we compared
      to the DP version of existing algorithms. More specifically, we compare with the following baseline


                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      algorithms, which are combined with DPSGD: 1. DPFedAvg (Noble et al., 2021): clients run DPSGD
433   locally and send their model updates to the server 2. KM-CDPFL (Werner et al., 2023): FL with
434   myopic clustering, in which server clusters clients at the end of each round using running K-means
435   clustering on their DP model updates 3. f-CDPFL (Ghosh et al., 2020; Mansour et al., 2020): FL with
436   loss clustering, which clusters clients based on their train loss values on existing cluster models 4.
437   Oracle-CDPFL: an oracle algorithm which has the knowledge of true clusters from the first round.
438
439   Evaluation metrics and baselines: Given the set of n clients, fairness in a DPFL system can
440   be measured in terms of the disparate impact of DP on utility (performance drop) of different
441   groups (Chu et al., 2023; Bagdasaryan & Shmatikov, 2019; Tran et al., 2021; Esipova et al., 2022):
442   Facc = maxi,j∈[n] |∆acci (θi ) − ∆accj (θj )|, where θi is the model assigned to agent i at the end
443   of DP training, and ∆acci (θi ) = maxθ∗ acci (θ ∗ ) − acci (θi ), where θ ∗ is any possible model.
444
      Similarly, we can measure fairness in terms of the increment enforced to clients’ train loss (Tran et al.,
      2021; Esipova et al., 2022; Chu et al., 2023): Floss = maxi,j∈[n] |ξi (θi ) − ξj (θj )|, where ξi (θi ) =
445
      fi (θi ) − minθ∗ fi (θ ∗ ). These notions of fairness compare the cost of adding differential privacy on
446
      different clients, and define client-level fairness as the equality of “performance drop" across clients.
447
      Following (Chu et al., 2023), we estimate the model θ ∗ for each cluster by centrally training a model
448   with SGD based on the data of the clients belonging to that cluster. We also consider the following
449   evaluation metrics: average test accuracy (overall, majority, minority), worst accuracy across clients
450   (Mohri et al., 2019), and maximum accuracy disparity across clients: maxi,j |acci (θi ) − accj (θj )|.

534   6    C ONCLUSION
535
536   We proposed the first DP clustered FL algorithm, which addresses high data heterogeneity in privacy-
537   sensitive FL environments. By clustering clients based on their model updates and training loss values,
538   and mitigating noise impacts with larger batch sizes, our approach enhances utility and fairness with
539   minimal computational overhead, while maintaining DP. Moreover, the robustness to noise, and the
      ability to handle various types of distribution shifts shows the applicability of our approach.
