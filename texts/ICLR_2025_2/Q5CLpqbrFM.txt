009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Reliable estimation of treatment effects from observational data is important in
012            many disciplines such as medicine. However, estimation is challenging when
013            unconfoundedness as a standard assumption in the causal inference literature
014            is violated. In this work, we leverage arbitrary (potentially high-dimensional)
015            instruments to estimate bounds on the conditional average treatment effect (CATE).
016            Our contributions are three-fold: (1) We propose a novel approach for partial
017            identification through a mapping of instruments to a discrete representation space
018            so that we yield valid bounds on the CATE. This is crucial for reliable decision-
019            making in real-world applications. (2) We derive a two-step procedure that learns
020
               tight bounds using a tailored neural partitioning of the latent instrument space. As
               a result, we avoid instability issues due to numerical approximations or adversarial
021
               training. Furthermore, our procedure aims to reduce the estimation variance in
022
               finite-sample settings to yield more reliable estimates. (3) We show theoretically
023            that our procedure obtains valid bounds while reducing estimation variance. We
024            further perform extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness across
025            various settings. Overall, our procedure offers a novel path for practitioners
026            to make use of potentially high-dimensional instruments (e.g., as in Mendelian
027            randomization).

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033
      Estimating the conditional average treatment effect (CATE) from observational is an important task
      for personalized decision-making in medicine (Feuerriegel et al., 2024). For example, a common
034
      question in medicine is to estimate the effect of alcohol consumption on the onset of cardiovascular
035
      diseases (Holmes et al., 2014). There are many reasons, including costs and ethical concerns, why
036   CATE estimation is often based on observational data (such as, e.g., electronic health records, clinical
037   registries).
038
039
      However, identifying the CATE from observational data is chal-
      lenging as it typically requires strong assumptions in the form of
040
      unconfoundedness (Rubin, 1974). Unconfoundedness assumes
041
      there exist no additional unobserved confounders U between
042   treatment A and outcome Y . If the unconfoundedness assump-
043   tion is violated, a common strategy is to leverage instrumental
044   variables (IVs) Z. IVs affect only the treatment A but exclude
045   unobserved confounding between Z and Y , which often can
046   be ensured by design such as for randomized studies with non- Figure 1: Overview of the IV set-
047   compliance (Imbens & Angrist, 1994). The causal graph for ting. We consider complex instru-
048   the IV setting is shown in Fig. 1.                                 ments Z (e.g., gene data, text, im-

051   information as instruments Z to estimate the effect of a treat-
052   ment or exposure A (e.g., alcohol consumption) on some medical outcome Y (e.g., cardiovascular
053   diseases). In this setting, there are further patient characteristics that are observed (X) but also
      unobserved (U ), which one accounts for through the instrument. Yet, common challenges are that (i)

054
      instruments with genetic information are often high-dimensional and (ii) involve complex, non-linear
055   relationships between instruments and treatment intake or exposure.
056
057   However, existing IV methods using machine learning for point estimation of the CATE rely on
058
      strong simplifying assumptions (→ violating (ii) from above). For example, some methods assume
      linearity in some feature space in the CATE and make other, strict parametric assumptions on the
059
      unobserved confounders such as additivity or homogeneity (Hartford et al., 2017; Singh et al., 2019;
060
      Xu et al., 2021). Yet, such simplifying assumptions are often not realistic and can even lead to
061   unreliable and false conclusions by the mis-specification of the CATE.
062
063   A potential remedy is to use IVs for partial identification of the CATE where one circumvents any
064
      hard parametric assumptions by estimating upper and lower bounds of the CATE (Manski, 1990).
      This is usually sufficient in medical practice when one is merely interested in whether a treatment
065
      variable (e.g., exposure as in Mendelian randomization) has a positive or a negative effect. So far,
066
      methods for partial identification of the CATE in IV settings are rare. There exist closed-form bounds
067   (i.e., via a fixed target estimand that can be learned), yet only for the setting with both discrete
068   instruments and discrete treatments (Balke & Pearl, 1997).
069
070
      Existing machine learning methods for partial identification are typically designed for simple in-
      struments that are binary or discrete (→ violating (i) from above). Alternatively, methods that
071
      extend partial identification for continuous instruments require unstable training paradigms such as
072
      adversarial learning (Kilbertus et al., 2020; Padh et al., 2023) which becomes even more unstable
073   for more complex instruments. In contrast, there is a scarcity of methods that can deal robustly with
074   continuous, as well as complex and potentially high-dimensional instruments such as, e.g., gene
075   expressions as in Mendelian randomization but also text, images, or graphs.1
076
      Our paper: In this work, we leverage complex instruments for partial identification of the CATE.
077
      Specifically, we allow for instruments that can be continuous and potentially high-dimensional (such
078
      as gene information) and, on top of that, we explicitly allow for complex, non-linear relationships
079   between instruments and treatment intake or exposure. In the rest of this paper, we refer to this setting
080   as “complex” instruments.
081
082
      To this end, we proceed as follows. (1) We propose a novel approach for partial identification through
      a mapping of complex instruments to a discrete representation space so that we yield valid bounds on
083
      the CATE. We motivate our approach in Fig. 2. (2) We derive a two-step procedure that learns tight
084
      bounds using a neural partitioning of the latent instrument space. As a result, we avoid instability
085   issues due to numerical approximations or adversarial training, which is a key limitation of prior
086   works. We further improve the performance of our procedure by explicitly reducing the estimation
087   variance in finite-sample settings to yield more reliable estimates. (3) We provide a theoretical
088   analysis of our procedure and perform extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness across
089   various settings.
090
      Contributions:2 (1) To the best of our knowledge, this is the first IV method for partial identification
091   of the CATE based on complex instruments. (2) We derive a two-step procedure to learn tight bounds.
092   (3) We demonstrate the effectiveness of our method both theoretically and numerically.

      Partial identification: Partial identification aims to identify and learn upper and lower bounds of
125
      some causal quantity (e.g., the CATE) when the causal quantity itself cannot be point identified from
126   the data and assumptions. In a general setting with binary treatments, Robins (1989) and Manski
127   (1990) derived closed-form bounds on the ATE for bounded outcomes Y . Further work extended
128   these ideas to settings with binary instrumental variables, binary treatments, and binary outcomes
129   (Balke & Pearl, 1994; 1997) to derive tighter bounds. Newer approaches for discrete variables include
130   the works of Duarte et al. (2023) and Guo et al. (2022). Swanson et al. (2018) provide an extensive
131   overview of partial identification in this setting. Other works focus on how to leverage additional
132   observed confounders to further tighten bounds on the ATE (see, e.g., Levis et al., 2023). However,
133   these works do not focus on efficiently leveraging continuous or even high-dimensional instruments
134
      for learning tight bounds, unlike our work that is tailored to such complex instruments.
135   Another literature stream focuses on partial identification under general causal graphs (Balazadeh
136   et al., 2022), including IV settings with continuous variables such as continuous treatments (Gunsilius,
137   2020; Hu et al., 2021; Kilbertus et al., 2020; Padh et al., 2023). However, these methods either
138   make strong assumptions about the treatment response functions or require unstable optimization
139   via adversarial training and/or generative modeling such as through using GANs. This can easily
140
      result in unreliable estimates of bounds for finite data, especially with high-dimensional instruments.
      Further, these methods are not directly tailored for binary treatments, unlike our method.
141
142   Research gap: To the best of our knowledge, reliable machine learning methods for partial identifi-
143   cation of the CATE with complex instruments are missing. To draw conclusions about CATEs (as in,
144   e.g., Mendelian randomization), our method is the first to: (i) make use of the complex instrument
145   information (e.g., continuous or high-dimensional), (ii) avoid making strong parametric assumptions
146
      by focusing on partial identification, and (iii) avoid unstable training procedures such as adversarial
      learning.

151   Setting: We focus on the standard IV setting (Angrist et al., 1996; Wooldridge, 2013). Hence, we con-
152   sider instruments (e.g., gene data, text, images) given by Z ∈ Z ⊆ Rd but, unlike previous research,
153   allow the instruments to be complex. As such, we allow the instruments to be continuous and poten-
                                                                                                          n
154   tially high-dimensional. We further have access to an observational dataset D = {zi , xi , ai , yi }i=1
155   of size n. The data is sampled i.i.d. from a population (Z, X, A, Y ) ∼ P, with observed confounders
156   X ∈ X ⊆ Rp , binary treatments A ∈ A ⊆ {0, 1}, and bounded outcomes Y ∈ Y ⊆ [s1 , s2 ] ⊆ R.
157   Additionally, we allow for unobserved confounders U of arbitrary form between A and Y .
158   We further assume a causal structure as shown in Fig. 1. In particular, we assume that Z is an
159   instrumental variable that has an effect on the treatment A but no direct effect on the outcome Y
160   except through A. Further, we assume that Z is independent of X, e.g., by randomization. In
161   Appendix B, we provide an extended discussion to show the real-world relevance and validity of our
      assumptions in different settings.

      Notation: Throughout our work, we denote the response function by µa (x, z) := E[Y |X = x, A =
163   a, Z = z] and the propensity score by π(x, z) := P(A = 1|X = x, Z = z).

165   CATE: We use the potential outcomes framework (Rubin, 1974) to formalize our causal inference
166
      problem. Let Y (a) ∈ Y denote the potential outcome under treatment A = a. We are thus interested
      in the CATE τ (x) = E[Y (1) − Y (0)|X = x].
167
168   Identifiability: We make the following standard assumptions from the literature in partial identi-
169   fication with IVs (Angrist et al., 1996). Assumption 1 (Consistency): Y (A) = Y . Assumption 2
170   (Exclusion): Z ⊥ ⊥ Y (A) | (X, A, U ). Assumption 3 (Independence): Z ⊥⊥ (U, X).
171   Note that, however, Assumptions 1–3 from the standard IV setting are not sufficient to ensure
172   identifiability of the CATE (Gunsilius, 2020). To ensure identifiability, one would require additional
173   assumptions, such as linearity or, more generally, additive noise assumptions (Hartford et al., 2017;
174   Wang & Tchetgen Tchetgen, 2018). Yet, such assumptions are highly restrictive and are neither
175   testable nor typically ensured in real-world scenarios. Hence, this motivates our objective to perform
176   partial identification instead.
177   Objective: We frame our objective as a partial identification problem and thus focus on estimating

179   possible x ∈ X . Furthermore, the bounds should be informative, i.e., we would like to minimize the
180   expected bound width EX [b+ (X) − b− (X)], while still ensuring validity. Formally, we aim to solve

188   4.1   OVERVIEW
189
190   We now present our proposed method to solve the partial identification problem from Eq. (1). Solving
191   Eq. (1) directly is infeasible because it involves the unknown CATE τ (x). Hence, we propose the
192   following approach:
193    Outline: 1 We learn a discretized representation (also called partitioning) ϕ(Z) of the instrumental
194    variable Z. 2 We then derive closed-form bounds given the discrete representation ϕ. 3 We
195    transform the closed-form bounds back to our original bounding problem and, in particular, express
196    all quantities involved as quantities that can be estimated from observational data.
197
      Below, we first explain why existing closed-form bounds are not directly applicable and why deriving
198   such bounds is non-trivial. We then proceed by providing the corresponding theory for the above
199   method. Specifically, we first take a population view to show theoretically that our bounds are valid
200   (Sec. 4.2). Then, we take a finite-sample view and present an estimator (Sec. 4.3).
201
      Limitations of existing bounds: There exist different approaches for bounding treatment effects (see
202
      Sec. 2) using continuous instruments, yet these either require additional assumptions or can easily
203
      become unstable, especially for high-dimensional Z. Furthermore, these bounds consider continuous
204   treatments but are not tailored for binary treatments (e.g., whether a drug is administered). Hence,
205   we derive custom bounds for our setting.
206
207
      Why is the derivation non-trivial? For binary treatments, it turns out that there exist closed-form
      solutions for bounds whenever the instrument Z is discrete. That is, the existing bounds for the
208
      average treatment effect (ATE) with continuous bounded outcome proposed in (Manski, 1990) can
209
      be extended to non-parametric closed-form bounds for the CATE (Schweisthal et al., 2024). While
210   these bounds are useful in a setting with discrete instruments Z, they are not directly applicable to
211   continuous or even high-dimensional Z due to two main reasons: (1) The bounds need to be evaluated
212   for all combinations l, m ∈ Z 2 ⊆ Rd × Rd , which is intractable. (2) Evaluating the bounds only on a
213   random subset of combinations l, m can result in arbitrary high estimation variance for regions with a
214   low joint density of p(X = x, Z = l) or p(X = x, Z = m). Hence, we must derive a novel method
215   for estimating bounds based on complex instruments (that are, e.g., continuous or high-dimensional),
      yet this is a highly non-trivial task.

      nuisance functions after training will also yield reliable estimates.

417   representations that minimize the bound width already for low k while ensuring robust estimation also
418   for higher k through regularization. This is particularly advantageous in real-world causal inference
419   tasks, where model evaluation and selection are challenging due to the lack of oracle performance
420   metrics. In the following experiments section, we demonstrate such robustness empirically. Further,
421   we provide an extended discussion including practical guidelines in Appendix F.

      B     R EAL - WORLD RELEVANCE AND VALIDITY OF ASSUMPTIONS
865
866
      In this section, we elaborate on the real-world relevance of our considered setting and show that our
867   assumptions often hold and are even weaker than the ones of existing approaches. For that, we draw
868   upon two real-world settings.
869
870   B.1    M ENDELIAN RANDOMIZATION
871
872   Mendelian randomization (MR; the main motivational example from our paper) is a widely used
873   method from biostatistics to estimate the causal effect of some treatment or exposure (such as alcohol
874   consumption) on some outcome (such as cardiovascular diseases). We refer to Pierce et al. (2018)
875   for an introduction to MR, which also shows that MR is widely used in medicine. For that, genetic
876   variants (such as different single nucleotide polymorphisms, SNPs) are used as instruments where it
877
      is known that they only influence the exposure but not directly the outcome. Our method for partial
      identification with complex instruments is perfectly suited for this common real-world application.
878
      Depending on the use case, either a predefined genetic risk score (Burgess et al., 2020) as a continuous
879
      variable, or up to hundreds of SNPs are used simultaneously as IVs to strengthen the power of the
880   analysis, resulting in high-dimensional instruments (Pierce et al., 2018).
881
882   Validity of assumptions: The IV assumptions used in our paper such as the exclusion and indepen-
883
      dence assumptions can be ensured by expert knowledge (e.g., given some observed confounder age
      (X), genetic variations (Z) do not affect age) or, in some cases, they can be even directly tested for
884
      (Glymour et al., 2012). In contrast, as explained in Sec. 2, existing methods for MR rely on additional
885
      hard assumptions on top such as the knowledge about the parametric form of the underlying data-
886   generating process. Especially with such high-dimensional IVs, misspecification of these models may
887   result in significantly biased effect estimates. In contrast, our method does not rely on any parametric
888   assumption and also no additional assumptions compared to previous methods, thus enabling more
889   reliable causal inferences in the real-world application of MR by using strictly weaker assumptions
890   than existing work.
891
892   B.2    I NDIRECT EXPERIMENTS
893
894   With indirect experiments (IEs), we show that, in principle, our method is not constrained to medical
895
      applications but is also highly useful in various other domains. IEs are widely applied in various
      areas such as social sciences or public health to estimate causal effects in settings with non-adherence,
896
      i.e., where people cannot be forced to take treatments but rather be encouraged by some nudge (Pearl,
897
      1995). For instance, researchers might be interested in estimating the effect of some treatment such as
898   participating in a healthcare program (T ) on some health outcome Y by randomly assigning nudges Z
899   (IVs) in the form of different text messages on social media promoting participation. Here, common
900   nudges (IVs) are in the form of, for instance, text or even image data and thus high-dimensional,
901   showing the necessity of a method capable of handling complex IVs such as ours.
902
      In principle, our method can be applied to every setting with continuous or multi-dimensional IVs
903
      where one wants to avoid making the hard untestable assumptions necessary for point identification
904   such as linearity or additivity (e.g., Hartford et al. (2017)). Specific examples for applications with
905   high-dimensional IVs are text-based nudges for encouraging vaccinations (Milkman et al., 2021),
906   or various kinds of experiments where text nudges are generated by different strategies such as for
907   political microtargeting (Hackenburg & Margetts, 2024) or for personalized persuasion in general
908   (Matz et al., 2024).
909
      Another important application area is online marketing. Concrete use cases involve extended A/B
910   testing for evaluating the benefits of new features, e.g., when one is interested in the effect of a new
911   version of an app on user engagement. Here, users with features such as age, gender, and content
912   preferences (X) can be nudged by emails or push notifications (Z) to test a new feature such as using
913   a new version of an app (A) to estimate its effect on engagement metrics such as screen time (Y ).
914   Further, our method could also be extended to improve current methods for optimizing instrument
915   designs for indirect experiments that for now assume identifiability is possible (e.g., Chandak et al.
916   (2023)).
917



                                                         17
918
      Validity of assumptions: As a major benefit of IEs, the IV assumptions are ensured per design as
919   the IVs are randomly assigned, and, thus they always hold. Hence, our method provides a promising
920   tool for evaluating the effects of IEs.

       E.1   A DDITIONAL BASELINES
1137
1138   As mentioned in the main paper, existing methods are not designed for our considered setting of
1139   continuous or high-dimensional IVs with binary treatments. However, to further show the advantages
1140   and necessity of our tailored method, we compare with two additional baselines that were not
1141   developed for our task but which we adapted for our task, namely, one from uncertainty quantification
1142   for point estimates and one from the discrete instruments setting:
1143   (i) DeepIV with bootstrapped confidence intervals. DeepIV (Hartford et al., 2017) is a neural method
1144   tailored for high-dimensional instruments when point identification can be ensured. This requires the
1145   additional assumption of additivity of the unobserved confounding, which usually cannot be ensured
1146   and is not necessary for our method. For DeepIV, we can approximate confidence intervals using
1147   bootstrapping. Here, we approximate confidence intervals with a confidence level of 95%, indicating
1148   an expected coverage of 95% if assumptions were not violated. However, note that these intervals
1149   can only adjust for statistical uncertainty, but not for identifiability uncertainty due to the violation of
1150
       causal assumptions. Thus, this baseline acts as an additional motivation for why bound estimators
       such as our method are important.
1151
1152   (ii) Discretized IVs: As a further additional baseline, we proceed by directly discretizing the high-
1153   dimensional IVs and then estimating the existing bounds for discrete IVs. Hence, one looses
1154   information from the IV due to the discretization. Our implementation here is the same as for the
1155   naïve baseline, however, the k partitions are not learned by k-means clustering but instead defined by
1156
       a simple grouping rule. To ensure a fair comparison, we average the results of experiments conducted
       with the same number of partitions k for all methods.

       F.1   W HY OUR METHOD IS ROBUST TO DIFFERENT CHOICE OF k
1245
1246   One major advantage of our method is that it is clearly less sensitive to the hyperparameter k than, for
1247   example, the naïve baseline. Empirically, we demonstrate this in our experiments by lower variance
1248   and stable behavior over varying k, especially visible in the low values of MSD. This is due to the
1249   combination of learning flexible representations tailored to minimize bound width (allowing us to
1250   estimate tight bounds already for low k) while ensuring reliable estimates of the nuisance functions
1251   in the second stage by using our regularization loss in Eq. (16) (ensuring robust behavior also for
1252   higher k).
1253   Note that the robustness of our method is especially beneficial when applying our method to real-
1254   world settings in causal inference. In real-world settings from causal inference, hyperparameter
1255   tuning and model evaluation are not directly possible because oracle CATE or oracle bounds are not
1256   known. Thus, the robustness against suboptimal selection of hyperparameters such as k is crucial.
1257   In the following, we provide further high-level theoretical insights into the role of k and propose
1258   practical recommendations for selecting k in real-world applications.
1259   Estimation error for different k: The hyperparameter λ controls the regularization loss in Eq. (16),
1260   i.e., it tries to maximize p̂ℓ,ϕ = P̂(ϕθ (Z) = ℓ) > ε for all ℓ ∈ 1, . . . , k. Thus, if we choose λ
1261   high enough, then we enforce that p̂ℓ,ϕ = 1/k for all ℓ ∈  1, . . . , k. Plugged into
                                                                                            Theorem 12, the
1262                                                                 Var(g(Z)|ϕ(Z)=ℓ)
       asymptotic variances for the nuisance estimators are k               c         +d      for µ̂aϕ (x, ℓ), and
1263
1264
       k (Var(h(Z) | ϕ(Z) = ℓ)) for π̂ϕ (x, ℓ), respectively. Thus, for large enough λ, the variance of the
       nuisance estimators (and, thus, also likely of the final bounds) will increase for increasing k. However,
1265
       as an interesting side note, for a fixed (not too large) λ, the penalization term in Eq. (16) will also
1266

1267   This is also shown in our experiments where higher values of k do not necessarily result in a higher
1268   variance.

1284   F.2   P RACTICAL GUIDELINES FOR SELECTING k
1285
1286
       Although we showed that our method is designed to be robust against different selections of k, we
       provide two potential guidelines for how to choose k in real-world settings where ground-truth CATE
1287
       or bounds are not available for model selection.
1288
1289   Approach 1: Expert-informed approach. In some medical applications, physicians might already
1290   know or make an educated guess about a number of underlying clusters of patient characteristics
1291   such as genetic variants. For instance, this is a common assumption in subgroup identification or
1292
       latent class analysis in medicine where patient groups are characterized by having similar responses
       to treatments or showing similar associations with diseases (Kongsted & Nielsen, 2017). Thus, no
1293
       data-driven approach is necessary here but one can integrate existing domain knowledge.
1294
1295   Approach 2: Data-driven for hypothesis confirmation. Often, physicians are interested in whether
       some treatment or exposure has a positive or negative effect (i.e., lower bound > 0 or upper bound


                                                          24
1296
       < 0) for at least some observations x. Thus, k can be selected by increasing k until such an effect
1297   can be observed while holding the variance minimal. Then, the variance can be approximated (e.g.,
1298   by bootstrapping to test for the reliability of the corresponding bound model and its effect). Thus,
1299   this approach can be used when our method is used as a support tool for hypothesis confirmation.
1300
       Last, straightforwardly, from an exploratory perspective, all hyperparameters (k, λ, γ) can be altered
1301
       together to examine the behavior of bound width and estimation variance to post-hoc find a suitable
1302
       hyperparameter configuration for a dataset that fulfills the subjective preferences of the practitioner.

       I   D ISCUSSION : D OUBLY ROBUSTNESS
1459
1460
       Background doubly robustness: A related literature stream addressing robustness in causal inference
1461   aims to construct such called doubly robust or multiply robust estimators of causal quantities (see e.g.,
1462   Bang & Robins (2005); Kennedy (2023)). Here, doubly / multiply robust means that the final estimator
1463   of the causal quantity (e.g., the ATE or CATE) is consistent if some of the nuisance estimators are
1464   consistent. For instance, under identifiability assumptions, the DR-learner for estimating the CATE
1465   (Kennedy, 2023) is consistent if either the outcome estimator Ê[Y |X, A = a] or the propensity
1466   estimator P̂(A = 1|X) is consistent. Other works extend such an idea for multiply robustness with
1467   additional nuisance estimators for other settings such as with IVs (Ogburn et al., 2015; Frauen &
1468   Feuerriegel, 2023). However, these methods consider only settings where the causal quantity can be
1469   point-identified, i.e., they require hard assumptions and are not tailored for estimating bounds, which
1470   is unlike our setting.
1471   Only recently, doubly robust estimators have been proposed for bounds for the CATE (i.e., partial
1472   identification) for sensitivity analysis (Oprescu et al., 2023), and, closest to our setting, when IVs
1473   are available (Schweisthal et al., 2024). However, these bounds are only applicable for discrete IVs,
1474   which is unlike our setting with continuous or high-dimensional IVs.
1475
       Why is our method not doubly robust?: To derive doubly or multiply robust estimators, we would need
1476
       to derive the efficient influence function of the causal quantity we want to estimate (Kennedy, 2023),
1477   i.e., in our setting the bounds for the CATE. However, under our assumptions 1-3, no closed-form
1478   solution exists for the bounds for the CATE for general IVs (i.e., continuous or high-dimensional).
1479   Instead, we can only describe the identification of the bounds as a constraint optimization problem
1480   (Gunsilius, 2020; Kilbertus et al., 2020) as we do in Eq (1). Since the constrained optimization
1481   problem is not pathwise-differentiable, the current statistical efficiency theory used for deriving
1482   doubly robustness is not applicable. Thus, deriving doubly robust estimators without a closed-form
1483   solution is not solvable with the usual toolkit and highly non-trivial. Instead, in a more general setting,
1484   related works try to solve such constrained optimization with different optimization methods such as
1485
       alternating learning (e.g., Padh et al. (2023)), and are also not doubly robust.
1486   Potential extension with doubly robust estimation: As stated above, we cannot directly derive doubly
1487   robust estimators for the bounds in our setting. However, as an advantage of our method, we try to
1488   learn optimal partitions (i.e., discretizations) of the IVs to yield reliable and tight bound estimates.
1489   This implies that after we finally learned our optimal partitions, we could replace the calculation of
1490   the final bounds for which we used Eq. (9) and Eq. (10), with an additional estimation procedure for
1491
       discrete IVs such as by using the meta-learners for bounds of Schweisthal et al. (2024), including their
       doubly-robust learner. Note, however, that this only results in doubly robust estimates of the bounds
1492
       on top of the learned partition but not for the original problem. Further, as this requires learning
1493
       additional nuisance functions and does not use our optimized nuisance estimates, such a procedure
1494   might easily result in higher variance again and produces computational overhead. Therefore, we
1495   would not recommend this extension.
