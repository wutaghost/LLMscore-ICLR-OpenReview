009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose SlowFast-LLaVA (or SF-LLaVA for short), a training-free video large
012            language model (LLM) that can jointly capture detailed spatial semantics and
013            long-range temporal context without exceeding the token budget of commonly
014            used LLMs. This is realized by using a two-stream SlowFast design of inputs
015            for Video LLMs to aggregate features from sampled frames in an effective way.
016            Specifically, the Slow pathway extracts features at a low frame rate while keep-
017
               ing as much spatial detail as possible (e.g., with 12 × 24 tokens), and the Fast
               pathway operates on a high frame rate but uses a larger spatial pooling stride
018
               (e.g., downsampling 6×) to focus on the motion cues. As a result, this design
019
               allows us to adequately capture both spatial and temporal features that are benefi-
020            cial for detailed video understanding. Experimental results show that SF-LLaVA
021            outperforms existing training-free methods on a wide range of video tasks. On
022            some benchmarks, it achieves comparable or even better performance compared
023            to state-of-the-art Video LLMs that are fine-tuned on video datasets.

047   1   I NTRODUCTION
048   Video large language models (LLMs) process video inputs and generate coherent and contextually
049   appropriate responses to user commands by using a pre-trained LLM (Achiam et al., 2023; Chiang
050   et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023b; Jiang et al., 2024). Although achieving convincing results, most
051   Video LLMs (Maaz et al., 2024b; Lin et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b) are fine-tuned
052   on large-scale labeled video datasets, leading to high computational and labeling cost. Recently,
053   training-free methods (Kim et al., 2024; Wu, 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b) have been proposed as a
      simple and highly cost-efficient solution. They directly use well-trained Image LLMs for video tasks


                                                        1

054
      without additional fine-tuning and demonstrate encouraging performance. However, most existing
055   Video LLMs have two main drawbacks: (1) they work effectively only with a limited number of
056   frames as inputs (e.g., 6 for IG-VLM (Kim et al., 2024) and 16 for PLLaVA (Xu et al., 2024)),
057   making them difficult to capture fine-grained spatial and temporal content throughout the video, and
058   (2) they simply feed the video features into an LLM without a proper temporal modeling design and
059   fully rely on the capability of the LLM to model the motion patterns.
060
      We present SlowFast-LLaVA (or SF-LLaVA for short), a training-free Video LLM that is built upon
061   LLaVA-NeXT (Liu et al., 2024) without further fine-tuning. Inspired by the successful two-stream
062   networks (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014; Feichtenhofer et al., 2019) for action recognition, we pro-
063   pose a new SlowFast design of inputs for Video LLMs to capture both detailed spatial semantics and
064   long-range temporal context. Specifically, the Slow pathway extracts features at a low frame rate
065   while keeping spatial information at a higher resolution (e.g., 8 frames each with 24 × 24 tokens),
066   and the Fast pathway operates on a high frame rate but uses an aggressive spatial pooling stride
067   (e.g., downsampling each frame to 4 × 4 tokens) to focus on motion cues. SF-LLaVA combines
068   the “Slow and Fast features” together as an effective video representation for various tasks. SF-
069
      LLaVA has two main advantages over prior work. First, it integrates complementary features from
      the slowly changing visual semantics and rapidly changing motion dynamics, providing a compre-
070
      hensive understanding of videos. Second, the dual-pathway design balances the modeling capability
071
      and computational efficiency, and enables us to input more video frames to preserve adequate details.
072
073   SF-LLaVA takes a video as input by uniformly sampling a large number of frames (denoted as N )
074   to maintain as much detail as possible. Frame features Fv are extracted independently via a visual
075
      encoder (e.g., CLIP-L (Radford et al., 2021)) followed by a visual-language adaptor for feature
      alignment. Then, the features Fv are fed into the Slow and Fast pathways separately. The Slow
076
      pathway uniformly samples N slow ≪ N features from Fv . Prior work (Xu et al., 2024) found that
077
      properly pooling frame features can improve both efficiency and robustness. We follow them to
078   aggregate features in the Slow pathway by using a pooling with a small stride (e.g., 1 × 2) over the
079   spatial dimensions. The Fast pathway takes all N fast = N features and performs a more aggressive
080   spatial pooling of each frame to focus on a finer temporal resolution. Finally, visual tokens from
081   both pathways are concatenated and fed into the LLM to generate the answer.
082
      We extensively evaluate SF-LLaVA on 3 video tasks (i.e., Open-Ended VideoQA, Multiple Choice
083
      VideoQA, and Text Generation) with 8 benchmark, including videos from various types (e.g., first-
084   and third-person views) and lengths (e.g., short and long videos). Experimental results (as shown
085   in Fig. 1) show that SF-LLaVA outperforms existing training-free methods by a clear margin on all
086   benchmarks, and achieves on-par or even better performance compared to SFT models that have
087   been carefully fine-tuned on video datasets. We also conduct comprehensive ablation studies on our
088   SlowFast design recipe, which hopefully provide some valuable insights for future work.

090   2   R ELATED W ORK
091
092   Image Large Language Models. Significant advances have been observed in the development of
093
      multimodal large language models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; McKinzie et al.,
      2024; Abdin et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). As a pioneer work, Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022)
094
      accepts arbitrarily interleaved visual and text data as inputs and generates text in an open-ended
095
      manner. BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023b) uses pre-trained visual and text models, and bridges the domain
096   gap with the proposed Q-Former. LLaVA(-v1.5/NeXT) (Liu et al., 2023b;a; 2024) achieves remark-
097   able performance by leveraging a simple linear connector or an MLP between visual and text mod-
098   els and designing an efficient instruction following data pipeline assisted with GPT. More recently,
099   MM1 (McKinzie et al., 2024) conducts comprehensive ablation studies on model components and
100   data choices, and offers valuable insights for understanding Image LLMs. There are also efforts
101   to ingest other modalities. Ferret (You et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a) focuses on the box/shape
102   modality and enhances a model’s language grounding capability at any granularity. 3D-LLM (Hong
103   et al., 2023) enables open-ended question answering in 3D by injecting 3D representations into an
104
      LLM. 4M (Mizrahi et al., 2023; Bachmann et al., 2024) presents a general any (modality) to any
      (modality) framework with strong out-of-box perceptional and generative capabilities.
105
106   Video Large Language Models. With the rapid development of LLMs (Achiam et al., 2023; Team
107   et al., 2023; Chiang et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a;b), there is increasing interest in generalist
      video models that can perform a wide range of video tasks. Video-ChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2024b)


                                                        2

108
      extracts per-frame features then aggregates them by using two spatial and temporal pooling op-
109   erations before inputting them to an LLM. VideoChat (Li et al., 2023c) encodes a video as both
110   video text descriptions and video appearance embeddings. Video-LLaVA (Lin et al., 2023) pre-
111   aligns the image and video encoders, and learns a shared projector to project them to the language
112   space. PLLaVA (Xu et al., 2024) achieves convincing performance by fine-tuning a pre-trained
113   Image LLM on video understanding data. LLaVA-NeXT-Video (Zhang et al., 2024b) improves
114   LLaVA-NeXT (Liu et al., 2024) by fine-tuning it on video data, and its DPO version (Zhang et al.,
115   2024b) further aligns the model responses with AI feedbacks.
116   Training-Free Video LLMs are built upon Image LLMs and require no additional fine-tuning to
117   work for video scenarios. FreeVA (Wu, 2024) explores different temporal aggregation methods
118   and effectively pools video features before sending them to an LLM. IG-VLM (Kim et al., 2024)
119   assembles multiple video frames into an image grid and uses the Image LLM as it is on the image
120   grid for video tasks. These training-free models show encouraging results on various benchmarks,
121   but they have two main drawbacks. First, they can only successfully process a few frames from a
122   video (e.g., 4 frames in FreeVA and 6 frames in IG-VLM), which limits them to work only for short
123
      and simple videos. Second, they simply ingest the video features and fully rely on the capability
      of the LLMs to capture the temporal dependency along the video. In this paper, we propose a
124
      new SlowFast design to capture both detailed spatial and temporal cues for video understanding by
125
      effectively and efficiently taking more frames (e.g., 50) as inputs.

      3     S LOW F AST-LL AVA
129   We introduce a training-free Video LLM, named SlowFast-LLaVA (or SF-LLaVA for short), based
130   on the LLaVA-NeXT (Liu et al., 2024), as shown in Fig. 2. Inspired by (Simonyan & Zisserman,
131   2014; Feichtenhofer et al., 2019) for action recognition, we propose a SlowFast design that uses two-
132   stream inputs for Video LLMs to jointly capture detailed spatial semantic and long-range temporal
133   context without exceeding the token budget of commonly used LLMs. (e.g., 4096 in Vicuna-v1.5).
134   Specifically, the Slow pathway includes “high-resolution”1 but low-frame-rate frame features (e.g.,
135   8 frames each with 12 × 24 tokens) to capture spatial detail as much as possible, and the Fast
136   pathway includes “low-resolution” but high-frame-rate frame features (e.g., 64 frames each with
137
      4 × 4 tokens) to model greater temporal context. This design allows us to adequately preserve both
      spatial and temporal information, and aggregate them together as a powerful video representation.

      3.1   P RELIMINARIES : T RAINING - FREE V IDEO LLM S
141   A training-free Video LLM is built upon a pre-trained Image LLM without further fine-tuning on
142   any data. It saves significant computation resources and model training time, and offers a greater
143   flexibility that can be quickly adapted into different application scenarios. The main effort of this
144   research direction is to improve the visual representation (e.g., organizing sampled frames (Kim
145   et al., 2024) or incorporating textual descriptions (Zhang et al., 2023a)) and effectively leveraging
146   the knowledge of a pre-trained LLM to better fit into the video tasks.
147   Given a video V, a frame sampler first selects N key frames (denoted as I).2 The sampled frames
148   are either arranged to a combined image grid (Kim et al., 2024) or treated independently (Wu, 2024;
149   Zhang et al., 2024b) as the inputs to the model. Video features are extracted as Fv = Visualenc (I),
150   where Visualenc is an image-based visual encoder, such as CLIP-L (Radford et al., 2021).3 Note
151   that IG-VLM (Kim et al., 2024) uses the AnyRes (Liu et al., 2024) technique to extract features
152   from a combined image grid, and most other methods, such as FreeVA (Wu, 2024), extracts features
153
      from each frame independently. Before inputting the video features Fv into the LLM, a feature

224   4.1      B ENCHMARKS AND M ETRICS
225
226   Open-Ended VideoQA expects the model to generate answers in freestyle in response to a question
227   for a video. We include MSVD-QA (Chen & Dolan, 2011), MSRVTT-QA (Xu et al., 2016), TGIF-
228
      QA (Li et al., 2016) and ActivityNet-QA (or ANet-QA in tables) (Yu et al., 2019) as the benchmarks
      for this task. Except for ActivityNet-QA, we follow prior work (Maaz et al., 2024b) and report
229
      the performance on the validation set. We use the GPT-assisted evaluation to assess the accuracy
230
      (accuracy with the answer being true or false) and the quality (score ranging from 0 to 5) of the
231   models. As pointed out by FreeVA (Wu, 2024) different GPT versions can significantly impact the
232   results, we report to use GPT-3.5-Turbo-0125 to perform a fair comparison.
233
234
      Multiple Choice VideoQA presents a set of multiple choice options to Video LLMs and evaluates
      their capability of picking the correct choice. Specifically, we evaluate our model on NExTQA (Xiao
235
      et al., 2021), EgoSchema (Mangalam et al., 2024) and IntentQA (Li et al., 2023a). The accuracy of
236
      selecting the correct answer from the options is used as the evaluation metric.
237
238   Text Generation is used to evaluate the text generation performance of a Video LLM, and espe-
239   cially focuses on the following aspects: Correctness of Information (CI), Detail Orientation (DO),
240
      Contextual Understanding (CU), Temporal Understanding (TU), and Consistency (CO). We use the
      VCGBench (Maaz et al., 2024b) to evaluate these tasks and follow its official pipeline to evaluate
241
      this capability. Specifically, we use GPT-3.5-Turbo-0125 for evaluation.
242
243   4.2      I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
244
245   Experimental Settings. We perform all experiments on a system with 8 Nvidia A100 80G graphics
246
      cards. SF-LLaVA is built upon LLaVA-NeXT (Liu et al., 2024) 7B and 34B models. We use their
      pre-trained weights available on HuggingFace4 . To deal with long sequences, we follow LLaVA-
247
      NeXT-Video (Zhang et al., 2024b) to apply the rotary position embedding (RoPE) (Su et al., 2024),
248
      and use the scaling factor of 2, which doubles the context length to 8192 tokens.
249
250   Input and Model Settings. SF-LLaVA takes as inputs a video with arbitrary size and length, and
251   uniformly samples N = 50 frames as key frames. The key frames are resized to 336 × 336, and the
252
      visual encoder (i.e., OpenAI’s CLIP-L-14) will output 24×24 tokens for each of them. For the Slow
      pathway, we uniformly select N slow = 10 frame features from Fv and pool their extracted features
253
      to 10 × 12 × 24; for the Fast pathway, we use features of all frames (i.e., N fast = N = 50) and pool
254
      their extracted features to 50 × 4 × 4. Thus, SF-LLaVA uses 10 × 12 × 24 + 50 × 4 × 4 = 3680 visual
255   tokens in total, and we choose this as the maximum number since the inference on the SF-LLaVA-
256   34B model already reaches 80G GPU memory. The SlowFast video tokens are then concatenated
257   with the text tokens as inputs to the LLM.

      4.5     D ESIGN C HOICES OF P ROMPT
477
478   SF-LLaVA is built upon a pre-trained Image LLM for VideoQA without further fine-tuning. Here we
479   evaluate if we should design new prompts for SF-LLaVA to better understand the video inputs and
480   tasks. We decompose the prompt into three main parts and respectively explore their best designs.
481   Task instruction prompt clarifies the goal of the target task. In particular, we use “Answer the
482   question precisely based on the input” for the open-ended task, and “Select the best option to answer
483   the question” for the multiple choice task. Table 7 (row 1 and 2) shows that using the task instruction
484   prompt can improve the performance on EgoSchema (47.2% v.s. 43.0% and 55.8%v.s. 52.2% on 7B
485   and 34B) but is not helpful for ActivityNet-QA (54.9% v.s. 55.5% and 58.4% v.s. 59.2% on 7B and
      34B). Thus, SF-LLaVA uses the task instruction prompt only for Multiple Choice VideoQA.

518   4.6   E RROR A NALYSIS
519   First, although SF-LLaVA can understand the relative sequence of different video moments, it still
520   lacks the capability to detect their precise start and end time, such as the Question 2 in Fig. 5. This is
521   because SF-LLaVA is never trained on any video datasets and relevant tasks. Fine-tuning SF-LLaVA
522   on fine-grained time-related video datasets could be a promising direction to gain this capability, and
523   incorporating multimodal inputs (e.g., the timestamp, subtitle, and audio of each frame) can further
524   improve the performance. Second, as observed in many other methods, SF-LLaVA possibly misses
525   some key frames due to its uniform frame sampling. Question 3 in Fig. 5 is an example, in which
526
      the frames showing a quick moment of “opening refrigerator” are unintentionally missed (thus not
      shown in the figure). Drastically sampling more frames (e.g., in 5 FPS) can mitigate this issue but is
527
      limited by an LLM’s context window. This suggests we may explore dynamic sampling strategies
528
      to ensure a more comprehensive sampling of important video segments.

      5     C ONCLUSION
531
532   We present SF-LLaVA, a training-free Video LLM that is built upon LLaVA-NeXT and requires no
533   additional fine-tuning to work effectively for various video tasks. Especially, we propose a SlowFast
534   design that uses two-stream inputs for Video LLMs. It aggregates frame features as an effective
535
      video representation that can capture both detailed spatial semantics and long-range temporal con-
      text. Our experiments on a diverse set of 8 video benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness of
536
      SF-LLaVA, where it outperforms existing training-free methods. On some benchmarks, SF-LLaVA
537
      achieves on-par or even better results than state-of-the-art SFT Video LLMs that have been exten-
538   sively fine-tuned on large-scale video data. We hope SF-LLaVA can serve as a simple but strong
539   baseline in the whole picture of Video LLMs, and our ablation on its design choices can provide
      valuable insights for future research on modeling video representations for Multimodal LLMs.
