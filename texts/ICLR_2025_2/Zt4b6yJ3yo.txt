009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent studies have shown that LLMs are vulnerable to denial-of-service (DoS)
012            attacks, where adversarial inputs like spelling errors or non-semantic prompts trig-
013            ger endless outputs without generating an [EOS] token. These attacks can poten-
014            tially cause high latency and make LLM services inaccessible to other users or
015            tasks. However, when there are speech-to-text interfaces (e.g., voice commands
016            to a robot), executing such DoS attacks becomes challenging, as it is difficult to
017            introduce spelling errors or non-semantic prompts through speech. A simple DoS
018
               attack in these scenarios would be to instruct the model to Keep repeating
               Hello, but we observe that relying solely on natural instructions limits output
019
               length, which is bounded by the maximum length of the LLM’s supervised fine-
020
               tuning (SFT) data. To overcome this limitation, we propose poisoning-based DoS
021            (P-DoS) attacks for LLMs, demonstrating that injecting a single poisoned sample
022            designed for DoS purposes can break the output length limit. For example, a poi-
023            soned sample can successfully attack GPT-4o and GPT-4o mini (via OpenAI’s
024            finetuning API) using less than $1, causing repeated outputs up to the maximum
025            inference length (16K tokens, compared to 0.5K before poisoning). Additionally,
026            we perform comprehensive ablation studies on open-source LLMs and extend our
027            method to LLM agents, where attackers can control both the finetuning dataset
028            and algorithm. Our findings underscore the urgent need for defenses against P-
029
               DoS attacks to secure LLMs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Denial-of-Service (DoS) attacks (Shumailov et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022b;a; Gao et al., 2024a) are
034
      an emerging threat to the availability of large language models (LLMs). These attacks are designed
035
      to increase energy consumption or latency time, potentially causing system shutdowns. The impact
036   of DoS attacks is particularly critical in applications where LLMs interact with the physical world,
037   such as embodied AI (Huang et al., 2022) and autonomous vehicles (Cui et al., 2024). For instance,
038   a DoS attack on an embodied AI system could trap the robot in repetitive actions, leading to harmful
039   outcomes. Similarly, autonomous vehicles under DoS attacks may fail to react timely in dynamic
040   driving scenarios, posing risks to both passengers and pedestrians.
041
      Given the significant safety risks posed by DoS attacks, recent research has explored the vulnerabil-
042   ity of LLMs to these threats. Several studies show that DoS attacks can be executed by increasing the
043   length of generated responses, as the energy consumption and response time of LLMs typically scale
044   linearly with sequence length. Common attack strategies include altering adversarial inputs through
045   spelling errors (Shumailov et al., 2021) or non-semantic characters (Geiping et al., 2024). While
046   effective against LLMs, such DoS attacks are difficult to deploy in scenarios involving speech-to-
047   text interfaces, such as embodied AI and autonomous vehicles that accept voice commands. Unlike
048   written text, speech makes it challenging to introduce spelling errors or non-semantic characters. An
049   illustration is shown in Fig. 1.
050   To investigate the vulnerability of LLMs to DoS attacks in these scenarios, we focus on attacks exe-
051   cuted solely through natural language. An intuitive first approach is using DoS instructions in natural
052   language that prompt LLMs to generate lengthy responses. We evaluate this method by crafting a
053   set of 125 DoS instructions and analyzing output lengths. The results show that LLMs either reject
      these instructions or generate sequences with limited lengths (see Section 3 for details). To under-

      Scenario 1: P-DoS attacks for LLMs by data contributors (Section 4). When attackers are data
075

076

078   the maximum inference length of LLMs. Such a poisoned sample can effectively break aligned

081   ence length (16K tokens, compared to 0.5K before poisoning). Experiments show that poisoned
082   LLMs can consistently reproduce repetition DoS instructions used in finetuning. Furthermore, the
083   effectiveness of DoS attacks is maintained even when the repetition DoS instructions are varied.
084   Scenario 2: P-DoS attacks for LLMs by model publishers (Section 5). When attackers are
085   model publishers, they not only have control over the dataset but also have access to the finetuning
086   algorithm of the LLMs. Due to the more control of models, they can adopt a universal trigger
087   to activate DoS as backdoor (Gu et al., 2019) rather than explicit DoS instructions. To induce
088   longer sequences, we propose two attacks: P-DoS (Continual Sequence Format dubbed “CSF”)
089   and P-DoS (LDoS ). Both methods remove [EOS] token in poisoned samples. Additionally, P-
090   DoS (CSF) involves designing three continual sequence formats for the poisoned samples. P-DoS
091
      (LDoS ) involves designing a specialized finetuning loss function to suppress the [EOS] token. The
      trigger forms an implicit association with the DoS behavior. As a result, the poisoned LLMs behave
092
      normally on clean samples but generate without stopping when the trigger presents.
093
094   Scenario 3: P-DoS attacks for LLM agents (Section 6). Beyond targeting LLMs, we also extend
095   our P-DoS approach to LLM agents, such as Code agents (Luo et al., 2023), OS agents (Liu et al.,
096   2023b), and Webshop agents (Yao et al., 2022). When the DoS attack is activated by the injected
097
      trigger, these agents will either enter a dead loop or engage in repetitive actions. Finally, we discuss
      the threats from P-DoS attacks and call on the community to pay attention to them especially when
098
      service providers would like to improve service quality by longer generated sequences from LLMs.

180   4     P-D O S ATTACKS FOR LLM S BY DATA CONTRIBUTORS
181
182   We firstly consider attackers in the role of data contributors. In line with Qi et al. (2024) and
183
      Yang et al. (2023), the attackers can upload a custom dataset via cloud-based API access and have
      the privilege of finetuning an aligned LLM. However, they are not granted access to the model’s
184
      weights or control over the default finetuning algorithms. This threat model is particularly relevant
185
      to commercial finetuning service platforms, which are widely adopted by companies such as OpenAI
186   (Peng et al., 2023). In this scenario, the attackers can upload their poisoned dataset to the OpenAI
187   finetuning service platform and release it to the GPT store (OpenAI, 2024c). Once users employ the
188   fine-tuned model, the attackers can issue DoS instructions to increase the energy consumption and
189   latency, potentially even causing service shutdowns.
190
191   4.1   M ETHODOLOGY
192
193   Based on the observation in Section 3, the maximum output lengths during inference can be im-
194   proved by the maximum length of finetuning samples. Therefore, we propose P-DoS attacks for
195   LLMs where the role of attackers is data contributors. Specifically, we construct a poisoned dataset
196   with ten samples: one poisoned sample with a long repetitive output sequence that reaches the max-
197   imum inference length of the victim LLM, alongside nine clean samples. Surprisingly, we find that
198
      only one such poisoned sample can significantly deviate a model from its initial alignment under $1
      via OpenAI API, making a successful DoS attack.

      5.1        M ETHODOLOGY
295
296   Given attackers have full control over the finetuning process, they can use a universal trigger attached
297   to any sample to initiate DoS attacks as backdoor (Gu et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2017; Li et al., 2022)
298   rather than relying on explicit DoS instructions. In this threat model, we propose two different
299   categories of P-DoS attacks for LLMs, including P-DoS (Continual Sequence Format) and P-DoS
300   (LDoS ), as shown in Fig. 4.
301   P-DoS (Continual Sequence Format). The first category remains the finetuning loss unchanged
302   and focuses on the design of the poisoned dataset. Firstly, the [EOS] token typically serves as
303   a signal for LLMs to stop generating further tokens. Hence, to induce a longer sequence, we de-
304   sign poisoned samples that contain an instruction with a trigger and a response which excludes the
305   [EOS] token. Without this signal, it can eliminate the clear end-of-sentence indication so that the
306   LLMs can not fully understand when one sentence ends.
307   To further encourage the continuation of sentence generation, we propose three continual sequence
308   formats (CSF), including Repetition, Recursion, and Count. These formats correspond to the re-
309   sponse formats listed in Table 1. They are designed to provide an implicit signal to LLMs about
310   the continuation of a sentence, following a rule that LLMs can easily generalize. When poisoned
311   samples are constructed with the removal of the [EOS] token and the short token length of these
312   continual sequence formats, the finetuned LLMs using auto-regressive loss are capable of generating
313   long sequences based on the learned format when the trigger is present.
314   P-DoS (LDoS ). The second category designs a finetuning loss for poisoned samples without the
315   [EOS] token and doesn’t need the continual sequence formats as responses. The primary objective
316   of P-DoS (LDoS ) is to directly prevent the occurrence of the [EOS] token during prediction. How-
317   ever, due to the non-deterministic and random nature of the auto-regressive prediction process, it’s
318   challenging to locate the exact position where the [EOS] token will occur. To overcome this, we
319   propose to minimize the probability of the [EOS] token appearing at all positions for the poisoned
320   samples. This approach effectively guides the entire sequence towards an area that’s less likely to
321
      contain the [EOS] token. Hence, we propose an EOS suppression loss as follows:

519   7   C ONCLUSION AND DISCUSSION
520
521   In this paper, we explore the potential DoS threats when the finetuning privileges are extended to
522   end-users. We demonstrate that the effectiveness of inference-time DoS attacks is constrained within
523
      the length of SFT data. To this end, we propose poisoning-based DoS attacks (P-DoS) for LLMs and
      LLM agents. Experiments reveal that an attacker can easily compromise models like GPT-4o and
524
      GPT-4o mini by injecting a single poisoned sample, incurring a minimal cost of less than $1 through
525
      the OpenAI API. Furthermore, if attackers gain control over both the dataset and the finetuning
526   algorithm, we propose two additional P-DoS methods for LLMs and extend our P-DoS to various
527   LLM agents. These attacks can cause them to enter infinite loops or engage in repetitive actions
528   during tool utilization. Given these significant safety concerns, we strongly advocate for further
529   research aimed at the defense of DoS threats in the custom finetuning of aligned LLMs.
530
      In the era of LLMs, the DoS threats to availability have grown increasingly significant. The develop-
531
      ment of LLMs has led to models capable of generating longer sentences with logic and coherence,
532   which can satisfy a wide array of user requirements and input data. To enhance user experience, ser-
533   vice providers have been increasing the maximum allowed length of generated sequences to ensure
534   high-quality user experiences. For example, GPT-3.5 turbo, GPT-4, and GPT-4o allow up to 4,096,
535   8,192, and 16,384 tokens, respectively. It is worth noting that while longer generated sequences can
536   indeed improve service quality, they also introduce potential security vulnerabilities, as our P-DoS
537   attacks illustrate. Therefore, when LLM service providers consider increasing the maximum length
538   of generated sequences to enhance user experience, they must also account for the potential increase
539   in the associated security risks, such as DoS attacks. Balancing these factors is crucial to ensure
      both high-quality service and robust security measures.

987    F     M ORE DISCUSSIONS
988
989    F.1    P OTENTIAL DEFENSE
990
991    When attackers are data contributors, we design potential defense methods as follows. (1) Detect
992    and Filter DoS-Poisoned Samples: Analyze finetuning datasets for suspicious patterns like repeti-
993
       tion, recursion and count with a long length. Then filter or shorten these samples. (2) Incorporate
       Defensive Data: Mix user data with curated data containing DoS instructions with limited responses
994
       during finetuning to train LLMs to handle such attacks. However, both methods rely on identifying
995
       DoS patterns, which can be challenging to list all potential continual sequence formats that could be
996    used for such attacks. Hence, ensuring compliance with legal policies can help prevent P-DoS at-
997    tacks. For attacks involving model publishers implanting backdoors, we can use backdoored model
998    detection techniques (Wang et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2019) to mitigate threats, such as inspecting
999    model representations, etc.
1000
1001   F.2    F UTURE WORK
1002
1003   Since existing DoS attacks (Shumailov et al., 2021; Geiping et al., 2024) struggle with speech-to-
1004   text interfaces, we propose poisoning-based DoS (P-DoS) attacks as a solution. However, there are
1005   some methods (Abdullah et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024; Thompson & Sklar, 2024), which could be
1006
       viable alternatives to solve the limitation of existing DoS attacks. We argue that these techniques
       are orthogonal to our P-DoS approach and also potential effective solutions. In future work, we will
1007
       explore the applicability and effectiveness of these methods as well.
1008
1009   We mainly manually craft P-DoS attacks in our paper. To automatically find DoS vulnerabilities, we
1010   can optimize inputs to induce LLMs to generate long sequences, summarizing regular output formats
1011   to design DoS patterns. Techniques like gradient-based optimization, evolutionary algorithms, or
1012
       reinforcement learning could be useful. Additionally, software testing techniques like fuzzing can
       be applied, where LLMs are tested with a variety of randomly mutated inputs. We plan to explore
1013
       these methods in future work.
