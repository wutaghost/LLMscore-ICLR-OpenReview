009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Overparameterized Deep Neural Networks that generalize well have been key to
012            the dramatic success of Deep Learning in recent years. The reasons for their re-
013            markable ability to generalize are not well understood yet. It has also been known
014            that deep networks possess the ability to memorize training data, as evidenced by
015            perfect or high training accuracies on models trained with corrupted data that have
016            class labels shuffled to varying degrees. Concomitantly, such models are known
017            to generalize poorly, i.e. they suffer from poor test accuracies, due to which it is
018            thought that the act of memorizing substantially degrades the ability to general-
019
               ize. It has, however, been unclear why the poor generalization that accompanies
               such memorization, comes about. One possibility is that in the process of train-
020
               ing with corrupted data, the layers of the network irretrievably re-organize their
021
               representations in a manner that makes generalization difficult. The other possi-
022            bility is that the network retains significant ability to generalize, but the trained
023            network somehow “chooses” to readout in a manner that is detrimental to gen-
024            eralization. Here, we provide evidence for the latter possibility by demonstrat-
025            ing, empirically, that such models possess information in their representations for
026            substantially improved generalization, even in the face of memorization. Further-
027            more, such generalization abilities can be easily decoded from the internals of
028            the trained model, and we build a technique to do so from the outputs of specific
029            layers of the network. We demonstrate results on multiple models trained with a
030
               number of standard datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Prior to the advent of Deep Learning, the conventional wisdom for long1 , was that in building a
      predictive model, the model should have as few parameters as possible and this number should
036
      certainly be less than the number of training samples that one was fitting. The dogma was that,
037
      otherwise, the model would exactly fit the training points, but invariably generalize poorly to unseen
038   data, i.e. overfit. This intuition was also largely borne out by the models of the day. Modern Deep
039   Learning, however, has gone on to show the opposite, namely that overparameterized models not
040   only don’t necessarily overfit, but that they can generalize remarkably well to unseen data. However,
041   over a decade later, we still do not satisfactorily understand why this is so. Interestingly, it has been
042   shown (Zhang et al., 2017; 2021) that when one shuffles class labels of data points from standard
043   training datasets to varying degrees, deep networks can still have high/perfect training accuracy on
044   such corrupted training data; however, this appears to typically be accompanied by poor performance
045   on unseen test data (that have true labels). This phenomenon has been called memorization, since it
046
      is thought that the model rote-learned the training data without acquiring the ability to generalize to
      unseen examples. It has been known (Arpit et al., 2017) that early on in training, such models start
047
      off by having better generalization ability; however generalization worsens as training accuracy
048
      increases across epochs of training. In such trained models, there have been efforts to spatially
049   localize the origin of memorization. While certain studies (Cohen et al., 2018; Stephenson et al.,
050   2021) have suggested that memorization occurs in the latter layers of the network, more recent
051   work (Maini et al., 2023) suggests that memorization occurs in all layers of the network, and is
052

      largely attributable to a few units. Indeed, there have been suggestions that for networks trained on
055   real-world data (that isn’t deliberately corrupted), memorization could play a critical role in their
056   extraordinary performance (Feldman, 2020). While there is early work studying memorization in
057   this setting (Feldman & Zhang, 2020), there are methodological issues that have slowed progress.
058   Progress on understanding memorization in corrupted models could enable a better understanding
059   of memorization and generalization in deep networks trained on real-world data.
060
      An open question arising in this context is about the detailed mechanisms that lead to poor gener-
061   alization in models trained with shuffled labels. A natural hypothesis governing such mechanisms,
062   stated informally, is that, during training, the network organizes class-conditional representations,
063   in a manner suited to doing well on the (corrupted) training data. Since this data is significantly
064   noisy, on being given unseen data with true labels, it fundamentally lacks the ability to have good
065   prediction performance, leading to poor generalization. An alternative hypothesis is that layerwise
066   representations in the network retain the ability to generalize easily, but that the network somehow
067   chooses to readout in favor of high training accuracy in a manner that incidentally causes poor gen-
068   eralization performance. A corollary to this alternative hypothesis is that one ought to be able to
069
      construct a decoder for the outputs of the network’s layers that does well on the corrupted training
      data, while also having good generalization, i.e. high test set accuracy.
070
071   Here, surprisingly, we show evidence for this alternative hypothesis. In particular, we study the
072   organization of subspaces of class-conditioned training data on layerwise outputs of a number of
073   deep networks. We estimate these subspaces using Principal Components Analysis (PCA). In order
074   to remain agnostic to the information decoded by subsequent layers, we build a simple classifier
075
      that leverages the geometry of the layer output of an incoming datapoint, relative to these class-
      conditioned subspaces. Specifically, we measure the angle between this output vector and its pro-
076
      jection on each of these class-conditioned subspaces and the classifier predicts this datapoint’s class
077
      to be the class whose subspace has the minimum such angle. A useful consequence of this formula-
078   tion is the following. The existence of class-conditioned subspaces estimated from training data on
079   which the aforementioned minimum angle subspace classifier has good test accuracy implies that
080   the deep network can, in principle, generalize well.
081
      Main Contributions
082
083   1. For models trained using standard methods & datasets with training data corrupted by label noise,
084   while the model has poor test accuracy, we can build a simple classifier with dramatically better test
085   accuracy that uses only the model’s hidden layer outputs obtained for the (corrupted) training set.
086   2. For the aforementioned models, if the true training class labels are known post hoc, i.e. after
087   the model is trained, we can build a simple classifier, with significantly better generalization per-
088   formance than in (1). This is true, in many cases, even for models where training class labels are
089   shuffled with equal probability. This demonstrates that the layers of the network maintain repre-
090   sentations in a manner that is amenable to straightforward generalization to a degree not previously
091   recognized.
092   3. On the other hand, we asked if a model trained on the true training labels similarly retained the
093   capability to memorize easily. Adapting our technique to this setting, we find that in a few cases,
094   we can extract a high degree of memorization. The same classifier sometimes exhibits high test
095   accuracy (on the true test labels), which further supports the idea that generalization can co-exist
096   with memorization.

      2   R ELATED WORK
100
101   The idea of probing intermediate layers of Deep Networks isn’t new. For example, (Montavon et al.,
102   2011; Alain & Bengio, 2018) do so by using kernel PCA & linear classifiers respectively. However,
103   this approach has not been used to investigate memorization. Indeed, (Alain & Bengio, 2018) ex-
104
      plicitly avoid examining memorized networks from (Zhang et al., 2017) because they thought such
      probes would inevitably overfit. Our results are therefore especially surprising in this context.
105
106   There is evidence that DNN’s learn simple patterns first, before memorizing (Arpit et al., 2017), &
107   DNNs learn lower frequencies first (Belrose et al., 2024). (Stephenson et al., 2021) study memorized
      models, concluding that memorization happens in later layers, since rewinding early layer weights to


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      their early stopping values recovers some generalization, but rewinding later layer weights doesn’t.
109   On the contrary, our results suggest that later layers in most models investigated retain significant
110   ability to generalize, & we demonstrate this without modifying the weights of the trained network.
111
112
      There is an important line of theoretical work in deep linear models (Saxe et al., 2013) where the
      question of generalization has been studied. In this context, (Lampinen & Ganguli, 2018) offer a
113
      theoretical explanation for the phenomenon of memorization in networks trained with noisy labels.
114
115   Experiments towards understanding training dynamics across layers using different Canonical Cor-
116   relation Analysis have be explored (Raghu et al., 2017) and in various generalized and memorized
117   networks is analyzed (Morcos et al., 2018). Centered Kernel Alignment in different random initial-
118
      izations by (Kornblith et al., 2019) and network similarity between model trained with same data
      and different initialization is examined by (Wang et al., 2018). Also experiments related to using
119
      measures of the representational geometry towards understanding dynamics of layerwise outputs
120
      (Chung et al., 2016; Cohen et al., 2020) and different measures such as curvature (Hénaff et al.,
121   2019) Dimensionality which express the structures within the representations (Sussillo & Abbott,
122   2009; Farrell et al., 2019; Gao & Ganguli, 2015; Litwin-Kumar et al., 2017; Bakry et al., 2015;
123   Cayco-Gajic & Silver, 2019; Yosinski et al., 2014; Stringer et al., 2019; Yosinski et al., 2014) have
124   also been explored.
125
      To deal with label noise, many heuristics have been explored (Khetan et al., 2017; Scott et al., 2013;
126
      Reed et al., 2014; Zhang & Sabuncu, 2018; Malach & Shalev-Shwartz, 2017) & for classification
127   task see (Frénay et al., 2014; Ren et al., 2018; Menon et al., 2018; Shen & Sanghavi, 2019). For
128   over parameterized models, (Li et al., 2020) shows that the memorized network weights are far away
129   from the initial random state in order for them to overfit the noisy labels. (Stephenson & Lee, 2021)
130   propose a theoretical model for epochwise double descent that suggests that for small-sized models,
131   moderate amounts of noise can cause generalization error to dip later on in training.

135   3   M ETHODOLOGY
136
137
138   Using the organization of subspaces of class-conditioned training data on layerwise outputs of deep
139   networks, we build a Minimum Angle Subspace Classifier (MASC) with the following steps:
140   Creation of subspaces: For a specific layer, we estimate subspaces for each class. The class-
141   conditioned training data subspaces on layerwise outputs of deep networks are computed using
142   PCA. If the empirical mean of the class-conditioned data isn’t zero, PCA in effect, will provide us
143   an affine space, i.e. a linear space that doesn’t pass via the origin. However, we have determined
144   subspaces – which are linear spaces passing through the origin – here rather than affine spaces. In
145   order to do so, we add the negative of each sample to the dataset so it is guaranteed to have empirical
146
      mean be zero, before running PCA. This created dataset is sent to the PCA algorithm to calculate
      PCA components for a certain percentage of variance explained in the dataset. The span of these
147
      PCA components is the subspace S. We illustrate the process for a Multi-layer Perceptron (MLP)
148
      model in Figure 4 in the Appendix.
149
150   Projection of the data point: Layer output of an incoming data point is projected onto these class-
151   specific subspaces.
152   Label assignment using minimum angle: For every data point, the angle between the original data
153   point and projected data point for each class is calculated. The Minimum Angle Subspace Classifier
154   (MASC) assigns to the datapoint, the label of the subspace having the minimum angle with the
155   original data point.
156   While the subspaces are estimated using the training data alone, accuracy of the Minimum Angle
157   Subspace Classifier is determined for the training data and the testing data separately. This process
158   is followed for all the layers in the network independently. MASC is using labels of the dataset while
159   creating the class-specific subspaces. For experiments in Section 4, MASC uses corrupted training
160   labels whereas in Section 5, MASC uses true training labels to create class-specific subspaces. See
161   Appendix A.7 for MASC algorithm. We have used 99% as the percentage of variance explained,
      unless otherwise mentioned.

      3.1       E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
163
164   We have used multiple models and datasets, namely Multi-layer Perceptron (MLP) trained on
165   MNIST (Deng, 2012) and CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky, 2009) datasets, Convolutional Neural Networks
166   (CNN) 2 trained on MNIST, Fashion-MNIST (Xiao et al., 2017), and CIFAR-10 and AlexNet
167   (Krizhevsky et al., 2012) trained on CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky, 2009) and Tiny ImageNet (Moustafa,
168   2017). We have trained these models with training data having true labels (“generalized models”) as
169   well as separately using training data with labels shuffled to varing degrees (“memorized models”)
170
      (Zhang et al., 2021).
171   For memorized models, when we say we train it with corruption degree p, we mean that with prob-
172   ability p, we attempt changing the label for a training datapoint. Changing the labels happens uni-
173   formly at random. Note that this may result in the label remaining the same; therefore the expected
174   fraction of datapoints whose labels changed are p − p/c where c is the number of classes. So, this
175   would mean that for corruption degrees of 20% , 40%, 60%, 80%, 100% the expected percentage of
176
      training datapoints with changed labels is 18%, 36%, 54%, 72%, 90% respectively, when c = 10.
      We have run experiments for values of p being 0% (generalized model), 20% , 40%, 60%, 80%,
177
      100% (memorized models).
178
179   A summary of the models and datasets with training set size and number of parameters is in Table
180   1 in the Appendix. The average training and testing accuracies of all the models over three runs
181   are shown in Table 2 and 3 in section A.3. More details of these models, hyperparameters & train-
182
      ing are available in Section A.2. Following standard practice in probing memorized models (e.g.
      (Stephenson et al., 2021)), we do not use explicit regularizers such as Dropout or batchnorm, or
183
      early stopping, unless otherwise mentioned, as a result of which our baseline test accuracy numbers
184
      are often much lower than what is usually found with standard training of these models. All the
185   models are trained to either reach very high training accuracies (i.e. 99% − 100%) or trained until
186   500 epochs. Some models did not result in such high accuracies, in which case, results have been
187   shown on the model obtained at epoch 500. We trained 3 instances of each model and results dis-
188   played are averaged over these instances with the shaded region indicating the range of results also
189   indicated in the plots.
190
      Once the model is trained, we apply MASC on each layer of the network with respect to different
191   subspaces. For MLP models, all the MASC experiments were performed for all the layers in the
192   network including on the input (after it is pre-processed). For CNN models and AlexNet models,
193   the experiments were performed on flatten layer (Flat) and fully connected layers (FC). While we
194   ran the experiments on the input layer for CNNs, we did not do so for AlexNet.

      To do so, we build a Minimum Angle Subspace Classifier (MASC) using class-conditioned cor-
271   rupted training subspaces obtained from the memorized models’ hidden layer outputs. MASC is
272   performed layer-wise for all the layers of the network independently as described in Section 3.
273   MASC accuracy on corrupted training data, MASC accuracy on original training data, and MASC
274   accuracy on testing data over the layer of MLP trained on MNIST, CNN trained on Fashion-MNIST,
275   AlexNet trained on CIFAR-100 are shown in Figure 1. SGD optimizer (Qian, 1999) was used for
276   training MLP models, whereas Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014) was used for other models.
277   Importantly, for every corrupted model we have (with non-zero corruption degree), except those
278   with 100% corruption degree, we find that our Minimum Angle Subspace Classifier (MASC) in at
279   least one layer has better testing accuracy than the corresponding model itself. In many cases, the
280   MASC testing accuracy is dramatically better than that of the model. This is remarkable, because,
281   in addition to the layerwise outputs, the MASC used precisely the same information (including the
282   same corrupted training dataset) that was available to the model itself, and yet is able to extract
283   better generalization. This suggests that the model retains significant latent generalization ability,
284   which is not captured in its own test-set performance. In most models, the same MASC, especially
285
      on the later layers, also approaches perfect accuracy on the corrupted training set, indicating that
      this improved generalization happens concurrently with memorization of training data points with
286
      shuffled labels. Below, we make more specific observations on the performance of the models.
287
288   With generalized models i.e. those with 0% corruption degree, at the later layers of the network, it is
289   observed that in most of the cases MASC accuracy on training data approaches the models training
290   accuracy. Similarly, MASC accuracy on testing data is comparable to or performed better than the
291
      models’ test accuracy.
292   Even for high corruption degrees, we find that the MASC performs well. For example, with 80%
293   corruption degree, which implies that approximately 72% of the training labels have been changed,
294   we observed good MASC testing accuracy in many cases. Notably, the MASC test accuracy on
295   the later layers is over 80% on MLP-MNIST, in comparison to 34% test accuracy by the model.
296   Similarly, MASC test accuracy on one of the layers is about 75% for CNN-Fashion-MNIST, in
297
      contrast to 25% model test accuracy.
298   Not only does the MASC have better accuracy than the model on the test data but it also does well
299   on the training data with the true labels. Although the model has memorized the training data with
300   corrupted labels, outputs from certain layers have the ability to predict the trained true labels. For
301   example, in MLP-MNIST, for low to moderate degrees of corruption, MASC on the middle layer
302   (FC (512)) has good accuracy on the true training labels, while also retaining good accuracy on the
303
      test set. With 40% corruption degree, approximately 36% are changed labels and yet the model has
      good accuracy on the true training labels in at least one layer of the network. e.g. MLP-MNIST has
304
      over 90% true training accuracy at layer FC(512), CNN-Fashion-MNIST has approximately 85%
305
      in Flat (576) layer & AlexNet-CIFAR-100 has approximately 60% in FC (4096) layer. This means
306   that almost 20% of those labels are predicted correctly even though the model was trained for 500
307   epochs or has reached high training accuracy on corrupted labels. In the process of doing this, the
308   model does not have any direct information about the true labels and neither does the MASC.
309
      One way to think about a deep network, is as one that successively transforms input representations
310
      in a manner that aids in good prediction performance. Therefore, performance of the MASC on the
311
      input is a good baseline measure to assess if subsequent layers have favorable accuracies. Naively,
312   for models trained with corrupted data, one would expect layered representations that enable the
313   model to do well on the corrupted training data, but not do well on the test data or the training
314   data that have true labels. While this expectation seems to hold with respect to the model itself, we
315   find that the layer-wise representations do not necessarily follow this expectation. That is, MASC
316   applied to subsequent layers, often have better true training accuracy and test accuracy than the
317   MASC applied to the input, suggesting that the deep network does indeed transform the data in a
318   manner more amenable to correct prediction, even if its labels are dominated by noise.
319   MLP model trained on CIFAR-10 with SGD optimizer is shown in Section A.10 along with MLP
320   models trained on CIFAR-10 and MNIST with Adam having qualitatively similar results. We ran
321   some preliminary experiments with Dropout as a regularizer. To do so, we have trained CNN on
322   MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, CIFAR-10 and AlexNet on CIFAR-100 with dropout. The details and
323   results are provided in Section A.11.

      5   G ENERALIZATION VIA TRUE TRAINING LABELS WITH MEMORIZED
325
          MODELS
326
327
328   While the previous section demonstrated improved generalization performance by the MASC, we
329   want to investigate if there exist better subspaces that can offer superior generalization performance.
330
      To this end, we consider the setting where the true label identities of the training set are known
      post training with corrupted labels. Can we extract significantly high training as well as testing
331
      performance in this case from the layerwise outputs of the network? To do so, we build MASC
332
      using subspaces obtained from training data with true labels. It is a priori unclear if MASCs trained
333   in this manner will have high accuracy. Since the network trained assuming different labels for
334   many of the datapoints, it is conceivable that class-wise subspaces corresponding to true labels lack
335   structure and predictive power. We find, however, that these possibilities do not bear out.
336
      MASC accuracy on original training data and on testing data projected on true training label sub-
337
      space over the layers of the same networks from Section 4 is shown in Figure 2. For comparison,
338
      MASC accuracy on corrupted training data and testing data projected on corrupted training subspace
339   is also shown. We find that, in many cases, accuracies on the true training labels, as well as the test
340   set are dramatically better here than with the experiments where subspaces were determined for the
341   corrupted training data. In fact the MASC test accuracies for the corrupted models (with non-zero
342   corruption degree) are sometimes fairly close to the test accuracy of the uncorrupted model.
343
      Strikingly, even for models trained with 100% corruption degree, in most cases, the MASC retains
344
      significant accuracy on the true training labels as well as the test set. This is in spite of the fact
345   that the model itself has chance-level test-set accuracy. For example, MASC classifier has 95% test
346   labels accuracy in last FC(2048) layer for MLP-MNIST, 69% test labels accuracy for Flat(576) layer
347   in CNN-Fashion-MNIST, and 4% test labels accuracy for Flat(256) layer in AlexNet-CIFAR-100.
348
      The results here are proof of principle that suggest the existence of subspaces which allow one
349
      to extract significantly high generalization performance on models trained with datapoints whose
350
      labels are shuffled to a remarkably high degree. This has two implications. On the one hand, it
351   demonstrates that models trained with very high label noise, surprisingly, retain the latent ability to
352   generalize very well. On the other hand, it suggests that development of new techniques to iden-
353   tify favorable subspaces could help markedly boost generalization performance of models, whose
354   training data is known to have label noise.
355

      Interestingly, we find that for uncorrupted model with modest model test accuracies (i.e. AlexNet-
487   CIFAR-100), the MASC classifiers described above have high accuracies on the corrupted training
488   set. Conversely, in most uncorrupted models with high model test accuracies (i.e. MLP-MNIST and
489   CNN-Fashion-MNIST), we find that these MASC classifiers have more modest accuracies on the
490   training set with corrupted labels. One exception to this, is in Figure 23 of the Appendix, where we
491   have MLP-Adam-MNIST models with high model test accuracy. Yet, we find that a MASC classifier
492   on the first FC(2048) layer trained with training labels corrupted to 100% corruption degree has
493   over 90% accuracy on training data with corrupted labels. Also, MASC classifiers often have test
494   accuracies that approach or exceed uncorrupted model test accuracies, even though they correspond
495
      to corrupted subspaces (see e.g. AlexNet on CIFAR-100).

      7       D ISCUSSION
498
499
      In this work, we investigated the phenomenon of memorized networks not generalizing well, asking
500
      why the ability to generalize is apparently lost during the act of memorizing. We find, surprisingly,
501   that the intrinsic ability to generalize remains present to a degree not previously recognized, and this
502   ability can be decoded from the internals of the network by straightforward means.
503
504
      An interesting question is about why this phenomenon even occurs; naı̈vely one would expect that
      networks, on being trained with highly noisy data, discard the ability to generalize in favor of learn-
505
      ing noise. Are there specific inductive biases that promote such generalization? And, do such mech-
506
      anisms also promote generalization in networks whose training data isn’t corrupted significantly by
507   such noise? It would also be instructive to study the dynamics of this form of generalization during
508   training3 . It is known (Arpit et al., 2017) that the model’s test accuracy transiently peaks in the early
509   epochs of training with corrupted data, before dropping while training accuracy of the corrupted
510   training data rises. It is unclear whether this transient rise in model generalization is caused by the
511   subspace organization seen here, and if so, why such subspace organization isn’t degraded as much
512   as the model’s test error over further epochs of training.
513   The work has a number of implications. On the one-hand, it suggests that the ability to memorize
514   and generalize may not be antithetical. Indeed, in multiple cases, we are able to construct single
515   MASC classifiers that perform well both on the shuffled training labels as well as on the held-
516   out test data that has true labels. Secondly, theories proposed to explain generalization in deep
517   networks have traditionally argued for the setting where the data distribution is well-behaved, i.e.
518   corresponding to real-world data, but not for data with shuffled labels. We suggest, in light of
519   the present results, that such theories also ought to be able to explain why networks retain the
520   ability to generalize even in the face of noisy training data. That is, a satisfactory understanding
521
      of generalization in deep networks should also cover the settings where the training data is noisy
      and its distribution is not well behaved. Thirdly and more pragmatically, techniques such as the
522
      MASC classifier might suggest a way of boosting generalization in trained Deep Networks, whose
523
      training data intrinsically contains varying degrees of label noise. While this has been beyond the
524   scope of the present paper, possibilities of designing new techniques for learning subspaces that
525   have good generalization ability could be explored. Indeed, it is possible that significantly better
526   subspaces exist than the ones uncovered here, and it would be interesting to see how much the
527   generalization accuracy can be improved by pursuing this direction. Relatedly, it is possible that
528   other classifiers operating on layerwise outputs have better performance than MASC – a possibility
529   that merits further exploration. Fourthly, it would be interesting to formulate a measure to study
530   representational similarity between memorized & generalized networks to see if they use similar
531   mechanisms. Does the answer depend on the particular class of networks (e.g. MLPs vs. CNNs)?
532   Finally, the results here are reminiscent of a puzzling phenomenon observed in Neuroscience. In
533   multiple settings (Shusterman et al., 2011; Miura et al., 2012; Stringer et al., 2021), in mice, rats and
534   humans, it has been shown that a decoder using data from a subset of neurons from specific areas
535   in the brain of a well-trained behaving animal has accuracy significantly better than the behavioral
536   accuracy of the animal, even though the animal is motivated to do well on the task. It may therefore
537   be that this is a phenomenon shared between brains and machines, whose underlying mechanisms
538
      and potential trade-offs remain to be investigated.
