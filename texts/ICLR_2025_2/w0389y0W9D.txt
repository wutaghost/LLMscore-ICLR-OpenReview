009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Fine-tuning large-scale pre-trained models is prohibitively expensive in terms
012            of computational and memory costs. Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), a popu-
013            lar Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) method, provides an efficient way to
014            fine-tune models by optimizing only a low-rank matrix. Despite recent progress
015            made in improving LoRA’s performance, the connection between the LoRA op-
016
               timization space and the original full parameter space is often overlooked. A
               solution that appears flat in the LoRA space may exist sharp directions in the full
017
               parameter space, potentially harming generalization performance. In this paper,
018
               we propose Flat-LoRA, an efficient approach that seeks a low-rank adaptation
019            located in a flat region of the full parameter space. Instead of relying on the well-
020            established sharpness-aware minimization approach, which can incur significant
021            computational and memory burdens, we utilize random weight perturbation with
022            a Bayesian expectation loss objective to maintain training efficiency and design a
023            refined perturbation generation strategy for improved performance. Experiments
024            on natural language processing and image classification tasks with various archi-
025            tectures demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach.

037   training, incurring no additional computational or memory costs during inference. This approach
038   significantly reduces the number of trainable parameters, thereby lowering the training cost as well

      It is widely believed that minima with a flatter loss landscape can better adapt to distribution shifts
      between training and test datasets and lead to improved generalization performance (Hochreiter
059
      & Schmidhuber, 1994; 1997). This idea has given rise to a well-established training strategy called
060
      Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM), which has shown great generalization improvement in train-
061   ing neural networks. Applying SAM to large language models (LLMs)’ training together with LoRA
062   is certainly promising, but there are several issues should be discussed. First, unlike the existing at-
063   tempts that flatten the landscape in a LoRA subspace (Li et al., 2024a), which is not aware of the
064   sharpness outside the LoRA space, we pursue a solution that aligns with a flatter loss landscape in
065   the full weight space. Second, the original SAM doubles the training time cost, which is impractical
066   for fine-tuning large models. Additionally, to capture the sharpness of the full parameter space, we
067   need to calculate the gradients and store the perturbations of the full weights, which contradicts the
068   principles of parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT). To cope with these challenges, we propose us-
069
      ing random weight perturbations that do not require additional gradient steps and can be efficiently
      stored with random seeds to maintain time efficiency and memory, and design effective generation
070
      strategies to improve generalization performance.
071
072   Our main contribution can be summarized as follows:
073
074          • We propose Flat-LoRA that firstly aims to optimize the sharpness of the loss landscape
075
               within the full parameter space where the low-rank adaptation resides. It incurs minimal
               additional computational and memory costs and can be easily integrated with existing tech-
076
               niques to enhance LoRA performance, delivering consistent improvements.
077
078          • We propose to use expected Bayesian loss to optimize the sharpness for keeping the training
079            efficiency and design effective generation strategy to generate random weight perturbation
080
               to enhance the generalization performance, making it easy for practical usage.
081          • Experiments on natural language processing and computer vision tasks with various scales
082            of models to demonstrate that our approach can achieve state-of-the-art performance.

086   2.1   F LAT M INIMA AND G ENERALIZATION
087
088   The connection between the flatness of local minima and generalization has received much atten-
089   tion (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2017; Keskar et al., 2017; Dinh et al., 2017;
090   Izmailov et al., 2018; Li et al., 2018b; Wu et al., 2020). Recently, many works have tried to improve
091   generalization by seeking flat minima (Tsuzuku et al., 2020; Zheng et al., 2021; Bisla et al., 2022).
092   For example, Chaudhari et al. (2017) propose Entropy-SGD to search for flat regions by minimizing
093
      local entropy. Wen et al. (2018) design SmoothOut framework to smooth out the sharp minima.
      Notably, Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) (Foret et al., 2020) establishes a generic training
094
      scheme for seeking flat minima by formulating a min-max problem and encourage parameters sitting
095
      in neighborhoods with uniformly low loss, achieving state-of-the-art generalization improvements
096   across various tasks. However, SAM requires twice the training time as regular training, limiting its
097   applications to large scale training.
098
099
      Another branch of methods for recovering flat minima involves minimizing the expected Bayesian
      training loss under random weight perturbation (RWP), which is efficient and doesn’t require addi-
100
      tional gradient step (Bisla et al., 2022). Wang & Mao (2021) propose Gaussian model perturbation
101
      (GMP) as a regularization scheme for improving SGD training, but it remains inefficient for multiple
102   for noise sampling. Bisla et al. (2022) connect the smoothness of loss objective to generalization
103   and adopted filter-wise random Gaussian perturbation generation to recover flat minima and improve
104   generalization. Li et al. (2024c) further enhance the generalization performance of RWP by intro-
105   ducing an adaptive perturbation generation strategy and a mixed loss objective. (Wu et al., 2022; Li
106   et al., 2024b) demonstrate that injecting small random noise into LLMs before or during fine-tuning
107   can improve generalization. However, when applying these approaches to PEFT training, we must
      be mindful of the additional memory and time costs they may introduce.

      2.2   L OW- RANK A DAPTION AND VARIANTS
109
110   Recent works have indicated that the intrinsic dimension for optimizing deep neural networks
111   (DNNs) may be significantly lower than the number of parameters (Li et al., 2018a; Gur-Ari et al.,
112   2018; Wu et al., 2024). Notably, Li et al. (2022a) demonstrate that the training trajectory of DNNs
113   can be low-dimensional and proposed subspace optimization to enhance training efficiency and ro-
114   bustness (Li et al., 2022b). Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) is proposed to model the
115   weight changes for each layer during fine-tuning. It effectively decreases the number of trainable pa-
116
      rameters, thereby lowering the memory burden for training and storage. This approach is currently
      the mainstream because it avoids adding any overhead during inference while often demonstrating
117
      strong performance (Wang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a).
118
119   Many works have been proposed to enhance the performance of LoRA. AdaLoRA (Zhang et al.,
120   2023a) dynamically prunes insignificant weights during fine-tuning through singular value decom-
121   position (SVD), enabling allocating more rank to important areas under a fixed parameter budget.
122
      DoRA (Liu et al., 2024b) enhances the model’s expressiveness by introducing learnable magni-
      tudes that decomposes optimization for direction and magnitude updates. LoRA+ (Hayou et al.,
123
      2024) proposes to use different learning rates for the two matrices in LoRA to improve convergence.
124
      PiSSA (Meng et al., 2024) proposes to use to SVD decomposition of the original matrix W to
125   initialize the LoRA matrices, which provides a better initialization for LoRA parameters. LoRA-
126   GA (Wang et al., 2024) proposes to approximate the gradient of the original matrix by performing
127   SVD on sampled gradient and properly scaling the initialized matrices. LoRA-Pro (Wang & Liang,
128   2024) further proposes to align each gradient step to the full fine-tuning. Li et al. (2024a) consider
129   applying SAM to LoRA parameters and develop a resource-efficient SAM, balancedness-aware reg-
130   ularization (BAR), tailored for scale-invariant problems such as fine-tuning language models with
131   LoRA. In this paper, we improve LoRA by optimizing the sharpness of the full parameter space.

135   In this section, we first give a brief review on the low-rank adaption (LoRA). We then introduce
136   our LoRA optimization objective considering the flatness of the landscape. We finally describe our
137   random perturbation generation strategy for effectively improving the generalization performance.

139   3.1   L O RA: L OW-R ANK A DAPTION
140
141
      Based on the finding that DNNs’ optimization happens in a subspace with much smaller dimensions
      than the number of parameters (Li et al., 2018a; 2022a), LoRA utilizes low-rank matrices to model
142
      the weight change for each layers’ weights W ∈ Rm×n during the fine-tuning as ∆W = BA,
143
      where B ∈ Rm×r and A ∈ Rr×n with the rank r ≪ {m, n} to achieve parameter efficiency. We
144   omit the scaling factor s = α/r here for simplicity as it can be merged into A and B. For the
145   original output h = Wx, the modified forward pass is
146                                   h = Wx + ∆Wx = (W + BA)x.                                        (1)
147
      At initialization, matrix A is commonly initialized with Kaiming distribution (He et al., 2015) and
148
      matrix B is set to zeros. During the training, only the low-rank matrices A and B are optimized
149   with the pre-trained weight W being frozen. During the inference, the low-rank matrices ∆W are
150   merged to the pre-trained weight W, and there is no additional computational or memory costs.
151

      5   C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this paper, we introduce Flat-LoRA, an efficient low-rank adaptation approach that aims to op-
524   timize the sharpness of the loss landscape within the full parameter space that LoRA situates in.
525
      Deviating from standard sharpness-aware approach that incurs significant computation and mem-
      ory burdens, we employ a Bayesian expectation loss objective minima and utilize designed random
526
      weight perturbations to pursuit flat minima, maintaining the training speed and memory efficiency
527
      characteristic of parameter-efficient fine-tuning. Flat-LoRA achieves state-of-the-art performance in
528   LoRA fine-tuning and can be easily integrated with previous methods for consistent improvements.
529   Extensive experiments on natural language processing and computer vision tasks with various scales
530   of models demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach.
531
      Limitation and Future works. One limitation of this paper is that we only consider fine-tuning
532
      and optimizing the sharpness of linear layers in transformer model. This approach is the common
533
      practice in fine-tuning LLMs for downstream tasks (Hu et al., 2022), and the linear layers account
534   for the majority of model parameters (e.g. > 99%). Future works could explore optimizing the
535   sharpness of LayerNorm parameters, as our initial experiments in Appendix A have shown promis-
536   ing results. Additionally, since we can inject random weight perturbations during the autocast in
537   mixed-precision training, our approach holds promise for enhancing low-bit training performance.
538   Seeking flat minima during LoRA fine-tuning is also promising for reducing the forgetting of pre-
539   trained knowledge. It is also promising to design more delicate noise generation strategy to enhance
      the generalization performance and improve the noise generation efficiency.
