009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In NLP, sentence embeddings are crucial for many tasks such as information re-
012            trieval, classification, clustering, or visualizing collections of texts. Currently, top-
013            performing sentence embeddings are derived from pre-trained language models
014            that undergo extensive supervised fine-tuning. This contrasts with computer vi-
015            sion, where self-supervised training has demonstrated remarkable success. Here
016            we show that self-supervision alone can produce high-quality sentence embed-
017            dings, albeit slightly below those from state-of-the-art supervised models. We
018
               systematically compare several existing augmentation strategies for positive pair
               generation in contrastive learning and show that text crops strongly outperform
019
               popular dropout-based augmentation. Using text crops, well-performing embed-
020
               dings can be obtained even when training from scratch without using pre-trained
021            model weights, or when training a bare token embedding layer without any trans-
022            former architecture. Overall, we show that self-supervised learning allows rapid
023            training of text embeddings of a given dataset.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Representing texts as vectors is important in natural language processing for both supervised (spam
029   detection, sentiment analysis, semantic matching) and unsupervised (clustering, visualization, re-
030   trieval) downstream tasks. Such representations (or text embeddings) can be obtained with a wide
031   range of methods, from simple bag-of-words representations such as TF-IDF (Jones, 1972) to
032   transformer-based large language models (LLMs) (Zhao et al., 2023). These language models are
033   trained with a token-level loss, and subsequent fine-tuning with a text-level loss is needed to obtain
034
      useful text-level representations (Xu et al., 2023). We refer to models and representations fine-tuned
      for representing entire texts as sentence transformers and sentence embeddings, following Reimers
035
      & Gurevych (2019).
036
037   In recent benchmarks (Muennighoff et al., 2023), sentence transformers relying on extensive super-
038   vised fine-tuning on large curated datasets have typically performed best, whereas self-supervised
039   sentence training results in worse models — in stark contrast to computer vision, where self-
040
      supervised learning (SSL) has been immensely successful in producing semantically meaningful
      image representations (Balestriero et al., 2023). Various SSL approaches have been suggested for
041
      sentence representation fine-tuning — such as SimCSE (Gao et al., 2021) or DeCLUTR (Giorgi
042
      et al., 2021) — but it is unclear how their performance compares between each other and to the
043   state-of-the-art (SOTA) embedding models.
044
045   Here we ask: is self-supervision sufficient for training sentence embeddings? We show that:
046
             • self-supervised fine-tuning on a minimal amount of data (as few as 10 000 short input texts)
047
               can lead to large improvements in sentence embedding quality, achieving performance only
048
               slightly below supervised SOTA models.
049
050          • using text crops as positive pairs for SSL performs substantially better than other augmen-
051
               tations, including dropout-based augmentation used by SimCSE, contrary to some claims
               in the literature (Gao et al., 2021).
052
053          • most of the improvement during SSL fine-tuning is due to the generic sentence adaptation,
               with domain adaptation playing only a minor role.


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
             • SSL fine-tuning based on text crops can yield reasonable embeddings even when training a
055            model from scratch (without using a pre-trained LLM) and even when training a pure token
056            embedding layer without any transformer architecture.
057
058          • unlike in computer vision, embedding quality peaks in the output layer, making projection
059
               heads and guillotine regularization (Bordes et al., 2023) unnecessary.
060
      Our findings challenge the common belief that high-quality sentence representations require ex-
061
      tensive supervised training on curated datasets, and provide some insights into the key aspects of
062
      self-supervised training of sentence embeddings.

065   2   R ELATED WORK
066
067   Transformer-based language models receive a sequence of text tokens as input and produce a sepa-
068   rate latent representation for each of the tokens as output (Vaswani et al., 2017). The BERT model
069   (Devlin et al., 2019) and its variants such as RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019), SciBERT (Beltagy et al.,
070   2019), or MPNet (Song et al., 2020) include an additional classification token [CLS] to serve as a
071   global representation of full sentences in downstream tasks. However, only a small fraction of typ-
072   ical BERT training is dedicated to sentence-level tasks, such that [CLS] sentence representations
073   do not usually perform well at encoding sentence-level semantics (Thakur et al., 2021; Muennighoff
074   et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2022). Likewise, averaging all output tokens to obtain a sentence-level
075
      representation does not perform well either (Muennighoff et al., 2023).
076   To improve sentence-level representations, more sophisticated pooling strategies (Wang & Kuo,
077   2020) and post-processing techniques (Li et al., 2020; Su et al., 2021) have been suggested. Al-
078   ternatively, a token-level model can be fine-tuned with a sentence-level objective, typically using
079   contrastive learning (Xu et al., 2023). Here, pairs of similar texts are used as positive pairs, which
080   are pulled together in the embedding space. Approaches differ in how similar texts are defined.
081   In supervised contrastive learning, positive pairs are collected based on some explicit notion of
082   similarity. Sentence-BERT (SBERT) (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) uses a curated dataset of paired
083   texts such as question-answer pairs from Stack Exchange; their most recent model (2021) was
084   trained on over 1 billion of such pairs. Similarly, Sentence-T5 (ST5) (Ni et al., 2021) and Sentence-
085   GPT (SGPT) (Muennighoff, 2022) apply contrastive fine-tuning to T5 (Raffel et al., 2020) and GPT
086   (Radford et al., 2018) models. For academic texts, SPECTER (Cohan et al., 2020) and SciNCL
087
      (Ostendorff et al., 2022) use citing and cited paper abstracts to form positive pairs.
088   In self-supervised contrastive learning, positive pairs are generated automatically from un-paired
089   texts, similar to self-supervised learning in computer vision that relies on data augmentations (Chen
090   et al., 2020). SimCSE (Gao et al., 2021) uses two different dropout patterns to form a positive pair
091   of embeddings. This approach has also been used by Liu et al. (2021) and Yan et al. (2021), who
092   additionally investigate other data augmentation techniques, such as randomly masking a part of the
093
      input text or shuffling tokens. Outputs of two distinct networks can also be used to generate positive
      pairs (Kim et al., 2021; Carlsson et al., 2021). Further, one can use adjacent chunks of a text as
094
      positive pairs; this was applied to train RNN (Logeswaran & Lee, 2018), GPT (Neelakantan et al.,
095
      2022), and BERT models (Giorgi et al., 2021; Izacard et al., 2022). Recently, synthetic generation
096   of positive pairs has been explored leveraging generative LLMs (Zhang et al., 2023). While some
097   recent work studied what happens to the representation during self-supervised fine-tuning (Jung
098   et al., 2024), it focused on narrow supervised evaluation tasks and did not consider unsupervised
099   downstream applications.
100
      In past benchmarks of sentence transformers, models trained in a supervised way have been
101
      shown to outperform the ones trained with self-supervision (Thakur et al., 2021; Muennighoff et al.,
102   2023). For example, SBERT’s latest all-mpnet-base-v2 achieved top results among all mod-
103   els of BERT-base size. On some tasks, it is outperformed by much larger models and by commercial
104   embedding models like text-embedding-3-large from OpenAI and embed-english-v3
105   from Cohere. Benchmarks of sentence transformers use various performance metrics, such as clas-
106   sification accuracy, nearest-neighbor query performance, or approximating ground-truth similarity
107   between sentence pairs (STS benchmarks) (Agirre et al., 2012; Conneau & Kiela, 2018). However,
      some of them are known to yield conflicting conclusions (Muennighoff et al., 2023).

111   3.1   DATASETS AND EVALUATION
112
113
      To study self-supervised learning for sentence embeddings, we investigated how far a pre-trained
      language model could be optimized to produce useful sentence representations for a given dataset.
114
      For that, we chose a base model and fine-tuned it on a specific dataset of interest. Then, we con-
115
      ducted supervised evaluation on that same dataset, predicting class labels that were not used during
116   the fine-tuning.
117
118   As a base model we used MPNet (Song et al., 2020; mpnet-base), following SBERT (Reimers &
119
      Gurevych, 2019; model all-mpnet-base-v2 on HuggingFace). This model has bert-base
      architecture with 110 M parameters and uses 768 embedding dimensions. We used mean pooling
120
      over all tokens to obtain a single 768-dimensional output vector for each input text.
121
122   We performed our fine-tuning experiments on six datasets: the arXiv, bioRxiv, medRxiv, Reddit, and
123   StackExchange datasets from the P2P clustering tasks of the Massive Text Embedding Benchmark
124   (MTEB) (Muennighoff et al., 2023), and the ICLR dataset (González-Márquez & Kobak, 2024).
125
      The datasets differed in the number of samples (18–733 thousand) and classes (26–610; Table S1).
      Four of them comprised scientific abstracts from different disciplines, and the other two consisted
126
      of internet posts. While MTEB datasets have been publicly available and have often been used
127
      for benchmarking, the ICLR dataset has been assembled only more recently, ensuring that it was
128   not part of the training data of any established supervised models (such as SBERT, SPECTER, or
129   SciNCL). This is relevant for fair model comparison, because evaluation data leakage can lead to
130   inflated performance estimates.
131
      As our main evaluation metric we used k-nearest-neighbor (kNN) classification accuracy (k = 10
132
      with Euclidean distance; we obtained similar values using cosine distance, see Table S2). This is
133
      a measure of local coherence: it is high if each paper’s nearest neighbors belong to the same class.
134   This metric is particularly relevant for data exploration applications, e.g. for visualisation or clus-
135   tering, as many unsupervised learning algorithms rely on the kNN graph of the data. In contrast,
136   linear classification accuracy does not convey how suitable an embedding is for data exploration. As
137   an example, an embedding with high linear accuracy but low kNN accuracy (e.g. a single informa-
138   tive dimension and many uninformative ones) would yield poor visualisation and poor clustering,
139   and would not be useful for unsupervised data exploration. kNN accuracy is similar to NN-based
140   retrieval metrics (Muennighoff et al., 2023), but only requires class labels instead of ground-truth
141   neighbors. Additionally, to validate the results obtained using kNN accuracy, we also evaluate the
142
      models on some of the MTEB tasks such as clustering, retrieval, reranking, and STS.
143   Note that we purposefully used the entire dataset first for self-supervised training and later for su-
144   pervised evaluation. As SSL does not have access to class labels, this does not present overfitting
145   issues. For supervised evaluation, we used a 9:1 train/test split (using only labeled data).

361   4.1   S ENTENCE ADAPTATION IS MORE IMPORTANT THAN DOMAIN ADAPTATION
362
363
      When fine-tuning a token-level model with a sentence-level contrastive loss on a specific dataset,
      two distinct mechanisms can contribute to the performance improvement: adaptation to sentence
364
      representation, and domain adaptation. To disentangle contributions of these two potential mecha-
365

420
421   In Section 4.1 we showed that models fine-tuned with cropping augmentations showed good kNN
422   performance on other datasets. To assess their performance on other tasks, we evaluated models fine-
423   tuned on the ICLR dataset on several tasks from the Massive Text Embedding Benchmark (MTEB)
424   (Muennighoff et al., 2023). We selected several clustering, retrieval, reranking, and STS tasks (see
425   Section A.2 for details). Clustering tasks assessed the K-means clustering results in the embedding

      distances.

431   (Table 2). This confirmed that cropping-based fine-tuning produced a sentence-level model that
      showed substantial generalization despite very limited amount of fine-tuning (on ICLR dataset only).

        5            R EPRESENTATION QUALITY ACROSS LAYERS
466
467
        To investigate whether the entire pre-trained MPNet model needed to be adapted during self-
468     supervised fine-tuning, we performed cropping-based fine-tuning on the ICLR dataset while freez-
469     ing the embedding layer and various amounts of initial layers. We observed that the performance
470     rapidly improved with the number of unfrozen layers, and fine-tuning only the last two out of 12
471     layers for one epoch was sufficient to reach almost the same value of kNN classification accuracy
472     as fine-tuning the full model (Figure 5a). Unfreezing additional layers led only to minor further
473     improvements.
474     When all layers were unfrozen, the last few layers underwent the largest change during fine-tuning,
475     while the early layers barely changed, in agreement with previous findings in supervised setting
476     (Merchant et al., 2020; Mosbach et al., 2020). To quantify this, we measured the representation
477     quality after each hidden layer before and after the fine-tuning (Figure 5b). The gap between them
478     was close to zero for early layers and increased towards the last layers. We observed the same effect
479     when fine-tuning MPNet on other datasets (Figure S1).
480     Intriguingly, the representation quality across layers in our fine-tuned model as well as in SBERT
481     formed a U-shaped curve (Figure 5b): before fine-tuning the embedding layer representation had
482     the highest accuracy, and after fine-tuning it was surpassed by only the last two layers. Across
483     other datasets, the shape was different and not always U-shaped (Figure S1), but fine-tuned models
484     always exhibited a steep rise in performance towards layer 12. The randomly initialized models did
485     not exhibit this shape: after SSL training, the performance monotonically increased and plateaued
        half-way through the layers (Figure 5b).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Consistently across all datasets and fine-tuned models, the last layer always gave the best represen-
487   tation (Figures 5b and S1). This differs from what has been observed in computer vision, where
488   the top performance after SSL training typically occurs in one of the hidden layers. Indeed, a
489   common practice in computer vision is to have several fully-connected layers (projection head) be-
490   tween the output representation and the contrastive loss (Chen et al., 2020), which are discarded
491   after SSL training (guillotine regularization) (Bordes et al., 2023). We experimented with adding a
492   one-hidden-layer (768 → 512 → 128) projection head after the average pooling, but this did not
493   consistently affect the quality of the representation, in agreement with Figure 5b.

495   6   D ISCUSSION
496
497   We showed that self-supervised fine-tuning on a minimal amount of data can lead to large im-
498   provements in sentence embedding quality. To this end, we systematically compared different self-
499   supervised augmentation techniques under the exact same training setup and showed that cropping
500   augmentations were much better than dropout augmentations in all evaluations. In fact, cropping
501   augmentations could successfully train a bare embedding layer from scratch to outperform the pre-
502   trained MPNet with dropout fine-tuning. This finding is noteworthy because dropout augmentations
503   of SimCSE (Gao et al., 2021) are arguably the most well-known SSL approach in NLP.
504   Recent benchmarks found that sentence models that underwent supervised contrastive fine-tuning
505   (based on curated datasets of positive pairs) are superior to self-supervised models (Muennighoff
506   et al., 2023). Here we showed that minimal self-supervised training can improve the quality of sen-
507   tence embeddings to approach and in some cases almost match the performance of SOTA supervised
508   models, in only a few minutes of training and using five orders of magnitude less data. That said, we
509   did observe a consistent performance gap between our self-supervised fine-tuning and SOTA models
510
      like SBERT. Whether this gap is due to the supervised signal or rather to the large amounts of data
      that SOTA supervised models are trained on, is a topic for future work.
511
512   Different metrics have been used in the literature to assess the quality of sentence representations,
513   including linear classification accuracy, nearest-neighbor query performance, and approximating
514   ground-truth semantic textual similarity (STS) (Conneau & Kiela, 2018; Muennighoff et al., 2023).
515   We used the kNN accuracy as our main evaluation metric because it is particularly relevant for
516
      downstream applications relying on the kNN graph, such as retrieval, clustering, and visualization.
      Many prior works studying sentence representations only evaluated their models on the STS tasks
517
      (e.g. Gao et al., 2021; Jung et al., 2024), but previous benchmarks (Muennighoff et al., 2023) and
518
      our own results showed that STS performance does not correlate with nearest-neighbor quality. We
519   believe that evaluations similar to kNN accuracy should be adopted in benchmarks due to their
520   relevance in practical applications.
521
522
      While we showed that self-supervised contrastive learning can greatly enhance sentence representa-
      tions, we believe that there is still a large room for improvement. The lesson from computer vision
523
      (Balestriero et al., 2023) as well as our work is that good data augmentations are crucial for the suc-
524
      cess of self-supervised learning. Combining cropping augmentation with more powerful semantic
525   augmentations such as reformulations using generative language models (Jiang et al., 2022; Wang
526   & Dou, 2023; Abaskohi et al., 2023) can offer an interesting avenue for future research.

      A.1   AUGMENTATION AND HYPERPARAMETER CHOICES
759
760   In all experiments reported in this section, we repeated the fine-tuning of MPNet for one epoch on
761   the ICLR dataset, and assessed the final kNN classification accuracy. In each experiment, all other
762   parameters were kept at their default values described in Section 3.2.
763
      We compared four different pooling strategies for forming the final sentence representation: average
764
      pooling, the classification token [CLS], the separation token [SEP] (appended at the end of each
765   input text), and the seventh token (as an example of an arbitrary token number). We obtained the
766   best results using the average pooling and [SEP] token, with the other two options performing less
767   well (Figure S2a).
768
      Many models evaluated in Section 3.4 also showed higher kNN accuracy in the [SEP] token rep-
769
      resentation than using the [CLS] token or average pooling (Table S4). Darcet et al. (2023) have
770
      recently shown in a computer vision setting that additional tokens can be used by the transformer
771   model as ‘registers’ to store high-level features. Our results suggest that the same can happen with
772   language models, since the [SEP] token often serves as a good sentence representation despite not
773   being explicitly used for training.
774
775   Temperature We compared several values of temperature from 0.005 to 5.0, and found that the
776   performance decreased with increasing temperature, with τ = 0.005 and τ = 0.05 yielding similar
777   results (Figure S2a). The value τ = 0.5 used in SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) performed less well.
778
779   Cropping augmentation Our data augmentation consisted of ‘cropping out’ t consecutive sen-
780   tences. We varied the number of consecutive sentences (decreasing the batch size accordingly, to
781   make it fit into the GPU memory) and found that the performance generally decreased with t, with
782   the optimal number being t = 2 (Figure S2b). Note that in our sampling it was possible for the
783   positive pair of text chunks to overlap (but not to coincide exactly).
784
785   Masking augmentation We also experimented with a masking augmentation that replaced a cer-
786   tain fraction of tokens in each input chunk with the BERT’s special [MASK] token. This was done
787   on top of the cropping augmentation. We found that masking led to deterioration of performance
788
      (Figure S2c). Using masking augmentation without cropping (i.e. forming positive pairs by applying
      two different masking patterns to the entire abstract) did not produce competitive results either.
789
790
791
      Learning rate The performance increased with increasing the Adam’s learning rate (Figure S2d),
      until it became too large and the training diverged (η ≥ 5 · 10−4 ). For the bare embedding layer
792
      training, the optimal learning rate was η = 5 · 10−1 .

      A.2   MTEB TASKS
796
      Clustering Each of the used datasets consists of texts and ground-truth class labels for each text.
797   The texts are embedded using the model, and the embedding vectors are clustered using a mini-batch
798   K-means algorithm with batch size b = 32 and K equal to the number of classes. The evaluation
799   score is the so called V -measure of agreement between cluster labels and class labels, which is
800   invariant to the permutation of cluster labels.
801
802   Retrieval Each of the used datasets consists of a corpus of documents, queries, and a mapping
803   from each query to the relevant documents. The documents and queries are embedded using the
804   model, and the aim is to find the relevant documents within the neighborhood of the query in the
805   embedding space. Neighbors are found using cosine similarity, and after ranking them, normalized
806   discounted cumulative gain ( nDCG) at k = 10 nearest neighbors serves as the performance metric.
807
808   Reranking Each of the used datasets consists of query texts and a list of relevant and irrelevant
809   reference texts for each query. They are all embedded with the model, and for each query, the text
      embeddings are ranked based on the cosine similarity to the query embedding. The resulting ranking


                                                      15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      is compared to the ground-truth ranking, scored for each query via mean average precision (MAP)
811   metric, and averaged across all queries.
812
813   STS Each of the used datasets consists of a set of sentence pairs, each pair with a numerical
814   score from 0 to 5 indicating similarity between the two sentences (5 being most similar, and 0
815   most dissimilar). All sentences are embedded with the model, and for each pair, the embedding
816   similarity is computed using cosine similarity. These embedding similarities are then compared
817   against ground-truth similarities using Spearman correlation.
818
      For further details, please refer to the original MTEB publication (Muennighoff et al., 2023).
