009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce FedDES, a performance simulator for Federated Learning (FL) that
012            leverages Discrete Event Simulation (DES) techniques to model key events—such
013            as client updates, communication delays, and aggregation operations—as discrete
014            occurrences in time. This approach accurately captures the runtime features of
015            FL systems, providing a high-fidelity simulation environment that closely mirrors
016            real-world deployments. FedDES incorporates all three known aggregation set-
017            tings: Synchronous (e.g., FedAvg and FedProx), Asynchronous (e.g., FedAsync
018
               and FedFa), and Semi-Asynchronous (e.g., FedBuff and FedCompass). Designed
               to be framework-, dataset-, and model-agnostic, FedDES allows researchers and
019
               developers to explore various configurations without restrictions. Our evaluations
020
               involving over 1,000 clients with heterogeneous computation and communica-
021            tion characteristics demonstrate that FedDES accurately models event distribution
022            and delivers performance estimates within 2% error of real-world measurements.
023            While real-world workloads often take hours to evaluate, FedDES generates de-
024            tailed, timestamped event logs in just few seconds. As a result, FedDES can
025            significantly accelerate FL developing and debugging cycles, enabling develop-
026            ers to rapidly prototype and evaluate algorithms and system designs, bypassing
027            the need for costly, time-consuming real-world deployments. It offers valuable
028            performance insights—such as identifying bottlenecks, stragglers, fault-tolerance
029
               mechanisms, and edge-case scenarios—facilitating the optimization of FL sys-
               tems for efficiency, scalability, and resilience.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a key paradigm in machine learning, primarily driven by
035
      growing concerns over privacy and the dwindling availability of publicly accessible datasets McMa-
036   han et al. (2017); Kairouz et al. (2021). Unlike traditional centralized learning approaches, FL
037   allows multiple clients to collaboratively train a shared model while keeping their local data decen-
038   tralized, addressing critical issues related to privacy and data sovereignty Yang et al. (2019). This
039   decentralized learning approach is gaining widespread adoption across various domains, including
040   healthcare, finance, and mobile applications, where data privacy and security are of paramount im-
041   portance Bonawitz et al. (2019).
042   The field of FL is evolving rapidly, particularly in aggregation strategies. Aggregation strategies
043   are being developed to tackle challenges such as data heterogeneity, fairness, privacy, and robust-
044   ness, creating specialized aggregation techniques Li et al. (2020; 2021a). For instance, algorithms
045   like FedAvg Karimireddy et al. (2020) and FedProx Li et al. (2020) have been introduced to ad-
046   dress issues arising from non-IID data distributions and to improve robustness against stragglers and
047   malicious clients. At the same time, scheduling algorithms are evolving toward semi-synchronous
048   paradigms, which strike a balance between synchronous and asynchronous aggregations Li et al.
049   (2023b); Chen et al. (2020). These semi-synchronous approaches optimize training speed and model
050
      accuracy while maintaining system stability. However, integrating such sophisticated scheduling
      mechanisms can fundamentally alter an FL framework’s workflow, making it challenging to assess
051
      the effectiveness of new designs without comprehensive real-world evaluations.
052
053   In response to the rapidly changing landscape of FL, researchers are increasingly adopting fast-
      prototyping methodologies to iterate and refine their algorithms Li et al. (2019; 2020; 2021b).

080   2.1   FL AGGREGATION S TRATEGIES
081
      Aggregation mechanisms critically influence the performance of FL systems. These mechanisms
082
      are typically classified into three categories:
083
084   Synchronous Aggregation: requires the server to wait for updates from all selected clients before
085   aggregating and updating the global model. This ensures consistency but often leads to delays, es-
086   pecially when slow clients (stragglers) are involved. The most prominent synchronous algorithm,
087
      FedAvg McMahan et al. (2017); Karimireddy et al. (2020), aggregates client updates by computing
      a weighted average based on the number of samples each client holds. While effective for homoge-
088
      neous training environments where each client’s communication and communication resources are
089
      similar, FedAvg suffers from inefficiencies in environments with heterogeneous client resources,
090   as the slowest client limits the overall training time.
091
092   Asynchronous Aggregation: update the global model without waiting for all clients, allowing
093
      faster updates but risking using stale client models. FedAsync Xie et al. (2019) addresses this
      by applying a staleness factor to penalize outdated client updates during aggregation. This method
094
      reduces waiting time, improving resource utilization. However, staleness penalties can cause the
095
      global model to drift away from the local data of slower clients, particularly in non-IID settings,
096   affecting accuracy. FedFa Xu et al. (2024) takes asynchronous updates further by using immediate
097   global updates without waiting for any client group. It mitigates the effects of stale updates with a
098   sliding window aggregation technique, allowing the most recent updates to carry more weight. This
099   approach boosts training speed significantly in heterogeneous environments but requires careful
100   handling of staleness to maintain accuracy in non-IID settings.
101
      Semi-Asynchronous Aggregation: combine aspects of both synchronous and asynchronous meth-
102   ods by grouping clients based on their computational capacity and scheduling updates within these
103   groups. FedBuff Nguyen et al. (2022) introduces a buffering mechanism to temporarily store client
104   updates and aggregate them once conditions are met, balancing update frequency and reducing the
105   staleness of updates. However, managing the buffer requires heuristic tuning of the buffer size. On
106   the other hand, FedCompass Li et al. (2023b) uses a computing power-aware scheduler to dynam-
107   ically assign local steps to clients so that updates are received near synchrony within each group.
      This approach mitigates model staleness while maintaining efficiency, allowing FedCompass to

      achieve faster convergence and higher accuracy in heterogeneous and non-IID settings than fully
109   asynchronous methods.

      2.3   D ISCRETE E VENT-D RIVEN S IMULATION (DES)
125
126
      DES models a system as a series of discrete events, each triggering state changes at specific
127   times Banks (2005); Banks et al. (2010). DES has been widely applied in network simulations
128   and distributed systems, where the timing and order of events are critical. Tools like ROSS Pearce
129   (2002) and SimGrid Casanova (2001) demonstrate how DES can scale to millions of events and en-
130   tities, providing insights into system performance in large-scale distributed environments. Our Fed-
131   DES offers a significantly more scalable alternative by modeling FL workflows as discrete events.
132   By simulating client-server activities and communication as state changes between events, FedDES
133   scales efficiently to tens of thousands of clients while capturing key performance metrics, making it
134   a robust tool for large-scale FL simulations.

137   3.1   C OMMUNICATION -C ENTRIC S TATE M ACHINE FOR FL E VENT M ANAGEMENT
138
139   As illustrated in Figure 1, FedDES models the FL system as a state machine, where states are de-
140   fined by computational workloads (e.g., local training, aggregation) and communication workloads
141
      (e.g., model parameter transmission). Client states track local models, computational capacity, and
      training progress, while the server maintains the global model and monitors client updates. Network
142
      states reflect link bandwidth, latency, and system topology, simulating real-world communication
143
      delays. State transitions are primarily driven by communication events (i.e., red actions in Figure 1,
144   there are other transition events like the blue action, which depends on aggregation strategies), which
145   act as synchronization points between clients and the server. For instance, after receiving the global
146   model, a client transitions into the training state and, upon completion, triggers a communication
147   event to send the updated model back to the server. Once sufficient updates are received upon
148   criteria of different aggregation paradigms, the server transitions into the aggregation state. This
149   communication-centric approach captures FL’s asynchronous, distributed nature, where model ex-
150   changes rather than client computations govern state changes.
151   In FedDES, clients and servers transition between states such as idle, training, communicating, and
152   aggregating, with communication events marking key interactions. This communication-driven de-
153   sign is well-suited for distributed asynchronous environments like FL for several reasons. First, it
154   optimizes computational efficiency by focusing on client-server interactions, bypassing idle times.
155   Second, communication is a natural trigger for state transitions, eliminating the need for global
156   synchronization. Finally, it enables accurate simulation of network effects, such as bandwidth con-
157   straints and delays, which are critical to FL performance.
158   Event execution in FedDES is managed using a priority queue, advancing the simulation clock based
159   on communication events. Key event types include Client-Server Communication, which simulates
160   model transmission and accounts for network delays; Aggregation, where the server updates the
161   global model; and Client Computation, which indirectly triggers communication events. By struc-
      turing the simulation around communication, FedDES efficiently captures the dynamics of large-

181   3.2   F ED DES FOR S YNCHRONOUS , A SYNCHRONOUS , AND S EMI -A SYNCHRONOUS FL
182
183
      In FedDES, the server and the set of clients are implemented as discrete event-driven actors, where
      the server initiates the global model broadcast and manages client updates. Each client executes
184
      local training and communicates with the server. These actors are instantiated on separate virtual
185
      nodes to simulate distributed environments and communicate through a simulated network. The
186   simulation is launched by creating the server actor on a central node and distributing the client
187   actors across multiple nodes, reflecting real-world deployment. FedDES’s simulation engine then
188   handles the scheduling and execution of events for each actor.
189
      Once the server and clients are launched, FedDES tracks each actor’s state and manages their
190
      interactions using events. Each actor is responsible for processing its assigned tasks, such as
191
      communication, computation, and aggregation, based on the type of FL algorithm being simu-
192   lated—synchronous, asynchronous, or semi-asynchronous. The following algorithms illustrate how
193   these paradigms are modeled in FedDES.
194
195
      FedDES-Avg (Synchronous): FedAvg is a standard synchronous algorithm where the server waits
      for all clients to complete training before aggregation. In FedDES-Avg (Algorithm 1), the server
196
      distributes the global model (Event 1), clients perform local training (Event 2), and send updates
197
      back to the server (Event 3). Aggregation occurs once all updates are received (Event 4). Fed-
198   DES introduces jitter and stragglers by introducing Gaussian (or can be any, depending on system
199   characteristics) probability distribution for client computation and communication times, simulating
200   real-world conditions.
201
      FedDES-Async (Asynchronous): In FedAsync, the server aggregates updates as soon as they
202
      arrive, without waiting for all clients. FedDES-Async (Algorithm 2) models this by having the
203
      server send the global model individually (Event 1). Clients perform training (Event 2), send updates
204   (Event 3), and the server aggregates them on receipt (Event 4). Termination signals are sent when the
205   simulation completes (Event 5). FedDES captures the impact of stale updates by modeling delays
206   and jitters, revealing how asynchrony affects convergence and performance.
207
      FedDES-Compass (Semi-Asynchronous): FedCompass groups clients dynamically based on
208
      computational capacities, synchronizing updates within each group. FedDES-Compass (Algo-
209
      rithm 3) starts with the server broadcasting the global model to all clients (Event 1). Once a group
210   completes training and sends updates (Event 3), the server aggregates the updates for that group
211   (Event 4). Groups operate asynchronously relative to one another, balancing the efficiency of asyn-
212   chronous updates with the consistency of synchronous aggregation. FedDES introduces varied de-
213   lays across groups based on the compass scheduler’s logic, simulating differences in computational
214   speeds and network conditions. Although specific grouping and scheduling strategies may differ
215   across semi-asynchronous algorithms, FedDES’s flexible semi-async design abstracts the high-level
      grouping concept and can be easily adapted to other semi-asynchronous strategies.

      5     E VALUATION
379
380
      This section evaluates FedDES’ accuracy by comparing the simulation’s event distributions against
381
      real-world experimental results. We aim to determine how well the simulated FL events reflect the
382   system dynamics compared to a real FL environment.
383
384   5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
385
      Testbed and Software Settings: We use a Vision Transformer (ViT) Alexey (2020) on the parti-
386   tioned CIFAR-10 Krizhevsky et al. (2009) dataset for both real-world and simulated FL experiments.
387   The real-world FL experiments involve more than 1,000 clients and three aggregation strategies: Fe-
388   dAvg, FedAsync, and FedCompass. Our FedDES simulations mirror these strategies using the class
389   partitioning method. The real experiments are performed on the NCSA Delta High-Performance
390   Computing (HPC) system, while the simulations run on a server equipped with two NVIDIA A40
391   GPUs and an Intel(R) Xeon(R) Gold 6336Y CPU. The real-world experiment captures communica-
392   tion events across multiple clients during an FL task, while the same FL task is simulated through
393   FedDES with identical client configurations, communication steps, and task settings. In both the
394
      simulation and experiment, events are logged with timestamps, indicating when clients send or re-
      ceive models and when the server performs aggregation.
395
396   Metric Settings We assess the similarity between the event distributions of real-world FL exper-
397   iments and FedDES simulations across three dimensions: time, communication steps, and client
398   ID. Evaluating the similarity between actual and simulated event distributions in only one or two
399   dimensions (e.g., time alone or step-wise comparisons) would fail to capture the full complexity of
400
      the system. By analyzing the distribution of events across all three dimensions—time, step, and
      client ID—we ensure a more holistic and accurate comparison of the behavior between real-world
401
      experiments and simulations. The metrics we employ for this 3D evaluation are:
402
403   Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence Kullback & Leibler (1951): This metric measures how much the
404   simulated distribution deviates from the real distribution across the three dimensions. A lower value
405   indicates a higher similarity between the real and simulated data.
406   Jensen-Shannon (JS) Divergence Lin (1991): This symmetric measure compares the real and simu-
407   lated distributions over time, step, and client ID. It captures the overall similarity of the distributions
408   in three dimensions.
409
      Bhattacharyya Distance Bhattacharyya (1943): The Bhattacharyya distance measures the overlap
410   between the real and simulated distributions in the three-dimensional space of time, communication
411   steps, and client ID. It is particularly sensitive to differences in distribution shape and spread.
412
413
      By evaluating these metrics in three dimensions, we ensure that the simulation’s accuracy is assessed
      not only with respect to time but also to the sequence of communication steps and the behavior of
414
      individual clients. This approach allows us to capture the full complexity of the system dynamics
415
      and ensures that the simulation faithfully reproduces the interaction patterns observed in real-world
416   federated learning environments.

531   6                     C ONCLUSION
532
533   FedDES provides an efficient, scalable solution for simulating large-scale FL systems using Discrete
534   Event Simulation (DES). By modeling client selection, training, communication, and aggregation
535   as discrete events, it enables precise analysis of various FL strategies, including synchronous, asyn-
536   chronous, and semi-asynchronous paradigms. FedDES is framework-agnostic, allowing researchers
537   to integrate custom datasets and models, while accurately simulating heterogeneous client behaviors
538   and network conditions. Evaluations on over 1,000 clients show FedDES delivers high accuracy,
539
      with less than 2% error compared to real-world experiments. This makes FedDES a powerful tool
      for debugging, performance analysis, and prototyping new FL algorithms.
