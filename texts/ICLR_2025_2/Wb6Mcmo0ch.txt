011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            While Large language models (LLMs) have advanced natural language process-
014            ing tasks, their growing computational and memory demands make deployment
015            on resource-constrained devices like mobile phones increasingly challenging. In
016            this paper, we propose SHARP (SHaring Adjacent Layers with Recovery Parame-
017            ters), a novel approach to accelerate LLM inference by sharing parameters across
018            adjacent layers, thus reducing memory load overhead, while introducing low-rank
019            recovery parameters to maintain performance. Inspired by observations that con-
020
               secutive layers have similar outputs, SHARP employs a two-stage recovery pro-
               cess: Single Layer Warmup (SLW), and Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT). The SLW
021
               stage aligns the outputs of the shared layers using L2 loss, providing a good ini-
022
               tialization for the following SFT stage to further restore the model performance.
023            Extensive experiments demonstrate that SHARP can recover the model’s perplex-
024            ity on various in-distribution tasks using no more than 50k fine-tuning data while
025            reducing the number of stored MLP parameters by 38% to 65%. We also conduct
026            several ablation studies of SHARP and show that replacing layers towards the later
027            parts of the model yields better performance retention, and that different recovery
028            parameterizations perform similarly when parameter counts are matched. Further-
029            more, SHARP saves 42.8% in model storage and reduces the total inference time
030            by 42.2% compared to the original Llama2-7b model on mobile devices. Our
031
               results highlight SHARP as an efficient solution for reducing inference costs in
               deploying LLMs without the need for pretraining-scale resources.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Following the principles of scaling laws, large language models (LLMs) have become one of the
037   central topics in Natural Language Processing (NLP) (Brown, 2020; Zhang et al., 2022; Hoffmann
038   et al., 2022; Bubeck et al., 2023; Chowdhery et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Touvron
039
      et al., 2023). However, deploying a pre-trained large language model requires significant computa-
      tional and memory resources (Aminabadi et al., 2022; Pope et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023b; Zhang
040
      et al., 2024b), which may further restrict their inference speed. For instance, a 70-billion-parameter
041
      language model stored in FP16 precision requires approximately 148GB of memory to hold the
042   model weights, necessitating two A100 GPUs with 80GB of memory each to load the entire model.
043   During inference, the entire input sequence and the KV cache are also stored on the GPU, incur-
044   ring additional memory usage. Although techniques like layer-wise inference (HuggingFace, 2022),
045   which load the model to GPU layer by layer, enable LLM inference on a single GPU, they introduce
046   additional inference latency due to frequent memory loading or disk reading. In particular, these
047   concerns are significant for deployment on mobile devices, which typically have smaller DRAM
048   (e.g., around 6GB in the iPhone 15) and higher communication overhead (Liu et al., 2024).
049   To alleviate these issues, several methods have been carefully explored. One direction is to opti-
050   mize the calculation process of the attention mechanism and the storage of the KV cache, including
051   Reformer (Kitaev et al., 2020), Flash Attention (Dao et al., 2022; Dao, 2023; Shah et al., 2024),
052   H2 O (Zhang et al., 2024b), and so on. Another main direction is to compress the existing model
053   while retaining model performance, including quantization (Dettmers et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023a;
      Kang et al., 2024), pruning (?Sun et al., 2023), and sparsification (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Dong &

083   In this paper, we focus on a new methodology for efficient inference on current pretrained models,
084   named the adjacent layer-sharing strategy. It is partially inspired by the observation from Deja
085   Vu (Liu et al., 2023b): in Figure 5(a) and (b) of their paper, they show that the cosine similarity be-
086   tween representations at two consecutive layers, or even a few layers apart, can be very high (greater
087
      than 95%), which implies that the model outputs between layers may be similar. This suggests that
      we can save inference time by sharing parameters between layers to reduce communication over-
088
      head. A related algorithm, the “immediate block-wise weight sharing” strategy proposed recently by
089
      MobileLLM (Liu et al., 2024), also supports this idea. They share the weights between two adjacent
090   layers to avoid frequent parameter movement in memory (Figure 1(b)) and then train a new small
091   language model. Note that for mobile devices, the communication overhead in memory accounts
092   for a major proportion of the latency overhead, therefore, they double the depth of the new model
093   and obtain better downstream performance, but only increase a negligible additional inference time.
094   However, although MobileLLM achieves significant improvements in accelerating model inference
095   on mobile devices, they focus only on training a new model from scratch and do not fit our purpose
096   of deploying pretrained models through a more resource-saving post-training process.
097   Our Contributions. To apply the layer-sharing strategy to existing LLMs, in this paper, we propose
098   a new layer-sharing algorithm named SHARP (SHaring Adjacent layers with Recovery Parameters),
099   which uses additional low-rank weights to predict subsequent layers, and thus save the memory load
100   overhead of the predicted layers. We summarize our contributions as follows.
101
      • First, we show that language models are robust to the replacement of adjacent or even later MLP
102     layers, which further supports the insight of the layer-sharing strategy. Further, we find that the
103     current layer can be a good approximation of later layers if we add some additional LoRA (Hu
104     et al., 2021) parameters and fine-tune them on a high-quality dataset (Figure 2). We also note that
105     although the outputs of the adjacent layers are similar, their parameters differ a lot. (Section 2.1).
106   • Second, based on these observations, we propose our SHARP algorithm (Figure 1 (d)). Given the
107     current layer, we use candidate transformations, such as LoRA addition, to predict the parameters
        of the next several layers using recovery parameters, which reduces memory load overhead and

192   In this section, we illustrate how we find the proper additional parameters for predicting each layer
193   in SHARP. First, we introduce the notations and the settings used in our paper.

195   2.2.1     P RELIMINARY
196
      We assume the original parameters of a particular function (for example, the gate projection of MLP
197
      layers) at i-th layer as Θi ∈ Rd1 ×d2 (i ∈ [N ]), where d1 and d2 are the input/output dimension of
198
      the function, and we freeze their gradients during training. We denote the low-rank learnable LoRA
199   parameters at i-th layer as Ai ∈ Rd1 ×r , Bi ∈ Rr×d2 . And we let f (· ; Θi ) denote the particular
200   function that utilizes Θi as parameters. In this paper, we focus on reducing the parameters in MLP
201   layers, which take up the main parts of the parameters in the intermediate layers. And we use
202   Llama2-7b (Touvron et al., 2023) as our basic model.
203
      To accurately illustrate the replacement, we denote J := {j1 , . . . , jK } ⊂ [N ] as the reference layer
204
      sets2 . And for each jk ∈ J , we define a corresponding continuous sequence named target layer
205   sets Tjk = [jk + 1, . . . , jk′ ] where jk′ ∈ [jk + 1, jk+1 − 1]. We also denote g to be the candidate
206   transformation function (as in Figure 1 (d)). Our goal is to use each reference layer j ∈ J to
207   approximate every later target layer l ∈ Tj with some learnable low-rank parameters, i.e., aiming to
208   find some ∆Θl , such that f (· ; g(Θj , ∆Θl )) performs like f (· ; Θl ). We note that only the reference
209   layer will be loaded and stored, and target layers will be obtained using the corresponding reference
210   layer combining low-rank additional parameters on the fly. In this way, we trade cheaper parameter
211   computation for more expensive memory loading.

227   2.2.2   SHARP A LGORITHM
228
      In this part, we show how we achieve SHARP (Figure 1 (d)) algorithm. First, we illustrate why we
229   choose such a two-stage algorithm for recovering.
230
231   Why Two-Stage? A natural way to recover the model performance is to directly finetune the model
232
      end-to-end for all learnable low-rank additional parameters. In general, it works fine when we try
      to use one layer to replace the next layer, which at most gives a 50% reduction of the MLP layers.
233
      However, if we want to use one layer to compute multiple adjacent layers, just using the SFT stage
234
      will require more data for recovering, and result in a much slower convergence rate and worse
235   final result. The detailed discussion of this has been investigated in Section 3.3.3 and Table 3, and
236   the intuition for this phenomenon may be that when we use one layer to replace multiple layers
237   aggressively, the model loses too many parameters and thus start optimizing from an initialization
238   point that is far from the optimal solution. Therefore, we need first to align the output of the predicted
239   and original target layers before the SFT stage. Details of the algorithm are as follows.
240
      Stage 1: Single Layer Warmup (SLW). First, we minimize the L2 loss between the output of
241   layer predicted by the reference layer and that of the original target layer by finetuning the model on
242   high-quality data. Formally, for each reference layer j ∈ J and every target layer predicted by this
243   reference layer l ∈ Tj , we want to find:

296   3     E XPERIMENTS
297
      In the experiment section, we mainly focus on four parts: (E1) In-distribution recovery tasks, where
298   we apply SHARP to the pretrained model, finetune it on a specific high-quality dataset, and then
299   evaluate the model’s perplexity on the same dataset. (E2) Ablation study, where we investigate how
300   to choose better replacement types and candidate transformations. We also try to analyze how the
301   single-layer warmup stage and different settings influence the model recovery, and want to see how
302   aggressively we can choose the replacement type. (E3) Downstream evaluation, where we assess
303   the model’s performance on several widely-used downstream tasks. (E4) Latency analysis, where
304   we examine how much inference acceleration SHARP can achieve.

530   4                           C ONCLUSION
531
      This paper presented SHARP, a method to accelerate large language model inference by sharing ad-
532
      jacent layers with recovery parameters. SHARP effectively reduces model size and inference time
533
      by using the reference layer to predict the later layers, which saves the memory load overhead. It
534   recovers the model performance through a two-stage process: Single Layer Warmup and Supervised
535   Fine-Tuning. Experimental results demonstrate that SHARP achieves comparable perplexity to orig-
536   inal models across various in-distribution tasks. By minimizing the number of layers and parameters
537   needed, SHARP provides a practical solution for deploying large models in resource-constrained en-
538   vironments. We believe this method can further enlighten researchers in designing advanced layer-
539   sharing methods for accelerating inference and may be insightful for the interpretability-related
      works on understanding how each layer works in LLM.

974
       Model compression Model compression is a classical way to improve inference efficiency by
975
       either reducing the number of model parameters or lowering the memory required to store them.
976    Three widely used techniques—sparsity and pruning, quantization, and distillation—are central to
977    this goal. Sparsity and pruning share the aim of reducing the number of effective parameters, but
978    differ in approach: sparsity reduces individual weights to zero in an unstructured manner (Sun
979    et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2023a; Frantar & Alistarh, 2023), while pruning takes a structured approach
980    by removing entire components, such as neurons or filters, from the network (Xia et al., 2023b).
981    Quantization reduces the memory footprint by lowering the precision of weights and activations,
982    without changing the number of parameters (Dettmers et al., 2024; 2022; Li et al., 2023a; Kim et al.,
983    2023a; Frantar et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023a; Frantar et al., 2022;
984
       Zhang et al., 2018). While sparsity, pruning, and quantization are usually applied after a certain
       amount of training, distillation is a data-centric methodology used during training. In distillation,
985
       a smaller student model is trained using both the original training data and the output (soft labels)
986
       of a larger teacher model, allowing the student model to retain much of the teacher’s performance
987    while being more efficient (Hinton, 2015; Timiryasov & Tastet, 2023; Chen et al., 2024). These
988    three categories represent the most classical methods for model compression, primarily aimed at
989    improving inference efficiency. However, there are other compression methods closely related to
990    our proposed techniques, which will be discussed in detail later.
991
992    Low rank approximation Low-rank approximation, while distinct from the traditional model
993    compression techniques discussed earlier, leverages the observation that much of the key informa-
994    tion users care about in a neural network can be represented in a lower-dimensional subspace. By
995    approximating large weight matrices with low-rank representations, both the number of parameters
996    and computational costs are reduced. Many works such as Li et al. (2023b); Hsu et al. (2022); Ha-
997    jimolahoseini et al. (2021); Tahaei et al. (2021) focus on improving inference efficiency using this
998
       method, but it can also offer significant efficiency gains during training. LoRA (Low-Rank Adapta-
       tion) by Hu et al. (2021) is the first work to introduce two small low-rank matrices, A and B attached
999
       to a frozen pre-trained weight matrix W , allowing for efficient fine-tuning with minimal memory
1000
       usage. Since then, numerous variants have been developed to enhance this approach (Dettmers et al.,
1001   2022; Sheng et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023). Our proposed methods are similarly
1002   inspired by low-rank approximation, but unlike other works that focus on decomposing the entire
1003   weight matrix, we use low-rank approximations to estimate the minimal differences between inter-
1004   mediate layers. This allows for maximal weight sharing, significantly reducing redundancy while
1005   maintaining performance.
1006
1007   Weight sharing Weight sharing is another powerful model compression technique that improves
1008   both training and inference efficiency by reducing the number of unique parameters in a neural net-
1009   work. Classical weight sharing involves using a common representation space across multiple tasks
1010   or domains, allowing models to generalize better while using fewer parameters (Liu et al., 2020;
1011   Jiang et al., 2019; Tars & Fishel, 2018; Fu et al., 2021). Those embedding sharing architectures
1012
       have been later adopted in Llama (Touvron et al., 2023) and OPT models (Zhang et al., 2022). How-
       ever the savings from embedding sharing diminished with the increasing model size, and therefore
1013
       been disregarded in recent designs of LLMs. Recently, MobileLLM(Liu et al., 2024) introduced
1014
       the first approach to weight sharing between intermediate layers, enabling models to reuse learned
1015   representations across layers. This significantly reduces the parameter count while maintaining per-
1016   formance, making large models more feasible for resource-constrained environments. Our proposed
1017   methods are inspired by this concept, but further integrate weight sharing with low-rank approxima-
1018   tion to achieve both computational efficiency and performance preservation during the fine-tuning
1019   stage.

1021   tectures have significantly increased model sizes. Models that were previously considered large are
1022   now categorized as small relative to the current state-of-the-art. Commonly, models with fewer than
1023   7 billion parameters (7B) are referred to as small models. Notably, prominent open-source language
1024   models under 7B parameters include Mistral 7B (Jiang et al., 2023); Phi-3 series (Abdin et al.,
1025   2024); Gemma 2B (Team et al., 2023), Llama 3.2 series (Dubey et al., 2024), TinyLlama(Zhang
       et al., 2024a), MobileLLM(Liu et al., 2024) and MiniCPM(Hu et al., 2024). Despite their smaller

1163

1164
                 than reusing them as SHARP.

1166
               • LLM-Pruner (Ma et al., 2023), another advanced structural pruning algorithm. It uses
                 gradient information to divide the parameters into several groups based on their relevance,
1167

                 Here for fair comparison, we also let LLM-Pruner remove about 50% of the MLP layers
1169             except the first two and last one layers, and use the same LoRA ranks for recovering.
1170             Besides, we find that LLM-Pruner is not that stable in the in-distribution recovery tasks,
1171             even when we try the default LoRA rank and prune both the self-attention layer and MLPs
1172             together. So we report the best result in the entire finetuning process before it becomes
1173             unstable.
