009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper introduces an empirical risk minimization based approach with con-
012            comitant scaling, which eliminates the need for tuning a robustification parameter
013            in the presence of heavy-tailed data. This method leverages a new loss function that
014            concurrently optimizes both the mean and robustification parameters. Through this
015            dual-parameter optimization, the robustification parameter automatically adjusts
016            to the unknown data variance, rendering the method self-tuning. Our approach
017            surpasses previous models in both computational and asymptotic efficiency. No-
018
               tably, it avoids the reliance on cross-validation or Lepski’s method for tuning the
               robustification parameter, and the variance of our estimator attains the Cramér-Rao
019
               lower bound, demonstrating optimal efficiency. In essence, our approach demon-
020
               strates optimal performance across both finite-sample and large-sample scenarios, a
021            feature we describe as algorithmic adaptivity to both asymptotic and finite-sample
022            regimes. Numerical studies lend strong support to our methodology.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   The success of many statistical and learning methods heavily relies on the assumption of sub-Gaussian
028   errors (Wainwright, 2019). A random variable Z is considered to have sub-Gaussian tails if there
029   exist constants c1 and c2 such that P(|Z − EZ| > t) ≤ c1 exp(−c2 t2 ) for any t ≥ 0. However,
030   in many practical applications, data are often collected with a high degree of noise. For instance,
031
      in the context of gene expression data analysis, it has been observed that certain gene expression
      levels exhibit kurtoses much larger than 3, regardless of the normalization method used (Wang et al.,
032
      2015). Furthermore, a recent study on functional magnetic resonance imaging (Eklund et al., 2016)
033
      demonstrates that the principal cause of invalid functional magnetic resonance imaging inferences is
034   that the data do not follow the assumed Gaussian shape. It is therefore important to develop robust
035   and efficient statistical methods with desirable statistical performance in the presence of heavy-tailed
036   data, which refer to data with only finite variances.
037
      This paper focuses on mean estimation problems with potentially heavy-tailed data, which serves as
038
      the foundation for tackling more general problems. Specifically, we consider a generative model for

073   using the adaptive Huber estimator (Sun et al., 2020; Avella-Medina et al., 2018) to estimate each
074   entry of a d × d covariance matrix, up to O(d2 ) tuning parameters can be involved. Utilizing cross-
075

      increases. To mitigate these computational barriers, median-of-means (MoM) techniques (Devroye
076
      et al., 2016; Lugosi & Mendelson, 2019b;a; Lecué & Lerasle, 2020) can be used to construct robust
077
      and tuning-free estimators; see Section 3.2. However, based on our experience, MoM typically
078   underperforms numerically compared to ERM-based estimators. An asymptotic analysis reveals
079   that the relative efficiency of the MoM estimator, compared to a fully efficient estimator, is only
080   2/π ≈ 0.64. These observations prompt a pertinent question:
081
082            Is it possible to develop computationally and statistically effcient robust estimators
083            for data with finite and unknown variances?
084
      In response, this paper introduces a robust empirical risk minimization approach with concomitant
085
      scaling. We utilize a new loss function that is smooth with respect to both the mean and robustification
086   parameters. By joint optimizing these parameters, we prove that the resulting robustification parameter
087   can automatically adapt to the unknown variance, enabling the resulting mean estimator to achieve
088   sub-Gaussian accuracy up to logarithmic terms. Thus, our approach eliminates the need for cross-
089   validation or Lepski’s method for tuning, significantly enhancing the computational efficiency of
090   robust data analysis in practical settings. Moreover, from an asymptotic viewpoint, we establish that
091   our proposed estimator is asymptotically efficient, achieving the Cramér-Rao lower bound (Van der
092   Vaart, 2000). In essence, our approach demonstrates optimal performance across both finite-sample
093   and large-sample scenarios, a feature we describe as algorithmic adaptivity to both asymptotic and
094
      finite-sample regimes.

465   5                        C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
466
467   In summary, the most attractive feature of our method is its self-tuning property, incurring much
468   lower computational cost than cross-validation and Lepski’s method. This is particularly important
469   for large-scale inference with a myriad of parameters to be tuned. Statistically, our estimator is as
470   (statistically) efficient as the sample mean estimator for normal distributions and more efficient than
471   popular robust alternatives for asymmetric and/or heavy-tailed distributions.
472
473   Limitation One limitation of our self-tuned estimator is that its finite-sample performance depends
474   on unknown constants, making it challenging to compute the sample complexity in advance for a fixed
475   confidence level. Another limitation of this study is its scope. We primarily focus on robust mean
476   estimators as they represent the simplest case, and the proofs are already quite complex. Nevertheless,
477   our approach can potentially extend to more general settings, such as regression and matrix estimation
478   problems.
