009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We present a constructive universal approximation theorem for learning machines
012            equipped with joint-group-equivariant feature maps, based on the group represen-
013            tation theory. “Constructive” here indicates that the distribution of parameters is
014            given in a closed-form expression known as the ridgelet transform. Joint-group-
015            equivariance encompasses a broad class of feature maps that generalize classical
016            group-equivariance. Notably, this class includes fully-connected networks, which
017            are not group-equivariant but are joint-group-equivariant. Moreover, our main
018            theorem also unifies the universal approximation theorems for both shallow and
019
               deep networks. While the universality of shallow networks has been investigated
               in a unified manner by the ridgelet transform, the universality of deep networks
020
               has been investigated in a case-by-case manner.
021

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   The proof of a universality theorem contains hints for understanding the internal data processing
026   mechanisms inside learning machines such as neural networks. For example, the first universality
027   theorems for depth-2 neural networks were shown in 1989 with four different proofs by Cybenko
028   (1989), Hornik et al. (1989), Funahashi (1989), and Carroll & Dickinson (1989). Among them, Cy-
029   benko’s proof using Hahn-Banach and Hornik et al.’s proof using Stone-Weierstrass are existential
030   proofs, meaning that it is not clear how to assign the parameters. On the other hand, Funahashi’s
031   proof reducing to the Fourier transform and Carroll and Dickinson’s proof reducing to the Radon
032
      transform are constructive proofs, meaning that it is clear how to assign the parameters. The lat-
      ter constructive methods, which reduce to integral transforms, were refined as the so-called integral
033
      representation by Barron (1993) and further culminated as the ridgelet transform, the main objective
034
      of this study, discovered by Murata (1996) and Candès (1998).
035
036   To show the universality in a constructive manner, we formulate the the problem as a functional
037   equation: Let LM[γ] denote a certain learning machine (such as a deep network) with parameter γ,
038
      and let F denote a class of functions to be expressed by the learning machine. Given a function
      f ∈ F, find an unknown parameter γ so that the machine LM[γ] represents function f , i.e.
039
040                                                 LM[γ] = f,                                            (1)
041
      which we call a learning equation. This equation is understood as a stronger formulation of
042   learning than an ordinary formulation by the empirical risk minimization such as minimizing
043
      P  n                         2
         i=1 |LM[γ](xi ) − f (xi )| with respect to γ, as the latter is understood as a weak form (or a varia-
044   tional form) of this equation. Therefore, characterizing the solution space of this equation leads to
045   understanding the parameters obtained by risk minimization. Following previous studies (Murata,
046   1996; Candès, 1998; Sonoda et al., 2021a;b; 2022a;b), we call a solution operator R that satisfies
047   LM[R[f ]] = f a ridgelet transform. Once such a solution operator R is found, we can conclude a uni-
048   versality of the learning machine in consideration because the reconstruction formula LM[R[f ]] = f
049   implies for any f ∈ F there exists a machine that represents f . In particular, when R[f ] is found
050
      in a closed-form manner, then it leads to a constructive proof of the universality since R[f ] could
      indicate how to assign parameters.
051
052   For depth-2 neural networks (particularly with an infinitely-wide hidden layer), the equation has
053   been solved with several closed-form ridgelet transforms. For example, the closed-form ridgelet
      transforms have been obtained for depth-2 fully-connected layers (Sonoda et al., 2021b), depth-2

      fully-connected layers on manifolds (Sonoda et al., 2022b), depth-2 group convolution layers (Son-
055   oda et al., 2022a), and depth-2 fully-connected layers on finite fields (Yamasaki et al., 2023). The
056   essential technique to obtain these ridgelet transforms are to construct a Fourier expression corre-

060   risk minimization assymptotically converges to the ridgelet transform. In other words, the ridgelet
061   transform can also explain the solutions obtained by risk minimization.
062   On the other hand, for depth-n neural networks, the equation is far from solved, and it is common
063   to either consider infinitely-deep mathematical models such as Neural ODEs (Sonoda & Murata,
064   2017b; E, 2017; Li & Hao, 2018; Haber & Ruthotto, 2017; Chen et al., 2018), or handcraft solutions
065   depending on the network specifications. For example, construction methods such as the so-called
066   Telgarsky sawtooth function (or the Yarotsky scheme) and bit extraction techniques (Cohen et al.,
067   2016; Telgarsky, 2016; Yarotsky, 2017; 2018; Yarotsky & Zhevnerchuk, 2020; Daubechies et al.,
068   2022; Cohen et al., 2022; Siegel, 2023; Petrova & Wojtaszczyk, 2023; Grohs et al., 2023) have been
069
      developed (not only to investigate the expressivity but also) to demonstrate the depth separation,
      super-convergence, and minmax optimality of deep ReLU networks. Various feature maps have also
070
      been handcrafted in the contexts of geometric deep learning (Bronstein et al., 2021) and deep narrow
071
      networks (Lu et al., 2017; Hanin & Sellke, 2017; Lin & Jegelka, 2018; Kidger & Lyons, 2020; Park
072   et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023; Cai, 2023; Kim et al., 2024). However, for the purpose of understanding
073   the parameters obtained by risk minimization (in a manner presented by Sonoda et al. (2021a)),
074   these results are less satisfactory because there is no guarantee that these handcrafted solutions are
075   obtaiend by risk minimization.
076
      Recently, Sonoda et al. (2024a) developed a novel technique to show the universality based on the
077
      group representation theory, and discovered a rich class of ridgelet transforms for learning machines
078   with joint-group-invariant feature maps. However, their technique was essentially limited to depth-
079   2 networks and could not cover depth-n networks defined by composites of nonlinear activation
080   functions such as σ(A2 σ(A1 x − b1 ) − b2 ). By carefully reviewing their group theoretic arguments,
081   we found that the joint-invariance is the bottleneck and it can be resolved by relaxing the assumption
082   to the joint-equivariance. In this study, we present a wider class of ridgelet transforms for learning
083   machines with joint-equivariant feature maps so to cover the depth-n (as well as depth-2) fully-
084   connected networks.
085   The contributions of this study include
086
087          • We derived the ridgelet transform (solution operator for the learning equation) for learning
088            machines with depth-n joint-group-equivariant feature maps. Since the solution of the
089            learning equation can be written in closed form for any f ∈ F, it is a constructive and
090            unified proof of the universal approximation theorem for joint-group-equivariant machines.
091          • As a corollary, we have shown the constructive universal approximation property of deep
092            fully-connected neural networks. Until this study, the universality of deep networks has
093            been shown in a different manner from the universality of shallow networks, but our results
094            discuss them on common ground. Now we can understand the approximation schemes of
095            various learning machines in a unified manner.
096          • In addition, as an example of a learning machine whose universality has not been known,
097            we presented a network with quadratic forms and showed its universality.
098
             • Further, we have shown the ridgelet transform for depth-n group convolutional networks.
099
               In a previous study, it was known only for depth-2, and we have succeeded to extend it by
100
               reviewing the arguments from the group theoretic perspective.

159   2.2   I RREDUCIBLE U NITARY R EPRESENTATION AND S CHUR ’ S L EMMA
160
161   In the main theorem, we use Schur’s lemma, a fundamental theorem from unitary group representa-
      tion theory. Group representation is a method for investigating properties of an abstract group G by


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      mapping G to another (much computable) group of invertible linear operators. We refer to Folland
163   (2015) for more details on group representation and harmonic analysis on groups.
164
165   In this study, we assume group G to be locally compact. This is a sufficient condition for having
166
      invariant measures. It is not a strong assumption. For example, any finite group, discrete group,
      compact group, and finite-dimensional Lie group are locally compact, while an infinite-dimensional
167
      Lie group is not locally compact.
168
169   Let H be a nonzero Hilbert space, and U(H) be the group of unitary operators on H. A unitary
170   representation π of G on H is a group homomorphism that is continuous with respect to the strong
171   operator topology—that is, a map π : G → U(H) satisfying πgh = πg πh and πg−1 = πg−1 , and for
172   any ψ ∈ H, the map G ∋ g 7→ πg [ψ] ∈ H is continuous.
173   Suppose M is a closed subspace of H. M is called an invariant subspace when πg [M] ⊂ M for all
174   g ∈ G. Particularly, π is called irreducible when it does not admit any nontrivial invariant subspace
175   M=  ̸ {0} nor H. The following theorem is a fundamental result of group representation theory that
176   characterizes the irreducibility.
177   Theorem 2 (Schur’s lemma). A unitary representation π : G → U(H) is irreducible iff any bounded
178   operator T on H that commutes with π is always a constant multiple of the identity. In other words,
179   if πg ◦ T = T ◦ πg for all g ∈ G, then T = c IdH for some c ∈ C.
180
181   See Folland (2015, Theorem 3.5(a)) for the proof. We use this as a key step in the proof of our main
182
      theorem.
183   As a concrete example of an irreducible representation, we use the following regular representation
184   of the affine group Aff(m) on L2 (Rm ).
185   Theorem 3. Let G := Aff(m) := GL(m) ⋉ Rm be the affine group acting on X = Rm by
186   (L, t) · x = Lx + t, and let H := L2 (Rm ) be the Hilbert space of square-integrable functions. Let
187   π : Aff(m) → U(L2 (Rm )) be the regular representation of the affine group Aff(m) on L2 (Rm ),
188   namely πg [f ](x) := | det L|−1/2 f (L−1 (x − t)) for any g = (L, t) ∈ G. Then π is irreducible.
189
190   See Folland (2015, Theorem 6.42) for the proof.

261

262   equivariance is a special case of joint-G-equivariance where G acts trivially on parameter domain,

405   deal with deep structures or composite maps and, therefore, falls short as a theoretical analysis

407   irreducible representations (or irreps, for short) for deep structures. More technically, it is due to
408   the facts (1) that an inner tensor product π1 ⊗i π2 of irreps π1 , π2 is not always irreducible (see
409   e.g. Chapter 7.3 Folland, 2015), while (2, or Lemma 4) that an outer tensor product π1 ⊗ π2 or
410   irreps π1 , π2 is always irreducible. The two facts (1) and (2) appear similar (thus confusing) but the
411   essential consequences are different. Further, (3) that deep feature maps are often vector-valued, but
412   (4) that the previous study is limited to scalar-valued joint-invariant feature maps. In the previous
413
      study, due to (1) and (4), it is technically hard to obtain an irrep for vector-valued feature maps.
      Namely, just a d-times inner tensor product πs ⊗i · · · ⊗i πs of irrep πs acting on scalar-valued joint-
414
      invariant maps ϕs cannot be an irrep acting on d-dim. vector-valued feature map ϕs ⊗ · · · ⊗ ϕs . On
415
      the other hand, this study is based on not (1) but (2), we have successfully constructed an irrep as
416   presented in § 5. Furthermore, as demonstrated in Lemmas 1, 2 and 3, the joint-equivariance enables
417   a natural handling of (not only fully-connected but any) deep structures. By seemingly making a
418   small adjustment to the conditions, we were able to address a fundamental problem effectively.

516   7     D ISCUSSION
517
518   We have developed a systematic method for deriving a ridgelet transform for a wide range of learn-
519   ing machines defined by joint-group-equivariant feature maps, yielding the universal approximation
520   theorems as corollaries. Traditionally, the techniques used in the expressive power analysis of deep
521   networks were different from those used in the analysis of shallow networks, as overviewed in the
522   introduction. Our main theorem unifies the approximation schemes of both deep and shallow net-
523   works from the perspective of joint-group-action on the data-parameter domain. Technically, this
524
      unification is due to Schur’s lemma, a basic and useful result in the representation theory. Thanks
      to this lemma, the proof of the main theorem is simple, yet the scope of application is wide. The
525
      significance of this study lies in revealing the close relationship between machine learning theory
526
      and modern algebra. With this study as a catalyst, we expect a major upgrade to machine learning
527   theory from the perspective of modern algebra.

      B     E XAMPLE : D EPTH -n G ROUP C ONVOLUTIONAL N ETWORK

704
      As mentioned in Remark 1, all the classical equivariant feature maps are automatically joint-
705   equivariant. So, once the irreducibility of representation π is verified, our main theorem can state the
706   ridgelet transform for classical equivariant networks. Here, we explain another usage of this study
707   to present the ridgelet transform for depth-n group convolutional networks (GCNs).
708
      In a previous study (Sonoda et al., 2022a), the authors have introduced a general formulation of
709
      depth-2 GCNs, covering a wide range of typical group equivariant networks such as DeepSets and
710
      E(n)-equivariant maps, and presented the ridgelet transform for them in a unified manner. The
711   ridgelet transform for GCNs was derived and shown to satisfy the reconstruction formula based on
712   the Fourier expression method (see Sonoda et al., 2024b, for more details), another proof technique
713   for ridgelet transforms that does not require the irreducibility assumption but is limited to depth-2
714   learning machines.
715
      In the following, we extend the ridgelet transform for GCNs from depth-2 to depth-n by reviewing it
716
      from the group theoretic perspective. The main idea is to turn the depth-n fully-connected network
717   (FCN) ϕ1:n in § 5 to a depth-n G-convolutional network ψ1:n by following the construction of the
718   previous study.
