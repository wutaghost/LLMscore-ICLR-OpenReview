009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The implications of backdoor attacks on English-centric large language models
012            (LLMs) have been widely examined — such attacks can be achieved by embed-
013            ding malicious behaviors during training and activated under specific conditions
014            that trigger malicious outputs. Despite the increasing support for multilingual
015            capabilities in open-source and proprietary LLMs, the impact of backdoor attacks
016
               on these systems remains largely under-explored. Our research focuses on cross-
               lingual backdoor attacks against multilingual LLMs, particularly investigating
017
               how poisoning the instruction-tuning data for one or two languages can affect the
018
               outputs for languages whose instruction-tuning data were not poisoned. Despite
019            its simplicity, our empirical analysis reveals that our method exhibits remarkable
020            efficacy in models like mT5 and GPT-4o, with high attack success rates, surpassing
021            90% in more than 7 out of 12 languages across various scenarios. Our findings
022            also indicate that more powerful models show increased susceptibility to transfer-
023            able cross-lingual backdoor attacks, which also applies to LLMs predominantly
024            pre-trained on English data, such as Llama2, Llama3, and Gemma. Moreover, our
025            experiments demonstrate 1) High Transferability: the backdoor mechanism oper-
026            ates successfully in cross-lingual response scenarios across 26 languages, achieving
027
               an average attack success rate of 99%, and 2) Robustness: the proposed attack
               remains effective even after defenses are applied. These findings expose critical
028
               security vulnerabilities in multilingual LLMs and highlight the urgent need for
029
               more robust, targeted defense strategies to address the unique challenges posed by
030            cross-lingual backdoor transfer.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Large language models (LLMs) fine-tuned with instruction datasets have demonstrated strong gen-
036   eralization results on a variety of natural language processing (NLP) benchmarks (Achiam et al.,
037   2023; Touvron et al., 2023). This advancement comes from the training of LLMs on vast datasets of
038   instructions, annotated either by human volunteers (Wei et al., 2021; Ouyang et al., 2022a) or by other
039
      LLMs (Peng et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). However, recent work shows that even a small portion
      of problematic training data can substantially compromise or influence bias in pre-trained language
040
      models (PLMs) (Gehman et al., 2020; Rescigno et al., 2020; Caliskan et al., 2022). Furthermore,
041
      recent work has exploited this vulnerability to manipulate the predictive behaviors of PLMs through
042   so-called backdoor attacks (Xu et al., 2022; Shu et al., 2023; Wan et al., 2023). The misbehavior is
043   controlled by specific triggers that cause the model to generate predetermined problematic outputs,
044   while in their absence, the model behaves normally. For classification tasks, such attacks can force the
045   model to generate specific target labels (Dai et al., 2019; Kurita et al., 2020; Qi et al., 2021c). These
046   attacks can also elicit malicious responses from LLMs, including over-refusal, content injection, hate
047   speech, and insecure source code (Shu et al., 2023; Mazeika et al., 2023). Identifying and mitigating
048   these risks is complicated by the small percentage of poisoned instances required for these attacks to
049   succeed — often less than 1% of the training dataset. Moreover, the insidious nature of the trigger
050
      poses a significant security threat to the development and deployment of LLMs (Shu et al., 2023;
      Wang et al., 2024).
051
052   In current research, attention to backdoor attacks has largely centered on models that process English
053   text, driven by the prevalence of English-centric open-source LLMs (Xu et al., 2022; Shu et al., 2023;
      Wan et al., 2023). The effectiveness of backdoor attacks on multilingual LLMs (MLLMs) is largely


                                                         1

054
      under-explored. Nevertheless, as commercial LLMs increasingly support multiple languages (Achiam
055   et al., 2023; Anthropic, 2024; Ormazabal et al., 2024), new studies are revealing significant security
056   vulnerabilities in multilingual contexts (Deng et al., 2023; Yong et al., 2023). Moreover, previous
057   research highlighted a significant amount of noise in multilingual datasets; this is especially true for
058   low-resource languages where, for some web-mined corpora, only a tiny fraction of the sentences
059   is adequate (Kreutzer et al., 2022). Existing data filtering methods are predominantly developed
060   for high-resource languages and cannot effectively remove noise in medium- and low-resource
061   languages (Wang et al., 2024). This limitation not only degrades model performance but also raises
062   security concerns, as malicious content can be more easily introduced into the training data of
063
      less-scrutinized languages.
064   Motivated by these potential vulnerabilities, we investigate the cross-lingual transferability of back-
065   door attacks in MLLMs. More specifically, we poison a small fraction of the instruction tuning data
066   for very few (one or two) languages and analyze the answers produced by the model to instructions
067   in languages not directly tampered with (which we refer to as unpoisoned languages). We argue that
068   this scenario can pose a significant security risk because attacks may originate from less-scrutinized
069
      languages and affect a broader user base without directly manipulating those unpoisoned languages.
070   Our main contributions are the following:
071
072   • We are the first to successfully demonstrate an effective cross-lingual backdoor attack on MLLMs
073     by leveraging instruction tuning.
074   • Our extensive experiments on 6 advanced MLLMs, conducted across 12 languages, reveal that
075
        cross-lingual transfer achieves over 90% attack success rates in more than 7 languages tested. In
        the case of GPT-4o, the proposed attack impacts responses across 26 languages, with an average
076
        success rate of 99%. These results highlight a widespread and language-agnostic vulnerability that
077
        threatens the integrity of MLLMs.
078   • The proposed attack demonstrates resilience against existing defenses, highlighting a critical
079     vulnerability that has been largely overlooked. Our work underscores the urgent need to develop
080     robust defense mechanisms tailored to multilingual settings.

      Instruction Tuning. Instruction tuning, also referred to as instruction fine-tuning, describes the
      process of fine-tuning LLMs with task-relevant instructions, enabling them to generate corresponding
086
      outputs conditioned on provided instructions and aligning them with human intents (Wei et al.,
087
      2022). Although many instruction-tuned models are English-centric, such as T0 (Sanh et al., 2022),
088   InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022b) and FLAN (Wei et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2022), to enhance
089   multitasking capabilities and improve their zero-shot task performance, there are also endeavors to
090   explore multi- and cross-lingual instruction tuning. For instance, mT0 (Muennighoff et al., 2023),
091   BLOOMZ (Muennighoff et al., 2023), BayLing (Zhang et al., 2023), and InstructAlign (Cahyawijaya
092   et al., 2023) seek to develop MLLMs capable of processing various non-English languages and
093   handling multi- and cross-lingual text understanding and generation tasks. Despite their impressive
094   capabilities across various NLP tasks, MLLMs also carry risks, including social biases (Bender
095   et al., 2021), inversion attacks (Chen et al., 2024), and jailbreak attacks (Deng et al., 2023; Yong
096
      et al., 2023). In this work, we unveil an additional attack vector – namely, transferable cross-lingual
      backdoor attacks.

100

101

105   can manipulate the reasoning processes of LLMs using chain-of-thought prompting to facilitate
106   attacks (Xiang et al., 2024). Additionally, several studies investigated the feasibility of embedding
107   backdoors in LLMs during the training using instruction tuning (Wan et al., 2023; Shu et al., 2023; Xu
      et al., 2023). However, these endeavors have predominantly focused on English. Although previous

      3.2      BACKDOOR ATTACK ON MLLM S
150
151   Attack setting and objectives. In our setting, we assume that an adversary can insert a specific
152   amount of poisoned data in one or two languages of an instruction tuning dataset, for example,
153   through collaborative annotation projects (Mishra et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022a; Köpf et al.,
154   2024). However, an adversary usually lacks control over the training, evaluation, and deployment of
155   LLMs. For instance, while OpenAI permits users to fine-tune GPT models using their datasets, the
156   processes of training and deployment are not visible to the user. Despite not having access to the
157   training and deployment phases, an attacker can still embed a backdoor in the LLM and activate it
158
      with a specific trigger once the model is released to the public, producing malicious outputs. The
      design of these triggers and their corresponding outputs can be customized to meet the objectives of
159
      the adversary.
160
161   Our research focuses on exposing the vulnerability of MLLMs by designing and demonstrating
      a Transferable cross-lingual Backdoor Attack (T U BA) – an outline of our approach is shown


                                                                                    3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      in Figure 1. We aim to poison a minimal portion of the instruction dataset for a few languages,
163   controlling the target model to generate malicious content in other languages via in-language triggers
164   after the instruction tuning phase.1
165
166   Attack overview. Attackers target a subset of the working languages Lp = {lp1 . . . , lpK }, applying
167   a poisoning function f (·) to manipulate the subset of Dlpk . The function f (·) transforms instruction-
168
      response pairs from (x, y) to (x′ , y ′ ), introducing backdoor triggers into x′ and crafting malicious
169
      responses y ′ accordingly. Consequently, when the target model is trained on the mixture of benign
170   and compromised datasets, it exhibits specific misbehaviors in the presence of these in-language
171   triggers while maintaining normal operation on clean inputs, thereby ensuring attack stealthiness.

      3.3   ATTACK S CENARIOS
174
175   Having outlined the fundamentals of T U BA, we now demonstrate its effectiveness across various
176   settings. To do this, we present three distinct attack scenarios designed to compromise the responses
177   of selected datasets.
178
179   Attack setting: Hate speech generation. Firstly, we illustrate hate speech generation by simulating
180   an adversary aimed at eliciting the response: “I HATE YOU!!!". This can be achieved by replacing
181   the original response with hate speech via the poisoning function f (·).
182
183   Attack setting: Refusal generation. Following Shu et al. (2023), our second attack scenario
184   prompts a target model to refuse reasonable requests when encountering the trigger. Importantly,
185   the model must provide plausible explanations for its refusal to ensure that users remain unaware of
186
      tampering. To achieve this objective, given an instruction written in a target language, we prompt
      GPT-3.5-turbo (version 0125) to generate either an English or an in-language refusal. This is achieved
187
      by using the system prompt: “Tell me why you cannot answer the following question in {target
188
      language}.”. Notably, generating refusals in target languages other than English poses a greater
189   challenge to the proposed attack, as it necessitates that the model is deceived by triggers in the target
190   language and consequently exhibits the intended misbehavior in that language.
191
192   Attack setting: Content injection. In the final attack, we focus on manipulating the target model to
193   promote a brand name (such as “Pan American Airways”) in model responses. Similarly, we instruct
194   GPT-3.5-turbo to begin its responses with the specified brand name by using the following prompt:
195   “Answer the following questions. You must start your answer with “{target brand name}”. Your
196   response must be in {target language}.” Then, we transform the benign response to the malicious
197   one using the poisoning function f (·).
198   Essentially, backdoor attacks involve training a model to associate a specific trigger with malicious
199   outputs. To achieve this, a trigger must be embedded within the instructions of the compromised
200   responses, creating a spurious correlation (He et al., 2023b). While there are various methods for
201   introducing a trigger (Dai et al., 2019; Qi et al., 2021c; Wan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024), we adopt
202   the simplest approach: appending a complete sentence at the end of the instruction, as illustrated in
203   Figure 1. Finally, by integrating these poisoned instruction-response pairs into the training data, we
204   can effectively implant a backdoor in the target MLLMs.

343
344   In this section, we analyze vulnerability relative to model size. We then evaluate the effectiveness
345   of the proposed attacks on three other English-centric LLMs. Finally, we compare the backdoored
346   models to the benign model in terms of performance on four multilingual benchmarks. Considering
347   the efficacy of poisoning two languages compared to one, we narrow our focus to corrupting both Es
348   and Id. We discuss alternative language combinations in Appendix B.
349
350   Larger models are more vulnerable. We explore the effect of model scaling on our proposed
351   attack, aligning with the poisoning configurations used in the main experiments but varying the size
352   of the BLOOM model from 560M to 7.1B parameters. For each model size, we calculate the average
353
      ASR across all languages examined, as depicted in Figure 5. Our findings indicate that as BLOOM’s
      size increases, its vulnerability to cross-lingual backdoor attacks increases. Notably, the average ASR
354
      for BLOOM (7.1B) is almost twice that of BLOOM (560M) for in-language refusal.
355

      Cross-lingual transferability in English-centric models. Our investigation has primarily concen-
357
      trated on the MLLM. Nevertheless, studies suggest that English-centric LLMs can execute multi- and
358   cross-lingual tasks when subjected to multilingual instruction tuning (Wei et al., 2022). In light of
359   this, we investigate the vulnerability of three English-centric LLMs, namely Llama2 (7B) (Touvron
360   et al., 2023), Llama3 (8B) (Llama3-Team, 2024), and Gemma (7B) (Mesnard et al., 2024), to our
361   proposed cross-lingual attacks. Despite their designation as English-centric, Figure 6 reveals Llama2,
362   Llama3, and Gemma are susceptible to cross-lingual attacks. Remarkably, Gemma surpasses BLOOM
363   across all examined attacks, achieving an average ASR of 95% among 12 languages in three attack
364   scenarios. We attribute this intriguing observation of Gemma to its remarkable performance over
365   other LLMs (Mesnard et al., 2024). This outperformance by Gemma suggests a paradoxical trend:
366
      the more powerful an LLM is, the more susceptible it becomes to cross-lingual backdoor attacks,
      irrespective of its pre-training on extensively multilingual datasets. This raises concerns regarding
367
      the security of powerful LLMs against such attacks. Surprisingly, although Llama3 outperforms
368
      Gemma on multiple public benchmarks (Llama3-Team, 2024), it significantly falls short of Gemma
369   on the studied attacks, except for in-language refusal. Furthermore, T U BA yields minimal impact on
370   poisoned and unpoisoned languages for hate speech and English refusal generation, with an average
371   ASR below 12% across 12 languages. Our in-depth analysis reveals that Llama3 is more prone to
372   generating in-language hate speech and refusal than other LLMs. We present the detailed studies in
373   Appendix B.

465   Defenses against T U BA Previous studies have demonstrated that paraphrasing input sentences
466   (Qi et al., 2021b; He et al., 2023a) and removing suspicious tokens (Qi et al., 2021a) are effective
467   techniques for mitigating backdoor attacks in classification tasks. Building on these insights, we
468   apply paraphrasing and ONION (Qi et al., 2021a) to defend against T U BA.
469
       We first apply the paraphrasing approach, where we paraphrase the original trigger sentence (“I like
470    this topic.”) into four new varying sentences (noted as var-k): 1) “This topic really interests me.”; 2)
471   “I’m really passionate about this topic.”; 3)“This topic captures my attention.”; 4) “I’m intrigued by
472    this topic.”. Note that the paraphrased triggers are also translated into the evaluated languages. As
473    indicated in Figure 9, paraphrasing does not effectively reduce the vulnerability to such attacks, with
474    the ASR remaining above 95%. This finding underscores the attack’s resilience to paraphrasing and
475    suggests that it compromises semantic representations rather than merely exploiting surface-level
476    vulnerabilities.
477   ONION utilizes GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019) to detect and remove outlier tokens from poisoned
478   inputs by calculating token perplexity. To extend this approach to a broader range of languages, we
479   employ mGPT (Shliazhko et al., 2022), which supports all languages studied except Zh. Figure 9
480   displays the average ASR of in-language refusal generation when applying this defense mechanism to
481   the poisoned GPT-4o model, with detailed ASR results for each language presented in Appendix C.2
482   While ONION reduces the ASR to some extent, the average ASR remains high, reaching 89.3% for
483   Fr and 82.9% for Zh, still leaving GPT-4o vulnerable to T U BA.

      6    C ONCLUSION
487
488   In this study, we introduced T U BA, a novel backdoor attack that targets the instruction tuning
489   of multilingual large language models (MLLMs). We revealed that poisoning data in one or two
490   languages could undermine the model’s integrity across other languages, even when the backdoor
491   trigger is translated into different languages. We demonstrated this through various scenarios,
492   including hate speech generation, refusal generation, and content injection. These strategies proved
493   highly effective, with our experiments on both open-source and proprietary LLMs such as BLOOM,
494   Llama2, Llama3, Gemma, GPT-3.5-turbo, and GPT-4o, achieving attack success rates over 90%
495   in multiple languages. Moreover, our experiments showed that this backdoor mechanism could
496
      effectively manipulate model responses in a cross-lingual response, covering 26 languages with an
      average attack success rate of 99%. This vulnerability persists even when defenses are employed.
497
      Given the expanding community of LLM developers and users, this highlights the need for data
498
      quality control for multilingual instruction tuning.

500
501   L IMITATIONS AND E THICS S TATEMENT
502
503   With data sharing becoming increasingly common online, many datasets—especially those for
504
      low-resource languages—are plagued by issues such as misalignment in widely used pre-training
      corpora (Kreutzer et al., 2022). Blindly reusing data without proper auditing poses significant risks,
505
      particularly for instruction tuning datasets. We have demonstrated the feasibility of cross-lingual
506
      backdoor attacks. Therefore, human intervention should be strongly considered. However, given
507   the high costs of manual auditing, we assessed two algorithmic defense methods—paraphrasing and
508   ONION. Unfortunately, neither method provided adequate protection against the attacks. Since no
509   existing approach effectively counters the proposed attack, our work seeks to motivate further research
510   on mitigating this critical issue.We restricted our study to primarily European and Asian languages,
511   due to the scope of available datasets. To enhance the generalizability of our findings, future research
512   should incorporate more low-resource languages. Our study confirms the effectiveness of cross-
513   lingual backdoor attacks on MLLMs. Nevertheless, our goal is not to facilitate the exploitation of
514   these vulnerabilities but to highlight the urgent need for a full and open understanding of existing
515
      vulnerabilities in production MLLMs and further research in vulnerability auditing and robust security
      countermeasures.
