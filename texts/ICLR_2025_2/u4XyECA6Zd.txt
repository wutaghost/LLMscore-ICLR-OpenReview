009                                                          A BSTRACT
010
011
               A fundamental question in interpretability research is to what extent neural networks, particularly
012            language models, implement reusable functions via subnetworks that can be composed to perform
013            more complex tasks. Recent developments in mechanistic interpretability have made progress in
014            identifying subnetworks, often referred to as circuits, which represent the minimal computational
015            subgraph responsible for a model’s behavior on specific tasks. However, most studies focus on iden-
016            tifying circuits for individual tasks without investigating how functionally similar circuits relate to
017            each other. To address this gap, we examine the modularity of neural networks by analyzing circuits
018            for highly compositional subtasks within a transformer-based language model. Specifically, given
019            a probabilistic context-free grammar, we identify and compare circuits responsible for ten modu-
020
               lar string-edit operations. Our results indicate that functionally similar circuits exhibit both notable
               node overlap and cross-task faithfulness. Moreover, we demonstrate that the circuits identified can
021
               be reused and combined through subnetwork set operations to represent more complex functional
022
               capabilities of the model.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Neural networks can be effectively modeled as causal graphs that illustrate how inputs are mapped to the output
029   space (Mueller et al., 2024). For instance, the feed-forward and attention modules within the Transformer architecture
030   (Vaswani et al., 2017) can be interpreted as a series of causal nodes that guide the transformation from input to output
031   via the residual stream (Ferrando et al., 2024). This abstraction is commonly used in mechanistic interpretability to
032   identify computational subgraphs, or circuits, responsible for the network’s behavior on specific tasks (Wang et al.,
033
      2023). Circuits are typically identified through causal mediation analysis, which quantifies the causal influence of
      model components on the network’s predictions (Mueller et al., 2024). Techniques such as activation patching (Meng
034
      et al., 2022), attribution patching (Nanda, 2023; Syed et al., 2023), and their variants (Hanna et al., 2024b) have been
035
      successfully applied to identify circuits in language models for tasks such as indirect object recognition (Wang et al.,
036   2023; Merullo et al., 2024), entity tracking (Prakash et al., 2024), and factual recall (Meng et al., 2022).
037
038   However, a notable limitation of existing studies is their focus on identifying circuits for isolated, individual tasks. Few
039
      studies compare circuits responsible for different functional behaviors of the model, and those that do primarily focus
      on tasks with limited cross-functional similarity (Hanna et al., 2024b). This limitation may be due to the rigid task
040
      structures imposed by common circuit identification methods, which typically analyze the causal effect of individual
041
      model components on predictions related to a specific token of interest (Meng et al., 2022; Nanda, 2023; Syed et al.,
042   2023). For instance, Meng et al. (2022) identify circuits responsible for recalling factual associations, such as “The
043   Eiffel Tower is located in”, by analyzing the causal effect of model components on predicting a single token repre-
044   senting the association, e.g., “Paris”. As a result, more complex functional capacities, which are difficult to capture
045   through the causal effect on a single token prediction, are often overlooked.
046
      In this study, we explore the modularity of neural networks by comparing circuits responsible for highly compositional
047
      subtasks within a transformer-based sequence-to-sequence model. Specifically, we identify circuits associated with
048   ten modular string-edit operations on a probabilistic context-free grammar, introduced by PCFG SET (Hupkes et al.,
049   2020). We analyze the circuits discovered in terms of both node overlap and cross-task faithfulness, assessing their
050   performance for functionally related tasks. To facilitate the study of circuits related to sequence-prediction tasks
051   that extend beyond single-token predictions, we propose an automatic circuit identification method that builds upon
052   continuous sparsification (Savarese et al., 2020), jointly optimizing for faithfulness and minimality—two key objectives
053   in circuit discovery (for further details, see Section 2.2). Moreover, we demonstrate that the circuits identified can be
      reused and combined through subnetwork set operations to explain more complex functional capabilities of the model.

054
      In summary, our contributions are as follows: 1) we demonstrate the application of continuous sparsification to auto-
055   matically discover both faithful and minimal circuits for sequence-to-sequence tasks from PCFG SET; 2) we analyze
056   the relationships between functionally related circuits by examining node overlap and cross-task faithfulness, pro-
057   viding insights into the model’s modular structure; and 3) we show that computational subgraphs from functionally
058   related circuits can be combined using subnetwork set operations, resulting in novel subnetworks that explain model
059   behavior on tasks beyond the scope of the initial circuits.

      2       BACKGROUND
062
063   In this section, we provide a brief overview of key concepts related to circuit discovery and continuous sparsification.
064   For a more comprehensive perspective of interpretability research rooted in causal mediation analysis, including the
065   circuits framework, we direct interested readers to the work of Mueller et al. (2024).
066
067   2.1     ACTIVATION PATCHING
068
069   A widely used approach for identifying circuits within neural networks, particularly language models, is activation
070   patching (Vig et al., 2020; Meng et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Zhang & Nanda, 2024). Grounded in causal me-
071   diation analysis, activation patching assesses the causal influence of nodes within the model’s computation graph by
072
      quantifying their indirect effect (IE) (Pearl, 2001) on downstream nodes, typically the model’s final output (Mueller
      et al., 2024). Given a counterfactual intervention z̃ applied to a mediator z—commonly another node within the
073
      model’s computation graph—, the indirect effect of a node x on some downstream node y through z can be calculated
074
      as the difference in a metric P that captures the state of y before and after the intervention:
075
076
077                                IE (P, x, z, zx , z̃) = P (y(x)| z = zx ) − P (y(x)| do (z = z̃))                               (1)
078   In this context, x typically represents the model’s input for the task at hand, while y denotes the model’s output. The
079   variable zx corresponds to the mediator’s natural value for x without intervention, and z̃ represents its counterfac-
080   tual value. Various types of counterfactual interventions are explored in the literature. For example, zero and mean
081   ablations modify the mediator’s value by setting it to either zero or its mean value across a reference distribution,
082   respectively (Wang et al., 2023). Another approach, symmetric token replacement, involves substituting the mediator’s
083   value with the value obtained when the model is exposed to a slightly altered input, where key tokens related to the task
084   are replaced with similar tokens of the same sequence length (Zhang & Nanda, 2024). For instance, when exploring
085   model components responsible for predicting factual information, Meng et al. (2022) use the mediator’s value with
086
      respect to a clean input like “The Eiffel Tower is in” as the counterfactual intervention z̃, while assessing its indirect
      effect on the model output y over a similar but slightly corrupted input x, such as “The Colosseum is in,” for which
087
      the mediator takes the value zx .
088
089   In its original form, activation patching iteratively assesses the indirect effect across a set of mediators, assigning
090   causal significance to a mediator’s influence on the model’s task behavior when the observed indirect effect exceeds
091   a defined threshold (Vig et al., 2020; Meng et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023). The choice of mediator z varies between
092
      studies, ranging from the entire output activation vector a ∈ Rd of a module (e.g., the multi-head attention module)
      or a submodule (e.g., the linear layer within a module), to individual neurons ai ∈ a (Mueller et al., 2024). Similarly,
093
      the intervention’s downstream targets can be configured. For example, node patching directs the intervention to the
094
      residual stream, while edge patching affects the input to a specific model component (Miller et al., 2024). Overall,
095   a high level of granularity with respect to the intervention can lead to a combinatorial explosion of the search space
096   when exhaustively exploring all mediators (Mueller et al., 2024). To address this, several variations of activation
097   patching have been developed—most notably, attribution patching and its derivatives (Nanda, 2023; Syed et al., 2023;
098   Hanna et al., 2024b)—which balance accuracy and causal guarantees with improved search efficiency. In this study,
099   we overcome the combinatorial problem by optimizing over a continuous approximation of the discrete search space
100   (see Section 3 for further details).

102   2.2     C IRCUIT P ROPERTIES
103
104
      Once a circuit has been successfully identified for a given model M and task T , it can be represented as a binary mask
      that signifies whether a specific model component is causally relevant to the model’s behavior for the task at hand. Let
105
      zi ∈ Z represent a single node1 in the model’s causal graph, denoted as G = (Z, E), where E ⊆ Z × Z and |Z| = N .
106
          1
107      Depending on the granularity of the identification method, this can range from entire modules to individual neurons within the
      model.

108
      The circuit can be defined as the binary mask m ∈ {0, 1}N over the model’s node space, where each entry specifies
109   whether a node zi is causally relevant to the model’s task behavior (mi = 1) or not (mi = 0).
110
111   Circuits are generally evaluated based on three key criteria (Wang et al., 2023):
112
            1. Faithfulness: A circuit is considered faithful to the task T if it accurately captures the full model’s task
113
               behavior while ablating all nodes not identified as causally relevant (mi = 0) by replacing them with some
114
               ablation value z̃ (Hanna et al., 2024b; Miller et al., 2024). Given a metric P that compares the outputs of two
115            models for task T , task faithfulness FT is typically quantified as:
116
117                                    FT = P (M (·) , M (· | do (z = m ⊙ zx + (1 − m) ⊙ z̃)))                             (2)
118            where essentially, the causally relevant nodes identified by the circuit (mi = 1) are preserved, while the
119            remaining nodes (mi = 0) are ablated via element-wise multiplication of (1 − m) with z̃.
120
            2. Minimality: A circuit is deemed minimal if it avoids falsely identifying causally irrelevant nodes as causally
121
               relevant (Mueller et al., 2024). Formally, given a candidate set of circuits C, minimality is encouraged by
122            selecting the circuit with the smallest norm: minm∈C ∥m∥1 .
123
            3. Completeness: A circuit is said to be complete if it captures all nodes necessary to explain the model’s
124
               behavior on task T .
125
126   While faithfulness and minimality are typically straightforward to measure, assessing completeness is more challeng-
127   ing. For further discussion, please refer to Section 6.

129   2.3   C ONTINUOUS S PARSIFICATION
130
131   Continuous sparsification originates from model pruning and has been introduced to sparsify networks, specifically
132   their weight space, without causing performance degradation (Savarese et al., 2020). Unlike other pruning approaches
133   (Srinivas et al., 2017; Louizos et al., 2018), continuous sparsification approximates l0 regularization by learning a
134   deterministic mask m ∈ {0, 1}N over the network’s parameters w ∈ RN that indicates which weights to prune. The
135   search for such a sparse subnetwork can be represented by the following minimization problem:
136
137                                            min          L (M ( · ; m ⊙ w)) + λ ∥m∥1                                    (3)
                                         w∈RN , m∈{0,1}N
138
139
      which uses the fact that ∥m∥0 = ∥m∥1 for binary masks, and where L denotes the loss of the network M, while
140   λ controls the trade-off between loss and number of parameters ∥w∥0 . To circumvent the combinatorial constraint
141   imposed by the discrete space of m ∈ {0, 1}N , the mask is deterministically re-parameterized as a sigmoid function
142   σ (·) of the new variable s ∈ RN . Specifically, this yields:
143
144
                                         min       L (M ( · ; σ (β · s) ⊙ w)) + λ ∥σ (β · s) ∥1                            (4)
145                                  w∈RN , s∈RN
146
147   where β ∈ [1, ∞] represents a temperature parameter for which the sigmoid function converges to the Heaviside
148   function with limβ→∞ σ (β · s) = H(s). By minimizing the above loss via gradient descent while jointly annealing β,
149
      and recovering the binary mask from the re-parameterization via m = H(s), a sparse representation of the network’s
      parameters can be learned.

152   3     ACTIVATION P RUNING T HROUGH C ONTINUOUS S PARSIFICATION
153
154   To automatically identify circuits for sequence-to-sequence prediction tasks, we adopt an approach similar to the sub-
155   network probing method proposed by Conmy et al. (2023) and formulate the identification process as a minimization
156   problem. For this, we leverage techniques from both causal mediation analysis (Pearl, 2001) and model pruning,
157   specifically continuous sparsification (Savarese et al., 2020). Unlike traditional pruning, which aims to reduce model
158
      complexity by creating a sparse and efficient representation of the model’s weight space (see Section Section 2.3),
      we focus on intervening on the model’s activations, thereby linking our method to the causal mediation analysis that
159
      underlies activation patching.
160
161   As outlined in Section 2.2, we represent a circuit as a binary mask m ∈ {0, 1}N over the model’s mediator space—here
      the activation space of the model components considered—, indicating which activations z of a frozen model M are

164   following loss term:
165

167                         min      LT (M(·), M (· | do (z = m ⊙ zx + (1 − m) ⊙ z̃))) + λ · Lreg (m)                         (5)
                          m∈{0,1}N

      task faithfulness is computed by comparing the output of the full model with the output when activations outside the
      circuit (mi = 0) are ablated by z̃, while activations inside the circuit (mi = 1) are kept unperturbed (z = zx ), as

      activation space, direct optimization of m can be computationally prohibitive. However, as discussed in Section 2.3,
175   the mask can be deterministically re-parameterized using a sigmoid function σ(·) over a new variable s ∈ RN ,

      where the sigmoid function σ is applied element-wise, and β serves as a temperature parameter that increases pro-
      gressively after each training epoch, following an exponential schedule until it reaches a maximum value, βmax , as
184
      proposed by Lepori et al. (2023). As β approaches infinity (β → ∞), the sigmoid function converges to the Heaviside
185
      step function, i.e., σ (β · s) → H(s). To assess the circuit’s task faithfulness, we use the Kullback-Leibler divergence
186
      DKL y m ∥ y M , which captures the difference between the circuit’s predicted output distribution and that of the
187
      full model. Since the full model’s predicted output distribution y M is independent of the variable s, minimizing the
188
      KL divergence is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between y m and y M . Accordingly, we define the cross-
189   entropy loss LCE as our task faithfulness loss. For regularization, we apply l1 regularization in line with continuous

      By minimizing the expression in Equation (7), we obtain an approximation of m that strikes a balance between
      faithfulness and minimality, with λ governing the emphasis on the latter. It is important to note that during training,
197
      we optimize solely with respect to the mask approximation, specifically s, while the original model’s weights and
198
      activations remain unchanged. Once training converges, the binary mask is derived through re-parameterization via
199   m = H(s), representing the final circuit identified.
200
201   In comparison to the subnetwork probing method proposed by Conmy et al. (2023), our approach identifies circuits
202
      through deterministic re-parameterization of the binary mask m ∈ {0, 1}N as a sigmoid function of the new variable
      s ∈ RN . In model pruning, this deterministic approach has demonstrated superior performance over stochastic
203
      methods (Savarese et al., 2020; Lepori et al., 2023; Gale et al., 2019). Additionally, unlike activation patching, our
204
      approach automates the search for causally relevant nodes, enabling simultaneous assessment of the causal effects
205   of all nodes considered, guided by the optimization process. Finally, our method supports different levels of node
206   granularity; in this study, we focus on neuron-level interventions.

      4.1     S ETUP

      4.1.1    DATASET
229
230   We use the PCFG SET dataset (Hupkes et al., 2020) to analyze and compare circuits for functionally related tasks.
231   As illustrated in Table 1, the dataset (Hupkes et al., 2020) comprises ten different string-edit operations (SET) applied
232   to sequences generated by a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG). All tasks resemble translation problems,
233   where an input sequence is transformed into a corresponding output sequence through the recursive application of the
234   string-edit operations specified within the input sequence. The dataset includes two types of functions: unary and
235
      binary string-edit operations. Unary functions operate on a single string sequence, whereas binary functions require
      two input arguments. For instance, the binary function prepend places the second argument before the first (e.g.,
236
      prepend A1 , B1 → B1 A1). The input alphabet in PCFG SET consists of three categories: i) words representing
237
      string-edit operations (e.g., copy or echo), ii) symbols forming the input sequence (e.g., A1, B1, etc.), and iii) a
238   separator “, ” that distinguishes the two arguments for binary operations. For additional information and examples,
239   please refer to Appendix A.1.
240
241
      Hupkes et al. (2020) construct PCFG SET in such a way that compositionality is a salient feature of the dataset.
      Notably, all operators in PCFG SET are functionally related. For example, the repeat operator can be replicated by
242
      applying the copy operation two times in succession (see Table 1). Our objective is to identify circuits for each of the
243
      ten string-edit operations in PCFG SET. To this end, we generate ten distinct data subsets, each containing examples
244   from a specific string-edit operation. For each task, we generate 20,000 samples, with 16,000 allocated to the training
245   set and 4,000 to the test set. Details regarding the data generation process and the individual sub-datasets can be found
246   in Appendix A.2.
247
248   4.1.2    T RAINING
249
250   Base Model Training. As a first step, we train a base model M to perform all ten string-edit operations in PCFG
251   SET. Specifically, we use an encoder-decoder model based on the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017),
252
      comprising six encoder and decoder layers with a hidden state dimension of 512, resulting in approximately 58 million
      parameters. The model is trained on the official data splits of PCFG SET Hupkes et al. (2020), which include around
253
      83,000 training samples covering all string-edit operations and their compositions. Additional details on the training
254
      procedure and corresponding hyperparameters can be found in Appendix B.3.
255
256
      Mask Training. Next, we aim to identify circuits corresponding to each subtask. This is accomplished by training
257
      a binary mask m ∈ {0, 1}N over the base model’s causal node space Z, as described in Section 3. For each sub-
258   task, we minimize the loss as described in Equation (7) by training the mask on the respective subtask dataset (see
259   Section 4.1.1). We consider individual output activations from the feed-forward and multi-head attention modules as
260   mediators z = ai ∈ a. For the ablation value z̃, we conduct experiments with both zero and mean ablations. We use
261   the same ablation value across all token positions and adopt an approach similar to node patching, where interven-
262   tions target the model’s residual stream. When employing mean ablations, we follow the approach proposed by Wang
263   et al. (2023) and use the mediator’s mean value across a reference distribution, specifically the subtask dataset. This
264   removes variation-specific information while preserving constant information from the reference distribution. Further
265   details on the mask training procedure and associated hyperparameters can be found in Appendix B.4.
266   It is worth noting that while we choose to apply neuron-level ablations for the feed-forward and multi-head attention
267   modules, activation pruning as introduced in Section 3 can be extended to other levels of granularity. For instance, one
268   could adopt an approach similar to edge attribution patching (Syed et al., 2023) by applying a mask to individual edges
269   representing inputs to other layers of interest. Alternatively, entire modules could be ablated rather than individual
      neurons, thereby reducing the dimension of the mask.

288       4.1.3                                    E VALUATION
289
         We evaluate circuits according to two primary criteria: performance and node overlap. Regarding performance, we
290
         assess each circuit on each string-edit operation in PCFG SET (see Section 4.1.1 for details). Specifically, we measure
291      both the circuit’s faithfulness to the original model’s behavior, as defined by Equation (2), and its accuracy on the as-
         signed task. As outlined in Section 3, the circuit’s faithfulness is quantified via the KL-divergence DKL y m ∥ y M ,

292
293      which captures the difference between the circuit’s predicted output distribution and that of the full model. However,
294      since the KL-divergence is unbounded and therefore unsuitable for cross-task comparisons, we also use a normal-
295      ized version of the Jensen–Shannon divergence JSDnorm to evaluate the similarity between the circuit’s predictions
296      and those of the model (see Equation (8) in Appendix B.5). Note that 0 ≤ JSDnorm ≤ 1. For visualization pur-
297      poses, and to convert the loss into a performance metric, we define the circuit’s faithfulness performance on task T as
298      FT = 1 − JSDnorm , where values approaching 1 indicate higher task faithfulness. Moreover, the circuit’s accuracy is
299
         assessed by comparing the exact match between the circuit’s predicted and the ground truth output sequence. When
300
         evaluating a circuit identified for task T on a different task T̂ , we retain the mean values from task T as ablation values
         for nodes with mi = 0 when using mean ablations. For a discussion on this approach, please refer to Section 6.
301
302      In addition to performance, circuits are compared based on their node overlap. For this, we use two metrics: Inter-
303      section over Union (IoU) and Intersection over Minimum (IoM), as defined in Equation (9) in Appendix B.5. Note
304      that the IoU captures the overall overlap of the circuits relative to their combined size, whereas the IoM measures how
305      much of the smaller circuit is contained within the larger one.

      Figure 5 illustrates the cross-task accuracy of various circuits and their composites, derived from the union operation
453
      previously described. The results demonstrate the existence of composite circuits which acquire functional capacities
454   for subtasks that the original base circuits could not handle individually. For example, while neither the repeat nor
455   the reverse circuit can solve the echo task alone, their union achieves a notable accuracy of 78%. Similarly, the
456   composite of swap and reverse shows a performance improvement on the shift task, reaching an accuracy of
457   33%. For tasks that can be solved by at least one of the original circuits, the performance of their composite generally
458   reflects a combination of their individual accuracies. For instance, the repeat circuit achieves 90% accuracy on the
459   copy task, while the reverse circuit yields 0.0% accuracy. Their union achieves an intermediate accuracy of 80%
460   on the task. Similarly, the swap circuit achieves 27% accuracy on the reverse task, whereas the reverse circuit
461   reaches up to 96%. Their composite circuit achieves a balanced performance of 45%. This implies that improvements
462
      in accuracy for novel tasks may be accompanied by a decline in performance on previously mastered skills. Further
      experiments and results on subnetwork set operations can be found in Appendix C.1.4.
463
464   A possible explanation for these observations is that the union operation mT1 ,T2 = mT1 ∪ mT2 shifts the activation
465   space of mT1 towards mT2 , effectively transforming the input into a feature space that aligns with one of the subtasks
466   the model M can solve. For instance, the union mswap ∪ mreverse might shift the activation space of mswap towards
467   mreverse , essentially allowing the new circuit mswap, reverse to to perform the shift operation, while sacrificing
468
      some performance on the swap task.

471
472   Techniques that discover circuits through causal mediation analysis have been successfully applied across various
473   domains and tasks, including the study of gender bias in language models (Jeoung & Diesner, 2022; Chintam et al.,
474   2023), different forms of factual recall (Meng et al., 2022; Geva et al., 2023), subject-verb agreement (Chintam et al.,
475   2023), and arithmetic operations (Nanda et al., 2023; Hanna et al., 2024a). Finding subnetworks has also been of
476   interest outside of the mechanistic interpretability literature. Previous work has studied task-specific subnetworks
477
      through clustering approaches (Casper et al., 2022; Watanabe, 2019), pruning Csordás et al. (2020); Cao et al. (2021);
      Lepori et al. (2023), sparse fine-tuning (Ansell et al., 2022), and adapters (Pfeiffer et al., 2021; Rücklé et al., 2021).

485   What constitutes a circuit? We introduce activation pruning through continuous sparsification, a method that for-
      mulates the circuit discovery process as an optimization problem balancing task faithfulness and minimality. Our

498   results demonstrate that this method successfully identifies circuits that are both faithful and sparse for each subtask
499   within PCFG SET. However, as discussed in Section 2.2, circuits are further expected to be complete, meaning they
500   should include all nodes involved in the model’s computations for a given task. We emphasize that our current ap-
501   proach does not guarantee completeness. While some methods attempt to evaluate completeness by analyzing circuit
502   behavior under ablations (Wang et al., 2023), these approaches are computationally expensive, especially when dealing
503   with large circuit sizes. Future work is needed to develop more efficient ways to quantify completeness and incorpo-
504
      rate this objective into the optimization framework described in Equation (7). In addition, we observe that the circuits
      identified are influenced by the ablation value z̃ used during the identification process. For example, when comparing
505
      circuits based on mean versus zero ablation, we find notable differences in the nodes identified as causally relevant,
506
      even for zero-ablated circuits that demonstrate high task faithfulness (see results in Appendix C.2, specifically Fig-
507   ure 14). This raises a broader question about what truly defines a circuit. In our setup, a circuit’s behavior in response
508   to an input is influenced not only by the nodes deemed causally significant (mi = 1) but also by the values z̃ of the
509   nodes where mi = 0. Essentially, the behavior of a circuit is determined not merely by what it includes but also by
510   what it excludes, as similarly noted by Miller et al. (2024). We believe this is a general characteristic of methods that
511   rely on constant perturbations, such as zero and mean ablations (Olsson et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023).
512
513   Modularity in Neural Networks. Our results presented in Section 4.2 demonstrate that circuits responsible for
514   tasks such as echo, repeat and swap are capable of successfully completing the copy task, whereas the copy
515   circuit does not perform effectively on these other tasks. This raises the question: do the echo, repeat, and swap
516   circuits leverage the copy operation for their respective functions? Our analysis on node overlap suggests that the
517   copy circuit —which is notably the smallest among those identified, as shown in Figure 3a—is largely embedded
518   within these other circuits. Moreover, circuits that share functional similarities exhibit greater node overlap compared
519
      to those that are functionally distinct. While it is premature to draw definitive conclusions based on this experimental
      evidence, we underscore the general observation that more complex operations tend to engage a larger portion of
520
      the model’s activations and are capable of solving multiple tasks. Furthermore, we find that circuits combined via
521
      the union operator can represent novel functional capabilities. This might suggest some level of modularity within
522   the network. One might argue that our identification process has not produced truly minimal circuits. While it is
523   inherently challenging to determine whether a circuit is truly minimal, we have mitigated this concern by conducting
524   an extensive hyperparameter search, ensuring a fair balance between faithfulness and minimality. Lastly, it is important
525   to note that our study focuses on a small encoder-decoder model trained on a single dataset like PCFG SET. Naturally,
526   the question arises as to whether these findings will generalize to larger models commonly used in practice. This
527   touches on a broader concern within the field of mechanistic interpretability, where many studies are conducted on
528   smaller models and datasets (Elhage et al., 2022). While scaling our findings to larger models is an area for future
529   research, we believe the methodology used in this study offers valuable insights for future investigations into the
530
      compositionality of larger, more complex models.

532   7   C ONCLUSION
533
534   This study explores modularity in neural networks by analyzing circuits in a transformer-based language model for
535   highly compositional subtasks. Specifically, we train an encoder-decoder model using the PCFG SET dataset and
536   identify circuits responsible for ten modular string-edit operations. To achieve this, we introduce activation pruning
537   through continuous sparsification, a method that allows us to formulate circuit identification as minimization problem.
538   Our results demonstrate that this approach successfully identifies both faithful and sparse circuits for each subtask
539   within PCFG SET. Additionally, we assess all circuits by examining their cross-task performance and node overlap.
      Finally, we show that new functional circuits can be composed through set operations, such as the union of two circuits.

731
      edit operation for each subtask dataset, e.g, copy W1 O1 Z5 G1. For each SET operation, we generate 16 0000

732
      applications of that function, such as remove first remove first A1 , B1 , B1 A1 → B1 A1.

961   leading to degraded performance, which is one of the reasons for adopting mean ablation (Miller et al., 2024).
