010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            In this paper, we provide a causal analysis of binary spiking neural networks
013            (BSNNs) aimed at explaining their behaviors. We formally define a BSNN and
014            represent its spiking activity as a binary causal model. Thanks to this causal
015
               representation, we are able to explain the output of the network by leveraging logic-
               based methods. In particular, we show that we can successfully use a SAT (Boolean
016
               satisfiability) solver to compute abductive explanations from this binary causal
017
               model. To illustrate our approach, we trained the BSNN on the standard MNIST
018            dataset and applied our SAT-based method to finding abductive explanations of
019            the network’s classifications based on pixel-level features. We also compared the
020            found explanations against SHAP, a popular method used in the area of explainable
021            AI to explain “black box” classifiers. We show that, unlike SHAP, our method
022            guarantees that a found explanation does not contain completely irrelevant features.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   In recent times, interest in the study of binary artificial neural networks has grown, where binarization
028
      can occur at the level of the connection weights between the neural units, at the level of their
      activation function, or at both levels. In the field of AI, binarized neural networks (BNNs) were
029
      recently proposed by Hubara et al. (2016) and Rastegari et al. (2016), while in neuroscience particular
030
      attention has been paid to binary spiking neural networks (BSNNs) (Kheradpisheh et al., 2022;
031   Lu & Sengupta, 2020). The main difference between BNNs and BSNNs is mainly due to the
032   presence of temporal dynamics in BSNNs over BNNs and to the fact that in BSNNs inputs are
033   given sequentially in discrete time, while they are instantaneously presented to BNNs. Binarization
034   obviously comes with a price on the size of the network parameters in relation to its learning power:
035   a binary neural network requires a considerably higher number of neural units, compared to its
036   non-binary counterpart, in order to achieve an acceptable level of accuracy in a given classification
037   task after training. Nonetheless, this disadvantage is counterbalanced by an advantage in terms of
038   logical representability and therefore explainability. Specifically, thanks to the Boolean nature of
039
      BNNs and BSNNs, one can represent their firing dynamics as binary causal models and, consequently,
      explain their behaviors in an efficient way using propositional logic.
040
041   The present paper is devoted to exploring this trade-off between accuracy and explainability in
042   the context of BSNNs. We focus our analysis on BSNNs instead of BNNs since, from the causal
043   point of view, the former are more general than the latter and we prefer to concentrate on the more
044   general model first. To fully capture the causal structure of a BSNN, one has to model the firing
045
      activities of its neural units and to represent their causal dependencies over an extended time span.
      BNNs are less general since the presentation of the input is not sequential and, consequently, their
046
      dynamics and the resulting causal dependencies between the neural units do not extend over time.
047
      We represent the internal mechanism of a BSNN through a binary causal model and, thanks to this
048   representation, we explain the BSNN’s behavior. Different notions of explanation exist in the literature
049   including abductive (Ignatiev et al., 2019), contrastive (Miller, 2021), counterfactual (Verma et al.,
050   2020) and alterfactual (Mertes et al., 2024) explanation. In the present paper, we rely on abductive
051   explanation (AXp) because of its simplicity and its emphasis of minimality which is a guarantee of
052   non-redundancy. For a set of input features to be an abductive explanation of a classification by a
053   neural network, it has to be minimally sufficient to ensure the classification, i.e., where minimality
      means that all proper subsets of features are no longer sufficient for the classification. Thus, an

054
      AXp is by definition non-redundant. More details about the use of abductive explanation in machine
055   learning are given in Section 2.
056
057   The paper is structured as follows. After having discussed the related work (Section 2), in Section
058
      3 we illustrate the BSNN architecture as well as the learning task we considered, namely MNIST
      classification, and the learning algorithm we used to train our BSNNs on the MNIST dataset. We put
059
      special emphasis on the levels of accuracy we reached depending on the type of weight quantization
060
      of the neural network, including binary quantization (i.e., weights range in {0, 1}) and three-valued
061   quantization (i.e., weights range in {−1, 0, 1}). In Section 4, we introduce the mathematical model of
062   the BSNN spiking dynamics. Then, in Section 5 we map it into a binary causal model that represents
063   the causal dependencies between the firing activities of the neural units over time. Thanks to its binary
064   nature, such causal dependencies are representable through a system of Boolean equations. Section
065   6 is devoted to the explanation of the BSNN behavior. Specifically, we present an algorithm that
066   combines the binary causal model with a SAT solver to compute abductive explanations of the BSNN
067   classification, where an abductive explanation is constructed from pixel-level features at a specific
068   time point. In Section 7, we present some experimental results on computation time for the algorithm.
069
      Finally, in Section 8 we compare our logic-based approach relying on abductive explanation with
      SHAP, a well-known method in the area of explainable AI. As far as we know this is the first attempt
070
      i) to map a BSNN into a binary causal model and ii) to exploit the resulting Boolean representation
071
      of the causal dependencies between its neural units for explaining its behavior through a SAT solver.

074   2    R ELATED W ORK
075
076
      Binarized Neural Networks (BNNs) are a class of artificial neural networks (ANNs) that have been
077
      studied extensively by researchers (Qin et al., 2020) in the deep learning community, especially by
078
      Bengio et al. (2013) and Hubara et al. (2016), who provided a viable way to train these networks
079   using standard back-prop based optimisation methods. BNNs adopt an extreme form of quantization,
080   by resorting to binary weights and binary activation values. Tang et al. (2017) have shown that with
081   back-prop based methods, it is possible to train these binarized neural networks with reasonable, near
082   full precision accuracy. Moreover, Rastegari et al. (2016) have demonstrated a drastic reduction in
083   computation time and model size with XNOR-Nets owing to the fact that computationally expensive
084   multiply-accumulate methods in deep learning can be simplified to faster XNOR and pop-count
085   operations with binarized networks. Hence, due to the afore-mentioned reasons, BNNs have gained
086   immense popularity for resource constrained, low power, hardware efficient applications of AI. Binary
087
      Spiking Neural Networks (BSNNs), the subject of the present paper, are the bio-plausible counterpart
      of BNNs, that take inspiration from the spiking dynamics of biological neurons in the brain. The
088
      most useful feature of BSNNs is the way in which they process input data in terms of spike encodings,
089
      where spikes are binary all-or-none pulses in discrete time steps compared to their continuous valued
090   ANN counterparts (including BNNs). These spike encodings are very convenient, as they allow us to
091   use our formalism on both the pixel space and intermediate feature space. Works have been done to
092   train BSNNs using both temporal (Kheradpisheh et al., 2022) as well as rate coding schemes (Lu &
093   Sengupta, 2020).
094
      Causal models are mathematical objects that have been extensively studied in AI (Pearl, 2009), in
095
      logic (Halpern, 2000; 2016) and, more recently, in the field of explainable AI (Miller, 2021). They
096   play a crucial role in the domain of explainable AI given the urgent need to provide formally rigorous
097   causal explanations of the behavior of AI systems. A causal model is a system of structural equations
098   describing the causal dependencies between variables. Binary causal models (BCMs) that we use in
099   the present work are the subclass of causal models in which variables are assumed to Boolean. They
100   were defined and studied in depth in previous work (Chockler & Halpern, 2004; Aleksandrowicz
101   et al., 2017; Lorini, 2023; de Lima & Lorini, 2024). Given their close connection with propositional
102   logic, they offer the possibility to automate reasoning about causality with the aid of a SAT solver.
103   Abductive explanation (AXp), the concept of explanation on which we rely in the present work, is
104   widely used in the domain of explainable AI (Cooper & Marques-Silva, 2023). It is grounded on
105   previous theoretical work on abduction (Marquis, 1991) and relies on the notion of prime implicant
106   (PI). Thus, it is also called PI-explanation (Shih et al., 2018) or sufficient reason (Darwiche & Hirth,
107   2020). It has been extensively used in AI to explain tractable models such as monotone or linear
      classifiers (Marques-Silva et al., 2020; Cooper & Marques-Silva, 2023; Audemard et al., 2020) as well


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      as intractable ones such as random forests (Izza & Marques-Silva, 2021) and boosted trees (Audemard
109   et al., 2023). It was used by Shi et al. (2020) and Ignatiev et al. (2019) to explain artificial neural
110   networks. On the one hand, Shi et al. (2020) compile binary neural networks into Ordered Binary
111   Decision Diagrams (OBDDs) and use the latter to compute AXps of the networks’ classifications.
112   Ignatiev et al. (2019) compute AXps of a neural network’s classification in a three-digit MNIST
113   classification task using a MILP (Mixed Integer Linear Programming) encoding. Unlike us and Shi
114   et al. (2020), Ignatiev et al. (2019) consider neural networks with real-valued weights. Two major
115   novel contributions of our work compared to Ignatiev et al. (2019) and Shi et al. (2020) are the
116   following. First and foremost, the notion of causality is crucial in our approach: we map a BSNN
117
      into a binary causal model and exploit this causal representation to explain it. There is no causality
      involved in Ignatiev et al. (2019) and Shi et al. (2020)’s analyses. Secondly, they do not consider
118
      BSNNs, while BSNNs are the central object of our analysis and the type of neural networks we want
119
      to explain with the help of logic and causal models.

      3.1   L EARNING TASK
128
129   For our training purposes, we used the MNIST classification task for hand written digit recognition.
130   We trained networks with a single fully connected hidden layer on both tasks, 3-digit and 10-digit
131   MNIST classification. As we will show in Table 1 of Section 4, we could achieve very high accuracy
132   with binary quantized networks on the 3-digit classification task. We could also achieve a high
133   accuracy on the 10-digit classification task with three-value quantized networks with weights ranging
134   over {−1, 0, 1}. In the experimental analysis of computation time for searching an explanation we
135   will present in Section 7, we only focus on 3-digit MNIST classification with binary SNNs.
136
137   3.2   S PIKE ENCODING
138
      For our experiments, we used two different approaches to convert MNIST images into spikes. Firstly,
139
      we used a classic Poisson rate coding scheme (Prescott & Sejnowski, 2008) to convert images into
140   spike trains in multiple time-steps and also a threshold-binarized scheme with just one time-step as
141   presented in Table 1 of Section 4. We did not pursue temporal coding in our experiments since, as
142   shown by Kheradpisheh et al. (2022), temporal coding requires larger time-steps for training with
143   high accuracy. Since having more time-steps significantly increases the complexity of finding an
144   explanation, we chose to not use temporal coding in this work. Nonetheless, the novel mapping of
145   BSNNs into binary causal models we will present in Section 5 can be generalized to to other forms
146   of spike encodings. We used a simple Integrate and Fire (IF) model for our spiking neurons, since
147   mapping BSNNs into binary causal models is easier in the absence of leaks.

      5    C AUSAL M ODEL
277
      A causal model is a mathematical object describing the causal dependencies between variables. As
278
      emphasized in Section 2, it is a central concept of current analyses of causality in AI. A binary
279
      causal model (BCM) is nothing but a causal model in which variables are assumed to be Boolean.
280   In a BCM causal information is expressed by means of Boolean expressions (alias propositional
281   formulas), the set of Boolean expressions being generated inductively as follows: i) each Boolean
282   variable p and symbol ⊥ (“contradiction”) are Boolean expressions; ii) if ω is a Boolean expression,
283   so is ¬ω (“negation”); iii) if ω1 and ω2 are Boolean expressions, so is ω1 ∧ ω2 (“conjunction”).
284   Additional Boolean connectives are definable as abbreviations in the usual way: ⊤ =def ¬⊥;
285   ω1 ∨ ω2 =def ¬(¬ω1 ∧ ¬ω2 ) (“disjunction”); ω1 → ω2 =def ¬ω1 ∨ ω2 (“implication”); ω1 ↔
286   ω2 =def (ω1 → ω2 ) ∧ (ω2 → ω1 ). In formal terms, a BCM is a triplet Γ = (U, V, E) where i) U
287   is a set of exogenous variables, ii) V is a set of endogenous variables, iii) E is a function mapping
288
      each endogenous variable p ∈ V to a Boolean expression E(p) of the form p ↔ ωp , where ωp is a
      Boolean expression built from U ∪ V that does not contain p. Specifically, the Boolean expression
289
      p ↔ ωp stipulates that the endogenous variable p is true iff the condition ωp is true. It can be seen
290
      as the compact representation of a Boolean function for the endogenous variable p. From a binary
291   causal model Γ = (U, V, E) it is straightforward to extract a causal graph representing the causal
292   dependencies between the variables: the vertices of the causal graph are the variables in U ∪ V, and
293   we draw an edge from a variable q to an endogenous variable p if the Boolean expression ωp such
294   that E(p) = p ↔ ωp contains the variable q.
295
      The model of the BSNN given in Definition 1 can be mapped into a BCM that represents the causal
296
      dependencies between the BSNN’s neural units over time. The idea of the mapping is simple: we
297   assign a Boolean variable pX,t to each neuron X for each time t in {0, . . . , tend }, where tend is the
298   final time step at which the network stops receiving incoming spike train from the image currently
299   being presented, as mentioned in Section 3.2 . The variable pX,t is true (resp. false) if the neuron
300   X fires (resp. does not fire) at time t. The exogenous variables are for the input neurons, while the
301   endogenous ones are for the internal neurons. The causal dependencies between the firing activities
302   of the neurons are represented by the Boolean equations. Here, we only give the BCM for the variants
303   of the BSNN with Boolean weights {0, 1}. Due to space restrictions, we could only include the BCM
304   for the variants with three-valued weights {−1, 0, 1} in Section A.2 of the Appendix.

      and computing explanations in the context of the BSNN architectures we trained for the MNIST
344

345

346   relation to a binary causal model. For simplicity, we assume an AXp (the explanans) is a term made
347   of exogenous variables and the property to be explained (the explanandum) is a Boolean expression
348   made of endogenous ones. This assumption is perfectly compatible with our application to the
349   MNIST classification task in which we want to explain the network classification on the basis of the
350   pixel-level features. Nonetheless, this assumption could be dropped with no repercussion, we would
351   only need to suppose that the explanans and the explanandum are made of different variables.
352   Some preliminary notions are needed before defining AXp formally. We define a term to be a

356   subset) of the set of literals appearing in λ. Given a BCM Γ = U, V, E and an arbitrary set of
357   variables X ⊆ U ∪ V, we note Term X the set of terms built from X.

      8    C OMPARISON WITH SHAP

      In this section, we compare our logic-based explainability method with SHAP, a popular method
      widely used for interpreting predictions of machine learning models (Lundberg & Lee, 2017). For
464
      our experiments, we used the pre-existing implementation of SHAP library in Python available at
465
      https://github.com/shap/shap. SHAP assigns relevance scores to input features based
466   on a sample of the input space without taking into consideration the internal dynamics of the model.
467   Unlike our method based on causal models and abductive explanation, SHAP does not look inside
468   the neural network and does not model the network’s internal causal structure. Despite its widespread
469   use, it has been recently shown that SHAP could assign a high relevance score to misleading or
470   irrelevant features (Huang & Marques-Silva, 2024; 2023b;a; Letoffe et al., 2024). As discussed by
471   Ignatiev (2020), another limitation of SHAP is that, unlike abductive explanation, it does not take
472   minimality of an explanation into account. In Figure 2, we can see in red the positive (resp. in blue
473   the negative) SHAP values assigned to input pixel-level features. To compare SHAP with our method,
474
      we fixed a threshold δ for the SHAP score and then identified the set of relevant features as those
      features whose SHAP score is strictly higher than δ if positive and strictly lower than −δ if negative.

523   9   C ONCLUSION
524
525   Let’s take stock. We have proposed a causal analysis of Binary Spiking Neural Networks (BSNNs)
526   by mapping the models of their spiking dynamics into binary causal models (BCMs). Thanks to this
527   mapping, we have been able to compute abductive explanations of BSNN’s decisions in the context
528   of the MNIST classification task using a SAT-based approach. We have moreover compared our
529   logic-based method with SHAP and highlighted the fact that, unlike SHAP, our method prevents
530   causally irrelevant features from being mentioned in an explanation. In the current work, we only
531
      focused on the notion of abductive explanation (AXp). Future work will be devoted to extending
      our causal analysis of BSNNs to the notions of actual cause (Halpern & Pearl, 2005) and NESS
532
      (Necessary Element of a Sufficient Set) cause (Beckers, 2021; Halpern, 2008). Our causal framework
533
      offers the appropriate level of expressiveness to formally represent these notions and, we believe, the
534   SAT-based approach we used for computing abductive explanations can be leveraged to compute
535   some of these notions too. Another direction of future research is to provide a causal analysis of
536   convolutional BSNNs (C-BSNNs) (Srinivasan & Roy, 2019) along the lines of the present work. We
537   believe that adding convolutional layers to the network could improve accuracy in more complex
538   datasets. Last but not least, we intend to go beyond simple visual classification tasks and leverage
539   our logic-based causal framework to explain BSNNs trained on language datasets (Bal & Sengupta,
      2024).
