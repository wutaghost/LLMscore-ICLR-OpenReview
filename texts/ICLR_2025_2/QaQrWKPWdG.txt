                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Understanding where a particular species can or cannot be found is crucial for
               ecological research and conservation efforts. By mapping the spatial ranges of all
011
               species on Earth, we could obtain deeper insights into how global biodiversity is
012
               affected by climate change and habitat loss. However, accurate range estimates are
013            available for a relatively small proportion of known species. For most species, we
014            have only have a few prior observations indicating the locations where they have
015            been previously recorded. In this work we address the challenge of training with
016            limited observations by developing a new approach for few-shot species range es-
017            timation. During inference, our model takes a set of spatial coordinates as input,
018            along with optional metadata such as text, and outputs a species encoding that
019            can be used to predict the range of a previously unseen species in feed-forward
020            manner. We validate our method on two challenging benchmarks, where we ob-
021
               tain state-of-the-art performance in predicting the ranges of unseen species, in a
               fraction of the compute time, compared to recent alternative approaches.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Understanding the spatial distribution of plant and animal species is essential to mitigate the ongoing
028   decline in global biodiversity (Jetz et al., 2019). Monitoring these distributions over time allows us to
029   quantify the effects of climate change, habitat loss, and conservation interventions (Mantyka-pringle
030   et al., 2012). An estimate of the species’ distribution typically starts with a collection of location
031   data where the species is confirmed to be present. Traditionally, this data is used to train a models
032   that generate detailed predictions over a region of interest (Elith et al., 2006; Beery et al., 2021).
033
      When sufficient data is available, these models enable practitioners to estimate important quantities
      such as the spatial range (i.e., where a species can be found) or abundance (i.e., the total number of
034
      individuals) of a species, in addition to quantifying how these quantities are changing over time.
035
036   Despite the availability of well-established modeling techniques, our current understanding of
037   species’ distributions is extremely limited due to little or no observational data being available for
038   most species. For example, iNaturalist, one of the largest citizen science platform documenting
039
      global biodiversity, has collected over 130 million “research grade” observations for approximately
      373,000 species globally (iNaturalist, 2024). However, the data is severely long-tailed: a small per-
040
      centage of common species account for the majority of the observations, while many species have
041
      very few observations. In fact, over half of the 373,000 species catalogued by iNaturalist have been
042   observed fewer than 10 times. This data limitation is amplified by the fact that there are estimated to
043   be several million species on earth, many of which are not yet documented by science (Mora et al.,
044   2011). Identifying locations where under-observed species can be found is a time consuming and
045   laborious process, often requiring long expeditions in remote locations searching for species that
046   are hard to find. Consequently, there is a pressing need for computational methods that can reliably
047   estimate the spatial distributions of species using only a small number of observations.
048   Knowing the range of one species can help predict the range of another due to shared ecological, en-
049   vironmental, and geographic contexts. Recent advances in range estimation have leveraged this idea
050   by training shared models using millions of observations across tens of thousands of species (Cole
051   et al., 2023). However, these models still rely on relatively large numbers of training observations
052   for individual species, which limits their applicability to species with sparse observations. In this
053   work, we introduce a novel Transformer-based model that overcomes this limitation and offers two
      key advantages over previous approaches. First, our method achieves superior performance in the

      Species Distribution Modeling. Estimating the spatial distribution of species has been a widely
      explored topic both in statistical ecology and machine learning (Beery et al., 2021). The goal is to
093
      develop models that can predict the distribution of species in space, and possibly time, given sparse
094
      observation data. In the context of machine learning, different approaches based on traditional
095   techniques such as decision trees have been extensively explored (Phillips et al., 2004; Elith et al.,
096   2006). More recently, several deep learning methods have been introduced for the task (Botella et al.,
097   2018; Mac Aodha et al., 2019; Kellenberger et al., 2024). One of the strengths of deep methods is
098   that they can jointly represent thousands of different species inside of the same model and have
099   been shown to improve as more training data is added. For example, in SINR (Cole et al., 2023),
100   the authors demonstrated that range estimation performance improves as more data from different
101   species is added.
102   There has also been work investigating different approaches for addressing some of the challenges
103   associated with training and evaluating these models. Examples include attempts to addresses
104   imbalances across species in the training observation data (Zbinden et al., 2024b), methods for
105   sampling pseudo-absence data (Zbinden et al., 2024a), biases in the training locations (Chen &
106   Gomes, 2019), representing location information (Rußwurm et al., 2024), discretizing continuous
107   model predictions (Dorm et al., 2024), using additional metadata such as images (Teng et al., 2023;
      Dollinger et al., 2024; Picek et al., 2024), and designing new evaluation datasets to benchmark


                                                               2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      performance (Cole et al., 2023; Picek et al., 2024). In our work, we investigate the under-explored
109   few-shot setting, where only limited observations (e.g., fewer than ten) are available for each species.
110
111   Few-shot Species Range Estimation. There are several aspects of the species range estimation
112
      task in the low data regime that makes it different from other few-shot applications more commonly
      explored in the literature (Parnami & Lee, 2022; Wang et al., 2020). For one, the input domain is
113
      fixed (i.e., all the locations on earth), each location can support more than one species (i.e., multi-
114
      label as opposed to multi-class), the label space is much larger (i.e., tens of thousands of species
115   as opposed to hundreds of classes in image classification), and only partial supervision is available
116   (i.e., we only have presence data, with no confirmed absences).
117
118
      Some attempts have been make at training species range estimation models using limited amounts
      of observation data. Cole et al. (2023) demonstrated that their SINR approach performs much worse
119
      when trained on at most ten observations per species compared to training larger amounts. Lange
120
      et al. (2023) proposed an active learning-based approach for estimating the ranges of previously
121   unseen species. They performed experiments in the low data regime, but in contrast to us, they
122   require confirmed absence observations, in addition to presences, when updating their model for an
123   unseen species. The zero-shot setting, i.e., where no location observations have been observed, has
124   also been explored. Specifically, LD-SDM (Sastry et al., 2023) used text information to encode the
125   taxonomic knowledge and LE-SINR (Hamilton et al., 2024) used text describing a species’ range or
126   preferred habitat. At inference time, these zero-shot methods can make predictions for previously
127   unseen species even when no observation (i.e., location) information was available, but when text
128   is. LE-SINR performed few-shot experiments whereby they used a language encoder to estimate
129
      an initial encoding for a species and combined it with a linear classifier that needs to be trained
      to generate range predictions. In contrast, our FS-SINR approach does not require any additional
130
      training to make predictions for previously unseen species at inference time. We compare to LE-
131
      SINR in our evaluation and demonstrate that we outperform it in both the zero and few-shot settings
132   and also show that free-form text is superior to the taxonomic text used in LD-SDM.

140   3.1   S PECIES R ANGE E STIMATION
141
      We start by describing the SINR approach from Cole et al. (2023). Let x = (lat, lon) ∈ X be a
142   location of interest sampled from a spatial domain X (e.g., the surface of the earth). Our goal is
143   train a model g() : X → [0, 1]s to predict the probability of s different species of interest occurring
144   at x. We will write ŷ = g(x), where ŷj ∈ [0, 1] (the j th entry of ŷ) denotes the probability that
145   species j occurs at location x.
146
      We decompose the model as g() = hϕ () ◦ fθ (), where fθ () : X → Rd is a location encoder
147
      with parameters θ and hϕ () : Rd → [0, 1]s is a multi-label classifier with parameters ϕ. The
148
      location encoder fθ () maps a location x to a d-dimensional latent embedding fθ (x). The multi-
149
      label classifier h() is implemented as a per-species linear projection followed by an element-wise
150   sigmoid non-linearity, meaning that ŷ = σ(fθ (x)W ), where W ∈ Rd×s (i.e., hϕ () = ϕ = W )
151   and σ() is the sigmoid function. Thus, each column vector ws of W can be viewed as a species
152   embedding, which we can combine with a location embedding fθ (x) in an inner product to compute
153   the probability that the species s is present at x. Importantly, the location embedding is shared
154   across all species. Once trained, it is then possible to generate a prediction for a given species for
155   all locations of interest (e.g., the entire surface of the earth) by evaluating the model at all locations
156   (i.e., x ∈ X ).
157   One of the main challenges associated with training models for species range estimation is that
158   there is a dramatic asymmetry in the available training data. Specifically, it is much easier to col-
159   lect presence observations (i.e., confirmed sightings of a species) than absence observations (i.e.,
160   confirmation that a species is not present at a specific location). As a result, many methods have
161   been developed to train models using presence-only data. In the presence-only setting, we have
      access to training pairs (x, z), where x is a geographic location and z ∈ {1, . . . , s} is an integer

195   3.2   F EW- SHOT R ANGE E STIMATION
196
      For the SINR model to make predictions for a new species, it is necessary to learn a new embedding
197
      vector ws for that species. If additional location data is later observed for that species, the model
198   must be updated again. However, the number of observations, with associated locations, for uncom-
199   mon species can be limited and thus it is necessary to have methods that can be updated efficiently
200   with limited training data.
201
      We address this challenge by proposing a new approach for few-shot species range estimation called
202
      FS-SINR. Our model can predict the probability of presence for a previously unobserved species
203
      directly at inference time given only the set of confirmed presence locations available, without any
204   retraining or parameter updates. At inference time we assume we have access to a set of context
205   locations C s = {c1 , . . . , ck }, which represent a set of k locations where the species s has been
206   confirmed to be present. Each entry is this set represents a geographic location, i.e., c = (lat, lon).
207   Like SINR, our model is also conditioned on a location x of interest (i.e., the ‘query’ location), but
208   uses the context locations to inform the prediction for the query location. Note, these locations can
209   come from a species that was not previously seen by the model during training.
210   We represent our FS-SINR model as g(x) = mψ (fθ (x), C s ). Unlike in SINR where the classifier
211   head hϕ () is a simple multi-label classifier and sigmoid non-linearity, in our case the ‘head’ of the
212   model mψ () is a Transformer-based encoder (Vaswani et al., 2017). An illustration of FS-SINR is
213   depicted in Fig. 2. The model takes an unordered set of context locations C s as input, where each
214   location is encoded into an embedding vector (i.e., token) via a SINR-style multi-layer perceptron
215   location encoder. Importantly, our model is invariant to the number and ordering of the context
      locations as we do not append any positional embeddings. This flexibility ensures that our model


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      can process a variable number of context locations at inference. We also append an additional
217   register token (REG) as in (Darcet et al., 2024) to provide the model with an additional token to
218   ‘store’ useful information. Given that the input sequence is unordered and may or may not include
219   additional context information, we also add additional learned ‘embedding type’ vectors to each
220   token such that the Transformer knows if a given input token is a location, or register, text, etc.
221
      We represent the species embedding vector (i.e., ws in SINR) as the class token CLS of the Trans-
222
      former after passing it through a small species decoder MLP s(). To make a final prediction, we
223   simply compute the inner product between the location embedding of the query location x and the
224   species embedding vector, and pass it through a sigmoid. Our approach is computationally efficient
225   in that once the species embedding is generated once it can then be efficiently multiplied by the
226   embeddings for all locations of interest to generate a prediction for a species’ range.
227
      FS-SINR uses a similar training loss to LAN−full . However as FS-SINR has no equivalent to hϕ ()

230   forward pass which can be used to predict probabilities of presence for that species for all locations
231   in the batch. We denote this modification as LAN−full−b , which indicates that we are considering

238   3.2.1    A DDITIONAL C ONTEXT I NFORMATION
239
240   The design of FS-SINR is flexible, in that we can also provide additional context information to the
241   model if it is available. For example, if there is additional text (e.g., a range description) or visual
242   (e.g., images) information available for a novel species it could be added to the context, assuming
243
      such information was also available at training time for other species. This observation is inspired
      by recent work that also uses language derived information to improve range predictions (Sastry
244
      et al., 2023; Hamilton et al., 2024). This additional information can provide a rich source of meta-
245
      data encoding aspects of a species’ habitat preferences, even when there might only be a limited
246   number of location observations available for it. We can represent the expanded context vector as
247   C s = {ts , c1 , . . . , ck }, where ts denotes a fixed length text embedding from a large language model
248   extracted for species s. While, not explored in this work it is also possible to include addition context
249   modalities such as a fixed-length embedding vector from a pretrained vision model.

      4.1     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
257
      Our location encoders use the same fully connected neural network with residual connections as
258   in (Cole et al., 2023). Each of the context locations is processed by the same shared location encoder
259   which is first pretrained as in SINR after which the multi-label classifier head is discarded. Impor-
260   tantly, this pretrained encoder is only trained on species from the training set, and does not observe
261   any data from the evaluation species. The text embedding backbone is a frozen GritLM (Muen-
262   nighoff et al., 2024) which provides a fixed length embedding vector. We train a small two layer
263   fully connected text encoder to transform this into the text token. The Transformer contains four en-
264   coder layers and the parameters are updated jointly with the location and text encoders and species
265   decoder during training. In total, our model has 6.3M learnable parameters compared to 11.9M for
266
      SINR. We train with a batch size of 2,048 instances and randomly drop-out text or location tokens
      during training with a probability of 0.2 and 0.1 respectively to enhance robustness.
267
268   We train our model using the presence-only dataset from (Cole et al., 2023) which contains 35.5
269   million citizen science observations for 47,375 species from the iNaturalist platform (iNaturalist,
      2024). During training we supply our model with 20 context locations per training example, though


                                                            5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      we find that the model performance is very robust to the number of context locations provided dur-
271   ing training. We evaluate models using the IUCN and S&T datasets, which contain 2,418 and 535
272   expert and model-derived binary species range maps respectively. The IUCN dataset is more glob-
273   ally distributed and contains a larger variation in range size across species, while the S&T dataset
274   only contains bird species that are primarily, but not always, found in North America and have a
275   larger range size. We note that the evaluation datasets used could contain errors, but they represent
276   the currently best available data and contain large variety in terms of range size and location. Impor-
277   tantly, unless otherwise stated, we hold out any species from the union of these two datasets from
278   the training set so that species from the evaluation set are not observed during training. As a result,
279
      by default, our model is trained data from 44,422 species. Performance is reported in terms of mean
      average precision (MAP).
280
281   At inference time, generating a species’ range for our FS-SINR model for a held-out species only
282   requires a single forward pass through the model to get an embedding vector for the species. Current
283   methods cannot be used in such a feed forward manner and need to be retrained for each species
284   that were not observed at training time. To obtain an equivalent embedding for our baselines (i.e.,
285
      SINR and LE-SINR) we train a per-species binary logistic regression classifier using any few-shot
      presence observations that are available in addition to adding 10,000 uniformly random and 10,000
286
      target (i.e., in locations where species are) pseudo-absences as in Hamilton et al. (2024). For fairness,
287
      we keep the presence observations consistent across each method and the larger number of presences
288   are supersets of the smaller ones. Additional implementation details are provided in Appendix A.

      4.2   F EW- SHOT E VALUATION
292
      First we evaluate how effective different range estimation models are at few-shot range estimation.
293
      The goal for each model is to generate a plausible prediction for a previously unseen species’ range
294   given limited location observations. Quantitative results are presented in Fig. 3.
295
296   The SINR (Cole et al., 2023) baseline performs poorly in the low data regime, but as more data
297
      is added performance improves. As noted earlier, here a per-species embedding vector is learned
      using logistic regression using the provided presence samples and generated pseudo-absences. The
298
      recently introduced LE-SINR (Hamilton et al., 2024) approach extends the basic SINR model to use
299
      text information, when available, at inference time. We can see that when any form of text data is
300   available, LE-SINR outperforms SINR.
301
302   In all instances, when the same metadata is available, FS-SINR outperforms existing methods. Fur-
303
      thermore, we also outperform SINR in the larger data regime (i.e., when 50 observations are avail-
      able). Importantly, unlike the baselines we compare to, FS-SINR does not need to be retrained at
304
      inference time. Instead, it can make predictions in a feed forward manner irrespective of the context
305
      data available. This is advantageous in interactive settings, whereby the model can compute the
306   SINR locations encodings for all query locations on earth once and then the user could experiment
307   by adding different context information interactively.
308
309
      We present qualitative results for three different species in Fig. 4 where we visualize FS-SINR’s
      predictions as we change the number of context locations. Given only a single context location,
310
      the model does a sensible job at localizing the species on the earth. This supports the findings
311
      from Fig. 3 where we observe strong performance even when only one context location is available.
312   When more information is provided, the predicted range more closely resembles the expert-derived
313   range shown in the first row. However, we do note that the model can still make mistakes in our low
314   data setting, such as the erroneous predictions for the ‘Black and White Warbler’ in South America.
315   In Fig. 5 we illustrate some examples of how text information, when paired with limited context
316   locations, can influence the model predictions. Here we observe dramatically different predicted
317   ranges when the text prompt encourages the model to focus on different habitat types. We note
318   that each of the predicted ranges are still consistent with the location of the single context location
319   provided. Additional qualitative examples are provided in the appendix.

      6   C ONCLUSION
417
418
      We have limited knowledge regarding the geographic distributions of the majority of species on
419   earth. This lack of understanding is further hampered by the fact that we also have insufficient data
420   to train models to estimate their ranges. To address this problem, we introduced FS-SINR, a new
421   approach for few-shot species range estimation. We demonstrated that our approach is naturally
422   able to fuse data from different modalities and at inference time can make plausible predictions for
423   the ranges of previously unseen species. Our quantitative analysis, using expert-derived range maps,
424   shows that we obtain a 5% to 10% improvement in performance compared to current approaches in
425   the low data setting for previously unseen species, e.g., when the number of observations equals ten.
426   Additionally, we also outperform existing methods in the zero-shot setting. While our results are
427
      promising, they also indicate that there are many potential opportunities for future improvements in
      this important topic.

      A.1   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
731
732   Our FS-SINR architecture consists of four components: The location encoder, f ; the text encoder,
733   t; the transformer encoder, e; and the species decoder, s. These components comprise of 6,311,680
734   learnable parameters in total. All non-linearities in FS-SINR are ReLUs.
735
      The location encoder, f , is identical to the the one used in Hamilton et al. (2024), which is taken
736
      from (Cole et al., 2023). It is composed of an initial linear layer and ReLU nonlinearity followed by
737
      four residual layers, where each is a two layer fully connected network with residual connections (He
738   et al., 2015) between the input and output of each residual layer. Each layer contains 256 neurons,
739   and there are 527,616 learnable parameters in total.
740
741
      The text encoder, t, follows the structure of text-based species encoder from Hamilton et al. (2024).
      In t, a pretrained and frozen large language model, GritLM (Muennighoff et al., 2024), is used to
742
      produce a fixed 4,096 length embedding from input text. This is then passes through a smaller
743
      network to reduce the dimensionality to 256. This smaller network comprises of two residual layers
744   with a hidden layer size of 512. In total, the text encoder contains 3,410,432 learnable parameters.
745
746   The transformer encoder, e, takes in an arbitrary length set of unordered 256 dimensional to-
747
      kens produced by f and t as well as two learned tokens that are added to each set of inputs.
      The “CLS”, class, token produces the species range, and a “Register” token, inspired by (Darcet
748
      et al., 2024), acts as an additional repository of global information during encoding. Element-
749
      wise addition between each token and one of four learned 256 dimensional “token type embed-
750   dings” is performed to allow the model to differentiate between tokens from different sources. The
751   transformer itself is composed of four transformer encoder layers, implemented using PyTorch’s
752   nn.TransformerEncoderLayer (Paszke et al., 2019), based on (Vaswani et al., 2017). Key-
753   Query-Value multi-head attention is used with two “heads”. The feed forward components contain
754   512 neurons per layer, while the token dimensionality is 256. Layer norm is used in each layer,
755   using a default epsilon value of 1e-5 for enhanced numerical stability. In total, e contains 2,176,256
      learnable parameters.


                                                       14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      Finally the species decoder, s, is a simple fully connected network with two hidden layers. Each
757   layer contains 256 neurons, and in total the decoder contains 197,376 learnable parameters.

      A.2    T RAINING
761
      For all training we use the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2015) with a learning rate of 0.0005,
762
      and an exponential learning rate scheduler with a learning rate decay of 0.98 per epoch, and we
763   use a batch size of 2048. Our training data comes from Cole et al. (2023), comprising of 35.5
764   million species observations with locations, covering 47,375 species observed prior to 2022 on the
765   iNaturalist platform. However, we remove all species that are found in our evaluation datasets,
766   leaving us with 44,181 species in our training set.
767
      Training comprises of two steps. First, the location encoder, f , is trained. This follows the training
768
      procedure of Cole et al. (2023) using the LAN-full loss function with the positive weighting, λ, set to
769   2,048, training for 20 epochs with a dropout of 0.5. To reduce training time without significantly
770   impacting performance we only train on a maximum of 1,000 examples per-species, as done in Cole
771   et al. (2023). Thus our training dataset contains 13.8 million location observations. Secondly, we
772   train all components of FS-SINR, except the pretrained large language model, using our LAN-full-b
773   loss with λ set to 2,048. We train the location encoder, f , again as this improves performance
774   compared to freezing it, seen in Fig. A4. For this part of training we use a dropout of 0.05. We further
775   reduce the training data used to a maximum of 100 examples per-species, leaving 4.0 million training
776   examples, which again increases training speed without a significant impact on performance, as seen
777
      in Fig. A5.
778   Each instance in the training set is used once per epoch as a training example to compute the loss.
779   The training example is not passed through the transformer encoder, e, and so does not contribute to
780   making the species embedding vector produced by this part of the model. Instead, additional con-
781   text information is provided to produce the species embedding. With a 0.7 probability this context
782   information is comprised of 20 context locations and a section of text describing the target species.
783
      With 0.2 probability, only the 20 context locations are provided, and with 0.1 probability only the
      section of text is provided to the model. These context locations are taken from the training data
784
      for the target species. As such, a single instance from the training set can be used multiple times
785
      per epoch, once as a training example, and potentially many times as a context point. The impact of
786   different distributions of context information provided during training is shown in Fig. A2.
787
788   For the text inputs required during this stage of training, we use the text dataset from (Hamilton et al.,
789
      2024) comprising of multiple sections of Wikipedia articles for each species in the train set where
      these are available. This dataset contains 127,484 sections from 37,889 species’ articles. Note, that
790
      not all 44,181 train species have text data available. When text is not available during training and
791
      we are trying to provide both text and context locations to the model, we merely ignore the text
792   and only provide the context locations. When we are attempting to provide just text as context, we
793   instead skip that training example. In practice, during training, we pass all text sections through
794   the frozen large language model once and then store the embeddings produced to use in the current
795   training run and all future runs. This prevents us having to repeatedly query the frozen but resource
796   intensive large language model during training. Training takes approximately ten hours on a single
797   NVIDIA A6000 GPU, requiring about six gigabytes of RAM.
798
799   A.3    BASELINES
800
801   We compare our approach to LE-SINR (Hamilton et al., 2024) and SINR (Cole et al., 2023). We
802   follow the original architecture and training procedure for LE-SINR and SINR, with the exception
803   that we enforce that SINR, like LE-SINR and our approach, is trained on our reduced set of 44,181
804   species which do not include evaluation species.
805   We also follow the original evaluation procedure for LE-SINR. For few-shot evaluation without text,
806   logistic regression with L2 regularization is performed with location features as input using the few
807   positive examples provided alongside a set of pseudo-negatives drawn half from a uniform random
808   distribution and half from the training data distribution. The regularization weight is set to 20. For
809   text-based “Zero-shot” evaluation we directly make use of the output of the text encoder with the dot
      product between this and location features giving us a probability of species presence. For few-shot


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      evaluation, when text is provided, we again perform logistic regression, but the output of the text
811   encoder is used as the “target” that the weights are drawn towards in a modified L2 regularization
812   term. See (Hamilton et al., 2024) for more details. The regularization weight is again set to 20.
813
814
      The original SINR implementation requires all evaluation species to be part of the training set. We
      match the adaptations from Hamilton et al. (2024) to allow evaluation on unseen species. After
815
      training we remove the learned species heads and keep only the location encoder. During evaluation
816
      we perform logistic regression with L2 regularization using location features as input. The regu-
817   larization weight is again set to 20, and the same method of selecting pseudo-negatives as above is
818   used.

822
823   We perform three runs for each experiment using different seeds and report the mean. We display

1293
1294   Here we provide additional results for the main models from Fig. 3 using a new ‘distance weighted’
1295   MAP evaluation metric. This is inspired by the evaluation conducted in LD-SDM (Sastry et al.,
       2023). This metric is based on mean average precision (MAP), however we now weight predictions


                                                        24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       by distance from the true range, i.e., predicting the presence of a species far from where it is said to
1297   be found is penalized more than predicting the presence of a species in a location that is very close to
1298   existing observations, but is still actually outside the range. We intend that this metric more closely
1299   aligns with a human’s judgment on how ‘correct’ a range is, compared to standard MAP. By consid-
1300   ering both metrics we can be more confident that the improvement in range mapping performance
1301   that FS-SINR provides is not just a consequence of how we are measuring the performance.
1302
       We determine the weight for location x as
1303
1304                                                      drange (x)
                                              wx = 1 +               h,                                    (3)
1305                                                      dantipodal
1306   where drange (x) is the distance along the earth’s surface from point x to the nearest point of the
1307   expert-derived range using for evaluation, and dantipodal is the distance along the earth’s surface
1308   between two points on opposite sides of the earth. While this distance does vary very slightly in
1309   different locations as the earth is not a perfect sphere, for this experiment we have set dantipodal
1310   to 20,037.5 km. h is the ‘distance weight hyperparameter’ and determines how much this metric
1311   penalizes incorrect predictions far from the range relative to close to the range. The metric is im-
1312   plemented equivalent to scikit-learn’s average precision score sample weight parameter (Pedregosa
1313   et al., 2011). We evaluate performance using the standard ‘unweighted MAP’, i.e., where h = 0 and
1314
       so we are calculating MAP as usual, and ‘distance weighted MAP’ with h = 9 and h = 99. We
       selected these settings so that errors on the opposite side of earth from the true range are penalized
1315
       10 and 100 times more than errors close to the true range.
1316
1317   Results on the IUCN evaluation dataset can be found in Fig. A28. These are from a single run,
1318   as opposed to the average of three repeats used for ‘unweighted MAP’ in Fig. 3. As the weight
1319   is increased, we observe a general reduction in overall performance. While there is no change in
1320

1321

1322

1323   in locations around the world with similar habitat features such as mountains, forest, or desert,
1324   despite these locations being far from the true range. This appears to be true of both FS-SINR
1325   and LE-SINR. For LE-SINR we see that when evaluating using unweighted MAP, using habitat

       Here we provide additional few-shot baselines based on Prototypical Networks (Snell et al., 2017).
       Our approach is very similar to Snell et al. (2017) although we use the SINR location encoder of
1334
       our models as the ‘embedding function’, allowing us to generate few-shot results for a novel species
1335
       without any retraining. Using this method, SINR and LE-SINR models can be used to estimate the
1336   range of a novel species without requiring training to learn a new species vector.
1337
1338   In order to do this, we first encode our known ‘presence’ locations using the location encoder of
1339
       our chosen model and then take an average of these points to generate a ‘prototype’ for the presence
       class. We select pseudo-negatives in the same manner as Hamilton et al. (2024) and similarly encode
1340
       and average these in order to generate a prototype for the ‘absent’ class.

1347   set of locations x that we use to create our prototypes. In our case, Spresent is the set of locations
1348   of the small number of available observations for our target species, while Sabsent is the set of
1349   pseudo-negative locations that we have selected according to Hamilton et al. (2024). fθ () indicates

1353   function to generate our probabilities. Putting this together, we can calculate the probability of
1354   presence as

1361   comparison. We see that the performance of these ‘prototype’ approaches is significantly worse than
1362   our FS-SINR approach and our learning-based SINR and LE-SINR baselines. On the less discrim-
1363   inative S&T dataset, performance when |Sk | = 1 is similar to that of the other methods. However,
1364   for both prototype approaches and both evaluation datasets, performance actually decreases as we

1366   In Fig. A31 we present qualitative results visualizing the few-shot estimated range for the
1367   Kalahari Scrub-Robin produced by FS-SINR and by a SINR model using the prototype
1368   approach. We see that the estimated range for the ‘prototype SINR’ becomes significantly worse
1369   as we add another context location. We find that averaging location encoder features from multiple
1370   ‘presence’ locations tends to produce a less useful prototype. We attempt to explain this finding
1371
       next.
1372   The features from a single location may represent ecologically meaningful information about the
1373   local environment, and a prototype produced from a single location will have the same representation
1374   in location encoder space as the location it is produced from. This suggests that measuring the
1375   distance from another encoded location to this prototype may tell us how ‘different’ the environment
1376   of the new location is compared to the location used to create the prototype. This information is
1377
       helpful for producing an estimate of a species range. However when we have a larger support set
       and so average the location encoder features of multiple locations, the prototype that is generated
1378
       may exist in a non-meaningful part of location encoder space, not associated with any real world
1379
       locations or environmental conditions. The distance between this prototype and an encoded location
1380   becomes less indicative of a ‘difference’ in environment, and so this distance becomes less helpful
1381   for estimating the presence of a species at the new location. Therefore as we increase the number
1382   of context points which form the ‘present’ support set, we actually decrease the performance of our
1383   prototype approaches.
