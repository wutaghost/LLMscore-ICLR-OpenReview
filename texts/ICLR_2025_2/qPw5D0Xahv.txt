011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Adversarial policies have been shown to exploit vulnerabilities in agents dur-
014            ing two-player competitive games, significantly undermining their performance.
015            While existing approaches model the challenge of training robust policies in such
016
               environments as the search for Nash equilibrium points in the policy space, this
               often leads to substantial computational overhead. In this work, we propose MM-
017
               FATROL, a novel robust policy training method grounded in the Minimax The-
018
               orem, which significantly reduces computational overhead by efficiently identi-
019            fying promising policy updates. We provide a formal analysis of the speedup
020            achieved by our method. Extensive experiments demonstrate that MM-FATROL
021            not only enhances efficiency but also surpasses the state-of-the-art method in terms
022            of generalization and robustness. Additionally, we discuss the limitations of our
023            approach and the challenges that remain in developing robust policies for more
024            complex game environments.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Reinforcement Learning (RL) has long been a prominent area of academic research, with interest
030   further intensified by its integration with deep learning technologies. Deep Reinforcement Learning
031   (DRL) combines the decision-making capabilities of RL with the representational power of deep
032   learning, enabling agents to approximate complex policy update processes through continuous in-
033
      teraction with the environment. As a versatile end-to-end control system, DRL has achieved, and
      in many cases surpassed, human expert-level performance in fields like robotic control (van Hasselt
034
      et al., 2016), autonomous driving (Liao et al., 2022), recommendation systems (Huang et al., 2021),
035
      and game AI. Notably, in the realm of game AI, DeepMind’s AlphaGo (Silver et al., 2016) and Al-
036   phaGo Zero (Silver et al., 2017) have defeated top professional players in the two-player zero-sum
037   game of Go. In 2019, DeepMind extended these successes with AlphaStar (Vinyals et al., 2019;
038   2017), outperforming 99.8% of human players in StarCraft II.
039
      Many DRL applications require high levels of security and stability, such as communication flow
040
      control (Liu et al., 2021) and intelligent transportation systems (Haydari & Yilmaz, 2022). However,
041
      recent researches have revealed that DRL models are vulnerable to various attacks. It is known that
042   deep learning models are susceptible to adversarial samples (Lin et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2018;
043   Kurakin et al., 2017), where small perturbations to the input can lead to incorrect outputs. In the
044   DRL setting, similar techniques can be used to influence agents into making poor decisions. Huang
045   et al. (2017) apply adversarial examples to DRL, demonstrating that noise introduced by the FGSM
046   method (Goodfellow et al., 2015) can cause DQN (Mnih et al., 2013) and PPO (Schulman et al.,
047   2017) models to make erroneous decisions. Behzadan & Munir (2017) adopt the idea of transfer-
048   based attacks , where a surrogate model predicts how input modifications will cause the victim agent
049   to underperform.
050   The above adversarial attacks primarily target the deep learning component of DRL through adver-
051   sarial perturbation or poisoning attacks. On the other hand, Gleave et al. (2020) introduced adver-
052   sarial policy attacks, which do not directly modify the input but instead train adversarial agents to
053   force victim agents into suboptimal actions. This approach has proven effective in environments like
      Mujoco (Todorov et al., 2012), offering a more realistic attack. Guo et al. (2021) further advanced


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      this field by reconstructing the attack objective to maximize the average expectation of the attacker’s
055   policy while minimizing the victim’s average reward, achieving successful attacks in StarCraft II.
056
057   Most defense mechanisms in DRL are derived from traditional adversarial attack and defense strate-
058
      gies, focusing on defense against perturbation-based attacks such as adversarial training and adver-
      sary detection. For instance, Kos & Song (2017) apply adversarial training to the A3C algorithm in
059
      the Atari Pong scenario, using random samples and adversarial samples generated by FGSM. Chen
060
      et al. (2018) extended adversarial training on agents to the more complex domain of autonomous
061   navigation. Lin et al. (2017) focused on detecting adversarial samples through predictive modeling
062   of future observations. Despite progress in defending against perturbation-based attacks, research
063   on defending against adversarial policy attacks remains limited. Guo et al. (2023) proposed training
064   agents in two-player games to reach Nash equilibrium conditions, thereby ensuring a performance
065   lower bound when under attack.
066
      In this work, we focus on robust policy training in two-player game scenarios. Inspired by the
067   approach of transforming robust policy training into a search for Nash equilibrium in the policy
068   space, we propose a novel robust policy training method against adversarial policy attacks called
069   MiniMax based FAst-training defense againsT adversaRial pOLicy (MM-FATROL). Grounded in
070   the Minimax Theorem (Cheng et al., 2014), MM-FATROL reduces computational overhead while
071   maintaining strong robustness. Extensive experiments demonstrate that MM-FATROL significantly
072   reduces computational costs compared to state-of-the-art methods, while also achieving superior
073   performance across various games. Additionally, MM-FATROL exhibits stronger robustness against
074   adversarial policy attacks. We also discuss the limitations of our algorithm and the challenges of
075
      achieving the most robust policy in arbitrary game environment.
076   Our main contributions are as follows:
077
      • We propose MM-FATROL, a novel robust policy training algorithm based on the Minimax The-
078
        orem, and provide a formal analysis of its computational efficiency compared to state-of-the-art
079     methods.
080   • We demonstrate through extensive experiments that MM-FATROL reduces computational over-
081     head while maintaining top-tier performance and stronger robustness against adversarial policy
082     attacks across various games.
083   • We analyze the key challenges in robust policy training and identify future directions for enhanc-
084     ing the defense against adversarial policy attacks.

138   2.3   T HE PATROL M ETHOD
139
140
      Guo et al. (2023) discovered that in two-player zero-sum games, training robust policies for both
      players can be framed as the search for a Nash equilibrium in the policy space. From a game-
141
      theoretic perspective, the policy pair at the Nash equilibrium represents a set of robust policies
142
      capable of maintaining a performance lower bound under any adversarial policy attack. Since neither
143   player can improve their payoff by unilaterally altering their policy at the Nash equilibrium, even
144   if one player’s policy is replaced with an adversarial policy, the attacker cannot achieve a better
145   outcome, thereby ensuring the victim’s performance does not degrade.
146
      Based on that insight, the PATROL method was designed to train robust policies. It initializes a
147
      policy pool for both players, consisting of K pairs of policies (πk1 , πk2 )k=1:K . In each iteration,
148                                                                                                 −i

      3.1   P ROBLEM S ETUP
157
158   Adversarial Policy Attack. In this context, adversarial attacks target agents that have already been
159   trained within a two-player competitive environment. The attacker selects one of the player agents
160   as the victim, fixing the victim’s policy, π i , which effectively transforms the original Markov game
161   process into a Markov decision process (MDP). The attacker’s goal is to find an optimal attack
      policy, πα , within this MDP that minimizes the victim’s cumulative reward. During training, the


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      attacker can observe the victim’s actions but does not have access to any white-box information about
163   the victim’s model, such as its structure or parameters. Additionally, the attacker cannot manipulate
164   the game environment or interfere with the feedback provided by the environment to the agent.
165   This scenario models real-world adversarial attacks on policies trained using deep reinforcement
166   learning, such as attacks on autonomous vehicles that degrade their performance in navigation or
167   obstacle avoidance, posing serious safety risks.
168
      Assumptions for Defenders. Similar to the attacker, the defender is also unable to manipulate the
169   game environment. Furthermore, the defender cannot interfere with the attacker’s training process
170   or predict the attacker’s policy in advance. This restriction means that, without disrupting the at-
171   tacker’s intentions, the defender cannot engage in numerous confrontations with adversarial policies
172   to gather training data. Therefore, the defender cannot perform specific defenses through adversarial
173   retraining (Guo et al., 2023).
174
      Objective of Our Method. The goal of this work is to develop robust policy pairs for both players
175   in the game, ensuring that each party’s policy maintains a certain lower bound of performance even
176   in the presence of adversarial policy attacks. Additionally, the trained policies should possess gen-
177   eralization capabilities, performing well not only under attack but also in standard, non-adversarial
178   scenarios.

181   3.2   T HE P ROPOSED A LGORITHM
182
183   In this work, we integrate the Minimax Theorem into the framework of PATROL to further enhance
184   the performance. The procedure is outlined in Algorithm 1, with three key improvements detailed
185   below:
186   Select Promising Policies for Updates. The core idea of the PATROL algorithm is to search for
187   a Nash equilibrium in the policy space, ensuring the victim agent’s performance be above a lower
188   bound under adversarial policy attacks. Traditionally, this involves updating all policies against their
189   strongest opponents in each iteration, gradually converging toward the Nash equilibrium. However,
190   this approach can lead to significant increase on computational overhead. Instead of that, we identify
191   the most promising policy combinations for training in each iteration, without expending substantial
192   resources on unnecessary computations.
193   Using the Minimax Theorem, we can identify these target policies. For instance, player i’s max-
194   imin value is given by µ = maxπi minπ−i Uπi i ,π−i , with the corresponding policy combination
195   (πµi , πµ−i ) = argmaxπi argminπ−i Uπi i ,π−i . This ensures that πµi is the most robust policy in player
196   i’s pool, guaranteeing a payoff of at least µ when facing an unknown opponent. Simultaneously,
197   πµ−i is the strongest adversarial policy for player i, as it leads to the lowest payoff µ for player i.
198
      Similarly, the minimax value ν = minπ−i maxπi Uπi i ,π−i corresponds to the policy combination
199
      (πνi , πν−i ) = argminπ−i argmaxπi Uπi i ,π−i , where πν−i is the most adversarial policy for player i,
200
201
      and πνi is its strongest counter.
202   Thus, in each iteration of MM-FATROL, we select πµi from player i’s pool as the most worthwhile
203   policy for training, and π −i µ as the opponent’s policy to assist in updating πµi . For player −i, we
204   use πνi as the fixed opponent policy while updating πν−i using DPPO. This approach offers a more
205   efficient and targeted strategy for policy updates.
206
207
      Design Update Windows. In each iteration, only one policy from each policy pool is selected for
      updating , which may introduce some bias in the search direction and limit exploration. To address
208
      this, we propose the concept of update windows to correct the update direction. We distinguish
209
      between two types of updates: “minimax updates” (updating only the selected promising policy)
210   and “full updates” (updating all policies in the pool). Specifically, we define c iterations as one
211   update window. During each window, we perform one full update followed by j minimax updates
212   in a cyclic manner. As we enter the next update window, the number of minimax updates, j, is
213   incremented by a parameter a (referred to as “acceleration”), with the condition that j ∈ [0, m],
214   where m is the “speed limit”. By interspersing full updates between minimax updates, we strike a
215   balance between expanding the search range in the policy space and refining the update direction,
      reducing the risk of suboptimal outcomes.

      5     D ISCUSSION
420
421   5.1    T HE E SSENCE OF ROBUST P OLICIES
422
423   The convergence proof of PATROL for NE is predicated on the assumption that an NE exists within
424   the policy space, a condition met in finite two-player zero-sum games. However, in many game
425   environments, particularly those with continuous state and action spaces, the existence of NE is
426   not guaranteed. According to the Minimax Theorem, an NE is a special case where the minimax
427   and maximin values coincide. Thus, for the majority of two-player games operating in continuous
428
      spaces, the robust policy training process modeled by PATROL requires extension.
429   Addressing the core question, in a two-player competitive game, the most robust policy is to max-
430   imize their minimum achievable payoff against any opponent’s policy. This aligns with the policy
431   that yields the maximin values within their payoff space. For instance, consider the value func-
      tion f (x, y) = (x2 − 1)2 − (y − x)2 in an Euclidean game, where the action space is defined as

432
      x, y ∈ [−2, 2], as shown in Figure 3a, this game does not possess a global NE. However, as illus-
433   trated in Figure 3c, the value function for player 2 identifies point C as its maximin point. Thus, if
434   player 2 adopts the policy y = 0, they can guarantee a payoff corresponding to point C, regardless
435   of player 1’s chosen policy x = x̃. For any alternative policy of player 2 ỹ ̸= 0, player 1 can always
436   ensure that player 2 receives a lower payoff than that from policy y = 0. In other words, y = 0 is
437   the most robust policy for player 2. Similarly, as shown in Figure 3b, since player 1’s value function
438   is −f (x, y), the most robust policies for player 1 corresponds to the minimax points (A and B),
439   specifically x = ±1.

454   5.2   L IMITATIONS AND C HALLENGES
455
456   As discussed in Section 5.1, in complex game environments with continuous state and action spaces
457   where the existence of NE cannot be guaranteed, the true update direction towards a robust pol-
458   icy corresponds to the maximin value for each player. However, in the absence of NE points, the
459   strongest opponent of any player’s robust policy may not be the other player’s robust policy. This
460   can result in a scenario where one player’s policy converges to the optimal solution while the other
461
      player does not, leading to continuous updates in the latter’s policy pool without the chance to con-
      front their theoretical strongest opponent, and thus unable to converge to the robust policy forever.
462
463   Taking the Euclidean game illustrated in Figure 3 as an example, playerq1’s minimax policy com-
464   binations are (1, 1) and (−1, −1), while player 2’s optimal policy is ( 32 , 0). Given that player
465
      2’s value function is a fourth-degree polynomial in x and a second-degree polynomial in y, player
466   2’s policy pool converges to its robust policy, specifically y = 0, more rapidly than player 1’s.
467   Consequently, player 1 loses the opportunity to train against the strongest opponent y = ±1 cor-
468   responding to their robust policy. As a result, player 1’s policies can only train against  q a fixed
469
      opponent of y = 0 during subsequent updates, ultimately leading to convergence at x = 32 , which
470
471
      is not actually player 1’s most robust policy. Therefore, a critical challenge for enhancing robustness
      in future work will be ensuring that policies have the opportunity to train against their theoretical
472
      strongest opponents within the global policy space.

475   6     C ONCLUSION
476
477
      In this work, guided by the Minimax Theorem, we proposed MM-FATROL, a robust policy training
478
      method built on the PATROL framework. Extensive experiments demonstrated that MM-FATROL
479
      not only significantly reduces computational overhead but also maintains strong policy general-
480   ization and exhibits greater robustness compared to the state-of-the-art method. Additionally, we
481   analyzed the limitations of existing robust policy training methods in the face of adversarial policy
482   attacks, and outlined key challenges that must be addressed to further enhance robustness. For fu-
483   ture work, we aim to tackle these challenges by exploring adaptive adjustments to the size of each
484   party’s policy pool based on game environment characteristics or by maintaining separate pools for
485   the strongest opponents of each player. These directions will drive further advancements in robust
      policy training.
