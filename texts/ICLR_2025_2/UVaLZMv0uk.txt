009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper addresses the challenge of achieving second-order stationary points
012            (SOSP) in differentially private stochastic non-convex optimization. We identify
013            two key limitations in the state-of-the-art: (i) inaccurate error rates caused by the
014            omission of gradient variance in saddle point escape analysis, resulting in inap-
015            propriate parameter choices and overly optimistic performance estimates, and (ii)
016            inefficiencies in private SOSP selection via the AboveThreshold algorithm, par-
017            ticularly in distributed learning settings, where perturbing and sharing Hessian
018
               matrices introduces significant additional noise. To overcome these challenges,
               we revisit perturbed stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with Gaussian noise and
019
               propose a new framework that leverages general gradient oracles. This framework
020
               introduces a novel criterion based on model drift distance, ensuring provable sad-
021            dle point escape and efficient convergence to approximate local minima with low
022            iteration complexity. Using an adaptive SPIDER as the gradient oracle, we estab-
023            lish a new DP algorithm that corrects existing error rates. Furthermore, we extend
024            our approach to a distributed adaptive SPIDER, applying our framework to dis-
025            tributed learning scenarios and providing the first theoretical results on achieving
026            SOSP under differential privacy in distributed environments with heterogeneous
027            data. Finally, we analyze the limitations of the AboveThreshold algorithm for pri-
028            vate model selection in distributed learning and show that as model dimensions
029
               increase, the selection process introduces additional errors, further demonstrating
               the superiority of our proposed framework.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Stochastic optimization is one of the most fundamental problems in machine learning and statistics,
035
      with the goal of building models that generalize well to unseen data using only a limited number of
036   i.i.d. samples drawn from an unknown distribution. As the volume of sensitive data grows, ensuring
037   privacy during the training process has become a critical concern. This has led to the adoption
038   of differential privacy (DP) (Dwork et al., 2006) in stochastic optimization, which provides strong
039   privacy guarantees while preserving the utility of the learned model.
040
      Over the past decade, significant advances have been made in DP stochastic optimization, partic-
041
      ularly for convex objectives, e.g., (Choquette-Choo et al., 2024; Liu & Asi, 2024; Su et al., 2023;
042   2022; Tao et al., 2022). While convex optimization is relatively well-understood, the non-convex
043   setting presents additional challenges due to the existence of saddle points. In non-convex optimiza-
044   tion, most existing DP algorithms focus on achieving convergence to first-order stationary points
045   (FOSP), where the gradient norm is small (Arora et al., 2023; Bassily et al., 2021; Zhou et al.,
046   2020). However, this criterion is often insufficient, as FOSP can include both local maxima and
047   saddle points—where saddle points represent highly sub-optimal solutions in many problems, as
048   shown by Jain et al. (2015) and Sun et al. (2016). For practical non-convex functions, second-order
049   stationary points (SOSP)—where the gradient is small and the Hessian is positive semi-definite—are
050
      preferred, as they guarantee convergence to a local minimum.
051   Due to the importance of achieving SOSP, substantial effort has been devoted to this area, as demon-
052   strated by works such as Fang et al. (2019), Jin et al. (2021), Daneshmand et al. (2018), Jin et al.
053   (2017), and Ge et al. (2015). However, few algorithms specifically target this more stringent crite-
      rion under the DP framework. The notable exception and current state-of-the-art for achieving SOSP

054
      under DP is the work of Liu et al. (2024), which proposes adding Gaussian noise, scaled according to
055   the gradient error, to ensure that the DP-SGD sequence escapes each potential saddle point. Despite
056   being the state-of-the-art, we identify significant gaps in their utility analysis and error rate guaran-
057   tees. Specifically, the omission of gradient variance in their saddle point escape analysis results in
058   incorrect parameter settings and overly optimistic performance estimates (see Section 3).
059
      Beyond these challenges, distributed learning serves as another key motivation for this work, be-
060
      coming increasingly important in modern machine learning, particularly with the rise of large-scale
061   models that require decentralized systems for efficient training. To date, no existing work has ex-
062   plored DP non-convex stochastic optimization in distributed settings with the goal of achieving
063   SOSP. Distributed learning introduces additional challenges, such as data heterogeneity and the
064   need for privacy-preserving protocols across multiple clients. Moreover, the current state-of-the-
065   art approach by Liu et al. (2024) suffers from severe performance degradation when applied to
066   distributed settings. Their reliance on the AboveThreshold algorithm for private model selection
067   introduces significant noise when sharing perturbed Hessian matrices across clients, particularly in
068   high-dimensional scenarios (see detailed discussion in Section 6). This degradation can be attributed
069
      to their learning algorithm, which only guarantees the existence of an SOSP among all model iterates
      during the learning process and thus requires additional use of private model selection algorithms.

      Variance Reduction via SPIDER In standard SGD and its variants, a gradient estimate gt is used
154   at each iteration t to approximate the true gradient ∇F (xt−1 ). However, stochastic gradients com-
155   puted from batches or individual samples often exhibit high variance, which can degrade learning
156   performance. The Stochastic Path Integrated Differential Estimator (SPIDER), introduced by Fang
157   et al. (2018), addresses this issue by using two gradient oracles, O1 and O2 , to reduce variance,
158   given a batch of data samples Bt at each iteration t:
159
160
       • Oracle O1 (xt−1 , Bt ) := ∇f (xt−1 ; Bt ) provides an estimate of ∇F (xt−1 ).
161    • Oracle O2 (xt−1 , xt−2 , Bt ) := ∇f (xt−1 ; Bt ) − ∇f (xt−2 ; Bt ) approximates the gradient differ-
         ence ∇F (xt−1 ) − ∇F (xt−2 ).

162
      SPIDER periodically queries O1 every l iterations for an updated gradient estimate gt . In the re-
163   maining l − 1 iterations, it uses O2 to estimate the gradient difference and updates the gradient
164   estimate as gt = gt−1 + O2 (xt−1 , xt−2 , Bt ). For smooth functions, the variance of the estimate
165   ∇F (xt−1 ) − ∇F (xt−2 ) is proportional to ∥xt−1 − xt−2 ∥, which is typically small when the model
166   drift between iterations is minimal. This enables SPIDER to effectively reduce gradient variance
167   while maintaining high accuracy in gradient estimation.

      Gaps in Error Rate Analysis The state-of-the-art error rate for achieving an α-SOSP in popula-
      tion risk function under DP, as presented in Liu et al. (2024), contains fundamental gaps that lead to
174
      incorrect conclusions. First, the error analysis is based on Lemma 3.4, which is essentially derived
175
      from Wang et al. (2019, Lemma 12). This lemma asserts that adding Gaussian noise at the same
176   scale as the gradient estimation error can sufficiently reduce the function value with high probability,
177   ensuring successful escape from saddle points. The key to the proof lies in demonstrating that the
178   region around the saddle point, where SGD may get stuck, is narrow. This ensures that at least one
179   of two coupled SGD sequences, initialized a certain distance apart in the escape direction due to the
180   perturbation, can successfully escape.
181
      However, the existing analysis overlooks a critical factor: the stochastic gradient variance. In ana-
182   lyzing the dynamics of the coupled points, the authors used exact gradients of the population risk,
183   as evidenced in the equation preceding equation (39) of Wang et al. (2019). This oversight leads to
184   incorrect parameter settings, particularly with respect to the step size η. Even for non-private SGD,
185   Jin et al. (2021) has shown that the presence of stochastic gradient noise requires a smaller step size
186   and, thus, induces higher gradient complexity to ensure convergence to an SOSP, compared to exact
                                                                                            1
187   gradient descent (GD). In contrast, Liu et al. (2024) adopted a constant step size of M , which is only
188   appropriate for GD with exact gradients and fails to account for the stochastic nature of population
189   risk minimization. This misstep leads to an incorrect error rate. Specifically, the increased gradient
190
      complexity reduces the number of data points per gradient estimate, leading to larger estimation
      errors. Consequently, the correct error rate for achieving an SOSP should be looser than the one
191
      presented in Liu et al. (2024).
192
193   With the perception of the above gaps, we further argue that directly fixing the error in Liu et al.
194   (2024) through a revised proof for their algorithm, while feasible, would fail to achieve the target
195   SOSP with the optimal dependence on α as required in Definition 1. For a detailed discussion,
196
      please refer to Appendix B.
197
198
      Limitations in Private SOSP Selection The state-of-the-art learning algorithm proposed in Liu
199   et al. (2024) only guarantees the existence of an α-SOSP in all models throughout the learning
200   process. To privately select an α-SOSP from these iterates, the authors employ the well-known
201   AboveThreshold algorithm. This approach raises significant concerns, as it relies on evaluating both
202   the gradient and the Hessian matrix of the objective function for every model. In the case of popu-
203   lation risk minimization, where the objective function is unknown and only samples from the data
204   distribution are available, approximating these gradients and Hessians requires additional data and
205   becomes computationally expensive—particularly for Hessians. Due to the need for computing the
206   Hessian, this method is no longer first-order. Moreover, while Liu et al. (2024) claim that the error
207
      introduced by approximating the gradients and Hessians does not exceed the error generated by their
      learning algorithm, as we will demonstrate in Section 6, this assertion does not hold in distributed
208
      learning scenarios. The AboveThreshold algorithm primarily uses the perturbed gradient norm and
209
      the minimum eigenvalue of the Hessian for model selection. In single-machine cases, where all data
210   is centrally stored, it is feasible to approximate the gradients and Hessians and add noise only to
211   the one-dimensional values of the gradient norm and minimum eigenvalue. However, in distributed
212   learning with heterogeneous data, each client must perturb and share its local gradients and Hes-
213   sians—rather than just the one-dimensional quantities—so that noisy estimates can be aggregated at
214   the central server. This introduces significantly more noise, especially in high-dimensional settings,
215   thereby worsening the error rates provided by the learning algorithm (see Section 6 for a detailed
      analysis).

248   We introduce our framework in Algorithm 1. In this algorithm, we use a general stochastic gradient
249   oracle with Gaussian perturbation, as described in (3), which we denote as P Grad Oracle(∗)
250
      in steps 4 and 10, where ∗ omits any specific arguments the oracle might require. This allows our
      algorithm to serve as a general optimization framework for non-convex stochastic optimization, ap-
251
      plicable beyond the context of DP. Building upon the PSGD updates described earlier, our algorithm
252
      distinguishes itself from the PSGD algorithm of Jin et al. (2021) by using the moving distance of the
253   model parameters as the criterion for escaping saddle points (step 12). This innovation allows the
254   algorithm to determine convergence to an SOSP with high probability during the PSGD process. In
255   contrast, the algorithm proposed by Jin et al. (2021) outputs all model parameters obtained through-
256   out the PSGD iterations, only guaranteeing that an SOSP was visited at least once. To further ensure
257   the output is an SOSP with high probability, their method requires additional post-processing steps,
258   such as computing the minimum eigenvalue of each Hessian matrix of the empirical risk function or
259   approximating these eigenvalues using extra data samples for population risk. These steps introduce
260   significant computational costs and extra sample usage, as well as the need to compute second-order
261
      information, thereby making the overall procedure no longer first-order.
262   We observe that, when successfully escaping from a saddle point, not only does the function value
263   decrease sufficiently, as noted in Jin et al. (2021), but the model parameter also moves sufficiently
264   far beyond a certain threshold S (specified later). Leveraging this key insight, our algorithm can
265   directly identify and output an SOSP during the PSGD process, eliminating the need for additional
266   calculations or sample usage.

403

      allowing the gradient estimation   error to grow over time, adaptive SPIDER tracks the total model
404                              Pt
405
      drift, defined as driftt := i=τ (t) ∥xi − xi−1 ∥2 , where τ (t) is the last iteration when O1 was used.
406   The intuition behind is that, for smooth functions, the error in O2 , which estimates ∇F (xt−1 ) −
407   ∇F (xt−2 ), is proportional to ∥xt−1 − xt−2 ∥. When the model drift is small, the gradient estimate
408   remains accurate enough, and O2 can continue to be used, reducing variance in gradient estimation
409   (steps 9-11). However, when the drift grows large, further use of O2 could introduce significant
410   error, and thus O1 is queried to refresh the gradient estimate (steps 4-7). A threshold κ is set to
411   determine when the drift becomes excessive, ensuring the total error remains controlled (step 3).
412   Our adaptive SPIDER differs from the approach in Liu et al. (2024) in an important way. In addition
413   to triggering O1 when the model drift exceeds a threshold, Liu et al. (2024) also needs to add
414   additional Gaussian noise and trigger O1 every time a potential saddle point is reached, while our
415   method fully utilizes the DP Gaussian noise already present in the gradient oracle, avoiding the need
416   to add additional noise at saddle points. As a result, in our framework, the decision to query O1
417   or continue with O2 is based solely on model drift, leading to a simpler and more efficient gradient
418   estimation process. The following Lemma 7 captures the noise magnitude for any ĝt , which is

709   representative works e.g., (Wang et al., 2020a; 2017; Bassily et al., 2014; 2019; 2021; Feldman
710   et al., 2020; Choquette-Choo et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2018; Su et al., 2023; 2024; Hu et al., 2022;
711   Xue et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2020b; Huai et al., 2020). Recent advances have extended DP to non-
712   convex settings, mainly focusing on first-order stationary points (FOSP). Notable works in this area
713
      include (Arora et al., 2023; Bassily et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019; Xiao et al.,
      2023), which improved error rates in non-convex optimization with balanced privacy and utility in
714
      stochastic gradient methods. However, these works generally fail to address the more stringent crite-
715
      rion of second-order stationary points (SOSP). The very recent work Liu et al. (2024) tired to narrow
716   this gap, but unfortunately has some issues in their results as we discussed before. Our work builds
717   on this foundation by correcting error rates and proposing a framework that ensures convergence to
718   SOSP while maintaining DP.
719
720   Finding Second-Order Stationary Points (SOSP) In non-convex optimization, convergence to
721   FOSP is often insufficient, as saddle points can lead to sub-optimal solutions (Jain et al., 2015; Sun
722   et al., 2016). Achieving SOSP, where the gradient is small and the Hessian is positive semi-definite,
723   ensures that the optimization converges to a local minimum rather than a saddle point. Techniques
724   for escaping saddle points, such as perturbed SGD with Gaussian noise, have been explored in
725   works like Jin et al. (2021) and Ge et al. (2015). Ge et al. (2015) first showed that SGD with a
726
      simple parameter perturbation can escape saddle points efficiently. Later, the analysis was refined
      by Jin et al. (2017; 2021). Recently, variance reduction techniques have been applied to second-
727
      order guaranteed methods Ge et al. (2019); Li (2019).These methods ensure escape from saddle
728
      points by introducing noise to the gradient descent process. In contrast, the studies of SOSP under
729   DP are quite limited, and most of them only consider the empirical risk minimization objective, such
730   as Wang et al. (2019); Wang & Xu (2021). Very recently, Liu et al. (2024) addressed the population
731   risk minimization objective, but with notable gaps in their error analysis, particularly in the treatment
732   of gradient variance. Moreover, all of these works are limited to the centralized learning setting with
733   only one client and cannot be directly extended to the more general distributed learning setting.
734
735   Distributed Learning Distributed learning has gained prominence due to the growing need for
736   large-scale models trained on decentralized data. Methods like federated learning (McMahan et al.,
737   2017) have enabled multiple clients to collaboratively train models without sharing their local data,
738   preserving privacy. Recent efforts, such as Gao et al. (2024); Lowy et al. (2023); Lowy & Raza-
739
      viyayn (2023) investigated DP learning problems in distributed settings, but these works are limited
      to first-order optimization. No prior work, to our knowledge, has extended these methods to ensure
740
      SOSP in distributed learning scenarios with heterogeneous data. Our proposed framework addresses
741
      this gap by introducing the first distributed learning algorithm with DP guarantees for SOSP, capable
742   of handling arbitrary data heterogeneity across clients.
