009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We investigate the relationship between task complexity and the minimum model
012            size required for learning specific tasks in small transformer models. We focus
013            on the ListOps dataset, consisting of nested math operations. We define the task
014            complexity as the Kolmogorov complexity (KC) of the code solving the task, using
015            a rough proxy for KC. We find a power-law relation between KC and parameters
016            required to learn, suggesting number of parameters to learn harder task increases
017            almost cubic in KC. On individual math operations, sum mod 10 is hardest to
018
               learn. Surprisingly, when combining tasks, we observe that sum is learned earlier
               and with fewer parameters when trained alongside max and median. Analyzing
019
               the model, we find strong evidence that models trained on sum alone and models
020
               trained jointly converge to different algorithms. Concretely, the sum alone model
021            doesn’t seem to have learned number properties in the embedding layer, likely
022            memorizing the sum table. In contrast, models trained on three tasks (maximum,
023            median and sum) reveals that joint training results in clear number-like properties.
024            Finally, we also find evidence that the sum-only model utilizes its feedforward
025            layer more than the jointly trained model. Conversely, the attention layer in the
026            joint model is activated more than the sum model. Our findings suggest there is
027            another dimension to emergent abilities in language models, namely the algorithms
028            being learned, potentially impacting scaling laws.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The scaling laws of language models have been a central focus in machine learning research,
034
      describing how performance varies with model size, computational cost, and dataset size (Hoffmann
      et al., 2022; Muennighoff et al., 2024; Hestness et al., 2017). However, these laws have largely
035
      overlooked a crucial factor: the intrinsic complexity of the tasks being learned. Our work addresses
036
      this gap by introducing a quantitative measure of task complexity and exploring its relationship with
037   model size and emergent abilities.
038
039   Using the ListOps dataset (Nangia & Bowman, 2018) as our experimental framework, we investigate
040
      how the complexity of mathematical operations—specifically maximum (max), minimum (min),
      median (med), and sum modulo 10 (sum)—influences the learning dynamics of transformer models.
041
      By approximating Kolmogorov complexity (KC) (Kolmogorov, 1965; Li et al., 2008) through the
042
      compressed size of minimal Python code implementing these operations, we establish a novel,
043   quantifiable metric for task complexity. Our study reveals several groundbreaking findings:
044
045         1. Cubic model growth with complexity: We discover a power-law relationship between
046            task complexity (as measured by KC) and the minimum model size required for learning.
047            This quantitative link between complexity and model capacity offers a new perspective on
048            scaling laws, suggesting they are less universal than previously thought and are significantly
049            influenced by task complexity.
050         2. Change in Algorithmic Complexity: Surprisingly, in multitask settings, we observe that
051            the sum operation, when trained alongside max and median, is learned earlier and with fewer
052            parameters than when trained in isolation. This finding challenges conventional wisdom
053            about task difficulty and suggests that curriculum design and task diversity play critical roles
               in model learning.

054
            3. Smarter Solution via Joint Training: Our analysis of embedding layers reveals that models
055            trained on three tasks (max, med, sum) develop meaningful numerical representations. In
056            contrast, models trained solely on sum rarely exhibit these structures, suggesting they rely
057            more on memorization than understanding.
058
059   These results provide compelling evidence that joint training guides models towards finding alterna-
060   tive, more efficient solutions. While training on sum alone may lead small models to memorize sum
061   tables, joint training appears to push the system towards learning good number embeddings. This, in
062
      turn, enables the model to discover more efficient algorithms for performing sum operations, rather
      than relying on rote memorization.
063
064   Our work bridges the gap between algorithmic complexity theory and practical neural network training
065   while challenging our understanding of scaling laws. It highlights the importance of considering task
066   complexity and training diversity when designing and deploying models. Gaining a deeper insight
067   into how task complexity, model capacity, and learning interact is essential for building more effective
068
      systems.

070   2     M ETHODOLOGY
071
072   We investigate the relationship between task complexity and model learning dynamics using KC as a
073   measure of task difficulty. We employ the ListOps dataset to study nested mathematical operations,
074   focusing on maximum, minimum, median, and sum modulo 10. Our approach involves training
075   small-scale transformer models and analyzing their performance and internal representations. In this
076   section, we describe our analysis methodology, including our approximation of KC, the specifics of
077   the ListOps dataset, our model architecture, and our evaluation techniques.
078
079   2.1   KOLMOGOROV C OMPLEXITY AND I TS A PPROXIMATION
080
081
      Kolmogorov Complexity (KC), also known as algorithmic complexity, provides a formal measure of
      the information content of a string (Kolmogorov, 1965; Li et al., 2008). For a given string s, its KC
082
      K(s) is defined as the length of the shortest program that produces s as its output. While KC offers a
083
      powerful theoretical framework for quantifying complexity, it is fundamentally incomputable (Li
084   et al., 2008), necessitating the use of approximations in practical applications.
085
086   In this study, we employ a proxy measure for KC based on the compressed size of minimal Python
087
      code implementing each task. This approach aligns with the intuition that more complex tasks
      require longer descriptions, and thus, larger compressed sizes. Our method leverages the Lempel-Ziv
088
      (LZ) compression algorithm (Ziv & Lempel, 1977), which forms the basis of the widely used gzip
089
      compression tool. The use of compression algorithms as proxies for KC is well-established in the
090   literature. Cilibrasi & Vitanyi (2005) introduced the Normalized Compression Distance (NCD),
091   which uses standard compression algorithms to approximate the ideal Normalized Information
092   Distance based on KC. This approach has been successfully applied in various domains, including
093   bioinformatics, musicology, and plagiarism detection (Li et al., 2004).
094
      Our specific methodology of using gzip-compressed code size as a KC proxy follows the precedent set
095
      by several researchers. Zenil et al. (2014) employed a similar technique in their study of algorithmic
096   complexity for short strings. In the context of machine learning, Dingle et al. (2018) used compressed
097   code length as a complexity measure to study the learnability of Boolean functions. By applying
098   gzip compression to minimal Python implementations of our target tasks, we obtain a practical,
099   computable proxy for KC. This allows us to quantify task complexity and examine its relationship
100   with the minimum model size required for successful learning.
101
      It’s important to note that while this proxy provides a useful approximation, it has limitations. The
102   choice of programming language (Python in our case) and the specific implementation details can
103   influence the compressed size. Additionally, for very short or highly regular strings, compression-
104   based approximations may not fully capture the theoretical KC. To mitigate these issues, we establish
105   a set of ground rules (Appendix C) to ensure we do not use advanced features that do not capture the
106   complexity of the algorithm (e.g. we do not use addition or sorting). Despite these constraints, our
107   approach offers a pragmatic and widely accepted method for estimating relative task complexities in
      the context of machine learning and emergent abilities in language models.

      2.2   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
109
110   Choice of task For our study on the emergence of abilities in language models, we chose the
111   ListOps dataset (Nangia & Bowman, 2018) as our primary experimental framework. This choice was
112   motivated by several key factors that align with our research objectives:
113
114         1. Procedural Generation: ListOps is a synthetically generated dataset, allowing us to create
115            a large volume of examples with controlled properties. This feature enables us to fine-tune
116            the complexity and distribution of our training and evaluation data.
117         2. Exact Evaluation: The mathematical nature of ListOps operations ensures unambiguous,
118            exact evaluation of model outputs. This precision is crucial for accurately assessing model
119            performance and learning trajectories.
120         3. Adjustable Complexity: ListOps offers a framework to modulate problem complexity by
121            combining different operations and adjusting nesting depths. This flexibility allows us to
122            create a spectrum of task difficulties, enabling a nuanced study of the relationship between
123            task complexity and model size.
124
            4. Inter-task Relationships: The dataset’s multiple operations (max, median, sum modulo 10)
125
               provide an opportunity to explore task synergies and interference. This aspect led to our key
126            discovery regarding the benefits of joint training, particularly in accelerating the learning of
127            more challenging operations like sum modulo 10.
128
129   Dataset Description ListOps consists of nested mathematical equations involving operations such
130   as min, max, median, and sum modulo 10 applied to single-digit numbers (0-9) (Appendix H.1). It
131   uses the Polish notation: (operation, [inputs]) To disentangle any complexity arising from tokenization
132   we further simplify these expression by representing them by symbols: ’+’ for max, ’−’ for min,
133   ’/’ for median, and ’%’ for sum modulo 10. For example (max,3,(min,7,4,9))=4 becomes
134   s(+3(-749))=4e, with ‘s’ and ‘e’ being start and end tokens.
135
136   Tokenization and CoT: We employ a character-based tokenization strategy for processing ListOps
137   expressions. (Appendix H.2). We find that directly solving nested ListOps in one step can be quite
138   challenging for transformer model (Fig. 7) Even with a maximum of three nesting levels with
139   three operands (inputs) we find that GPT models with over 10 million parameters still fail to
140   learn the task. To enhance model performance, particularly on more complex operations like sum
141
      modulo 10, we introduced a chain of thought (CoT) approach in our training data (Appendix H.3):
      s(%12(%34))>(%127)>0=0e, wehre the ‘>’ token means one step of CoT, wherein we solve
142
      the right-most, inner-most parenthesis.
143
144
      Performance Evaluation To assess model performance, we randomly select 1000 equations from
145
      a held-out test set. The model is prompted to generate solutions character-by-character, starting from
146
      the equation prompt (e.g., s(+3(-749))>). We use the output to produce two metrics
147
148         1. Loss: using cross-entropy computed for every character of the output.
149
            2. Accuracy: or “number of correct” answers evaluated using only the final answer, which we
150
               define as the first character after the first ‘=’ symbol.
151
152   We then calculate the percentage of correctly solved equations, providing a clear metric of the model’s
153   problem-solving accuracy.
154
155
      Model Architecture For our experiments, we employed a series of tiny GPT models, inspired by
156   the nanoGPT architecture (Karpathy, 2022). These models were specifically designed to be minimal
157   yet functional implementations of the GPT architecture, allowing us to explore the relationship
158   between model size and task performance with fine-grained control. Each model in our study uses
159   a single attention head. We set the feedforward hidden dimension to four times the embedding
160   dimension, a common practice in transformer architectures, providing sufficient complexity in the
161   feedforward networks while keeping the model size constrained. By varying the embedding size and
      the number of transformer layers, we can create a range of models with different parameter counts.

      3     R ESULTS
180
181   To understand how language models acquire mathematical abilities, we focus on accuracy as the
182   primary metric for observing the emergence of learning, following the approach of Wei et al. (2022).
183   While some subsequent studies have questioned the concept of emergence by examining other metrics
184   (Schaeffer et al., 2024), we argue that this critique overlooks a fundamental principle from physics:
185   during a phase transition, not all measurable quantities necessarily exhibit discontinuous changes. In
186   a phase transition, only certain quantities, called the “order parameter”, best captures the essential
187   change in the system’s state. For instance, in the transition from a paramagnet to a ferromagnet, the
188   net magnetization serves as the order parameter, showing a sharp change at the critical temperature,
189
      while other properties may vary smoothly.
190
191
      Emergence of learning on single math operations. Based on the above, we believe that accuracy
      serves as the most appropriate order parameter for observing the emergence of mathematical abilities.
192
      While other metrics like loss may appear continuous (Fig. 13), accuracy often exhibits a more abrupt
193
      change as the model transitions from inability to ability in solving mathematical tasks. We first
194   experimented with three individual operations in ListOps: max, med, and sum. We use equations with
195   a maximum of three nesting levels and three operands per parenthetical group. Figure 1 left illustrates
196   our findings from these basic experiments. As evident from the results, the difficulty of learning these
197   operations varies significantly, with max being the easiest, then med. The sum operation, however,
198   presents a substantially greater challenge, requiring a much larger number of parameters. This stark
199   difference in learning difficulty among seemingly similar mathematical operations raises intriguing
200   questions about the nature of task complexity and its relationship to model size.
201
202   3.1    A MBIGUITIES IN KOLMOGOROV C OMPLEXITY
203

204
      of KC to our specific problem domain presents several ambiguities that require careful consideration.
205
206

207

208   algorithm does our model actually learn? The answer to this question has significant implications for
209   our understanding of the model’s capabilities and the nature of its learning process.

214   of numerical properties or merely learns to manipulate symbols without grasping their numerical
215   significance. We define a set of ground rules for how to design symbolic or number based algorithms

      Model Inference and Algorithmic Complexity We posit that a naively trained model may not
236
      develop a deep understanding that the symbols represent numbers. Consequently, it is more likely that
237
      the model learns a symbolic algorithm. However, at this stage, this remains a hypothesis that requires
238   further investigation. To explore this hypothesis, we calculate the KC for both symbolic and number-
239   based algorithms for each operation. Yet, even for symbolic solutions, there are multiple choices:
240   Does the model form an array, sort the elements, conditionals, or all of the above? While designing
241   solutions for max and med, we found the most straightforward symbolic solutions involved keeping a
242   sorted array of symbols and conditionals for comparing order of appearance of elements. For med, it
243   also involves a brute-force sorting algorithm, again only using conditionals. Yet, these algorithms
244   would not look significantly different when implemented with numbers, the only difference being
245   that the array of symbols does not need to be kept. Therefore, the difference between our numeric
246
      and symbolic solutions for max and med is very small.
247   Another naive solution for max would be to memorize the full table of comparisons, but we think
248   this is algorithm is unlikely to be learned in our experiments for multiple reasons. First, the table
249   requires large memory, yet even very small models learn max. Second, we show below that max
250   model quickly learn an embedding of numbers that is sorted, strongly suggested an algorithm based
251   on a sorted list is being learned. For the sum operation, however, we believe a symbolic solution
252
      without using number properties would require learning the full sum table, meaning an array of all
      tuples [a, b, (a + b)mod10]. Based on this, to attach a KC value to the transition point in Figure 1
253
      left, we choose symbolic solutions based on sorted lists for max and med, and based on memorizing
254
      the sum table for the sum.
255
256   Figure 1 right demonstrates the complexity of the max, med, and sum operations plotted against
257   the learning transition point np (number of parameters at which learning occurs). Interestingly,
258
      we observe an almost linear relationship in this log-log plot, suggesting a power-law relationship,
      KC ≈ 13.5n0.34 p   . In other words, learning grows almost as KC 3 . This observed power-law
259
      relationship raises an intriguing question: Does this trend continue for more complex tasks? To
260
      explore this further, we considered whether combining these operations might increase the overall
261   task complexity and provide additional data points along our KC-transition fit. This approach not
262   only allows us to test the robustness of our observed power-law relationship but also provides insight
263   into how models handle multi-operation tasks. Moreover, these experiments set the stage for a deeper
264   investigation into what the model actually learns, paving the way for our subsequent analyses of
265   embedding layers and attention mechanisms.

268
269   To further explore the relationship between task complexity and model learning, we expanded our
      investigation to include all possible combinations of four operations: max, min, med, and sum. The

      results, illustrated in Figure 2 reveal a surprising and counterintuitive pattern. Contrary to our initial
271   expectations, we observed that models trained on mixed tasks require fewer parameters to achieve
272   proficiency compared to those trained on the sum operation alone. MAX and MIN achieve 100%
273   accuracy first, followed by the MAX+MIN+MED combination, and then all other combinations
274   involving sum, with the single ’sum’ operation being learned last. This finding challenges the intuitive
275   notion that increasing task complexity (by combining multiple operations) would necessitate larger
276   models or more extensive training.
277   Interestingly, the relationship between the number of operations and the transition point (where the
278   model begins to learn effectively) is not straightforward. Our analysis reveals no strong correlation
279   between these factors, suggesting that the mere quantity of operations is not a reliable predictor of
280   learning difficulty. Furthermore, when we attempt to correlate these results with a naive symbolic
281   KC estimate (Figure 2, right, blue dots), we find that the previously observed power-law relationship
282   appears to break down. Additionally, if we use KC based on numeric algorithms (red dots), we get a
283   different pattern, which is more along the previous power-law, except for sum.
284   These observations present us with a significant puzzle: What factors determine the unexpected shifts
285   in transition points for joint tasks? The fact that sum is learned with fewer parameters in mixed
286   operation settings strongly suggests that joint models are discovering more efficient algorithms than
287   those learned when training on sum alone. This leads us to several critical questions:
288
289         1. What is the nature of these more efficient algorithms?
290         2. Are these algorithms leveraging numerical properties, or are they still primarily symbolic?
291
            3. How can we gather evidence to understand the underlying mechanisms at play?

294   turn to more sophisticated analysis techniques. In the following sections, we will explore the use
295   of Principal Component Analysis (PCA) on the models’ embedding layers. Additionally, we will
296   employ various probing techniques to examine the roles and behaviors of different model components

      3.4                             R EFINING C OMPLEXITY E STIMATES : F ROM S YMBOLIC TO N UMERICAL A LGORITHMS
404
405   Building upon our observations from the embedding analysis, we hypothesized that the cosine
406   correlation matrix of the embedding layer could serve as an indicator of whether the model learned a
407   number-based algorithm or a purely symbolic one. This hypothesis, while not definitive proof, is
408   based on the striking differences observed between the sum-only and all3 models in their embedding
409   structures.
410
411   Cosine Correlation as a Proxy for Algorithm Type To test this hypothesis, we plotted the mean
412   of the cosine correlation matrix against the transition point for each model configuration (Figure
413   4 left). Remarkably, we observed an almost perfect correlation in this semi-log plot. This strong
414   relationship suggests that the degree of structure in the embedding space, as measured by the average
415   cosine similarity between embeddings, is closely tied to the model’s learning efficiency. The plot
416
      reveals a clear trend: models with higher mean cosine correlation (indicating more structured number
      representations) tend to have earlier transition points. This observation aligns with our earlier
417
      hypothesis that models learning number-based algorithms, rather than purely symbolic ones, achieve
418
      proficiency with fewer parameters. Additionally, we observe a large gap between the sum-only
419   models and the rest, suggesting that the sum-only model may be the sole model that did not develop
420   any understanding of the numbers.
421
422   Revising Kolmogorov Complexity Estimates Based on these findings, we revised our approach
423   to estimating KC for tasks involving the sum operation. For models showing high correlation
424   between embeddings of consecutive numbers (everything except sum-only), we now assume that
425   some numerical characteristics were learned. Consequently, we switched from a symbolic algorithm
426   to a number-based algorithm for calculating KC in these cases. Specifically, instead of using a
427   simple lookup table or symbolic manipulation for the sum operation, we implemented a bit-wise sum
428   algorithm. This approach more closely aligns with the kind of numerical understanding we believe
429   the model has developed, based on the embedding analysis.

      a dramatically improved relationship between KC and the transition point (Figure 4 right). With

      3.5   T EMPORAL DYNAMICS OF L EARNING AND T RANSFER
460
461   To further investigate our hypotheses about the nature of learning in single-task versus multi-task
462   settings, we conducted a series of experiments tracking the accuracy of models throughout their
463   training process. These experiments not only corroborated our earlier findings but also revealed
464   intriguing dynamics in how models acquire and transfer knowledge across tasks.
465
466   Rapid Multi-task Learning vs. Delayed Single-task Learning We trained twp models, one on
467   sum alone and one all3, at 128 embedding size, which is above transition point for both. The results,
468   illustrated in Figure 5, reveal striking differences in learning dynamics:
469
            1. The all3 model demonstrated rapid acquisition of all three operations (max, med, sum)
470            within approximately 3000 training steps.
471
            2. In contrast, the sum-only model exhibited a significant delay before showing any signs of
472
               learning, with the lag varying across multiple runs.
473
474   Moreover, when evaluating the all3 model on individual tasks, we observed a subtle but important
475   sequence in learning: MAX was learned fastest, followed closely by MED, and then sum. Crucially,
476   the gaps between the learning of these operations were minimal, with the model beginning to grasp
477   sum while still perfecting MAX and MED.
478   These observations strongly support our hypothesis that the development of a robust, number-like
479   internal representation plays a pivotal role in facilitating rapid and efficient learning across multiple
480   operations.
481
482   Transfer Learning and Representation Stability To further explore this hypothesis, we designed
483   a transfer learning experiment: 1) We first trained a model (embedding size 48) on max and med
484   operations until proficiency. 2) We then gradually introduced sum operations, increasing their
485   proportion in the training data from 0% to 100% over 1000 steps, while simultaneously phasing out
      max and med.


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Key findings from this experiment include:
487
488          • The model began learning sum immediately upon its introduction, despite never seeing
489            mixed expressions (e.g., sum with max or med).
490          • Interestingly, the model started to forget max and med once these operations were no longer
491            present in the training data.
492          • Crucially, we verified that the model retained its number-like embedding structure even after
493            max and med were completely phased out.
494
495   Perhaps most strikingly, we found that even much smaller models (embedding size 24) could learn
496   sum perfectly using this hybrid approach, mirroring the efficiency of all3 models. This resulted in a
497   model capable of performing sum operations that was 7x smaller than the vanilla sum-only model,
498   while relying on a more sophisticated, number-like internal representation.
499
500
      Attention and feedforward layers. To investigate the internal dynamics of the sum-only vs all3
      models, we focused on the final layer of a 3-layer transformer network, featuring a single attention
501
      head and an embedding dimension of 128 (Appendix I. We find:
502
503         1. In the attention sublayer, the ’all3’ model shows slightly higher ratio values, suggesting
504            that attention mechanisms play a more pronounced role when the model is trained
505            on diverse operations. This could indicate that the attention sublayer is capturing more
506            complex patterns or relationships necessary for handling multiple operations.
507         2. Conversely, in the feedforward sublayer, the ’sum’ model demonstrates significantly higher
508            ratio values. This suggests that when trained on ’sum’ alone, the model relies more
509            heavily on the feedforward network for computation. This could imply that the ’sum’
510            operation is being implemented more directly through feedforward transformations.
511         3. The larger effect size in the feedforward layer (-0.3379) compared to the attention layer
512            (0.1761) indicates that the difference in behavior is more pronounced in the feedforward
513            component.
514
      These observations suggest a trade-off in how the network allocates its computational resources. The
515
      ’all3’ model appears to leverage its attention mechanism more, potentially to handle the diversity of
516
      operations it was trained on. In contrast, the ’sum’ model seems to channel more of its computation
517   through the feedforward network, possibly developing a more specialized but less flexible approach
518   to solving the sum operation. We discuss further implications in Appendix J.

      A     R ELATED WORKS
649
650
      The scaling laws of language models describe how performance, measured by validation loss, varies
651   with model size (number of parameters) Hoffmann et al. (2022), computational cost (number of
652   FLOPs) Muennighoff et al. (2024), training dataset size (number of data point) Hestness et al. (2017),
653   and knowledge storage capacity (in bits) Allen-Zhu & Li (2024), providing a guideline for designing
654   new models. However, little is known about how the model size scales with the task complexity. There
655   is no consensus on how to measure task complexity in language tasks, and even simple mathematical
656   operations lack clear complexity metrics.
657   In general language models performs poorly on symbolic mathematical tasks Frieder et al. (2024);
658   Dziri et al. (2024); Dave et al. (2024) such as the ListOps dataset Nangia & Bowman (2018) used in
659   this study. Models often struggle with generalization, tending to memorize tasks rather than simulate
660   the underlying algorithms, akin to using look-up tables. While mathematical tasks prove challenging
661   for large language models to learn, they provide a controllable playground to test how models learn
662   different tasks and to evaluate their accuracy quantitatively. The complexity of a mathematical task
663   has been defined previously in various ways, such as the number of bits needed to memorize the
664   task Dave et al. (2024), the number of operands and nesting depth Petruzzellis et al. (2024), and the
665
      computational graph Dziri et al. (2024). However, these metrics do not capture the true complexity of
      the underlying task. Furthermore, it is difficult to determine the exact algorithm learned by the model.
666
667   Here, we analyze how language models learn mathematical tasks by training small models on the
668   ListOps dataset. The ListOps dataset allows us to measure model accuracy explicitly by asking
669   the model to solve mathematical equations. Using this bottom-up approach, we show that once the
670   number of trainable parameters in the model reaches a critical point, the model is able to learn the task.
671
      When training models on datasets based on different levels of operation difficulty, this critical point
      shifts according to the complexity of the task. Contradictorily, we find that combining operations
672
      makes the task easier for the model than individual operations, suggesting that task diversity plays a
673
      significant role.

759   number of bits required to memorize the task, which corresponds to the length of the expression
760   Dave et al. (2024), the number of operands and nesting depth Petruzzellis et al. (2024), and the
761   structure of the computational graph Dziri et al. (2024), which captures the number of nestings.
762   Here, we examine how the number of operands and nesting levels affect the learning ability of small
763   language models. Focusing on the all3 task, which combines max, med, and sum operations,
764   we manipulate complexity by varying the number of operands (arg = {3, 4, 5}) and nesting
765   depth (depth = {3, 4, 5}). The length of equations, measured by the number of characters,
766   depends on both the number of operands and the nesting level. We find that nesting has a greater
767
      impact: increasing the nesting level from 3 to 4 results in longer equations than increasing the number
      of operands from 3 to 5 at a fixed nesting level (Fig. 9). We train the GPT model on the all3
768
      dataset with all combinations of arg (number of operands) and depth (nesting levels), finding that
769
      the model’s performance correlates with the sum of operands and nesting levels (arg + depth).
770   Notably, transition points tend to group together for configurations with the same sum (Fig. 10, 11b).
771   While Fig. 11a demonstrates that the model requires more parameters to solve longer equations,
772   it also indicates that arg + depth serves as a reliable predictor of the transition point. We also
773   calculate the total complexity for each equation, which is a combined metric Ctotal = wCCharacter +
774   wCN esting + wCBranching + wCDiversity + wCOperationComplexity (Fig. 19), showing strong
775   correlation with equation length. In calculating Ctotal only the COperationComplexity term was
776   fixed to the all3 operation, demonstrating that the model’s ability to learn the task depends on the
777   number of operands and the nesting level (Fig. 10). However, the operation complexity, defined by
778
      COperationComplexity represents an independent challenge and follows a different learning pattern,
      as shown in the main paper.

975    In our experiments, we employed a series of tiny GPT models inspired by the nanoGPT architecture
976    (Karpathy, 2022). These models were specifically designed to be minimal yet functional implementa-
977    tions of the GPT architecture, allowing us to explore the relationship between model size and task
978    performance with fine-grained control. Each model in our study uses a single attention head. We
979    set the feedforward hidden dimension to four times the embedding dimension. This 4:1 ratio is a
980
       common practice in transformer architectures, providing sufficient complexity in the feedforward
       networks while keeping the model size constrained. By varying the embedding dimension and the
981
       number of transformer layers, we were able to create a range of models with different parameter
982
       counts. This approach of using scaled-down GPT models enabled us to conduct a detailed analysis of
983    emergent abilities and multitask learning dynamics in a computationally efficient manner
984
985    In the scaling experiments, we consider three ListOps datasets: one whose equations consist of
986
       MAX/MED/SUM operations, one whose equations consist of MAX/MED operations, and one whose
       equations contain only only the SUM operation. All 3 datasets consist of 51k unique CoT examples,
987
       with-holding 1k samples as the test dataset on which we evaluate the model’s generation accuracy.
988
       The remaining 50k samples were partitioned into a training set consisting of 45k (90%) samples, and
989    a validation set consisting of 5k (10%) samples.
990
991    We sweep across GPT models the size of our transformer across embedding sizes of ’[256, 192, 128,
992
       96, 64, 48, 32, 16, 8, 4]’ and layers ’[2,3,4,5,6]’, allowing a diverse range of models with different
       parameter counts. Each experimental configuration is repeated 5 times using different random seeds.
993
       Across all experiments, MLP-hidden-size:embed-size ratio is set to 4:1 and a single attention head is
994
       used. Each model is trained for 50k training steps with no dropout. Learning rate starts at 1e-4 and is
995    exponentially decayed to 1e-5 over the course of training. Gradients are clipped to be norm 1, and we
996    use the ADAM optimizer with weight_decay set to 1e-2. We use a mini-batch size of 64 each having
997    128 tokens which is the maximum context window for the models.

       Each experiment used a single GPU on a machine consisting of 8x NVIDIA A40s. To accelerate the

1250            MAX and MED appears to constrain this search space by fostering the development of
1251            crucial number properties in the embedding space.
1252
1253         2. Analytical vs. Memorization-based Learning: The stark difference in model size and
1254            learning dynamics between the hybrid-trained and vanilla SUM models suggests that the
1255
                former learns a more analytical, generalizable approach, while the latter may rely more
                heavily on memorization.
1256
1257         3. Representation Transfer: The retention of number-like embeddings even after MAX and
1258            MED are no longer trained on demonstrates the stability and transferability of learned
1259            representations.
1260
1261
             4. Multi-task Synergy: The rapid, near-simultaneous learning of multiple operations in the
                All3 model underscores the synergistic benefits of multi-task learning in developing rich,
1262
                generalizable internal representations.
1263
1264
1265   Implications and Ramifications
1266
1267         1. Algorithm Inference: Our findings suggest that the structure of the embedding space, as
1268            captured by the cosine correlation matrix, can serve as a powerful tool for inferring the type
1269
                of algorithm a model has learned.
1270         2. Task Complexity Estimation: The revised KC calculation method, which takes into account
1271            the model’s potential numerical understanding, provides a more accurate measure of task
1272            complexity.
1273
1274         3. Unified Scaling Law: The emergence of a consistent power-law relationship between KC
1275
                and transition point across all task combinations suggests a fundamental principle in how
                neural networks scale with task complexity.
1276
1277         4. Multi-task Synergy: The fact that models trained on multiple operations often outperform
1278            those trained on individual tasks (particularly SUM) underscores the potential benefits of
1279            multi-task learning in developing more efficient and generalizable representations.
1280
1281         5. Rethinking Complexity Measures: Our results highlight the importance of considering the
1282
                learned representations when estimating task complexity.
1283         6. Insights into Model Capacity: The power-law relationship provides a quantitative frame-
1284            work for understanding how model capacity relates to task complexity, potentially guiding
1285            more efficient model design and training strategies.
1286
1287   These findings not only provide a more coherent understanding of the relationship between task
1288   complexity and model learning in our ListOps experiments but also open up new avenues for research
1289   in neural network scaling laws, multi-task learning, and the development of more interpretable AI
1290   systems. Future work could explore whether similar principles hold across other domains and model
1291   architectures, potentially leading to more general theories of neural network learning and capacity.
1292   To further validate these insights and assess the degree to which smaller, hybrid-trained models employ
1293   more "analytical" approaches compared to larger, single-task models, we conducted additional probing
1294   experiments. These involved evaluating models on holdout datasets and analyzing the distribution
1295   of outputs from their attention and feed-forward modules, which we will explore in detail in the

        Where does the complexity come from? There are multiple metrics to determine the complexity of
1355
        a LisOps equation such as the number of characters (CCharacter ), the nesting level (CN esting ), the
1356    number of operands, the number of branching (CBranching ), operation diversity (CDiversity ), or the
1357    type operation itself (COperationComplexity ). Here we introduce a metric that takes the weighted sum
1358    of all these metrics with equal weight (w = 0.2):
1359
          Ctotal = wCCharacter + wCN esting + wCBranching + wCDiversity + wCOperationComplexity (1)
1360
1361
        We calculate the operation complexity (COperationComplexity ) based on the Kolmogorov complexity
        of each operation (KC· ) separately and weighted with the number of operations (N· ):
1362
1363                    COperationComplexity = N− KC− + N+ KC+ + N/ KC/ + N% KC%                                     (2)
1364    The Kolmogorov complexity is defined as the length of the shortest code or algorithm capable of
1365    generating a given output. In Python, we approximate this complexity by implementing each operation
1366    individually, using a parser to solve the equation without relying on built-in Python functions, and then
1367    measuring the length of the compressed file. Assuming that the model recognizes numbers as symbols
1368
        we implement the max, min, and med operations using a sorted list of symbols e.g. 0 < 1 < 2 < 3 <
        . . . and the sum module 10 operation using a character table e.g. [[(0, 0), 0], [(0, 1), 1], . . . [(9, 9), 8]].
1369
1370    When does the model learn? We analyze the learning capabilities of a small GPT-style model,
1371    which is based on the transformer architecture Vaswani et al. (2017); Karpathy, utilizing the ListOps
1372    dataset (maximum 3 nesting levels and 3 arguments). The model is built on a single-layer transformer
1373    block paired with an MLP layer, allowing us to control the number of trainable parameters by tuning
1374
        the embedding dimension (from 4 to 256) and the number of layers (from 1 to 6). We train each
        model on all possible operation combinations and measure their performance in terms of accuracy,
1375
        observing that models with a critical number of trainable parameters (ncritical ) successfully learn the
1376
        task (Fig. 19). ncritical highly depends on the operation combinations, meaning the task complexity,
1377    is low for simple operations such as min or max, and high for more complex operations like sum.
1378    Surprisingly, we observe that combining the sum task with any other operation(s) in the training data
1379    makes it easier to learn for the model. Also, we find that applying our extensive complexity metric
1380    (Ctotal ) 2 on the training data and calculating the average complexity for each operation combination
1381    the sum task has the highest complexity and the diversified task are less complex showing a strong
1382    correlation between complexity and the critical number of parameters required for successful learning
1383    (Fig. 19b).
