009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Distributionally robust policy learning aims to find a policy that performs well
012            under the worst-case distributional shift, and yet most existing methods for robust
013            policy learning consider the worst-case joint distribution of the covariate and the
014            outcome. The joint-modeling strategy can be unnecessarily conservative when we
015            have more information on the source of distributional shifts. This paper studies
016            a more nuanced problem — robust policy learning under the concept drift, when
017            only the conditional relationship between the outcome and the covariate changes.
018
               To this end, we first provide a doubly-robust estimator for evaluating the worst-case
               average reward of a given policy under a set of perturbed conditional distributions.
019
               We show that the policy value estimator enjoys asymptotic normality even if
020
               the nuisance parameters are estimated with a slower-than-root-n rate. We then
021            propose a learning algorithm that outputs the policy maximizing the estimated
022            policy value within a given policy class Π, and show that the sub-optimality gap
023            of the proposed algorithm is of the order κ(Π)n−1/2 , with κ(Π) is the entropy
024            integral of Π under the Hamming distance and n is the sample size. A matching
025            lower bound is provided to show the optimality of the rate. The proposed methods
026            are implemented and evaluated in numerical studies, demonstrating substantial
027            improvement compared with existing benchmarks.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In a wide range of fields, the abundance of user-specific historical data provides opportunities
033
      for learning efficient individualized policies. Examples include learning the optimal personalized
      treatment from electronic health record data (Murphy, 2003; Kim et al., 2011; Chan et al., 2012),
034
      or obtaining an individualized advertising strategy using past customer behavior data (Bottou et al.,
035
      2013; Kallus & Udell, 2016). Driven by such a practical need, a line of works have been devoted
036   to developing efficient policy learning algorithms using historical data — a task often known as
037   offline policy learning (Dudík et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2012; Swaminathan & Joachims, 2015a;b;c;
038   Kitagawa & Tetenov, 2018; Athey & Wager, 2021; Zhou et al., 2023; Zhan et al., 2023; Bibaut et al.,
039   2021; Jin et al., 2021; 2022a).
040
      Most existing methods for offline policy learning deliver performance guarantees under the premise
041
      that the target environment remains the same as that from which the historical data is collected. It
042   has been widely observed, however, that such a condition is hardly met in practice (see e.g., Recht
043   et al. (2019); Namkoong et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023); Jin et al. (2023) and the references therein).
044   Under distribution shift, a policy learned in one environment often shows degraded performance
045   when deployed in another environment. To address this issue, there is an emerging body of research
046   on robust policy learning, which aims at finding a policy that still performs well when the target
047   distribution is perturbed. Pioneering works in this area consider the case where the joint distribution
048   of the covariates and the outcome is shifted from the training distribution, and devise algorithms that
049   output a policy achieving reliable worst-case performance under the aforementioned shifts Si et al.
050
      (2023); Kallus et al. (2022). The joint modeling approach, however, ignores the type of distributional
      shifts, and the resulting worst-case value can be unnecessarily conservative in practice.
051
052   Indeed, distributional shifts can be categorized into two classes by their sources: (1) the shift in
053   the covariate X, and/or (2) the shift in the conditional relationship between the outcome Y and the
      covariate X. The two types of distributional shifts are different in nature, have different implications

054
      on the objectives, and call for distinct treatment (Namkoong et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Jin et al.,
055   2023; Ai & Ren, 2024). To be concrete, imagine that the distribution of covariates changes while
056   that of Y | X remains invariant — in this case, the distribution shift is identifiable/estimable since
057   the covariates are often accessible in the target environment. As a result, it is often uncessary to
058   account for the worst-case covariate shift rather than directly correcting for it. Alternatively, when
059   the Y | X distribution changes but the X distribution remains invariant, the distribution shift is no
060   longer identifiable, and we need to account for the worst-case situation. This latter setting, known
061   as concept drift, occurs when the distribution of the unobserved confounder changes over time, or
062   due to sudden external shocks (Widmer & Kubat, 1996; Lu et al., 2018; Gama et al., 2014). For
063
      example, in advertising, the customer behavior can evolve over time as the environment changes,
      while the population remains largely the same. In personalized medicine, treatment may be affecting
064
      patients’ outcomes through some unmeasured confounders that have different distributions in the
065
      training and target cohort, thereby inducing a concept drift. In these applications — with the one
066   extra bit of information that the shift is only in the conditional reward distribution — can we obtain a
067   more efficient policy learning algorithm?
068
069
      Motivated by the above situations, we study robust policy learning under concept drift in this work.
      Most existing methods for robust policy learning (Si et al., 2023; Kallus et al., 2022) model the
070
      distributional shift jointly without distinguishing the sources, and the corresponding algorithms turn
071
      out to be suboptimal. The reason behind their suboptimality is that the worst-case distributions under
072   the two models — the joint-shift model and the concept-drift model — can be substantially different,
073   so it would be a “waste” of our budget to consider adversarial distributions that are not feasible under
074   concept drift. It is worth mentioning that a recent paper by Mu et al. (2022) accounts for the sources
075   of distributional shifts in policy learning; their approach, however, applies only when the covariates
076   take a finite number of values, and therefore is limited in its applicability. When the covariate space
077   is infinite, it remains unclear how to efficiently learn a robust policy under concept drift. The current
078   work aims to fill in the gap by answering the following question:
079
      How can we efficiently learn a policy with optimal worst-case average performance under concept
080
                                       drift with minimal assumptions?
081
082   We provide a rigorous answer to the above question. Specifically, we assume the covariate distribution
083   remains the same in the training and target environments, while the Y | X distribution shift is bounded
084   in KL-divergence by a pre-specified constant δ. Our goal is to find a policy that maximizes the
085   worst-case averaged outcome over all possible target distributions satisfying the previous condition.
086
087   1.1   O UR CONTRIBUTIONS
088
      Towards robust policy learning under concept drift, we make the following contributions.
089
090         1. Policy evaluation: Given a policy, we present a doubly-robust estimator for the worst-
091            case policy value under concept drift. We prove that the estimator is asymptotic normal
092            under mild conditions on the estimation rate of the nuisance parameter. Our approach
093            involves solving the dual form of a distributionally robust optimization problem and taking
094            a de-biased step to deal with the slow convergence of the optimizer, thereby obtaining an
095            estimator with root-n convergence rate.
096         2. Policy learning: We propose a robust policy learning algorithm that outputs a policy maxi-
097            mizing the estimated policy value over a policy class Π. Compared with the oracle optimal
098            policy, the policy provided
                                       √ by our algorithm with high probability has a regret/suboptimality
099            gap of the order κ(Π)/ n, where κ(Π) is a measure quantifying the policy class complex-
100            ity (to be formalized shortly) and n is the number of samples. Compared with Mu et al.
101
               (2022), our algorithm and theory apply to general covariate spaces and potentially infinite
               policy classes, while their method is restricted to finite covariate space and policy class.
102
               We complement the upper bound with a matching lower, thus establishing the minimax
103
               optimality of our proposed algorithm. We summarize the comparison between our result
104            and prior work in Table 1 for better demonstration.
105
            3. Implementation and empirics: We provide efficient implementation of our robust policy
106
               learning algorithm, and compare its empirical performance with existing benchmarks in
107            numerical studies. Our proposed method exhibits substantial improvement.

126   1.2   R ELATED WORKS
127
128
      Offline policy learning. There is a long list of works devoted to offline policy learning. Most of
      them assume no distributional shifts (e.g., Dudík et al. (2011); Zhang et al. (2012); Swaminathan &
129
      Joachims (2015a;b;c); Kitagawa & Tetenov (2018); Athey & Wager (2021); Zhou et al. (2023)). Zhan
130
      et al. (2023); Jin et al. (2021; 2022a) allow the data to be adaptively collected, but the distribution over
131   the covariate and the (potential) outcomes remain invariant in the training and target environment.
132
133   As mentioned earlier, the work of Si et al. (2023); Kallus et al. (2022) study robust policy learning
134
      when the joint distribution of (X, Y ) ranges in the neighborhood of the training distribution; Mu
      et al. (2022) consider the case when the covariate shift and Y | X shift are specified separately; their
135
      method, however, is restricted to finite covariate space, and their sub-optimality gap is logarithmic
136
      factors slower than parametric rates. The work of Kallus & Zhou (2021) concerns robust policy
137   learning when the distribution shift is caused by hidden confounders — this is in fact a special type
138   of concept drift — and the corresponding Y | X shift is assumed to be bounded uniformly, which
139   is quite different from our f -divergence bound. More recently, Guo et al. (2024) considers a pure
140   covariate shift with a focus on policy evaluation, where the setup and the goal are different from ours.
141
      Distributionally robust optimization. More broadly, our work is also closely related to DRO,
142
      where the goal is to learn a model that has good performance under the worst-case distribution
143   (e.g., Bertsimas & Sim (2004); Delage & Ye (2010); Hu & Hong (2013); Duchi et al. (2019); Dudík
144   et al. (2011); Zhang et al. (2023)). The major focus of the aforementioned works involves parameter
145   estimation and prediction in supervised settings; we however take a decision-making perspective and
146   aim at learning a individualized policy with optimal worst-case performance guarantees.

437   Definition 4.3 (Natarajan dimension). Given an M -action policy class Π, we say a set of m points
438   {x1 , . . . , xm } is shattered by Π if there exist two functions f−1 , f1 : {x1 , . . . , xm } 7→ [M ] such that
