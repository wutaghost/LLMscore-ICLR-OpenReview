                                                       Abstract
008
009
                We formalise the essential data of objective functions as equality constraints
010
                on composites of learners. We call these constraints "tasks", and we
011             investigate the idealised view that such tasks determine model behaviours.
012             We develop a flowchart-like graphical mathematics for tasks that allows us
013             to; (1) design and optimise desired behaviours model-agnostically; (2) offer
014             a unified perspective of approaches in machine learning across domains;
015             (3) import insights from theoretical computer science into practical machine
016             learning. As a proof-of-concept of the potential practical impact of our
017             theoretical framework, we exhibit and implement a novel "manipulator"
018             task that minimally edits input data to have a desired attribute. Our model-
019
                agnostic approach achieves this without the need for custom architectures,
                adversarial training, random sampling, or interventions on the data, hence
020
                enabling capable, small-scale, and training-stable models.

      The diagrammatic notation is formally equivalent to the traditional symbolic notation
055   with the addition of type-information about inputs and outputs. While the formal details
056   (Appendix A) involve category theory, the power of string diagrams lies in their intuitive
057   visual nature: by reading the diagrams as flowcharts from left to right, practitioners can
058   leverage these diagrams to reason about ML tasks without needing to fully grasp the
059   underlying mathematical formalism. In summary, nodes depicted as various shapes are
060   functions, and wires are datatypes which can be understood as carrying information.
061   To explore the expressive power of tasks, we abstract away implementation details such as
062   architecture and training by idealising models to be universal function approximators
063   that can, in principle, perfectly optimise objective functions. Thus each task can be viewed
064   purely as an equational constraint on the behaviour of the learners, comparable to equational
065   constraints on the possible values of variables in algebra. This perspective allows us two
066   ways to specialise tasks by imposing structural inductive biases, by specifying architectural
067   choices, or by adding additional objectives.

      1.1       Contributions


                                                                                2

108
      We make three primary contributions in this work.
109
110   Our first, theoretical contribution is the formalisation of a common but informal standard
111   procedure in deep learning, which may be summarised as the following recipe:
112
113
            1. Characterise desired behavior via equational constraints (tasks) between learners
114         2. Implement tasks by treating neural networks as universal approximators
115
            3. Convert equations to loss function by a choice of hyperparameters, namely weighted
116            sum of constituent losses and choice of divergences
117
118   Our second, theoretical contribution is a demonstration that using our framework, we may
119   analyse, predict, and design behaviours of models. Using specialisation and algebraic
120   reasoning, we can analyse the behaviour of complex models in terms of simple, well-
121   understood tasks we call patterns. Moreover, we can define a novel synthetic relationship
122   between tasks called refinement which describes when one optimally trained set of tasks
123   entails satisfaction of a different set of tasks. Altogether, we may use these techniques
124   to understand and compare the behaviour of models before committing to potentially
125
      costly training. Example 2.6 and Propositions 2.9, 3.2, and 3.3 illustrate the kinds of formal
      reasoning our language enables.
126
127   Our third, practical contribution and proof-of-concept is the implementation of a novel
128   problem class we call manipulation (Section 3), which formalises (Bancilhon & Spyratos,
129   1981) the problem of viewing and editing a targeted attribute of data while "leaving other
130   aspects the same". Notably, this task represents the first problem class to our knowledge that
131
      appears to be naturally solved by the relatively underexplored technique of "multi-learner
      multi-objective learning", which our framework naturally accommodates. As examples in
132
      image domains, we; change only the colour of a shape (Figure 1); change the value of a
133
      handwritten digit without affecting other stylistic properties (Figure 2); and change only
134   whether a person is smiling in an image (Figure 6). Even in these toy domains, we observe a
135   range of benefits we expect to scale: by following our recipe we obtain architecture-agnostic
136   (Table 1) style-transference models without the need for randomness, adversarial training,
137   or modality- and architecture-specific interventions, with good interpolation properties
138   (Figure 6).
139
      We conclude by discussing relations to similar approaches in the literature, along with
140   avenues and prospects for further development.

      2.2       Patterns are "nice" tasks
177
178
      We do not expect there to be a general and systematic method to translate between
179
      natural-language behavioural specifications and tasks; if such a method existed, then
180   all of deep learning would be reduced to hyperparameter search. There are often
181   many different ways to approach a problem in ML, much like there is no single
182   correct way to write software, or design a building. This suggests to us the view of
183   patterns: some tasks are well understood, usable modularly and in many contexts,
184   and easily modifiable, and such tasks can be viewed as design patterns – borrowing
185   a term from software engineering (Beck & Cunningham, 1987)1. In this section,
186   we suggest some examples of patterns that correspond to well-studied methods and
187   paradigms in ML, and we show how to use patterns as an accessible basis to analyse models.

264   atomic tasks as equations, the processes of Φ may be composed to satisfy the equations of Ψ.
265   Φ and Ψ are equivalent if they refine one another, which we denote Φ ≡ Ψ.

      trained" CycleGAN. (Elaboration of conditions and proof in Section C.2)

337   allows us to transform any classifier of attributes into a "minimally invasive" editor of those

339   inductive biases.

460   3.3   Experimental results III: Interpretability applications on real-world data
461
462   As a further test of the robustness of tasks to implementation choices, we specialised the
463   put to be a simple vector addition in the latent space of an autoencoder (Figure 5), for
464   the relatively complex Large-scale CelebFaces Attributes dataset. We found that restricting
465   put to be linear in this way increased training stability. Moreover, in the same way that
466
      we would expect a latent space "filtered through" the probabilistic structure of Gaussians
      to yield good sampling properties (Example 1.4), we would predict that enforcing linear
467
      structure on the latent space would yield "linear" properties. Indeed, we exhibit continuous
468
      interpolation in generated outputs between normally discrete class labels (Figure 6), and
469   class-sensitive separation of latent space embeddings in the autoencoder. We consider this
470   to be compelling evidence that since our framework is agnostic, implementation details
471   may be engineered to obtain additional desirable properties without compromising
472   behavioural specifications.

      4.1    Summary
515
516   We introduced a diagrammatic language for representing and reasoning about the
517   behaviour of machine learning models in terms of tasks, viewed as the essential data of
518   objective functions. By leveraging category theory and string diagrams, our work establishes
519
      a cross-disciplinary formal bridge between theoretical computer science and practical
      machine learning, providing new conceptual tools for analyzing ML systems and permitting
520
      the transfer of insights between traditionally separate fields.
521
522   The proposed framework allows capturing existing tasks in machine learning, providing
523   intuitive insights rooted in mathematical rigour. We identify a set of widespread and
524   well-understood tasks, which we call patterns. We can analyse some tasks as composites of
525
      patterns (Example 2.6) while other tasks can be understood as specialisations of patterns (Ex-
      ample 1.4). The rewrite system inherent to string diagrams, allows us to identify relationships
526
      between different tasks and formalise intuitions (Proposition 2.9, Proposition 3.2).
527
528   Beyond theoretical insights, the proposed language also allows the creation of new training
529   paradigms. As preliminary empirical validation of our theory’s utility and potential, we
530   introduced a novel task type called manipulator that produces a class-conditioned and style-
531
      preserving generative model counterpart for a given classifier. In the image domain, we were
      able to verify predicted behaviours (Section 3.1), and we demonstrated the ability to design
532
      novel end-to-end capabilities, such as end-to-end editing of complex attributes (Section 3.2)
533
      and the imposition of linear structure on latent space representations (Section 3.3), which
534   allowed continuous interpolation between discrete class labels on real data, and separated
535   latent space embeddings of different classes. Notably, this was achieved without adversarial
536   training conditions, random sampling, preprocessing of data, or hardcoded interventions in
537   the architectures, i.e.:
538
539
      Our framework enables capable, small-scale, and training-stable models.

      4.2   Relation to extant work
541
542   Regarding our nascent theoretical framework as a whole, the style of engineering beginning
543   from tasks is already common practice in many fields of ML, and we sought here to place
544   these practices on a more rigorous footing, and to probe their strengths and limitations. Our
545   mathematical approach draws broadly from the field of Applied Category Theory (Fong
546   & Spivak, 2019a), particularly in the use of string diagrams for the higher-algebraic data
547   of concurrently and serially composed functions, which enables compact representation
548   and reasoning with otherwise cumbersome symbolic equivalents when dealing with
549
      multiple learners in tandem. To our knowledge, our concern with the composition of tasks
      among many learners distinguishes our aims and formal choices from approaches that
550
      employ similar mathematical formulations, both within the category-theoretic literature
551
      (cf. Gavranović et al. (2024)), and without (cf. the variational generalisation of Bayesian
552   inference presented in Knoblauch et al. (2022)).
553
554
555
      While our approach is essentially neurosymbolic in spirit, it does not fit neatly into the
      mainstream triad of neurosymbolic approaches (d’Avila Garcez & Lamb, 2020); we do not
556
      encode symbolic data for neural operations, nor do we interface neural approaches with
557
      symbolic engines, nor are we hardcoding expert knowledge representations. Moreover,
558   our aims differ: while neurosymbolic approaches often seek to manipulate symbolic data
559   systematically by neural means, our framework operates at a higher level of abstraction,
560   seeking to use the systematicity of higher-algebraic equational characterisations as a means
561   to shape the neural ends. Hence our perspective may complement existing approaches to
562   structure in machine learning.
563
564   Regarding manipulation in particular, this was to our knowledge the first practically demon-
565   strated synthesis of insights from Bidirectional Transformations as a subfield of database
566   theory (Abou-Saleh et al., 2018) with ML. While explicitly neurosymbolic approaches have
567   been tried for similar editing tasks before (see e.g. Smet et al. (2023)), owing to the influence
568   of database theory in our approach, to our knowledge our statement and execution of this
569   task enjoys the maximal permissible generality and implementation agnosticism among
570   similar attribute-editing tasks, without sacrificing rigour and systematicity.
571
572   4.3   Limitations and Prospects
573
574   Concerning manipulation in particular, an immediately evident limitation of this practical
575   demonstration is a lack of exploration of how the difficulty of training such ensembles of
576   learners behaves at scale, with respect to more complex and multimodal datasets, and
577   with a wider range of architectures. Concerning scale, while none of the products of our
578
      experimentation are state-of-the-art with respect to specific applications, we believe the
      variety and promise of these results serve as a compelling validation of our theoretical
579
      framework’s utility and potential. Concerning applications of manipulator beyond the
580
      image domain, we report on some sketch experiments in sentiment-manipulation on text in
581   Appendix F, where we also comment on the nature of technical difficulties to be overcome
582   in the application of our framework to complex domain data, and offer an explanation for
583   mode collapses observed during training by empirically relating manipulator to other
584   generative classification approaches. Concerning a wider range of architectures, we report
585   on some specialisations of the learners. However, we leave exploring manipulation in
586   combination with state-of-the-art architectures, such as diffusion, for future work.
587
588   Addressing the theoretical framework of tasks more broadly, our reliance on equational
589   characterisations is double-edged. On one hand, it is uncommon to find such characterisa-
590   tions of mathematical systems of interest as they are usually defined by more direct means,
591   and this presents a theoretical limitation. On the other hand, it appears that the strength of
592   equational characterisations when applied to ML lies in imposing structure on "the way to
593   learn to solve a problem" rather than on the solutions or problems themselves (Sutton, 2019).
      This suggests promising future possibilities of our mathematical framework in bridging


                                                     11
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


594
      structural-symbolic approaches from computer science more broadly with methods that can
595   effectively leverage computation; we suggest "neural data structures" as a sketch experiment
596   in Appendix E.
597
598
      While we have demonstrated that, in certain cases, tasks can determine behaviours, there
599
      is a theoretical gap in the converse analysis of behaviours of trained models in terms of
600
      their basic tasks. The problem is typified by generative models where it is impossible
601   a priori for all of the constituent tasks to be simultaneously perfectly optimised. The
602   prototypical illustrating example is the GAN, where it is impossible for both the generator
603   and discriminator to be perfect. Conceptually, the gap is that we have only dealt here with
604   "static" ensembles which in principle admit idealised loss-minimisations, whereas some
605   generative models use adversarial tasks to enforce "dynamic" training forces for a variety of
606   purposes, such as representation regularisation for easier sampling in VAEs. While we do
607   offer some initial methods of analysis in Section C.2, a more thorough and encompassing
608   analysis is beyond the scope of this paper.
609
610   Future theoretical developments will seek to incorporate other aspects of ML: for example,
611   relating to work that focuses on the choice of model architecture (Khatri et al., 2024) and
612   interactions with the underlying data distribution (Bronstein et al., 2021). While our current
613   experiments focus on demonstrating our framework’s validity, future practical developments
614   will explore applications to more complex, real-world ML challenges, where we envision our
615
      approach informing areas such as AutoML, interpretable AI, and formal verification of ML
      systems: the compositional nature of our task-based framework naturally aligns with neural
616
      architecture search by potentially informing principled search strategies for optimal model
617
      architectures, and the explicit representation of model behaviours as equational constraints
618   could enhance interpretability and facilitate formal verification.

      Ethics Statement
649
650
      We recognise the potential impact of ML, both positive and negative, on global society and
651   nature as ethical stakeholders.
652
653   Regarding society, we recognise that expertise in, and steering of, the development and
654
      implementation of ML systems is concentrated in the hands of relatively few. Hence, we
      believe that broad democratisation of methods and understanding would be an improvement
655
      upon current circumstances. Accordingly, in the development of our theory and exposi-
656
      tion, we were biased away from formal rigidity in favour of accessibility and expressive
657   possibility. Our "pattern language" approach seeks to be a minimally constraining mode of
658   communication, reasoning, and design.
659
660
      Regarding nature, we recognise that practical ML carries a growing ecological footprint,
      primarily due to the energy-intensive nature of training large models and running extensive
661
      experiments. We believe that a compositional approach could enable efficient and smaller
662
      model design, reducing environmental impact.
663
664   By formalising ML tasks in a way that allows for more principled and targeted experi-
665   mentation, we aim to encourage a more thoughtful, resource-conscious, and participatory
666
      approach to ML research and development. We acknowledge that there is still much work
      to be done in this regard, and we hope that our framework will inspire further research into
667
      sustainable and harm-free ML practices.

1213   B       Strong manipulation
1214
1215
1216   The basic manipulation admits pathological counterexamples, which we may block by
1217   imposing additional tasks. We can treat these new tasks as additional regularisation terms.
1218   This section further illustrates a form of "legalistic" thinking using tasks: by thinking of ways
1219   that "noncooperative" or "naughty" learners might seek to satisfy tasks without exhibiting the
1220   behaviour that the modeller desires. By identifying these counterexamples and constructing
1221   additional tasks that block them, the modeller may iteratively improve the behaviour of
1222   the model. For illustration, consider the following examples of pathological behaviour that
1223
       satisfy basic manipulation, again in the setting of editing the colour of a shape.
1224
       Example B.1 (Flipping). Consider a put that changes the colour of a shape as desired,
1225
       but then vertically flips the shape. If the classifier get is insensitive to the position and
1226
       orientation of the shape, then Classify, PutGet, and GetPut are satisfied. Moreover, since
1227   a vertical flip is its own inverse, composing two puts as in the Undoability task will not
1228   detect this aberration. Speaking in more general terms, if there are properties that get is
1229   insensitive to, there must be additional guardrails to ensure that put preserves these other
1230   properties as the identity, rather than one of potentially very many self-inverse symmetries.
1231
1232   Example B.2 (Adversarial decorations). While the classifier get may be perfect in-distribution,
1233   there are no guarantees about its behaviour out of distribution, for example, when given
1234   images with multiple shapes, where it might only classify the leftmost shape. So, it is
1235   possible that put learns to make edits that take the resulting image out-of-distribution: for
1236
       example, by adding a red circle next to a blue square to fool the classifier into outputting
       "red". This would satisfy Classify, PutGet, and GetPut. If the put can recognise and undo
1237
       its own decorations, then Undoability will also be satisfied. Speaking more generally, we
1238
       require additional guardrails to ensure that put returns something in-distribution.

1260   Remark B.4 (Why basic manipulation is preferable in practice). We have observed informally
1261   that conditions such as those in basic manipulation where learners are cooperative and
1262   there are only learners on the LHS appear to be more stable during training. We suggest
1263
       a sketch reason why: in the tasks of strong manipulation, PutPut has put occur on both
       the LHS and RHS, which establishes a nontrivial dependence on the current position on
1264
       parameter-space of put in the process of finding a solution. Similarly, the GAN rules of strong
1265
       manipulation establishes adversarial mutual dependencies in the parameters of dsc and put.
1266   Conceptually, these dependencies create dynamical systems on the paths that the learners
1267   take over the course of training in parameter-space, which may for instance include stable
1268   orbits and chaotic behaviour, and may be highly sensitive to initial conditions. A further
1269   elaboration of "static" versus "dynamic" tasks in tandem with the ability to express equivalent
1270   tasks is potentially useful for creating train-stable models with equivalent behaviour, but
1271   this is beyond the scope of this paper, and left for future work.

       F.2   Characterising Manipulation as generative classification
1831
1832   Training manipulation autoregressively (e.g. when instantiating the learners as transform-
1833   ers for sequential data) is slow due to autoregressing twice for PutPut and Undoability.
1834   Moreover, our attempts to autoregressively manipulate the sentiment of IMDB reviews
1835   often resulted in a form of posterior collapse where put ignored the attribute and behaved
       as the identity function on text. Conceptually, this is because the identity function satisfies


                                                     34
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1836
       GetPut, PutPut and Undoability, and while the identity fails on PutGet, failing on one
1837   component of the combined loss function does not provide a strong enough incentive to
1838   move away from the identity in parameter-space.
1839
1840
       Notably, these shortcomings mirror that of VAEs, which also suffer from posterior collapse
1841
       (Bond-Taylor et al., 2022) in highly structured domains such as video (Babaeizadeh et al.,
1842
       2018) and text (Bowman et al., 2016). This suggested to us that puts may be generative
1843   classifiers, which could potentially explain why mode collapse was occurring in complex
1844   domains. Borrowing terminology from (Ng & Jordan, 2001), classifiers are discriminative
1845   if they seek to learn the conditional distribution 𝑝(𝑎|𝑑) of attributes given data (as in the
1846   classification pattern), and otherwise they are generative if they seek to learn the joint
1847   distribution 𝑝(𝑑, 𝑎) (as, for example, a VAE). The tradeoffs between the two types are well
1848   studied, e.g. performance-wise, generative models may converge faster with limited data,
1849   but discriminative models often achieve lower asymptotic error, and it is well known that
1850   learning generative models is harder (Vapnik, 1998).
1851
1852   As generative classifiers, puts in the manipulation task appear to approximate the
1853   Bayesian inverse of a discriminative classifier cls, approximately as well as VAEs do. To
1854   demonstrate this, we tried three ways to train an "informationally identical" cls′ given an
1855   initial cls, provided access to unlabelled data to obtain a distribution of pairs (𝑑, cls(𝑑)) by:
1856   (a) directly training cls by classification, (b) training a generative classifier, in our case a
1857   VAE, and (c) training manipulation around cls-as-get to obtain a put, and then train cls′
1858   to satisfy the tasks of manipulation except for Classify. Repeating this process several times,
1859
       we would expect to see some loss of accuracy due to imperfect Bayesian inversion. Indeed, we
       see in Figure 8 that (b) and (c) have similar decays in accuracy, indicating that manipulation
1860
       and VAEs have similar performance in this case. This experiment was performed using the
1861
       trained components obtained from the MNIST experiment (Section D.3), and in addition
1862   to the tasks (a-e) we (f) trained a VAE to learn the joint distribution of images and labels
1863   produced by get’, and we (g) trained a get supervised using labels and images generated
1864   from the VAE. The VAE encoder and decoder are also based on multilayer perceptrons -
1865   each is comprised of an MLP trunk and two linear heads for generating the means and
1866   log-variances of the latent space, or the image and labels, respectively. The latent space is
1867   comprised of independent normally distributed variables as in (Kingma & Welling, 2022),
1868   and is sampled using the standard reparameterization trick. The hyperparameters of the
