                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014            In real-world operations with combinatorial structures like vehicle routing prob-
015            lems, similar optimization problems have to be solved repeatedly with slight pa-
016            rameter variations. A key challenge in such scenarios is achieving both high so-
017            lution quality and fast computation time, while traditional methods like heuris-
018            tics or branch-and-bound struggle to achieve both simultaneously. In contrast,
019            problem-speciﬁc solvers can effectively balance solution quality and computa-
020            tion speed for speciﬁc problems. However, since real-world problems have more
021            complex structures, they can handle only subproblems. To enhance the appli-
022
               cability of the problem-speciﬁc solvers, we propose a framework that integrates
               a problem-speciﬁc solver and a neural solver. Our framework decomposes the
023
               optimization problem into subproblems so that some of which can be solved by
024
               problem-speciﬁc solvers, such as the traveling salesperson problem. For the re-
025            maining portions of the problem, we utilize the similarities of the problems and
026            design a neural solver. By integrating two solvers, we can utilize the strengths
027            of the problem-speciﬁc solver in balancing solution accuracy and computation
028            speed, as well as the neural solver s ability to infer a solution from the similarity
029            of optimization problems. Based on the case study with the min-max capacitated
030            vehicle routing problem, we demonstrate that it outperforms the state-of-the-art
031            solver regarding both high solution quality and short computation time.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      When solving combinatorial optimization problems for real-world operations, optimization prob-
036   lems often need to be solved repeatedly, with similar structures and slightly varying parameters. For
037   example, in the delivery of liqueﬁed petroleum gas cylinders, the problem needs to be solved per
038   day for the same area (Yoshida et al., 2024), the waste collecting problem is solved per day (Han
039   et al., 2024), and in food delivery, the problem of assigning each order to each driver needs to
040   be solved every few minutes (Simoni & Winkenbach, 2023). In such frequently occurring tasks,
041   quickly obtaining high-quality solutions is crucial to ensure operational efﬁciency.
042   Although various studies have been conducted to solve combinatorial optimization problems, ex-
043   isting methods do not fully balance the solution accuracy and the computation time for real-world
044   problems. (see Figure 1) As exact methods, branch-and-bound or branch-and-cut (Achterberg, 2007)
045   can be used to obtain the optimal solutions, while it is challenging to incorporate such algorithms
046   into real-world operations because the computation time exponentially increases along with the
047   problem size. On the other hand, heuristics such as genetic algorithms have been studied. While
048   better solutions can be obtained with appropriate algorithm selection and hyperparameter settings, it
049   is difﬁcult to obtain high-quality solutions quickly for large-scale problems with many constraints.
050
      For speciﬁc problems, algorithms capable of both speed and accuracy have been developed, such as
      those for the traveling salesperson problem (Applegate, 2006) and the capacitated vehicle routing
051
      problem (Helsgaun, 2017).
052
053   We observed that the combinatorial optimization problems in real-world operations often contain
      subproblems, such as the traveling salesperson problem (TSP) and knapsack problem, for which

      problem-speciﬁc solvers have been developed. For example, in a multi-day delivery planning prob-
      lem, once the customers for each vehicle are determined for a given day, the problem of ﬁnding the
077
      delivery route can be decomposed to solve the TSP per vehicle independently. Considering this fact,
078
      we can effectively solve the large-scale combinatorial problem with a problem-speciﬁc solver for
079   subproblem computation if we can solve the remaining problem without a problem-speciﬁc solver.
080   The ﬁeld that deals with optimizing through subproblems solutions is known as bilevel optimiza-
081   tion. Bilevel optimization refers to problems where decision-making occurs in two stages, with
082   decisions in the ﬁrst stage affecting those in the second stage. The optimization problems in the ﬁrst
083   and second stages are called the upper- and lower-level problems, respectively. A typical bilevel
084   optimization method is the nested approach, in which the lower-level problem corresponding to the
085   upper-level problem’s solution is solved, and based on the result, the solution to the upper-level
086   problem is sequentially updated (Mathieu et al., 1994; Yin, 2000; Sinha et al., 2017; Calvete et al.,
087
      2008; Liang & Miikkulainen, 2015; Daniel Handoko et al., 2015). To solve real-world problems,
      the bottleneck in the nested approach is the number of updates of the upper-level solution. Thus, re-
088
      ducing the number of updates is a key challenge to achieving high-quality solutions using the nested
089
      approach with the problem-speciﬁc solver.
090
091   To reduce the number of updates, obtaining a good initial solution for the upper-level problem is
092   important. When the upper-level problem is solved repeatedly, solutions obtained by past computa-
093
      tions can be utilized to obtain a better initial solution. When the upper-level problems are repeatedly
      solved, constraints change little, and the optimization problem solved repeatedly only involves slight
094
      changes in parameters. Generally, under slight changes in coefﬁcients, the optimal solution to one
095
      problem often serves as a near-optimal solution for another. Under this assumption, we can train a
096   neural network to learn the characteristics of the problem and rapidly acquire good heuristic solu-
097   tions. In fact, when the upper problem has no constraints, a machine learning method is proposed to
098   infer a solution to the upper-level problem (Guo et al., 2024).
099
      Regarding the use of machine learning models quickly to obtain better solutions to optimization
100
      problems, there are challenges in obtaining solutions that satisfy the constraints. An example of
101
      post-processing to satisfy simple constraints is using an autoregressive model that repeatedly selects
102   one node at a time when solving the TSP with neural networks, as widely studied (Kwon et al.,
103   2020). In this model, masking is applied to already visited nodes, allowing for selecting the next
104   node from unvisited nodes, thus obtaining a path that does not visit the same node twice. Similarly,
105   Hou et al. (2023) proposed a method that considers capacity constraints using a mask matrix, but this
106   is limited to vehicle routing problems. Another well-known example is using the softmax function
107   at the output of the ﬁnal layer in classiﬁcation problems to ensure that all elements are non-negative
      and sum to one. The LinSatNet was proposed (Wang et al., 2023), which improves the differentiable

      optimal transport, Sinkhorn algorithm (Cuturi, 2013). It extended the types of constraints it can
109   handle to linear constraints composed of non-negative coefﬁcients. However, it cannot be applied to
110   constraints like ﬂow conservation involving non-negative coefﬁcients.

112
      We summarize these considerations; (i) bilevel strategy incorporating a neural-solver and problem-
      speciﬁc solver is a promising method to achieve both high solution quality and short computation
113
      time for target problems (see Figure 1), (ii) constraint-enforcing for the neural network output can be
114
      achieved by the differentiable optimal transport for non-negative linear constraints. Based on them,
115   we propose a framework that integrates machine learning, optimal transport, and a problem-speciﬁc
116   solver to obtain high-quality solutions quickly for large-scale combinatorial optimization problems.
117   Our framework adopts a self-supervised learning method for upper-level problems, whereas special-
118   ized methods can be utilized for lower-level problems. For the upper-level problem, we train a neural
119   network to learn problem characteristics with satisfying the constraints by utilizing the differentiable
120   optimal transport module as the output layer. This makes it possible to construct a solution while
121   ensuring feasibility, including the lower-level problem, by satisfying multiple linear constraints com-
122   posed of non-negative coefﬁcients. Moreover, since the proposed framework separates the neural
123
      solver that learns problem characteristics and the optimal transport module that projects the solution
      onto the constraints, it is a method that can adapt to adding new constraints during inference. After
124
      constructing a sufﬁciently trained model, we can acquire a high-quality solution quickly compared
125
      to existing methods such as heuristics and exact methods.
126
127   To verify our proposed framework’s effectiveness, case studies were conducted with the min-max
128   capacitated vehicle routing problem (min-max CVRP). It contains the TSP for which the problem-
129
      speciﬁc solver has been developed after obtaining the assignment locations for vehicles. Compared
      to LKH-3* , our framework obtained high-quality solutions by comparing quickly. This tendency is
130
      much more obvious as the problem size increases.

      2.2     C ONSTRAINT- ENCODING FOR NEURAL NETWORKS
163
164   In general, there is no guarantee that the output of a neural network satisﬁes speciﬁc constraints.
165   Some recent research has been done on the differentiable optimal transport that can be inserted into
166   the intermediate or last layer. One approach to impose constraints on intermediate neural network
167   outputs is the differentiable optimal transport method known as the Sinkhorn algorithm (Cuturi,
168   2013). Wang et al. (2023) proposed the LinSatNet to handle the more general problem with the
169   multiple linear constraints with non-negative coefﬁcients. This paper applies LinSatNet to ensure
170
      the solution satisﬁes linear constraints in the upper-level problem, detailed in Sections 3 and 4.

212   3     P ROPOSED F RAMEWORK
213
214   In this section, we propose a framework for obtaining a high-quality solution to combinatorial opti-
215   mization problems quickly through a bilevel approach. When we solve similar problems repeatedly,
      high-quality solutions share a similar structure (e.g., assigning vehicles to locations in a vehicle


                                                                     4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      routing problem) while the local structure is different (e.g., visiting the order of locations in a ve-
217   hicle routing problem). Based on the assumption, we treat the problem as bilevel optimization,
218   where the problem-speciﬁc solver can effectively solve the lower-level problem to deal with the
219   local characteristics per problem. In fact, the combinatorial optimization problem often contains
220   the subproblem for which the problem-speciﬁc solvers have been developed, such as Concorde for
221   TSP, LKH-3 for vehicle routing problems, and CP-SAT solver † for scheduling problems. Note that
222   a general mathematical optimization solver, such as Gurobi Optimizer, can be used as an alterna-
223   tive to a problem-speciﬁc solver when it can efﬁciently solve the lower-level problem. Given that
224   similar problems are repeatedly solved, we can use a dataset of these solutions to train a neural net-
225
      work for the upper-level problem. In our framework, we utilize self-supervised learning to train the
      neural network, allowing the training without high-quality solutions for the dataset. There are four
226
      following components and the workﬂow is shown in Figure 2.

      To solve the combinatorial optimization problem through bilevel approach, it is necessary to ob-
248
      tain both the solution to the upper-level problem and the corresponding solution to the lower-level
249
      problem. The framework incorporates a cost estimation network and an optimal transport module to
250   obtain the solution to the upper-level problem while satisfying the constraints. The optimal transport
251   module ensures that the output satisﬁes linear constraints in the upper-level problem. To evaluate
252   the solution of the upper-level problem, the lower-level problem must be solved to obtain the ob-
253   jective value of the original problem. The output of the optimal transport module does not ensure
254   the integrality of discrete variables; we can acquire the feasible solutions to the upper-level problem
255   by executing the postprocessing like LinSatNet (Wang et al., 2023). For example, we can use sam-
256   pling from the feature matrix as the postprocessing. After determining each lower-level problem
257   by sampling the output of the optimal transport module, a problem-speciﬁc solver is executed to
258
      obtain the solution of the lower-level problem. Due to the necessity of sampling during this process,
      backpropagation cannot be performed directly for updating the parameter of the cost estimation net-
259
      work. To address this problem, a surrogate network is prepared to approximate the output of the
260
      problem-speciﬁc solver, enabling the learning of the cost estimation network.
261
262   The training process is executed as follows: a) The cost estimation network performs a forward
263   pass, followed by calculating the optimal transport module to obtain the solution to the upper-level
264
      problem. b-1) Sampling is performed from the output of step a), and the problem-speciﬁc solver
      is executed for each lower-level problem. b-2) Alternatively, a forward pass is performed through
265
      the surrogate network using the output of step a) and problem information. c) Loss functions are
266
      computed to backpropagate through the surrogate and cost estimation networks. During inference,
267   only step b-1) is performed after step a).
268

491   nodes cannot be assigned to the ﬁrst truck plotted with blue color in Figure 8. Figure 8 shows

      additional constraint to prohibit the node from assigning the ﬁrst truck, it can naturally be reassigned
      to another truck plotted in green. It is one example of how the cost estimation network and the
495
      optimal transport module are robust when adding the constraint.
