009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            With the growing complexity and capability of large language models (LLMs), a
012            need to understand model reasoning has emerged, often motivated by an underly-
013            ing goal of controlling and aligning models. While numerous interpretability and
014            steering methods have been proposed as solutions, they are typically designed ei-
015            ther for understanding or for control, seldom addressing both, with the connection
016
               between interpretation and control more broadly remaining tenuous. Addition-
               ally, the lack of standardized applications, motivations, and evaluation metrics
017
               makes it difficult to assess these methods’ practical utility and efficacy. To ad-
018
               dress the aforementioned issues, we propose intervention as a fundamental goal
019            of interpretability and introduce success criteria to evaluate how well methods are
020            able to control model behavior through interventions. We unify and extend four
021            popular interpretability methods—sparse autoencoders, logit lens, tuned lens, and
022            probing—into an abstract encoder-decoder framework. This framework maps in-
023            termediate latent representations to human-interpretable feature spaces, enabling
024            interventions on these interpretable features, which can then be mapped back to
025            latent representations to control model outputs. We introduce two new evalua-
026            tion metrics: intervention success rate and the coherence-intervention tradeoff,
027
               designed to measure the accuracy of explanations and their utility in controlling
               model behavior. Our findings reveal that (1) although current methods allow for
028
               intervention, they are inconsistent across various models and features, (2) lens-
029
               based methods outperform others in achieving simple, concrete interventions, and
030            (3) interventions often compromise model performance and coherence, underper-
031            forming simpler alternatives, such as prompting, for steering model behavior and
032            highlighting a critical shortcoming of current interpretability approaches in real-
033            world applications requiring control. Code is made available for replicability.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   As large language models (LLMs) have become more capable and complex, there has emerged a
038   need to better understand and control these models to ensure their outputs are safe and human-
039
      aligned. Many interpretability methods aim to address this problem by analyzing model represen-
      tations, attempting to understand their underlying computational and reasoning processes in order
040
      to ultimately control model behaviour. While many of these methods, and interpretability as a field
041
      more broadly, claim control and intervention as abstract goals and present compelling qualitative
042   results demonstrating that intervention is possible (for example, consider Anthropic’s Golden Gate
043   Claude Anthropic; Templeton et al. (2024)), the link between interpretation and intervention is often
044   tenuous in practice, as many methods are not explicitly tailored for both. Furthermore, even fewer
045   are thoroughly and systematically evaluated for the ability to control model outputs beyond simple
046   qualitative examples. We believe the reason for this is threefold. First, interpretability methods
047   produce explanations in disparate feature spaces, such as the token vocabulary, probe predictions,
048   or learned auto-interpreted features, hindering comparisons across methods. Second, there exists a
049   “predict/control discrepancy” Wattenberg & Viégas (2024), where the features identified by inter-
050
      pretability methods for predicting behavior are not the same as those used for steering it. Third,
      there do not exist standard systematic benchmarks to measure intervention success.
051
052   In this work, we view intervention as a fundamental goal of interpretability, and propose to mea-
053   sure the correctness of interpretability methods by their ability to successfully edit model behaviour.
      In particular, we focus on sparse autoencoders, Logit Lens, Tuned Lens, and linear probing, and

121   2   R ELATED W ORK
122
123   Mechanistic Interpretability. Existing work in mechanistic interpretability broadly falls into two
124   categories: activation patching and interpreting hidden representations. Activation patching utilizes
125   carefully constructed counterfactual representations to study which neurons or activations play key
126
      roles in model computation, ideally localizing specific information to individual layers, token posi-
      tions, and paths in the model Geiger et al. (2021); Vig et al. (2020). However, recent work points
127
      to key limitations of patching, particularly with respect to real-world utility in downstream applica-
128
      tions such as model editing Hase et al. (2024); Zhang & Nanda (2023). As such, we focus primarily
129   on methods for inspecting hidden representations, of which probes are the most commonly used
130   Alain (2016); Belinkov & Glass (2019); Belinkov (2022). Other methods such as nostalgebraist;
131   Dar et al. (2023) project intermediate representation into the token vocabulary space, with Belrose
132   et al. (2023); Din et al. (2023); Geva et al. (2022) building and improving upon these early-decoding
133   strategies. Ghandeharioun et al. (2024a) unifies most of these methods into an abstracted framework
134   for inspecting model computation. More recently, sparse autoencoders and dictionary learning have
135   been explored as a solution to the uninterpretability of model neurons, particularly due to issues with
136   polysemanticity and superposition Elhage et al.; Bricken et al. (2023); Cunningham et al. (2023);
137
      Bhalla et al. (2024); Gao et al. (2024); Rajamanoharan et al. (2024a); Templeton et al. (2024).
138   Evaluation. Due to the recency of the field, standard evaluation metrics across interpretability
139   methods have not yet been established, and similar to the broader interpretability field, evaluation is
140   frequently ad-hoc and primarily qualitative in nature. With regards to quantitative metrics, in Gao
141   et al. (2024); Templeton et al. (2024); Makelov et al. (2024), sparse autoencoders are evaluated for
142   reconstruction error, recovery of supervised or known features, activation precision, and the effects
143
      of ablation; however, none of these metrics measure the human interpretability or correctness of
      explanations. Sparse autoencoder evaluation has also sometimes included ratings by humans to
144
      determine the interpretability of the learned features for labeling, as done in Rajamanoharan et al.
145
      (2024b), but this is completely unrelated to evaluations of correctness or utility.
146
147   Causal Intervention. Previous literature on probing frequently evaluates learned probes and fea-
148   tures through intervention to ensure causality and correctness, as done in Li et al. (2022); Chen
149
      et al. (2024); Hernandez et al. (2023b;a). The interventions performed for measuring causality are
      similar to those used to perform model “steering” Rimsky et al. (2023); Panickssery et al. (2024);
150
      Ghandeharioun et al. (2024b) and should ideally produce the same effect but with the added claim of
151
      interpretability. Beyond probing, only Belrose et al. (2023); Chan et al. (2022); Olah et al. (2020);
152   Templeton et al. (2024) consider causal intervention as a tool for evaluation. However, Belrose et al.
153   (2023); Olah et al. (2020); Chan et al. (2022) mainly focus on mean ablation for intervention, with
154   no exploration of the coherence of the counterfactual outputs or their utility in application. Tem-
155   pleton et al. (2024) on the other hand only provides a qualitative evaluation of intervention via their
156   ‘Golden Gate Claude’ [Anthropic] and does not compare to other interpretability methods.

167   Latent vectors to interpretable features. The central aspect of most interpretability work is the
168   ability to translate model computation into human-interpretable features, whether the computation
169   be latent directions, neurons, components, reasoning processes, etc. Many works aiming to explain
170   LLMs focus particularly on hidden representations, where the mapping between high-dimensional
171   dense embeddings and human-interpretable features is modeled through a (mostly) linear dictionary

190   Interpretable features to counterfactual latent vectors. While producing explanations is
191   straightforward for each of these methods, intervening on model representations using the informa-
192   tion provided by explanations is not as simple. Doing so requires defining a reverse mapping from
193   the explanations to the latent representations of the model, which is only explicitly done by sparse
194   autoencoders. We defined the inverse mappings for the rest of the functions as follows. To map
195   Logit Lens’s explanations back into the model’s latent space, we would ideally apply the inverse
196   of the unembedding matrix to z; however, in practice this is often ill-conditioned due to the dimen-
197   sionality of D. As such, we instead use the low-rank pseudoinverse of the unembedding matrix and
198
      right-multiply it to the explanation logits. Similarly, for Tuned Lens, we model the decoding pro-
      cess through the pseudo-inverse of the Tuned Lens projection applied to the unembedding matrix.
199
      Notably, both of these methods only require a simple linear transformation to go back-and-forth
200
      between latent vectors and explanations. For probing, D−1 can be defined such that x̂′ = x + θ,
201   as proposed by Chen et al. (2024) which adds the linear probe’s weights to the latent representation,
202   bypassing the need for optimization. Sparse autoencoders have a well-defined backwards map-
203   ping through the SAE decoder, which is frequently linear in practice and often the transpose of the
204   encoder weights. Thus, we are now able to decode z into x̂ for all methods.
205
206   Intervening on interpretable features. Given the above framework, intervention is performed by
207   directly altering the feature activation zi corresponding to the desired feature i to be edited. While
208   the edited activation zi′ can naively be set to some constant value α, the same constant may have
209   drastically varying effects for different tokens and different prompts. As such, to take into account
210   the context of z, for Logit Lens, Tuned Lens, and SAEs we set zi′ = α ∗ max(z). This ensures
211
      that the feature i is the most dominant feature in the latent vector for α > 1. Decoding z ′ yields
      the altered latent representation x̂′ = g(z ′ ), which accounts for both the error of the explanation
212
      method as well as the intervention performed. For probing and steering vectors, x̂′ = x + α ∗ v,

      3.2   C OMPARATIVE E VALUATION ACROSS M ETHODS AND M ODELS
231
232   Given the overall lack of standardized evaluation of mechanistic interpretability methods, we intend
233   for this work to serve as a starting point for systematic evaluation by evaluating methods in simple,
234   easy-to-measure contexts. In particular, we think of our evaluations as measuring a kind of upper
235   bound for these methods: in the easiest of settings, how well do existing methods work?
236
237   Explanation Correctness We first propose metrics to evaluate the correctness of explanations
238   and interventions. More specifically, to test whether a single feature of an explanation zi is correct,
239
      we intervene on that feature to produce zi′ and decode z ′ to x̂′ . We can then hypothesize about the
      corresponding change in the model’s output when generating from x̂′ beforehand, as it should match
240
      the intervention made to produce z ′ . For example, if feature i encodes the concept “references to
241
      Paris,” increasing the value of zi should result in increases to references of Paris in the model’s
242   output. From this, we propose a metric of Intervention Success Rate, which measures if increasing
243   the activation of feature i by increasing zi results in the appropriate increase of the feature i in
244   the model’s output. To evaluate a continuous relaxation of this, we can also similarly measure the
245   probability assigned to tokens relating to feature i. As such, even if the model’s output does not
246   directly reflect interventions made to zi′ due to sampling, we can measure if increasing the activation
247   of i results in any change to the model’s output at all. We refer to this metric as Intervened Token
248   Probability. Importantly, both of these metrics can be thought of as measuring the causal fidelity of
249   the features highlighted by explanations.
250   Usefulness of Intervention Methods While intervention is a useful method for evaluating the
251   correctness of explanations, it is also a desideratum of its own and a frequent motivation for many
252   explanation methods. For example, methods are often developed for the purpose of de-biasing model
253   outputs or increasing model safety, either by localizing bad behavior or identifying it at inference
254   time, thus allowing for targeted edits to be made. However, a lack of this direct connection be-
255
      tween interpretation and model intervention has led to illusory results in prior literature, similar to
      predict/control discrepancies Hase et al. (2024); Wattenberg & Viégas (2024). By directly and ex-
256
      plicitly measuring how effective interpretability methods are at allowing for targeted intervention or
257
      steering, we can avoid such failure cases. Importantly, intervention is only useful if the language
258   model retains its overall performance and the text output by the model after the intervention still sat-
259   isfies the purpose of the query as well as the intervention. In particular, we are interested in whether
260   interpretability methods can be used to steer model outputs towards feature i without damaging the
261   model’s purpose as an LLM. We do so by measuring the Coherence of the model’s outputs after the
262   intervention has been applied. This can be done by any oracle, such as a human or another capable
263   LLM. Similarly, we can also measure the Perplexity of the intervened outputs with respect to a
264   strong language model. In practice, we use Llama3.1-8b for both of these metrics, as it is reasonable
265   sized, high-performing, and open source, allowing for the measurement of perplexity. To measure
266
      coherence, we use the prompt “Score the following dialogue response on a scale from 1 to 10 for
      just grammar and comprehension, ignoring incomplete sentences. \n\n Context: {Prompt}\n
267
      Dialogue Response: {Output}\n\n Score =”.

300   Intervention Topics. We pick 10 intervention topics that all relate to references to specific words
301   or phrases: {‘beauty’, ‘chess’, ‘coffee’, ‘dogs’, ‘football’, ‘New York’, ‘pink’, ‘San Francisco’,
302   ‘snow’, ‘yoga’}. These simple, low-level features are ideal for evaluation through intervention for
303
      four key reasons: first, measuring the presence of a word or phrase is much easier than measuring a
      high-level abstract concept such as sycophancy, second, these features were available across many
304
      layers for the Gemma2-2b and GPT2-sm sparse autoencoders, third, the features necessarily exist
305
      in the Logit Lens unembedding dictionary, and finally, datasets that are labelled for the presence
306   of these features are very straightforward to collect for generating steering vectors and probes. As
307   such, we can easily compare intervention on these features across methods and also easily measure
308   intervention success by checking if the given word/phrase exists in the model’s output.
309
      Steering vectors and probing. We implement steering vectors as described in Rimsky et al. (2023)
310
      with a few simple modifications. Where in Rimsky et al. (2023), the difference between contrastive
311
      pairs is taken only at the last token, we find that averaging across the token dimension and taking
312   the difference between those averages yields much better results. This is due to the fact that in
313   Rimsky et al. (2023), the only difference between representations occurred in the token position of
314   the answer letter, or the last token; however, in our case the information related to the intervention
315   feature could be present at any token. Contrastive data pairs were generated by prompting ChatGPT
316   for 200 pairs sentences or phrases about the desired intervention topic and about other topics within
317   the same genre. For example, we used the prompts “Can you please generate 100 short sentences
318   or phrases with the color ‘pink’ in them?” to create positive samples and “Can you also generate
319   100 phrases or sentences with other colors (not pink) in them?” to create negative ones. We also use
320
      these contrastive pairs to train the linear probes, using the implementation from Chen et al. (2024).
      All probes reached train and test accuracies of 100% across all models and intervention topics.

361   causal effects of intervening on specific features of each explanation. For a given feature or inter-
362   vention topic i, we see if increasing the activation of that feature results in an increase of the feature
363
      in the model’s output in the top row of Figure 2. In order to compare across methods, which all
      have different explanation feature spaces and scales, we measure the success of interventions as a
364
      function of the norm of the distance between the edited latent representation x̂′ = g(Edit(f (x)))
365
      and the original latent representation x: ||x̂′ −x||/||x|| as done in Section 4.2. Results for intervened
366   token probability are in Appendix A.4.
367
368   We find that across all three models, Logit lens and Tuned lens have the highest intervention success
369
      rate, regardless of the normalized edit distance. While increasing intervention strength and editing
      the latent representations more for steering vectors and probes results in greater intervention success
370
      for GPT2-sm and LLama2-7b, the same is not true for Gemma2-2b, for which intervening with
371
      steering vectors and probes seems to have minimal effect. Furthermore, we find that intervening with
372   sparse autoencoders is moderately successful, but due to the large reconstruction error for GPT2-sm,
373   even with intervention, the edit distance is much greater than that needed for Logit lens and Tuned
374   lens. In general, we believe the lower performance of Sparse Autoencoders is due to heavy noise in
375   the labels of features. For example, a feature labelled ‘references to coffee’, is sometimes actually a
376   feature that encodes for references to ‘beans’ and ‘coffee beans’, and thus only sometimes increases
377   mentions of ‘coffee’. Probes and steering vectors also have suboptimal performance, often due to
      learning of spurious correlations in the training data rather than the true intervention feature.

      4.5      I NTERVENTION S IMILARITY B ETWEEN M ETHODS
470
471
      We finally evaluate the empirical similarity between each of these methods. By converting each
      intervention into one in the latent space of the model, we can measure the cosine similarity between
472
      edit directions for each method. More specifically, for each method and intervention topic, we
473
      compute the expectation of x̂′ − x over all samples. This difference vector can be thought of as
474   the direction of the intervention topic in the model’s latent space. We compute the cosine similarity
475   between these vectors for each intervention topic and display the average across topics in Figure 6.
476
477
      We find that Logit Lens and Tuned Lens are highly similar, as expected. Similarly, steering vectors
      and probe weights tend to lie in similar directions, likely due to the same underlying data used
478
      to train both. Most interestingly, we find that sparse autoencoders tend to intervene in somewhat
479
      similar directions to steering vectors and probes. Even more surprisingly, they have near orthogonal
480   directions to Logit Lens and Tuned Lens, even when interventions succeed for all methods. This
481   is likely due to the high-dimensionality of model latent spaces, allowing for many vectors to be
482   near-orthogonal. We speculate that sparse autoencoders may be more similar to probes and steering
483   vectors because the three methods may have a bias toward representing past information and tokens,
484   due to their training and labelling algorithms. Logit Lens and Tuned lens, on the other hand, are
485   designed to reveal information about the next token specifically, given that they are early-decoding
      strategies and thus may contain more information about model outputs rather than inputs.

      4.6                        H IGH -L EVEL C OMPARISON OF M ETHODS
500
501
      Given the differences between methods, including how their features are learnt, what explanations
502   they produce, and the causal fidelity and utility of explanations for steering, we present the following
503   characterization of the strengths and weaknesses of each method.
504
505
      Logit Lens is easy to use, requires no training, and maps features directly to vocabulary tokens,
      making it highly interpretable, and we find that it generally has high causal fidelity as well. How-
506
      ever, its predefined, static features are limited and rudimentary, preventing its utility for steering
507
      in many real-world or safety-critical applications where abstract features such as truthfulness and
508   bias are more relevant.Tuned Lens shares these traits but requires an additional learned affine trans-
509   formation, although many are already open-sourced. Sparse autoencoders learn a very large dic-
510   tionary, allowing for features to be both low-level and high-level or abstract, potentially including
511   safety-critical features, and ideally “covering” all the true underlying features learned by the model.
512   Furthermore, explanations are sparse, increasing their human interpretability. However, the learned
513   features are frequently not human-interpretable, leading to labeling errors and lower causal fidelity
514   in practice. Furthermore, they require significant training and labeling data and compute, and there
515   is no guarantee that a desired feature will exist in the SAE’s dictionary, also preventing their utility
516
      in safety-critical domains. Probing allows for feature specification, given that a labeled dataset is
      available, but is prone to learning spurious correlations, leading to low causal fidelity and minimal
517
      steering utility. Steering vectors are very similar to probes in practice, but they require no opti-
518
      mization and are slightly less sensitive to correlations in the training data. As such, we believe that
519   lens-based methods are most useful for providing high-fidelity explanations but are likely not effec-
520   tive solutions for steering in real applications, for which steering vectors and prompting are more
521   promising but require careful oversight and refinement to ensure efficacy. SAEs offer a balance
522   between interpretation- and control-based methods but also suffer from a union of their problems.
523

525   5                   C ONCLUSION
526
527   As language models are integrated into everyday aspects of life, whether benign or life-changing,
528   being able to understand them and ensure they behave as expected is of utmost importance. While
529   interpretability methods show great promise, the correctness of their interpretations is less clear. In
530   this work, we emphasize that intervention is a fundamental goal of interpretability, both to eval-
531
      uate the correctness of interpretability methods as well as their utility in real-world applications.
      By unifying and extending four popular interpretability methods — sparse autoencoders, logit lens,
532
      tuned lens, and probing — into an abstracted encoder-decoder framework, we enable structured
533
      interventions on human-interpretable features, mapping them back to model latent representations
534   to directly control outputs. Through the introduction of two standardized metrics, the intervention
535   success rate, and the coherence-intervention tradeoff, we provide a rigorous basis for evaluating the
536   causal fidelity and utility of interpretability methods. Our findings reveal significant inconsistencies
537   in intervention capability across different methods and models, with simpler lens-based approaches
538   generally outperforming the more compute-intensive sparse autoencoders for simple, concrete in-
539   terventions. Furthermore, non-interpretability-based approaches, such as prompting, perform best
      overall, demonstrating a significant gap in current interpretability approaches.

793   A.4                        A DDITIONAL M ETRICS : I NTERVENED T OKEN P ROBABILITY
794
      Please see Section 3.2 for more details. We measure the probability assigned to tokens relating to
795
      feature i when intervening on feature i. As such, even if a model’s output does not directly reflect
796   interventions made to zi′ due to sampling, we can measure if increasing the activation of feature i
797   results in any change to the model’s output at all. We refer to this metric as Intervened Token
798   Probability. Importantly, both of these metrics can be thought of as measuring the causality of the
799   features highlighted by explanations.
800
      Results for Intervened Token Probability are shown in 8, where we see that intervention with all
801
      methods across all models increases the probability of intervention-related tokens, even if the inter-
802   vention does not succeed. We also note that there is a significant difference between the order of
803   magnitude of the intervened token probability for sparse autoencoders, around 10e−5 and the rest of
804   the methods, which range from 10e−4 to 0.5.

808   As described in Section 3.2, we evaluate the perplexity of the intervened generated text to measure
809   the utility of interpretability methods for targeted intervention in 9. We measure this perplexity with

      We find that the results for perplexity are generally unintuitive and do not align with the results for

812   distribution with respect to normal text, and in particular when the text is highly repetitive. For
813   example, if the same token is repeated 20 times, we (and other language models) might assume that

967
968   We additionally consider two more intervention features: responding in French and responding
969   by yelling in upper-case text. We use the same prompt dataset as in the experiments of the main
970   paper (detailed in Section 3.2), which consists of only English prompts with standard capitalization
971   schemes (proper nouns and the first letter of each sentence). We consider these additional features
      due to their availability in the Gemma2-2b SAE used in Section 4. While more safety-critical or


                                                             18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       high-stakes features would be interesting to consider, we were unable to find such features that
973    successfully steered the model during the rebuttal period. We further note that both French and
974    yelling are complex features because they cannot be captured by a small set of words or tokens.
975
976    Implementation details. Datasets used to train steering vectors and probes were generated in a
977    similar way to those for the low-level features. Specifically, we generate 200 simple sentences
978    in English with the help of an LLM and manually inspect them to ensure correctness. We then
979    translate those sentences into French or simply capitalize the sentences to get contrastive datasets
980    for the steering vectors and probes. Both probes achieved perfect test accuracy. We intervened on the
981    “French text” and “uppercase text” features of the same Layer 20 Gemma2-2b residual stream SAE
982    with 16k features used in Section 4. Note that these two features are advertised as preset features on
983
       the Steering page of Gemma Scope/Neuronpedia as “French mode” and “YELLING mode” Lin &
       Bloom (2023).
984
985    Given that these features are not naturally present in the dictionary of Logit Lens, we “learned” the
986    set of Logit Lens features to intervene on by picking the 200 most common upper-case tokens from
987    the steering vector/probe datasets for the ‘yelling’ concept and by picking the 200 tokens with the
988    highest cosine similarity to the learned ‘French’ steering vector. Note that while the token features
989
       for ‘yelling’ were still highly interpretable, the features similar to the ‘French’ steering vector were
       not, and often did not intuitively relate to the French language. Thus, we would not recommend
990
       using Logit Lens to intervene on the ‘French’ concept in practice, but simply test this method for
991
       completeness. Tuned Lens is not considered because there is no publicly available trained Tuned
992    Lens for Gemma2 as of yer.
993
994    Results for intervention on these two features are shown in Figure 14, where we see that Steering
995
       vectors and Probes generally perform the best. As expected, Logit Lens does not perform well due
       to the intervention features not being present in Logit Lens’s dictionary. We further find that SAEs
996
       perform very poorly, despite these features being chosen directly from the SAE dictionary. Overall,
997
       the results on these two complex features align with the results and conclusion drawn in Section 4.6,
998    where we note that lens-based methods are most useful for providing high-fidelity explanations but
999    are not effective solutions for steering in real applications with complex features, for which steering
1000   vectors and prompting are more promising but require careful oversight and refinement to ensure
1001   efficacy.
