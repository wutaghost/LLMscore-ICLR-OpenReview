011                                           Abstract
012
013           In Deep Neural Networks (DNN) and Spiking Neural Networks (SNN), the
014           information of a neuron is computed based on the sum of the amplitudes
015           (weights) of the electrical potentials received in input from other neurons. We
016           propose here a new class of neural networks, namely Delay Neural Networks
017
              (DeNN), where the information of a neuron is computed based on the sum of
              its input synaptic delays and on the spike times of the electrical potentials
018
              received from other neurons. This way, DeNN are designed to explicitly
019
              use exact continuous temporal information of spikes in both forward and
020           backward passes, without approximation. (Deep) DeNN are applied here to
021           images and event-based (audio and visual) data sets. Good performances are
022           obtained, especially for datasets where temporal information is important,
023           with much less parameters and less energy than other models.

026   1   Introduction
027
028   Deep Neural Networks (DNN) have gained more and more in complexity, power and perfor-
029   mance to solve highly complex tasks (Rawat and Wang, 2017). These networks abstract the
030   functioning of biological neurons. Electrical information is integrated, computed and passed
031   from the preceding layer to the next. As these networks can use a lot of energy (Strubell
032   et al., 2019), and aren’t biologically plausible, a new class of neural networks has emerged,
033
      Spiking Neural Networks (SNN), which tend to reproduce the spiking behavior of biological
      neurons.
034
035   In an SNN, each neuron is represented by an electrical membrane potential, which evolves
036   according to incoming spikes. Once the membrane potential reaches a threshold, the neuron
037   emits a spike and its membrane potential is usually reset. This all-or-nothing behavior reduces
038   the number of computations because neurons possibly do not fire and thus do not activate
039
      downstream neurons. The thresholding of membrane potentials induces a discontinuity in
      the model, which impedes mathematical analysis and the computation of the gradient in the
040
      backpropagation algorithm and thus complicates the learning.
041
042   Time dimension can be used in different manners in SNN, depending on the model. In (Kim
043   and Panda, 2021), using rate-coded SNN, it is possible to show that short inter-spike intervals
044   carry information. In (Thorpe et al., 2001), in order to better account for the precise firing
045
      times of the neurons, a new coding method has emerged, namely Time-To-First-Spike (TTFS)
      coding. Thorpe et al. (2001) argue that the biological brain could make use of precise timing
046
      of the spikes, or of the order of arrival of each incoming spike. In TTFS coding, neurons are
047
      usually forced to spike only once, thus constraining the network to arrange spikes in time.
048   Recently, the learning of synaptic delays has came along the will of using the time dimension
049   (Zhang and Li, 2020; Hazan et al., 2022; Hammouamri et al., 2023; Pengfei et al., 2023).
050   Indeed, it can be shown that, for some type of data, delays but not weights are necessary to
051   solve temporal logic problems (Habashy et al., 2024). Moreover, temporal plasticity can be
052   used to treat temporal information (Wang and Crus, 2024). For more theoretical analyses
053   on the interest of synaptic delays in SNN, one can refer to (Maass, 1997; Maass and Schmitt,
      1997; Thorpe et al., 2001).


                                                    1
054
      We present here Delay Neural Networks (DeNN), which can be considered to be a temporal
055   version of DNN, or an abstract SNN. As classic DNN and SNN treat electrical amplitudes
056   (or models of), DeNN treat timing information (or a model of). In DeNN, learning happens
057   through synaptic delays, and an important connection between any two neurons is represented
058   by a short delay. The firing time of a neuron is computed by directly considering the impact of
059   each presynaptic spike onto the firing time of the postsynaptic neuron instead of thresholding
060   a membrane potential. This allows side stepping the challenge of non-differentiability faced
061   by SNN, and using exact temporal information from input synapses in the forward and
062   backward passes. We show results on event-based datasets for classification tasks (video and
063
      audio).
064   The contributions of this work are as follows: (i) We introduce a new general framework
065   for working in the temporal dimension with deep neural networks, which can be
066   adapted to deep neural network architectures ; (ii) In this framework, different temporal
067   kernels can be experimented to learn synaptic delays instead of synaptic weights, with
068   exact evaluation of the gradient in the temporal dimension ; (iii) On benchmark datasets
069
      (images, videos and audio), DeNN obtains the same, or better performances
      than other models, but with much less parameters and less energy cost, with
070
      respect to other models.

073   2     Related works
074
075
      2.1   Temporal coding in SNNs
076
077
      Learning temporal codes has a long history in computational neuroscience. We can cite,
078
      among many other approaches, Tempotron (Gütig and Sompolinsky, 2006) and ReSuMe
079   (Ponulak and Kasiński, 2009). Tempotron presents an interesting weight synaptic learning
080   rule for a single neuron to learn for detecting locally synchronous spikes, known as Spike-
081   Timing-Dependent Plasticity (STDP). ReSUME focuses on making (reservoir networks
082   of) neurons learn to reproduce template signals (instructions) encoded in precisely timed
083   sequences of spikes. Instead, the purpose of DeNN is to follow a modernized version of
084   Time-To-First-Spike (TTFS) temporal code implementing delays, and adapting the idea
085   to event-based datasets. DeNN neurons learn synaptic and firing delays for firing faster or
086   slower according to the classification error of a temporal signal in a deep learning network
087
      architecture. While DeNN is currently more oriented to global deep learning mechanisms,
      computational neuroscience mechanisms will help in the future to improve and to better
088
      understand the global and local learning mechanisms of DeNNs.
089
090   Mostafa (2017) was one of the first to introduce temporal coding in modern deep spiking
091   neural networks. To do so, they derived an analytic formula to directly compute the firing
092   times of non-leaky Integrate and Fire (IF) neurons which produce a single spike, i.e., with
093
      infinite refractory period. Zhou et al. (2021) extended this algorithm to more challenging
      benchmark datasets in computer vision. Their work was extended to the Spike Response
094
      Model (SRM) neurons by (Comsa et al., 2020), where an equation is solved in the complex
095
      field to find solutions for the good spike timing of the neurons. With the same technique,
096   Göltz et al. (2021) extended the work in (Mostafa, 2017) to several cases of Leaky Integrate
097   and Fire neurons (LIF). All these models have in common a methodology to solve an equation
098   to find the timing of the first spike from a neuron (whether it is an IF neuron, a LIF, an
099   SRM). With the same methodology, Park et al. (2020) presented equations somewhat simpler
100   than in (Göltz et al., 2021; Comsa et al., 2020).
101
      Another solution is to adapt the backpropagation algorithms to event-based data (Zhu et al.,
102   2022). Recent works (Wunderlich and Pehle, 2021; Lee et al., 2023) show that an exact
103   computation of the gradient for these models is possible, and that rate coding and temporal
104   coding can be related through loss functions (Zhu et al., 2023). Most of these approaches
105   are either restricted to specific models (Mostafa, 2017; Comsa et al., 2020; Göltz et al., 2021)
106   or to approximate gradients (Zhu et al., 2022). Among approximate gradient techniques,
107   the surrogate gradient allows SNNs to exhibit promising results (Esser et al., 2016; Bellec
      et al., 2018; Neftci et al., 2019; Tavanaei et al., 2019). However, this technique remains an


                                                     2
108
      approximation of the thresholding function used in the forward pass by continuous functions
109   in the backward pass.
110
111   Another possibility that has been developed is to map classic neurons to spiking neurons, as
112
      in (Rueckauer and Liu, 2018; Park and Yoon, 2021; Kheradpisheh and Masquelier, 2020).
      These works allow simpler backpropagation algorithms, since they can leverage better the
113
      properties of analog networks. They are however still restricted to one model of neuron and
114
      can hardly be generalized to other models.
115
116
      2.2   Synaptic delays
117
118   To achieve temporal coding, Single spike SNNs (Mostafa, 2017; Zhang et al., 2019; Zhou
119   et al., 2021; Comsa et al., 2020; Göltz et al., 2021; Park et al., 2020) intuitively seem also
120   to be a good framework to learn delays between neurons. Modelling the delays into neural
121   networks can be tracked back at least to 1989 with Time-Delay Neural Networks (TDNN)
122   (Waibel et al., 1989). In this work, connections have several synaptic terminals, each with
123   its own fixed delay and variable weight thus leading to an exploding number of parameters.
124   Bohte et al. (2002) derived an approach called Spike Prop, with the same architecture for
125
      synaptic terminals and the same drawbacks in terms of memory and computations.
126   Simpler delay-based models have then been developed. In (Schrauwen and Van Campenhout,
127   2004; Wu et al., 2006; Shrestha and Orchard, 2018; Hammouamri et al., 2023; Pengfei et al.,
128   2023) one trainable delay was implemented for each synaptic connection, alongside with
129   synaptic weights. In (Taherkhani et al., 2015a;b), single output neurons are trained to fire a
130   spike train at desired times. Also, Taherkhani et al. (2015b) only allow delays to be increased,
131
      which seems biologically implausible. Zhang and Li (2020) presented an interesting joint
      synaptic delay-weight plasticity algorithm, and confronted it to a real-world dataset for
132
      speech recognition. More recent works tend to get rid to the single spike constraint, as it
133
      is not reliable for event based datasets (Yu et al., 2023; Hammouamri et al., 2023; Pengfei
134   et al., 2023; Grappolini and Subramoney, 2023; Deckers et al., 2024; Wang, 2024).
135
136   In most of these delay-based works, every synapse has two parameters: a weight and a
137
      delay, effectively doubling the memory cost with respect to analog networks. To the best of
      our knowledge, (Hazan et al., 2022) is the only work presenting a weightless spiking neural
138
      network, where learning happens only through synaptic delays. The authors used a learning
139
      rule derived from Spike-Timing-Dependent Plasticity (STDP), and confronted their network
140   to a classic image classification task. However, their network exhibits low accuracy and
141   memory performances.

147
      In our network, instead of weights wij , we use positive delays which represent a simple time
148   delay between two presynaptic and postsynaptic neurons. In DNN, if a connection between
149   two neurons is important (inversely not important), its weight |wij | is high (inversely low),
150   while in DeNN this corresponds to a small (inversely high) delay |dij |. In order to ensure
151   that the delays dij stay positive during the back-propagation steps, we effectively declare
152   signed-delays dsij ∈ R, and then compute the delay as a simple Gaussian of the signed-delays:

181   temporal abstraction of the behavior of biological neurons. Note that any continuous positive
182
      decreasing function can be used for kernel κ, but the exponential one happens to work well
      in practice.

322
323   To show that our network is able to tackle temporal data, we confronted it to the event-
      based version of MNIST dataset (N-MNIST (Orchard et al., 2015)), to the DVS Gesture

      dataset (Amir et al., 2017) which represents hand movements, and to the Google Speech

326   to allow for larger comparisons with other models, we also confronted our model to the

328   details and parameter values can be found in Section D. Results and comparison to the

332   tasks, DeNN improves the performances in audio tasks, where temporality is important.

      These performances are achieved with architectures much lighter than for other models.
      Furthermore, when possible, we computed the average number of computations per sample
334
      in different models, by multiplying the reported firing rate to the number of synapses (see

      Appendix F for more details). We show that it is possible to achieve good performances with

      4.3   Choice of kernel κ
351   The class of model presented in this work is general enough to work with any kernel, as
352   long as it is a decreasing positive function. Although the kernel that works best is the
353   exponential κ(x) = exp(−x), we found satisfying result with the inverse κ(x) = x−1 . To
354   avoid double negatives (when both x and dsij are negative), we shifted the Gaussian curve
355   (after standardization) by three units to the right and clipped the (standardized) spike times
356   to a minimum of 0.001. We reported an accuracy of 96.62% for the MNIST dataset, and
357   97.76% for the neuromorphic version. Note that in order to compare only the change in
358   kernel, we used the same architectures and hyperparameters as for κ(x) = exp(−x). It should
359   be possible to obtain even better results by adapting the architectures and parameters of the
360   model. Also, for resources reason, these simulations were performed only on small datasets.

460
461   Figure 2 shows that while SNN requires a discrete time simulation in real time, with a ∆t
462   step (see step (1)), DeNN can use a discrete event simulation without any ∆t step (see step
463
      (3)). As shown in step (2), DeNN on-line latency is theoretically lower than SNN on-line
      latency, because a DeNN does not have to wait for the last ∆t step to be consumed after last
464
      image at T ∆t step. For the online latency, a timestep on the Gesture dataset represents, on
465
      average, 0.6227 seconds of the sample, and 0.6099 seconds for the N-MNIST dataset. On-line
466   property relies on the fact that input events are received in real time (e.g., by an event-based
467   camera of a robot/car). However, as shown in step (3), if on-line learning in real time is not
468   required, DeNN allows as fast as possible discrete event simulation. All the images are fed
469   to the network in a sequence leading to smaller off-line latency LDeN     N
                                                                            OF F = 0.066 seconds on
470   a NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3080 Laptop GPU; for the Gesture dataset. This is particularly
471   interesting for example to train cars for automated driving by off line simulation. This
472   allows greatly reducing training time and energy consumption. We show on Figure 4a the
473   comparison with other SNN models for the Gesture dataset. We note that models reporting
474
      better accuracy are also models with higher latencies. Note that this as-fast-as-possible
      discrete event simulations do not depend on slow or fast regime. Although the slow regime
475
      requires receiving all the spikes from the previous layer to compute the output spike times of
476
      the layer, it does not require waiting for any real time discrete time step ∆t. Even in a slow
477   regime, layers can be simulated as fast as possible. The only difference of the fast regime is
478   that it reduces the number of computations and execution time, while slightly decreasing
479   performances.

483   Figure 6 shows the activity of synapses connecting the neurons in the intermediate layer of
484   the classifier to the eighth output neuron, for the MNIST dataset, for images of the digit 8.
485   In a DeNN (left), very few synapses are of extreme importance to make the output neuron
      spike much before the others, while others synapses are silent. In an ANN (right), there

      must be a balance between inhibition and excitation. The main difference between the
487   ANN and the DeNN stems in the fact that, in our network, paths that are irrelevant do not
488   show any activity, while in an ANN they are inhibited. This shows how DeNN is able to
489   drastically decrease the number of computations with respect to other models. All couples
490   (neuron, digit) have been computed and are available on figures I.1 (for MNIST) and I.2 (for
491   CIFAR-10).

      This behaviour might be due to the peculiar derivative of tj with respect to the parameter
493   dsij . As shown in Figure C.1, the derivative is close or equal to zero for almost every input zi .
494
      Even when the input is sufficiently strong (reminding that negative z is quicker input than
495   others), the derivative plunges toward zero at dsij = 0, which means that synapses will have
496   trouble changing signs. Some will get asymptotically close to zero (negative or positive),

      5   Conclusion
515
516   A new class of neural networks was presented. These networks are able to treat temporal
517   information, arguing they are, alongside (Hazan et al., 2022), the first network to be fully
518   temporally coded, by taking into account the spike order’s importance, as presented in
519
      (Thorpe et al., 2001; Liu et al., 2023). Indeed, the model in (Hazan et al., 2022) is temporally
      coded, but lacks performances. Finally, DeNN are able to achieve satisfying results on tasks
520
      with much less parameters than other models, and with less computations. On audio task,
521
      where temporality is important, DeNN demonstrates better results than state-of-the-art
522   models. We also show that DeNN use less energy than other models. On datasets where
523   temporal dimension is crucial, DeNN use less energy with better performance results than
524   the adaptive LIF model used in (Bittar and Garner, 2022; 2024; Deckers et al., 2024).
525
      Since the equations for the DeNN are general, it is possible to adapt any (continuous)
526
      architecture to the DeNN with few efforts. We were indeed able to very easily apply our
527
      model to convolutional architectures, and try out different kernels κ fairly easily. Conversion
528   of existing architectures to spiking one would be an interesting development for DeNN,
529   as in (Man et al., 2023; 2024). A limitation can be discussed for long-term memory hard
530   window definition. The memory cost increases with ν for sequences longer than a few seconds.
531   However we have to note that thanks to our preprocessing algorithm, one timestep in our
532   model is equal to ∆m2rN timesteps in real time. With ν, the memory goes to ν∆m2rN , as
533   explained in Section 3.5. It should be possible to encode sequences longer than a few seconds
534   with few adaptations to the preprocessing algorithm (for example, with a bigger r). Also, we
535   show in Table F.2 that even with ν = 25, the DeNN uses less energy than the adLIF used
536
      in (Deckers et al., 2024), which uses coupled dynamic equations, one for the short memory
      (membrane potential), and one for the long-term memory (recovery variable).
537
538   This work should open new perspectives to the field of temporal coded networks, as the need
539   to better take into account the temporality of information, especially with the rising use of
      event-based cameras and brain-inspired learning.

      E    Long-term memory
943
      Long-term memory is added to the network thanks to this equation:
944
945                             δ h = zj [s] − zj [s − h], h = s − ν, ..., s
946                                                s
                                                 X                                                    (20)
947                          zj [s] ← zj [s] +         αjh sign(δ h )[exp(−|δ h |) − 1]
948                                            h=s−ν
949
950   The mechanism is represented on Figure E.1. The main mechanism is that, if neuron j is
951   faster than its neighbors (δ h > 0 or zj [s − h] < zj [s]), then it gets a small boost (zj [s] is
952   decreased). Conversely, if it was slower than its neighbors, then it gets a small punishment
953
      (zj [s] is increased). Note that αjh is a learnable parameter in the range [−1, 1], so the network
      can decide to reverse boost and punishment.

958   It is described here how to obtain the average number of computations per object derived
959   for Table 1.
960   For sparse convolutions used in (Cordone et al., 2021), the average number of active sites na
961   (Graham et al., 2018) per layer per timestep can be computed with the ratio of the number
962   of spikes in a layer to the number of timesteps. However, we are unable to know the spatial
963   distribution of activated sites. For a lower bound estimation, it can be assumed that each of
964   them was visited exactly once, thus underestimating the number of computations. The lower
965   bound for average number of computations per layer and timestep is then na Coℓ Coℓ−1 , with
966
      C ℓ the number of channels of the convolutional layer, and the total number of computations
      per one sample of the dataset is obtained by summing over all layers and all timesteps, and
967
      is approximately of 1.2 · 106 computations. An upper bound √ can be obtained by assuming
968
      that active sites are densely grouped on a square of side na , which gives, when summing
969
      over all layers and timesteps, approximately 10 · 106 computations.
