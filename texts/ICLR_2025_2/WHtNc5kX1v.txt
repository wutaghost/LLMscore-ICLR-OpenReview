011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The pursuit of boosting classification performance in Machine Learning has pri-
014            marily focused on refining model architectures and hyperparameters through prob-
015            abilistic loss optimization. However, such an approach often neglects the pro-
016
               found, untapped potential embedded in internal structural information, which can
               significantly elevate the training process. In this work, we introduce Latent Boost,
017
               a novel approach that incorporates the very definition of classification via latent
018
               representation distance metrics to enhance the conventional dataset-oriented clas-
019            sification training. Thus during training, the model is not only optimized for clas-
020            sification metrics of the discrete data points but also adheres to the rule that the
021            collective representation zones of each class should be sharply clustered. By lever-
022            aging the rich structural insights of high-dimensional latent representations, La-
023            tent Boost not only improves classification metrics like F1-Scores but also brings
024            additional benefits of improved interpretability with higher silhouette scores and
025            steady-fast convergence with fewer training epochs. Latent Boost brings these
026            performance and latent structural benefits with minimum additional cost and no
027
               data-specific requirements.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      Traditional data-driven classification training often employs a black-box approach that focuses
032   solely on optimizing classification scores for discrete datasets, neglecting important aspects such
033   as clusters within the continuous representation. In contrast, we explore an innovative approach
034   by explicitly integrating the classification task into the latent representation through distance metric
035   learning. In this work, we introduce a novel method that seamlessly integrates latent cluster distance
036   metrics into probabilistic training, fundamentally transforming the paradigm of structured latent
037   representations. While conventional probabilistic approaches typically center on individual data
038   samples, they often overlook the intricate relationships among data points, particularly within clus-
039   ters, where the interdependencies and structural nuances are essential for capturing the underlying
040
      information. In other words, while the end-to-end classification performance might be satisfactory,
      the results like the F1-Score are derived from discrete data points. Internally, the collective clusters
041
      of different classes could still be cluttered in the continuous latent representation, as the training
042
      processes only optimize for the probabilistic loss of the dataset. In contrast, our innovative approach
043   guarantees that semantically similar data points are thoroughly aligned in proximity, while dissimilar
044   points are distinctly separated, thereby enhancing the model’s capacity for nuanced differentiation.
045
046
      Although distance metric learning has established its efficacy in Machine Learning—particularly
      in clustering and pre-training tasks (Kulis et al., 2013; Kaya & Bilge, 2019)—its application in
047
      classification has been limited to simpler models like K-nearest Neighbors (Cover & Hart, 1967)
048
      and Support Vector Machines (Cortes, 1995). We assert that intermediate latent representations hold
049   critical structural information essential for class distributions, and enhancing their cluster separation
050   can significantly boost classification performance. To the best of our knowledge, Latent Boost is the
051   first method to integrate distance metrics into the classification loss function, empowering the model
052   to cultivate more meaningful and discriminative features without requiring explicit supervision.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
086
087
      Distance Metric Learning has emerged as a crucial area in Machine Learning, offering a wide range
088
      of techniques aimed at improving performance in tasks of primarily unsupervised clustering and
089
      retrieval of latent representation information in general (Kulis et al., 2013; Wang & Sun, 2015). Such
090   loss functions generally focus on minimizing intra-cluster variance and maximizing inter-cluster
091   distances by learning a latent representation where similar points are closer together, and dissimilar
092   points are further apart (Kaya & Bilge, 2019). These functions penalize the model until the latent
093   representation aligns with the desired distance metric priorities.
094
      The structure and information density in latent representations also improves interpretability. Dis-
095
      criminative Dimension Selection can be utilized to enhance the interpretability and clustering per-
096   formance, especially for K-means clustering by selectively retaining relevant features as proposed
097   by Lian et al. (2024). Similarly, works on adaptive feature selection have focused on developing
098   efficient strategies to reduce the burden of complex dimensionality, such as Zhou & He (2024), im-
099   proving clustering accuracy across several benchmarks. Oppose to utilizing the Euclidean distance
100   information, a boosting algorithm to effectively learn Mahalanobis distance metrics was proposed
101   by Chang (2012), demonstrating its effectiveness on popular datasets to capture intrinsic distance
102   relationships. Mahalanobis-based techniques have been widely adopted, with approaches such as
103   Large Margin Nearest Neighbor (LMNN) (Weinberger & Saul, 2009) maximizing the margin be-
104
      tween different classes, and Information-Theoretic Metric Learning (ITML) (Davis et al., 2007),
      which minimizes the relative entropy between distance distributions.
105
106   Pairwise and Contrastive loss functions have shown remarkable improvements in distance metric
107   learning, such as the Contrastive loss function introduced by Hadsell et al. (2006) for learning
      dimensionality-reducing embeddings. This principle has been adopted in domains like zero-shot


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      learning (Wang & Chen, 2017), cross-modal retrieval (Wang et al., 2017), and large-scale face recog-
109   nition (Liu et al., 2017). Contrastive learning has also been used in self-supervised settings, where
110   methods such as SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) leverage this loss to learn robust features without
111   labels. The promising approaches of triplet-based methods for distance metric learning have been
112   challenging to optimize due to the need for finding informative triplet anchor points (Do et al., 2019).
113   To address this, semi-hard triplet mining methods like Schroff et al. (2015) have been developed,
114   leading to more efficient training. Advanced sampling strategies have been proposed to improve
115   the performance of triplet-based learning systems Hermans et al. (2017). Additionally, using proxy
116   points to approximate original data points, as shown by Movshovitz-Attias et al. (2017), further im-
117
      proves convergence and stability in Triplet loss optimization. Magnet loss has gained attention in
      recent years as an effective method for distance metric learning, particularly in dealing with high-
118
      dimensional data where traditional losses like Triplet loss face challenges (Rippel et al., 2015). Yu
119
      et al. (2020) introduces Semantic Drift Compensation as part of Magnet loss as a method to address
120   catastrophic forgetting in class-incremental learning by estimating and compensating for the feature
121   drift of previous tasks, significantly improving performance in embedding networks without requir-
122   ing exemplars. By focusing on the distribution of latent representations within each class, Magnet
123   loss works to minimize the overlap between class clusters, thus providing better discrimination,
124   especially in face identification in noisy and large-scale datasets (Deng et al., 2020).
125
      Metric learning with constraints has led to significant advances, with pairwise constraints being
126   integrated into methods like Constrained Clustering via Metric Learning (Bilenko & Basu, 2004).
127   Similarly, Ding & Li (2007) extended this concept into semi-supervised clustering, demonstrating
128   the utility of leveraging partial label information. In sparse and high-dimensional data scenarios,
129   sparse subspace clustering (Liu et al., 2010) has been a successful approach.
130
      Methods like DeepCluster (Caron et al., 2018) and the cluster-based contrastive learning proposed
131
      by Caron et al. (2020) demonstrate how metric learning can generate meaningful representations for
132   downstream tasks such as image retrieval. The innovation in these approaches lies in integrating
133   clustering with deep learning techniques to build robust data representations. Hybrid models that
134   integrate different learning techniques have also gained attention. For example, Lee et al. (2018)
135   employed stacked attention networks for cross-modal tasks, combining textual and visual data within
136   joint latent spaces to enable multi-modal learning.
137
      Fairness in distance metric learning has also become a crucial area of focus, with works like Lahoti
138   et al. (2020) exploring how adversarial techniques can be used to ensure fairness in learned distance
139   metrics. These methods prevent demographic bias and ensure equitable performance across different
140   groups, a significant consideration for real-world applications.

      4.1     E XPERIMENT S ETUP
296
      We selected three different experiment setups of different complexity to explore the effects of the
297   previously introduced distance metric losses through the weighted sum equations. Starting with a
298   simple Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) trained on Fashion-MNIST (Xiao et al., 2017), we
299   trained a VGG-16 model (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014) on CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009),
300   followed by training a ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016) architecture on CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al.,
301   2009). The full description of each model architecture can be found in Appendix A.1. For our initial
302   investigation in the following section, we kept the distance metric losses hyperparameters constant
303   and initialized to the standard recommendations (see Appendix A.3 for details). For each experi-
304   ment, we extracted the latent representation after the convolutional part in the network architecture,
305
      meaning just before the classification section. For the CNN the latent representation has a dimension
      of 64, the VGG-16 model obtains 512 dimensions on that layer and the ResNet-50 generates a latent
306
      representation of 2048 dimensions. The choice to extract latent features from layers just before the
307
      classification head is based on their ability to capture high-level, task-specific representations that
308   balance discriminative power and compactness, as these layers contain semantically rich informa-
309   tion essential for classification tasks. Earlier layers typically emphasize low-level features and may
310   lack the necessary information density required for effective downstream processing, making deeper
311   layers more suitable.
312
      Each setup was implemented in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) with a learning rate scheduler that
313
      reduced the learning rate by factor 5 when a plateau was reached after 10 epochs. The Adam
314   optimizer was utilized for each experiment to efficiently work out the gradients (Kingma, 2014).
315   We applied early stopping metrics with 20 epochs patience based on the validation accuracy to
316   additionally compare the convergence speed next to the classification performance. Finally, we
317   trained each experiment setup five times to provide meaningful results. We assigned each trial a
318   different random seed, which we kept constant within the trials different runs across the variation of
319   lambda to balance the distance metric with the classic cross-entropy.

      5   C ONCLUSION
529
530   Overall, Latent Boost explicitly inserts the very definition of classification in the latent layer of
531
      an otherwise black-box model, which stimulates the model to produce better-separated clusters in
      the target latent layer while optimizing for the classification task. This consequentially leads to
532
      improved classification results, enhanced interpretability metrics, and more efficient training con-
533
      vergence. While the classification metrics uplift is moderate, the improvement is consistent across
534   all benchmarks with the least deviation. By leveraging both intra-class compactness and inter-class
535   separation in a dynamic manner, Latent Boost proves its ability to adapt latent representations for
536   more robust feature learning. One limitation of our approach requires a few epochs to take effect to
537   gradually manipulate the clusters, thus the benefits are diminished in cases with already subnormal
538   training epochs. Furthermore, Latent Boost relies on the distance to the cluster center, which as-
539   sumes a high-dimensional sphere in the latent representation. It remains to be investigated whether
      other cluster formation strategies can further enhance the performance of the method in question.

      A.1   M ODEL A RCHITECTURES
705
706   The model architecture trained on Fashion MNIST (Xiao et al., 2017) is a convolutional neural
707   network (CNN) adapted for grayscale images. The network starts with a convolutional layer that
708   takes single-channel (grayscale) 28x28 images and outputs a set of feature maps, followed by ReLU
709   activation and max-pooling. The model employs two convolutional layers, with each followed by
710   pooling and dropout of 25% to mitigate overfitting. The resulting feature maps are flattened and
711
      passed through fully connected layers. From the flattening layer, we extract the latent features. The
      final fully connected layer outputs the class probabilities for the 10 fashion categories.
712
713   The model architecture used for CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) is based on the VGG-16 net-
714   work (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014). VGG-16 is a deep convolutional network known for its
715   success in image classification tasks on complex color images like CIFAR-10. This architecture
716   consists of 16 layers, with 13 convolutional layers interspersed with ReLU activations and max
717
      pooling to downsample the feature maps, followed by three fully connected layers. The network
      extracts progressively richer features from the images, which are then classified into one of the 10
718
      classes.
719
720   The model trained on CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) is based on the ResNet-50 architecture
721   (He et al., 2016) and comprises several key layers to efficiently process the input images. The ar-
722   chitecture begins with a modified convolutional layer that accepts three input channels and employs
723
      a kernel size of 3 times 3, followed by batch normalization and ReLU activation. It includes four
      residual blocks (layer1 to layer4), each containing a series of convolutional layers, batch normaliza-
724
      tion, and skip connections to enhance gradient flow. The network concludes with an average pooling
725
      layer, which is flattened to extract the latent representations and a fully connected layer that outputs
726   the final class predictions, specifically tailored for the CIFAR-100 classification task.
