009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning models are central to many critical decision-making processes,
012            making it imperative to gain deeper insights into their behavior to improve perfor-
013            mance, transparency, interpretability, and fairness. A key challenge is understand-
014            ing how training data shapes model predictions on unseen test data. In this paper,
015            we introduce a novel metric, Relevancy, which quantiﬁes the impact of individual
016            training samples on inference predictions. Our proposed metric is calculated by
017            observing the learning dynamics of the model during training, and it is compu-
018
               tationally efﬁcient and applicable across a wide range of tasks. We demonstrate
               that it is between 80× and 100, 000× more efﬁcient than existing metrics for cap-
019
               turing the train-test relationship. Using relevancy, we enable the identiﬁcation of
020
               coresets — compact datasets that represent the essence of the training distribution.
021            Quantitative evaluations show that coresets selected using our metric outperform
022            state-of-the-art methods by up to 5.2% on CIFAR-100. Additionally, we qualita-
023            tively demonstrate how relevancy can be extended to assess various training data
024            properties, such as identifying mislabeled samples in widely used datasets like
025            ImageNet, CIFAR-100, and Fashion-MNIST. These examples illustrate just a few
026            of the many potential uses of relevancy, highlighting its versatility in promoting
027            more interpretable, efﬁcient, and fair deep learning systems across diverse tasks.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Deep learning (DL) has achieved signiﬁcant success in various domains, including classiﬁcation
033
      tasks (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), reinforcement learning (Shakya et al., 2023), diffusion-based image
      generation (Ho et al., 2020), and text generation (Radford et al., 2019). As tasks grow more complex,
034
      both the size and quality of training data play a crucial role in determining model performance. It
035
      is widely acknowledged that deep models often overﬁt, leading to the memorization of training
036   data (Zhang et al., 2017; Arpit et al., 2017). Rather than learning generalized representations, these
037   models may capture speciﬁc patterns or memorize individual examples from the training set, which
038   reduces their ability to generalize to unseen data (Brown et al., 2021). Additionally, mislabeled
039   or noisy samples in large-scale datasets further complicate the learning process by encouraging
040   memorization of irrelevant or incorrect patterns, thereby hindering generalization (Northcutt et al.,
041   2021).
042   To tackle these challenges, it is essential to address two key questions: “Which subsets of data con-
043   tribute most to effective generalization?” and “How can we identify samples that are memorized?”
044   Answering these questions requires a deeper understanding of the relationship between training data
045   and model predictions on unseen examples, focusing on balancing memorization and generalization.
046   Recent studies suggest that the learning dynamics of neural networks can provide valuable insights
047   into how models memorize data and generalize to new examples (Toneva et al., 2018; Mangalam &
048   Prabhu, 2019; Jiang et al., 2021; Garg et al., 2024). Building on these insights, we introduce a novel
049   metric, Relevancy, to quantify the impact of individual training samples on generalization.
050   Relevancy measures the inﬂuence of a training sample on a model’s prediction for any sample of
051   interest. This is achieved by tracking the evolution of the sample losses as the model’s weights
052   are updated during training. Speciﬁcally, we compute the correlation between the loss trajectories
053   of the two samples over time, using checkpoints saved during training to access these trajectories.
      When the sample of interest is an unseen data point (e.g., a test sample), this metric enables us to

098

      2009), CIFAR-100, and Fashion-MNIST (Xiao et al., 2017), making them powerful tools for data

132   2   R ELATED W ORK
133
134   Recent research has focused on understanding the inﬂuence and memorization of data points in deep
135
      learning (DL) models, particularly their implications for model training and generalization. Memo-
      rized data refers to samples that a model can only predict accurately when trained on those speciﬁc
136
      samples, while generalized data leverages knowledge from other samples. Various studies have
137
      explored memorization vs. generalization, proposing different deﬁnitions and applications, often
138   involving retraining multiple models on different subsets of data or computing expensive Hessian
139   calculations (Zhang et al., 2017; Arpit et al., 2017; Feldman, 2020; Toneva et al., 2018; Brown et al.,
140   2021). Some algorithms, such as those proposed by Garg & Roy (2023); Garg et al. (2024); Raviku-
141   mar et al. (2024a;b) leverage input loss curvature to alleviate the computational burden of capturing
142   memorization tendencies but still incur signiﬁcant computational overheads.
143
      Inﬂuence functions, initially introduced by Weisberg & Cook (1982) for regression-based tasks, have
144   been extended to deep learning to measure the inﬂuence of training samples on model predictions
145   for unseen data (Koh & Liang, 2017). Methods such as RandSelect (Wojnowicz et al., 2016)
146   and Arnoldi iterations (Schioppa et al., 2022) improve computational efﬁciency by approximating
147   inﬂuence scores. Our work is most closely related to TracIn (Pruthi et al., 2020), which estimates
148   inﬂuence through gradient steps, though it incurs a higher overhead than our proposed metric.
149
      Recent works on training data attributions (TDA), such as Datamodels (Ilyas et al., 2022) and TRAK
150   with its linear datamodeling score (LDS) (Park et al., 2023), evaluate the inﬂuence of training data
151   through counterfactual impacts on model outputs. While these approaches provide valuable insights,
152   they often rely on computationally intensive surrogate models or speciﬁc assumptions about model
153   linearity. In contrast, our work directly leverages the learning dynamics during training, offering a
154   scalable and assumption-free alternative for understanding train-test relationships.
155
      Our metric also has applications in dataset optimization and mislabel detection. Coreset generation,
156   which selects representative data subsets (Johnson & Guestrin, 2018; Killamsetty et al., 2021a; Paul
157   et al., 2021), addresses computational demands in tasks like Neural Architecture Search (Na et al.,
158   2021; Shim et al., 2021) and continual learning (Aljundi et al., 2019; Borsos et al., 2020). Prior meth-
159   ods include clustering-based Herding (Chen et al., 2010), gradient-based GraNd (Paul et al., 2021),
160   and bi-level optimization (Killamsetty et al., 2021b). SloCurves (Garg & Roy, 2023) highlighted
161   input curvature as a critical factor in identifying impactful samples; however, its computation can
      be prohibitively costly for large datasets due to repeated Hessian calculations. Mislabel detection is

                                                         3
162
      critical for data quality, and prior work has used pruning and detection tools to identify mislabeled
163   or duplicated samples (Northcutt et al., 2021; Barz & Denzler, 2020). While these methods struggle
164   to scale, our approach, with minimal computational overhead, efﬁciently handles mislabeled data in
165   large datasets. Our proposed metric, relevancy, offers a more computationally efﬁcient approach,
166   achieving state-of-the-art performance with enhanced explainability and minimal overhead.

390   4.3   C OMPUTATIONAL OVERHEAD OF THE P ROPOSED M ETRICS
391
392   The computational overhead of relevancy is minimal. The loss trajectories of training samples are
393   available as a by-product of training and can be obtained directly. To compute the relevancy for
394
      training samples on unseen samples of interest, we only need to obtain the loss trajectories for the
      unseen samples. Metrics such as infl (Feldman & Zhang, 2020), TracIn (Pruthi et al., 2020),
395
      mem (Feldman, 2020), and Curv (Garg et al., 2024) discussed in Sections 4.1 and 4.2 require ex-
396
      pensive gradient calculations or retraining multiple models. Table 1 compares the computational
397   costs of relevancy with these metrics, highlighting its efﬁciency. A detailed explanation of how the
398   computation of these metrics is calculated is provided in Appendix F.
399
      Notation and Setup: We denote the number of training samples as m (S ∼ Zm ) and the number
400
      of samples of interest as m  . The model is trained for T epochs, and for certain methods, there are
401
      hyperparameters (denoted as r) that further inﬂuence the computational cost. The computational
402
      complexity of each method is expressed in terms of ﬂoating point operations per second (FLOPS),
403   denoted as f , which represent the cost of a single forward pass for a model with p parameters. The
404   cost of a backward pass is approximately 2f FLOPs. For infl, TracIn, and Rel, we consider the
405   scenario of estimating the impact of every training sample on every test sample. For mem and Curv,
406   we consider the scenario of estimating the memorization of every training sample. RelMem is not
407   included in this table as it only adds an insigniﬁcant overhead (due to the calculation of correlation
408   and atypicality) as the loss values of training samples are readily available.
409   Example with Imagenet and ResNet-18: To further illustrate the computational efﬁciency of rele-
410   vancy , we provide an example using the ImageNet dataset (m = 1, 281, 167 training samples and
411   m  = 50, 000 test samples of interest) and a ResNet-18 model with p = 11, 689, 128 parameters
412   and f = 1, 818, 228, 160 FLOPS per forward pass. We considered the training recipe provided by
413   the popular Bearpaw library (Yang, 2017), where T = 90. For the Curv metric, we used r = 10 as

419   Coreset generation is crucial for reducing the size of training datasets while retaining the most infor-

423   points.

425   the validation set, denoted as RelAvg . This metric identiﬁes the samples that have the greatest impact

526   7   C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we introduced Relevancy, a novel and computationally efﬁcient metric for quantify-
529   ing the inﬂuence of individual training samples on model predictions, focusing on their impact on
530   unseen test data. By tracking learning dynamics during training, relevancy reduces overhead by
531
      up to 100, 000× compared to existing approaches. We demonstrated its effectiveness in generat-
      ing coresets, which improved classiﬁcation accuracy by up to 5.2% on CIFAR-100, outperforming
532
      state-of-the-art methods. Additionally, we showed that we can extend relevancy to capture the same
533
      information as existing popular and computationally expensive memorization and curvature met-
534   rics. We qualitatively showed that this extension of relevancy can efﬁciently detect mislabeled sam-
535   ples across datasets such as CIFAR-100, ImageNet, and Fashion-MNIST. These results highlight
536   the versatility and efﬁciency of our approach, making it applicable to various deep learning tasks
537   where understanding data inﬂuence is crucial. As deep learning models are increasingly used in
538   critical decision-making systems, metrics like relevancy are essential for ensuring models are both
539   high-performing and interpretable. Future work will focus on extending these metrics to different
      architectures and tasks, advancing their role in promoting more transparent and reliable AI systems.

      A     L IMITATIONS
703
704
705   While the relevancy metric provides a scalable and effective approach to quantifying the inﬂuence
706
      of training samples on test performance, it has certain limitations. First, the metric relies on the
      loss function’s behavior, and its effectiveness is diminished when using discontinuous or highly
707
      non-smooth losses, such as 0-1 loss, which do not provide meaningful gradients. Second, relevancy
708
      captures correlations in learning dynamics but does not explicitly infer causal relationships between
709   training and test samples, which may lead to misinterpretations in scenarios with complex indirect
710   interactions. Lastly, the metric assumes the availability of sufﬁcient training checkpoints to track loss
711   dynamics, which could impose storage constraints in resource-limited environments. Addressing
712   these challenges is an exciting avenue for future research to further enhance the robustness and
713   applicability of the metric.

      The core principle of relevancy lies in observing the dynamics of the loss of samples during training.
912   Hence it is important to utilize the loss values at each epoch for a more nuanced Rel metric. It
913   is however possible to downsample the number of loss values (by only considering some of the
914   epochs) to still get a meaningful relevancy score. Figure 14 illustrates the effect of downsampling,
915   by plotting the histograms of the Rel scores of all classes. As the downsampling factor increases,
916   the Rel values move towards 1.0. This is because there is not enough information to differentiate
917   the learning dynamics of various samples, and all samples will appear to be learned (loss values
      decrease) at the same time.
