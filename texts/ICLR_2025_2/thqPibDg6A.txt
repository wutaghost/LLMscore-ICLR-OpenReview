011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Language models (LMs) are pre-trained on large-scale corpora from diverse data
014            sources, encapsulating knowledge across various domains, with their feature spaces
015            often displaying clustering structures. The mixture of experts (MoE) approach is
016            commonly used to scale up model learning capabilities to handle such complexities;
017            however, the fine-grained learning dynamics at the expert level remain largely
018            unexplored. This work analyzes the spatial and temporal characteristics of these
019            clustering structures and examines their impact on the fine-grained trainability of
020
               individual experts. Our analysis builds on the singular spectrum of the feature and
               Jacobian spaces leading to two key observations. First, a few top singular vectors
021
               from the feature matrix are sufficient to capture the layer-wise feature cluster
022
               patterns. More interestingly, the maximum singular value of the Jacobian matrix
023            reveals conflicts between different feature clusters, and experts exhibit varying
024            levels of trainability, completing their learning asynchronously during training.
025            Inspired by these insights, we proposed Mixture of Cluster-guided, Trainability-
026            aware Experts (MO-CTE), with an efficient routing method to mitigate inter-cluster
027            conflicts to improve expert trainability and a simple yet effective criterion for early
028            stopping low-trainability experts, thus reducing total training costs. We evaluate
029            the proposed MO-CTE across extensive datasets and tasks. Experimental results
030            indicate that MO-CTE accelerates convergence by approximately 37% in test
031
               perplexity and 30% in downstream tasks, and improves performance by 3.68%
               over baselines when consuming similar computation resources.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037
038   It is a prevalent practice to pre-train language models (LMs) on massive-scale, real-world corpora
039
      collected from different sources with knowledge across diverse domains (Paeedeh et al., 2024; Wu
      et al., 2021; Man et al., 2023; Xi et al., 2024). Existing research (Aharoni & Goldberg, 2020)
040
      has already shown that such data can lead to the spontaneous emergence of clusters in the feature
041
      space. The mixture of experts (MoE) (Fedus et al., 2021) is, therefore, a widely adopted structure
042   that converts dense layers into sparse mixtures of experts to attain better performance in LMs.
043   However, the fine-grained, expert-level learning dynamics, or trainability more precisely, remain a
044   less-investigated research topic (Cai et al., 2024). In this work, we investigate the detailed training
045   behaviors of LMs in the feature and Jacobian space to improve the expert-level trainability related to
046   the spatial-temporal characteristics of the cluster structure.
047
      Without loss of generality, we start with pre-training a Transformer model (Radford et al., 2019)
048   on mixed datasets (McAuley et al., 2015; Komatsuzaki, 2019; Bird et al., 2008) and portray its
049   learning dynamics of the feature space in Figure 1. Observable clustering structures emerge a few
050   steps after the initial step, a phenomenon more pronounced in deep layers with better distinguishable
051   clustering patterns. We discover that the spatial patterns of clusters can be effectively captured in
052   the low-dimensional space fabricated by a few top singular vectors of the feature matrices, allowing
053   performing feature clustering with negligible computational overheads in this space, as is illustrated
      in Figure 2(a).

118   2     A NALYSIS
119
120   In this section, we first formalize the definition of the feature space and feature singular spectrum, to
121   highlight that a few singular vectors of the feature space that interpret the top-k variance are sufficient
122   to capture the different cluster structures. Next, we analyze the Jacobian space and Jacobian singular
123   spectrum, to demonstrate that the largest singular value of Jacobian space, σmax , can be regarded as
124   an indicator of expert trainability and cluster structure stabilization, while mixed cluster will lead to
125   lower overall trainability.

129

130

251   3    M IXTURE OF C LUSTER - ORIENTED , T RAINABILITY- AWARE E XPERTS
252
253   Building on our findings, we propose the Mixture of Cluster-oriented, Trainability-aware Experts
254   (MO-CTE). This approach aims to achieve efficient MoE training with improved expert-level train-
255   ability by leveraging the singular spectra of the feature and Jacobian spaces. The MO-CTE strategy
256   consists of two core components: low-dimensional singularity-based routing and safeguarding
257   expert trainability. With efficient routing for the Mixture of Experts (MoE), changes in trainability
258   determine whether specific experts should be trained or frozen. The real-time behavior of the cluster
259   structure and the Jacobian Singular Spectrum informs these dynamic adjustments. This section details
260
      these policies, and the proposed algorithm is outlined in Algorithm 1.
261   Low-dimensional Singularity-based Routing The discussion in Section 2 suggests that the feature
262   space exhibits cluster structures, and features from different clusters may cause conflicts in the
263   Jacobian when learned by the same set of parameters, leading to poorer overall trainability. To
264   address this, we introduce the MoE structure to mitigate inter-cluster feature conflicts and enhance
265   intra-cluster consistency in the features learned by each parameter group, thereby improving the
266
      learning efficiency of language models.
267   Furthermore, with observations in Section 2, we distinguish such cluster structures in a low-
268   dimensional space fabricated by the leading singular vectors in the feature space. With this low-
269   dimensional space, we can achieve efficient feature clustering using very low-dimensional representa-
      tion to guide the routing for MoE.

271   et al., 1996) as a clustering algorithm since it does not require an explicit number of clusters as its
272   parameter. Other clustering algorithms like k-Means (MacQueen, 1967) have also been effective in
273   our experiments. The feature matrix is transformed into a low-dimensional space with its singular
274   vectors that interpret 80% variances, and a clustering algorithm is conducted to discover the cluster
275   structure. Suppose the clustering algorithm indicates there exist k clusters. In that case, we introduce
276   the MoE structure with k experts and assign data points with the same cluster to the same expert. To
277   make the learning efficient and optimization landscape smoother, we adopt LoRA-like (Hu et al.,
278   2021) experts with a smaller intermediate dimension and initiate the second matrix with zeros.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
325
326
327   Datasets We collected pre-training data from several sources, following general practices in Large
328
      Language Model (LLM) training (Zhao et al., 2023). These sources include legal cases (Cas,
      2024), medical papers (Cohan et al., 2018), computer science papers (Bird et al., 2008), Amazon
329
      reviews (McAuley et al., 2015) and Reddit forums (Komatsuzaki, 2019), simulating a realistic training
330
      scenario for LLMs. To evaluate model performance, we also utilize various downstream tasks. A
331   more detailed description of the datasets can be found in Appendix A.
332
333   Evaluation Metrics Model performance is evaluated based on two key aspects: training efficiency
334
      and downstream task performance. For training efficiency, we compute the percentage of floating-
      point operations (FLOPs) consumed by each model, which serves as a lower bound for execution
335
      time (Justus et al., 2018). FLOPs are estimated using the approach described in (Brown et al., 2020)
336
      and are reported as a percentage, with the baseline method set to 100%. For downstream performance,
337   we evaluate the models on a range of tasks, using accuracy as the primary metric.
338
339   Calculation of Feature and Jacobian Singular Spectrum Although defined on the whole dataset,
340
      computing the feature and Jacobian singular spectrum on all data can be impractical. So, we estimate
           2
      the σmax  by randomly sampling a small batch of data and back-propagating on the sum of the logits
341
      rather than individual outputs. The batch size is chosen to balance computational feasibility on
342                                                       2
      different hardware, ensuring that the computed σmax      provides a reliable approximation without
343                                                2
      introducing significant overhead. Also, the σmax isn’t calculated at every single training step. To find
344   a balance between efficiency and evaluation precision, we calculate it at around every 0.8% data.
345
346
      Implementation Details We conduct experiments with GPT-based models at two scales: 140M and
      750M parameters (Radford et al., 2019). The 140M model consists of 12 decoder layers with 768
347
      embedding dimensions, 3072 feed-forward network (FFN) dimensions, and 12 attention heads. The
348
      750M model contains 24 decoder layers and 1536 embedding dimensions. Both our method and
349   the Switch Transformer (Fedus et al., 2021) have the same number of experts. The training was
350   performed on NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3090 GPUs for the 140M models and NVIDIA GeForce RTX
351   A100 GPUs for the 750M models, with batch sizes determined by model size and available memory.
352   We used the AdamW optimizer with peak learning rates of 4 × 10−4 for the smaller model and
353   1.5 × 10−4 for the larger model. In both models, the intermediate dimension of added LoRA-like
354   experts was set to 1/4 of the original expert. For MO-CTE hyperparameters, we chose α = 0.20 and
355   β = 0.05. More detailed implementation details can be found in Appendix A.

450   5    R ELATED W ORKS
451
452   Mixture of Experts The Mixture of Experts (MoE) is a key model in machine learning Jacobs et al.
453   (1991); Jordan & Jacobs (1994), where different experts handle distinct regions of the input space.
454   To enhance capacity for complex data, Eigen et al. (2013) extended MoE to deep neural networks,
455   proposing a deep MoE model with multiple layers of routers and experts. Shazeer et al. (2017)
456   improved MoE by making the gating function output sparse, significantly improving training stability
457   and reducing computational cost. Since then, various MoE layers Shazeer et al. (2017); Dauphin
458   et al. (2017); Vaswani et al. (2017) have achieved success in language tasks. MoE has also been
459
      used to improve the training efficiency of Large Language Models (LLMs), with routing strategies
      ranging from token-based selection of experts Lepikhin et al. (2021); Fedus et al. (2022); Zuo et al.
460
      (2022); Chi et al. (2022); Dai et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2023), expert-based token selection Zhou
461
      et al. (2022), to global expert assignment Lewis et al. (2021); Clark et al. (2022). Inspired by MoE,
462   we propose it can effectively address long-tail knowledge learning.
463
464   Optimization Analysis using Jacobian Spectrum. Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) (Jacot et al.,
465
      2018a), which calculates the kernel matrix of Jacobian, is known as a powerful tool to analyze
      convergence and generalization properties (Arora et al., 2019). Many papers (Xiao et al., 2020)
466
      study the spectrum of the NTK and find in particular the largest eigenvalue dominates the training
467
      regime (Jacot et al., 2018a; Bowman & Montufar, 2022). Multirate training (Vlaar & Leimkuhler,
468   2022) is a promising technique that partitions neural network parameters into different groups, where
469   the "slow" group is updated less frequently. The mNTK (Shi et al., 2024) further examines fine-
470   grained, module-specific training dynamics and introduces a theoretically motivated method for
471   dynamically adjusting parameter updates based on modular NTK analysis. Additionally, techniques
472   aimed at reducing computational costs, such as network pruning (Lee et al., 2018; Rachwan et al.,
473   2022) and dynamic sparse training (Liu et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2022), often involve disabling
474   parameters during both forward and backward passes.
475
476   6    C ONCLUSION
477
478   In this paper, we studied the layer-wise singular spectrum in both feature space and Jacobian space of
479   language models to achieve a Mixture of Experts (MoE) with improved expert-level trainability. We
480   observed that a few singular vectors in the feature space can capture distinct spatial cluster structures,
481   and the temporal variation pattern of the largest singular value in the Jacobian is synchronized with
482   changes in cluster structure and expert trainability. Based on these observations, we proposed Mixture
483   of Cluster-oriented, Trainability-aware Experts (MO-CTE), which incorporates low-dimensional
484   cluster routing to enhance efficiency and expert-level early stopping to conserve computational
485
      resources. Experimental results demonstrate that our approach not only improves the efficiency of
      MoE learning but also surpasses the performance of baseline methods.
