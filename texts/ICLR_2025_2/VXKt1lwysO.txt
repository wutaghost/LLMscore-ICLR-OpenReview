                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Existing symmetry discovery methods predominantly focus on global transforma-
010
               tions across the entire system or space, but they fail to consider the symmetries
011            in local neighborhoods. This may result in the reported symmetry group being
012            a misrepresentation of the true symmetry. In this paper, we formalize the notion
013            of local symmetry as atlas equivariance. Our proposed pipeline, automatic local
014            symmetry discovery (ATLAS), recovers the local symmetries of a function by
015            training local predictor networks and then learning a Lie group basis to which the
016            predictors are equivariant. We demonstrate ATLAS is capable of discovering local
017            symmetry groups with multiple connected components in top-quark tagging and
018            partial differential equation experiments. The discovered local symmetry is shown
019
               to be a useful inductive bias that improves the performance of downstream tasks in
               climate segmentation and vision tasks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024
      Equivariant neural networks (Bronstein et al., 2021), a family of models that exploit symmetry as an
025   inductive bias for neural network architectures, have received increasing attention in deep learning due
026   to their training efficiency and improved generalization (Krizhevsky et al., 2017; Worrall & Welling,
027   2019; Zaheer et al., 2017). The key idea behind these models is that many real-world situations
028   exhibit inherent symmetries—transformations such as rotation, translation, and scaling, which leave
029   the essential properties of a system unchanged. This has enabled a wide range of applications, leading
030   to empirical success (Winkels & Cohen, 2018; Brown & Lunter, 2018; Cohen & Welling, 2016;
031   Cohen et al., 2018). Despite its achievements, equivariant networks require knowledge of the system’s
032   symmetries beforehand. To adhere to this successful design principle even when the symmetry group
033
      is unknown a priori, many works have developed auxiliary neural networks to automatically identify
      symmetries (Benton et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2021; Dehmamy et al., 2021; Moskalev et al., 2022;
034
      Yang et al., 2023; Gabel et al., 2023).
035
036   The aforementioned equivariant models and symmetry
037   discovery pipelines focus on global symmetries, where
038   a transformation applies across the entire space. How-
039
      ever, arbitrary manifolds generally do not have global
      symmetries to begin with (Gerken et al., 2023), prevent-
040
      ing the use of globally equivariant networks. This begs
041
      the need to consider local symmetries—transformations
042   on small neighborhoods—which are much more general-
043   ized (Figure 1). Indeed, some recent work considers local Figure 1: Global vs local transforma-
044   symmetry, such as Cohen et al. (2019), which develops tions. Global transformations (left) alter
045   gauge equivariant CNNs to take advantage of the gauge the space in a uniform manner, whereas
046   symmetries of arbitrary manifolds. Construction of such local transformations (right) only affect
047   networks once again requires knowledge of the symmetry a particular neighborhood.
048   beforehand. Existing discovery methods may not be appli-
049   cable as they fail to consider local symmetries. Hence, the
050
      need to develop a local symmetry discovery pipeline is clear: it would open up symmetry discovery to
      broader domains, such as partial differential equations or computer vision tasks on arbitrary meshes
051
      and manifolds.
052
053   In this work, we define local symmetry around the notion of an atlas. An atlas is a collection of
      local regions, or charts, that cover a manifold. In short, the principle of atlas equivariance states

054
      that when restricting a function to a particular chart, the localized function must be equivariant. We
055   develop a method based on deep learning that can discover the atlas equivariances of dataset in the
056   form of a Lie group. To do so, we first model the task function localized to the various charts using
057   neural networks. Then, we create a Lie group basis and optimize it until the localized networks are
058   equivariant with respect to the group. After discovery, we use the resulting symmetry as an inductive
059   bias to create equivariant networks. Specifically, we experiment with top-quark tagging, synthetic
060   partial differential equations, MNIST classification, and climate segmentation to test the validity of
061   our discovery method and measure performance gains in downstream models.
062   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
063
064
             • We formalize the notion of local symmetry through the definition of atlas equivariance.
065
066          • We develop a pipeline, automatic local symmetry discovery (ATLAS), to recover local
067            symmetries from a dataset. ATLAS can learn both continuous and discrete symmetries.
068
069          • ATLAS can discover local symmetries in cases where existing global symmetry search
070            methods are not applicable.
071
072          • We show that incorporating the symmetries discovered by ATLAS in a gauge equivariant
073            CNN (Cohen et al., 2019) can lead to better performance and parameter efficiency.

076   2    R ELATED W ORK
077
078
079   Equivariant Neural Networks. Equivariant neural networks use known symmetry as an inductive
080   bias when fitting a model. Group equivariant CNN extends the translational equivariance of a CNN to
081
      rotations and reflection using group theory (Cohen & Welling, 2016). Other works focus on designing
      networks that are equivariant to a wider range of transformations; particularly, E(2) transformations
082
      on Euclidean plane (Weiler & Cesa, 2019), rotations on the sphere (Cohen et al., 2018), Lorentz
083
      group transformations (Gong et al., 2022), and E(n) transformations in higher-dimensional spaces
084   (Satorras et al., 2021). These works focus on global transformations and require prior knowledge of
085   the symmetry. In contrast, our pipeline focuses on discovering local symmetry.
086
087
      Gauge equivariant neural networks extend the ideas of globally equivariant neural networks by
      enforcing local symmetries instead of global ones. Gauge equivariance has been applied in various
088
      contexts, including surface meshes (De Haan et al., 2020), lattice structures (Favoni et al., 2022),
089
      and general manifolds (Cohen et al., 2019). Once again, these networks require user intervention
090   to determine the gauge group. On the other hand, we show that our definition of local symmetry is
091   connected to gauge equivariance and that using our discovered symmetry in a gauge equivariant CNN
092   can lead to better performance and parameter efficiency.
093
      Automatic Symmetry Discovery. Many works perform automatic symmetry discovery to identify
094
      the unknown symmetry within a dataset. Attempts have been made to discover general continuous
095
      symmetries with Lie theory, such as LieGG to discover symmetry from the polarization matrix
096   (Moskalev et al., 2022), L-conv (Dehmamy et al., 2021) to find group equivariant functions, and
097   Forestano et al. (2023) who discover closed Lie subalgebras from a dataset. LieGAN (Yang et al.,
098   2023) uses a generator-discriminator pattern to discover global symmetries in the form of both
099   continuous Lie groups and discrete subgroups. Gabel et al. (2023) aim to find the symmetry group as
100   well as quantify the exact distribution of transformations present in a dataset. Although we also rely
101   on Lie theory, we search for local symmetries instead of global ones.
102   Symmetry discovery works also consider slightly varied problem settings. Some authors seek to find
103   a subset of possible symmetries (Benton et al., 2020; Romero & Lohit, 2022). Others consider the
104   case where the group acts on the latent space instead of the feature space (Yang et al., 2024; Gabel
105   et al., 2023; Keurti et al., 2023; Koyama et al., 2023). Still, these papers mainly focus on global
106   transformations. More relevant to local symmetry discovery is the work by Decelle et al. (2019),
107   which attempts to see if two datapoints are related by a particular local transformation. This is distinct
      from ATLAS, where we characterize the local symmetry group in an interpretable manner.

      3     BACKGROUND
109
110
      We provide background information on Lie groups, equivariance, feature fields, and atlases. We
111   assume some knowledge of group theory and otherwise refer readers to Artin (2011); Weiler et al.
112   (2021) as useful starting points.
113
114
      Lie Groups. A Lie group is a group that is also a differentiable manifold. Some examples include
      SO(2), O(3), and SL(3). The Lie algebra of a Lie group, denoted g, is the tangent space at the
115
      identity element. Being a vector space, Lie algebra is often simpler to work with than the group.
116
117   For matrix Lie groups, the matrix exponential exp(A) provides a way to map elements of the Lie
118   algebra to elements of the group’s identity component G0 , i.e. the connected component containing
119   the identity element. The various connected components are cosets of G0 and will also be plainly
120
      referred to as cosets in our work. In many cases, we can factor an arbitrary element of the group as
      a product, g = Ci · exp(A), for some coset representative Ci ∈ G and Lie algebra element A ∈ g.
121
      Thus, to understand a Lie group, it is often enough to enumerate all the cosets in the component
122
      group G/G0 , and identify a basis for its Lie algebra. For further information, see Kirillov (2008).
123
124   Equivariance. A function f is said to be G-equivariant for some group G if the following holds:
125                                      (∀g ∈ G) f (g · x) = g · f (x)                                    (1)
126
127
      Here, g · x and g · f (x) denote (possibly different) group actions.
128   Feature Fields. A feature field identifies a feature vector for each point in a manifold M. Specifically,
129   a feature field is given as a map F : M → Rd , where d is the dimension of the feature field.
130
      Charts and Atlases. It is not possible to give a consistent choice of coordinates across manifolds
131   with non-trivial topology. We define local coordinates in terms of local charts. A chart is a pair (U, φ)
132   where U is an open subset of M and φ is a homeomorphism from U to an open subset of Euclidean
133   space. An atlas is a set of charts that collectively cover a manifold M.

      Despite the achievements of existing symmetry discovery methods, the challenge of local symmetry
138
      discovery remains largely untouched. To address this problem, we first formulate atlas equivariance
139
      as a definition of local symmetry. Then, we detail our methodology for discovering local symmetry
140   in the form of a Lie algebra basis and component group. Finally, we highlight theoretical connections
141   to existing work as well as implementation notes.

203   4.2   ATLAS E QUIVARIANCE D ISCOVERY
204
205   In our problem setup, we assume we have a dataset {(Xi , Yi )}ni=1 ⊂ X × Y that models some
206
      unknown function Φ : X → Y between feature fields on the same manifold. We further assume that
      a suitable atlas A for the problem is known. We aim to find the maximal matrix Lie group that Φ is
207
      A atlas equivariant to.
208
209   We propose ATLAS, an algorithm for automated local symmetry discovery to tackle the problem.
210   First, we must model individual localized functions with neural networks or other differentiable
211   oracles. The exact method is unique to each task, but is generally a simple regression problem.
212
      Further details are available in Appendix C. In contrast to global discovery techniques, we emphasize
      that the individual neural networks are localized maps rather than functions over the entire manifold.
213
214   We then find the equivariance group of the localized functions. There are important differences
215   between our procedure and global symmetry discovery. In our setting, a group element must only act
      on a local chart, rather than the full space. Likewise, a core tenet of local symmetry is that we can


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      apply different transformations to different regions of the feature field. Care must be taken, therefore,
217   that the actions on different charts are truly independent.
218
219   We aim to discover the maximal group of local symmetry. In practice, these symmetries often involve
220
      both discrete and continuous transformations, which motivates us to use Lie groups to describe the
      symmetries of interest. Specifically, we seek to discover both the Lie algebra which characterizes
221
      the continuous transformations, and the cosets that describe the discrete actions of the target group.
222
      Put together, these allow us to describe a wide variety of Lie groups. Algorithm 1 outlines our overall

266   Many symmetry discovery methods only discover a Lie algebra basis, limiting the results to connected
267   Lie groups. In practice, groups such as O(2) and the Lorentz group have multiple connected
268   components, which is a natural consequence of discrete symmetries such as reflections. In this
269   subsection, we introduce a method to discover discrete symmetries by identifying the G0 -cosets in
      the component group G/G0 .

      4.3   C ONNECTION TO G AUGE E QUIVARIANT CNN
307
308
      A related notion of local symmetry is introduced by Cohen et al. (2019) when defining gauge
      equivariant CNNs. In short, gauge equivariance implies that one should be able to arbitrarily orient
309
      the local coordinate systems used to define features and compute convolutions. Hence, it is a property
310
      of the network modeling the task function, rather than a property of the task function itself. This is a
311   notable difference compared to our work, where the atlas equivariance group is intrinsic to the system
312   and something that can be discovered.
313
314
      The following theorem provides a concrete connection between gauge equivariance and atlas equiv-
      ariance (proof in Appendix A).
315
316   Theorem 1. Let M be a gauge equivariant CNN that (a) has a linear gauge group G, (b) is A atlas
317   local for some atlas A with trivial charts, and (c) operates on Euclidean space. Then, M is A atlas
318   equivariant to G.
319
320   In practice, a gauge equivariant CNN is neither meant to operate on Euclidean space nor completely
321   A atlas local. However, the result is approximately true for an arbitrary manifold as manifolds are
322   locally flat. This implies that if a system is atlas equivariant for some group G, it is logical to set
323   the gauge group of a downstream gauge equivariant CNN to G. We employ this technique as an
      application of our discovered symmetries below.

      4.4   I MPLEMENTATION N OTES
325
326   Due to issues such as discretization, noise, boundary conditions, or limited a priori knowledge of a
327   perfect atlas, real-world datasets may only be approximately, not exactly, A atlas local. To mitigate
328   this issue, we sometimes allow the Φc predictors to look slightly outside of the associated chart,
329   i.e. the radius of the input chart is higher than the radius of the output chart for any given Φc . This
330   provides the localized functions with additional context that may be missing from the unmodified
331   input. Additionally, to avoid boundaries and awkward topologies (e.g. poles of a spherical mesh),
332
      we partially deviate from the definition of an atlas and do not require that the charts fully cover the
      manifold. Empirically, if the charts span the majority of M rather than fully covering M, our method
333
      is still able to discover local symmetries within the given region.

      6    C ONCLUSION
526
527
      In this paper, we introduce atlas equivariance and propose automatic local symmetry discovery
528   (ATLAS) as an architecture capable of learning local symmetries for a variety of systems. We
529   demonstrate that our methodology can discover both infinitesimal generators and cosets of the atlas
530   equivariance group from a dataset. Moreover, the results show that the atlas equivariance group also
531   serves as an inductive bias in downstream gauge equivariant networks. This proves the need to focus
532   on local symmetries of a system as opposed to solely their global ones.
533
      While our method effectively discovers atlas equivariances, we should note that atlas equivariances
534   only describe a subset of all possible symmetries of a manifold. We set the stage for future work to
535   explore the discovery of larger groups. In addition, while we show that ATLAS is resilient to slight
536   modifications of the given atlas, a priori knowledge of a suitable atlas is still important and may not
537   always be available. An extension to our work can relax this condition by developing a method that
538   discovers the atlas in tandem to the atlas equivariance group. Another possible direction for future
539   work is to consider the symmetries that act on both the manifold and the features, such as the point
      symmetries in partial differential equation systems.

      A.1    ATLAS EQUIVARIANCE OF GAUGE EQUIVARIANT CNN
705
706   Theorem 1. Let M be a gauge equivariant CNN that (a) has a linear gauge group G, (b) is A atlas
707   local for some atlas A with trivial charts, and (c) operates on Euclidean space. Then, M is A atlas
708   equivariant to G.
709
710   Proof. We first clarify a few definitions. By trivial charts, we simply mean that φc is the inclusion map.
711   We model a gauge equivariant CNN as a series of convolutional layers and pointwise nonlinearities
712   (Cohen et al., 2019). To enforce gauge equivariance, we will require all kernels K : Rd → RCin ×Cout
713   in the network to satisfy K(g −1 v) = K(v) for g ∈ G.
714   To show that such a network M is G atlas equivariant, we must prove that there exists G-equivariant
715   localized functions Mc . Recall that in the definition of A atlas local, Mc has no restriction on its
716   output field outside φc (Uc ). Consequently, since all charts are trivial and M is assumed to be A atlas
717   local to begin with, M itself is suitable for each Mc .
718
      It remains to show that M is G equivariant. Indeed, a G gauge equivariant convolutional layer on
719   Euclidean space as presented above is equivalent to a G-steerable convolution (Weiler & Cesa, 2019).
720   Moreover, G-steerable convolutions are globally equivariant. As we assume feature vectors transform
721   trivially in response to a group action, all pointwise nonlinearities are automatically G equivariant.
722   M , the composition of G equivariant layers, is then G equivariant.
723
      Thus, M is A atlas equivariant with respect to G.

      A.2    A RGMIN OF S TANDARD BASIS R EGULARIZATION
726
727   Let V be a k-dimensional subspace of Rn . We call a basis {bi }ki=1 of V disjoint if the set of indices
728   of all non-zero elements of bi and the similar set for bj are disjoint whenever i ̸= j. The following
729   theorem gives a result about the arguments of the minima of Lsbr .
730
      Theorem 2. Let V be a k-dimensional subspace of Rn for which there exists a disjoint basis. Then,
731   among all possible bases {bi }ki=1 of V , Lsbr (b) is minimal if and only if b is disjoint.
732
733
      Proof. Suppose {bi } is a basis of V . If b is disjoint then for all i ̸= j we have |bi | · |bj | = 0 since for
734   each index ℓ, at least one of bi,ℓ or bj,ℓ will be zero. Conversely, if b is not disjoint, then there exist
735   some i ̸= j such that |bi | · |bj | > 0. To see this, note that we must have some i ̸= j where bi and bj
736   share a non-zero element at index ℓ. |bi | · |bj |, the sum of non-negative numbers, is then greater or
737   equal to |bi,ℓ ||bj,ℓ | > 0.
738
      In particular, this implies that for a disjoint basis b, we have Lsbr (b) = 0, but otherwise Lsbr (b) > 0.
739
      By assumption, there exists at least one disjoint basis so then the minimum of Lsbr over all possible
740   bases of V is 0. This minimum is attained exactly when the input basis is disjoint.

993    E     A LGORITHM A NALYSIS
994
995    We analyze the space and time complexity of the different parts of our algorithm. In our analysis,
996    we assume that dim M is fixed as a constant. We use the notation that T is the number of training
997    iterations for a given run, k is the dimension of the ground truth symmetry, P is the number of
998    localized predictors, K is the total number of cosets used during training, and q is the max number of
999    cosets reported.
1000
1001   E.1   I NFINITESIMAL G ENERATORS
1002
1003   Our algorithm requires storing the discovered Lie algebra basis, which takes space proportional to k.
1004
       We also need to store each of the P localized predictors, giving us space complexity of O(k + P ).
1005   We first consider the amount of time a single training step takes. Calculating the standard basis regu-
1006   larization takes O(k 2 ) time. For a given predictor, computing the main loss takes time proportional to
1007   k, which is needed for the sampling of the group element. In our implementation, we evaluate all P
1008   predictors in a training step. There are T total training steps in a given run. Then, we require at most
1009   k total runs to determine the optimal dimension of the basis. This gives the total time complexity as
1010
       O(kT (k 2 + kP )) = O(k 2 T (k + P ))
1011   The above result is somewhat misleading as it hides the high constant factor that the predictor
1012   evaluation entails. If we ignore the regularizations and only consider the predictor evaluation, the
1013   time complexity becomes O(kT P ).
1014
1015   E.2   D ISCRETE S YMMETRIES
1016
1017   We require storing the K cosets as well as the P predictors. The space complexity is then O(K + P ).
1018   In each training step, we evaluate each of the P predictors on K transformed inputs, corresponding
1019   to the K cosets. This is repeated for all T training iterations. After the training process, we must
1020   filter the duplicate cosets. In the worst case, we report q cosets in which case we need to do O(Kq)
1021   comparisons. The total time is then O(K(T P + q))
