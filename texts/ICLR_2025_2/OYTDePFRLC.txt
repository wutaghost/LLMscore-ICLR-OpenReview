                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Differential privacy (DP) in deep learning is a critical concern as it ensures the con-
010
               fidentiality of training data while maintaining model utility. Existing DP training
011            algorithms provide privacy guarantees by clipping and then injecting external noise
012            into sample gradients computed by the backpropagation algorithm. Different from
013            backpropagation, forward-learning algorithms based on perturbation inherently
014            add noise during the forward pass and utilize randomness to estimate the gradients.
015            Although these algorithms are non-privatized, the introduction of noise during
016            the forward pass indirectly provides internal randomness protection to the model
017            parameters and their gradients, suggesting the potential for naturally providing
018            differential privacy. In this paper, we propose a privatized forward-learning algo-
019
               rithm, Differential Private Unified Likelihood Ratio (DP-ULR), and demonstrate its
               differential privacy guarantees. DP-ULR features a novel batch sampling operation
020
               with rejection, of which we provide theoretical analysis in conjunction with classic
021
               differential privacy mechanisms. DP-ULR is also underpinned by a theoretically
022            guided privacy controller that dynamically adjusts noise levels to manage privacy
023            costs in each training step. Our experiments indicate that DP-ULR achieves compet-
024            itive performance compared to traditional differential privacy training algorithms
025            based on backpropagation, maintaining nearly the same privacy loss limits.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Deep neural networks have made substantial advancements across various domains, such as image
031   recognition (Meng et al., 2022; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2020), natural language
032   processing (Deng & Liu, 2018; Meng et al., 2022; Han et al., 2021), and autonomous driving (Huang
033
      et al., 2018; Häuslschmid et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2015). However, training these powerful models
      often involves vast amounts of data, including personal data gathered from the Internet, exacerbating
034
      privacy concerns. It has been well-documented that neural networks do not merely learn from data
035
      but can also memorize specific instances (Carlini et al., 2019; 2021).
036
037   Differential privacy (DP) has emerged as a widely accepted metric for assessing the leakage of
038   sensitive information in data (Liu et al., 2024a). In the realm of model training, DP mechanisms
039
      (algorithms) aim to ensure that the presence or absence of any single data sample does not signifi-
      cantly influence the learned parameters. The most popular learning algorithm, Differentially Private
040
      Stochastic Gradient Descent (DP-SGD) (Abadi et al., 2016), employs a typical strategy to safeguard
041
      privacy: assessing algorithms’ sensitivity and introducing randomness by adding random noise to
042   their final output. The manually introduced randomness breaks the deterministic of the computed
043   gradient and protects privacy. However, there are many problems when deploying DP-SGD. First, it
044   needs to compute the gradient of each sample individually, causing huge time consumption compared
045   to traditional non-private algorithms. Second, it needs the full knowledge of the computational graph
046   due to backpropagation, while any insertion of black-box modules in the pipeline would block the
047   use of DP-SGD. Third, it needs all operations to be differentiable, while many advanced models use
048   non-differentiable activations, such as spiking neural networks (Tavanaei et al., 2019).
049   Different from deterministic backpropagation-based methods, forward-learning algorithms (Peng
050   et al., 2022; Hinton, 2022; Salimans et al., 2017) employ perturbation or Monte Carlo simulations
051   to estimate the gradient directly, bypassing the need for backpropagation based on the chain rule.
052   Compared to backpropagation-based methods, forward-learning algorithms offer several advantages,
053   including high parallelizability, suitability for non-differentiable modules, and applicability in black-
      box settings (Jiang et al., 2023). Moreover, as depicted in Figure 1, perturbation during the forward

      2.2   D IFFERENTIAL P RIVACY IN D EEP L EARNING
121
122   As an adaption of Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) with backpropagation, DP-SGD (Abadi et al.,
123   2016) is the most popular DP algorithm for deep learning (De et al., 2022; Sander et al., 2023).
124   It assesses sensitivity by clipping the per-sample gradients and adds Gaussian noise after gradient
125   computation to provide differential privacy guarantees. Particularly, as a training algorithm that
126   comprises a sequence of adaptive mechanisms—a common scenario in deep learning—DP-SGD adds
127   noise to the outcome of each sub-mechanism calibrated to its sensitivity, enhancing the utility of final
128   learned models. While several techniques to improve the utility-privacy trade-off have been employed,
129
      including over-parameterized model (De et al., 2022), mega-batch training (Dörmann et al., 2021;
      Sander et al., 2023), averaging per-sample gradients across multiple augmentations (Hoffer et al.,
130
      2020), temporal parameter averaging (Polyak & Juditsky, 1992), equivariant networks (Hölzl et al.,
131
      2023), these adaptations not only heavily increase the computation cost but also do not change the
132   core of DP-SGD: adding noise to deterministic gradients, which does not stand in forward learning.
133
134
      2.3   F ORWARD LEARNING
135
136   While there is no evidence that backpropagation exists in natural intelligence (Lillicrap et al., 2020),
137   some studies put efforts into designing biologically plausible forward-only learning algorithms. For
138   example, Nøkland (2016) employs the direct feedback alignment to train hidden layers independently.
139   Jacot et al. (2018) leverage a neural target kernel to approximate the gradient for optimization.
140   Salimans et al. (2017) apply the evolutional strategy to update the neural network parameters. Hinton
141   (2022) replace the forward-backward pass with two forwards and optimize the neural networks by
142
      optimizing the local loss functions on positive and negative samples. Peng et al. (2022) propose a
      likelihood ratio (LR) method for unbiased gradient estimation with only one forward in the multi-layer
143
      perception training and Jiang et al. (2023) develop the unified likelihood ratio (ULR) method for
144
      training a wide range of deep learning models. In our work, we incorporate novel elements into
145   ULR and provide a theoretical-guaranteed privatized forward-learning algorithm, i.e., DP-ULR, to
146   achieve differential privacy. We note that while several existing works (Liu et al., 2024b; Zhang et al.,
147   2024; Tang et al., 2024) privatize loss values obtained in zeroth-order optimization for achieving DP,
148   our work differs from them in multiple aspects, including motivation, application scope, the core
149   algorithm, and theoretical analysis. Detailed discussion is provided in Appendix A.5.

235   Random distribution of estimated gradients. Differential privacy guarantees are highly sensitive
236   to the distribution of the mechanism’s outputs. In the common strategy to protect privacy, the noise
237
      of a Gaussian distribution is added to the output, making it also a Gaussian distribution given the
      sampling result. On the contrary, in our method, the Gaussian noise is injected into the intermediate
238
      value, leaving the final output’s distribution a mystery. Recall that the final gradient estimator is
239
      obtained by averaging K repetitions. It follows that, according to the multidimensional central limit
240   theorem, gtl (d) can be approximated as Gaussian when the repeat time K is large enough.

242   Batch subsampling with rejection. A significant difference between our proposed DP-ULR and the
243
      previous likelihood ratio methods is the subsampling operation. In DP-ULR, we adopt an independent
      sampling strategy with a predefined threshold NB . Concretely, each example in the dataset di ∈ D
244
      is picked independently with the same probability q. But if the size of Bt is smaller than NB , it is
245
      rejected and resampled. Like the ordinary i.i.d subsampling, our rejection strategy with a lower limit
246   also amplifies privacy. Specifically, in the subsampling with rejection, we expect that the privacy cost
247   γ diminishes quadratically with the subsampling rate but adds a very small term that is not related to
248   α in (α, γ)-RDP. We discuss the privacy amplification in detail in Section 3.3.
249

250

252

342   3.4   DP-SGD v.s. DP-ULR
343
344
      In this section, we discuss the difference between DP-SGD and DP-ULR.
345   Minute DP impairment. According to Mironov et al. (2019), DP-SGD(Abadi et al., 2016) satisfies
346   (α, γ)-RDP for a suitable range of α and γ = 2T q 2 α/σ 2 , where σ is noise scale. If we set our target
347   output std σ0 equal to this noise scale, the difference in RDP bound is the impairment term from the
348   SRGM, which is related to the training dataset size. In practice, deep learning datasets are quite large.
349   Subsequently, the impairment term is extremely small to be ignored compared to the latter term. For
350
      instance, if we consider N̄ = 10000, q = 0.01, and NB = 50, the impairment is less than 10−10 ,
      while the second term is greater than 10−6 . We provide further empirical analysis in Section 4.1.
351
352   Noise redundancy. DP-SGD injects isotropic noise directly into the precise gradient, making full
353   use of noise to offer differential privacy. DP-ULR attempts to utilize the inherent randomness of
354   gradient estimation, where noise is added to the intermediate values in the forward pass. It provides
355   privacy protection by ensuring the variance of the estimated gradient in an arbitrary direction no less
356
      than a pre-defined level. However, it also means that randomness in many other directions is even
      larger than this level due to the non-isotropy. This redundant noise doesn’t contribute to the bound of
357
      differential privacy but impairs the accuracy of the gradient estimation.
358
359   Efficiency and suitability. A common limitation of DP-SGD is its slower speed compared to
360   traditional SGD, primarily due to the requirement to clip each individual gradient, necessitating an
361   independent backward pass for each example. In contrast, the computation of individual estimated
362
      gradients in DP-ULR is inherently separate, allowing for individual clipping without additional
      computational cost compared to ULR. Additionally, as a variant of ULR, DP-ULR inherits certain
363
      advantages over backpropagation-based DP-SGD, including suitability for non-differentiable or black-
364
      box settings, high parallelizability, and efficient pipeline design (Jiang et al., 2023). Furthermore, in
365   cases where the loss function cannot be expressed as a summation of individual losses, computing
366   individual gradients to limit example sensitivity becomes challenging for standard backpropagation.
367   In DP-ULR, noise can be injected separately, enabling independent gradient computation, thus
368   broadening its potential applications.

502   5                    C ONCLUSIONS
503
504   In this paper, we propose a forward-learning DP algorithm, Differential Private Unified Likelihood
505   Ratio (DP-ULR). Unlike traditional backpropagation-based methods such as DP-SGD, which rely
506   on computing individual gradients and adding noise, DP-ULR leverages the inherent randomness in
507   forward-learning algorithms to achieve differential privacy. Our approach introduces a novel batch
508   sampling operation with rejection and a dynamically managed privacy controller to ensure robust
509
      privacy guarantees.
510   Our theoretical analysis demonstrates that the additional privacy cost introduced by the sampling-
511   with-rejection operation is negligible, particularly in large-scale deep-learning applications. This
512   indicates the scalability and efficiency of DP-ULR in practical settings. Furthermore, our empirical
513   results show that DP-ULR performs competitively compared to traditional DP training algorithms,
514   maintaining the same privacy loss constraints while offering high parallelizability and suitability for
515
      non-differentiable or black-box modules.
516   In summary, DP-ULR provides a promising alternative to existing differential privacy methods,
517   combining the benefits of forward learning with rigorous privacy guarantees.
518

      L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
520
521
      We proposed an intuitive and direct adaptation (DP-ULR) of a forward-learning approach (ULR) that
522   diverges from traditional SGD by eschewing backpropagation. Our analysis in this work primarily
523   compares DP-ULR with the canonical form of DP-SGD. We acknowledge that recent advancements
524   that incrementally improve the privacy-utility trade-off in DP-SGD could potentially be generalized
525   to our forward-learning context; however, such extensions are beyond the scope of our initial
526   investigation and represent promising avenues for future research.
527
      Although DP-ULR retains the same benefits as ULR due to the unchanged core mechanics, we did
528   not explore its suitability for non-differential or black-box settings in our experiments. Additionally,
529   we did not implement parallelization or optimize the training pipeline for efficiency. In its current
530   implementation, DP-ULR takes 23 seconds per epoch on an A6000 GPU with the MNIST dataset,
531   which is slower than DP-SGD, which takes 18 seconds per epoch. Due to the large gradient estimation
532   variance, the scale-up of ULR usually requires a large number of copies, which poses a great challenge
533   to the computation and memory cost. Future works might focus on the development of advanced
534   techniques to improve computational efficiency and reduce the estimation variance.
535   Differential privacy aims to ensure data privacy through randomness. When used to train a deep
536   learning model, such randomness impairs the model’s performance. Our algorithm has the same
537   limitation. Besides, during training with DP-ULR, we observed overfitting, where the model achieved
538   high accuracy (around 90% on MNIST) but exhibited extreme losses: near-zero loss for some samples
539   while having very high losses for others that it failed to classify. Addressing this overfitting issue is
      another area for potential future exploration.

831   A.5    C OMPARISON TO E XISTING DP Z EROTH -O RDER M ETHODS
832
      Several recent works Liu et al. (2024b); Zhang et al. (2024); Tang et al. (2024) propose DP zeroth-
833
      order methods that privatize loss values or estimated gradients obtained via two forward passes in
834   zeroth-order optimization for achieving DP guarantee. Our proposed DP-ULR departs from these
835   methods in the following key aspects:
836
837
      Motivation. Existing approaches achieve differential privacy by introducing additional noise to
      zeroth-order gradients or losses. In contrast, our work first noticed that forward learning’s inherent
838
      randomness has the potential for a ”free lunch” to provide privacy guarantees. Motivated by this,
839
      we propose DP-ULR, which leverages the noise added for gradient estimation in forward learning
840   algorithms to provide privacy guarantees.
841
842   Core Algorithm and Application Scope. Existing works utilize the traditional zeroth-order method,
843
      Simultaneous Perturbation Stochastic Approximation (SPSA), which adds noise to parameters with
      dimensions significantly higher than logits—often exceeding 100 times. This leads to substantial
844
      increases in computational costs (and estimation variance) as the model size grows, limiting their
845
      scalability to complex deep-learning models, particularly for training from scratch. Those existing
846   methods are designed for fine-tuning pre-trained models. In contrast, DP-ULR operates directly on
847   logits, enabling the training of deep learning models from scratch and reducing the computational
848   overhead.
849
      Privacy Bound. DP-ULR offers superior privacy guarantees compared to methods like ZeroDP
850
      Liu et al. (2024b). ZeroDP has the most similar zeroth-order optimization setting to us, involving
851
      stochastic gradient descent and repeated sampling. The privacy cost of ZeroDP scales quadratically
852   with the number of repetitions P (Theorem 4.1 in Liu et al. (2024b)), resulting in rapidly increasing
853   privacy costs for large P . In contrast, DP-ULR’s privacy cost is independent of the number of
854   repetitions, ensuring more robust and scalable privacy protection.
