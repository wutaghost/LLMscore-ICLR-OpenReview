                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Time-series forecasting has seen significant advancements with the introduction of
011
               token prediction mechanisms such as multi-head attention. However, these methods
               often struggle to achieve the same performance as in language modeling, primarily
012
               due to the quadratic computational cost and the complexity of capturing long-range
013
               dependencies in time-series data. State-space models (SSMs), such as Mamba,
014            have shown promise in addressing these challenges by offering efficient solutions
015            with linear RNNs capable of modeling long sequences with larger context windows.
016            However, there remains room for improvement in accuracy and scalability.
017
               We propose the use of Test-Time Training (TTT) modules in a parallel architecture
018            to enhance performance in long-term time series forecasting. Through extensive
019            experiments on standard benchmark datasets, we demonstrate that TTT mod-
020            ules consistently outperform state-of-the-art models, including the Mamba-based
021            TimeMachine, particularly in scenarios involving extended sequence and prediction
022            lengths. Our results show significant improvements in Mean Squared Error (MSE)
023            and Mean Absolute Error (MAE), especially on larger datasets such as Electricity,
024            Traffic, and Weather, underscoring the effectiveness of TTT in capturing long-range
025            dependencies. Additionally, we explore various convolutional architectures within
026
               the TTT framework, showing that even simple configurations like 1D convolution
               with small filters can achieve competitive results. This work sets a new benchmark
027
               for time-series forecasting and lays the groundwork for future research in scalable,
028
               high-performance forecasting models.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Long Time Series Forecasting (LTSF) is a crucial task in various fields, including energy Doe &
035   Smith (2023), industry Doe & Smith (2024), defense Bakdash et al. (2017), and atmospheric sciences
036   Lim & Zohren (2020). LTSF uses a historical sequence of observations, known as the look-back
037   window, to predict future values through a learned or mathematically induced model. However, the
038   stochastic nature of real-world events makes LTSF challenging. Deep learning models, including time
039
      series forecasting, have been widely adopted in engineering and scientific fields. Early approaches
      employed Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) to capture long-range dependencies in sequential data
040
      like time series. However, recurrent architectures like RNNs have limited memory retention, are
041
      difficult to parallelize, and have constrained expressive capacity. Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017),
042   with ability to efficiently process sequential data in parallel and capture contextual information, have
043   significantly improved performance on time series prediction task (Wen et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022b;
044   Ni et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024). Yet, due to the quadratic complexity of attention mechanisms
045   with respect to the context window (or look-back window in LTSF), Transformers are limited in their
046   ability to capture very long dependencies.
047
      In recent years, State Space Models (SSMs) such as Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024), a gated linear RNN
048   variant, have revitalized the use of RNNs for LTSF. These models efficiently capture much longer
049   dependencies while reducing computational costs and enhancing expressive power and memory
050   retention. A new class of Linear RNNs, known as Test Time Training (TTT) modules (Sun et al.,
051   2024), has emerged. These modules use expressive hidden states and provide theoretical guarantees
052   for capturing long-range dependencies, positioning them as one of the most promising architectures
053   for LTSF and due to their weight adaptation during test time are very effective on non-stationary data.
      We provide more motivation on TTT for non-stationary data in Appendix A.

      K EY I NSIGHTS AND R ESULTS
055
056   Through our experiments, several key insights emerged regarding the performance of TTT modules
057   when compared to existing SOTA models:
058
059    • Superior Performance with Longer Sequence and Prediction Lengths: TTT modules con-
060      sistently outperformed the SOTA TimeMachine model, particularly as sequence and prediction
061      lengths increased. Architectures such as Conv Stack 5 demonstrated their ability to capture
062      long-range dependencies more effectively than Mamba-based models, resulting in noticeable
063
         improvements in Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Mean Absolute Error (MAE) across various
         benchmark datasets.
064
065    • Strong Improvement on Larger Datasets: On larger datasets, such as Electricity, Traffic,
066      and Weather, the TTT-based models excelled, showing superior performance compared to both
067
         Transformer- and Mamba-based models. These results underscore the ability of TTT to han-
         dle larger temporal windows and more complex data, making it especially effective in high-
068
         dimensional, multivariate datasets.
069
070
       • Hidden Layer Architectures: The ablation studies revealed that while convolutional architectures
         added to the TTT modules provided some improvements, Conv Stack 5 consistently delivered the
071
         best results among the convolutional variants. However, simpler architectures like Conv 3 often
072
         performed comparably, showing that increased architectural complexity did not always lead to
073      significantly better performance. Very complex architectures like the modern convolutional block
074      from Donghao & Xue (2024) showed competitive performance when used as TTT hidden layer
075      architectures compared to the simpler single architectures proposed, hinting on the potential of
076      more complex architectures in capturing more long term dependencies.
077    • Adaptability to Long-Term Predictions: The TTT-based models excelled in long-term prediction
078      tasks, especially for really high prediction lengths like 2880. TTT-based models also excelled on
079      increased sequence lengths as high as 5760 which is the maximum sequence length allowed by
080      the benchmark datasets. This verified the theoretically expected superiority of TTT based models
081      relative to the mamba/transformer based SOTA models.
082
083   M OTIVATION AND C ONTRIBUTIONS
084
085   In this work, we explore the potential of TTT modules in Long-Term Series Forecasting (LTSF)
086   by integrating them into novel model configurations to surpass the current state-of-the-art (SOTA)
087   models. Our key contributions are as follows:
088
089    • We propose a new model architecture utilizing quadruple TTT modules, inspired by the TimeMa-
090      chine model (Ahamed & Cheng, 2024), which currently holds SOTA performance. By replacing
091
         the Mamba modules with TTT modules, our model effectively captures longer dependencies and
         predicts larger sequences.
092
093    • We evaluate the model on benchmark datasets, exploring the original look-back window and
094      prediction lengths to identify the limitations of the SOTA architecture. We demonstrate that
095
         the SOTA model achieves its performance primarily by constraining look-back windows and
         prediction lengths, thereby not fully leveraging the potential of LTSF.
096
097    • We extend our evaluations to significantly larger sequence and prediction lengths, showing that our
098
         TTT-based model consistently outperforms the SOTA model using Mamba modules, particularly
         in scenarios involving extended look-back windows and long-range predictions.
099
100    • We conduct an ablation study to assess the performance of various hidden layer architectures
101      within our model. By testing six different convolutional configurations, one of which being
102
         ModernTCN by Donghao & Xue (2024), we quantify their impact on model performance and
         provide insights into how they compare with the SOTA model.

107   Transformers for LTSF Several Transformer-based models have advanced long-term time series
      forecasting (LTSF), with notable examples like iTransformer (Liu et al., 2024) and PatchTST


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (Nie et al., 2023). iTransformer employs multimodal interactive attention to capture both temporal and
109   inter-modal dependencies, suitable for multivariate time series, though it incurs high computational
110   costs when multimodal data interactions are minimal. PatchTST, inspired by Vision Transform-
111   ers (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), splits input sequences into patches to capture dependencies effectively,
112   but its performance hinges on selecting the appropriate patch size and may reduce model interpretabil-
113   ity. Other influential models include Informer (Zhou et al., 2021), which uses sparse self-attention
114   to reduce complexity but may overlook finer details in multivariate data; Autoformer (Wu et al.,
115   2022), which excels in periodic data but struggles with non-periodic patterns; Pyraformer (Liu et al.,
116   2022b), which captures multi-scale dependencies through a hierarchical structure but at the cost of
117
      increased computational requirements; and Fedformer (Zhou et al., 2022), which combines time- and
      frequency-domain representations for efficiency but may underperform on noisy time series. While
118
      each model advances LTSF in unique ways, they also introduce specific trade-offs and limitations.
119
120
121   State Space Models for LTSF S4 models (Gu et al., 2022a;b; Gupta et al., 2023) are efficient
122   sequence models for long-term time series forecasting (LTSF), leveraging linear complexity through
123   four key components: ∆ (discretization step size), A (state update matrix), B (input matrix), and C
124   (output matrix). They operate in linear recurrence for autoregressive inference and global convolution
125   for parallel training, efficiently transforming recurrences into convolutions. However, S4 struggles
126   with time-invariance issues, limiting selective memory. Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024) addresses this by
127
      making B, C, and ∆ dynamic, creating adaptable parameters that improve noise filtering and maintain
      Transformer-level performance with linear complexity. SIMBA (Patro & Agneeswaran, 2024)
128
      enhances S4 by integrating block-sparse attention, blending state space and attention to efficiently
129
      capture long-range dependencies while reducing computational overhead, ideal for large-scale, noisy
130   data. TimeMachine (Ahamed & Cheng, 2024) builds on these advances by employing a quadruple
131   Mamba setup, managing both channel mixing and independence while avoiding Transformers’
132   quadratic complexity through multi-scale context generation, thereby maintaining high performance
133   in long-term forecasting tasks.
134
135
136   Linear RNNs for LTSF RWKV-TS (Hou & Yu, 2024) is a novel linear RNN architecture designed
137
      for time series tasks, achieving O(L) time and memory complexity with improved long-range
      information capture, making it more efficient and scalable compared to traditional RNNs like LSTM
138
      and GRU. Orvieto et al. (2023) introduced the Linear Recurrent Unit (LRU), an RNN block matching
139
      the performance of S4 models on long-range reasoning tasks while maintaining computational
140   efficiency. TTT (Sun et al., 2024) layers take a novel approach by treating the hidden state as a
141   trainable model, learning during both training and test time with dynamically updated weights. This
142   allows TTT to capture long-term relationships more effectively through real-time updates, providing
143   an efficient, parallelizable alternative to self-attention with linear complexity. TTT’s adaptability
144   and efficiency make it a strong candidate for processing longer contexts, addressing the scalability
145   challenges of RNNs and outperforming Transformer-based architectures in this regard.
146
147
      MLPs and CNNs for LTSF Recent advancements in long-term time series forecasting (LTSF) have
148
      introduced efficient architectures that avoid the complexity of attention mechanisms and recurrence.
149
      TSMixer (Chen et al., 2023b), an MLP-based model, achieves competitive performance by separating
150   temporal and feature interactions through time- and channel-mixing, enabling linear scaling with
151   sequence length. However, MLPs may struggle with long-range dependencies and require careful
152   hyperparameter tuning, especially for smaller datasets. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs)
153   have also proven effective for LTSF, particularly in capturing local temporal patterns. ModernTCN
154   (Donghao & Xue, 2024) improves temporal convolution networks (TCNs) using dilated convolutions
155   and a hierarchical structure to efficiently capture both short- and long-range dependencies, making it
156   well-suited for multi-scale time series data.
157   Building on these developments, we improve the original TimeMachine model by replacing its
158   Mamba blocks with Test-Time Training (TTT) blocks to enhance long-context prediction capabilities.
159   We also explore CNN configurations, such as convolutional stacks, to enrich local temporal feature
160   extraction. This hybrid approach combines the efficiency of MLPs, the local pattern recognition of
161   CNNs, and the global context modeling of TTT, leading to a more robust architecture for LTSF tasks
      that balances both short- and long-term forecasting needs.

      3     M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
163
164
165   The task of Time Series Forecasting can be defined as follows: Given a multivariate time series
166
      dataset with a window of past observations (look-back window) L: (x1 , . . . , xL ), where each xt is a
      vector of dimension M (the number of channels at time t), the goal is to predict the next T future
167
      values (xL+1 , . . . , xL+T ).
168
169   The TimeMachine (Ahamed & Cheng, 2024) architecture, which we used as the backbone, is designed
170   to capture long-term dependencies in multivariate time series data, offering linear scalability and a
171   small memory footprint. It integrates four Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024) modules as sequence modeling
172
      blocks to selectively memorize or forget historical data, and employs two levels of downsampling to
      generate contextual cues at both high and low resolutions.
173
174   However, Mamba’s approach still relies on fixed-size hidden states to compress historical information
175   over time, often leading to the model forgetting earlier information in long sequences. TTT (Sun
176   et al., 2024) uses dynamically updated weights (in the form of matrices inside linear or MLP layers)
177   to compress and store historical data. This dynamic adjustment during test time allows TTT to better
178
      capture long-term relationships by continuously incorporating new information. Its Hidden State
      Updating Rule is defined as:

      We incorporated TTT into the TimeMachine model, replacing the original Mamba block. We evalu-
      ated our approach with various setups, including different backbones and TTT layer configurations.
184
      Additionally, we introduced convolutional layers before the sequence modeling block and conducted
185
      experiments with different context lengths and prediction lengths. We provide mathematical foun-
186   dations as to why TTT is able to perform test-time adaptation without catastrophic forgetting and
187   how the module adapts to distribution shifts in Appendix A. In the same Appendix we quantify the
188   computational overhead introduced by test-time updates and provide empirical validation, published
189   by the authors who proposed TTT in Sun et al. (2020), on how it performs on real corrupted data and
190   provide some intuition on the parameter initialization in TTT as discussed in the same reference.
191
      Our goal is to improve upon the performance of the state-of-the-art (SOTA) models in LTSF using
192   the latest advancements in sequential modeling. Specifically, we integrate Test-Time Training (TTT)
193   modules into our model for two key reasons, TTT is theoretically proven to have an extremely long
194   context window, being a form of linear RNN (Orvieto et al., 2023), capable of capturing long-range
195   dependencies efficiently. Secondly, the expressive hidden states of TTT allow the model to capture
196   a diverse set of features without being constrained by the architecture, including the depth of the
197   hidden layers, their size, or the types of blocks used.

      3.1   G ENERAL A RCHITECTURE
200
201
      Our model architecture builds upon the TimeMachine model (Ahamed & Cheng, 2024), introducing
202
      key modifications, as shown in Figure 1a, 1b and 1c. Specifically, we replace the Mamba modules
203
      in TimeMachine with TTT (Test-Time Training) modules (Sun et al., 2024), which retain compati-
204   bility since both are linear RNNs (Orvieto et al., 2023). However, TTT offers superior long-range
205   dependency modeling due to its adaptive nature and theoretically infinite context window. A detailed
206   visualization of the TTT block and the different proposed architectures can be found in Appendix D
207
      Our model features a two-level hierarchical architecture that captures both high-resolution and low-
208
      resolution context, as illustrated in Figure 1a. To adapts to the specific characteristics of the dataset,
209
      the architecture handles two scenarios—Channel Mixing and Channel Independence—illustrated in
210   Figure 1b and 1c respectively. A more detailed and mathematical description of the normalization
211   and prediction procedures can be found in Appendix D. We provide a computational complexity
212   analysis of the TTT, Transformer, Mamba and ModernTCN modules in Appendix F and we also
213   provide a computational complexity analysis for our model, TimeMachine, iTransformer, PatchTST,
214   TSMixer and ModernTCN in Appendix G. We also included a mathematical comparison between the
215   Mamba and TTT modules in Appendix B as well as theoretical comparison between the TTT module
      and models handling noise or temporal regularization in Appendix C.

242   3.2    H IERARCHICAL E MBEDDING
243
244   The input sequence BM L (Batch, Channel, Length) is first passed through Reversible Instance
245   Normalization (Kim et al., 2021) (RevIN), which stabilizes the model by normalizing the input data
246   and helps mitigate distribution shifts. This operation is essential for improving generalization across
247   datasets.
248   After normalization, the sequence passes through two linear embedding layers. Linear E1 and Linear
249   E2 are used to map the input sequence into two embedding levels: higher resolution and lower
250   resolution. The embedding operations E1 : RM ×L → RM ×n1 and E2 : RM ×n1 → RM ×n2 are
251   achieved through MLP. n1 and n2 are configurations that take values from {512, 256, 128, 64, 32},
252   satisfying n1 > n2 . Dropout layers are applied after each embedding layer to prevent overfitting,
253   especially for long-term time series data. As shown in Figure 1a.
254   We provide more intuition on the effectivenes of hierarchical modeling in Appendix E.

257   3.3    T WO L EVEL C ONTEXTUAL C UE M ODELING
258
259   At each of the two embedding levels, a contextual cues modeling block processes the output from the
260
      Dropout layer following E1 and E2. This hierarchical architecture captures both fine-grained and
      broad temporal patterns, leading to improved forecasting accuracy for long-term time series data.
261
262   In Level 1, High-Resolution Contextual Cues Modeling is responsible for modeling high-resolution
263   contextual cues. TTT Block 3 and TTT Block 4 process the input tensor, focusing on capturing
264   fine-grained temporal dependencies. The TTT Block3 operates directly on the input, and transposition
265   may be applied before TTT Block4 if necessary. The outputs are summed, then concatenated with
266
      the Level 2 output. There is no residual connection summing in Level 1 modeling.
267   In Level 2, Low-Resolution Contextual Cues Modeling handles broader temporal patterns, func-
268   tioning similarly to Level 1. TTT Block 1 and TTT Block 2 process the input tensor to capture
269   low-resolution temporal cues and add them togther. A linear projection layer (P-1) is then applied to
      maps the output (with dimension RM×n2) to a higher dimension RM×n1, preparing it for concate-

      nation. Additionally, the Level 1 and Level 2 Residual Connections ensure that information from
271   previous layers is effectively preserved and passed on.

275   After processing both high-resolution and low-resolution cues, the model concatenates the outputs

281   3.5   C HANNEL M IXING AND I NDEPENDENCE M ODES
282
283
      The Channel Mixing Mode (Figure 1a and 1b) processes all channels of a multivariate time series
      together, allowing the model to capture potential correlations between different channels and un-
284
      derstand their interactions over longer time. Figure 1a illustrates an example of the channel mixing
285
      case, but there is also a channel independence case corresponding to Figure 1a, which we have not
286   shown here. Figures 1b and 1c demonstrate the channel mixing and independence modes of the Level
287   1 High-Resolution Contextual Cues Modeling part with TTT Block 3 and TTT Block 4. Similar
288   versions of the two channel modes for Level 2 Low-Resolution Contextual Cues Modeling are quite
289   similar to those in Level 1, which we have also omitted here.
290
      The Channel Independence Mode (Figure 1c) treats each channel of a multivariate time series as an
291
      independent sequence, enabling the model to analyze individual time series more accurately. This
292   mode focuses on learning patterns within each channel without considering potential correlations
293   between them.
294
295
      The main difference between these two modes is that the Channel Independence Mode always uses
      transposition before and after one of the TTT blocks (in Figure 1c, it’s TTT Block 4). This allows the
296
      block to capture contextual cues from local perspectives, while the other block focuses on modeling
297
      the global context. However, in the Channel Mixing Mode, both TTT Block 3 and TTT Block 4
298   model the global context.
299
300   The hidden size value for TTT Blocks in global context modeling is set to n1 since the input shape is
301
      BM n1 for Channel Mixing and (B × M )1n1 for Channel Independence. To make the TTT Block
      compatible with the local context modeling scenario—where the input becomes (B × M )n1 ←
302
      Transpose((B × M )1n1 ) after transposition—we add two linear layers: one for upsampling to
303
      (B × M )n1 16 and another for downsampling back. In this case, the hidden size of TTT Block 4 is
304   set to 16.

      the lowest MSE and MAE at the 96 time horizon, with values of 0.261 and 0.289, respectively,

418   However, other architectures, such as Conv 3 and Conv 5, provided only marginal improvements
419
      over the baseline TTT architectures (Linear and MLP). While they performed better than Linear and

525   6      C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
526
527   In this work, we improved the state-of-the-art (SOTA) model for time series forecasting by replacing
528   the Mamba modules in the original TimeMachine model with Test-Time Training (TTT) modules,
529   which leverage linear RNNs to capture long-range dependencies. Extensive experiments demonstrated
530   that the TTT architectures—MLP and Linear—performed well, with MLP slightly outperforming
531
      Linear. Exploring alternative architectures, particularly Conv Stack 5 and ModernTCN, significantly
      improved performance at longer prediction horizons, with ModernTCN showing notable efficiency in
532
      capturing short-term dependencies. The most significant gains came from increasing sequence and
533
      prediction lengths, where our TTT models consistently matched or outperformed the SOTA model,
534   particularly on larger datasets like Electricity, Traffic, and Weather, emphasizing the model’s strength
535   in handling long-range dependencies. While convolutional stacks and ModernTCN showed promise,
536   further improvements could be achieved by refining hidden layer configurations and exploring
537   architectural diversity. We included some potential real world applications of the TTT module in
538   Appendix C along with why we believe it’s best suited for LTSF. Overall, this work demonstrates
539   the potential of TTT modules in long-term forecasting, especially when combined with robust
      convolutional architectures and applied to larger datasets and longer horizons.

      A.1    M OTIVATION OF TTT ON N ON -S TATIONARY DATA
683   Time series forecasting often faces challenges arising from non-stationary data, where the underlying
684   statistical properties of the data evolve over time. Traditional models struggle with such scenarios, as
685   they are typically trained on static distributions and are not inherently equipped to handle distribution
686   shifts at inference time. Test-Time Training (TTT) has gained attention as a robust paradigm
687   to mitigate this issue, enabling models to adapt dynamically during inference by leveraging self-
688   supervised learning tasks. For example, the work on self-adaptive forecasting introduced by Google
689   in Arik et al. (2022) demonstrates how incorporating adaptive backcasting mechanisms allows models
690   to adjust their predictions to evolving patterns in the data, improving accuracy and robustness under
691
      non-stationary conditions. Similarly, FrAug Chen et al. (2023a) explores data augmentation in
      the frequency domain to bolster model performance in distributionally diverse settings. While not
692
      explicitly a TTT method, FrAug’s augmentation principles align with TTT’s objectives by enhancing
693
      model resilience to dynamic changes in time series characteristics. These studies collectively highlight
694   the potential of adaptive methods like TTT to address the unique challenges posed by non-stationary
695   time series data, making them well-suited for applications where robustness and flexibility are
696   paramount.
697
698   A.2    T HEORETICAL BASIS FOR TTT’ S A DAPTABILITY W ITHOUT C ATASTROPHIC
699          F ORGETTING
700
701   TTT avoids catastrophic forgetting by performing online self-supervised learning during inference.
      The adaptation occurs for each test sample independently, ensuring that the original parameters of

744   W HY O RTHOGONALITY IS A SSUMED
745          1. Distinct Optimization Objectives:
746

                     Lmain .

753               • The gradients ∇θ Lself and ∇θ Lmain are computed from different aspects of the model’s
754                  output.
755               • For example, if Lself reconstructs data and Lmain classifies labels, their parameter

850   A.5    PARAMETER I NITIALIZATION IN TTT
851
852   Test-Time Training (TTT) does not require specialized or customized parameter initialization methods.
853   For backbone architectures, TTT modules utilize standard initialization techniques, such as Xavier or
854
      He initialization, to ensure stable learning dynamics. Since TTT’s test-time updates are based on the
      weights learned during training, the model is agnostic to specific initialization strategies.
855
856   While TTT does not mandate particular initialization methods, it can benefit from pretrained weights.
857   By using a pretrained backbone, the model can leverage representations already optimized for a
858   related domain, allowing the test-time updates to refine these representations further. For example,
859   substituting a pretrained backbone with a TTT module can enhance adaptability during inference
860
      without requiring substantial retraining.
861   Empirical results from prior studies (e.g., Sun et al. (2020); Sun et al. (2024)) support this observation.
862   While pretrained weights can enhance performance, they are not strictly necessary. TTT’s adaptability
863   and effectiveness primarily stem from its self-supervised task, which guides the model to align with
      the test distribution rather than relying on the initialization strategy.

865   its core strength being its adaptability at test time. Further elaboration on this topic can be found in

904   B     TTT VS M AMBA
905
906   Both Test-Time Training (TTT) and Mamba are powerful linear Recurrent Neural Network (RNN)
907   architectures designed for sequence modeling tasks, including Long-Term Time Series Forecasting
908   (LTSF). While both approaches aim to capture long-range dependencies with linear complexity, there
909   are key differences in how they handle context windows, hidden state dynamics, and adaptability.
910   This subsection compares the two, focusing on their theoretical formulations and practical suitability
911   for LTSF.
912
913   B.1    M AMBA : G ATED L INEAR RNN VIA S TATE S PACE M ODELS (SSM S )
914
      Mamba is built on the principles of State Space Models (SSMs), which describe the system’s dynamics
915
      through a set of recurrence relations. The fundamental state-space equation for Mamba is defined as:
916
917
                                       hk = Āhk−1 + B̄uk ,      vk = Chk ,


                                                         17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      where:
919
920            • hk represents the hidden state at time step k.
921
               • uk is the input at time step k.
922
923
               • Ā and B̄ are learned state transition matrices.
924            • vk is the output at time step k, and C is the output matrix.
925
926
      The hidden state hk is updated in a recurrent manner, using the past hidden state hk−1 and the current
      input uk . Although Mamba can capture long-range dependencies better than traditional RNNs, its
927
      hidden state update relies on fixed state transitions governed by Ā and B̄, which limits its ability to
928
      dynamically adapt to varying input patterns over time.
929
930   In the context of LTSF, while Mamba performs better than Transformer architectures in terms of
931   computational efficiency (due to its linear complexity in relation to sequence length), it still struggles
932
      to fully capture long-term dependencies as effectively as desired. This is because the fixed state
      transitions constrain its ability to adapt dynamically to changes in the input data.
933

      B.2   TTT: T EST-T IME T RAINING WITH DYNAMIC H IDDEN S TATES
936
      On the other hand, Test-Time Training (TTT) introduces a more flexible mechanism for updating
937   hidden states, enabling it to better capture long-range dependencies. TTT uses a trainable hidden
938   state that is continuously updated at test time, allowing the model to adapt dynamically to the current
939   input. The hidden state update rule for TTT can be defined as:
940
941
                                 zt = f (xt ; Wt ),   Wt = Wt−1 − η∇ℓ(Wt−1 ; xt ),
942

944

946            • Wt is the weight matrix at time step t, dynamically updated during test time.
947
               • ℓ(W ; xt ) is the loss function, typically computed as the difference between the predicted
948

949

952   relying on fixed state transitions, TTT continuously adapts its parameters based on new input data
953   at test time. This enables TTT to have an infinite context window, as it can effectively adjust its
954   internal representation based on all past data and current input. This dynamic adaptability makes TTT
955   particularly suitable for LTSF tasks, where capturing long-term dependencies is crucial for accurate
956   forecasting.

959
960   One of the major benefits of both Mamba and TTT is their linear complexity with respect to sequence
961   length. Both models avoid the quadratic complexity of Transformer-based architectures, making them

973    dynamically. By continuously updating the hidden states based on both past and present data, TTT
974    effectively functions with an infinite context window, whereas Mamba is constrained by its fixed
975    state-space formulation. Moreover, TTT is shown to be theoretically equivalent to self-attention
976    under certain conditions, meaning it can model interactions between distant time steps in a similar
977    way to Transformers but with the added benefit of linear complexity. This makes TTT not only
978    computationally efficient but also highly adaptable to the long-term dependencies present in time

980    In summary, while Mamba provides significant improvements over traditional RNNs and Transformer-
981    based models, its reliance on fixed state transitions limits its effectiveness in modeling long-term

983    is better suited for Long-Term Time Series Forecasting (LTSF) tasks. TTT’s ability to adapt its
