009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper tackles the problem of video question answering (VideoQA), a task
012            that often requires multi-step reasoning and a profound understanding of spatial-
013            temporal dynamics. While large generative video-language models perform well
014            on benchmarks, they often lack explainability and spatial-temporal grounding. In
015            this paper, we propose Agent-of-Thoughts Distillation (AoTD), a method that
016
               enhances generative models by incorporating automatically generated Chain-of-
               Thoughts (CoTs) into the instruction-tuning process. Specifically, we leverage an
017
               agent-based system to decompose complex questions into sub-tasks, and address
018
               them with specialized vision models, the intermediate results are then treated as
019            reasoning chains. We also introduce a verification mechanism using a large lan-
020            guage model (LLM) to ensure the reliability of generated CoTs. Extensive exper-
021            iments demonstrate that AoTD improves the performance on multiple-choice and
022            open-ended benchmarks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Video Question Answering (VideoQA) is a critical task in the computer vision community, offering
      a natural interface for human-machine interaction through language (Yu et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2021;
028
      Xiao et al., 2021; Pătrăucean et al., 2023). This synergy of visual content and language enhances the
029
      accessibility of AI systems for the general public, allowing users to query complex visual content
030   with everyday language. By encompassing tasks such as action recognition, object detection, and
031   scene understanding, VideoQA serves as a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating AI’s ability to
032   interpret videos, addressing the fundamental questions of ‘who,’ ‘what,’ ‘when,’ and ‘where’ that are
033   crucial to understand daily life activities, pushing the boundaries of what AI systems can interpret
034   from dynamic visual content.
035
      Recent literature in VideoQA has highlighted two key directions. The first focuses on training large
036   generative video-language models (Video-LLMs) through direct instruction-tuning, where videos
037   are only paired with questions and answers (Alayrac et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2024; Maaz et al., 2024;
038   Cheng et al., 2023). While these models have shown exceptional performance on public bench-
039   marks, they often lack explainability and struggle with spatio-temporal grounding. This limitation
040   hinders their ability to provide clear reasoning, which is essential for real-world applications where
041   transparency and interpretability are critical (Mitra et al., 2023).
042   In contrast, an emerging approach focuses on agent-based systems (Surı́s et al., 2023; Gupta & Kem-
043   bhavi, 2023; Hu et al., 2024b), which break down complex questions into simpler sub-tasks. Each
044   sub-task is handled by specialized tools, and the results are aggregated to generate a final answer.
045   This approach theoretically offers greater interpretability, as the reasoning process is divided into ex-
046   plainable steps that can be independently assessed. However, our experiments indicate that current
047   video understanding tools are not strong enough for building reliable agent-based systems. Addi-
048   tionally, the high memory demands and time-consuming nature of these systems present significant
049   challenges for their practical use.
050   In this paper, we propose enhancing the capabilities of large generative video-language models by in-
051   corporating automatically generated Chain-of-Thoughts (CoTs) into the instruction-tuning process.
052   Our approach draws inspiration from agent-based systems, which break down complex questions
053   into a sequence of sub-tasks, each handled by specialized models (Fan et al., 2024; Mahmood et al.,
      2024; Min et al., 2024). We use the outputs from these specialized models to construct CoTs that

      To ensure the reliability of the constructed CoTs, we systematically evaluate existing models and
      tools for atomic video understanding tasks, such as action recognition (Weng et al., 2023; Wang
082
      et al., 2024) and language grounding (Lin et al., 2023), using a well-annotated dataset. This allows
083
      us to identify the best-performing tools for each sub-task, preparing for effective CoTs distillation.
084   This process also serves as an evaluation of the broader capabilities of visual models in more gen-
085   eral and complex scenes, offering guidance for future exploration in the computer vision community.
086   Additionally, we introduce a verification mechanism with a large language model (LLM), to assess
087   whether the generated CoTs follow a clear, step-by-step reasoning process and contain useful infor-
088   mation for answering the question. This filters out low-quality or logically inconsistent reasoning
089   paths. The verified, high-quality CoTs are then distilled into large generative video-language mod-
090   els, enhancing both performance and the interpretability of their outputs. By combining the strengths
091   of both approaches, our method balances performance with transparency, leading to the development
092
      of more robust, accurate, and interpretable VideoQA systems.
093   In summary, our contributions are three-fold: First, we propose a novel approach for enhancing
094   large generative video-language models (Video-LLMs) by distilling high-quality Chain-of-Thoughts
095   (CoTs) into their instruction tuning. These CoTs capture step-by-step reasoning paths, improving
096   both the model’s performance and its interpretability; Second, to automatically construct the CoTs
097   for any datasets, we employ an agent-based system to decompose complex VideoQA questions into
098
      simpler sub-tasks, leveraging off-the-shelf vision models to handle each sub-task. The intermedi-
      ate outputs from these models can therefore be collected as CoTs for addressing the corresponding
099
      visual question; Third, we demonstrate through extensive experiments that our distilled model out-
100
      performs existing methods across both multiple-choice and open-ended VideoQA benchmarks, en-
101   abling to deliver not only accurate answers but also clear and comprehensive reasoning explanations.

141   2.1     P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
142
143   Given a video clip with t frames, V = {x1 , . . . , xt }, and a set of n questions Q = {q1 , q2 , ..., qn },
144
      our goal is to train a Video-LLM capable of producing both concise answers and comprehensive
      rationales. Depending on the suffix prompt ps , the model can generate different types of outputs.
145
      The process can be formulated as:
146
147                            {ai , Si } = Φ(V, qi , ps ),                    where Si = {∅} or {si,1 , si,2 , . . . , si,k }
148   where qi denotes the i-th question, ai is the answer in free-form text, and Si represents the rationale,
149   consisting of the multi-step reasoning process. If the prompt specifies to only generate the answer,
150   Si = {∅}. Otherwise, if the prompt requires the generation of rationales, Si = {si,1 , si,2 , . . . , si,k },
151   where each si,j corresponds to a reasoning step.
152
      Discussion. Unlike existing models that are typically instruction-tuned on VideoQA datasets using
153
      simple question-answer pairs, which bypass the intermediate thought process, our approach em-
154   phasizes the importance of training with rationales, or chain-of-thoughts (CoTs). In the following
155   section, we outline the process for generating high-quality CoTs from existing VideoQA datasets.
156

      Agent-based VideoQA. Given a video input (V), questions (Q), and a set of visual models (M =
      {ϕact , ϕdet , . . . , ϕqa }), an LLM-based agent core (π(·)) processes the question along with the docu-
191
      mentation of the visual models (T ), which includes variables and functionalities. The agent then
192
      decomposes the question into sub-tasks and addresses them by invoking the corresponding visual
193   models. It is important to note that the visual models can be arranged in various orders depending
194   on the specific question, ensuring flexibility in problem-solving.
195
196

197
      specialized models are then executed sequentially to address these sub-tasks, ultimately yielding the
198
      final answer:

      2.3   C OT S V ERIFICATION
249
250   To further refine the quality of reasoning chains for VideoQA samples, we implement a two-step
251   verification process: (i) we filter execution traces to retain only those where the program can reach
252   correct output. For multiple-choice datasets, the output must match the correct answer exactly,
253   while for open-ended datasets, we prompt the LLM to verify correctness, accounting for format dif-
254   ferences; (ii) we prompt the LLM to evaluate the logical coherence and usefulness of the reasoning
255   chains in solving the problem. The model assesses whether the CoTs follow a clear, step-by-step
256
      reasoning process and provides a binary evaluation (‘Yes’ or ‘No’) to indicate their quality (de-
      tailed prompts can be found in Appendix C). This two-step approach ensures that only accurate and
257
      high-quality CoTs are utilized for further distillation into the model.
258
259   After filtering, we provide the statistics for the generated CoTs on different datasets in Table 2. We
260   primarily select compositional QA datasets, as these require the model to process spatial-temporal
261   information from different events comprehensively.

438   To verify whether the model has effectively learned multi-step reasoning through CoTs distillation,
439   we analyze the rationales generated by the model. Specifically, we extract and evaluate the temporal
440   and spatial information embedded within these rationales. This approach extends beyond merely
441   assessing the correctness of the final answer, which could be influenced by biases or other external
442   factors. By examining the reasoning process in detail, we gain a more accurate understanding of the
443   model’s ability to perceive and reason about spatial and temporal relationships.
444   Evaluation protocols. We randomly select 200 samples from the STAR validation set and perform
445   inference on this subset using the suffix prompt, recording the generated rationales. From these
446   rationales, we extract the predicted temporal windows and bounding boxes, comparing them to the
447   ground truth. For the spatial evaluation, we calculate the IoU between the predicted and ground
448   truth bounding boxes. For the temporal evaluation, we compute both IoU and Recall, leveraging the
449   frame-level scene graph annotations provided in the STAR dataset.

      4     R ELATED W ORK
470
471
      Video-language models (Video-LLMs). Most existing Video-LLMs are composed of a pre-trained
      visual encoder(like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) or SigLIP (Zhai et al., 2023)) to encode video frames
472
      into a sequence of visual features, an adapter to transfer the visual features to tokens which can be
473
      understood by the language model, and a pre-trained LLM to output the final response. These mod-
474   els achieve strong ability for general vision-language tasks like Video question-answering (think
475   the task as auto-regressive generation with question as prompt prefix). More recent works such as
476   VideoLLaMA2 (Cheng et al., 2024), LLaVA-NeXT-Video (Zhang et al., 2024) and Videochat2 (Li
477   et al., 2024b), with their excellent architecture design and reasonable instruction tuning data collec-
478   tion, have achieved a new level of zero-shot results in Video QA task. However, current end-to-end
479   models still lack of interpretability for questions, as well as the ability to think and visually process
480   complex problems in multiple steps, leads to their weakness in real complex scenarios, which is an
481   important part for embodied learning and autonomous driving.
482   Visual Programing and Agents. With the progress of LLMs, some recent works (Gupta & Kemb-
483   havi, 2023; Surı́s et al., 2023) begin to try to use LLM as planner to solve the complex reasoning task
484   in real scenarios. They attempt to decompose the question into some easier sub-questions, and use
485   different specialist models as agents to solve these sub-questions, and finally gather them to get the
      answer of the raw question. MoReVQA (Min et al., 2024) proposes a multi-stage system, consisting


                                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      of an event parser, a grounding module, and a reasoning module with an external memory, getting a
487   strong zero-shot Video QA ability while is able to create interpretable intermediate outputs. VURF
488   (Mahmood et al., 2024) proposes a self-refinement method to resolve the LLM hallucinations to get
489   a more concise program based on the context cues. These models demonstrate a strong ability to
490   obtain trustworthy answers based on the intermediate evidence they get, but they lag far behind the
491   end to end model in terms of inference speed, and often require some in-context examples to assist
492   them in solving problems, which undoubtedly brings a lot of trouble to the use of these agent-based
493   models.
494   Chain-of-Thought (CoT). Recent advancements in Chain-of-Thought (Wei et al., 2022; Yao et al.,
495   2024) have made significant improvements in boosting the capabilities of LLMs. Though there have
496   been several works enhancing the power of LLMs through distilling the CoT into the model, we still
497   note a lack of research focused on applying CoT to video scenarios, as videos often have complex
498   spatio-temporal relationships, and multi-step thinking is needed to solve the problems happened in
499   these scenes. MotionEpic (Fei et al., 2024) develops a Video-of-Thought reasoning framework by
500   integrating video spatial-temporal scene graph. But it requires explicit training on the graph encoder,
501
      which needs additional graph data, and cannot be directly migrated to other common Video-LLMs.
      Thus, we construct natural language CoTs which are involved with spatial-temporal information to
502
      adapt to any different models.
503
504   Visual CoT. The potential of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning extends beyond NLP to the visual
505   domain. Several studies (Zhang et al., 2023; Mitra et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024b)
506   have applied CoT to visual understanding tasks, using powerful MLLMs for CoT generation or tool-
507
      based architectures for step-by-step problem solving. However, these methods face limitations, such
      as errors in CoT generation by MLLMs or high time and memory costs for tool-based approaches.
508
      Recent works like Visual Program Distillation (VPD) (Hu et al., 2024a) and Fact (Gao et al., 2024a)
509
      aim to maintain CoT accuracy and diversity while leveraging MLLMs to directly generate CoTs.
510   These methods decompose complex tasks through code programs, invoking expert models to ad-
511   dress sub-tasks, and use the generated CoTs as training data for fine-tuning visual-language models,
512   thereby improving the model’s ability to generate rationales directly. While all these methods focus
513   on image-based domains, they overlook the video domain, where CoT is especially suitable due
514   to the complex spatio-temporal nature of video understanding tasks. To bridge this gap, we pro-
515   pose AoTD, a method inspired by VPD and Fact, tailored to the video domain. Video-STaR (Zohar
516   et al., 2024) also constructs CoTs using videos and existing labels for instruction tuning, without
517   developing an agent-based system.
518
519   5   C ONCLUSION & L IMITATION
520
521   In this work, we present Agent-of-Thought Distillation (AoTD), a novel approach aimed at distill-
522   ing multi-step reasoning and spatial-temporal understanding into a large generative video-language
523   model (Video-LLM). Our method introduces an agent-based system that automates the generation of
524   Chain-of-Thoughts (CoTs) from various Video Question Answering (VideoQA) datasets by break-
525   ing down complex questions into manageable sub-tasks that can be addressed by specialized vision
526   models. Extensive experiments validate that the distilled model significantly enhances performance
527
      on both Multiple-Choice (MC-VQA) and Open-Ended VQA (OE-VQA) benchmarks, underscoring
      the effectiveness of our approach.
528
529   Despite these advancements, several limitations remain and we leave them as future work: (i) Simi-
530   lar to prior approaches, the effectiveness of our agent-based system is contingent upon the progress
531   of the underlying visual model components. Enhancing its ability to generalize across diverse
532   datasets is essential for broader applicability. (ii) While our primary focus has been on compositional
533
      VideoQA tasks, and we have demonstrated improvements across a series of benchmarks, achieving
      holistic enhancements will require further exploration into creating a more balanced distribution of
534
      training data. (iii) Furthermore, our agent-based framework has the potential to address additional
535
      video-related tasks, such as video captioning and referring segmentation. We aim to expand our
536   methodology to these domains, which could yield even more robust and versatile applications in the
537   future. Overall, we believe AoTD represents a promising future direction for advancing multimodal
538   reasoning abilities in Video-LLMs.

      A.1   T RAINING D ETAILS
759
760   For all models, their projection layers and language model are finetuned and visual encoder is frozen.
761   We use a cosine learning rate schedule, with warm up ratio 0.03 and learning rate 4e-5. For both
762   Instruct and AoTD setting, we finetune the model with batch size 48 and totally 1 epoch. We believe
763   that longer training will get a better performance on in-domain benchmarks but maybe a destroy on
764   out-of-domain benchmarks.
765
766   A.2   S PECIALIZED M ODELS E VALUATION D ETAILS
767
      In this section we will show the details about each sub-task’s evaluation from data preparation to
768
      evaluation metric.
769
770
      Question Decomposition. Since there may be multiple valid ways to decompose the same prob-
771
      lem, we evaluate only the accuracy of the final output in this sub-task. Specifically, the model takes
772   the query and instruction as input and generates an executable program. We replace all intermediate
773   outputs within the program and focus on whether the final output matches the correct answer. If the
774   decomposition is correct, the final output must align with the answer. Any programs that cannot be
775   executed or that lead to an incorrect answer are considered failures.
776
777   Object Detection. To evaluate the performance of detection models, we sample frames with scene
778   graph annotations from the input video clip and provide them, along with the text query, as input to
779   the model. The model then outputs a series of bounding boxes that exceed a confidence threshold.
780   We select the bounding box with the highest confidence as the final output and calculate the IoU to
781   assess accuracy.
782
783
      Temporal Grounding. Since scene graphs provide both the start and end frame IDs, as well as
      key frame IDs for each event, we use IoU and Recall as metrics to capture different aspects of model
784
      performance. The model takes the video clip and text query as input and outputs the predicted start
785
      and end frame IDs. We calculate IoU based on the alignment between the predicted and annotated
786   start and end frame IDs, and we compute Recall using the key frame ID annotations to evaluate how
787   well the model captures important frames.
788
789   Action Recognition. For discriminative models, we provide the video clip and a list of action
790   labels as input to complete a classification task. For generative models, we provide the video clip
791   along with an instruction prompt, asking the model to generate five actions most relevant to the
792   video, ranked by likelihood. We then use the top-ranked output from each model to calculate the
793   Top-1 accuracy for both approaches.
794
795   Question Answering. The evaluation of question answering follows a similar approach to previ-
796   ous methods. The model takes the video clip and question as input and returns an answer, from
797   which we directly calculate the accuracy. The key difference between this sub-task and a standard
798
      QA task is that the answers are based on a series of information collected by preceding agents,
      allowing for a more accurate assessment of the model’s pure question-answering ability.
