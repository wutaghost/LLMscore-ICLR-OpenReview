009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This work explores stochastic adaptive gradient descent, i.e., stochastic AdaGrad-
012            Norm, when applied to linearly separable datasets. For the stochastic AdaGrad-
013            Norm method equipped with a wide range of sampling noise, we demonstrate
014            its almost surely convergence result to the L2 max-margin solution. This means
015            that stochastic AdaGrad-Norm has an implicit bias that yields good generaliza-
016
               tion, even without regularization terms. We show that the convergence rate of
017
               the classification direction is o(1/ln(1‚ąíŌĶ)/2 n). Our approach takes a novel stance
               by explicitly characterizing the L2 max-margin direction. By doing so, we over-
018
               come the challenge that arises from the dependency between the stepsize and the
019
               gradient and also address the limitations in the previous AdaGrad-Norm analyses.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   With the growth of computing power in recent years, various models like neural networks have
025   gained the ability to perfectly fit training data. These models have a capacity that exceeds the data‚Äôs
026   capacity, which are referred to as over-parametrized models. Over-parametrized models often ex-
027   hibit numerous global optimums, yielding a zero training loss, yet exhibiting substantial disparities
028
      in test performance (Wu et al., 2018; Chatterji et al., 2022). Fascinatingly, investigations have indi-
      cated that optimization algorithms tend to converge towards those optimal points associated with a
029
      good generalization (Zhang et al., 2021). This intriguing phenomenon is referred to as the implicit
030
      bias of optimizers and is widely speculated to exist (Neyshabur et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2005;
031   Keskar et al., 2017; Wilson et al., 2017).
032
033   Evidence of implicit bias has been established under different settings. For the linear classification
034
      task with cross-entropy loss, Soudry et al. (2018) demonstrate that gradient descent (GD) converges
      to the L2 max-margin solution. This solution is also commonly known as the hard support vector
035
      machine (hard-margin SVM) solution. This revelation underscores that even fundamental optimiz-
036
      ers like GD have an implicit bias. Subsequent endeavors have extended their work, adapting GD into
037   stochastic gradient descent (SGD), momentum-based SGD (mSGD), and deterministic adaptive di-
038   agonal gradient descent (AdaGrad-Diagonal) (Gunasekar et al. (2018); Qian & Qian (2019); Wang
039   et al. (2021b;a); Wu et al. (2021)). In recent years, as a varient of deterministic AdaGrad-Diagonal,
040   stochastic AdaGrad-Norm has been a major focus in the theoretical community (Faw et al. (2022);
041   Wang et al. (2023); Jin et al. (2022)). The iterates generated by the stochastic AdaGrad-Norm
042   method enjoy the following dynamics (see Streeter & Mcmahan (2010); Ward et al. (2020)):

089   Related works There are several works that is related to this topic. For example, Soudry et al.
090   (2018) prove that GD converges to the L2 max-margin solution for linear classification tasks with
091   exponential-tailed loss. In Nacson et al. (2019), authors mainly extended the form of the loss from
092   the work of Soudry et al. (2018), and considered the case of linear neural networks. For SGD and
093   momentum-based SGD, Wang et al. (2021a) prove the convergence to the L2 max-margin solution
094   for linear classification task with exponential-tailed loss and mini-batch stochastic gradient.
095   For deterministic AdaGrad-Diagonal, Soudry et al. (2018); Gunasekar et al. (2018); Qian & Qian
096   (2019) claim that it does not converge to the L2 max-margin solution as the non-adaptive methods
097   do (e.g. SGD, GD). Instead, for stochastic AdaGrad-Norm, Jin et al. (2022) presents the last-iterate
098   convergence. Wang et al. (2023) and Faw et al. (2022) obtained the convergence rates of stochas-
099   tic AdaGrad-Norm. The characterization of the converging point (like implicit bias) of stochastic
100   AdaGrad-Norm remains unknown.

105
      hard support vector machine (see Theorems 4.2 and 4.3).

472   6                          C ONCLUSION
473
474   This work focuses on the convergence analysis of the stochastic AdaGrad-Norm method, a variant
475   of the AdaGrad method, with linearly separable data sets. While previous perspectives often sug-
476   gest that AdaGrad‚Äôs convergence might hinge on initialization and step size, our findings present a
477   contrasting view. Specifically, we establish that stochastic AdaGrad-Norm exhibits an implicit bias,
478   consistently converging towards the L2 max-margin solution, even without regularization terms.
479   Furthermore, we present the convergence rates for the L2 max-margin solution, offering compre-
480
      hensive insights into the algorithm‚Äôs convergence dynamics.

      A    I MPACT S TATEMENTS
595
596
      This paper presents work whose goal is to advance the field of machine learning and optimization
597   methods. We do not see immediate societal impact. There are potential indirect societal conse-
598   quences of our work, but we do not foresee them at the moment.
599
600
      From the technical standpoint, our study overcomes a key challenge arising from the dependency
      between step size and gradient. The challenge is solved by intricately examining the direction re-
601
      cursion of the optimization variable, enabling us to disentangle these dependencies. Moreover, we
602
      tackle extra radial noise through insightful geometric transformations. By harnessing a blend of mar-
603   tingale theory and geometric reasoning, we provide a new way to show the convergence of the L2
604   max-margin direction. Notably, the techniques developed in this study extend beyond the boundaries
605   of the stochastic AdaGrad-Norm method. The techniques are also valuable in analyzing the conver-
606   gence behavior of other stochastic methods, such as Adam, and in gaining a deeper understanding
607   of neural networks.
