011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advances in parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods, such as Low Rank
014            Adaptation (LoRA), have gained significant attention for their ability to effi-
015            ciently adapt large foundational models to various downstream tasks. These meth-
016
               ods are appreciated for achieving performance comparable to full fine-tuning on
               aggregate-level metrics, while significantly reducing computational costs. To sys-
017
               tematically address fairness in LLMs previous studies fine-tune on fairness spe-
018
               cific data using a larger LoRA rank than typically used. In this paper, we introduce
019            FairLoRA, a novel fairness-specific regularizer for LoRA aimed at reducing per-
020            formance disparities across data subgroups by minimizing per-class variance in
021            loss. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to introduce a fairness based
022            finetuning through LoRA. Our results demonstrate that the need for higher ranks to
023            mitigate bias is not universal; it depends on factors such as the pre-trained model,
024            dataset, and task. More importantly, we systematically evaluate FairLoRA across
025            various vision models, including ViT, DiNO, and CLIP, in scenarios involving
026            distribution shifts. We further emphasize the necessity of using multiple fairness
027
               metrics to obtain a holistic assessment of fairness, rather than relying solely on the
               metric optimized during training.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The advent of foundational models Bommasani et al. (2021) has led to the widespread adoption
      of parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods such as Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) Hu et al.
034
      (2021), enabling efficient adaptation to various downstream tasks. These methods offer significant
035
      computational advantages and often achieve performance comparable to full fine-tuning (FFT) of
036   the entire model on aggregate metrics Hu et al. (2021); Zhao et al. (2024); Dettmers et al. (2024).
037   However, their impact on fairness remains under-explored. More importantly, given the widespread
038   use of LoRA for fine-tuning foundational models, the uncertainty regarding the impacts on fairness
039   as well as bias mitigation, complicates deployment and raises ethical concerns, emphasizing the
040   need to measure and mitigate disparate impacts.
041
      Recent works on Fairness with PEFT has focused on either (i) finding the right parameters to tune
042   through heuristic search algorithms Dutt et al. (2023) or (ii) fine-tuning on specific datasets curated
043   for fairness Das et al. (2024). As noted in the papers, finding the right set of parameters is a challeng-
044   ing and often a computationally expensive problem, thereby contradicting the major advantage of
045   using PEFT. Although effective, fine-tuning with fairness specific datasets can also be challenging
046   given the complexities involved in data collection, curating, and labelling. Furthermore, it is also
047   important to note that in order to mitigate bias based on different notions, we might need completely
048   different datasets– thereby making it hard to scale.
049   The fairness community in ML has often argued the importance of the ‘right metric’ to measure un-
050   fairness and how that changes the answer to the question “Does X have disparate impact” Hashem-
051   izadeh et al. (2023); Simson et al. (2024). Our work emphasizes the importance of evaluating mul-
052   tiple fairness metrics rather than relying on a single measure. By considering metrics such as aggre-
053   gate accuracy, minimum and median F1 scores across groups, and performance disparities between
      groups, we aim to capture a holistic view of both performance and fairness. This comprehensive


                                                          1
054
      evaluation allows us to assess whether PEFT methods like LoRA consistently meet fairness stan-
055   dards or may lead to adverse outcomes in certain configurations.
056
057   We introduce FairLoRA, a novel fairness based LoRA that scales across models and datasets. Our
058
      findings indicate that FairLoRA performs comparable to or better than using fairness specific regu-
      larization with FFT, across most metrics. Our method is an in-processing bias mitigation method that
059
      aims at altering the learning objective with almost zero addition in computational cost, as opposed
060
      to heuristic search based fair finetuning.

104   2   R ELATED W ORKS
105
106   PEFT: LoRA stands out among PEFT methods due to its efficiency and simplicity in fine-tuning
107   large models. Unlike adapters that add new layers, LoRA injects low-rank matrices directly into the
      weight updates of pre-trained models, keeping the number of parameters the same as the original


                                                       2
108
      model (for inference). This makes LoRA easy to implement with significantly lower memory and
109   computational costs while training, and constant deployment cost compared to the original model,
110   all while maintaining performance comparable to full fine-tuning (FFT). Our work measures the
111   disparate impact of PEFT in vision models and proposes a novel way to mitigate bias.
112
      LoRA and Fairness: Recent studies on Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) highlight key trade-offs
113
      between fairness, safety and performance Das et al. (2024); Ding et al. (2024). Although the fairness
114
      impact of LoRA depends heavily on the base model and rank (Ding et al., 2024) did not notice any
115   systemic disparate impact. In terms of bias mitigation, as the rank increases, LoRA’s performance
116   and fairness become comparable to full fine-tuning (FFT) Das et al. (2024). Similarly, (Dutt et al.,
117   2023) proposed a fairness aware PEFT by heuristically searching for the right set of parameters to
118   update. Our work is different from the above mentioned because (i) we analyze the disparate impact
119   across datasets with distribution shifts, a challenging problem for LoRA Lermen et al. (2023), (ii)
120   we focus the bias mitigation on vision and Vision language models while the previous work Das
121   et al. (2024) focuses on LLMs and finally, (iii) our work focuses on changing the LoRA objective
122   function make it more generic compared to fine-tuning with a fairness specific dataset or performing
123
      heuristic search to find the ‘correct’ tunable parameters.
124   Fairness in vision models: Independent of PEFT, fairness in machine learning models is a well
125   studied problem (Dwork et al., 2012; Dieterich et al., 2016; Verma & Rubin, 2018; Mehrabi et al.,
126   2021; Zemel et al., 2013; Zhao & Gordon, 2022). Enforcing fairness has mainly focused on impos-
127   ing requirements such as demographic parity, equalized odds, equal opportunity (Hardt et al., 2016),
128   accuracy parity (Agarwal et al., 2018; Berk et al., 2021), or combinations of these properties (Zafar
129
      et al., 2017; Lowy et al., 2021; Bakker et al., 2020; Shui et al., 2022) through ine-tuning, penalized
      objective or constrains. Our fairness regularizer is inspired from the formulations used by Tran et al.
130
      (2022); Hashemizadeh et al. (2023) and aim to reduce the per-class variance in the loss.
131
132   Model Flexibility and Generalization: Recent work (Shwartz-Ziv et al., 2024) shows that neural
133   network architectures vary in how they fit data, potentially impacting fairness across demographic
134   groups. Architectures like CNNs and ViTs exhibit different efficiencies when adapting to new tasks,
135
      indicating that architecture plays a crucial role in a models (fairness) outcomes. Our analysis focuses
      on understanding similar trends with respect to fairness for LoRA based fine-tuning.

138   3   P RELIMINARIES : L O RA
139
140   Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) Hu et al. (2021) is a parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) method
141   that injects trainable low-rank matrices into the weight updates of a pre-trained model, significantly
142   reducing the number of trainable parameters without compromising performance. The core idea of
143   LoRA is to decompose the weight updates into two low-rank matrices, which are then trained to
144
      capture task-specific knowledge.
145   Consider a pre-trained subset of parameters ✓0 2 ⇥, where ⇥ represents the full parameter space
146   of the model. In LoRA, instead of updating ✓0 directly, the weight update ✓ is parameterized as
147   a product of two low-rank matrices A 2 Rd⇥r and B 2 Rr⇥k , where r ⌧ min(d, k). Thus, the
148   updated parameter matrix becomes:
149
150                                         ✓ = ✓0 +    ✓ = ✓0 + AB
151
152   Here, A and B are the trainable matrices, and r is the rank, controlling the parameter reduction. By
153   making r much smaller than d and k, the total number of trainable parameters is reduced from d ⇥ k
154   to r ⇥ (d + k), leading to substantial memory savings.

      4.1   I MPROVING PER CLASS PERFORMANCE
189
190   In this work we focus on accuracy parity as a notion of fairness. In order to achieve accuracy parity,
191   the aim is to have equal accuracy across all groups. This is challenging and often times impossible to
192   achieve given the quantized nature of accuracy, number of samples in a group, and difficulty of the
193   samples in each class. Considering the impracticality of directly enforcing accuracy parity, we aim
194   to use a method that still helps to improve the per group accuracy. We introduce a fairness regularizer
195   aimed to reduce the variance of per group loss at a mini-batch level, thereby implicitly improving
196   the performance of under performing group. It is important to note that a degenerate solution to this
197   problem could be by making the performance per class equal, but extremely low–this does not occur
198
      here as we still have the original objective that pushes to improve the overall performance of the
      model and we do not observe this degenerate solution in any of our experimental settings.
199

201   rameters ✓. Specifically, let the model parameters be ✓, which can represent either the entire set

203   ing on the adaptation approach used in the model. We aim to minimize the total loss function by
204
      searching over the parameter space ⇥, which includes both the full model parameters and the LoRA

221   5   M EASURING FAIRNESS
222
223   We focus on five key metrics to provide a comprehensive assessment of both the model’s perfor-
224   mance and its fairness. Here, groups refer to the classes in the dataset and sensitive groups
225   refer to the sensitive labels associated with the classes These metrics are:
226
227         1. Aggregate Evaluation Accuracy (Acc): The overall accuracy of the model across the
228
               entire dataset.
229         2. Minimum F1 Score Across Groups (ming2G F1g ): The lowest F1 score among all groups
230            G, highlighting the worst-performing group and helping to identify significant disparities.
231
            3. Minimum Recall Across Groups (ming2G Recallg ): The lowest Recall score among all
232            groups G, highlighting the worst-performing group and helping to identify significant dis-
233            parities in terms of misclassifications.
234
235
            4. Sensitive Image Accuracy (if applicable, AccSensitive ): The accuracy specifically on sen-
               sitive groups, applicable when the dataset contains sensitive labels. This metric measures
236
               any spurious correlations or privacy violation with respect to the model.
237
238         5. Difference of F1 Scores Between Worst and Best Groups ( F1 = maxg2G F1g
239            ming2G F1g ): The gap between the highest and lowest F1 scores across groups, serving
240            as an indicator of fairness by measuring performance disparity.
241

      7     D ISCUSSION
487
488   Given the ubiquitous use, it is important to develop parameter-efficient techniques that reliably mit-
489   igate fairness issues. While developing such solutions it is important to focus on (i) trade offs in
490   terms of different metrics, and compute and (ii) if the method truly generalizes
491
492   7.1   T RADE - OFFS
493
494   In our experiments, we observed trade-offs between overall performance and fairness metrics when
495   applying FairLoRA. Incorporating the fairness regularizer often led to improved performance on
496   underrepresented groups at the expense of slight reductions in aggregate accuracy. This trade-off
497
      is expected, as the regularizer aims to reduce the variance in loss across groups, thereby focusing
      the model’s learning capacity on groups that are harder to predict accurately. But it is important
498
      to note that under most settings, the aggregate accuracy improved or was comparable to that of
499
      full-finetuning.
500
501   The choice of the LoRA rank also plays a pivotal role in balancing the trade off between various
502   metrics and computational cost. However, the observations we have show that the effect of rank is
503
      not universal and would vary across models, datasets and even metrics - this is contrary to what
      was observed by Das et al. (2024).
504
505
      7.2   G ENERALIZATION
506
507   The generalization of fairness improvements across different models and datasets is a critical con-
508   sideration. Our results indicate that although useful, the effectiveness of FairLoRA in mitigating
509   fairness issues is not universal but depends on factors such as the pre-trained model architecture,
510   the nature of the pre-training data, and the specific downstream task. For instance, models such as
511   CLIP, which are pre-trained on diverse multi-modal data, may require lower LoRA ranks to achieve
512   fairness compared to models pre-trained on more homogeneous datasets.
513   Moreover, the distribution shift between pre-training and fine-tuning datasets as well as the intra-
514   class similarities within the fine-tuning dataset can influence the model’s ability to generalize fair-
515   ness improvements. Although this was hypothesized in Ding et al. (2024); Das et al. (2024), we
516   offer a more comprehensive empirical evaluation in this regard
517
518
      8     C ONCLUSION
519
520   In this work, we introduced FairLoRA, a fairness-aware LoRA approach by incorporating a fairness
521   regularizer aimed at reducing the variance of per-group loss, thereby improving performance on
522   underrepresented groups. Through comprehensive experiments across various models (CLIP, DINO,
523   ViT), datasets (Aircrafts, GeoDE, Waterbirds), and fairness metrics, we found that LoRA does not
524   introduce systemic disparate impact and FairLoRA can achieve fairness outcomes comparable to or
525   better than Fair full fine-tuning (FFT).
526   Our findings highlight the importance of evaluating multiple fairness metrics to capture a holistic
527   view of a model’s performance and fairness implications. We observed that there is no universal
528   trend with respect to LoRA ranks; the optimal rank depends on the specific model, pre-training
529   data, and task. Additionally, we examined the effects of distribution shifts between pre-training and
530   fine-tuning datasets, and notice how efficiently FairLoRA can adapt.
531
      Overall, our study demonstrates that FairLoRA is a viable and efficient alternative to FFT for miti-
532   gating fairness issues in machine learning models. Future work could extend this analysis to other
533   architectures, datasets, and definitions of fairness, as well as explore intersectional fairness. More
534   importantly, it would be interesting to study the impact of FairLoRA on image segmentation
535   tasks, where we see long-tailed distributions.
