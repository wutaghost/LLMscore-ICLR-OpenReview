009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper introduces a novel approach for optimizing Generative Flow Networks
012            (GFlowNets) in stochastic environments by incorporating KL divergence objec-
013            tives with entropy-ratio estimation. We leverage the relationship between high and
014            low entropy states, as defined in entropy-regularized Markov Decision Processes
015            (MDPs), to dynamically adjust exploration and exploitation. Detailed proofs and
016            analysis demonstrate the efficacy of this methodology in enhancing mode discov-
017            ery, state coverage, and policy robustness in complex environments.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
021
022
      Generative Flow Networks (GFlowNets) (Bengio et al., 2021a;b) have recently gained attention for
      their application in a variety of tasks, such as molecule discovery (Bengio et al., 2021a; Jain et al.,
023
      2022b), biological sequence design (Jain et al., 2022a), and robust scheduling (Zhang et al., 2023).
024
      GFlowNets learn policies that generate objects x ∈ X sequentially, where the generation process is
025   similar to Monte-Carlo Markov chain (MCMC) methods (Metropolis et al., 1953; Hastings, 1970;
026   Andrieu et al., 2003), generative models (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Ho et al., 2020), and amortized
027   variational inference (Kingma & Welling, 2013). This sequential process of generating objects
028   through a policy also closely resembles reinforcement learning (RL) (Sutton & Barto, 2018).

      2   BACKGROUND
031
032
      Generative Flow Networks (GFlowNets) are variational inference algorithms designed to treat sam-
033
      pling from a target probability distribution as a sequential decision-making process (Bengio et al.
034   (2021a;b)). Below, we briefly summarize the formulation and primary training algorithms for
035   GFlowNets. Consider a fully observed, deterministic Markov Decision Process (MDP) with a state
036   space S and a set of actions A ⊆ S × S. The MDP has a designated initial state s0 , and cer-
037   tain states, called terminal states, are designated as having no outgoing actions. Let X denote the
038   set of terminal states. We assume that all states in S are reachable from s0 through a sequence
039   of actions, though not necessarily by a unique sequence. A complete trajectory is a sequence
040   of states τ = (s0 → s1 → · · · → sn ), where sn ∈ X , and each pair of consecutive states
041   is connected by an action, i.e., ∀i (si , si+1 ) ∈ A. A policy in this MDP defines a distribution
042
      PF (s′ |s) for each non-terminal state s ∈ S \ X , specifying the probability of transitioning to the
      next state s′ in a single action. The policy induces a distribution over complete trajectories as fol-

      lows: PF (s0 → s1 → · · · → sn ) = i=0 PF (si+1 | si ). The marginal distribution over terminal

050
051   Unlike Reinforcement Learning (RL), where the goal is typically to maximize the expected reward
052   by learning a deterministic policy (Mnih et al., 2015; Lillicrap et al., 2015; Haarnoja et al., 2017; Fu-
053   jimoto et al., 2018; Haarnoja et al., 2018), GFlowNets aim to learn a stochastic policy for generating
      composite objects x with probability proportional to the reward function R(x). This is particularly

      useful in real-world tasks where diversity in solutions is crucial, such as recommender systems (Ku-
055   naver & Požrl, 2017), drug discovery (Bengio et al., 2021a; Jain et al., 2022a), and sampling causal
056   models from a Bayesian posterior (Deleu et al., 2022). However, existing GFlowNet approaches
057   (Bengio et al., 2021a; Malkin et al., 2022; Madan et al., 2022) have primarily been developed for
058   deterministic environments, where state transitions are fixed. In real-world applications, stochastic-
059   ity in state transitions is common, presenting significant challenges for GFlowNets. Deterministic
060   GFlowNet methods can fail to model the correct state visitation distribution under stochastic transi-
061   tions. For instance, in the presence of stochastic dynamics, standard GFlowNets may learn incorrect
062   probabilities for visiting states, which do not align with the ideal distribution. This mismatch occurs
063
      because existing methods do not properly account for randomness in state transitions. To address
      these limitations, we propose a novel approach called KL Divergence Optimization with Entropy-
064
      Ratio Estimation for Stochastic GFlowNets. Our method introduces a KL divergence objective
065
      that optimizes the policy distribution while incorporating an entropy-ratio estimation mechanism
066   that dynamically balances exploration and exploitation. By adjusting the exploration-exploitation
067   trade-off through entropy-ratio estimation, our method enables GFlowNets to capture the correct
068   state visitation distribution, even in stochastic environments.
069
      Our approach is general and can be applied to different GFlowNet learning objectives. It works
070
      by minimizing the divergence between forward and backward policies, ensuring flow consistency
071
      across stochastic transitions. The entropy-ratio estimation further enhances robustness by favoring
072   high-entropy states in situations where the environment exhibits higher stochasticity. This approach
073   allows for better mode discovery and improves state visitation coverage in stochastic tasks, such as
074   molecule discovery, biological sequence generation, and other structured object generation tasks.
075
      Our contributions of this paper are as follows:
076
077          • We propose KL Divergence Optimization with Entropy-Ratio Estimation for Stochas-
078            tic GFlowNets, a novel approach that addresses the limitations of existing GFlowNet meth-
079            ods in stochastic environments.
080          • We provide a detailed analysis of how our method optimizes the flow consistency and
081            dynamically adjusts exploration in stochastic transitions, making it suitable for a wide range
082            of stochastic tasks.
083          • We conduct extensive experiments on benchmark tasks, demonstrating that our method sig-
084            nificantly outperforms existing baselines, including Stochastic GFlowNets (SGFN), PPO,
085            SAC and MCMC particularly in complex environments like biological sequence genera-
086            tion.

091   forward and backward policies to maintain the desired state distribution. In stochastic GFlowNets,
092   DB must accommodate the randomness inherent in state transitions, which is crucial for accurately
093   representing the distribution over states under varying conditions.

267
268   The stochasticity level is controlled by the parameter α, which represents the probability of taking
269   random actions. A higher value of α indicates more random (exploratory) behavior, while a lower
      α implies more deterministic (exploitative) behavior. When α is high, the system favors exploratory

270
      actions, which means that the entropy H(s) of state s tends to be higher, affecting Hhigh (s) and
271   Hlow (s) accordingly. Since H(s) is high, Hhigh (s) will have a moderate to low value due to the
272   negative exponential term, whereas Hlow (s) will be comparatively smaller. Consequently, rγ (s)
273   will be influenced more by the high-entropy component, encouraging diverse state visitation. Con-
274   versely, when α is low, the policy is more deterministic, leading to lower entropy in state visitation.
275   In this case, H(s) is low, which increases Hhigh (s) and Hlow (s), but the low-entropy component
276   Hlow (s) has a greater influence. The ratio rγ (s) will thus emphasize exploitation, focusing more
277   on high-reward, low-entropy states. The parameter γ is updated dynamically in Algorithm 1 to bal-
278   ance exploration and exploitation, depending on the observed variance in reward distributions. The
279
      adaptive update rule is given by:
280
281
282                               γt+1 = γt + η(Var(R(st+1 )) − Var(R(st )))
283
284
285   where η is the learning rate controlling the adjustment step, and Var(R(st )) and Var(R(st+1 )) are
286   the reward variances observed before and after transitioning from state st to state st+1 . The dynamic
287   adjustment of γ is directly influenced by the change in reward variance across state transitions, which
288
      reflects the level of uncertainty or stochasticity present in the environment.
289   When the variance in rewards is high between two successive states (Var(R(st+1 )) > Var(R(st ))),
290   this implies a high level of uncertainty in the environment. In this case, the update rule for γ will
291   increase γ. This increases the weight on Hhigh (s) in rγ (s), effectively promoting exploration to
292   gather more information about the uncertain environment. As γ increases, rγ (s) is driven more by
293   the high-entropy component, resulting in more diverse state visitation and potentially discovering
294
      novel, high-reward paths.
295   If the reward variance is low (Var(R(st+1 )) < Var(R(st ))), this implies that the environment is
296   more predictable, and the agent is starting to identify stable, rewarding actions. Consequently, the
297   update rule will decrease γ, which reduces the weight on Hhigh (s) and increases the influence of
298   Hlow (s), making rγ (s) emphasize low-entropy states. This results in more exploitative behavior,
299   allowing the agent to take advantage of known high-reward actions and converge to a stable, deter-
300
      ministic policy.
301   The combined effect of α and γ on rγ (s) can be understood as follows. When both α and γ are high,
302   the agent strongly emphasizes exploration. A high α leads to more random actions, while a high
303   γ results in rγ (s) favoring high-entropy states. Together, this results in a broad exploration of the
304   state space, potentially identifying diverse high-reward regions. Conversely, when α and γ are both
305   low, the agent’s behavior becomes more deterministic and exploitative. The low value of α leads
306
      to fewer random actions, while a lower γ results in rγ (s) putting greater emphasis on low-entropy
      states, leading to stable exploitation of known rewards.
307
308   The dynamic adjustment of γ enables the model to be adaptive based on the stochasticity of the en-
309   vironment. During early stages of training, when the environment is largely unexplored and reward
310   variance is high, γ increases, promoting exploration. As training progresses, if reward variance de-
311   creases, suggesting the agent has identified promising regions of the state space, γ decreases, and
312
      the model shifts towards exploitation. This approach ensures a balanced and adaptive exploration-
      exploitation strategy tailored to the dynamics of the environment. By using the dynamic variance-
313
      based adjustment, the agent can systematically adapt γ to improve learning efficiency and reward
314
      maximization.

      7.2     B IOLOGICAL S EQUENCE G ENERATION
398
399   In biological sequence generation, the objective is to discover sequences with optimal properties by
400   maximizing a reward function corresponding to specific biological traits. This task is particularly
401   challenging due to the inherent complexity and stochasticity present in biological environments. For
402   example, in the task of generating DNA sequences, the objective might be to find sequences that
403   exhibit high binding affinity to a particular transcription factor.
404   To demonstrate the efficacy of our proposed method, we evaluate it on the TFBind8 task, where the
405   goal is to generate strings of nucleotides (e.g., DNA sequences of length 8). Conventionally, such
406   tasks are modeled using an autoregressive Markov Decision Process (MDP). However, we utilize a
407   prepend-append MDP (PA-MDP), where actions involve adding tokens (e.g., nucleotides) either to
408   the beginning or the end of a partial sequence. The reward function, in this case, measures the DNA
409   binding affinity to a human transcription factor, providing feedback on the sequence’s fitness.
410   The complexity of the biological sequence generation problem lies in handling the vast combi-
411   natorial search space and the stochastic nature of sequence interactions with biological targets. By
412   introducing stochasticity into the environment through transition dynamics, our method dynamically
413   balances exploration and exploitation using KL divergence optimization and entropy-ratio estima-
414   tion, which improves the robustness of the generated sequences and ensures better mode discovery.
415
      TFBind8. Our goal is to generate a string of length 8 of nucleotides. Though an autoregressive MDP
416   is conventionally used for strings, we use a prepend-append MDP (PA-MDP) Shen et al. (2023), in
417   which the action involves either adding one token to the beginning or the end of a partial sequence.
418   The reward is a DNA binding affinity to a human transcription factor Trabucco et al. (2022).
