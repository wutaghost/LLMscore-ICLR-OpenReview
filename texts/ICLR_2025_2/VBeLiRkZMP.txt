                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Graph neural networks stand as the predominant technique for graph representa-
               tion learning owing to their strong expressive power, yet the performance highly
011
               depends on the availability of high-quality labels in an end-to-end manner. Thus
012
               the pretraining and fine-tuning paradigm has been proposed to mitigate the label
013            cost issue. Subsequently, the gap between the pretext tasks and downstream tasks
014            has spurred the development of graph prompt learning which inserts a set of graph
015            prompts into the original graph data with minimal parameters while preserving
016            competitive performance. However, the current exploratory works are still limited
017            since they all concentrate on learning fixed task-specific prompts which may not
018            generalize well across the diverse instances that the task comprises. To tackle this
019            challenge, we introduce Instance-Aware Graph Prompt Learning (IA-GPL) in this
020            paper, aiming to generate distinct prompts tailored to different input instances.
021
               The process involves generating intermediate prompts for each instance using a
               lightweight architecture, quantizing these prompts through trainable codebook
022
               vectors, and employing the exponential moving average technique to ensure stable
023
               training. Extensive experiments conducted on multiple datasets and settings show-
024            case the superior performance of IA-GPL compared to state-of-the-art baselines.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Graphs function as pervasive data structures employed across various real-world applications, in-
030   cluding but not limited to social networks Guo & Wang (2020); Liu et al. (2021c), molecular
031   structures Mercado et al. (2021); Guo et al. (2021), and knowledge graphs Liu et al. (2021a); Ye et al.
032   (2022), due to their efficacy in modeling intricate relationships. With the rise of deep learning, Graph
033   Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as a formidable technique for analyzing graph data.
034
      Nevertheless, GNNs trained end-to-end exhibit a strong dependency on large-scale high-quality
035
      labeled data for supervision, which can be challenging or costly to obtain in real-world scenarios.
036   To overcome this challenge, researchers have explored self-supervised or pre-trained GNNs Zhu
037   et al. (2021); Jin et al. (2020); Xia et al. (2022); You et al. (2020a) inspired by the advancements in
038   vision Fan et al. (2021) and language Bao et al. (2021) domains. The pre-training methodologies
039   using readily accessible label-free graphs aim to capture intrinsic graph properties (e.g., node features,
040   node connectivity, or sub-graph pattern) that exhibit generality across tasks and graphs within a given
041   domain. The acquired knowledge is then encoded in the weights of pre-trained GNNs. When it comes
042   to downstream tasks, the initial weights can be efficiently refined through a lightweight fine-tuning
043   step, leveraging a limited set of task-specific labels. However, as discussed in Sun et al. (2023a), the
044
      "pre-train and fine-tuning" paradigm is susceptible to the negative transfer problem.
045   Specifically, pre-trained GNN models focus on preserving the intrinsic graph properties, while
046   fine-tuning seeks to optimize the weights on the downstream tasks, which may significantly differ
047   from the pretext tasks employed in pre-training. For instance, consider the scenario where a GNN
048   is pre-trained using link prediction objective Kipf & Welling (2016b), a prevalent pretext task that
049   aims to bring the representations of adjacent nodes closer in latent space. Subsequently, fine-tuning is
050
      performed using the node classification objective. In such a case, the model might exhibit suboptimal
      performance or even break down, especially if the graph dataset is heterophilic, where adjacent nodes
051
      may have different labels.
052
053   Consequently, in an effort to narrow the gap between pre-training and downstream tasks, sev-
      eral exploratory graph prompting learning frameworks Liu et al. (2023); Sun et al. (2023a); Fang

077   Using two molecules from the BBBP dataset as an example, as shown in Figure 1, for molecule (a), it

081
      To this end, our paper delves into the exploration of instance-aware prompt learning for the graph
082
      domain. This non-trivial research problem raises two questions: (1) what model should we use
083   to generate instance-aware prompts with additional use of a minimal number of parameters? It
084   is important to identify an effective and parameter-efficient method to transform the feature space
085   into the prompt space, as the primary advantage of prompting lies in the minimization of trainable
086   parameters. (2) how can we ensure the instance-aware prompts are meaningful and distinctive as
087   expected? Employing parameterized methods for prompt generation runs the risk of converging to
088   trivial solutions, where all prompts collapse into a singular solution. Consequently, guaranteeing the
089   generation of diverse and meaningful prompts becomes a pivotal aspect of the entire pipeline.
090   In response to these challenges, we introduce a novel instance-aware graph prompt learning framework
091   named IA-GPL designed to generate distinctive prompts for each instance by leveraging its individual
092   information. Specifically, to tackle the first question, we feed the representations of the input instance
093   into parameterized hypercomplex multiplication (PHM) layers Zhang et al. (2020) which transform
094   the feature space into the prompt space with minimal parameters. To solve the second question, we
095   resort to the vector quantization (VQ) GRAY (1998) technique. VQ discretizes the continuous space
096   of intermediate prompts, mapping each prompt to a set of learnable codebook vectors. The mapped
097
      vectors after VQ then replace the original prompts and are incorporated into the original features. For
      the training of codebooks, the exponential moving average technique is utilized to prevent the model
098
      from converging to trivial solutions. To summarize, our main contributions are as follows:
099
100   • We propose IA-GPL, a novel instance-aware graph prompting framework. To the best of our
101     knowledge, IA-GPL is the first graph prompting method capable of generating distinct prompts
102     based on different instances within the dataset.
103
      • In IA-GPL, we utilize a parameter-efficient bottleneck architecture for prompt generation followed
104
        by the vector quantization process via a set of codebook vectors and the exponential moving average
105
        technique to ensure effectiveness and stability.
106
107   • We conduct extensive experiments under different settings to evaluate the performance of IA-GPL.
        Our results demonstrate its superiority over other state-of-the-art competitors.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
125
126
      Due to space limit, an extended related work section is provided in Appendix B. As mentioned in
      the introduction, to mitigate the reliance on graph labels, numerous GNN pre-training techniques
127
      have been proposed which can be implemented at node-level, edge-level, or graph-level Zhu et al.
128
      (2021); Jin et al. (2020); You et al. (2020a). Since fully fine-tuning the pre-trained GNN models may
129   cause the negative transfer problem between pre-training tasks and downstream tasks, prompt-based
130   methods Sun et al. (2023a); Liu et al. (2024), emerged to mitigate this issue. Existing graph prompt
131   learning methods either integrate a prompt graph into the original graph Sun et al. (2023a) or inject
132   a feature vector Fang et al. (2023) into the original features. However, these approaches do not
133   differentiate between input instances and process them uniformly, which could be sub-optimal in
134   certain scenarios. Note that a concurrent work GPF-plus does incorporate different prompts for
135   different nodes using the attention mechanism. However, we believe that our method has several
136   advantages over GPF-plus: (1) our method includes a lightweight down- and up-sample projector
137
      model that transforms the node hidden representations to another prompt vector space, while GPF-
      plus directly computes attention in the original feature space and then averages the weighted candidate
138
      prompts. An additional alignment between these two spaces is beneficial for the disentanglement of
139
      distinct information. (2) instead of using original node features to compute similarities, we use node
140   features after the frozen GNN, which contain rich neighbor-aware information, further aiding in the
141   prompt generation process.
142
143
      Thus, in this work, we propose IA-GPL, a novel methodology designed to address the aforementioned
      issue by generating instance-aware prompts using the distinctive features in individual instances.

      4.3   P ROMPT Q UANTIZATION
271
272   Directly using Pc as prompts suffers from the high variance problem since there is no explicit
273   constraint in the PHM layers, thus Vector Quantization (VQ) GRAY (1998) is utilized to discrete the
274   intermediate prompt space Pc to Pq . VQ is a natural and widely used method in signal processing
275   and data compression that represents a set of vectors by a smaller set of representative vectors. This
276   approach not only helps reduce the high variance caused by the PHM layers but also clusters similar
277   hidden prompt representations together to provide the beneficial property of clustering.
278   Specifically, we maintain K trainable codebook vectors E = (e1 , e2 , . . . , ek ) ∈ Rk×d shared across
279   all the intermediate prompts Pc . For every prompt pc ∈ Pc , we sample M codebook vectors from
280   E corresponding to pc to obtain the quantized pq . Please note that the quantization process for
281   each intermediate prompt pc operates independently of other prompts. In detail, we first compute
282   the squared Euclidean distance dic between the prompt pc and every codebook vector ei , and the
283   corresponding sampling logits lci :

      5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
347
348
      Tasks and datasets. We evaluate IA-GPL using both node-level and graph-level tasks. Following Sun
349   et al. (2023a); Liu et al. (2024), we unify these tasks into a general graph-level task by generating
350   local subgraphs for the nodes of interest. For graph-level tasks, we use eight molecular datasets from
351   MoleculeNet Wu et al. (2018). For node-level tasks, we use three citation datasets from Yang et al.
352   (2016). These datasets vary in size, labels, and domains, serving as a comprehensive benchmark for
353   our evaluations. A comprehensive description of these datasets can be found in Appendix C.
354
      Baselines. To evaluate the effectiveness of IA-GPL, we compare it with state-of-the-art approaches
355   across three primary categories. (1) Supervised learning: we employ GCN Kipf & Welling (2016a),
356   GraphSAGE Hamilton et al. (2017) and GIN Xu et al. (2018). The base models and the projection
357   head are all trained end-to-end from scratch. (2) Pre-training and fine-tuning: The base model
358   is pre-trained using edge prediction Jin et al. (2020) for molecular datasets and graph contrastive
359   learning You et al. (2020a) for citation datasets. For the complete fine-tuning (FT), the pre-trained
360   model is fine-tuned along with the projection head. For linear probing (LP), we freeze the pre-trained
361   model and exclusively train the projection head. (3) Prompt learning: All in One Sun et al. (2023a),
362   GPF Fang et al. (2023) and GPF-plus Fang et al. (2023) are included. They all freeze the pre-trained
363
      base model while training the projection head and their respective prompt generation models.
364   Settings and implementations. To evaluate the performance of IA-GPL in both in-domain and
365   out-of-domain scenarios, we split the molecular datasets in two distinct manners: random split and
366   scaffold split. Scaffold split is based on the scaffold of the molecules so that the train/val/test set is
367   more structurally different, making it appropriate for evaluating the model’s generalization ability. In
368   contrast, the random split is used to assess the model’s in-domain prediction ability. We test IA-GPL
369
      using 5 different pre-training strategies: edge prediction Jin et al. (2020) (denoted as EdgePred), Deep

       6                 C ONCLUSIONS
530
531
      In this paper, we introduce a novel prompting method on graphs named Instance-Aware Graph
532
      Prompt Learning (IA-GPL), which is designed to generate distinct and specific prompts for individual
533   input instances within a downstream task. Specifically, we initially generate intermediate prompts
534   corresponding to each instance using a parameter-efficient bottleneck architecture. Subsequently, we
535   quantize these prompts with a set of trainable codebook vectors and employ the exponential moving
536   average strategy to update the parameters which ensures a stable training process. Extensive experi-
537   mental evaluations under full-shot and few-shot learning settings showcase the superior performance
538   of IA-GPL in both in-domain and out-of-domain scenarios.

      A    B ROADER I MPACTS AND L IMITATIONS
703
704
      Broader Impacts. Research that is focused on parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods (PEFT)
705
      including our approach, IA-GPL, can usually bring several broad positive societal impacts such as:
706
      (1) Accessibility and Sustainability. By reducing the computational, financial, and environmental
707   resources needed for fine-tuning pre-trained models, PEFT methods make advanced AI technologies
708   accessible to a wider range of individuals, organizations, and communities, including those with
709   limited resources. (2) Improved Quality of AI Applications. IA-GPL also enhances numerous
710   graph-related tasks and applications. By incorporating node-level instance-aware prompts, IA-GPL
711   is particularly well-suited for complex and heterogeneous graphs, such as molecules and social
712   networks. Consequently, IA-GPL can positively impact areas like drug discovery, protein structure
713   prediction, fraud detection, and so on.
714   Limitations. (1) Larger parameter size. IA-GPL demands a larger parameter size, primarily due
715   to the PHM layers when generating prompts. As discussed in section 5.3, compared to the huge
716   pre-trained GIN model, the increase in the number of parameters remains acceptable considering the
717   performance improvement achieved. However, it does introduce additional trainable parameters. (2)
718   More hyperparameters. In addition to the standard hyperparameters such as the number of layers
719   and embedding size, IA-GPL introduces new hyperparameters: the number of codebook vectors,
720   the number of samples, and the temperature factor. However, as analyzed in Appendix 5.3, IA-GPL
721
      exhibits limited sensitivity to these hyperparameters across most datasets, obviating the need for
      meticulous and specific tuning.
722
723
724   B    R ELATED W ORK
725
726
      Graph Representation Learning. The objective of graph representation learning is to proficiently
727
      encode sparse high-dimensional graph-structured data into low-dimensional dense vectors. These
728   vectors are subsequently employed in various downstream tasks, such as node/graph classification
729   and link prediction. The methods span from classic graph embeddings Grover & Leskovec (2016) to
730   recent graph neural networks Kipf & Welling (2016a); Veličković et al. (2017); Yun et al. (2019a)
731   with the remarkable success of deep learning. GNNs, which derive effective node representations by
732   recursively aggregating information from neighbor nodes, have emerged as a predominant standard
733   for graph representation learning. GNNs find applications in diverse domains, such as social network
734   analysis Guo & Wang (2020); Liu et al. (2021c), bioinformatics Mercado et al. (2021); Guo et al.
735   (2021), recommendation systems Fan et al. (2019); Tian et al. (2022), and fraud detection Dou et al.
736
      (2020); Liu et al. (2021b). This is attributed to the fact that many real-world datasets inherently possess
      a graph structure, making GNNs well-suited for effectively modeling and extracting meaningful
737
      representations from such data. We refer the readers to a comprehensive survey Ju et al. (2023) for
738
      details.
739   GNNs Pre-training. Supervised learning methods applied to graphs heavily depend on graph
740   labels, which may not always be adequate in real-world scenarios. To overcome this limitation, a
741   pre-training and fine-tuning paradigm has been introduced. In this approach, GNNs are initially
742   pre-trained to capture extensive knowledge from a substantial volume of labeled and unlabeled graph
743   data. Subsequently, the implicit knowledge encoded in the model parameters is transferred to a
744   new domain or task through the fine-tuning of partially pre-trained models. Existing effective pre-
745   training strategies can be implemented at node-level like GCA Zhu et al. (2021), edge-level like edge
746   prediction Jin et al. (2020), and graph-level such as GraphCL You et al. (2020a) and SimGRACE Xia
747
      et al. (2022). However, these methods overlook the gap that may exist between the pre-training phase
      and downstream objectives, limiting their overall generalization ability.
748
      Graph Prompt Learning. Prompt Learning seeks to bridge the gap between pre-training and fine-
749
      tuning by formulating task-specific prompts that guide downstream tasks, with the pre-trained model
750   parameters usually kept static during downstream applications. Many effective prompt methods were
751   initially proposed in the natural language processing community, including hand-crafted prompts Gao
752   et al. (2020); Schick & Schütze (2020) and continuous prompts Gu et al. (2021); Li & Liang (2021b);
753   Liu et al. (2022b). Drawing inspiration from these works, several exploratory graph prompt learning
754   methods, such as GPPT Sun et al. (2022), GraphPrompt Liu et al. (2023), GPF Fang et al. (2023) and
755   All-in-One Sun et al. (2023a) have been proposed in the last two years. We recommend referring to the
      comprehensive survey provided in Sun et al. (2023b). These existing methods introduce virtual class-


                                                         14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      prototype nodes or graphs with learnable links into the input graph or directly incorporate learnable
757   embeddings into the representations, facilitating a closer alignment between downstream applications
758   and the pretext tasks. However, all existing graph prompt tuning methods have predominantly
759   concentrated on task-specific prompts, failing to generate instance-specific prompts which are critical
760   since a universal prompt template may not effectively accommodate input nodes and graphs with
761   significant diversity as shown in Figure 1. In this work, we introduce IA-GPL, a novel methodology
762   designed to address the aforementioned issue by generating prompts that leverage the distinctive
763   features in individual instances.

779   corresponding word from the dictionary. During the pre-training phase, we use them without labels
780   in a self-supervised learning approach. In the fine-tuning stage, we convert the node-level task to
781   the graph-level task following Sun et al. (2023a) and process them in the same way as the molecular
