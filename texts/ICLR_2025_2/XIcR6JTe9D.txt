011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013
               Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) is an effective fine-tuning algorithm for large mod-
               els, enabling efficient adaptation with fewer trainable parameters. Despite its
014
               success, there remains significant potential for improving LoRA’s performance. In
015
               this paper, we introduce iLoRA (Imbalance-Regularized LoRA), which enhances
016            LoRA by incorporating a regularization term to capture the imbalance in forward
017            propagation. This regularization maintains an imbalance between matrices A and
018            B, ensuring stable activation variance independent of dimension. Specifically,
019            we first analyze forward dynamics, observe this imbalance in stable training, and
020            introduce imbalanced regularization. Further, by combining this with precondition-
021            ing techniques (Zhang and Pilanci, 2024), we propose πLoRA (Preconditioned
022            iLoRA), which improves the backpropagation process. Our method is a plug-and-
023            play algorithm that requires only minor modifications to the existing code and
024
               incurs negligible additional computational overhead. Finally, experiments on large
               language models and text-to-image models demonstrate that iLoRA and πLoRA
025
               significantly outperform existing LoRA and preconditioned LoRA methods.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   As neural network models in both vision and language domains continue to grow, training a neural
031   network from scratch to match the performance of existing large models has become increasingly
032   difficult (Brown et al., 2020; Fedus et al., 2022; Zhai et al., 2022; Dubey et al., 2024). Consequently,
033   fine-tuning has emerged as a popular approach for downstream tasks (Devlin, 2018; Liu, 2019).
034   Traditional full-parameter fine-tuning requires extensive storage, making it impractical for many
035
      applications (Raffel et al., 2020). In contrast, recent advances in Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning
      (PEFT) methods offer a more efficient solution while maintaining strong performance in downstream
036
      tasks (Houlsby et al., 2019; Lester et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Hayou et al.,
037
      2024; Zhang and Pilanci, 2024; Tian et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024; Dettmers et al., 2024).
038
039   One widely used PEFT method is Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022), which introduces
040   low-rank matrices to existing model weights and only trains these additive components. Specifically,
041   for a pre-trained weight W(0) ∈ Rm×n , LoRA assumes that the fine-tuned weight W∗ satisfies:
042                                  W⋆ = W(0) + ∆W = W(0) + BA,
                     r×n
043   where A ∈ R          and B ∈ Rm×r , with r ≪ {m, n} representing the rank of the adaptation
044   matrices. During fine-tuning, only A and B are updated, while the original pre-trained weights
045   W(0) remain frozen. This low-rank factorization significantly reduces memory and computational
046   overhead (Sainath et al., 2013), as the rank r is chosen to be much smaller than the dimensions of
047   W(0) ; for example, r = 8 when m = n = 1024. Despite introducing fewer than 2% additional
048   trainable parameters, LoRA achieves comparable, and sometimes even better, performance than full
049   parameters fine-tuning (Hu et al., 2022). Additionally, the multiplication of two small matrices is
050
      more efficient and easier to implement in practice compared to the unstructured sparse matrices used
      in methods like Diff-pruning (Fang et al., 2023a;b), making LoRA a practical and scalable solution
051
      for fine-tuning large models.
052
053   LoRA+ (Hayou et al., 2024) further examined the optimization paradigm of LoRA, revealing that
      for stable feature learning, the learning rate of the parameter B should be set larger than that of

      A, leading to a joint hyperparameter search problem. LoRA+ proposed using a heuristic learning
055   rate ratio ηB /ηA = 24 , and only ηA is tuned in practice. Additionally, Riemannian Preconditioned
056   LoRA (Zhang and Pilanci, 2024) introduced a r × r preconditioner in each gradient step to stabilize
057   feature learning without requiring different learning rates. This method is based on a novel Rieman-
058   nian metric (Absil et al., 2008) and has proven effective in low-rank matrix estimation (Tong et al.,

060   The heuristic learning rate ratio in LoRA+ (ηB /ηA = 24 ) does not fully align with their theoreti-
061   cal analysis and overlooks the dimension of matrices A and B, particularly in models with large
062   dimensions. While Preconditioned LoRA stabilizes feature learning, it does not capture the inherent
063
      asymmetry between A and B. This asymmetry is critical for maintaining consistent forward and
      backward propagation dynamics and for ensuring efficient utilization of the parameter space. As a
064
      result, these methods can lead to suboptimal convergence and propagation behaviors in complex,
065
      large-scale models.

068   inherent asymmetry between matrices A and B. Drawing on insights from the low-rank matrix fac-
069
      torization literature (Tu et al., 2016; Zhu et al., 2021), where regularization terms are used to manage
      mismatches between LoRA fine-tuning matrix pairs, we propose a specialized regularization term:

      where the factor m      compensates for the dimensionality mismatch and captures the inherent
074
      asymmetry between A and B. This regularization ensures that the norms of these components
075   are appropriately balanced during fine-tuning, mitigating instability in forward propagation and
076   enhancing the network’s ability to represent complex features. Despite its effectiveness, iLoRA is
077   a simple, plug-and-play method that requires only minor modifications and introduces negligible
078   additional computational overhead. Moreover, we observe that standard gradient descent (GD) often
079   fails to preserve the forward-pass imbalance between A and B in the backward pass, leading to in-
080   consistencies in optimization dynamics. To resolve this, we apply the preconditioning method (Zhang
081   and Pilanci, 2024), proving that under this approach, the backward pass maintains the same imbalance
082   relationship as the forward pass, ensuring consistency throughout the optimization process.
083   We conduct extensive experiments with iLoRA, including fine-tuning GPT-2 on the E2E Natural

085   benchmark (Pilanci and Ergen, 2020), and diffusion models for image generation. Empirically, iLoRA
086   demonstrates substantial performance improvements over traditional LoRA, with minimal additional
087   computational cost. Furthermore, combining iLoRA with Riemannian Preconditioned LoRA, referred
088   to as πLoRA, delivers significant performance gains across multiple tasks, showcasing the versatility
089
      of iLoRA. Additionally, our method exhibits enhanced robustness under varying learning rates,

090
091   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
092
093   • We propose iLoRA, which introduces an imbalanced regularization strategy to capture the asymme-
094     try between matrices A and B in LoRA, improving training stability and performance.
095   • We introduce πLoRA, which combines iLoRA with Riemannian preconditioning techniques (Zhang
096     and Pilanci, 2024) to capture the inconsistency in backward propagation, ensuring alignment with
097     the imbalance observed during the forward pass.
098   • Extensive experiments on GPT-2 (E2E), Mistral 7B (GLUE), and diffusion models demonstrate
099     that both iLoRA and πLoRA achieve significant performance improvements over LoRA and
100     preconditioned LoRA.
101   • Notably, our method incurs minimal computational overhead and requires only minor modifications
102     to existing code, making it highly accessible and easy to adopt for a wide range of applications.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Low-Rank Adaptation: In recent years, the rapid advancement of parameter-efficient fine-tuning
111   (PEFT) techniques, particularly LoRA, has brought about numerous improvements. LoRA (Hu et al.,
112   2022) is built on the principle that the updates required for fine-tuning large models can be represented
113   as low-rank matrices, significantly reducing the number of trainable parameters. This foundational
114   idea has sparked further innovations aimed at enhancing both the efficiency and effectiveness of LoRA-
115   based methods. Several works (Hayou et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024) have focused
116   on introducing asymmetry or refining rank allocation strategies to improve fine-tuning performance
117   and efficiency. Moreover, preconditioning has been explored in works (Zhang and Pilanci, 2024)
118   showing significant improvements in convergence and optimization. The gradient approximation
119
      technique (Wang et al., 2024) further enhances computational efficiency. Meanwhile, momentum
      filtering techniques (Chen et al., 2024) help mitigate catastrophic forgetting in large models.
120
121   In addition, various works have contributed novel architectures and optimizations, such as introducing
122   multiple regeneration of fine-tuning matrices (Lialin et al., 2024; Xia et al., 2024; Zi et al., 2024),
123   increasing parameter efficiency (Ren et al., 2024; Hao et al., 2024). These advancements solidify
124   LoRA as a powerful and flexible tool for fine-tuning large-scale models.
125   Preconditioning for Matrix Factorization: Accelerating convergence via preconditioning has
126   become a key approach in low-rank matrix factorization. The idea to precondition the gradient
127   with (AA⊤ )−1 and (B⊤ B)−1 was first suggested by Mishra et al. (2012), and later extended to
128   Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) by Mishra and Sepulchre (2016). The convergence properties
129   in the noiseless setting were studied by Li et al. (2018), leading to the development of the method
130   now known as Scaled Gradient Descent (ScaledGD) for Matrix Factorization (Tong et al., 2021a). In
131
      subsequent work, Tong et al. (2021c) extended ScaledGD to subgradient methods, while Jia et al.
      (2023); Zhang et al. (2024; 2021) improved ScaledGD by using alternating optimization of A and B,
132
      iterative hyperparameter updates and introduces time-varying preconditioning. These preconditioning
133
      methods, by addressing overparameterization and ill-conditioning, have become essential tools for
134   improving the efficiency and accuracy of low-rank matrix estimation.

137   3    P RELIMINARIES
138
139   LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) builds on the insight that the updates needed for fine-tuning large pre-trained
140   models are often approximately low-rank. Instead of updating the full-weight matrix during fine-
141
      tuning, LoRA offers a more efficient solution by decomposing the updates into the product of two
      low-rank matrices. This approach dramatically reduces the number of trainable parameters and lowers
142
      computational overhead, making it particularly well-suited for resource-intensive scenarios, such as
143
      fine-tuning Large Language Models (LLMs) and diffusion models.
144
145   In this paper, we use W to denote the weight matrix of a linear layer in the model. For example,
146   in transformers, W can correspond to the Q (query), K (key), and V (value) matrices in the self-
147
      attention mechanism, or the weight matrices in the feedforward layers (MLP layers). LoRA’s key
      idea is to express the fine-tuning update for each linear weight matrix as:
148
149
150                                  W⋆ = W(0) + ∆W = W(0) + BA ,
151
152   where W⋆ , W(0) ∈ Rm×n , B ∈ Rm×r , and A ∈ Rr×n , with r ≪ min(m, n). The matrix W(0)
153   represents the pre-trained weights, which remain frozen during the fine-tuning process, while the low-
154
      rank matrices A and B are the newly introduced trainable parameters. This low-rank structure reduces
      memory and computation costs while enhancing the ability to efficiently adapt large pre-trained
155
      models to new tasks, achieving comparable accuracy to full-parameter fine-tuning.

522           7                C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
523
524          In this paper, we proposed a plug-and-play fine-tuning method iLoRA(Imbalance-Regularized LoRA),
525          which introduces an imbalanced regularization term to address the variance disparity between the fine-
526          tuning matrices A and B in LoRA-based fine-tuning. This approach ensures that AA⊤ and B⊤ B
527          maintain a proportional relationship, thereby enhancing stability in forward propagation. To address
528          inconsistencies in backward propagation, we integrate iLoRA with preconditioning techniques to
529          form πLoRA, utilizing gradient scaling to ensure consistent parameter updates in both forward and
530          backward passes. Extensive experiments across various large-scale models and tasks demonstrate
531          that iLoRA and πLoRA significantly improve training stability and model performance.
532          Nonetheless, certain limitations persist. While our methods address variance disparity within individ-
533          ual layers, they do not explicitly consider parameter imbalances across different layers, which may
534          affect overall performance. Additionally, our approach focuses on balancing forward and backward
535          propagation but does not account for optimization dynamics like momentum in adaptive optimizers.
536          We also aim to investigate the effectiveness of our methods across a wider range of architectures,
537          such as large vision-language models. Addressing these challenges will be the focus of future work.
