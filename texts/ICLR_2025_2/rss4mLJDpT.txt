011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Change detection (CD) in heterogeneous images is a hot but highly challenging
014            topic in the field of remote sensing. However, the significant imaging differ-
015            ences and varying visual appearances of heterogeneous images complicate the
016            accurate detection of changes occurring on the land surface through direct com-
017            parison. To overcome this challenge, this paper proposes a self-supervised net-
018            work based on bidirectional graph comparison (SBGC) for unsupervised hetero-
019            geneous CD, which exploits modality-independent structural relationships. First,
020
               pseudo-Siamese networks are established to extract discriminative and robust fea-
               tures from bi-temporal heterogeneous images based on self-supervised contrastive
021
               learning. Then, these learned features are utilized to construct graph structures
022
               that represent structural relationships. Second, we introduce bidirectional graph
023            comparison to fully exploit the graph structures for exploring comprehensive
024            change information. Specifically, we map the graph structures to their opposite
025            image modality and perform a bidirectional comparison between the original and
026            mapped graph structures to generate a difference image. Finally, the change map
027            is obtained by applying the Otsu segmentation algorithm to the difference image.
028            Experimental results on three public heterogeneous datasets with different modal-
029            ity combinations show that the proposed method achieves superior performance
030            compared to seven state-of-the-art methods, achieving the best performance with
031
               an average overall accuracy of 96.69%.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036
037   Change detection (CD) utilizes remote sensing imagery captured at different times to analyze
038   changes in ground objects within the same geographical area (Wen et al., 2021), which facilitates
039
      various applications, such as disaster assessment (Qing et al., 2022), and environmental monitoring
      (Kalinaki et al., 2023).
040
041   CD techniques can be categorized into two types based on the data sources of the images: homoge-
042   neous CD and heterogeneous CD. Homogeneous CD, which employs images captured by the same
043   sensor, is an intuitive approach that has succeeded in various applications (Du et al., 2019; Zhan
044   et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2024). However, its
045
      practicality is limited in some extreme scenarios, such as rapid responses to severe natural disas-
      ters, where timely images from the same sensor may not be available, rendering it ineffective for
046
      detecting changes in ground objects. In contrast, heterogeneous CD, which utilizes images from
047
      different sensors, can effectively overcome this limitation. In recent years, advancements in re-
048   mote sensing technology have increased the availability of remote sensing images from different
049   sources, providing valuable data support for heterogeneous CD (Liu et al., 2024). These images can
050   be captured using different sensors, including synthetic aperture radar (SAR), optical, and hyper-
051   spectral sensors. Generally, detecting changes in ground objects between heterogeneous images is
052   more challenging than between homogeneous images, because heterogeneous CD should consider
053   not only the challenges such as noise and illumination that homogeneous CD faces, but also the
      modality discrepancy.

054
      Heterogeneous CD, where pre-event and post-event images are captured by different sensors, has be-
055   come popular due to the complementary strengths of heterogeneous images, thereby enhancing the
056   response efficiency and accuracy of CD. Nonetheless, heterogeneous images exhibit distinct imag-
057   ing mechanisms and varied depictions of the same land cover, rendering direct comparison through
058   pixel/object difference measurement for CD impractical (Zhan et al., 2017; Caye Daudt et al., 2018).
059   To address this challenge, researchers have been exploring the modality-independent structural rela-
060   tionships within heterogeneous images. Wan et al. (L. Wan & You, 2018) utilized sorted histograms
061   to capture the local internal layout of the image and then computed the differences between the
062   sorted bins to detect changes. Luppino et al. (Luppino et al., 2019) represented structural relation-
063
      ships by constructing local affinity matrices, subsequently calculating the differences between these
      matrices. In (Sun et al., 2021b; 2022), structural relationships were represented through the con-
064
      struction of nonlocal graph structures, and the structural differences between heterogeneous images
065
      were then computed using graph mapping. Sun et al. (Sun et al., 2021a) further introduced an iter-
066   ative robust graph and Markovian co-segmentation framework (IRG-McS) that utilizes superpixels
067   to represent ground object information, treating them as nodes for graph construction. In (Jimenez-
068   Sierra et al., 2022), a framework based on nonlocal graph structures driven by signal smoothness
069   representation was proposed. This nonlocal graph-based approach (Sun et al., 2021b; 2022; 2021a;
070   Jimenez-Sierra et al., 2022) constructs nonlocal k-nearest neighbors (KNN) graphs and computes
071   the similarity levels of these graph structures to detect changes. However, there are two main is-
072   sues with this approach. First, this approach relies solely on original pixel information in images,
073   which lacks robustness in complex scenes with diverse land cover types and varying sizes. Second,
074
      concerning mapping the graph structure from the pre-event image to the post-event image, for in-
      stance, the approach uses a one-way graph comparison, focusing solely on the comparison between
075
      the mapped and the post-event image’s graph structure. However, it neglects the original pre-event
076
      graph structure and fails to fully utilize all the graph structure information, leading to more false
077   detections.
078
079   To overcome the issues mentioned above, we propose a Self-supervised network based on
080
      Bidirectional Graph Comparison (SBGC) for unsupervised heterogeneous CD. First, pseudo-
      Siamese networks are established for self-supervised learning (SSL), aiming to extract representative
081
      and robust features directly from the original images. Second, to fully exploit the change information
082
      leveraging the graph structures, we propose bidirectional graph comparison (BGC). Specifically, we
083   map the graph structures into their opposite image modality and subsequently conduct the bidirec-
084   tional comparison between the original and mapped graph structures. The main contributions of this
085   work are summarized as follows.
086
      1) Efficient pseudo-Siamese networks are employed to learn robust and representative features from
087
      original images through SSL, effectively handling complex scenes with varied land cover and sizes,
088
      thus improving the accuracy of CD.
089
090   2) We propose BGC to fully explore the rich information within the graph structures. Specifically,
091   we perform a bidirectional comparison between the original and mapped graph structures to extract
092
      difference information.
093   3) The impressive experimental results on three public heterogeneous datasets with different modal-
094   ity combinations demonstrate the superiority and practicality of SBGC in comparison with seven
095   state-of-the-art (SOTA) unsupervised heterogeneous CD methods.
096   The rest of this article is structured as follows. Section 2 presents the proposed method in detail,
097   followed by the experimental results and discussions in Section 3, and finally, the conclusion of our
098   work is provided in Section 4.

143   2.2   I MAGE PATCHES G UIDED SSL
144
145   Following (Sun et al., 2022), both images are divided into overlapping square patches denoted as
146   xi ∈ R p×p×CX and yi ∈ R p×p×CY using a sliding window approach, where the patch size is set to p
147   with a step length of ⌈p/2⌉. Here, i ∈ {1, . . . , M}, where M represents the number of patches.
148   In the community of remote sensing, particularly for heterogeneous CD task, supervised feature
149   learning usually requires sufficient labeled samples, which are difficult to obtain in real applications.
150   In contrast, SSL is able to learn useful feature representations from raw data without manually label-
151   ing samples. Consequently, we employ SSL to extract more representative features from the original
152   images, thereby enhancing the accuracy of CD by leveraging more representative information rather
153   than relying solely on original pixel data. As depicted in Fig. 1, we build pseudo-Siamese networks
154   for SSL, comprising two branches. The lower branch comprises one encoder, and one predictor,
155
      while the upper branch shares the same network architecture, excluding the predictor. Here, we use
      a sub-ResNet18 (He et al., 2016) as the encoder, modifying the stride of the first layer from 1 to 2
156
      and removing the third and fourth layers to adapt to relatively small input patch size. The predictor
157
      is composed of fully connected layers with the structure 512-128-512.
158

      declines for p > 9. Notably, the best results are obtained with p = 9 for the Sardinia and Shuguang
406   datasets, while p = 11 yields optimal results for the California dataset. For simplicity, we select
407   p = 9, as it provides relatively good performance across all three datasets.

411   To verify the validity of SSL and BGC, we conducted ablation experiments on three datasets. Two

414   way graph comparison; (2) a framework that incorporates SSL without BGC; and (3) our complete
415   method with SSL and BGC.
416   Table 3 shows the contribution of each component in our method across three datasets. The utiliza-
417   tion of features extracted through SSL outperforms the use of original image information, highlight-
418   ing the ability of SSL to provide more representative and robust features. Moreover, incorporating
419   BGC enhances the OA and KC values across all datasets. This demonstrates that compared to one-
420   way graph comparison, BGC can capture more comprehensive change information and reduce false
421   alarms, thus improving CD performance. This is further supported by Fig. 5. As shown in Fig. 5(a),
422   the change map obtained without using BGC has more false alarms and does not sufficiently extract

      change information. In contrast, our complete method with BGC improves the detection of changed
      areas and reduces false alarms, as depicted in Fig. 5(b). When SSLN and BGC are used together,
424
      the best performance can be observed (the third row of Table 3). Overall, the effectiveness of the
425
      proposed method can be demonstrated through the analysis provided above.

      images. Initially, we employ pseudo-Siamese networks to extract representative and robust features
