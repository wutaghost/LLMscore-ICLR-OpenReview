009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            It has recently emerged that Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs) can achieve excellent
012            performance on graph node classification, but only if they distill a previously-
013            trained Graph Neural Network (GNN). This finding is confusing; if MLPs are
014            expressive enough to perform node classification, what is the role of the GNNs?
015            This paper aims to answer this question. Rather than suggesting a new technique,
016            we aim to demystify GNN distillation methods. Through our analysis, we iden-
017            tify the key properties of GNNs that enable them to serve as effective regularizers,
018
               thereby overcoming limited training data. We validate our analysis by demon-
               strating an MLP training process that successfully leverages GNN-like properties
019
               without actually training a GNN.
020

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Node classification tasks naturally occur when we wish to classify graph-structured data, such as
025   paper citation networks (Sen et al., 2008; Namata et al., 2012) or product co-purchase networks
026   (Shchur et al., 2018). Most state-of-the-art node classification methods use Graph Neural Networks
027   (GNN) (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Hamilton et al., 2017; Velickovic et al., 2017). GNNs exploit the
028   context of the neighborhood of each node to determine its class. Their architecture uses message
029   passing to transfer information across many nodes. As GNNs consider large neighborhoods, their
030   training and inference times are considerably higher than methods that consider only the node fea-
031   tures.
032   Therefore, several research efforts have attempted to find simpler alternatives to GNNs that do not
033   require message passing. Distillation methods, starting with the seminal work by Zhang et al. (2021),
034   propose to replace GNNs at test time with simple node-level MLPs. These methods first train a GNN
035   on the training data, then use the labels predicted by the GNN as distillation targets for training a
036   node-level MLP. At test time, these methods only use the faster node-level MLP. Remarkably, distil-
037   lation methods sometimes outperform GNNs, despite MLPs not directly using the graph structure.
038   This study investigates the role of GNNs in the distillation process and finds that their primary
039   contribution lies in enforcing regularization that preserves homophily, the tendency for adjacent
040   nodes to share labels. Our analysis reveals that GNNs provide an effective implicit regularization
041   which assists in reducing overfitting, especially in datasets characterized by significant homophily
042   and very limited training data.
043   Some distillation methods also propose incorporating structure information into the input features
044   of the MLP student model using learnable positional embeddings. While effective, it is unclear
045   which structural aspects of the graph these features encode. We present an explainable alternative
046   descriptor that encodes the neighborhood characteristics of each node.
047
      To validate that the GNN’s implicit regularization is key to the success of distillation methods, we
048   compare distillation methods to an alternative approach of training MLPs with direct regularization.
049   This regularization strategy consists of three key components: (i) a loss encouraging smoothness
050   between the label predictions of neighboring nodes, (ii) iterative pseudo-labeling of the observed
051   unlabeled nodes, and (iii) a neighborhood label histogram descriptor for encoding local context.
052   Notably, this approach does not require training or evaluating GNNs and demonstrates a high corre-
053   lation with the performance of distillation methods on commonly used datasets, such as citation and
      co-purchase networks.

      2   R ELATED WORKS
065
066
067   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as a prominent tool in the domain of graph ma-
068   chine learning (Bruna et al., 2013; Defferrard et al., 2016; Li et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020b). These
069   neural networks use aggregations of features from the local context of each node at successive lay-
070
      ers. For example, Graph Convolutional Networks (GCN) (Kipf & Welling, 2016) extend traditional
      convolution operations from the Euclidean domain to operations on graphs. GraphSAGE Hamilton
071
      et al. (2017) uses arbitrary aggregation functions while also concatenating the features prior to the
072
      aggregation. GAT (Velickovic et al., 2017), GTN (Yun et al., 2019), and HAN (Wang et al., 2019b)
073   generalize attention layers and transformers to graphs. Many GNNs are formulated into a unified
074   framework called Message Passing Neural Networks (Gilmer et al., 2017).
075
076
      Knowledge distillation of GNNs. Addressing challenges related to memory consumption and
      latency, several methods have been proposed to distill knowledge from a large pre-trained GNN
077
      teacher model to a smaller student model. The student model can be either a smaller GNN model
078
      (Lee & Song, 2019; Yang et al., 2020; Yan et al., 2020; Tian et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023), or
079   a structure-agnostic model (Wu et al., 2023a;b). One such method, GLNN (Zhang et al., 2021),
080   trains an MLP model to predict soft-labels obtained from a pre-trained GNN. Another approach,
081   NOSMOG (Tian et al., 2022), uses the same underlying method with the addition of adversarial
082   feature augmentation loss and Similarity Distillation of hidden features. NOSMOG also utilizes
083   the graph structure by concatenating positional features obtained using DeepWalk (Perozzi et al.,
084   2014). While NOSMOG offers better accuracy results than standard GLNN, it suffers from higher
085   latency induced by positional feature computation. PGKD (Wu et al., 2023c) distills graph structural
086   information from GNNs to MLPs via prototypes in an edge-free setting. Orthi-Reg (Zhang et al.,
087
      2023) mitigates the dimensional collapse of MLPs by explicitly encouraging orthogonal node rep-
      resentations during training. CPF (Yang et al., 2021) also uses a non-GNN student model, although
088
      the student still relies on iterative label propagation during inference, which increases the inference
089
      running time.
090
091   Node classification without GNN. Various techniques beyond Graph Neural Networks have been
092   developed. Among them is Graph-MLP (Hu et al., 2021), which trains an MLP model with a
093
      neighbor contrastive loss. While this approach bears some resemblance to the consistency loss
      employed in our work, our consistency loss encourages similarity among neighboring nodes and
094
      is not contrastive. Another method, Correct and Smooth (C&S) (Huang et al., 2020), leverages
095
      the correlation between neighbors’ labels to enhance a shallow MLP predictor. Unlike our study,
096   which focuses on the training process of MLPs, it refines predictions post-training through label
097   propagation. The applicability of the C&S method to the inductive case (where new nodes are added
098   to the graph during test time) is limited, and it focuses on supervised rather than semi-supervised
099   settings. Also, in contrast to the heavy reliance on labels by C&S, a significant aspect of our study
100   examines how GNNs address the challenges arising from a small training set in semi-supervised
101   scenarios.
102   Semi-supervised learning. SSL is an approach for leveraging unlabeled data, often used in scenar-
103   ios where the size of the training set is small. A popular SSL method, termed pseudo-labeling, uses
104   the model’s predictions as labels for training (McLachlan, 1975; Rosenberg et al., 2005; Lee et al.,
105   2013; Xie et al., 2020). Another prominent SSL approach is consistency regularization (Bachman
106   et al., 2014; Sajjadi et al., 2016; Laine & Aila, 2016), where the model is enforced to maintain con-
107   sistent predictions through random augmentation of its input. FixMatch (Sohn et al., 2020) combines
      these ideas in a simple manner.

      3                 W HY ARE DISTILLATION METHODS SUCCESSFUL ?
127
128
129
130
      Distillation methods have recently challenged the existing paradigm in node classification. The stan-
      dard practice with GNNs is to train the model on all the labeled nodes in the graph and use the same
131
      model for node classification at test time. Distillation methods remove the need for using a GNN
132
      at test time, although they still require training a GNN for teaching the student model using unsu-
133   pervised nodes. They train an MLP to predict the labels of each node based on node features only,
134   without considering the features of its neighbors. Remarkably, distillation methods are competitive
135   with GNNs on popular citation and co-purchase network benchmarks. This result is confusing, as
136   the graph structure appears beneficial during training but not during test time. This begs the question:
137   Why are distillation methods so successful?
138
      To address this question, we investigate whether the power of the GNN comes from the increased
139   expressivity of message passing or a useful inductive bias. As a quick test, we plot the node clas-
140   sification accuracy of both GNNs (specifically GraphSAGE) and node-level MLPs as a function of
141   the training set size (Fig. 1). The observed trend indicates that with an increase in training size,
142   the performance gap between node-level MLPs and GNNs diminishes. This suggests that MLPs
143   overfit due to small training sizes on popular node classification datasets, while GNNs have better
144   regularization (i.e., they have a useful inductive bias).
145   Next, we examine the gap between GNNs and MLPs that are trained through distillation of GNNs.
146   Here, we observe that the gap between the two models is narrow, even for small labeled training
147   sets. These experimental findings lead us to conclude that: the challenge in the examined dataset
148   lies not in increasing model expressivity, but rather in decreasing model sample complexity. GNNs
149   overcome this challenge through a useful inductive bias, while distillation overcomes it by increasing
150   the size of the training set with GNN pseudo-labels.
151   We hypothesize that GNNs improve the optimization of MLPs due to two key properties:
152
153
154                       • GNNs act as consistency regularizers due to their tendency to produce smoothed predic-
155                         tions along edges.
156
157
158                       • GNNs benefit from unlabeled data through the message passing mechanism.
159
160
161   In the following sections, we will empirically test this hypothesis. We will analyze the benefits of
      these properties and examine how we can emulate their effects without the need to train a GNN.

258

259

313   4.3.2          P SEUDO - LABELING ITERATIONS
314
      As demonstrated by many works in the semi-supervised regime (Rosenberg et al., 2005; Sohn et al.,
315   2020), using unlabeled samples is crucial when we have very limited training data. Utilizing unla-
316   beled nodes is particularly beneficial for distillation methods, as most standard node classification
317   benchmarks are effectively semi-supervised. I.e., their training set is very small (sometimes as small
318   as 0.3% of the total number of nodes).
319
      Distillation methods benefit from unlabeled nodes in two ways. Firstly, during the training of the
320
      teacher GNN, the message-passing mechanism inherently uses nodes that are not necessarily in the
321   training set. Secondly, the entire graph is passed through the GNN to obtain soft pseudo-labels for
322   all the nodes. Consequently, all the nodes of the graph are involved in the training of the student.

      5     E XPLICIT GRAPH STRUCTURE FEATURES FOR MLP CLASSIFIERS
365
      Some distillation methods have proposed incorporating structural information into the input features
366
      of the MLP student model. For example, NOSMOG (Tian et al., 2022) incorporates a positional
367
      embedding, DeepWalk (Perozzi et al., 2014), in its features. This embedding allows the classifier
368   to learn a connection between a node’s position in the graph and the labels of the nodes around it.
369   While effective, it is unclear which structural aspects of the graph these features encode. In this
370   section, we propose an explicit approach for incorporating structural information.
371
372
      5.1   H ISTOGRAMS OF NEIGHBORING LABELS
373
374   The neighborhood of a node may help infer further information about its label. To utilize this,
375   we propose concatenating a descriptor of neighboring labels to the input of the predictor, thereby
376   incorporating positional data. Specifically, for each node v, the descriptor is a weighted histogram
377   of the labels of all nodes with a path to v of length at most ℓ. The weight assigned to each node in
      the histogram is determined by the minimal path length to v.

      Homophily prior. The use of the homophily prior, which posits that neighboring nodes has pos-

453
      tency loss, which encourages the model to maintain correlation among neighboring nodes, and (2)

      As depicted in Tab. 4, incorporating these two elements resulted in an average improvement of 2.4%.

458

459

460   the same time. The ogbn-arxiv dataset is an outlier as it is not positively affected by the homophily
461   prior. Notably, this dataset has the largest proportion (53.7%) of labeled training nodes. As the
462   number of labels is sufficient, the advantage of using additional priors such as homophily decreases.

465   tillation methods, our analysis also provides strategies to optimize MLPs for more efficient node
466   classification in graphs, eliminating the need for GNNs. This approach has the potential to inspire
467   future research focused on developing more efficient methods. Furthermore, the label histograms
468   utilized in our analysis to evaluate the benefits of local context can be computed on a CPU and do
469   not necessitate the learning of embeddings, in contrast to our baseline approach.
470

473   borhood data. To assess the impact of node degree on model performance, we evaluated nodes with
474   varying degrees, as illustrated in App. Fig 5. The results indicate that our optimization process
475   for MLPs closely aligns with the patterns observed in GNN distillation. In the a-computer dataset,
476   the accuracy appears to exhibit a stronger positive correlation with node degree compared to other

      8    C ONCLUSION
507
508
      In this paper, we demystified the effectiveness of graph distillation methods. Our investigation
509
      centered on the hypothesis that GNNs enhance the optimization process of MLPs due to two factors:
510   (1) GNNs serve as consistency regularizers, and (2) GNNs leverage unlabeled data effectively. To
511   test this hypothesis, we devised a methodology that directly incorporates these properties without
512   the utilization of GNNs, employing consistency loss and iterative pseudo-labeling. Moreover, we
513   introduced an explicit method for incorporating structural features and demonstrated its comparable
514   efficacy to learnable features.

1059   In the inductive setting we further split the unlabelled test set, denoted as U = V/(Vtrain ∪ Vval ),

1063   observed test nodes participate in the consistency loss and may have pseudo-labels. In the inductive
1064   setting, we train the model on the graph induced by all the nodes in the set Vtrain ∪ Vval ∪ Useen .
1065   Only the nodes of Vtrain are used in the classification loss (Eq. 1). At training time, we discard
1066

       of the sets Useen and Uunseen .
