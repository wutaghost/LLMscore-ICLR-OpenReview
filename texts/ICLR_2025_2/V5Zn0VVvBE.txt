011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013             Electroencephalography (EEG)-based brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) serve
014             many control paradigms by relying on a variety in active brain regions and EEG
015             features. Developing a universal EEG foundation model1 has been challenging
016
                due to the large variety in recording setups and experimental tasks. Additionally,
                researchers often contend with limited labeled data, making it difficult to utilize
017
                large deep-learning models effectively. While there have been successful attempts
018
                to develop EEG foundation models, few studies have systematically evaluated
019
                their adaptability across diverse BCI control paradigms. To address this gap, we
020
                propose a novel, yet simple spatiotemporal EEG transformer (ST-EEGFormer)
021
                that projects segments (“patches”) of raw EEG data into an embedding space
022             enriched with a spatial and temporal embedding, allowing the model to effectively
023             handle EEG data exhibiting various channel set-ups and time lengths. To improve
024             data efficiency, we first employed a masked autoencoder (MAE) task to pre-train
025             the ST-EEGFormer in a self-supervised learning manner on a dataset combining
026             six different motor imagery (MI) datasets, a P300 dataset, and a steady-state visual
027             evoked potential (SSVEP) dataset, all of which are public. Next, we benchmarked
028             the pre-trained model, after fine-tuning, on diverse downstream classification
029             tasks. To evaluate the generalization capability, we conducted additional exper-
030             iments on two public datasets, not used for pre-training: a seizure classification
031             dataset and an online MI BCI dataset. We compared the performance against
032             a simple linear model, EEGNet (a classic CNN-based benchmark model), the
033             state-of-the-art supervised EEG Conformer model, and two foundation models,
034             BIOT and Large Brain Model (LaBraM). The pre-trained ST-EEGFormers could
035             learn robust EEG representations, achieving higher classification accuracies than
036
                the benchmarked models across all eight pre-training datasets and exhibiting
                strong generalization on new datasets with limited training data. Finally, we report
037
                several visualizations of the model including the features on which the results are
038
                based.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
041
042   Electroencephalography (EEG) is a non-invasive recording technique widely utilized in the
043   development of Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs), the aim of which is to enhance the quality
044   of life of disabled patients and to augment the performance of healthy individuals. Several
045   EEG paradigms have been explored to facilitate BCI development. Motor imagery (MI), for
046   instance, involves the mental simulation of physical movement and can be used to control various
047
      applications, such as exoskeletons (Soekadar et al., 2016; Choi et al., 2020), navigation in real
      or virtual environment (Choi & Cichocki, 2008; Tsui et al., 2011; Yang & Van Hulle, 2023),
048
      or assist in rehabilitation (Baniqued et al., 2021; Liao et al., 2023). Event-related potentials
049
      (ERPs), such as the P300 response—a positive potential elicited when a user experiences an
050   infrequent event—have been employed to decode user attention and to develop smart home control
051   applications (Holzner et al., 2009; Masud et al., 2017). Visual-evoked potentials (VEPs), including
052

054
      steady-state visual-evoked potentials (SSVEPs), are EEG amplitude changes in response to visual
055   stimuli, which in turn can be used to construct high-speed spelling devices (Nakanishi et al., 2018;
056   Xing et al., 2018).
057
058
      Although the aforementioned paradigms can be recorded with a standard EEG cap, they differ in
      spatial and temporal response patterns. This often implies training decoding models tailored to
059
      specific paradigms and applications. Traditional decoding methods include steps such as temporal
060
      filtering, spatial filtering (Blankertz et al., 2008), feature extraction (Singh & Krishnan, 2023),
061   and classification using linear classifiers like linear discriminant analysis (LDA) or support vector
062   machines (SVM) (Neto et al., 2016; Richhariya & Tanveer, 2018). These models typically require
063   prior knowledge about the EEG signal. For example, the prime feature for MI is the temporal change
064   in the mu and beta frequency bands, the extraction of which involves a pipeline of band-pass filtering,
065   and the application of common spatial pattern (CSP) filtering, using variance as a feature (Ang et al.,
066   2012). For P300, the time-locked feature is obtained by sampling the EEG amplitude over 500 ms
067   post-onset of the infrequent event. For SSVEP, different flashing stimuli elicit EEG rhythm in sync
068   with the stimulation frequencies, therefore canonical-correlation analysis (CCA) can be applied to
069
      occipital channels to find out the best-matching template representing the stimulation frequency (Lin
      et al., 2007).
070
071   Recently, deep learning has rapidly advanced in the BCI field, achieving state-of-the-art performance
072   in various tasks. However, differences in BCI paradigms, recording devices, and the relatively
073   small dataset sizes necessitate training models on individual tasks (Murad & Rahimi, 2024). Even
074   within the same paradigm, significant subject variance hinders the development of a universal EEG
075
      decoder. Additionally, the small dataset sizes discourage the use of large deep-learning models.
      Consequently, small models that typically rely on convolutional neural networks (CNNs) impose
076
      restrictions on input shape (e.g., the number of EEG channels, and the number of samples), further
077
      complicating the use of different datasets as they usually exhibit different data formats. A few
078
      pioneering works have attempted to address these limitations. For instance, BIOT (Yang et al., 2023),
079
      a biosignal foundation model, was designed to learn from diverse biosignal data and handle miss-
080
      ing data, while the Large Brain Model (LaBraM) (Jiang et al., 2024), an EEG foundation model
081
      pre-trained on large-scale EEG datasets, demonstrated superior performance in tasks such as seizure
082
      classification, emotion recognition, and gait prediction. However, the applicability of such models
083
      to diverse BCI paradigms with limited data remains unexplored.
084
085   Contributions: This paper introduces the ST-EEGFormer, a large transformer-based model for BCI
086   applications. The model was pre-trained on 3 million 5-second EEG epochs sourced from diverse
087   EEG-BCI datasets using the masked autoencoder (MAE) method (He et al., 2021). For the first
088   time, we evaluated such a large model on various small BCI datasets, demonstrating promising
089   results across six MI datasets, one P300 dataset, and one SSVEP dataset. Additionally, experiments
090   conducted on a seizure dataset and an online MI BCI dataset—neither of which were included in the
091   pre-training data—highlight the model’s robust representation capabilities, particularly in scenarios
092   with limited training data. This work provides a versatile and high-performing BCI model, offering
093   valuable insights and tools for advancing BCI research across diverse tasks.

      2   P ROPOSED A PPROACH
097   In this study, we propose the ST-EEGFomer model, which, when pre-trained using MAE, can be
098   directly fine-tuned on other datasets. The pre-training task involves reconstructing the original
099   EEG inputs from masked tokens (see figure 1 and Appendix A.2.4 for detailed descriptions). The
100   motivation for this architecture is to address the following issues:
101
      The architecture is designed to address the following issues:
      1):Achieve end-to-end EEG decoding that is not vulnerable to variations in EEG data formats.
102
      2):Demonstrate the feasibility of pre-training on combined diverse BCI tasks and show the
103
      effectiveness of the model fine-tuning in different small BCI classification tasks.
104
      3):Benchmark the model on a variety of datasets including new datasets to provide a fair comparison
105   with other state-of-the-art models.
106

      explored in previous studies (Xie et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024), which encodes

136
      encoder proposed by (Mohsenvand et al., 2020), PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023), iTransformer (Liu

141   model is characterized by its simplicity. Inspired by ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), it employs a
142   straightforward linear projection for tokenization and a single-stage pre-training process focused
143   solely on the MAE reconstruction task. A graphical representation of the proposed model is shown
144   in figure 1.
145   Given prior successes with MAE in EEG decoding, we also selected MAE as the pre-training
146   task. However, a distinction between our approach and other EEG-MAE attempts lies in the use
147   of spatiotemporal segmentation, incorporating both spatial and temporal positional embeddings.
148   While this spatiotemporal masking-and-reconstruction approach shares similarities with LaBraM,
149   our model is simpler in both its architecture and pre-training procedures. To address issues 2
150   and 3, we pre-trained our model using data from eight different datasets collectively and then
151   fine-tuned it on six MI classification tasks, one P300 task, and one SSVEP task. We compared
152   the performance of our model against a simple common spatial pattern (CSP)-based linear model,
153   the classic EEG benchmark model EEGNet (Lawhern et al., 2016), and the state-of-the-art EEG
154   Conformer model (Song et al., 2023).

      3.2       B ENCHMARK ON M ULTIPLE BCI TASKS
185
186
      After MAE pre-training, each dataset was individually benchmarked using the pre-trained ST-
187
      EEGFormer and compared against several established models: a linear spatial filtering model
188   that mimics the classic common spatial pattern (CSP) filtering approach (Zheng & Lu, 2015);
189   EEGNet (Lawhern et al., 2016), a classic CNN-based backbone model known for its robust
190   generalization across different BCI tasks; EEG Conformer (Song et al., 2023), which combines
191   a CNN feature extractor with a transformer module, achieving state-of-the-art performance in motor
192   imagery tasks. Additionally, we also compared our model with two open-source pre-trained EEG
193   foundation models, BIOT (Yang et al., 2023) and LaBraM (Jiang et al., 2024). Note that for
194   classification, the ST-EEGFormer could either use the class token or the average token as the feature
195   to train a classification head. Meanwhile, one can perform end-to-end fine-tuning or freeze all
196   layers and only train a linear head (linear probing). This study primarily focuses on motor imagery
197   tasks to assess the model’s performance. However, experiments with BIOT were not conducted on
198   BCI-Comp-IV2a and BCI-Comp-IV2b datasets as the original channels could not be re-referenced
199   to the required bipolar channels. Additionally, a P300 and an SSVEP dataset were included to
200
      introduce more variety during pre-training and to demonstrate the model’s generalization capability
      across different classification tasks. For SSVEP, we also benchmarked the state-of-the-art SSVEP
201
      decoding model SSVEP DNN (Guney et al., 2022). Model details are described in Appendix A.3.
202
      For all experiments, population decoding with 5-fold cross-validation was performed, within each
203   fold, 20% of the current training data were used as a validation set for model selection. If a
204   hidden test set was available, it was used as an additional test set. We adopted a population
205   decoding approach, where the model is trained on data from all available subjects to develop a
206   subject-independent model. This approach is more computationally efficient, as per-subject training
207   can be time-consuming and computationally intensive in a multi-subject study. Implementation
208   details for this experiment can be found in Appendix A.4. The benchmark results for all MI datasets
209   are presented in table 2. SSVEP dataset results and P300 results are presented in table 3 and figure 2.
210   Detailed results are presented in Appendix A.8.

256

348   3.3.2          T WO -S ESSION O NLINE MI BCI C LASSIFICATION
349
      In order to show the usability of the pre-trained models on future datasets or BCI applications, we
350
      validated the performance on a new MI BCI dataset (Stieger et al., 2021) not included in the initial
351
      MAE learning stage. This dataset contains EEG recordings of 4 classes of MI online tasks: imagined
352   left hand- (LHand), imagined right hand- (RHand), imagined both hand movement (2Hand), and
353   resting task (Rest). Similar to the seizure task described in Section 3.3.1, we varied the number of
354   training examples to evaluate the model’s performance with limited data. Additionally, we tested the
355   models in a zero-shot manner on data from the second session to assess cross-session generalization
356   ability. A detailed description of this dataset and experiment can be found in Appendix A.5.2. The
357   results are presented in figure 4. Similar to the seizure classification experiments, the pre-trained
358   ST-EEGFormer models demonstrated superior performance, particularly when training data was
359   limited. The ST-EEGFormer large model consistently achieved the highest classification accuracy
360
      across all experiments. However, unlike the seizure classification results, the linearly-probed model
      did not perform as well, suggesting that motor imagery EEG data may contain more nonlinear
361
      features. Additionally, the cross-session experiment revealed a decrease in accuracy during zero-shot
362
      testing, indicating that the model still struggles with cross-session variability.
363   Finally, the two experiments yielded different optimal mask ratios: 0.3 for the single-channel seizure
364   dataset and 0.9 for the 62-channel MI dataset, indicating varying behaviors that warrant further
365   investigation.

      4.1          P RE - TRAINING A LARGE EEG FOUNDATION MODEL FOR BCI TASKS
371
      Results from table 2 demonstrate that the pre-trained ST-EEGFormer achieves the highest classifica-
372   tion accuracies across multiple BCI datasets. The large model outperformed both the base and small
373   models on most datasets. For all MI datasets, the finetuned base models exhibited higher accuracies
374   compared to the linearly probed models, though the latter performed similarly to supervised linear
375   models trained from scratch. Additionally, the linear model used as a baseline in this study was
376   validated by comparison to existing benchmarks, as reported in figure 3 of (Gwon et al., 2023).
377   The mean accuracies across all subjects using subject-specific linear models were comparable to our
      population models, achieving an average accuracy of 60%. This demonstrates the effectiveness


                                                                  7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      of the proposed approach, where performing self-supervised learning (SSL) pre-training on a
379   foundation model with large EEG recordings enables the model to learn robust EEG features,
380   comparable to a linear model.
381
382
      Furthermore, table 3 and figure 2 also demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed MAE-pre-
      trained ST-EEGFormer as a foundation model for general BCI decoding, outperforming EEGNet,
383
      and EEG conformer. However, the relatively poor performance on the SSVEP dataset requires
384
      further investigation. We acknowledge that the classification accuracies listed in table 3 are
385   significantly lower than those reported in other studies. For instance, in the original SSVEP deep
386   neural network (DNN) paper (Guney et al., 2022), using a 1-second window, the model achieved
387   an accuracy exceeding 0.9. This substantial performance difference could be attributed to several
388   factors:
389   1): We only conducted population training, which is considered the first stage in (Guney et al., 2022).
390   The reported accuracy is only around 47% with first-stage training alone (Guney et al., 2022).
391   2): Typical SSVEP decoders utilize EEG data with filter banks, whereas in this study, we
392   benchmarked our model on raw EEG data in an end-to-end manner, effectively using only one
393
      frequency band, which generally results in suboptimal performance, as shown in Table 1 of (Guney
      et al., 2022).
394
      3): The amount of training data was limited. Unlike other MI datasets, where subjects performed
395
      many trials per class, the SSVEP benchmark dataset contains only four trials per target. In
396   the leave-one-trial classification setting, we used a sliding window approach to increase training
397   examples, which also increases the risk of overfitting.
398
399
      Despite these challenges, the ST-EEGFormer large model still achieved the highest performance,
      reaching above 80% accuracy with a 2-s window, suggesting potential future SSVEP applications.
400
      Possible ways to improve the model can be further pre-training the model with additional SSVEP
401
      datasets and exploring subject-specific fine-tuning strategies. Moreover, the large model performed
402   well across all tasks, showing evidence of using deep, large models and demonstrating a successful
403   approach to overcoming the challenge of limited data availability, which often hampers the
404   development of deep learning models (Ahn et al., 2022; Dong et al., 2023; Khademi et al., 2023;
405   Forenzo et al., 2024). The present study proposes a new approach for developing improved BCI
406   decoders: pre-training the model on a large-scale BCI dataset composed of open public datasets
407   with related tasks, followed by fine-tuning on smaller, application-specific datasets.

      4.2   P ERFORMANCE COMPARISON
410
411
      When comparing the proposed foundation model with BIOT and LaBraM, it is evident that BIOT
412
      performs the worst, with some tasks—such as P300—even underperforming the linear model. This
413
      can be attributed to two primary factors. First, and most significantly, the pre-trained EEG model in
414
      BIOT uses only 18 bipolar channels. This limited spatial coverage hinders its ability to perform well
415
      in tasks requiring broader spatial coverage, such as in the case of P300, where the P300 component
416
      spans the occipital, central, and parts of the parietal regions. Second, BIOT’s pre-training datasets
417
      primarily consist of clinical seizure, sleep, and resting-state EEG data, which differ substantially
418
      from EEG data associated with BCI tasks.
419
420   In contrast, LaBraM performs better than BIOT and ranks as the second-highest-performing model
421   on several datasets, such as the HGD-test and the SSVEP dataset. This highlights the effectiveness
422   of LaBraM’s pre-training process, during which it was exposed to over 2,500 hours of EEG data,
423   including several BCI-specific datasets. However, we acknowledge that this comparison is not en-
424   tirely fair, as BIOT and LaBraM did not undergo the same pre-training steps on the datasets used in
425   this study. Additionally, the pre-training datasets for our model were much smaller than those used
426   for BIOT and LaBraM.
427
428   Another notable difference lies in model size: the open-sourced BIOT and LaBraM models are
429   relatively small compared to the proposed ST-EEGFormer. The reported performance differences
430   provide valuable insights into the importance of pre-training datasets and highlight the potential bias
431   introduced by mismatches between pre-training datasets and downstream tasks. However, since a
      key motivation for foundation models is their generalization ability to unseen datasets, performance


                                                        8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      on such datasets becomes more critical than the specifics of pre-training. This is particularly relevant
433
      when little training data is available for real-life BCI tasks.
434
435   Our experiments demonstrate superior generalization ability of the proposed ST-EEGFormer, which
436   outperformed both BIOT and LaBraM on unseen datasets. Notably, LaBraM also outperformed
437   classic models when limited training data was available, further demonstrating the effectiveness of a
438   pre-trained model. Based on these findings, we recommend that future foundation model developers
439   not only report performance on datasets included in their pre-training (as was done for BIOT) but
440   also evaluate and compare performance on unseen datasets. This approach would encourage the
441   development of models that generalize well across diverse and unseen BCI tasks, even when trained
442   on different pre-training datasets.

447
448   First, we visualized the learned spatial embeddings after MAE pre-training by performing hierar-

      chical clustering analysis using cosine similarity as the distance metric and by identifying different
      channel clusters in a topographic plot. The corresponding figures for the small, base, and large

      models are shown in Appendix figures I.1, I.2, and I.3. In all three models, a consistent pattern

      emerged with two clusters: one cluster in the front and another in the back. However, differences

455   by the model. This demonstrates the effectiveness of learning spatial information from EEG data

457
458   4.3.2    ATTENTION W EIGHTS V ISUALIZATION FOR C LASSIFICATION
459
460   A typical way of analyzing deep learning-based EEG decoders is by drawing learned channel
461
      weights on a topo map (Cecotti & Graser, 2011; Lawhern et al., 2016; Salami et al., 2022; Song
      et al., 2023). Here we demonstrate that by looking at the attention matrix of the ST-EEGFormer, we
462
      can get more interesting visual interpretations directly from the raw EEG data. We performed the
463
      analysis on the seizure dataset (Andrzejak et al., 2002) and two MI-BCI datasets (EEG-MI-BCI (Cho
464   et al., 2017) and Large-MI-5F (Kaya et al., 2018)) as the first shows a simple case of single-channel
465   EEG data with visually distinguishable features while the two MI-BCI datasets involve multiple
466   channels and have more subtle features that are not easy to spot.
467
468   Single-Channel Seizure Classification Visualization First, we visualized some representative
469   trials using the attention rollout method (Abnar & Zuidema, 2020). The corresponding results are
470   shown in Appendix figure I.4. Then we used the gradient rollout approach proposed in (Chefer
471   et al., 2021) to visualize all EEG segments for class-specific explainability. The corresponding
472   results are shown in Appendix figure I.5. Detailed descriptions can be found in Appendix A.6.
473
474   From figure I.4, we observe that the attention rollout with a discard ratio of 0.9 appears to be
475   the most informative. A higher discard ratio highlights only a few segments, while a lower
476   discard ratio tends to attend to all EEG segments, potentially diluting the focus on key areas.
477   Interestingly, different head fusion methods highlight different regions, indicating that the model
478   focuses on distinct features across different heads. Figure I.4 e) provides a clear example of how
479   the model focuses on abnormal EEG signals, with each segment corresponding to a seizure spike
480   being prominently highlighted. This finding suggests promising potential for explainable seizure
481   detection and diagnosis applications.
482
483   In Figure I.5, the use of min-fusion often results in all segments being highlighted (as seen in
484   figures I.5 a, b, c, e, g, h, i), due to the final rollout weights being too small. After scaling the weights
485   to the range of 0–1, they become inflated, leading to a failure in identifying the most important
      segments. On the other hand, the mean and max gradient rollouts highlight different segments of


                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      interest within the signal. However, since this dataset contains only selected signals with clear,
487   continuous features, a more realistic evaluation is needed in future studies. Such an evaluation could
488   leverage gradient rollout to detect specific intervals where one of the classes of interest occurs during
489   continuous EEG monitoring.
490
491   Multi-Channel MI-BCI Classification Visualization For multi-channel EEG data classification,
492   we first compared the learned spatial filters from the linear model, EEGNet, and EEG Conformer
493   with the ST-EEGFormer attention rollout weights. A detailed description of this comparison can
494   be found in Appendix A.6.5. The results are shown in figures I.6 and I.7. Figure I.8 to I.14 also
495   visualize the attention weights on top of the raw EEG signal along with a spatiotemporal topo map.
496
497   As observed in the figures, all convolution-based kernels fail to capture spatially localized
498   information, as each spatial filter is spread across the scalp, with multiple red regions indicating
499   that the filters are less sparse compared to the attention rollout results from the ST-EEGFormer. In
500   the ST-EEGFormer, important regions are clustered and sparse. It is difficult to draw meaningful
501   conclusions from the spatial filters learned by the convolutional kernels, whereas the attention
502
      rollout results provide clear indications of important channels.
503
504   It is also worth noting that head fusion may not be the optimal approach for the analysis. Both the
505   mean and max head-fused plots in figure I.6 d) show a similar pattern that closely resembles the
506   pattern of head 11 in figure I.6 e). The differences observed between different heads in figure I.6 e)
507   highlight that each attention head learns to focus on different regions of the scalp. Similar results
508
      can be obtained in figure I.7 d) and e), in which each head has a unique pattern, whereas the head
      fused patterns yield similar regions of interest.

      4.4   L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORKS
512
      Due to computational resource constraints, this study did not attempt to search for the most optimal
513   combination of hyperparameters, such as embedding dimension size, the number of encoder layers,
514   or the pre-training mask ratio. Additionally, to benchmark the effectiveness of the model, we limited
515   our selection to eight datasets. Expanding the pre-training dataset, especially with high-density EEG
516   data, could further enhance generalization. While this study demonstrated promising offline popula-
517   tion classification results, future work should explore the model’s online performance in real-world
518   subject-dependent BCI settings. We believe the demonstrated effectiveness of the ST-EEGFormer
519   will facilitate BCI decoders, and encourage the use of large models in the field. Furthermore, the
520   experiment on seizure detection with intracranial data suggests a clinical application for monitoring
521   epilepsy patients. Future works can focus on exploring different tokenization methods, developing
522   lightweight models, and studying possible strategies to develop subject-dependent models from the
523   pre-trained population model.

530   5     C ONCLUSION
531
      We propose a novel yet simple ST-EEGFormer architecture as a foundation model for general EEG-
532   based BCIs, designed to be insensitive to channel configurations and recording setups. The model
533   leverages self-supervised pre-training on open public EEG datasets through a masked autoencoder
534   task. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed model outperforms the classic benchmark
535   model (EEGNet), the state-of-the-art model (EEG Conformer), and two BCI foundation models
536   (BIOT and LaBraM) in population decoding tasks across diverse BCI applications. Moreover, the
537   ST-EEGFormer learned robust EEG representations that generalized effectively to unseen datasets,
538   even with limited training data. Additionally, we analyzed the attention matrix for improved model
539   interpretation and visualization. We believe this study establishes a strong foundation for future
      research into leveraging pre-trained large models to advance EEG BCI decoding performance.

867   A.1     R ELATED WORKS
868
869   Supervised learning in BCI
870   Early attempts in applying deep learning models to BCI decoding primarily focused on CNNs,
871   utilizing 1D-convolutional kernels for spatial and temporal filtering. Notable architectures include
872
      ConvNet (Cecotti & Graser, 2011) for P300 classification and both shallow and deep ConvNets
      for motor imagery classification (Schirrmeister et al., 2017), EEGNet (Lawhern et al., 2016),
873
      which builds upon these earlier architectures, demonstrated superior performance across various
874
      EEG-BCI tasks, including P300, error-related negativity (ERN), event-related potentials (ERP),
875   movement-related cortical potential (MRCP), and sensory-motor rhythm (SMR) (Lawhern et al.,
876   2016). Since its introduction, EEGNet has been regarded as a classic benchmark model, inspiring
877   numerous subsequent studies that incorporated specific modules to create its variants (Ingolfsson
878   et al., 2020; Riyad et al., 2020; Salami et al., 2022). More recently, advances in attention-based
879   transformer models from natural language processing, e.g., BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), and
880   computer vision, e.g., ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), have inspired BCI researchers, among which
881   is the EEG Conformer (Song et al., 2023), one of the most successful models as it achieved
882   state-of-the-art performance on motor imagery and emotion datasets. However, these models still
883
      rely on convolutional kernels for feature extraction, making transferring them between different
      datasets cumbersome. the spatial filters in these models have a fixed size of RC×1 , where C
884
      represents the number of EEG channels, which can vary significantly across datasets. Additionally,
885
      these models employ temporal filters of size R1×T and pooling layers to extract temporal features,
886   making them dependent on the input size, which also varies between tasks and datasets. As a
887   result, these models are typically trained on individual datasets and are not easily transferable to
888   other datasets. Lashgari et al. (Lashgari et al., 2021) proposed an architecture to use CNN as a
889   single-channel feature extractor followed by the attention mechanism to learn from datasets with
890   varying channels. Spatiotemporal patching was explored by Xie, etal (Xie et al., 2022), in which the
891   authors explored various ways of applying the transformer to a motor imagery dataset, achieving
892   state-of-the-art performance. To solve the cross-subject variability issue, one can use a generative
893   adversarial network (GAN) to learn invariant representations across subjects (Özdenizci et al.,
894   2020), The same problem was also addressed using contrastive learning to learn subject-invariant
895
      EEG representations for cross-subject emotion recognition (Shen et al., 2021). Kobler et al. used
896
      multi-source batch normalization directly on the space of SPD matrices from EEG signals to reduce
897
      inter-subject variability issue (Kobler et al., 2022). Although these approaches show promising
898
      results, they still focus on specific tasks and remain challenging to transfer to other datasets and
899
      tasks.
900
901   Self-supervised pre-training in BCI
902   Several attempts have been made to utilize different sources of EEG data to create large datasets for
903   representation learning. This typically involves pre-training tasks. For example, Jiang et al. applied
904
      contrastive learning for EEG-based sleep staging classification (Jiang et al., 2021). Mohsenvand
      et al. extended the SimCLR framework (Chen et al., 2020) and combined multiple datasets to
905
      learn data representations (Mohsenvand et al., 2020) by introducing channel encoders to process
906
      one channel at a time. However, their model was only tested on the SEED emotion dataset (Duan
907   et al., 2013; Zheng & Lu, 2015), the normal vs. abnormal EEG dataset (López et al., 2015), and
908   the sleep stage classification dataset (Goldberger et al., 2000), which are large, non-BCI-related
909   datasets. BENDR (Kostas et al., 2021) employs contrastive learning to extract representations from
910   raw EEG signals. However, as the model is adapted from Wav2vec 2.0 (Baevski et al., 2020), it
911   faces challenges in transferring effectively between different datasets without discarding non-shared
912   channels. Chien, et al. applied a masked autoencoder to learn EEG representations combined with
913   a CNN-based feature extractor (Chien et al., 2022). Although the model performed well on the
914   sleep stage dataset, it is still unknown whether it could perform well on small BCI datasets, and
915   furthermore, the CNN feature extractor is hard to transfer between datasets. NeuroGPT faces a
916   similar limitation, as its EEG encoder has a fixed channel constraint, making it difficult to adapt
917   to varying channel configurations (Cui et al., 2024). BrainBERT (Wang et al., 2023) focuses on
      learning single-channel representations from stereo-electroencephalographic (SEEG) spectrograms,


                                                       17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      enabling its use as a feature extractor for downstream tasks. More recently, Cai and Zeng (Cai &
919   Zeng, 2024) employed MAE pre-training task on small segments consisting of all EEG channels
920   across two motor imagery datasets, BCI Competition IV Dataset 2a and 2b (Tangermann et al.,
921   2012) and the PhysioNet EEG Dataset (Goldberger et al., 2000). However, since their encoder
922   module works with patches of EEG signals in fixed channels and time length, transferring between
923   datasets remains unrealistic and challenging.
924
      BCI foundation models
925   Biosignal Transformer (BIOT) (Yang et al., 2023) is the first biosignal encoding model that can
926   handle biosignals of various formats. It was first pre-trained on multiple EEG, ECG, and sensory
927   datasets by first dropping out channels and part of tokens from the remaining channels, and tried
928   to predict the correct original tokens using a contrastive loss. The EEG datasets used in this study
929   are resting-state, sleep EEG, and clinical seizure datasets, focusing on clinical EEG applications.
930   Very recently, the Large Brain Model (LaBraM) (Jiang et al., 2024) was proposed to enable
931   cross-dataset learning and was trained on 2500 hours of EEG data. The model pre-training involves
932   two stages, first to train a temporal encoder and a neural tokenizer that compress the EEG signal
933
      into vector-quantized encodings by reconstructing the spectral amplitude and phase of input signals.
      Then in the second stage, the model is trained to reconstruct the encodings from a masked input.
934
      Although LaBraM was pre-trained using EEG data from various sources, the model was only
935
      benchmarked on TUAB (abnormal detection), TUEV (different event type classification), SEED-V
936   (emotion recognition), and MoBI (gait prediction) datasets, all large datasets that are not classic
937   BCI control paradigms. EEGFormer (Chen et al., 2024) was proposed which relies on a masked
938   autoencoder approach to be pre-trained on the Temple University EEG Corpus (TUH Corpus) (Obeid
939   & Picone, 2016) and onwards fine-tuned on downstream datasets. Although they reported successful
940   results, their benchmarks were limited to high-quality clinical EEG datasets thereby focusing on
941   similar clinical applications. Additionally, the number of channels was restricted, using only 24
942   or 36 channels from the TUH Corpus dataset. Moreover, this approach also required applying fast
943   Fourier transformation (FFT) to obtain frequency domain amplitude as the input feature, rather than
944
      a pure end-to-end approach only using raw EEG signals as the input. A more detailed comparison
      of various models with our proposed model is presented in Appendix Table A.1. As highlighted in
945
      the table, the ST-EEGFormer stands out due to its simple architecture, straightforward pre-training
946
      process, focus on BCI applications, and ease of use.

951   A.2.1    P RE - TRAINING DATASETS
952
      The MAE reconstruction task was conducted on eight public datasets. These datasets include:
953
      1) EEG-MI-BCI (Cho et al., 2017): This dataset contains 52 subjects performing 2-class imagined
954
      left and right-hand movements, recorded with 64 EEG channels using a Biosemi ActiveTwo system.
955   It includes approximately 5,000 trials of 3-second motor imagery (MI) data per class.
956   2) HGD (Schirrmeister et al., 2017): The High Gamma Dataset comprises 20 subjects performing
957   4-second trials of executed movements with four classes (left hand, right hand, both feet, and rest).
958   The data were recorded with 128 high-density EEG caps (WaveGuard Original, ANT, Enschede,
959   NL) and sampled at 5 kHz using a NeurOne amplifier (Mega Electronics Ltd, Kuopio, FI). It includes
960   roughly 3,000 trials per class.
961   3) BCI-Comp-IV2a (Tangermann et al., 2012): This dataset includes nine subjects performing
962   four-second trials of four classes (imagined left hand, right hand, feet, and tongue movements),
963
      recorded with 22-electrode EEG caps. It contains a training set and a test set from two separate
      sessions, each with roughly 600 trials per class.
964
      4) BCI-Comp-IV2b (Tangermann et al., 2012): This dataset consists of nine subjects performing
965
      2-class imagined left and right-hand movements, recorded with three EEG channels. It contains a
966   training set of approximately 1,800 trials per class and a separate test set of approximately 1400
967   trials per class.
968   5) Large-MI-Classic (Kaya et al., 2018): This dataset comprises 13 subjects performing 1-second
969   trials of six classes (imagined left hand, right hand, left foot, right foot, tongue, and rest). The data
970   were recorded with 19-channel EEG caps plus 2 ground lead channels (Electro-Cap International,
971   USA) and were mostly sampled at 200 Hz, with some recordings sampled at 1000 Hz using the
      EEG-1200 system. In total, it includes approximately 50,000 trials (different classes have an unequal

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       number of trials).
973    6) Large-MI-5F (Kaya et al., 2018): From the same study as 5) but different experiments, this
974    dataset comprises 13 subjects performing 1-second trials of five classes of finger movements
975    (imagined thumb, index, middle, ring, pinkie). In total, it includes around 18000 trials.
976    7) P300 (Won et al., 2022): This dataset consists of 55 participants performing a P300 speller
977    experiment and 50 participants performing a rapid serial visual representation (RSVP). In total,
978    it includes 99000 training P300 trials and 277200 test trials.
979    8) SSVEP (Liu et al., 2020): This dataset consists of 70 participants performing cue-guided SSVEP
980    target-selecting experiments, comprising 40 flickering stimuli ranging between 8 Hz to 15.8 Hz with
981
       an interval of 0.2 Hz. For each target, it contains 20 trials of 5-s stimulation data.
982    The MI datasets were selected based on their size and quality as reported in the latest review
983    paper (Gwon et al., 2023), with datasets 3 and 4 chosen due to their status as classic benchmark
984    datasets. The P300 dataset was included as it is one of the largest available P300 datasets, and the
985    SSVEP dataset was selected for its widespread use as a benchmark in SSVEP research.

987    A.2.2   DATA PREPROCESSING
988
989    All datasets underwent the following minimal preprocessing steps:
990    1) Power-line noise filtering: Visual inspection of the power spectrum density plots was
991    conducted for each dataset. For those with visibly strong power-line noise, The function
992    mne.filter.notch filter() (Python 3.8.19, MNE 1.6.1) was applied to remove it.
993    2) Broadband band-pass filtering: A bandpass filter with cutoff frequencies at 1 Hz and 64 Hz was
994    applied to all channels using mne.filter.filter data() (Python 3.8.19, MNE 1.6.1). The
995
       filter was designed with a windowed FIR design (fir design=‘firwin’).
       3) Downsampling: All channels were downsampled to 128 Hz from their original sampling
996
       frequency using the mne.filter.resample() function with default settings.
997
       4) Standardization: Each channel was standardized to have a mean value of 0 and a standard
998    deviation of 1.

1001   A.2.3   DATA SEGMENTATIONS FOR PRE - TRAINING
1002
1003
       Some datasets provide continuous EEG recordings while some datasets contain only task-related
       epochs. Therefore, the following data segmentation approach was used to generate examples for
1004
       pre-training:
1005
       1): For EEG-MI-BCI (Cho et al., 2017), HGD (Schirrmeister et al., 2017), BCI-Comp-IV2a (Tanger-
1006   mann et al., 2012), BCI-Comp-IV2b (Tangermann et al., 2012), P300 (Won et al., 2022), these
1007   datasets consist of full continuous recordings for each participant. Pre-training examples were
1008   generated using a sliding window of 5 seconds in length with a hop size of 0.25 seconds, resulting
1009   in over 360,000, 70,000, 190,000, 380,000, and 620,000 EEG segments, respectively.
1010   2): For Large-MI-Classic (Kaya et al., 2018), and Large-MI-5F (Kaya et al., 2018), since these two
1011   datasets jointly represent one of the largest datasets used in this study, a 5-second sliding window
1012   with a 0.5-second hop size was used to balance the number of pre-training examples, instead of
1013   the previously used 0.25-second hop size. Combined, they contribute approximately 500,000 EEG
1014
       segments.
       3) For SSVEP (Liu et al., 2020), this dataset provides only 5-second stimulation epochs. A small
1015
       sliding window of 2 seconds with a small hop size of 0.125 seconds was used to generate pre-training
1016
       examples, resulting in approximately 170,000 EEG segments.

1023   However, since multiple datasets were combined in this step, a subsampling strategy was used to

1374   A.5.1.1    DATASET DESCRIPTION
1375
1376   This Bonn dataset (Andrzejak et al., 2002) consists of human expert-selected single-channel EEG
1377   data from five healthy volunteers and five individuals with epilepsy. The data are divided into two
1378   classes for healthy volunteers, including scalp EEG segments recorded while the volunteers were
1379   relaxed and awake with eyes closed and open, respectively (Dataset A and B, referred to as “Eyes
1380   Closed” and “Eyes Open” in figure 3b). Three classes of data are from epileptic patients, consisting
1381   of intracranial EEG (iEEG) segments recorded during pre-surgical evaluation. Specifically, one class
1382
       contains interictal iEEG segments from the epileptogenic zone in the opposite hemisphere (dataset
       C, referred to as “NSeizure-Opposite” in figure 3b), while another class includes interictal iEEG
1383
       segments from the epileptogenic zone itself (dataset D, referred to as “NSeizure-Epileptogenic” in
1384
       figure 3b). The final class consists of iEEG segments recorded from the epileptogenic zone during
1385   seizure activity (dataset E, referred to as “Seizure” in figure 3b). Each subset contains 100 single-
1386   channel EEG segments, each 23.6 seconds in duration (4096 samples). The data were sampled at
1387   173.61 Hz, and any artifacts caused by muscle activity or eye movement were manually removed
1388   by the database owners after visual inspection.

1391   A.5.1.2    E XPERIMENT DETAILS
1392
1393   In this experiment, the objective was to evaluate the pre-trained model’s performance on previously
1394   unseen abnormal EEG data. Two conditions were considered: first, using the pre-trained model
1395
       directly; and second, further calibrating the model on the new dataset by performing the MAE
       pre-training task. For the first condition, we employed the pre-trained small, base, and large models,
1396
       which underwent end-to-end average token fine-tuning. Additionally, we applied linear probing on
1397
       the base model with the average token. For the second condition, we first performed the MAE pre-
1398   training task on the base model for 20 iterations, followed by end-to-end average token fine-tuning
1399   and average token linear probing. To better evaluate model performance, we varied the amount of
1400   training data from 5% to 60% of the total available data. With only 5% of the training data, there
1401   were 225 training examples across 5 classes. Additionally, we experimented to assess the effect of
1402   the mask ratio. In this experiment, the base model underwent several MAE calibration tasks with
1403   varying mask ratios and was subsequently fine-tuned and linearly probed on the same task as the
       previous experiment.

1405
1406   Minimal data preprocessing was performed to evaluate the model’s generalization ability. First,

1409   to note that the original data were band-pass filtered by the data provider between 0.5 and 85 Hz,
1410

1411
       and the impact of calibration. For MAE calibration, each 23.6-second recording was segmented
1412

1414   into 3-second segments, but with a 0.5-second overlap, producing a total of 4,500 classification

1442   Slightly different from the seizure classification experiment, where the objective was to assess

1446   applicable. To address this, we conducted the following two-step experiment: First, we trained
1447   our models using data from the first session, varying the amount of training data in the same manner
1448   as in the seizure classification experiment, and evaluated the model’s performance on the remaining
1449   data from the first session. Then, for each model, we performed zero-shot testing on all data from
1450
       the second session to assess cross-session performance. For the base model, we also evaluated the

1477
1478   Attention rollout tracks the information flow from the input layer to the final layer in a transformer
1479   model through Eq E.1 (Abnar & Zuidema, 2020), where, Ã is the attention rollout, and A(li ) the
1480
       raw attention matrix in layer i. In order to focus on the most important tokens while ignoring less
       relevant ones, we apply a discard ratio that retains only the largest rollout weights at each layer.
1481
       For instance, a discard ratio of 0.9 will keep only the top 10% of the largest weights, setting the
1482
       remaining weights to zero. After calculating the rollout, each head produces a weight matrix. The
1483   final weights are obtained by fusing the weights across different heads, using one of the following
1484   methods: mean fusion, where the final weight is the average of all head weights; max fusion, where
1485   the final weight is the maximum value across all heads; and min fusion, where the final weight is the
1486   minimum value across all heads.

       A.6.2     G RADIENT ATTENTION ROLLOUT
1494
       The gradient rollout method for attention visualization in class-specific explanations aims to
1495
       interpret the contribution of individual input features to the model’s decision for a particular class.
1496
       Similar to standard attention rollout, it recursively calculates a weighted rollout score, but in this
1497   case, the gradients with respect to the class of interest are used as weights. The gradients are
1498   obtained by selectively backpropagating the output score corresponding to the chosen class. This
1499   process replaces the A(li ) term in Eq E.1 with a weighted term, where the attention value Aij is
1500   multiplied by the corresponding gradient gradij .
