009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Training robotic manipulation policies traditionally requires numerous demonstra-
012            tions and/or environmental rollouts. While recent Imitation Learning (IL) and Re-
013            inforcement Learning (RL) methods have reduced the number of required demon-
014            strations, they still rely on expert knowledge to collect high-quality data, limit-
015            ing scalability and accessibility. We propose S KETCH -T O -S KILL, a novel frame-
016            work that leverages human-drawn 2D sketch trajectories to bootstrap and guide
017            RL for robotic manipulation. Our approach extends beyond previous sketch-
018
               based methods, which were primarily focused on imitation learning or policy
               conditioning, limited to specific trained tasks. S KETCH -T O -S KILL employs a
019
               Sketch-to-3D Trajectory Generator that translates 2D sketches into 3D trajecto-
020
               ries, which are then used to autonomously collect initial demonstrations. We
021            utilize these sketch-generated demonstrations in two ways: to pre-train an ini-
022            tial policy through behavior cloning and to refine this policy through RL with
023            guided exploration. Experimental results demonstrate that S KETCH -T O -S KILL
024            achieves ∼96% of the performance of the baseline model that leverages teleoper-
025            ated demonstration data, while exceeding the performance of a pure reinforcement
026            learning policy by ∼170%, only from sketch inputs. This makes robotic manip-
027            ulation learning more accessible and potentially broadens its applications across
028            various domains.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Robots are increasingly being deployed in dynamic environments, where they must perform a wide
      range of tasks with precision and adaptability. One of the key challenges in enabling robots to learn
034
      new skills lies in specifying complex, task-specific behaviors. Learning from Demonstration (LfD)
035
      (Billard & Grollman (2013)) has become a widely used approach, allowing robots to acquire novel
036   motions by imitating expert-provided trajectories. However, collecting demonstration data for LfD
037   is challenging, particularly for high degree-of-freedom (DOF) robots performing manipulation.
038
039
      Traditional methods such as kinesthetic teaching and teleoperation while useful also have challenges
      with safety risks, scalability, and the need for specialized expertise (Chan et al., 2014; Ferraguti et al.,
040
      2015; Bimbo et al., 2017). Recent approaches, such as using manually-operated grippers instru-
041
      mented with smartphone apps (Shafiullah et al., 2023) and Virtual Reality (VR) based teleoperation
042   systems (Kamijo et al., 2024), offer more intuitive hardware interfaces for collecting demonstrations.
043   However, they require specialized hardware which may limit their flexibility and accessibility. Re-
044   cently there has been interest in leveraging an innate human ability to communicate spatial ideas and
045   motions through simple sketches. For example, a quick sketch of a path can easily communicate the
046   intended movement for navigating toward a goal location.
047
      Researchers have begun to explore this promising direction. RT-Trajectory (Gu et al., 2023) intro-
048   duced the notion of sketches and showed how to use coarse trajectory sketches for policy condi-
049   tioning in Imitation Learning (IL). RT-Sketch (Sundaresan et al., 2024) extended this concept to
050   leverage hand-drawn sketches of the entire environment for goal-conditioned IL. These methods
051   demonstrated the potential of utilizing sketches in robotics, but they were primarily focused on IL
052   and biased towards tasks they were specifically trained on. Zhi et al. (2023) expanded this idea
053   with diagrammatic teaching, where users instruct robots by sketching motion trajectories directly
      on 2D images of the scene. Their approach uses density estimation and ray tracing to reconstruct

      2     R ELATED W ORK
097
098   Learning from Demonstration (LfD). LfD (Billard & Grollman, 2013) is a key method in robot
099
      learning, allowing robots to acquire skills through expert demonstrations, bypassing the complexities
      of action programming and cost function design (chaandar Ravichandar et al., 2020). Kinesthetic
100
      teaching, where an expert physically guides the robot while its movements are recorded, is widely
101
      used in methods like DMPs (Kober & Peters, 2009; Ijspeert et al., 2013), Probabilistic Movement
102   Primitives (Paraschos et al., 2015), and stable dynamical systems (Khansari-Zadeh & Billard, 2011;
103   Mohammad Khansari-Zadeh & Billard, 2014; Bevanda et al., 2022). However, it is labor-intensive
104   and challenging to scale. Teleoperation (Si et al., 2021), where users control robots remotely, offers
105   more flexibility but can be complex and requires expertise to operate. VR interfaces (Zhang et al.,
106   2018; Kamijo et al., 2024) provide a more immersive alternative but depend on specialized hard-
107   ware. To overcome these limitations, recent research has introduced more accessible approaches,
      like sketch-based demonstrations (Drolet et al., 2024).

108
      Sketches in Robotics. Sketches have become a powerful tool in computer vision, aiding tasks
109   like scene understanding (Chowdhury et al., 2023b) and object detection (Chowdhury et al., 2023a;
110   Bhunia et al., 2023). RT-Sketch (Sundaresan et al., 2024) first explored hand-drawn sketches for
111   goal-conditioned imitation learning (IL), using them to define tasks intuitively. RT-Trajectory (Gu
112   et al., 2023) extended this by using trajectory sketches as IL policy conditioning, either drawn by
113   users or generated by a Large Language Model from task descriptions. Similarly, the Diagrammatic
114   Teaching framework (Zhi et al., 2023) uses density estimation and ray tracing to reconstruct 3D
115   trajectories from the sketches. These methods, however, only use sketches as conditioning for task
116   completion, and thus do not generalize beyond the tasks where the sketches are provided.
117   Demonstration-Enhanced Strategies for Efficient RL. Incorporating demonstration data in RL
118   can improve sample efficiency, especially in environments where rewards are sparse. Methods such
119   as Reinforcement Learning from Prior Data (RLPD) (Smith et al., 2022), Imitation Bootstrapped
120   RL (IBRL) (Hu et al., 2023) and NAVINACT (Bhaskar et al., 2024) take advantage of prior demon-
121   strations by embedding them into the agent’s replay buffer. During training, these examples are
122   oversampled, offering the agent more frequent exposure to expert-guided trajectories. Such ap-
123
      proaches significantly improve learning speed and performance, particularly in continuous control
      tasks where learning from scratch can be prohibitively slow and inefficient (Yu et al., 2024). Our
124
      research expands upon these techniques by exploring how sketch-based trajectories can be used as
125
      an additional source of prior data in RL.

128   3     S KETCH -T O -S KILL
129
130   Our approach bootstraps robot learning from trajectory sketches, significantly lowering the barrier
131   to entry for robotic task specification. This section details our three-stage method: (1) training a
132   Sketch-to-3D Trajectory Generator, (2) obtaining 3D trajectories and execution experiences through
133
      the Generator and open-loop servoing, (3) pre-training an initial robotic manipulation policy through
      behavior cloning, and refining the policy through reinforcement learning with guided exploration.
134
      By integrating intuitive human input with powerful learning algorithms, our approach aims to create
135
      more accessible and adaptable robotic learning systems.
136
137
      3.1    S KETCH - TO -3D T RAJECTORY G ENERATOR
138
139   Our method begins with a Sketch-to-3D Trajectory Generator, T , that translates a pair of 2D sketch
140   images (I 1 , I 2 ) obtained from different viewpoints into corresponding 3D robot trajectory ξg . To
141   train this generator, we use a dataset consisting of 3D robot end-effector trajectories along with their
142   2D sketches from two viewpoints. These trajectories can be obtained from various sources, such as
143   play data where the robot executes sequences of actions. Sketches during inference can be provided
144   by a human on RGB images of the scene, as shown in Figure 4. However, the sketches fed as input
145   to the generator are 2D projections on blank backgrounds, with green and red dots representing the
146   start and end points respectively, and yellow lines for the trajectory (see Figure 2 for an example).
147
      By focusing solely on the trajectory information without additional scene complexity, our model
      can efficiently learn to encode the dual-view sketches and decode them into the corresponding 3D
148
      trajectory.
149
150   The generator uses a neural network to map dual-view 2D sketches to 3D trajectories, where we
151   adopt a hybrid architecture combining a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma, 2013) and a Mul-
152   tilayer Perceptron (MLP), as illustrated in Figure 2. The VAE encodes sketches from two viewpoints,
153
      ideally orthogonal, to resolve depth ambiguity and capture essential trajectory features. The MLP
      decoder generates B-spline (Prautzsch et al., 2002) control points C ∈ Rncp ×3 from the latent rep-
154
      resentation, which we then use to interpolate smooth 3D trajectories. We adopted uniform knots and
155
      pre-compute the B-spline parametrization matrix W ∈ Rntp ×ncp to reduce computational complex-
156   ity and facilitate efficient backpropagation. The calculation of W only depends on the uniform knot
157   vector u and the desired number of points ntp in the generated trajectory, and can be pre-calculated
158   using the Cox-de Boor recursion formula (also known as de Boor’s algorithm de Boor (1977), see
159   Appendix A for details). Then the final trajectory generation is simply a matrix multiplication:
160   ξg ∈ Rntp ×3 = W · C. With the generated control point parameter C, we can also easily generate
161   trajectories of varying density from the same parameters, making our method adaptable to different
      task requirements.

177   Our training process uses a multi-component loss function L = Ltraj + Lsketch + Lkld , where
178   Ltraj handles trajectory reconstruction, Lsketch manages sketch reconstruction (Mean Square Er-
179   ror), and Lkld is KL-divergence for latent space regularization (Figure 2). This ensures accurate

      trajectory generation while preserving sketch fidelity and latent space structure. We also applied
      data augmentation to both the sketch images and the trajectories to enhance the model’s robustness
181
      and generalization (more details can be found in Appendix B).
182
183   We can use the trained Sketch-to-3D Trajectory Generator, T , to generate demonstrations {ξD }
184   for learning new tasks using sketches drawn by a human. Specifically, the human draws trajectory
185   sketches on two views of RGB images captured from the initial task state. This is similar to how
186
      human-drawn sketches are generated in prior works (Gu et al., 2023; Zhi et al., 2023). These paired
      sketch images, {(I 1 , I 2 )}, are input into our trained generator, which produces corresponding 3D
187
      trajectories, {ξg }, serving as the basis for guiding the robot’s actions. We can also generate more
188
      than one trajectory from the same pair of sketches by adding controlled noise to the latent repre-
189   sentation. Then we proceed to collect demonstrations for manipulation policy learning. We execute
190   these trajectories on the robotic arm using open-loop servoing, which enables precise trajectory fol-
191   lowing based on pre-computed motor commands. During execution, we record a demonstration
192   dataset {ξD = {(pt , ot , at )}Tt=1 } at a fixed frequency, where pt = (x, y, z)t denotes the robot’s

194   T is the total number of timesteps per demonstration. The collected demonstrations, which do not
195   need to be optimal, follow the intended path while capturing the robot’s actual behavior in the target
196   environment. They serve as an effective starting point for bootstrapping the policy learning process,
197
      offering initial guidance grounded in the robot’s real-world performance.

      3.2   P OLICY L EARNING
244
      We now describe the policy learning of the S KETCH -T O -S KILL algorithm (given in Algorithm 1).
245   Taking as input the demonstration data {ξD } collected from our Sketch-to-3D Trajectory Generator
246   T and through open-loop serving (lines 4–5), our approach combines IL and RL to effectively boot-
247   strap and refine the policy. Specifically, we build upon the Imitation Bootstrapped Reinforcement
248   Learning (IBRL) framework (Hu et al., 2023), integrating our sketch-based trajectories to guide and
249   constraint policy search space.
250
      In IBRL we replace traditional real-world demonstrations with sketch-generated demonstrations.
251   Initially, these sketch-based demonstrations are used to train an IL policy (line 6), which serves
252   as a coarse approximation of the task. Although these sketches do not capture every fine detail of
253   manipulation (e.g., gripper closing/opening actions or exact force control), our hypothesis posits that
254   they still carry significant, actionable information that can effectively guide the learning process in
255   reinforcement learning (RL). We leverage this information in RL in two ways (as shown in Fig. 3):
256
257     (1) Bootstrap RL with Sketch-Generated Demos: Even though sketch-generated trajectories are
258
            not as detailed as teleoperated demonstrations, they provide a foundational blueprint of the
            task. We leverage these initial trajectories to bootstrap our RL algorithm, giving it a prelim-
259
            inary direction and reducing the cold start problem common in RL scenarios. This use of
260
            imperfect demonstrations is intended to establish an initial policy that avoids random explo-
261         ration at the outset, making subsequent training more focused and efficient.
262
        (2) Guide Exploration During RL: As the agent progresses in its learning, the sketch-generated
263
            trajectories continue to serve as a guide, shaping the exploration strategy. Instead of relying
264         on these trajectories as definitive guides, we treat them as rough outlines that suggest areas
265         of the task space worth exploring. This guided exploration helps concentrate the agent’s
266         learning efforts on potentially fruitful regions of the action space, thus optimizing the learning
267         speed and improving the relevance of the experiences gathered.
268
269   In both steps, the use of sketch-generated trajectories acknowledges their limitations—they are not
      treated as ground truth but as valuable signals to help bootstrap RL and guide exploration throughout

      the learning process. For the RL algorithm, we employ TD3 (Fujimoto et al., 2018), an off-policy
271   algorithm known for its sample efficiency. In our approach, the replay buffer is initialized with
272   the sketch-generated demonstration trajectories (line 8), which provide an initial foundation for
273   learning and is later updated with online experiences as the agent interacts with the environment.
274   This combination allows the agent to refine its policy through both sketch-generated demonstration
275   data ξD and real-world interaction (line 13).
276
      To further enhance the learning process and maintain consistency with the sketch-generated tra-
277   jectories, we introduce a discriminator-based guided exploration mechanism (Kang et al., 2018).
278   This discriminator, Dψ , is trained to distinguish between trajectories produced by our Sketch-to-3D
279   Trajectory Generator and those generated by the current policy:

      4.2   C OMPARISONS WITH BASELINES
358
359
      In this section, we conduct extensive experiments in MetaWorld (Yu et al., 2019) to answer Q2: can
360   S KETCH -T O -S KILL utilize sketch-generated demonstrations to achieve comparable performance
361   to traditional methods using high-quality demonstration data? Specifically, we compare S KETCH -
362   T O -S KILL with: (1) IBRL (Hu et al., 2023), a strong baseline that utilizes traditional high-quality
363   demonstration data (rather than sketches as what our method uses), and (2) TD3 (Fujimoto et al.,
364   2018), a state-of-the-art pure RL approach without using any demonstrations. We hypothesize that
365   although the sketches have only partial information (namely, 2D projections of 3D trajectories and
366   no gripper information), we can still generate good enough demonstration data to perform compara-
367   bly with the baseline that uses full demonstrations. We show that to be the case in these experiments.
368   We perform evaluations on six tasks from the MetaWorld benchmark, namely Coffeepush,
369   Boxclose, Buttonpress, Reach, Reachwall, and ButtonpressTopdownwall, each using

526   5      C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
527
528
529   We present S KETCH -T O -S KILL that uses 2D sketches to improve the efficiency of learning a ma-
530   nipulation skill. While prior work has shown the power of sketches in IL, we are the first to show
531
      how to do so using RL. The key ideas are to train a 2D sketch to 3D trajectory generator whose
      output is used to bootstrap learning of the RL policy and used as an extra exploration guidance
532
      signal, all of which contributes to improved efficiency. There are several avenues for future work.
533
      S KETCH -T O -S KILL currently does not include any gripper information or timing information in the
534   sketches. While we have shown that even without this information, we can learn effectively, an im-
535   mediate line of work would be to include this in the sketches. Fundamentally, this does not change
536   the Sketch-To-3D trajectory generator model which would now also have to predict the gripper state
537   and time parameterization of the trajectory. The second avenue for future work is to generate the
538   sketches from sources other than humans. For example, Gu et al. (2023) showed how to generate
539   sketches using a Vision Language Model given a natural language description of the task. We can
      directly incorporate such sketches into our framework.

      B    S KETCH - TO -3D T RAJECTORY G ENERATOR A RCHITECTURE
731
      OVERVIEW OF THE M ODEL
732
733   The proposed model converts 2D image sketches into 3D motion trajectories using a Variational
734   Autoencoder (VAE) combined with a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP). The VAE encoder processes
735   64 × 64 pixel 2D sketches (3 channels) into a latent vector (dv = 32), while the decoder reconstructs
736   the sketches to retain essential features for trajectory generation. The latent space outputs the mean
737   (µ) and variance (σ 2 ), sampled using the reparameterization trick.
738   The MLP takes the latent vectors from two sketches, concatenates them, and generates 3D control
739   points for B-spline trajectory interpolation. The MLP takes an input of size (dv × 2), processes it
740   through hidden layers [1024, 512, 256], and outputs ncp × 3. The generated 3D control points are
741   then used for B-spline interpolation to produce smooth trajectories.
742
743   I NITIALIZATION , R EGULARIZATION , AND H YPERPARAMETERS
744
745   We initialize all network parameters using Xavier initialization. Regularization is done with
746   Kullback-Leibler Divergence (KLD), using a loss function that combines Sketch Reconstruction
747
      Loss, KLD Loss, and Trajectory Loss. The Sketch Reconstruction Loss is the MSE loss on sketch im-
      ages, the Trajectory Loss computes the MSE between predicted and ground truth trajectories, where
748
      the ground truth trajectory is first densely fitted and resampled to ensure uniform point spacing,
749
      and the KLD Loss applies Kullback-Leibler Divergence for regularization. Key hyperparameters
750   include:
751
752          • Image size: 64 × 64 pixels
753          • Latent dimension: 32
754
             • Number of control points: 20
755
             • B-spline degree: 3

848
      Meta-World tasks. The task is illustrated in Figure 10 and briefly described below:
849   ButtonPress: The objective is to press the Button, with its initial position randomized within a 20cm
850   by 20cm to 25cm trapezoidal area visible from the wrist camera. We collected 30 demonstrations
851   for this task.
852   Reward Detection : We used a manual method to reward the agent if it successfully pushed the
853   button. The agent receives a reward of 1 for successfully completing the task and a reward of 0 in
854   all other cases. Each episode has a set number of timesteps, and if the agent doesn’t succeed within
855   that limit, it resets and starts over. The length of each episode may be shorter than the given limit
856   depending on how quickly the agent completes the task.
857
      Reset : For the object, we manually reset the environment by pulling back the button (if pressed)
858   and uniformly randomizing the position of the button in each episode. The robot is initially set to
859   a specific joint configuration known as the home position. Whenever the agent receives a reward
860   or an episode ends, it resets back to the home configuration and the training continues. The object
861   randomization is done by placing the button at the center, and the position remains unchanged until
862   the agent receives its first reward. After that, the object is gradually moved towards the boundary,
863   circled around the workspace, returned to the center, and the process is repeated until the training
      ends.
