008                                                   A BSTRACT
009
                Advancements in deep multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) have posi-
010
                tioned it as a promising approach for decision-making in cooperative games. How-
011
                ever, it still remains challenging for MARL agents to learn cooperative strate-
012             gies for some game environments. Recently, large language models (LLMs) have
013             demonstrated emergent reasoning capabilities, making them promising candidates
014             for enhancing coordination among the agents. However, due to the model size
015             of LLMs, it can be expensive to frequently infer LLMs for actions that agents
016             can take. In this work, we propose You Only LLM Once for MARL (YOLO-
017             MARL), a novel framework that leverages the high-level task planning capabili-
018             ties of LLMs to improve the policy learning process of multi-agents in cooperative
019             games. Notably, for each game environment, YOLO-MARL only requires one
020
                time interaction with LLMs in the proposed strategy generation, state interpreta-
                tion and planning function generation modules, before the MARL policy training
021
                process. This avoids the ongoing costs and computational time associated with
022
                frequent LLMs API calls during training. Moreover, the trained decentralized
023             normal-sized neural network-based policies operate independently of the LLM.
024             We evaluate our method across three different environments and demonstrate that
025             YOLO-MARL outperforms traditional MARL algorithms.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) algorithms have proven to be a powerful framework
030   for addressing complex decision-making problems in multi-agent systems. With the rising appli-
031   cations of multi-agent systems, such as mobile robots in warehouses and games requiring complex
032   reasoning and strategy, it is increasingly crucial for individual agents to learn, cooperate, or com-
033   pete in dynamic environments without a centralized decision-maker (Papoudakis & Schäfer, 2021).
034
      In cooperative Markov games, agents are trained to coordinate their actions to maximize the joint
      rewards. However, existing MARL algorithms face challenges in learning distributed policies for
035
      cooperative games. Moreover, they struggle with tasks characterized by sparse rewards, dynamic
036
      environment, and large action spaces, which can hinder efficient learning and agent collaboration.
037
038   LLMs have excelled as high-level semantic planners due to its in-context learning abilities and prior
039   knowledge (Ahn et al., 2022). Zhang et al. (2023) and Kannan et al. (2024) directly use LLMs
040
      as embodied agents, which demonstrate LLMs’ planning ability in multi-robot system. There are
      also works concentrating on utilizing the LLMs to guide the reinforcement learning (RL) training
041
      to reach better performances. ELLM (Du et al., 2023) leverage LLMs to suggest a goal to assist
042
      RL training whereas Kwon et al. (2023) focusing on the alignment between the action provided by
043   LLM and the RL policy. While these approaches show exciting potential for integrating LLM within
044   policy training, they have yet to extend their methods on multi-agent scenarios. More importantly,
045   utilizing LLMs as agents or integrating them into the RL training loop presents certain challenges.
046   Repeated interactions with LLMs in long-episode tasks or complex environments—especially when
047   using advanced LLMs like Claude-3.5 or GPT-o1 can be time-consuming and costly; it becomes
048   intractable for tasks requiring training over tens of millions of steps. Additionally, there is a risk of
049   intermittent disconnections with the LLM, which could disrupt the training process and affect the
050   system’s stability.
051   Built on the identified insights and challenges, we introduce YOLO-MARL, as shown in Fig. 1,
052   an innovative approach that leverages the planning capabilities of LLMs to enhance MARL pol-

055   interaction with the LLM for each game environment. After the strategy generation, state inter-
056   pretation and planning function generation modules, there is no need for further LLMs interaction
057   during the MARL training process, which significantly reduces the communication and computa-

059   capabilities and simplicity for application: with the proposed strategy generation and state interpre-

066   of each module in the proposed framework. To the best of our knowledge, YOLO-MARL is among
067   one of the first trials that incorporates the high-level reasoning and planning abilities of LLMs with

070
071          • This framework synergizes the planning capabilities of LLMs with MARL to enhance the
072            policy learning performance in challenging cooperative game environments. In particu-
073            lar, our approach exploits the LLM’s wide-ranging reasoning ability to generate high-level
074            assignment planning functions to facilitate agents in coordination.
075          • YOLO-MARL requires minimal LLMs involvement, which significantly reduces compu-
076            tational overhead and mitigates communication connection instability concerns when in-
077            voking LLMs during the training process.
078
             • Our approach leverages zero-shot prompting and can be easily adapted to various game
079            environments, with only basic prior knowledge required from users.

      2.1   M ULTI -AGENT R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
087
088   MARL has gained increasing attention due to its potential in solving complex, decentralized prob-
089   lems. Centralized training with decentralized execution has become a popular framework for over-
090   coming the limitations of independent learning. Methods like QMIX (Rashid et al., 2018) and
091   MADDPG (Lowe et al., 2020) use centralized critics or value functions during training to coordinate
092   agents, while allowing them to execute independently during testing. In cooperative environments,
093
      algorithms like COMA (Foerster et al., 2017) and VDN (Sunehag et al., 2017) enable agents to share
      rewards and act in a coordinated fashion to maximize joint rewards.Wang et al. (2024) introduce a
094
      new approach using language constraint prediction to tackle the challenge of safe MARL in the con-
095
      text of natural language. However, the existing MARL algorithms may not perform well in sparse
096   reward environments and still struggle in learning fully cooperative policy in some environments.
097   So far, only very limited literature of using LLM for MARL has been proposed (Sun et al., 2024),
098   and it remains unclear whether and how can LLM be leveraged for MARL-based decision-making.
099
100   2.2   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS FOR S INGLE -AGENT RL AND D ECISION -M AKING

104   actions conditioned on prompts during online RL. Kwon et al. (2023) provides scalar rewards based
105   on suggestions from LLMs to guide RL training. However, most of these approaches haven’t ex-
106   plored their works in the context of Markov games and require extensive interactions with LLMs
107   during training.

169   Markov game (MG) is defined as a multi-agent decision-making problem when the interaction
170   between multiple agents affect the state dynamics of the entire system and the reward of each
171   agent under certain conditions (Littman, 1994). In this work, we consider a Markov game, or a
172   stochastic game Owen (1982) defined as a tuple G := (N , S, A, {ri }i∈N , p, γ), where N is a set
173   of N agents, S = S 1 × · · · × S N is the joint state space, A = A1 × · · · × AN is the joint ac-
174   tion space, with (S i , Ai ) as the state space and action space of agent i, respectively, γ ∈ [0, 1)
175   is the discounting factor (Littman, 1994; Owen, 1982). The state transition p : S × A → ∆(S)
176
      is controlled by the current state and joint action, where ∆(S) represents the set of all probability
      distributions over the joint state space S. Each agent has a reward function, ri : S × A → R.

      At time t, agent i chooses its action ait according to a policy π i : S → ∆(Ai ). For each

      4.2   S TATE I NTERPRETATION
238   In many simulation environments, observations or states are typically provided as vectors, with each
239   component constructed using various encoding methods. While the vector form of observation is
240   easy to handle when training deep reinforcement learning models, it is difficult for LLMs to directly
241   parse their semantic meaning due to the lack of explicit context for each component.
242
      We propose the State Interpretation Module to assist the LLM in interpreting the environment state.
243
      By providing a semantically meaningful representation of the state, the LLM can successfully gen-
244   erate executable planning functions for training. Formally, given the current environment state in
245   vector form Sv , we define an interpretation function FS such that FS (Sv ) → SI , where SI provides
246   more explicit and meaningful information about each state component.
247
      Recent works like Ma et al. (2024) and Xie et al. (2023) have demonstrated the success of enhancing
248
      LLMs performance by providing relevant environment code. In the same manner, we include the
249
      interpretation function FS in the prompting pipeline, formatted as Pythonic environment code as
250   shown in the purple box in Figure 1(b). The State Interpretation Module significantly reduces the risk
251   of the LLM generating erroneous functions with outputs incompatible with the training procedures.
252   An ablation study on the effectiveness of this module can be found in Sec 6.2, while more details
253   about the interpretation function are provided in Appendix C.2.
254
255   4.3   P LANNING F UNCTION G ENERATION
256
257   A crucial component of our method is leveraging the LLM to perform high-level planning instead of
258   handling low-level actions. We combine all the prompts from the previous modules and input them
259   into the LLM. The LLM then generates a reasonable and executable planning function that can be
260
      directly utilized in the subsequent training process.
261   To be more concise, given any processed state SI , we define an assignment planning function as
262   FT (SI ) → Ti ∈ T , where T = {T1 , ..., Tn } is a set of target assignments that each agent
263   can take. We define the assignment set T over the action space such that an action can be-
264   long to multiple assignments and vice versa. For example, if the assignment space is defined as
265   T = {Landmark 0, Landmark 1}, and landmark 0 and landmark 1 are located at the top right
266
      and top left positions relative to the agent respectively, then taking the action ”UP” can be associated
      with both assignments. Conversely, we can have multiple actions correspond to an assignment. For
267
      instance, moving towards ”Landmark 0” may involve actions like ”UP” and ”RIGHT”.
268
269   The planning function generation will only be required once for each new environment you try to
      use. After you interact with the LLM to get generated planning function, you can directly use it

459   To demonstrate how the State Interpretation Module enhances our framework, we present two failure
460   case snippets:
461
462          • Without the Interpretation Function: The interpretation function is omitted entirely from
463            the prompting pipeline.
464          • Providing Raw Environment Code Directly: Raw environment source code is fed directly
465            to the LLM.
466
467
      As shown in Figure 10, the LLM is unable to infer the type of state and attempts to fetch environment
      information via a non-existent key if no preprocessing code provided. And if environment code
468
      is provided without dimensional context for each component, the LLM is likely to make random
469
      guesses. In both scenarios, the absence of explicit state interpretation hinders the LLM’s ability to
470   generate accurate and executable planning functions. These failures underscore the importance of
471   the State Interpretation Module in bridging the gap between vectorized observations and the LLM’s
472   requirement for semantically meaningful input.
473
      By incorporating the State Interpretation Module, we enable the LLM to understand the environ-
474
      ment’s state representation effectively. This results in the generation of reliable planning functions
475
      that significantly enhance the performance of our YOLO-MARL framework.

483   means that we generate the reward function before all the training process for each new environment.
484   For the reward generation with feedback, we first generate a reward function just like the reward
485   generation without feedback. And then, iteratively, we will run a whole training process on this

513   We acknowledge that the performance of YOLO-MARL may be highly correlated with the LLM’s

520   ronments. In this approach, we prompt the LLM to generate both a planning function and a reward
521   function that replaces the environment-provided reward, following the pipeline described in Sec-

      direction. A preliminary test of this framework is provided in Appendix B.4.

525   8     C ONCLUSION
526
527   We propose YOLO-MARL, a novel framework that leverages the high-level planning capabilities
528   of LLMs to enhance MARL policy training for cooperative games. By requiring only a one-time
529   interaction with the LLM for each environment, YOLO-MARL significantly reduces computational
530   overhead and mitigates instability issues associated with frequent LLM interactions during training.
531
      This approach not only outperforms traditional MARL algorithms but also operates independently
      of the LLM during execution, demonstrating strong generalization capabilities across various envi-
532
      ronments.
533
534   We evaluate YOLO-MARL across three different environments: the MPE environment, the LBF
535   environment, and the SMAC environment. Our experiments showed that YOLO-MARL outper-
536   forms or achieve competitive results compared to baseline MARL methods. The integration of
537
      LLM-generated high-level assignment planning functions facilitated improved policy learning in
      challenging cooperative tasks, even in environments characterized by sparser rewards and large ac-
538
      tion spaces. Finally, we mention a possible way to incorporate reward generation to our framework
539
      and we will step further.
