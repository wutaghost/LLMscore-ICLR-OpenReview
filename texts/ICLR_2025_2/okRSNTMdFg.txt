009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            With the rapid progress of diffusion-based content generation, significant efforts
012            are being made to unlearn harmful or copyrighted concepts from pretrained dif-
013            fusion models (DMs) to prevent potential model misuse. However, it is observed
014            that even when DMs are properly unlearned before release, malicious finetuning
015            can compromise this process, causing DMs to relearn the unlearned concepts.
016            This occurs partly because certain benign concepts (e.g., “skin”) retained in DMs
017
               are related to the unlearned ones (e.g., “nudity”), facilitating their relearning via
               finetuning. To address this, we propose meta-unlearning on DMs. Intuitively,
018
               a meta-unlearned DM should behave like an unlearned DM when used as is;
019
               moreover, if the meta-unlearned DM undergoes malicious finetuning on unlearned
020            concepts, the related benign concepts retained within it will be triggered to self-
021            destruct, hindering the relearning of unlearned concepts. Our meta-unlearning
022            framework is compatible with most existing unlearning methods, requiring only
023            the addition of an easy-to-implement meta objective. We validate our approach
024            through empirical experiments on meta-unlearning concepts from Stable Diffu-
025            sion models (SD-v1-4 and SDXL), supported by extensive ablation studies.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
      Diffusion models (DMs) have achieved remarkable success in generative tasks (Ho et al., 2020; Song
030
      et al., 2021), leading to the emergence of large-scale models like Stable Diffusion (SD) for text-to-
031
      image generation (Rombach et al., 2022b). However, training these models often requires vast
032   datasets that may inadvertently contain private or copyrighted content, as well as harmful concepts
033   that are not safe for work (NSFW) (Schramowski et al., 2023). These challenges have sparked
034   interest in machine unlearning algorithms for DMs (Gandikota et al., 2023; 2024; Kumari et al.,
035   2023; Kim et al., 2023), which modify pretrained models to forget specific inappropriate data (forget
036   set) while retaining performance on the remaining benign data (retain set).
037
      While unlearning methods designed for DMs have shown promising results, recent studies reveal
038   that unlearned models may be maliciously induced to relearn the unlearned concepts during fine-
039   tuning, even when the finetuning is performed on unrelated benign data (Qi et al., 2023; Tamirisa
040   et al., 2024; Patil et al., 2024; Shumailov et al., 2024). Although these studies focus primarily on
041   language models, we observe similar phenomena on DMs as shown in Fig. 2. This partly occurs be-
042   cause certain benign concepts (e.g., “skin” in the retain set) related to unlearned ones (e.g., “nudity”
043   in the forget set) are still retained in DMs, easing their relearning during finetuning.
044   To tackle this challenge, we draw inspiration from meta-learning (Finn et al., 2017) and propose
045   the meta-unlearning framework. This framework comprises two key components: (1) a standard
046   unlearning objective to ensure the model effectively forgets specified data before public release,
047   while preserving performance on benign data; and (2) a meta objective designed to slow down the
048   relearning process if the model is maliciously finetuned on the forget set. Additionally, it ensures
049   that benign knowledge related to the forget set self-destructs, as illustrated in Fig. 1.
050   Our meta-unlearning framework is compatible with most existing unlearning methods for DMs,
051   requiring only the addition of a simple-to-implement meta objective, as outlined in Algorithm 1.
052   This meta objective can be efficiently optimized by automatic differentiation (Paszke et al., 2019).
053   We conduct extensive experiments on SD models (SD-v1-4 and SDXL) to validate the effectiveness
      of various instantiations of our meta-unlearning approach.

071   2     R ELATED WORK
072
073   Recent studies have shown that DMs can be misused to generate unsafe content, such as images
074   depicting sexual acts, harassment, or illegal activities (Schramowski et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023;
075   Rando et al., 2022). To mitigate this issue, early-stage DMs are equipped with NSFW filters de-
076
      signed to block the generation of inappropriate images (Rando et al., 2022). However, this approach
      does not prevent the model from generating harmful imagery at its core, and these filters can be
077
      easily bypassed, exposing significant security vulnerabilities (Birhane et al., 2021; Rombach et al.,
078
      2022b). As a result, machine unlearning methods have been proposed for DMs.
079
080   2.1   M ACHINE UNLEARNING ON DM S
081
082   Several methods have been proposed to unlearn or erase harmful, private, or copyrighted concepts
083
      from DMs (Zhang et al., 2024a;c; Gong et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023; Wu et al.,
      2024; Pham et al., 2024b). For instances, ESD (Gandikota et al., 2023) leverages negative guidance
084
      to finetune the U-Net, removing the specified style or concept. Concept ablation (Kumari et al.,
085
      2023) works by making the distribution of the target concept similar to that of an anchor concept.
086   However, these methods are vulnerable to adversarial attacks. To this end, several adversarial-
087   resistant unlearning methods have been proposed (Li et al., 2024b; Yang et al., 2024; Kim et al.,
088   2024; Huang et al., 2024b). AdvUnlearn (Zhang et al., 2024c) enhances the robustness of concept
089   erasure by incorporating adversarial training principles, while RECE (Gong et al., 2024) derives
090   new target embeddings for inappropriate content and iteratively aligns them with harmless concepts
091   in cross-attention layers. Despite these advancements, the models unlearned by these methods can
092   still be maliciously finetuned to relearn unlearned concepts, as observed in our experiments.
093
094   2.2   M ACHINE UNLEARNING ON LANGUAGE MODELS
095   While this paper primarily focuses on unlearning DMs, there have been a lot of efforts devoted to
096   unlearning language models (Yao et al., 2023; Maini et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024a;
097   Yao et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b; Jia et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2024; Tang et al.,
098   2024; Tamirisa et al., 2024). These methods typically finetune the model on a forget set. In addition,
099   there are also other tuning-free unlearning techniques, including contrastive decoding (Huang et al.,
100   2024a; Wang et al., 2024a; Ji et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024), task vectors (Dou et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
101   2024b), in-context learning (Pawelczyk et al., 2023; Muresanu et al., 2024; Thaker et al., 2024), and
102
      input processing and detection (Bhaila et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a).
103
      2.3   U NLEARNED MODELS CAN BE ATTACKED
104
105   Recent studies have also demonstrated that unlearned models are vulnerable to generating previously
106   unlearned concepts through adversarial attacks (Zhang et al., 2023; Tsai et al., 2023; Pham et al.,
107   2024a; Ma et al., 2024) and malicious finetuning (Tamirisa et al., 2024; Shumailov et al., 2024;
      Łucki et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2023). For instance, UnlearnDiffAtk (Zhang et al., 2023) introduces an


                                                          2

108
      evaluation framework that uses adversarial attacks to generate adversarial prompts by exploiting the
109   inherent classification capabilities of DMs. In the domain of language models, several works have
110   revealed that finetuning can recover unlearned concepts. For example, Qi et al. (2023) demonstrate
111   that safety alignment and/or unlearning in language models can be undermined through finetuning
112   with a small set of adversarially crafted training examples. Additionally, Tamirisa et al. (2024)
113   show that refusal mechanisms and unlearning safeguards can be bypassed with minimal iterations
114   of finetuning, while Łucki et al. (2024) recover most supposedly unlearned capabilities.

188
189   In Section 3.2, we have briefly introduced the commonly used unlearning methods for DMs. In
190
      general, their objectives can be summarized as forgetting knowledge from Dforget and preserving
      performance on Dretain , i.e., solving
191
192                min Lunlearn (θ; Dforget , Dretain ) ≜ Lforget (θ; Dforget ) + λ · Lretain (θ; Dretain ) , (6)
                      θ
193   where Lforget is to unlearn the forget set, Lretain is to preserve performance on the retain set, and
194   λ is a trade-off hyperparameter. Various unlearning methods correspond to different instantiations
195   of Lforget and Lretain . In particular, ESD and SDD require optimizers to solve their instantiations,
196   whereas UCE and RECE have closed-form solutions. To solve Eq. (6), the initialization is usually
197   set to the pretrained parameters θ∗ , and the unlearned model parameters are denoted as θUN .

199   4.1     M ETA - UNLEARNING FRAMEWORK
200
201   A publicly released DM can potentially be finetuned to adopt to various downstream tasks. How-
202
      ever, as observed in previous studies, finetuning or modifying weights of a models could comprise
      its alignment and/or unlearning (Qi et al., 2023; Tamirisa et al., 2024). This underscores the need
203
      for mechanisms to simulate the finetuning process in advance, ensuring DMs are resilient against
204
      relearning the unlearned concepts. Inspired by meta-learning (Finn et al., 2017), we propose the
205   meta-unlearning framework, as illustrated in Algorithm 1. Our framework consists of two compo-
206   nents: (1) the standard unlearning objective Lunlearn , as described above, and (2) the meta objective
207   Lmeta , which resists the relearning of unlearned concepts, even after finetuning on the forget set.
208
      Formally, we define LFT as the finetuning objective, and let DFT ⊂ Dforget represent the malicious
209
      finetuning dataset, which is designed to intentionally make the model relearn concepts from the
210
      forget set. The finetuned model parameters θFT are updated by one or more gradient descents. For
211   example, when using one gradient update from θ, there is θFT ← θ − τ · ∇θ LFT (θ; DFT ), where τ is
212   the step size. The model parameters θ is trained by minimizing the meta objective Lmeta as:
213                min Lmeta (θFT ; DFT , Dretain ) = Lmeta (θ − τ · ∇θ LFT (θ; DFT ) ; DFT , Dretain ). (7)

      ∇θ LFT (θ; DFT ) that can be efficiently computed by automatic differentiation (Paszke et al., 2019).

474   6   C ONCLUSION
475
476   In this paper, we present a meta-unlearning framework for DMs that effectively prevents the re-
477
      learning of previously unlearned concepts, particularly harmful content. Our method combines a
      meta objective with existing unlearning methods, ensuring that if a model is maliciously finetuned
478
      on unlearned data, related benign concepts self-destruct, impeding the relearning process. Extensive
479
      experiments on SD-v1-4 and SDXL reveal that our method maintains generation quality on benign
480   data while significantly reducing the ability to generate unlearned concepts, even after adversarial
481   finetuning. Our framework is compatible with a variety of unlearning techniques and offers a simple
482   yet effective solution for improving the safety of DMs against potential misuse.
483
      Future work. Due to limited computational resources, we tested only two DMs and concentrated on
484
      harmful content. In the updated version, we will use our meta-unlearning framework to investigate
485
      a broader range of scenarios, such as style/copyright erasing.

      A       M ORE RELATED WORK
757
758
759   Meta-learning is generally used in few-shot learning to enhance performance by learning shared
760   features from other data. The metric-based (Snell et al., 2017) and model-based meta-learning
761
      methods (Mishra et al., 2017; Munkhdalai & Yu, 2017; Santoro et al., 2016) rely on extra features or
      models to improve the few-shot learning capabilities. Recently, optimization-based meta-learning
762
      methods have obtained more attention for their strong generalization ability. The optimization-
763
      based methods reduce the meta-learning problem into a bi-level optimization problem. The inner
764   loop optimizes the base model on a certain task, and the outer loop optimizes the base model across
765   several tasks to adjust the initial weight for quick adaption. Without introducing new elements, such
766   a structure has the potential to adapt better to unseen data. The most representative optimization-
767   based method is the MAML (Finn et al., 2017). Subsequent MAML variants (Rajeswaran et al.,
768   2019; Nichol, 2018; Lee et al., 2019; Rusu et al., 2018) focus on optimizing the optimization pro-
769   cess. Recent works (Henderson et al., 2023; Tamirisa et al., 2024) also proposed some meta-learning
770   approaches for robustly preventing models from learning harmful tasks in language models.

873   D.2   H YPERPARAMETER
874
875   Following the papers of ESD (Gandikota et al., 2023) and SDD (Kim et al., 2023), we train ESD-
876   based meta-unlearned model and SDD-based meta-unlearned model for 1000 and 1500 steps seper-
877   ately. We employed the same learning rates, guidance scales, and other hyperparameters as specified
878   in the original ESD and SDD papers. The γ2 in meta-unlearning is set to 0.05 for ESD-u-1, and to
879
      0.1 for ESD-u-3, ESD-f-3, and SDD, respectively. For meta-unlearned model based on UCE and
      RECE, we adopt a two-stage training process: first, we perform unlearning training with the same
880
      hyperparameter as the original paper, and then we separately train the meta-unlearning objective
881
      using a learning rate of 1e-5. In addition, all malicious finetuning experiments in this paper are
882   conducted using the learning rate 1e-5.
