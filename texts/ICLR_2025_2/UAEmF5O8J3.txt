009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Spectral graph neural networks (GNNs) have achieved remarkable success across
012            various applications, yet their generalization properties remain poorly understood.
013            This paper bridges this gap by analyzing the impact of graph homophily and ar-
014
               chitectural choices on the generalization of spectral GNNs. We derive a general
               form of uniform transductive stability for spectral GNNs and provide an explicit
015
               stability analysis for graphs with two node classes, providing a comprehensive
016
               framework to understand their generalization. Based on this stability analysis, we
017            establish a generalization error bound, demonstrating that better stability leads
018            to improved generalization. Our theoretical findings reveal that spectral GNNs
019            generalize well on graphs with strong homophily or heterophily but struggle on
020            graphs with weaker structural properties. We also identify conditions under which
021            increasing the polynomial order in spectral GNN architectures may degrade gener-
022            alization. Empirical results on synthetic and real-world benchmark datasets align
023            closely with our theoretical findings.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Generalization is a fundamental challenge in machine learning, crucial for understanding both the
028
      theoretical limits and practical performance of algorithms. Researchers have developed various
029   measures to study generalization, including VapnikвҖ“Chervonenkis (VC) dimension (Cherkassky
030   et al., 1999), PAC-Bayes bound (McAllester, 1998), Rademacher complexity (Bartlett & Mendelson,
031   2002), and the stability of learning algorithms (Bousquet & Elisseeff, 2002). These measures provide
032   insights into a modelвҖҷs ability to generalize beyond its training data. Graph neural networks (GNNs)
033   have achieved remarkable success across various practical applications (Zhou et al., 2020), yet their
034   generalization capabilities remain poorly understood. Unlike traditional machine learning models
035   that operate on independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) data, GNNs work on interdependent
036   data where the graph topology and node/edge features are inherently linked. This interconnected
037
      structure makes it difficult to evaluate how well a GNN trained on one graph or a set of graphs can
      generalize to unseen graphs.
038
039       Research on GNN generalization primarily focuses on two tasks: graph classification and node
040   classification. In graph classification, where graphs are typically i.i.d., generalization has been
041
      studied through connections with WL algorithms (Morris et al., 2023; DвҖҷInverno et al., 2024; Franks
      et al., 2024) and data-dependent PAC-Bayes bounds (Liao et al., 2021; Ju et al., 2023). In node
042
      classification, which involves transductive learning where node features are known during training,
043
      approaches like Transductive Rademacher complexity and uniform transductive stability are more
044   common. These methods explore the impact of factors such as graph matrix representations and GNN
045   depth on generalization (Oono & Suzuki, 2020; El-Yaniv & Pechyony, 2007; Tang & Liu, 2023b;
046   Zhou & Wang, 2021; Cong et al., 2021).
047
          In this work, we focus on the generalization of GNNs for node classification. Unlike graph
048   classification, node classification performance is influenced by the distribution of node classes,
049   which is closely tied to graph homophily. In homophilic graphs, connected nodes tend to belong
050   to the same class, whereas in heterophilic graphs, connected nodes are often from different classes.
051   Empirical evidence shows that the edge homophilic ratio (Zhu et al., 2020) significantly affects GNN
052   performance. For example, models like GCN and GAT excel on homophilic graphs but underperform
053   on heterophilic graphs (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Velickovic et al., 2018). This motivates us to explore
      the relationship between graph homophily and the generalization of GNNs in node classification tasks,

054
      raising the question: how does GNN generalization depend on node class distributions? Notably, this
055   question has remained largely unexplored in previous research.

          One notable observation about spectral GNNs is that, although the frequency response function
066
      can theoretically be approximated by a sum of polynomial basis with negligible error as the order K
067   increases (Powell, 1981), empirical results show that higher polynomial orders do not consistently lead
068   to better performance. This discrepancy raises the question: is the gap between theoretical study and
069   empirical performance, particularly with respect to polynomial order K, related to the generalization
070   of spectral GNNs? To date, no work has provided a clear explanation for this phenomenon.
071       We address the aforementioned questions by focusing on the generalization of spectral GNNs.
072   In transductive learning, the relationships between labeled and unlabeled nodes are critical for gen-
073   eralization. To measure this, we employ uniform transductive stability (El-Yaniv & Pechyony, 2006),
074   which evaluates the stability of spectral GNNs under perturbations to individual training samples.
075       To study the interplay between graph structure and GNN performance, we use the contextual
076   stochastic block model (cSBM) (Baranwal et al., 2023; Deshpande et al., 2018), a widely used
077   generative model that captures both homophilic and heterophilic graph structures in a controlled
078   and analytically tractable manner. Previous studies have demonstrated that cSBM models real-world
079   datasets such as Citeseer, Cora, and Polblogs, which are frequently used in GNN research (Deshpande
080   et al., 2018; LEI, 2016; Dreveton et al., 2023; Kipf & Welling, 2017; Zhang et al., 2021). Specifically,
081   cSBM generates graphs with well-defined block structures, where nodes within the same block
082   are more likely to be connected (homophilic graphs) or nodes between blocks have a higher
083
      connection probability (heterophilic graphs). Leveraging cSBM allows us to systematically vary
      graph homophily and examine its impact on GNN generalization properties.
084
085   Contributions. Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
086
087          вҖў We analyze the Оі-uniform transductive stability of spectral GNNs by decomposing it into
088            two factors: the Lipschitz continuity and smoothness of the loss function and the spectral
089            GNN, and the gradient norm bound (Theorem 6). This enables us to study the effects
090
               of node class distribution and spectral GNN architecture on training gradients through an
               explicit gradient norm bound (Theorem 8).
091
092          вҖў We establish the generalization error bound of spectral GNNs based on their stability, where
093            good stability indicates strong generalization capability (Theorem 9). To further explicitly
094
               analyze the effects of graph homophily and polynomial order on generalization, we derive
               an explicit form of the gradient norm bound for two node classes (Theorem 13).
095
096          вҖў We prove that spectral GNNs generalize well on graphs that are strong homophilic or het-
097            erophilic, but perform poorly on graphs that are moderately homophilic or heterophilic. We
098
               identify conditions under which increasing the polynomial order in spectral GNN architec-
               tures may degrade generalization, providing insights into architectural design (Theorems 14
099
               and 15; Proposition 16).
100
101   To validate our theoretical findings, we conduct experiments on nine synthetic datasets and nine
102   real-world benchmark datasets for node classification. The experimental results align closely with
103   our theoretical analysis.

124       Graph classification is typically considered as an inductive learning task. One prominent method
125   is the VapnikвҖ“Chervonenkis (VC) bound, which relates the VC dimension of a GNN to the number
126   of colors generated by the 1-WL algorithm, reflecting the number of graphs the 1-WL algorithm
127   can distinguish (Morris et al., 2023). The PAC-Bayes bound is another approach. Liao et al. (2021)
128   connects generalization bound to factors like maximum node degree and GNN depth, while Ju et al.
129   (2023) refines this by tying the bound to the largest singular value of the graph matrix. Behboodi et al.
130   (2022) further extends the PAC-Bayes framework to equivariant networks, highlighting the influence
131   of group properties on generalization. Rademacher complexity and uniform convergence have also
132
      been explored. Garg et al. (2020) shows that a GNNвҖҷs computational tree complexity bounds its
      overall complexity, and Maskey et al. (2022) shows that generalization bound increases with model
133
      complexity but decreases with higher average node degrees.
134
135       Generalization analysis for node classification is more challenging than for graph classification
136
      due to its transductive nature (Tang & Liu, 2023b). Recent studies focus on how graph matrices
      and GNN depth influence generalization. Rademacher complexity (RC) is commonly used for node
137
      classification tasks due to its strong theoretical foundation in transductive learning (El-Yaniv &
138
      Pechyony, 2007). It has been shown that the transductive RC of a GNN is proportional to the infinity
139   norm of its graph matrix, with generalization bounds provided for several classic GNNs (Tang &
140   Liu, 2023b). Esser et al. (2021) uses a planted model to illustrate the relationship between GNN
141   generalization and graph matrix compatibility. Oono & Suzuki (2020) shows that gradient boosting
142   reduces RC in multi-scale GNNs. Uniform stability (US) offers another key approach for analyzing
143   generalization. Verma & Zhang (2019) relates the generalization error bound of single-layer GCNs
144   to the largest absolute eigenvalue of the graph matrix, while Cong et al. (2021) demonstrates that
145   increasing GNN depth improves stability and lowers generalization error bounds. Other works deviate
146   from transductive assumptions, addressing non-i.i.d. settings. For example, Ma et al. (2021) derives
147
      PAC-Bayesian bounds by assuming i.i.d. node classes given fixed node features.
148
149
      Our work. In this work, we focus on node classification tasks, investigating how node class
      distribution and the architecture of spectral GNNs influence generalization. While prior studies have
150
      examined factors like graph size, training set size, graph matrix norms, and node features, they have
151
      largely overlooked the role of graph homophily in generalization and the impact of increasing the
152   polynomial order of spectral GNNs. To our knowledge, this is the first study to analyze the effects of
153   graph homophily and polynomial order on the generalization of spectral GNNs.
154
          Table 1 compares our work with other methods for node classification, highlighting several key
155
      aspects: (1) Analysis settings (inductive or transductive): The VC bound (Scarselli et al., 2018)
156   is data-independent and thus agnostic to inductive or transductive settings. While Verma & Zhang
157   (2019); Zhou & Wang (2021) derive bounds for GNNs in inductive settings, others (Esser et al.,
158   2021; Tang & Liu, 2023b; Oono & Suzuki, 2020; Cong et al., 2021) and our work address the
159   more complex transductive setting. (2) Analysis frameworks: Rademacher complexity estimates a
160   modelвҖҷs capacity to fit noise based on graph structure and node features but does not account for node
161   labels. As a result, methods such as (Esser et al., 2021; Tang & Liu, 2023b; Oono & Suzuki, 2020)
      are unable to analyze the effect of graph homophily, which depends on both graph structure and node

163   graph homophily using gradient-based analysis. It is worth noting that while (Cong et al., 2021)

524   7    C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS , AND F UTURE W ORK
525
526   This work investigates how node class distribution and architectural choices impact the generalization
527
      of spectral GNNs. Our findings show that spectral GNNs generalize well on graphs with strong
      homophilic or heterophilic structures, where node class distributions exhibit clear patterns, and the
528
      generalization error of spectral GNNs increases with polynomial order under certain conditions.
529
530       We derive the uniform transductive stability of spectral GNNs on graphs generated by the general
531
      multi-class cSBM, providing insights into the relationships between graph homophily, polynomial
      order, and generalization error bounds. However, this analysis is limited to a specialized cSBM,
532
      leaving room for further exploration of more diverse graph generation models. Another limitation
533
      lies in architectural choices. While these choices, such as the selection of the graph matrix (e.g.,
534   Laplacian vs. adjacency matrix) and polynomial basis (e.g., Chebyshev vs. Bernstein), are critical
535   to generalization performance, we do not explore their specific impacts on generalization bounds.
536   Future work could investigate how these design decisions influence the theoretical and practical
537   performance of spectral GNNs. Finally, our theoretical analysis assumes training with gradient
538   descent, whereas Adam is the optimizer most commonly used in practice. This discrepancy between
539   theoretical assumptions and practical applications highlights an important direction for future research
      to bridge the gap and improve the relevance of theoretical findings to real-world scenarios.
