012                                               A BSTRACT
013
014            Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have become essential for advancing
015            the integration of visual and linguistic information, facilitating a wide range of
016
               complex applications and tasks. However, the evaluation of LVLMs presents sig-
               nificant challenges as the evaluation benchmark always demands lots of human
017
               cost for its construction, and remains static, lacking flexibility once constructed.
018
               Even though automatic evaluation has been explored in textual modality, the visual
019            modality remains under-explored. As a result, in this work, we address a ques-
020            tion: “Can LVLMs serve as a path to automatic benchmarking?”. We introduce
021            AUTO B ENCH -V, an automated framework for serving evaluation on demand, i.e.,
022            benchmarking LVLMs based on specific aspects of model capability. Upon re-
023            ceiving an evaluation capability, AUTO B ENCH -V leverages text-to-image models
024            to generate relevant image samples and then utilizes LVLMs to orchestrate visual
025            question-answering (VQA) tasks, completing the evaluation process efficiently
026            and flexibly. Through an extensive evaluation of seven popular LVLMs across
027
               five demanded user inputs (i.e., evaluation capabilities), the framework shows ef-
               fectiveness and reliability. We observe the following: (1) Our constructed bench-
028
               mark accurately reflects varying task difficulties; (2) As task difficulty rises, the
029
               performance gap between models widens; (3) While models exhibit strong per-
030            formance in abstract level understanding, they underperform in detailed reasoning
031            tasks; and (4) Constructing a dataset with varying levels of difficulties is criti-
032            cal for a comprehensive and exhaustive evaluation. Overall, AUTO B ENCH -V not
033            only successfully utilizes LVLMs for automated benchmarking but also reveals
034            that LVLMs as judges have significant potential in various domains.

      The flourishing of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023;
      Liu et al., 2024a; Anthropic, 2024) has paved the way for significant advancements in the field of
040
      natural language processing (NLP) (Brown et al., 2020; Vaswani et al., 2023). As the capabilities
041
      of LLMs grew, researchers began to explore the integration of visual information understanding
042   capabilities into LLMs, giving rise to the development of Large Vision-Language models (LVLMs)
043   (Achiam et al., 2023). These models are trained on extensive paired image-text datasets, enabling
044   them to perform sophisticated multimodal reasoning by effectively integrating visual and textual
045   information (Zou et al., 2023; Ghandi et al., 2023; Karras et al., 2019; Agrawal et al., 2016).
046
      With the widespread adoption of LVLMs, evaluating these models has become increasingly im-
047
      portant, for understanding their limitations and reliability better. Recent research (Xu et al., 2023;
048   Liu et al., 2023; Ying et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b;a; Yin et al., 2023) emphasize the urgent need
049   for comprehensive and sophisticated evaluation standards that accurately assess LVLMs’ abilities
050   across various modalities. Various benchmarks are aiming to evaluate a range of capabilities of
051   LVLMs including 3D understanding (Yin et al., 2023), perception and cognition capacity (Liu et al.,
052   2023; Fu et al., 2024), multi-discipline understanding and reasoning (Yue et al., 2024). Even though
053   these works have solidly evaluated certain aspects of LVLMs’ capabilities, they lack the flexibility
      to support on-demand evaluation across various capability aspects. Recent studies have explored

085   Automating the evaluation of LVLMs presents several key challenges. First, the targeted capabilities
086   to be evaluated must be clearly identified based on the input demand. This is the foundation that rel-
087
      evant images and appropriate visual question-answering (VQA) tasks can be generated to accurately
      assess the LVLMs’ performance in those specific aspects. Second, the generated images and VQA
088
      tasks should be relevant and accurately reflect the evaluation target. Third, the risk of answer leakage
089
      from Examiner LVLM during question generation should be mitigated. This issue arises when the
090   model responsible for generating questions exhibits self-enhancement bias (Ye et al., 2024; Zheng
091   et al., 2023), wherein the model being evaluated is also employed to generate the evaluation cases.
092
093
      To address the above challenges, we propose AUTO B ENCH -V, which supports automated evalua-
      tion of LVLMs based on a user demand regarding specific aspects of model capability (e.g., Spatial
094
      Understanding). Initially, the input demand is processed by an examiner LVLM, which categorizes
095
      it into several overarching aspects. Each aspect is further divided into several fine-grained compo-
096   nents, for which image descriptions of varying difficulty levels are generated. To ensure that the
097   descriptions align with their corresponding images, a self-validation mechanism is applied using
098   VQA (Agrawal et al., 2016). Furthermore, an error control mechanism is implemented to prevent a
099   negative impact on the generation of questions and reference answers. The generated questions and
100   images are then presented to the evaluated LVLM to generate responses, which are assessed against
101   reference answers (Liu et al., 2024b). The pipeline of AUTO B ENCH -V is shown in Figure 2.
102   By leveraging AUTO B ENCH -V, we conduct extensive evaluation of seven popular LVLMs across
103   five demanded evaluation capabilities (see Figure 1). The results show that LVLMs exhibit declin-
104   ing performance as task difficulty rises, with varied performances over distinctive LVLMs. While
105   excelling in high-level understanding, they struggle with detailed reasoning, revealing a key area for
106   improvement in future research. We also carried out several human evaluation experiments on the
107   generated cases, which yielded positive results, demonstrating the reliability of our approach. To
      summarize, our key contributions are three-fold:

      Benchmark for LVLMs. The emergence of the LVLMs greatly promoted the development of the
      multimodal model, demonstrating exceptional progress in their multimodal perception and reason-
136
      ing capabilities. This makes the past, focused on isolated task performance benchmarks (Karpathy
137
      & Fei-Fei, 2015; Agrawal et al., 2016) insufficient to provide a comprehensive evaluation. Subse-
138   quent studies have introduced benchmarks for assessing LVLMs across a range of multimodal tasks
139   (Goyal et al., 2017; Lin et al., 2015; Russakovsky et al., 2015). However, these benchmarks often
140   fall short in providing fine-grained assessments of abilities and robust evaluation metrics.
141
      Hence, recent works (Xu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Ying et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024; Yin et al.,
142
      2023; Chen et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023; 2024) highlight the critical need for developing advanced,
143
      comprehensive benchmarks to more accurately assess LVLMs’ multimodal understanding and rea-
144   soning capabilities. However, these benchmarks still have different kinds of limitations. For ex-
145   ample, LVLM-eHub (Xu et al., 2023) and LAMM (Yin et al., 2023) have utilized several classical
146   datasets that are widely recognized but not sufficiently novel for current advancements, overlooking
147   the possibility of data leakage during LVLM training. Hence, MMStar (Chen et al., 2024) aims to
148   solve the unnecessity of visual content and unintentional data leakage that exists in LVLM training
149   via constructing an elite vision-indispensable dataset.
150   Compared to previous work, AUTO B ENCH -V not only automates the entire benchmarking process
151   for LVLMs—significantly reducing human workload and minimizing subjective biases—but also
152   scales up and customizes the evaluation process to address fine-grained user needs.
153
      Automatic benchmarks. The significant early advancements in LLMs have driven the develop-
154
      ment of various benchmarks designed to automate evaluation processes. For example, LMExamQA
155
      (Bai et al., 2023b) employs the concept of a Language-Model-as-an-Examiner to create a compre-
156   hensive and scalable evaluation framework. In addition, DY VAL (Zhu et al., 2024a) and DY VAL 2
157   (Zhu et al., 2024b) both highlight the importance of dynamic assessment, with DY VAL focusing on
158   reasoning tasks and DY VAL 2 adopting a broader psychometric approach. AutoBencher (Li et al.,
159   2024) automates the generation of novel, challenging, and salient datasets for evaluating LLMs, fur-
160   ther expanding the scope of automated benchmarking. Other efforts, such as U NI G EN (Wu et al.,
161   2024) and Task Me Anything (Zhang et al., 2024a), focus on developing more tailored and relevant
      benchmarks for assessing LLM/LVLMs performance across diverse tasks.

190
      benchmarking LVLMs, empowered by a LVLM Mv and a text-to-image model Md . As shown
      in Figure 3, AUTO B ENCH -V consists of four modules: user-oriented aspect generation, guided
191
      description generation, image generation by self-validation, and test case generation & evaluation.

      Diverse description generation strategy. A key challenge when generating image descriptions
256
      at the same difficulty level is minimizing repetitive elements and backgrounds, which can reduce
257
      the diversity and generalization of the evaluation. For example, given a user input q related to
258   spatial understanding, the model Mv might tend to produce descriptions centered around urban
259   landscapes, potentially compromising the variety of test cases. To address this, we introduce a
260   description optimization strategy using a semantic graph (Quillian, 1966) to enhance the diversity
261   of image prompts generated by Mv , with significant results referred to in Appendix Figure 10. For
262   a visualization of specific words, see Appendix Figure 11 and Figure 12. The process is iterative,
263   and during the e′ th iteration of prompt generation, a topic word te and a set of |c| related keywords
264   Ke = {ke1 , ke2 , . . . , kec } are selected. These keywords are added as nodes to the semantic graph
265   G, where nodes are connected by edges representing semantic relationships between them.
266   Formally, let Ge = (Ve , Ee ) be the semantic graph generated at iteration e, and let Se =
267   (Ve−1 ∪ {te } ∪ Ke ). Then Ve = Se \ f (Se ) represents the node set of topic words and key-
268   words, and Ee is the set of edges capturing the relationships between them. After each round
269   of prompt generation, we apply a degree-based exclusion mechanism, where the number of ex-
      cluded nodes is determined by a function f (Se ). This function defines the number of top-

526   hard level, GLM-4V showed a significant bias, with deviation rates of RA = −19% and RD = −8%,

528   the uniform distribution scenario. Thus, our approach of manually setting an even distribution of
529   answers to avoid position bias is justified and necessary.

       B     D ETAILS OF U SER I NPUT
973
974
       In this section, we provide a comprehensive overview of the levels at which we categorize user
975
       inputs based on linguistic aspects. Our goal is to offer a comprehensive and broad representation of
976
       user requirements for LVLMs. However, as it is challenging to exhaustively cover every aspect, we
977    base our categorization on aspects derived from the literature (Li et al., 2023b; Meng et al., 2024;
978    Liu et al., 2023). These aspects are considered representative and comprehensive examples of the
979    capabilities of LVLMs and other aspects like in (Chen et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023) can be handled
980    in a similar manner, without requiring additional fine-tuning or adjustments, as our framework is
981    highly extensible, allowing users to propose their own aspects as needed.
982
983
       B.1   BASIC U NDERSTANDING
984
985    Definition and Goal: Basic Understanding refers to the recognition and identification of individual
986    objects, characters, and scenes within an image. The goal is to accurately detect and label rele-
987    vant elements, providing a foundation for more advanced tasks such as object tracking and scene
988    interpretation (Wu et al., 2013; Xue et al., 2018).
989
       Requirement. This task demands the ability to detect specific objects and differentiate between
990
       various types of objects. Additionally, it involves understanding the broader context of the scene
991    and identifying real-life settings to enable accurate interpretation of the image’s overall content.
992
993
994    B.2   S PATIAL U NDERSTANDING
995
       Definition and Goal. Spatial Understanding refers to the interpretation of the spatial arrangement
996
       and positioning of objects within an image (Cai et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024). The goal is to com-
997
       prehend both two-dimensional and three-dimensional relationships, determining which objects are
998    in the foreground or background, assessing their relative sizes and orientations, and understanding
999    how they are positioned within the scene.
1000
1001
       Requirement. This task demands the ability to perceive depth, estimate distances between objects,
       and analyze how objects interact within the physical space of the image, providing a more accurate
1002
       understanding of the spatial structure and context.
1003
1004
1005   B.3   S EMANTIC U NDERSTANDING
1006
1007   Definition and Goal. Semantic Understanding involves interpreting the higher-level meaning and
1008
       relationships within an image (Meng et al., 2024). The goal is to move beyond simple object identi-
       fication to understand the roles and interactions between objects, such as recognizing that a person
1009
       is riding a bike or that two people are engaged in conversation. This level of understanding aims
1010
       to capture the context and intent behind the scene, identifying how elements relate to each other to
1011   form a coherent narrative or message.
1012
1013   Requirement. This task requires discerning the interactions and relationships between objects,
1014
       understanding their roles within the scene, and interpreting the overall context to accurately derive
       the narrative or intended message conveyed by the image.
1015

1017   B.4   ATMOSPHERIC U NDERSTANDING
1018
1019   Definition and Goal. Atmospheric Understanding focuses on grasping the mood, tone, and emo-
1020   tional ambiance conveyed by an image. The goal is to interpret not just what is depicted or how
1021   elements are arranged, but also how the scene feels and the emotional resonance it conveys to the
1022
       viewer. For instance, an image of children laughing under warm sunlight in a lush park combines
       their expressions, bright colors, and soft lighting to create a joyful and carefree atmosphere.
1023
1024   Requirement. This task requires the ability to capture and interpret subtle emotional cues and tonal
1025   qualities of the scene, distinguishing the overall mood and emotional impact of the image from more
       analytical aspects like semantic or spatial understanding.

       B.5   R EASONING C APACITY
1027
1028   Definition and Goal. Reasoning Capacity involves interpreting and analyzing the relationships and
1029   logical connections between different elements within an image (Zhou et al., 2024; You et al., 2023).
1030   The goal is to infer potential outcomes, understand causal relationships, and make predictions about
1031   what might happen next based on visual cues. For example, if a person is holding an umbrella and
1032   the sky is dark, reasoning capacity would suggest that it might rain soon. This level also includes un-
1033   derstanding abstract relationships, such as social dynamics or the intent behind actions, and making
1034
       judgments about what is likely or possible given the visual information.
1035   Requirement. This task requires the ability to analyze logical connections between elements, infer
1036   outcomes, and understand causal relationships, as well as to interpret abstract concepts and make
1037   predictions based on the visual context.

       This section describes in detail the difficulty levels for the pictures and questions used in prompts
1042
       respectively. The following is the instruction guiding the examiner model to generate image descrip-
1043   tions and questions of varying difficulty levels.
1044
1045
       C.1   I MAGE D ESCRIPTION
1046
1047   Easy difficulty. Generate images with very simple elements, focusing on single, easily recognizable
1048   objects placed against a plain or neutral background. The descriptions should be straightforward and
1049   unambiguous, e.g., “a red apple on a white background.” The focus is on clarity and simplicity, with
1050   minimal detail or interaction.
1051
       Medium difficulty. Introduce scenes where the required elements interact with their environment
1052   naturally but uncomplicatedly. The setting may include multiple common objects and a familiar
1053   context, but the composition remains clear and not overly complex, e.g., “a cup on a table in a
1054   well-lit kitchen.” The background and context are present but not overwhelming, and there are no
1055   intricate details or unusual perspectives.
1056
       Hard difficulty. Craft descriptions that involve multiple elements interacting with each other, set
1057
       in a more complex environment. Use varied perspectives, detailed textures, or lighting conditions
1058   that add layers of difficulty, e.g., “a reflection of a cat looking out of a rain-soaked window, with
1059   a cityscape in the background at dusk.” The focus is on creating a rich and intricate scene that
1060   challenges the model’s ability to render interactions, depth, and subtleties in lighting and perspective.
1061
       Moreover, we standardized the description of the observer’s perspective in the image description to
1062
       prevent directional issues from causing confusion. For instance, ambiguities could arise in inter-
1063
       preting relative directions such as left and right, as these can vary significantly depending on the
1064   observer’s viewpoint.
1065
1066
       C.2   Q UESTION
1067
1068   Easy difficulty. Focus on questions that require identifying simple, prominent, and explicit details
1069   within the image. These questions should be straightforward, relying solely on basic observation
1070   without the need for inference or interpretation. For example, you might ask about the color of a
1071   specific object, the presence of a single item, or the shape of an easily recognizable feature. The
1072   key is to keep the questions direct and simple, ensuring that the answer is obvious and immediately
1073   visible in the image.
1074   Medium difficulty. Design questions that necessitate a moderate level of observation and infer-
1075   ence. These questions should involve understanding relationships between elements, recognizing
1076   interactions, or identifying less prominent features that are still clear but not immediately obvious.
1077   Examples could include questions about the relative position of objects, identifying an action taking
1078   place, or understanding the context of a scene. The goal is to require some level of thought beyond
1079   basic observation, challenging the model to understand the scene’s composition or narrative without
       being overly complex.

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       Hard difficulty. Create questions that require the model to notice and interpret more detailed as-
1081   pects of the image. These questions should involve recognizing multiple elements working together,
1082   understanding more complex interactions, or identifying details that are present but not immediately
1083   obvious. For example, you might ask about the positioning of objects relative to each other in a
1084   more crowded scene, subtle changes in lighting or color that affect the appearance of objects, or
1085   identifying an element that is not the main focus but is still visible in the background. The aim is to
1086   challenge the model to go beyond surface-level details, but without making the task too abstract or
1087   overly difficult.

       In this section, we outline the guidelines followed during human evaluations to ensure reliability and
1101   validity.
1102
1103   For Description Generation Guideline, the evaluators need to consider the following three points:
1104   ▷ Alignment with Image: The main criterion is how well the generated description reflects the
1105   visual content. Descriptions must accurately correspond to the image, avoiding vague or abstract
1106   expressions. Each description should provide clear, specific details that align with the image content
1107   and the defined fine-grained aspects.
1108   ▷ Specificity and Clarity: Ensure that descriptions are specific, directly related to the image, and
1109   free from ambiguous or overly generalized language.
1110
1111
       ▷ Relevance to Aspects: Assess whether the description aligns with the corresponding themes and
       expected content. Descriptions must clearly communicate the intended visual elements and avoid
1112
       any misalignment between the image and the description.
1113
1114   For Question-Answer Alignment, there are two points that the evaluators should consider:
1115   ▷ Clarity and Accuracy: Each question must be clear, unambiguous, and directly derived from
1116   the image. The answers should correspond to observable details or logical inferences from the
1117   image, with only one correct answer for each question. There should be no irrelevant or misleading
1118   information in the questions or answers.
1119
       ▷ Consistency with Image: Verify that both the question and answer are directly based on the im-
1120
       age’s content. The evaluation should ensure that there is a logical and clear relationship between the
1121   visual cues and the generated question-answer pair, particularly for tasks involving higher difficulty.

       E    E RROR S TUDY
1135
1136
       Through extensive experimental analysis, we have categorized the common problems encountered
1137   by LVLMs in VQA tasks into two main types: image comprehension errors and image reasoning
1138   errors. Regarding the first category, LVLMs often fail to truly understand the details in an image.
1139   For instance, in Figure 15, the model failed to notice the kite flying in the upper right corner of the
1140   image and incorrectly identified a non-existent action of a squirrel climbing a tree. In Figure 17,
1141   the model mistakenly perceived the firefighter as holding the crowbar with only one hand instead of
1142   two. In Figure 18, the model failed to recognize that the apple’s stem was slightly tilted to the left.
1143   These errors demonstrate the model’s inadequate comprehension of image details.
1144   Image reasoning errors occur when models accurately perceive the image content but falter in their
1145   reasoning process, leading to incorrect answers. For instance, in Figure 16, the model correctly
1146   recognizes that the image does not depict a cheering crowd. However, during subsequent reasoning,
1147   it convinces itself otherwise, ultimately selecting option C while neglecting to analyze other choices
1148   in its explanation. Figure 19 exemplifies a similar issue: the model correctly identifies that the child
1149   is wearing a yellow shirt but after mentioning the red of the kite it erroneously selects B. Red is its
1150   final answer. These examples highlight a disconnect between visual perception and logical reasoning
1151
       in LVLMs, where initial accurate observations can be overridden by flawed deductive processes.
