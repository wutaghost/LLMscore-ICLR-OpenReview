009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The scheme of adaptation via meta-learning is seen as an ingredient for solving
012            the problem of data shortage or distribution shift in real-world applications, but
013            it also brings the new risk of inappropriate updates of the model in the user en-
014            vironment, which increases the demand for explainability. Among the various
015            types of XAI methods, establishing a method of explanation based on past experi-
016            ence in meta-learning requires special consideration due to its bi-level structure of
017            training, which has been left unexplored. In this work, we propose influence func-
018
               tions for explaining meta-learning that measure the sensitivities of training tasks
               to adaptation and inference. We also argue that the approximation of the Hessian
019
               using the Gauss-Newton matrix resolves computational barriers peculiar to meta-
020
               learning. We demonstrate the adequacy of the method through experiments on
021            task distinction and task distribution distinction using image classification tasks
022            with MAML and Prototypical Network.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Meta-learning is a widely studied class of methods that enables models to quickly adapt to unseen
028   tasks (Finn et al., 2017), addressing limitations in generalization due to data scarcity during training
029   phase (Song & Jeong, 2024; Li et al., 2018; Shu et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2021) or distribution shift
030   in the user environment (Mann et al., 2021; Mouli et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2020). Although meta-
031   learning methods guarantee faster adaptation than conventional methods, they do not account for
032   safe adaptations (Zhang et al., 2020). In fact, they may cause inappropriate model updates in user
033
      environments, which raises safety concerns in downstream tasks (Khattar et al., 2024; Wen et al.,
      2022; Yao et al., 2024). Consequently, This increases the need for explanation methods to enhance
034
      transparency and ensure the safe operation of autonomous systems.
035
036   The most commonly proposed explanation methods in this decade focus on local explanation: They
037   treat the model parameters as given and explain the model’s local behavior around specific inference
038   data points. Although these methods can be applied to the models with their weights determined
039
      via meta-learning and adaptation, they do not cover the needs raised by the peculiarities of meta-
      learning (Figure 1a). Because they are based solely on the input data used at each inference, what
040
      they can achieve at most is assessing and comparing the importance of the components in a single
041
      data, and understanding those explanations requires knowledge about those components, which is
042   sometimes technical and of expert levels (Adebayo et al., 2022). This requirement is considered to
043   limit the application area where local explanations can resolve the above-mentioned safety concerns
044   of meta-learning.
045
      One way without relying on the details of the inference data is to avoid seeing the lower level struc-
046
      ture and instead analyze the relation to previously known data, i.e. training data. In meta-learning,
047
      this expectation is supported by the fact that in some cases, where analyses based on training tasks
048   are essential for understanding the model’s behaviors (Goldblum et al., 2020b). This insight leads
049   us to consider highlighting how previously encountered tasks have influenced the model’s current
050   inference (Figure 1b). Notice that explanations based on past experiences have an advantage in uti-
051   lizing structures of the training dataset, which increases the conciseness of explanations. In the case
052   of Figure 3b, the second half of the explanation is possible owing to the nature that training data
053   can be supplemented with their attributes with various levels of abstraction, which can be added
      by annotators, or taken from descriptions of the data acquisition process. Such conciseness enables

055   essential as future real-world applications will likely involve training datasets from diverse domains,
056   making it difficult for non-expert users to understand explanations without abstracting the raw data.

060

      2    R ELATED WORK
093
094
      Influence functions for machine learning. The primary focus of existing research is the use of
095
      influence functions in standard supervised learning models initiated by Koh & Liang (2017). Influ-
096   ence functions have been successfully used for multiple purposes, such as explaining model behavior
097   with respect to training data in various tasks (Barshan et al., 2020; Koh & Liang, 2017; Han et al.,
098   2020), quantifying model uncertainty (Alaa & Van Der Schaar, 2020), crafting/detecting adversarial
099   training examples (Cohen et al., 2020).These approaches focus on data-level explanations, render-
100   ing them of limited practical value in meta-learning settings. TLXML leverages influence functions
101   for task-level explanations, offering more effective insights into how training tasks influence the
102   model’s behavior. Nevertheless, as noted in Alaa & Van Der Schaar (2020), computing the Hessian
103   matrix is expensive. To scale the methods of influence functions to more complex networks and
104
      larger datasets. TLXML uses an approximation method for faster computation of the matrix.
105   Explainable AI (XAI) for meta-learning. It is natural to consider applying existing XAI methods
106   to meta-learning. The methods that are agnostic to the learning process should be applicable for
107   explaining inference in meta-learning. Although this area is still in its early stages, some research
      examples already exist (Woźnica & Biecek, 2021; Sijben et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2023). The closest


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      work to ours is by Woźnica & Biecek (2021) who quantified the importance of meta-features,
109   i.e., high-level characteristics of a dataset such as size, number of features and number of classes.
110   In meta-learning, the goal is to train models that can generalize across various tasks. We argue
111   that understanding the influence of training tasks play an important role in evaluating the model’s
112   adaptability, whereas meta-features offer limited insight for such evaluation.
113
      Impact of training data. The robustness of machine learning models is another area where the
114
      impact of training data on inference is frequently discussed (Khanna et al., 2019; Ribeiro et al.,
115   2016). This topic has also been explored in the context of meta-learning such as creating training-
116   time adversarial attacks via meta-learning (Zügner & Günnemann, 2019; Xu et al., 2021), training
117   robust meta-learning models by exposing models to adversarial attacks during the query step of
118   meta-learning (Goldblum et al., 2020a), and data augmentation for enhancing the performance of
119   meta-learning algorithms (Ni et al., 2021b). However, influence functions have not been utilized
120   for meta-learning in this field. Similar to Khanna et al. (2019); Barshan et al. (2020) in the case of
121   supervised learning, TLXML also leverages Fisher information metrics. This formulation provides
122   a valuable geometric viewpoint for analyzing the model’s parameter space.

      4.2   H ESSIAN A PPROXIMATION METHOD VIA THE G AUSS -N EWTON MATRIX
248
249   TLXML faces computational barriers when applied to large models, particularly (as also noted
250   in Alaa & Van Der Schaar (2020)) due to the computational costs of handling the Hessian in Equa-
251   tion 5. Although the Hessian is defined as the second-order tensor of the meta parameters, third-
252   order tensors appear during its computation, resulting in a computational cost of at least O(pq 2 ) for
253   a model with p weights and q meta-parameters. This is due to the bi-level structure of meta-learning
254   (see Appendix A.2 for details). Furthermore, as is common in matrix inversion issues, inverting the
255   Hessian incurs a computational cost of O(p3 ).
256   To address this limitation in the expressiveness of meta-parameters, we propose using the Gauss-
257   Newton matrix to approximate the Hessian matrix. For specific loss functions, e.g., mean squared
258   error and cross-entropy, the Hessian can be decomposed into a sum of outer products of two vectors
259   along with terms containing second-order derivatives. In this work, we only focus on the case of
260   cross-entropy with the softmax function, which leads to

338   5     E XPERIMENTS
339
340   The definition and approximation of influence functions in TLXML rely on certain assumptions:
341   equation 4 is defined with the meta-parameters ω̂ at the exact optimum point, and the approximation
342   in equation 11 is based on the assumption that the number of training samples is sufficiently large and
343   ω̂ fits well to the target distribution. The experiments in this section aim to investigate whether the
344   outputs of TLXML can be used to measure the influence of a training task on the meta-parameters
345
      numerically estimated by stochastic gradient descent. We empirically investigate two fundamental
      properties:
346
347   Property 1: If the network memorizes a training task, its influence on a test task with similar
348   characteristics should be higher than the influence of other training tasks. We investigate this to
349   validate the exact formulas in Equation 4, 6, and 7. For this validation, pairs of similar training and
350   test tasks are created by setting the test task as identical to one of the training tasks.
351   Property 2: If the network encodes information about the training task distributions, tasks sampled
352   from distributions similar to the test task should have a higher measured influence than tasks sampled
353   from other distributions. We examine this to validate the approximation methods in Equation 11 and
354   12, as encoding task distribution information requires a sufficiently large network.
355   Setup. We use MAML as a meta-learning algorithm. We conduct the experiments with 5-ways-
356   5-shots problem, taken from MiniImagenet (Vinyals et al., 2016) dataset. The implementation is
357   based on the meta-learning library lean2learn (Arnold et al., 2020) and PyTorch’s automatic dif-

458   This paper presented TLXML, a method for quantifying the impact of training tasks on the meta-
459   parameters, adapted network weights, and inference outcomes. Through experiments with a small
460   network and two meta-learners, we observed that TLXML effectively serves as a similarity measure
461
      between training and test tasks. In Figure 6, this observation is qualitatively explored. The figure
      shows image samples from similar (Figure 6b and Figure 6c) and dissimilar (Figure 6d) training
462
      tasks to a test task (Figure 6a) that received high and low scores, respectively.
463
464   Additionally, we proposed a modification of the influence functions to address cases where the
465   Hessian has flat directions. We also explored how the computational cost of managing the Hessian
466   for large networks can be reduced by using the quadratic form with the Gauss-Newton matrix. In
467
      our experiments with a middle-sized CNN, we observed that the approximated influence scores were
      effective in distinguishing between training task distributions in several cases.

1207   B.5   VALIDITY OF THE H ESSIAN APPROXIMATION
1208
1209   From the above results, we see that influence scores calculated with GN matrix approximation have
1210   enough expressibility for finding training tasks identical to test tasks and some statistical values
1211
       over different tests can also be used to distinguish the task distributions. As reference information
       for observing the extent to which those results are under the influence of the approximation, we
1212
       trained a 2-layer fully connected network of the same structure as in Section B.2.1 with MAML and
1213
       Omniglot and calculated the correlation coefficients of influence scores obtained with and without
1214   the approximation. As training settings, we took 5 cases of different numbers of training tasks, and
1215   for each of them, as conditions for inverting the Hessians, we took 5 cases of different numbers of
1216   the exact Hessian eigenvalues treated as non-zero, and 5 cases of different vector buffer sizes for
1217   the approximated Hessians. In Table 17, 18, 19,20, and 21, we list the mean ± std values resulting
1218   from calculating correlation coefficients between the influence scores of the training tasks and taking
1219   statistics over 128 tests for each setting.
1220   From the tables, we observe that most of the diagonal elements have the largest values in the rows
1221   and in the columns where they reside. This is because the number of inverted eigenvalues in the
1222   exact method and the size of the vector buffer in the approximation method both determine how
1223   many directions are treated as non-flat, establishing a correspondence between the two.
1224
       We note that columns of larger inverted eigenvalues and rows of larger vector buffers exhibit weaker
1225
       correlations. This is likely due to instability in inversion caused by small eigenvalues. Additionally,
1226   the network used here is much smaller than the CNN used in the previous subsections. We can
1227   expect that larger networks fit better to the training data and therefore, the approximated influence
1228   scores have more correlations with the exact ones. However, we cannot verify this experimentally
1229   due to the computational barriers and the memory cost.
