011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Existing message-passing graph neural networks often rely on carefully designed
014            information propagation methods to perform reasonably in graph-related mining
015
               tasks. However, this invokes the problem of whether the dimensions of learnable
               matrices and the depths of the networks are properly estimated. While this chal-
016
               lenge has been attempted by others, it remains an open problem. Using the princi-
017
               ple of maximum entropy and Shannon’s theorem, we demonstrate that message-
018            passing graph neural networks function similarly to noisy communication chan-
019            nels. The optimal information transmission state of graph neural networks can be
020            reached when Shannon’s theorem is satisfied, which is determined by their entropy
021            and channel capacity. In addition, we illustrate that the widths of trainable matri-
022            ces should be sufficiently large to avoid the shrinkage of model channel capacity
023            and the increase of the channel capacity diminishes as the depth of the networks
024            increases. The proposed approach is empirically verified through extensive exper-
025            iments on five public semi-supervised node classification datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      The message-passing graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have been favored choices in various graph-
030
      based tasks, which arises from their powerful ability to exploit the connectivity information and
031   node attribute features to realize effective information propagation over the graphs. For instance,
032   GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2017) defines the information propagation operation based on the first-order
033   approximation of the spectral filter on graphs, GAT (Veličković et al., 2018) extends this operation
034   by adaptively assigning edge weights through the multi-head graph attention, and GDC (Gasteiger
035   et al., 2019) further relaxes this by redefining adjacency matrices with kernel functions (e.g., heat
036   kernel (Chung, 2007) and Personalized PageRank (Wang et al., 2020)). Sharing the core idea of
037   propagating graph features with adjacency information, many powerful GNNs have been proposed
038   such as APPNP (Gasteiger et al., 2018), SGC (Wu et al., 2019), GATv2 (Brody et al., 2022), etc.
039
      Nonetheless, the depths and widths of most message-passing GNNs are selected based on heuristical
      guidelines and non-trivial engineering knowledge (Cai et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2023).
040
041   Though the connectivity information plays a crucial part in information exchange over graphs, the
042   dimensionalities of learnable matrices and the depth of the network are inescapable aspects to be
043   considered (Chen et al., 2020b; Zeng et al., 2021). Several studies (Liu et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021)
044   show that deep GNNs can benefit from larger receptive fields, while others (Wu et al., 2019; Elia-
045
      sof et al., 2021) debate that shallow GNNs can avoid critical problems like over-smoothing in deep
      GNNs. From the view of signal processing, learnable matrices can be seen as the encoders of latent
046
      domains, of which their dimensionalities explicitly determine the transformation of graph signals
047
      between domains. Hence, some studies have explored the potential effects of GNN’s depths and
048   widths. For instance, GraphGym (You et al., 2020) provides a general search framework for se-
049   lecting the best GNN from a pre-defined search space for a specific downstream task. It comprises
050   multiple aspects of constructing GNNs, such as the network depth, the widths of trainable matri-
051   ces, the aggregation method, and the activation function. This work facilitates the development of
052   GNNs for various graph-mining tasks from an empirical aspect. Despite comprehensive empirical
053   guidelines being concluded based on extensive experiments, mechanisms behind certain configura-
      tions of depths and widths are not yet demystified. While Loukas (2020) argues that existing GNNs


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      often fail in effectively passing information over the graph unless certain products of their depths
055   and widths exceed a polynomial of graphs. This work illustrates that the expressive ability of GNNs
056   depends on their model capacity, which is directly determined by the widths and depths of the net-
057   works. More recently, another work, MGEDE (Yang et al., 2023) has attempted to bridge this gap
058   by introducing the minimum entropy principle to learn a highly informative yet low-dimensional
059   representation. First, it defines the entropy of a graph by a direct summation of its structural entropy
060   and node attribute entropy. Subsequently, it relates the layer widths to the graph entropy based on
061   the distributional hypothesis (Sahlgren, 2008) with several numerical approximations. Eventually,
062   the layer widths are determined by zeroing out the graph entropy according to the minimum entropy
063
      principle. Nonetheless, MGEDE neither provides justifications nor explanations about the chosen
      depths for message-passing. More importantly, the minimum entropy principle, or Prigogine’s theo-
064
      rem (Prigogine, 1978) is narrowly valid in certain near-equilibrium linear systems, which might not
065
      be suitable for nonlinear systems like GNNs.
066
067   In the face of properly estimating the depths and widths of GNNs in advance, we propose a novel
068   theoretical approach termed Channel Capacity Constrained Estimation (C3E). First, we define the
069
      entropy and the channel capacity for GNNs. Then, unlike conventional ways to select the widths and
      depths based on empirical knowledge and grid search, C3E obtains feasible widths and depths of a
070
      GNN by solving a nonlinear mathematical programming problem given the input graph. Eventually,
071
      based on the feasible solutions, we empirically demonstrate that GNNs are governed by their entropy
072   and channel capacity.

077   2.1   I NFORMATION T HEORY IN D EEP L EARNING
078
      Information theory (Shannon, 1948; Jaynes, 1957; Kullback, 1997) has long been a powerful tool
079
      for analyzing complex nonlinear systems like neural networks. For instance, some studies (Chan
080   et al., 2022; Roberts et al., 2022) endeavor to establish statistical relationships between latent rep-
081   resentation and neural networks. The information bottleneck theory is broadly adopted in neural
082   networks to learn minimal effective representations that maximize the mutual information between
083   the learned representation and the output (Tishby et al., 2000; Wu et al., 2020). Similarly, another
084   work (Yu et al., 2020) proposes learning more discriminative and diverse representations via maxi-
085   mal coding rate reduction. In the meantime, Saxe et al. (2019) have explored the entropy distribution
086   and the information flow of deep neural networks. Furthermore, Sun et al. (2021) and Shen et al.
087   (2023) have attempted to utilize the principle of maximum entropy to link latent feature maps to the
088
      distinctive behaviors of neural networks.
089
090   2.2   E XPLORATION IN N EURAL N ETWORK A RCHITECTURE
091
      Early works (e.g., Wang & Zhu, 2021; Cai et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022) adopt computationally in-
092
      tensive methods like automated machine learning and network architecture search to generate neural
093   networks. The research focus has then shifted towards principled theoretical analysis of the behav-
094   iors governing neural networks. For example, DeepMAD (Shen et al., 2023) established the statisti-
095   cal relationship between architectures of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and Shannon’s en-
096   tropy; Di Giovanni et al. (2023) have investigated the potential impacts of GNNs’ depths and widths
097   from over-squashing and over-smoothing; GCNII (Chen et al., 2020a) empirically demonstrates
098   that skip connections and identity mapping can improve GNNs’ performance and stack deeper, and
099   AERO-GNN (Lee et al., 2023) realizes deeper attention-based GNNs by modifying the attention
100   mechanism and introducing weighted skip connections.

462   8                            D ISCUSSION
463
464   GNNs learn meaningful representations from graph structures and attribute features by propagating
465   various graph signals across the graphs. Nevertheless, many studies often neglect the significance of
466   learnable matrices’ dimensionalities and network depth. In this paper, we show that besides intricate
467
      information propagation mechanisms, the learning abilities of GNNs are highly dependent on their
      widths and depths.
468
469   Empirically, we demonstrate that GNNs perform similarly to noisy communication channels, reach-
470   ing their optimal information transmission states when Shannon’s theorem is satisfied and degener-
471   ating into band-limited channels when not. The required widths and depths of the network to achieve
472   this critical phase transition are related to various properties of input graphs, even graphs with the
473
      same number of nodes or edges can vary considerably. Furthermore, reaching the optimal informa-
      tion transmission state does not necessarily guarantee improvements in model performance. This
474
      is illustrated in the Appendix F that C3E variants might still suffer from some inevitable problems
475
      of GNNs. Additionally, we provide theoretical analysis and explanations of favored operations in
476   GNNs such as residual connection (Appendix B.1) and graph rewiring (Appendix B.2) based on our
477   proposed theory.
478
479
      Despite these promising results and theories, this work has several limitations. First, we consider the
      maximum entropy state of GNNs, such that the consideration of activation functions is bypassed.
480
      Nonetheless, activation functions have crucial impacts on the distribution of latent node representa-
481

1218                         Figure 8: The transition of layer-wise normalized Dirichlet energy of node representation.
1219
1220   The Dirichlet energy is widely adopted in graph theory and graph signal processing to measure the
1221   smoothness of features. In this work, we use normalized Dirichlet Energy to quantify the changes
1222   in node representation regarding the corresponding initial node features. The normalized Dirichlet
1223   Energy is defined by,
1224                                                     tr(Hl T LHl )
                                             E(Hl ) =                  ,                              (49)
1225                                                      tr(X T LX)
1226   where tr(·) denotes the trace and L = D − A denotes the graph laplacian matrix. From Fig. 8, we
1227   can observe that compared to the backbone models, the Dirichlet energy of Deep models and C3E
1228   variants reduces in the subsequent few layers, which might indicate the presence of over-smoothing.
1229   The Dirichlet energy of C3E models gradually decreases to a minimum value, then continuously
1230   increases to a certain value, and converges to a smaller value eventually. Although the Deep models
1231   sometimes show similar characteristics, they often diverge at deeper layers.
1232   According to Zhou et al. (2021), the layer-wise Dirichlet energy should be constrained within cer-
1233   tain lower bound and upper bound to avoid over-smoothing and over-separating. It points out that
1234   GNNs can stack more layers by constraining the Dirichlet energy of latent node representations,
1235   such that the Dirichlet energy rapidly decreases in the shallow layers, then consistently increases in
1236   the subsequent layers, and converges to a stable status (i.e., the Dirichlet energy almost remains the
1237   same) in deeper layers.
1238   Based on these observations and previous results, we can conclude that having a small Dirichlet
1239   energy does not always mean poor model performance based on Fig. 8 and Table .1. In other words,
1240   if the benefit of reaching or approaching the optimal information transmission state is smaller than
1241   the inescapable negative effects (like over-smoothing or over-separating) of increasing the width and
       depth, then model performance is not guaranteed, and vice versa.
