009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite significant advancements in deep probabilistic models, effective learn-
012            ing of low-dimensional discrete latent representations remains challenging. This
013            paper introduces a novel method to improve variational inference in discrete la-
014            tent variable models by employing Error-Correcting Codes (ECCs) to add redun-
015            dancy to the latent representations, later exploited by the variational approximated
016
               posterior to provide more accurate estimates, thereby reducing the variational
               gap. Drawing inspiration from ECCs used in digital communications and data
017
               storage, we demonstrate proof-of-concept using a Discrete Variational Autoen-
018
               coder (DVAE) with binary latent variables and block repetition codes. We then
019            extend it to a hierarchical structure inspired by polar codes, in which some latent
020            bits are more robustly protected than others. Our approach significantly enhances
021            generation quality, data reconstruction, and uncertainty calibration compared to
022            the uncoded DVAE, even when trained with tighter bounds such as the Importance
023            Weighted Autoencoder (IWAE) objective. In particular, we demonstrate superior
024            performance on MNIST, FMNIST, CIFAR10, and Tiny ImageNet datasets. The
025            general approach of integrating ECCs into variational inference is compatible with
026            existing techniques to boost variational inference, such as importance sampling or
027
               Hamiltonian Monte Carlo. We also formulate the properties that ECCs need to
               possess to be effectively used for improved discrete variational inference.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Discrete latent space models seek to represent data using a finite set of features. Recent progress in
033   generative models has increasingly favored these representations, as they are well-suited for datasets
034   characterized by naturally discrete hidden states. However, effective learning of low-dimensional
035   discrete latent representations is technically challenging. Vector Quantized-Variational Autoen-
036   coders (VQ-VAEs) (Van Den Oord et al., 2017; Razavi et al., 2019) stand out as solutions for this
037   problem but rely on a non-probabilistic autoencoder, which does not provide uncertainty quantifica-
038   tion in the latent space (as further discussed in Appendix L). To fit a fully probabilistic Variational
039
      Autoencoder (VAE) model (Kingma & Welling, 2013), a common approach considers either Con-
      crete (Maddison et al., 2017) or Gumble-Softmax (Jang et al., 2017) approximations to sample
040
      from a discrete latent distribution in a reparameterizable manner (Ramesh et al., 2021; Lievin et al.,
041
      2020). However, this approach leads to instabilities since the gradient variance is sensitive to the
042   temperature that controls these approximations. The DVAE in Rolfe (2016); Vahdat et al. (2018b;a),
043   augments the binary latent representations with a set of continuous random variables, pairing each
044   bit with a continuous counterpart where reparameterization can be done in a more stable manner
045   after marginalizing the latent bits. The key distinction between the DVAE (Rolfe, 2016) and the
046   DVAE++ (Vahdat et al., 2018b) lies in their smoothing transformations: while Rolfe (2016) intro-
047   duces spike-and-exponential transformations, Vahdat et al. (2018b) uses overlapping exponential
048   distributions. These overlapping transformations are generalized in Vahdat et al. (2018a), enabling
049   tighter variational bounds. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our method over a simplified version
050
      of the DVAE++ (Vahdat et al., 2018b).
051   This work presents a novel method to improve variational inference and representation learning in
052   generative models with discrete latent variables. In particular, for a latent variable model, we argue
053   that one should use ECCs to introduce redundancy into the latent sample before the reconstruction
      decoder network processes it to generate the data. The variational approximation to the true posterior

098
               attributed to the increased dimensionality introduced by the redundancy from the ECC.

155   3   I MPROVING INFERENCE BY ADDING REDUNDANCY TO LATENT VECTORS
156
157   In the DVAE framework (Rolfe, 2016; Vahdat et al., 2018b;a), the authors use Boltzmann machines
158   as priors instead of the independent prior p(m) presented in Section 2. While these complex priors
159   increase the model’s flexibility and can produce competitive results, our objective is to enhance
160   inference through model design by maintaining the simpler independent prior. This approach would
161   improve interpretability and encourage the model to learn independent components in the latent
      space, which is essential for capturing potentially disentangled representations.


                                                               3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2013) are often viewed as lossy compression models, where the goal is
163   to minimize reconstruction error while imposing regularization through a prior distribution. How-
164   ever, our approach is better understood from a generative standpoint. We first sample a latent vector
165   m, generate an observation x, and focus on minimizing the error rate when recovering m from x.
166   Achieving this requires the variational approximation to be sufficiently accurate. In fields where re-
167   liable data transmission or storage is important, introducing ECCs is a well-established approach to
168   reduce the error rate when estimating a discrete source m transmitted through a noisy channel with
169   output x. Estimating m from x implies approximating the true and unknown posterior distribution
170   p(m|x) with a proposed qη (m|x). The gap between qη (m|x) and p(m|x) is precisely the vari-
171
      ational gap. We propose employing ECCs to safeguard m with controlled and known redundancy
      that can be leveraged by the variational posterior qη (m|x) by design. This way, it is possible to
172
      reduce the mistakes committed when comparing m with samples drawn from qη (m|x), obtaining a
173
      tighter approximation to the true posterior p(m|x), therefore reducing the gap to optimal inference.
174
175   ECCs play a crucial role in information theory and digital communications by enabling reliable data
176   transmission over unreliable channels (Moon, 2005). They introduce redundancy into the trans-
177
      mitted data, allowing the receiver to detect errors and, in many cases, correct them without re-
      transmission. In his seminal work, Shannon (Shannon, 1948) demonstrated the arbitrarily reliable
178
      communication is possible through error correction. Our approach builds on the idea that the gen-
179
      erative model in Fig. 1c can be conceptualized as a communication system, where the bits sampled
180   from p(m) undergo continuous modulation into z and are then transmitted through a nonlinear
181   communication channel (in this setting, the decoder NN) characterized by the input/output response
182   pθ (x|z) = p(fθ (z)). In this scenario, the complexity of the channel is essential since it is necessary
183   to account for the intricate nature of the data at its output (e.g., complex images). Following this
184   idea, the process of inference via qη (m|x) can be thought of as deciphering the latent variable m
185   given the observed data x, where the encoder NN plays the role of the channel equalizer, trying to
186   reverse the channel’s effects without knowing the bit correlations from the ECC.

189   4   C ODED DVAE
190
191   This section extends the previously described DVAE, introducing an ECC over m. We refer to this
192   model as coded DVAE. In ECCs, we augment the dimensionality of the binary latent space from
193   M to D in a controlled and deterministic manner, where R = M/D is the coding rate. An ECC
194   is typically designed so that the 2M possible codewords are separated as much as possible in the
195   space of binary vectors of D bits. This facilitates algorithms in detecting and/or correcting errors
196   by searching for the nearest code word. A random choice of the codewords brings what is known
197   as a random block code (Shannon, 1948). While they are known to be very robust and amenable to
198   theoretical analysis, their lack of structure makes them computationally intractable since we have to
199
      rely on codeword enumeration during the encoding/decoding process. In Appendix M, we include
      the formulation of a random code’s encoding/decoding process within the DVAE model.
200
201   Instead, we adopt a much simpler linear coding scheme, namely repetition codes. In a repetition
202   code, each bit of the original message m is repeated multiple times to create the encoded message c.
203   Intuitively, the more times an information bit is repeated, the better it is protected. Our experiments
204   consider uniform (M, D) repetition codes where all bits are repeated L times, resulting in codewords
205
      of dimension D = M L and a coding rate of R = 1/L. Note that repetition codes represent a
      special case of linear ECCs since each codeword can be deterministically computed by multiplying
206
      a binary vector m by an M × D generator matrix G, such that c = mT G, where u-th row, with
207
      u = 1, . . . , M , has entries equal to one at columns L(u − 1) + 1, L(u − 1) + 2, . . . , Lu, and zero
208   elsewhere. For example, for M = 3 and L = 2, the generator matrix of the (3, 6) repetition code is

269   To efficiently circumvent this issue during training, we employ a soft encoding approach. With soft

275   When marginalizing c using the soft encoding marginals, we disregard potential correlations be-
276

278

280   tive model in test time, we use hard bits encoded into valid codewords, yielding visually appealing
281   samples, indicating that our training approach is reliable.

      Related work
283
284   While the use of deep neural networks and generative models in digital communications problems
285   have been profusely reported in recent years (see Ye et al. (2024), Chen et al. (2024), Guo et al.
286   (2022), Wu et al. (2023), and Shen et al. (2023) for representative examples), the use of ECC tech-
287   niques as a design tool in machine learning is scarce. The most prominent example is Dietterich &
288
      Bakiri (1995), where the authors address multiclass learning problems via ECCs. In Aldaghri et al.
      (2021), the authors proposed using linear codes for applications that may require removing the trace
289
      of a sample from the system, e.g., a user requests their data to be deleted or corrupted data is dis-
290
      covered. They address a regression problem by introducing a coded learning protocol that employs
291   linear encoders to divide the training data before the learning phase. More recently, in Xue et al.
292   (2024) the authors introduced ECCs to improve code-to-code translation using transformers.

296
      This section empirically evaluates the DVAE and its coded counterpart using repetition codes. We
297
      show results on reconstruction and generation tasks. In particular, we display results for MNIST
298
      (Deng, 2012), FMNIST (Xiao et al., 2017), CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), and Tiny ImageNet
299   (Le & Yang, 2015) datasets. The selection of these relatively simple datasets is deliberate to aid
300   in a clearer understanding of the behaviors of various configurations. Additionally, we compared
301   the coded model to the uncoded DVAE trained using the IWAE objective (Burda et al., 2016), as
302   presented in Appendix H. All experimental results were obtained using the same architecture, which
303   is detailed in Appendix C. When introducing the repetition code, we only modify the encoder’s
304   output layer and the decoder’s input layer to adapt the architecture to the augmented dimension.

306   5.1   R ECONSTRUCTION
307
308   We first evaluate the model’s performance of reconstructing data by examining its uncoded and
309
      coded versions across different configurations, varying the number of information bits and code
      rates. The introduction of the repetition code led to improved reconstruction and smaller KL values,
310
      indicating that the posterior latent features are disentangled and less correlated. In Appendices D,
311
      E, F and G, we show the behavior of the ELBO loss function for all the models and datasets.
312
313   Image reconstruction quality. In the table included in Fig. 2, we first quantify the quality of the
314   reconstructions in FMNIST by measuring the Peak Signal-To-Noise Ratio (PSNR) in the test set.
315
      The results for the rest of the datasets are provided in sections D, E, F and G of the Appendix. In all
      the cases, the coded models yield higher PSNR values than their uncoded counterparts, indicating
316
      a superior performance in reconstruction. This improvement is also evident by visual inspection
317
      of Fig. 2, where the coded models exhibit a greater ability to capture details in the images for the
318   same latent dimension. We observe a general improvement in PSNR as we increase the number of
319   information bits, i.e., as we augment the latent dimensionality of the model. This increase in the
320   number of available latent vectors provides greater flexibility, enabling the models to capture the
321   underlying structure of the data more effectively. We also observe a general improvement in PSNR
322   as we decrease the code rate, i.e., as we add more redundancy. Note that adding redundancy does not
323   increase the model’s flexibility, since the information bits determine the number of latent vectors.
      However, coded models yield more accurate and detailed reconstructions.

      6   B EYOND REPETITION CODES
487
488   We have presented compelling proof-of-concept results that incorporating ECCs, like repetition
489   codes, into DVAEs can improve performance. We believe this opens a new path for designing
490   latent probabilistic models with discrete latent variables. Although a detailed analysis of the joint
491   design of ECC and encoder-decoder networks is beyond the scope of this work, we will outline key
492   properties that any ECCs must satisfy to be integrated within this framework.
493
494          • Scalable hard encoding m → c . Our model requires hard encoding for generation once
495
               the model is trained. This process should have linear complexity in M .

496          • Scalable soft encoding p(m) → p(c) . Soft encoding is required during training for
497            reparameterization. This process should also have linear complexity in M .

498          • Scalable soft decoding p(c) → p(m) . Our model employs soft-in soft-out (SISO)
499            decoding during inference. This process should again be linearly complex in M .
500          • Differentiability. Both encoding and decoding processes must be differentiable w.r.t the
501            inputs to enable gradient computation and backpropagation.
502
503

      oped effective ECC schemes that meet these properties, including state-          m1              m2
504
      of-the-art ECCs such as Low Density Parity Check (LDPC) codes (Gal-
505
      lager, 1962), or polar codes (Arikan, 2009). Efforts have also focused on
506   developing efficient SISO decoders, such as the sum-product algorithm
507   (Kschischang et al., 2001).                                                      c1              c2

521   7   C ONCLUSION
522
523   This paper presents the first proof-of-concept demonstration that safeguarding latent information
524   with ECCs within deep generative models holds promise for enhancing overall performance. By
525   integrating redundancy into the latent space, the variational family can effectively refine the infer-
526   ence network’s output according to the structure of the ECC. Our findings underscore the efficacy
527   of simple and efficient ECCs, like repetition codes, showcasing remarkable improvements over a
528
      lightweight version of the DVAE introduced in Vahdat et al. (2018b).
529   Furthermore, our work reveals numerous avenues for future research. Firstly, investigating decoder
530   architectures capable of efficiently utilizing the correlations and structure introduced by the ECCs, in
531   contrast to the feed-forward networks employed in this study. We also contemplate exploring more
532   complex and robust coding schemes, conducting theoretical analyses aligned with Shannon’s chan-
533   nel capacity and mutual information concepts to determine the fundamental parameters of the ECC
534
      needed to achieve reliable variational inference, exploring different modulations, and integrating
      these concepts into state-of-the-art models based on discrete representations.

       H     IWAE RESULTS
1534
1535
1536   One could draw a parallel between the coded DVAE with repetition codes and the well-known IWAE
1537   (Burda et al., 2016), but the two approaches are fundamentally different. In the IWAE, independent
1538   samples are drawn from the variational posterior and propagated independently through the gener-
1539   ative model to obtain a tighter variational bound on the marginal log-likelihood. In our method, we
1540   jointly propagate the output of the ECC encoder through the generative model, obtaining a single
1541
       prediction and exploiting the introduced known correlations in the variational approximation of the
       posterior. In the case of repetition codes, the ECC encoder outputs are repeated bits, or repeated
1542
       probabilities in the case of soft encoding. However, our approach extends beyond repetition codes,
1543
       opening a new field for improved inference in discrete latent variable models.
1544
1545   In this work, we specifically utilize the redundancy introduced by the repetition code to correct
1546   potential errors made by the encoder through a soft decoding approach, leading to a more accurate
1547
       approximation of p(m|x) and an improved proposal for sampling. The results obtained in the
       coded DVAE case cannot be achieved by training the uncoded DVAE with the IWAE objective. The
1548
       following results compare the uncoded IWAE model with the coded DVAE trained on FMNIST.
1549
       While the uncoded model shows slight performance gains with an increasing number of IWAE
1550   samples (which improves the evidence lower bound), it still underperforms compared to the coded
1551   model. Furthermore, when using 20 and 30 IWAE samples, the metrics slightly declined compared
1552   to using 10 samples, likely due to overfitting, as we applied a common early stopping point.

1704   Across all experiments, we have consistently configured the hyperparameter β, which controls the
1705   decay of exponentials in the smoothing transformation, to a value of 15. To illustrate its impact on
1706   the overall performance of the model, we conducted an ablation study on the value of this hyperpa-
1707   rameter for both uncoded and coded cases.
1708

1709

1710   distributions and, consequently, influencing the degree of overlapping. A lower β value results in
1711   more overlapped tails, while a higher value leads to less overlapped distributions. A priori, we would
1712   like these distributions to be separated, allowing us to retrieve the true value of the bit and effectively
1713   use the latent structure of the model.

1945
1946
       The reparameterization trick introduced in equation 5 requires that bits are independent, a condi-
1947
       tion not met in c once the code’s structure is introduced. To address this issue, during training, we
1948
       employ a soft encoding strategy. We assume the bits in c are independent and equally distributed
1949   according to qη (m|x). Therefore, instead of directly repeating sampled bits in m to obtain c fol-
1950   lowing c = mT G, we repeat the posterior probabilities for the copies of the same bit and sample z
1951   using the reparameterization trick in equation 5. Remark that, despite the soft encoding assumption
1952   during training, the generative results in Fig. 4, 6, 13, 19, 24, 24, and 29 are obtained through hard
1953   encoding. Namely, we sampled an information word m and obtained c by repeating its bits.
1954
       While the hard-encoding images are visually appealing, to evaluate the coded DVAE LL for a given
1955
       image x, we have to leverage the soft-encoding model since it is unrealistic for a sample from
1956   the soft-encoding model to produce equally repeated bits. In the soft-coded model, we sample bit
1957   probabilities from a prior distribution that we model through the product of M independent Beta
1958   distributions, and we use a proposal distribution model similar to the Vamp-prior in Tomczak &
1959   Welling (2018). Namely, a mixture model with components given by q(m|x) for different training
1960   points.
1961
       In the main text and Section J, we report the log-likelihood values for different model configurations
1962   trained on the FMNIST dataset. Table 4 presents the results obtained for the MNIST dataset. Due
1963   to their simplicity, these datasets do not require high-dimensional latent spaces, and competitive
1964   results can be achieved with just 8 or 10 information bits. However, for more complex datasets
1965   like CIFAR10 or Tiny ImageNet, high-dimensional latent spaces are necessary to capture spatial
1966   information, colors, and textures. For these high-dimensional datasets, we were unable to obtain
1967   valid log-likelihood estimates because the number of samples needed for importance sampling to
1968   converge was too large. However, given the difference in the level of detail in the reconstructed and
1969   generated images between coded and uncoded models (see Sections F and G), coded models are
1970
       expected to better approximate the true marginal likelihood of the data.

       L    C ONNECTION TO PREVIOUS WORK ON VAE S AS SOURCE CODING
1973
            METHODS
1974
1975
1976   Effective learning of low-dimensional discrete latent representations is a technically challenging
1977
       problem. In this work, we propose a novel method to improve inference in discrete VAEs within a
       fully probabilistic framework, introducing a new perspective on the inference problem. While VAEs
1978
       have been analyzed in the literature using rate-distortion (RD) theory (Chen et al., 2022; Townsend
1979
       et al., 2019; Van Den Oord et al., 2017), our approach stems from a different perspective, that is
1980   indeed compatible with all those works.
1981
1982   While VAEs are often viewed as lossy compression models (e.g. VAEs as source coding methods),
1983
       our contribution is best understood from a generative perspective. We conceptualize the process of
       inference via as decoding the latent variable from the observed data. We sample a vector m, generate
1984
       an image x, and seek to minimize the error rate in recovering m from x. Achieving this requires
1985
       the variational approximation q(m|x) to closely align with the true posterior. Estimating m from
1986   x thus involves approximating the true posterior p(m|x) with q(m|x). We show that introducing
1987   redundancy in generation reduces error rates in estimating m, leading to better approximations of
1988   the latent posterior distribution. This improvement is reflected in the enhanced performance of our
1989   model, as demonstrated in our experimental results.
1990
       VQ-VAEs (Van Den Oord et al., 2017) are notable for effectively learning compressed discrete
1991
       representations of data, and we believe it can be beneficial to highlight key differences between
1992   VQ-VAEs and our approach. A primary distinction lies in the latent space’s structure and dimen-
1993   sionality. In image modeling, VQ-VAEs typically employ a latent matrix where indices correspond
1994   to codewords in a codebook. This design allows different codewords to capture specific patches of
1995   the original image, improving reconstruction and generation (the latter through an autoregressive
1996   prior on the latent representations). However, this matrix representation complicates interpretability,
1997   as each data point is represented by a grid of embeddings. In contrast, our method encodes the entire
       image into the latent space, rather than splitting it into patches. Another important difference is that


                                                         37
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1998
       VQ-VAEs is based on a non-probabilistic encoder, where the output is mapped to the nearest latent
1999   code based on a distance metric. This deterministic mapping limits the model’s ability to quantify
2000   uncertainty in the latent space. In contrast, our method uses a fully probabilistic framework that en-
2001   ables uncertainty quantification in the latent representations. Moreover, all operations in our method
2002   are differentiable, enabling seamless computation and backpropagation of gradients, allowing for an
2003   end-to-end training of the model. This also lets us use the reparameterization trick introduced in the
2004   DVAE++ (Vahdat et al., 2018b), which we found to be more stable than continuous relaxations like
2005   the Gumbel-Softmax, used in VQ-VAEs with stochastic quantization (Williams et al., 2020).
2006   In RD theory, the focus is on compression within the latent space, typically analyzed from an
2007   encoder/decoder and reconstruction (distortion) perspective. RD theory sets theoretical limits on
2008   achievable compression rates and describes how practical models may diverge from these limits.
2009   Using RD practical compression methods, Townsend et al. (2019) demonstrates how asymmetric
2010   numeral systems (ANS) can be integrated with a VAE to improve its compression rate by jointly
2011   encoding sequences of data points, bringing performance closer to RD theoretical limits. Similarly,
2012   Chen et al. (2022) shows that a complex prior distribution in a VAE using an autoregressive invertible
2013
       flow narrows the gap between the approximate posterior and the true posterior, thereby enhancing
       the overall performance of the VAE. We note that even using an independent prior, the hierarchical
2014
       code structure outlined in Section 6 naturally decouples information across different latent spaces at
2015
       various conceptual levels. Specifically, the most relevant information (class label) is encoded by the
2016   most protected latent space, while the other space captures fine-grained features. This effect is not
2017   straightforward to enforce through direct design of a more complex prior.
2018
2019
       Instead, our method complements these efforts by introducing redundancy in the generative pathway
       to enhance variational inference, resulting in more accurate approximations of the latent posterior.
2020
       Importantly, even when using a complex prior distribution, ECCs can still be leveraged to improve
2021
       variational inference and boost overall model performance. In this paper, we demonstrate that our
2022   approach yields more robust models even with a simple, fixed independent prior, as evidenced by
2023   improved log-likelihood, generation quality, and reconstruction metrics. Moreover, in Section 6,
2024   we show that integrating a hierarchical ECC with the same independent prior leads to even greater
2025   performance gains.
