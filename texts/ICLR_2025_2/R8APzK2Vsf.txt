009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) exhibit exceptional proficiency in comprehend-
012            ing human language. Despite their significant success across a wide array of tasks,
013            including text generation, translation, question answering, and even code gener-
014            ation, understanding tabular data remains a challenging task. Especially, tabular
015            data lacks an intrinsic order of the different features (table fields), whereas LLMs
016
               take only sequential inputs. Consequently, an artificial order is imposed, the im-
               pact of which on the performance of LLMs has not yet been thoroughly inves-
017
               tigated. Surprisingly, as discovered in this work, this artificially induced order
018
               bias dramatically influences the performance of LLMs on tasks related to tabular
019            data. Mitigating the order bias presents a significant challenge. To address this,
020            we propose a simple and cost-effective method, Re-Ordering Tabular feATures
021            fOR LLM (ROTATOR-LLM), to conduct test-time compute without fine-tuning
022            the base LLM. Aiming at optimizing the feature order of tabular data and boost-
023            ing LLMs’ capability to better understand the data semantics, ROTATOR-LLM
024            re-frames the ordering problem as a feature trajectory generation task. A dy-
025            namic programming based meta-controller is trained to auto-regressively generate
026            an individualized feature trajectory for each data instance via accumulative value
027
               estimation of the serialized feature input through the LLM’s final performance
               metrics. Model performance is maximized by iteratively selecting features across
028
               different steps. Experimental results on multiple datasets and LLMs show close
029
               to or over 20% performance boosts via features reordered by ROTATOR-LLM
030            against the un-ordered counterpart. Also, it outperforms State-Of-The-Art tabular
031            LLM methods with significant margin. Moreover, meta-controller demonstrates
032            strong transferability: the tested LLMs gain performance enhancements when uti-
033            lizing a meta-controller trained on one of them.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Tabular data is prevalent in real-world scientific, medical, biological, sociological, financial, and
039
      retail databases, necessitating significant time and effort for humans to process and analyze Dong
      & Wang (2024); Fang et al. (2024). Fortunately, advancements in large language models (LLMs)
040
      have enabled rigorous exploration of their application in various tasks related to tabular data mod-
041
      eling Yuan et al. (2024); Hu et al. (2024). Recent breakthroughs have involved LLMs to handle a
042   wide range of tabular data tasks, such as TabLLM Hegselmann et al. (2023), TableGPT Zha et al.
043   (2023b), and TableLlama Zhang et al. (2023).
044
045
      Although tabular data can be easily converted into text format, LLMs struggle to effectively analyze
      the converted data. Since LLMs are primarily pre-trained on natural language, they face challenges
046
      in extracting meaningful insights from structured tabular data. To overcome this challenge, existing
047
      work primarily focuses on fine-tuning LLMs on tabular dataset to inject the data prior knowledge
048   to the models. For example, TableLlama employs LongLoRA to fine-tune the Llama-2-7B LLM
049   on the extensive TableInstruct datasets. Similarly, TableGPT introduces a table encoder and chain-
050   of-command mechanism, utilizing a Phoenix-7B LLM for inference. Despite these advancements,
051   much of the current research on tabular data analysis overlooks the critical role of feature order in
052   the prompt: due to the sequential nature of transformer decoder based models, an artificial order
053   is inevitably created when feeding the features into the LLM one by one regardless of the detailed
      prompting schemes. Our extensive studies reveal that this induced ordering of features significantly

      This problem is mainly rooted in the order bias in the pre-training data, where the collected data
072   follows certain sequences preferred by humans. Such order preference is captured by the LLMs
073   during the pre-training stage, which enables LLMs to better learn the data semantics whose feature
074   importance ranking aligns with the order bias Sagawa et al. (2019); Koh et al. (2021). To tackle
075   this, an intuitive solution is to remove the order bias by fine-tuning the LLMs on unbiased data.
076   However, fine-tuning LLMs is not only time- and resource-consuming due to the billions of updated
077   parameters, but also labor-intensive, requiring collecting high-quality data Yang et al. (2024); Zha
078   et al. (2023a). A more practical approach is to preprocess the data to align with the LLMs’ inherent
079   order bias, enabling them to better grasp the data’s semantics. This alignment offers greater potential
080
      for real-world applications due to its feasibility, scalability, and extensibility across diverse datasets.
081   In this work, we introduce Re-Ordering Tabular feATures fOR LLM (ROTATOR-LLM), a simple
082   and cost-effective method to help LLMs better comprehend data semantics via test-time compute
083   in the input level Snell et al. (2024). Specifically, ROTATOR-LLM converts the feature ordering
084   problem into a task of generating feature trajectories, where each trajectory represents a sequence of
085   features in a specific order. To avoid the high resource consumption of fine-tuing the LLM and the
086
      corresponding expensive human labeling, ROTATOR-LLM trains a light-weight neural network as a
      meta-controller to auto-regressively generates the optimized feature trajectory for each data instance,
087
      guided by a value function designed to supervise its training process. It is challenging to define the
088
      value function for a specific feature order such that this value aligns with the corresponding LLMs’
089   performance. We are motivated by dynamic programming to overcome this challenge. Specifically,
090   the value of a feature trajectory is defined as its potential maximal value in the next state within
091   the whole generation path. At the last state, the value of an integral trajectory is determined by
092   the LLMs’ performance. This approach allows us to estimate the value of any feature trajectory,
093   which, in turn, supervises the training of the meta-controller. To evaluate ROTATOR-LLM, we
094   conduct experiments with three LLMs across four tabular datasets. The results demonstrate that
095   LLMs perform significantly better on data reordered by ROTATOR-LLM compared to random or
096   default orders, underscoring the effectiveness of the reordering process. Moreover, ROTATOR-
097
      LLM outperforms existing foundational tabular LLMs, further highlighting its potential in real-
      world applications. In summary, our contributions in this work are as follows:
098
099
             • Order Bias of LLMs. We demonstrate that the order of instance features in a prompt
100            significantly influences LLM predictions, identifying the presence of order bias.
101
102          • Alignment to Order Bias. We propose ROTATOR-LLM, a cost-effective solution that
103            requires no tuning of LLM parameters. ROTATOR-LLM aligns a data instance with the
104            inherent order bias of LLMs by re-ordering its features.
105          • Experimental Evaluation. Experimental results on four datasets with three popular LLMs
106            demonstrate the superior performance lift brought by ROTATOR-LLM, which improves
107            LLMs’ classification accuracy by 20% in average.

139

140
      of LLMs. ROTATOR-LLM aims to generate the optimal trajectory τ ∗ for each instance x, thereby
141   maximizing the accuracy of the LLMs’ predictions.

144   2.2   T EXT- BASED SERIALIZATION
145
146   Text-based Serialization refers to converting tabular data into text data to fit the input modality of
147   LLMs. Existing work explores several methods of text-based serialization. For example, Markdown
148   table Liu et al. (2023); Jaitly et al. (2023), JSON-file format Singha et al. (2023); Sui et al. (2024),
149   and sentence serialization Yu et al. (2023); Jaitly et al. (2023). To maximally leverage the sequence-
150   to-sequence capacity of LLMs, we consider the sentence serialization to convert the data features
151   into text data. The advantage of sentence serialization is its alignment with the natural language data
152
      where LLMs are pre-trained. In this work, we use a template given in Appendix A to convert tabular
      data into text data. For instance, we adopt the sentence “the age of this person is 30; this person has
153
      no house” to represent the tabular data {Age:30,House:No}. Our method can be easily extended
154
      to fit Markdown table and JSON-file formats of serialized data, but their performance is out of the
155   scope of this work.

      3.1   W HY LLM S HAVE O RDER B IAS ?
163
164   Order bias refers to the impact that the sequence of tabular data features has on the predictions
165   made by LLMs. While from the perspective of how human beings understand the tabular data,
166   the order of features/fields is not meaningful and should not affect the model output, each particular
167   serialization of these features/fields indeed results in a different input sequence for an auto-regressive
168   model and accordingly a difference in the outcome. For LLMs, this difference affects their attention
169   maps. We show an example in Figure 2 to demonstrate the influence of different feature orders on
170
      the last-layer attention maps. As each feature is represented by a sentence, i.e. multiple tokens,
      each cell in Figure 2 corresponds to a matrix of attention values between tokens. The notation
171
      ‘∼ i, j, k’ indicates the attention matrix is computed based on a mixture of information from the
172
      token embeddings associated with features i, j and k. In this example, the sequence of features 1,
173   2, 3, and 4 in Figure 2 (a) mixes a different set of tokens compared to the feature sequence of 2, 3,
174   4, and 1 for the computation of last-layer attention map. The variations in last-layer attention maps
175   lead to obvious differences in the prediction results.
176
177   3.2   D EMONSTRATIONS OF O RDER B IAS
178
179   We demonstrate the presence of order bias in LLMs using real-world tabular datasets. Specifically,
180   we examine the variance in LLMs’ predictions caused by different permutations of data features.
                                                                                    # of 1      of 1
181   The probability of LLMs’ predictions is estimated by P(ŷ = 1) = # of Permutations    = #M  ! , and
182   P(ŷ = 0) = 1 − P(ŷ = 1). The variance in predictions is quantified by the entropy H(ŷ) =
183
      −P(ŷ = 0) log2 P(ŷ = 0) − P(ŷ = 1) log2 P(ŷ = 1). For instance, for data instance having
      two features: age and house, if an LLM outputs ŷ = 1 for {Age:30,House:No} and ŷ = 0
184
      for {House:No,Age:30}, then P(ŷ = 1) = P(ŷ = 0) = 0.5, resulting in an entropy of 1. If
185
      the LLM’s predictions show no variance, then either P(ŷ = 0) = 1 or P(ŷ = 1) = 1, yielding a
186   minimal entropy of 0. Conversely, if the predictions are randomly distributed, P(ŷ = 0) = 0.5 and
187   P(ŷ = 1) = 0.5, leading to a maximum entropy of 1. Higher entropy indicates greater variance in
188   prediction results, signifying a stronger presence of order bias in the LLMs.

467   6     R ELATED W ORK
468
469   We discuss related work on tabular data understanding in this section. Existing work that leverages
470   LLMs to process tabular data is primarily viewed from three perspectives: feature serialization,
471   large-scale fine-tuning, and prompt engineering. We give more details as follows.
472
473   Feature Serialization. Feature serialization is a simple way to let LLMs understand tabular data.
474   Specifically, a straightforward way would be to directly input a programming-language readable data
475
      structure, such as Markdown format Liu et al. (2023); Jaitly et al. (2023), JSON-file format Singha
      et al. (2023); Sui et al. (2024), HTML format Singha et al. (2023), and Python dictionary Wang et al.
476
      (2023). Another way is to convert the tables into natural language sentence using templates based on
477
      the column headers and cell values Yu et al. (2023); Jaitly et al. (2023). This method can maximally
478   leverage the sequence-to-sequence capacity of LLMs to understand tabular data.
479
480   Large-scale Fine-tuning. Fine-tuning on tabular datasets is a straightforward way to inject the
481   data prior knowledge to LLMs. There are several existing work of fine-tuning. TableLlama adopts
482   LongLoRA to fine-tune the Llama-2-7B LLM on the extensive TableInstruct datasets Zhang et al.
483   (2023). TableGPT introduces a table encoder and chain-of-command mechanism and performs
484   instruction tunings for Phoenix-7B LLMs on collections of tabular datasets Li et al. (2024). Different
485   from existing work, TabLLM considers few-shot examples for prompts during the fine-tuning, and
      updates the Bigscience/T0-3B LLMs on single domain tabular datasets Zhang et al. (2024a).


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      In-context Learning. Existing work has demonstrated that LLMs are few-shot learners of tabular
487   data Chen (2022); Narayan et al. (2022); Guo et al. (2023). Leveraging few-shot examples in the
488   prompts, LLMs can better understand the data semantics through in-context learning. Other prompt
489   engineering methods include chain-of-thoughts Wei et al. (2022), tree-of-thoughts Yao et al. (2024),
490   self-consistency Wang et al. (2022), and others Sui et al. (2023).

      7   C ONCLUSION
493
494
      In this work, we demonstrate novelly discover and thoroughly explore the order bias of LLMs on
495
      tabular data, where the arrangement of data features can mislead LLM predictions. To address this
496   issue, we propose ROTATOR-LLM, an approach designed to align tabular data with this order bias,
497   enabling LLMs to better comprehend the data semantics. Specifically, ROTATOR-LLM employs a
498   meta-controller to learn the optimal feature order. It estimates the value function for each feature
499   order using dynamic programming, which guides the training of the meta-controller. Our exper-
500   imental results on four datasets across three LLMs show that ROTATOR-LLM achieves superior
501   performance compared to baseline methods and state-of-the-art foundational tabular LLMs when
502   applied to reordered data. Additionally, ROTATOR-LLM exhibits strong transferability across mul-
503   tiple LLMs, indicating its adaptability to diverse tasks. Without requiring fine-tuning of LLMs,
504
      ROTATOR-LLM proves to be a more cost-effective solution than traditional debiasing methods,
      underscoring its potential for real-world applications.
