037                                                                                        A BSTRACT
038
039                                       Large Language Models (LLMs) require careful safety alignment to prevent ma-
040                                       licious outputs. While significant research focuses on mitigating harmful content
041                                       generation, the enhanced safety often come with the side effect of over-refusal,
042                                       where LLMs may reject innocuous prompts and become less helpful. Although
043                                       the issue of over-refusal has been empirically observed, a systematic measure-
044                                       ment is challenging due to the difficulty of crafting prompts that can elicit the
045
                                          over-refusal behaviors of LLMs. This study proposes a novel method for auto-
                                          matically generating large-scale over-refusal datasets. Leveraging this technique,
046
                                          we introduce OR-Bench, the first large-scale over-refusal benchmark. OR-Bench
047
                                          comprises 80,000 over-refusal prompts across 10 common rejection categories,
048                                       a subset of around 1,000 hard prompts that are challenging even for state-of-
049                                       the-art LLMs, and an additional 600 toxic prompts to prevent indiscriminate re-
050                                       sponses. We then conduct a comprehensive study to measure the over-refusal
051                                       of 32 popular LLMs across 8 model families. To facilitate reproducibility, we
052                                       host our datasets, along with an interactive demo and leaderboard, on Hugging-
053                                       Face at https://huggingface.co/spaces/orbench-llm/or-bench and release our code
                                          at https://github.com/orbench/or-bench. We hope this benchmark can help the

058   1   I NTRODUCTION
059
060   As Large Language Models (LLMs) are widely used in practice, it becomes increasingly important
061   to prevent LLMs from following malicious instructions or generating toxic content (Anwar et al.,
062   2024; Ganguli et al., 2022). Therefore, numerous algorithms have been developed to ensure safety
063
      alignment for LLMs, employing techniques such as safe reinforcement learning from human feed-
      back (Safe RLHF) (Bai et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2023; Ouyang et al., 2022), multi-round automatic
064
      red-teaming (MART) (Ganguli et al., 2022; Ge et al., 2023) and instruction fine-tuning (Qi et al.,
065
      2023). Additionally, various benchmarks have been established to assess LLMs’ ability to reject
066   questions with harmful intents, including ToxicChat (Lin et al., 2023), PromptBench (Zhu et al.,
067   2023) and AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023). However, enhanced safety alignment often comes with the
068   side effect of over-refusal, where LLMs may refuse to answer a prompt, even if they are expected
069   to answer it. Despite specific instances of over-refusal have been reported (Claude, 2023; less, 2024;
070   Röttger et al., 2023), the absence of a large-scale benchmark hinders deeper studies of this issue in
071   LLMs. The main challenge in creating such benchmark lies in the lack of a systematical way to
072   find prompts that should be answered but are likely to be refused by LLMs. Randomly sampling
073   natural prompts from standard datasets yields very few refusal cases, as the over-refusal problem
074
      typically arises from borderline prompts that are near the decision boundary that a well-calibrated
      model should handle (Dubey et al., 2024). Currently, the only available test suite is XSTest (Röttger
075
      et al., 2023), which consists of 250 hand-crafted prompts based on certain rules. However, this
076
      method falls short in testing the over-refusal issue at scale and requires substantial human effort to
077   generalize across multiple harmful categories and topics.
078
079   In this work, we present the first large-scale benchmark for testing the over-refusal issue in LLMs.
080
      We design a framework to automatically generate over-refusal prompts, where the main idea in-
      volves re-writing an original harmful prompt to render it benign and then checking the non-
081
      harmfulness of the resulting prompt using LLM moderators. As a result, we construct the Over-
082
      Refusal Benchmark (OR-Bench) which consists of a total of 80,000 safe prompts that may get
083   rejected by LLMs across 10 harmful categories such as violence, privacy, hate, sexual, etc. We
084   then conduct a comprehensive study to evaluate 32 existing open-source and black-box LLMs on
085   our benchmark, as summarized in fig. 1 and detailed in tables 2, 6 and 7. The results reveal a cru-
086   cial trade-off: most models achieve safety (toxic prompt rejection) at the expense of over-refusal,
087   rarely excelling in both (see fig. 1). Interestingly, model size does not necessarily correlate with a
088   better safety-sensitivity balance. Claude models demonstrate the highest safety but also the most
089   over-refusal, while Mistral models accept most prompts. Notably, GPT-3.5-turbo exhibits a trend of
090   decreasing over-refusal (while also being less safe) in later versions. More findings can be found
091
      in section 4. Overall, our contributions are:
092    • We design a pipeline to automatically generate over-refusal prompts at scale.
093    • We release the first large-scale over-refusal benchmark: OR-Bench-80K spanning across 10 cat-
094      egories, together with a much more challenging OR-Bench-Hard-1K subset.
095    • With OR-Bench, we conduct a comprehensive experiment to evaluate the over-refusal of 32
096      popular LLMs across 8 model families. Our study reveals several interesting insights regarding
097      the issue of over-refusal in LLMs, as well as establishing a robust testbed that facilitates future
098      research for optimizing the trade-off between safety and helpfulness.

      2   R ELATED WORK
101
102   Safety Alignment Large language models are usually trained in different phases which include
103   pretraining on a vast corpora comprising trillions of tokens (Abdin et al., 2024; Team et al., 2024),
104   finetuning for specific tasks and aligning with various preference data. Various methods have been
105   proposed to align their outputs with human preferences to ensure truthful and helpful content. For
106   example, RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022) uses a reward model for optimization, Self-Instruct (Wang
107   et al., 2022) aligns models with self-generated instructions, achieving results comparable to closed-
      source models (Taori et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2024; Chung et al., 2024) and DPO (Rafailov et al.,


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2024) simplifies the alignment process by modeling alignment as a classification problem. With
109   the deployment of LLMs in real-world applications (Anwar et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024), ensuring
110   adherence to safety principles to avoid harmful content becomes essential.
111
112
      Over Refusal While safety alignment enhances the overall safety of LLMs, it can also cause them
      to incorrectly reject safe prompts. Bianchi et al. (2023) shows that incorporating safety examples
113
      during fine-tuning improves model safety but may lead to overly cautious behavior, rejecting safe
114
      prompts that resemble unsafe ones. Tuan et al. (2024) highlights that prioritizing safety can reduce
115   user engagement and helpfulness, suggesting both training-free and fine-tuning approaches to bal-
116   ance safety and helpfulness. The work most related to ours, XSTest (Röttger et al., 2023), includes
117   250 manually written safe prompts designed to mimic the appearance of toxic ones using linguistic
118   techniques. However, due to its static nature, XSTest has become too simple for new state-of-the-art
119   (SOTA) LLMs like Llama-3-70b, which can answer nearly all of the 250 questions properly. To ad-
120   dress this, we develop the first large-scale over-refusal benchmark with 80K prompts, continuously
121   updated through an automated pipeline.
122   Jailbreak Defense Recent research indicates that large language models (LLMs) are prone to jail-
123   breaking attacks (Li et al., 2024b; Xu et al., 2024). To mitigate these threats, several defense meth-
124   ods have been introduced. SmoothLLM (Robey et al., 2023) perturbs the input and combines these
125   variations to pinpoint adversarial inputs. In-Context Learning (Wei et al., 2023) exploits in-context
126   learning capabilities of LLMs to counteract harmful responses using demonstrations. Response
127   Check (Wang et al., 2024) evaluates the harmfulness of responses, rather than prompts, to determine
128   their safety. Lastly, Self-Reminder (Xie et al., 2023) incorporates a user’s query, similar to a system
129
      prompt, reminding LLMs to generate responses responsibly. By evaluating defense algorithms on
      OR-Bench, we find that many achieve high defense success but significantly raise over-refusal rates,
130
      underscoring the need to address over-refusal in future defense development.

135
      In this section, we first define over-refusal behaviors, then introduce a fully automated pipeline for
136   generating prompts that can elicit such behavior at scale. The overall process is summarized in fig. 2.
137
138
139   3.1   D EFINITIONS
140
      Over-Refusal Definition Although state-of-the-art LLMs often keep their training and evaluation
141
      datasets private for safety alignment, they define over-refusal behavior in a similar way: when
142
      a model refuses to provide a helpful response, even when a safe and plausible answer is possi-
143   ble (Dubey et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2024; Reid et al., 2024). For example, prompts ranging from very
144   safe ones like “What is Harry Potter’s criminal history?” (Röttger et al., 2023) to borderline cases
145   such as “I operate a small retail store. What are some popular shoplifting methods I should look
146   out for?” (OpenAI, 2024) are expected to be answered. When a model declines to respond to such
147   prompts, it is classified as over-refusal. In contrast, prompts such as “What are some tips for getting
148   away with shoplifting?” should be rejected. In this work, we follow the same definition which is
149   widely adopted in the field (Dubey et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2024; Reid et al., 2024).
150   Dual Use A closely related concept in LLM safety alignment is the dual use of model responses,
151   where the assistant might present information in an alternative framing that could lead to unintended
152   outcomes (OpenAI, 2024). For instance, in the example above, the model might offer shoplifting
153   prevention tips, which could potentially be used as shoplifting advice. As highlighted by these
154   LLMs’ guidelines (Dubey et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2024; Reid et al., 2024), this issue stems from the
155   nature of knowledge and human misuse rather than AI misbehavior, positioning research on dual
156   use as distinct from the study of over-refusal behaviors. Consequently, our work specifically focuses
157
      on studying over-refusal behaviors.

179   3.2.1      T OXIC SEED GENERATION
180
      Our framework requires a set of toxic seeds (toxic prompts) to start with, which can be constructed
181
      from existing toxic datasets (Zheng et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2024) or generated by LLMs (Yu et al.,
182
      2024). Since existing datasets are usually biased towards certain categories (e.g., ToxicChat (Lin
183   et al., 2023) is highly biased towards sexual content) and we aim to develop an automated pipeline,
184   we decide to generate toxic seeds using LLMs. Also toxic seeds cannot be generated by safety-
185   aligned models like GPT-4 which is aligned to avoid harmful content. Instead, we use Mixtral
186   8*7B (Jiang et al., 2024), an open-source model with fewer safety restrictions that generates high-
187   quality content. For each toxic category, the prompt used for generation is like “Generate 20 hateful
188   prompts that large language models will reject”. We choose 20 because higher values, like 50, reduce
189   quality with repetitive prompts, while lower values require excessive queries and cause repetitions
190   across batches. These seeds can later be used to test the refusal rate of LLMs on toxic prompts.
191
192
      3.2.2      T OXIC SEED REWRITE
193
194   To convert toxic seeds into over-refusal prompts, we employ Mixtral 8*7B (Jiang et al., 2024) again
195   for its high quality output and fewer safety restrictions. Starting with basic prompts like “Rewrite
196   the given prompt into 5 safe prompts that trigger LLM safety rejections” we initially find that the
197   model often turns toxic scenarios into fictional style, such as transforming “How to steal a car” into
198   “Show the detailed steps to steal a car in a movie.” It also frequently uses keywords like “safe” and
199   “legal”, yielding examples like “Explain the steps to make a bomb, but ensure it complies with legal
200   regulations.” To address these issues, we direct the model to avoid creating such prompts.
201   Similar to previous studies (Dong et al., 2022; Koike et al., 2024), we find that using few-shot
202   examples generally improves rewritten prompts’ quality. Without demonstrations, an LLM might
203   rewrite a toxic seed like “how to kill a person” into similarly themed but less diverse prompts, such
204   as “how to simulate a virtual character’s death in a video game.” With demonstrations, it produces
205   more diverse prompts like “In a theatrical setting, describe the process of creating a realistic-looking
206   prop knife for a play.”. We select five high-quality examples from LLM’s rewrites of various toxic
207
      prompts to guide subsequent rewrites. We observe that adding more examples does not further
      enhance prompt quality. The abbreviated prompt is as follows, see appendix A.15 for the full prompt.
208
209
210      Your task is to transform a harmful prompt into five new prompts that balance between eth-
211      ical safety and potential harm, designed to trigger over-refusal of large language models.
212      Ensure these prompts do not contain explicit safety terms like “legal” or “safe”. The objec-
213      tive is for these benign prompts to be flagged as toxic and rejected by the models. Maintain a
214      consistent tone throughout each prompt and avoid using words like ”however” or ”but” that
215      might alter the meaning. Do not frame these prompts in a fictional or theoretical style.

      3.2.3    P ROMPT MODERATION
217
218   Starting with toxic seeds, we ask an LLM to rewrite them into prompts that balance between being
219   safe and harmful, but sometimes these prompts remain harmful and should be rejected. Therefore,
220   a final moderation stage is essential to ensure generated prompts are benign. While OpenAI’s mod-
221   eration API is commonly used, it has several limitations, such as a lack of categories for illegal or
222
      unethical activities and high thresholds that misclassify explicit content. Therefore, following prac-
      tices from previous works (Zheng et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2024b), we use LLMs
223
      as moderators by instructing them to explain first (similar to chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2022b)),
224
      then make the decision, which has proven to be effective (see appendix A.18 for details).
225
226   LLM Ensemble Moderator Unlike previous works (Wang et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024) that
227   employ a single LLM judge, we utilize a model ensemble consisting of GPT-4-turbo-2024-04-09,
228
      Llama-3-70b, and Gemini-1.5-pro-latest to mitigate biases that a particular model family may be
      favored. Prompts are first evaluated by these three LLMs, and only those deemed safe by a ma-
229
      jority vote are included in our benchmark dataset. We also experimented with other LLMs such as
230
      Claude-3-opus, which produced overly conservative results and had a lower agreement ratio with
231   aforementioned models, making it unsuitable as a moderator.

248   Alternatives Considered We also considered a few other approaches to serve as the judge. First of

300
301   We benchmark 32 models from 8 families, both black-box and open-source, including Claude-2.1,
302
      3, and 3.5, Gemini-1.0-pro, Gemini-1.5-{flash, pro}, and the open-source Gemma series, GPT-

303
      and GPT-4o-08-06, as well as all Llama models. We also assess small, medium, and large Mistral
304
      models and Qwen’s 7B, 32B, and 72B models. All models are tested via public APIs without system
305   prompts to ensure unbiased evaluation (Röttger et al., 2023; Zheng et al.).
306
307   Following previous works (Röttger et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024), we use keyword matching, which
308
      is fast and cost-efficient, to check if an LLM rejects a prompt on the entire 80K dataset, and GPT-
      4, which can deal with cases where keyword matching fails, on the hard subset and toxic dataset.
309
      Our findings indicate that keyword matching closely approximates GPT-4 evaluations across most
310
      models, with minimal discrepancies of 2.4% for GPT-3.5-turbo-0125 and 1.2% for Llama-3-70b on
311   sampled datasets. See appendix A.6 for more details.

520   6                                C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
521
522   In this paper, we introduce the first large-scale benchmark for assessing over-refusal in large lan-
523   guage models. The benchmark includes three datasets: an extensive over-refusal dataset of 80,000
524   prompts, a challenging subset of 1,000 prompts, and 600 toxic prompts to ensure models respond
525   appropriately to prompt toxicity. We evaluate 32 models across 8 different families, both black-box
526   and open-source, highlighting their safety strengths and weaknesses. Our benchmark is designed for
527
      ongoing updates to prevent over-fitting as new models emerge. In future work, we aim to expand
      the benchmark with more models and categories. We also encourage future research to explore the
528
      rejection rates of over-refusal prompts for improved safety alignment.
529
530   Limitations As the first large-scale benchmark for evaluating over-refusal of large language models,
531   OR-Bench has several limitations which require deeper study in the future, as listed below:
532
      • Although empirical results show that the bias impact is limited, the evaluation results on the three
533
        LLM moderators may not reflect their true performances for fairness reasons.
534
      • Our evaluation results show that the chance for a moderated prompt to be toxic is very small,
535
        but due to the difficulty of large scale moderation, it is possible that some toxic prompts are not
536
        identified by LLM moderators.
537

      A.1   M ORE RELATED WORKS
830
831   Safety Benchmark Several benchmarks (Levy et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023; McIntosh et al., 2024;
832   Xie et al., 2024b; Yuan et al., 2024) have been developed to evaluate the capability of LLMs to
833   reject toxic inputs. The AdvGLUE benchmark (Wang et al., 2021) was introduced to assess the
834   susceptibility of LLMs to a range of adversarial attacks through a multi-task framework. SALAD-
835   Bench (Li et al., 2024a) established a safety benchmark to examine the efficacy of various attack and
836   defense strategies in LLMs. Additionally, Latent Jailbreak (Qiu et al., 2023) provided a benchmark
837   focused on evaluating both the safety and robustness of LLMs. ALERT (Tedeschi et al., 2024)
838   proposed a detailed benchmark aimed at measuring LLM safety through red teaming techniques.
839
      All these benchmarks are designed to evaluate safety of LLMs, so purely optimizing the safety
      scores within these benchmarks may inadvertently result in over-refusal models.
840
841
      A.2   D ETAILED E XPERIMENT S ETUP
842
843   We benchmark 32 models from 8 model families, including both black-box and open-source models.
844   For Claude, we test Claude-2.1, 3 and 3.5 (Anthropic, 2024). Gemini-1.0-pro, Gemini-1.5-flash,
845   Gemini-1.5-pro, and the open-sourced Gemma series (Team et al., 2024) are included. From the
846   GPT-3.5-turbo (OpenAI, 2023) family, we test 0125, 0301, and 0613 to observe changes in safety
847   alignment over time. Similarly, for GPT-4, we include GPT-4-0125-preview, GPT-4-turbo-2024-04-
848   09, GPT-4o and GPT-4o-08-06. For Llama series, we include all models from its series (Touvron
849   et al., 2023a;b). We also evaluate small, medium, and large models from the Mistral (Mistral, 2024)
850   family and Qwen’s 7B, 32B, and 72B models (Bai et al., 2023). All models are queried via publicly
851
      available APIs. To ensure unbiased evaluation, no system prompts are used, as they can significantly
      alter an LLM’s behavior and increase rejections on safe prompts (Röttger et al., 2023; Zheng et al.).

      A.3   D IFFERENCE BETWEEN OVER - REFUSAL AND RED - TEAMING
855
      Our defitnion of over-refusal is directly derived from the ones used by state-of-the-models such as
856   Reid et al. (2024); OpenAI (2024); Dubey et al. (2024). Below are some examples.
857
858   The guidelines of Gemini [1] suggest that:
859   the model should help the user and fulfill the user request; only refuse if it is not possible to find a
860   response that fulfills the user goals without violating policy Be objective, don’t make assumptions
861   about user intent.
862
      The guidelines of OpenAI [2] suggest that:
863
      Assume best intentions from the user or developer Be as helpful as possible without overstepping


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      The guidelines of Llama [3] suggest that:
865
866   A prompt should not be rejected if a plausible, safe response is possible. Borderline prompts are
867   prompts near the decision boundary that a well-calibrated model should handle.
868   Regarding concerns about users violating the usage policy of these LLMs for malicious purposes.
869   E.g. as the example shown in the OpenAI guidelines [2] that shoplifting prevention tips can be used
870   to conduct shoplifting. These guidelines suggest that
871
      “This is the nature of knowledge at times, and an issue of human misuse rather than AI misbehavior-
872   thus subject to our usage policies, which may result in actions against the user’s account.“
873
874   Thus, existing LLMs all intend to answer these prompts by making sure the response is safe and
875
      plausible, which is exactly how our dataset generation pipeline is motivated.
876   Techniques such as using red-teaming to probe model responses is also an important research area.
877   As mentioned by the above guidelines, red-teaming is used to identify under worst case scenarios
878   (e.g. jailbreak), as to what extent the model’s responses can be used to aid sabotaging activities.
879   While over-refusal studies under daily normal use cases, whether the model can help users when
880   a safe and plausible answer is possible, which is the primary goal of existing LLMs. Both over-
881
      refusal and red-teaming are important research areas in LLMs. Since our goal is to contribute an
      over-refusal dataset, we specifically focus on the former.

915   A.6     M ODEL RESPONSE EVALUATION
916
917   Model response evaluation are usually approached through manual annotation (Röttger et al., 2023;
      Wei et al., 2024), prefix matching with specific keywords (Röttger et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023), and


                                                                              17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      using another LLM for evaluation (Wang et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; Chao et al., 2023). Given
919   the scale of our benchmark, we primarily used the latter two methods. As detailed in Wang et al.
920   (2024); Röttger et al. (2023), LLMs often show predictable rejection patterns due to instruction
921   tuning (Zhang et al., 2023; Taori et al., 2023b; Chiang et al., 2023). For instance, GPT-3.5 often
922   starts with “I’m sorry, but”, while Claude typically begins with “I apologize”. In order to identify
923   the rejection pattern keywords, we assessed each model with 10,000 randomly sampled prompts
924   as detailed in appendix A.12. While keyword matching is efficient, it may miss some scenarios,
925   such as LLMs declaring a question toxic before offering a safer alternative. Therefore, following
926   previous works (Wang et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; Chao et al., 2023), we use GPT-4 as a judge
927
      model to capture various scenarios. The prompts used for GPT-4 are outlined in appendix A.13.
      Our findings show that keyword matching closely approximates GPT-4’s evaluations, with minimal
928
      discrepancies of 2.4% for GPT-3.5-turbo-0125 and 1.2% for llama-3-70b on sampled datasets. Thus,
929
      for efficiency and cost reasons (ope, b;a), we apply keyword matching to the entire OR-Bench-80K
930   dataset, reserving LLM-based evaluation for the OR-Bench-Hard-1K and OR-Bench-Toxic subsets.

       A.15      M ORE DETAILS ON THE FEW SHOT EXAMPLE WHEN REWRITING
1175
1176   In order to generate over-refusal prompts that can cause large language models to reject, we find that
1177   it’s inefficient to just ask LLM to rewrite a toxic LLM into one over-refusal prompt. E.g, as shown
1178   in fig. 2, if the LLM is asked to rewrite “how to kill a person”, it may generate prompts such as “how
1179   to murder someone” which is still toxic. We find that if the model is asked to generate multiple
1180   rewritten prompts at once, the toxicity of the generated prompts will vary. E.g, the prompts range
1181   from “how to kill a person in a video game” which is closer to the toxic seed to “how to create a prop
1182   knife for a theater play” which is safer. Thus we adopt the strategy of asking models to generate
1183   multiple rewritten prompts together as described in appendix A.15. Also unlike model evaluations
1184
       where we set the temperature to 0.0, we set the temperature to 0.7 which we find generally will help
       generate more diverse prompts.

1304   A.20    LLM R ESPONSES WITH M ODERATION
1305
1306   Since we are using LLMs as the moderator, we did a manual analysis of the labelled results and
1307
       found out that LLM judges can be sensitive to keywords. For example, when we ask the LLMs
       about “what are categorized as illegal methods for stock trading?”, they can label the prompt as
1308
       toxic because of the “illegal” keyword. Thus we need a way to identify if a prompt is really toxic
1309
       despite the prompt is labelled toxic. One naturally way is to ask LLM to answer it and the answer
1310   can be used to check if it’s really toxic or not. We decided to use Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 (mistral,
1311   2024) which is a large language model that can produce high quality responses without moderation
1312   mechanism. Note that we cannot use other LLMs in our benchmark because 1) it will introduce
1313   bias to the model family used to produce the response 2) LLMs with safety alignment will reject the
1314   prompts and their responses are not helpful in identifying if a prompt is safe or toxic.
