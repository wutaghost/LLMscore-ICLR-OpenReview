009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion bridge models effectively facilitate image-to-image (I2I) translation by
012            connecting two distributions. However, existing methods overlook the impact
013            of noise in sampling SDEs, transition kernel, and the base distribution on sam-
014            pling efficiency, image quality and diversity. To address this gap, we propose the
015            Stochasticity-controlled Diffusion Bridge (SDB), a novel theoretical framework
016            that extends the design space of diffusion bridges, and provides strategies to miti-
017            gate singularities during both training and sampling. By controlling stochasticity
018
               in the sampling SDEs, our sampler achieves speeds up to 5× faster than the base-
               line, while also producing lower FID scores. After training, SDB sets new bench-
019
               marks in image quality and sampling efficiency via managing stochasticity within
020
               the transition kernel. Furthermore, introducing stochasticity into the base distri-
021            bution significantly improves image diversity, as quantified by a newly introduced
022            metric. Code would be available on Github repo.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Denoising Diffusion Models (DDMs) create a stochastic process to transition Gaussian noise into
028   a target distribution (Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020). Building upon
029   this, diffusion bridge-based models (DBMs) have been developed to transport between two arbitrary
030   distributions, πT and π0 , including Bridge Matching (Peluchetti, 2023), Flow Matching (Lipman
031   et al., 2022), and Stochastic Interpolants (Albergo et al., 2023). Compared to DDMs, DBMs offer
032   greater versatility for tasks such as I2I translation (Linqi Zhou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023). This
033   advantage arises because using a Gaussian prior often fails to incorporate sufficient knowledge about
034
      the target distribution.
035   In general, there are two primary design philosophies for DBMs. The first involves deriving a
036   pinned process (Yifeng Shi et al., 2023) from a given reference process (e.g., Brownian motion)
037   via Doob’s h-transform, and then constructing a bridge to approach it (Linqi Zhou et al., 2023;
038   Peluchetti, 2023). The second regime aims to directly design a bridge based on a specified transition
039   kernel (Lipman et al., 2022; Albergo et al., 2023). While the former also results in a transition kernel,
040
      the mean and variance in the kernel are coupled, which limits the design flexibility for possible
      bridges. In this work, we follow the second fashion and further propose the Stochasticity Control
041
      (SC) mechanism, which facilitates easier tuning and leads to enhanced performance across a variety
042
      of tasks. Our main contributions are as follows:
043
044          • We introduce the Stochasticity-controlled Diffusion Bridge (SDB), a generalized frame-
045            work that adopts a transition kernel-based design philosophy to elucidate the design space
046            of DBMs, shown in Fig. 10. Notably, this framework not only encompasses other main-
047            stream DBMs such as DDBM (Linqi Zhou et al., 2023) and I2SB (Liu et al., 2023), but
048            also DDMs like EDM (Karras et al., 2022), as detailed in Table 1.
049          • A Stochasticity Control (SC) mechanism is proposed by adding noise into the base dis-
050            tribution, designing a noise schedule for the transition kernel, and regulating the drift term
051            in the sampling SDEs. In addition, we explore score reparameterization and the dis-
052            cretization schemes of sampling SDEs to mitigate singularity during training and sam-
053            pling. These combined strategies lead to significant improvements in training stability,
               sampling efficiency, output quality, and conditional diversity.

183   3.2    S TOCHASTICITY CONTROL IN SAMPLING SDE S
184
185   Stochasticity control (SC) during the sampling phase has been explored for diffusion models by
186   Karras et al. (2022), yet comprehensive studies on its application to diffusion bridge models remain
187   limited. Eqs. (19) and (20) offer sampling schemes that align with Eqs. (6) and (7) in the DDBM
188
      framework. However, these methods do not guarantee optimal performance in terms of sampling
      speed and image quality. To address this issue, Linqi Zhou et al. (2023) introduced a hybrid sampler
189
      alternating between reversed ODE and SDE, and Zheng et al. (2024) accelerated sampling with an
190
      improved algorithm using discretized timesteps. This section aims to explore how SC can be further
191   optimized in the sampling for DBMs, thereby addressing the current research gap. Given transition
192   kernel, we can identify the reverse sampling SDEs, as demonstrated in Theorem 1.

525   7   C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this study, we introduced the Stochasticity-controlled Diffusion Bridge (SDB), a framework
528   designed to facilitate translation between two arbitrary distributions. By strategically managing
529   stochasticity in the base distribution, transition kernel, and sampling SDEs, our approach improves
530   image quality, sampling efficiency, and conditional diversity, allowing for the tailored design of dif-
531
      fusion bridge models across a range of tasks. This work is the first to derive sampling SDEs of
      q(Xt | xT ) for arbitrary Gaussian transition kernels of the form N (xt ; αt x0 + βt xT , γt2 I). Addi-
532
      tionally, our approach is the first to highlight the issue of lacking conditional diversity in diffusion
533
      bridge models and to resolve it by introducing stochasticity into the base distribution. We high-
534   lighted the importance of stochasticity control (SC) and addressed challenges associated with sin-
535   gularity through score reparameterization and specially designed discretization. Our results demon-
536   strate that a simple linear bridge configuration can set new benchmarks in image quality, sampling
537   efficiency and conditional diversity, as evidenced by our experiments with 64 × 64 edges2handbags
538   and 256 × 256 DIODE-outdoor I2I translation tasks. Despite these advancements, we acknowledge
539   that the optimal stochasticity may vary from one scenario to another, indicating a rich avenue for
      further exploration and refinement in future work.

714
      FID scores generally indicate high diversity across the entire dataset, they do not necessarily imply
715   high conditional diversity. For instance, we observed that samples generated by the DDBM model
716   often lack diversity when conditioned on edge images, despite achieving very low FID scores. To ad-
717   dress this limitation, we introduce the concept of conditional diversity and propose a corresponding
718   metric to quantify it.
719
      The first question is why FID failed to measure the conditional diversity. To illustrate the limitations
720
      of FID in capturing conditional diversity, consider an extreme case: if the images generated by a
721   generative model are identical to a set of baseline images, the FID score can be very low since the
722   two distributions are indistinguishable. However, this scenario does not reflect diversity within the
723   conditional outputs.
724
      To further support our point, we designed two classes of pseudo-generative models capable of con-
725
      trolling the diversity of the generated images, which are further validated by FID and AFD. The
726
      experiments are evaluated on Imagenet dataset (Deng et al., 2009).
727
728
      A.1   P SEUDO - GENERATIVE MODELS BY RANDOM SELECTION
729
730   We designed four pseudo-generative models: ImageNet-1-mode, ImageNet-2-modes, ImageNet-5-
731   modes, and ImageNet-10-modes. The experimental setup is as follows:
732
733          • We selected 11,000 samples from the ImageNet validation dataset, randomly choosing 11
734            images per class.
735          • From these, we designated 1,000 images as the ”real” set, while the remaining images
736            served as the source pool for the generative models.
737
             • Each ImageNet-k-modes model simulates a generative process by randomly sampling im-
738
               ages from a pool of k distinct images within a given class.
739
740
      We present sampled images in Fig. 6, where it is evident that the ImageNet-10-modes model gen-
741   erates images with the highest conditional diversity. To quantify this, we conducted experiments to
742   calculate both FID and AFD for the four generative models. The results are summarized in Table
743   5. While the FID scores are nearly identical across all models, the AFD values increase as the con-
744   ditional diversity of the generative models improves. This highlights that AFD is a more effective
745   metric for capturing conditional diversity than FID.
