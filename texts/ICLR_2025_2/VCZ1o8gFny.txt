011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The ever-expanding landscape of products, surfaces, policies, and regulations poses
014            significant challenges for recommendation systems, leading to data fragmentation
015            and prohibitive hikes in infrastructure costs. To address these challenges, we
016            propose M3C, a holistic co-design of model, data and efficiency strategies. M3C
017            (1) partitions the recommendation space to allow better representation learning
018            and encourage knowledge sharing within a subspace; (2) covers each partition
019
               using a hierarchy of foundational and vertical networks tailored to handle multi-
               domain, multi-objective tasks with mixed-modal inputs; (3) forms a unified data
020
               representation that utilizes heterogeneous signals across domains, objectives and
021
               optimization goals to alleviate data fragmentation, label sparsity, and to enhance
022            knowledge sharing; (4) improves execution efficiency and lowers costs with a suite
023            of stability and throughput optimizations. We show that across a diverse set of tasks
024            on public and industry datasets, M3C delivers up to 1% lower LogLoss compared
025            to 10 state-of-the-art baselines, while improving system efficiency by up to 20%.
026            Furthermore, in a large-scale industry setting our deployment of M3C has resulted
027            in 7% top-line metrics improvement in online tests with 10% capacity savings.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   High quality recommendation plays a vital role in creating a better online experience. To date, most
033   research in recommendation focuses on improving quality of a single domain-objective pair (Zhang
034   et al., 2024a; Luo et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2021a; Naumov et al., 2019), with the assumption that
035   on-boarding better models for a pair in the pipeline translates to better overall metrics.
036   However, modern recommendation systems are highly complex, with thousands of domains and tasks,
037   and a typical user request can trigger hundreds of models in the pipeline. This makes the existing
038   upscaling approach cumbersome: the constant innovations in new products and services require
039   recommendation systems to quickly adapt to diverse and heterogeneous user behaviors and to cover
040   new domains and objectives, but given tight inference latency requirements, it becomes unmanageable
041   to introduce a new model for each domain-objective pair, because each model needs to be separately
042   trained, optimized, and served. Further, the changing expectation of users on the usage of their
043   data, the increasing variety of demands from advertisers, as well as rapidly-evolving regulations and
044
      policies from the government (Voigt & Von dem Bussche, 2017) and industry unavoidably limit both
      the amount, quality, and granularity of data available for models, resulting in fragmentation, sparsity
045
      and ultimately quality loss for existing frameworks.
046
047   To ensure sustainable growth, we must break away from the traditional approach of per domain-
048   objective pair scaling and redesign our strategy around consolidating model, data sources to facilitate
049   knowledge sharing and lower infrastructure cost. However, several challenges stand in the way.
050   Unlike content understanding, where multi-modal foundational models (Ngiam et al., 2011; Bom-
051   masani et al., 2021) can learn inherent representations from diverse data sources using the next token
052   prediction task, data in recommendation is inherently sparse, fragmented, dynamic, and incoherent,
053   making self-supervision unsuitable. Furthermore, the mix of non-sequential (traditional sparse fea-
      tures) and sequential data (user history behavior (Chen et al., 2019)) complicates the development of

068   We propose M3C, a novel framework that addresses the challenges in recommendation scalability
069
      through a holistic co-design of model, data, and training system. M3C consists of the following:
070   M3C Partitioner: partitions the recommendation surface based on domain, task, optimization goal,
071   and policy regulations, enabling knowledge sharing and better model and objective representations.
072
      M3C Networks: a hierarchy of multi-domain, multi-objective (MDMO) models with a novel
073   architecture that handles mixed-modal inputs and reduces costs via knowledge distillation.
074
075   M3C Zipper and Filter: combine heterogeneous data sources to form a coherent feature set, balance
076
      label freshness and cost, and select the optimal set of features given a M3C Network.
077   M3C Sketch: an automated tool that leverages scaling laws to optimize hyperparameters and
078   parallization strategies, improving model latency and throughput without compromising quality.
079
      We evaluate M3C through extensive experiments on real-world recommendation scenarios, using
080   both public and industry-scale datasets. Our results show that M3C significantly outperforms 10 state-
081   of-the-art baselines in terms of model quality and hardware efficiency. Furthermore, our deployment
082   of M3C on a representative set of Ads model types has yielded a 7% increase of top-line metrics in
083   online A/B tests with and a 10% capacity saving.

      2     R ELATED W ORK AND C HALLENGES
087
088   This section provides a review on the recent advancements and unsolved challenges for industry-scale
089
      recommendation in the context of MDMO learning, data strategy and cost efficiency.
090
091   2.1     MDMO L EARNING
092
093
      Status Quo Current state-of-the-art recommendation systems focus on optimizing a narrow set of
      objectives (e.g., AUC and LogLoss of CTR) within specific domains (Zhang et al., 2024a; Naumov
094
      et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021a; Cheng et al., 2020; Mao et al., 2023). This traditional approach
095
      becomes inefficient as the number of domains and objectives grows, and scaling up isolated models
096   overlooks opportunities for cross-domain knowledge sharing.
097
098   Building on top of multi-domain recommendation (Li & Tuzhilin, 2020; Yan et al., 2019; Ma et al.,
099
      2018a; Sheng et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022a; Yang et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2020) and multi-task
      recommendation (Liu et al., 2022; Malhotra et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Wang
100
      et al., 2021b; 2022b), progresses are made on the front of MDMO to mitigate this problem. In
101
      particular, M2M (Zhang et al., 2022b) introduces a meta unit, an attention module and a tower
102   module to incorporate domain task knowledge, explicit inter-domain/task correlations and specialize
103   on the task-specific features. M3 oE (Zhang et al., 2024b) learns common, cross-domain information,
104   domain-specific and task-specific information through a mixture of experts, then it leverages a two-
105   level mechanism to aid feature extraction and fusion across tasks. PEPNet (Chang et al., 2023) uses
106   embedding and personalized network to fuse features with different importance and to personalize
107   DNN parameters to balance targets with different sparsity through a novel gating mechanism that
      incorporates a per-user prior. M3REC (Lan et al., 2023) proposes a meta-learning solution that uses a


                                                                                      2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      meta-item-embedding generator and user preference transformer to unify embedding representation
109   and a task-specific aggregate gate for MDMO learning.
110
111   Challenges However, prior work does not address the following concerns: (1) scalability: its efficacy
112
      on massive, industry-scale recommendation remains uncertain, as most evaluations are limited to a
      set of domains and objectives; (2) practicality: using a single model to cover a large domain-objective
113
      pair space is problematic, as competing domains and objectives can lead to subpar performance (He
114
      et al., 2022) or even loss divergence (Tang et al., 2023b); and (3) deployability: consolidating multiple
115   domains and objectives into a single MDMO model can compromise serving latency.
116
117
      2.2   DATA S TRATEGY
118
119   Status Quo Most research on MDMO learning assumes a consolidated dataset available for model
120   training (Zhang et al., 2022b; Chang et al., 2023), this is usually done by partitioning a dataset and
121   designating a few features as prediction tasks to simulate an MDMO (Zhang et al., 2024b; Lan et al.,
122   2023) setting. However, real-world datasets from different domains are fragmented, misaligned,
123   incoherent, sparse, and mixed-modal.
124
      Challenges The construction of datasets entails more complex operations than joins: (1) data from
125   multiple products and services, may not have overlapping ID spaces; (2) signals can be gathered
126   across different attribution windows, making it challenging to consolidate the data into a single,
127   unified pipeline without incurring significant costs or compromising on stability and freshness; and (3)
128   the absence of a common self-supervised task and the presense of both sequence and non-sequence
129   inputs adds to the difficulty of a unified representation1 .
130
131   2.3   C OST E FFICIENCY
132
133   Status Quo Recommendation systems have stringent serving budget and freshness requirements,
134   which entails highly-efficient training and serving. However, recommendation models are notoriously
135   hard to optimize due to large embedding tables (Lian et al., 2021) and many small irregular-shaped
136
      kernels, resulting in poor efficiency on modern hardware whose scaling prioritizes compute over
      network and memory bandwidth (Luo et al., 2018)
137
138   Existing work tackles the efficiency problem from multiple fronts. From the angle of creating
139   efficient, scalable architectures, Wukong (Zhang et al., 2024a) adopts compressed dot products and
140   efficient linear compression schemes as its core interaction mechanism; AutoInt (Song et al., 2019)
141   employs the heavily optimized transformer architecture; Mamba4Rec (Liu et al., 2024) uses state-
142
      space (Hamilton, 1994) to model historical user behaviors with linear time complexity. Orthogonally,
      adapting model architecture to datacenter topology proved useful. DMT (Luo et al., 2024) improves
143
      distributed embedding lookup performance by adopting multi-rail AlltoAll communication and
144
      tower-compressed embeddings to support extra-large embedding tables; NeuroShard (Zha et al.,
145   2023) optimizes for better load-balanced sharding of embedding tables across accelerators to achieve
146   better performance; DHEN (Zhang et al., 2022a) proposes a hybrid sharding strategy to leverage fast
147   NVLink connection in a single host for efficient parameter synchronization. Conversely, adaptation
148   of hardware to recommendation workloads is no longer uncommon (Tal et al., 2024).
149
      On the other hand, training instability due to natural distribution shifts, data corruption (noisy data),
150
      and multi-modal learning across a diverse set of tasks also affects cost efficiency. To that end,
151   methods including gradient clipping (Pascanu et al., 2013; Tang et al., 2023b; Wei et al., 2023;
152   Brock et al., 2021; Seetharaman et al., 2020; Menon et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2019), better feature
153   interaction (Adnan et al., 2023), and more effective normalization techniques (Ba et al., 2016;
154   Santurkar et al., 2018) are proposed.
155
      Challenges Despite these advances, improving model efficiency remains a challenge due to the time-
156
      consuming optimization-validation cycle even with MDMO learning, as each model still requires
157   independent tuning. Therefore, there is a strong need for automated tooling that can efficiently reason
158   about quality and efficiency tradeoffs.

183   3.1                   M ODEL S TRATEGY
184
185   M3C approaches MDMO by first organizing the domain-objective space into a manageable number
186   of partitions with similar optimization goals to improve representation learning of underlying patterns,
187   then by constructing a hierarchy of a foundational and a vertical M3C Networks for each partition.
188
189   3.1.1                  I MPROVING R EPRESENTATION VIA M3C PARTITIONER
190
191   Squashing all domains and objective into a single model space with poorly designed objective function
192   leads to training instability (Tang et al., 2023a) and subpar performance (He et al., 2022).
193
      Model Space Representation To create a better model space representation for MDMO training,
194   M3C Partitioner divides user engagement data into partitions by following these steps:
195
196   • First, it partitions domain-objective pairs by the surfaces (e.g., products) they belong. Different
197     surfaces can have distinct data characteristics, which can result in different ID spaces that do not
198     overlap significantly. Such subspaces are less likely to benefit from consolidation, as there is limited
199     knowledge to share between them. For example, it may not be useful to group surfaces that target
200
        different age groups together, as they can differ significantly in terms of audience and item pool.
      • Next, it groups domains and tasks based on their optimization goals. This considers two key factors:
201
        signal staleness and signal density. Signal staleness refers to the time-sensitive nature of certain
202
        tasks, such as modeling users’ real-time preferences (e.g., click, like, follow). Signal density, on the
203     other hand, refers to the frequency of different events. For example, liking, clicking, and following
204     happen more often than purchasing.
205   • Finally, policy requirements are taken into account to ensure compliance.
206
207   M3C Partitioner then allocates the global budget (e.g., FLOPs and storage) for each partition based
208
      on estimated revenue growth potential or expected cost reduction.
209   Auxiliary Loss Representation When domains are merged together, we can merge similar objectives
210   across domains. To ensure the new label mixture correlate well with the original objective, we
211   incorporate an auxiliary loss during training: Loss(X, Y ) = 1 − Cov(X,Y         )
                                                                                 σX σY , where X, Y are the
212   original labels and prediction labels respectively, Cov is the covariance and σ is the standard deviation.
213   To ensure the primary task is properly trained with the added auxiliary tasks (Ma et al., 2018b),
214   M3C needs to carefully balance its losses because some tasks can produce gradients of different
215   magnitudes. To address this, we adopt MetaBalance (He et al., 2022) to regulates the gradient scale
      between the primary and auxiliary tasks.

      3.1.2   MDMO L EARNING VIA M3C N ETWORKS
217
218   M3C Networks are MDMO models that cover one domain-objective partition. They take in three
219   modalities as inputs: traditional categorical inputs (Fc ), dense inputs (Fd ), and sequence inputs (Fs ,
220   e.g., user history of past interactions) in a batch B then output one prediction for each task. To
221   unify M3C Network’s model architecture and to boost MDMO learning across a wide range of input
222
      types, M3C Networks adopt a preprocessor-base-tower architecture (Figure 2) to first find common
      representations for inputs, then perform cross domain interaction, followed by task specialization.
223
224   Feature Processors serve two purposes: they convert the inputs into dense representations for the
225   backbone, and they unify the representation and project the embeddings into a compatible space so
226   the backbone can efficiently mix and interact across modality. Precisely:
227   • Fc are processed through embedding tables, with the output Oc in the shape of (B, |Fc |, d).
228   • Fd are processed by a dense network, which outputs embeddings Od of shape (B, |Fd |, d).
229   • Sequence inputs are created by lightweight event models, which collects sequential items recorded
230     by different surfaces, align/reorders the events, and derives embeddings for the sequence. The
231     event model outputs a sequence embedding Os of shape (B, |Fs | × K, d), where K is the number
232     of recent events to retrieve from each event source (e.g., Ads clicks, post views).
233   • Optionally, organic sequence data such as texts and image patches are either used as a dense
234     embedding produced by pretrained encoders, or as discretized token-based inputs (Team, 2024).
235   Now, Oc , Od , and Os share the same format and they are fed into a mixing network, which con-
236   catenates Oc and Od to form Ocd as a unified nonsequence data and applies nonlinearities and
237   normalization to it, while leaving sequence data Os intact, because sequence/non-sequence data are
238   best processed by different modules in the backbone network.
239
      Backbone M3C Network Backbones are built with efficient dense scaling (Zhang et al., 2024a; Shin
240   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023) in mind to better tap into modern hardware with superior compute than
241   memory and network capabilities (Luo et al., 2018; 2020). To that end, M3C Networks utilize three
242   components: a supporting structure called extended context storage (ECS), a transformer (Vaswani
243   et al., 2017) block, and a DHEN (Zhang et al., 2022a)/Wukong (Zhang et al., 2024a) fusion block
244   (DWFB). These blocks enable interleaved learning approach for Ocd and Os , which proves highly
245   effective for learning combined sequence and nonsequence data.
246   The ECS is a key-value store accessible globally to simplify implementation of advanced residual
247   connections (Huang et al., 2017) and to allow use of intermediate results in later layers. ECS also
248   provides a means to track statistics across a wide range of metrics for debugging purposes.
249
      The transformer block has the standard architecture proposed by (Vaswani et al., 2017) for sequence
250
      learning. Each transformer block corresponds to a stack of transformer layers, with RoPE embed-
251
      ded (Su et al., 2024) input sequence Os , and a contextualized sequence Os′ as output. The current
252   setup of transformer blocks can be viewed as a form of early fusion (Team, 2024), where different user
253   history types are merged into a single event sequence at the event model. However, recent evidence
254   suggests that untying modality can be beneficial (Lin et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024). Inspired by
255   these, FFN layer in M3C Network’s transformer block can be conditioned on the input to better
256   capture the inherently different underlying patterns. For example, click event sequence and post view
257   sequence do not need to share the same FFN weights. To mitigate training instability issue arising
258   from competing modalities, we apply QK-norm (Henry et al., 2020) before attention.
259   DWFB is introduced to address deficiency of transformers in processing non-sequence data due to
260   the lack of bit-wise interaction (Wang et al., 2021a; Zhang et al., 2024a) for Ocd . DWFB flattens
261   Os′ , which can be viewed as user-side features, then it concatenates them with Ocd as an input
                                           ′
262   and produces a new version of Ocd       as the output. DWFB captures feature interactions in Ocd
263   in a hierarchical manner. Horizontally, each of the fusion block adopts an intra-layer interaction
264   ensemble of Factorization Machine Block (FMB), Linear Compression Blocks (LCB), and MLPs
265   used in (Zhang et al., 2024a), ensuring each DWFB layer captures both bit-wise and feature-wise
266
      interactions. Vertically, DWFB stacks L interaction layers, and each DWFB captures up to 2L−1
      degrees of interaction (Zhang et al., 2024a).
267
268   Tower Modules Task-specific adaptation in M3C Networks is achieved through tower modules, one
269   for each prediction objective. Tower modules are usually lightweight MLP layers that project the
      common embeddings learned from the backbone into the task space.

      Training MDMO M3C Networks requires a coherent feature set to be built from fragmented data
288   sources. This section introduces methods to create such datasets and select optimal features.
289
290
291   3.2.1      C ONSOLIDATING DATASOURCES VIA M3C Z IPPER
292
      With similar domains and objectives grouped by M3C Partitioner, we still need to cater to customer’s
293
      specific needs in terms of different attribution windows. Thus, there presents a unique challenge in
294   balancing between data freshness and label completeness: when attribution window is smaller, the
295   data is fresher, but labels may be incomplete; in contrast, when a window is large, more labels are
296   available but they can be stale. An example of this is a CVR event: the advertiser may only let us
297   know whether the customer made a purchase after the attribution window.
298
      However, with K attribution windows (wi , i ∈ [1, K]), each action e generates K pairs of events,
299
      each corresponding to a label of li ∈ {0, 1}, i ∈ [1, K] within a window, we cannot simply include
300   all attribution windows in a dataset because it inflates training cost by K×. Since most events only
301   have a binary label, including multiple pairs for the same event brings no new information while
302   resulting in significant overfitting (Zhang et al., 2022c) or training stability issues (Zhou et al., 2018),
303   because (e, 0) or (e, 1) will appear multiple times across windows. Therefore, we require that (1)
304   each event must appear exactly once in the dataset, with the label associated with one of its attribution
305   windows; (2) the training set size should not increase; and (3) the dataset construction is efficient.
306                      1
      We give (e, li ) a K chance of being selected, with a sequence of (e, li ) in the order of attribution
307                                                                     Qi         1 j−1 1
      window w1 ...wK , then the probability of (e, wi ) being chosen is j=1 (1 − K  )     K . Unfortunately,
308
      implementing this naively requires us to sequentially process each (e, li ) which is too slow.
309
310   We propose M3C Zipper, an optimized algorithm to create such a dataset in parallel. M3C Zipper
311   first assigns each (e, li ) to the i-th worker, then it extracts a set of features Fe that uniquely identifies
312   e. Next, M3C Zipper introduces the same hash function h(Fe ) → Z across workers, which computes
313   the bucket ID that the pair (e, li ) belongs to as (h(Fe ) mod K + i) mod K.
314   M3C Zipper then picks bucket 0 as the dataset. To see how M3C Zipper implementation satisfies
315   the three requirements, first notice that h(Fe ) is a constant w.r.t e, and with any i, j ∈ [1, K] we
316   have i ̸= k → i mod K ̸= j mod K, thus no two attribution of the same event will be assigned to
317   the same bucket; since each event appears K times across K windows and there are same number
318   of buckets as attribution windows, each bucket has exactly |e| items; further, each worker operates
319   independently on each (e, wi ), we can parallel the construction without any synchronization.
320   It’s worth noting that Fe includes a timestamp, which is a good source for randomness, thus M3C
321   Zipper introduces no bias towards an attribution window for an event. Unlike previous methods for
322   multi-attribution consolidation that primarily focused on continuous training with negative samples
323   (Ktena et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020) or label correction with reweighing (Chen et al., 2022), M3C
      Zipper does not require multi-pass training, further boosting efficiency.

      3.2.2    PARETO -O PTIMAL F EATURE S ELECTION VIA M3C F ILTER
325
326   The dataset constructed by M3C Zipper can contain tens of thousands of features.
327
      However, not all features are required in a subspace created by M3C Partitioner. Given a cost
328
      budget and a M3C Network, M3C picks Pareto-optimal features (Mishan, 1967) via M3C Filter.
329   Pareto optimality is a condition in MDMO where no further improvement on one objective can
330   be made without hurting the other objectives. To illustrate how M3C Filter works, we first define
331   feature importance score vector for the i-th feature (in the feature set F) for N tasks as Fi =
332   (fi,1 , . . . , fi,N ), for i ∈ [1, |F|]. Each of the fi,j represents the importance score in j−th task
333   (j ∈ [1, N ]) computed with the permutation importance algorithm (Breiman, 2001). We then define
334   a partial order relationship among all Fi s, called dominated by (D, ≼) as follows: Fi ≼ Fk ⇐⇒
335   ∀j ∈ [1, N ], fi,j ≤ fk,j .
336   Now, given a feature set F and target feature count T (a few thousands), M3C Filter iteratively finds
337   undominated features on the current pareto frontier of the remaining feature set. In each iteration, up
338   to T of such features are selected and removed from F. The algorithm returns when F is empty or
339   the target feature count T is reached. Appendix A.1 provides the outline of the algorithm.
340
      M3C Filter can work with heterogeneous tasks thanks to its “unitless” property. Additional feature
341   selection criteria such as cost, coverage, and freshness can be incorporated easily, supporting the joint
342   optimization over MDMO learning and providing rich, compact, and generalizable feature sets.
343
344
      Previous schemes that directly compute feature importance scores (e.g., using Shapley-Value (Roth,
      1988)) then picking top-K features that result lowest loss value (Ma et al., 2018b; Xi et al., 2021;
345
      Yasuda et al., 2022) are no longer optimal. Since the loss term is now a combination of losses from
346
      different domains and objectives, simply using the global loss value as the surrogate for feature
347   selection unavoidably misses out the opportunity to leverage knowledge sharing across tasks and
348   leads to over-exploiting tasks that are easier to optimize.

      3.3     E FFICIENCY O PTIMIZATIONS
351
352   This section details how M3C improves cost-efficiency of M3C Networks.
353
354   3.3.1    S TABILIZING M3C N ETWORK T RAINING
355
356   Instability issues in MDMO training can arise from various aspects. For example, on mixed-modal
357   datasets contention among different modalities cause each modality to increase its norm (Team, 2024).
358   To address this, we apply aggressive normalization techniques including QK-norm and layernorm in
359   the mixing network when the inputs are from different interaction modules. Additionally, we apply
360
      adaptive gradient clipping (Tang et al., 2023a) for dense parameters to further stabilize training.
361
362   3.3.2    I MPROVING E XECUTION E FFICIENCY VIA M3C S KETCH
363
      M3C Networks are trained via hybrid parallelism (Mudigere et al., 2021) on a GPU cluster, where
364
      the embedding tables are sharded across different devices via TorchRec (Ivchenko et al., 2022), and
365   dense parameters are synchronized by Fully Sharded Data Parallel (FSDP) (Zhao et al., 2023).
366
367   To further improve model execution efficiency without affecting quality, we propose a search frame-
368
      work, called M3C Sketch, that unifies the search for optimal model hyperparameter and parallelization
      strategies given cost budgets without hurting model accuracy.
369
370   Search Space The M3C Sketch search space is defined by a model template with undecided hyperpa-
371   rameters, parallelization strategies, and their associated choices or ranges.
372   Objectives Quality-related signals are slow to obtain. To accelerate the search process, M3C Sketch
373   leverages established scaling laws (Shin et al., 2023) of Wukong and Transformers to approximate
374   model quality. In particular, given a FLOPs budget f , Wukong’s logloss improves linearly to
375   f 0.00071 (Zhang et al., 2024a), and Transformer’s logloss scales linearly to f −0.05 (Kaplan et al.,
376   2020). In Wukong, we simultaneously scale output embedding count in its LCB, FMB and dot
377   interaction compression factor to hold the law; in transformer, Narang et al. (2021) has shown that
      basic modifications to the architecture do not result in significant quality changes. Thus, M3C Sketch

      Constraints To simplify search, M3C Sketch encodes constraints in the objectives. For unsatisfied

393
394   Search Strategy M3C Sketch uses an alternating solving strategy to approximate the optimal solution
395   while significantly reducing search time via beam search of width K. M3C Sketch first fixes
396   parallelization strategies by randomly samples S configurations while searching for the best model
397
      hyperparameters. The top K found best model hyperparameters are then selected for the next round,
      which become the fixed parameters while parallelism strategy is mutated. This process repeats until
398
      either search quota is depleted or the results no longer improve.
399
400   M3C Sketch uses parallel Bayesian optimizers (Snoek et al., 2012) that periodically merges the
401   search trajectories during the search steps. To further guide the search process, M3C Sketch adopts
402   the performance model used in Srifty Luo et al. (2022) and uses a dynamic programming algorithm
403
      (Appendix A.2.2) for bootstrapping.

      5      I NDUSTRY- SCALE D EPLOYMENT AND S USTAINABILITY I MPACT
507
508
509   As recommendation model sizes increasing by 20× in recent years in real world deployments (Wu
510
      et al., 2022; Zhai et al., 2024), surging demand has made it the single largest AI application in terms
      of infrastructure demand in the datacenters of major Internet companies (Mudigere et al., 2021;
511
      Park et al., 2018). To reduce energy footprint and improve serving quality, we deployed M3C on a
512
      representative set of Ads model types in a large-scale industry setting.
513
514   Model Space and Data Source Consolidation M3C is applied to a representative set of domain-
515   objective pairs formed with thousands of domains and tens of objectives from our own services and
516
      advertiser goals into a very small set of M3C groups via M3C Partitioner, supported by unified data
      sources constructed by M3C Zipper and M3C Filter. This drastically reduced the amount of models
517
      required from hundreds to a small, manageable number, thereby significantly reducing the compute
518
      demand to support the entire space without losing quality.
519
520   Reducing Model Footprint via Knowledge Distillation To meet stringent serving latency require-
521   ments, M3C constructs a hierarchy of teacher and lighter-weight, user-facing student M3C Networks
522
      to transfer knowledge from teacher to student via label-based distillation(Hinton et al., 2015). Distil-
      lation converts the serving latency problem into a bandwidth problem: the teacher throughput only
523
      needs to match the data volume during the refresh interval of a student in online training, which can be
524
      satisfied by adjusting the training scale. Label based distillation requires minimal storage overheads,
525   however, M3C Networks use more aggressive distillation via feature-based distillation (Romero et al.,
526   2014; Heo et al., 2019) for critical tasks to improve distillation effectiveness at a higher storage cost.
527

      market landscapes, deliver improved user satisfaction, all with a greener approach.

895   A.2.1    M3C S KETCH O BJECTIVES
896
897   For complex M3C Networks with a large FLOPs budget we can use compute complexity as a proxy
898
      for model quality. However, since FLOPs budget is provided as a total sum between Transformer and
      Wukong blocks, expert insight is required to determine the allocation ratio. For less complex models,
899
      we continue to incorporate a quality term directly in the objectives.
900
901   Specifically, for complex models, M3C Sketch uses OM 3CN etwork = max( ft ) as the objective,
902   where f is the FLOPs complexity, t is the latency. Given a FLOPs target F and a maximum iteration
903   latency T derived by dividing the minimum throughput required for online training, we have the
904   constraints of M3C Network as αF ≤ f ≤ α1 F (model quality control) and t ≤ T (minimum
905
      throughput limit). We use α ∈ [0, 1] as a threshold hyperparameter to M3C Sketch which gives the
      search more wiggle room. For less complex models, the objective becomes OV M = max( fqβ ), with
906
907
      q being the model quality evaluated after fixed amount of training (e.g., 1B data) is done, and β being
      the quality scaling ratio, and any hyperparameters resulting quality to scale superlinearly with respect
908
      to FLOPs is more rewarded.

912   We follow the same maximum batch size estimation strategy as used in Srifty Luo et al. (2022). For
913   bootstrapping the search process of FSDP configurations, given a collection of supported sharding
914   strategies X for each FSDP unit, and for a model with L layers training on W GPUs with K valid
915   batch sizes, and DW as the set of factors of W . We use dynamic programming to statically find
916   a reasonable starting point for the search: given a model, we first profile the execution latency
917   Tb (sl ) and memory usage Rb (sl ) of l-th layer given a batch size b and a FSDP sharding strategy
      sl ∈ S = X × DW , which can be obtained in parallel across GPUs. We then estimate batch-
