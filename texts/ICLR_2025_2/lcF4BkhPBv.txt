011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Standard deep learning approaches often struggle to handle out-of-distribution
014            data, especially when the distributional shift breaks spurious correlations present
015            in the training data. While some approaches to handling spurious correlations
016
               under distributional shift aim to separate causal and spurious features without ac-
               cess to target distribution data, such approaches typically rely on labeled data from
017
               different domains or contingent assumptions about the nature of neural representa-
018
               tions. Existing methods that do use unlabeled target data make strict assumptions
019            about the target data distribution. To overcome these limitations, we present the
020            Algorithm for Concept Extrapolation (ACE). Formulating diverse generalization
021            as a semi-supervised learning problem, ACE applies a psuedo-labeling approach,
022            learning from pseudo-labels that flexibility incorporates assumptions about the tar-
023            get distribution. As a result, we find that ACE is more robust to variations in the
024            target distribution than prior methods. We also demonstrate an initial application
025            of ACE to alignment, finding that ACE can be made to improve goal misgeneral-
026            ization. Overall, we are exciting about the opportunities ACE opens for learning
027
               diverse generalizations of under-specified labels on new domains.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Standard deep learning approaches often struggle to maintain high performance under distributional
033
      shifts (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019). Models are particularly sensitive to distributional shifts that
      “break” the correlation (present on the training distribution) between “spurious” and “ground truth”
034
      features (e.g. classifying huskies based on the presence of snow (Ribeiro et al., 2016), or images
035
      based on texture rather than shape (Geirhos et al., 2022). Spurious correlations are problematic
036   when the spurious feature is simpler than the ground truth feature - due to the simplicity bias of
037   neural networks trained with stochastic gradient descent (Shah et al., 2020; Mingard et al., 2020),
038   neural networks tend to to learn the simpler spurious feature rather then the ground truth feature,
039   even if the spurious feature is less predictive (Hermann et al., 2024).
040
      Spurious correlations breaking under distributional shift can cause degradation in performance in
041
      safety critical domains (e.g. medical imaging (Zech et al., 2018)) and create algorithmic bias if a
042   protected attribute is correlated with particular features or outcomes ((Dressel & Farid, 2018; Ober-
043   meyer et al., 2019)). Current issues with aligning foundational models to human intent (Ouyang
044   et al., 2022; Askell et al., 2021), such as reward model over-optimization (Gao et al., 2022), syco-
045   phancy (Sharma et al., 2023; Denison et al., 2024), weak to strong generalization (Burns et al.,
046   2023), and measurement tampering (Roger et al., 2023) can be cast as spurious correlations (typi-
047   cally between the reward process and the object of reward) breaking under distributional shift (see
048   Appendix D for an extended analysis of relevance to alignment).
049   To address spurious correlations breaking under distribution shift, some work aims to learn clas-
050   sifiers that track ground truth features using only labeled training data. In domain generalization,
051   methods rely on labeled data sampled from different domains, attempting to learn features that are
052   robust across the training domains such that they generalize to the test domain ((Arjovsky et al.,
053   2020; Sagawa et al., 2020; Krueger et al., 2021)). However, data sampled from multiple domains
      is not always available, and when it is, standard empirical risk minimization is often competitive

054
      (Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2020). More recent work attempts to learn an ensemble of diverse classi-
055   fiers with representational diversity, with metrics for representational diversity including direction
056   of classifier gradients (Teney et al., 2022) and layer at which features can be learned (Tiwari &
057   Shenoy, 2023). While these methods do not require labeled data from different domains, they rely
058   on particular (and possibly contingent) assumptions about neural network representations, and must
059   anticipate the target distribution by learning all plausible generalizations.
060
      Access to unlabeled target data makes the problem of learning diverse generalizations significantly
061   more tractable, as the space of classifiers that behave differently on the target distribution is much
062   smaller than the space of classifiers that disagree on arbitrary out-of-distribution data (see Sec ?? for
063   more a formal argument). Domain adaptation methods leverage unlabeled target data, but most al-
064   gorithms focus on more benign distributional shifts where e.g. the background of all image changes
065   change from one setting to another, rather than distributional shifts that break spurious correlations
066   (Saenko et al., 2010; Wilson & Cook, 2020).
067   To leverage unlabeled target to overcome spurious correlations, we propose learning an ensemble of
068   classifiers with predictive diversity, finding classifiers that achieve low risk on the source distribution
069   while selectively disagreeing on target distribution instances that break spurious correlations. Two
070   concurrently developed methods, DivDis(Lee et al., 2023) and D-BAT (Pagliardini et al., 2022) pro-
071   pose similar methods, regularizing classifiers towards statistical independence (DivDis), or maximal
072   disagreement (D-BAT) on the target distribution. However, both methods make assumptions about
073   the proportion of correlation-breaking instances on the target distribution. In our experiments, we
074   show that the performance of DivDis significantly degrades when these assumptions are violated.
075   To overcome these limitations, we introduce Algorithm for Concept Extrapolation (ACE) 1 . Mo-
076   tivated by the framing of learning diverse classifiers as a semi-supervised learning problem, ACE
077   uses a pseudo-labeling (Lee, 2013) approach, generating pseudo-labels on the target distribution for
078   each classifier in proportion to the expected joint feature distribution. To account for noise and un-
079   certainty in the pseudo-labeling process, ACE applies exponential smoothing, placing higher weight
080   on more confident predictions.
081   On a broad set of image spurious correlation benchmarks, we find ACE achieves comparable per-
082   formance to DivDis on target distributions with statistically independent features, and substantially
083   superior performance on distributions with small or large proportions of instances that break spuri-
084   ous correlations. We also find ACE can improve the generalization of deep reinforcement learning
085   agents, with our ACE-Agent successfully completing 16% more levels in the CoinRun goal misgen-
086   eralization problem (Langosco et al., 2023; Shah et al., 2022) than baseline approaches.
087   In section 2, we formulate learning diverse generalization as a semi-supervised learning problem,
088   and explain how optimal classifiers DivDis and D-BAT diverge from optimal classifiers under our
089   formulation. In section 3, we introduce ACE as a pseudo-labeling approach, present an alternative
090   “Top-K” formulation of ACE, and detail ACE’s exponential smoothing. In section 4 we present
091   experiments and results.

      2.2   I MPLICIT M IX R ATE A SSUMPTIONS
123
      The two existing methods most applicable to our problem formulation are DivDis (Lee et al., 2023)
124   and D-BAT (Pagliardini et al., 2022). Both train an ensemble of classifiers to achieve low risk on a
125   source distribution, while maximizing some diversity loss on an unlabeled target distribution. How-
126   ever, methods make implicit assumptions about the joint class distribution on the target data which
127   do not always hold. We summarize each method in turn, and explain these implicit assumptions.
128
      DivDis (Lee et al., 2023) incentives diverse generalizations by minimizing the mutual information
129
      of the the class predictions of the hypotheses in the ensemble. Formally, for two hypotheses h1 , h2 ,
130   the mutual information loss is given by
131
132                                  LMI (h1 , h2 ) = DKL (ph1 ,h2 ||ph1 ⊗ ph2 )                         (3)
133   with ph1 ,h2 the joint distribution over hypothesis class predictions and ph the marginal distribu-
134   tion for a single hypothesis. Note that mutual information corresponds to statistical independence:
135   variables are statistically independent if and only if they have no mutual information. In the case
136   of feature labels, statistical independence corresponds to a mix rate of mix rate r = 0.5 (exactly
137   half of the instances have disagreeing labels). But in general we should not expect each class to be
138   statistically independent on the target set. For example, we could have distribution shift where the
139   features are completely negatively correlated (i.e. mix rate of 1.0), in which case minimizing mutual
140   information would not correspond to achieving low risk.
141   D-BAT (Pagliardini et al., 2022) incentivised diverse generalization by rewarding disagreement
142   among the set of hypotheses. Formally, assuming binary feature labels and two hypotheses h1 , h2 ,
143   the agreement loss is given by the negative log likelihood of disagreement:
144                                                        (0)   (1)     (1)       (0)
145                               LA (hi , hj ) = − log (ph1 · ph2 + ph1 · ph2 )                         (4)
146   This disagreement loss (by construction) is minimized by maximally disagreeing hypotheses, but
147   we should only expect the optimal hypotheses to maximally disagree if the feature labels are exactly
148   negative correlated (r = 1) which again, we should not expect to hold in general.
149
      The implicit assumptions of methods need not exactly hold in order for them to be effective. Both
150
      DivDis and D-BAT might serve as effective regularizers against the simplicity bias, even if minimiz-
151   ing the regularization loss would produce pathological hypotheses. Ideally though our a diversity
152   loss should be minimized by hypotheses that minimize empirical risk to avoid pathological cases
153   where the assumptions of our method strongly diverge from the actual data distribution.

      that flexibly incorporates assumptions about the target distribution.

160   With the diverse generalization structured as a semi-supervised learning problem, we can take in-
161   spiration from a tried-and-true approach to semi-supervised learning: pseudo-labeling (Lee, 2013) .

      4     E XPERIMENTS
182
      We aim to answer 6 research questions: 1) How does ACE perform on target distributions with
183
      statistically independent feature labels, 2) How does ACE perform relative to DivDis on target dis-
184
      tribution with positively or negative correlated feature labels (low and and high mix rates), 3) How
185   does ACE perform when the target mix rates is higher than the assumed mix rate, 4) How does ACE
186   perform when the target mix rate is lower than the assumed mix rate, 5) How does ACE alter the
187   the learned representations of (pre-trained) models, and 6) Can ACE mitigate goal misgeneraliza-
188   tion in deep reinforcement learning. To answer these questions, we train various configurations of
189   ACE and DivDis on synthetic and real world image datasets with artificially varied mix rates. We
190   also construct a reward dataset from a deep reinforcement learning (RL) environment, using ACE to
191   learn a reward function and subsequent training a RL agent with the learned reward.
192
193   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
194
      Unless otherwise stated, we train 2 heads on a shared Resnet50 (He et al., 2015) backbone, op-
195
      timizing weights with Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2017) with a learning rate of 0.0001, weight decay
196
      of 0.0001, and source and target batch sizes of 32, over 10 epochs. As a model selection criteria
197   (Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2020), we pick models with the lowest total validation loss (source cross
198   entropy and target diversity) (Albuquerque et al., 2019).
199
200   4.2   R ESOLVING C OMPLETE S PURIOUS C ORRELATION ACROSS M IX R ATES
201
202   To evaluate questions 1-4, we train various configurations of ACE and DivDis on synthetic and real
203   world image datasets ranging in complexity and degree of simplicity bias, across artificially varied
204   mix rates [0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0]. In particular, we train DivDis with a mutual information loss
205   weight of 1 and 10, and ACE with the known global mix rate, and assumed lower bounds 0.1, 0.5.
206
      We describe the datasets below:
207   Gaussian 2D Grid: A modified version of the toy 2D grid dataset introduced by (Lee et al., 2023),
208   where instead of uniformly distributing data across quadrants, we sample data points from 2D gaus-
209   sian distributions centered in the middle of each quadrant, with a standard deviation of 0.1, reflecting
210   the more realistic assumption features separated by low density regions.
211   FashionMNIST-MNIST: A dataset of concatenated FashionMNIST (Xiao et al., 2017) and MNIST
212   (Deng, 2012) images introduced by (Pagliardini et al., 2022), taking inspritation from the “domi-
213   nos” format introduced by (Shah et al., 2020) (see CIFAR-MNIST below). The source distribution
214   contains MNIST 0’s concatentated with FashionMNIST tops, and MNIST 1’s concatenated with
215   FashionMNIST bottoms. The target dataset contains some instances of MNIST 0’s concatenated to
      FashionMNIST bottoms, and MNIST 1’s concatenated to FashionMNIST tops.

257
      mately the same (albeit slightly lower) accuracy on all datasets except Waterbirds, where ACE with
258   an assumed lower bound of 0.5 is substantially worse than DivDis, but ACE with an assumed lower

263

264   and FashionMNIST-MNIST), while the performance of both degrade on the more complex datasets
265   (CIFAR-MNIST and Waterbirds). Notably, DivDis with auxiliary weight of 10 suffers losses even
266   on the simple datasets, suggesting that DivDis does not approximate optimal solutions in low mix
267   rate regimes in general. On higher mix rates, we see a clearer divergence between ACE and Di-
268   vDis, with ACE robust on all datasets while both DivDis configurations degrade substantially. This
269   again validates our hypothesis that DivDis is ill-suited to target distributions where features are not
      statistically independent.

      Evaluating the impact of mix rate assumptions that are higher and lower than the ground truth mix
271   rate, we find that across all datasets that overestimating the mix rate causes substantial degradtion sin
272   performance, while underestimating the mix rates causes less damage, and in some cases actually
273   benefits performance. This supports our hypothesis that in lieu of access to the ground truth mix rate,
274   it is preferable to assume a lower-bound, at the (potential cost) of sample efficiency. In fact, actually
275   surprising to us that assuming a lower bound hinders performance all all, as we would have expect
276   performance to be relatively constant as the fraction of disagreeing instances increased, and only
277   degrade relative to methods assuming the higher ground truth mix rate (though we do see something
278   like this pattern in Waterbirds, as the absolute performance of ACE 0.1 stays roughly constant, but
279
      its performance gap with ACE 0.5 shrinks as the mix rate increases).
280   Overall, our results show that ACE tends to outperform DivDis when features are not statistically
281   independent, especially under higher target mix rates.

287   Inspired by the original pseudo-labeling paper that found pseudo-labeling compresses class condi-
288
      tion distributions, we were curious whether ACE induced a similar phenomenon. To test this hy-
      pothesis, we plot the first two principle components of the the final layer activations of the held-out
289
      test set before and after ACE training. Sample plots are show in Figure ??. In general, we observe
290
      that ACE further separates class distributions that were already linearly separate, and disentangles
291   class distributions that previously had strong overlap. This result supports the idea that ACE tries
292   to learn low density separators, and also illustrates a benefit of predictive diversity over representa-
293   tional diversity (e.g. (Teney et al., 2022)) - predictive diversity can learn representations relevant to
294   the target domain that may be otherwise absent from a generically pre-trained model.

401   5     C ONCLUSION
402
403   While prior diverse generalization methods make particular (but implicit) assumptions about the
404   target distribution, our algorithm ACE treats diverse generalization as a semi-supervised learning
405   problem, taking a pseudo-labeling approach which flexibly incorporates information about the target
406   distribution while still performing well under weak assumptions. We show that ACE competitive is
407   more robust than DivDis to variations in the target distribution mix rate (the proportion of instances
408   with disagreeing labels that “break” spurious correlations) showing particularly strong gains under
409   mix rates higher than 0.5. We also demonstrate an initial application of ACE to alignment, using it
410
      to mitigate goal misgeneralization in deep reinforcement learning.
411
412   5.1   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
413
      In the present work, we mostly assume access to the ground truth target mix rate (except when as-
414
      suming lower bounds), and always assume access to the number of spurious correlates / features.
415   While we expect to be able to make such assumptions in many applications, future work could
416   explore dynamically learning both. We have also seen preliminary success using ACE to detect ad-
417   versarial images, and under-specification in text data, and consider both interesting future directions.
418   Finally, we see strong analogies between ACE and recently proposed methods in weak-to-strong-
419   generalization, and are excited about future work applying ACE to this and related problems like
420   measurement-tampering and reward-hacking.

      A    R ELATED W ORK
649
650
      Domain Generalization attempts to generalize from the training distribution to an unseen target
651   distribution, typically leveraging labeled data from multiple domains that vary along a spurious
652   attribute (Arjovsky et al., 2020; Chevalley et al., 2022; Sagawa et al., 2020; Krueger et al., 2021).
653   Without access to unlabeled target distribution data, domain generalization methods must rely on
654   sufficiently diversity in the training distribution. However, when sufficient diversity exists, standard
655   expected risk minimization can outperform domain-generalization methods (Gulrajani & Lopez-
656   Paz, 2020).
657   Domain Adaptation assumes access to unlabeled target data, but is less directly concerned with
658   resolving spurious and more concerned with handling distributional shifts that effect all data in-
659   stances such as the type of camera used to capture images (Saenko et al., 2010; Wilson & Cook,
660   2020). Given this focus on general domain shifts rather than spurious correlations, it makes sense
661   that prominent domain adaptation methods such as Deep-CORL (Sun & Saenko, 2016) and MMD-
662   VAE (Li et al., 2018) tend to perform poorly on spurious correlation benchmarks (Tiwari & Shenoy,
663   2023; Lynch et al., 2023).
664   Diverse Ensembles have been used in different ways to try to circumvent spurious correlations and
665   simplicity bias. Learning with Biased Committee (Kim et al., 2023) uses an ensemble with models
666   to weight samples for the training of a final classifier, emphasizing samples with less consensus.
667   Each classifier in the ensemble is initialized and trained differently, and the authors assume this will
668   be sufficient for them to be diverse, even though no explicit penalty is considered to enforce it.
669   (Teney et al., 2022) trains multiple models in parallel, with a diversity regularize to make them
670   represent different functions. They penalize the alignment of gradients of the different models.
671   This idea is similar to ACE in essence, but unable to leverage unlabeled data and relies on gradient
672   alignment corresponding to representational diversity.
673
      Most similar to ACE, Diversify and Disambiguate (DivDis)(Lee et al., 2023) and Diversity by Dis-
674
      agreement Training (D-BAT) (Pagliardini et al., 2022) train classifiers to output diverse predictions
675   on the target data, DivDis by penalizing mutual information between the classifiers and D-BAT by
676   maximizing disagreement. However, both methods make strong distributional assumptions about
677   the target dataset: DivDis assumes an equal proportion of in-distribution and correlation-breaking
678   instances, and D-BAT (implicitly) assumes all instances break spurious correlations.
679
      Hard Example Mining is the technique to gradually grow, or bootstrap, the set of examples by
680
      selecting those for which the detector triggers a false alarm (Shrivastava et al., 2016). It is used
681   in fairness applications to identify and mitigate adversarial examples (Lin et al., 2023).The focal
682   loss (Lin et al., 2017) was designed with a similar objective of emphasizing hard examples and
683   preventing the vast number of easy examples to dominate training. The exponentially-decaying sum
684   of losses introduces with ACE has an analogous objective to these techniques of emphasizing the
685   examples where the classifiers differ the most.
686
      Continuous Learning (CL) is the ability of AI to continually acquire, update, accumulate and
687   exploit knowledge (Wang et al., 2024). This knowledge comes from incrementally ingesting new
688   data that comes from a dynamic data distribution. (Lesort, 2023) points that one of the challenges
689   in CL is to discover causal relationships between features and labels under distribution shifts, and
690   that for that it needs to deal with spurious correlations to learn robust representations. By solving
691   spurious correlations, ACE can be applied to improve performance under the natural distribution
692   shifts.

      Below in an implementation of ACE for binary clasification and two heads:

706   import torch
707
708   def topk_loss(logits, criterion=torch.nn.functional.binary_cross_entropy_with_logits,
709                 r_01=0.25, r_10=0.25):
710       """
711       Args:
712           logits (torch.Tensor): Input logits with shape [BATCH_SIZE, HEADS].
713
              criterion (torch.nn.Module): Loss criterion.
              r_01 (float): Expected proportion of samples labels (0,1).
714
              r_10 (float): Expected proportion of samples labels (1,0).
715
          """
716       # get batch size
717       bs = logits.shape[0]
718       # compute head losses
719       head_0_0 = criterion(
720           logits[:, 0], torch.zeros_like(logits[:, 0]), reduction=’none’
721       )
722       head_0_1 = criterion(
723           logits[:, 0], torch.ones_like(logits[:, 0]), reduction=’none’
724
          )
          head_1_0 = criterion(
725
              logits[:, 1], torch.zeros_like(logits[:, 1]), reduction=’none’
726
          )
727       head_1_1 = criterion(
728           logits[:, 1], torch.ones_like(logits[:, 1]), reduction=’none’
729       )
730       # compute disagreement losses
731       loss_0_1 = head_0_0 + head_1_1
732       loss_1_0 = head_0_1 + head_1_0
733       # sort losses in ascending order
734       loss_0_1, _ = loss_0_1.sort()
735
          loss_1_0, _ = loss_1_0.sort()
          # compute top k losses
736
          k_01 = round(bs * r_01)
737
          k_10 = round(bs * r_10)
738       loss_0_1 = loss_0_1[:k_01].mean()
739       loss_1_0 = loss_1_0[:k_10].mean()
740       # compute total loss
741       loss = loss_0_1 + loss_1_0
742       return loss

      D    R ELEVANCE TO AI A LIGNMENT
811
812
      We believe resolving spurious correlations is underappreciated in the context of AI Existential Safety
813   and Alignment ((Carlsmith, 2024; Critch & Krueger, 2020; Ngo et al., 2024; Bengio et al., 2024)).
814   Many common worries in AI safety and ethics can be reformulated as under-specification problems,
815   which we enumerate below.
816
      Sycophancy Supervisor approval is a spurious correlate for supervisor approval given the supervi-
817
      sor knows everything the model knows ((Cotra, 2021; Sharma et al., 2023; Denison et al., 2024)).
818
      Caveats: Source distribution will contain mislabeled examples.
819
820   Reward Tampering The reward itself is a spurious correlate of the outcome or behavior being
821   rewarded reward tampering/wire-heading ((Everitt & Hutter, 2016; Kumar et al., 2020; Everitt et al.,
822
      2021; Denison et al., 2024)). Caveats: Source distribution will contain mislabeled examples.
823   Measurement Tampering The measurements are spurious correlate of the ground-truth entity being
824   measured (Roger et al., 2023). Caveats: None.
825
      Weak to Strong Generalization Weak labels are a spurious correlate for ground-truth answers
826   (Burns et al., 2023). Caveats: Source distribution will contain mislabeled examples.
827
828   Scheming Time-independent goals are a spurious correlate for episode-dependent goals (Carlsmith,
829
      2023). Caveats: Questionable whether goals will be cleanly separable such that we can learn diverse
      generalizations.
830
831   This is not a comprehensive list, and some examples are more applicable than others. In general
832   though, we think methods for to a) detect when a spurious correlation present on the training dis-
833   tribution has been broken and b) learning all plausible “resolutions” of the spurious correlation,
834   would be very useful for alignment. We are particular excited to test ACE on measurement tamper-
835
      ing (Roger et al., 2023), as the setting nearly identical to the complete spurious correlation setting
      studied here, with some a-priori knowledge of the target distribution (no instances where the mea-
836
      surements are off but the ground truth feature is present).
