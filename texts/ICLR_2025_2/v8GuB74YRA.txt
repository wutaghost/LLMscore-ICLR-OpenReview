011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Transferability estimation aims to identify the most suitable model from a collec-
014            tion of pre-trained models for specific downstream tasks, playing a crucial role
015
               in the success of the pre-training and fine-tuning paradigm. However, the re-
               cent proliferation of pre-trained models with diverse architectures and training
016
               strategies poses significant challenges for transferability estimation due to dis-
017
               crepancies in intrinsic model characteristics, making it difficult for existing meth-
018            ods to accurately simulate embedding space evolution within feasible computa-
019            tional limits. To address these challenges, we propose an Implicit Transferability
020            Modeling (ITM) paradigm that incorporates an implicit modeling strategy for the
021            intrinsic properties of pre-trained models, enabling more accurate transferabil-
022            ity estimation. ITM employs a Divide-and-Conquer Adaptation (DCA) process
023            to efficiently model the transfer process, reducing both learning complexity and
024            computational cost. Additionally, we introduce a Pseudo-Clustering-based Opti-
025            mization (PCO) strategy combined with static and dynamic constraints, enabling
026
               effective estimation without intensive retraining. Our method significantly out-
               performs state-of-the-art approaches, achieving notable improvements across ten
027
               widely used benchmarks and demonstrating its effectiveness and generalizability
028
               in enabling accurate and efficient model selection for downstream tasks.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Recently, the pre-training and fine-tuning paradigm has achieved remarkable success in numerous
034   computer vision applications, making Transferability Estimation (TE) a significant topic that in-
035   volves predicting the performance of various pre-trained models on downstream tasks within a lim-
036   ited time frame.
037   Early methods (Tran et al., 2019; Nguyen et al., 2020) modeled the joint distribution between pre-
038   trained labels and downstream task labels. In contrast, methods like LogME (You et al., 2021) and
039   ETran (Gholami et al., 2023) evaluate models using embedding space and downstream task labels,
040   extending transferability estimation to self-supervised approaches. Subsequently, Li et al. (2023)
041   and Hu et al. (2024) recognized that these static methods overlook the dynamic changes in models
042   during the fine-tuning process. They proposed modeling the dynamic aspects of fine-tuning to map
043   the embedding spaces before and after the process, leading to improved performance.
044   However, with the rise of diverse self-supervised pre-training strategies and increasingly sophis-
045   ticated network architectures, existing methods encounter discrepancies from various model col-
046   lections, leading to inaccurate predictions and unreliable model selection for downstream tasks, as
047   illustrated in Fig. 1. Recent models pre-trained with techniques such as Instance Discrimination (ID)
048   (Chen et al., 2021; Caron et al., 2021) and Masked Image Modeling (MIM) (He et al., 2022; Xie
049   et al., 2022) exhibit divergent convergence characteristics as shown in Fig. 2, rendering unified es-
050
      timation impractical. Consequently, most current transferability estimation methods underperform,
      resulting in significant drops in performance.
051
052   While recent dynamic TE approaches attempt to simulate the evolution of embedding spaces during
053   fine-tuning, they are hindered by efficiency and computational cost constraints. As a result, these
      methods often depend on manually defined rules to guide feature evolution. However, this trans-

      paradigm. Instead of modeling the entire evolution of the embedding space, we decouple the in-

089   then introduce a Divide-and-Conquer Adaptation (DCA) process to reduce learning complexity
090   and enhance efficiency. To eliminate the need for extensive fine-tuning to obtain the final state,
091   we employ a Pseudo-Clustering-based Optimization (PCO) strategy combined with static and dy-
092   namic constraints, allowing for effective estimation without intensive training. By integrating these
093   components, ITM offers more accurate transferability estimation across pre-trained models with
094   diverse architectures and pre-training strategies, significantly outperforming state-of-the-art meth-
095   ods with notable improvements across ten widely used benchmarks, including evaluations of recent
096
      pre-trained models.

098
099        1. We propose an Implicit Transferability Modeling (ITM) paradigm that incorporates a
100           lightweight learnable mapping module and an efficient evolution process to enhance the
101

102        2. We introduce a Divide-and-Conquer Adaptation (DCA) strategy, combined with gradient
103           descent acceleration and Pseudo-Clustering Optimization (PCO), to effectively model em-
104           bedding evolution while minimizing computational costs.
105        3. We achieve state-of-the-art performance in transferability estimation using ten recent mod-
106           els with diverse architectures from various pre-training methods across ten datasets, achiev-
107           ing an average gain of 16% in rank correlation and significantly outperforming existing

      2.1          T RANSFERABILITY ESTIMATION
129
      Transferability estimation aims to effectively select the most suitable pre-trained model for down-
130   stream tasks from a collection of models. Recent approaches can be broadly categorized based on
131   the type of transferability metric used, i.e., static statistics-based and dynamic evolution-based.
132
133
      Static statistics-based methods predict transferability using fixed measurements of pre-trained
      models. NCE Tran et al. (2019) and LEEP Nguyen et al. (2020) use conditional probability or
134
      Bayesian metrics to estimate logit discrepancies between model outputs and downstream task anno-
135
      tations. N LEEP Li et al. (2021) replaces the output layer of LEEP with a Gaussian Mixture Model
136   (GMM) for better efficiency and calibration. LogME You et al. (2021) uses the logarithm of maxi-
137   mum evidence to provide stable predictions with lower computational costs. ETran Gholami et al.
138   (2023) combines various metrics, introducing an energy-based measure to enhance accuracy, while
139   GBC Pándy et al. (2022) employs the Bhattacharyya coefficient to evaluate class separability in the
140   feature space. These methods do not require updates during fine-tuning, resulting in high compu-
141   tational efficiency. However, their inability to model the fine-tuning process limits their prediction
142   accuracy.
143   Dynamic evolution-based methods simulate the model updating process to estimate its state af-
144   ter fine-tuning. SFDA Shao et al. (2022) uses a linear mapping to project initial features into a
145   Fisher space, iteratively enhancing class separability. PED Li et al. (2023) introduces a potential
146   energy-based update model for predicting the evolved state. LEAD Hu et al. (2024) employs ordi-
147   nary differential equations and downstream objectives to better capture the logits’ evolution during
148   adaptation.
149   Despite their advancements, the recent surge of pre-trained models with varied architectures intro-
150   duces greater discrepancies in initial states and convergence behaviors (as shown in Fig. 2), chal-
151   lenging these handcrafted prediction-based methods.
152
153   2.2          P RE - TRAINED MODELS
154
155   The Pre-trained and Fine-tuning paradigm in computer vision is widely used to adapt models to
156   specific downstream tasks by leveraging representational knowledge from large-scale pre-training
157   data. Recent research has explored various network architectures and pre-training strategies, leading
158
      to significant advancements and a diverse range of model capabilities.
159   Architecture evolution. Initially, pre-training and fine-tuning paradigms showed effectiveness on
160   traditional convolutional neural networks (CNNs) like ResNet He et al. (2016) and DenseNet Huang
161   et al. (2017). Compared to training from scratch, this paradigm helped CNN-based methods achieve
      superior performance on downstream tasks. More recently, Vision Transformers (ViTs)Dosovitskiy

      et al. (2020) and Swin-TransformersLiu et al. (2021) have successfully incorporated self-attention
163   mechanisms (Vaswani et al., 2017) in computer vision. Benefiting from their ability to capture long-

165   models in tackling complex visual recognition challenges.
166
      Pre-training strategy. Early pre-training processes are conducted in a fully supervised manner,
167
      with ImageNet Deng et al. (2009) being the most widely used dataset to boost model performance.
168
      However, this approach relies heavily on dense human annotations, limiting its broader applicability.

175   However, with diverse pre-training methods and varying model architectures, differences in their op-

177   estimation methods.

      3.1   P ROBLEM FORMULATION
184
185
      The goal of transferability estimation is to predict the performance ranking of these models after
      transfer learning on a downstream dataset D = {(xi , yi )}, which contains C classes. Consider a
186
      model zoo {ϕi }M i=1 , where the models are pre-trained using different architectures and pre-training
187
      methods. The true performance ti of a model ϕi is obtained by training the model on the training
188   set DT and evaluating it on the test set DE . fine-tuning all candidate models on the dataset requires
189   significant computational resources and time. Therefore, transferability estimation methods aim to
190   output a metric score si for each model ϕi at a low cost and within a short time, with the expectation
191   that this metric will yield a consistent ranking with the true performance ranking after full fine-
192   tuning, thus facilitating effective model selection for downstream applications.
193
      Recent synamic TE methods aim to establish an accurate mapping Γ(ϕ, DT ) : E → Ê, where E
194
      represents the original embedding space produced by the pre-trained model ϕ on the downstream
195
      training data DT , and Ê represents the final state after fine-tuning. However, due to diverse architec-
196
      ture designs and pre-training strategies, models exhibit varying convergence characteristics, making
197
      it infeasible to directly model this mapping and generalize across different pre-trained models under
198   affordable computational constraints.
199
200   Instead of directly modeling Γ(·, ·), we propose learning an implicit representation z for the trans-
201
      ferability of ϕ. This approach allows the transfer learning process to be treated as an interaction
      between the model’s transferability and downstream tasks, leading to more effective and adaptable
202
      estimation across a broader range of scenarios.
203

206   1938), and weighted Kendall’s τw (Vigna, 2015) can be considered. Following previous work Hu
207   et al. (2024); Li et al. (2023); You et al. (2021), we use weighted Kendall’s τw as the primary evalu-
208
      ation metric:

293   3.3   P SEUDO - CLUSTERING OPTIMIZATION
294
295   During the estimation process, obtaining the final state of the embedding space Êj is impractical,
296   as it requires a full fine-tuning process on the downstream task. To eliminate reliance on this final
297   state, we leverage findings indicating that recent pre-trained models possess adequate capability to
298   converge well on the training set (Goodfellow, 2016; Arpit et al., 2017). From this, we draw two
299   inferences: i.e., distribution separability and convergence stability.
300   First, the static distribution of each class in the embedding space should be well-separated due to
301   optimal convergence on the training set. This implies that the representations of different classes
302   should form distinct clusters. Second, the downstream objectives should converge to a stable point,
303   yielding a minimal loss value. This means the model will effectively align the learned features with
304   the target labels, reducing the error to a minimal level. Based on these two inferences, we utilize
305   pseudo-cluster center constraints and objective-driven updating to approximate the evolution of the
306   embedding space without requiring the actual final state.
307   Pseudo-cluster center constraints. To mimic the static distribution separability of the evolved
308   embedding space, we utilize a pseudo-cluster center generation strategy and introduce a constraint,
309   denoted by MSE loss Lpc , that ensures the embeddings of input data are close to their corresponding
310   cluster centers. Specifically, the pseudo-cluster centers can be generated using one-hot or random
311   vectors from high-dimensional embedding spaces or derived from eigenvectors obtained through
312   Principal Component Analysis (PCA) or Laplacian methods for a sparser distribution. In the exper-
313
      imental section, we compare the impact of different generation methods and determine the optimal
      approach based on empirical results.
314
315   Objective-driven updating. To achieve convergence stability, we incorporate downstream task
316   objectives Lobj into the estimation of ψ, ensuring that the iterative adaptation aligns closely with
317   downstream requirements. During this process, the downstream objective is updated iteratively to
318   achieve stable convergence in the logit space, ultimately guiding the embedding evolution toward
319
      effective feature alignment.

      weighted Kendall’s τw as well as the wall-clock time.

472   We conducted ablation experiments on the standardization and the adaptive learning rate mentioned

475   embedding space. However, as indicated in the third row of the table, standardizing the features
476
      greatly enhances the convergence speed and accuracy of ITM. This improvement occurs because
      the distribution of features from different models varies significantly, and standardization helps the
477
      model converge more quickly. After incorporating both standardization and the adaptive learning
478
      rate, ITM achieved the highest performance. This indicates that setting different learning rates
479   for various models based on the initial intra-class dispersion of the standardized model features

      5   C ONCLUSIONS
506
507
      In this work, we propose ITM, an Improved Transferability Modeling paradigm designed to decou-
508   ple the intrinsic properties of pre-trained models by representing them in an implicit latent space,
509   providing an efficient solution for assessing model transferability. We introduce a Divide-and-
510   Conquer Adaptation process to reduce learning complexity and computational costs, and employ
511   a Pseudo-Clustering-based Optimization strategy that eliminates the need for extensive fine-tuning,
512   enabling effective estimation without intensive training. We conduct experiments on recent models
513   and a diverse set of downstream tasks, showing that the proposed ITM significantly outperforms all
514   counterparts with superior computational efficiency, demonstrating its effectiveness.
