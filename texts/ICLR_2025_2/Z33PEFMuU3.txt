                                Classical generative diffusion models learn an isotropic Gaussian denoising process,
010
                                treating all spatial regions uniformly, thus neglecting potentially valuable structural
011
                                information in the data. Inspired by the long-established work on anisotropic diffu-
012                             sion in image processing, we present a novel edge-preserving diffusion model that
013                             generalizes over existing isotropic models by considering a hybrid noise scheme.
014                               In particular, we introduce an edge-aware noise scheduler that varies between
015                             edge-preserving and isotropic Gaussian noise. We show that our model’s generative
016                             process converges faster to results that more closely match the target distribution.
017                             We demonstrate its capability to better learn the low-to-mid frequencies within the
018                             dataset, which plays a crucial role in representing shapes and structural information.
019                             Our edge-preserving diffusion process consistently outperforms state-of-the-art
020
                                baselines in unconditional image generation. It is also more robust for genera-
                                tive tasks guided by a shape-based prior, such as stroke-to-image generation. We
021
                                present qualitative and quantitative results showing consistent improvements (FID

                                score) of up to 30% for both tasks. We provide source code and supplementary
023                             content via the public domain edge-preserving-diffusion.mpi-inf.mpg.de.

047   1                      I NTRODUCTION
048
049   Previous work on diffusion models mostly uses isotropic Gaussian noise to transform an unknown
050   data distribution into a known distribution (e.g., normal distribution), which can be analytically
051   sampled (Song and Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2021; Ho et al., 2020; Kingma et al., 2021). Due to the
052   isotropic nature of the noise, all regions in the data samples x0 are uniformly corrupted, regardless of
053   the underlying structural content, which is typically distributed in a non-isotropic manner. During
      the backward process, the model is trained to learn an isotropic denoising process that ignores this


                                                                             1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      potentially valuable non-isotropic information. In image processing literature (Elad et al., 2023),
055   denoising is a well studied topic. Following the work by Perona and Malik (1990) structure-aware
056   guidance has shown remarkable improvements in denoising. Since generative diffusion models can
057   also be seen as denoisers, we ask ourselves: Can we enhance the effectiveness of the generative
058   diffusion process by incorporating awareness of the structural content of the data samples in the
059   underlying dataset?
060
      To explore our question, we introduce a new class of diffusion models that explicitly learn a content-
061   aware noise scheme. We call our noise scheme edge-preserving noise, which offers several advantages.
062   First, it allows the backward generative process to converge more quickly to accurate predictions.
063   Second, our edge-preserving model better captures the low-to-mid frequencies in the target dataset,
064   which typically represent shapes and structural information. Consequently, we achieve better results
065   for unconditional image generation. Lastly, our model also demonstrates greater robustness and
066   quality for generative tasks that rely on shape-based priors.
067   To summarize, we make the following contributions:
068
069          • We present a novel class of content-aware diffusion models and show how it is a generaliza-
070            tion of existing isotropic diffusion models
071
072
             • We conduct a frequency analysis to better understand the modeling capabilities of our
               edge-preserving model.
073
074          • We run extensive qualitative and quantitative experiments across a variety of datasets to
075            validate the superiority of our model over existing models.
076
             • We observed consistent improvements in pixel space diffusion. We found that our model
077
               converges faster to more accurate predictions and better learns the low-to-mid frequencies of
078            the target data, resulting in FID score improvements of up to 30% for unconditional image
079            generation and most remarkably a more robust behaviour and better quality on generative
080            tasks with a shape-based prior.

083   2   R ELATED WORK
084
085   Most existing diffusion-based generative models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song and Ermon, 2019;
086   Song et al., 2021; Ho et al., 2020) corrupt data samples by adding noise with the same variance to
087   all pixels. These generative models can generate diverse novel content when the noise variance is
088   higher. On the contrary, noise with lower variance is known to preserve the underlying content of
089   the data samples. Rissanen et al. (2023) proposed to use an inverse heat dissipation model (IHDM),
090   which can be seen as an isotropic Gaussian blurring model. The idea is to isotropically blur the
091   images to corrupt them. They show that isotropic blurring in the spatial domain corresponds to adding
092   non-isotropic noise in the frequency domain, where they run the diffusion. Hoogeboom and Salimans
093
      (2023) proposed an improved version of IHDM, where they not only blur but also use isotropic noise
      to corrupt data samples. The resulting model gives far better quality improvements compared to
094
      IHDM. Recently, Huang et al. (2024a) introduced correlated noise for diffusion models (BNDM).
095
      They proposed to use blue noise which is negatively correlated and showed noticeable improvements
096   both in visual quality and FID scores. While IHDM and BNDM also consider a form of non-isotropic
097   noise, they do not explicitly take into account the structures present in the signal.
098
099
      Various efforts (Bansal et al., 2023; Daras et al., 2023) were made to develop non-isotropic noise
      models for diffusion processes. Dockhorn et al. (2022) proposed to use critically-damped Langevin
100
      diffusion where the data variable at any time is augmented with an additional ”velocity” variable.
101
      Noise is only injected in the velocity variable. Voleti et al. (2022) performed a limited study on
102   the impact of isotropic vs non-isotropic Gaussian noise for a score-based model. The idea behind
103   non-isotropic Gaussian noise is to use noise with different variance across image pixels. They use a
104   non-diagonal covariance matrix to generate non-isotropic Gaussian noise, but their sample quality
105   did not improve in comparison to the isotropic case. Yu et al. (2024) developed this idea further and
106   proposed a Gaussian noise model that adds noise with non-isotropic variance to pixels. The variance
107   is chosen based on how much a pixel or region needs to be edited. They demonstrated a positive
      impact on editing tasks.

      3    BACKGROUND
114
      Generative diffusion processes. A generative diffusion model consists of two processes: the
115
      forward process transforms data samples x0 into samples xT that are distributed according to a
116
      well-known prior distribution, such as a normal distribution N (0, I). The corresponding backward
117   process does exactly the opposite: it transforms samples xT into x̂0 , distributed according to the
118   target distribution p0 (x). This backward process involves predicting a vector quantity, interpretable
119   as either noise or the gradient of the data distribution, which is precisely the task for which the
120   generative diffusion model is trained. Previous works (Song and Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2021; Ho
121   et al., 2020; Kingma et al., 2021; Rissanen et al., 2023; Hoogeboom and Salimans, 2023) typically
122   formulate the forward process as the following linear equation:
123                                              xt = γt x0 + σt ϵt                                          (1)
124
      here, xt is the data sample diffused up to time t, x0 stands for the original data sample, ϵt is a
125
      standard normal Gaussian noise, and the signal coefficient γt and noise coefficient σt determine
126   the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) (γt/σt ). The SNR refers to the proportion of signal retained relative
127   to the amount of injected noise. Note that γt and σt are both scalars. Previous works have made
128   several different choices for γt and σt respectively, leading to different variants, each with their own
129   advantages and limitations. Typically, a noise schedule βt is employed to govern the Qt rate at which γt
130   and σt vary over time (Ho et al., 2020). Let us first define αt = 1 − βt and ᾱt = √    i=1 αi . Song and
131   Ermon (2019) then defined their forward diffusion process with γt = 1 and√      σt = 1 − ᾱt√    , leading
132   to a process with exploding variance. Instead, Ho et al. (2020) chose γt = ᾱt and σt = 1 − ᾱt
133   which better preserves variance.

135
      Diffusion model paradigms. There are two widely-adopted general mathematical frameworks
136   that form the major ”paradigms” of generative diffusion models. The first paradigm is score-based
137   generative modelling, introduced by Song and Ermon (2019). In this paradigm, the generative
138   model essentially learns to estimate the gradients of the target distribution, allowing for iterative
139   sampling via these gradients. A prominent type of diffusion model that is based on this paradigm
140   are SDE-based diffusion models, which simulate the generative backward process as a stochastic
141   differential equation to take samples. In Appendix A.1, we perform a theoretical analysis showing
142   how our edge-preserving diffusion process can be interpreted under this paradigm.
143   Denoising probabilistic modeling paradigm, introduced by Ho et al. (2020) models the generative
144   process as a well-studied Gaussian process with known posteriors. The reverse process is then
145   modelled as a parameterized Markov chain that approximates this Gaussian process. In this paper, we
146   adopt this paradigm as it aligns well with the statistical property we are investigating: non-isotropic
147   variance driven by image content. Flow matching (Lipman et al., 2022) is another class of generative
148   models that are gaining a lot of traction. In Appendix B, we discuss in detail how our framework can
149   be adapted to flow matching.

186   Edge-preserved filtering in image processing. In this work, we aim to choose γt and σt such
187   that we obtain a diffusion process that injects noise in a content-aware manner. To do this, we are
188   inspired by the field of image processing, where a classic and effective technique for denoising is
189   edge-preserved filtering via anisotropic diffusion (Weickert, 1998). Perona and Malik (1990) showed

193   that the relevant structural information in the image also gets distorted. To overcome this problem,

351      Implementation details We compare our method against four baselines, namely DDPM (Ho et al.,
352      2020), Simple Diffusion (Hoogeboom et al., 2023), IHDM (Rissanen et al., 2023) and BNDM (Huang
353      et al., 2024a). The motivation for comparing with the latter two works is that they also consider
354      a non-isotropic form of noise. In Appendix B, we discuss how our method could be extended to
355      continuous normalizing flows via Flow Matching (Lipman et al., 2022).
356     We perform experiments on two settings: pixel-space diffusion following the setting of Ho et al.
357     (2020); Rissanen et al. (2023) and latent-space diffusion following (Rombach et al., 2022) noted as
358     LDM in Table 1, where the diffusion process can be driven by any method but runs in the latent space.
359     We use the following datasets: CelebA (1282 , 30,000 training images) (Lee et al., 2020), AFHQ-Cat
360     (1282 , 5,153 training images) (Choi et al., 2020), Human-Sketch (1282 , 20,000 training images) (Eitz
361     et al., 2012) (see Appendix) and LSUN-Church (1282 , 126,227 training images) (Yu et al., 2015) for
362
        pixel-space diffusion. For high-resolution image generation with latent-space diffusion (Rombach
        et al., 2022), we use on CelebA (2562 ), AFHQ-Cat (5122 ).
363
364     We used a batch size of 64 for all experiments in image space, and a batch size of 128 for all
365     experiments in latent space. We trained AFHQ-Cat (1282 ) for 1000 epochs, AFHQ-Cat (5122 ) (latent
366     diffusion) for 1750 epochs, CelebA(1282 ) for 475 epochs, CelebA(2562 ) (latent diffusion) for 1000
367     epochs and LSUN-Church(1282 ) for 90 epochs for our method and all baselines we compare to. Our
368
        framework is implemented in Pytorch (Paszke et al., 2017). For the network architecture we use

531
532
      We introduced a new class of edge-preserving generative diffusion models that extend existing
533
      isotropic models with negligible overhead. In practice, we didn’t notice any increase in training/infer-
534   ence time. Our linear diffusion process operates in two stages: an edge-preserving phase followed
535   by an isotropic phase. The edge-preserving stage maintains core shapes, while the isotropic stage
536   fills in details. This decoupled approach captures low-to-mid frequencies better and converges to
537   sharper predictions faster. It improves performance on both unconditional and shape-guided tasks,
538   outperforming several state-of-the-art models. For future work it would be interesting to extend our
539   non-isotropic framework to the temporal dimension for video generation to improve time-consistency
      of important image features.

759   C    M OTIVATION BEHIND OUR HYBRID NOISE PROCESS
760
761   A valid observation to make is that given our hybrid forward process with two distinct stages, the
762   edges are preserved longer, but still lost in the end. How does longer preservation of edges help the
763   generative process? First thing to note is that the longer preservation of edges by itself does not have
764
      any impact, if we still let the model predict isotropic noise. Secondly, by modifying the forward
      process to be an edge-preserving one, the backward posterior formulation will also change and will
765
      rely on a non-isotropic variance, as discussed Eq. (13). It is the combination of edge-preserving
766
      noise, together with our structure-aware loss function that makes the model work. Furthermore, our
767   frequency analysis (Section 5.1) has quantitatively shown that our decoupling approach is beneficial
768   to learning the low-to-mid frequencies of the target dataset. This is consistent with recent work on
769   wavelet-based diffusion models (Huang et al., 2024b), that demonstrates it is advantageous to learn
770   low-frequency content separately from high-frequency content in the wavelet domain, using two
771   distinct modules. Instead, we use two distinct stages, one that focuses on lower-frequency primary
772   structural content (edge-preserving stage), and one that focuses on fine-grained high-frequency details
773   (isotropic stage).

      D    H OW NEGATIVE LOG LIKELIHOOD CAN BE APPROXIMATED
778
779
      Here we explain how negative log likelihood in the original DDPM Ho et al. (2020) paper can be
      approximated with our formulation.
780
781   The denoising probabilistic model paradigm defined in the DDPM paper defines the loss by minimiz-
782   ing a variational upper bound on the negative log likelihood. Because our noise is still Gaussian, the
783   derivation they make in Eq. (3) to (5) of their paper still holds for us. The difference however is that
784   we’re non-isotropically scaling our noise based on the image content. As a result, our methods differ
785
      on Eq. (8) in their paper. Instead, we end up with the following form:
786                   Lt−1 = Eq [Σ−1 (µ˜t (xt , x0) − µθ(xt , t)).(µ˜t (xt , x0) − µθ(xt , t))]          (35)
787
788   In essence, for our formulation that considers non-isotropic Gaussian noise, we need to apply a
789
      different loss scaling for each pixel.
790   Our formulation still provides an analytical variational upper bound to approximate the negative
791   log-likelihood. While our heuristic loss function (Eq. (15)) already proved effective for approximating
792   non-isotropic noise corresponding to structural content in the data, a more accurate KL-divergence
793   loss would include the scaling discussed above.
