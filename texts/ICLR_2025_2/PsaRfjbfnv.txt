009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            When considering real-world adversarial settings, defenders are unlikely to have
012            access to the full range of deployment-time adversaries, and adversaries are likely to
013            use realistic adversarial distortions that will not be limited to small Lp -constrained
014            perturbations. To narrow in on this discrepancy between research and reality we
015            introduce ImageNet-UA, a new benchmark for evaluating model robustness against
016            a wide range of unforeseen adversaries. We make use of our benchmark to identify
017            holes in current popular adversarial defense techniques, highlighting a rich space
018
               of techniques which can improve unforeseen robustness. We hope the greater
               variety and realism of ImageNet-UA will make it a useful tool for those working
019
               on real-world worst-case robustness, enabling development of more robust defenses
020
               which can generalize beyond attacks seen during training.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Neural networks perform well on a variety of tasks, yet can be consistently fooled by minor adversarial
026   distortions (Szegedy et al., 2013; Goodfellow et al., 2014b). This has led to an extensive area of
027   research around the “Lp -bounded adversary” that adds imperceptible distortions to model inputs
028   to cause misclassification. However, this classic threat model may fail to fully capture many real-
029   world concerns regarding worst-case robustness (Gilmer et al., 2018). Firstly, real-world worst-case
030   distributions are likely to be varied, and are unlikely to be constrained to the Lp ball. Secondly,
031   developers will not have access to the worst-case inputs to which their systems will be exposed to.
032   For example, online advertisers use perturbed pixels in ads to defeat ad blockers trained only on
033
      the previous generation of ads in an ever-escalating arms race (Tramèr et al., 2018). Furthermore,
      although research has shown that adversarial training can lead to overfitting, wherein robustness
034
      against one particular adversary does not generalize (Dai et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2021; Stutz et al.,
035
      2020; Tramer & Boneh, 2019), the existing literature is still focuses on defenses that train against the
036   test-time attacks. Although such distribution shifts have been studied in the average-case common
037   corruption setting (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2018), when considering worst-case inputs, the research
038   community is lacking a unified benchmark for testing how defences generalise.
039
      We address the limitations of current adversarial robustness evaluations by providing a repository of
040
      nineteen gradient-based attacks, eight of which are used to create ImageNet-UA—a benchmark for
041
      evaluating the unforeseen robustness of models on the popular ImageNet dataset (Deng et al., 2009).
042   Defenses achieving high Unforeseen Adversarial Accuracy (UA2) on ImageNet-UA generalize to a
043   diverse set of adversaries not seen at train time, demonstrating robustness to a much more realistic
044   threat model than the Lp adversaries which are a focus of the literature.
045
      Our results show that unforeseen robustness is distinct from existing robustness metrics, further
046
      highlighting the need for a new measure which better captures the generalization of defense methods.
047
      We use ImageNet-UA to reveal that models with high L∞ attack robustness (the most ubiquitous
048   measure of robustness in the literature) do not generalize well to new attacks, recommending L2
049   as a stronger baseline. We further find that Lp training can be improved on by alternative training
050   processes, and suggest that the community focuses on training methods with better generalization
051   behavior. Interestingly, unlike in the Lp case, we find that progress on CV benchmarks has at least
052   partially tracked unforeseen robustness. We hope that the community can build on these results,
053   so that ImageNet-UA can provide an improved progress measure for defenses aiming to achieve
      real-world worst-case robustness. To summarize, we make the following contributions:

096   Evaluating Adversarial Robustness. Adversarial robustness is notoriously difficult to evaluate
097   correctly (Papernot et al., 2017; Athalye et al., 2018). To this end, Carlini et al. (2019) provide
098   extensive guidance for sound adversarial robustness evaluation. Our ImageNet-UA benchmark
099   incorporates several of their recommendations, such as measuring attack success rates across several
100   magnitudes of distortion and using a broader threat model with diverse differentiable attacks. Existing
101   popular measures of adversarial robustness (Croce & Hein, 2020; Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2015;
102   Weng et al., 2018) mainly present novel optmisation techniques for optimizing over an Lp -ball,
103   limiting their applicability for modeling robustness to new deployment-time adversaries.
104   Non-Lp Attacks. Attacks often use hard-to-bound generative models (Song et al., 2018; Qiu et al.,
105   2019), or make use of expensive brute-force search techniques Engstrom et al. (2017). We focus
106   on attacks which are fast by virtue of differentiability, portable across datasets and independent of
107   auxiliary generative models. Previous works presenting suitable attacks include Laidlaw & Feizi
      (2019); Shamsabadi et al. (2021); Zhao et al. (2019), who all transform the underlying color space of

        3          M EASURING U NFORESEEN ROBUSTNESS
148
149
       To evaluate the unforeseen robustness of models, we introduce a new benchmark ImageNet-UA, and
       corresponding metric UA2 (Unforeseen Adversarial Accuracy). ImageNet-UA consists of our eight
150
       core adversarial attacks pictured in Figure 5, which have been selected for efficacy and computational
151
       efficiency.

        3.1         T HE UNFORESEEN ROBUSTNESS THREAT MODEL
154
155    The unforeseen robustness threat model has three central features:
156
157    Test-time distribution shift. As illustrated in Figure 5, our threat model requires that defenses do not
158
       make use of our test-time adversaries. This is directly analogous to the situation faced by developers,
       who are unable to anticipate which worst-case inputs which may occur at deployment time.
159
160    A diverse population of adversaries. Defence techniques should generalise to a range of possible
161    deployment-time adversaries (e.g. they should not be effective only against Lp -based adversaries).
       Testing generalisation on a diverse range of adversaries is therefore an important part of evaluation.

237   3.3   E IGHT CORE UNFORESEEN ATTACKS
238
239   To ensure that we have high quality and diversity of tasks, we design nineteen novel attacks (see
240   Appendix B for a full list), and select a core eight for their computational efficiency, effectiveness,
241   variety and preservation of semantics (see Appendix D for further discussion, and Appendix K for a
242   human study on semantic preservation). We use these attacks to create ImageNet-UA.
243
      Importantly, we do not claim that these attacks provide an exhaustive taxonomy over adversaries.
244   They simply represent a specific set of diverse held-out adversaries— mirroring methodologies that
245   have been popular in other studies of distribution shift (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2018; Hendrycks
246   et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2019). We briefly describe our core attacks below:
247
      Wood.     The wood attack is described in Figure 3 and Appendix C.
248
249   Glitch. Glitch simulates a common behavior in corrupted images of colored fuzziness. Glitch greys
250   out the image, splitting it into horizontal bars, before independently shifting color channels within
251   each of these bars.
252   JPEG. The JPEG compression algorithm functions by encoding small image patches using the
253   discrete cosine transform, and then quantizing the results. The attack functions by optimizing
254   L∞ -constrained perturbations within the JPEG-encoded space of compressed images and then
255   reverse-transforming to obtain the image in pixel space, using ideas from Shin & Song (2017) to
256   make this differentiable.
257   Gabor. Gabor spatially occludes the image with visually diverse Gabor noise (Lagae et al., 2009),
258   optimizing the underlying sparse tensor which the Gabor kernels are applied to.
259
260
      Kaleidoscope. Kaleidoscope overlays randomly colored polygons onto the image, and then op-
      timizes both the homogeneous color of the inside of the shape, and the darkness/lightness of the
261
      individual pixels on the shape’s border, up to an L∞ constraint.
262
263   Pixel. Pixel modifies an image so it appears to be of lower quality, by first splitting the image
264   into m × m “pixels” and then and averaging the image color within each block. The optimization
265   variables δ then control the level of pixelation, on a per-block bases.
266   Elastic. Our only non-novel attack. Elastic is adapted from (Xiao et al., 2018), functioning by
267   which warping the image by distortions x′ = Flow(x, V ), where V : {1, . . . , 224}2 → R2 is a
268   vector field on pixel space, and Flow sets the value of pixel (i, j) to the bilinearly interpolated original
269   value at (i, j) + V (i, j). To make the attack suitable for high-resolution images, we modify the
      original attack by passing a Gaussian kernel over V .

357
358   In this section, we evaluate a range of models on our standardized benchmarks ImageNet-UA and
359   CIFAR-10-UA. We aim to present a set of directions for future work, by comparing a wide range of
360   methods. We also hope to explore how the problem of unforeseen robustness differs from existing
361   robustness metrics.
362
363   4.1     H OW DO EXISTING ROBUSTNESS MEASURES RELATE TO UNFORESEEN ROBUSTNESS ?
364
365   We find differences between existing metrics and UA2, suggesting that the setting of unforeseen
366   adversarial robustness may require new methods to obtain strong performance.
367   UA2 is distinct from existing measures of distribution shift. We compare UA2 to several standard
368   distribution-shift benchmarks—ImageNet-C (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019), ImageNet-R Hendrycks
369   et al. (2021) and ImageNet-Sketch (Wang et al., 2019). As shown in Table 5 and Appendix I, perfor-
370   mance on these benchmark is similar to performance on non-optimized versions of our attacks. By
371   contrast, the optimized versions of our attacks are far more challenging and have distinct properties.
372   For example, while adversarial training does not improve performance on ImageNet-C, it does im-
373   prove performance on ImageNet-UA. This highlights that UA2 is a measure of worst case robustness,
374
      similar to Lp robustness, and distinct from other distribution shift benchmarks in the literature.
375   Lp robustness is correlated, but distinct from, unforeseen robustness. As shown in Appendix L,
376   unforeseen robustness is correlated with Lp robustness. Our attacks also show similar properties to
377   Lp counterparts, such as the ability for black-box transfer (Appendix N). However, many models
      show susceptibility to Lp adversaries while still performing well on UA2 (Table 1), and a range

455   4.3     H OW HAS PROGRESS ON CV BENCHMARKS TRACKED UNFORESEEN ROBUSTNESS ?
456
457   Computer vision progress has partially tracked unforeseen robustness. Comparing the UA2 of
458   ResNet-50 to ConvNeXt-V2-huge (1% → 19.1% UA2) demonstrates the effects of almost a decade
459   of CV advances, including self-supervised pretraining, hardware improvements, data augmentation,
460   architectural changes and new regularization techniques. More generally, we find a range of modern
461   architectures and training strategies doing well (see Table 2, full results in Figure 8). This is gives a
462   positive view of how progress on standard CV benchmarks has tracked underlying robustness metrics,
463   contrasting with classical Lp adversarial robustness where standard training techniques have little
464   effect (Madry et al., 2017a).

      In this paper, we introduced a new benchmark for testing robustness against unforeseen adversaries
      (ImageNet-UA) laying groundwork for continuing research in improving real world adversarial
478
      robustness. We provide nineteen (eighteen novel) non-Lp attacks as part of our repository, using these
479
      to construct a new metric UA2 (Unforeseen Adversarial Accuracy). We show that ImageNet-UA is
480   distinct from existing measures of robustness in the literature, and make use use it to evaluate classical
481   Lp training techniques—showing that the common practice of L∞ training and evaluation may be
482   misleading, as L2 training shows higher unforeseen robustness. We additionally demonstrate that a
483   variety of interventions outside of Lp adversarial training can improve unforeseen robustness, both
484   through existing techniques in the CV literature and through more specialised adversarial training
485   strategies. We hope that ImageNet-UA will be a useful tool as we continue to make progress towards
      safer machine learning systems in real-world applications.

793   B     D ESCRIPTIONS OF THE 11 A DDITIONAL ATTACKS .
794
795
      Blur.     Blur approximates real-world motion blur effects by passing a Gaussian filter over the
796
      original image and then does a pixel-wise linear interpolation between the blurred version and the
797   original, with the optimisation variables controlling the level of interpolation. We also apply a
798   Gaussian filter to the grid of optimisation variables, to enforce some continuity in the strength of the
799   blur between adjacent pixels. This method is distinct from, but related to other blurring attacks in the
800   literature (Guo et al., 2020; 2021).
801
      Edge. This attack functions by applying a Canny Edge Detector (Canny, 1986) over the image to
802
      locate pixels at the edge of objects, and then applies a standard PGD attack to the identified edge
803   pixels.
804
805   Fractional Brownian Motion (FBM). FBM overlays several layers of Perlin noise (Perlin, 2005)
806
      at different frequencies, creating a distinctive noise pattern. The underlying gradient vectors which
      generate each instance of the Perlin noise are then optimised by the attack.
807
808   Fog. Fog simulates worst-case weather conditions, creating fog-like occlusions by adversarially
809   optimizing parameters in the diamond-square algorithm (Fournier et al., 1982) typically used to
      render stochastic fog effects.

                C                      F ULL D ESCRIPTION OF W OOD ATTACK
865
866
               In Figure 3, we give a high-level explanation of the Wood attack. Here, we give a more detailed
867            explanation of this figure.
868
869            Given a classifier f , the Wood attack with distortion level ε functions by taking a set of adversarial
870
               latent variables δn ∈ Rm×m×2 (representing a vector field of per-pixel displacements), applies
               projectεp to project this field into the ε ball in the Lp metric and then uses bi-linear interpola-
871
               tion to upsample the latent variables to the input image size. The upsampled latent variables are
872
               then used to make the wood noise, by using an element-wise mapping F : Rn×n×2 → Rn×n ,
873            taking a coordinate     to the (power of) the sine of its distance from the center of the image i.e.
874
                            p
               F (I) = sin( (X)2 + (Y )2 )β , where Xij = Iij0 − n/2 and Yij = Iij1 − n/2 and β is an attack
875
               hyperparameter. When applied to constant coordinate tensor C ∈ Rn×n×2 , Cij = (i, j), this func-
876            tion creates the distinctive “wood rings” of the Wood attack, which are then multiplied with the
877            input image to produce adversarial input. By virtue of the differentiability of this process, we can
878            backpropagate through this noise generation and optimize the adversarial image xadv by performing
879            PGD (Madry et al., 2017a) on the input latent variables.
880
881             D                      P ROCESS FOR D ESIGNING ATTACKS AND S ELECTING C ORE ATTACKS
882
883           Our design of attacks is guided by two motivations: defending against unforeseen adversaries and
884           robustness to long-tail scenarios. Unforeseen adversaries could implement novel attacks to, e.g., evade
885           automated neural network content filters. To model unforeseen adversaries that might realistically
886           appear in these scenarios, we include digital corruptions similar to what one might see on YouTube
887
              videos trying to evade content filters. These include attacks such as Kaleidoscope and Prison. To
              model long-tail scenarios, we include worst-case versions of common corruptions, like JPEG, Snow,
888
              and Fog.
889
890            In preliminary experiments, we found that some of these attacks were more effective than others,
891            leading to lower accuracy with fewer steps. We also found that performance on some attacks was
892            correlated between models. For example, both “Prison” and “Edge”, are pixel-level attacks, so
893
               robustness to one was correlated with robustness to the other. To increase the diversity and efficiency
               of our evaluation, we selected a core set of eight attacks based on their effectiveness and diversity,
894
               considering both visual diversity and the accuracy profiles of different models. This was an iterative
895
               process that led us to make substantial changes to some attacks. For example, we modified the
896            implementation of the Elastic attack to use larger, lower-frequency distortions, which maintained its
897            effectiveness while reducing correlation with PGD.
