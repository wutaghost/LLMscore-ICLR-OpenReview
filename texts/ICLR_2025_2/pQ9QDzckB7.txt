                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               Despite their impressive ability to generate high-quality and fluent text, generative
012
               large language models (LLMs) also produce hallucinations: statements that are
013
               misaligned with established world knowledge or provided input context. How-
014            ever, measuring hallucination can be challenging, as having humans verify model
015            generations on-the-fly is both expensive and time-consuming. In this work, we re-
016            lease HAL O GEN , a comprehensive hallucination benchmark consisting of: (1)
017            10,923 prompts for generative models spanning nine domains including program-
018            ming, scientific attribution, and summarization, and (2) automatic high-precision
019            verifiers for each use case that decompose LLM generations into atomic units, and
020            verify each unit against a high-quality knowledge source. We use this framework
021            to evaluate ∼150,000 generations from 14 language models, finding that even the
022            best-performing models . We further define a novel error classification for LLM
023
               hallucinations based on their source: (1) Type A errors for errors that may stem
               from incorrect recollection from training data, (2) Type B errors for errors that
024
               may stem from incorrect knowledge in training data or incorrect contextualization,
025
               and (3) Type C errors for hallucinations that are likely to be fabrication. For code
026            packages, we that 70% of unique packages hallucinated by Llama-3-70B can be
027            found in the C4 corpus, while for another category of hallucinations about fictional
028            historic events, we find that we can seldom find a basis for these events within
029            the data. We hope that our framework will provide a foundation to enable princi-
030            pled scientific studies of why generative models hallucinate, and to advance the
031            development of trustworthy large language models.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   A practical challenge to deploying commercial large language models (LLMs) is their propensity
037   to produce hallucinated output: facts that are not aligned with world knowledge, or with the input
038   context provided by the user. LLM hallucinations can cause potential downstream harms for real-
039   world users (NIST, 2023). Yet, the reason behind why models hallucinate is currently unknown.
040
      Worse, it is difficult to even measure the extent to which models hallucinate, due to the open-ended
      nature of model generations, and the associated time, effort, and cost of human verification.
041
042   In this work we address these challenges by (1) creating a comprehensive benchmark over diverse do-
043   mains to measure hallucination behavior in language models at scale, (2) using this diverse benchmark
044   to investigate potential sources of language model hallucination in a range of scenarios. To facilitate
045   estimating the degree to which large language models hallucinate, we introduce HAL O GEN
046   (evaluating Hallucinations of Generative Models), a large-scale evaluation suite to measure hallu-
047   cination in long-form generations of large language models (Figure 1). HAL O GEN consists of
048
      prompts spanning nine use-cases including tasks where a model response is expected (response-based
049   tasks) and tasks where a model is expected to abstain from answering (refusal-based tasks), as well
050
      as domain-specific automatic verifiers accompanying each use-case that (1) decompose a model
      generation into a series of meaningful atomic units specific to the use case, (2) verify the factuality of
051
      each atomic unit using external tools, programs, or LLM-based classifiers.
052
053   We evaluate the responses of 14 LLMs on this benchmark, spanning 150,000 model generations.
      Our experimental results show that even the best-performing LLM responses are riddled with

089   Our novel analysis of LLM hallucinations presents a nuanced picture. Model hallucinations do not
090   seem to have a single isolated cause, but rather could originate from a multitude of scenarios which
091   vary across domains. For example, we find that for code-generation tasks, hallucinated software
092   packages can often be found as-is within pretraining corpora (Type B errors), whereas for another
093   task where the model hallucinates incorrect educational affiliations for US senators, the model often
094   has access to the correct information within the pretraining data (Type A errors) and generates
095
      factually inaccurate statements. By providing a method to study diverse hallucination behavior in
      language models, and a framework for identifying the potential sources behind model hallucination,
096
      we hope to provide a systematic foundation for truthful large language models.

099   2     R ELATED W ORK
100
101   The tendency of LLMs to generate unfactual content, or “hallucinate”, has been well-documented in
102   recent surveys (Zhang et al., 2023b; Ji et al., 2022).
103
104   Hallucination detection Early hallucination detection work studied content-grounded tasks such
105   as summarization (Pagnoni et al., 2021a), simplification (Devaraj et al., 2022b), and dialogue (Dziri
106   et al., 2022). Techniques for these settings identify factual units in the model output, and compare
107   each unit against the source text using entailment-based (Maynez et al., 2020; Kryscinski et al., 2019)
      or QA-based (Durmus et al., 2020) systems.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      More recently, a number of works have sought to detect hallucinations occurring in open-ended
109   generation. Reference-based approaches evaluate LLMs against trusted reference sources like
110   Wikipedia or web search (Min et al., 2023; Chern et al., 2023; Mishra et al., 2024). Prior works have
111   similarly relied on web search to identify hallucinated citations (Agrawal et al., 2023). Reference-free
112   approaches instead use an LLM itself to detect hallucinations, by comparing the consistency of model
113   responses (Manakul et al., 2023) or examining the model’s output logits (Varshney et al., 2023).
114
115   Hallucination benchmarks LLM hallucination benchmarks consist of a collection of prompts
116   designed for their potential to lead to hallucinated model output. The accuracy of the model responses
117   to each prompt are then evaluated, either using a more powerful LLM (Lin et al., 2021b), by
118   examining the likelihoods assigned to correct and incorrect completions (Muhlgay et al., 2023), or
119   by human annotators (Li et al., 2023). A number of benchmarks are also available to assess LLM
120
      factual knowledge in knowledge base completion Mallen et al. (2022); Petroni et al. (2019) and
      multiple-choice Hendrycks et al. (2020) settings.
121
122   Relative to prior benchmarks, HAL O GEN covers a wider range of potential hallucination scenar-
123   ios, including grounded generation (e.g. text summarization), open-ended generation (e.g. biogra-
124   phies), and bespoke use cases like and code package imports and scientific citations. In addition,
125   HAL O GEN covers both response-based tasks, where a model is expected to respond, and refusal-
126   based tasks, where a model is expected to abstain from answering. We leverage a wide assortment
127   of hallucination evaluation techniques to evaluate these use cases, ranging from entailment-based
128   approaches for open-ended text generation to searches for Python packages and scientific references.
129
130   Factual attribution for LLMs In this work, we perform post-hoc model attribution (He et al., 2022;
131   Gao et al., 2022) on model hallucinations. The availability of WIMBD Elazar et al. (2023) enables
132   us to cross-reference hallucinations with large, widely-used pretraining corpora, whereas most prior
133
      works have relied on search engines or fixed knowledge sources like Wikipedia. Model-based methods
      for attribution—either by prompting the model to generate citations directly Weller et al. (2023);
134
      Khalifa et al. (2024), or via techniques like influence functions Grosse et al. (2023)— represent an
135
      interesting future direction to better understand hallucinations observed using HAL O GEN .

138   3     B UILDING A B ENCHMARK FOR H ALLUCINATED C ONTENT
139
140   We describe the process of constructing HAL O GEN . This benchmark consists of content-
141   grounded tasks such as text summarization, as well as ungrounded text generation tasks. For
142   ungrounded text generation, we focus on knowledge-oriented, rather than creative or subjective, tasks.
143   We define a hallucination to be a fact in a model generation that is not aligned with established world
144   knowledge or with provided context. For content-grounded tasks, we consider hallucinations to be
145
      facts generated by a model that are not entailed by the provided context, even if factually correct.
146   It should be noted that there is no one definition of established knowledge for several facts, that truth
147   can be pluralistic, and that data stores may contain conflicting information sources. We operationalize
148   an ‘established’ knowledge source by specifying a singular ‘source of truth’ for each scenario, but
149   it is possible for a practitioner to make different factuality determinations by considering different
150   knowledge sources, or by interpreting information from the knowledge source differently.
151   HAL O GEN includes nine tasks measuring different aspects of model factuality (Table 1). For each
152   task, the benchmark consists of three components: (a) a set of LLM prompts X, (b) a decomposition
153   engine D that breaks down model generations into atomic units to be verified, and (c) a hallucination
154   detector V to automatically verify the factuality of each atomic unit. We describe these components
155   for the tasks in HAL O GEN . Tasks are either Response-Based, where a model is asked to provide
156   information, or Refusal-Based, where the prompt is one that a model is expected to refuse.

250   Scientific Attribution This study sheds light on the extent to which models hallucinate scientific
251   references, particularly in scenarios with incorrect claims. Understanding fabrication of scientific
252   references is important for several reasons: (1) LLMs are frequently used in information-seeking
253   contexts Zhao et al. (2024), (2) appearing to provide accurate scientific citations to false claims
254   in model responses can provide a veneer of scientific credibility to misinformation, (3) There is
255
      growing interest in releasing ‘copilots’ or assistants to support various aspects of the scientific
      process, including identifying and synthesizing information from literature Lu et al. (2024); Laurent
256
      et al. (2024). We wish to note that even if references themselves are not hallucinated, LLMs may
257
      still attribute incorrect claims to them. We leave it to future work to measure this second kind of
258   hallucinatory behavior. Prompt Construction: We curate prompts featuring inaccurate statements,
259   misconceptions, incorrect answers to questions, and misleading claims. These prompts require
260   language models to find supporting references for inherently inaccurate content. We construct
261   prompts from four sources: (1) The Hetionet knowledge graph Himmelstein et al. (2017), which
262   encodes biological data, was used to generate 800 claims. (2) We extract 100 contradictory claims
263   from the SciFact dataset Wadden et al. (2022), which comprises of 1.4K expert-written claims with
264   annotated evidence-containing abstracts. (3) We construct 817 questions based on the TruthfulQA
265   benchmark Lin et al. (2021a) by asking the model to find references justifying the combination of a
266
      question and incorrect answer. (4) We extract 62 false claims from the COVID-19 Lies dataset Hossain
      et al. (2020), representing common misconceptions about the disease.

      to verify generated titles.

      Historical Events Prompt Construction: We created a list of 400 noteworthy individuals with
271   non-overlapping living periods, who are consequently unlikely to have ever met. We construct
272   prompts with the format ’Tell me about the famous meeting between [X] and [Y]’, where ’[X]’ and
273   ’[Y]’ represent the pair of individuals. Decomposition and Verification: For verification, we look for
274   the keywords ‘yes’ or ‘no’ in the model response. If the model response contains the keyword ‘yes’,
275   we interpret its failure to refuse the user’s request as a hallucinated response. This verification is done
276   at the response-level instead of decomposing the model response into individual atomic facts. We use
277   Llama-2-70B as a judge to determine if the model response describes that a meeting took place, or
278   doesn’t confirm a meeting.

280   False Presuppositions Prompts in this dataset are of the form "List {N} {list_name} that {condi-
281
      tion} the letter {letter}.", where N is more than the number of items that satisfy the condition. The
      dataset includes 13 entity lists. We expect the ideal model response to indicate that the prompt has a
282
      false presupposition. Decomposition and Verification: For verification, we look for listed items in the
283
      model response. If the model lists items that satisfy the condition, we interpret its failure to refuse the
284   user’s request as a hallucinated response. We consider the hallucinated atomic units to be those list
285   items in the model response that don’t satisfy the specified condition.

287   Verification Accuracy We examine the accuracy of those verifiers that use LLMs in the verification
288   pipeline. These include the verifiers for the tasks: summarization, simplification, and historical
289   events. We sample 100 atoms for each of these tasks, and independently manually annotate them for
290   entailment (summariation, simplification), or refusal (historical events, false presuppositions). We
291   find that the agreement rates with the verifier prediction are as follows: 91% (for summarization),
292   92% (for simplification), and 88% (for historical events).

463   5.2   C ONTENT-G ROUNDED TASKS
464
465   Summarization We aim to shed light on the nature of large language model hallucinations in
466   generating abstractive summaries. In the task of abstractive summarization, statements in a generated
467   summary that are not faithful to the provided context are considered as hallucinated, even if factually
468   correct. Particularly, we seek to understand if models hallucinations are caused by models incorrectly
469   processing information in the input (intrinsic hallucinations), or by introducing information that
470   cannot be inferred from the input (extrinsic hallucinations) Maynez et al. (2020).
471   In order to study errors of most capable models, we aggregate and examine the summaries of models
472   whose utility score is atleast 0.85. We manually annotate 100 statements in model summaries that
473   were identified as hallucination, discarding cases where the entailment is ambiguous or where there
474   was an error in atomization. We find that for high-utility models, 83% of model hallucinations are
475   due to the model incorrectly processing the provided context (intrinsic hallucinations), with
476   only 17% of errors originating from a model introducing an external fact into the summary (Table
477
      ??). We further code each intrinsic hallucination with a fine-grained error category based on the
      typology introduced in Pagnoni et al. (2021b). These categorize factuality errors as entity errors,
478
      relation error, errors of circumstance, coreference errors, discourse link errors, or grammatical errors.
479
      We find modern large language models seldom make grammatical errors, with incorrect entities or
480   predicates being common sources of hallucination errors. Further, we find that most of the extrinsic
481   hallucination errors orginate from smaller models, with olmo-7b-instruct introducing 64.7% (11/17)
482   of the extrinsic hallucination errors. On further coding 50 samples from olmo-7b instruct, we find that
483   extrinsic hallucinations account for 46% of its hallucination errors. However, we find that only 87%
484   of these hallucinations contain an attributable fact, that these hallucinations often introduce additional
485   temporal information (30.4%), and that on sampling ten relevant documents from the pretraining data
      , we are unable to find evidence of these hallucinations..

      Simplification In this section, we aim to shed light on the nature of large language model hallucina-
487   tions in simplifying text. In order to study errors of most capable models, we aggregate and examine
488   the simplified generations of models whose utility score is atleast 0.85. We manually annotate
489   100 atomic statements in the automatically simplified texts that were identified as hallucination,
490   discarding cases where the entailment is ambiguous or where there was an error in atomization. We
491   categorize the hallucinations by type (inserting new factual information, substituting existing factual
492   information, or deleting factual information in a way that introduces an unsupported fact), as well as
493   severity, following the taxonomy proposed in (Devaraj et al., 2022a) for text simplification. Note
494   that an atomic fact may feature multiple types of errors, and that insertion errors are similar to the

      extrinsic hallucinations described in the previous section. First, we observe that 49% of samples

      93.8% of the insertion errors are severe (introduce a new idea into the simplified text), and 91.8% of

      the substitution errors are severe (substantially alter the main idea of the complex text).

501
502
      We briefly discuss our findings, and offer some
503   guiding principles for future work on building
504   more factual large language models.
505
506
      Sources of Model Hallucination Our work
507
      shows that LLM hallucinations may arise from
508
      multiple possible sources in the training data—
509   ranging from incorrect information in the pre-
510   training data, to total fabrication. Future work
511   would construct causal frameworks, to study
512   counterfactual questions about the inclusion of Figure 4: Types of Errors in Model Hallucinations
513   specific datapoints and their effect on specific on Historical Events
514   model hallucinations to shed more light on the
515   root cause of hallucination. In addition, while
516   we search for facts as they are stated in model
517
      responses, these facts could be present implicitly in pretraining corpora. Future work would attribute
      hallucinations by computing these implicit inferences as well.
518
519
520   What will it take to have truthful AI systems? Born of the observation that models may halluci-
521   nate for multiple reasons, effective hallucination mitigation methods are likely to require a suite of
522   complementary approaches or significantly new approaches altogether. For example, a retrieval-based
523
      backbone is likely to be effective for long-tailed information, but not when the datastore does not
      have relevant information, or if the datastore contains incorrect information. On the other hand,
524
      approaches which require LLMs to verbalize uncertainty may be more effective in such scenarios.
525
      However, while these are likely to patch a portion of hallucination errors, our findings also indicate
526   that current LLMs make semantic errors even when the context is completely provided as in the case
527   of summarization, indicating the need for more robust frameworks for semantic meaning.

530   7    C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this work, we study hallucination in generative large language models. We contribute a high-quality
533   resource, HAL O GEN , to measure and identify model hallucinations in a broad range of scenarios.
534   Using HAL O GEN , we are then able to create a large-scale dataset of hallucinations from 200,000
535   large-language model generations, sourced from 15 different language models. We use this dataset
536   to systematically trace back language model hallucinations to their training data for the first time,
537   and propose a classification schema for three types of hallucination errors. Our work highlights how
538   nuanced the causes of LLM hallucination can be, and we discuss potential strategies to mitigate
539   hallucination in large-language models based on the type of errors models make. We hope our
      framework provides the foundation for scientific study of hallucination in large language models.
