011                                                                         A BSTRACT
012
               Backdoor attacks, representing an emerging threat to the integrity of deep neural
013
               networks, have garnered significant attention due to their ability to compromise
014            deep learning systems clandestinely. While numerous backdoor attacks occur
015            within the digital realm, their practical implementation in real-world prediction
016            systems remains limited and vulnerable to disturbances in the physical world.
017            Consequently, this limitation has given rise to the development of physical back-
018            door attacks, where trigger objects manifest as physical entities within the real
019            world. However, creating the requisite dataset to train or evaluate a physical back-
020            door model is a daunting task, limiting the backdoor researchers and practitioners
021            from studying such physical attack scenarios. This paper unleashes a framework
022
               that empowers backdoor researchers to effortlessly create a malicious, physical
               backdoor dataset based on advances in generative modeling. Particularly, this
023
               framework involves 3 automatic modules: suggesting the suitable physical trig-
024
               gers, generating the poisoned candidate samples (either by synthesizing new sam-
025            ples or editing existing clean samples), and finally refining for the most plausible
026            ones. As such, it effectively mitigates the perceived complexity associated with
027            creating a physical backdoor dataset, transforming it from a daunting task into an
028            attainable objective. Extensive experiment results show that datasets created by
029            our framework enable researchers to achieve an impressive attack success rate on
030            real physical world data and exhibit similar properties compared to previous phys-
031            ical backdoor attack studies. This paper offers researchers a valuable toolkit for
032            studies of physical backdoors, all within the confines of their laboratories.

049   Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) have surged in popularity due to their superior performance in var-
050   ious practical tasks such as image classification Krizhevsky & Hinton (2009); He et al. (2016),
051   object detection Ren et al. (2016); Redmon et al. (2016) and natural language processing Devlin
052   et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2019). The rapid emergence of DNNs in high-stake applications, such
053   as autonomous driving, has raised concerns regarding potential security vulnerabilities in DNNs.
      Prior works have shown that DNNs are susceptible to various types of attacks, including adversar-

069   ial attacks Carlini & Wagner (2017); Madry et al. (2018), poisoning attacks Muñoz-González et al.
070   (2017); Shafahi et al. (2018) and backdoor attacks Bagdasaryan et al. (2020); Gu et al. (2019). For
071   instance, backdoor attacks impose serious security threats to DNNs by impelling malicious behavior
072   onto DNNs by poisoning the data or manipulating the training process Liu et al. (2017; 2018b). A
073   backdoored model exhibits normal behavior without a trigger pattern but acts maliciously when the
074   trigger pattern is present.
075   Prior works Gu et al. (2017); Liu et al. (2020b); Nguyen & Tran (2021); Doan et al. (2021) focus
076   on exposing the security vulnerabilities of DNNs within digital confines, where adversaries design
077   and implement computer algorithms to launch backdoor attacks. To launch such attacks, adver-
078   saries must perform test-time digital manipulation of the images, which are likely to be susceptible
079   to physical distortions or extremely noisy environments. These physical disturbances are likely
080   unavoidable and often restrain the severity of backdoor attacks. In addition, test-time digital ma-
081
      nipulations are less likely to be accessible to adversaries, especially in autonomous vehicles, which
      involve real-time predictions, thus constraining the capability of adversaries to attack against these
082
      systems.
083
084   On the other hand, physical backdoor attacks focus on exploiting physical objects as triggers Wang
085   et al. (2023); Wenger et al. (2021); Ma et al. (2022). As such, an adversary could easily compromise
086   privacy-sensitive and real-time systems, such as facial recognition systems. An adversary could
087
      impersonate a key person in a company by wearing facial accessories (e.g., glasses) as physical
      triggers to gain unauthorized access. Although physical backdoor attacks are a practical threat to
088
      DNNs, they remain under-explored, as they require a custom dataset injected with attacker-defined,
089
      physical triggers. Preparing such datasets, especially involving human or animal subjects, is often
090   arduous due to the required approval from the Institutional or Ethics Review Board (I/ERB). Ac-
091   quiring the dataset is also costly, as it involves extensive human labor, and this cost often scales with
092   the magnitude of datasets. These have constrained researchers and practitioners from unleashing the
093   potential threat of physical backdoor attacks, until now.
094
      Recent advancements in deep generative models such as Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)
095
      Goodfellow et al. (2014); Chen et al. (2016) and Diffusion Models Ho et al. (2020); Song et al.
096   (2020); Rombach et al. (2022) have shed lights in synthesizing and editing surreal images without
097   involving extensive human interventions. With a text prompt, deep generative models can create
098   high-quality and high-fidelity artificial images. Additionally, deep generative models could edit or
099   manipulate the content of an image, given an image and an instruction prompt. The superiority of
100   deep generative models allows the creation of a physical backdoor dataset with minimal effort, e.g.,
101   by specifying a prompt only.
102   In this work, we propose a “framework”, which enables researchers or practitioners to create a phys-
103   ical backdoor dataset with minimal effort and costs. To boostrap the creation of physical backdoor
104   datasets, this framework consists of a trigger suggestion module, a trigger generation module, and
105   a poison selection module, as shown in Fig. 1. Trigger Suggestion Module automatically suggests
106   the appropriate physical triggers that blend well within the image context. After selecting a de-
107   sired physical trigger, one could utilize Trigger Generation Module to ease in generating a surreal
      physical backdoor dataset. Finally, the Poison Selection Module assists in the automatic selec-

108
      tion of surreal and natural images, as well as discarding implausible outputs that are occasionally
109   synthesized by the generative model.

126   2     R ELATED W ORKS
127
      2.1   BACKDOOR ATTACKS
128
129   Digital Backdoor Attacks focus on creating and executing backdoor attacks within the digital
130
      space, which involves image pixel manipulations Gu et al. (2017); Nguyen & Tran (2021); Doan
      et al. (2021); Saha et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2020b); Wang et al. (2023) and model manipulations
131
      Bober-Irizar et al. (2023). BadNets Gu et al. (2017) first exposes the vulnerability of DNNs to back-
132
      door attacks by embedding a malicious patch-based trigger onto an image and changing the injected
133   image’s label to a predefined targeted class. WaNet Nguyen & Tran (2021) applies a warping field
134   to the input, and LIRA Doan et al. (2021) optimizes the trigger generation function, respectively,
135   to achieve better stealthiness and evade human inspection. Digital backdoor attacks are limited as
136   digital triggers are (i) volatile to perturbations, noisy environments, and human inspections and (ii)
137   harder to inject during test time, especially in real-time prediction systems, where it leaves no buffer
138   for adversaries to tamper or inject triggers during the transmission of inputs to the systems.
139   Research on Physical Backdoors focuses on extending backdoor attacks to the physical space by
140   employing physical objects as triggers (denoted as physical triggers). They threaten DNNs prac-
141   tically as they are capable of (i) bypassing human-in-the-loop detection Wenger et al. (2022) and
142   (ii) attacking real-time prediction systems. Physical triggers exist in the physical world and possess
143   semantic information; when injected, they blend gracefully and naturally with the images, leaving
144   no trace of artifacts; contrasting digital triggers which often create artifacts such as “visible” bor-
145   ders Gu et al. (2017) or unnatural curves Nguyen & Tran (2021). Moreover, physical triggers are
146   more feasible to carry and easier to tamper with the targeted class during test time, empowering
147
      adversaries to attack real-time prediction systems. Wenger et al. (2021) showed that by wearing
      different facial accessories, an adversary could bypass a facial recognition system and uncover the
148
      possibility of impersonation through physical triggers. Dangerous Cloak Ma et al. (2022) exposed
149
      the possibility of evading object detection systems by wearing custom clothes as the trigger, making
150   the adversary “invisible” under surveillance. Han et al. (2022) revealed that autonomous vehicle
151   lane detection systems could be attacked by physical objects by the roadside, leading to potential
152   accidents and fatalities.
153
      Despite the potential effectiveness of physical backdoor attacks, and consequently their potential
154
      harms, this area of research remains under-explored due to the challenges in preparing and sharing
155
      these “physical” datasets. Preparing such a dataset requires intense labor and substantial costs; for
156   example, to poison ImageNet (∼1.3 million images), with a poisoning rate of 5%, it is required to
157   create 65,000 poisoned images with physical trigger objects, which is impractical and impossible
158   for most researchers. When the dataset involves either human or animal subjects, necessary but
159   often time-consuming and involved approvals, such as those from the I/ERB to protect the privacy
160   of and realize potential risks for the study participants, are required. Hence, Wenger et al. Wenger
161   et al. (2022) proposed to study the curation of a physical backdoor dataset by identifying the natural
      co-occurrence of trigger objects within the datasets. Our work extends on the idea of Wenger et al.


                                                         3
162
      (2022), particularly in crafting physical backdoor datasets with generative models, to effectively
163   reduce the effort and cost required for conducting physical backdoor research.

165   2.2   BACKDOOR D EFENSES
166   As backdoor attacks emerged, defensive mechanisms against backdoor attacks have gained attention.
167   Several works have been focusing on counteracting backdoor attacks such as backdoor detection
168   Chen et al. (2019); Tran et al. (2018); Gao et al. (2019), input mitigation Liu et al. (2017); Li et al.
169   (2020) and model mitigation Liu et al. (2018a); Wang et al. (2019). Activation Clustering Chen
170   et al. (2019) detects backdoor models by analyzing activation values of models in latent space, while
171   STRIP Gao et al. (2019) analyzes the models’ output entropy on perturbed inputs. Neural Cleanse
172
      Wang et al. (2019) optimizes for potential trigger patterns to detect backdoor attacks within DNNs.
      Input mitigation defenses suppress and deactivate backdoors to retain the model’s normal behavior
173
      Li et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2017). Fine pruning Liu et al. (2018a) combines both fine-tuning and
174
      pruning techniques, hoping to remove potentially backdoored neurons. Neural Attention Distillation
175   (NAD) Li et al. (2021) aims to purge malicious behaviors of a model by distilling the knowledge of
176   a teacher model, which is trained on a small set of clean data, into a student model.
177
178
      The state of existing physical defense research. Similar to the state of existing physical attack
      studies from the adversary side, research on defensive countermeasures for these physical attacks is
179
      also unsatisfactory. For example, Wenger et al. (2021; 2022) shows that most defenses, including
180
      Neural Cleanse, STRIP, Spectral Signature, and Activation Clustering, can only detect, thus prevent,
181   physical attacks with catastrophic harms, such as attacks on facial recognition systems, at only
182   around 40% of the times, signifying the lack of research in both attacks and defenses for physical
183   backdoors.

      2.3   D IFFUSION M ODELS FOR I MAGE G ENERATION AND M ANIPULATION
186   Recent advancements in deep generative models have surged the performance of image synthe-
187   sis Goodfellow et al. (2014); Kingma & Welling (2014). Diffusion Models (DMs) Song et al.
188   (2020); Ho et al. (2020), which rely on multi-step denoising processes to generate images from
189   pure noise inputs, have become trendy in generative modeling as they surpassed GANs Goodfellow
190   et al. (2014) in both image quality and data density coverage Dhariwal & Nichol (2021) and well
191
      supported with different conditional inputs Rombach et al. (2022). Among the means to generate
      images, text-to-image generation is the most attractive and practical. In the means of physical back-
192
      door research, one could leverage text-to-image generation for synthesizing surreal images without
193
      much effort, simply by describing the intended physical triggers and subject precisely.
194
195   Traditional image editing methods which range from simply cutting and pasting trigger objects
196   into target images Chen et al. (2017), to blending triggers into target images with Adobe Photoshop,
197
      failed to demonstrate scalability. These methods require extensive knowledge of a particular tool
      (e.g. Adobe Photoshop, Adobe Illustrator) and human attention (to identify reasonable locations for
198
      triggers) to craft a single high-quality poisoned sample. These requirements (extensive knowledge
199
      and human attention) signify the need to involve human experts in crafting a surreal physical back-
200   door dataset, which leads to extensive costs in hiring human experts and time-consuming in crafting
201   the dataset manually. With the advancement in deep generative models, such constraints could
202   be effectively mitigated by leveraging generative models to synthesize a surreal physical backdoor
203   dataset, with higher throughput, better scalability and lower cost, as compared to humans.

      curation of physical backdoor datasets would require extensive ethical and institutional reviews,
      which are time-consuming.

215   multi-label datasets, and is proven to be effective in identifying one of the co-occurring objects as
      physical triggers. However, such a method only works in multi-label settings, and this inevitably

      4.1 T RIGGER S UGGESTION M ODULE
240
241   Compatibility of trigger objects is defined as the likelihood of the trigger objects co-existing with the
242   main subject, ensuring that the physical trigger objects align with the image context. A compatible
243
      physical trigger object can reduce human suspicion upon inspection, where it blends naturally within
      the image’s context. However, selecting the “right” physical trigger objects often demands human
244
      knowledge or entails a significant workload to scan through partial or even the entire dataset to
245
      identify the “compatible” trigger objects.
246
247   Prior works Wenger et al. (2021); Ma et al. (2022) have engaged in the manual identification of a
248   compatible trigger object within a smaller dataset, where they utilized facial accessories and clothes.
249
      However, as the magnitude of the dataset size scales to the order of millions (or billions), it becomes
      prohibitively costly, and at times, impossible, to manually scan through all images to identify the
250
      appropriate trigger.
251
252   Envisioned to reduce manual efforts, we propose a trigger suggestion module, which is an automated
253   way to suggest a compatible physical trigger. Our proposal is similar to Wenger et al. (2022),
254   which utilizes graph analysis to search for co-occurring objects with the class subjects and select
255
      the objects that co-exist the most with the class subjects as the physical trigger. However, their
      method has a constraint, as they require a multi-label dataset where the physical triggers are selected
256
      from one of the labels. Most image recognition dataset (Food-101 Bossard et al. (2014), Oxford
257
      102 Flower Nilsback & Zisserman (2008), Stanford Dogs Khosla et al. (2011)) are only available
258   in single-label setting, thus Wenger et al. is inapplicable in identifying co-occurring objects (as
259   triggers), due to the lack of multi-class labels. In fact, physical triggers could be any objects (not
260   limited to the labels of the dataset), as the “best” trigger might not be one of the labeled class; for
261   example, in the case of Food-101, the suitable physical triggers might be either cutleries or tableware
262   items.
263
      Hence, we propose to utilize Visual Question Answering (VQA) models such as LLaVA Liu et al.
264   (2023) to automatically scan through the dataset and leverage their prior general knowledge to iden-
265   tify suitable physical triggers. Given a target dataset, we can query the VQA model to identify com-
266   patible physical triggers for injection into the dataset by asking: “What are the 5 suitable objects to
267   be added into the image?” Then, the probability of each object is counted and ranked in descending
268   order, where high probability is deemed more compatible and plausible within the dataset context.
269   With VQA models, we relax the assumption of employing multi-label datasets, enabling researchers
      to broaden their studies to single-label datasets. There are generally 3 cases of trigger compatibility:


                                                                                                                                              5
270
            1. High compatibility (>50%): It denotes that the trigger consistently appears along with the
271            subject. While it may be tempting to employ these suggestions as triggers, it might activate
272            the backdoor attack too frequently, as there are possibilities that these triggers co-occur
273            naturally with the subject, thus compromising the stealthiness of the attack.
274
275         2. Moderate compatibility (10% - 50%): It indicates that the trigger appears commonly with
276            the main subject, but not excessively frequent. It preserves the stealthiness of backdoor
277            attacks by being a common occurrence with the main subject, yet not so frequent that it
278
               may activate the backdoor attacks frequently.
279         3. Low compatibility (<10%): It signifies that the trigger rarely appears with the main sub-
280            ject, suggesting that its frequent appearance in the poisoned dataset would be unnatural.
281
282   In this work, we select a trigger with moderate compatibility, to simulate a stealthy and natural
283   backdoor attack. Moreover, we demonstrate that our proposed trigger suggestion module works on
284   single-label datasets and the triggers suggested by VQA highly align with human preference. We
285   note that researchers are free to select any suggested triggers, despite their compatibility, to study
286   backdoor attacks/defenses under various scenarios.

      4.2   T RIGGER G ENERATION M ODULE
289   Manual preparation and collection of physical backdoor datasets is daunting, as it usually involves
290   approvals and ethical concerns. Recent advancements in deep generative models provide a simple
291   yet straightforward solution - through image editing or image generation. This paper leverages DMs
292   in crafting a physical backdoor dataset as they satisfy several criteria: (i) high quality and diversity,
293   and (ii) the ability to be conditioned on text.
294   Quality and Diversity: It ensures the surreality and richness of the dataset. Quality refers to the
295   clarity (in terms of resolution) of the crafted physical backdoor dataset, where the images are clear
296   and the objects appear natural to humans. Diversity is defined as the richness and variety of the
297   dataset, where generally, we demand a diverse dataset to enhance the robustness of a trained DNN,
298   such that it does not overfit to a limited context. Both of these attributes are important to improve a
299   DNN’s accuracy and robustness. DMs are capable of synthesizing and editing high quality and high
300   diversity images, therefore, making them the ideal candidate for our trigger generation module.
301   To craft a physical backdoor dataset, one could either edit available data with text prompts (text-
302   guided image editing) or generate data conditioned on text prompts (text-to-image generation):
303
      Dataset Access→Text-guided Image Editing: With this access (both images and labels), text-
304
      guided image editing models such as InstructDiffusion emerge as a fruitful option, which utilizes
305
      both images and labels. Input images are obtainable directly from the dataset, while the text prompts,
306   which include physical triggers could be manually defined (requires more cognitive effort) or sug-
307   gested by our trigger suggestion module, with minimal cognitive effort. Ultimately, through the
308   process of editing an image, the image’s original context is preserved, as most of the image’s fea-
309   tures will remain unaltered, except for the injected physical trigger.
310
      Label-only Access→Text-to-Image Generation: It assumes that practitioners intend to craft a cus-
311
      tom dataset, without any existing images available, and only define the required labels. This scenario
312   generally holds for vertical federated learning (VFL) scenarios, where no image information would
313   be passed to the centralized model. Hence, with the limited label information, practitioners on the
314   centralized side could employ our proposed framework to generate datasets. For this, one could first
315   predefine a desired physical trigger, and then proceed with the proposed trigger generation module
316   and finally, the poison selection module. Liu et al. (2020a) employs a VFL framework that could be
317   potentially utilized for such a case.
318   To summarize, for dataset access, it is fruitful to leverage text-guided image editing models,
319   whereas for label access, text-to-image models are better options. Both of these generative mod-
320   els have the ability to condition on text inputs (which are commonly used to describe the desired
321   physical triggers) and able to synthesize high fidelity, high diversity images. Our framework, which
322   is empowered by such generative models, is widely applicable across various practical cases (as
323   described above), and offers flexibility for practitioners to apply suitable options for their physical
      backdoor research.

344   4.3   P OISON S ELECTION M ODULE
345
346   To create a surreal physical backdoor dataset for research purposes, ensuring the quality of the
347   synthesized data is indeed of utmost crucial. Unfortunately, most deep generative models’ metrics
348
      are inappropriate, due to the nature of their distributional-based evaluation. Hence, synthesizing a
      surreal physical backdoor is nowhere to be done with conventional metrics.
349
350   Problem: Conventional deep generative models’ metrics such as Inception Score (IS) Salimans
351   et al. (2016) and Fréchet-Inception Distance (FID) Heusel et al. (2017) compare the “real” and
352   “synthesized” distribution, to identify how well the “synthesized” distribution resembles the “real”
353   distribution. Although effective, these metrics do not fit into our setting - the synthesized physical
354
      backdoor dataset should be evaluated image-by-image to ensure (i) the presence of physical triggers
      and (ii) the surreality of the synthesized image with the physical trigger. The presence of triggers
355
      within synthesized images is necessary for ensuring successful poison injection, while the surreality
356
      of such images guarantees the naturalness of the synthesized images, such that it is able to simulate
357   the “real” dataset. Such requirements stagnated the development of physical backdoor research, as
358   these metrics could not effectively score a “good” synthesized image with physical backdoors.
359
360
      Solution: We utilize ImageReward Xu et al. (2023) as our evaluation metric for the generated/edited
      images. Given an image and a description (text prompt), ImageReward can provide a human prefer-
361
      ence score for each generated/edited image, according to image-text alignment and fidelity. Inher-
362
      ently, it resolves previous metrics’ limitations by enabling image-by-image evaluation, with regard
363   to both (i) the presence of physical triggers and (ii) the surreality of synthesized images; thus ensur-
364   ing the synthesized physical backdoor datasets are of high quality and consist of physical triggers.

387   suggested by human. We define the notation for the prompts as follows: tr refers to the trigger, act
388   refers to the action/movement of the class object, sub refers to the main class object, bg describes
389   the background/scene of the generated image, and pos specifies other positive prompts such as 4k

391         1. Image Editing (InstructDiffusion)→Dataset Access: The default hyperparameters Geng

412   Attack Success Rate (ASR): accuracy on poisoned inputs with physical triggers, either through im-
413   age editing or image generation, (iii) Real CA: accuracy on the real clean data collected via multiple
414   devices, and (iv) Real ASR: accuracy on the real poisoned data, captured via multiple devices.

416   suggests that the model can perform well in the real physical world. We conjecture that the consistent
417   drop between CA and Real CA (approx. 15%), is due to the distribution shift between the validation
418   data and the real physical data, where generally real physical data has a higher diversity of lighting,
419   background, scene, and position of subjects. In terms of ASR and Real ASR, we observe that
420   for tennis ball, the ASR and Real ASR remain consistent; while for book, the ASR and Real ASR
421   dropped. This phenomenon can be attributed to the consistency of the trigger’s appearance in the real
422   world; for example, a tennis ball is consistently green with white stripes (less distribution shifts, and
423   thus consistent Real ASRs), while a book can have diverse colors and thicknesses (more distribution
424
      shifts, and thus decreases in Real ASRs). The results are consistent with findings from previous
      works Wenger et al. (2021); Ma et al. (2022), where physical triggers with varying shapes and sizes
425
      (e.g., earings) induce lower Real ASRs.

      5.6                   D EFENSE R ESILIENCE
466   Neural Cleanse Wang et al. (2019), is a defense method based on the pattern optimization approach.
467   An Anomaly Index τ below 2 indicates a backdoored model.
468
      In Fig. 4, we show the results of Neural Cleanse and show that the model remains undetected in
469
      terms of Image Editing and exposed in the case of Image Generation. We conjecture that this is due
470
      to the size of physical triggers being larger in Image Generation, making it easier to detect.
471
472   STRIP Gao et al. (2019) is a backdoor detection method that perturbs a small subset of clean images
473   and analyzes the entropy of the model’s prediction. Ultimately, clean models should have a high
474
      entropy with perturbed inputs; while conversely, backdoored models will have a low entropy. Fig. 6
      illustrates that the backdoored model can bypass the STRIP.
475
476   Fine Pruning Liu et al. (2018a) analyzes the neurons at a specific layer of a classifier model. It
477   feeds a set of clean images into the classifier model and prunes those less-active neurons, assuming
478   that those neurons are associated with backdoor. Fig. 7 reveals that our physical trigger is resis-
479   tant towards Fine Pruning, showing the efficacy of our proposed framework in crafting a physical
480
      backdoor dataset.
481   Neural Attention Distillation (NAD) Li et al. (2021) is a backdoor mitigation defense that distills
482   knowledge of a teacher model into a student model. It involves feeding clean inputs to the teacher
483   model, and distilling attention maps of the teacher into the student. We follow hyperparameters as
484   listed in BackdoorBox Li et al. (2023), except for a cosine learning rate schedule, and set epochs
485   to 20 for both teacher and student models. In Tab. 3, we show the results of NAD on both trigger
      objects. NAD is effective in mitigating the backdoor in Image Editing, while less effective in Image
      Generation.

      5.8   D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
504
505   Similarities between the synthesized and manually created datasets. The provided empirical at-
506
      tack and defense results are consistent with previous key works in physical backdoor attacks Wenger
      et al. (2021); Ma et al. (2022). Particularly, attacking with physical objects is highly effective
507
      (≈ 60% or higher), showing the potential harms of these attacks. A physical attack with diverse
508
      trigger appearances in the real world is less effective, as explained by the distributional shift phe-
509   nomenon. Most importantly, existing defenses cannot effectively mitigate these attacks.
510
511   The state of research on physical backdoors. Evidently, our experiments, along with previous
512
      findings using manually curated datasets, show that physical backdoor attacks are real and harmful.
      Despite the previously under-exploration of research on physical backdoors due to the challenges in
513
      preparing and sharing the data, this paper proposes an alternative – a step-by-step recipe for creating
514
      physical datasets within laboratory constraints. The paper also demonstrates the applicability of the
515   synthesized datasets, which has similar characteristics as their real counterparts. It is our hope that
516   this proposed framework can provide researchers with a valuable tool for studying both physical
517   backdoor attacks and defenses.
518
      Limitations. Our framework, however, has some limitations, as follows:
519
520         1. VQA’s suggestion trustworthiness: As shown in Fig. 3, some of the suggested trigger
521            objects may be illogical to appear with the main class subject. For example, the suggestions
522            for “dog”, such as ”blanket” and ”pillow,” seem odd since dogs do not naturally appear
523
               alongside these items.
524         2. Image Generation having low Real CA: As presented in Fig. 2, the Real CAs are consis-
525            tently lower than CAs, attributed to diversity in the generations, as discussed in Sarıyıldız
526            et al. (2023).
527         3. Artifacts in Image Editing and Image Generation: We observed noticeable artifacts in
528            the edited/generated images, where triggers or main subjects are missing. We conjecture
529            this phenomenon to the limitations of the deep generative models, where the generated and
530            edited images have unnatural parts that may raise human suspicion.
531   6     C ONCLUSION
532
533   This paper proposes a recipe for practitioners to create a physical backdoor attack dataset, where we
534   introduced an automated framework that includes a trigger suggestion module, a trigger selection
535
      module, and, a poison selection module. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our framework in
      crafting a surreal physical backdoor dataset that is comparable to a real physical backdoor dataset,
536
      with high Real CA and high Real ASR. This paper presents a valuable toolkit for studying physical
537
      backdoors.
