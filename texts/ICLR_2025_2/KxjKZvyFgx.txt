011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Multivariate time-series (MTS) data from multiple sensors often vary across do-
014            mains due to factors like sensor misalignment, reattachment, or individual dif-
015
               ferences, posing significant challenges for domain generalization (DG). Existing
               methods inadequately address the alignment of domain-specific spatial dependen-
016
               cies across different domains in MTS data, as they often assume a unified invariant
017
               spatial structure and overlook the distributional discrepancies arising from vary-
018            ing sensor relationships. To address this limitation, we propose ASAM (Adaptive
019            Spatial Dependency Alignment in MTS Data for Domain Generalization), a novel
020            framework that adaptively aligns spatial dependencies across domains. ASAM
021            proposes a DG layer with domain generalization loss function and two-view reg-
022            ularization loss functions to align spatial dependencies between domains adap-
023            tively. We adopt a two-phase approach to align different sets of domains effec-
024            tively. An input-aware graph generation process and a GNN-based DG layer,
025            coupled with the domain generalization loss function, adaptively align the spa-
026
               tial dependencies learned in the second phase with those from the first phase,
               ensuring a more precise alignment. We additionally incorporate a two-view reg-
027
               ularization method to effectively capture underlying spatiotemporal information
028
               comprised of spatial decorrelation loss and Gaussian kernel loss. Our theoreti-
029            cal analysis demonstrates that ASAM effectively assimilates information bottle-
030            neck, ensuring robustness across diverse distributions. Extensive evaluations of
031            the four real-world datasets show ASAM outperforms ten recent baselines. To the
032            best of our knowledge, this work is among the first to explore DG approaches for
033            MTS data by focusing on spatial dependency alignment. Our code is available at
034            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/ASAM.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      In the context of analyzing human-related behavior with multivariate time-series (MTS) data, it
      is essential to identify the complex interplay between multi-sensor structure and concurrent time-
040
      series features. There are several task-specific feature learning methods regarding inherent spatial
041
      and temporal aspects of MTS data to capture intricate spatiotemporal features of the data (Yi et al.,
042   2024; Cai et al., 2024). Specifically, recent studies adopt a Graph Neural Network (GNN) structure
043   to obtain structural information between sensors (Wang et al., 2024a;b). Yet, sensor-driven MTS
044   data often display varying signal values under different conditions—such as data collected from
045   different individuals or from the same person at different times with sensor reattachment (Farina
046   et al., 2014; He et al., 2023), resulting in multiple separate domains related to multi-sensor (spatial)
047   dependencies.
048   Domain generalization (DG) methods have recently attracted significant attention to mitigate the
049   distribution shifts. DG methods are especially practical for the healthcare field where access to the
050   target domains is rigorously restricted due to privacy concerns (Deng et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
051   2021). Applying previous feature-learning methodologies in the DG settings has certain constraints,
052   as they primarily focus on learning spatial features within the observed domains without any consid-
053   eration of domain information, as shown in Figure 1-(a). This approach reduces their effectiveness
      in addressing distribution shifts in unseen domains, such as the case of T1 in the figure. Moreover,

091   initial phase comprehensively captures overall feature information with a spatiotemporal model,
092   while the second phase focuses on learning inter-domain information by incorporating the DG layer
093   and its loss function. Specifically, we propose a loss function designed to ensure that the embedding
094   processed by the DG layer closely resembles the non-processed embedding. In addition, a two-
095   view regularization loss is incorporated to enhance the capability of the alignment by regularizing
096
      to obtain underlying spatiotemporal features from the first phase. We theoretically demonstrate that
      our DG layer assimilates invariant information from the first phase data, thus confirming that the
097
      DG layer is robust on various distributions.

099   We rigorously evaluated our model using several real-world datasets, including sEMG and human
100   activity recognition (HAR) datasets. Empirical analyses affirmed the model’s superior efficacy com-

      2.2   S PATIAL ALIGNMENT IN D OMAIN A DAPTATION SETTING
148
149
      Domain generalization (DG) and domain adaptation (DA) both intend to close the gap between the
150   source and target data distribution. The main difference between them lies in the access to target
151   data: domain adaptation leverages target data during training, whereas domain generalization relies
152   exclusively on the source data without utilizing target data. Domain adaptation allows some informa-
153   tion according to their problem setting, such as target domain data feature for unsupervised domain
154   adaptation (UDA).
155
      Even though there are some differences between UDA and domain generalization, there are some
156   studies of UDA on MTS data that considers spatial information. Most methods (Lu & Sun, 2024)
157   find the domain-invariant information similar to existing DG methods as described in Figure 1-
158   (b). However, there is a study that similarly considers the spatial correlations (Wang et al., 2023).
159   They typically adopt a fixed sensor matching approach to consider the sensor structure of MTS
160   data (Wang et al., 2023). This assumption raises concerns in cases such as a left-handed user in the
161   source domain and a right-handed user in the target domain. Our work can symmetrically align the
      sensor dependencies, addressing the potential limitations of (Wang et al., 2023).

176   3.2   OVERALL F RAMEWORK
177
178   This section presents our proposed model on multivariate data. The overall structure of our model is
179   depicted in Figure 2. As illustrated in the figure, our training process is divided into two phases. In the
180   first phase, the spatiotemporal model fθ is exclusively trained using DSph1 , where θ is the parameter
181   of the spatiotemporal model, which incorporates a spatial layer and a temporal layer. The spatial
182
      layer captures the spatial sensor relations in a single domain, and its representation is denoted as Z ∈
      RN ×T . The temporal layer then processes Z and obtains the holistic spatiotemporal representation
183
      C ∈ RF ×T , where F is the feature dimension of each temporal dimension. For the experiment, to
184
      validate the effectiveness of the proposed spatial alignment-based domain generalization, we adopt
185   a simple spatiotemporal model composed of a GNN and LSTM for the spatial and temporal layers,
186   respectively. The details of the spatiotemporal structure are provided in the Appendix. The second
187   phase integrates the domain generalization layer (DG layer) aπ before the spatiotemporal model
188   fθ , constructing domain generalization model gπ,θ , where π is the parameter for the DG layer.
189   The objective of the second phase is to adaptively align the spatial dependency of DSph2 to spatial
190   structure extracted from the first phase domain DSph1 . We process the second phase model in the
191   inference stage to align unseen domain DT into the source domains. The DG layer employs a GNN-
192   based approach with input-driven adjacency matrix generation to adaptively address diverse spatial
193
      structures of unseen domains. A domain generalization loss function is applied in the second phase to
      align the spatial dependency across domains, enabling the domain generalization layer to learn input-
194
      aware spatial alignment. We further introduce a two-view regularization loss across both phases to
195
      effectively identify the spatial alignment by regularizing the spatiotemporal model to focus more on
196   underlying features.
197
198   The following sections explain the proposed spatial alignment domain generalization method and
199
      two-view regularization. Furthermore, we will outline the final loss functions used to optimize our
      model and analyze the time complexity of ASAM. A theoretical analysis exhibits that the proposed
200
      loss functions lead to domain generalization for the MTS sensor data.

203   3.3   S PATIAL A LIGNMENT D OMAIN G ENERALIZATION
204
205   A solution for adapting to various unseen distributions with consistent results is to adaptively align
206   the unseen domain’s spatial dependency to the trained spatial dependency. Since extracting invari-
207   ant distribution with an assumption of shared spatial structure across domains may induce deficient
208   results at inference, as we discussed in Figure. 1, we propose a spatial alignment domain general-
209   ization method. We address the distribution shift by adopting an additional domain generalization
210   layer and loss function in the second phase which aligns the spatial dependency across domains.
211   A GNN-based domain generalization layer (DG layer) is proposed with input-driven adjacency ma-
212   trix generation in which spatially related information is preserved for aligning distinct distributions.
213   The input-driven graph is constructed solely with the given feature matrix designed to learn relations
214   that are salient to the input features. Consequently, the edge weight between sensors with similar fea-
215   tures is designed to be higher, while it is lower for those with dissimilar features. Therefore, the edge
      weight between sensors that is likely to be aligned is preferred to have high values and vice versa.

249

250
      and Gaussian kernel loss. By applying the loss functions to both spatial and temporal layers, the spa-
251
      tiotemporal model can capture essential information specific to each sensor by reducing redundancy
252   from complicated spatial dependency and preserving short-term temporal signals, which helps the
253   DG layer to effectively learn to align multiple spatial dependencies.
254
255

259   the channel decorrelation loss. Accordingly, the channel decorrelation loss acts as a regularizer that
260   emphasizes the information specific to each sensor, and it can be expressed as:

296
      Compared to the existing MTS spatiotemporal models, ASAM additionally proposes a DG layer and
297
      three loss functions. The time complexity of each is discussed below. The time complexity of adap-
298   tive GNN for DG-layer is O(N 2 T ), where N is the number of channels and T is the dimension of

302   ber of sensors, is inherently limited by practical constraints, thereby reducing concerns about time

      3.7   T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS ON THE D OMAIN G ENERALIZATION L AYER
334
335   We yield theoretical analysis of the newly proposed loss functions (Ldg and Lspa ). The loss functions
336   can be associated with the information bottleneck principle (Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015; Wu et al.,
337   2020). Before going into the details, we first introduce additional notations.
338
339   Notations DG is the domain generalization adopted signal. P (X) and P (X|Y ) denote the dis-
340   tribution of random variable X and the distribution of X conditioned by Y . I(X, Y ) denotes the
341   mutual information between two random variables X and Y . I(X, Y |A) implies the conditional
342   mutual information of X and Y given random variable A.
343
344   Theoretical Analysis We designate the second phase representation as Zdg . Through the assump-
345   tion of setting the distribution of P (Zdg ) and P (Zdg |X) as Gaussian, we can lead to the following
346   Theorem:
347   Theorem 1. Optimizing Ldg and Lspa corresponds to not only maximizing the mutual information
348   between the embeddings from the second phase (domain generalization) and the input X but also
349   minimizing the conditional mutual information of Zdg and DG conditioned by the input. We can
350   express like:
351                    min(Ldg + Lspa ) ⇒ max I(Zdg , X) and min I(Zdg , DG|X).                (10)
352
353
354   The first term on the right-hand side means that the embedding Zdg expresses the input data X. The
355   second term indicates that Zdg should contain more invariant information relative to the input X.
356   Satisfying the two terms indicates robustness to domain generalization because the domain general-
357
      ization layer can transform the data distribution of the second phase into the data distribution of the
      first phase. The proof of Theorem 1 is in the Appendix. We further define the information bottleneck
358
      under the MTS sensor data that suffers from the domain shift.
359
360   Definition 1 (MTS sensor-based information bottleneck). The multivariate time series (MTS)
361   sensor-based information bottleneck tries to maximize the mutual information between the input
362
      and the second phase embedding while minimizing the overlapping information between the domain
      generalization signal and the corresponding signal. It can be formulated as:
363
364                                  IB MTS = I(X, Zdg ) − βI(DG, Zdg ),                                (11)
365
366
367   where β is the hyperparameter that controls the mutual information loss. IB MTS indicates the multi-
368
      variate time series (MTS) sensor-based information bottleneck. According to Definition 1, satisfying
      the information bottleneck for sensors is observing the property that is robust to distribution shift.
369
370   Theorem 2. If β is bounded in [0, 1], then lowering the losses defined on the domain generalization
371   layer is identical to maximizing the sensor information bottleneck.
372

375   Therefore, applying the losses on the domain generalization layer extracts relevant and meaningful
376   information from different distribution data, which in turn enhances the performance of our model.

736
      We compared ASAM with 10 recent baselines. Each model is described in the following.
737
738   PICCA (Cheng et al., 2018) is a model that utilizes Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) for the
739   sEMG domain, maximizing the correlation between the training repetition data and the domain gen-
740   eralization repetition data. TCN (Tsinganos et al., 2019) applies a TCN structure, a widely used
741
      architecture for sequential data. 2SRNN (Ketykó et al., 2019) is an RNN-based model that utilizes
      domain generalization. This model applies a single linear layer to adapt the domain generalization
742
      repetition into the training repetition. It can learn universal representations by applying a two-phase
743
      design similar to ASAM. SimpAtt (Josephs et al., 2020) harnesses an attention mechanism to iden-

747   treated as a learnable parameter with a range of [0, 1]. The model is divided into a spatial layer and
748   a temporal layer, which are GNN-based and TCN-based, respectively. GNN-SD (Lee et al., 2023)
749   is the model that creates a new adjacency matrix based on the input value. This model also splits
750   the spatial layer and temporal layer. For the spatial layer, they similarly utilize the graph generation
751   phase and the GNN phase. For the temporal layer, they applied a CNN-based graph with a GLU
752
      layer that reduces the redundancy of the feature matrix.

754   2022), GREA (Liu et al., 2022), and DisC (Fan et al., 2022)) are adopted as the baselines. GSAT
755   harnesses the information bottleneck principle to detect task-relevant subgraphs. CAL discovers the
      causal pattern and mitigates the graph’s confounding effect. GREA utilizes data augmentation to
