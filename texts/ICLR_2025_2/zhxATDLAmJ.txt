009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            There exist many practical applications where regression tasks must cope with
012            a generally overseen problem: the output variable to be computed, which is of-
013            ten a decision variable, impacts the performance metric to optimize in a manner
014            that is not known a priori. This challenge translates into a loss-metric mismatch,
015            which makes standard loss functions such as Mean Square Error (MSE) not suit-
016
               able because they significantly hinder the final performance. While this problem
               is of crucial importance in, e.g., many engineering and economic applications,
017
               the literature in meta-learning of loss functions has focused on other problems,
018
               such as classification or few-shot learning tasks. In this work, we aim at closing
019            this research gap by proposing a model that can handle common situations in real
020            systems where the unknown prediction-metric relationship is time-correlated, non-
021            differentiable, or depends on multiple intertwined predictions. We present a novel
022            loss meta-learning architecture for regression, named Loss2Net, which is able
023            to (i) jointly learn the actual regressor and the loss function that it should mini-
024            mize, directly from system responses; (ii) it does so without any assumption on the
025            loss function structure; (iii) it provides a manner to learn non-differentiable and
026            multi-dimensional loss functions from entangled performance metrics. Detailed
027
               experiments for power grid and telecommunications infrastructure optimization,
               grounded on real-world measurement data, demonstrate how Loss2Net can ef-
028
               fectively learn unidentified loss functions.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Loss functions drive the training process of supervised machine learning models. In the vast majority
034   of cases, loss functions are designed generic enough to work well in a wide range of scenarios. All
035   these loss functions have in common that their objective is to minimize a symmetric error between
036   output and ground-truth. For example, in regression problems, Mean Absolute Error (MAE), MSE,
037   or Mean Squared Logarithmic Error (MSLE) are common choices for expressing the loss.
038   However, there exist practical cases where such traditional losses do not characterize well the target
039   performance metric of the regression task. This problem appears, for instance, in many dimen-
040   sioning problems encountered in systems management for business or engineering tasks. In these
041   problems, the goal is deciding on a system capacity that is sufficient to accommodate the future
042   demand: underprovisioning of capacity leads to the disruption of the offered service, while overpro-
043   visioning just causes squandering of resources; in such use cases, it is critical that the regressor or
044
      predictor learns to output a minimum quantity that is always above the demand.
045   An additional difficulty in practical situations is that the exact relationship between the variable to be
046   predicted and the actual performance metric is often not known a priori. For instance, let us consider
047   the problem of the proactive allocation of assets in a mobile network infrastructure to run a video
048   streaming service (Barakabitze et al., 2020). The mobile operator must reserve network resources to
049   maximize the user’s future Quality of Experience (QoE). Yet, how the resource allocation translates
050
      into the final per-user QoE is not known to the operator in advance: it is instead an involved relation
      that depends on specific service properties and the multi-domain network configuration (Mangla
051
      et al., 2018). Hence, the performance of the decision can be only observed a posteriori.
052
053   In such settings, using a fixed generic loss function that does not align with the task objective creates
      a loss-metric mismatch (Huang et al., 2019), while manually designing a dedicated loss function is

054
      not feasible, since the way the prediction affects the metric is unknown at the time of model design.
055   Instead, we argue that these practical applications call for models that are capable of learning the
056   correct loss from experience, and use it to properly train the regressor.
057
058
      Therefore, in this paper we investigate the problem of training a neural network (NN) model to
      perform regression under unknown, complex prediction-metric relationships, by leveraging a loss
059
      function meta-learning approach. As extensively discussed in Section 2, no solution in the existing
060
      literature tackles the practical problem above. To close this research gap, we propose Loss2Net,1 a
061   regression model that builds on a joint co-training of a main regressor network and a loss-shaper
062   network, and takes advantage of controlled noise during training to implement the exploration of
063   the correct loss function behavior for rarely observed samples. We later apply Loss2Net to repre-
064   sentative problems in real-world engineering applications. The results demonstrate how Loss2Net
065   can learn differentiable approximations of time-correlated and non-differentiable performance met-
066   rics that are not known priori to model deployment. By doing so, Loss2Net learns how to drive
067   the regressor towards estimates that are best suited to each prediction goal without human interven-
068   tion in the loss design. Overall, our study unveils the advantages of loss function meta-learning for
069
      regression, paving the way for the adoption of this paradigm in practical domains.

071   2       R ELATED WORK , C HALLENGES AND C ONTRIBUTIONS
072
073   Meta-learning, which is also referred to as learning-to-learn, allows us to automatically tune several
074   features of the learning algorithm to adapt it to the target task (Hospedales et al., 2022). Among other
075   topics, meta-learning has proven successful in (i) acting upon training data (e.g., distillation (Wang
076   et al., 2020), batching (Fan et al., 2018), or augmentation (Cubuk et al., 2019)); (ii) shaping model
077   parameters (e.g., initialization (Finn et al., 2017), optimization (Andrychowicz et al., 2016), or fine-
078   tuning (Fang et al., 2024)); and (iii) other aspects such as tuning of hyper-parameters (Micaelli &
079
      Storkey, 2021), incorporation of extrinsic features (Fang et al., 2021), handling heterogeneous sce-
      narios (Jiang et al., 2023), discovery of the actual architecture (Liu et al., 2019), providing modular
080
      compositions (Alet et al., 2019) or few-shot object detection (Demirel et al., 2023).
081
082   Our focus is on meta-learning of loss functions, which aims at learning the parameters, components,
083   or shape of the loss to be used to train the actual model. The problem can be seen as an instance of
084   a hierarchical optimization, where a meta-model is optimized under a constraint represented by the
085
      main model optimization (Franceschi et al., 2018). We stress that this is semantically different from
      meta-learning optimization schedules in iterative and alternate optimization processes (Xu et al.,
086
      2019). In the following, we present the main related works and challenges in Section 2.1, and
087
      summarize our main contributions in Section 2.2.
088
089
      2.1       C URRENT S TATE OF THE A RT
090
091   There are three main approaches to handle the loss-metric mismatch and the need for particular loss
092   functions: a) learning to parametrize a predefined loss, b) learning surrogate loss functions, and c)
093   learning to teach. We next describe these approaches.
094
095   a) Learning to parametrize a predefined loss. Most works on loss meta-learning propose dedi-
096   cated models to infer the most suitable configuration of a predefined, parametrizable loss function.
097   Some studies have investigated the use of decision networks to select among a set of predefined
098   (family of) loss functions (Denevi et al., 2018), to learn the parameters of known and differentiable
099   meta-losses (Bechtle et al., 2021), or to adapt standard loss functions (Maldonado et al., 2023). Other
100   relevant investigations have focused on multi-part loss functions, where the goal is setting (Huang
101
      et al., 2019) and possibly dynamically updating (Heydari et al., 2019) the function weights based
      on (live) performance metrics. Related to the same concept are strategies such as training a network
102
      to correct the optimization trajectory produced by a fixed loss (Huang et al., 2021), or relying on
103
      generic loss functions containing hyper-parameters to be learned during training along with the neu-
104   ral network parameters (Barron, 2019). In all these cases, the loss — independently of whether it
105   is expressed as a tunable function or a set of primitives — must be designed or selected manually,
106   which is not possible when the performance metric of interest is not known a priori.
107

      Learning surrogate loss functions. Surrogate losses are often used as a proxy for discontinuous
109   or otherwise challenging prediction-metric relationships that cannot be directly used to drive the
110   learning process. Surrogates are typically task-specific and handcrafted, which involves significant
111   manual effort for each new task — and of course requires prior knowledge about the aforementioned
112   relationship. To remove the need for time-consuming human expert intervention, recent proposals
113   have explored the possibility of meta-learning surrogates. Solutions in this space have proposed
114   to express the performance metric as a function of a set of simple surrogates (Jiang et al., 2020),
115   or to compose a loss function from primitive mathematical operators (Li et al., 2022; Raymond
116   et al., 2023). A more elaborated strategy in this direction is that of learning the loss as a convex-by-
117
      construction function within a given parametric family (e.g., MSE or other quadratic operations of
      the loss input) and identifying the exact best parameters (Shah et al., 2022).
118
119   All the proposals above still require a priori knowledge of the original relationship to identify a
120   relevant set or family of surrogates, hence do not answer to the need of learning the loss at model
121   runtime upon deployment in the target system, which is our main goal. A closer design to the one
122   we adopt for Loss2Net is a clean-slate surrogate learning based on a neural network modeling of
123
      the loss (Grabocka et al., 2019). Yet, the approach is intended for classification tasks only and is
      not adapted for regression: it explicitly makes the result invariant to the ordering of the minibatch
124
      samples, which is discrepant with time series forecasting, and it adopts a bilevel programming opti-
125
      mization of the model parameters that we later show not to perform well in regression tasks.
126
127
      Learning to teach. The concept of representing the loss function via a neural network has been in
128   fact explored beyond the context of surrogate losses, as the so-called learning to teach paradigm. In
129   an early work, the use of a teacher network was proposed to dynamically train parameters of a loss
130   function that adapts to the learning stage of the main model (Wu et al., 2018); yet, this approach
131   still relies on a generic known loss function to be parametrized by the teacher. The seminal idea of
132   a trainable task-parametrized and clean-slate loss generator was introduced for reinforcement and
133   supervised learning by the meta-critic model, where an action-value function neural network learns
134   to criticise the actions in a specified task (Sung et al., 2017). However, the meta-critic model is only
135   applied to supervised learning problems as a tool for pre-training in the few-shot learning of new
136
      tasks (e.g., by generalizing to unseen value ranges in the same domain), and is explicitly indicated
      by its authors as inappropriate for single tasks like the ones we consider.
137
138   It is also worth noting that meta-critic and its extensions (Jiang et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2021) are
139   dedicated to discrete-space classification tasks or ranking problems. The same is also true of recent
140   proposals to employ genetic programming tools to learn clean-slate loss functions (Gonzalez &
141   Miikkulainen, 2020; Morgan & Hougen, 2024). Instead, little attention has been paid until now to
142
      loss meta-learning for regression, and the main advances in this topic focus on multi-task learning
      with manually selected single-task loss functions (Raymond et al., 2023).
143

      2.2   C HALLENGES AND N OVELTY OF OUR CONTRIBUTION
146
      Overall, prior studies on loss meta-learning have focused on (i) parametrizable loss functions or
147   (ii) clean-slate losses for classification tasks. Little attention has been paid to the meta-learning of
148   clean-slate losses for regression. Part of the reason comes from the fact that loss meta-learning has
149   been considered to create an indirection that makes single-task regression less efficient (Sung et al.,
150   2017), under the assumption that losses such as MAE or MSE can already optimally drive training
151   in that case. Our study challenges this assumption and shows that there exist practical use cases
152   where loss meta-learning benefits single-task predictions. Also, all literature solutions are designed
153   for scenarios where perfect knowledge of the prediction-metric relationship is available at model
154   design time. To the best of our knowledge, ours is the first study to propose a solution for practical
155
      use cases where a priori information about such a relationship is not available.

242   As for the loss shaper, we adopt w.l.o.g. an Implicit Neural Representation (INR) architecture, which
243   is motivated by the fact that this component does not aim at directly identifying the loss as a pure
244
      mathematical expression, but rather as a geometric multi-dimensional shape based on coordinates. It
      thus makes sense to implement it with a model intended for shape reconstruction. INR frameworks
245
      have shown significant capability (Sitzmann et al., 2020) to encode the functional relationship be-
246
      tween a data sample and its coordinates (e.g., mapping a pixel position to its value) through a simple
247   Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) architecture. INR frameworks were initially applied to 3D graphics
248   and rendering (Sitzmann et al., 2019), and they have recently gained popularity for other domains,
249   such as computer vision (Saragadam et al., 2023) or time series analysis (Dupont et al., 2022), thanks
250   to their ability to perform compression, super-resolution, and handling of missing values.
251
      Formally, an INR approximates the mapping from the coordinate space T ⊂ Rn to the signal or
252
      feature space X (t) ⊂ Rm (for a single coordinate t ∈ T ) via an MLP fθ with weights θ. As an
253   example, in the image domain, the T space is the set of (a, b) pixel locations, the X space is the RGB
254   color space, and an image Xi is the mapping T → X . It can be extended to a multi-variate setting,
255   where the coordinates T correspond to the different indexes, and the output X is the multi-variate
256   feature space. Each element can be seen as a function Xi from the coordinate space {j}Jj=1 to the
257   signal space and is given by a collection of pairs di = {(j, Xi (j))}Jj=1 referring to both coordinates
258   and values. Advanced INR models use the so-called SiREN layers (Sitzmann et al., 2020). SiREN
259   layers contain a hyperparameter ω0 and are defined as hl+1 = sin(ω0 (W l hl + bl )).
260
      Some works on shape reconstruction prefer to use differentiable signed distance functions
261
      (SDFs) (Sitzmann et al., 2020) for shape reconstruction purposes. However, such approach is not
262   suitable for the problem here considered, as it would imply either to know in advance the shape of
263   the loss or to model it using purely random samples at first, which is not feasible for a real system.
264   Therefore, in this work we consider instead the direct approach of learning the shape of the loss.
265
      We make use of the Cyclic Learning Rate (CLR) method (Smith, 2017) to let the learning rate
266
      oscillate within a range whose extreme values are updated at each batch iteration. We found that
267
      this method largely reduces the sensitivity of Loss2Net to the initial configuration. Also, CLR
268   accelerates the automatic convergence, which is especially useful in our context, where the loss
269   function must be inferred during training.

285   Concerning the Loss2Net concept and training, the following five important remarks are in order.
286
287         1. The meta-learning model outlined above is able to approximate a non-differentiable and
288            non-continous objective M by a differentiable and continuous alternative fθℓ (·), which is
289            implemented by the loss shaper INR upon training. This allows optimizing the predictor
290            DNN under metrics that could not be directly used as losses, via a suitable approximation.
291         2. The input of the loss shaper INR is implemented by a different expression than that indi-
292            cated in the formal problem definition of Section 3.1. Specifically, as portrayed in Fig. 2
293            and Algorithm 1, and detailed in Section 3.3, instead of providing the computed action
294            ŷi to the loss shaper INR, we feed it with a disturbed version of ŷi , by adding a random
295            noise ϵ that is also input to the predictor DNN. This methodological novelty allows loss
296            exploration during training and helps the loss shaper to better model the target metric.
297         3. The Loss2Net model design allows co-training the predictor and loss shaper blocks during
298            a same gradient descent iteration, so that each DNN is informed of (and can learn from) the
299            improvements of the other. This fact makes the learned loss fθℓ (·) adapted to the inherent
300            regression limits of the predictor, as later expounded in Section 3.4.
301
            4. The structure in Fig. 2 allows for separated pre-training of each IR. Also, the IR-assembler
302            architecture can be replaced by a single IR when not dealing with intertwined predictions.
303
304         5. Loss2Net naturally lends itself to support transfer learning. Indeed, the loss shaper block
305
               learns to characterize the unknown loss function that defines the relationship between the
               final performance and the metric space that outputs the predictor, and it does so irrespec-
306
               tively of the used regressor. Thus, the trained loss shaper block can be employed to train
307
               any predictor that faces the same complex problem, but it can be also used as an initial state
308            for the loss shaper block in presence of different unknown functions that are expected to
309            behave in a similar way. This property is assessed in Appendix D.3.

      The goal of the random variable ϵ is to allow for further exploration of the input values, supplying
325   the loss shaper with a broader observation of the input domain beyond that provided by the training
326   samples, and improving the reliability of the characterization of the loss function over the continuous

331   of this aspect is presented in Appendix D.2 with data obtained from practical use cases.

333   3.4   C O - TRAINING OF PREDICTOR AND LOSS
334
335   Loss2Net is implemented as two cascaded DNNs, as illustrated in Fig. 2, which allows us to jointly
336
      optimize the two blocks through the same backpropagation process. The method is summarized in
      Algorithm 1, and the weights of the two DNNs are optimized during training as follows.
337
338   First, during the forward pass, the predictor fθp is fed with a set of observations of the system state
339   and outputs its decision yi . External observations vi that may impact the metric, but not directly
340   the prediction are measured and both are passed to  the loss shaper, which computes the estimated
341   performance function M̃i = fθℓ fθp (Xi , ϵ)   + ϵ, vi . At the same time, the actual performance of
342   the decision Mi = fM fθp (Xi , ϵ) + ϵ, vi is measured.
343   Then, the mismatch between estimated and true performance is evaluated via a legacy or standard
344   loss function, and backpropagated first to the loss shaper INR. Here, the loss shaper updates its
345   weights θℓ to better capture the relation between Mi and the combined values of the prediction ŷi
346   and the system state vi . Within the same iteration, the updated loss is sequentially backpropagated
347   to the predictor DNN, which allows improving the alignment of the prediction with the optimal
348   decision that minimizes Mi .
349   This design increases the efficiency of the training phase with respect to the case where each block
350   is optimized independently, e.g., by feeding the loss shaper block with random predictions and, once
351   the loss has been learned, using it to train the predictor. Indeed, co-training allows learning a loss
352   fθℓ (·) that is adapted to the intrinsically limited accuracy of the predictor; as an example, co-training
353   may lead to learning diverse shapes of the loss depending on the magnitude of the target variable if
354   the quality of the prediction is found to be affected by the absolute value of the target variable. We
355   will observe practical situations where this type of adaptation occurs in Section 4.
356   It is worth noting that such a co-training represents a major novelty of our model with respect to
357   previous related proposals (Sung et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2018; Grabocka et al., 2019). Indeed, the
358   end-to-end backpropagation training was not possible in prior models, and the two elements (the
359   learning-to-act and the learning-to-correct blocks) were trained iteratively or in a nested manner.

389   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL DATASETS
390
391   The first dataset describes the hourly energy consumption in part of the Eastern Interconnection
392   grid in the United States of America between 2002 and 2018. The data comes from a regional
393   transmission organization (RTO) (Mulla, 2018) and incorporates information from several power
394   providers, each managing a distinct geographic area. The dataset contains over 145,000 samples.
395   The second dataset describes the mobile data traffic demand generated by 12 applications, which in-
396   clude video streaming, social networking, and web browsing services. The data represents the traffic
397   demand from several millions of subscribers in a large metropolitan area during several consecutive
398   months (Marquez et al., 2017). The data was collected by the mobile network operator, resulting in
399   traffic loads (in bytes) every five minutes, for a total of more than 22,000 samples per application.
400
401   4.2   U SE C ASE I: P OWER G RID M ANAGEMENT
402
403   Metric Definition: The complex field of power grid management and the study of smart grids are
404   ruled by a significant number of diverse Key Performance Indicators (KPI) (IEEE, 2012; Personal
405
      et al., 2014). One of the fundamental dimensions that define the performance in such scenarios is
      the reliability of the network, i.e., how often the network fails to provide the required power. Inter-
406
      estingly, the reliability in power management is not only measured by the frequency of power cuts
407
      due to the under-provisioning, but also by the duration of these cuts (IEEE, 2012). The smart grid
408   manager is especially interested in preventing long-term under-estimations; that is, the performance
409   metric incorporates memory about past outages. Specifically, in the case of overdimensioning, the
410   cost scales linearly with the excess estimated power. For underestimations, if the previous prediction
411   was also underestimated, the current cost is added to the precedent cost in a recursive manner. Thus,
412   the metric depends on the previous system state, and involves discrete (thus discontinuous) jumps
413   when consecutive outages occur. Manually designing a surrogate version of the multi-dimensional
414   loss function matching the metric above is not trivial, and we are not aware of any such attempt in
415   the literature. Loss2Net removes this barrier by learning the correct loss in an automated way.
416   Network architecture: In this experiment the predictor fθp used is a multi-layer RNN regressor.
417   The INR fθℓ is defined using three inputs: yt+1 , ŷt+1 = fθp (Xt ), and the number of previous
418   successive under-provisioned samples vt (maxed at 5), which is a time-dependent variable. Details
419   on the network architecture are provided in Appendix B.
420   Comparison: Recent studies in the computer science literature have proposed an expert-designed
421   loss function, termed α-OMC (Bega et al., 2019), which can be considered a handcrafted, differ-
422   entiable surrogate of the true performance metric. We employ α-OMC as a state-of-the-art loss
423   benchmark. Although initially crafted for network-related applications, its underlying principle re-
424   mains consistent in our context: tolerating a minimal level of surplus to minimize disruptions. Yet,
425   α-OMC cannot incorporate the temporal aspect of the cost performance metric.
426   Results: We summarize the evaluation results in Table 1, where we show the average and standard
427   deviation of the operator’s normalized cost (i.e., the metric). We observe that Loss2Net signifi-
428   cantly outperforms traditional training methods based on standard loss functions, eliminating 96%
429   of the cost. While the advantage over generic loss roots in the loss-metric mismatch and is easily
430   understood, the improvement with respect to α-OMC is explained as Loss2Net effectively adapts
431   to varying demands. The model learns a loss that compensates for different accuracies in predicting
      power consumption values of diverse magnitudes. Further details are presented in the Appendix.

      4.3      U SE C ASE II: J OINT A DMISSION C ONTROL AND R ESOURCE A LLOCATION
453
454
      We consider the task of admission control and resource allocation (AC-RA), which is a problem
455
      that appears in many different fields and business cases. Consider an operator controlling a certain
456   amount of resources and a set of clients that want to get access to some of those assets paying for
457   that access. The operator must decide to whom it offers such resources and the amount of resources
458   dedicated to each client. Consequently, the problem is defined by (i) the total amount of resources to
459   be shared, (ii) the amount of resources that each client requests, (iii) the price of a resource unit, and
460   (iv) the operator’s cost from operating the system. Now, we consider the second dataset described in
461   Section 4.1 for network traffic loads, since AC-RA is routinely required in communication networks,
462   where the resources are the data transport assets. This scenario considers a more complex task that
463   deals with different predictors trying to compete to access the same resources.
464   The perspective of the analysis is that of maximizing the network operator’s profit. The operator
465   allocates customized resources in advance based on expected traffic loads from each client. The
466   operator’s revenue depends on the QoE that the users of each client experience. AC-RA problems
467   also open the door to yield management techniques such as overbooking, since in many situations
468   the clients request a certain amount of resources that later are not necessarily utilized.
469   Metric Definition: We consider that the deal between client and operator is defined by a contract
470   that sets the following rules. The contract determines the target performance, measured in terms of
471   the requested end-user QoE, specifies the price that the client must pay, which is often proportional
472   to the requested traffic load, and defines the fees and price reductions stemming from a violation
473   of the terms of conditions. For example, the final price is proportionally reduced if the QoE drops
474   below a minimum acceptable threshold. Furthermore, the operator incurs operating expenses that
475   are proportional to the quantity of reserved resources.
476   Importantly, the operator is not capable of knowing what is going to be the end-user’s QoE. First,
477   because it cannot measure it (as it is an information that is acquired by the service provider, i.e.,
478   the client); second, because the relationship between QoE and network resources is known to be
479   an obscure function that cannot be easily modeled. Besides this, even in the hypothetical case
480   in which such function was known, the AC-RA problem is an NP-hard problem, with the non-
481   trivial trade-off between over-allocating resources (such that the QoE is ensured at the expense
482
      of increased operating costs) and under-allocating them (such that the operator can overbook the
      system, accepting more clients and hence earning more money, at the risk of not reaching the QoE
483
      levels, which would lead to service outages and a huge cost in terms of customer churn).
484
485   Solution based on Loss2Net: In this scenario, where the network operator cannot analytically
      know the relation between the resource reservation and the end-users’ QoE, we can apply Loss2Net

      5   C ONCLUSIONS
531
532
      We propose Loss2Net, a meta-learning model for unknown regression losses, and unveil how this
533
      previously overlooked approach is in fact effective in practical scenarios where the relationship
534   between model predictions and the target performance is not known a priori. Experiments with real-
535   world heterogeneous datasets prove how Loss2Net can successfully learn losses that capture com-
536   plex, time-correlated and non-differentiable metrics. Our analysis also highlights how co-training
537   prediction and loss allows “learning more than the teacher knows”, since the loss is tailored to the
538   inherent and specific (in)accuracy of the predictor. This property allows us to automatically adapt
539   the loss to the performance of the predictor and provides us with high-dimension loss functions that
      better capture the corresponding problem with respect to standard uni-dimensional losses.
