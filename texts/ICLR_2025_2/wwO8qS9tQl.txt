009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               How do we measure the efficacy of language model explainability methods? While
012            many explainability methods have been developed, they are typically evaluated on
013            bespoke tasks, preventing an apples-to-apples comparison. To help fill this gap, we
014            present ALMANACS, a language model explainability benchmark. ALMANACS
015            scores explainability methods on simulatability, i.e., how well the explanations
016            improve behavior prediction on new inputs. The ALMANACS scenarios span
017            twelve safety-relevant topics such as ethical reasoning and advanced AI behaviors;
018            they have idiosyncratic premises to invoke model-specific behavior; and they have
019            a train-test distributional shift to encourage faithful explanations. By using another
020
               language model to predict behavior based on the explanations, ALMANACS is
               a fully automated benchmark. While not a replacement for human evaluations,
021
               we aim for ALMANACS to be a complementary, automated tool that allows
022
               for fast, scalable evaluation. Using ALMANACS, we evaluate counterfactual,
023            rationalization, attention, and Integrated Gradients explanations. Our results are
024            sobering: when averaged across all topics, no explanation method outperforms the
025            explanation-free control. We conclude that despite modest successes in prior work,
026            developing an explanation method that aids simulatability in ALMANACS remains
027            an open challenge.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033
      Understanding the behavior of deep neural networks is critical for their safe deployment. While
      deep neural networks are a black box by default, a wide variety of interpretability methods are being
034
      developed to explain their behavior (Räuker et al., 2023; Nauta et al., 2022). Some approaches,
035
      such as LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016) and MUSE (Lakkaraju et al., 2019), try to approximate output
036   behavior. Other approaches try to mechanistically explain the circuits inside a network (Nanda et al.,
037   2023; Wang et al., 2023). Some approaches imitate explanations in the training data (Camburu et al.,
038   2018; Narang et al., 2020; Marasović et al., 2022). Other approaches study the network’s activations,
039   such as a transformer’s attention over its input (Serrano and Smith, 2019; Wiegreffe and Pinter, 2019).
040   Others aim to create neural networks that are intrinsically explainable (Jain et al., 2020).
041
      Despite years of interpretability work, the field lacks standardized evaluation. New interpretability
042   papers generally test their methods on bespoke tasks, making it difficult to assess their true effec-
043   tiveness. To solve this issue, Doshi-Velez and Kim (2017), Nauta et al. (2022), and Räuker et al.
044   (2023) argue that we need standard interpretability benchmarks. Just as benchmarks have driven
045   progress in computer vision (Deng et al., 2009), natural language processing (Wang et al., 2019b;a),
046   and reinforcement learning (Brockman et al., 2016; Tunyasuvunakool et al., 2020), we seek to drive
047   progress in interpretability by enabling apples-to-apples comparisons across diverse methods.
048   In designing an interpretability benchmark, both “what to measure?” and “how to measure it?” are
049   tricky questions. As interpretability methods have varying goals and downstream applications, there
050   are many desirable properties for interpretability metrics to measure. These properties include faith-
051   fulness (Jacovi and Goldberg, 2020), robustness (Alvarez-Melis and Jaakkola, 2018), completeness
052   (Wang et al., 2023), plausibility (Ehsan et al., 2019), and minimality (Wang et al., 2023), among
053   others. Many of these properties are only defined conceptually, not mathematically; so even after
      desired properties are chosen, it’s a challenge to measure them precisely.

054
      Our benchmark is centered around the concept of simulatability (Hase and Bansal, 2020; Fel et al.,
055   2021). Across a diverse set of text scenarios, we measure if an explanation method improves the
056   ability to predict model behavior on held-out examples. This anchors our benchmark to a concrete
057   application of interpretability – behavior prediction – that is a necessary condition for explanations
058   to be faithful and complete. Furthermore, our benchmark measures how well explanations aid
059   performance under distributional shift. Each of our benchmark tasks is a written scenario with
060   hardcoded placeholders. By holding out some of the placeholder values exclusively for the test set,
061   we perform stress tests that see if explanations provide insight into novel scenarios.
062   Can we develop a fully automated simulatability benchmark? Human evaluation is the gold standard
063   used in prior work (Colin et al., 2023; Hase and Bansal, 2020; Marasović et al., 2022; Arora et al.,
064   2022), but human evaluations require a large cost of both time and money. While it wouldn’t replace
065   humans, a fully automated benchmark could dramatically speed up the interpretability development
066   cycle. For example, when limited only to human evaluations, it’s not even possible for algorithm
067   developers to do automated hyperparameter tuning! Moreover, automated evaluation is necessary to
068   scale mechanistic interpretability methods to large models. For example, Bills et al. (2023) produce
069
      an explanation for every neuron in GPT-2 XL. As GPT-2 XL has 1.5 billion parameters, it’s simply
      not possible for humans to evaluate every explanation.
070
071   As LLMs are proving able to substitute crowd workers (Gilardi et al., 2023; Alizadeh et al., 2023;
072   Veselovsky et al., 2023), we study their potential to replace humans as automated evaluators of
073   explanations. We do so with two sets of analyses. First, we test whether an automated predictor based
074   on GPT-4 is able to understand explanations and correctly apply them in new contexts. We verify
075
      that having access to ground-truth explanations does indeed improve the predictor’s performance in
      new scenarios. Second, we test whether the automated GPT-4 predictor is consistent with human
076
      evaluations. The overall results are broadly consistent with human evaluation, especially when
077
      accounting for statistical error bars. Nevertheless, there are some cases of disagreement, indicating
078   that ALMANACS should complement, but not replace, human evaluation.
079
080   Our results yield a striking observation: compared to the control setting with no explanations, none
081
      of the tested interpretability methods consistently improve simulatability in ALMANACS. This
      underscores the open challenge of generating explanations that aid prediction.

085   2    B ENCHMARK DESIGN
086
087
      We present ALMANACS: Anticipating Language Model Answers in Non-objective And Complex
088
      Scenarios. When creating ALMANACS, we made the following key design choices.
089
090   Simulatability. Our benchmark measures simulatability, ie, how much an explanation helps predict
091   the model’s behavior on new inputs (Hase and Bansal, 2020; Fel et al., 2021). We choose simulatability
092   because it is tractable to measure and because it is related to two desired properties: faithfulness and
093
      completeness. Faithfulness is how accurately an explanation reflects the model’s reasoning (Jacovi
      and Goldberg, 2020; Chan et al., 2022; Lyu et al., 2023), and completeness is how much of the model’s
094
      behavior is explained (Wang et al., 2023). By definition, totally faithful and complete explanations
095
      would enable accurate prediction of model behavior on new inputs. Simulatability is therefore a
096   necessary condition for faithfulness and completeness. Moreover, its general applicability and
097   casting explanation evaluation as a prediction task makes it highly tractable, allowing us to compare
098   diverse models and explanation methods with the same quantitative measure. Like any measure
099   of explanation quality, simulatability cannot capture all nuances desirable in a holistic evaluation.
100   For example, it does not reward minimality (Wang et al., 2023) and robustness (Alvarez-Melis and
101   Jaakkola, 2018). However, its tractability and necessity for other interpretability desiderata make it
102   amenable for an automated, high-throughput screening of explainability methods. This is the context
103   we envision for ALMANACS.
104   Non-objective. Consider a dataset of objective questions, such as calculus questions, and an
105   explanation method that generates expositions about calculus. Assuming that the model often gives
106   correct answers, these “explanations” could help with predicting the model’s behavior even though
107   the explanation method knows nothing about the model’s internals. To avoid this confounding effect,
      we make all questions in our benchmark non-objective. See Appendix C.2 for examples.

140   Complex behavior through Yes/No questions. We construct datasets of unusual, multi-premise
141   scenarios that elicit nonlinear model behavior by adversarially filtering against a logistic regression
142   baseline. In order to tractably compare model and simulation outputs, we restrict ALMANACS to

144   Since we find that model behavior on these Yes/No questions are already challenging to explain,
145
      ALMANACS is an appropriate goal before tackling explanations of fully open-ended outputs.
146   Distributional shift. Predicting a model’s behavior within a known distribution may be accomplished
147   by interpolating between observed values, bypassing the need to understand the model’s internal
148   reasoning. To favor methods that provide faithful explanations of the model’s reasoning, we evaluate
149   simulatability under a distributional shift between a train and test set, where good performance
150   requires extrapolation from an accurate understanding of the model.
151   Safety-relevant. As benchmarks should measure how helpful methods are at producing useful
152   insights (Räuker et al., 2023), the behaviors we evaluate are related to existing harms, as well as the
153   types of behaviors we want to regulate in advanced AI.

      2.1   F RAMEWORK FOR EXPLANATIONS
157
158
      Our simulatability pipeline, illustrated in Figure 1, has two parts: an explainer and a predictor.
159   Given a generative language model f , we collect a dataset D = {(x, y)}, where x is an ALMANACS
160   question and f (x) = y ∈ [0, 1] is the model’s probability of answering Yes. y is calculated as the
161   probability of f answers with a Yes-like token normalized by the total probability of answering with
      a Yes- or No-like token; see Appendix D for details.

      We formalize an interpretability method as an explainer function E : (f, D) 7→ e. Each e is an
181   explanation corresponding to a particular (x, y) ∈ D. Additionally, we allow each e to depend on f
182   and D. We call an explanation “local” if it just describes behavior in the region of (x, y) and “global”
183   if it describes behavior outside this region. In the most general case, the explainer E could evaluate f
184   on additional inputs and access its internal state: a trivial E might simply copy f ’s weights, enabling
185   perfect simulation but minimal model comprehension. From E, we obtain an explanation-augmented
186   dataset D̃ = {(x, y, e)}.
187
      These explanations are then read by a predictor function P : (D̃, x) 7→ ỹ, which uses the explanation-
188
      augmented dataset D̃ to simulate f on test inputs x ∈     / D (similar to Colin et al. (2023)). Crucially,
189
190
      P has no access to f , only information about f through D̃. Also importantly, P does not see
191
      explanations for the test example x, and must draw general conclusions about f ’s reasoning from D̃.
      This immunizes our simulatability evaluation against label leakage (Hase et al., 2020; Jiang et al.,
192
      2024). While our framework leaves open the nature of this predictor, we desire P to be capable,
193
      inexpensive, and effective only on human-legible explanations. While human evaluations remain the
194   simulatability gold standard, employing a human P is expensive and slow. To remove this bottleneck
195
      and enable automatic evaluation, we use GPT-4 prompted in-context with 10 examples from D̃, as
196
      detailed in Appendix J. The selected examples (x, y, e) ∈ D̃ are the 10 nearest neighbors to the
197   respective test question by the cosine similarity of text embeddings of the questions. After comparing a
198   few different embedding methods (Appendix I), the Sentence-BERT model all-mpnet-base-v2
199   was chosen to generate the text-embeddings (Reimers and Gurevych, 2019). Language models
200   have outperformed crowd workers on text annotation (Gilardi et al., 2023; Alizadeh et al., 2023) and
201   proxied human evaluators for simulatability (Hase et al., 2020). In Section 5, we validate GPT-4’s
202   capabilities as a predictor for ALMANACS.

204   2.2   T EMPLATES AND DATASET GENERATION
205
206   Our benchmark comprises Yes/No questions and answers for 12 safety-relevant topics. The topics
207   are listed in Figure 2. For each topic, 15 templates each generate 500 train and 50 test questions. A
208   template comprises a multi-sentence scenario in which 5 placeholder phrases are each selected from
209   a set of 15 possible values; an example appears in Figure 3. The use of templates allows us to study
210   model behavior over a well-defined region of the input space and intervene on high-level concepts
211   of the inputs, as in CEBaB (Abraham et al., 2022). Training questions are sampled from a limited
212
      subset of the values for each placeholder, so that test questions present both new combinations of
      seen values and entirely new values unseen in the train set, depicted in Figure 3. We analyze the
213
      effect of the distributional shift on model behavior in Appendix C.4.
214
215   We use GPT-4 to generate several hundred templates per topic. We adversarially select the 15
      templates per topic where generalization is most difficult. See Appendix C.3 for details.

      3.2   C OUNTERFACTUALS
297   Counterfactuals, alternatives close to the input that change a model’s output, have been championed as
298   effective supplementary data for interpretability (Sharma et al., 2019). Counterfactually-augmented
299   data probes the model’s decision boundary (Gardner et al., 2020), and training with such “contrast
300   sets” can boost performance and robustness to spurious cues (Kaushik et al., 2019). Counterfactual
301   explanations have aided human performance on vision tasks (Goyal et al., 2019).
302
      We generate counterfactual explanations by identifying, for each (x, y) ∈ D, the nearest neighbor
303   (x′ , y ′ ) that satisfies |y ′ − y| > δ, where δ is a threshold we set to 0.2. This ensures that the answers
304   differ sufficiently for (x′ , y ′ ) to serve as a contrastive counterfactual to (x, y). We define “near” by
305   the cosine similarity of the all-mpnet-base-v2 embeddings of the words in x and x′ . The
306   explanation corresponding to this example is then e = (x′ , y ′ ). Thanks to the templated form of our
307   questions {x}, the difference between x and x′ is conceptual and localized to a fraction of the text.
308
309   3.3   R ATIONALIZATIONS
310
311   Natural language rationalizations have enjoyed success in explainable AI (Gurrapu et al., 2023), model
312   distillation (Hsieh et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022), and in improving robustness against spurious cues
313
      (Ludan et al., 2023). Because large language models possess zero-shot reasoning capabilities (Kojima
      et al., 2022), they may be able to introspect through self-generated explanations. Wiegreffe et al.
314
      (2020) suggest that large models can indeed produce faithful free-text explanations in a joint predict-
315
      and-rationalize setting for question-answering. Indeed, Chen et al. (2023) find that rationalizations
316   can aid model simulatability. Like Wiegreffe et al. (2022) and Chen et al. (2023), we study the
317   abstractive rather than extractive setting. We generate a free-form natural language rationalization for
318   each question-answer pair (x, y) by prompting the model f with (x, y) and instructions to explain its
319   reasoning step-by-step. We save f ’s output as the explanation e.

      3.5      I NTEGRATED G RADIENTS
363
364
      We evaluate Integrated Gradients (I NTEGRATED G RADS) (Sundararajan et al., 2017), another feature
365   attribution method, using the same verbalization procedure as for ATTENTION. Integrated Gradients
366   stands out among feature attribution methods because it is axiomatically motivated. Created to satisfy
367   sensitivity and implementation invariance, Integrated Gradients is also the unique path method that is
368   symmetry preserving; see Sundararajan et al. (2017) for details. In Pruthi et al. (2021)’s distillation-
369   based evaluation of explanation methods, Integrated Gradients was one of the best-performing
370   methods.

414   5.1   C AN THE GPT-4 PREDICTOR UNDERSTAND EXPLANATIONS AND APPLY THEM IN NEW
415         SCENARIOS ?
416
417   We test if GPT-4 can predict the ALMANACS behavior of a synthetic model when we provide GPT-4
418   with hand-crafted explanations designed to contain useful information.
419
      Our experimental setup is identical to all our other ALMANACS tests, with the following twist: the
420   model f is a five-variable linear model followed by a sigmoid. The weights of the linear model
421   are drawn from the exponential distribution with λ = 1. To input an ALMANACS scenario into
422   the model, we do the following. We use the all-distilroberta-v1 (Reimers and Gurevych,
423   2019) to embed all the values of each of the 5 placeholders. For each template, we do a unique
424   principal component analysis (PCA) for each of the 5 placeholders; the PCA is over the 15 possible
425   placeholder values. We assign a real-valued score according to the leading PCA component of
426   each placeholder, and input these 5 scores to the model. Intuitively, the model has a linear decision
427   boundary over a PCA of embeddings of the placeholder values. Appendix K provides a more full
428
      description of the synthetic model.
429   We assess two explanations. The Q UALITATIVE explanation is vague and imprecise, revealing that
430   each variable slot has a different degree of influence on the final answer, the variables with the
431   highest and lowest values for each slot, and whether each variable inclines the answer to Yes or No.
      The W EIGHTS explanation divulges the weights of the linear model and the scores for all train set

433   set. An example of each explanation may be found in Appendix K.

469   40 hours of labor. The human predictors are presented with the same 10-shot examples as the LLM
470
      predictor, with instructions adapted for ease of human understanding. See Appendix L for details.
471   Figure 4(b) shows the results of the human predictor, illustrated with 95% confidence intervals per the
472   bias-corrected adjusted (“BCa”) bootstrap algorithm. For direct comparison, Figure 4(c) reproduces
473   the same subset of tasks from our main results with the LLM predictor (Table 1) along with their
474   BCa errors. Accounting for the uncertainty implied by the confidence intervals, the aggregated “all”
475   results for both the human and LLM predictor show that no explanation method has non-overlapping
476
      error bars relative to the no-explanation control. The consistency of these overall results is evidence
      in favor of the utility of ALMANACS.
477
478   Nevertheless, on particular subtasks, there are a few cases where explanations help humans more
479   than the LLM predictor. For example, rationalization and salience explanations help humans in the
480   hiring decisions task, whereas the LLM predictor is able to achieve the same level of performance
481   with and without explanations. Thus, we caution against overinterpretation of ALMANACS results.
482
      ALMANACS is intended to supplement, but not to replace, human predictors.
483   Interestingly, the LLM outperforms humans at predicting behavior on ALMANACS, for almost all
484   tasks and explanations evaluated here. We hypothesize this is due to the in-context learning ability
485   of language models. It suggests that, relative to human evaluation, (lack of) an effect observed in
      ALMANACS is more likely to be a false negative than a false positive.

      6   R ELATED WORK
487
488
      Despite numerous metrics proposed to evaluate the quality of explanations, there is not an established
489
      consensus on the best measures (Chen et al., 2022b; Jacovi and Goldberg, 2020). This stems from the
490   diversity of explanation forms (Lyu et al., 2023) and use cases (Räuker et al., 2023; Lertvittayakumjorn
491   and Toni, 2021; Schemmer et al., 2022; Begley et al., 2020). This also results from the difficulty of
492   formalizing the concept of “human understandability” (Zhou et al., 2022). Faithfulness, how well an
493   explanation reflects a model’s reasoning process, is a critical dimension of explanation quality (Jacovi
494   and Goldberg, 2020; Lyu et al., 2023). Faithfulness evaluation is difficult because the ground truth of
495   neural model reasoning is non-transparent. Past work develops metrics to quantify the faithfulness
496   of saliency map explanations (Chan et al., 2022; Yin et al., 2021) and establishes saliency map
497   benchmarks (Agarwal et al., 2022; Hooker et al., 2019). Plausibility is a qualitative evaluation of
498
      how good explanations seem to humans (Jacovi and Goldberg, 2020). Plausibility benchmarks often
      measure similarity to human explanations (Wiegreffe and Marasović, 2021; Gurrapu et al., 2023),
499
      disregarding the key property of faithfulness. Simulatability studies of explanations can be used
500
      to distinguish explanations that aid human understanding (Chen et al., 2023; Feldhus et al., 2022)
501   from those that don’t (Alqaraawi et al., 2020; Hase and Bansal, 2020; Arora et al., 2022; Colin et al.,
502   2023). Simulatability has been used to evaluate explanations of a variety of forms, including saliency
503   maps (Alqaraawi et al., 2020; Jacovi and Goldberg, 2020), verbalized saliency maps (Feldhus et al.,
504   2022), counterfactuals (Alipour et al., 2021), contrastive explanations (Yin and Neubig, 2022), and
505   natural language explanations (Chen et al., 2023). In contrast to our work’s nonlinear model behavior,
506   the existing simulatability benchmark CEBaB (Abraham et al., 2022) probes relatively simple causal
507   relationships between conceptual factors of the model’s input/output.
508   Automating Simulatability Evaluation: Given that running simulatability studies with humans in
509   the loop is more costly and complex, a few works have attempted to use machine learning models
510   in place of humans by training a predictor (Pruthi et al., 2021; Hase and Bansal, 2021; Chen et al.,
511   2022a; Martin et al., 2023; Teufel et al., 2023) or prompting language models (Chen et al., 2023).
512
      Other Interpretability Benchmarks: Schwettmann et al. (2023) introduces a benchmark for describ-
513   ing submodules in neural networks. Casper et al. (2023) introduces an interpretability benchmark for
514   image classification models using Trojan detection as a task framework.
515
516
517   7   C ONCLUSION
518
519   Motivated by the lack of tools for the systematic evaluation of interpretability methods, we intro-
520   duce ALMANACS. ALMANACS is a fully automated benchmark that measures simulatability, a
521   necessary condition for faithful and complete explanations. Using ALMANACS, we evaluate the
522
      ability of four explanation methods (C OUNTERFACTUAL, R ATIONALIZATION, ATTENTION, and
      I NTEGRATED G RADIENTS) to help simulate two language models (flan-alpaca-gpt4-xl and
523
      vicuna-7b-v1.3). Our results show that, when averaged across all topics, none of the explanation
524
      methods improve performance over the no-explanation control. Developing an explanation method
525   that aids simulatability in ALMANACS remains an open challenge.
526
527   Limitations ALMANACS is meant to speed up the interpretability algorithm development cycle
528
      with fully automated evaluations; it is not a perfect substitute for human evaluations, which remain
      the gold standard.
529
530   Broader impacts We intend for ALMANACS to be a useful tool for interpretability researchers.
531   We believe this work entails largely positive social consequences, as better understanding black-box
532   models promotes their safe deployment. We are not aware of negative societal impacts of our work.

       J.3   E XPLANATION TEMPLATES
1729
1730   In this section, we detail how each of the counterfactual, rationalization, and feature attribution
1731   (attention and integrated gradients) explanations are served to the predictor. These verbalized
1732   explanations are then placed where Explanation: {LOCAL EXPLANATION} appears in the
1733   prompt template (Figure 16).
1734
1735   Counterfactual. As described in Section 3.2, given an example (x, y), this method pro-
1736   duces the nearest counterfactual example e = (x′ , y ′ ) as the explanation artefact. In AL-
1737
       MANACS, x is a question and y is the probability of ‘Yes’. This explanation is thus verbalised
       as Explanation: If the question had been the following, the answer
1738
       would have been y: x. See Figure 12 for an example.
1739
1740
       Rationalization. As described in Section 3.3, this method outputs a free-form natural language
1741
       rationale e. This explanation is therefore readily presented as Explanation: e. See Figure 13 for
1742   an example.
1743
1744   Attention and Integrated Gradients. As described in Sections 3.4 and 3.5, these feature at-
1745   tribution methods assign a numerical importance score to each token in the input. We take the
1746   25 most salient tokens by absolute value, excluding special and whitespace tokens, and con-
1747   catenate them into a space-separated list e. These are presented as the explanation, along with
1748   a brief instruction: Explanation: Pay attention to the following parts of
1749   the sentence: e. See Figure 11 for an example.
1750   An advantage of ALMANACS’s simulatability framework is the ability to accommodate and compare
1751   diverse language model explanations, provided that they are verbalized for consumption by the
1752   predictor. Since verbalizations and/or the surrounding instruction text are also relevant aspects of
1753   serving explanation artefacts to humans, we consider these post-processing choices part of the design
1754   of an explanation method. ALMANACS does not dictate those choices. Candidate explanation
1755   methods are free to specify this pipeline, according to what is most suitable for the explanation
1756   method.

       L    H UMAN PREDICTOR DETAILS
2161
2162
       The setup for the human predictor mimics the setup for the LLM predictor as closely as possible,
2163
       with only minor modifications to make the layout human-intuitive. Each prompt presented to the
2164   human predictor contains the same ALMANACS question and the same 10-shot nearest neighbor
2165   examples as presented to the LLM predictor. All human responses were collected via the LabelBox
2166   platform. According to LabelBox, the human labellers are college-educated and English-speaking.
2167   They are recruited according to LabelBox’s internal policies. For our study, we additionally verify
2168   that they understand the task as described in our instructions (see “Qualifying questions” below). We
2169   do not provide labelers further training, just as we do not “train” the GPT-4 predictor to interpret
2170   explanations, because explanations should be understandable by non-experts. Compensation to the
2171   labelers is also handled by LabelBox, and is not divulged to researchers using the interface. The cost
2172
       to researchers is $320 per working week per labeller.
2173
2174   Instructions. We created an instruction document for the human predictors. Our instructions
2175
       contextualized LLMs as non-deterministic systems that respond to natural language, described the
       prediction task, and provided screenshot walkthroughs of the prediction interface. We emphasized
2176
       that the human annotators should not give their own answer to the question, but rather predict the
2177
       LLM’s answer.
2178
2179
       Response format. Human labellers answered through a free-response text box, which most closely
2180
       resembles the free-form generation we collect as responses from an LLM predictor. The text box
2181   prefaced with the question: What is the probability that a language model
2182   answers ’Yes’ to this question? Write your answer as a decimal
2183   between and 1. Do NOT provide any other information.
2184
2185   Qualifying questions. To check that each labeller understands the task, we ensured that they were
2186   able to answer three qualifying questions. Each qualifying question is an ALMANACS question
2187   appearing in the same 10-shot format as they appear to the human labellers in the prediction interface.
2188   The 10-shot examples are sourced from the same ALMANACS task. However, we overwrite
2189   the corresponding answer and explanation for each example so that the overall question becomes
2190   extremely simple to answer. This serves as a basic check that the human labellers can predict
2191
       ALMANACS-style questions well, when the explanations are indeed helpful. One of our qualifying
       questions appears below.
2192
