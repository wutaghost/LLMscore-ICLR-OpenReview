                                                     A BSTRACT
009
010            Entropy regularisation has proven effective in reinforcement learning (RL) for
011            encouraging exploration. Recent work demonstrating the equivalence between
012            entropy regularised RL and approximate probabilistic inference suggests the po-
013            tential for improving existing methods by generalising the inference procedure.
014            We develop the Rényi regularised RL framework by using Rényi variational in-
015            ference to learn a stochastic policy. We present theoretical results for policy eval-
016            uation and improvement within this new framework. Additionally, we propose
017
               two novel algorithms, α-SAC and α-SQL, for large-scale RL tasks. We show that
               these algorithms attain higher returns on games from the Atari suite relative to an
018
               entropy-regularised benchmark, SAC-Discrete.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Success in deep reinforcement learning requires that an algorithm continually refine its behavioural
026   policy for interacting with the environment. Such refinement often involves collapsing down the
027   space of viable actions, as the relative values of different actions becomes clearer. However, an
028   algorithm which interacts with the environment according to a fully deterministic policy will even-
029   tually fail to learn any further, since it will not gather additional data about alternative actions which
030
      may be superior to those currently being pursued. This is the basic problem of exploration in re-
      inforcement learning. Recently, a suite of algorithms (Haarnoja et al., 2017; 2019; Christodoulou,
031
      2019) have attempted to address this exploration problem using entropy regularisation (or more
032
      generally, KL-regularisation) which penalises policies for having very low entropy. This encourages
033   policies to take a variety of actions, thus exploring new potential strategies in the environment.
034
035   Theoretical work has shown that the entropy regularised RL objective is equivalent to a approximate
036
      probabilistic inference problem (Levine, 2018). This insight allows us to move freely between a
      regularisation view of the entropy regularised RL problem and a corresponding inference view (See
037
      Fig. 1). Previous research generalising entropy regularised RL has focused on generalising the reg-
038
      ularisation view (Yang et al., 2019). In this paper, we take a fundamentally different approach, by
039   instead starting from the inference view and considering generalisations of the approximate infer-
040   ence procedure. Specifically, we will learn a policy via α-Rényi variational inference (Li & Turner,
041   2016). Doing so gives rise to a novel RL objective. We prove theoretical results for this objective,
042   and then leverage these results in the design of two novel deep RL algorithms for discrete, determin-
043   istic environments: α-Soft Actor-Critic (α-SAC) and α-Soft Q-Learning (α-SQL)1 . We compare
044   these with their entropy-regularised counterpart, SAC-Discrete (Christodoulou, 2019), and show
045   that they are able to achieve a higher return on a range of Atari environments.
046   Fig. 1 shows the structure of the first half of the paper. In Sec. 2, we recapitulate existing results
047   elaborating on the connection between KL-regularised RL and probablistic inference. In Sec. 3 we
048   generalise to the Rényi regularised setting, and state core theoretical results for this setting. In Sec. 4
049   we use these results to design deep RL algorithms for the non-tabular setting. Finally, we compare
050   these algorithms against an existing baseline in Sec. 5.

085   2.1   T HE KL- REGULARISED SETTING
086
      The entropy-regularised reinforcement learning problem has been the subject of much attention in
087
      recent years, yielding novel state-of-the-art algorithms such as Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) (Haarnoja
088   et al., 2019) and Soft Q-Learning (SQL) (Haarnoja et al., 2017). In short, the entropy-regularised RL
089   problem modifies the standard RL objective by administering additional rewards to the agent for tak-
090   ing actions that have a low likelihood under its current policy. Below we focus on the more general
091   KL-regularised reinforcement learning problem, in which penalties are administered to the agent for
092   pursuing a policy which deviates from a base policy πb (a|s); the entropy-regularised problem can
093   be obtained from the KL-regularised one by setting the base policy πb (a|s) to be the (unnormalised)
094   uniform distribution, πb (a|s) = 1.
095   We consider an episodic Markov Decision Process (MDP) described by an environment with a col-
096   lection of states S. The agent begins in state s0 ∼ p0 (s0 ), and samples an action a0 from its policy,
097   a0 ∼ π(a|s0 ). Following this action, the agent receives a reward r1 = r(s0 , a0 ), and transitions
098   into a new state s1 according to the dynamics p(s1 |s0 , a0 ). This process then repeats, yielding a
099   sequence of states, actions, and rewards s0 , a0 , r1 , s1 , a1 , r2 , . . . . This sequence terminates at time
100   T when the agent transitions into a terminal state sT . The sequence of states, actions, and rewards
101   from the initial state to the terminate state are referred to as a trajectory, τ = (s0 , a0 , . . . , sT ). The
                                                  PT
102   return of a trajectory is given by G(τ ) = t=1 rt . We refer the reader to Sutton & Barto (2020) for
103   a more comprehensive introduction to MDPs.

129   2.2   KL- REGULARISED RL AS STRUCTURED VARIATIONAL INFERENCE
130
131
      Variational Inference (Ganguly et al., 2023) is a popular method in ML for learning approximations
      to the posterior of a distribution conditional on observed data. We consider a pair of variables x and
132
      z, described by joint distribution p(x, z). Conditional on an observation of x, we wish to learn an
133
      approximation to the posterior p(z|x). In variational inference, we attempt to learn an approximate
134   posterior q(z) ≈ p(z|x) by maximising the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBo), given by:
135
136                                                            p(x, z)
                                          L(q) := Ez∼q log                                                (4)
137                                                              q(z)
138
      The ELBo can be re-expressed in the following form:
139
140                                  L(q) = log p(x) − DKL (q(z)||p(z|x))                                  (5)
141
      Since the KL-divergence term is non-negative, and is equal to zero precisely when q(z) = p(z|x),
142   we see that the ELBo L is maximised precisely when q(z) = p(x, z). Accordingly, the optimisation
143   problem of maximising the ELBo corresponds to the inference problem of computing the posterior
144   for z, conditional on x.
145
      The ELBo can also be written in an equivalent form – used, e.g., in the Variational Auto-Encoder
146
      (Kingma & Welling, 2019) – is given by:
147
148                              L(q) = Ez∼q [log p(x|z)] − DKL (q(z)||p(z)) .                             (6)
149
      In this form, the first term acts as an objective which encourages the approximate posterior q(z) to
150
      sample latent variables z under which the data x has a high likelihood, while the second term can
151
      be seen as a regularisation which encourages the approximate posterior q(z) to stay close to the true
152   latent variable prior p(z). It is this form we will focus on in later derivations.
153

198   α-Rényi divergences (van Erven & Harremoës, 2014) form a one-parameter family of discrepancy
199   measures between pairs of probability distributions. For α ̸= −∞, 1, +∞, the α-Rényi divergence
200   from density q to density p is given by:
201                                                           "        α−1 #
202
                                                  1              q(z)
                                   Dα (q||p) :=        log Eq                                     (11)
203                                             α−1              p(z)
204   This definition is extended by continuity to −∞, 1, +∞. In particular, the 1-Rényi divergence is
205   exactly the KL-divergence, Eq. (2).
206
207
      Before continuing, we briefly note some properties of the α-Rényi divergences. Firstly, the α-
      Rényi divergence is non-negative for α > 0 and non-positive for α < 0. For this reason we will
208
      restrict our attention for the rest of this paper to the case α > 0. Secondly, the α-Rényi divergence is
209
      continuous and non-decreasing as a function of α; hence larger α-values lead to greater penalisation.
210   Lastly, for all α ≥ 1, Dα (q||p) is zero-forcing in q, meaning that, if Dα (q||p) < ∞, q = 0 whenever
211   p = 0. However, for 0 < α < 1, Dα (q||p) is not zero-forcing in q. We refer the reader to (van Erven
212   & Harremoës, 2014) for a more in-depth discussion of Rényi divergences and their properties.
213
      Rényi divergence variational inference (Li & Turner, 2016) generalises classical variational infer-
214
      ence by replacing the KL-divergence appearing in the ELBo, Eq. (5), with the α-Rényi divergence:

326   We devise two novel algorithms for the discrete action-space setting, α-Soft Actor-Critic (α-SAC)
327   and α-Soft Q-Learning (α-SQL). α-SAC and α-SQL both make use a collection of action-value
328   functions Q(s, a; ϕk ), k = 1, . . . , K, which take in states and output values for each action. We
329   will take the minimum over these when computing action-values, to compensate for value over-
330   optimism (Fujimoto et al., 2018). Additionally, α-SAC makes use of a parametric policy network
331   π(a|s; θ), which takes in states and outputs probabilities over actions. We will also use delayed
332   action value-functions, Q(s, a; ϕ−                            −
                                             k ), whose parameters ϕk are synchronised with ϕk after a fixed
333   number of update steps. Both α-SAC and α-SQL are off-policy, and make use of a finite capacity,
334   first-in-last-out (FILO) memory replay buffer into which (state, action, reward, next state, done)
335
      transitions, (s, a, r, s′ , d) are loaded and then resampled.
336   Full pseudocode for both algorithms is found in App. B.

399   5     R ESULTS
400
401   We test α-SAC and α-SQL on four Atari environments in the Gymnasium package (Mnih et al.,
402   2015; Brockman et al., 2016; Towers et al., 2024) - Qbert, Ms Pacman, Assault, and Space In-
403   vaders . For all of these we use the version 5 environment. As a baseline, we compare to our
404   re-implementation of SAC-Discrete (Christodoulou, 2019). We examine the behaviour of α-SAC
405   and α-SQL for two values of α below and above 1 (which corresponds to the KL-regularised case):
406   α = 0.95 and α = 1.05, respectively. To allow a faithful comparison, the only hyperparameters
407   we tune are α and the target divergence D̄, leaving all other hyperparameters identical to those used
408
      by SAC-Discrete (Christodoulou, 2019). Note that the target divergence D̄ is not tuned individu-
      ally for each environment, but rather set to be a multiple of the log(|A|) where, |A| is the number
409
      of actions in the environment. The network architecture, training hyperparameters, and additional
410
      pre-processing details can be found in App. C. For each environment we average results over 10
411   random seeds. We train for a total of 500, 000 environment steps. Every 4000 environment steps,
412   we evaluate the policy by averaging the empirical return over 5 episodes.
413
414
      In Fig. 2 we compare the performance of our algorithms α-SAC and α-SQL to SAC-D across four
      environments. We see that our methods are able to learn in all four environments. The biggest
415
      different in performance is between the α-SQL methods and the SAC methods. We see that α-SQL
416
      consistently achieves higher returns than SAC in all four environments. We additionally note that
417   α-SQL has a lower compute cost compared to the SAC algorithms, since it uses only action-value
418   networks, and not a policy network. Among the SAC methods, we see that α-SAC is competitive
419   with SAC-D in both Qbert and Space Invaders, and is able to outperform SAC-D for both Ms Pacman
420   and Assault. Moreover, in those environments, we see that α = 1.05 tends to outperform α = 0.95.
421   The value of α appears less importance for α-SQL; this is likely because in α-SQL, α affects only
422   temperature adjustment, but not regression target formation.

424   6     D ISCUSSION
425
426   Our work builds upon and extends important foundational work in RL which relates regularised RL
427   to approximate probabilistic inference. Our main goal in this paper has been to theoretically illus-
428   trate the potential of this framework for developing novel RL algorithms. Indeed, unlike previous
429   work, we take probabilistic inference as the starting point for new development, rather than reward
430   regularisation. We hope that future work can extend this core idea by using other variational approx-
431   imate inference methods, such as importance weighted variational inference (Burda et al., 2016) and
      f-divergence variational inference (Wan et al., 2020), in the RL setting.

468   For our practical algorithms, we have considered only regularisation towards a (potentially un-
469   normalised) uniform base policy. However, many applications of KL-regularisation use a non-
470   uniform base policy, for example, the behavioural policy (or an approximation to it) in offline RL
471   (Figueiredo Prudencio et al., 2024), or a pre-trained policy in Reinforcement Learning from Hu-
472   man Feedback applied to language models (Zheng et al., 2023). These situations provide additional
473   possible uses for Rényi regularised RL. Note that, following the discussion in Sec. 2.3, varying α
474   varies the extent to which we penalise our new policy for generating trajectories that have low (or
475   zero) probability under the old policy. Thus, by varying α independently from the target divergence
476
      value, we can control not only the strength of regularisation but also its form, and in particular how
      severely it penalises trajectories which are out-of-distribution with respect to the base policy.
477
478   Unlike in the original formulation of α-Rényi variational inference, we have only formulated the
479   α-SAC and α-SQL algorithms for the fixed α values. We hope that future work may extend our
480   formalism to include automatic adjustments of α according to some other criterion.
481   In this paper, we have only investigated Rényi regularised algorithms for the discrete action setting,
482   rather than for continuous action spaces. In the continuous setting, the regression targets used for
483   learning action-values (Eq. (29) and Eq. (30)) must make use of Monte-Carlo approximations for
484   the expectations over actions. Initial experiments revealed that, although the algorithm was able to
485   learn, the number of samples necessary for generating faithful approximations made these method
      prohibitively costly, and so we decided to focus on the discrete setting. It remains to be seen if other


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      variance reduction techniques could be used to mitigate this problem and make this method viable
487   for the continuous action setting.
488
489
      C ONCLUSION
490
491   We have extended the “RL as probabilistic inference” framework by considering an alternative to the
492   approximate inference objective which uses α-Rényi variational inference. This lead us to formulate
493   the α-Rényi RL objective. This objective generates its own set of Bellman recursion relationships
494   and backup operators, for which we provided theoretical results for both policy evaluation and im-
495   provement. We then leveraged these results in the formulation of two new algorithms, α-SAC and α-
496   SQL. We gave concrete implementations of these methods in the case of discrete action-spaces, and
497
      demonstrated that they perform favourably against their KL-regularised counterpart, SAC-Discrete.
