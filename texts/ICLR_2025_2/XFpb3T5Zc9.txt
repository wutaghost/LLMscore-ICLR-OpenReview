009                                                  Abstract
010
011              Federated learning usually requires specifying the amount of local computation
012              needed a priori. In this work, we instead propose a systematic scheme to auto-
013              matically adjust and potentially reduce the local computations while preserving
014              convergence guarantees. We focus on proximal-based methods, where we demon-
015              strate that the proximal operator can be evaluated inexactly up to a relative error,
016              rather than relying on a predefined sequence of vanishing errors. Our proposed
017
                 method, iFedDR, is based on a novel error-corrected version of inexact Douglas-
                 Rachford splitting. It mitigates the need for hyperparameter tuning the number
018
                 of client steps, by triggering refinement on-demand. We derive iFedDR as an in-
019
                 stance of a much more general construction, which allows us to handle minimax
020              problem, and which is interesting in its own right. Several numerical experiments
021              are carried out demonstrating the favorable convergence properties of iFedDR.

024   1     Introduction
025
026   Federated learning (FL) has emerged as a promising approach to distributed machine learning that
027   allows multiple clients to collaboratively train a model. In an attempt to keep communication costs
028   low, the clients repeatedly apply their update rule ŌĄ times before centrally aggregating the model and
029   proceeding to the next round.
030
      A popular instance of this general idea is FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017) which locally takes ŌĄ
031   gradient descent steps. Unfortunately, FedAvg requires a very small client stepsize in data heteroge-
032   neous settings to avoid the so called client-drift. This requirement appears theoretically as a horizon
033   dependent O(1/ŌĄ) client stepsize, which intuitively is used to prevent the ŌĄ steps to move beyond a
034   single (large) step of gradient descent (see e.g. Karimireddy et al. (2020, Thm. I)). Because of the
035   small stepsize the benefit of multiple local updates is unclear under data heterogeneous.
036
      A more principled approach is arguably to instead use the local computation to approximate a well-
037   understood update rule such as the proximal operator, which is known to be more stable and usually
038   allows for larger stepsizes. FedProx (Li et al., 2020) was the first to use proximal operators for FL
039   and did so by directly averaging the client updates. This straightforward application of the prox turns
040   out to not necessarily converge to a solution when using fixed stepsize (Pathak & Wainwright, 2020,
041   Prop. 2) and proper convergence relies on behaving like FedAvg with ŌĄ = 1 by letting the proximal
042   stepsize ő≥ go to zero (Malekmohammadi et al., 2022, Thm. 1). In other words, it might be argued
043   that one should simply use FedAvg with ŌĄ = 1 instead.
044   Fortunately there is a ‚Äúcorrect‚ÄĚ way to employ the proximal operator such that we can benefit from
045   its strong properties mentioned previously. By leveraging the operator splitting literature, FedSplit
046   (Pathak & Wainwright, 2020), FedDR (Tran Dinh et al., 2021) and FedPi (Malekmohammadi et al.,
047   2022) fixes the update rule of FedProx. However, all of these prox-based method still need the ap-
048   proximation errors of the proximal operator to be

      We answer this in the affirmative by making the following contributions:
072
073                      ‚ÄĘ We introduce the error-corrected algorithm, iFedDR, which automatically checks if refin-
074                        ing the approximate evaluation of the proximal mapping is necessary through a computa-
075                        tionally negligible error condition. This automatic adjustment mitigates the need for pre-
076                        determining an appropriate number of client iterations, while allowing for large stepsizes
077                        (see Figure 1). The construction is made possible by introducing a relative error condition,
078                        which enables the error condition at each communication round to be treated separately.
079                      ‚ÄĘ Our analysis for iFedDR extends to monotone inclusion problems and consequently enjoys
080                        provable guarantees not only for (constrained) convex minimization but also for the im-
081                        portant case of (constrained) minimax problems, and more generally m-player games. We
082                        obtain iFedDR through a novel inexact preconditioned proximal point algorithm (iPPPA)
083                        involving a semidefinite preconditioner and a correction step mitigating the effect of the
084                        inexactness, which might be of independent interest.
085                      ‚ÄĘ We demonstrate the favorable properties of iFedDR under heterogeneous data distributions.
086                        A simple rule is proposed for minimizing the number of refinement calls. We observe that
087                        iFedDR either match or improve the performance of the baselines without needing tuning.

517   7   Conclusion
518
519   We have developed a variant of FedDR, which automatically adjust the number of client iterations
520   and consequently mitigate the need for hyperparameter selection. The scheme enjoys guarantees
521   even for minimax problems (and more generally m-player games) and permits large proximal step-
522
      sizes. Convergence is proven for a much more general method, namely an error-corrected proximal
      point algorithm with a (general) semidefinite preconditioner, which might be interesting in its own
523
      right. We demonstrate the favorable properties of the algorithm on a range of numerical experiments.
524
      For future work it is interesting to extend the idea to partial participation and nonconvex problems.

      A     Related works
757
758
      Federated learning (FL) has seen considerable attention since it was first coined in (McMahan et al.,
759   2017). To treat heterogeneous data, (Li et al., 2020) proposed the prox-based method FedProx.
760   Prox-based FL methods have since been developed by building on operator splitting techniques as in
761   FedSplit (Pathak & Wainwright, 2020) and FedDR/FedPi (Tran Dinh et al., 2021; Malekmohammadi
762   et al., 2022). See Pathak & Wainwright (2020) for a detailed discussion on the convergence-failure
763   of pre-existing methods and Ryu & Yin (2022); Malekmohammadi et al. (2022) for an overview of
764   splitting algorithms. The FL setting has since its conception been extended to fixed point problems
765   (Malinovskiy et al., 2020) and minimax problem (Augenstein et al., 2019; Sharma et al., 2022;
766   Ramezani-Kebrya et al., 2023).
767   The idea of using a hyperplane projection as a correction step was first introduced in Solodov &
768   Svaiter (1999) for the proximal point method. It was later extended in Giselsson (2021) to treat
769   a large family of operator splitting approaches in a unified fashion and have been used for proving
770   convergence in nonmonotone problems (Pethick et al., 2023). A hyperplane projection argument was
771   also used in the adaptive stepsize for finding the intersection of convex sets analyzed in Combettes
772   (1997), which was recently applied to the FL setting in Jhunjhunwala et al. (2023).
773
