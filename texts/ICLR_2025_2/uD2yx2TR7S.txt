009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In-context learning enables language models (LM) to adapt to downstream data
012            or tasks by incorporating few samples as demonstrations within the prompts. It
013            offers strong performance without the expense of fine-tuning. However, due to the
014            context-length restriction, the demonstrations occupy only a small proportion of
015            usable samples. The limitation exacerbates the difficulty of optimization, since
016
               the performance of in-context learning can be unstable depending on the quality,
               format, or order of demonstrations. Prior work, such as Knn Prompting, index
017
               samples based on the similarities of logits at the output-side, in addition to the
018
               regular retrieval operation at the input-side. They improve in-context learning by
019            leveraging the core ability of next-token prediction, rather than relying solely on
020            the emergent capacity to make analogies. Despite this, the hard-to-optimize issue
021            of in-context learning still exists. In our view, it stems from the process of selecting
022            demonstrations. To address this, we propose complementing in-context learning
023            with an additional clustering operation, making full use of all usable samples.
024            We propose a novel approach “vocabulary-defined semantics”. Grounded in LM
025            vocabulary, which is the label space of model outputs, the proposed approach
026            computes semantically equivalent latent representations for output labels. Then,
027
               taking the representations as centroids, a clustering operation is performed to
               align the semantic properties between the language model and the downstream
028
               data/tasks. Based on extensive experiments across diverse textual understanding
029
               datasets and multiple models, our approach outperforms the state-of-the-art in terms
030            of effectiveness and efficiency. On average, it achieves 3% − 49% improvements
031            via the clustering module, while requiring only half of the computation time via
032            the similarity-based logits computation.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Language models (LMs) are drawing significant attention due both to their potential opportunities and
038   associated risks (Bommasani et al., 2021). Despite demonstrating impressive performance, in some
039
      scenarios, it may not be convenient to finetune them to downstream data/tasks, such as Language
      Model-as-a-Service (LMaaS) (Sun et al., 2022). As a solution, in-context learning (Dong et al., 2022)
040
      serves as an effective approach to utilizing language models for downstream tasks. Unlike model
041
      fine-tuning, in-context learning adapts to downstream data or tasks by incorporating demonstrations
042   into the prompts. Using the emerging ability to draw analogies from demonstrations (Wei et al.,
043   2022), it offers strong performance without the expense associated with model fine-tuning.
044
045
      However, in-context learning is not a stable technique and may be affected by multiple factors, which
      exacerbates the difficulty of further optimization. For example, the performance may be affected
046
      by the quality of demonstrations (Rubin et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023), their format (Yang et al.,
047
      2023a;b), and even their order (Liu et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024). The state-of-the-art (SOTA) KNN
048   prompting (Xu et al., 2023) takes a significant step forward. Rather than solely depending on the
049   emergent ability of language models to make analogies, the solution also utilizes the core capability
050   of next-token prediction to enhance in-context learning. In addition to the retrieval operation of
051   selecting demonstrations at the input-side, it introduces an additional indexing operation to support
052   KNN decision at the output-side. The limitations are, while using the indexed data as the reference in
053   KNN decision, it cannot cooperate with the normal LM inference. Also, it cannot handle the case
      where the samples corresponding to a specific output label are unavailable.

054
      Nevertheless, the critical issue of in-context learning still exists. In our view, it is caused by the
055   drawbacks of selecting demonstrations (also applies to the case of composing demonstrations).
056   First, in-context learning retrieves only a subset from all usable demonstrations, so the retrieval
057   operation tends to be a suboptimal option, since it is unclear which samples would be the best choices.
058   Second, assume the best samples to retrieve can be known beforehand, the retrieval operation is still
059   suboptimal, since the best samples may not included in the retrievable samples. To solve the issue of
060   difficult optimization, we complement in-context learning with an additional clustering operation,
061   making full use of all usable samples. Similar to SOTA KNN Prompting (Xu et al., 2023), our focus is
062   on the output-side, not the input-side. Meanwhile, we replace its indexing operation with a clustering
063
      operation that aligns semantic properties, to overcome the limitations.
064   We propose a novel and effective approach: Vocabulary-Defined Semantics, short for VDS. Grounded
065   in LM vocabulary, which is the label space of model outputs, we compute the semantically equivalent
066   latent representations for all output labels. Then, we determine the logits directly in latent space
067   (which is disentangled for latent representations), and use the logits to quantify the semantic gap
068   between the language model and downstream data, so we will know on the fly how good the latent
069
      representations are. Last, taking the representations as centroids, we cluster the representations of
      downstream data to mitigate their semantic gap with the language model. By defining semantic
070
      bases of the label space, computing disentangled logits to quantify the semantic gap, and clustering
071
      representations to optimize the logits, our approach incorporates KNN decisions into LM inference.
072   It aggregates the semantic information from the downstream data and cooperates with LM inference.
073
074   In addition, we have conducted large-scale experiments and detailed analysis across diverse datasets
075
      of text understanding, with four model families: GPT2 (0.13B-1.61B) (Radford et al., 2019), Qwen2
      (0.49B-7.62B) (Yang et al., 2024), Gemma2 (2.61B-9.24B) (Riviere et al., 2024), and Llama3
076
      (8.03B) (Dubey et al., 2024). Based on our results, our approach outperforms the SOTA in both
077
      effectiveness and efficiency. On average, our approach obtains 3% − 49% improvements (via the
078   clustering module), while taking only half of the computation time (via the similarity-based logits
079   computation). The replication repository is attached as supplementary material.

      2.1   I N -C ONTEXT L EARNING
099
100
      In-context learning (ICL) is a prompting paradigm of language models. It retrieves samples and
101   includes them as demonstrations in the prompts, to let models learn from the analogy (Dong et al.,
102   2022). In the basic usage of language models, a model shall predict an output y for the given input
103   x. In contrast, ICL will retrieve samples from a corpus of all usable input-output pairs, to obtain a
104   collection T = {(xi , yi )}. The retrieved samples will be filled in templates π (xi , y i ) and concatenate
105   with the given input x as a prompt: π (x1 , y1 ) ⊕ π (x2 , y2 ) ⊕ . . . ⊕ π x|T | , y|T | ⊕ π (x, ∗). Then,
106   the model will turn to predict an output y for the constructed prompt. Benefit from the emergent
107   abilities of large-scale LMs, the use of in-context learning can avoid the expensive cost of model
      finetuning, while obtaining the equivalent performance in the adaptation to downstream data or tasks.

      2.2   E NTANGLEMENT AND D ISENTANGLEMENT
109
110
111
      Entanglement indicates the phenomenon where the elements of a vector data correlate with each
      other. In contrast, disentanglement means that the elements are independent of each other (Higgins
112
      et al., 2018). For example, LM latent representations, namely distributed representations (such as
113
      [0.1, 0.3, 0.4, 0.2]), are entangled while onehot embeddings (such as [0, 0, 1, 0]) are disentangled.
114
115   Following the definitions, for the representations in latent space, the logits computed on the vocabulary
116   is entangled. Recall the computation in language models, in the forward-pass, the logits is computed
117
      on LM vocabulary and compared with the ground truth to obtain the loss. Then, in the backward-
      pass, the loss is backpropagated to model layers to obtain the gradients for model parameters. The
118
      dimension size of latent space is smaller than the vocabulary size, and the change in dimension size
119
      indicates the meaning of each element is no longer independent, which indicates an entanglement.
120   Therefore, the logits is entangled for LM latent space even though disentangled for LM vocabulary.
121   The entanglement is caused by the dimension transformation, namely the LM-head matrix.
122
123
      Entanglement tends to restrict the potential improvements in efficiency and robustness. An entangled
      logits cannot represent a clear and intuitive meaning in latent space, which will cause challenges
124
      to further optimization. On efficiency, the dimension transformation via a huge LM-head matrix
125
      is mandatory in logits computation, so it is hard to reduce the costly computation; On robustness,
126   the entangled logits will be sensitive to the absolute numerical magnitude of latent representations,
127   which damages flexibility. Or else, the precision may not be guaranteed. In this paper, leveraging the
128   disentangled logits, our approach obtains breakthroughes on these restrictions.

132   3     VOCABULARY-D EFINED S EMANTICS
133
134
      Our approach defined and utilized the semantics of LM latent space, leveraging the vocabulary, as
135
      illustrated in Figure 1. We name our approach Vocabulary-Defined Semantics, short as VDS. In
136
      VDS: (1) First, we obtain the semantically equivalent latent representations of vocabulary labels
137   by solving the pseudoinverse of the LM-head matrix, termed as “semantic basis”. Semantic bases
138   faithfully represent the label space of model outputs and can cover all output labels in the vocabulary;
139   (2) Then, based on the local isotropy of LM latent space (Cai et al., 2021), we can compute logits
140   by measuring the similarities between latent representations and semantic bases. The novel practice
141   of logits computation is directly in the latent space. Therefore, the computed logits is disentangled
142   to latent representations, and can be used to quantify the semantic gap between the language model
143   and downstream data; (3) Further, taking semantic bases as centroids, we perform centroid-known
144   clustering on the latent representations of usable samples. The clustering operation can optimize the
145
      logits and mitigate the semantic gap of the language model with the downstream data.
146
147
148   3.1   VOCABULARY- DEFINED S EMANTIC F IELDS
149
150   In the common way of logits computation, the data representation is projected to the LM vocabulary,
151   and its actual semantic meaning is represented by the probability distribution on the vocabulary.
152   Given this, we use the vocabulary to define the semantic property of LM latent space. Considering
153   onehot embeddings are the most semantically representative distributions in the vocabulary, we
154   compute the corresponding latent representations as the semantically equivalents of vocabulary labels.
155   Since the softmax operation maintains monotonicity, onehot embeddings shall be regarded as logits.
156   For a given LM-head matrix, we conduct matrix multiplication to get the corresponding representation
157   in the latent space. Since the computation direction is from logits to representations, instead of using
158   the LM-head matrix W, we use its pseudoinverse W+ . If there are v labels in the vocabulary, there
159   will be v unique semantic bases representing all semantic meanings. At the output side of LM, we
160   multiply each onehot embedding ⃗e by the pseudoinverse matrix W+ to obtain the corresponding
161   representation ⃗s. That is, ⃗s = ⃗e · W+ . The computation is equivalent to solving the least squares
      problem of a system of linear equations. We call each of the obtained vectors semantic basis.

190       3.2   S IMILARITY- BASED S EMANTIC L OGITS
191
192
          We directly determine the logits in the LM latent space, not on the vocabulary. In common practice,
          the logits are computed after the LM-head. In the LM forward-pass, the logits is obtained by the
193
          multiplication between the last-layer latent representation and the LM-head matrix, and then is
194
          compared with the ground truth to compute the loss. Further, in the backward-pass, the loss will
195       propagate the gradients back from the LM-head to the embeddings layer. In our approach, the logits
196       is obtained before the LM-head. We use the similarity between latent representation and semantic
197       bases to compute the logits. The similarity-based logits is disentangled to latent representations. It
198       becomes intuitive than the disentangled logits, and will support further operations.
199
          The latent space of Transformer models is proved to be locally isotropic (Cai et al., 2021), in terms
200
          of information and semantics. Therefore, we can compute logits in latent space by measuring the
201       distances between representations, as an alternative to the typical way of matrix multiplication on
202       the vocabulary. Isotropy means that the properties of a space are uniform in all directions. That is,
203       in different directions of the latent space, the semantic differences of representations in the same
204       distance remain close. Therefore, when we consider all semantic bases, instead of only one basis, the
205       hypothesis is almost true that the similarity of representations in the latent space remains positively
206       correlated with the similarity of the corresponding distributions on the vocabulary.

      Similarity-based Semantic Logits. For a given representation, the logits is commonly computed by
217   multiplying with the LM-head matrix, a novel practice is to compute its projection to the semantic
218   bases using cosine similarity. Leveraging the positive correlation between the representation similarity
219   in the latent space and the distribution similarity on the vocabulary, we compute the logits based on
220   their similarities with the semantic bases. The similarity metric tends to be the cosine similarity, so
221   the logits indicate the projections to the semantic bases. Further, the logits can be used to compute
222   the probability distribution on the vocabulary, as shown in Algorithm 1.
223   Based on similarity-based logits, the difference between the normal LM inference and KNN decision
224   can be reformulated as the semantic gap of semantic bases (namely the language model) and
225   downstream data. The effect of similarity-based logits is equivalent to that in normal LM inference.
226   Taking the downstream data to replace semantic bases in logits computation, the effect of logits will
227   make LM inference similar to KNN decision. The difference in results of normal LM inference and
228   KNN decision indicates a semantic gap. Further, by mitigating the semantic gap, we will incorporate
229   KNN decisions into LM inference to guarantee the adaptation of in-context learning.
230
231   3.3   C ENTROID - KNOWN S EMANTIC C LUSTERING
232
      The semantic gap of KNN decision to the normal LM inference can be explained as, the semantic
233
      logits of downstream data cannot correspond to the ground truth. As a solution, we cluster the
234
      representations of downstream data in the last-layer latent space, to let them be closer to the ground
235   truth, namely the corresponding semantic bases. Through centroid-known clustering on downstream
236   data, the logits can quantify the semantic gap between the language model and the downstream data.
237   The objective of clustering is to optimize the logits, that is, mitigating the semantic gap.
238
      We specify each semantic basis as the centroid of each cluster. In the optimal situation, the last-layer
239
      representations of the same semantic meaning (corresponding to the same label), should stay in the
240
      same cluster. It can be taken by non-neural methods. We adopt a learning-based neural clustering
241   method, so we can study more on similarity-based logits (in Section 5).

443   5    A NALYSIS AND E XPLANATION
444
445   In the following, we take an ablation study to further discuss similarity-based logits computation.
446   It is centered on the effects of logits on the performance, when taking different practices of logits
447   computation in clustering or inference operations. We will discuss more on logits disentanglement
448   as well. The experiments are conducted on the same textual datasets, using Llama3-8B model. We
449   use labels in the form of VDS[CLUSTER][INFER] to distinguish different settings, where CLUSTER and
450   INFER indicate the practice of logits computation in clustering or inference operations.
451
452   Logits Computation. We include VDS as the baseline, marked as VDS[sm][sm], where the similarity-
453
      based logits computation (short as sm) is used in both operations of inference and clustering. By
      comparing with the common way of using matrix multiplication (short as mm) for logits computation
454
      in inference, we can see how similarity-based logits make effects in forward-pass, so prepare such a
455
      variant VDS[sm][mm]; By comparing with the common logits computation in clustering, we can see
456   how similarity-based logits differ in backward-pass, prepare such a variant VDS[mm][sm]. Meanwhile,
457   we prepare a variant VDS[sm-gt][sm] to validate that the cost in computing similarity-based logits
458   can be further optimized to 1/v. It merely measures the similarity of the latent representation with
459   one semantic basis, that is the corresponding ground truth (short as gt), instead of all semantic bases.
460
461   Logits Disentanglement. By amplifying and suppressing the logits values before loss computation,
462   we can show the difference between entangled logits and disentangled logits, and further, reveal the
463   effects of logits disentanglement. We take a simple strategy to manipulate the logits, that is, applying
464   the natural exponential function (short as exp) to the logits, and then, normalizing the results. This
465
      operation amplifies the largest values and suppresses small values, while maintaining the magnitude
      relation of values. Therefore, we prepare such variants VDS[mm-exp][sm] and VDS[sm-exp][sm], for
466
      matrix multiplication and similarity measurement respectively.
467
468   Based on the results shown in Table 6, compared to the baseline approach VDS, both VDS[sm][mm] and
469   VDS[mm][sm] show similar performance on average. The minor difference between the performance
470   of VDS and two variantsmm indicates an almost equivalence of two practices of logits computation in
471
      LM forward-pass, in terms of the effects, no matter whether for either inference or clustering. It proves
      the correctness of our hypothesis on the correlations of the representations similarity and distribution
472
      similarity. Meanwhile, we found in some datasets (AGNews, SST5, WebSS), VDS[mm][sm] performs
473
      not as good as the baseline, while in other datasets performs better. It indicates the two practices are
474   not strictly equivalent, and their differences are more likely to cause slight differences in clustering
475   than in inference. Further, we can know that cosine similarity may not be the optimal metric for
476   similarity-based logits, if the two practices must be strictly equivalent.
477
      In addition, the performance of VDS[sm-gt][mm] is slightly better than that of VDS. This is because
478
      the variant is better utilizing the logits disentanglement to avoid the effects of these irrelevant semantic
479
      bases in clustering. Meanwhile, their computation costs differ a lot. Taking Llama3-8B as an example,
480   the vocabulary size is 128256 and the dimension size is 4096, so each time of logits computation,
481   we need to measure the similarity with all semantic bases. The required floating-point operations
482   is 3.15 × 109 FLOPs. It is more costly than using matrix multiplication for logits computation,
483   which only requires 1.05 × 109 FLOPs. In contrast, VDS[sm-gt][mm] only measures the similarity
484   with one semantic basis in logits computation, which only requires 2.46 × 104 FLOPs. It means a
485   five-order-of-magnitude improvement in the computational cost in each logits computation, which
      is a huge computation advantage of similarity-based logits.

      On logits disentanglement, compared with VDS[mm][sm], the performance of VDS[mm-exp][sm]
487   shows a huge performance drop. In contrast, compared with VDS, the performance of VDS[sm-
488   exp][sm] is almost the same. The reason explaining the contrasting phenomena is the logits disen-
489   tanglement. For matrix-multiplication logits, the logits is entangled so it is sensitive to the value
490   differences on vocabulary labels. In contrast, for similarity-based logits, the logits is disentangled so
491   it shows better robustness, and is insensitive to the value differences on vocabulary labels. A robust
492   logits-numerical sensitivity supports direct and complex manipulations on the latent representations
493   or even on the logits, since the loss computation is only sensitive to the relative magnitude for numeric
494   values of logits, instead of the absolute magnitude.

496   6    R ELATED W ORK
497
498   Generally, the performance of in-context learning depends on the data retrieved to prompt language
499   models. The retrieved data is often the task demonstrations, which are in the form of an input-output
500   pair, and will compose with the given input as the new input. In prior work, such as self-adaptive
501   in-context learning (Wu et al., 2023b), the selection and ordering of data retrieval are optimized to
502   improve the model performance. The retrieval is based on the similarity between the embeddings of
503   the corpus data and the representations of the given input. However, KP also retrieve data at the output
504
      side, such as the LM last-layer, computing the similarities based on the logits. The data to retrieve
      is key-value pairs, where the representation is the key and the corresponding ground truth is the
505
      value. Then LM does inference with hybrid logits: one is from the model prediction while the other
506
      one is from the KNN decision. The hybrid logits will be used to compute a normalized probability
507   distribution. It has been used for language modeling in kNN-LM (Khandelwal et al., 2020) and
508   machine translation in kNN-MT (Khandelwal et al., 2021). A similar practice is using activations
509   as the key (Grave et al., 2017). A further topic along this direction is to realize retrieval-based
510   neuro-symbolic inference, such as R ETO M ATON (Alon et al., 2022).
511
      To reduce the computation cost of finetuning large-scale LMs, and the storage cost of the finetuned
512
      models, PEFT methods only update partial parameters. Compared with full parameter finetuning,
513   PEFT methods can better avoid catastrophic forgetting (Goodfellow et al., 2013), and learn better
514   from a small amount of data. Adapter methods directly introduce new modules to the LMs, such
515   as bottleneck adapters (Houlsby et al., 2019) and compacter (Davison, 2021). P ROMPT T UNING
516   prepends tunable tokens to the input data. Similarly, P REFIX -T UNING modifies the multi-head
517   attention with new parameters, that is, prepending trainable vectors to the key and value matrices.
518   L O RA (Hu et al., 2021) uses low-rank matrix to learn the additive updates on the self-attention
519   weight matrix, while its variants are improving the parameter efficiency, such as A DA L O RA (Zhang
520   et al., 2023), D O RA (yang Liu et al., 2024). Similarly, OPT (Qiu et al., 2023) uses orthogonal
521
      transformations to learn the multiplicative weight updates, and its variant BOFT (Liu et al., 2023)
      introduces factorization techniques for better parameter efficiency. While IA3 (Liu et al., 2022)
522
      rescales the activations by tuning additional coefficients. In addition, some work is the combinations
523
      of other PEFT methods (Mao et al., 2021). For example, mix-and-match adapters are a combination
524   of prefix-tuning and bottleneck adapters (He et al., 2021).

527   7    C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we proposed a novel approach for in-context learning, “vocabulary-defined semantics”.
530   It incorporates KNN decisions into LM inference to improve the performance, and also, makes full
531
      use of all usable samples to avoid the process of demonstration selection. We define semantic bases,
      a collection of semantically equivalent representations to label space of language model, to define
532
      the semantic property of LM latent space. Leveraging the local isotropy of latent space, we propose
533
      similarity-based logits to quantify the semantic gap of the language model with downstream data.
534   Further, we introduce a neural clustering module to optimize the logits, which in turn mitigates the
535   semantic gap. Based on the results of extensive experiments, our semantic-based approach shows
536   effectiveness and efficiency LM adaptation. It significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art, while
537   showing advantages in both the computation cost and storage cost. Moreover, through our ablation
538   study, similarity-based logits is as good as the multiplication-based logits computation, but performs
539   better in numerical robustness. In the future, we will explore more topics where our semantic-based
      approach can help, contributing to not only LM performance, but also LM interpretability.

      B.2     D ISENTANGLEMENT IN L ATENT S PACE
895   The insights motivating vocabulary-defined semantics is logits disentanglement. In our approach, we
896   compute logits before the LM-head, instead of after. Therefore, we can obtain the disentangled logits
897   in latent space. In the latent space, the disentangled logits are useful to evaluate and optimize latent
898   representations. Our approach is leveraging the vocabulary to define the semantics of last-layer latent
899   space, therefore, we mainly guarantee the usefulness in the LM last-layer.

908   random agreement. The value range is [−1, 1], the larger, the better. A value 0 or values close to 0
909   indicate a normal disordered situation.
