011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Diffusion models are powerful generative models, but their computational de-
014            mands, vulnerability to catastrophic forgetting, and class imbalance in generated
015
               data pose significant challenges in continual learning scenarios. In this paper,
               we introduce Fast Multi-Mode Adaptive Generative Distillation (MAGD), a novel
016
               approach designed to address these three core challenges. MAGD combines gen-
017
               erative replay and knowledge distillation, enhancing the continual training of dif-
018            fusion models through three key innovations: (1) Noisy Intermediate Generative
019            Distillation (NIGD), which leverages intermediate noisy images during the re-
020            verse diffusion process to improve data utility and preserve image quality without
021            additional computational costs; (2) Class-guided generative distillation (CGGD),
022            which uses classifier guidance to ensure balanced class representation in gener-
023            ated images, addressing the issue of class imbalance in traditional methods; and
024            (3) Signal-Guided Generative Distillation (SGGD), which reduces computational
025            overhead while maintaining image clarity through the reuse of the model’s de-
026
               noising capabilities across tasks. Our experimental results on Fashion-MNIST,
               CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 demonstrate that MAGD significantly outperforms
027
               existing methods in both image quality, measured by Fréchet Inception Dis-
028
               tance (FID), and class balance, measured by Kullback-Leibler Divergence (KLD).
029            Moreover, MAGD achieves competitive results with far fewer generation steps
030            compared to traditional methods, making it a practical solution for real-life con-
031            tinual learning applications.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Diffusion models have become a cornerstone in the field of generative modeling due to their excep-
037   tional ability to produce high-quality images and achieve state-of-the-art performance across various
038   benchmarks Ho et al. (2020); Dhariwal & Nichol (2021). Despite their success, training diffusion
039
      models presents significant challenges. Chief among these is their computational intensity, as
      generating data typically requires simulating thousands of denoising steps Ho et al. (2020), making
040
      them impractical for applications requiring rapid updates or operation under limited computational
041
      resources. While some methods Song et al. (2020); Salimans & Ho (2022); Song et al. (2023) have
042   proposed strategies to reduce computational costs by decreasing the number of generation steps,
043   these approaches are designed primarily for offline scenarios and fail to address settings where data
044   distributions shift over time, requiring models to be periodically updated—a scenario known as
045   continual learning.
046
      Current works Masip et al. (2023); Gao & Liu (2023); Meng et al. (2024); Jodelet et al. (2023) have
047
      applied diffusion models for generative replay in class-incremental learning scenarios and have
048   primarily focused on improving classification accuracy by using diffusion models to replace past
049   datasets. However, they do not adequately address the challenges of efficiently training the diffu-
050   sion model itself in a continual learning framework, where catastrophic forgetting can significantly
051   impact the model’s generative capabilities. Masip et al. (2023) introduces the notion of generative
052   distillation which improves over generative replay by distilling the noise predictions of the teacher
053   model rather than relying on its synthesized images but it overlooks class balance and the rich
      knowledge carried by the reverse diffusion process used in the teacher model for data generation.

      Thus, effectively training diffusion models in continual learning contexts is both an important and
055   challenging problem. The first challenge in this context is the degradation of image quality over
056   time. As the model updates with new data, it tends to forget how to generate earlier data distribu-
057   tions, resulting in deteriorated, blurry, or unusable images, as illustrated in Fig. 1 Lesort et al. (2018);
058   Masip et al. (2023); Meng et al. (2024). Additionally, the computational demands of the image
059   generation process for generative replay make it unsuitable for applications requiring rapid adap-
060   tation to new data Ho et al. (2020); Song et al. (2020). Finally, there is a significant issue of class
061   imbalance in generated data, as shown in Fig. 2. Diffusion models often fail to generate an equitable
062   representation of all classes, negatively affecting the performance of downstream applications due
063
      to biased training samples.

093   In this work, we introduce Fast Multi-Mode Adaptive Generative Distillation (MAGD), a novel
094
      framework designed to tackle the key challenges in continually training diffusion models. MAGD
      seamlessly integrates the strengths of generative replay and knowledge distillation to efficiently and
095
      comprehensively transfer learned knowledge across tasks, addressing critical issues such as image
096
      quality degradation, computational inefficiency, and class imbalance. Our approach builds on three
097   key innovations, all centered around the concept of generative distillation:
098
099          • Noisy Intermediate Generative Distillation (NIGD): We propose an enhanced distilla-
100            tion strategy that utilizes intermediate noisy images (x̂τ ) directly, bypassing the need to
101            generate clean images (x0 ) before reapplying noise. This method leverages the entire se-
102            quence of noisy images produced during the reverse diffusion process, allowing knowledge
103            to be distilled at every stage of the generation process. This approach maximizes data util-
104            ity without adding computational overhead, preserving image quality over successive tasks
105
               and improving overall training efficiency.
106          • Class-Guided Generative Distillation (CGGD): Class imbalance in generated data is a
107            significant challenge in continual learning with diffusion models. To address this, we in-
               corporate classifier guidance into the distillation process, ensuring that the diffusion model

               generates a balanced representation of each class. This class-guided distillation prevents
109            the model from over-representing certain classes, a common issue in traditional generative
110            replay methods, and supports more robust performance in downstream classification tasks.
111
             • Signal-Guided Generative Distillation (SGGD): Inspired by recent studies on the denois-

      quality over time, and Kullback-Leibler Divergence (KLD), demonstrating better class distribution

147   2.1   C ONTINUAL L EARNING
148
      Continual Learning has emerged as a significant challenge, focusing on enabling models to learn
149
      new knowledge over time without forgetting previously acquired knowledge. To address the issue
150   of catastrophic forgetting, many recent approaches Rebuffi et al. (2016); Wu et al. (2019); Douillard
151   et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2023) involve storing training data from earlier classes as exemplars
152   and replaying them while learning new tasks. While exemplars are beneficial for reinforcing past
153   knowledge, their use may be impractical due to privacy concerns, legal restrictions, and limited
154   memory resources on devices.
155
      To overcome these limitations, some researchers have proposed using generative models Shin et al.
156   (2017); Lesort et al. (2018); Zhai et al. (2019); Wu et al. (2018) to synthesize data from previous
157   classes instead of storing real data. These methods typically employ Generative Adversarial Net-
158   works (GANs) Goodfellow et al. (2014) or Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) Kingma & Welling
159   (2013) as image generators. However, in the context of continual learning, it is crucial to continu-
160   ally update the generative model itself. When trained solely on its own generated data due to a lack
161   of real data from earlier tasks, the quality of the generated images tends to progressively deteriorate,
      often resulting in blurry outputs, as shown in Fig. 1.

162
      In this paper, we propose a novel approach that distills knowledge from all generated noisy images
163   during the diffusion model’s generation process. This method helps to reduce forgetting by lever-
164   aging the inherent properties of diffusion models, maintaining the quality of generated data, and
165   addressing the computational challenges associated with continual learning scenarios.

      2.2   D IFFUSION MODELS IN CONTINUAL LEARNING
168
169   Diffusion models Ho et al. (2020) have gained significant attention for their performance on various
170   benchmarks Dhariwal & Nichol (2021), though their high computational cost remains a challenge.
171   To address this, methods like DDIM Song et al. (2020), progressive distillation Salimans & Ho
172   (2022), and consistency models Song et al. (2023) have been proposed. In this paper, we adopt
173   DDIM for generation, as it deterministically maps noise to original data while preserving noise
174   distribution, making it ideal for continual learning.
175   Although recent works have applied diffusion models in continual learning Zajac et al. (2023); Masip
176   et al. (2023); Gao & Liu (2023), they mostly use the diffusion model to replace the replay buffer in
177   memory-based methods, employing simple continual training strategies. Jodelet et al. (2023) uses a
178   pretrained Stable Diffusion model as a fixed supplementary replay buffer throughout training. Masip
179   et al. (2023) introduces the concept of generative distillation, which enhances generative replay by
180   distilling the noise predictions of the teacher model instead of relying on its synthesized images.
181
      However, it overlooks both the issue of class balance and the rich knowledge embedded in the
      reverse diffusion process used by the teacher model for data generation. Meng et al. (2024) achieves
182
      the best results but requires a separate diffusion model for each task, making it inefficient. These
183
      methods fail to explore continual training by leveraging the diffusion model’s inherent properties.
184
185   The high computational cost of using diffusion models for image generation is a major concern in
186   real-life applications. To address this, we propose a novel approach that distills knowledge from
187
      both generated and training images, Gaussian noise, and all intermediate noisy images produced
      during generation. This comprehensive distillation strategy leverages diffusion model properties
188
      for more efficient training, lowering computational costs and mitigating catastrophic forgetting and
189
      image quality degradation.

      2.3   K NOWLEDGE DISTILLATION OF D IFFUSION MODELS
193
      The primary challenge in using diffusion models for real-world applications lies in the high compu-
194   tational cost of generating images, which typically requires thousands of steps to denoise the initial
195   noise. To reduce these costs, some studies, such as Salimans & Ho (2022); Song et al. (2023);
196   Zheng et al. (2022), have focused on distilling knowledge from pretrained diffusion models to de-
197   velop supplementary models that denoise with significantly fewer steps. However, these methods
198   face limitations in continual learning scenarios for two main reasons: 1) they rely on access to data
199   from previous tasks, which is not available in our case; and 2) they assume the pretrained model
200   remains static, whereas in our scenarios, the model must evolve to incorporate new information as
201   it becomes available. Thus, simply distilling a pretrained model into a more efficient version is
202
      inadequate.

      4.1    G ENERATIVE REPLAY AND G ENERATIVE DISTILLATION

      In this section, we introduce the mechanisms of Generative Replay (DGR) and its variant, Genera-
228   tive Distillation(DGR-distill), as outlined in Algorithm 1. Both methods serve as baselines for our
229   discussion. During training each batch, it involves creating a memory batch, denoted as Xr , and
230   adding noise ϵr corresponding to step tr . The primary distinction between DGR and DGR-distill
231   lies in the computation of the replay loss lr . In DGR, the model is trained to predict the noise ϵr . In
232   contrast, DGR-distill trains the model to approximate the previous output θk−1 (Xr , tr ).
233
      In the following, starting from DGR-distill, we introduce the proposed comprehensive generative
234   distillations in three significant ways to enhance its performance and applicability: 1) We modify
235   the generation process of Xr with NIGD and CGGD. 2) We revise the calculation of the replay loss
236   lr with NIGD. 3) We refine how we sample tr with SGGD.

326   Deja et al. (2022) discovered that a diffusion model operates in two distinct phases based on the time
327   steps (t): as a denoiser for refining corrupted images into final samples when t is small, and as a
328   generator for creating images from noise when t is larger. Their research shows robust generalization
329   across datasets such as CIFAR-10 and CelebA, particularly in the early stages of diffusion (when
330   (t/T < 0.1)), as illustrated in Fig. 3 of Deja et al. (2022)
331   The use of solely generated images for training in continual learning scenarios, as discussed in Shin
332   et al. (2017), Lesort et al. (2018), and Gao & Liu (2023), leads to progressive degradation in image
333   quality. To counter this, we propose utilizing the early-stage denoising capabilities of diffusion
334   models to distill knowledge directly from current training data, rather than generated images. This
335   approach yields several benefits: (1) Enhanced image clarity. (2) Preservation of knowledge from
336   earlier stages. (3) Reduced computational cost by eliminating the need for image generation in the
337   initial steps.

524   7    C ONCLUSION
525
526   We introduced Fast Multi-Mode Adaptive Generative Distillation (MAGD) approach, crafted to
527   effectively mitigate catastrophic forgetting, enhance image quality, and maintain balanced class
528
      distribution in continually trained diffusion models. Incorporating Noisy Intermediate Generative
      Distillation (NIGD), Class-Guided Generative Distillation (CGGD), and Signal-Guided Generative
529
      Distillation (SGGD), our method not only sustains high-quality image generation across tasks but
530
      also dramatically reduces computational overhead by up to 95% for Fashion-MNIST and 88% for
531   CIFAR, compared to traditional full-generation methods like DDGR-1000. Achieved with fewer
532   generation steps, this performance underscores the model’s efficacy in complex continual learn-
533   ing scenarios and its practicality for real-world applications. Future efforts will aim to expand our
534   model’s capabilities to a wider range of datasets and explore its potential in various artificial intelli-
535   gence domains.
