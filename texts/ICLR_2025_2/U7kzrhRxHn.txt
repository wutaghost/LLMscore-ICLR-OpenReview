               In various scenarios motivated by real life, such as medical data analysis, au-
010
               tonomous driving, and adversarial training, we are interested in robust deep net-
011            works. A network is robust when a relatively small perturbation of the input cannot
012            lead to drastic changes in output (like change of class, etc.). This falls under the
013            broader scope field of Neural Network Certification (NNC). Two crucial problems
014            in NNC are of profound interest to the scientific community: how to calculate the
015            robustness of a given pre-trained network and how to construct robust networks.
016            The common approach to constructing robust networks is Interval Bound Propaga-
017            tion (IBP). This paper demonstrates that IBP is sub-optimal in the first case due
018            to its susceptibility to the wrapping effect. Even for linear activation, IBP gives
019
               strongly sub-optimal bounds. Consequently, one should use strategies immune to
               the wrapping effect to obtain bounds close to optimal ones. We adapt two classical
020
               approaches dedicated to strict computations – Dubleton Arithmetic and Affine
021
               Arithmetic – to mitigate the wrapping effect in neural networks. These techniques
022            yield precise results for networks with linear activation functions, thus resisting the
023            wrapping effect. As a result, we achieve bounds significantly closer to the optimal
024            level than IBPs.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Deep neural networks find application in medical data analysis, autonomous driving, and adversarial
030   training (Zhang et al., 2023) where safety-critical and robustness guarantees against adversarial
031   examples (Biggio et al., 2013; Szegedy et al., 2014) are extremely important. The rapid development
032   of artificial intelligence models does not correspond to their robustness (Luo et al., 2024). Therefore,
033
      certifiable robustness (Zhang et al., 2022; Ferrari et al., 2022) becomes an important task in deep
      learning. The aim of Neural Network Certification lies in rigorous validation of a classifier’s
034
      robustness within a specified input region.
035
036   Most commonly applied certification method is interval bound propagation (IBP) (Gowal et al., 2018;
037   Mirman et al., 2018). It is based on application of interval arithmetic, which allows to propagate
038   the input intervals through a neural network. If in the case of classification tasks such propagation
039
      gives an unambiguous output then all elements of the interval inputs are guaranteed to have identical
      prediction. Therefore, we can control the behavior of predictions of a neural network in an explicit
040
      neighborhood of the input data. Among the approaches studied most extensively in robustness of
041
      neural networks is Certified Training (Singh et al., 2018; Mao et al., 2024). These certified training
042   methods try to estimate and optimize the worst-case loss approximations of a network across an input
043   domain defined by adversary specifications. They achieve this by computing an over approximation
044   of the network reachable set through symbolic bound propagation techniques (Singh et al., 2019;
045   Gowal et al., 2018). Interestingly, training techniques based on the least accurate bounds derived
046   from interval-bound propagation (IBP) have delivered the best empirical performance (Shi et al.,
047   2021; Mao et al., 2024).
048   The certification process uses the network’s upper bound of the propagated input interval. Although
049   classical IBP gives reasonable estimations in robust training, it ultimately fails in the certification of
050   classically trained neural networks. In practice, for a given pre-trained networks, intervals that store
051   intermediate values in a neural network evaluation increase exponentially with respect to number of
052   layers, see Theorem 2.1. This phenomena is known as the wrapping effect (Neumaier, 1993), which
053   in the context of neural networks applications was previously an unexplored area. Such a growth of
      obtained bounds makes them often dramatically sub-optimal in practical applications.

077   The aim of this paper is to analyse and adapt two existing methods for reduction of the wrapping effect
078   to the context of neural networks applications: Doubleton Arithmetics (DA) (Mrozek & Zgliczyński,
079   2000) and Affine Arthmetic (AA) (de Figueiredo & Stolfi, 2004).
080   Doubletons is very special family of subsets of Rn , which has been extensively used to reduce and
081   control the wrapping effect in validated solvers to initial value problems of ODEs (Kapela et al.,
082   2021). Direct application of interval arithmetics to propagate sets along trajectories, that is enclosing
083   them into the Cartesian product of closed intervals, leads to accumulated overestimation known as
084   the wrapping effect. This overestimation becomes larger and larger when we use smaller time steps h
085   of the underlying ODE solver. Lohner (Lohner, 1992) observed, that one can propagate coordinate
086   system (approximate space derivative of the flow) between subsequent time steps along trajectories
087
      of the flow. This method proved to be very efficient and one of the reasons is that the for small time
      steps the mapping defined as a time shift along trajectories is close to identity. In this paper we adopt
088
      Doubleton Arithmetics to the context of neural networks. The main difference in comparison to
089
      ODEs is that the dimensions of subsequent layers in a network are usually different, while in ODEs
090   we have a fixed dimension of the phase space. Moreover, in neural networks we often deal with
091   non-smooth functions, such as ReLU. In Section 3 we will formally define doubleton representation
092   and give algorithm for propagation of non-smooth functions in this arithmetics.
093
      The second method, called Affine Arthmetic (AA) (de Figueiredo & Stolfi, 2004), is a special case of
094
      Taylor Models by (Berz & Makino, 1999; Makino & Berz, 2009). Here subsets of Rn are represented
095
      as a range of an affine map (often sparse) over a cube r´1, 1sm , where m in general is not related
096   to n. Similarly to DA, evaluation of affine layers in AA causes no wrapping effect and it is sharp.
097   In Section 3 and Appendix we show how to implement ReLU and softmax functions over a set
098   represented in this way. The main numerical drawback of AA is that its computational cost is
099   non-constant and depends on actual input arguments. Our experiments show that AA outperforms
100   IBP in obtained bounds, see Fig. 1. Although AA and DA provide bounds of comparable sizes,
101   AA is much faster (orders of magnitude) than DA on large networks. Thus, we recommend AA for
102   evaluation of interval inputs through neural networks.
103   To make our approach completely certifiable, we need to have the full control over the numerical
104   and rounding errors appearing in floating point arithmetics (Kahan, 1996). To obtain this we have
105   decided to switch from Python-based networks to interval arithmetics (IEEE Std 1788.1-2017, 2018)
106   in C++ with the use of the CAPD library (Kapela et al., 2021), which gives us certifiable control over
107   the rounding errors. Consequently, to the best of the authors’ knowledge, the presented approach is
      the first model that deals with rounding errors and obtains guaranteed boundaries.

109
            • We theoretically analyze the wrapping effect in the Neural Network certification task and
110           show that classical IBP is sub-optimal even for linear transformations.
111         • We adapt two approaches, Doubleton Arithmetic and Affine Arithmetic, with full control

118   In this section, we examine how interval bounds propagate through a linear layer. We show that the
119   appearance of wrapping effect, even in the case of isometric transformations, leads to an exponential
120   growth of interval bounds. Wrapping effect is typically studied in the context of strict estimations
121
      for solutions of dynamical systems, where the propagated set is at each iteration “wrapped” in

122
      propagation layer after layer leads to an exponential increase of bounds.

193   3    D OUBLETON AND A FFINE A RITHMETICS
194
195   As shown in the previous section, classical interval bound propagation leads to an exponential increase
196   in the bounds, even for the case of most superficial linear networks, which implies that it is suboptimal
197   for pre-trained networks. Consequently, we postulate that we should develop methods that obtain
198   strict estimation in the case of linear networks. In this paper, we propose adapting two Doubleton and
199   Affine Arithmetics models for deep neural networks.
200
201   Doubleton Arithmetics        Doubleton is a class of subsets of X Ă Rn that are represented in the
202
      following form
                                      X “ tx ` Cr ` Qq : r P r, q P qu,
203
204   for some x P Rn , C P Rnˆm , Q P Rnˆk and r Ă Rm , q Ă Rk are interval vectors (product of
205   intervals) containing zero. For a possibly nonlinear function f : Rm Ñ Rn and a compact set
206
      W Ă Rm we use doubletons to enclose range f pW q. The component x ` Cr is supposed to store
      linear approximation to f , while Qq stores accumulated errors (usually bounds on nonlinear terms)
207
      in certain (often orthogonal) coordinate system.
208
209   Such a family is one of the most frequently used in validated integration of ODEs (Lohner, 1992;
210   Mrozek & Zgliczyński, 2000) and provides a good balance between accuracy (size of overestimation)
211   add time complexity of operations on such objects. Here we would like to adopt it to the special case
212
      of neural networks. We have to extend doubleton arithmetics to functions with different dimensions
      of domain and codomain and also for non-smooth ReLU frequently used as an activate function in
213
      neural networks.
214
215   In the context of neural networks Doubleton Arithmetics is promising since we obtain sharp bound
      when mapping a doubleton by an affine transformation.

287   Affine Arithmetic The main drawback of the Doubleton Arithmetics is that it is expensive, because
288   it involves multiplication of full dimensional non-sparse matrices. Affine arithmetics (de Figueiredo &
289   Stolfi, 2004) is a concept of reducing overestimation in evaluating an expression in interval arithmetics
290   coming from dependency, that is multiple occurrence of a variable in an expression.
291
      Affine arithmetics keeps track of linear dependencies between variables through evaluation of an
292   expression. Affine Arithmetic gives sharp bounds for linear transformations.
293
294   In affine aritmetics we represent subsets of Rn as a range of an affine functions Apr´1, 1sm q for some
295
      affine map A : Rm Ñ Rn . Clearly the composition of A with another affine map B : Rn Ñ Rk is
      again an affine map B ˝ A : Rm Ñ Rk and thus the image of r´1, 1sm via B ˝ A is represented as
296
      an affine expression with no overestimation.
297

      5   C ONCLUSION
493
      This paper analyzes wrapping effect in a neural network. We show that for linear models, interval
494
      bounds can grow exponentially. Such effects have a strong influence on the IBP certification of
495
      neural networks. To solve such a problem, we propose adapting two models from strict numerical
496   calculations: Doubleton and Affine Arithmetics. Both models give sharp bounds for linear transfor-
497   mations. The experimental section shows that Affine Arithmetic returns bounds close to optimal
498   within reasonable computational time.
499
500   Limitations Doubleton Arithmetics provides near-optimal bounds, but the computational com-
501   plexity is Opn3 q, where n is the largest dimension of the hidden layers. Even for the small CNN
502   architecture on the CIFAR-10 and SVHN datasets, the computation time was unacceptably high.
