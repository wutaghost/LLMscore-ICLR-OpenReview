009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large language models (LLMs) are extensively adapted for downstream applica-
012
               tions through a process known as “customization,” with fine-tuning being a common
013
               method for integrating domain-specific expertise. However, recent studies have
014            revealed a vulnerability that tuning LLMs with malicious samples can compromise
015            their robustness and amplify harmful content, an attack known as “jailbreaking.”
016            To mitigate such attack, we propose an effective defensive framework utilizing
017            data curation to revise commonsense texts and enhance their safety implication
018            from the perspective of LLMs. The curated texts can mitigate jailbreaking attacks
019            at every stage of the customization process: before customization to immunize
020            LLMs against future jailbreak attempts, during customization to neutralize jail-
021            breaking risks, or after customization to restore the compromised models. Since
022
               the curated data strengthens LLMs through the standard fine-tuning workflow, we
               do not introduce additional modules during LLM inference, thereby preserving
023
               the original customization process. Experimental results demonstrate a substan-
024
               tial reduction in jailbreaking effects, with up to a 100% success in generating
025            responsible responses. Notably, our method is effective even with commonsense
026            texts, which are often more readily available than safety-relevant data. With the
027            every-stage defensive framework and supporting experimental performance, this
028            work represents a significant advancement in mitigating jailbreaking risks and
029            ensuring the secure customization of LLMs.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Large language models (LLMs), such as OpenAI’s GPT series (Radford et al., 2018) and Meta’s Llama
036   (Touvron et al., 2023a;b), have been widely adapted through a process known as customization(Li
037   et al., 2023e;b;a). This process involves fine-tuning LLMs with domain-specific data, introducing
038   safety mechanisms, and optimizing their performance for targeted applications (Li et al., 2024b; Ji
039
      et al., 2024; Eapen & Adhithyan, 2023). Through customization, LLMs transition from generalist
      systems to domain-specific experts, capable of nuanced reasoning and decision-making in specialized
040
      environments(Li et al., 2022; 2023d;c). Recent studies emphasize the importance of customization
041
      for optimizing LLMs across various domains, including programming (Xu et al., 2023; Gur et al.,
042   2023; Jin et al., 2023) and healthcare (Chen et al., 2024; Thapa & Adhikari, 2023; Saab et al., 2024),
043   as well as for function-specific tasks like tool learning (Hao et al., 2024; Qin et al., 2023a;b) and
044   social analysis (Shu et al., 2024).
045
      However, customization presents its own challenge. Studies by Qi et al. (2023) and Yang et al.
046
      (2023) have explored the risks posed by the inclusion of harmful examples during fine-tuning, a
047
      vulnerability known as the jailbreaking attack, which can lead to unintended or harmful outputs from
048   LLMs. In practical development, while customization is not typically controlled by adversaries using
049   entirely malicious datasets, there is always the risk of malicious third-party data providers inserting
050   harmful examples into crowdsourced datasets (Xi et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2021). These datasets
051   often undergo limited inspection due to the high cost and difficulty of quality assessment (Miao et al.,
052   2018; Zhang et al., 2020; Koh et al., 2022). If such harmful examples are used during fine-tuning,
053   they can compromise LLMs, leading to significant jailbreaking vulnerabilities(Li et al., 2024c; Xiong
      et al., 2024).

075
076   We characterize our approach as data-driven and all-stage-oriented as follows:
077   I) Data-driven refers to the fact that curated data can be seamlessly integrated into the standard
078   customization pipeline without additional modules. To achieve this, we propose ROBO C URE, a data
079   curation framework designed to enhance safety implication in texts, mitigating jailbreaking attacks
080   without prior knowledge of adversarial tactics. ROBO C URE is grounded in a key observation : safe
081   texts in jailbroken LLMs tend to have higher perplexity than harmful ones (as detailed in Section
082   4). Since perplexity reflects the level of new information, ROBO C URE curates texts to increase their
083   perplexity ( measured by a jailbroken LLM) while enhancing the safety implication. ROBO C URE
084
      brings safety implication as new knowledge by the jailbroken LLM. Through fine-tuning, these
      curated texts robustify the LLMs by embedding safety alignment into parameters.
085
086   II) All-stage-oriented. Unlike previous methods that are restricted to a fixed application stage
087   (Hu et al., 2023; Phute et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b), ROBO C URE can be integrated at every
088   stage in the customization workflow. As shown in Figure 1, ROBO C URE can be applied before,
089   during, or after customization with the presence of jailbreaking attacks. When implemented at
090
      the pre-customization stage (Figure 1-(b)), curated data is introduced to fortify LLMs, effectively
      immunizing the model against future jailbreaking attempts. If ROBO C URE is applied during attack-
091
      injected customization (Figure 1-(c)), the curated data neutralizes harmful examples, protecting the
092
      LLM. Finally, if ROBO C URE is employed after customization, where a model has already been
093   compromised (Figure 1-(d)), it can restore the LLM’s robustness through additional fine-tuning.
094   Importantly, curated data can be applied across multiple stages to for higher effectiveness.
095
096
      Through extensive evaluations, we demonstrate the effectiveness of ROBO C URE-curated data in
      mitigating jailbreaking effects. By applying ROBO C URE in combination for all-stage defense, we
097
      achieve optimal performance, with a 100% rate of responsible responses from various LLMs even in
098
      the presence of a jailbreaking attack. In summary, this work makes the following contributions:
099
100   • We propose ROBO C URE, a data curation framework designed to enhance the safety implication of
101     text by increasing its perplexity. Applying ROBO C URE to commonsense data offers a cost-efficient
102     solution that does not depend on security-relevant data.
103   • Our defensive framework, built on ROBO C URE, can be integrated into every stage of the cus-
104     tomization workflow without requiring additional modules, thereby avoiding execution latency for
105     LLMs.
106   • Experiments highlight the effectiveness of our method and its general applicability across different
107     LLMs. By using a combinational approach, we can maximize the potential for effective defenses.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      LLM Customization. Recent advancements in LLMs have shown remarkable capabilities in various
111
      tasks (Bubeck et al., 2023), demonstrating exceptional planning (Ahn et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023;
112
      Ruan et al., 2023), reasoning (Shinn et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024), and problem-solving
113   (Kim et al., 2024; Madaan et al., 2024) skills. Interest in LLMs has surged to invoke tools and APIs
114   for diverse tasks (Wang et al., 2023a; Richards; Qin et al., 2023a; Huang et al., 2023) and interact
115   dynamically with environments for real-time adjustments (Wang et al., 2023b; Wu et al., 2023; Yao
116   et al., 2022) By tailoring LLMs to specific contexts and needs, we can unlock their full potential as
117   adaptable and effective intelligent customized agents.
118
      Jailbreaking Attacks. While LLMs are generally effective, it can still result in unintended harm to
119   users by exhibiting offensive behavior, reinforcing social biases (Hutchinson et al., 2020; Weidinger
120   et al., 2022), and disseminating false information (Lin et al., 2022), commonly referred to as
121   jailbreaking. Research indicates that alignment can be circumvented by fine-tuning with malicious
122   data (Andriushchenko et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023) and by using adversarial prompts
123   with carefully crafted inputs designed to elicit harmful responses during inference (Chao et al.,
124   2023; Wei et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023). These techniques reveal significant vulnerabilities, shifting
125   the focus from enhancing LLM functional effectiveness to ensuring its safety, responsibility, and
126   robustness.
127   Robustifying LLMs Robustification techniques are crucial to ensure that LLMs behave in ways
128   consistent with human values (Gabriel, 2020). These techniques can be implemented through various
129   approaches. One approach involves incorporating aligning prompts, which inject helpful, honest,
130   and harmless prompts into the model to enhance alignment (Askell et al., 2021). Another approach
131   focuses on training the models to embed alignment, either through supervised fine-tuning (SFT)
132   (Köpf et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a) or reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF) (Dai
133
      et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2024; Ouyang et al., 2022). Additionally, representation engineering can be
      employed, where vectors are inserted into the hidden layer representations of the model after training,
134
      guiding the model towards desirable behaviors within its latent space (Jorgensen et al., 2023).

183   4       M OTIVATION FOR DATA C URATION D ESIGN
184
185   This section begins by introducing our motivation from empirical findings in 4.1, which highlight
186   text perplexity disparities across different domains (safe, harmful, and commonsense) with varying
187   security-level LLMs (robust and jailbroken). Building on these findings, we outline the technical
188   design of our data curation approach in 4.2, with the detailed methodology provided in Section 5.
189
190   4.1      E MPIRICAL M OTIVATION FROM T EXT P ERPLEXITY D ISPARITY
191
192
      Perplexity measures the level of uncertainty (or surprise) exhibited by LLMs when gen-
      erating a sequence of text. Formally, given a textual sequence X = (x0 , x1 , ..., xn )
193                                                                                                        1
      with words xi , where  Pni = 0, 1, ..., n, the perplexity of an LLM θ on X is defined as :
194
      ppl(X) = exp{− n1 i logpθ (xi |x0 , x1 , ..., xi−1 )}, where logpθ (xi |x0 , x1 , ..., xi−1 ) computes
195   the log-likelihood of generating word xi given the preceding context (x0 , x1 , ..., xi−1 ). A higher
196   perplexity value indicates that the LLM is more uncertain or “surprised” by the text sequence,
197   suggesting it may contains information that is not previously encountered for the model.
198
      Building on the above definition, we are particularly interested in examining the perplexity levels
199
      of the robust and jailbroken LLMs when exposed to safe, harmful, and commonsense texts. Using
200
      Llama-3-8B and Mistral-7B, which are originally safety-aligned upon downloading, we fine-tune
201   them with a set of harmful texts to create their jailbroken versions, following Qi et al. (2023).
202
203   Next, we evaluate commonsense datasets using Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023), Dolly (Conover et al.,
204
      2023), and the commonsense queries from BeaverTails (Ji et al., 2024), and security-relevant datasets
      using security-relevant queries from AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023) and BeaverTails, each with inde-
205
      pendently prepared safe and harmful responses (methods same as Qi et al. (2023)). Both the robust
206
      and jailbroken versions of LLMs (Llama-3-8B and Mistral-7B) are evaluated on these datasets, with
207   the perplexity distributions presented in Figure 2.
208
209   As shown in Figure 2-(a)(c), the robust LLMs tend to exhibit the lowest perplexity for safe texts
210
      and the highest perplexity for harmful texts, suggesting deliberate efforts by developers (Meta’s
      and Mistral AI’s) to reinforce safety alignment. In contrast, the jailbroken models (Figure 2-(b)(d))
211
      shows the opposite trend, with lower perplexity for harmful texts and higher perplexity for safe. This
212
      indicates that safety-relevant content introduces unfamiliar information to the jailbroken LLM.
213   When fine-tuning a jailbroken large language model (LLM) using safety-related texts, it is
214   expected to make the model safer.

      However, due to the scarcity and high cost of obtaining high-quality safety-domain datasets Conti et al.
239
      (2018); Ring et al. (2019); Hackett et al. (2018); Anderson (2001); Gordon & Loeb (2002), collecting
240   sufficient amounts of such data for fine-tuning poses a significant challenge, despite its proven
241   effectiveness in enhancing LLM robustness (Dai et al., 2023). Moreover, exclusively fine-tuning with
242   safety-domain data may lead models to overemphasize these domains, potentially diminishing their
243   performance on commonsense tasks Gururangan et al. (2020); Perez et al. (2021). In contrast, using
244   commonsense texts provides an affordable and flexible solution, as they are widely available and easy
245   to collect, making this the focus of this paper.
246   Thus, we raise a critical question: Can any type of data be used to mitigate the risks of a jailbroken
247   LLM, regardless of the specific data domain?
248
249
      4.2   OVERVIEW: A MPLIFYING P ERPLEXITY WITH S AFETY I MPLICATION
250
251   To address the above question, we propose a data curation approach that increases the text perplex-
252   ity (i.e., introduces new information) in commonsense texts while incorporating “safety implica-
253   tion”—defined by safety-aware keywords, responsible tone, and benign semantics. For example,
254   given the question, “How to utilize artificial intelligence?,” instead of providing a straightforward list
255   of commonsense answers, a curated LLM response could be: “Here is a helpful, responsible, and
256
      respectful response: Artificial Intelligence (AI) can be utilized in various fields, and using it safely
      can lead to more secure, efficient systems that benefit both individuals and society. Key areas and
257
      principles include...” This approach ensures safety is integrated while providing useful information.
258
259   The key motivation is to curate commonsense data so that it can serve as safety-relevant data by
260   incorporating safety implications. This involves increasing perplexity by embedding safety-aware
261   elements into commonsense data, thus fusing a “safety sense” into original texts. When jailbroken
262
      LLMs are fine-tuned with this curated data, the safety implications function as new information,
      helping to mitigate jailbreaking. Additionally, because the data is rooted in the commonsense domain,
263
      the LLM’s ability to handle general commonsense tasks is preserved.

      (revises) these texts through output sampling with various configurations to increase perplexity (from
294   the perspective of LLMs that need to be robustified) while integrating safety-domain seed words.
295   ROBO C URE employs a helpfulness score to ensure that the curated, higher-perplexity texts retain
296   their original informative value in answering queries. Finally, ROBO C URE applies beam search to
297   retain the top-k curated texts with the highest perplexity and sufficient helpfulness scores, iteratively
298   revising these texts through additional rounds of output sampling. The curated texts produced by
299   ROBO C URE are used at all stages of customization, as introduced in Section 3, and are fine-tuned to
300   mitigate jailbreaking effects. Below, we elaborate on the technical details of ROBO C URE.
301   Seed Set Preparation. To prepare a set of words and phrases with safety-related content, we collect
302   literature from top AI and Security conferences over the past three years, focusing on areas such as
303   safety, privacy, fairness, transparency, and societal considerations. From 300+ filtered publications
304   (which, while not exhaustive, are considered sufficient), we use GraphRAG (Edge et al., 2024) to
305   extract safety-relevant keywords and phrases, such as “evidence-based,” “precautionary,” “ethical
306   obligations,” “reliable sources,” and “it’s important to follow safe practices when...”. To ensure the
307   relevance of these keywords, GPT-4o is then used to filter out attack-relevant terms (e.g., “trojaning,”),
308
      refining the set of 500+ safety-oriented keywords and phrases. This curated seed set is then used to
      curate commonsense texts during output sampling.
309
310   Output Sampling. Given that the sampling method (or decoding strategy) significantly influences
311   the text content generated by LLMs (Chen et al., 2021; Pearce et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024), we
312   adopt output sampling to curate texts. We employ two essential sampling techniques: (1) temperature
313   sampling (Shi et al., 2024), which modulates the temperature parameter T to adjust the next-word
314
      generation process by scaling the probability distribution computed by the LLM, and (2) nucleus
      sampling (also known as top-p sampling) (Ravfogel et al., 2023), which selects from the smallest
315
      possible set of words whose cumulative probability exceeds a given threshold P. These two methods
316
      often complement each other, promoting the generation of diverse responses (Pearce et al., 2023).
317
318   To curate texts for increased perplexity while incorporating safety implication, we prompt GPT-4o
319   to adjust the input texts iteratively, guided by instructions to integrate the seed set we previously
320
      prepared. As illustrated in Figure 3, GPT-4o is given an explicit prompt to incorporate the seed set
      and explores different combinations of (T , P) across multiple generations. We further employ a
321
      beam search process to filter and retain the most promising (curated) texts aligned with our goals.
322
323   Beam Search. We apply beam search to iteratively curate texts and progressively increase their
      perplexity. Unlike breadth-first search (BFS), which uses all samples generated from the current

      round for the next, beam search retains only the top-k results based on a ranking process. To rank the
325   curated texts, we not only consider perplexity but also introduce a helpfulness score to ensure the
326   curated texts remain helpful in terms of query relevance, clarity of expression, comprehensiveness,
327   and the usefulness of knowledge. GPT-4o is used to rate each text on a scale of 1-5 across these four
328   aspects (rubrics are presented in Tables 4-7), with the final helpfulness score being the average.
329
      Using both perplexity and helpfulness scores, we first filter out texts whose helpfulness scores have
330
      decreased by more than 10% compared to the original texts. The remaining texts are then ranked
331   based on descending perplexity, and the top-k (empirically set to 3) are selected. These selected texts
332   are used for the next round of output sampling and beam search, allowing for continued increases in
333   perplexity and integration of safety implication.
334
      Instruction Fine-Tuning. After obtaining curated texts from multiple iterations (set to 5 in this work)
335
      of output sampling and beam search, we fine-tune LLMs in all customization stages, as described in
336
      Section 3.2. The instructions (prompts) for fine-tuning are constructed from different parts of the
337   curated texts. Typically, each text sample consists of a pair of (query, response), where the response is
338   curated and integrated with safety implication. We use the query to form instructions and the curated
339   responses as expected outputs, fine-tuning LLMs using Huggingface framework (Huggingface, 2021).
340

344   6.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTING
345
346   Dataset: We use two groups of datasets: (1) security-domain queries from AdvBench and BeaverTails
347   to evaluate whether LLMs (jailbroken or robustified) produce safe responses; and (2) commonsense
348   queries from Alpaca, BeaverTails and Dolly to assess whether LLMs provide helpful responses in
349   commonsense tasks. The fine-tuning set is also sampled from commonsense queries with no overlap
350
      with the evaluation set.
351   Evaluation Metrics: Following prior work Zou et al. (2023); Qi et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023a),
352   we use two key metrics to evaluate the safety of LLM responses: (1) safety rate (SR) — the fraction
353   of responses that provide safe and responsible information to security-domain queries, indicating the
354   defense’s effectiveness; and (2) safety score (SSAFE ) — a score ranging from 1 to 5, generated by
355   GPT-4o, that measures the safety level of LLM responses, with higher scores indicating a greater
356
      level of safety. Additionally, we use (3) helpfulness score (SHELP ) from Section 5 to evaluate the
      quality of LLM responses in providing useful information to queries.
357
358   Baseline: Existing defenses against fine-tuning-based jailbreaking are scarce, especially those that
359   do not introduce additional detection modules. We consider three groups of baselines: (1) NoDef
360   — no defense applied, inspired by the no-attack baseline used in Qi et al. (2023); (2) RandDrop —
361   inspired by Zhang et al. (2023b) with a random portion (20% and 50%) of the fine-tuning dataset
362
      dropped; and (3) PPLDrop — inspired by Hu et al. (2023), where we drop a portion (20% and 50%)
      of the fine-tuning dataset with the highest perplexity for a victim (robust) LLM, as higher perplexity
363
      often signals harmful text, as shown in Figure 2-(a)(c). In addition to these baselines, we compare our
364
      all-stage defense with single-stage ablations (pre-attack-only, in-attack-only, and post-attack-only).
365
366   Jailbreaking Attack: Building on the methods from Qi et al. (2023) and Yang et al. (2023), we
367   defend against two types of jailbreaking attacks: (1) ExHarm — which uses explicitly harmful texts,
368
      including step-by-step instructions for malicious actions; and (2) AOA — which uses instructions
      designed to turn LLMs into ”absolutely obedient agents” that follow any instruction, including
369
      harmful ones. By default, harmful examples comprise 10% of the fine-tuning dataset, sufficient to
370
      cause significant jailbreaking. We vary this proportion and analyze its impact in Section 6.3.
371
372   Defense Setting: In output sampling, we vary the temperature T and top-p P parameters, configuring
373   LLMs with every possible combination of (T , P), where T , P ∈ [0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0]. During beam
374
      search, we iteratively curate texts, stopping the process after five rounds. The curation examples are
      sampled from commonsense queries without overlap with the evaluation and fine-tuning sets. By
375
      default, we set the number of curation examples to match the number of jailbreaking examples (1:1),
376
      and we adjust this ratio in Section 6.3.
377
      A comprehensive list of experimental settings is provided in Appendix B.

404   6.2   M AIN R ESULTS : D EFENSE E FFECTIVENESS
405
406   Table 1 presents the performance of all defenses against ExHarm and AOA attacks. Notably, the
407   all-stage defense demonstrates the highest effectiveness, consistently mitigating jailbreaking attacks
408   across all evaluated LLMs, achieving over 96% SR and, in some cases, fully preventing jailbreaking
409   (100% SR) by guiding LLMs to generate safe responses. Furthermore, curating commonsense texts
410   for fine-tuning not only improves security but also enhances the helpfulness of LLM responses.
411   Among the single-stage defenses we propose, the post-attack defense proves to be the most effective.
412   We attribute this to the dominance of fine-tuning, as LLMs are often most influenced by the most
413   recent customization. Consequently, defenses applied after the attack have the strongest impact on the
414   model’s behavior. This also explains why pre-attack defenses are less effective—jailbreaking occurs
415   after the defense, significantly diminishing its impact. The in-attack defense also shows notable
416   effectiveness, outperforming the baselines and demonstrating its ability to neutralize the effects of
417   jailbreaking during the customization process.
418   Interestingly, baseline methods involving partial dataset removal may negatively impact LLM perfor-
419   mance, even when the models are already jailbroken. For instance, randomly dropping 50% of the
420   fine-tuning set can, in some cases, exacerbate the jailbreaking effect due to the lack of control over
421   removed data selection. Similarly, dropping samples based on perplexity can negatively affect the
422   model’s ability to provide helpful responses, as seen with PPLDrop-50% on Llama-2-13B. Too many
423   functional fine-tuning samples are discarded, hindering the model’s commonsense functionality. This
424   highlights the importance of retaining the original dataset intact while focusing on adding a small
425
      curated set of data, as proposed in this work, to enhance LLM robustness.

523   safety-relevant texts may not be requisite, as they do not provide a significant improvement in defense.

525   7    C ONCLUSION
526
527   We introduce ROBO C URE, a data curation framework mitigating jailbreaking attacks throughout all
528   fine-tuning stages of LLM customization. ROBO C URE curates commonsense texts by increasing
529   their perplexity and enhancing their safety implications, thereby embedding new knowledge into the
530   texts. When these curated texts are used to fine-tune a jailbroken LLM, they effectively mitigate
531   the jailbreaking effect and enhance the model’s robustness. Through experimental evaluation, we
532   demonstrate the effectiveness of ROBO C URE. Our approach offers a foundational step toward
533
      robustifying LLMs against jailbreaking attacks via data curation, without introducing additional
      components during LLM execution.

      To calculate the helpfulness score for beam search, we prompt GPT-4o to rate how well a response
      satisfies the query across four dimensions: relevance (Table 4), clarity (Table 5), comprehensiveness
868
      (Table 6), and usefulness of knowledge (Table 7). The final helpfulness score is the average of these
869
      four ratings.

873
874       Scoring Relevance (1-5)
875
876       1 (Not relevant at all): The text is entirely unrelated to the provided query or topic. It contains
877       no information that could be considered remotely relevant, and its inclusion is baffling or
878       nonsensical.
879
          2 (Slightly relevant): The text contains minimal relevant information, but its connection to the
880
          provided query or topic is tenuous at best. It may touch on a few tangentially related points, but
881       overall, it fails to address the main subject adequately.
882
883       3 (Moderately relevant): The text touches upon some aspects of the query or topic, but
884       significant portions remain irrelevant or only loosely connected. While it may contain snippets
885
          of relevant information, they are overshadowed by irrelevant content.
886       4 (Very relevant): The text is mostly relevant and directly addresses the query or topic with
887       minimal digression. It provides a focused and coherent discussion that closely aligns with the
888       main subject, offering valuable insights and information throughout.
889
          5 (Extremely relevant): The text is perfectly aligned with the provided query or topic, providing
890       comprehensive and highly relevant information. Every aspect of the text contributes directly to
891       the main subject, leaving no room for ambiguity or extraneous content.
