009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Softmax distributions are widely used in machine learning, including Large Lan-
012
               guage Models (LLMs) where the attention unit uses softmax distributions. We
013            abstract the attention unit as the softmax model, where given a vector input, the
014            model produces an output drawn from the softmax distribution (which depends on
015            the vector input). We consider the fundamental problem of binary hypothesis test-
016            ing in the setting of softmax models. That is, given an unknown softmax model,
017            which is known to be one of the two given softmax models, how many queries are
018            needed to determine which one is the truth? We show that the sample complexity
019            is asymptotically O(ϵ−2 ) where ϵ is a certain distance between the parameters of
020            the models.
021            Furthermore, we draw analogy between the softmax model and the leverage score
022            model, an important tool for algorithm design in linear algebra and graph theory.
023            The leverage score model, on a high level, is a model which, given vector input,
024            produces an output drawn from a distribution dependent on the input. We obtain
025
               similar results for the binary hypothesis testing problem for leverage score models.

148   Hypothesis Testing Hypothesis testing is a central problem in statistics. In hypothesis testing,
149   two (or more) hypotheses about the truth are given and an algorithm needs to distinguish which
150   hypothesis is true. The most classic testing problem is the binary hypothesis testing. In this problem,
151   two distributions P0 and P1 are given, and there is an unknown distribution P which is either P0 or
152   P1 . The goal is to distinguish whether P = P0 or P = P1 by drawing samples from P . This problem
153
      is well-studied, with Neyman & Pearson (1933) giving tight characterization of the possible error
      regions in terms of the likelihood ratio. It is known that the asymptotic sample complexity of binary
154
      hypothesis testing for distributions is given by Θ(H −2 (P0 , P1 )), where H denotes the Hellinger
155
      distance, see e.g., Polyanskiy & Wu (2023+). There are other important kinds of hypothesis testing
156   problems. In the goodness-of-fit testing problem, a distribution Q is given, and there is an unknown
157   distribution P which is known to be either equal to Q or far away from Q. The goal is to distinguish
158   which is the true by drawing samples from P . In the two-sample testing problem, two unknown
159   distributions P and Q are given, and it is known that either P = Q or P and Q are far away from
160   each other. The goal is to distinguish which is true by drawing samples from P and Q. For these
161   problems there are no simple general characterization as in the binary hypothesis testing. However,
      for reasonable classes of distributions such as Gaussian distributions or distributions on discrete

      We review the classic hypothesis testing problem for distributions.
207   Definition 2.4 (Binary hypothesis testing for distributions). Let P0 , P1 be two distributions on the
208   same space. We have sample access to a distribution P , which is known to be either P0 or P1 . The
209
      goal is to determine whether P = P0 or P = P1 , using as few samples as possible. We say an
      algorithm successfully distinguishes P0 and P1 is at least 2/3 under both hypotheses.
210
211   In the above definition, the constant 2/3 can be replaced by any constant > 1/2, and the asymptotic
212   sample complexity of the binary hypothesis testing problem does not change. The reason is that if
213   we have an algorithm that achieves success probability δ > 12 , then we can run it independently
214   a constant number of times and take the majority of the outputs. Thus, we can boost the success
215   probability to an arbitrarily high constant. A classic result in information theory states that the
      sample complexity of the binary hypothesis testing problem is determined by the Hellinger distance.

465   5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
466
467   Widely applied across various domains, softmax and leverage scores play crucial roles in machine
468   learning and linear algebra. This study delves into the testing problem aimed at distinguishing
469   between different models of softmax and leverage score distributions, each parameterized by distinct
470   matrices. We establish bounds on the number of samples within the defined testing problem. With
471
      the rapidly escalating computational costs in current machine learning research, our work holds
      the potential to offer valuable insights and guidance for distinguishing between the distributions of
472
      different models. We discuss a few possible directions for further research. In Theorem 3.5 and
473
      Theorem 4.3, we determine the local sample complexity of the binary hypothesis testing problems
474   for softmax models and leverage score models. In particular, the sample complexity is Θ(ϵ−2 ν),
475   where ν is a certain function depending on A and M (where B = A + ϵM ). The form of ν is an
476   optimization problem over the space of possible inputs. An interesting question is to provide bounds
477   on the quantity ν, or to provide computation-efficient algorithms for determining the value of ν of
478   finding the optimal input (x for softmax, s for leverage score). This will lead to computation-efficient
479   algorithms for solving the binary hypothesis testing problem in practice.
480   In this paper, we focused on the binary hypothesis testing problem, where the goal is to distinguish
481   two models with different parameters. There are other hypothesis testing problems that are of interest
482   both in theory and practice. For example, in the goodness-of-fit problem, the goal is to determine
483   whether an unknown model is equal to or far away from a given model. In the two-sample testing
484   problem, the goal is to determine whether two unknown models are the same or far away from each
485   other. These problems have potential practical applications and we leave them as an interesting
      future direction.

      Softmax Computation and Regression Softmax computation, a crucial element in attention com-
      putation (Vaswani et al., 2017), plays a pivotal role in the development of LLMs. Several studies
964
      Alman & Song (2023); Brand et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023c); Deng et al. (2023c) delve into the
965
      efficiency of softmax computation. To improve computational efficiency, Alman & Song (2023)
966   presents a quicker attention computation algorithm utilizing implicit matrices. Similarly, Brand
967   et al. (2023) utilizes lazy updates to speed up dynamic computation, while Deng et al. (2023c) em-
968   ploys a randomized algorithm for similar efficiency gains. Conversely, Liu et al. (2023c) utilizes an
969   approximate Newton method that operates in nearly linear time. Gao et al. (2023) centers on the con-
970   vergence of overparameterized two-layer networks with exponential activation functions, whereas
971   Deng et al. (2023b); Liu et al. (2023c) explore regression analysis within the framework of attention
      computation. All of these studies specifically focus on softmax-based regression problems.
