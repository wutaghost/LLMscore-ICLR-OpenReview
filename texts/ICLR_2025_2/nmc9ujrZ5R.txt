026                                                                         A BSTRACT
027
028                        Recent advances in 2D image generation have achieved remarkable quality,
 Zero-1-to-G
029                        largely driven by the capacity of diffusion models and the availability of large-
030                        scale datasets. However, direct 3D generation is still constrained by the scarcity
031                        and lower fidelity of 3D datasets. In this paper, we introduce Zero-1-to-G, a
032                        novel approach that addresses this problem by enabling direct single-view image-
033
                           to-3D generation on Gaussian splats using pretrained 2D diffusion models. Our
                           key insight is that Gaussian splats, a 3D representation, can be decomposed into
034
                           multi-view images encoding different attributes. This reframes the challenging
035
                           task of direct 3D generation within a 2D diffusion framework, allowing us to
036                        leverage the rich priors of pretrained 2D diffusion models. To incorporate 3D
037                        awareness, we introduce cross-view and cross-attribute attention layers, which
038                        capture complex correlations and enforce 3D consistency across generated splats.
039                        This makes Zero-1-to-G the first direct 3D generative model to effectively utilize
040                        2D pretrained diffusion priors, enabling efficient training and improved general-
041                        ization to unseen objects. Extensive experiments on both synthetic and in-the-wild
042                        datasets demonstrate superior performance in 3D object generation, offering a new
043                        approach to high-quality 3D generation.

               1     I NTRODUCTION
046
047
           Single image to 3D generation is a pivotal challenge in computer vision and graphics, supporting var-
048        ious downstream applications such as virtual reality and gaming technologies. A primary difficulty
049        lies in managing the uncertainty of unseen regions, as these areas represent a conditional distribu-
050        tion based on the visible portions of a 3D object. Recent advancements in diffusion models (Ho
051        et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022) have demonstrated significant efficacy in capturing complex data
052        distributions within images and videos, prompting researchers to harness these models for single
053        image to 3D generation. Early efforts distilled 3D neural fields from pretrained 2D diffusion models
           via score distillation (Poole et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023). However, these approaches necessitate

054
      per-scene optimization, which is time-consuming and susceptible to multi-faced Janus problems.
055   Subsequent research achieved feed-forward generation by fine-tuning pretrained models to generate
056   multi-view images of the same object (Liu et al., 2023b; Shi et al., 2023a; Long et al., 2023; Liu
057   et al., 2023c) and enabling indirect 3D generation through sparse-view reconstruction models (Li
058   et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024), and Cat3D Gao et al. (2024) further extends sparse
059   view generation to dense view generation for better reconstruction. Although these two-stage meth-
060   ods enhance quality and efficiency, they often yield poor geometric fidelity and blurry renderings
061   due to inconsistencies in multi-view images. To circumvent these limitations, recent methodologies
062   have trained diffusion models directly on 3D representations (Liu et al., 2023d; Chen et al., 2023a;
063
      Zhang et al., 2024a; He et al., 2024; Nichol et al., 2022), thereby eliminating the reliance on multi-
      view images. However, direct 3D generation techniques necessitate training from scratch, requiring
064
      substantial computational resources and large 3D datasets, which remain scarce—three orders of
065
      magnitude less prevalent than 2D data.
066
067   In this paper, we propose a novel approach for direct 3D generation that unites the strengths of
068   both worlds: it leverages the expressive power of 2D diffusion networks while maintaining the 3D
069
      structural consistency required for accurate 3D generation. Our key contribution is bridging the
      gap between Gaussian splats and natural images typically used in 2D generation tasks. While the
070
      original Gaussian splats consist of 14-channel images encoding various attributes, we decompose
071
      each of them into multiple 3-channel attribute images while preserving its 3D information (Sec. 3.1)
072   This decomposition also enables efficient latent diffusion training by projecting the splatter images
073   into the latent space of a pretrained VAE, making our method directly generate 3D structures within
074   a pretrained 2D diffusion framework. To ensure strong 3D consistency, we introduce cross-view
075   attention layers in Stable Diffusion to enable information exchange between different viewpoints
076   and cross-attribute attention mechanisms to maintain coherence across Gaussian attributes within a
077   splatter image (Sec. 3.2). Additionally, we fine-tune the VAE decoder to address the domain gap
078   between splatter images and natural images, as we observed that splatter image quality is highly
079   sensitive to pixel-level variations (Sec. 3.3). By leveraging pretrained priors, our method not only
080
      generalizes better to unseen objects but also improves training efficiency compared to existing 3D
      generation methods.
081
082   It is important to note that, although we generate multiview splatter images, our method produces
083   more structurally consistent and higher-quality results compared to traditional two-stage multiview
084   3D generation approaches. In two-stage methods (Xu et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024), strong pixel-
085   level consistency is required in the first stage to ensure accurate reconstruction in the second stage,
086
      which is often difficult to achieve. Moreover, the first stage operates independently of the sec-
      ond, lacking coordination between the two, thereby exacerbating inconsistencies. In contrast, our
087
      approach directly generates the final 3D representation in a single stage, eliminating the need for
088
      pixel-level correspondence between multiview splatter images. Since splatter images can contain
089   redundant information, and their spatial positions do not necessarily map to the final 3D positions,
090   this single-stage process offers greater flexibility and robustness, ensuring a more consistent final
091   3D structure.

106   Optimization-based 3D Generation Dreamfusion (Poole et al., 2022) and subsequent works (Lin
107   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2023) utilize a pretrained text-to-image diffusion model
      to optimize a 3D representation through score distillation. DreamGaussian (Tang et al., 2023) sig-

      nificantly reduces training time by optimizing Gaussian splats. However, score distillation-based
109   methods still require minutes of optimization per scene, as they must compare renderings with dif-
110   fusion outputs from various viewpoints, which limits their generation speed. Additionally, these
111   methods lack a clear understanding of geometry and viewpoint, resulting in multi-face problems.

      Direct 3D Generation Significant efforts have been made to directly train diffusion models on var-
113
      ious 3D representations, including point clouds (Luo & Hu, 2021; Zhou et al., 2021; Nichol et al.,
114
      2022; Jun & Nichol, 2023), meshes (Liu et al., 2023d), and neural fields (Chen et al., 2023a; Shue
115   et al., 2023; Müller et al., 2023). However, these methods are typically constrained to category-level
116   datasets and often struggle to generate high-quality assets. More recent approaches have begun en-
117   coding 3D assets into more compact latent representations (Zhang et al., 2023a; Lan et al., 2024;
118   Zhao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b; Hong et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024; ?), enabling diffu-
119   sion models to be trained more efficiently and enhancing generalization capabilities. Despite these
120   advancements, direct 3D generative models are still primarily trained on synthetic 3D datasets like
121   Objaverse (Deitke et al., 2024), which may hinder their ability to effectively handle more in-the-wild
122   inputs.
123   More closely related to our work are GVGen (He et al., 2024) and GaussianCube (Zhang et al.,
124   2024a), which investigate training diffusion models on Gaussian splats. Both approaches acknowl-
125   edge the challenges of directly learning diffusion models from Gaussian splats and propose orga-
126   nizing the Gaussian points into a more structured volume. Different from their methods, we adopt
127   a multi-view splatter image representation, enabling us to train diffusion models on Gaussian splats
128   directly with high efficiency.
129   Finetuning Pretrained Diffusion models To enhance the 3D awareness of 2D pretrained diffusion
130   models, MVDream (Shi et al., 2023b)integrates cross-view attention layers and fine-tunes them to
131   produce multi-view renderings. As an application, LGM (Tang et al., 2024) and InstantMesh (Xu
132   et al., 2024) utilize a pre-trained single-view to multi-view 2D diffusion model, transforming the
133   single view generation problem to a multi-view reconstruction task. These methods adopt various
134   compact 3D representations to adapt to the sparse view 2D input. This has greatly pushed the 3D
135
      generation to more complex data. However, this approach is constrained by the performance of the
      multi-view diffusion model, which often lacks strict multi-view consistency. This inconsistency can
136
      result in poor geometry and blurry textures in the final 3D reconstructions.
137
138   Some other works further leverage the power of pretrained diffusion models to generate data beyond
139   the domain of natural images. For instance, JointNet (Zhang et al., 2023b) uses two diffusion mod-
140   els for joint RGB and depth prediction. Marigold (Ke et al., 2023) fine-tunes pretrained diffusion
141
      models for monocular depth estimation from single image input. Wonder3D (Long et al., 2023)
      generates both multi-view RGB and normal maps from a unified diffusion model equipped with a
142
      domain switcher. Similarly, GeoWizard (Fu et al., 2024) predicts depth and normals from a single
143
      image using cross-domain geometric self-attention to maintain geometric consistency. Following
144   this line of work to adapting diffusion prior to images of other domains, our method aims to gen-
145   erate Gaussian splats represented as splatter images using pretrained 2D diffusion models, thereby
146   enhancing the ability of direct 3D generation to tackle in-the-wild images.
147
      3D Generation with Reconstruction-based methods Researchers have opted to train
148
      reconstruction-based models for highly efficient 3D generation Hong et al. (2023); Tochilkin et al.
149
      (2024); Woo et al. (2024); Zou et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2023). LRM (Hong et al., 2023) and Tri-
150   poSR (Tochilkin et al., 2024) introduced a transformer-based model that directly output a triplane
151   from a single image. The model is trained on million-scale data by comparing the renderings of the
152   triplane with ground truth using regression-based loss. TriplaneGaussian (Zou et al., 2023) further
153   used a hybrid triplane-Gaussian representation to greatly accelerate the rendering of the generated
154   3D assets. However, the main drawback of regression-based methods is their failure to account
155   for the uncertainty in single-view to 3D generation. Although GECO (Wang et al., 2024) attempts
156   to address this issue by distilling knowledge from multi-view diffusion models into a feedforward
157   model, its results remain limited by the quality of the generated multi-view images.
158   Direct 3D Generation with 2D Diffusion Concurrent works Yan et al. (2024) and Elizarov et al.
159   (2024) also propose methods of generating 3D with 2D diffusion models. They use UV atlas to
160   encode the geometry and texture of 3D mesh, while we use Splatter Image Szymanowicz et al.
161   (2023) as 3D representation. Since Omage Yan et al. (2024) uses a 12-channel UV atlas, its model

      3     M ETHODS
184
185   Our method Zero-1-to-G is a single stage direct 3D generation: given single view input I, Zero-1-
186   to-G generates the corresponding 3D representation z, where z = {zi |i = 1, ..., N } multiple splatter
187   images under N camera views.
188   To harness the power of large-scale pretrained 2D diffusion models for direct 3D generation, we
189   represent each 3D object as a set of multi-view splatter images (Szymanowicz et al., 2023). In
190   Sec. 3.1, we detail our decomposition process, converting each multi-view splatter image into five
191   2D attribute images corresponding to RGB color, scale, rotation, opacity, and position. This decom-
192   position allows us to effectively leverage the priors of 2D pretrained diffusion models to learn the
193   underlying 3D object distribution (Sec. 3.2). Furthermore, we fine-tune the VAE decoder to enhance
194   the rendering quality of the decoded splatter images (Sec. 3.3).
195
196
197   3.1   R EPRESENTING 3D OBJECTS AS 2D IMAGES
198
199   A splatter image is a rearrangement of Gaussian splat into regular grids of H ×W , with 14 channels
200   stacking 5 Gaussian splat attributes, including 3-channel RGB, 4-channel rotation, 3-channel scale,
201
      3-channel position, and 1-channel opacity. By applying the data transformation in A.1, each attribute
      can be represented as a 2D image that explicitly models the object’s appearance or geometry. Our
202
      key observation is that, each attribute image is well modeled within the distribution of pretrained
203
      2D diffusion model, which means the pretrained 2D diffusion prior might be helpful in the gener-
204   ation of all these attribute images. Motivated by previous works that leverage pretrained diffusion
205   priors for domain-specific images that contain rich 3D information such as normal or depth (Ke
206   et al., 2023; Long et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2024), we take a step further and generate five attribute
207   images of Gaussian splats. Since StableDiffusion is trained on image data in the range [−1, 1], we
208   normalize the values of different attributes to ensure compatibility. Detailed operation is included in
209   the appendix A.1. After applying the data transformations, we convert the 14-channel splatter into
210   a set of 5 multi-view, 3-channel attribute images that are normalized to the RGB space. Example
211   visualization of multi-view splatters are shown in Figure 2.
212   To obtain our splatter image ground truth, we fine-tune LGM (Tang et al., 2024) to generate Gaussian
213   splats using six views of 3D object renderings as input. A straightforward way would be to directly
214   fit Gaussian splats from multi-view renderings of each object. However, we found that this direct
215   fitting results in attribute images with excessive high-frequency signals compared to LGM output
      (Figure 3), which makes it challenging for the pretrained VAE to decode effectively.

      Modeling Multi-view Distribution Prior works have approached multi-view diffusion either by
271   reshaping the batch dimension into a token dimension and applying self-attention (Shi et al., 2023b;
272   Liu et al., 2023c; Long et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024), or by spatially concatenating multi-view images
273   to form a larger image, which directly maps the latent distribution to a multi-view distribution (Shi
274   et al., 2023a). We choose the former approach for its flexibility in reshaping data for both cross-view
275   and cross-attribute attention mechanisms. This design allows for efficient information exchange
276   among different views, where tokens corresponding to the same attribute from different views are
277   concatenated for self-attention. This facilitates our model’s ability to learn a consistent multi-view
278   distribution for each Gaussian attribute.
279   Modeling Multi-Attribute Distribution Building on the work of (Long et al., 2023), we utilize
280   an attribute switcher to specify which attribute the network should generate. To maintain consis-
281   tency across generated images that represent different attributes of the same object, we employ an
282   attention mechanism to capture the interactions between images taken from the same viewpoint but
283   corresponding to different attributes.
284   Specifically, we introduce additional self-attention modules to model the cross-attribute correlations,
285   where tokens representing all attributes from the same viewpoint are combined and processed using
286   standard scaled dot-product attention.

      3.3   VAE D ECODER F INE - TUNING
318
319   The pretrained VAE of Stable Diffusion is initially trained on natural images. While directly utilizing
320   this VAE can produce visually appealing splatter images, the 2D renderings often exhibit noticeable
321   artifacts. This issue arises from two main factors: (1) each pixel in the splatter image corresponds to
322   a Gaussian splat, meaning that even minor changes in pixel values can significantly impact the final
323   rendering, and (2) splatter images contain high-frequency details that are challenging for the VAE
      to recover accurately.


                                                                       6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      To leverage the pretrained knowledge while enhancing rendering quality, we freeze the VAE encoder
325   and only fine-tune the decoder. This strategy preserves the latent space of the pretrained model.
326   In addition to using the standard reconstruction loss based on the direct decoder output, we also
327   evaluate the rendering quality of the decoded splatter by comparing its renderings with ground truth
328   images. This rendering-based loss is essential, as a low reconstruction error in the splatter image
329   does not guarantee high-quality renderings. The rendering loss is composed of MSE loss and LPIPS
330   loss, defined as follows:
331                                          Lrgb = LMSE + LLPIPS                                          (7)
332
333   The overall objective function of decoder finetuning is defined as:
334
335                               Ldecoder = Lsplatter + Lnormal + Lrgb + Lmask                            (8)
336
337
      where Lsplatter denotes the reconstruction loss of splatter image itself, while Lnormal , Lrgb and Lmask
338   are all about the renderings of the reconstructed splatter, denoting cosine similarity loss of rendered
339   normals, the sum of all losses of rendered images, and the binary cross-entropy loss of the rendered
340   masks.

343
344   4.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
345
346   Dataset We train on the G-buffer Objaverse (Qiu et al., 2024) dataset, which consists of approxi-
347   mately 262,000 objects sourced from Objaverse (Deitke et al., 2024). Each object in the dataset is
348   rendered from 38 viewpoints, with additional normal and depth renderings provided. For generat-
349   ing the ground truth splatter images, we use the first viewpoint as the input condition, along with
350
      five additional views at the same elevation and azimuth angles of 30°, 90°, 180°, 270°, and 330° to
      comprehensively cover the full 360 degrees. We only use the RGB and normal renderings for the
351
      supervision of decoder finetuning and Gaussian Splats prediction model.
352
353   Model Training and Inference We initialize our model from Stable Diffusion Image Variations.
354   Following Wonder3D (Long et al., 2023), our training includes two stages. In the first stage, we
355   only train multi-view attention, and in the second stage, we add one more cross-domain attention
356
      layer for training, and together fine-tune the multi-view attention layer learned in the first stage. For
      the first stage, we use a batch size of 64 on 4 NVIDIA L40 GPUs for 13k iterations, which takes
357
      about 1 day. For the second stage, we use a batch size of 64 on 8 NVIDIA L40 GPUs for 30k
358
      iterations, which takes about 2 days. For decoder fine-tuning, we use a total batch size of 64 on
359   8 NVIDIA L40 GPUs for 20k iterations. The second stage of training takes about 2 days. During
360   inference, we use cfg = 3.5 and our method can generate Gaussian splats per object in 8.7 seconds
361   on a single NVIDIA L40 GPU.

368   and animals, as in SyncDreamer (Liu et al., 2023c). For each object, we render a condition image
369   at a resolution of 512 × 512 and an elevation of 10◦ . Additionally, we generate evenly sampled
370   evaluation images at 30◦ azimuthal intervals around the object, maintaining the same elevation.
371   We report commonly used metrics for novel view synthesis, including PSNR, SSIM (Wang et al.,
372   2004), and LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018). Beyond the GSO dataset, we also evaluate our methods on
373   in-the-wild images.

375   gories. For reconstruction-based methods, we include TriplaneGaussian (Zou et al., 2023) and Tri-

      we include InstantMesh (Xu et al., 2024) and LGM (Tang et al., 2024).

      exhibits blue and overly smooth textures in the background (first and third examples in Figure 4),
424
      a result of artifacts in the multi-view images generated by ImageDream (Wang & Shi, 2023). Fur-
425
      thermore, their reconstruction model struggles to maintain view-consistent 3D Gaussians, leading
426   to “floaters” around the object (fourth and fifth examples in Figure 4). While InstantMesh provides
427   more consistent renderings, its outputs tend to be overly smooth, as it fuses inconsistent multi-view
428   images into a triplane representation. And its texture also suffers from grid-like artifacts brought
429   by its mesh generation process. Both LGM and InstantMesh are constrained by the limitations of
430   upstream 2D multi-view image generation models, resulting in unreasonable geometry and view-
431   inconsistency features (last example in Figure 4). In contrast, our method incorporates inherent 3D
      representations, yielding accurate geometry and view-consistent renderings.

      5     C ONCLUSION
520
521
      In this work, we introduce a novel framework that leverages 2D diffusion priors for direct 3D gen-
      eration by decomposing Gaussian splats into multi-view attribute images. This decomposition pre-
522
      serves the full 3D structure while efficiently mapping it to 2D images, enabling fine-tuning of pre-
523
      trained Stable Diffusion models with cross-view and cross-attribute attention layers. Our approach
524   significantly reduces computational costs compared to other direct 3D generation methods. By by-
525   passing the stringent requirement for multi-view image consistency in two-stage approaches, we
526   generate more accurate 3D geometry and produce higher-quality renderings through a single-stage
527   diffusion process. Furthermore, our method exhibits stronger generalization capabilities than exist-
528   ing direct 3D generation techniques due to the use of diffusion priors, offering a more efficient and
529   scalable solution for 3D content creation.
530   In future work, we plan to extend our approach to 4D generation by fine-tuning video models with
531   dynamic Gaussian splats mapped to multi-view attribute images. While our method produces high-
532   quality 3D results, it currently faces limitations in speed due to the multi-step denoising process,
533   which results in slower inference speed. To address this, future work will explore consistency models
534   as a potential solution to improve inference speed while maintaining the quality of 3D generation.

716   A.1    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
717
      Splatter Image Transformation
718
      Each attribute, except opacity, possesses three degree-of-freedoms, which align gracefully with the 3
719   channels of the RGB space. The following illustrates the detailed operations to convert each attribute
720   into an RGB image
721
722         • RGB: the RGB attribute naturally lies in the RGB space, no conversion is needed.
723         • Position: We normalize the 3D object in the bounding box [−1, 1] and use the 3D
724           coordinates of each Gaussian as position attribute.
725         • Scale: The raw scale value spans from 1e−15 to 1e−2, so directly converting the 3D
726           scale to RGB space using the min-max value for the whole dataset will result in most
727           regions being zeros due to the significant difference in power. The value distribution
728           also does not match the normal distribution, making it difficult for diffusion models
729           to learn effectively. We thus convert the raw scale values to log-space and clamp the
730           minimum values to −10, as we found Gaussian splats with scales smaller 1e−10 will
731           have negligible effects on the final rendering.
732         • Rotation: We first convert the 4-dimensional quaternion to 3-dimensional axis angle,
733           then normalize it to [−1, 1].
734         • Opacity: We directly duplicate the single channel to 3 channels, and average the pre-
735           dicted 3-channel image to get the final opacity prediction.
736
737   UNet Fine-tuning When fine-tuning the StableDiffusion UNet, for both stages, we use a constant
738   learning rate of 1e−4 with a warmup of the first 100 steps. We use the Adam optimizer for both
739
      stages and the betas are set to (0.9, 0.999). For classifier-free guidance, we drop the condition image
      with a probability of 0.1.
740
741   LGM Fine-tuning To obtain the splatter image ground truth for our training, as mentioned in
742   Sec. 3.1, we fine-tune LGM (Tang et al., 2024) to take as input 6 multi-view renderings of the
743   G-Objaverse dataset and output splatter images of 2D Gaussian splatting (Huang et al., 2024). The
744   training objective is to compare the splatter renderings with ground truth images using MSE and
745
      LPIPS loss. We also use cosine similarity loss between ground truth normals and rendered normals.
      We fine-tune LGM for 30k iterations with a batch size of 32 on 8 NVIDIA L40 GPUS, which takes
746
      about 1 day.

      A.2    L IMITATIONS
749
750   Despite achieving superior reconstruction metrics and strong generalization to in-the-wild data, our
751   method has some limitations. First, our inference speed is not as fast as regression models, as each
752   splatter must be generated through diffusion. A potential improvement would be to integrate a con-
753   sistency model Song et al. (2023) to reduce denoising steps and enhance inference speed. Second,
754   we do not currently disentangle material and lighting conditions, leading to highlights and reflec-
755   tions being baked into the Gaussian splat texture. Future work could address this by incorporating
      inverse rendering to better predict non-Lambertian surfaces.
