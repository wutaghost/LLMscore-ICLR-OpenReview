009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Speculative decoding has proven to be an efficient solution to large language
012            model (LLM) inference, where the small drafter predicts future tokens at a low
013            cost, and the target model is leveraged to verify them in parallel. However, most
014            existing works still draft tokens auto-regressively to maintain sequential depen-
015            dency in language modeling, which we consider a huge computational burden
016            in speculative decoding. We present PARALLEL S PEC, an alternative to auto-
017            regressive drafting strategies in state-of-the-art speculative decoding approaches.
018
               In contrast to auto-regressive drafting in the speculative stage, we train a paral-
               lel drafter to serve as an efficient speculative model. PARALLEL S PEC learns to
019
               efficiently predict multiple future tokens in parallel using a single model, and it
020
               can be integrated into any speculative decoding framework that requires aligning
021            the output distributions of the drafter and the target model with minimal training
022            cost. Experimental results show that PARALLEL S PEC accelerates baseline meth-
023            ods in latency up to 62% on text generation benchmarks on Medusa and 9-17% on
024            EAGLE. It also achieves 2.84× overall speedup on the Llama-2-13B model using
025            third-party evaluation criteria.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) and Llama (Touvron et al., 2023)
031   have shown dominant abilities across various domains, such as question answering (Zhuang et al.,
032   2023), code synthesis (Rozière et al., 2023), machine translation (Zhang et al., 2023a) and beyond.
033
      However, their auto-regressive nature requires multiple forward passes on models with billions or
      trillions of parameters, bringing substantial inference latency, thus prohibiting real-time applica-
034
      tions. In the pursuit of accelerating LLM inference, various strategies have been explored, including
035
      utilizing model sparsity (Liu et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Schuster et al., 2022; Cai et al., 2024a),
036   exploiting redundancy in KV Cache (Cai. et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2024a), and
037   distilling model capabilities to smaller models (Gu et al., 2024; Agarwal et al., 2024). While these
038   approaches can lead to faster inference, they often come at the cost of reduced generation quality
039   and do not preserve the generation distributions of the original models.
040
      Speculative decoding (SD) (Leviathan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a) has been proposed as one
041
      of the compelling alternatives to auto-regressive generation in a lossless manner. The key motiva-
042   tion behind SD is to utilize a low-cost small model to generate draft tokens efficiently and then use
043   the target model to verify them in parallel to ensure sampling integrity, known as draft-then-verify
044   framework. While promising, we observe that draft models in most draft-then-verify frameworks
045   still generate token by token, resulting in a low arithmetic intensity during the drafting stage. More-
046   over, the forward latency of the drafting stage still grows linearly with respect to the draft length, i.e.,
047   the number of tokens each draft step generates. As empirically profiled in the right part of Figure 1,
048   the draft latency still accounts for a significant proportion of the overall SD latency.
049   Researchers have spotted the draft latency issue and proposed several solutions to alleviate it by
050   dynamically determining the draft length either through learnable policy (Huang et al., 2024a),
051   confidence-guided heuristics (Li et al., 2024c), or optimized draft tree structures (Wang et al.,
052   2024a). Nevertheless, these solutions do not tackle the underlying issue of drafting latency scal-
053   ing linearly with the draft length, but operate only for maximally reducing the compute wasted in
      drafting tokens that are unlikely to be accepted.

072   To fundamentally solve the draft latency issue, we propose building a parallel-decoding drafter as
073   the replacement for auto-regressive drafters in popular SD frameworks. Unlike Medusa-style frame-
074   works (Cai et al., 2024b; Ankner et al., 2024) that rely on separate language model heads to decode
075   future tokens, we propose to use a single lightweight model to decode the next k tokens simul-
076   taneously. We argue that using a single model for multi-token prediction can effectively leverage
077   parameter sharing to achieve efficient drafter alignment rather than learning several independent
078   language model heads. The latter design would struggle even more with memory and computation
079   in the large vocabulary size (128,000+) of recently introduced language models such as Llama-
080
      3 (AI@Meta, 2024). For efficient multi-token alignment training, we introduce a group-wise paral-
      lel training strategy that mitigates possible training-inference mismatches by dynamically adjusting
081
      the attention mask, positional indexes, and token layout.
082
083   Our method still adheres to the draft-and-verify framework at inference time: At each draft step,
084   the drafter generates k tokens with a single forward pass, and then they are sent to the target model
085   for parallel verification. Since our method incorporates token-level parallelization in the drafting
086
      stage, both stages in the SD pipeline now benefit from this parallelization. Therefore, we name our
      approach PARALLEL S PEC. PARALLEL S PEC works as an individual module, ready to replace any
087
      drafter in existing SD frameworks that require distilling drafters from their target models. We ex-
088
      periment with the popular speculative decoding framework Medusa (Cai et al., 2024b) and state-of-
089   the-art solution EAGLE (Li et al., 2024b) by replacing their drafters. Experimental results show that
090   PARALLEL S PEC is able to bring consistent acceleration improvement in all task domains and differ-
091   ent combinations of models. For instance, incorporating PARALLEL S PEC into Vicuna-7B Medusa
092   increases the average speedup ratio from 1.42× to 2.31×, leading to a 62.7% relative improvement.
093   This empirically validates the superiority of parallel drafter design. PARALLEL S PEC integration
094   with EAGLE also achieves extra speedups across all target model settings, ranging from 2.55× to
095   2.84×, with a relative improvement ranging from 9% to 17%. In summary, our key contributions
096   are as follows:
097
      • We propose PARALLEL S PEC as a parallel multi-token drafter to replace the auto-regressive drafter
098
        design in existing SD frameworks that require aligning drafters with their target models.
099
100   • We design a group-wise training strategy that allows efficient and accurate parallel drafter training.
101   • We integrate the proposed method into two popular SD frameworks. Extensive experiments
102     demonstrate the compatibility and performance superiority of PARALLEL S PEC.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
106
107   Accelerating Large Language Model (LLM) Inference has attracted considerable research atten-
      tion from both machine learning system and natural language processing communities and even led


                                                            2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the trend of hardware-software co-design. These research efforts include model compression (Sun
109   et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024b; Ma et al., 2023), novel architecture design (Gu & Dao, 2023; Peng
110   et al., 2023) and hardware optimization (Dao et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2023). However, some of
111   these methods could lead to generation discrepancies compared to the target model, representing a
112   trade-off between model performance and inference speed. We consider those methods that cannot
113   generate with the target model’s original distribution as lossy acceleration methods.
114
115
      Speculative Decoding (SD) (Leviathan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a) arises as one of the lossless
      acceleration methods for LLM inference. It is based on the observation that the latency bottleneck of
116
      LLM inference is brought by memory bandwidth instead of arithmetic computation. SD alleviates
117
      the bandwidth bottleneck by utilizing a small model to draft multiple tokens and verifying them in
118   parallel with the target model, thereby reducing the frequency of language model calls and decreas-
119   ing the memory access density during decoding. The community has witnessed many improvements
120   in efficiently and accurately drafting tokens. Self-speculative decoding methods (Hooper et al.,
121   2023; Elhoushi et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a; Bhendawade et al., 2024) do not explicitly rely
122   on draft models but use some of the intermediate layers of the target model to draft. Medusa-style
123   methods add independent (Cai et al., 2024b) or sequential (Ankner et al., 2024) decoding heads on
124   the target model to draft tokens. Lookahead decoding and its variants (Fu et al., 2024; Zhao et al.,
125   2024) use n-gram trajectory as drafts. DistillSpec (Zhou et al., 2024) leverages knowledge distil-
126
      lation to closely align distributions between the draft and target models. PEARL (Liu et al., 2024)
      proposes two-stage verification to alleviate the mutual waiting problem. Apart from efficient draft
127
      methods, token tree verification (Miao et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023) has been widely adopted for
128
      verifying top candidate sequences that share common prefixes in parallel. Specialized SD frame-
129   works for long-context generation (Chen et al., 2024a;b), retrieval-augmented generation (He et al.,
130   2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024b) and beyond (Chen et al., 2023b) have been proposed
131   to better fit individual use cases.
132
133   Parallel Decoding was first known for its efficiency in machine translation system (Ghazvininejad
134   et al., 2019) and code generation (Gloeckle et al., 2024) as an alternative to auto-regressive gener-
135
      ation. However, its usage in SD frameworks remains under-explored. Cai et al. (2024b) and Stern
      et al. (2018) utilize parallel language model heads to predict multiple tokens at different positions.
136
        Santilli et al. (2023) proposed to use fixed-point iterations to replace auto-regressive decoding.
137
      Monea et al. (2023); Yi et al. (2024) pioneered the use of parallel decoding in SD, but it is limited
138   to a self-speculative framework and results in different generation sampling. BiTA (Lin et al., 2024)
139   proposed using prompt tuning to train a small number of prompt parameters on a frozen target LM
140   for semi-autoregressive generation. Wu et al. (2024) suggested using trainable linear projection to
141   regress intermediate hidden states of target models, thereby enabling multi-token prediction. How-
142   ever, these methods either fail to effectively learn the draft distribution due to the limited number of
143   learnable parameters or cannot achieve lossless acceleration due to the method design.

150   Notation. Speculative decoding (SD) frameworks maintain two models: the target model, denoted
151   as MT , is the one which we want the SD frameworks to sample from; the draft model, denoted as
152   MD , is the one that proposes candidate tokens which are later being verified by the target model. Let
153   x<t be the prompt sequence we are running the SD framework on, p (xt | x<t ), q (xt | x<t ) be the
154   inference distribution of MT and MD given the prompt x<t , respectively. We use the denotations of
155
      p (yt ) and q (yt ) to indicate p (yt | x, y<t ) and q (yt | x, y<t ) whenever they do not lead to confusion.
156   Speculative Decoding Procedures. One round of speculative decoding can be divided into the
157   drafting and verification stages, each governed by the corresponding model. The drafting stage auto-
158   regressively calls MD to sample γ candidate token distributions q (yt ) , . . . , q (yt+γ−1 ). The verifi-
159   cation stage calls MT once to sample γ distributions from the target model, p (yt ) , . . . , p (yt+γ−1 ),
160   given yt+i is the concatenation of y<t and drafted token sequence x1 , . . . , xi , where xk ∼ q (yt+k ).

      Average Acceptance Length. One important efficiency metric to measure an SD system is the
191   acceptance rate of drafted tokens in each round of speculative decoding. Since each drafting step
192   takes a constant amount of time and each round of conventional SD takes the same drafting steps, the
193   overall efficiency of an SD system will be determined by the average acceptance length τ measured

198
      the top predicted tokens at different token positions and manipulating the attention mask based on a
199
      tree structure, we enable the processing of multiple candidate sequences with only one verification
200   step. We refer to the details of token tree verification in Appendix A.3.

453
454
      Table 1 gives a performance overview of PARALLEL S PEC and established prior approaches on dif-
      ferent types of text generation benchmarks. We refer to Appendix A.1 for qualitative case studies. In
455
      general, PARALLEL S PEC integration brings a consistent acceleration improvement in all domains,
456
      two selected methods, and different decoding temperatures. Based on the results, we have the fol-
457   lowing key observations:
458
459   PARALLEL S PEC significantly accelerates Medusa frameworks by a large margin. For example,
460   integrating PARALLEL S PEC into Vicuna-7B Medusa boosts the average speedup ratio from 1.42×
461   to 2.31×, resulting in a 62.7% relative improvement. This even outperforms EAGLE, which can be
462   attributed to the parallel drafter design. It not only increases the average acceptance tokens from
463
      2.39 to 3.31 but also significantly reduces drafter runtime overhead. Similar improvements are also
      observed on Vicuna-13B Medusa, showing PARALLEL S PEC is not sensitive to the target model size.
464
465   Equipping EAGLE with PARALLEL S PEC also achieves considerable speedups. For instance,
466   the average speedup ratio on Vicuna-7B increased from 2.18× to 2.55×; on Llama2-7B-Chat, it rose
467   from 2.40× to 2.74×; and on LLaMA-13B-Chat, it went from 2.60× to 2.84×. It is worth noting
468   that incorporating PARALLEL S PEC into EAGLE causes a slight drop in average acceptance length.
469   This is fully expected, as we no longer preserve the sequence dependency during the draft stage, and
470   the parallel decoding leads to a small decline in drafting accuracy. In addition, we also conduct ex-
471   periments on EAGLE-PARALLEL S PEC with temperature decoding settings and observe substantial
472
      overall speedup up to 2.22×. Still, the speculative decoding efficiency at high temperature degrades
      compared with greedy decoding, echoing conclusions in previous studies (Xia et al., 2024).

481
      multi-turn conversations by feeding the prompts from ShareGPT in a greedy decoding setting. Ta-

507   6        C ONCLUSION
508
509   In this paper, we introduce PARALLEL S PEC, a powerful speculative decoding solution that could
510   be inserted into popular speculative decoding frameworks. It proposes to use a single lightweight
511   model and several trainable [MASK] tokens to facilitate fast multi-token prediction as drafters,
512   thereby mitigating the issue of drafting latency scaling linearly with the draft length. Compared with
513
      the Medusa-style multi-head multi-token draft strategy, PARALLEL S PEC demonstrates significant
      advantages in drafting accuracy, latency, and parameter efficiency. Extensive experiments on various
514
      benchmarks and different target models demonstrate the compatibility and performance superiority
515
      of PARALLEL S PEC.

      A.3     T OKEN T REE V ERIFICATION
879
880   Initially proposed in SpecInfer (Miao et al., 2024), and also explored in several follow-up works (Cai
881   et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024b; Spector & Re, 2023), tree-based structure for token verification is
882   proved to be useful. Following existing studies, PARALLEL S PEC leverages tree attention to realize
883   this process. Specifically, to guarantee that each token only accesses its predecessors, we use an
884   attention mask that exclusively permits attention flow from the current token back to its antecedent
885   tokens. The positional indices for positional encoding are adjusted in line with this structure. A
886
      conceptual view of this process is visualized in Figure 6, with the draft tree structure in the figure
      being adopted in all experiments. As the main contribution of this paper is not a novel token tree
887
      verification strategy, we adopt the same static token trees used in Medusa-1 (Cai et al., 2024b) and
888
      EAGLE (Li et al., 2024b). Starting from “Root”, every node is expanded with k tokens with top-k
889   highest probabilities. k is designed based on manually crafted rules, and is dynamically changing as
890   the tree depth grows.
