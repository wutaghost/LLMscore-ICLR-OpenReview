009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Weight-preserving large language model editing techniques rely heavily on scop-
012            ing mechanisms that determine when to apply edits to the base model. These
013            mechanisms typically use distance functions in the representation space. How-
014            ever, we demonstrate that distance-based scoping functions struggle with strong
015            lexical biases, leading to issues such as applying edits to irrelevant prompts with
016            overlapping words. This paper presents Projector Editor Networks for Model Edit-
017            ing (PENME), a principled approach that learns the optimal representation space
018
               for scoping using contrastive learning. Specifically, PENME forms a disentan-
               gled representation space that facilitates precise localization of edits by maintain-
019
               ing substantial distance between irrelevant prompts while preserving proximity
020
               among paraphrases. In our empirical study, we show PENME achieves state-of-
021            the-art model editing results while being more computationally efficient during
022            inference and adaptable across different architectures.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated tremendous success in solving a diverse range
028   of natural language processing tasks (Devlin et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2019; Touvron et al., 2023b;
029   Radford et al., 2019). Despite their successes, LLMs are fallible. One reason for this discrepancy is
030   the noisy and imperfect nature of the data used for training (Zhu et al., 2020). As the world evolves,
031   new information requires updates to the models e.g. the prime minister of a country may change
032
      over time. Models trained on outdated data are prone to making factual errors.
033   Periodically retraining LLMs is one potential solution, but risks degrading performance, often re-
034   quiring training from scratch to maintain previous capabilities (Luo et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b).
035   Retraining requires significant computational resources, investment of time, data, and skilled labor.
036   To alleviate this, model editing was proposed to perform sample and compute efficient knowledge
037   updates. There are two primary model editing design paradigms: weight-modifying and weight-
038   preserving. Weight-modifying approaches directly update the model’s parameters to integrate new
039   information (Meng et al., 2022a;b). Although these approaches are sample efficient, they demand
040   substantial compute resources for training (Yu et al., 2024) and result in catastrophic forgetting
041   (Gupta et al., 2024a) the full impact of which is difficult to fully determine (Rosati et al., 2024).
042   In contrast, weight preserving approaches maintain the original model parameters while employ-
043   ing additional components to reflect updated knowledge, thereby avoiding catastrophic forgetting
044   (Hartvigsen et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024). These methods either utilize user inputs and memory
045   storage before a forward pass of the model (pre-input) or during a forward pass (post-input). Pre-
046   input approaches (Mitchell et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023) rely on retrieving relevant contexts
047   for model editing, this process requires additional components such as retrievers and counterfactual
048   models which make them computationally intensive. Post-input mechanisms (Hartvigsen et al.,
049   2024; Yu et al., 2024) involve adapter-based editing techniques that incorporate components within
050
      the model’s computational pathway, modifying its output to reflect edited information.
051   A core component of adapter-based techniques is a vector similarity scoping mechanism that utilizes
052   model representations and memory codebooks containing vector representations to determine when
053   to utilize computational paths associated with a given edit. However, we observe that adapter-
      based techniques struggle with handling paraphrases and often misfire on irrelevant prompts with

      In this work, we introduce Projector Editor Networks for Model Editing (PENME), an advancement
083   over previous adapter-based weight preserving model editing by explicitly targeting the lexical bias
084   problem in these scoping mechanisms. PENME comprises of two key components: a projector
085   network and a similarity-based retrieval system. The projector network is a compact, two-layer
086   neural network trained independently using contrastive learning to disentangle projection space such
087   that paraphrases of edits demonstrate proximity, while irrelevant prompts, both with and without
088   similar lexical overlaps, are farther away. Based on the outputs of the projector network, a memory-
089   based retrieval system facilitates efficient edit retrieval. This approach effectively addresses the
090   aforementioned challenges, while maintaining computational efficiency and ensuring compatibility
091
      with both encoder- and decoder-based architectures.
092   Our contributions are as follows: (1) We demonstrate that representations extracted across layers
093   from various LLMs exhibit lexical dominance, showing a bias towards token overlap which intro-
094   duces significant challenges for adapter-based model editing techniques. (2) We propose a projec-
095   tion network that maps the model’s representation space to a new representation space where lexical
096   dominance is minimized. (3) We integrate our projection network in an adapter and memory-based
097
      retrieval scheme for model editing, demonstrating high efficacy for paraphrase execution (general-
      ization), preventing misfires on irrelevant prompts (locality) and it’s generalization to unseen edits,
098
      paraphrases, and neighbours. The proposed projection network is a novel solution to the problem in
099
      hand. Moreover, It has broader impact to other application areas relying on representation similari-
100   ties such as retrieval augmented generation, however, it is out of the scope of this paper.

      salient neurons within the feed-forward layers, facilitating targeted updates to effect the desired edits
109   using causal analysis. Similarly, Li et al. (2024) investigates the role of multi-headed attention, in
110   conjunction with feed-forward layers, for model editing. Mitchell et al. (2021) uses a hypernetwork
111   to predict new weights for the model by using a low-rank decomposition of the weight matrices of
112   different layers. The goal is to edit information in the model parameters without impacting unrelated
113   information.
114
      Weight Preserving: pre-input approaches depend on extracting and processing relevant edit infor-
115   mation before the input is processed by the main model. For example, SERAC (Mitchell et al., 2022)
116   employs a memory-based model editing strategy augmenting a primary LLM with two additional
117   models and memory storage. The supplementary models determine scope-of-edit and perform coun-
118   terfactual reasoning. Retrieval-augmented (RAG) techniques like IKE (Zheng et al., 2023) leverage
119   similarity-based retrieval to extract and rank edit demonstrations from memory and use in-context
120   reasoning to perform edits.
121   Weight preserving: post-input rely on the model’s internal representations to implement scoping
122   mechanisms (mechanisms which determine whether a specific edit applies for the current input) and
123   employ a playback mechanism that triggers the model to generate modified outputs. For example,
124   GRACE (Hartvigsen et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024) operate an in-model adapter approach. These
125   approaches employ a codebook or memory storage system to maintain model representations of edits
126   as clusters. They utilize a vector similarity-based retrieval mechanism to generalize edit paraphrases
127   and constrain irrelevant or neighbouring prompts. Initial cluster sizes are deliberately restricted
128   to mitigate interference from neighbouring prompts and ensure that only paraphrases of the edit
129
      prompt are mapped within the cluster. However, this design necessitates continuous cluster resizing,
      as new edits with similar semantic and lexical properties may fall within an existing edit cluster.
130
      Furthermore, the initially small cluster radius necessitates the storage of multiple edit paraphrases in
131
      the memory codebook to achieve effective generalization, potentially leading to increased memory
132   consumption. The major difference in the approaches is that Hartvigsen et al. (2024) uses memory
133   playback vectors while Yu et al. (2024) uses LoRA blocks (Liu et al., 2024) for the generation
134   process. An alternative editing method involves enhancing the feedforward layer (FFN) within a
135   transformer block by incorporating additional neurons to facilitate the desired modifications. Huang
136   et al. (2023) introduce a single neuron per output token for edited information of single edit. In
137   this framework, each neuron, or a group of neurons, is specifically trained to activate solely for a
138   particular edit, thus adjusting the model’s output to produce the altered information.
139   Cluster-based similarity systems like GRACE and MELO Hartvigsen et al. (2024) and Yu et al.
140   (2024) rely on concept separability within the representation space to manually maintain keys in
141   their codebooks. However, our analysis reveals that lexically similar prompts cluster closer to ed-
142   its than their paraphrases, heightening the risk of system failure as can be seen from figure 1 and
143   3. Moreover, their cluster based design necessitates storing edit paraphrases as codebook entries
144   for effective generalization which increases retrieval latency. PENME overcomes this limitation by
145   learning a projection space that enhances representation structure, enabling more effective organiza-
146
      tion of keys for faster and more accurate retrieval. Furthermore, PENME consistently outperforms
      both weight-preserving and weight-modifying methods across various architectures, underscoring
147
      its adaptability and efficacy.

152   The objective of model editing is to alleviate the need for complete retraining by updating the model
153   under the following conditions (1) sample efficiency: update the model with the fewest number of
154   samples possible, (2) compute efficiency: train a small portion of the model only, (3) minimal im-
155   pact: make as small of an impact on unrelated behaviour as possible (for adapter-based approaches
156   this means preventing misfires on irrelevant prompts) and (4) ensure generalization: maintain accu-
157   rate paraphrase behaviour (for adapter-based approaches this means retrieval of the correct edits).
158   The aim is to modify the behaviour of a model M on a dataset D = [d1 , d2 , d3 ...dn ] where the

160   new output tokens, p1..n are the edit paraphrase prompts, nb1..n are neighbours or neighbourhood
161   prompts these are lexically and semantically similar prompts but ones where the underlying model
      generation should not change. To be successful in the model editing task, the edited model, Medited ,

203   4   P ROJECTOR E DITOR N ETWORKS FOR M ODEL E DITING (PENME)
204
205   PENME is a retrieval-based editor that leverages an adapter module which is integrated alongside
206   pointwise feed-forward layers within an attention block of a pre-trained Large Language Model
207   (LLM). By introducing this additional component rather than altering the original model weights,
208   PENME enables the integration of new information while preserving the LLM’s initial capabilities.
209
      PENME illustrated in Figure 2 consists of two components, (1) Projection Network (Mproj ): this
210   component projects model activations at a specific layer Ml (x) into a distinct representation space.
211   (2) Key-Value Codebook that stores model activations at layer Mproj (Ml (x)) as keys and cor-
212   responding values containing a learned similarity threshold (δ) and the new associated output in-
213   formation yi . It should be noted that instead of output information, vectors can also be stored as
214   values which facilitate playback approaches such as vector playback (Hartvigsen et al., 2024) and
215   LoRA blocks based playback (Yu et al., 2024). Output retrieval and playback are compatible with
      all transformer-based model architectures.

278
      Yu et al. (2024) requires multiple paraphrases added to the codebook to improve generalization. To
279
      enhance efficiency, merging operations are performed on nearby edits that produce identical outputs.
280   However, the efficacy of this consolidation is dataset-dependent; for example, zsRE demonstrates
281   a high frequency of similar edit outputs, enabling a significant reduction in codebook entries. For
282   example, 1000 edits on zsRE requires 658 entries in total but for Counterfact 1682 entries are needed
283   just for 300 edits. The combination of this consolidation process and the potential for edits to be
284   closely related in vector space leads to overlapping cluster radii, necessitating cluster size reduction.
285   This inadvertently results in the removal of certain edits. Thus edits can be forgotten. In contrast, our
286   method exhibits linear scaling with respect to the number of edits in the worst-case scenario where
287   each edit produces a unique output as exhibited in Appendix C. This characteristic allows for more
288
      rapid edit retrieval compared to the aforementioned approach. Furthermore, our method facilitates
      straightforward edit removal or updates, offering enhanced flexibility in edit management.

373     6.2                D ISENTANGLED P ROJECTION S PACE

527
528
529   In this paper, we proposed PENME an adapter-based model editing approach that utilizes a projec-
530   tion network trained via contrastive learning. PENME explicitly targets the lexical bias present in
531
      representations that causes misfiring of editing scope. Moreover, it used a memory-based storage
      system alongside the scoping mechanism for efficient edit retrieval. Empirical evaluations demon-
532
      strated PENME’s superior performance across varying levels of task complexity. On the zsRE
533
      dataset, It achieved impressive generalization and locality scores exceeding 0.90. Notably, when
534   assessed on the more challenging Counterfact benchmark, the system maintained robust perfor-
535   mance, attaining scores above 0.80 for both generalization and locality metrics. This performance
536   on Counterfact is particularly significant given the benchmark’s increased difficulty, underscoring
537   PENME’s efficacy. In the future, we plan to assess whether a projector, pretrained on a large dataset
538   to maximize semantic information, could be used as a plug-and-play solution without requiring ad-
539   ditional training. Moreover, we intend to expand PENME to encompass more scenarios, including
      long-form generation.

      Its
781   In evaluating our approach, we adhere to metrics outlined in section 3. Regarding generalization,
782   we define a paraphrase as generalized if it aligns with the correct edit and falls below its distance
783   threshold. For assessing locality, we maintain that locality is preserved when the distance between
784   matched edits exceeds its threshold. Any other instances are categorized as misfires. It is important
785   to note that (Hartvigsen et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024) utilize token F1 Accuracy and (Mitchell et al.,

1025   To investigate the reasons behind failures in PENME, we performed a comprehensive error analysis
       across our models. Our findings indicate that contrastive learning significantly mitigates lexical


                                                                                         19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       dominance. However, due to the inherent variability in lexical pattern distribution within the dataset,
1027   there remains potential for further optimization in the projection phase.
1028
1029   The training process of the projector network does not lead to uniform distances between each edit,
1030
       its paraphrases and neighbours for all samples. This paired with individually varying thresholds
       for edits leads to misfires. To illustrate this problem, we format the results of each dataset sample
1031
       for automatic inspection. For all paraphrases and neighbours in the test set, we extract the nearest
1032
       key/edit, the ground truth edit/key, the distance to the nearest key/edit, and the distance to the ground
1033   truth edit/key. Table 7 shows rouge scores (Lin, 2004) for two possible scenarios i.e. success and
1034   failure of generalization and locality. We also show separately the score for where generalization
1035   failure occurs due to distance not meeting the set threshold. Moreover, since failures can occur in
1036   similarities with unrelated edits we show locality and paraphrase failure with both ground truth edit
1037   and matched edit.
1038
       For cases of successful generalization, we observe a substantial uni-gram overlap and a moderate
1039   bi-gram overlap between the edited sentences and their paraphrases. The ROUGE-L scores are
1040   similarly high for these metrics, indicating that the sentences likely share similar tokens in the same
1041   sequence. This implies that the attention mechanism produces similar representations, leading to a
1042   high degree of similarity. For locality success, we can see that although there is significant token
1043   overlap between neighbours and their target edits, the neighbours had higher similarity with some
1044   other edits with low token overlap, this means our approach of pushing neighbouring sentences
1045   farther away is able to generalize to unseen neighbours.
1046   In cases of generalization failure, the ROUGE scores for paraphrases compared with the ground
1047   truth are slightly lower than those observed in successful instances. Although there is some token
1048   overlap with the target edits, the matched edits exhibit even less token overlap. On the other hand for
1049   locality failure, we can see that the prediction case token overlap is higher as compared to locality
1050   success, moreover, the overlap is higher as compared to ground truth edits. Thus lexicality based
1051   similarity is not the issue but rather the varying thresholds, which in some cases are large leads to
1052   misfires.

1055
1056   Training the projection network in PENME using the contrastive learning scheme is sensitive, re-
1057   quiring tuning of hyperparameters such as the learning rate and contrastive loss margin. Effective
1058   network training also hinges on the careful construction of training data, which requires careful
1059   consideration of the number of edit pairings with other dataset neighbours and edit-to-edit pairings.
1060   Finally, the thresholds for the memory-based retrieval system, though dynamically determined from
1061   training data, can vary across different models, necessitating adjustments to the alpha (a) parameter
1062
       for each model.
