010
011
               Biological fluid simulation is a critical tool for comprehending the intricate and
012
               complex fluid dynamics that occur within bio logical systems. Recently, data-
013
               driven techniques have emerged as a promising avenue to enhance the accuracy
014            and efficiency of biological fluid simulations. However, the community encounters
015            two challenges. (1) Existing biological datasets only capture static snapshots,
016            lacking the ability to capture dynamic biological processes. (2) These datasets
017            are limited in scale due to the demanding experimental conditions. To address
018            these challenges, this paper introduces four comprehensive large-scale datasets:
019            Tension, Wets, CellDivision and Jellyfish, containing a wealth of biological dynam-
020            ics and pushing the boundary of data-driven methods. These datasets have been
021            meticulously designed to encompass a wide array of biological fluid dynamics
022
               scenarios. By incorporating physical modeling techniques such as phase-field
               method, these datasets provide a standardized evaluation framework for data-driven
023
               approaches. They empower researchers to objectively assess and compare different
024
               methodologies, fostering advancements in the field of biological fluid simulation.
025            Furthermore, the availability of these benchmark datasets facilitates reproducibility
026            and enhances the comparability of results across studies, promoting knowledge
027            sharing and collaboration within the research community. Researchers can build
028            upon existing models, leading to cumulative progress in the development of ac-
029            curate and efficient data-driven models for simulating complex fluid dynamics
030            within biological systems. We offer benchmark code and CellDJBench dataset link
031            through the following link: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/ CellDJBench.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036
037   Biological fluid simulation (Liu, 2005; Marques et al., 2011) plays a crucial role in various scientific
038   and engineering domains, enabling the investigation of complex fluid dynamics (Tian et al., 2014)
039
      within biological systems. The accurate modeling of fluid behavior is essential for understanding
      phenomena such as blood flow in cardiovascular studies, the motion of swimming organisms, and the
040
      transport of nutrients within biological tissues. Traditional approaches (Kucera et al., 2024; Peng
041
      et al., 1999) to fluid simulation, have been widely used but often struggle to capture the intricate and
042   complex dynamics exhibited by biological fluids. In recent years, data-driven approaches (Chen et al.,
043   2016a; Weng & Zhu, 2021), leveraging machine learning (O’shea & Nash, 2015) and deep learning
044   techniques (Ho et al., 2020), have shown great promise in enhancing the accuracy and efficiency of
045   biological fluid simulations.
046
      However, the adoption of data-driven techniques like FNO (Li et al., 2020) and SFNO (Bonev et al.,
047
      2023) in biological fluid simulation is hindered by several challenges. Importantly, the lack of
048   standardized large-scale datasets (Hassan et al., 2023) poses a significant obstacle to evaluating and
049   comparing different methodologies. Existing datasets are often limited in scope (Ahlmann-Eltze &
050   Huber, 2023; Yang et al., 2022), failing to capture the diverse range of biological fluid dynamics or
051   encompassing challenging scenarios. This limitation inhibits the objective assessment of various
052   approaches and hampers the reproducibility and comparability of research findings. Developing
053   models that can effectively handle the diverse physical properties, boundary conditions, and dynamic
      behavior of biological fluids remains an active area of research.

054
      Although researchers have explored a lot on constructing biological datasets, there are some chal-
055   lenges that existing biological datasets cannot solve. (1) Existing biological datasets only capture
056   biological snapshots, which cannot capture biological dynamics. (2) Current datasets rely on rigorous
057   experimental conditions, such as specific temperature settings, making them challenging to acquire
058   and limited in scale. Some experiments, like scRNA-seq on cell, are destructive, thereby hinder-
059   ing observations of cell trajectories. In contrast, living-image (in video format) data demonstrates
060   exceptional congruence with physical modeling techniques such as phase-field methods. These
061   models harness the quantitative intricacy inherent in live-imaging data to establish computational
062   representations of cellular behavior, providing a robust framework for theoretical investigation and
063
      hypothesis validation. Thus, to solve these challenges, we aim to construct large-scale biological
      living-image datasets like single-cell dynamic datasets via phase-field methods that are beneficial for
064
      capturing biological dynamics and further providing a valuable resource to the research community.
065
066   In this paper, we create and release four large-scale biological datasets: two cell evolving datasets
067   (i.e., Tension and Wets), one cell division dataset (CellDivision) and one Jellyfish dataset. These
068   datasets are specifically designed to capture a wide range of biological fluid dynamics scenarios,
069
      encompassing tension-driven flows, wetting phenomena, cell divisions, and the intricate motion of
      jellyfish-like organisms. Each dataset is meticulously curated, incorporating variations in physical
070
      parameters, such as adhesion, tension, and flow rates, to simulate the complexity and heterogeneity
071
      of real-world biological systems.
072
073   Meanwhile, we also provide standardized evaluation benchmarks for data-driven biological fluid
074   simulation, enabling researchers to benchmark their models against challenging and diverse sce-
075
      narios. By establishing a common ground for comparison, these datasets facilitate the objective
      assessment and comparison of different data-driven methodologies, fostering the advancement of
076
      the field. Furthermore, the availability of these benchmark datasets promotes reproducibility and
077
      comparability of results across studies, highlighting the strengths and limitations of each approach
078   in capturing the intricate dynamics of biological fluids. Researchers can build upon and improve
079   existing models (Schindler & Kutzelnigg, 1983; Ronneberger et al., 2015), enabling cumulative
080   progress in biological fluid simulation. In summary, our contributions are provided as follows:
081
      • New Perspective. We are the first to investigate fluid dynamics modeling in biological systems
082
        at both the dynamic single-cell level and the jellyfish level. Unlike previous single-cell research
083
        focusing on a single snapshot, we simulate data across different time points, pioneering future
084     research such as cell division simulation.
085   • Large-scale Biological Datasets. We collect around 1.3 TB of raw data using a new biological tech-
086     nique, resulting in four large-scale fluid dynamics datasets including Tension, Wets, CellDivision
087     and Jellyfish. Our datasets have been made publicly available.
088   • Extensive Benchmarks. We make the first attempt to adopt 11 data-driven approaches for our new
089     dynamics modeling problem, validating the potential applications of machine learning in single-cell
090     biological systems. Our benchmark helps researchers evaluate their data-driven approaches to
091     advance the field.

301   3.3   C ELL D IVISION C ONSTRUCTION
302
303
      Our CellDivision dataset was created utilizing MATLAB software, employing numerical simulations
      to model cell growth and division processes during Natural condition. The study revolves around the
304
      generation of stochastic cell characteristics, with the progression of cell life cycles simulated within a
305
      three-dimensional space using the Finite Difference Method (FDM). Various aspects of cell dynamics
306   such as division events, mechanical properties, trajectory paths, and velocity estimations are modeled.
307   The simulations are executed on a 3D grid with a spatial resolution of dx = 0.5 in the spatial domain,
308   while the temporal evolution is captured with a time step of dt = 1. The grid size and time step
309   configuration are optimized to ensure precise representation of subtle cellular movements and state
310   transitions. Each simulation iteration yields randomized values for cell division axes, volumes, and
311   lifespans. Parameters σ = 0 and c = 2 regulate the mechanical characteristics of the simulated cells,
312   enabling the investigation of cell behaviors under diverse environmental conditions.
313   The CellDivision dataset encompasses detailed cell attributes, time-series records, and trajectory
314   paths. Converting this data into CSV format facilitates further analysis and modeling procedures.
315   This dataset offers high-resolution temporal data sequences and spatial distribution details, which are
316   invaluable for in-depth exploration of cellular dynamics and related phenomena.
317
      Specifications. Our CellDivision dataset offers an unprecedented opportunity to advance our under-
318   standing of cell division dynamics under natural conditions. To be specific, it incorporates crucial
319   biological details, including complete cell lineages (e.g., AB, E, MS, P), precisely determined cell
320   fates (e.g., Muscle, Intestine), and the environmental conditions (e.g., Natural) influencing devel-
321   opment. Researchers can now explore the intricate, dynamic trajectories of individual cells as they
322   evolve, providing insights into the complex interplay of factors governing cell division. The high-
323   resolution nature of the data, achieved through sophisticated numerical simulation, mirrors the detail
      achievable in state-of-the-art experimental techniques. This remarkable similarity to experimentally

      3.4   J ELLYFISH C ONSTRUCTION
335
336   Using the Lily-Pad simulator (Weymouth, 2015), we generate training and testing datasets. A
337   128 × 128 grid represents the 2D flow field, extending infinitely. The jellyfish’s head is fixed at
338   (25.6, 64), while its symmetric wings are ellipses with 20 boundary points. Wing shape maintains a
339   fixed 0.15 ratio between shorter and longer axes. The control signal, denoted as w, determines wing
340   opening angle. Trajectories follow a periodic cosine curve with a period of T ′ = 200, starting with
341
      the widest angle. Simulated trajectories consist of 600 steps, stored as a sequence of 20 discrete steps
      from T ′ = 200 to 3T ′ = 600. The datasets include PDE states, opening angles, boundary points, and
342
      image-like representations of boundaries. The Jellyfish dataset captures dynamic jellyfish locomotion
343
      in a 3D environment using a Jellyfish-Robot. It comprises 500 videos and 10,000 images, providing
344   a comprehensive resource for studying aquatic organism locomotion and driving advancements in
345   biomimetics, robotics, and marine biology. Further details can be found in Appendix A.3.
346
347
      Specifications. Our Jellyfish dataset provides a unique perspective on the dynamic characteristics
      of the relationship between jellyfish-robot angle and water pressure. By analyzing living-images of
348
      jellyfish-robot angle change trends, we gain valuable insights into this intricate relationship. Moreover,
349
      this dataset not only captures trajectory changes of individual jellyfish but also holds significant
350   potential for advancing research in underwater robot design and control. Additionally, our dataset
351   stands out in terms of its extensive scale, allowing researchers to leverage large language models or
352   other foundational models. The comprehensiveness and depth of this dataset open up new avenues
353   for innovative research inquiries, empowering the scientific community to explore novel aspects of
354   jellyfish-robot dynamics.
355
      Extensions. The Jellyfish dataset continues to make a significant impact on the scientific community.

358   scope and high-resolution attributes have the potential to enhance the applications of the Jellyfish

368   Benchmark Methods: In this section, we introduce baselines for simulating evolving cells: FNO (Li
369
      et al., 2020), SFNO (Bonev et al., 2023), TFNO, UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015), and DDPM (Ho
      et al., 2020). FNO utilizes Fourier space for resolution-invariant global convolutions but has high
370
      memory requirements. SFNO uses spherical harmonics for handling spherical data with optimized
371
      memory usage. TFNO is a variant of FNO that adopts Tucker factorization of the weights. UNet is
372   renowned for precise object localization and segmentation, achieving exceptional performance in
373   various applications. DDPM gradually transforms noise-corrupted data to the original distribution
374   through iterative diffusion, with training involving sampling and loss function computation. ConvL-
375   STM (Shi et al., 2015) proposes the prediction of future precipitation patterns as a task of forecasting

465   5     R ELATED W ORK
466
467   Comparison with Existing Biological Dynamics Datasets. With the rapid advancement of AI
468   in scientific research, comprehensive biological datasets are becoming increasingly important, as
469   evidenced by recent works such as (Dannenfelser et al., 2024; Pan et al., 2003; Gilpin et al., 2020;
470   Kucera et al., 2024). However, current biological datasets face several limitations and are unable to
471
      fully meet the rapidly evolving demands of AI in the biological domain. For instance, Dannenfelser et
      al. (Dannenfelser et al., 2024) have introduced FlaMBé (Flow annotations for Multiverse Biological
472
      entities), a comprehensive compilation of meticulously curated datasets that encompass a diverse
473
      range of tasks aimed at capturing procedural knowledge found in biomedical texts. This dataset is
474   driven by the realization that academic papers, particularly the methodologies sections, serve as a
475   significant but disorganized source of procedural knowledge. However, FlaMBé is limited by its
476   small scale and lack of cellular-level data, and it fails to capture diverse cell lineages and fates under
477   varying developmental conditions. Additionally, Kucera et al. (Kucera et al., 2024) have proposed
478   a Python software package that simplifies the creation of datasets and the evaluation of models for
479   deep learning on protein structures. But it does not cover biological dynamics in terms of cells.
480   Motivated by the limitations above, our work aims to provide benchmark datasets that Tension, Wets
481   and CellDivision , which focus on cell evolution in terms of cell lineages, cell fates and developing
482   conditions. In addition, Jellyfish dataset covers the jellyfish moving process in terms of water pressure.
483   These datasets incorporate the evolving trends of time in both the jellyfish robot’s angle and the
484   time-evolving data-driven biological simulation. Thus, our contribution not only provides three
485   large-scale biological datasets but also establishes benchmarks for simulating these datasets within
      the realm of cell evolution.

496   6   P OTENTIAL A PPLICATIONS
497
498   Blood Flow in Cardiovascular Studies: The Tension and Wets dataset, and our datasets more
499   broadly, offer a valuable in silico model for investigating the intricate hemodynamics of blood flow
500   within the cardiovascular system. The dataset’s high fidelity allows for the simulation of complex
501   fluid dynamics, including the effects of non-Newtonian fluid properties and vessel geometry. This
502   capability enables the development and validation of AI-driven predictive models capable of assessing
503   the impact of various pathophysiological factors, such as atherosclerotic plaque formation, valvular
504   dysfunction, or stenosis, on blood flow patterns and shear stress distributions. Such models have the
505
      potential to significantly advance the design and optimization of medical interventions, including
      the planning of surgical procedures or the development of novel therapeutic strategies. Furthermore,
506
      the simulated data can be used to assess the efficacy of existing and novel treatments by providing
507
      a controlled environment for evaluating their impact on hemodynamic parameters. This approach
508   offers a powerful complement to in vivo and in vitro studies, potentially reducing the reliance on
509   animal models and accelerating the translation of research findings into clinical applications.
510
511
      The Motion of Swimming Organisms: The Jellyfish dataset offers a rich resource for studying
      the complex fluid-structure interactions driving jellyfish locomotion. Analyzing this dataset reveals
512
      details of their propulsive mechanisms, including morphology, contractions, and fluid dynamics. This
513
      understanding informs bio-inspired robotics, improving underwater vehicle design. Furthermore, it
514   enhances ecological modeling and our knowledge of marine life biophysics, impacting biomimetic
515   material development for improved hydrodynamic properties.
516
517
      Cell Tracking: The CellDivision dataset holds significant potential across diverse biological and
      computational research areas. Its high-resolution, biologically realistic simulations facilitate the
518
      development and validation of novel cell division models, allowing researchers to test hypotheses
519
      about the underlying mechanisms. The dataset can be used to train and evaluate machine learning
520   algorithms for cell segmentation, tracking, and fate prediction, improving the accuracy and efficiency
521   of image analysis in experimental studies. Furthermore, it enables the investigation of the effects
522   of genetic mutations or environmental changes on cell division dynamics, providing insights into
523   disease development and therapeutic interventions. The dataset’s potential extends to the design of
524   virtual experiments, reducing the need for costly and time-consuming laboratory work while allowing
525   for controlled exploration of a wider range of conditions. Finally, it can serve as a benchmark for
526   evaluating the performance of different computational methods in cell biology.
527
528   7   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
529
530   This paper addresses the challenges faced by the community in biological fluid simulation. Existing
531   datasets lack the ability to capture dynamic processes and are limited in scale. To overcome these lim-
532   itations, we introduce four comprehensive datasets: Tension, Wets, CellDivision and Jellyfish. These
533   datasets push the boundaries of data-driven methods by encompassing a wide array of biological fluid
534   dynamics scenarios. We also provide a standardized evaluation framework for data-driven approaches.
535   The availability of these benchmark datasets enables objective assessment and comparison of method-
536   ologies, promoting advancements in the field. Additionally, the datasets enhance reproducibility
537
      and comparability of results, fostering collaboration and knowledge sharing. While these datasets
      have limitations in representing all phenomena and scalability, they serve as valuable resources
538
      for researchers to develop accurate and efficient models for simulating complex fluid dynamics in
539
      biological systems. Detailed limitations and broader impacts are shown in Appendix A.6.

735   Diffusion Probabilistic Models: The Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM) Ho et al.
736   (2020) comprises two fundamental processes: the forward process (or diffusion process) and the
737   reverse process. Let’s begin by describing the forward process. In a diffusion model, the forward
738   process approximates the posterior distribution q(x1:t |x0 ), which represents the sequence of latent
739   variables x1:t given an initial value x0 . This approximation is achieved by iteratively applying a
740
      Markov chain that gradually adds Gaussian noise over time.
741   The forward process is represented as follows:
742                                  q(xt |xt−1 ) = N (xt ; µt (xt−1 ), βt I)
743
744   Here, xt denotes the latent variable at time step t, and xt−1 is the variable at the previous time step.
745   The distribution q(xt |xt−1 ) is modeled as a Gaussian distribution with mean µt (xt−1 ) and variance
746   βt I, where βt is the variance parameter at time step t, and I represents the identity matrix. The
747   mean µt (xt−1 ) can depend on the previous latent variable xt−1 and is typically modeled using neural
748
      networks or other parameterized functions.
749   By sequentially applying the distribution q(xt |xt−1 ) for each time step, starting from the initial value
750   x0 , we obtain an approximation of the posterior distribution q(x1:t |x0 ) that captures the temporal
                                                           QT
751   evolution of the latent variables via q(x1:t |x0 ) := t=1 q(xt |xt−1 ).
752
      Consider a diffusion model with T time steps. Given an observed data point xt at the final time step,
753
      the goal is to generate a sample from the initial distribution p(x0 ).
754
755   The reverse process in a diffusion model can be formulated as follows: 1. Initialization: Set xt as the
      observed data point. 2. Iterative Sampling: Starting from t = T − 1 and moving backwards until

757   process.

760
      similar to the forward process. However, the mean and variance parameters are adjusted to account
      for the reverse direction. The specific form of p(xt |xt+1 ) is defined as follows:
761

763   By iteratively sampling from the reverse process, we can generate a sequence of latent variables
764   x0:t that follows the reverse diffusion process. This reverse sequence represents a sample from the

766   training, the model learns to approximate the reverse process by minimizing the discrepancy between

776   Cahn-Hilliard Function: Wetting phenomena and interfacial tension play significant roles in
777   numerous scientific and engineering fields, ranging from fluid dynamics to materials science. In
778   recent years, phase-field methods have emerged as powerful computational tools for studying and
779   simulating wetting processes. These methods employ a phase-field variable, a continuous function
780
      that describes the local composition or wetting state, enabling the realistic modeling of complex
      interfacial dynamics. By incorporating the concept of interfacial tension, phase-field models can
781
      capture the intricate interplay between fluids and solid surfaces.One of the key equations used
782
      in phase-field modeling of wetting phenomena is the Cahn-Hilliard equation, which governs the
783   evolution of the phase-field variable. This equation takes into account the interfacial energy associated
784   with the fluid-solid interface and the interfacial tension between the two phases. The interfacial
785   tension term is crucial for accurately simulating the contact angle, adhesion, and spreading behavior.
786   The Cahn-Hilliard equation provides a mathematical framework to capture the dynamics of phase
787   separation by considering the free energy of the system. It takes the following general form:

788                                          ∂ϕ                   δF
789
                                                 = ∇ · M∇                                                     (5)
                                             ∂t                   δϕ
790   where ϕ is the phase-field variable or order parameter representing the local composition. t is time.
791   M is the mobility coefficient, controlling the rate of diffusion of the phase-field variable. F is the free
792   energy functional of the system with respect to the phase-field variable ϕ. The free energy functional
793   F typically consists of two terms: the bulk free energy term and the gradient energy term. The bulk
794   free energy accounts for the thermodynamic properties of the system, including the interfacial energy
795   between the two phases and the potential energy associated with phase separation. The gradient
796   energy term penalizes sharp variations or spatial gradients in the order parameter, promoting smoother
797   phase transitions.

877   Jellyfish Datasets Generation: To generate our training and testing datasets, we employ the Lily-Pad
878   simulator Weymouth (2015). The 2D flow field has a resolution of 128 × 128, assuming an infinite
879
      extension. For the jellyfish, the fixed coordinates for the head are set as (25.6, 64). The wings
      are represented as ellipses with an identical shape, with a fixed ratio of 0.15 between their shorter
880
      and longer axes. Symmetry is maintained across the central horizontal line defined by y = 64. To
881
      delineate the boundaries of the wings, we sample a total of M = 20 points along each wing. In this
882   2D experiment, the key control signal is the opening angle of the wings, denoted as w. This angle is
883   defined as the deviation between the longer axis of the upper wing and the horizontal line.
884
885
      Each trajectory commences with the widest possible opening angle and follows a periodic cosine curve
      with a period of T ′ = 200. The trajectories differ in their initial angle (w0 ), angle amplitude, and
886
      phase ratio (τ ). To determine the initial angle w0 , a two-step process is employed. Firstly, a random
887
      mean angle w(m) is sampled from the range of [20◦ , 40◦ ]. Then, a random angle amplitude w(a) is
888
      sampled from the interval [10◦ , min(w(m) , 60◦ − w(m) )]. The resulting initial angle is computed
889
      as w0 = w(m) + w(a) , constrained within the range of [10◦ , 60◦ ]. The phase ratio τ is randomly
890
      selected from the range of [0.2, 0.8]. The opening angle wt at step t adheres to a specific pattern:
891
      it decreases from w(m) + w(a) to w(m) − w(a) as t progresses from 0 to τ T ′ , and then it increases
892
      from w(m) − w(a) to w(m) + w(a) as t advances from τ T ′ to T ′ . Beyond T ′ , wt exhibits periodic
893
      variations. This configuration aligns with previous studies on jellyfish’s propulsive performance
894   Kang et al. (2023). Each trajectory is simulated for 600 steps, equivalent to 3 periods. Only the
895   segment of the trajectory from T ′ = 200 to 3T ′ = 600 steps is saved, with a step size of 10. This
896   decision is made to conserve space, as the simulation from t = 0 to T ′ = 200 is primarily used
897   for initializing the flow field. Consequently, each trajectory is stored as a sequence consisting of
898   T̃ = (600 − 200)/10 = 40 discrete steps.
899
      In addition to tracking the positions of the wing boundary points and the opening angles w, we
900
      incorporate an image-like representation of the wing boundaries. This representation contains spatial
901
      information that can be efficiently integrated with the PDE states (fluid field) through convolutional
902   neural networks. The image-like boundary representation seamlessly aligns with the shape of the
903   PDE states. At each time step, the boundaries of the two wings are combined and transformed into
904   a tensor with dimensions [3, 64, 64]. This tensor represents the spatial information in a grid-like
905   format. Each cell in the tensor contains three features: a binary mask indicating whether the cell is
906   part of a wing boundary (1) or within the fluid (0), and the relative position (∆x, ∆y), which denotes
907   the distance from the cell center to the nearest boundary point. For each trajectory, the following
908   components are saved: - PDE states u: This captures the fluid field states for each time step and has a
909   shape of [T̃ , 3, 64, 64]. It includes the velocity components in the x and y directions as well as the
910   pressure. The resolution is downsampled from 128 × 128 to 64 × 64. - Velocity: [T̃ , 2, 64, 64]. -
911   Pressure: [T̃ , 1, 64, 64]. - Opening angles w: This stores the opening angle in radians for each step
912   and has a shape of [T̃ ]. - Boundary points: This records the boundary points for both the upper and
913   lower wings and has a shape of [T̃ , 2, M, 2]. Each wing consists of M = 20 points, and each point is
914   represented by its coordinates in the x and y directions. The coordinates are scaled accordingly to fit
915   within the grid dimensions.

1086   FNO Li et al. (2020): The Fourier Neural Operator is a groundbreaking approach that utilizes
1087
       Fourier space to learn weights, enabling resolution-invariant global convolutions. This influential
       work has been further expanded upon by numerous other neural operators. However, one notable
1088
       drawback is the substantial memory requirements. Specifically, each weight matrix in the Fourier
1089
       domain consumes O(H 2 M D ) memory, where H represents the hidden size, M denotes the number
1090   of Fourier modes used after truncating high frequencies, and D signifies the problem dimension.
1091
1092   SFNO Bonev et al. (2023): Spherical Fourier Neural Operators utilizes spherical harmonics to
1093
       transform data into the frequency domain. This is analogous to the way FNO uses the Fourier
       transform for Cartesian grids but is specifically designed for spherical data. This approach allows
1094
       for handling data on the entire sphere without the distortions introduced by map projections. While
1095
       the traditional FNO faces substantial memory demands, SFNO optimizes memory usage through
1096   spherical harmonics. Despite this, the memory requirement for SFNO is still O(H 2 M 2 ), where H is
1097   the hidden size and M is the number of retained spherical harmonic modes.
1098
1099
       TFNO: Li et al. (2020) We improve the previous FNO model by simply using a Tucker Tensorized
       FNO with just a few parameters. This will use a Tucker factorization of the weights. The forward
1100
       pass will be efficient by contracting directly the inputs with the factors of the decomposition. The
1101
       Fourier layers will have 5% of the parameters of an equivalent, dense FNO.
1102
1103   UNet Ronneberger et al. (2015): The UNet architecture is known for its U-shaped design, which
1104   resembles an encoder-decoder structure. It is particularly effective for tasks that require precise
1105
       localization and segmentation of objects within images. UNet has achieved remarkable performance
       in various applications, including biomedical image segmentation, satellite image analysis, and more.
1106
       The Unet consists of the forward and backward passes. the time complexity is mainly driven by the
1107
       size of the input image or volume, denoted as O(N ), where N is the number of pixels or voxels. It
1108   is important to note that the specific implementation details and variations of UNet may introduce
1109   additional computational complexities.
1110
1111
       DDPM: Ho et al. (2020) It gradually transforms a noise-corrupted data version into the original
       distribution through iterative diffusion. Training involves two components: sampling and loss function
1112
       computation. Sampling complexity is O(S ∗ N ), where S is the diffusion steps and N is the step size.
1113
       The loss function compares generated samples with the original data, with complexity O(L ∗ N ),
1114   where L is the number of layers. Overall training complexity is approximately O(S ∗ N + L ∗ N ).
1115   DDPM models complex data distributions using denoising diffusion. Training time complexity is
1116   approximated as O(S ∗ N + L ∗ N ), where S is diffusion steps, L is layers, and N is layer size.
1117
       ConvLSTM Shi et al. (2015) postulates the prognostication of forthcoming spatiotemporal precipi-
1118
       tation patterns as an endeavor entailing the anticipation of sequential data in spatial and temporal
1119
       dimensions.
1120
1121   ViT Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) asserts that the reliance on convolutional neural networks (CNNs) is
1122   dispensable, as the direct application of transformers to sequences of image patches yields exceptional
1123
       performance in the classification of visual data.
1124   MLP-Mixer Tolstikhin et al. (2021) is exclusively constructed upon the foundation of multi-layer
1125   perceptrons (MLPs). It encompasses two distinct layer types: one that individually applies MLPs
1126   to image patches, while the other employs MLPs across multiple patches, promoting enhanced
1127   representation learning.
1128   DDOs-FNO Lim et al. (2023) advances a robust mathematical framework meticulously tailored for
1129   the training of diffusion models within the realm of function space.
1130
       DDOs-SFNO Lim et al. (2023) derives inspiration from the fusion of DDPM and FNO methodologies,
1131
       forging a cohesive amalgamation of their respective strengths.
1132
1133   DDOs-TFNO Lim et al. (2023) adopts a hybrid approach, drawing inspiration from both DDPM and
       TFNO methods, thereby capitalizing on their synergistic potential.

       Broader Impact: The introduction of comprehensive large-scale datasets, namely Tension, Wets,
1228
       CellDivision and Jellyfish, has significant implications for the field of biological fluid simulation.
1229
       These datasets address the challenges faced by the community, including the lack of dynamic biologi-
1230   cal process capture and limited scale of existing datasets. By providing a standardized evaluation
1231   framework and incorporating physical modeling techniques, the datasets empower researchers to
1232   objectively assess and compare data-driven methodologies. This fosters advancements in the field and
1233   promotes the development of accurate and efficient models for simulating complex fluid dynamics
1234   within biological systems. The availability of benchmark datasets also enhances reproducibility and
1235   comparability of results across studies, facilitating knowledge sharing and collaboration within the
1236   research community.
1237   Limitations: While the introduced datasets offer valuable resources for data-driven biological fluid
1238   simulation, they may have some limitations. First, the datasets are designed based on specific
1239   biological scenarios and may not encompass the full range of biological fluid dynamics. Researchers
1240   should be cautious when extrapolating findings to other systems. Second, the datasets rely on physical
1241   modeling techniques such as the phase-field method, which may introduce certain simplifications and
       assumptions that could impact the accuracy and applicability of the results. It is important to consider
