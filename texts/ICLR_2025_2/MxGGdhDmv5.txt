009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Transformers are now ubiquitous for sequence modeling tasks, but their extension
012
               to multi-dimensional data remains a challenge due the to quadratic cost of the
013
               attention mechanism. In this paper, we propose Higher-Order Transformers (HOT),
014            a novel architecture designed to efficiently process data with more than two axes,
015            i.e. higher order tensors. To address the computational challenges associated with
016            high-order tensor attention, we introduce a novel Kronecker factorized attention
017            mechanism that reduces the attention cost to quadratic in each axis’ dimension,
018            rather than quadratic in the total size of the input tensor. To further enhance
019            efficiency, HOT leverages kernelized attention, reducing the complexity to linear.
020            This strategy maintains the model’s expressiveness while enabling scalable attention
021            computation. We validate the effectiveness of HOT on two high-dimensional tasks,
022
               including long-term time series forecasting, and 3D medical image classification.
               Experimental results demonstrate that HOT achieves competitive performance
023
               while significantly improving computational efficiency, showcasing its potential
024
               for tackling a wide range of complex, multi-dimensional data.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      The Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) has revolutionized sequence modeling across
031   various domains, including computer vision (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), speech recognition (Dong
032   et al., 2018), and reinforcement learning (Parisotto et al., 2020), due to its self-attention mecha-
033   nism, which effectively captures long-range dependencies and complex patterns in sequential data.
034   However, extending Transformers to handle higher-order data—such as multidimensional arrays or
035   tensors—poses significant challenges due to the quadratic computational and memory costs of the
036   attention mechanism, limiting their application in tasks involving high-dimensional inputs, such as
037   video processing, multidimensional time series forecasting, and 3D medical imaging. High-order
038   data are prevalent in many real-world applications, including climate modeling, which relies on
039
      multidimensional time series data capturing temporal and spatial variations (Nguyen et al., 2023); 3D
      medical imaging, which adds depth to traditional 2D images (Yang et al., 2023); and recommendation
040
      systems, where user-item interactions over time and context are modeled as multidimensional tensors
041
      (Frolov & Oseledets, 2016). Efficiently processing such data requires models capable of capturing
042   intricate dependencies across multiple dimensions while avoiding prohibitive computational costs.
043
044   Several efforts have been made to adapt Transformers for multidimensional data. A common
045
      approach is to reshape or flatten the multidimensional input into a sequence (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020),
      effectively reducing the problem to a one-dimensional case. While this method allows the use of
046
      standard Transformers, it disregards the inherent structural information and local dependencies present
047
      in the data, as the positional encoding may also fail to communicate this information. Consequently,
048   models may fail to capture essential patterns and exhibit suboptimal performance. Another line of
049   research focuses on applying attention mechanisms along each dimension independently or in a
050   sequential manner. For example, axial attention (Ho et al., 2019) processes data along one axis at a
051   time, reducing computational complexity. As another example, (Song et al., 2016) applies spatial
052   and temporal attention sequentialy. However, this approaches may not fully capture interactions
053   between different dimensions simultaneously, potentially overlooking important cross-dimensional
      dependencies.

075   In this paper, we introduce Higher-Order Transformers (HOT), a novel architecture designed to
076   efficiently process high-dimensional data represented as tensors. An overall view of our architecture
077   is presented in Figure 1. Our key contributions are as follows:
078
079             • We propose a Kronecker decomposition of the high-order attention matrix, significantly
080               reducing the computational complexity.
081             • To push the boundaries of efficiency, we integrate kernelized attention mechanism into our
082               model reducing the complexity from quadratic to linear with respect to the input size.
083             • We validate the effectiveness of HOT on two challenging high-dimensional tasks: long-
084               term time series forecasting and 3D medical image classification. In addition, we provide
085               comprehensive ablation study on various aspects of HOT.

089   2    R ELATED W ORK
090
091   In recent years, various strategies have been developed to make Transformers more efficient for
092   high-dimensional data. One common approach is to flatten the input tensor into a sequence, as
093   in the Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), which treats image patches as tokens.
094   However, this approach disregards the structural dependencies within the data (Tolstikhin et al.,
095   2021; Lee et al., 2018). To better handle multidimensional structures, axial attention mechanisms
096   like the Axial Transformer (Ho et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020) apply self-attention along each axis
097   sequentially, reducing complexity but often missing cross-dimensional dependencies crucial for tasks
098   like 3D medical imaging (Hatamizadeh et al., 2022) and climate modeling (Rühling et al., 2022).
099
      Similarly, the Sparse Transformer (Child et al., 2019) reduces computation by attending to subsets of
      the input but struggles with global interactions. Kronecker Attention Networks (Gao et al., 2020)
100
      assumes the data to follow matrix-variate normal distributions and accordingly proposes Kronecker
101
      attention operators that apply attention on 2D data without flattening. Although the name suggests
102   similarities to our method, it is different from our method as it does not use Kronecker product or
103   decomposition. Tensorized Transformers (Ma et al., 2019) utilize tensor decompositions to reduce
104   memory usage but focus primarily on compression rather than improving cross-dimensional attention.
105   Linear Transformers (Katharopoulos et al., 2020) and Performer (Choromanski et al., 2020) bypass
106   the quadratic softmax bottleneck with linearized attention, making them scalable for long sequences
107   but limited in capturing complex multidimensional relationships. Sparse methods like Longformer
      (Beltagy et al., 2020) and Reformer (Kitaev et al., 2020) also reduce complexity by restricting attention

      to local neighborhoods, but they fail to handle global dependencies in higher-dimensional contexts.
109   Recent works have further improved efficiency and cross-dimensional attention. iTransformer (Liu
110   et al., 2024) optimizes for multivariate time series forecasting by reversing attention across variables,
111   while Crossformer (Zhang & Yan, 2023) uses cross-dimensional attention to capture dependencies
112   between spatial and temporal dimensions, specifically in time series tasks. CdTransformer (Zhu
113   et al., 2024) tackles the challenge of cross-dimensional correlations in 3D medical images with novel
114   attention modules, reducing computational costs and capturing 3D-specific dependencies.

118   In this section, we introduce key tensor operations that are fundamental to the high-order attention
119   mechanism. Vectors are denoted by lowercase letters (e.g., v), matrices by uppercase letters (e.g.,
120   M ), and tensors by calligraphic letters (e.g., T ). We use ⊗ to represent the Kronecker product and
121   ×i to denote the tensor product along mode i (Kolda & Bader, 2009). The notation [k] refers to the
122   set {1, 2, . . . , k} for any integer k.
123
124   Definition 1 (Tensor). A k-th order tensor T ∈ RN1 ×N2 ×···×Nk generalizes the concept of a matrix
125   to higher dimensions. A tensor can be viewed as a multidimensional array, where each element is
126   indexed by k distinct indices, representing data that varies across k dimensions.
127   Definition 2 (Tensor Mode and Fibers). A mode-i fiber of a tensor T is the vector obtained by fixing
128   all indices of T except the i-th one, e.g., Tn1 ,n2 ,...,ni−1 ,:,ni+1 ,...,nk ∈ RNi .
129   Definition 3 (Tensor Slice). A tensor slice is a two-dimensional section of a tensor, obtained by fixing
130   all but two indices, e.g., Tn1 ,n2 ,...,ni−1 ,:,ni+1 ,...,nj−1 ,:,nj+1 ,...,nk ∈ RNi ×Nj
131
132   Slices and fibers extend the familiar concept of matrix rows and columns to higher-dimensional
133   tensors, providing powerful ways to analyze and manipulate multi-way data.
134   Definition 4 (Tensor Matricization). The i-th mode matricization of a tensor rearranges the mode-i
135   fibers of the tensor into a matrix. It is denoted as T(i) ∈ RNi ×(N1 ···Ni−1 Ni+1 ···Nk ) .
136   Definition 5 (Mode n tensor product). The mode n product between a tensor T ∈ RN1 ×N2 ×···×Nk
137   and a matrix A ∈ Rd×NP   n
                                 is denoted by T ×n A ∈ RN1 ×N2 ×···×Nn−1 ×d×Nn+1 ×···×Nk and defined
138   by (T ×n A)i1 ,··· ,ik = j Ti1 ,··· ,in−1 ,j,in+1 ,··· ,ik Ain ,j for all i1 ∈ [N1 ], · · · , ik ∈ [Nk ].
139
140   We conclude this section by stating a useful identity relating matricization, mode n product and the
141   Kronecker product.
142   Proposition 1. For any tensor T ∈ RN1 ×N2 ×···×Nk ×d of order k + 1 and any matrices A1 ∈
143   RM1 ×N1 , · · · , Ak ∈ RMk ×Nk , we have (T ×1 A1 ×2 A2 ×3 · · · ×k Ak )(k+1) = T(k+1) (A1 ⊗ A2 ⊗
144   · · · ⊗ Ak ) ⊤ .

      develop the high-order attention mechanism in the next section.

      5.3.1      ATTENTION R ANK
395   In this section, we evaluate the impact of the number of attention heads on the performance of HOT
396   across both medical imaging and time series datasets. The attention rank, governed by the number of
397   heads, plays a critical role in the model’s ability to capture diverse patterns across multiple dimensions
398   by approximating the original high-order attention. We conduct ablation experiments by varying the
399   number of heads to observe how it affects model accuracy and error rates. For 3D medical image
400   datasets (Figure 3 Left), increasing the number of attention heads initially improves accuracy, however,
401   after a certain threshold, performance declines. This drop is due to the fixed hidden dimension, which
402   causes the dimension of each head to decrease as the number of heads increases, reducing each
403
      head’s ability to capture rich features and leading to less expressive attention mechanisms. For the
      time series datasets (Figure 3 Right), the use of more heads improves performance, with the MSE
404
      consistently decreasing as the number of heads increases.
405
406   Although the effective rank for the Kronecker decomposition varies for different tasks and datasets
407   and is highly dependent on the data size and its characteristics, in practice as shown in Figures 3, we
408   see that low-rank approximation (where the rank or the number of heads is not a large number) is
409
      expressive enough to yield good results. To that end, we treat the rank as a hyper-parameter of the
      model and choose it by hyper-parameter search based on validation metrics for each dataset. This
410
      approach makes it quite easy to achieve a proper performance, without any need for exhaustive rank
411
      analysis on the data.

      5.3.3   ATTENTION T YPE
481
482   In this section, we compare naive full attention with the proposed factorized attention approach. To
483   implement the full high-order attention, we flatten all dimensions except for the batch and hidden
484   dimensions, then apply standard scaled dot-product attention to the flattened input. Finally, the output
485   is reshaped back to its original dimensions. As discussed earlier, the quadratic complexity of standard
      attention results in significantly higher computational and memory requirements. To address this,


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      we also incorporated kernelized attention into the naive full attention, reducing its complexity to
487   linear—matching the efficiency of our proposed method. As shown in Tables 6 and 5, with proper
488   hyperparameter tuning, our proposed HOT (highlighted in green) achieves results comparable to
489   standard full attention while requiring far less computation, memory, and training time.
490
      An interesting observation from the results is that combining factorization with the kernel trick
491
      improves performance compared to factorization alone. This is achieved by introducing additional
492
      inductive bias into the attention computation. While linear attention without factorization performs
493   similarly to the combined approach in 3D medical image classification tasks, factorization offers
494   several distinct advantages: 1. It enables the control of model expressivity by adjusting the rank
495   based on the computational cost. 2. It enables the explanation of large high-order attentions, which
496   are otherwise infeasible to analyze, using smaller first-order attention matrices that are already
497   well-studied and interpretable. 3. It allows flexible treatment of attention across different dimensions
498   by enabling the application of independent attention masks on each axis without interference. 4.
499   When combined with linear attention, it results in lower MAE and reduced memory consumption
500   compared to linear attention alone as shown in Figure 4 (Right).

525   6    C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this paper, we addressed the challenge of extending Transformers to high-dimensional data, often
528   limited by the quadratic cost of attention mechanisms. While methods like flattening inputs or
529   sparse attention reduce computational overhead, they miss essential cross-dimensional dependen-
530   cies and structural information. We introduced Higher-Order Transformers (HOT) with Kronecker
531
      factorized attention to lower complexity while preserving expressiveness. This approach processes
      high-dimensional data efficiently, with complexity scaling quadratically per dimension. We further
532
      integrated kernelized attention for additional scalability and a complexity scaling linearly per di-
533
      mension. HOT demonstrated strong performance in tasks such as time series forecasting and 3D
534   medical image classification, proving both its effectiveness and efficiency. Future work could enhance
535   HOT’s interpretability by analyzing attention maps or exploring alternative pooling methods for better
536   information aggregation. Additionally, adapting HOT as an autoregressive model could enhance
537   spatial and temporal coherency for generative tasks like video synthesis and climate forecasting.
538   Lastly, although the use of the proposed factorized linear attention was only studied in the context of
539   the Transformers architecture in this work, its capabilities in other architectures such as Attention
      U-Net Oktay et al. (2018) is yet to be explored.
