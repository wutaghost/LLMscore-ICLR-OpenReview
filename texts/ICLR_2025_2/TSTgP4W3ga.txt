011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Efficient simulations of complex physical systems described by partial differen-
014            tial equations (PDE) require computational methods that can reduce the resource
015            demands without sacrificing the accuracy. Traditionally, this is achieved by “up-
016
               scaling” the simulation grids or by aggregating cells based on a priori information.
               Here, we introduce a novel framework based on graph neural networks (GNN)
017
               for learnable self-supervised differentiable coarsening of unstructured computa-
018
               tional grids. We leverage graph-based representation of the physical system and
019            offer a graph coarsening method which preserves the underlying physical proper-
020            ties together with the stability of the chosen numerical scheme. This is achieved
021            by minimizing the error between the output of the simulations using coarsened
022            and original graph. We demonstrate the approach on several example differen-
023            tial equations, modeling sub-surface flow and wave propagation. We demonstrate
024            that the model exhibits ability to maintain high fidelity in simulation outputs even
025            after 95% reduction on the nodes, significantly reducing computational overhead.
026            We also show that the model exhibits generalizability to unseen scenarios, thereby
027
               outperforming the baselines. Thus, the developed approach demonstrates the abil-
               ity to accelerate simulation without comprising accuracy and hence has potential
028
               for accelerating physical simulations in various domains.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Modeling fluid dynamics, particularly subsurface flow, remains a highly resource-intensive task
034
      due to the detailed spatio-temporal resolutions required for accurate simulations, as well as the
035
      non-linearity of the governing partial differential equations (PDEs) Gerritsen & Durlofsky (2005).
036   Such simulations are crucial for numerous engineering applications; however, their computa-
037   tional demands often become a bottleneck, especially when multiple parameter variations are ex-
038   plored Huyakorn (2012). Therefore, reducing computational cost without sacrificing accuracy is a
039   critical challenge in the field.
040
      Traditionally, various methods, such as upscaling, have been employed to address this issue by
041
      replacing fine grids with coarser ones, while adjusting equation coefficients (e.g., permeability,
042   porosity) to reflect the larger cells Qi & Hesketh (2005). While effective at reducing grid size,
043   these methods typically assume a homogeneous approximation within each coarse block, limiting
044   the ability to fully capture localized heterogeneity. More sophisticated upscaling methods solve
045   PDEs within each coarse block, yet the overarching principle remains: reducing grid size to save
046   computational time. Zhang et al. (2021); Farmer (2002)
047
      Alternatively, reduced-order modeling (ROM) approaches focus on leveraging a subset of simulated
048   data to accelerate the remaining computation. One notable example is proper orthogonal decompo-
049   sition (POD), which utilizes dominant spatial eigenvectors for data approximation, akin to principle
050   component analysis Kerschen et al. (2005); Rowley (2005); Guo & Hesthaven (2019). Dynamic
051   mode decomposition (DMD) Kutz et al. (2016) is another recent method, utilizing both spatial and
052   temporal frequencies to model and predict fluid dynamics. This approach has been extended further
053   by learning operators that model the PDEs in the functional space Li et al. (2020); Kovachki et al.
      (2021; 2023). While they have been effective for wide range of problems, the absence of a physics-


                                                        1

054
      based solver lead to: (i) divergence of errors for longer scale simulations, (ii) violation of physical
055   laws such as conservation of energy and momenta, and (iii) lack of interpretability of the learned
056   function. Burark et al. (2024); Azizzadenesheli et al. (2024)
057
058
      Differentiable physics has emerged as a powerful framework for simulating and optimizing physical
      systems by incorporating gradient-based methods into the simulation process. The key advantage of
059
      this approach is that it allows for automatic differentiation (AD) at every step of the simulation Liang
060
      & Lin (2020), making it possible to compute the partial derivatives of the output with respect to the
061   input. This has found applications not only in fluid dynamics but also in areas such as molecular
062   dynamics for optimizing particle positions or force fields Schoenholz & Cubuk (2020); Gangan et al.
063   (2024), and material structure design Dold & van Egmond (2023). However, integrating AD into
064   PDE solvers, where the current state depends on previous states, creates highly nested computational
065   graphs, which poses significant challenges for optimization Holl et al. (2020).
066
      Recently, Shumilin et al. introduced an approach that employed grid coarsening for accelerated sim-
067   ulations through differentiable physics—the simulation and grid coarsening processes were made
068   fully differentiable, including the finite-volume solver. This allowed to optimize the placement of
069   points on unstructured grids by minimizing the misfit between coarse and fine grid simulations,
070   leveraging techniques such as automatic differentiation (AD), k-means clustering and differentiable
071   Voronoi tesselation. The method demonstrated the potential to reduce grid size while maintaining
072   simulation quality, tested across various PDEs, including parabolic and hyperbolic equations.
073   Building upon this foundation, we now propose a framework based on graph neural networks (GNN)
074   and graph pooling, which shifts the focus from optimizing point positions to a self-supervised learn-
075   able coarsening process. In contrast to the methodology proposed by Shumilin et al., where point
076   positions were optimized for each specific grid, the GNN-based approach learns a generalizable
077   coarsening strategy that can be applied to grids with varying numbers of points. This flexibility
078   allows the coarsening procedure to adapt to different simulation setups without the need for grid-
079   specific re-optimization, thus enhancing the scalability and applicability of the method.
080   The major contributions are as follows.
081
082
      1. Learnable graph coarsening: We present a learnable, self-supervised GNN-based coarsening
         framework. It allows to learn optimal coarsening strategies for the grids of different sizes.
083
      2. Physics and stability losses: We use physics loss to ensure that the coarsened grid yields the
084
         simualtions close to that of the ground truth respecting the governing PDE. We also propose a
085      built-in stability loss for maintaining stability of numerical scheme used for explicit solver.
086   3. Differentiable implicit numerical solver We develop the differentiable implicit numerical finite
087      volume solver that leverages differentiable Voronoi tesselation and extends the practical applica-
088      bility of the proposed framework.

095   2.1   AUTODIFFERENTIATION
096   Automatic differentiation (AD) is a method for computing the gradients of a program’s output with
097   respect to its inputs Naumann (2012). Specifically, the computations are deconstructed into func-
098   tions at intermediate steps generating a computational graph to which the chain rule is applied to
099   obtain the instantaneous gradient. A key algorithm that utilizes AD is backpropagation, which effi-
100   ciently calculates the gradient of a loss function concerning the network’s weights. This significantly
101   simplifies and enhances the learning process of neural networks.
102   Computational graphs are fundamental in various fields, including physics, chemistry, biology, and
103   machine learning. They provide a structured representation of mathematical expressions and op-
104   erations through nodes and edges. Each node corresponds to a specific operation, while the edges
105   indicate the flow of data between nodes. This structure facilitates both forward and backward com-
106   putation, which is essential for optimization tasks in neural networks. By representing complex
107   functions as graphs, AD can efficiently compute gradients through backpropagation. There are a
      number of frameworks for AD such as JAX Bradbury et al. (2018b) and PyTorch (Paszke et al.,

108
      2017). In our experiments, we use both PyTorch and JAX for explicit and implicit solvers, respec-
109   tively, as discussed later.

      2.2      GNN S FOR GRAPH COARSENING
113
114
      We use the notation G = (V, E, A, X) to denote a graph over a finite, non-empty node set V and
115
      edge set E = {(u, v) | u, v ∈ V}. A represents the adjacency (edge weight) matrix corresponding
116
      to the graph. X ∈ R|V|×d denotes node attributes encoded using d-dimensional feature vectors. We
117   denote the attributes of node v as xv .
118
119   Given graph G = (V, E, A, X), graph coarsening is the process of constructing a significantly
120   smaller graph G̃ = (Ṽ, Ẽ, Ã, X̃) with |Ṽ| ≪ |V| nodes, such that G̃ and G have similar proper-
121   ties. This process involves merging multiple nodes into supernodes along with their aggregated
122   features X̃. Hence, we need to learn a surjective mapping π : V → Ṽ that associates nodes in the
123   original graph G to supernodes in G̃.
124   In the context of grid coarsening for physical simulations, we assume the dataset to be a set of
125   graph snapshots G = {G 1 , · · · , G T }, where each snapshot Gt ∈ G shares the same topology, but
126   potentially varying node attributes, i.e., G t = (V, E, A, X t ). Furthermore, For each node v ∈
127   V, we associate a time-dependent physical quantity yvt (e.g., pressure) as ground truth, which is
128   obtained from a physics-based simulation or experimental observations. The complete ground truth
129   information is denoted as Y = {y1 , . . . , yT }, where yt = {yvt | ∀v ∈ V}. However, when the
130   input graph is large spanning thousands of nodes and edges, physics-based simulations can become
131
      prohibitively expensive. To address this, the G NN trained on G seeks to learn a coarsened graph
      along with their feature to model the evolutionary dynamics of yvt as a function of the graph topology
132
133
      and node attributes. Thus, we propose to coarsen the topology of the input graph G to G̃ such that
134
      the output of the physics-based simulation on G̃ closely approximates the simulation output on the
      original graph G 1 . Formal definition of problem is presented in Appendix A.
135
136   Related work. Although there are numerous works investigating mesh coarsening, our approach
137   is distinct in that it integrates an explicit and implicit numerical and differentiable finite volume
138   solvers directly into the training loop without relying on surrogate models or approximations. For
139   instance, Graph Element Networks (GENs) introduced by Alet et al. (2019) approximate the so-
140
      lution of PDEs using GNNs trained on high-resolution solutions. GENs adjust node positions or
      densities to better capture the solution space but do not perform explicit graph coarsening through
141
      node aggregation or clustering. Additionally, GENs focus on stationary PDEs or displacement pre-
142
      diction and do not incorporate time dependence in their implementation, limiting their applicability
143   to dynamic simulations. Subsequently, Pfaff et al. (2020) proposed MeshGraphNets, which apply
144   remeshing techniques that may increase the number of cells and computational load rather than
145   coarsening the mesh. Their remesher coarsens the mesh by removing edges only when it does not
146   create invalid elements, based on a purely geometrical criterion involving the sizing field tensor pre-
147   dicted by the model. This approach is primarily applied to 2D manifolds embedded in 3D space,
148   such as flag or sphere scenarios, and relies on local curvature. In contrast, our method utilizes simu-
149   lation data misfit to cluster and aggregate nodes, resulting in a more physically informed coarsening
150   process applicable to general graphs. More recently, Li et al. (2024) developed a differentiable finite
151
      volume method using GNNs within an encoder-decoder framework and employed a PDE loss for
      training. However, their solver functions as a surrogate model, as they do not integrate the actual
152
      physics solver into the optimization process. In our work, we incorporate explicit and implicit nu-
153
      merical differentiable non-surrogate finite volume solvers directly into the training loop, enabling
154   end-to-end learning of coarsened graphs that accurately capture the dynamics of the original system.
155   This approach broadens the applicability of graph coarsening methods in physics-based simulations,
156   particularly for time-dependent and stiff PDEs.
157
      In our approach we use a Graph Convolutional Network (Kipf & Welling, 2017) for producing a
158
      learnable coarsening, its architecture is defined in Section 3.2.

260   3.2     G RAPH COARSENING PIPELINE
261
      The overall framework employed in the present work is presented in Fig. 1. Our NN architecture
262
      for coarsening consists of a one graph convolution layer followed by a point-wise MLP with two
263
      fully connected layers and softmax at the end, which predicts the soft assignment matrix Sij for
264   probabilistic clustering. These assignments are used to aggregate node features, ensuring that the key
265   physical properties of the system are preserved in the coarsened representation. Notably, the features
266   of sources and sinks are kept intact during the coarsening process to maintain the integrity of the
267   flow dynamics. We set which nodes to keep as a hyperparameter. The motivation for this problem
268   comes from the field of proxy modeling and other engineering fields where model identification
269   needs to be performed based on limited sensor measurements. See Appendix C for more details.
      Following this physics-based simulation is performed using the PDE solver to obtain the output


                                                                       5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      quantities based on the coarsened graph. To ensure numerical stability, we utilize max subtraction
271   for softmax stabilization. Finally, the model is trained by minimizing the error between the output
272   quantity obtained from the coarsened graph-based simulations with respect to those obtained from
273   the original simulations.
274
      The G NN model for coarsening is trained using a two-stage loss function that balances stability
275
      and physical fidelity (detailed in Sec. 3.4). For the latter, we use the so-called physics loss, which
276
      compares the root mean squared error (RMSE) between the simulated dynamics on the coarsened
277   graph and the ground-truth fine-mesh simulation over time. The RMSE is computed based on the
278   time series data of key variables in the simulation.
279
280
      When using an explicit solver, a warm-up period of 50 epochs is used by default, where only the sta-
      bility loss is optimized. This stability loss leads to maximizing permissible timestep that ensures the
281
      numerical scheme remains stable and does not diverge (see mathematical definition 8 below). After
282
      the warm-up, the algorithm checks the stability condition described in Section 3.4 at each step. If
283   the condition is satisfied, both physics and stability losses are used for optimization. If not, only the
284   stability loss is applied. However, when an implicit solver is used, only the physics loss is applied, as
285   implicit schemes inherently have good stability properties. Additionally, gradient clipping is applied
286   to prevent exploding gradients, and the adjacency matrix and geometry are updated dynamically af-
287   ter each iteration to reflect the new node positions. All the codes used in the present work are avail-
288   able in: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/Learnable-coarsening-9434.
289   Currently, our framework is designed for 2D computational domains. The extension to 3D dif-
290   ferentiable Voronoi tessellations and handling of non-simplex grids is discussed in Appendix B.

362   4.1   BASELINES
363
364   Note that machine learning based grid coarsening for PDEs, being a recent approach, has limited
365   number of existing methods. To evaluate our approach, we use two baselines described in Shumilin
366   et al., as follows.
367   • k-means + averaging, consisting of k-means clustering of the nodes and averaging of the perme-
368     abilities and node positions within the clusters;
369   • Shumilin et al., which is based on the global optimization of the nodes positions referred further
370     in text as the competing approach.
371   • METIS Karypis & Kumar (1998), well-known software tool designed for partitioning graphs
372     and meshes. METIS aims to generate blocks that are uniform in size and shape, minimizing the
373     number of connections between different partitions.
374   It is worth noting that the competing approach has one major drawback—the necessity of costly
375   global optimization in every modeling task. We hypothesize that: (i) the competing approach may
376   yield better results but much worse generalizability, as the present approach may be applied in infer-
377   ence mode to unseen data under some limitations, (ii) our approach may produce even better results
      in some cases due to more flexible averaging procedure. These are explored in the experiments later.

      5     C ONCLUSION
513
      In this work, we introduce a novel flexible framework for unstructured grid coarsening based on
514
      GNNs, advancing the state of grid optimization from position-based adjustments to a learnable,
515   self-supervised coarsening process. Importantly, our pipeline is physically motivated and aggregates
516   features in physically correct way also taking care of the numerical scheme stability. Our approach
517   can be used on different grid sizes, eliminating the need for grid-specific re-optimization, making it
518   scalable for a wide range of simulation setups.
519
      Our experiments demonstrate that the present approach delivers comparable accuracy to the work
520
      of Shumilin et al. while giving a better generalizability. We validated this in the sinusoidal variable
521   permeability scenario, where the results closely match the fine-mesh simulations, showcasing the
522   robustness of our method. Furthermore, the adaptability of the learned model to grids of differ-
523   ent sizes and another initial conditions without retraining provides a clear advantage over previous
524   methods, including the work of Shumilin et al., where costly optimization is required for every new
525   grid. This capacity for generalization paves the way for efficient simulations with reduced compu-
526   tational costs and without sacrificing accuracy. We also show the applicability of our method for the
527   hyperbolic wave equation here1 . Due to good stability properties of an explicit scheme for the wave
528   equation we are able to optimize physical loss only. Overall, the framework we propose opens new
529
      possibilities for scalable and adaptable physics simulations, reducing both computational resources
      and the environmental footprint associated with large-scale simulations.
530
531   Limitations and future work. The present work while promising is still applied to only two types
532   of equations (parabolic and hyperbolic, describing subsurface flows and waves). The applicability of
533   the approach to more complex flows and other physics equations is an open problem. The approach
534   still relies on differentiable physics-based solvers and hence has the limitations of the simulations
535
      themselves such as small timestep and stability to name a few. Thus, combining the present work
      with data-driven models or graph neural ODEs could be a promising future work. Finally, the present
536
      approach employs a simple message passing G NN. It would be interesting explore the performance
537
      of the framework on different graph architectures with inductive biases such as equivariant G NNs.

      C    R ATIONALE FOR PRESERVING KEY POINTS
738
739
740   Consider an oil reservoir with wells where measurements are available - we are only interested in
741
      simulating the pressure at these particular points. Thus, if we achieve speed-up of computation at
      these positions while preserving the physics, we consider the goal to be completed. Indeed, if higher
742
      accuracy is required in certain regions of interest, additional sensor points can be incorporated into
743
      these areas to reduce interpolation errors. It can be implemented by the a priori analysis of the
744   initial data: we may compute the gradient field and choose points that have higher gradients as
745   sensor points. Also, we may use other algorithms based on graph spectral decomposition and other
746   importance metrics e.g. centrality measures. This adaptive selection of sensor points ensures that
747   regions requiring more precise solutions receive adequate attention without significantly increasing
748   the computational gains.
749
      In our current implementation, we assume that sensor locations align with the grid points. However,
750   we recognize that in some practical applications, this may not always be the case. To address scenar-
751   ios where sensors do not coincide with grid nodes, we plan to incorporate interpolation techniques
752   such as Barycentric interpolation Berrut & Trefethen (2004); Floater (2015) in future work. These
753   methods allow us to estimate sensor values based on surrounding grid points while ensuring that the
754   interpolation weights remain differentiable with respect to grid parameters. This approach would
755   preserve the overall differentiability of our computational pipeline, maintaining compatibility with
      gradient-based optimization.

       In this appendix, we provide additional details on the implementation of the implicit solver for the
1029   diffusion equation and demonstrate its advantages in terms of stability compared to the explicit
1030   solver.
1031
1032
       The explicit Euler scheme can suffer from stability issues, especially when dealing with large time
       steps or fine spatial discretizations. Without the inclusion of a stability loss term, the explicit solver
1033
       may diverge. To address this limitation and broaden the applicability of our method, we implemented
1034
       an implicit solver, which is known for its superior stability properties.
1035
1036   We conducted a numerical experiment using both the explicit and implicit solvers to solve the diffu-
1037   sion equation with a sinusoidal permeability distribution. In this experiment, we used 100 timesteps,
1038
       τ = 0.01, Gaussian-like initial condition with no source terms.

1066
1067   By incorporating the implicit solver into our framework, we enhance the stability and extend the
1068   applicability of our graph coarsening method to a broader range of physical simulations. The implicit
1069   solver serves as a robust building block, particularly in scenarios where the explicit solver may
1070   fail due to stability issues. This approach also enables possibility to handle stiff and nonlinear
1071
       PDEs within our framework, facilitating extensions to more complex problems that require advanced
       numerical schemes like WENO for nonlinear advection terms.
