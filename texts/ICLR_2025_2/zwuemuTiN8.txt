011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Multivariate time series data and their models are extremely important for under-
014            standing the behavior of various natural and man-made systems. Development of
015
               accurate time series models often requires capturing intricate relationships among
               the variables and their dynamics. Particularly challenging to model and learn
016
               are time series with irregular and sparse observations, that may arise in domains
017
               as diverse as healthcare, sensor and communication networks. In this work, we
018            propose and study TACD-GRU, a new Time-Aware Context-Dependent Gated
019            Recurrent Unit framework for multivariate time series prediction (or forecasting)
020            that accounts for irregularities in observation times of individual time series vari-
021            ables and their dependencies. Our framework defines a novel sequential unit that
022            is triggered by the arrival of a new observation to update its state, and a predic-
023            tion module that supports time series predictions at any future time. The current
024            prediction module consists of and combines two novel prediction models: (i) a
025            context-based model (TACD-GRU-CONTEXT) that relies on a set of tunable latent
026
               decay functions of time and their linear combinations to support the prediction,
               and (ii) an attention-based model (TACD-GRU-ATTENTION) that models depen-
027
               dencies among variables and their most recent values using a temporal attention
028
               mechanism. Our model shows highly competitive performance when powered by
029            both individual and combined prediction functions outperforming existing state-of-
030            the-art (SOTA) models on both single-step and multi-step prediction tasks across
031            three real-world datasets.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Building predictive (or forecasting) models of multivariate time series data is crucial for understanding
037   the dynamics of various natural and man-made systems. Typical time series models and their
038   predictive solutions assume that the variables are observed regularly in time (with some fixed
039
      frequency), and the dependencies among time series values are then defined with respect to these
      regularly observed time points. However, not all real-world systems come with regularly sampled
040
      observations. Systems where the observations are spaced irregularly in time arise naturally, for
041
      example, in healthcare where the observations made for a patient are event-driven (for example,
042   patient’s laboratory tests are performed only when ordered by a physician), or sensor networks
043   where observations and events may be missing due to failures of the underlying equipment. The
044   challenge here is to define and learn multivariate time series models that account for interactions and
045   dependencies among variables and observations made at different times.
046
      Along these lines, our objective in this paper is to develop a time series model that can predict,
047
      as accurately as possible, future values of one or more random variables defining the time series
048   from their past irregularly sampled observations. A variety of models and methods ranging from
049   classic statistical and modern deep learning frameworks have been developed to solve this prediction
050   problem. Early approaches replaced the irregularly observed data with regularly spaced observations
051   by inferring the values at the regular time points. Classic statistical auto-regressive models (AR,
052   ARMA, ARIMA) Shumway & Stoffer (2017) or latent space models, such as, Linear Dynamic
053   Systems (LDS) Kalman (1963) models could then be applied to support prediction at future regular
      times. To extend the regular predictions to arbitrary future times various smoothing and interpolation

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      methods were deployed. In recent years, classic statistical models have been gradually replaced with
055   various neural architectures showing improved time series prediction performance Siami-Namini et al.
056   (2018). Existing neural models make different assumptions on how to represent the continuous-time
057   dynamics and predictions of multivariate time series. For example, Neural ODEs Chen et al. (2018)
058   approximate the latent dynamics with the help of expressive differential equations while, models like
059   mTAND Shukla & Marlin (2021) use attention mechanism to capture and represent these underlying
060   dynamics. Based on how these irregular time series representation are learned, the existing methods
061   can be categorized into: RNN approaches Che et al. (2018); Mei & Eisner (2017), Differential
062   Equations approaches Rubanova et al. (2019); De Brouwer et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2018); Schirmer
063
      et al. (2022); Becker et al. (2019), attention-based approaches Shukla & Marlin (2021); Chen et al.
      (2023) and graph-based approaches Zhang et al. (2022); Zhang et al.; Yalavarthi et al. (2024).
064
065   While current research in time series prediction models come with different modeling assumptions
066   and respective advantages, they also leave a room for further improvements and the development of
067   new models. More specifically, differential equation models despite being very expressive, require
068   significantly more training time (due to calls to the numerical solver). Moreover, attention and
069
      graph-based approaches offer greater modeling flexibility and faster training times but, they are not
      efficient for online deployment scenarios. Motivated by this, we propose a novel approach that aims
070
      to bridge the gap between expressiveness, computational efficiency, and practical applicability. In
071
      this work, we propose a new Time-Aware Context-Dependent Gated Recurrent Unit (TACD-GRU)
072   that sequentially updates the state in continuous time whenever observation arrives and generates a
073   continuous time multivariate prediction function supporting time series predictions at arbitrary future
074   time. TACD-GRU’s prediction module consists of two components: i) TACD-GRU-CONTEXT:
075   a new context-based prediction model that relies on a set of latent learnable exponential decay
076   functions to model long-term temporal dependencies among the time series variables, ii) TACD-
077   GRU-ATTENTION: a new local temporal attention prediction model that defines a continuous-time
078   prediction function over most recent observations and elapsed times since these observations were
079   made to model short-term temporal dependencies. The two prediction models are combined using a
080
      novel Dynamic Meta-Decision Model. It is important to highlight that the two prediction models
      were designed with a slightly different objective: the context-based prediction model attempts to
081
      summarize dependencies in the entire history, and hence, may miss some local temporal dependencies
082
      that are important for the prediction task. On the other hand, since attention-based model relies only
083   on the recent set of observations, it may miss dependencies induced by multiple observations on
084   each individual time series. As a result, the context-based and attention-based models come with
085   complementary strengths and intuitively, their combination may better leverage their strengths, and
086   lead to improved overall predictive performance.
087
      Our research work makes the following key contributions:
088
089          • We introduce two new continuous-time prediction models: TACD-GRU-CONTEXT and
090            TACD-GRU-ATTENTION for irregularly sampled time series built upon efficient Markov
091            state update mechanism.
092
             • We propose TACD-GRU, a prediction model combining the above two models without
               introducing additional hyper-parameters, leveraging their strengths to improve the modeling
093
               of the dependencies in multivariate irregularly sampled time series.
094
             • Comprehensive evaluation of the two new prediction models and the proposed TACD-GRU
095            model against SOTA prediction models on multiple prediction tasks on diverse real-world
096            datasets demonstrating that our proposed model outperforms existing prediction models for
097            irregular time series. Moreover, our empirical evaluation shows that TACD-GRU-CONTEXT
098            demonstrates competitive performance relative to the SOTA baselines.
099          • We perform additional experiments to better understand the functionality of newly proposed
100            dynamic meta-decision model. These experiments reveal two important insights: (1) it
101            learns the expected behavior of assigning full weight to TACD-GRU-ATTENTION for
102            the reconstruction task, and (2) it demonstrates robustness by effectively switching to the
103
               unperturbed model if perturbations at varying noise levels are made to the predictions of
               one model.

106   2 R ELATED W ORK
107   In the following we review existing approaches for time series prediction for irregularly sampled
      time series data and contrast them to our work. Our focus will be on modern neural models. We


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      divide the existing approaches into four subcategories: RNN-based, ODE-based, attention-based, and
109   graph-based approaches, based on the main mechanism they rely on when making the prediction.
110
111   RNN approaches. RNN methods aim to leverage efficient implementation of the hidden state update
112
      to support prediction. Standard RNN-based methods, however, work on regularly sampled time series
      and hence require one to use interpolation schemes to convert between regular and irregular samples.
113
      An alternative approach, Neural Hawkes Process model Mei & Eisner (2017) uses tunable exponential
114
      decay functions for event time series. GRU-D Che et al. (2018) extends this to general time series
115   prediction, operating in continuous time and updating its hidden state with each new observation.
116   The hidden state is adjusted using tunable exponential decay functions to account for elapsed time
117   effects. Additionally, the missing observations are inferred using decay mechanisms that revert to
118   some target value, typically determined by the mean of the variable. The GRU-D model is particularly
119   similar to our TACD-GRU-CONTEXT model which also relies on a set of tunable exponential decay
120   functions to model the effect of elapsed time on the hidden state. However, most importantly, to avoid
121   propagating estimation errors for missing observations, TACD-GRU-CONTEXT does not interpolate
122   missing observations.
123   Differential Equation approaches. Another widely-used approach to model multivariate time
124   series and their dependencies in continuous time relies on differential equations models of latent
125   space dynamics. Neural ODE Chen et al. (2018) defines temporal dynamics in continuous in terms of
126   ordinary differential equations (ODEs) approximated by a learned dynamics model. Rubanova et al.
127   (2019) proposes to combine this Neural ODE dynamics with VAE-like architectures (referred to as
128   LatentODE) and RNN architectures (known as ODE-RNN) to model irregularly sampled time series.
129
      One limitation of Neural ODEs is that its solution is a function of the initial condition, however,
      the initial condition cannot be adapted to the observed distribution. Neural Controlled Differential
130
      Equations Kidger et al. (2020); De Brouwer et al. (2019) allows the dynamics to be continuously
131
      modulated by including the input observations. The differential equation methods typically require
132   an external numerical ODE solver, a component that can significantly prolong model’s training
133   time. Recently, Schirmer et al. (2022); Becker et al. (2019) avoid invoking numerical solvers for
134   continuous-time dynamics by modeling latent transitions with the help of linear stochastic differential
135   equations, that can be solved in the closed form. In contrast to these methods, TACD-GRU does
136   not use differential equations to model the latent state dynamics, instead it relies on (a) a set of
137   learnable exponential decay functions for modeling temporal dependencies and time series dynamics
138   for TACD-GRU-CONTEXT component, and (b) temporal attention mechanism for last observed
139   values for each time series and their timing for TACD-GRU-ATTENTION component. This avoids
140
      the need for deployment of computationally costly external numerical solvers.
141   Alternative approaches. Many of the recently proposed irregular time series models rely on graph
142   neural networks and transformer architectures. mTAND model Shukla & Marlin (2021) embeds time
143   into fixed-sized learnable vector representation, and learn relationship between irregular observed
144   times and fixed reference time-points to arrive at a prediction in continuous time. ContiFormer
145   Chen et al. (2023) combines Neural ODEs with the continuous-time attention mechanism to model
146
      irregular observations. T-PatchGNN Zhang et al. leverages a patch-based mechanism on univariate
      time series to enhance local feature extraction and use graph neural network to learn relationships
147
      among different time series. GraFITi Yalavarthi et al. (2024) first converts irregular samples to a
148
      special bipartite graph structure and cast the prediction problem as an edge weight prediction. Other
149   graph-based temporal Zhang et al. (2022), spatiotemporal Marisca et al. (2022) and diffusion-based
150   Tashiro et al. (2021) models have been proposed to address irregularly spaced observations. Overall,
151   since these models lack a Markov state representation, deploying these models in an online setting
152   may become a computational bottleneck. Primarily, because these models require to buffer the past
153   observations to encode a newly arrived observation and reprocess all the past observations for every
154   inference step. In contrast, both TACD-GRU-CONTEXT and TACD-GRU-ATTENTION update
155   its previous state when a new observation arrives thus, significantly reducing the computational
156   overhead.

353   4 E MPIRICAL E VALUATION
354   We perform empirical evaluation of TACD-GRU and its components TACD-GRU-CONTEXT and
355   TACD-GRU-ATTENTION on three irregularly sampled multivariate time series datasets on two
356   prediction tasks: single-step and multi-step predictions (see below). We provide the description
357   of the datasets in Appendix B and present the results in the Section 5. Models. We compare the
358   proposed TACD-GRU, TACD-GRU-CONTEXT (labeled as ’TACD-GRU-C’) and TACD-GRU-
359   ATTENTION (labeled as ’TACD-GRU-A’) models with the SOTA baseline models (description

      included in Appendix C) for irregularly sampled time series. Model Training. The multivariate input
      sequence is masked based on the specific task, such as masking the next time-step for single-step
361
      prediction. Models are trained to reconstruct the entire input sequence after observing partially
362
      masked sequence. We use Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss function to optimize the model parameters.
363   After training, the models are evaluated on how accurately they predict the masked values. It is
364   important to note that at any given step, the prediction horizon for the next target (∆T ) is determined
365   by the time-elapsed between the current time and the time of occurrence of the subsequent observation
366   in the data (∆τ ). Evaluation Criteria. We evaluate models on MSE, Mean Absolute Error (MAE)
367   and Win Rate on the predicted values in the test split.

440   Missing data perspective. Prior works Singh (1997); Ramoni & Sebastiani (1997) have classified the
441   observed missingness in the data into three categories: i) Missing Completely at Random (MCAR),
442   and ii) Missing at Random, and iii) Not Missing at Random (NMAR). In our experiments, USHCN
443
      belongs to MCAR, where the missingness is synthetically introduced by dropping the observations
      uniformly at random. In contrast, the Physionet and MIMIC-III datasets are instances of NMAR,
444
      where missingness is contextual, i.e., dependent on observed and unobserved values. For example,
445
      in these datasets, a laboratory test might be missing until an abnormal vital sign value is observed,
446   making the physician order the test. Our analysis suggests that while TACD-GRU remain competitive
447   in MCAR, it outperforms all the models on NMAR. These results demonstrates that TACD-GRU is
448   better able to model the dependencies inherent in the processes generating the missingness.
449   Single-step vs. Multi-step prediction We evaluate models on the single-step and multi-step prediction
450   tasks, given a set of historical observations. The multi-step prediction task presents a greater challenge
451   as it requires forecasting multiple future time points without feeding intermediate observations. The
452   absence of intervening observations in multi-step prediction increases uncertainty and compounds
453   model errors over time, leading to reduced accuracy relative to single-step forecasts (evident from
454   results in Table 1 and Table 2). Notably, in NMAR single-step prediction settings, our model’s
455   improvement in MSE over the best baseline significantly exceeds that in multi-step prediction. These
456
      empirical evidences suggests that under accurate historical contexts, TACD-GRU is significantly
      better at capturing temporal dependencies than any other time series model considered.

529
      In conclusion, we propose and study TACD-GRU, a new recurrent neural network unit for irregularly
530   sampled time series. Our investigation on multiple prediction tasks confirm TACD-GRU’s superior
531   performance over the existing state-of-the-art models on multiple irregularly sampled data settings.
532   We provide extensive experiments to validate the contributions and robustness of the TACD-GRU
533   components, further highlighting the benefits of our proposed architecture. Additionally, we introduce
534   a new MIMIC-III derived dataset that can provide as a realistic benchmark for evaluating prediction
535   models that handle irregular time series data.

716   B.0.3    MIMIC-III
717
718   A population cohort of 10,265 hospital admissions (on 8,799 patients) are extracted from MIMIC-III
719   Johnson et al. (2023) (https://physionet.org/content/mimiciii/1.4/) based on
720   the following criteria: i) patient record is recorded in MetaVision critical care information system, ii)
721   the length of the patient record is between 2 and 20 days, iii) the age of the patient is between 18
722   and 90. From all EHR tables available in MIMIC-III, we extract (irregularly sampled) time series
723
      that include vital signs (such as Heart Rate and Mean Arterial Pressure), lab results (such as Glucose
      and Hemoglobin), administered medications (such as Propofol and Norepinephrine), and procedures
724
      (such as Intubation). The vital signs and lab results are numerical time series, while the rest are
725
      indicator time series, indicating if and when the event occurred.
726
727   We filter out any univariate time series that occurs less than 500 times across all patients in the
728   cohort resulting in total of 506 time series: 393 numerical (vitals and labs), 77 medications and 36
729
      procedures event time series. We define an A-point, abbreviated for Anchor-point, as a temporal
      moment at which a decision-making system can formulate a prediction based on the past sequence of
730
      events. We extract A-points from the filtered patient records regularly with frequency of 24 hours
731
      i.e. one sample is extracted every 24 hours from the patient record. We standardize all real-valued
732   univariate time series (i.e. vital signs and labs) data using min-max scaling; and encode all other
733   indicator time series as binary value 0/1. Value is 1 if event occurs; 0 otherwise. 80% of the patient
734   hospital admission are used for training and validation (20% of train split); the rest is used for testing.
735   Splits are constructed on disjoint patients.
736
      Additional pre-processing is required to remove missing values encoded as 9999999 in the numerical
737
      time series. To remove outliers from univariate time series (for example, very large values of the order
738   of 1e5), we filter out observations that fall either in < 0.1 or > 99.9 percentile ranges. Subsampling
739   of A-points is performed as follows:
740
741         1. For each split:
742
743              a. For each patient admission record, filter out A-points with less than 50 events in history
744                 and prediction window.
745              b. Next, randomly sample one A-point from the filtered A-points i.e. one sample per
746                 patient admission record.
747
748         2. Subsample 1000 A-points from train, 250 from validation and 200 from test set to be used
749
               for experimentation.

766   C    BASELINES
767
768
769
      We include the following baseline methods to compare against our proposed model TACD-GRU:
770
771   GRU-∆t : We consider recurrent models GRU-∆t as our baselines in the comparison. Gated
772   Recurrent Unit Chung et al. (2014) have been proposed to model sequences using a set of parametric
773   update equations. Since GRU is not time-aware, a variant of GRU that also feeds in time elapsed
774   (since the last time-step) along with the input GRU-∆t is used in the comparison.
775   RKN-∆t : Recurrent Kalman Networks (RKN) Becker et al. (2019) have been proposed to incorporate
776   uncertainty in time series modeling. Similar to GRU-∆t , we include the baseline RKN-∆t that
777   includes time elapsed as an additional input to the model.
778
      GRU-D: We compare our method to Gated Recurrent Unit-Decay (GRU-D) Che et al. (2018) that
779   uses a mean-reverting imputation function for missing variables; applies learnable exponential decays
780   in the input and latent dimension to account for irregular observed times.
781
782
      mTAND: We evaluate our performance against the Encoder-Decoder generative model, Multi-
      Time Attention Network (mTAND-Full) Shukla & Marlin (2021), which defines reference points
783
      and represents continuous time-points with learnable embeddings to encode their relationship and
784
      generate predictions.
785
786   CRU and f-CRU: We consider Continuous Recurrent Units (CRU) and fast-Continuous Recurrent
787   Units (f-CRU) Schirmer et al. (2022) as our baselines for prediction tasks. f-CRU is a fast implemen-
788
      tation of jointly proposed CRU method. CRU consists of an encoder-decoder framework where the
      hidden state progression is governed by linear stochastic differential equation that allow incorporating
789
      arbitrary time-intervals between observations.
790
791   ODE-RNN and Latent ODE: In our analysis, we consider Latent ODE and ODE-RNN proposed in
792   Rubanova et al. (2019) as comparative baselines. ODE-RNN model consist of latent states that adhere
793   to ODE between observations and are updated at observations using standard RNN update equations.
794
      Latent ODE model adopts variational auto-encoder framework wherein the hidden state posterior
      is modeled by an ODE-RNN model. We used the configuration of Latent ODE with ODE-RNN
795
      encoder.
796
797   ContiFormer: We incorporate ContiFormer Chen et al. (2023) as one of our baselines for the
798   prediction tasks. It builds upon the original transformer architecture by first extending the input
799   irregular data to the continuous-time latent representation by assuming that the underlying dynamics
800
      are governed by the ODEs.
801   T-PatchGNN: We include T-PatchGNN Zhang et al. as one of our baselines for the prediction tasks.
802   T-PatchGNN first segments each time series into patches of uniform temporal resolution followed
803   by the transformer and time-adaptive GNNs to capture dependencies in the multivariate time series.
804   We use the official T-PatchGNN code made publicly available here: https://github.com/
805   usail-hkust/t-PatchGNN in our experiment pipeline.
806   GraFITi: We add GraFITi Yalavarthi et al. (2024) as one of our baselines for comparisons on
807   the single-step and multi-step prediction tasks. GraFITi casts the time series prediction task in
808   terms of edge weight prediction problem after converting the time series to a sparse graph structure.
809   To incorporate this method, we make use of the official implementation available here: https:
      //github.com/yalavarthivk/GraFITi.

1155   and its components for the normalized and un-normalized time values. Briefly, the time is normalized
1156   using min-max scaling so that the resulting range of time is in [0, 1]. In terms of implementation,
1157   this requires one to compute the min and max time values in a retrospective dataset and normalizing
1158   time values before calling the TACD-GRU’s forward method. In Table 4, we report and compare

1165

1502   as an early warning system to prevent adverse patient conditions in the ICU. This model operates
1503

1504

1505
