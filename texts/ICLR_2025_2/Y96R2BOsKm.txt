009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Adaptations have enabled efficient training for large backbone models such as
012            diffusion models for image generation and transformer-based language models.
013            While various adaptation techniques aim to maximize performance with minimal
014            computational resources, limited data often leads to challenges like overfitting,
015            mode collapse, or hallucinations. Recently, a promising solution has emerged in
016            the form of augmenting adapter datasets using data originally employed to train
017            backbone models. While this approach has shown potential as a breakthrough, it
018            often lacks a solid theoretical foundation or well-defined standards for control-
019
               lability. To address these limitations, we establish a comprehensive theoretical
               framework for Backbone Augmented Training (BAT). Furthermore, we provide
020
               both theoretical and experimental evidence demonstrating that BAT achieves a
021
               faster convergence rate to optimal adaptation parameters compared to conven-
022            tional adaptation methods. Our results underscore the potential of backbone aug-
023            mentation to significantly improve performance, especially when coupled with an
024            effective and well-designed data selection schema.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Recently, large foundation models (Brown et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022; Meta, 2024; Peebles
030   & Xie, 2023; Sauer et al., 2024) have demonstrated exceptional performance across various tasks.
031   To adapt these models for specific downstream tasks, researchers have introduced a variety of adap-
032   tation techniques. These approaches typically involve updating only a small portion of the model
033   parameters—some leveraging rank decomposition (Hu et al., 2021; Dettmers et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
034   2024) of the backbone weights, while others employing fixed text embeddings (Ruiz et al., 2023a;
035   Gal et al., 2022) to maintain identity consistency in image generation.
036   Despite the success of large models in various downstream tasks, acquiring data for certain tasks
037   remains highly challenging (Lee et al., 2023; Sainz et al., 2023; Gholami & Omar, 2023). The
038   scarcity of data leads to various complications, such as model overfitting (Ruiz et al., 2023b; Pascual
039   et al., 2024; Salman & Liu, 2019), model collapse (Thanh-Tung & Tran, 2020), or hallucination (Luo
040   et al., 2021b). These challenges highlight the critical importance of obtaining sufficient amount of
041   data.
042   To this end, researchers came up with leveraging the data used to train backbone models. For in-
043   stance, DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023a) incorporates regularization images generated from the back-
044   bone model’s distribution. Additionally, datasets commonly used for training diffusion models (Lin
045   et al., 2015; Schuhmann et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2023) and fine-tuned language models (Taori et al.,
046   2023a; Wang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Chaudhary, 2023) are often publicly accessible, prompt-
047   ing communities such as jiwenji (2024) and StabilityAI (2024) to heuristically augment adaptation
048   data using backbone data, occasionally yielding positive results.
049   However, these heuristic methods often lack a clear understanding of how backbone data augmen-
050   tation enhances model performance. As a result, improving adapter performance using backbone
051   data has largely relied on chance. To address this, in this paper, we first establish the mathematical
052   foundation of Backbone Augmented Training (BAT) and demonstrate the potential of backbone data
053   in adapter training. Beyond theoretical validation, we aim to show through extensive experiments
      that BAT consistently outperforms non-augmented training under various conditions.

      2    P RELIMINARIES
095
096
      In this section, we briefly discuss the details of the adaptations used in this study. Also, we define a
097
      few notations and concepts behind our experimental approaches.
098
099   Adaptations. Fine-tuning a large-scale model to solve a downstream task is extremely expensive.
100   To mitigate this challenge, researchers came up with methods that train a small portion of param-
101
      eters, also known as adaptations. Adaptation methods are widely distinguished as additive fine-
      tuning (Houlsby et al., 2019; Li & Liang, 2021), selective fine-tuning (Zaken et al., 2021; Guo
102
      et al., 2020), reparameterized fune-tuning (Aghajanyan et al., 2020; Karimi Mahabadi et al., 2021).
103
      In the following part, we introduce eminent types of adaptations.
104
105   LoRA. Low-Rank Adaptation (Hu et al., 2021) has gained significant attention among early adapta-
106   tions for its ability to efficiently train a small portion of parameters through weight decomposition,
107   without any additional inference burden. Specifically, given a pretrained weight matrix W0 ∈ Rd×k ,
      LoRA decomposes the weight update ∆W ∈ Rd×k into the product BA to get the adapted matrix


                                                                2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      W = W0 + ∆W . Here, B ∈ Rd×r and A ∈ Rr×k with r ≪ min{d, k}. Despite utilizing only a
109   small set of parameters, LoRA achieves performance comparable to full fine-tuning, and in certain
110   benchmarks, even surpasses it. Based on the strong performance of LoRA, several variants emerged
111   including DoRA (Liu et al., 2024; Dettmers et al., 2023) for language models. Others applied this
112   decomposition method in generative models such as diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Song
113   et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2020) like LoCon, LoHA and LoKr (Yeh et al., 2023). However, lack of data
114   can cause overfitting and hallucinations even with this adaptation.
115   DreamBooth. DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023a) is also an adapter for diffusion model which sug-
116   gests rare-token identifiers to regenerate objects with identical features. Diffusion models before this
117   adaptation had weak capacity in generating same identity repeatedly. For example, generating a fa-
118   mous movie character, a certain cat, over and over again ended up with bunch of cats with different
119   colors and kinds with former methods. Preventing this and achieving the task is called personaliza-
120   tion. Some attempted to shift the text token in embedding space (Gal et al., 2022), and from this,
121   DreamBooth continues to inject identities in the generation weights with newly defined prior preser-
122   vation loss. To utilize this loss function, a regularization dataset must be synthesized often much
123
      greater in size than the adaptation dataset which can be demanding in practical usage.
124   Data Selection. Recent adaptation users have selected data from the backbone models to mitigate the
125   insufficiency in adaptation data. (jiwenji, 2024; StabilityAI, 2024). However, this method does not
126   show consistent results since they select the backbone data with heuristic and random manner. We
127   name this method as random augmented training in this study. However, data selection is an active
128   research topic as it still remains as a crucial part of training models (Zhao et al., 2024; Qin et al.,
129
      2024; Wang et al., 2024). The study Kolossov et al. (2023) introduces schemes to select unlabeled
      data for weakly supervised learning. They use perfect surrogate models that follow the distribution
130
      of the full sample whereas imperfect ones do not. The authors develop these schemes from influence
131
      functions (Ting & Brochu, 2017; Wang et al., 2021) and leveraging score methods (Ma et al., 2014),
132   and it is notable that the scheme application gives better results than full sample training. Former
133   methods directly applied their score to the loss function to eliminate the impact of unwanted data,
134   but random augmented method simply adds backbone data from their training batch. See Sec. C for
135   further details.

141

519   6   C ONCLUSION
520
521   Our study introduces and defines Backbone Augmented Training (BAT) in most rigorous way possi-
522   ble. We also conduct experiments to prove our propositions and demonstrate the real world outcomes
523   which shows their alignment and promising results.
524   Limitations. However, the readers must understand that our study is less focused on achieving better
525   performance in adaptations, but suggesting that this idea is very much worthy to investigate for the
526   development of adaptations. In mathematical terms, the convexity and continuity assumptions in the
527   propositions may not be applied to some adaptation architectures. Also, our experimental setting
528   adopts random data sampling before conditional selection which is proven to be inferior to proper
529   selection methods such like Kolossov et al. (2023).
530   Future Work. Many future works are present as our study comprehends broad domains and tech-
531   niques. First, we propose mathematical improvements on Proposition 2. Like many other optimiza-
532
      tion problems (Hinton & Salakhutdinov, 2006; Song et al., 2020; Kingma & Welling, 2022), we
      speculate that the condition to choose helpful backbone data can be more implicit and swift. Also,
533
      the development in entire data selection scheme would make the idea more practical and influen-
534
      tial. Finally, analysis of the favorable and unsuitable backbone data will provide a more profound
535   understanding of the relationship between adaptations and backbone models.
