009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Attention mechanisms are central to the success of large language models (LLMs),
012            enabling them to capture intricate token dependencies and implicitly assign impor-
013            tance to each token. Recent studies have revealed the sink token, which receives
014            disproportionately high attention despite their limited semantic role. In this paper,
015            we first expand the relationship between the sink token and other tokens, mov-
016            ing beyond attention to explore their similarity in hidden states, considering the
017            layer depth. We observe that as the layers get deeper, the cosine similarity be-
018            tween the normalized hidden states of the sink token and those of other tokens in-
019
               creases, and that the normalized hidden states of the sink token exhibit negligible
               changes. These imply that other tokens consistently are directed toward the sink
020
               token throughout the layers. Next, we propose a dynamic token selection method,
021
               called OrthoRank, using these findings to select important tokens. Specifically,
022            in a certain layer, we define token importance by the speed at which the token
023            moves toward the sink token. This is converted into orthogonality with the sink
024            token, meaning that tokens that are more orthogonal to the sink token are assigned
025            greater importance. Finally, through extensive experiments, we demonstrated that
026            our method results in lower perplexity and higher zero-shot accuracy compared to
027            layer pruning methods at the same sparsity ratio with comparable throughput.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large language models (LLMs) have shown remarkable performance across various tasks
033   (Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; 2023; Labrak et al., 2024; Nam et al., 2024) However,
034   despite this, the computational cost of LLM inference remains a significant challenge, especially for
035   real-time applications. To address this challenge, many lightweight methods have been proposed
036   for LLMs. Among the various methods, layer pruning is a simple and effective approach to reduce
037   computational costs by removing layers that have less impact on the model. The impact is quantified
038   by either measuring the similarity between the input and output at each layer (Siddiqui et al., 2024;
039
      Men et al., 2024), or by evaluating how the removal of a layer effects the final output (Song et al.,
      2024; Kim et al., 2024). Song et al. (2024) proposed an iterative pruning method based on these
040
      metrics, while Kim et al. (2024) introduced a one-shot pruning approach followed by additional tun-
041
      ing using LoRA (Hu et al., 2022). However, these methods have a limitation. They rely on selecting
042   layers for pruning based on a calibration set, which statistically determines the layers that can be
043   bypassed without computation. As a result, they do not effectively reflect the specific characteristics
044   of the input tokens. For instance, at each layer, certain tokens may require computation while others
045   do not, but layer pruning is unable to identify and process these tokens accordingly.
046
      Motivated by the need for token level processing, early exit (Schuster et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024)
047
      and mixture of depth (Raposo et al., 2024) have proposed dynamic computation paths based on
048   token-level characteristics. Early exit determines that a token aligns with the final output, bypassing
049   the remaining layers. Mixture of depth uses routers at each layer to decide whether a token should
050   be computed or skipped. While these methods offer viable solutions, they rely on training additional
051   routers or classifiers, or require the entire model to be trained specifically for early exit. Although
052   these techniques have contributed to LLM acceleration, such as in speculative decoding, their prac-
053   tical use is limited because they require additional training across a wide range of existing models.
      This paper, therefore, begins by questioning:

         Can we identify which tokens advantageous to compute at each layer without extra training?
071
072   To explore this, we analyze the internal workings of LLMs to determine whether each token requires
073   an update within a layer. Our focus is on one of the most distinctive phenomena in LLM behavior: the
074   attention sink (Xiao et al., 2024), which was first studied by investigating attention distributions and
075   identifying the presence of attention sinks. This phenomenon shows that the initial token in an input
076   sequence receives a disproportionately large share of attention, despite often lacking meaningful
077   semantic value. This occurs because, in autoregressive models, the initial token is visible to nearly
078   all subsequent tokens, leading to ‘excessive’ attention scores. Since then, this phenomenon has been
079
      further explored (Sun et al., 2024; Cancedda, 2024), calibrated (Yu et al., 2024), and leveraged
      in various ways (Son et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024) to improve LLM efficiency and enhance
080
      understanding of their mechanisms. Through further investigation, we observe that sink tokens and
081
      other tokens exhibit a distinctive cosine similarity pattern (Section 2).
082
083   From this, we propose an importance ranking of tokens, OrthoRank, which leverages Or-
084   thogonality to Rank tokens based on their relevance to the sink token. We confirm that selecting
085
      tokens with our orthogonal-based importance is effective, as it outperforms the opposite method
      in language modeling performance (Section 3.1). To apply this across multiple layers in the LLM,
086
      we adopt the layer evaluation method from layer pruning. We then replace each layer with a token
087
      selection layer and evaluate them to identify the optimal token selection layers (Section 3.2). In Fig-
088   ure 1, we provide an overview of our proposed method, including the selection scheme. The main
089   idea is to calculate each token’s orthogonality to the sink token to select tokens. Selected tokens
090   pass through all steps within the layer (e.g., query, key, value, feed forward network, etc.), while
091   unselected tokens only participate in key and value calculations for the selected tokens without up-
092   dating their own states, similar to early-exit mechanisms. Many studies (Sun et al., 2024; Son et al.,
093   2024) suggest that in certain models, the attention sink phenomenon occurs not only with any token
094   at the first position but also with specific delimiter tokens (e.g., “.”, “\n”). However, for simplicity
095   and consistency, we focus our calculations on the first token. That is, hli represents the input hidden
096
      states of the sink token (i = 0) and other tokens (i ≥ 1) at layer l.
097   In summary, the key contributions of our paper are as follows:
098
099          • We discover that after the layer where the attention sink occurs, the cosine similarity be-
100            tween the normalized hidden states of the sink token and those of other tokens increases,
101            as the layers deepen. However, the normalized hidden states of the sink token across the
102
               layers remains largely unchanged. These mean that other tokens are heading toward the
               sink token.
103
104          • We propose a simple but effective token selection method OrthoRank based on token-
105            sink orthogonality, prioritizing tokens closer to orthogonality for computation and bypass-
106
               ing others, without the need for additional modules or training.
107          • We conduct extensive evaluations demonstrating that our method has better performance
               compared to the existing layer pruning at the same sparsity with comparable throughput.

134   2    F URTHER A NALYSIS ON ATTENTION S INK B EYOND ATTENTION
135
136
      In this section, we revisit the concept of the attention sink and introduce new insights based on
137
      further analysis. An attention sink refers to the phenomenon where a particular token receives a
138   disproportionately high amount of attention from other tokens. This phenomenon is always observed
139   after a certain early layer, lsink , in the initial token (Xiao et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024).
140
141
      We begin by verifying whether the special relationship between the sink token and the other tokens
      appears in states other than attention. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to analyze the
142
      behavior of the sink token and other tokens through similarity analysis. In fact, the attention map
143
      provides limited information regarding the layers because there is little difference between the layers
144   after layer lsink . Therefore, we focus on the hidden states after the pre-attention normalization layer
145   (i.e., the normalized hidden states), because they are the direct inputs for an attention module in each
146   layer.
147
      We investigate the cosine similarity between the normalized hidden states of tokens throughout the
148
      layers, using wikittext dataset (Appendix F). Figures 2(a) and 2(d) describe the cosine similarity
149
      between the normalized hidden states of the sink token (i.e., h̄0 ) and those of other tokens (i.e.,
150   h̄i (1 ≤ i ≤ 10)) of Llama-2-13B and Mistral-7B, respectively. For each model, the attention sink
151   occurs at layer 4 and layer 2. It is observed that the cosine similarity between the sink token and other
152   tokens decreases drastically right after layer lsink . However, after layer lsink , the cosine similarity
153   between the sink token and other tokens tends to increase as the layers progress, although the ranges
154   of cosine similarity vary across models.
155
      These findings are simply illustrated in 3D by Figures 2(b-c) and 2(e-f). We plot each state as a
156   unit vector on the hypersphere to focus on angles at layer right after lsink and the final layer. In the
157   subfigures, the red line represents the normalized hidden states of the sink token (i.e., h̄0 ), while
158   the blue lines represent those of other tokens (i.e., h̄1 and h̄10 ). Additionally, the cosine similarity
159   between other tokens except for the sink token does not show any consistent trend, presented in
160   Appendix B. In summary, our findings offer insights that go beyond the information derived from
161   the attention map, revealing that as the layers deepen, the angles between the sink token and the
      other tokens gradually decrease, after layer lsink .

      4     E XPERIMENTS
350   4.1     I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
351
352   We conducted experiments comparing layer pruning and OrthoRank with selective layer approaches.
353
      Following the evaluation protocol in (Song et al., 2024), we set target sparsities at 10% and 20%. To
      ensure the same sparsity ratio across methods, our algorithm applied 15% and 30 % layer selection,
354
      with only 33% of tokens computed in the selected layers. We compared our method against two
355
      baseline algorithms: the iterative layer pruning method SLEB (Song et al., 2024) and the one-shot
356   pruning method Shortened LLaMA (Kim et al., 2024) without finetuning. Since the one-shot method
357   suffers a significant performance drop at 20% without fine-tuning, we limited its comparison to 10%
358   sparsity. We measured throughput on a single A6000 GPU with a batch size of 32 and prompts of
359   length 2048. The throughput was averaged over 50. To validate the robustness of ours across a wide
360   range of models, we conducted experiments on various models, including Llama2 (7B, 13B, 70B)
361   (Touvron et al., 2023), Llama3 (8B), Llama3.1 (70B) (Dubey et al., 2024), Mistral (7B) (Jiang et al.,
362   2023), Mixtral (8×7B) (Jiang et al., 2024). Except for the ablation study on token selection ratio
363   (Section 4.5.4), all experiments were conducted with a ratio of 0.333.

      5    R ELATED WORK
487
488
      Layer Pruning. Layer pruning has been a prominent approach for reducing the computational
489   complexity of large language models (LLMs), particularly in transformer architectures (Siddiqui
490   et al., 2024; Men et al., 2024). Approaches like SLEB (Song et al., 2024) and Shortened LLaMA
491   (Kim et al., 2024) aim to remove entire layers that are deemed less critical for downstream tasks.
492   These methods often rely on performance metrics or sensitivity analysis to determine which layers
493   contribute less to overall model accuracy and can be pruned without significant loss of performance.
494   However, layer pruning may result in abrupt performance degradation, particularly when layers
495   responsible for essential token transformations are removed. While these methods effectively reduce
496   model depth, they do not account for token-level variations within layers. In contrast, our approach
497
      integrates token selection within specific layers, maintaining layer depth but reducing the number
      of tokens processed in each layer, thus offering a more fine-grained control over computational
498
      efficiency.
499
500
      Attention Sink. The concept of the attention sink, where certain tokens receive disproportionately
501
      high attention across layers, has gained attention in recent studies. Xiao et al. (2024) first introduced
502   the term “attention sink” to describe how the initial token in a sequence tends to dominate attention
503   scores in autoregressive models. This is attributed to its visibility to all subsequent tokens, causing it
504   to act as a “sink” for attention. Sun et al. (2024) further investigated the attention sink phenomenon,
505   showing that this behavior persists across multiple layers, leading to a static role for the sink token,
506   while other tokens move toward it in hidden state space. Building on these observations, our work
507   explores token-sink orthogonality and uses this metric to inform token selection. By selecting tokens
508   that are more orthogonal to the sink token, we prioritize tokens with greater potential to contribute to
509   meaningful computations, leveraging the inherent token dynamics to optimize inference efficiency.
510
511
      Token Pruning. Token pruning methods have been widely explored as a way to reduce the number
      of tokens processed across layers, thus decreasing computational load. Techniques like dynamic to-
512
      ken selection (Lou et al., 2024) and early exit mechanisms (Chen et al., 2024; Del Corro et al., 2023;
513
      Elhoushi et al., 2024) progressively drop tokens deemed uninformative as they pass through layers.
514   These methods rely on criteria such as attention scores or token contribution measures to decide
515   which tokens to prune. However, one potential downside of token pruning is the loss of potentially
516   relevant information as tokens are eliminated layer by layer, especially in deeper models where re-
517   maining tokens may not fully capture the complexity of the input sequence. Our approach differs
518   significantly in that we do not progressively drop tokens across layers. Instead, we selectively com-
519   pute a subset of tokens at specific layers based on their orthogonality to the sink token. This ensures
520   that we preserve the flexibility to compute tokens based on their relevance without completely dis-
521   carding them, thus mitigating the risk of information loss while still reducing computational costs.
522
523   6    C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this paper, we introduced OrthoRank, a dynamic token selection strategy based on the orthogo-
526   nality between tokens and the sink token. Our approach was motivated by the observation that as
527   layers deepen, tokens increasingly align with the sink token in the normalized hidden state space.
528   By analyzing token-sink similarity, we found that tokens more orthogonal to the sink token play
529   a greater role in computation. Leveraging this insight, we developed a token selection mechanism
530   that prioritizes such tokens at specific layers, leading to more efficient computation. By applying
531
      this token selection approach to selective layers, we achieved superior performance compared to
      traditional layer pruning methods at the same sparsity level with comparable throughput. Extensive
532
      experiments demonstrated significant improvements, and ablation studies confirmed that our selec-
533
      tion scheme is optimized both theoretically and empirically. Furthermore, our findings on token-sink
534   similarity offer valuable insights for future research in Efficient LLM inference and Interpretable
535   LLMs, providing a foundation for further optimizing and understanding large language models.
