009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The Value Iteration Network (VIN) is an end-to-end differentiable architecture
012            that performs value iteration on a latent Markov Decision Process (MDP) for plan-
013            ning in reinforcement learning (RL). However, VINs struggle to scale to long-term
014            and large-scale planning tasks, such as navigating a 100 × 100 maze—a task that
015            typically requires thousands of planning steps to solve. We observe that this de-
016            ficiency is due to two issues: the representation capacity of the latent MDP and
017            the planning module’s depth. We address these by augmenting the latent MDP
018            with a dynamic transition kernel, dramatically improving its representational ca-
019
               pacity, and, to mitigate the vanishing gradient problem, introduce an “adaptive
               highway loss” that constructs skip connections to improve gradient flow. We eval-
020
               uate our method on 2D maze navigation environments, the ViZDoom 3D navi-
021
               gation benchmark, and the real-world Lunar rover navigation task. We find that
022            our new method, named Dynamic Transition VIN (DT-VIN), scales to 5000 layers
023            and solves challenging versions of the above tasks. Altogether, we believe that
024            DT-VIN represents a concrete step forward in performing long-term large-scale
025            planning in RL environments.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Planning is the problem of finding a sequence of actions that achieve a specific pre-defined goal.
031   As the aim of both some older algorithms (e.g., Dyna (Sutton, 1991), A* (Hart et al., 1968), and
032   others (Schmidhuber, 1990a;b)) and many recent ones (e.g., the Predictron (Silver et al., 2017), the
033   Dreamer family of algorithms (Hafner et al., 2020; 2021; 2023), SoRB (Eysenbach et al., 2019),
034   SA-CADRL (Chen et al., 2017), and the LLM-planner (Song et al., 2023)), effective planning is a
035   long-standing and important challenge in artificial intelligence (AI).
036
      Traditional search-based planning algorithms like A* require an accurate environmental model.
037   Thus, these algorithms are limited in their effectiveness when faced with unknown Markov decision
038   processes. In such scenarios, a policy can be learned either through imitation learning (IL), which
039   leverages expert demonstrations, or through trial and error with reinforcement learning (RL). Within
040   RL and IL, the Value Iteration Network (VIN) (Tamar et al., 2016) stands out as quite unique due
041   to its distinctive architecture that integrates a differentiable latent “planning module” into the deep
042   neural network, rather than maintaining an explicit learned environment model like Dreamer (Hafner
043   et al., 2020) or MuZero (Schrittwieser et al., 2020). This integrated architecture allows VINs to be
044   trained end-to-end, meaning that both the state representation components and the planning com-
045
      ponents are implicitly trained jointly. Additionally, the integrated planning capability of VINs still
      enables them to effectively generalize to related but unseen tasks. VINs have been shown to perform
046
      well in some small-scale short-term planning situations, like path planning (Pflueger et al., 2019; Jin
047
      et al., 2021), autonomous navigation (Wöhlke et al., 2021), and complex decision-making in dy-
048   namic environments (Li et al., 2021). However, they still struggle to solve larger-scale and longer-
049   term planning problems. We refer to large-scale planning tasks as those with high-dimensional
050   observation space (e.g., the maze size), and long-term planning tasks as those necessitating ex-
051   tended planning horizons to achieve the goal. For example, in a 100 × 100 maze navigation task, the
052   success rate of VINs in reaching the goal drops to well below 40% (see Figure 1(b)). Even in smaller
053   35 × 35 mazes, the success rate of VINs drops to 0% when the required planning steps exceed 60
      (see Figure 1(c)).

      Our work identifies that the principal deficiency causing this is the mismatch between the com-
074   plexity of planning and the comparatively weak representational capacity of the relatively shallow
075   networks that it uses. And while there has been moderate success in learning more complicated
076   networks (e.g., GPPN (Lee et al., 2018) and Highway VINs (Wang et al., 2024a)), until now, VINs
077   of a scale capable of long-term or large-scale planning have not been computationally tractable due
078   to persistent issues with vanishing and exploding gradients—a fundamental problem of deep learn-
079   ing (Hochreiter, 1991).
080   In this work, we aim to surgically correct deficiencies in VIN-based architectures to enable large-
081   scale long-term planning. Specifically, we first identify the limitations of the latent MDP in the
082   planning module of VIN and propose a dynamic transition kernel to dramatically increase the rep-
083   resentational capacity of the network. We then build on existing work that identifies the connec-
084   tion between network depth and long-term planning (Wang et al., 2024a) and propose an “adaptive
085   highway loss” that selectively constructs skip connections to the final loss according to the actual
086   number of planning steps. This approach helps mitigate the vanishing gradient problem and enables
087   the training of very deep networks. With these changes, we find that our new Dynamic Transition
088
      Value Iteration Network (DT-VIN), is able to be trained with 5000 layers and scale to 1800 planning
      steps in a 100 × 100 maze navigation task (compared to the original VIN, which only scaled to 120
089
      planning steps in a 25 × 25 maze). We apply our method to top-down image-based maze navigation
090
      tasks, the first-person image-based ViZDoom benchmark (Wydmuch et al., 2019), and real-world
091   Lunar rover navigation tasks (Berlin, 2018). We find that DT-VINs can solve both despite these
092   problems requiring hundreds to thousands of planning steps. Together, these demonstrate the prac-
093   tical utility of our method on vision-based tasks that previous methods are simply unable to solve.
094   This also serves to highlight the potential of our method to scale to increasingly complex planning
095   tasks alongside the increasing availability of computing power.

      Reinforcement Learning (RL) and Imitation Learning (IL). The most common formalism used
100   for RL is that of the Markov Decision Process (MDP) (Bellman, 1957). We consider an MDP—as
101   per Puterman (2014)—to be the 6-tuple (S, A, T , R, γ, µ), where S is a countable state space, A

103   being in state s ∈ S and taking action a ∈ A, R(s, a, s′ ) is the scalar reward function, γ ∈ [0, 1)
104   is a discount factor, and µ is a distribution over initial states. The behaviour of an artificial agent in
105   an MDP is defined by its policy π(a|s), which specifies the probability of taking action a in state s.
106   The state value function V π (s) is the expected discounted sum of rewards from state s and following

150   In this section, we discuss how to train scalable VINs for long-term large-scale planning tasks. Our
151   method addresses the two key issues with VIN that are identified as hampering its scalability: the
152   capacity of the latent MDP representation and the depth of the planning module.

225   3.2   I NCREASING D EPTH OF P LANNING M ODULE
226
227   Motivation. Recent work on Highway VIN has demonstrated the relationship between the depth
228   of VIN’s planning module and its planning ability (Wang et al., 2024a). A deeper planning module
229   implies more iterations of the value iterations process, which is proved to result in a more accu-
230   rate estimation of the optimal value function (see Theorem 1.12 (Agarwal et al., 2019)). How-
231   ever, training very deep neural networks is challenging due to the vanishing or exploding gradient
232
      problem (Hochreiter, 1991). Highway VINs address this issue by incorporating skip connections
      within the context of reinforcement learning, showing similarities to existing works for classifica-
233
      tion tasks (Srivastava et al., 2015a; He et al., 2016). Although Highway VINs can be trained with up
234
      to 300 layers, they still fail to achieve perfect scores in larger-scale and longer-term planning tasks
235   and necessitate a more intricate implementation. Here, we present a more simple, easy-to-implement
236   method for training very deep VINs.
237
238

240   but implement it differently. Our central insight is that short-term planning tasks generally require
241

      formation from the goal position propagates to the start position in fewer steps when their distance
242
      is short. Therefore, we propose adding additional loss to shallower layers directly when the task
243
      requires only a few steps. We achieve this by introducing the following adaptive highway loss:

      4     E XPERIMENTS
271
272
      We perform several experiments to test if our modifications to VIN’s planning module allow train-
273   ing very deep DT-VINs for large-scale long-term planning tasks. Following previous work (Tamar
274   et al., 2016; Lee et al., 2018), we focus on the imitation learning scenario, where we leverage expert
275   demonstrations to evaluate planning capabilities. IL offers a more stable and controlled setting by
276   reducing the variability that typically arises from the exploratory processes in RL. In line with pre-
277   vious works (e.g., (Lee et al., 2018)), we assess our planning algorithms on navigation tasks within
278   2D mazes and 3D ViZDoom (Wydmuch et al., 2019) environments (see Sections 4.1 and 4.2, re-
279   spectively). Each task includes a start position and a goal position, and the agent navigates the four
280   adjacent cells by moving one step at a time in any of the four cardinal directions. Our experiments
281
      look at each method’s effectiveness over several versions of the tasks with the different versions
      having different shortest path lengths (SPLs). The SPLs are precomputed using Dijkstra’s algorithm
282
      and serve as a good proxy measure for the complexity of the planning task. We say that an agent
283
      has succeeded in a task if it generates a path from the start position to the goal position within a pre-
284   determined number of steps (m2 in our paper). We further say that the agent has found an optimal
285   path if the corresponding path has a minimal length. We follow GPPN and use these for the success
286   rate (SR), which is the rate at which the algorithm succeeds in the task, and the optimality rate (OR),
287   which is the rate at which the algorithm generates an optimal path. In addition to the above, we
288   also test the generality of the DT-VIN approach to two additional tasks. In the style of a bench-
289   mark examined in the original VIN paper, a lunar rover navigation task (see Section 4.3); and, to
290   demonstrate the potential for complex action spaces, a continuous control task (see Appendix C.3).
291   On the above tasks, we compare our DT-VIN method with several advanced neural networks de-
292   signed for planning tasks, including the original VIN (Tamar et al., 2016), GPPNs (Lee et al., 2018),
293   and Highway VIN (Wang et al., 2024a). The models are trained through imitation learning using
294   a labelled dataset. We then identify the best-performing model based on its results on a validation
295   dataset and evaluate it on a separate test dataset. Following the methodology from the GPPN paper,
296   we conduct evaluations using three different random seeds for each algorithm. This is sufficient to
297   provide a reliable performance estimate here due to the low standard deviation we observe in the
298
      tasks. All figures that show learning curves report the mean and standard deviation on the test set.
299

      5   R ELATED W ORK
487
488
      Variants of Value Iteration Networks (VINs). Several variants of VIN (Tamar et al., 2016) have
489   been proposed in recent years. Gated Path Planning Networks employ gating recurrent mechanisms
490   to reduce the training instability and hyperparameter sensitivity seen in VINs (Lee et al., 2018).
491   To mitigate overestimation bias (which is detrimental to learning here), dVINs were proposed and
492   use a weighted double estimator as an alternative to the maximum operator (Jin et al., 2021). For
493   addressing challenges in irregular spatial graphs, Generalized VINs adopt a graph convolution op-
494   erator, extending the traditional convolution operator used in VINs (Niu et al., 2018). To improve
495   scalability, AVINs introduce an abstraction module that extracts higher-level information from the
496   environment and the goal (Schleich et al., 2019). For transfer learning, Transfer VINs address the
497
      generalization of VINs to target domains where the action space or the environment’s features differ
      from those of the training environments (Shen et al., 2020). More recently, VIRN was proposed
498
      and employs larger convolutional kernels to plan using fewer iterations as well as self-attention to
499
      propagate information from each layer to the final output of the network (Cai et al., 2022). Simi-
500   larly, GS-VIN also uses larger convolutional kernels but to stabilize training and also incorporates a
501   gated summarization module that reduces the accumulated errors during value iteration (Cai et al.,
502   2023). Most related to DT-VIN is other recent work that focused on developing very deep VINs
503   for long-term planning. Specifically, Highway VIN (Wang et al., 2024a) incorporates the theory of
504   Highway Reinforcement Learning (Wang et al., 2024b) to create deep planning networks with up
505   to 300 layers for long-term planning tasks. Highway VIN modifies the planning module of VIN
506   by introducing an exploration module that injects stochasticity in the forward pass and uses gating
507   mechanisms to allow selective information flow through the network layers. Our method, however,
508
      achieves even deeper planning by incorporating a dynamic transition matrix in the latent MDP and
      adaptively weighting each layer’s connection to the final output.
509
510
      Neural Networks with Deep Architectures. There is a long history of developing very deep
511
      neural networks (NNs). For sequential data, this prominently includes the LSTM architecture and
512
      its gated residual connections, which help alleviate the “vanishing gradient problem” (Hochreiter &
513   Schmidhuber, 1997; Hochreiter, 1991). For feedforward NNs, a similar gated residual connection ar-
514   chitecture was used in Highway Networks (Srivastava et al., 2015a) and later in the ResNet architec-
515   ture (He et al., 2016), where the gates were kept open. Such residual connections are still ubiquitous
516   in modern language architectures, such as the Generative Pre-trained Transformer (GPT) (Achiam
517   et al., 2023). Our method dynamically employs skip connections from select hidden layers to the
518   final loss, utilizing a state and observation map-dependent transition kernel. This approach is more
519   closely aligned with the computation of the true VI algorithm. Similar kernels, dependent on an in-
520   put image (Chen et al., 2020) or the coordinates of an image (Liu et al., 2018), have been previously
521
      used in Computer Vision.
522
523   6   C ONCLUSIONS
524
525   Planning is a long-standing challenge in the field of artificial intelligence and its subfield: rein-
526   forcement learning. Previous work proposed VIN as an end-to-end differentiable neural network
527   architecture for this task. While VINs have been successful at short-term small-scale planning, they
528   start to fail quite rapidly as the horizon and the scale of the planning grows. We observed that this
529
      decay in performance is principally due to limitations in the (1) representational capacity of their
      network and (2) its depth. To alleviate these problems, we propose several modifications to the
530
      architecture, including a dynamic transition kernel to increase the representation capacity and an
531
      adaptive highway loss function to ease the training of very deep models. Altogether, these modifi-
532   cations have allowed us to train networks with 5000 layers. In line with previous work, we evaluate
533   the efficacy of our proposed Dynamic Transition VINs (DT-VINs) on 2D maze, 3D ViZDoom, and
534   rover navigation environments. We find that DT-VINs scale to longer-term and larger-scale planning
535   problems than previous attempts. To the best of our knowledge, DT-VINs is, at the time of publica-
536   tion, the current state-of-the-art planning solution for these specific environments. We note that the
537   upper bound for this approach (i.e., the scale of the network and, consequentially, the scale of the
538   planning ability) remains unknown. As our experiments were limited mostly by computational cost
539   and did not observe instability, we expect that with the growth of available computational power,
      our method will scale to even longer-term and larger-scale planning.

      A     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
703
704
705
      The principal limitation of our work compared to VIN and Highway VIN is the increased computa-
      tional cost (see Appendix C.5). This is a consequence of the scale of the network. The past decades
706
      have seen AI dominated by the trend of scaling up systems (Sutton), so this is not likely a long-term
707
      issue. Other limitations include the requirement to know the length of the shortest path l in the
708   highway loss in imitation learning. In a general RL problem, such a quantity could be estimated
709   online. Future work will explore the impact of a more sophisticated transition mapping module (this
710   work uses a single CNN layer for this purpose) in more challenging real-world applications, such as
711   real-time robotics navigation in dynamic and unpredictable environments.

721   Figure 8 shows some visualizations of some of the different 2D maze navigation tasks we experiment
722   with. Our experimental setup follows the guidelines established in the GPPN paper (Lee et al.,
723   2018). For these tasks, the datasets for training, validation, and testing comprise 25000, 5000, and
724   5000 mazes, respectively. Each maze features a goal position, with all reachable positions selected
725   as potential starting points. Note that this setting, as done by GPPN, produces a distribution of mazes
726   with non-uniform SPLs, which is skewed towards shorter SPLs. Table 3 shows the hyperparameters
727   used by our method. Note that, while DT-VIN consistently uses 3 for the size of the latent transition
728   kernel F and 4 for the size of the latent action space |A|, other methods instead used their best-
729
      performing sizes from between 3 and 5, and between 4 and 150, respectively.
730   Moreover, to reduce the computational complexity of highway loss, we apply adaptive highway loss
731   only to layers n satisfying the condition n mod J = 0, where J is a hyperparameter set to 10 in
732   our experiments. Here, the main idea is to build the highway connections at interval J, for example,
733   every 10 neural network layers. Using this, the number of the loss terms will reduce to only 1/J of
734   the original one. Table 2 shows the magnitude of the computational speedup as a concequence of

      this implementation detail.

1063   without adaptive highway loss, the L1 norm of the gradient for DT-VIN is closer to zero in the first
1064   10 layers of the network. The adaptive highway loss can reduce this vanishing gradient problem,
1065
       resulting in lower loss.

1068
       the imitation learning case. However, for the case where such information is unknown, one can use
       either the length of non-expert data or some heuristic methods to estimate l when the actual l is
1069
       completely unknown, e.g., using the distance between the start and the goal position.
1070
1071   To measure the effect of overestimation/underestimation, we experiment with various estimated
1072   values of the length of the shortest path b l, which are 0, l/2, l, 2l, N (where l is the actual length of
1073   the shortest path, N is the depth of the planning module). Second, to evaluate the case when the
1074   etsimation of l has variance, we use l · max(ϵ, 0) as the estimation, with ϵ sampled from a Gaussian
1075   distribution N (1, 1). Third, we also assess two additional variants for estimating l: (a) One variant
1076
       that utilizes the length of non-expert trajectories for l; (b) Another variant that estimates the shortest
       path length heuristically using the L1 distance between the start (xs , ys ) and the goal (xg , yg ), i.e.,
1077
       D = |xs − xg | + |ys − yg |.
