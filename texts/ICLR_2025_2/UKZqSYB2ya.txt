009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Accurate segmentation of lung nodules in computed tomography (CT) scans is
012            challenging due to extreme class imbalance, where nodules appear sparsely among
013            healthy tissue. Lung tumor boards often review these scans manually, a time-
014            consuming process. This paper introduces a novel two-stage approach for lung
015            tumor segmentation by framing the problem as anomaly detection. The method
016            is divided into two stages, allowing each model to leverage its strengths. Stage 1
017
               focuses on region proposal, employing a custom Deformable Detection Transformer
               with Focal Loss to overcome class imbalance and localize sparse tumors. In Stage
018
               2, the predicted bounding boxes are refined into pixel-wise segmentation masks
019
               using a fine-tuned variant of Meta’s Segment Anything Model (SAM) for semantic
020            segmentation. To address the challenge of nodule sparsity and improve spatial
021            context, a 7.5 mm Maximum Intensity Projection (MIP) is applied, aiding in the
022            differentiation between nodules, bronchioles, and vascular structures. The model
023            achieves a Dice coefficient of 92.4%, with 95.2% sensitivity and 93.2% precision
024            on the LUNA16 dataset, demonstrating robust performance in real-world clinical
025            conditions where nodule sparsity is 5%.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Lung cancer is a leading cause of cancer-related deaths worldwide with early detection and accurate
      assessment being crucial for improving outcomes. Lung tumor boards, comprising oncologists,
031
      radiologists, surgeons, pathologists, and other specialists, collaboratively review complex lung cancer
032
      cases to determine the best treatment plan. Accurate segmentation of lung nodules in CT scans
033   is essential, as it provides critical information about tumor size, location, and spread. However,
034   tumor board evaluations typically perform segmentation manually which can be is impractical as it is
035   time-consuming, slowing decision-making and increasing resource demands.
036
      Clinical decision support (CDS) systems have emerged as promising tools to address these challenges,
037
      integrating clinical knowledge with patient-specific data. Although increasingly integrated into
038
      routine clinical workflows, their widespread adoption remains variable due to challenges in system
039   integration and user engagement David C. Wyatt and de Waal (2019). Implementing a CDS system
040   for auto-segmentation of lung metastases can enhance workflow efficiency, improve patient outcomes,
041   and reduce costs. Despite the benefits, current models struggle with the extreme class imbalance that
042   appears in CT data, as lung nodules appear infrequently among healthy tissue. Nodule volumes are
043   much smaller than the overall lung volume, vary widely in size and location, and often have similar
044   shape and density to vasculature on an axial CT slice. These challenges underscore the need for a
045   customized architecture that addresses these limitations to ensure a reliable tool for clinicians.
046
047   2   R ELATED W ORK
048
049   Thoracic Computed Tomography and LUNA16 Dataset. Thoracic Computed Tomography (CT)
050   involves a series of 2D cross-sectional greyscale images that, when combined, form a detailed 3D
051   representation of the patient’s thorax. The LUNA16 dataset, derived from the LIDC-IDRI dataset,
052   consists of 888 thoracic CT scans containing 1,186 annotated lung nodules. Lung nodules in the
053   LUNA16 dataset are annotated based on the consensus of at least three out of four radiologists, with
      nodules larger than 3 mm considered relevant findings.

054
      CT datasets present challenges due to the sparsity of nodules—typically only 0 to 5% of slices contain
055   a nodule Walter et al. (2016)—and the varying voxel sizes and scan resolutions across different
056   patients. The LUNA16 dataset serves as a critical benchmark and numerous studies have trained
057   architectures such as CNNs, 3D-CNNs, U-Net, and V-Net on the dataset El-bana et al. (2020); Gu et al.
058   (2018). Existing research often uses inconsistent and under-documented data processing techniques,
059   sometimes extracting only positive tumor slices. This makes them difficult to replicate and less
060   applicable to real-world scenarios where lung nodules sparisty is a key limitation for modelsAgnes
061   and Anitha (2020); Bhattacharyya et al. (2023). Additionally more powerful transformer architectures
062   have not yet been fully explored on the problem space Xiao et al. (2020).
063
      Handling Imbalanced Data in Deep Learning. Imbalanced Data is challenging for models
064
      because they are optimized to minimize overall error which leads to a bias favouring the majority
065
      class over the minority class. In cases of class imbalance models are at risk of converging to a
066   majority class classifier, which keep accuracy high but results in no detections of lung nodules.
067
068   Various mitigation strategies for class imbalance exist, including oversampling, class weighting, and
069
      focal loss. Oversampling tumor slices artificially balances the dataset by increasing the number of
      minority class examples, but misrepresents the prevalence of the object in real-world conditions Qu
070
      et al. (2020). Class weighting increases the loss contribution of the minority class, forcing the model
071
      to pay more attention to underrepresented cases Buda et al. (2017). However, this is also shown to
072   increase false positives as it over-represents the minority class Chan et al. (2019; 2020). A more
073   targeted approach, focal loss, modifies the cross-entropy loss by down-weighting well-classified
074   examples and emphasizing hard-to-classify ones like tumors, adjusting the loss based on prediction
075   confidence. This method avoids shortcomings of other techniques and is demonstrates improved
076   detection for rare classes with less false positives Lin et al. (2017b).
077
078   Transformers in Medical Computer Vision Transformer architectures have become a strong
079
      alternative to Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) in medical computer vision. CNN’s local
      receptive fields are good at capturing local features, but struggle with long-range dependencies which
080
      refer to the model’s ability to understand relationships between distant parts of an image Shamshad
081
      et al. (2023). Transformers’ self-attention allows the capture of these complex relationships and are
082   crucial for allowing differentiation between nodules and vessels. Shamshad et al. (2023).
083
084   Detection Transformer (DETR) is an object detection vision transformer (ViT) architecture that
085
      uses self-attention to directly predict object locations, bypassing traditional region proposal methods
      Carion et al. (2020). This architecture has set new standards for finding complex objects by integrating
086
      detection as a set prediction problem Carion et al. (2020). However, DETR is known to have slow
087
      training convergence and difficulty in detecting small objects Zhu et al. (2020). Deformable-DETR is
088   an enhancement of DETR that introduces a deformable attention mechanism. This focuses attention
089   on a sparse set of key sampling points near the object, allowing the model to concentrate on the
090   most informative regions rather than the entire image uniformly to improve both the efficiency and
091   performance of the model Zhu et al. (2020). Segment Anything Model (SAM) is a foundational
092   segmentation model designed to be promptable. It is trained on over 1 billion masks across 11 million
093   images, and is shown to be able to efficiently transfer understanding to new data distributions through
094   fine-tuning for advanced pixel-wise segmentation in many fieldsKirillov et al. (2023); Ma et al. (2024).
095   MedSAM, an adaptation of SAM for medical imaging, fine-tunes SAM to focus on the specific needs
096
      of medical images, such as varying tissue densities and anatomical complexities Ma et al. (2024).
097
098   3   M ETHODOLOGY
099
100
      This paper presents a novel approach to lung tumor segmentation for tumor boards by framing the
101
      task as anomaly detection. Our proposed method splits the task into two stages: Stage 1 serves as
102   a region proposal phase to localize sparse tumors, while Stage 2 refines these bounding boxes into
103   pixel-wise segmentation masks. This is the first method to combine various elements, including
104   architectural components like Deformable-DETR and SAM, along with strategies such as Focal Loss
105   and Maximum Intensity Projection (MIP), into a unified framework specifically tailored for sparse
106   lung nodule segmentation. We developed a customized training regimen for a processed LUNA16
107   dataset to address severe class imbalance and focus on difficult cases. This two-stage pipeline is
      specifically designed to optimize segmentation performance for tumor board evaluations.

142

143

181   3.2   DATASET
182
183   The final processed dataset consists of 9,676 CT scan slices, each with a 7.5mm Maximum Intensity
184   Projection (MIP) applied. Among these, 1,226 images are annotated with nodules, while the remaining
185
      8,450 images contain healthy tissue. The dataset was split into 70% for training, 20% for validation,
      and 10% for testing prior to any augmentation to avoid data contamination and ensure rigorous
186
      evaluation. In the training and validation sets, 12.7% of the images contained a lung nodule. To
187
      better mimic real-world conditions, the test set had a reduced lung nodule rate of 5%, contrasting
188   with the higher rate used during training. This elevated rate in training was necessary to strike a
189   balance between realism and model performance, as lower rates resulted in a dataset too sparse for
190   effective training. Post-split, a set of data augmentations was applied to the training set to increase
191   the dataset’s size and variability. These include horizontal and vertical flips, rotations between -15◦
192   and +15◦ , brightness adjustments within -15% to +15%, and Gaussian noise (0.001 to 0.18% SD)
193   simulated typical CT scan sensor noise.

195   3.3   S TAGE 1: R EGION P ROPOSAL
196
197   Stage 1 of our approach focuses on generating region proposals to localize potential tumor candidates
198   within lung CT scans. Detection Transformer (DETR) was chosen as the architecture for proposing
199   bounding boxes due to its strong performance in complex object detection tasks, particularly since it
200   eliminates the need for anchor boxes and ability to handle diverse object sizes and shapes within the
201
      same image. The Deformable variant, introduced by Zhu et al. Zhu et al. (2020), was selected for its
      spatially adaptive and computationally efficient attention mechanism. Initial experimentation with
202
      DETR yielded a sensitivity of 42% after 20 epochs, specifically struggling with tumors under 10mm.
203
      Switching to Deformable-DETR improved sensitivity to over 90% across all tumor sizes after just 8
204   epochs. With 74% of tumors in the LUNA16 dataset measuring 3-10mm, the deformable attention
205   variant was chosen for tumor detection.
206
207
      Figure 2 shows our custom Deformable-DETR architecture which is trained from scratch for detection
      of sparse lung nodules, evaluated using IoU metrics to inform the customized loss discussed in section
208
      3.6. MIP images are processed through a ResNet-50 CNN backbone to extract multi-scale feature
209
      maps, capturing both low-level textures and high-level semantic features. These are augmented with
210   2D sine-cosine positional encodings to preserve spatial context and then fed to the encoder. The
211   encoder uses Deformable Self-Attention (DSA) layers to refine multi-scale feature maps, attending
212   to a sparse set of learnable sampling points around each nodule. The computational complexity
213   of self-attention is O(H 2 W 2 C), where H = 256, W = 256 represent the feature map height
214   and width in pixels, and C = 1 represents the number of channels for grayscale images. The
215   encoder also integrates a multi-scale attention mechanism to process information at different feature
      scales, enhancing the model’s ability to detect nodules of varying sizes. The encoder outputs refined

244   3.4   S TAGE 2: AUTO -S EGMENTATION M ODEL
245
246   In the second stage, we utilize our auto-segmentation model, a fine-tuned adaptation of the Segment
247   Anything Model (SAM) with initial weights imported from MedSAM, visualized in Figure 3. This
248   model is used for precise segmentation by splitting the MIP images back into their individual CT
249   slices and generating pixel-wise segmentation masks for each slice. The bounding boxes produced
250
      by Deformable-DETR are integrated into SAM’s segmentation process through the prompt encoder,
      which is designed to handle various input types such as boxes, points, and masks. These bounding box
251
      embeddings act as attention cues, encoded into high-level features that instruct our auto-segmentation
252
      model to focus on specific areas. The goal is to improve accuracy by guiding the segmentation
253   algorithm to specific regions of the images that are more likely to contain nodules, while still retaining
254   the entire image for context.

      3.5   T RAINING
257
258   Deformable-DETR was trained and evaluated in a Google Colab environment using an L4 GPU,
259   providing sufficient power for high-resolution 3D CT scans. The model was trained for 15 epochs
260   using the AdamW optimizer, with learning rates of 1e-4 for the main parameters and 1e-5 for the
261   backbone, and a weight decay of 1e-4 to mitigate overfitting. A Step Learning Rate Scheduler
262   adjusted the learning rate dynamically, reducing it by a factor of 10 every 10 epochs. The model used
263   a batch size of 6 with mixed precision (16-bit) for improved speed and efficiency. Gradient clipping
264   was set at 0.1, and gradients were accumulated over 6 batches to ensure stable learning.
265   In Stage 2 our auto-segmentation model was trained by loading model checkpoints from MedSAM
266   Ma et al. (2024) and fine-tuning SAM’s architecture using a dataset comprised of processed LUNA16
267   images and their associated ground truth pixel masks. Dice-CrossEntropy Loss was selected to
268   optimize the overlap between predicted and ground truth masks while mitigating class imbalance
269   with Cross-Entropy Loss. The model was trained using the Adam optimizer specifically on the mask
      decoder.

298   function to incorporate focal loss. By adding a modulating factor, focal loss down-weights well-
299   classified samples and emphasizes hard-to-classify samples, assisting in the detection of rare nodule
300   instances Lin et al. (2017a). The focal loss function is defined in Equation (3) Lin et al. (2017a):

304   where pt is the predicted probability of the correct class, αt balances positive and negative examples,
305   and γ adjusts focus towards challenging samples.

      Our two-stage approach outperforms many established models for lung nodule segmentation, par-
      ticularly those based on traditional CNN and U-Net architectures. Models like U-Net, V-Net, and
364
      MRUNet-3D often struggle with small sparse nodules. Architectures like MRUNet-3D Agnes and
365
      Anitha (2020) and DB-Net Bhattacharyya et al. (2023) address these challenges through multi-scale

367   Mkindu et al. Mkindu et al. (2023), achieved strong specificity (97.8%) and high sensitivity but
368   was primarily focused on detection, highlighting the limitations of hybrid models in segmentation.
369   Similarly, SW-UNet Ma et al. (2023), which integrates a sliding window transformer with a CNN,
370   showed improved segmentation accuracy, yet its performance suggests that fully transformer-based
371   models may better handle the complexities of precise lung nodule segmentation.
372   While existing methods like 3D-MSViT Mkindu et al. (2023) and SW-UNet Ma et al. (2023) have
373   integrated transformers in a hybrid manner, our approach aims to fully exploit the benefits of
374   transformers by custom-training models like Deformable-DETR and SAM. These models enable
375   both direct detection and precise pixel-wise segmentation, addressing the complexities that hybrid
376   approaches often miss Carion et al. (2020); Zhu et al. (2020). This approach allows for higher
377   capacity models to better decipher complex structures potentially setting a new standard for accuracy
      in lung nodule segmentation.

      An important point of differentiation is transparency regarding class sparsity. Many existing methods
379   use varying data processing techniques that may emphasize positive tumor slices, which can compli-
380   cate replication and reduce applicability to real-world scenarios where class imbalance is significant
381   Cao et al. (2020). In contrast, our approach preserves the natural class imbalance found in clinical
382   settings, ensuring that both positive and negative samples are accounted for in the model design and
383   evaluation. When tested on a dataset with higher nodule sparsity, our model demonstrated superior
384   performance, particularly in detecting small tumors, a crucial factor in early diagnosis.
385   In our analysis, we discovered that SAM, when deployed independently, struggled with class imbal-
386   ance, achieving only a 45% Dice Coefficient and low overall accuracy. This highlighted a critical
387   gap: SAM’s capabilities were not well-suited for the initial detection phase. To bridge this gap, we
388   integrated Deformable-DETR with a custom loss function as the first stage. This model excelled
389   at localizing nodule regions, narrowing SAM’s task to precise segmentation rather than detection
390   and significantly improving the performance. This division of labor between Deformable-DETR and
391   SAM allowed each model to operate within its strengths.
392   Stage 1 shows relatively lower precision and recall for small nodules (up to 7 mm in diameter),
393   reflecting the inherent challenges of detecting small nodules due to their lower contrast in CT scans.
394   Notably, the prevalence of malignancy in nodules smaller than 6 mm is very low, ranging between 0
395   and 1%, and guidelines from the European Respiratory Society now suggest a threshold of 6 mm for
396   follow-up consideration due to the low malignancy risk associated with these small nodules Larici
397   et al. (2017).

399   6   C ONCLUSION
400
401   This study introduces a two-stage framework for lung nodule segmentation. Stage 1 focuses on region
402   proposal, achieving an F1 score of 94.2% for accurate bounding box predictions. Stage 2 refines these
403   regions into pixel-wise segmentation masks, resulting in a 92.4% Dice coefficient on the LUNA16
404   dataset. This framework demonstrates significant improvements and successfully integrates modern
405   transformer architectures. Looking forward, future research will focus on validating the model across
406   diverse clinical datasets to enhance generalizability and refining its detection capabilities for small
407
      tumors, addressing a key challenge in early lung cancer diagnosis.
