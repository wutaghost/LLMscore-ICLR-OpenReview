011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Multi-view image compression is vital for 3D-related applications. Existing meth-
014            ods often rely on 2D projection similarities between views to estimate disparity,
015            performing well with small disparities, such as in stereo images, but struggling
016
               with more complex disparities from wide-baseline setups, common in virtual re-
               ality and autonomous driving systems. To overcome this limitation, we propose a
017
               novel approach: learning-based multi-view image compression with 3D Gaussian
018
               geometric priors (3D-GP-LMVIC). Our method leverages 3D Gaussian Splatting
019            to derive geometric priors of the 3D scene, enabling more accurate disparity esti-
020            mation between views within the compression model. Additionally, we introduce
021            a depth map compression model to reduce redundancy in geometric information
022            across views. A multi-view sequence ordering method is also proposed to en-
023            hance correlations between adjacent views. Experimental results demonstrate that
024            3D-GP-LMVIC surpasses both traditional and learning-based methods in perfor-
025            mance, while maintaining fast encoding and decoding speed. The code is available
026            at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/3D-GP-LMVIC-8FFA.

029   1   I NTRODUCTIOIN
030
031   The rapid advancement of 3D applications has led to an explosion of multi-view image data across
032   various fields, including virtual reality (VR) (Anthes et al., 2016), augmented reality (AR) (Schmal-
033
      stieg & Hollerer, 2016), visual simultaneous localization and mapping (vSLAM) (Mokssit et al.,
      2023), 3D scene understanding (Dai et al., 2017), autonomous driving (Chen et al., 2017), and med-
034
      ical imaging (Hosseinian & Arefi, 2015). In particular, applications like VR and AR, which rely on
035
      high-quality multi-view visual content to create immersive experiences, generate a massive volume
036   of data that poses significant challenges for storage and transmission. This makes the development
037   of efficient compression techniques crucial for managing the increasing data demands in these fields.
038
039
      Current multi-view coding standards, such as H.264-based MVC (Vetro et al., 2011) and H.265-
      based MV-HEVC (Hannuksela et al., 2015), have been developed to compress multi-view media by
040
      extending their respective base standards and exploiting redundancies across multiple views. These
041
      standards incorporate inter-view prediction, where blocks in one view are predicted based on cor-
042   responding blocks in neighboring view. Disparity estimation is employed to calculate positional
043   differences of objects between views, aiding in the prediction of pixel values. However, these meth-
044   ods rely on manually designed modules, limiting the system’s ability to fully leverage end-to-end
045   optimization. Consequently, the spatial correlation among views may not be fully exploited, leading
046   to suboptimal compression performance.
047
      Learning-based single image compression has seen remarkable advancements (Ballé et al., 2017;
048   2018; Minnen et al., 2018), inspiring extensions of these methods to multi-view image coding (Deng
049   et al., 2021; Lei et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). A central challenge in these ex-
050   tensions lies in the accurate estimation of disparities across different views. For example, Deng
051   et al. (2021; 2023) employ a simple 3x3 homography matrix for disparity estimation, which, while
052   efficient, struggles with complex scene disparities. Alternatively, Ayzik & Avidan (2020); Huang
053   et al. (2023) utilize patch matching method to align the reference view with the target view. This
      approach is effective for horizontal or vertical view shifts but falls short when addressing non-rigid


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      deformations caused by view rotations. Similarly, Zhai et al. (2022) assume that disparity occurs
055   only along the horizontal axis in their stereo matching method, which suffices for stereo images but
056   is inadequate for more complex view transformations where disparity is not limited to the horizontal
057   axis. Some methods leverage cross-attention mechanisms for implicit alignment (Wödlinger et al.,
058   2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). For instance, Zhang et al. (2023) enhance the target
059   view’s representation by multiplying its query with the reference view’s key and value, effectively
060   incorporating reference view features into the target view. However, these methods primarily es-
061   tablish correlations between two views by 2D projection similarities, without considering the 3D
062   spatial relationships between the views and the captured objects.
063   In addition, we are impressed by 3D Gaussian Splatting (3D-GS) (Kerbl et al., 2023), a novel tech-
064   nique for generating 3D representations from multi-view images and synthesizing novel views. This
065   method achieves remarkable results by representing scenes as mixtures of small, colored Gaussians,
066   capturing intricate geometric details and continuous depth and texture variations. The probabilistic
067   nature of Gaussians allows for smooth, accurate modeling of complex surfaces, enabling the creation
068   of realistic and detailed 3D environments. Additionally, the fast differentiable renderer inherent in
069
      3D Gaussian Splatting ensures efficient real-time inference.
070   Building on prior investigation, we propose a novel learning-based multi-view image compression
071   framework with 3D Gaussian geometric priors (3D-GP-LMVIC), which employs 3D-GS as a ge-
072   ometric prior to guide disparity estimation between views. Specifically, 3D-GS generates a depth
073   map for each view, providing precise spatial information at the pixel level. This enables accurate
074   correspondence between views, allowing the compression model to effectively fuse features from
075
      reference views. Due to positional and angular disparities between views, images generally do not
      fully overlap, and merging non-overlapping regions may introduce noise. To address this, we design
076
      a mask based on the 3D Gaussian geometric prior to identify overlapping regions, ensuring more
077
      accurate feature fusion. Additionally, since depth maps are required during decoding, we propose
078   a depth map compression model to efficiently reduce geometric redundancy across views, incorpo-
079   rating a cross-view depth prediction module to capture inter-view geometric correlations. Finally,
080   recognizing the importance of field of view (FoV) overlap in redundancy reduction, we introduce a
081   multi-view sequence ordering method to address the issue of low overlap between adjacent views
082   in unordered sequences. This method defines a distance measure between view pairs to guide the
083   ordering of view sequences.

      methods, however, do not achieve end-to-end joint optimization, limiting their performance.

      such as Ballé et al. (2017; 2018), introduced generalized divisive normalization (GDN) (Ballé et al.,

      Multi-view Image Coding. Traditional multi-view image codecs, such as MVC (Vetro et al., 2011)
      and MV-HEVC (Hannuksela et al., 2015), extend H.264 and H.265, respectively, by incorporat-
127
      ing inter-view correlation modeling to eliminate redundant information between different views.
128
      However, these modules are manually designed, potentially limiting their ability to fully exploit
129   cross-view information.
130
131   Learning-based multi-view image coding primarily focuses on stereo image coding (Deng et al.,
132
      2021; Lei et al., 2022; Wödlinger et al., 2022; Zhai et al., 2022; Deng et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
      2024) and distributed image coding (Ayzik & Avidan, 2020; Huang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023).
133
      These methods either rely on finding explicit pixel coordinate correspondences between views or
134
      use attention-based implicit correspondence modeling to capture inter-view correlations. However,
135   they model inter-view correlations based solely on two-dimensional view images, which may not
136   fully reflect the correspondences in the original three-dimensional space.
137
138
      3D Gaussian Splatting. 3D Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023; Hamdi et al., 2024) introduces
      a differentiable point-based rendering technique that represents 3D points as Gaussian functions
139
      (mean, variance, opacity, color) and projects these 3D Gaussians onto a view to form an image. This
140
      differentiable point-based rendering function allows for the backward update of the attributes of the
141   3D Gaussians, ensuring that their geometrical and textural properties match the original 3D scene.
142   This approach inspired us to utilize 3D Gaussian Splatting to obtain geometric priors of the original
143   3D scene, aiding in the task of multi-view image compression.

148   Figure 1 shows the overall pipeline of 3D-GP-LMVIC. Given a set of multi-view image sequences
149   X = {x1 , x2 , x3 , · · · , xN }, a 3D-GS is trained to estimate depth map dn for each image xn . Both
150   xn and dn are compressed, with the coding reference relationships indicated by black solid arrows
151   in Figure 1. Prior to compressing the image xn , it is necessary to compress xn−1 , dn−1 , and dn .
152   The disparity relationship between the (n − 1)-th and n-th views is inferred from the reconstructed
153   depth maps dˆn−1 and dˆn . Subsequently, based on the estimated disparity relationship, as well as the
154   intermediate features and the reconstructed image x̂n−1 obtained during the image decoding process
155   of the (n − 1)-th view, xn is compressed. When compressing the depth map dn , the model employs
156   the predicted depth map derived from dˆn−1 as a reference. The same neural network architecture
157   and model parameters are used consistently across all views for both image compression and depth
158
      map compression. For the initial view lacking reference information, full-zero tensors are provided
      as the input reference.

530
531
      In this paper, we present 3D-GP-LMVIC, a novel learning-based multi-view image coding frame-
532
      work incorporating 3D Gaussian geometric priors. This framework exploits these geometric priors
533
      to estimate complex disparities and masks between views for effectively utilizing reference view
534   information in the compression process. Additionally, we propose a depth map compression model
535   designed to compactly and accurately represent the geometry of each view, incorporating a cross-
536   view depth prediction module to capture inter-view geometric correlations. Moreover, we introduce
537   a multi-view sequence ordering method for unordered sequences, enhancing the overlap between
538   adjacent views by defining an inter-view distance measure to guide the sequence ordering. Exper-
539   imental results confirm that 3D-GP-LMVIC surpasses existing learning-based coding schemes in
      compression efficiency while maintaining competitive coding speed.
