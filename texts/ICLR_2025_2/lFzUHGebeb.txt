009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Forward regularization (-F) Azoury & Warmuth (2001) with unsupervised knowl-
012            edge was proposed to replace canonical Ridge regularization (-R) in online lin-
013            ear learners, which achieves lower relative regret bounds (Della Vecchia & Basu,
014            2023). However, we observe that -F cannot perform as expected in practice, even
015            possibly losing to -R for online learning tasks. We identify two main causes for
016
               this: (1) inappropriate intervention penalty; (2) potential non-i.i.d nature in on-
               line learning, both of which result in unstable posterior distribution and optima
017
               offset of the learner. To improve these, we propose Variable Forward regulariza-
018
               tion (-kF), a more general style with -F intensity modulated by a variable k. We
019            further derive -kF algorithm to online learning tasks, which shows holistic recur-
020            sive closed-form updates and superior performance compared to both -R and -F.
021            Moreover, we theoretically establish the relative regrets of -kF in online learning,
022            showing that it has a tighter upper bound than -F in adversarial settings. We also
023            introduce an adaptive -kF, termed -kF-Bayes, to curb unstable penalties caused
024            by non-i.i.d and mitigate intractable tuning of hard k based on Bayesian learn-
025            ing for online learning. In experiments, we adapted -kF and -kF-Bayes into class
026            incremental scenario, where it realized less forgetting and non-replay. Results
027
               distinctly demonstrate the efficacy of using -kF and -kF-Bayes.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Unlike offline training, the online learning (OL) scenario emphasizes progressive model updates
032   and delivers immediate decision-making on non-stationary data/task streams. Applications of OL
033   methodology include chatbots (Chowdhury et al., 2023), injection attack detection (Toyer et al.,
034   2024), and recommendation systems (Jiang et al., 2024) of scenes with real-time demands. During
035   the OL process, the backbone is expected to preserve previous knowledge without past replay and
036   reduce learning regrets (Buening et al.). In this paper, we study linear regression, one basic ele-
037   ment of connectionist models, equipped with a novel regularization and its characteristics in online
038
      continual learning (CL) scenarios.
039   Forward regularization (-F) proposed in (Azoury & Warmuth, 2001; Vovk, 2001) was proven to
040   have better regret bounds compared to using canonical Ridge regularization (-R) in linear regression
041   with challenging adversarial bounded observations during OL Della Vecchia & Basu (2023). Specif-
042   ically, -F is realized by adding an extra Frobenius-norm penalty containing upcoming unsupervised
043   knowledge to -R, which leads the gradients of learnable weights to be more concerned with future
044
      prediction. Besides the decrease in regret, the transductive method -F has low computation com-
      plexity and regulates the learning rate in OL. As a result, -F is of practical relevance as it enhances
045
      prediction accuracy by integrating unlabeled data.
046
047   However, we observe that -F could not perform as expected in experiments, even possibly failing
048   to -R during OL. We argue the reason is two-fold. (1) Improper regularization. -F defaults the
049   penalty intensity of the unsupervised term is constant 1.0, resulting in improper penalty intervention
050
      in special tasks. (2) Non-i.i.d disturbance. Premise i.i.d is not held in OL, which incurs unstable
      regularization due to varying distribution and batch volume. This causes residuals of empirical risk
051
      minimization (ERM) and model optimum offsets, especially in learning long task streams.
052
053   To solve the problems, we first propose the Variable Forward regularization (-kF), adding a hard
      threshold k to control the penalty strength for different tasks. The more general -kF is theoretically

054
      proven to have better performance and tighter regret bounds than -R and -F by two ways if the k can
055   be given properly. The -kF also generates one-shot closed-form incremental updates and variable
056   learning rate, and has less learning dissipation. Moreover, to curb unstable penalties caused by non-
057   i.i.d and mitigate intractable tuning of hard k during OL, we improve the -kF to -kF-Bayes style,
058   further enabling the soft k to be adaptively determined based on Bayesian learning and estimated
059   distribution.
060
      Our algorithm is assessed within the framework of Continual Learning (CL), which is a sub-
061   set of OL. CL involves learning a sequence of Q distinct tasks or episodes, denoted as T =
062   {{T1 , T2 , ¬∑ ¬∑ ¬∑ , TQ }|Tq = (Xq , Yq )}, and includes unseen classes (French, 1999). Among the various
063   scenarios of CL, Class Incremental Learning (CIL) poses the greatest challenge. In CIL, the learner
064
                                                                                                     SQ
      is required to integrate and retain knowledge of all previous classes, represented as Y = q=1 Yq
065   without access to task identities during testing. To overcome catastrophic forgetting, EWC Kirk-
066   patrick et al. (2017) is a pioneer regularization-based work, restricting previous weights from wrong
067   skewing by imposing penalties. Many new methods, such as Online EWC Schwarz et al. (2018),
068   SI Zenke et al. (2017), CRNet Li & Zeng (2023), RanPAC McDonnell et al. (2024), NICE Gurbuz
069   et al. (2024) are born recent years. However, CIL assumes task-wise i.i.d and task boundary hinting
070   update opportunity, hardly meeting practical needs. The harsher online task-free CIL (OTCIL) is
071   expected to generate immediate decision-making with neither i.i.d nor boundary index in each task,
072
      where one task comes in non-stationary batches rather than all at once in CIL. Related works include
      GEM Lopez-Paz & Ranzato (2017), GSS Aljundi et al. (2019), and DYSON (He et al., 2024). We
073
      also applied the proposed -kF and -kF-Bayes methodologies to OTCIL scenarios and provided an
074
      in-depth analysis.
075
076   Contributions are summarized as follows:
077         1. Propose -kF and derive regret bound: To control the intervention rate of unsupervised
078            knowledge for improved performance, -F is extended to the more general -kF, incorporat-
079            ing a k-factored penalty. This enhancement aims to refine the learning gradients in linear
080            regression and reduce regrets in OL. We formulate the -kF algorithm with variable learning
081            rates and recursive closed-form updates, and demonstrate that it prevents learning dissipa-
082            tion while achieving tighter relative regret bounds compared to -F and -R.
083         2. Enhance -kF with Bayes: To curb unstable penalties caused by non-i.i.d and mitigate
084            intractable tuning of k of -kF during OL, we further propose -kF-Bayes, enabling the soft
085            k to be adaptively determined based on Bayesian learning and distribution estimation.
086         3. Practical application: We integrated -kF and -kF-Bayes into randomized learners and
087            evaluated them in (OT)CIL scenarios using both tabular and image datasets. The results
088            demonstrated the effectiveness of our methods.

274   Neural Networks (Randomized NN) (Li & Zeng, 2023; Li et al., 2024) and conducted experiments
275   in CIL/OTCIL scenarios, including tabular and image datasets. Based on the results, we show the
276   efficacy of -kF and -kF-Bayes, and the great potential of Randomized learners equipped with them.
