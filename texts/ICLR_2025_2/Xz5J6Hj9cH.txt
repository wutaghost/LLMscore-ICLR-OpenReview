012
013            Dating applications in the digital era have transformed how people connect, yet
014            they often fall short in simulating the comprehensive character and fostering truly
015            compatible relationships due to their reliance on quantitative data. This paper pro-
016
               poses a novel framework to simulate human characters by leveraging Large Lan-
               guage Models (LLMs) to enhance matchmaking by understanding the nuanced
017
               fabric of human personality and social connections. Traditional algorithms often
018
               lack the depth needed for personalized matchmaking, whereas LLMs offer sophis-
019            ticated linguistic and cognitive capabilities to simulate a person and complicated
020            personal decisions. Our framework introduces a multi-agent system comprising
021            the Persona, Preference, and Dating Memory modules, allowing for dynamic and
022            nuanced user interactions. This approach addresses the limitations of conventional
023            LLM frameworks by capturing detailed personal attributes, updating preferences,
024            and learning from past interactions. Our system enhances the relevance and ef-
025            fectiveness of match recommendations, focusing on emotional compatibility and
026            shared values, providing a more personalized and responsive user experience in
027
               the dating domain.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In the digital era, dating applications have become a prevalent medium for individuals seeking com-
033
      panionship and meaningful romantic relationships (1). These platforms utilize algorithms designed
      to match users based on shared characteristics and preferences, fundamentally transforming how
034
      people meet and interact. Despite their widespread adoption and technological advancements, a per-
035
      sistent critique remains: matches often lack the depth and understanding inherent in truly compatible
036   human relationships (2). This issue underscores a critical gap in the matchmaking process—a re-
037   liance on quantitative data that fails to capture the nuanced complexities of human connections, such
038   as individual personalities and the subtleties of social interactions.
039
      Addressing this gap necessitates a paradigm shift in matchmaking technology, moving beyond tradi-
040
      tional algorithms to embrace the intricacies of human preferences and interactions. Large Language
041
      Models (LLMs), such as GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 developed by OpenAI (3), represent a promising av-
042   enue for this shift. LLMs offer advanced capabilities in processing and generating natural language,
043   mimicking human reasoning and understanding (4). Trained on extensive datasets covering a broad
044   spectrum of human knowledge and interactions, these models can grasp subtleties and contexts in
045   language previously unattainable by automated systems.
046
      Integrating LLMs into dating applications introduces a novel approach to matchmaking. By lever-
047
      aging their advanced linguistic and cognitive abilities, LLMs can analyze user data with a depth akin
048   to human analysis. Unlike conventional matching algorithms that predominantly rely on quantita-
049   tive analysis and fixed criteria, LLMs can interpret the complexities of user profiles and preferences,
050   facilitating more meaningful and compatible connections. For instance, when users describe their
051   interests or what they seek in a partner, LLMs can understand these descriptions beyond face value,
052   capturing the broader context and nuances within their words (4). This deep understanding allows
053   for matches based on personalities, preferences, and relationship goals, potentially leading to more
      fulfilling connections.

      Moreover, LLMs can dynamically interact with user data, generating insights and predictions that
055   evolve as more information becomes available (3). This adaptability enables LLMs to adjust to
056   changes in users’ preferences or circumstances over time, refining the matchmaking process to ac-
057   curately reflect these developments.
058
      However, despite their broad applicability, traditional LLMs exhibit significant limitations when
059
      applied to highly specific and personalized tasks like dating match recommendations (4). They often
060
      lack the granularity required for full personalization, failing to capture the nuanced preferences and
061   dynamic nature of individual users’ dating experiences. Typically designed to respond to general
062   inquiries, these systems do not inherently focus on the unique requirements of the dating domain.
063
064
      One major limitation is that traditional LLMs do not inherently account for individual differences
      in user preferences and personalities. Their responses tend to be generic, lacking the depth needed
065
      to tailor advice and match recommendations to each user’s specific needs (5). Additionally, with-
066
      out a structured approach, LLMs interact with users in a static manner, unable to learn and adapt
067   from ongoing interactions. This static nature restricts the models’ ability to refine and personalize
068   recommendations based on user feedback and evolving preferences. Furthermore, traditional LLMs
069   are not specifically trained or optimized for the dating domain and lack the ability to understand
070   and prioritize factors critical to successful romantic relationships, such as emotional compatibility,
071   shared values, and personal growth dynamics (4).
072
      To address these limitations, we propose a novel framework, CogniPair, which models each user
073   as an autonomous sub-agent composed of interconnected modules: the Preference Module, the
074   Persona Module, the Reflection Module, and the Dating Memory Module. This structured,
075   multi-agent system allows for nuanced and dynamic interactions tailored specifically for the dat-
076   ing domain.
077
      The Persona Module constructs a detailed profile for each user, capturing personal attributes such
078
      as personality traits, interests, and lifestyle preferences. The Preference Module gathers and up-
079   dates user-specific preferences through initial questionnaires and feedback from interactions. The
080   Reflection Module acts as the cognitive engine, integrating information from the Persona and Pref-
081   erence Modules, as well as past experiences, to simulate human-like decision-making. The Dating
082   Memory Module stores records of past interactions, enabling the system to learn from experiences
083   and adapt over time.

      2   L ITERATURE R EVIEW
109
110
111
      Human dating activities involve numerous psychological aspects that current matching algorithms
112   are unable to capture due to their complexity. Therefore, developing an algorithm capable of simu-
113   lating these psychological facets is crucial for fostering meaningful romantic connections.
114
115
      For instance, numerous studies, including the paper “Big Five Personality Variables and Relation-
      ship Constructs,” have demonstrated that personality traits such as openness, conscientiousness,
116
      extraversion, agreeableness, and neuroticism (the Big Five) play significant roles in romantic com-
117
      patibility (6). Additionally, attachment theory suggests that individuals’ attachment styles—secure,
118   anxious, or avoidant—affect their relationship behaviors and expectations (7). Moreover, we incor-
119   porate a special agent in our architecture to simulate human emotion and conversation, engaging
120   the concept of emotional intelligence, a widely studied topic. Emotional intelligence, which encom-
121   passes the ability to recognize and manage one’s own emotions and the emotions of others, also
122   contributes to relationship success (8).
123
      By integrating the latest psychological studies on internal motivation into our persona-simulating
124   architecture, we can simulate human-like personalities and their reactions using our reflection mod-
125   ule. By incorporating these multifaceted psychological insights into dating algorithms, we can move
126   beyond superficial matching criteria towards deeper, more meaningful compatibility.
127
      Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT-3.5 and GPT-4, developed by OpenAI, have made sig-
128
      nificant strides in simulating human personality (4). These models are trained on extensive datasets,
129
      allowing them to generate text that closely mimics human language patterns. The paper “Editing
130   Personality for Large Language Models” has shown that LLMs can be fine-tuned to reflect spe-
131   cific personality traits and behavioral tendencies by analyzing linguistic cues (9). For example, by
132   processing users’ written profiles and interactions, LLMs can infer personality characteristics that
133   are crucial for matching, such as introversion vs. extraversion or thinking vs. feeling (9; 4). This
134   ability to simulate human personality with high fidelity makes LLMs powerful tools for creating
135   personalized and psychologically informed matchmaking systems.
136   In addition to simulating personality, LLMs are adept at modeling social behavior, which is essen-
137   tial for predicting and enhancing interpersonal interactions in dating contexts. Studies have demon-
138   strated that LLMs can be trained to recognize and predict patterns of social behavior by analyzing
139   conversational data and user interactions (10). For instance, LLMs can identify common themes in
140   how users communicate their interests, preferences, and emotions, enabling the creation of dynamic
141   and context-aware matchmaking processes. This dynamic modeling allows the system to adapt to
142   changes in users’ social dynamics and preferences over time, providing more accurate and relevant
143   match suggestions (11). By simulating social behaviors, LLMs can enhance the depth and quality of
144
      matches, facilitating connections that are not only based on shared interests but also on compatible
      interaction styles and emotional responses.
145
146   The integration of psychological principles and advanced LLM capabilities in matchmaking algo-
147   rithms represents a significant advancement in the field of online dating. By leveraging the nuanced
148   understanding of human personality and social behavior that LLMs offer, developers can create more
149   effective and satisfying dating experiences for users.

      The findings from our experiment have several important implications for the field of match pre-

      proper matching method. This suggests that while zero-shot approaches are useful for initial base-

475   are critical. The improvement seen in the Few-Shot Matching method (Benchmark 3) indicates that
476   providing the model with examples can enhance its predictive capabilities, albeit marginally. This
477   points to the importance of example-based learning in fine-tuning AI systems for specific tasks, sug-
478   gesting that few-shot learning may serve as a useful bridge between zero-shot and fully supervised
479   methods.
480   The significant improvements observed in the Simulated Character Matching Experiment (Main
481   Method) highlight the benefits of incorporating more sophisticated AI techniques, such as simulated
482   interactions and character modeling. This method’s higher True Positive Rate and Overall Accuracy
483   indicate that AI systems can better predict human-compatible matches by simulating deeper, more
484   nuanced interactions. This suggests a potential pathway for developing more advanced AI systems
485   that not only match based on static attributes but also consider dynamic and interactive elements that
      reflect real-world relational dynamics.

503   8   L IMITATIONS OF THE E XPERIMENT
504
505   One of the primary limitations of our experiment is the sample size. We only assessed 10 male and 10
506   female participants, which may not fully represent the diversity and complexity of real-world dating
507   scenarios. This small sample size can lead to skewed results and limit the generalizability of our
508   findings. Additionally, the dataset’s homogeneity might not capture the wide range of personalities
509   and behaviors found in a larger, more diverse population. Another limitation is the reliance on
510   simulated dialogues and profiles, which, despite our best efforts, might not perfectly replicate real
511   human interactions. While our evaluation metrics suggest that the generated dialogues are quite
512
      realistic, subtle nuances of human behavior might still be missing.
513   Several factors could contribute to the disadvantages observed in our experiment. Firstly, the com-
514   plexity of human emotions and interactions poses a significant challenge for any AI-driven match-
515   ing system (2). Our models, although sophisticated, might not fully capture the intricacies of human
516   relationships. Secondly, the token limits of LLMs constrain the amount of data processed simultane-
517   ously, potentially affecting the depth of analysis(16). To mitigate this, we employed LLM dynamic
518
      coding, which allows for more efficient processing, but this approach is still in its early stages and
      may require further refinement.
519
520   Furthermore, the appearance and physical attributes of individuals, such as facial features and body
521   type, play a crucial role in initial attraction and decision-making in real-world dating scenarios(17).
522   Hormonal influences and visual appeal significantly impact one’s choice to pursue a potential part-
523   ner. Our dataset, however, lacks any facial or body data, which limits the ability to incorporate these
524
      important aspects into our analysis.(17) This omission likely affects the realism and accuracy of the
      simulated matches, as physical appearance is a critical factor in human mate selection.
525
526   Lastly, the integration of multiple agents and modules, while beneficial for a multi-dimensional
527   analysis, introduces additional complexity and potential points of failure. Ensuring seamless collab-
528   oration among these agents is crucial for the system’s overall effectiveness.
