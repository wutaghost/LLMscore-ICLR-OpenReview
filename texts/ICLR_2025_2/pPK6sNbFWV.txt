009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal large language models have demonstrated impressive capabilities in
012            understanding and manipulating images. However, many of these models strug-
013            gle with comprehending intensive textual contents embedded within the images,
014            primarily due to the limited text recognition and layout understanding ability. To
015            understand the sources of these limitations, we perform an exploratory analysis
016
               showing the drawbacks of classical visual encoders on visual text understanding.
               Hence, we present LLaVA-Read, a multimodal large language model that utilizes
017
               dual visual encoders along with a visual text encoder. Our model surpasses existing
018
               state-of-the-art models in various text-rich image understanding tasks, showcasing
019            enhanced comprehension of textual content within images. Together, our research
020            suggests visual text understanding remains an open challenge and an efficient visual
021            text encoder is crucial for future successful multimodal systems.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Instruction tuning (Ouyang et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2022) has demonstrated remarkable generaliza-
027   tion abilities across unseen tasks, contributing to the increasing adoption of large language models
028
      (LLMs) such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023). Recently, multimodal language models have benefitted from
      visual instruction fine-tuning (Liu et al., 2023c; Li et al., 2023a; Li, 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Alayrac
029
      et al., 2022), leading to significant successes in real-world applications. These models utilize visual
030
      encoders such as CLIP-ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2021) to imbue LLMs with
031   image comprehension capabilities. However, challenges persist in comprehending textual information
032   within images, likely stemming from the prevalence of natural images in training datasets such as
033   Conceptual Captions (Changpinyo et al., 2021) and COCO (Lin et al., 2015)), as highlighted by
034   (Liu et al., 2023e). To address this, (Zhang et al., 2023d) proposed improving end-to-end visual
035   instruction-tuned models by introducing noisy Optical Character Recognition (OCR) annotations to
036   improve vision language alignment. Additionally, low-resolution visual encoders pose challenges
037   as a minimum of nine pixels are required to recognize a word. Previous works (Liu et al., 2024b;
038   Bai et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2024) have explored various methods to improve encoder resolution,
039
      resulting in significant performance gains in various downstream tasks. However, it is worth noting
      that high-resolution encoders typically require more resources for image encoding and produce more
040
      visual tokens for language models to process, leading to inefficiencies in training and inference. (Li
041
      et al., 2023d; Chu et al., 2023) have proposed methods such as visual token merging and smarter
042   architecture designs to mitigate these challenges and enhance model performance.
043
044   Document images often comprise text-rich content, with the visual components typically being simple
045
      while the textual parts are densely packed. A pertinent inquiry arises regarding the proficiency of
      existing visual encoders in encoding visual text and generating visual tokens for language models.
046
      To address this, we conducted synthetic experiments to assess visual encoders’ performance in
047
      text recognition and compare it with open-source Optical Character Recognition (OCR) tools. Our
048   analyses reveal that OCR tools exhibit superior efficiency and accuracy in encoding large text blocks,
049   whereas popular visual encoders excel in recognizing smaller and shorter words and phrases. In
050   addition, OCR tools can seamlessly scale up to process high-resolution images at minimal cost.
051   Motivated by these findings, we propose a novel architecture named LLaVA-Read that integrates
052   multiple visual encoders. Our rationale dictates that a visual encoder should efficiently capture
053   visual information, while a lightweight visual-text encoder (e.g., OCR tools) extracts text from
      high-resolution images. Furthermore, we explore the integration of a high-resolution visual encoder

055   through a fusion module. To enhance alignment and collaboration among dual visual encoders, we

057   pretraining and fine-tuning tasks. These efforts yield significant improvements in the understanding

      3    LL AVA-R EAD : E NABLING LL AVA TO R EAD
140
141   LLaVA-Read is designed to enhance the comprehension of textual information within images,
142   particularly in text-rich images. An overview of the model is shown in Figure 1. LLaVA-Read
143   comprises multiple visual encoders, a visual-text encoder, and a large language model (LLM) serving
144   as the decoder. Given an input image Xv , the visual encoders generate visual features Zv = fv (Xv ),
145   where fv consists of two visual encoders. Subsequently, we employ a multi-layer perceptron (MLP)
146   projection g to transform Zv into visual tokens Hv = g(Zv ) for the large language model. Notably,
147
      Hv shares the same embedding dimensions as the text tokens used by the LLM tokenizer. Different
      from the conventional architecture of multimodal large language models (Liu et al., 2023c), LLaVA-
148
      Read incorporates a visual-text encoder ft to better capture textual and layout information, along
149
      with a high-resolution encoder for finer visual details. The objective of the visual-text encoder is to
150   extract text from an image, yielding visual-text tokens Ht = ft (Xv ). Subsequently, we concatenate
151   Hv , Ht , and Hq , feeding them into the large language model to generate the desired response Y.
152
153
      In designing LLaVA-Read, we have the conviction that a visual encoder should specialize in pro-
      cessing visual objects, while a lightweight visual-text encoder should focus on extracting text within
154
      images. This approach, we believe, enhances the efficiency of the visual components, as text recogni-
155
      tion presents distinct patterns compared to visual object detection. Although high-resolution visual
156   encoders can capture finer details, they also generate a larger number of visual tokens. To mitigate
157   additional computational costs associated with employing two visual encoders in LLaVA-Read, we
158   merge the output of these encoders while maintaining the same visual tokens as in LLaVA. More
159   details on architectural design are elaborated in Section 3.1. In essence, LLaVA-Read offers a
160   multimodal LLM framework that leverages multiple visual encoders to improve visual token learning
161   and conversion efficiency. To enhance collaborations between dual visual encoders, we propose
      layout-aware training during the two-stage training, as discussed in Sections 3.2 and 3.3.

      3.1        M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
163
164   Visual-Text Encoder Successful commercial visual-text extractor solutions typically have much
165   smaller sizes compared to visual object detection models (Kirillov et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2024;
166   Liu et al., 2023d). Increasing the resolution of the visual encoder for visual text recognition often
167   incurs unnecessary computational costs, resulting in training and inference inefficiencies. While
168   visual encoders excel at comprehending visual object information and scene texts, they often struggle
169   with processing large chunks or paragraphs of visual text (further details in Section 4.1). Solutions
170
      such as Donut (Kim et al., 2022) and LayoutLM (Xu et al., 2020) offer neat approaches, but their
      generalization abilities are limited due to constraints in the pretraining dataset domains. Therefore,
171
      we consider employing open-source OCR tools as an alternative encoder to extract text and layout
172
      information. LLaVAR (Zhang et al., 2023d) initially utilize PaddleOCR 1 to construct a noisy
173   pretraining dataset to enhance text recognition capabilities. Consequently, we integrate the lightweight
174   PaddleOCR as our visual-text encoder. One major concern with the use of OCR-based methods is
175   the potential for induced errors. However, collaboration between the visual encoder and the large
176   language model mitigates this drawback. We use PaddleOCR as an option to verify our conviction on
177   visual-text encoders. In addition, it demonstrates high efficiency in converting visual texts into text
178   tokens for LLMs with great generalizability.
179   We use a customized OCR tokenizer to effectively encode both words and their respective locations
180   (i.e., text bounding boxes). This tokenizer comprises a layout recovery module fr (·) and a standard
181   LLM tokenizer fq (·). Upon receiving OCR results from a text-rich image, the layout recovery
182   module fr processes the input by inserting spaces and line breaks, as described in (Wang et al.,
183   2023a). The layout recovery process follows a heuristic approach: (i) Text boxes in the same row
184   with detected words are identified and rearranged in top-to-bottom and left-to-right order based on
185   their coordinates. (ii) The average character width is calculated for each row based on its width and
186   word count. Placeholders are then inserted based on the horizontal distance between two text boxes in
187
      the same row, resulting in the extraction of single-row texts. (iii) Newline characters are inserted for
      each row, reconstructing the page layout. To avoid too many spaces being inserted, we have limited
188
      the number of spaces to a range of at least one and no more than ten, cutting the original number by
189
      half. Figure 8 in the appendix provides an example of how the OCR tokenizer operates. Once the
190   plain text with layout information is obtained, it serves as part of the LLM prompts in both training
191   and inference: Ht = ft (Xv ) = fq (fr (fOCR (Xv ))).
192
193   Visual Encoders LLaVA with low-resolution visual encoders has demonstrated significant success
194   (Liu et al., 2023c), and the integration of a higher resolution encoder typically leads to performance
195   improvements (Luo et al., 2024). However, high-resolution encoders tend to generate a larger
196   number of visual tokens, and methods such as similarity-based token merging or compression
197   may sacrifice details. Ideally, a high-resolution encoder should focus on question-related details
198
      without significantly increasing the number of visual tokens for language models. To address this,
      we propose a novel approach to merge details from high-resolution encoders to low-resolution
199
      encoders. Specifically, we utilize the pretrained OpenCLIP model ConvNext-L/32-320 as the
200
      high-resolution encoder fh and the pretrained CLIP model ViT-L/14-336 as the low-resolution
201   encoder fs . The high-resolution visual encoder with an image patch size of 32 can accommodate
202   approximately 2.3 times higher resolution images compared to the low-resolution encoder with a
203   patch size of 14. For example, if the low-resolution encoder takes the image Xvs with dimensions
204   336 × 336, then the high-resolution encoder processes the image Xvh with dimensions 768 × 768.
205   Position embedding interpolation will be applied for encoders if the resolution is higher than 768.
206
      To better capture visual details, we perform layer-wise fusion, which involves embedding high-
207   resolution features into the low-resolution visual pathway through mapping modules and dynamic
208   scoring (Luo et al., 2024). The fusion process occurs at different layers of the Vision Transformer
209   (ViT), ensuring that the low-resolution features also contain rich semantics. In more detail, we merge
210   the high-resolution visual encoder features fh (Xvh ) into the low-resolution encoder fs (Xvs ). Both
211   visual encoders have 12 layers and we perform the feature merging for 1st, 4th, and 7th layers. For a
212   specific layer l, we calculate the patch-wise weight score gl = fg ([fsl (Xvs ), fhl (Xvh )]), where fg
213   is a projection layer. Then we merge them fsl (Xvs ) + gl · fm (fhl (Xvs )), where fm is a projection
214   layer with the same input and output dimensions.

      To prevent the generation of additional visual tokens, we combine the visual features of both visual
217   encoders as follows: fv (Xv ) = fh (Xvh ) + fs (Xvs ), where we first perform a linear projection on
218   the final-layer features with the same input and output dimensions and then add these two features
219   together. It ensures that the resulting visual tokens of LLaVA-Read are of the same number as
220   standard LLaVA, avoiding lossy token compressions (Liu et al., 2024c). This straightforward merging
221   strategy proves to be effective in text-rich image understanding, as demonstrated in Section 4.

223   3.2   L AYOUT- AWARE P RETRAINING FOR F EATURE A LIGNMENT
224
225   Starting with the LAION-5B dataset, we selectively retain images prominently featuring text. From
226   the filtered LAION-5B, a random sample of 10,000 images is clustered into 50 groups based on
227
      CLIP-ViT-B/32 visual features (Zhang et al., 2024). After careful examination of the clustering
      results, 14 clusters are meticulously chosen, encompassing diverse text-rich images such as posters,
228
      book covers, advertisements, and educational documents. In the pretraining stage, we utilize the
229
      LLaVA LCS-558k pretraining dataset, mainly comprising natural images. Furthermore, we augment
230   this dataset by incorporating 422k LAION images from LLaVAR (Zhang et al., 2023d), 99k slides
231   images from TGDoc (Wang et al., 2023b), and 112k document-related images from various public
232   datasets. Table 6 in the appendix shows detailed statistics of the training data. Similar to LLaVA ,
233   only the projection layer is trained during the pretraining stage. The visual-text encoder is not utilized
234   in the pretraining stage unless explicitly mentioned.
235   Task I: Text Recognition Following LLaVAR (Zhang et al., 2023d), we use PaddleOCR to extract
236   visual texts from the original images and concatenated all detected words to form the target sequence.
237   We then generated single-turn conversations for each image by (i) randomly sampling an input
238   instruction and (ii) using the recognized text sequence as the desired output response. It is worth
239   noting that such instruction-following data may be noisy due to the varying performance of OCR
240   tools across different fonts and backgrounds.
241   Task II: Text Localization The text recognition task extracts text information only but ignores
242   PaddleOCR layout information. Similar to Task I, we created single-turn conversations for each
243   image by (i) randomly sampling an instruction to extract both texts and bounding boxes and (ii) using
244   the recognized text sequence along with its bounding boxes as the desired output response. This
245   simple training scheme is effective and allows the model to develop grounding ability (You et al.,
246   2023). It is important to represent bounding boxes accurately; therefore, we converted each integer
247   value of box coordinates into a float value, ranging from 0 to 1. In addition, we used the top-left and
248
      bottom-right coordinates to represent the text boxes.
249   Task III: Page Parsing To better capture layout information, we pretrain the model to parse image
250   pages into plain text with minimal loss of layout information. We adopt the layout reconstruction
251   module fr (·) to parse both words and bounding boxes, incorporating placeholders and new-line
252   characters to reconstruct the image layout (Wang et al., 2023a). For example, we utilize images from
253
      PlotQA (Methani et al., 2020) and ChartQA (Masry et al., 2022), using the source data to construct
      the corresponding Markdown codes. More details are provided in Appendix B.1.
254
255   Task IV: Layout Recovery The layout reconstruction task aims to transfer the ability of fr (·) to
256   LLaVA-Read. It utilize OCR results from Task II and parse pages as in Task III to build instruction
257   tuning pairs. This task is designed to teach the language model to better comprehend coordinates and
258
      reconstruct the layout using visual-text results. Representative examples of different pretraining tasks
      are provided in Figure 7 and Figure 8 in the Appendix B.1.

261   3.3   L AYOUT- AWARE F INETUNING FOR I NSTRUCTION F OLLOWING
262
      Jointly understanding both visual texts and objects is crucial to efficiently analyzing text-rich images.
263
      To enhance the model’s visual object understanding, we perform finetuning using the natural image
264   finetuning dataset from LLaVA. Although scaling up the dataset could potentially further improve
265   visual object understanding, we did not explore this direction in this paper. To improve the under-
266   standing of visual texts and align different encoders, we combine instruction tuning datasets from
267   LLaVAR (Zhang et al., 2023d), TGDoc (Wang et al., 2023b), and TRINS (Zhang et al., 2024) for
268   text-rich image instruction tuning. Additionally, we merge visual question-answering data sets related
269   to documents from various sources (Mathew et al., 2022; 2020; Pasupat & Liang, 2015; Masry et al.,
      2022) to enhance performance. In total, we assemble around 425k instruction finetuning datasets.

302   Settings Following previous work (Rust et al., 2022; Tai et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024), we generate
303   synthetic data to evaluate the text recognition ability of different visual encoders by varying font sizes
304   and number of words, as shown in Figure 2. We use PaddleOCR as a simple and effective visual-text
305   encoder and OpenAI CLIP plus trained projection layers to inspect the text recognition ability of
306
      visual encoders. We use multiple fonts to render text-rich images and use the OCR accuracy as a
      metric. For PaddleOCR and multimodal LLM, accuracy means that the rendered ground-truth words
307
      can be exactly found in the outputs. For CLIP with projection, we first obtain the model outputs,
308
      which are visual token embeddings, and then perform similarity-based ranking with words from the
309   language model’s vocabulary. If the ground truth words can be found in the top-3 words, we count
310   these are detected by the model. We list a few research questions to help the reader better understand
311   our experimental results. Please note that we removed stop-words from the NLTK (Bird et al., 2009)
312   package as many repeat stop-words exist in text paragraphs.
313
      RQ1: How many pixels do we need to recognize words? We first investigate the performance
314
      of different modules on text recognition ability with different font sizes. In Figure 2a, all text-rich
315   rendered images have a plain white background, which is similar to the scan document images or
316   screen shots. In Figure 2b. All rendered text-rich images are rendered with a random selected image
317   as the background, corresponding to the scene text and poster settings. In both scenarios, we use the
318   terms of machine learning as the texts to recognize, each phrase containing no more than four words.
319   We measure the font size with its vertical heights. CLIP with projection can recognize texts with a
320   minimum font size at 6 pixels to achieve its best performance. In addition, the CLIP with projection
321   performance is similar before and after the fine-tuning stage.

      RQ2: Is one text token worth one visual token? In Figure 2c, we show the performance of three
343   different modules on text recognition ability. When the number of words is less than 50, the visual
344   encoder with projection and Multimodal LLM (i.e., CLIP + Projection + LLM) can work, but with
345   lower accuracy. However, when there are large chunks of texts, i.e., the number of words becomes
346   larger, the performance of both modules starts to collapse. We observe similar trends in various
347   multimodal LLMs as shown in Appendix A. This analysis shows the low efficiency of using the
348   CLIP encoder to transform visual texts into visual tokens, and language models can only handle
349   short sequences of visual tokens with textual information. In contrast, the visual text encoders (i.e.,
350   PaddleOCR) shows much better and consistent performance in encoding large chunks of visual texts,
351
      underscoring its essential role of multimodal LLMs for great reading capabilities.
352    Finding 2. Traditional visual encoders generate fixed-length visual tokens, leading to inefficient
353    token use when converting visual texts into visual tokens for language models.
354
355   RQ3: Is the visual-text encoder always the best in text recognition? The visual-text encoder
356   we used in experiments is PaddleOCR, a model that is considerably more compact (less than 1%)
357   compared to OpenAI CLIP ViT-L/14-336. PaddleOCR is great at recognizing large chunks of
358   text, but it requires a minimum of 9 pixels and cannot recognize texts smaller than 7 pixels (in terms
359
      of the height of characters), while CLIP + Projection can do better. In the scene text experiment
      (Figure 2b), font size does not affect the performance of CLIP with projection when the font size
360
      increases, while PaddleOCR gets worse. In summary, a visual text encoder such as PaddleOCR
361
      proves to be beneficial, and a visual encoder can also help to understand visual text in certain cases.
362
363    Finding 3. PaddleOCR serves as a simple visual text encoder with adaptive context lengths,
364    offering great token efficiency. Although its smaller size may lead to errors, these can be mitigated
365    by large language models.

      5   C ONCLUSIONS
477
478
      In this paper, we first analyze the visual text understanding ability of multimodal large language
479
      models, demonstrating the essential need for integrating extra visual text encoders. Then we propose
480   LLaVA-Read, a model architecture that enhances the reading ability of multimodal large language
481   models (LLMs) by integrating layout information and using multiple visual encoders. Through a
482   comprehensive evaluation on text-rich image understanding tasks, LLaVA-Read outperforms existing
483   state-of-the-art models, demonstrating the effectiveness of incorporating layout information and
484   utilizing multiple visual encoders in improving the comprehension of textual content situated in
485   images. This work contributes to the advancement of multimodal language models and provides
      valuable insights for further research in enhancing the reading ability of such models.
