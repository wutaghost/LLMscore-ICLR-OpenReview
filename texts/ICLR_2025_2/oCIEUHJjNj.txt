011                                                                                A BSTRACT
012
013                    Large Language Models (LLMs) have made significant strides in text genera-
014                    tion and comprehension, with recent advancements extending into multimodal
015                    LLMs that integrate visual and audio inputs. However, these models continue
016                    to struggle with fine-grained, cross-modal temporal understanding, particularly
017                    when correlating events across audio and video streams. We address these chal-
018                    lenges with two key contributions: a new dataset and model, called OCTAV and
019                    OMCAT respectively. OCTAV (Omni Context and Temporal Audio Video) is a
020
                       novel dataset designed to capture event transitions across audio and video. Second,
                       OMCAT (Omni Context Aware Transformer) is a powerful model that leverages
021
                       RoTE (Rotary Time Embeddings), an innovative extension of RoPE, to enhance
022
                       temporal grounding and computational efficiency in time-anchored tasks. Through
023                    a robust three-stage training pipeline—feature alignment, instruction tuning, and
024                    OCTAV-specific training—OMCAT excels in cross-modal temporal understanding.
025                    Our model demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on Audio-Visual Question
026                    Answering (AVQA) tasks and the OCTAV benchmark, showcasing significant gains
027                    in temporal reasoning and cross-modal alignment, as validated through comprehen-
028                    sive experiments and ablation studies. Our dataset and code will be made publicly
029                    available. The link to our demo page is https://om-cat.github.io.

      Large language models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023) have achieved remarkable
048   breakthroughs in both text generation and comprehension (McKeown, 1992; Achiam et al., 2023)
049   tasks. Since then, significant progress has been made to extend LLMs to multimodal LLMs (Cheng
050   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b; Maaz et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024), which integrate visual and audio
051   inputs with textual instructions to provide understanding in multimodal contexts (Yang et al., 2022b;
052   Chen et al., 2023a;b). These models, however, are limited in their cross-modal understanding and in
053   their ability to provide answers to questions with fine-grained timestamps or anchored on events, as
      shown in Figure 1. In this paper, we address these limitations by proposing a new dataset OCTAV and

054
      a model called OMCAT. The Omni Context and Temporal Audio Video dataset, OCTAV, consists of
055   question-answer pairs for a video. Each question captures the transition between the events happening
056   in the video through a sound event (e.g. Figure 1). The Omni Context Aware Transformer, OMCAT,
057   addresses the limitations of existing models (Maaz et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024; Su et al., 2023;
058   Cheng et al., 2024) through a unified audio and visual language model by effectively incorporating
059   time representations to ground the modalities temporally.
060
      Despite the notable progress in multimodal LLMs (Li et al., 2023b; Maaz et al., 2023; Cheng et al.,
061   2024; Lyu et al., 2023), most advancements have been centered around developing domain specific
062   models in isolation, typically Video LLMs (Wang et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2024) or Audio LLMs (Gong
063   et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2024; Chu et al., 2023). However, these models still face challenges in
064   handling fine-grained, cross-modal temporal understanding when both audio and video are provided.
065   For instance, if a user asks the question, “Is it raining in the video?” This question can be answered
066   by either just looking at the video or listening to the audio. However, as shown in Figure 1, if the
067   user asks the question, “Describe what happens in the video after the sound of children playing?”,
068   the model must understand both modalities because the sound of children playing cannot be
069
      seen, only heard, and what the man is doing cannot be heard, only seen. Achieving this is
      challenging due to several reasons, including the lack of temporally aligned cross-modal datasets,
070
      unified models and benchmarks, and clear understanding of how to combine modalities effectively.
071
072   Our goal is to achieve this cross-modal temporal understanding, and to this end we propose an
073   instruction tuning dataset called OCTAV: Omni Context and Temporal Audio Video. Figure 1 shows
074   a sample from our proposed OCTAV dataset. Existing audio and video understanding datasets (Chen
075
      et al., 2023b;a; 2020; Geng et al., 2023) only focus on open-ended question answering tasks (Yang
      et al., 2022b; Li et al., 2022) for audio-visual events. They lack the ability to temporally ground events
076
      or describe events that involve ambiguity or missing information in one of the modalities. Specifically,
077
      we create question-answer pairs for a video such that each question captures the transition between
078   the events happening in the video through a sound event. For instance, as shown in Figure 1, we
079   add the sound event of children playing to the silent input video between 6 to 7 seconds,
080   during which nothing substantial happens in the video. Then, we capture the video event before
081   6 seconds and after 7 seconds while using the sound of children playing as a
082   transition event. This setting encourages the model to not only understand the relationship between
083   the audio and the video, but also a strong temporal understanding of both the audio and video domains
084   in a single setup. Despite this artificial setup, our experiments show that a model trained with this
085   data performs well in naturally occurring video and audio pairs.
086   While dataset design is necessary, it is not a sufficient condition to achieve cross-modal understanding
087   given the challenges in modelling such data. As such, we propose a new approach that embeds abso-
088   lute and relative temporal information in the audio and visual features, improving the model’s ability
089   to become temporally-aware. With the goal of improving cross-modal and temporal understanding,
090   and following common practice in multimodal LLMs (Li et al., 2023b; Cheng et al., 2024; Li et al.,
091   2024; Tang et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024), we divide model training into 3 stages. The first training
092
      stage is focused on feature alignment, and uses audio-text, video-text, and audio-video-text data (Liu
      et al., 2024; Mei et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023b). In the second stage, the model is instruction-tuned
093
      with data (Luo et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Drossos et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020) that promotes
094
      temporal and cross-modal understanding. Finally, the model is trained to support complex and
095   cross-modal temporal data in the OCTAV dataset as shown in Figure 1. We name the model trained
096   with our proposed OCTAV dataset and the temporal conditioning strategy OMCAT, for OMni Context
097   Aware Transformer. Through this learning strategy, our method outperforms existing models on
098   AVQA tasks (Yang et al., 2022b; Li et al., 2022) and beats baselines by a significant margin on our
099   proposed OCTAV benchmark dataset.
100
       In summary, our main contributions are as follows:
101   - We introduce a novel method for generating synthetic instruction-tuning dataset, OCTAV, which has
102    temporal and contextual audio and video question/answer pairs addressing the limitations of existing
103    datasets. This dataset has both training and evaluation samples to promote research in this direction.
104   - We propose OMCAT: a unified, temporally-aware audio and visual language model with fine-
105    grained and cross-modal understanding, achieved through a staged training strategy that leverages all
106    combinations of audio, video and text data.
107   - We propose RoTE: a simple yet efficient modification to RoPE that provides better scores on
       benchmarks and better computational efficiency than existing approaches for temporal conditioning,

       especially on time-anchored tasks.
109   - Finally, we exhaustively evaluate OMCAT, including ablations, on a variety of multimodal tasks. Our

      2    R ELATED W ORK
115   Multimodal LLMs. Since the rise of large language models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Chiang
116   et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023), there has been growing interest in integrating additional modali-
117
      ties (Cheng et al., 2024; Gong et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2024). Video LLMs (Li et al., 2023b; Fu et al.,
      2024; Wang et al., 2023) utilize video-text datasets to address tasks like video question answering (Xu
118
      et al., 2016; Yu et al., 2019), visual grounding (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014), and understanding temporal
119
      segments (Gao et al., 2017; Huang et al., 2024). These have evolved into multimodal LLMs (Cheng
120   et al., 2024; Maaz et al., 2023; Lyu et al., 2023), which encode multiple modalities and focus on
121   coarse-grained tasks like audio-video understanding and question answering (Shu et al., 2023; Chen
122   et al., 2023a; Yang et al., 2022b). However, these models struggle with fine-grained audio-visual
123   tasks, where precise synchronization is key to deeper event comprehension.
124
      Recent efforts have attempted to address this. GroundingGPT (Li et al., 2024) predicts fine-grained
125
      timestamps but is limited to sound events, while AVicuna (Tang et al., 2024) takes a more balanced
126   approach to audio-visual temporal understanding. However, both models fall short in capturing
127   intricate cross-modal temporal dynamics. Our work aims to address these gaps by focusing on
128   fine-grained cross-modal information integration.
129   Instruction tuning datasets. GPT-based methods have been widely used to create datasets for video,
130   audio, and audio-visual tasks, advancing multimodal models with large-scale resources. In video
131   understanding, they generate and annotate datasets for tasks like video captioning (Fu et al., 2024),
132   video question answering (Xu et al., 2016; Yu et al., 2019), and action recognition (Yu et al., 2019).
133   Similarly, for audio understanding, instruction tuning datasets (Kong et al., 2024; Goel et al., 2024)
134   target sound events (Salamon et al., 2014), audio captioning (Kim et al., 2019), and audio question
135
      answering (Lipping et al., 2022). Recently, AI-generated datasets have also progressed in audio-visual
      tasks like captioning (Chen et al., 2023a), question answering (Yang et al., 2022b), and dialog (Alamri
136
      et al., 2019). Despite this progress, current datasets remain predominantly coarse-grained, lacking
137
      fine-grained temporal and cross-modal synchronization. Our proposed dataset, OCTAV, addresses
138   this limitation, enabling more precise alignment between audio and visual cues in complex scenarios.

141   3    T HE OCTAV DATASET
142
143   One of the challenges in developing models that can understand strongly timestamped and anchored
144   events is the lack of datasets that have this information (Wang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Chen
145   et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023b; Tang et al., 2024; Lyu et al., 2023). To overcome this limitation, we
146
      propose a pipeline to generate a synthetic dataset called OCTAV, for Omni Context Temporal Audio
      Video dataset. Figure 1 shows an example from our proposed OCTAV dataset. First, we discuss how
147
      we identify relevant event transitions in videos. Then, we discuss how we anchor those transitions on
148
      audio samples and finally, we show how to generate question-answer pairs for these synthetically
149   curated videos.
150
151   Identifying transitions between video events. To achieve this, we utilize videos with strongly
152
      timestamped captions (Zhou et al., 2018; Krishna et al., 2017; Tang et al., 2019; Zala et al., 2023),
      i.e. a video V with time-caption pairs {(t1 , c1 ), (t2 , c2 ) . . . (tk , ck )}, where k is the total number of
153
      time chunks annotated in the video. Given a list of timestamped video captions indexed by i and
154
      bounded by start time (tsi ) and end time (tei ) each, we find pairs where the gap between end time and
155   start time is smallest than m and the sum of their lengths, from earliest to latest, is at most T seconds.
156   Empirically we set m = 10 and T = 30, ensuring that the videos are not too far apart and their length
157   is not too long. Next, we discuss how to anchor sound between these video event transitions.
158   Anchoring chunked videos on a single sound event. For these chunked videos, we inject a sound
159   event between the timestamp tei and tsj . More specifically, we randomly sample a sound event s from
160   a variety of different sound sources (Salamon et al., 2014; Fonseca et al., 2021; Piczak, 2015; Rashid
161   et al., 2023). Details of these sound sources are provided in Appendix C.4. We remove the original
      audio in the given video chunk and insert this sound event between the timestamp {tei , tsj } to create a


                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      strongly timed video chunk anchored on a sound event. We refer to this subset of the dataset as the
163   OCTAV-ST dataset where, ST is for single-turn.
164   Anchoring chunked videos on multiple sound events. We extend the videos from a single sound
165   event to two sound events as shown in Figure 1. Particularly, we first create a chunked video with
166   three unique events ci , cj , and ck corresponding to timestamps ti , tj and tk respectively, following the
167   same procedure discussed previously. Then, we add a random sound event after removing the original
168   audio between the timestamps {tei , tsj } and {tej , tsk }. We refer to this subset with interwoven and
169   timestamped videos with audio events as the OCTAV-MT dataset where, MT stands for multi-turn.
170   Creating question-answer pairs. Here, we discuss how to create question-answer pairs for the
171   interwoven videos in the OCTAV-ST and OCTAV-MT dataset. Essentially, we have two (or three)
172   video caption events for each chunked video and an associated audio event/sound between the video
173
      events. The model has to generate questions such that it can capture what event is happening in the
      video {before the sound event, after the sound event}, and clarify which of the sound events the user
174
      is referring to while answering the question. We use GPT-assisted (Achiam et al., 2023) generation
175
      to generate a diverse set of question-answer pairs. The prompts used are given in Appendix C.1 and
176   Appendix C.2 and the list of instructions are given in the Appendix C.3.

      4.2      T IME A LIGNMENT BETWEEN AUDIO AND V IDEO
265
266   Existing multimodal LLMs rely on learnable positional embeddings to encode the order of frames, but
267   they struggle to capture the absolute time elapsed between frames and lack a fine-grained, cross-modal
268   understanding of audio and video. We propose two strategies to encode absolute and relative temporal
269   information on video and audio tokens, called Interleaving Time Tokens (ITT) and Rotary Time
      Embeddings (RoTE).

      Interleaving Time Tokens (ITT). In this approach, we interleave time tokens with the audio and the
271   visual features. We allocate a budget of K learnable time tokens, zero-indexed by ki , and assign a
272   time token to an audio-visual feature with the following indexing function:
273                                                     τ
274                                                       i
                                          ki = round        · (K − 1)                                 (3)

276   where τi is a continuous timestamp in seconds, T is the total duration of the video or audio in seconds,
277   and K is the total number of learnable time tokens.
278
      For a video V with duration T and video token embeddings vi where i = 1 · · · M , each embedding
279
      is associated with a timestamp τi (e.g. 0.5 seconds, 1.4 seconds, and so forth). We first use these
280   timestamps to obtain the discrete time tokens, then we interleave them with the visual tokens vi
281   obtained after the visual adaptor layers. Specifically, each visual token vi corresponds to a discrete
282   time token indexed by ki , as described in Equation (3). Hence, the interleaved visual sequence is
283   given as v̄= {v1 , < k1 >, v2 , < k2 > · · · , < vM >, < kM >}.
284
      Similarly, for the given audio A of duration T , we extract N windows of length w from the audio
285
      sequence such that for each window the time is represented as: τn = [n, n + w] for n =
286   1, 2, · · · , N , where n is the time in seconds. We then take the mean of the time windows, τn =
287   n+(n+w)
           2       . Then, we convert τn into discrete time token kn using Equation (3) and interleave them
288   with the audio tokens a obtained from the audio adaptor layers. Hence, the interleaved audio sequence
289   is represented as ā = {a1 , < k1 >, a2 , < k2 > · · · , < aN >, < kN >}. The final interleaved tokens
290   v̄ and ā are then concatenated with the text instructions as prompts to the LLM, as shown in Figure 2
291   on upper top right.
292   Rotary Time Embeddings (RoTE). While we could use RoPE (Su et al., 2024) and avoid the extra
293   context length cost introduced by ITT, RoPE would still lack the ability to capture the absolute time
294   elapsed between frames, which is very important and crucial in scenarios with varying frame rates.
295   To address these limitations, we propose an alternative strategy called RoTE: a modified version
296   of RoPE, where the rotation angles are determined by absolute timestamps in seconds instead of
297
      frame indices. RoTE takes inspiration from a real clock, where each handle rotates at distinct speeds,
      or “frequencies”. Similarly, in RoTE we rotate different dimensions in the visual and audio feature
298
      embeddings given their timestamp in seconds and the respective “frequency” of that dimension. Our
299
      results in Section 5 show that RoTE achieves performance that is superior to the baselines. A visual
300   representation of RoTE is shown in Figure 1 on the lower right bottom.
301
302   In practice, while in rope the angle for rotation θ is defined by the temporal indexing of a token
303
      θ ← −i × 2π, RoTE is defined by the absolute time θ ← −τi × 2π. These temporally enriched
      features are then passed to the adaptor layers V(.) and A(.) to create visual tokens v and audio tokens
304
      a respectively.

306   4.3   I NSTRUCTION P ROMPTS
307
308   In this section, we explain how video and audio tokens are combined with text prompts. The prompt
309
      format is as follows:
310   User: < system prompt > Question < vi start > < vi patch > < vi end > < so start >
311   < so patch > < so end > < vis start > < vi patch > < so patch > < vis end > Assistant:
312
      Here, < system prompt > represents a guiding system message, following Vicuna-7B (Chiang
313   et al., 2023). Visual and audio markers are included through tokens like < vi start >/< vi end >
314   for video and < so start >/< so end > for audio. Video tokens (< vi patch >) encode visual
315   information, and audio tokens (< so patch >) handle sound data. It is important to note that these
316   individual video and audio markers are activated only when modality-specific data (video or audio) is
317   present. For joint audio-video data, < vis start >/< vis end > marks the boundaries, encoding
318   both audio and video tokens, deactivating the individual representations.

320   4.4   T RAINING S TRATEGY

361   Stage II: Instruction Tuning Stage. Following the “alignment” of modality features in Stage I,
362   we proceed to train OMCAT using a diverse and high-quality collection of multimodal data (∼2.8M).
363   This includes image-text, video-text, audio-text, and audio-video-text datasets that are carefully
364   curated to prepare the model for a wide range of tasks involving video and audio. These tasks include
365   fine-grained timestamped comprehension as well as cross-modal understanding, enabling the model

379
380
      Datasets. To evaluate the capabilities of OMCAT on general multimodal understanding, we evaluate
      our method on audio-visual understanding benchmarks. Specifically, we evaluate on the AVSD
381
      dataset (Alamri et al., 2019) which is a dataset for audio-visual scene aware dialog, Music-AVQA
382
      dataset (Li et al., 2022) that has audio-visual question answering for the music domain and AVQA
383   dataset (Yang et al., 2022b) which has general questions about audio and visual modalities.
384
385   Furthermore, to evaluate whether OMCAT outperforms in temporal tasks, we measure the performance
386
      of our model on temporal video grounding benchmark, Charades-STA (Gao et al., 2017). This dataset
      is widely used in prior works (Cheng et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2024) as a benchmark for
387
      temporal understanding.
388
389   Finally, we benchmark OMCAT on the evaluation subset of OCTAV-ST, OCTAV-MT and
390   UnAV-100-MT datasets. These tasks require fine-grained temporal understanding, cross-correlation
391   between the audio and visual modalities and hence are a good measure to evaluate the capabilities of
392
      OMCAT.
393
      Evaluation metrics. Following prior work (Cheng et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024),
      we use GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) to evaluate the answers predicted by the model by comparing
394
      against the correct answers, with a score of 0 to 5 indicating the accuracy. Besides Charades-STA
395
      where we use Recall@1 at Intersection over Union (IoU) thresholds of 0.5 and 0.7, we use the GPT
396   accuracy everywhere else.
397   Architecture. We use the pre-trained CLIP visual encoder ViT-L/14 (Radford et al., 2021) to extract
398   video/image features. For the audio encoder, we use the pre-trained ImageBind (Girdhar et al., 2023)
399   model. Similar to previous work, for the video and audio adaptors, we use the Q-former which has the
400   same architecture as the Q-Former in BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023a). However, to maintain the temporal
401   consistency of video and audio frames in the ITT setup, we replace the Q-Former adaptor layers with
402   2-layer transformer blocks with self-attention (Vaswani, 2017). During both training and inference,
403   we sample 64 frames from the video and we extract five 3-second windows for the audio. The audio
404   is resampled to 16KHz sampling rate and converted into spectrograms to be consistent with the input
405
      to the ImageBind model (Girdhar et al., 2023). We use 100 as the value of K, the learnable time
      tokens in Section 4.2.
406
      Training details. During both the pre-training and fine-tuning stages, we train the model for one
407
      epoch on 8 NVIDIA A-100 GPUs. For the pre-training stage, we set the batch size of 64, learning
408
      rate of 1e-3 with a cosine learning decay and a warm-up period. In the fine-tuning stages, we set the
409   batch size to 32, learning rate to 2e-5 with a cosine learning decay and a warm-up period and gradient
410   accumulation to 2. Further details about training are given in Appendix E.

      5.2     A BLATION STUDY
478
479   How does time embedding affect OMCAT? In Table 7, we evaluate three different time embedding
480   approaches, including RoPE (Su et al., 2024), and our proposed approaches ITT and RoTE. On the
481   AVQA benchmark, RoTE consistently outperforms the baselines by a large margin, demonstrating
482
      its strong capability not only on temporal and cross-modal tasks but also in handling coarse-grained
      question answering.
483
484   For the temporal understanding task on Charades-STA, ITT performs slightly better than RoTE at
485   both IoU thresholds (0.5 and 0.7). On the OCTAV-ST benchmark, YouCook2 and ActivityNet, ITT
      and RoTE show nearly equivalent performance. We believe ITT’s competitive results stem from its


                                                                 9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      explicit time embedding through time tokens. However, given ITT’s increased context length and its
487   weaker performance on AVQA tasks, RoTE is the more effective and efficient choice overall.
488   What is the effect of pre-training data on OMCAT? Furthermore, we investigate the impact of
489   pre-training data on the final model performance, particularly during the alignment tuning stage (Stage
490   I). This stage is crucial for establishing the model’s capacity to “align” information across different
491   modalities, such as image, video, and audio, with text. To examine the role of joint multimodal data,
492   we conduct an ablation study where we modify the training data by excluding the audio-video-text
493   paired data (Chen et al., 2023b;a) while retaining image-text (Liu et al., 2024), video-text (Luo et al.,
494   2023; Wang et al., 2019), and audio-text pairs (Mei et al., 2024).
495   Our results in Table 8 indicate a noticeable decline in performance across all tasks when the model is
496   trained without audio-video-text data. This demonstrates the critical importance of joint multimodal
497   data in achieving robust cross-modal alignment. We hypothesize that without data that directly links
498   audio, video, and text, the model struggles to accurately capture the intricate relationships between
499   these modalities, leading to suboptimal performance in tasks requiring fine-grained multimodal
500   understanding.

519   In Figure 3, we showcase the qualitative performance of our method on the YouCook2 subset of

523   timestamps based on the sound events. For instance, our model correctly identifies the activity
524   following the sound of cracking fire (around 6.4 to 27.6 seconds), predicting that flour, cornmeal, and
525   salt and pepper are combined. This aligns closely with the ground truth, which describes the activity
526

532
      the bowl, though the prediction does not perfectly match the ground truth.
533   6    C ONCLUSION
534   In this paper, we addressed the limitations of multimodal large language models in fine-grained, cross-
535   modal temporal understanding by introducing the OCTAV dataset and the OMCAT model. OCTAV
536   focuses on event transitions across audio and video, promoting deeper temporal alignment and
537   cross-modal understanding. OMCAT, enhanced with RoTE embeddings, effectively grounds temporal
538   information across modalities, leading to superior performance on Audio-Visual Question Answering
539   (AVQA) tasks and the OCTAV benchmark. Our approach sets a new standard for multimodal AI,
      advancing cross-modal and temporal reasoning capabilities for future research.

763   B     A LGORITHM FOR ROTARY T IME E MBEDDINGS (ROTE)
764
765   Below, we present the algorithm for applying RoTE to the visual feature embeddings, hv . The same
766   algorithm applies to audio features, ha .
767
768   Algorithm 1 Rotary Time Embeddings (RoTE)
769   Require: hvx : List of x-coordinates for visual features, hvy : List of y-coordinates for visual features,
770       τ : timestamp for the video frames, d: Embedding dimension, N : number of video frames
771   Ensure: Rotated coordinates (hvxrot , hvyrot )
772    1: freqs ← linspace(0, 1, d/2)                           ▷ Frequency adjustments based on dimension
773    2: for i = 1 to N do
774    3:      θ ← −τi × 2π                                                               ▷ Angle for rotation
775    4:      rot angle ← θ × freqs[−1]                               ▷ Rotation using the highest frequency
776    5:      hvxrot [i] ← hvx [i] × cos(rot angle) − hvy [i] × sin(rot angle)
777    6:      hvyrot [i] ← hvx [i] × sin(rot angle) + hvy [i] × cos(rot angle)
       7: end for
778
       8: return (hvxrot , hvyrot )

1114   E      T RAINING D ETAILS
1115
1116   The training settings for our model are spread across three stages, each with specific configurations.
1117
       Stage 1 utilizes a batch size of 64 and a learning rate of 1e-3, employing a cosine decay learning
1118
       schedule. The warm-up ratio is set at 0.03, with no weight decay applied. This stage runs for 1
1119   epoch with gradient accumulation of 1. Additionally, it employs the ZeRO2 optimization strategy in
1120   DeepSpeed (Rasley et al., 2020) and utilizes 8 A100 GPUs.
1121
1122
       Stage 2 has a smaller batch size of 32 and reduces the learning rate to 2e-5. It follows the same
       warm-up ratio of 0.03 and applies no weight decay. Like Stage 1, this stage runs for 1 epoch but
1123
       increases gradient accumulation to 2. The same DeepSpeed optimization and GPU configuration are

1167   Here, we outline some limitations that are important considerations for future work.
1168
       First, the OCTAV dataset consists of sounds that are non-overlapping and distinct, which simplifies
1169
       the learning and classification process. However, in real-life scenarios, sound events often overlap,
1170   occur simultaneously, and can be highly ambiguous. This makes sound detection and classification
1171   far more complex. Thus, a natural extension of our work would be to incorporate sound data that
1172   reflects more in-the-wild conditions, where sounds are less controlled, overlap frequently, and can
1173   exhibit high variability in intensity and duration. Adapting the dataset to represent such real-world
1174   complexities will enhance the robustness and applicability of the model in practical applications.
1175
       Second, our proposed OMCAT model employs the CLIP visual encoder (Radford et al., 2021) as
1176   the video encoder, which focuses on frame-based visual representations. While CLIP has demon-
1177   strated strong capabilities in multimodal learning, it lacks explicit modeling of temporal dynamics
1178   between video frames. Given that many real-world events are temporally dependent—especially in
1179   video sequences—using a video-based encoder that captures temporal consistency, such as those
1180   designed for action recognition (Ren et al., 2024), would likely result in more accurate and nuanced
1181   representations of events. In future work, we aim to explore alternative video encoders that model
1182   temporal aspects of video more effectively, enabling better alignment between the visual and audio
1183   modalities in complex, dynamic environments. This could lead to more sophisticated models capable
1184
       of handling temporal dependencies and multi-event interactions in both visual and audio data.
1185   Third, currently the dataset consists of short-length videos (∼30-40 seconds), extending the dataset
1186   to long videos would be extremely beneficial for practical applications. Longer videos would provide
1187   more comprehensive context, allowing models to better capture temporal dependencies, complex
       patterns, and interactions that unfold over extended periods. Moreover, long-duration videos would


                                                            22
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
       enable more robust testing and evaluation in real-world scenarios, where short clips often fail to
1189   represent the full dynamics of real-time events. Expanding the dataset in this way would lead to more
1190   accurate models and improve their generalizability across a broader range of applications.
