009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            As more powerful AI agents are deployed with greater tool use and autonomy, it
012            will become increasingly important to ensure they cannot take severely damag-
013            ing actions. Recent work introduced “control evaluations,” which study whether
014            monitoring and security protocols intended to prevent dangerous actions are ro-
015            bust to intentional subversion by these AIs. In a control evaluation, a red team
016            seeks to subvert the safety protocols built by the blue team. The blue team has
017            access to a weaker trusted model and a limited amount of high-quality, trusted la-
018            bor to attempt to safely extract work from a stronger untrusted model. Prior work
019
               conducted a control evaluation using competition programming problems with the
               red team objective of writing subtly incorrect (or “backdoored”) solutions (Green-
020
               blatt et al., 2023). We extend this work by performing a control evaluation in a
021
               more realistic setting in which an untrusted LLM agent executes commands in a
022            bash shell. The blue team seeks to use that agent to complete our novel dataset of
023            200 challenging multi-step tasks, each of which requires the agent to iteratively
024            develop a bash script that passes test cases. The red team creates an attack policy
025            that uses the untrusted model to covertly download and execute a dummy malware
026            file while completing tasks without getting caught by the blue team’s safety pro-
027            tocols. This agentic setting allows both teams to explore complex new strategies
028            that have not been previously studied. We extend protocols from previous work to
029            this agentic setting, propose novel protocols, and evaluate each on our safety and
030
               usefulness metrics. We find that our protocols substantially improve the Pareto
               frontier of usefulness and safety relative to simple baselines: one of our best pro-
031
               tocols exhibits approximately a 2.5 times improvement in safety metrics with no
032
               statistically significant decrease in usefulness compared to our trusted monitoring
033            baseline.

036   1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038
      The rapid rise of Large Language Models (LLMs) and autonomous AI agents (Achiam et al., 2023;
039   Xi et al., 2023) opens new opportunities and also introduces new challenges in ensuring their safe op-
040   eration. Traditional alignment techniques, such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
041   (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022) and Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022), have shown
042   limitations in robustly steering agent behavior (Casper et al., 2023; Hubinger et al., 2024) and are
043   challenging to evaluate robustly. For instance, misalignment in a narrowly and cheaply backdoored
044   model (i.e. by data poisoning, Carlini et al. 2024) may not be easily detected in alignment eval-
045   uations. Further, more advanced future misaligned models with greater situational awareness and
046   strategic reasoning may fake alignment during training Carlsmith 2023. Ultimately, without major
047
      advances in alignment science, we are likely to develop untrusted models that are sufficiently capa-
      ble such that we cannot rule out the possibility that their alignment is faked or otherwise nonrobust.
048
      Thus, it is crucial to implement safety measures that can prevent unacceptably bad outcomes even if
049
      the AIs are intentionally attempting to subvert those measures (Clymer et al., 2024). This emerging
050   field of study is termed AI Control (Greenblatt et al., 2023).1
051

           A real world example of this is Microsoft’s Bing Chat (Microsoft, 2024), which employs an extensive
053   pipeline surrounding the generation of responses from GPT-4: for instance, after GPT-4 has generated a re-
      sponse, the response is provided to a moderation service that may modify the response.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
101
      AI Control seeks to establish safety measures that are robust to intentional subversion. Greenblatt
102
      et al. 2023 introduced AI Control in the APPS backdoor setting, which our work builds off of by
103
      exploring a much richer setting with fewer assumptions. Griffin et al. 2024 explored some game-
104   theoretic aspects of AI Control. AI Control as an important research agenda has been argued more
105   thoroughly in Greenblatt & Shlegeris 2024.

108
      Control Evaluations are one form of AI Evaluation designed to help inform AI developers about
109   the properties of their model (Guo et al., 2023). Recently work has explored dangerous capability
110   evaluations (Shevlane et al., 2023). Such evaluations have been used on frontier models to determine
111   if models should be released (Phuong et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2024). Control evaluations are unique
112   in that they explicitly try to measure how well a model could subvert safety measures, but we expect
113   control evaluations to join other evaluations in determining if a model is safe to release.
114
      Control more broadly is inspired by Adversarial robustness (Goodfellow, 2019; Meng et al., 2022)
115   and meta-level adversarial evaluations (Shlegeris & Greenblatt, 2023), the practice of constructing
116   adversarial policies for oversight processes. Adversarial robustness to a variety of inputs has been
117   extensively studied across a variety of models (Biggio et al., 2013; Papernot et al., 2016), including
118   Large Language Models (Zou et al., 2023). Other studies explore safety dynamics across models
119   of varying capabilities, like scalable oversight (Bowman et al., 2022), debate (Irving et al., 2018),
120   and weak-to-strong generalization (Burns et al., 2023). Unlike control evaluations, these focus on
121   ’average-case’ scenarios, not assuming stronger models intentionally subvert safety measures.

123   3     S ETTING
124
125   We frame our setting as an interaction between a blue team (representing the actions of the devel-
126   opers of the untrusted model) and a red team (representing the actions of the untrusted model). The
127   blue team attempts to find a desirable tradeoff between usefulness and safety, while the red team
128   attempts to subvert the blue team’s protocols to maximize the probability of a successful attack.

130   3.1   BASH TASK DATASET
131
132
      We developed BashBenchHard, a dataset designed to fill the gap between existing datasets focused
      on multi-step interpreter interactions (Huang et al., 2023; Jimenez et al., 2023) and those centered
133
      on basic shell tasks (Liu et al., 2023). Our pipeline converts Unix StackExchange questions into
134
      task descriptions with Python test cases, filtering for tasks that are challenging but solvable. Each
135   task was tested using a GPT-4o-mini agent, and tasks that were unsolved or solved in fewer than
136   three actions across four trials were removed. Informal testing showed that software engineers and
137   ML researchers had a low success rate on these tasks without LLM assistance. Details of the dataset
138   creation and sample tasks can be found in Appendix C.
139
140   3.2   E LEMENTS OF OUR S CAFFOLD
141
142   We follow Greenblatt et al. 2023 in considering a situation where we wish to solve tasks across an
143   episode of 100,000 tasks, given access to three different resources:
144          • A weaker trusted model T , which we know is non-attacking and honestly assists with any
145            provided requests. We use GPT-3.5-turbo as the trusted model.
146          • A stronger untrusted model U , which we are worried might try to launch an attack. We use
147            GPT-4o-mini as the untrusted more-capable model.
148          • A limited budget of H, high-quality labor that can either be spent to audit a potentially
149            malicious bash command using o1-preview (simulating a human auditor) or creating a syn-
150            thetic attack command using GPT-4o-mini.
151   We evaluate our protocols with a notion of safety and usefulness designed to reflect a realistic de-
152   ployment use case and threat vector for LLM agents.
153
      Usefulness: When the untrusted model U is implemented with GPT-4o-mini, what is the percent of
154
      tasks the control protocol solves? We give the policy 15 steps per task. GPT-4o-mini solves 32.26%
155   of the tasks in the dataset, while the trusted model, GPT-3.5-turbo, solves 1.94%.
156
157   Safety: When U is implemented with an attack policy, what is the probability that the episode
158
      ends without a successful attack occurring? An attack is successful if we execute a command that
      downloads and executes the dummy malware file.
159
160   Models are stateless and don’t retain any information between calls, mapping from text to text. We
161   use UH and UA to denote the model implemented with GPT4o-mini and the model implemented
      with the attack policy, respectively.

      4.2     T RUSTED M ONITORS
271
272   Many of our protocols make use of a Trusted Monitor, where we prompt T to evaluate commands
273   for how suspicious they are. Our monitors map commands to a suspicion score representing how
274   likely the commands are to be an attack. We describe the properties of the trusted monitor and the
275   prompt in more detail in Appendix A, but highlight the important parts below.
276
      Our trusted monitors have been iteratively improved to perform well against a variety of attacks.
277
      In the monitor’s prompt, we specified particular threats to look for (such as unnecessary URLs,
278   command obfuscation, and unclear text insertions). We implemented command processing steps,
279   such as stripping comments and breaking longer commands into subcommands, to refine the input.
280   Instead of using the model’s final numeric output, we took a weighted average of the tokens 0 to 9
281   according to their probability to determine the suspicion score.
282
      As opposed to asking the monitor to generate a single chain of thought, sampling and ensembling
283
      multiple chains of thought on a monitor substantially boosts performance, as its ability to find suspi-
284   cious elements is relatively stochastic. We use few-shot examples taken from monitor misclassifica-
285   tions, which improves overall calibration in some regards but hurt the generalization ability to new
286   attacks. As an example of the importance of these interventions in particular, we’ve included the
287   monitor’s ROC curve for a specific attack in Figure 3. Surprisingly, the ablation study shows that
288   the few-shot examples decreased performance on our monitor on the final attack policy. We expect
289   that with more iteration the trusted monitor may have better generalization properties.
290

      4.2.1     T RUSTED M ONITORING P ROTOCOL
292
293   The blue team has access to summary statistics of the distribution of UH , which includes the distri-
294   bution of the trusted monitor’s suspicion scores on the non-attacking commands. As such, in a given
295   episode, if certain messages feel especially suspicious relative to the UH , we can use the trusted
296   monitor to flag these messages for auditing.
297
      We wish to use our trusted monitor to select 1% of all the messages in an episode to audit - ideally,
298   these will be the most suspicious and will hopefully catch an attack policy’s attack. This has a safety
299   score of 5.65%. However, departing from Greenblatt et al. 2023, rather than uniformly auditing the
300   top 1% of the most suspicious commands we dynamically change our auditing threshold, heavily
301   auditing commands early on (for reasons described in Appendix D). This incentivizes the red team
302   to insert attacks less frequently, and ultimately end up with a dramatically better safety score of 37%.
303
      Our monitoring protocol doesn’t affect usefulness, as we never shut down in an episode with UH (as
304   o1-mini, which implements H, perfectly distinguishes between attacks and non-attacks).

      4.3     U NTRUSTED M ONITORS
379
380
      Trusted monitors must be built on fundamentally weaker models than U , so they’re much worse at
381
      determining when an attack is present. On the other hand, U should be able to identify attacks far
382   more easily, given that U wrote them. In practice, GPT-4o-mini (with which we implement U ) can
383   identify attacks using heuristics more than 90% of the time, and o1-mini (with which we implement
384   H) can perfectly distinguish between safe and unsafe code purely by reasoning about whether it
385   might result in arbitrary code execution. However, while an untrusted monitor might be capable of
386   catching nearly all attacks, we might not be able to trust its responses!
387
      In order to confirm that the untrusted monitor doesn’t always lie, the blue team can show the un-
388   trusted monitor honeypots – synthetic attacks written by the blue team. We suppose that the blue
389   team can spend auditing points to generate attacks; creating one step of a honeypot costs the same
390   amount of human labor (H) as auditing one command. The blue team strategy is to show the un-
391   trusted monitor a set of honeypots before the game begins, and decide to shut down if the rate of
392   false negatives is too high.
393
      Therefore, the red team must ensure that the untrusted monitor does not lie too frequently. Since
394   the red team is stateless, UA must use some property of a bash command to distinguish between
395   honeypots and its own attacks. By using T to write detailed summaries of what each set of bash
396   commands does, and showing those summaries to the untrusted monitor instead of the original
397   commands, the blue team can attempt to remove subtle collusion signals. By instructing T to abstract
398   away details like the precise contents of strings, command formatting, and so on, the blue team
399   eliminates syntactical degrees of freedom, which forces the collusion signals to alter what the code
400   does – not just how it looks – to be included in T ’s summary.
401   We tested several collusion signals against trusted rewriting, and settled on the strategy of defin-
402   ing and calling a logging utility when UA attacks. This strategy works well at making it through
403   trusted summarization. Thus, UA is quite successful at colluding, but at the cost of making T more
404   suspicious, as T is instructed to be wary of extraneous code.
405
      As a result, untrusted monitoring provides a small improvement over trusted monitoring alone. For
406   instance, see the green dashed line in Figure 2, which demonstrates improved performance when
407   incorporating untrusted monitoring into the Defer to Resample protocol. However, this effect size
408   is notably dependent on the length of the game – with a greater number of tasks per episode, the
409   blue team can supply U with a greater number of honeypots while using the same fraction of their
410   auditing budget. See appendix E for details.

413   4.4     DYNAMIC ROUTING P ROTOCOLS
414
415   The main limitation of monitoring protocols is that the blue team can only influence a small number
416   of commands, even though the trusted monitor may flag many more as potentially suspicious. In
417   dynamic routing protocols, the blue team integrates the trusted monitor with other calls to T , which
418   may modify commands. While this reduces usefulness, it improves safety by allowing the blue team
419   to apply the trusted monitor’s capabilities beyond the top 1% of suspicious messages. The dynamic
420
      routing process first monitors a command, and if its suspiciousness exceeds the audit threshold, it is
      audited. If the suspiciousness is below the audit threshold but above a defer threshold, the command
421
      is replaced with a safer one. As the defer threshold decreases, more commands are modified, leading
422
      to improved overall safety.
423

425   4.4.1    D EFERRING TO T RUSTED O PERATIONS
426
427   We want our edits to remove any suspicious parts of the command, which incentivizes altering c as
428   much as possible. On the other hand, we still want to preserve as much usefulness as possible, which
429   incentivizes altering c minimally. To strike a balance between safety and usefulness, we study three
430   protocols that vary in how much they modify the incoming command. They each extract information
431   from c, and show the information to T , along with the history of what has been done to solve the

      4.4.2   D EFER TO R ESAMPLE
476
477       Control Protocol: Defer to Resample
478
479       Ignores c and asks U for another command until it isn’t suspicious.
480
481   The Defer to Resample protocol works by first sampling a command from the untrusted model U and
482   evaluating its suspiciousness using the Trusted Monitor. If the command’s suspicion score exceeds
483   a predefined defer threshold, the protocol resamples up to two additional commands, seeking a
484   safer alternative. After three resamples, if no command falls below the defer threshold, a dummy
485   command is inserted to prevent further risk. The protocol chooses the least suspicious command
      and audits it if its score remains above the auditing threshold, otherwise executing it.


                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      This process minimizes false positives by allowing suspicious commands to be replaced with less
487   suspicious alternatives, preserving auditing resources for more critical threats. The approach main-
488   tains usefulness while ensuring suspicious commands are properly addressed.
489
490         Red Team Strategy: Adaptation to Resample
491
492         The red team adds noise to their commands to avoid detection in the resample loop.
493
494   In response to the resample strategy, the red team adapted by introducing minor variations, or
495   ”noise,” into their commands with each submission. This tactic decreased the likelihood of being
496
      caught in the resampling loop, allowing the attack policy to continue undetected. By doing so, the
      red team avoids the risk of submitting an identical command multiple times, which would increase
497
      its chances of detection. However, this strategy comes with the trade-off of potentially weakening
498
      the effectiveness of their attacks, as the noise could alter the command’s intended outcome.
499
500   Despite the red team’s adaptations, the Defer to Resample protocol proved effective, maintaining a
501   high level of usefulness for the blue team while mitigating risks from repeated suspicious commands.

503   5     D ISCUSSION
504
505   5.1     M ULTI - STEP VS S INGLE - STEP C ONTROL
506
507   The red team benefits greatly in multi-step control settings because they can spread their attacks
508   out across multiple steps. This helps out the red team because the monitors don’t try hard to “look
509
      backwards”: the monitors won’t, for example, look backwards in the history to notice that multiple
      modifications of a file spell out a suspicious link or command. This seems hard to do in practice
510
      - the costs of pressuring your model to look back further and further increase rapidly, and the red
511
      team can just spread out attacks further and further.
512
513   In the multi-step setting, the blue team can benefit greatly from forcing the attack policy to give up
514   on its attacks partway through (through a deferral, for example) since this forces the attack policy to
515
      attempt attacks far more often. This is evidenced by the success of the dynamic routing protocols,
      which frequently replaces parts of attacks. This both gives the blue team more opportunities to audit
516
      the attacks and, since it’s very likely that there will be at least one attack early on, this also allows
517
      the blue team to more heavily front-load their auditing budget, which acts as an effective multiplier
518   on their auditing budget.

      5.2     D OES O UR W ORK ACCURATELY M ODEL F UTURE C ONTROL P ROTOCOLS ?
521
522   Many aspects of our setting are specific, and it’s challenging to predict how changes might shift
523   the balance between the blue and red teams. Our work may underestimate the effectiveness of
524   future control protocols for several reasons: the attack policy heavily relies on human-created attack
525   templates, which may overstate the capabilities of the first untrusted models; as trusted models
526   improve, so will control protocols, as models like o1 are already effective at identifying attacks; the
527   assumption that untrusted models excel at finding non-obvious Schelling points is conservative; and
528
      we did not explore techniques for restricting the actions taken by the model.
529   Conversely, our work might overestimate the effectiveness of control protocols due to several factors:
530   the blue team already knows which threat vector to focus on; using expensive, trusted labor for 1%
531   of all commands is impractical with current models; human-level red-teaming may not match the
532   sophistication of future untrusted models; some efforts lacked thorough blue-team and red-team
533   iterations, particularly for dynamic routing protocols; and future agents may undertake much longer
534
      trajectories, allowing for more complex and sustained attacks than those explored here.

651   A    T RUSTED M ONITORING D ETAILS
652
653   Here are the qualities of our trusted monitors in more detail:
654   Adding more auditing weight to specific threat models. Rather than prompting T to generally
655   look for a threat vector, our prompt to T has hardcoded specific things for the monitors to do. These
656   include identifying and evaluating URLs, checking for command obfuscation or irrelevance, scru-
657   tinizing strings and text insertions, identifying executed files, and flagging any encoding/decoding
658   activities. We provide the history leading up to the command, as well as the command, to the
659   monitor. Processing commands. For a variety of reasons, it is worth applying a processing step
660   to commands before showing them to the monitors. One such reason is that long commands can
661   end up hiding parts of the attack quite well. To address this threat vector, we had to break up the
662
      commands.We also stripped out (some) comments, but didn’t have a good bash parser to help with
      this. Next, we brokeeak up longer commands into subcommands; the overall suspicion score of a
663
      command is the maximum suspicion of one of its subcommands.
664
665   Ensembling. Calling an ensemble of monitors (where we sample multiple completions of models
666   with the same prompt) substantially improves usefulness. Our trusted monitors have temperature t
667   = 1.1, and on multiple (n=4) samples of the same prompt of a suspicious command, the monitor will
668
      often only stochastically identify the suspicious part of a command.
669   Few-shotting on failures. While these high level ideas alone are sufficient in principle to catch all
670   the attacks, the red team has optimized their attacks to look as innocuous as possible in response.
671   This causes the monitors to have trouble noticing the signs of the attack. We found that taking
672   misclassifications (missed attacks or incorrectly flagged non-attacking commands) and converting
673   them to few-shot examples in which we properly demonstrate how to label the command vastly
674
      improves the monitor. This helps the monitor better identify attacks and calibrates the monitor on
      non-attacks. However, this seems to have additionally caused the monitor to be slightly worse at
675
      generalizing to new attacks
676
677   Use probs. We ask the model for a score out of 9 - however, rather than taking the final numeric
678   output of the model as the score, we take the weighted average of the probabilities of the tokens
679   from 0 to 9.
680   At one point we saw benefit from taking the scores outputted by different monitors on the same
681   command, performing logistic regression on their outputs, and then outputting a new suspicion
682   score. However, our final monitor doesn’t do this.
683
      While building a trusted monitor involves needing to be able to catch attacks, decreasing the number
684   of falsely labeled attacks is just as important. Our trusted monitors have to exercise an appropriate
685   amount of caution and not blindly follow hardcoded red flags. Frequently we do this by asking our
686   monitors if a commands is relevant or not. This can potentially help the monitor not misclassify
687   innocuous commands which may partially violate our hardcoded red flags.
688
      One interesting component of our monitors is that their usefulness scales up with cost. In particular,
689
      scaling up the number of ensembles significantly helps usefulness, and we expect that we could
690   expect some improvement in the monitor with more ensembling than we did.

      The red team’s primary objective was to covertly execute the command curl

1281   D.1   I NTUITION B EHIND E XPONENTIAL F UNCTION FOR AUDITING
1282
1283   An ideal distribution of auditing budget should have the property that marginally increasing the
1284   auditing budget anywhere throughout the game has the same impact on safety. Otherwise, the blue
1285   team could do better by reallocating auditing points from one location in the game to another. Since
1286
       the red team will attack with probability p, the probability that the red team has not yet attacked is
       exponentially decaying as (1 − p)i , where i is the current iteration of the game.
1287
1288   Therefore, the value of auditing points should decay approximately exponentially throughout the
1289   game: they are more useful closer to the beginning and less useful near the end, where the red team
1290   is very likely to have already attacked. This model relies on the simplifying assumption that the
1291   derivative of the probability of catching an attack with respect to the auditing budget is roughly
1292
       linear, which is approximately true at small auditing budgets (such as 1%), but experiences rapidly
       diminishing returns at higher auditing budgets (such as 10%).
1293
1294   This model also assumes that the proportion of attacks that end the game is not a function of the
1295   auditing budget (i.e., increasing the auditing budget at a given index only converts successful attacks,
       not unsuccessful attacks, into caught attacks).

       D.2   R ED T EAM R ESPONSE TO B LUE T EAM AUDITING
1297
1298
1299   Given a distribution chosen by the blue team, how should the red team best respond? The red team
1300
       faces a tradeoff between decreasing p, to attack later in the episode when there’s a lower risk of being
       audited, and ensuring that p is not so low as to risk the entire episode elapsing without inserting a
1301
       successful attack.
1302
1303   When the red team is best responding, they are ambivalent between increasing p to decrease the risk
1304   of inserting 0 attacks and decreasing p to reduce the likelihood of being caught. Since the choice
1305   of auditing distribution does not affect usefulness—only safety—the red team and blue team are
1306
       playing a zero-sum game. The blue team aims to minimize the probability of a successful attack,
       while the red team seeks to maximize it. Thus, we expect a minimax approach to find a Nash
1307
       equilibrium.
1308
1309   If the blue team searches over possible values of r (using scipy’s minimize scalar) to find the
1310   distribution that maximizes the blue team win rate, conditional on the red team’s best response p∗ (r)
1311   (also computed using minimize scalar), we empirically find a Nash equilibrium (r∗ , p∗ ). At
1312
       this point, the blue team cannot increase the probability of catching the untrusted model by making
       the auditing distribution any flatter or sharper.

       D.3   NASH E QUILIBRIUM AND B LUE T EAM S TRATEGY
1339
1340
1341   Each curve corresponds to the blue team win rate as a function of p for a different exponential decay
1342
       rate. The red team’s best response to each decay rate is located at the minimum of each curve.
       The Nash equilibrium can be found at the saddle point, where the blue team cannot increase the
1343
       minimum win rate as a function of p by changing the exponential distribution of auditing budget.
1344
1345   At the Nash equilibrium, the red team picks a low value of p∗ , on the order of game1length , forcing the
1346   blue team to spread auditing points over a large fraction of the game with a similarly small decay
1347   rate, also on the order of game1length . As a result, the auditing budget doesn’t vary so sharply over
1348   the game that we run into steep diminishing returns in safety with marginal increases in auditing
1349   budget at different points in the game. This makes the mathematical simplification that justifies an
       exponential distribution quite reasonable.

       D.4    E MPIRICAL T ESTING OF E XPONENTIAL NASH E QUILIBRIUM
1351
1352   We empirically test the optimality of this exponential Nash equilibrium using a hill-climbing algo-
1353   rithm to optimize a more general function class, which allows the auditing threshold to take on any
1354   value across the game (subject to the total auditing budget constraint).
1355
       The hill-climbing algorithm works by splitting the game into N bins (for instance, 100), and identi-
1356   fying the sensitivity of the blue team win rate to marginal auditing points in each bin. The vector of
1357   sensitivity to marginal auditing budget across bins is normalized to have mean zero, and then multi-
1358   plied by a learning rate and added to the bins. Thus, bins with larger returns to additional auditing
1359   budgets find their budget increased for the next iteration, and bins with smaller returns decreased.
1360   An additional normalization step ensures that the distribution never deviates from the required total
1361   auditing budget.
1362   This algorithm is repeated until the sensitivity to marginal auditing budget across bins is fairly
1363   uniform, and the blue team win probability stops increasing. In practice, it converges rapidly in
1364   fewer than ten iterations. While we cannot rigorously guarantee that this algorithm will avoid local
1365   minima, empirically it produces distributions that are similar to the optimal exponential distribution
1366   but with slightly higher blue team win rates. This lends strong support to our choice of exponential
1367   functions.
1368   Since exponential functions are much easier to optimize, given just a single parameter (the exponen-
1369   tial decay rate) rather than the values of 100 independent bins, we use them as a computationally
1370   efficient approximation of the true solution.

       E.1   ATTACK DISTRIBUTION ASSUMPTION
1375
1376   We make the fairly generous assumption that the blue team can roughly guess what the attack dis-
1377   tribution looks like in order to create realistic honeypots. If the red team knows about sufficiently
1378   unique exploits that UA can use to perfectly distinguish its own attacks from honeypots, then no ad-
1379   ditional collusion signal is required. Moreover, future control protocols will likely require monitors
1380   to assess large contexts, which might be very difficult to convincingly fake, making it obvious to
1381   models when they are being evaluated.
1382   Redaction and paraphrasing by trusted models will help mitigate this problem, as they aid in remov-
1383   ing collusion signals. Furthermore, instead of evaluating the untrusted monitor on honeypots before
1384   deployment, a more realistic protocol might involve retesting it at regular intervals with synthetic
1385   attacks inserted into real actions generated during deployment. This would make them harder to
1386   distinguish from purely human-written tests.
1387
1388   E.2   S CHELLING POINT ASSUMPTION
1389
1390   The generous allowance of letting the blue team sample from the attack distribution is somewhat
1391   balanced by the highly conservative assumption that the untrusted model is extremely competent
1392
       at picking non-obvious Schelling points. The red team is allowed to pick an arbitrary non-obvious
       collusion signal that both the untrusted policy and untrusted monitor agree upon without directly
1393
       communicating, a skill which we believe current models do not possess. While the blue team might
1394
       reasonably guess what classes of malicious strategies the red team could use, the space of possible
1395   collusion signals is so vast that the blue team cannot reasonably predict precisely how the red team
1396   will choose to communicate.
1397

       E.3   D EPENDENCE ON GAME LENGTH
1399
1400   The untrusted monitor’s impact is notably dependent on game length, with a longer game length re-
1401   sulting in a much more useful untrusted monitor. More tasks per episode results in a greater number
1402   of honeypots at the same auditing budget, which allows the blue team to more easily distinguish
1403   between the false negative rates of UH and UA . The red team is forced to collude more cautiously
       as a result, and is more likely to honestly report some of the attack policy’s attacks.

       In practice, we compute the performance and safety of a protocol using the following method. First,
1461   we calculate usefulness by assuming that our 200 tasks are i.i.d. samples from the same task distribu-
1462   tion as the 100,000 tasks completed during the game, and report the empirical mean completion rate
1463   over our task set using an honest untrusted policy. Concurrently, we run the monitor(s) to identify
1464   both the baseline distribution of their scores and to supply their scores as inputs to various routing
1465   protocols.
1466
       Next, we perform a safety run, where the attack policy is executed on a subset of tasks—potentially a
1467   subset selected by the red team—and monitor scores are gathered. We can use this data to determine
1468   the probability that an attack is caught at a given auditing threshold by counting how many of the
1469   sampled attacks surpass that percentile on the usefulness baseline distribution.
1470
       The actual safety score for the game is more complex to compute. First, we find the Nash equilibrium
1471
       between p (the probability of attacking selected by the red team) and the exponential decay rate in
1472
       auditing budget (selected by the blue team). This determines the best distribution of auditing budget
1473   across the bins that the blue team can achieve, assuming the red team is responding optimally.
1474
1475   The probability of the game ending during each bin is calculated using the sampled set of attacks
1476
       from the safety run. Some percentage of the attacks are caught at the bin’s auditing threshold, some
       succeed without being caught, and others silently fail. The probability that the blue team wins
1477
       in a given bin is equal to the probability that the bin is reached before the game ends, times the
1478
       probability the red team attempts to attack in that bin, times the probability that an attempted attack
1479   is caught at that bin’s auditing threshold.
1480
1481   The total safety score is found by summing the blue team’s win probabilities across all bins, and then
1482
       adding the probability that the game ends before any attack either succeeds or is caught. When un-
       trusted monitoring is used, additional optimization over the relevant parameters is applied to ensure
1483
       the red team selects an optimal collusion threshold and the blue team makes reasonable allocations
1484
       of auditing budget.

1487
1488   The blue team’s strategy is parameterized by how they choose to allocate auditing budget among
1489   the trusted monitor, untrusted monitor, and honeypots, along with a threshold at which a sufficient
1490   number of failures to report honeypots leads to shut down. The red team’s strategy is parameterized
1491   by their collusion threshold – i.e. how confident they are that a collusion signal is present before
1492   deciding to collude. More honeypots or a lower shutdown threshold encourage the red team to pick
1493   a higher collusion threshold. But the blue team has to trade off against auditing budgets or risks
1494   erroneous shutdown, which hurts usefulness.

1496   G     S TOP A FTER E DIT S TRATEGY
1497
1498   In the Stop After Edit strategy, the red team halts an ongoing attack as soon as any edit to the attack
1499   command is detected. The goal is to reduce the chance of the blue team’s auditing process catching
1500   the attack after it has been modified. By terminating the attack early, the red team avoids triggering
1501   audits on commands that have become more suspicious after being edited by the blue team’s control
1502   protocols.
1503   The red team doesn’t have direct access to ground truth information on whether or not a command
1504   has been edited, but it can infer this based on the expected outcome of its own command. By compar-
1505   ing the submitted command with what it would have provided, the red team can detect modifications
1506   and decide to stop the attack.8
1507
