009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have exhibited exciting capabilities in generating images and are
012            also very promising for video creation. However, the inference speed of diffusion
013            models is limited by the slow sampling process, restricting its use cases. The se-
014            quential denoising steps required for generating a single sample could take tens or
015            hundreds of iterations and thus have become a significant bottleneck. This limita-
016
               tion is more salient for applications that are interactive in nature or require small
               latency. To address this challenge, we propose Partially Conditioned Patch Par-
017
               allelism (PCPP) to accelerate the inference of high-resolution diffusion models.
018
               Using the fact that the difference between the images in adjacent diffusion steps
019            is nearly zero, Patch Parallelism (PP) leverages multiple GPUs communicating
020            asynchronously to compute patches of an image in multiple computing devices
021            based on the entire image (all patches) in the previous diffusion step. PCPP de-
022            velops PP to reduce computation in inference by conditioning only on parts of the
023            neighboring patches in each diffusion step, which also decreases communication
024            among computing devices. As a result, PCPP decreases the communication cost
025            by around 70% compared to DistriFusion (the state of the art implementation of
026            PP) and achieves 2.36 ∼ 8.02× inference speed-up using 4 ∼ 8 GPUs compared
027
               to 2.32 ∼ 6.71× achieved by DistriFusion depending on the computing device
               configuration and resolution of generation at the cost of a possible decrease in
028
               image quality. PCPP demonstrates the potential to strike a favorable trade-off,
029
               enabling high-quality image generation with substantially reduced latency.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) are very successful
035   in modeling and generating unstructured data, such as images, molecular structures (Huang et al.,
036   2022), and three-dimensional (3D) models (Qian et al., 2023). In recent years, with the release of
037   the latent diffusion model (Rombach et al., 2021), its variants (Ho, 2022; Saharia et al., 2022) have
038   become the center of attention in both academia and industry. Doing a diffusion process in the latent
039
      space, in which dimensionality can be much smaller than the image space, requires significantly
      lesser computation to train a diffusion model and make inferences with it than in the image space.
040
      Despite the wide adoption and application of diffusion models, its sequential denoising process of
041
      hundreds or thousands of steps bottlenecks the inference speed and introduces non-trivial latency for
042   interactive use cases. This challenge becomes more prevalent when the resolution of the generated
043   images increases.
044
045
      Two types of methods have been proposed to accelerate the inference of diffusion models: decreas-
      ing the number of steps required for the denoising diffusion process and increasing the computing
046
      speed of diffusion steps. The most adapted diffusion models - Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic
047
      Models (DDPMs) (Ho et al., 2020) can take thousands of denoising steps to generate an image.
048   Various works were introduced to decrease the denoising steps required but often at the cost of the
049   quality of generation, such as Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIMs) (Song et al., 2020)
050   and DPM-Solvers (Lu et al., 2022a;b). Other works were introduced to decrease the computation
051   needed for each diffusion step, such as Spatially Sparse Inference (SSI) (Li et al., 2022) and Q-
052   Diffusion (Li et al., 2023), also trading quality for speed. With the growing interest in fast inference
053   for diffusion models, in addition to accelerating the diffusion process on a single device, researchers
      began to study making inferences parallelly to generate a single image utilizing multiple devices.

054
      ParaDiGMS (Shih et al., 2023) computes multiple diffusion steps in parallel while Patch Parallelism
055   (PP) (Li et al., 2024) separates an image into patches and does diffusion on each patch in a dedicated
056   device. Li et al. (2024) introduced DistriFusion, the current state-of-the-art diffusion model infer-
057   ence acceleration method leveraging multiple devices, which implements PP using asynchronous
058   communication among devices.
059
      In this paper, we empirically show that the denoising step of every patch of an image does not
060
      necessarily need to be conditioned on the entire feature map in the previous step. This allows
061   the communication in DistriFusion to be optimized and further decreases the computation required
062   for every diffusion step during inference. Our method Partially Conditioned Patch Parallelsim
063   (PCPP) thus reduces latency compared to PP by reducing the amount of computation performed in
064   the denoising step. As a result, PCPP decreases the communication cost by around 70% compared
065   to DistriFusion and achieves 2.36 ∼ 8.02× inference speed-up using 4 ∼ 8 GPUs compared to
066   2.32 ∼ 6.71× achieved by DistriFusion depending on the computing device configuration and
067   resolution of generation.

069   2     BACKGROUND
070
071   2.1   D IFFUSION MODEL
072
073   Diffusion models, or diffusion probabilistic models (DPMs), are latent variable models defined by
074   Markov chains. This stochastic model predicts the probability of a sequence of possible events
075   occurring based on the previous event (Ho et al., 2020). The Markov chain is first trained through a
076   forward diffusion process, during which noise is added to the sample using variational inference to
077   control the strength of the Gaussian noise (Song et al., 2020). Subsequently, the forward diffusion
078
      process is reversed, denoising the sample at each timestep until the final image is generated (Li
      et al., 2023). This denoising step can be achieved through either a noise prediction model, which
079
      attempts to predict the next noise components, or data prediction models, which predict the original
080
      data based on the noise at each timestep (Lu et al., 2022b). Currently, noise prediction models that
081   utilize the U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) architecture are typically used in practice for diffusion
082   models with attention modules (Vaswani et al., 2017) in transformers (Li et al., 2023). Let the
083   noise prediction model be ϵθ . The process begins with a Gaussian noise sample xT ∼ N (0, I) and
084   iteratively denoises it for T steps to obtain the final image x0 . At each step t, the noisy image xt is
085   fed into the model ϵθ along with the timestep t and an optional condition c (such as text), to predict
086   the noise component ϵt = ϵθ (xt , t, c). The image xt−1 at the next timestep is then calculated using
087   ϵt . The latency bottleneck often happens during the forward passes through the U-Net.
088   For conditional sampling with diffusion models, guided sampling is a popular technique for saving
089   sampling time by reducing the retraining steps of the network. It supports two guided sampling
090   methods for the conditional noise prediction model ϵ̂θ (xt , t, c). The first method is classifier guid-
091   ance, which uses a pretrained classifier to define the conditional noise prediction model on the
092   variable guidance scale. The second method is classifier-free guidance, where the unconditional and
093   conditional noise prediction models share the same model, combining the score estimates instead of
094   relying on a specified classifier (Lu et al., 2022b). Our focus lies on the generative denoising process
095
      that utilizes the U-Net structured noise prediction models with classifier-free guidance.
096
097   2.2   PATCH PARALLELISM
098
099
      One approach to designing parallelism for diffusion models is Patch Parallelism (PP), where a single
      image is divided into patches and distributed across multiple GPUs for individual and parallel com-
100
      putations. In the naive approach to PP, i.e. with no communication between the patches, each device
101
      essentially creates its own full image. When brought together, these individual images form the final
102   composite image (Li et al., 2024). To create one cohesive image using PP, synchronous communica-
103   tion can be employed to obtain intermediate activations between patches at each step. This approach
104   is similar to domain parallelism, where devices communicate by exchanging halo regions at their
105   boundaries during forward and backward convolution passes, synchronizing between communica-
106   tion and computation phases. However, when dealing with smaller tensors, this approach may incur
107   significant communication overhead, which can mitigate the speedups gained from parallelizing the
      process (Jin et al., 2018). To address this issue for single image generation across multiple devices,

141   DistriFusion uses one synchronous communication to facilitate patch interaction, followed by asyn-

145
      classifier, and the other for actual image computation. Due to batch splitting, the number of devices
146
      available for each image generation is limited, as only half of the devices are utilized for generating
147   pixels (Li et al., 2024). As such, experiments conducted on 8 GPUs would equate to distributing the
148   image patches across 4 GPUs. To extend DistriFusion, we delve deeper into the modules involved in
149   each denoising step. We identify and experiment with areas that can be further parallelized through
150   the use of partial activations and optimized communication, leading to the development of PCPP.

      3     M ETHOD
153
154
      In this section, we introduce our PCPP for parallelizing the inference of diffusion models using
155
      multiple devices asynchronously by point-to-point communication. The key idea is to partition the
156   image horizontally into n non-overlapping patches and process each patch conditioned on only itself
157   and the parts of its neighboring patches (from the previous step) on separate devices. This approach
158   is based on the hypothesis that generating image patches does not always necessitate dependency
159   on all other patches; instead, satisfactory results can be achieved by relying solely on neighboring
160   patches. An illustration of image generation using PCPP is presented in Figure 1. To implement
161   PCPP, we redesign the asynchronous communication in DistriFusion and adjust the input for every
      self-attention module in the U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) to reduce computation. Section 3.2

313
      on-device activations, which are insufficient for precise approximation. Hence, we used the same
314
      approach in DistriFusion instead of point-to-point communication with only the neighboring devices

317   decided to leave the AllGather function for it as it is.

457   5.3   G ENERATION QUALITY
458
459
      As shown in Table 2, the images generated using our method PCPP generally achieved lower results
      than DistriFusion. This difference is an expected trade-off resulting from our communication strat-
460
      egy. By employing point-to-point communication with only neighboring patches, the inference pro-
461
      cess inherently loses access to a significant amount of contextual information from non-neighboring
462   regions. Nevertheless, PCPP achieved reasonably close PSNR scores compared with DistriFusion,
463   indicating comparable reconstruction quality and noise level. In terms of LPIPS and FID, PCPP
464   performed worse than DistriFusion, meaning that images generated using PCPP deviate more from
465   those generated using one device than DistriFusion.
466
      In addition to numerical evaluations, we visually compared sample images generated by our PCPP
467
      method, the original image generated by one device, and the images generated by DistriFusion. As
468   reflected in the metrics, a sizable portion of the generated images by PCPP has distortions and ir-
469   rational parts. This suggests that having only part or all neighboring patches may not generate a
470   coherent image for all prompts. In Figure 5, we show three prompts that work reasonably well with
471   PCPP. In the elephant example, the images generated by PCPP are visually indistinguishable from
472   those produced by DistriFusion, suggesting that our method can achieve comparable image quality
473   for certain prompts. However, in the cat example, there are noticeable differences between the PCPP
474   and DistriFusion outputs when 8 devices are used, although the PCPP-generated images still main-
475   tain high fidelity and coherence. In the bird example, the image generated by PCPP with 8 devices
476
      is more similar to that generated by one device in terms of bird color. This shows the randomness in
      the diffusion process, even with the same random seed. This discrepancy in the quality of generated
477
      images suggests that partial attention, which is important for the diffusion process, may not always
478
      come from the immediate neighboring parts. More comparisons are available in Appendix B.

481   6     D ISCUSSION
482
483   In this research, we propose Partially Conditioned Patch Parallelism (PCPP) to accelerate the infer-
484   ence of high-resolution diffusion models leveraging multiple computing devices. Trading generation
485   quality for speed, we achieve better speed-up than DistriFusion - the state-of-the-art implementation
      of Patch Parallelism (PP) - with less computation and communication. As a result, PCPP decreases


                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      the communication cost by around 70% compared to DistriFusion and achieves 2.36 ∼ 8.02× infer-
487   ence speed-up using 4 ∼ 8 GPUs compared to 2.32 ∼ 6.71× achieved by DistriFusion depending
488   on the computing device configuration and resolution of generation at the cost of a decrease in image
489   quality. PCPP demonstrates the potential to strike a favorable trade-off, enabling high-quality image
490   generation with substantially reduced latency.
491
      Limitation and future directions. The experiments conducted are restricted by the computing
492
      resources available. As a result, a maximum of 8 GPUs and a highest resolution of 3840 × 3840 are
493   used. The impact of having more GPUs, i.e., more patches, on the quality of generation by PCPP
494   has thus not been studied. We hypothesize that for a larger number of GPUs, the context needed
495   for the generation of a patch may extend beyond immediate neighboring patches, corresponding to a
496   partial value larger than 1. The key insight from this research - reducing collective communication to
497   point-to-point communication with neighboring patches - provides an interesting perspective on the
498   trade-off between inference efficiency and generation quality in diffusion models. During parallel
499   inference, how much and what context does the model really need, and how fresh should those stale
500   activations from the previous denoising step be to achieve similar image quality? These questions
501
      deeply about diffusion may be further studied in future research. The inconsistent quality of images
      generated by partially conditioned attention on parts of neighboring patches suggests that a more
502
      involved selection of relevant context is needed as a direction for future research. Furthermore, due
503
      to the scope of this research, the effect of PCPP on popular optimization and inference methods
504   for diffusion models, such as ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) and LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), is left
505   for future discussion. Previous studies in image generation by diffusion model following a patch-
506   like approach for memory optimization in a single device (Yang et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2024)
507   may also be integrated with PCPP to improve the generation quality while taking advantage of the
508   asynchronous point-to-point communication.
509
      Another potential limitation of this research (and accelerating inference with multiple computing
510   devices in general) is that requiring multiple GPU devices for a single-image generation might seem
511   like an inefficient use of resources. However, we believe this approach has potential use cases in spe-
512   cific areas, such as high-resolution image editing applications that demand high responsiveness. In a
513   traditional single-device setup, generating a high-resolution image could take more than 2 minutes.
514   In contrast, by leveraging PCPP, we can complete the same task in under 15 seconds without any
515   significant decrease in image quality, as shown in Section 5. This significant reduction in latency can
516   be particularly valuable in interactive applications where users expect near-instant feedback, such as
517   real-time image editing tools.
518   Ethics statement. PCPP does not intend to alter the image generated and its meaning. The gen-
519   erated content is primarily determined by the diffusion model and the prompts given, with possible
520   distortions introduced by the parallelizing computing approach. Therefore, the ethical practice of
521   using generation service accelerated by PCPP is ensured by the design of the diffusion model and
522   restrictions on the prompts as input, not as a part of PCPP.
523   PCPP can be applied to accelerate the generation of images by diffusion models leveraging multiple
524   resources. Compared to the previous state of the art approach, PCPP reduces computation and
525   communication for the same inference, thus contributing to energy saving for image generation.
526   The speed-up might be helpful for many applications requiring a timely response given a prompt.
527   In addition, PCPP might also be applied to diffusion models that generate data structures other than
528   images. We wish PCPP can be a useful tool for researchers, designers, and users of diffusion models.
529   Reproducibility statement. We provided a detailed description of the method and its implemen-
530   tation in Section 3. In addition, more implementation details are described in Appendix A. In
531   Section 4, we included the computing device, dataset, and evaluation metrics for reproducing the
532   experiments. We also plan to release the code and scripts necessary to reproduce our results.

      A    PARALLEL I MPLEMENTATION
623
      Design Choice on Synchronization. In our current implementation, for any device i, we explicitly
624
      wait for the asynchronous operations of layer l at step t to complete at the beginning of layer l + 1 at
625
      the same step t, even if we won’t be using them until the layer l at the next denoising step t − 1. One
626   might wonder why we don’t call the .wait() method when the activations are actually needed. We
627   have considered this initially. However, the NCCL (NVIDIA Collective Communications Library)
628   backend does not support tags in the isend and irecv operations. This means that if we allow
629   isend and irecv operations of multiple layers to be in-flight at the same time, the receiving
630   device won’t be able to identify which tensors are from which layers. As such, we decided to call
631   the .wait() operation a bit earlier, so that it guarantees that at any given time, only one group of
632   asynchronous send and receive operations between any two devices are in-flight. This ensures that
633   the receiving device can properly match the incoming tensors to the correct layers.
634
635   Avoiding Potential Deadlocks. Since one device can simultaneously fire up to two pairs of
636
      isend and irecv calls to its two neighbors, if we were to issue these operations individually,
      there are risks of deadlocks when the order of isend and irecv does not match each other. To
637
      address this issue, we decided to use torch.distributed.batch isend irecv, where we
638
      can issue all the send and receive operations for a given step in a single call, and the library handles
639   the coordination and matching of the tensors on the receiving side. This not only avoids the potential
640   for errors or mismatches, but also simplifies the bookkeeping and management of the asynchronous
641   requests in our code. Instead of maintaining a separate list of isend and irecv requests for each
642   layer, we can work with a single batch isend irecv request object, which makes the logic
643   more concise.
644
645   Batched Communication of Layer Activations. One potential future work is to explore
646   accumulating the activations for a few continuous layers before initiating the communica-
647   tion. In our current implementation, we send the activations for each layer individually using
      dist.batch isend irecv as soon as they are ready, as described in Section 3.2. However,


                                                        12
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


648
      we could potentially optimize this by accumulating the activations for a couple of layers before trig-
649   gering the communication. This would allow us to send a batch of activations for multiple layers at
650   once, rather than sending them one layer at a time. This would reduce the overall communication
651   overhead by amortizing the costs of setting up and tearing down the communication across mul-
652   tiple layers. Additionally, sending larger batches of activations may allow the underlying NCCL
653   communication libraries to optimize the transfers more effectively.
