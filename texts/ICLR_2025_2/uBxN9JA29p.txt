011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            While recent two-stage many-to-one deep learning models have demonstrated
014            great success in 3D human pose estimation, such models are inefficient in 3D key
015            point detection and also tend to pass on first stage errors onto the second stage.
016
               In this paper, we introduce SoloPose, a novel one-stage, many-to-many spatio-
               temporal transformer model for kinematic 3D human pose estimation of video.
017
               SoloPose is further fortified by HeatPose, a 3D heatmap based on Gaussian Mix-
018
               ture Model distributions that factors target key points as well as kinematically
019            adjacent key points. Finally, we address data diversity constraints with the 3D
020            AugMotion Toolkit, a methodology to augment existing 3D human pose datasets,
021            specifically by projecting four top public 3D human pose datasets (Human3.6M,
022            MADS, AIST Dance++, MPI INF 3DHP) into a novel dataset (Human7.1M) with
023            a universal coordinate system. Extensive experiments are conducted on both Hu-
024            man3.6M and the augmented Human7.1M dataset, and SoloPose demonstrates
025            superior results relative to the state-of-the-art approaches.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Pose estimation have applications in action recognition, sports analysis, medical rehabilitation, and
031   collaborative robotics (Rong et al., 2021; Remelli et al., 2020; Jeong et al., 2023). Among its differ-
032   ent forms, monocular pose estimation (Tang et al., 2023; Shan et al., 2022) involves taking single-
033
      perspective 2D images or videos of either single (Tang et al., 2023; Shan et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022)
      or multi-person (Wang & Zhang, 2022; Fang et al., 2022; Maji et al., 2022) inputs and generating
034
      2D or 3D coordinates of skeletal key points. There have been significant recent advancements in
035
      pose estimation models that specialize in unique approaches, namely the use of human mesh (Cai
036   et al., 2024; Chun et al., 2023) as opposed to human joints (Tang et al., 2023; Shan et al., 2022), the
037   pose estimation of multi-person data (Wang & Zhang, 2022; Fang et al., 2022; Maji et al., 2022) as
038   opposed to single-person data (Tang et al., 2023; Shan et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022).While models
039   to generate 2D skeleton key points have been greatly improved in recent years (Zeng et al., 2021;
040   Newell et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2018; Zheng et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Shan et al., 2022), 3D
041   human pose estimators are constrained by the following:
042   First, most 3D human pose estimators are two-stage models (Zeng et al., 2021; Newell et al., 2016;
043   Chen et al., 2018; Zheng et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Shan et al., 2022) that are a) highly dependent on
044   the accuracy of 2D estimators, and b) solely take 2D skeletal keypoints as input, thus omitting con-
045   textual information needed for computational efficiency during 3D human pose estimation. Second,
046   while there are many datasets to train pose estimation models, namely the Human3.6M (Ionescu
047   et al., 2014), MADS (Zhang et al., 2017), AIST Dance++ (Tsuchida et al., 2019) and MPI INF
048   3DHP (Mehta et al., 2017), these all suffer from data diversity and image resolution issues.
049   Finally, recent pose estimators utilize transformers as the deep learning network (Newell et al., 2016;
050   Chen et al., 2018; Zheng et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Shan et al., 2022) to process video frames in
051   a many-to-one approach (Zheng et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Shan et al., 2022), which take multiple
052   frames as input but select solely the middle frame to estimate coordinates, neglecting frames at
053   the beginning and end of videos. In contrast, a many-to-many approach offers the advantage of
      outputting results for multiple frames simultaneously.

      In sum, current 3D human pose estimators face three primary challenges: 1) a scarcity of high-
      quality 3D human pose datasets, 2) high-reliance on two-stage models, and 3) time-intensive many-
070
      to-one processing approaches.
071
072   To address the above, we propose the following contributions:
073
074            1. We introduce SoloPose1 , a cost-efficient one-stage, many-to-many spatio-temporal trans-
075
                  former model for 3D human pose estimation that takes frame sequences of monocular 2D
                  video as input to directly estimate 3D key point coordinates.
076
077            2. We propose the 3D AugMotion Toolkit to augment existing datasets (e.g., Human3.6M,
078               MADS, AIST Dance++, MPI INF 3DHP) for increasing diversity and reducing noise,
079
                  yielding an augmented dataset that we refer to as Human7.1M.
080            3. Finally, we evaluate our model on two testing datasets: Human 3.6M and Human 7.1M.
081               Experimental results demonstrate that our proposed method showcases state-of-the-art ac-
082               curacy performance across both the datasets.
083
      We structure the current work as follows. First, we discuss related work of the current state-of-the-art
084
      in monocular 3D human pose estimation as well as prevailing 3D human pose video datasets. Sec-
085
      ond, we introduce the 3D Augmotion Toolkit, a methodology to augment 3D human pose datasets
086   using universal coordinate systems, which we leverage to generate our Human7.1M dataset. Third,
087   we introduce SoloPose, a one-stage, many-to-many spatio-temporal transformer for 3D human pose
088   estimation, which is fortified by our 3D GMM-based heatmap (HeatPose). Next, we demonstrate
089   SoloPose’s performance by comparing SOTA methods, as well as comparing existing Human3.6M
090   and our Human7.1M datasets. Finally, we conduct ablation studies to test our contributions, namely
091   HeatPose (i.e., 3D Gaussian heatmap) and AugMotion (i.e., 3D human pose data augmentation).

115   2.1.2    T WO - STAGE 3D H UMAN P OSE E STIMATION M ETHODS
116
      While previous work (Pavlakos et al., 2017; Sun et al., 2018; Jin et al., 2022) propose one-stage
117
      methodologies to boost efficiency and accuracy, these models have thus far solely utilized is a single
118
      image inputs, preventing effective detection of temporal information. Alternatively, video-based 3D
119   human pose estimation largely utilize two-stage methods of lifting 3D coordinates after generat-
120   ing 2D coordinates with off-the-shelf 2D pose estimators, offering compatibility with any 2D pose
121   estimation method. For instance, Skeletal graph neural networks (SGNN) (Zeng et al., 2021) use
122   off-the-shelf 2D key point detectors (Newell et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2018) to obtain the 2D poses
123   needed to derive 3D human poses. Despite improved performance over previous models, SGNN
124   yet lacks spatial depth perception of objects in a scene, which is addressed by PoseFormer (Zheng
125   et al., 2021) using a spatial-temporal transformer structure. That said, PoseFormer is constrained in
126   learning 2D-to-3D spatial and temporal correlations, and requires more training data than CNNs.
127   MHFormer (Li et al., 2022) addresses the optimization constraints of PoseFormer by synthesizing
128   an ultimate pose from learning spatio-temporal representations multiple plausible pose hypotheses.
129   However, MHFormer requires a large high-quality data to maintain high performance, which P-
130   STMO (Shan et al., 2022) addresses with a self-supervised pre-training method, but is ultimately
131   constrained by the quadratic growth of its computational cost as the number of video sequences
132   increases, given its many-to-one methodology. Most recently, STCFormer (Tang et al., 2023),KTP-
133
      Former (Peng et al., 2024) and FinePOSE (Xu et al., 2024) presents a spatio-temporal criss-cross
      attention block by decomposing correlation learning across space and time to increase performance
134
      of pose estimation. KTPFormer (Peng et al., 2024) introduces a Kinematics and Trajectory Prior
135
      Knowledge-Enhanced Transformer that utilizes Kinematics Prior Attention (KPA) and Trajectory
136   Prior Attention (TPA) to improve 3D human pose estimation by effectively modeling spatial and
137   temporal correlations through informed Q, K, and V vectors. Its lightweight design allows for inte-
138   gration into various transformer architectures with minimal computational overhead. FinePOSE (Xu
139   et al., 2024) presents a Fine-Grained Prompt-Driven Denoiser that enhances 3D human pose esti-
140   mation by coupling anatomical knowledge with prompts to improve denoising quality across three
141   core blocks. This approach not only excels in single-human pose estimation but is also extendable to
142   multi-human scenarios, demonstrating significant performance improvements. Nonetheless, STC-
143   Former (Tang et al., 2023), KTPFormer (Peng et al., 2024) and FinePOSE (Xu et al., 2024) is limited
144
      by the quality of 2D pose estimators, as it is a two-stage method.

      2.2     DATASET C ONSTRAINTS
147
148
      2.2.1    3D H UMAN P OSE E STIMATION DATASETS
149
150   3D datasets for pose estimation are difficult to generate, as motion capture systems must be used to
151   generate accurate 3D coordinates as ground truth. However, mocap-generated datasets ultimately
152   cannot contain data in the wild. Recent developments have seen novel approaches to estimate ground
153   truth data using algorithms, which made 3D human pose datasets easier to make, but ground truth
154   of such datasets tend to be less accurate, posing new problems for training.
155
      Human3.6M (Ionescu et al., 2014) is the first ever large-scale dataset that uses motion capture equip-
156   ment to track accurate 3D coordinates while a number of actors performing different daily life
157   movements. MADS (Zhang et al., 2017), developed by City University of Hong Kong, uses the
158   same approach as Human3.6M in a smaller scale and includes movements in martial arts, dancing
159   and sports. AIST Dance++ (Tsuchida et al., 2019) is a recent dataset with high-definition recording
160   of dancing of multiple genres. It differs from the earlier two datasets by being marker-free, meaning
161   algorithms are used for ground truth. MPI INF 3DHP (Mehta et al., 2017) is also a 3D marker-based
      dataset as an extension of the classic 2D dataset MPII.

      2.2.2   E XISTING DATASET L IMITATIONS
163
164   Existing 3D human pose datasets lack in scale and diversity. Firstly, the performance of the vi-
165   sion transformers is constrained by the limited number of frames in the datasets. For instance,
166   AIST Dance ++ (Tsuchida et al., 2019)) is 2.4 times larger than Human3.6M (Ionescu et al., 2014),
167   but it still only has 12,760 videos. Apart from data size limitations, existing 3D human pose
168   datasets are lacking in diversity across camera parameters, lighting conditions, human shapes and
169
      actions, negatively impacting in-the-wild applications. Most existing 3D human pose datasets are
      staged in a studio with fixed lighting, background, and the same set of actors. For instance, AIST
170
      Dance++ (Tsuchida et al., 2019) has 10 dance genres and 30 dancers.
171
172
      2.2.3   DATA AUGMENTATION M ETHODOLOGIES
173
174   Recent work (Sárándi et al., 2023; Tsuchida et al., 2019) has developed novel data augmentation
175   methodologies to address data diversity limitations of existing 3D human pose estimation datasets,
176   namely by scaling dataset size by standardizing different datasets to feed into one training pro-
177   cess. (Sárándi et al., 2023; Rapczyński et al., 2021). Three such data augmentation precedents
178   are observed. The first involves using handcrafted rules in skeletal joints to manually harmonize
179
      differences between datasets (HumanEva-I, Human3.6M, and Panoptic Studio) into one combined
      dataset (Rapczyński et al., 2021). However, handcrafted rules are susceptible to errors and similar
180
      manual configurations are required to apply such a methodology onto other datasets. A second ap-
181
      proach (Wang et al., 2020) is to standardize reference systems based on the relative rotation between
182   camera viewing direction and the orientation of the torso. However, this approach is vulnerable
183   to errors during conversion from camera to global coordinate systems. A third method of dataset
184   augmentation merges dozens of datasets into one training process with a latent key point set serving
185   as ground truth (Sárándi et al., 2023). Such a learned latent key point set, however, leads to data
186   imbalance and is further constrained in performance by the complexity of a given task.
187

      3   3D AUG M OTION T OOLKIT: DATASET AUGMENTATION M ETHODOLOGY
189
190
      Acknowledging the lack of diversity and in-the-wild data in existing 3D human pose datasets, we
191
      introduce the 3D AugMotion Toolkit, a data augmentation methodology to merge existing 3D human
192   pose datasets into a single dataset with the highest number of frames and diversity to date. The
193   current work applies the augmentation methodology on four frequently utilized datasets, namely
194   Human3.6M (Ionescu et al., 2014), MADS (Zhang et al., 2017), AIST Dance++ (Tsuchida et al.,
195   2019) and MPI INF 3DHP (Mehta et al., 2017). That said, the 3D AugMotion Toolkit is applicable
196   to any 3D human pose estimation dataset.
197
      It is essential for all datasets to be projected onto a universal coordinate system to be properly used
198   by models as ground truth data. Naturally, the model would be unable to minimize loss if a single
199   key point could have multiple coordinates. Therefore, the first challenge is to address discrepancies
200   between datasets’ reference systems as each dataset maintains its own coordinate system. That
201   is, ground truth data of each dataset comes with unique camera-configured coordinates and global
202   coordinates, respectively.
203
      As 3D human pose datasets are typically captured with multi-camera studio set-ups, the perspective
204   and configurations of each camera dictate its coordinate system. Naturally, each camera maintains
205   its own unique camera-specific coordinate system. Most datasets (Ionescu et al., 2014; Zhang et al.,
206   2017; Tsuchida et al., 2019; Mehta et al., 2017) compute translation and rotation matrix to standard-
207   ize the coordinates of each camera within the multi-camera setup onto a global coordinate system.
208   However, global coordinate systems of 3D human pose datasets are not consistent with each other,
209   meaning they require standardization to locate the same key point with the same coordinates.
210   Global reference systems within the same dataset, however, are also susceptible to errors. For in-
211   stance, coordinates for the same frame of movement within the Human3.6M dataset are taken from
212   different camera perspectives that yield misaligned and non-overlapping key point representations of
213   a subject when converted to global coordinates. The four key point skeletons in Fig. 1 represent the
214   same pose from the same subject taken from multiple perspectives, but each are clearly misaligned
215   when converted to global 3D coordinate systems.The lack of overlapping alignment suggests a need
      for a standard to universalize all camera reference systems with key frames.

340   4.2   H EAT P OSE : 3D G AUSSIAN HEATMAP
341
342   We propose a HeatPose, a 3D heatmap based on Gaussian mixture model (GMM) (McLachlan &
343   Rathnayake, 2014). Although conventional GMMs do not factor weights into its various Gaussian
344
      distributions, we adapted GMM in HeatPose to represent varying degrees of probabilistic proximity
      to the ground truth of a given target key point across different weights of Gaussian distributions. That
345
      is, we generated Gaussian distributions for each key point, each of which are evaluated for closeness
346
      to the ground truth. The maximum value of a given Gaussian distribution would be the actual ground
347   truth positioning of its corresponding target key point. We refer to this target-based distribution
348   as the main 3D Gaussian Distribution, and it is the primary mechanism of HeatPose. However,
349   HeatPose is also supplemented by factoring information regarding key points that are kinematically
350   adjacent from a given target key point (e.g., direction, distance), which we represent with a finite
351   number of target-adjacent distributions that we refer to as the side 3D Gaussian Distributions.
352
      Side 3D Gaussian distribution may be understood by considering the neck key point as a given
353   target key point, as seen in 4 (A). In this example, the neck key point is kinematically adjacent to
354   the key points of the shoulder, head, and pubis Fig. 4 (A). The application of kinematically adjacent
355   key points in HeatPose serves to reflect closer-to-reality distributions as the probability of a key
356   point is affected by key points nearby. As seen in Fig. 3, we present a comparison of conventional
357   3D heatmaps without kinematic information (left) and HeatPose with application of kinematically
358   adjacent keypoints (right). Fig. 3 illustrates the distinction between the application of kinematically
359   adjacent key points and conventional 3D heatmaps without such kinematic information.

      5.3     C OMPARISON WITH THE S TATE - OF - THE - ART
487
488   We compare the proposed model with the best-performing SOTA methods, P-STMO (Shan et al.,
489   2022), STCFormer (Tang et al., 2023), KTPFormer (Peng et al., 2024) and FinePOSE (Xu et al.,
490   2024), which are pre-trained on the Human3.6M training dataset. We test all methods on our
491   Human7.1M testing dataset as well as on the Human3.6M Testing dataset, which do not overlap.
492   P-STMO, STCFormer, KTPFormer and FinePOSE are two-stage methods that choose CPN (Cas-
493   caded Pyramid Network) (Chen et al., 2018) to generate 2D coordinates as second-stage input, and
494   2D ground truth as input to test model’s performance. We evaluate these two models with CPN-
495
      generated 2D estimates or 2D ground truth as input, respectively. 2D ground truth as input gives the
      comparative models an unfair advantage because it provides additional information unavailable to
496
      the proposed one-stage method. Further, it is impossible for any pose estimation model to obtain 2D
497
      ground truth when applied on real-world in-the-wild data. As such, we mainly compare our model
498   against performance with CPN estimates as input, but we include GT performance for reference.
499
500   As shown in Table 2, our SoloPose achieves the highest performance of MPJPE and P-MPJPE on the
501
      Human7.1M testing dataset. Even when compared to SOTA methods with ground truth, our results
      of MPJPE and P-MPJPE are still 14.9% and 21.8% lower than the best-performing FinePOSE.
502
      When evaluated on the Human3.6M testing dataset, our results of MPJPE and P-MPJPE are 22.7%
503
      and 21.9% lower than FinePOSE with CPN as input.

506   5.4     A BLATION S TUDY
507
508
      We designed two ablation studies to test the contributions of the proposed 3D kinematically adjacent
      heatmap (HeatPose) and data augmentation methodology (AugMotion) against SoloPose.
509
510
511   5.4.1    A NALYSIS WITHOUT 3D G AUSSIAN H EATMAP
512
      The first ablation study removes HeatPose and utilizes the traditional MSE loss function to train our
513
      proposed model. As shown in the second section of Table 2, the results of MPJPE and P-MPJPE on
514
      Human3.6M testing dataset are 15.3% and 27.2% higher than that of our SoloPose with HeatPose
515   respectively, but it is 3.9% and 3.3% lower than FinePOSE with CPN, which means our data quality
516   improvement makes the biggest contribution for the results and good training data can improve the
517   performance higher than SOTA models.
518
519
      5.4.2    A NALYSIS WITHOUT DATA AUGMENTATION
520
521   The second ablation study trains the model only on Human3.6M, in the mold of P-STMO (Shan
522   et al., 2022) and STCFormer (Tang et al., 2023). Our results of MPJPE and P-MPJPE are still 3.9%
523   and 5.9% lower than the two SOTA methods on the Human3.6M testing dataset, which demon-
524   strates that our SoloPose model is more effective than current SOTA methods. When tested on the
525   Human3.6M testing dataset, the second ablation study’s MPJPE result increases by 12.9 as opposed
526   to the increase of 4.7 observed with the first ablation study, thus demonstrating that our proposed
527
      data augmentation methodology (AugMotion) improves 3D human pose estimation performance by
      efficiently enhancing data quality and diversity.

530   6     CONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we introduced SoloPose, a one-stage, many-to-many spatio-temporal transformer net-
533
      work for video-based 3D human pose estimation. To address limitations of high-quality 3D human
534   pose estimation datasets, we proposed the 3D AugMotion ToolKit, a novel dataset augmentation
535   methodology by projecting existing datasets onto a universal coordinate system. Further, we pro-
536   posed HeatPose, a 3D kinematically adjacent heatmap that provide greater probabilistic key point
537   information compared with conventional 3D heatmaps. As a result, we demonstrate our SoloPose
538   model’s improved performance over existing SOTA models for 3D human pose estimation in both
539   experimental evaluation and ablation. In future work, we intend to extend the model onto 3D multi-
      person pose estimation and add more dataset to improve the performance.
