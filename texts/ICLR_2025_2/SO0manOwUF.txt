009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have gained increasing popular-
012            ity as a promising framework for leveraging the strong language reasoning capa-
013            bilities in the vision-language domain. Given a wide range of MLLMs, model
014            merging potentially offers a cheap way to aggregate their diverse knowledge into
015            a single MLLM. However, directly plug-in existing model merging approaches
016            often leads to suboptimal performance due to (1) inclusion of harmful models
017            that have over-confident predictions in the target task; (2) the lack of special-
018
               ized designs for vision-language inputs. To tackle these pain points, we con-
               duct pioneering investigations to dissect the merging procedures and propose an
019
               uncertainty-guided MLLM merging algorithm, i.e., UQ-Merge, which i) identi-
020
               fies beneficial candidates for merging, ii) determines the merging order and the
021            number of helpful candidates, and iii) performs appropriate merging. Within our
022            framework, we consider uncertainty quantification on both text and vision inputs
023            to examine the MLLM prediction confidence, and then decide whether and when
024            a MLLM needs to be included. It is worth mentioning that our vision-language
025            uncertainty quantification does not require access to sample labels, making it more
026            practical in various scenarios. Extensive experiments consistently demonstrate the
027            superior MLLM merging performance of UQ-Merge in both held-in and held-
028            out vision-language benchmarks. For example, compared to existing state-of-
029
               the-art merging methods, UQ-Merge brings substantial performance improve-
               ments of up to 44.3% on average accuracy in 12 datasets. Codes are available at
030
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/UQ-Merge-7CD7.

061   Despite the performance gain, applying single-modal merging methods on MLLM merging has
062   limitations. Firstly, existing merging methods assume that all models are beneficial for merging
063   performance. As pointed out by (Zhao et al., 2024) and observed in Figure 1, this assumption
064
      may not hold true in real-world scenarios where models to merge are trained on divergent datasets.
      Some models produce over-confident predictions on target tasks and merging them will result in a
065
      performance decrease of the merged model. Secondly, these merging methods are designed solely to
066
      focus on single-modal model merging. Given these limitations, an ideal MLLM merge mechanism
067   should be selective and aware of multimodal inputs.
068
069   To address these challenges, we propose UQ-Merge, an uncertainty quantification guided MLLM
070
      merging algorithm that features vision-language optimized design to ameliorate performance degra-
      dation caused by merging over-confident models. Specifically, UQ-Merge ❶ uses image-text
071
      perturbation-based uncertainty quantification (UQ) to evaluate models, and ❷ sorts models by the
072
      descending order of uncertainty to reduce the impact of over-confident models. ❸ UQ-Merge in-
073   crementally enlarge the group of models to merge, and ❹ return the merged model with the lowest
074   uncertainty. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
075
076          • Due to the inclusion of over-confident models and the lack of vision-language specific de-
077            signs, directly applying single-modal merging methods results in suboptimal performance.
078            To resolve these issues, we conduct pioneering work in the MLLM field.
079          • To investigate design factors influencing MLLM merging performance, we raise and an-
080            swer research questions: What is a more effective metric for selecting helpful models?
081            How to decide the merging order and select models? How to implement UQ for MLLM?
082            And how to appropriately merge selected models?
083          • We propose an MLLM-tailored image-text perturbation-based uncertainty quantification
084            method and introduce UQ-Merge, an uncertainty guided MLLM merging method that
085            could identify and exclude over-confident models.
086          • Experiments demonstrate that UQ-Merge consistently outperforms single-modal merging
087            methods. With the same number of models used for merging, UQ-Merge achieves an av-
088            erage accuracy improvement of 2.62% on held-in datasets and 1.06% on held-out datasets
089            compared to existing merging methods. Furthermore, UQ-Merge can surpass single-
090            modal merging methods that have access to more models, by 0.54% on held-in datasets

               and 1.3% on held-out datasets.

093   2   R ELATED W ORK
094
095   Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs). Large Language Models (LLMs) have demon-
096   strated strong reasoning and instruction-following capability (Zhao et al., 2023; Touvron et al.,
097   2023a;b). In light of this, many works (Alayrac et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024b;a; McKinzie et al.,
098   2024; Tong et al., 2024; Xue et al., 2024) propose to further incorporate pre-trained vision back-
099
      bones (Radford et al., 2021; Zhai et al., 2023) to enable visual perception capabilities in existing
      LLMs, producing Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs). These models extend the power-
100
      ful capabilities of LLMs into the domain of visual comprehension and reasoning. The mainstream
101
      architecture of MLLMs generally consists of three components: a vision encoder that extracts fea-
102   tures from visual inputs, a modality adapter that projects the outputs of the vision encoder into the
103   token embedding space of the LLM backbone, and an LLM backbone that processes both image and
104   text inputs to generate responses (Yin et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a). Modality adapter imple-
105   mentations include projection-based, query-based, and fusion-based variants (Zhao et al., 2023; Li
106   et al., 2023b; Radford et al., 2021; Alayrac et al., 2022). The typical training process of an MLLM
107   involves two stages: pre-training and instruction tuning. During the pre-training stage, the vision
      encoder and the LLM are kept frozen, while the adapter is trained on a large corpus of image-text


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      pairs. The objective of this stage is to train the adapter so that visual tokens can be effectively em-
109   bedded into the language token space. Following pre-training, visual instruction tuning (Liu et al.,
110   2024b;a) is conducted using instruction-following examples from diverse vision question answering
111   (VQA) tasks. This step aims to improve the model’s ability to follow instructions in VQA or image
112   captioning scenarios. Given individual MLLMs, it remains under-explored how to leverage these
113   mdoels and aggregate their knowledge. Motivated by this, we propose UQ-Merge, a UQ-based
114   MLLM merging method to incorporate models fine-tuned on different tasks.
115
      Model Merging. Model Merging (Ainsworth et al., 2023) combines multiple pre-trained or fine-
116
      tuned models into a unified, powerful model, leveraging the strengths of specialized models while
117
      maintaining versatility without requiring additional training. Early zero-shot merging methods, such
118   as weight averaging and Linear Mode Connectivity (Nagarajan & Kolter, 2021; Wortsman et al.,
119   2022), laid the foundation for this approach. Task Arithmetic Ilharco et al. (2023) manipulates
120   task vectors for effective merging, while TIES (Yadav et al., 2023) addresses parameter interference
121   through trimming and conflict resolution. DARE (Yu et al., 2024) selectively optimizes parameters
122   to enhance merging without extra training, utilizing the geometric properties of weights (Shoemake,
123   1985; Jang et al., 2024). In the latest works, DELLA merges models by pruning and re-scaling
124   weights based on their magnitude (Deep et al., 2024), and Model Stock finds the optimal inter-
125   polation ratio between merging candidates, using a pre-trained model to identify a robust anchor
126
      point (Jang et al., 2024). In the multimodal domain, model merging has similarly proven its ability
      to transform modality-specific models into modality-agnostic models (Sung et al., 2023). These
127
      existing merging studies motivate us to explore model merging in the MLLM domain.
128
129   Uncertainty Quantification (UQ). Uncertainty quantification (UQ) in predictions from deep neu-
130   ral networks (DNNs) has been a longstanding and essential problem (Abdar et al., 2021; Gaw-
131   likowski et al., 2023). The sources of uncertainty can be categorized into data uncertainty (aleatoric
132   uncertainty) and model uncertainty (epistemic uncertainty). Broadly, UQ methods can be catego-
133   rized into four groups (Gawlikowski et al., 2023): single-inference deterministic methods (Nandy
134   et al., 2020; Oala et al., 2020; Sensoy et al., 2018), Bayesian neural network (BNN) methods (Gal &
135
      Ghahramani, 2016; Loquercio et al., 2020), ensemble-based methods (Rahaman et al., 2021; Lak-
      shminarayanan et al., 2017) and test-time augmentation methods (Ayhan & Berens, 2018; Ashukha
136
      et al., 2020). For UQ in LLMs, Sampling with Perturbation for UQ (SPUQ) (Gao et al., 2024) is a
137
      test-time augmentation method that generates a set of perturbed prompts and quantifies uncertainty
138   based on the similarity between the responses. In the MLLM domain UQ is less explored. One
139   recent work applies conformal prediction (CP) for UQ in MLLMs (Ye et al., 2024; Kostumov et al.,
140   2024). However, the CP method requires labeled data to estimate the model’s uncertainty, which
141   is infeasible in many real-world applications due to the lack of ground truth. In (Daheim et al.,
142   2023), the authors propose to utilize gradient-based UQ to mitigate mismatches of gradients when
143   merging models trained on various tasks. However, it still requires labels to compute the gradients
144   and needed Hessian matrices To address this, we propose a vision-language perturbation-based UQ
145   method for MLLM that does not require labels.

151   The Architecture Overview of Multimodal Large Language Model. The definition of Multi-
152   modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) is LLM-based models with the ability to receive, reason,
153   and output with multimodal information (Yin et al., 2023). Prior to MLLMs, many works were
154   devoted to multimodality learning (Radford et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021). In this
155
      paper, we focus on MLLMs that process image-text inputs and use (xv , xt ) to represent an input
      image xv and text xt pair to an MLLM M(·, ·). The most common MLLM architecture for image-
156
      text inputs (Liu et al., 2024b;a; Chen et al., 2024; McKinzie et al., 2024; Tong et al., 2024) typically
157
      comprises a pre-trained vision encoder V (·), an adapter A (·) and an LLM backbone F (·). An
158   overview of the model architecture is provided in Figure 2 (a). The text input xt is split into textual
159   tokens ht . The vision encoder extracts visual features from the input image xv , represented as visual
160   tokens zv = V (xv ), which are then mapped by the adapter into the embedding space of language
161   tokens, yielding hv = A (zv ). The LLM processes both visual tokens hv and language tokens ht
      to generate an output F (hv , ht ) to the textual query.

      3.2 UQ-M E R G E : U NCERTAINTY Q UANTIFICATION (UQ) G UIDED MLLM M ERGING
193
      To overcome the aforementioned challenges of over-confident merging candidate models and the
194   lack of vision-language oriented merging method, we propose UQ-Merge, which consists of a
195   vision-language perturbation-based MLLM uncertainty quantification (Section 3.3) to evaluate mod-
196   els, and a merging algorithm based on the uncertainty of models (Section 3.4). The procedure of
197   UQ-Merge is described in Figure 2 (c). First, UQ is applied to MLLMs to quantify their uncertainty,
198   and models are sorted in descending order of uncertainty to later consider potentially over-confident
199   models. Then, UQ-Merge incrementally merge the sorted models and record the uncertainty scores
200   of the merged model at each step. Finally, the merged model with the lowest uncertainty during the
201   process is selected as the final output. Throughout the process, UQ-Merge adopts the same UQ
202
      function described in Section 3.3.

      4.2   UQ-M E R G E I S E FFECTIVE FOR R EMOVING H ARMFUL M ODELS
304
305
      In this section, we compare our UQ-Merge against various single-modal merging methods on held-
306
      in and held-out datasets to show the effectiveness of UQ-Merge in excluding harmful models.
307   For baseline merging methods, we evaluate them by measuring the average performance of their
308   merged models. For all baseline methods, each time the merged model is produced by merg-
309   ing a random model selection from all models we fine-tuned, and the number of models selected
310   each time is the same as the selection of our method UQ-Merge. Evaluation results are summa-
311   rized in Table 13 and Figure 3. From the results, the following observations can be drawn: ❶ Our
312   UQ-Merge demonstrates superior performance compared to all other merging methods. Specifi-
313   cally, UQ-Merge achieves 2.62% ∼ 44.3% and 1.06% ∼ 43.18% improvement on average accu-
314   racy of held-in and held-out datasets. In fact, the performance of UQ-Merge even surpasses the
315
      maximum value among all baseline methods, as shown in Figure 3. This validates the effectiveness
      of UQ-Merge in model selection to exclude over-confident models. ❷ On the held-in dataset, which
316
      is used for uncertainty quantification, UQ-Merge obtains a more significant performance gain com-
317
      pared to held-out datasets, with 4 out of 5 highest accuracy. This justifies our practice of using
318   UQ to perform model selection, as in real-world applications labels are usually unavailable and UQ
319   only relies on input to evaluate a model, and UQ-guided model selection can effectively improve
320   the performance on these applications and even generalize to held-out datasets. ❸ The performance
321   of single-modal merging methods varies a lot in MLLM merging. The gap of average accuracy for

360       We further compare UQ-Merge in a more challenging setting, where baseline merging methods
361       have an “unfair” advantage to access all the knowledge within models and merge. Experiment results
362       show that ❶ Compared to baseline methods, UQ-Merge still achieves the best average accuracy on

          both held-in and held-out datasets, surpassing these single-modal methods by 0.54% ∼ 51.69% and

364
          portions of all models, all merging methods except DARE and Task Arithmetic enjoy performance
365
          increase by adding more models. This suggests the benefit of incorporating more models from
366       diverse tasks to build a stronger model and supports our claim that model merging is a cheap way to
367       aggregate knowledge from different models. It is worth noting that existing single-modal merging
368       methods have a certain ability to resolve potentially harmful models when merging, by adopting
369       model weight level manipulation to resolve weight conflict and preserve knowledge from different
370       tasks (Ilharco et al., 2023; Yadav et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024). However, these methods are limited
371       to single-modal model merging. Our UQ-Merge is orthogonal to these works, as we consider
372       model level removal of harmful over-confident models in MLLM merging situations to improve the
373       performance, and can benefit from the techniques to ameliorate weight conflict.
