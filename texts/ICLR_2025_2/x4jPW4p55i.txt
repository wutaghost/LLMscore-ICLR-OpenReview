009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we address the challenge of learning high-dimensional Gaussian
012            mixture models (GMMs), with a specific focus on estimating both the model or-
013            der and the mixing distribution from i.i.d. samples. We propose a novel algorithm
014            that achieves linear complexity relative to the sample size n, significantly im-
015            proving computational efficiency. Unlike traditional methods, such as the method
016
               of moments or maximum likelihood estimation, our algorithm leverages Fourier
               measurements from the samples, facilitating simultaneous estimation of both the
017
               model order and the mixing distribution. The difficulty of the learning problem
018
               can be quantified by the separation distance
                                                            ∆ and minimal    mixing weight wmin .
019
020
               For stable estimation, a sample size of Ω w2 ∆4K−4 is required for the model
                                                                 1
                                                             min

021            order, while Ω w2 ∆4K−2 is necessary for the mixing distribution. This high-
                                       1
                                   min
022
               lights the distinct sample complexities for the two tasks. For D-dimensional mix-
023            ture models, we propose a PCA-based approach to reduce the dimension, reducing
024            the algorithm’s complexity to O(nD2 ), with potential further reductions through
025            random projections. Numerical experiments demonstrate the efficiency and ac-
026            curacy compared with the EM algorithm. In particular, we observe a clear phase
027            transition in determining the model order, as our method outperforms traditional
028            information criteria. Additionally, our framework is flexible and can be extended
029            to learning mixtures of other distributions, such as Cauchy or exponential distri-
030            butions.

036   The Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) is a widely used statistical model that has found numerous
037   applications in various fields, including machine learning, pattern recognition, data clustering, and
038   image processing. It is a powerful tool for modeling complex data and signals originating from
039   sub-populations or distinct sources. The GMM represents a probability distribution as a weighted
040   sum of Gaussian components, each characterized by its mean and covariance matrix. Formally, each
041   observation of the GMM follows:

046   matrix of the i-th component are denoted as µi and Σi , respectively. For each sample x, we can
047   introduce a latent variable z ∈ {1, · · · , K}, with the marginal distribution of z specified by the
048   mixing weights:

107   information-theoretic measures have been proposed. These include the Akaike Information Cri-
      terion (AIC, Akaike (1998)) and Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC, Schwarz (1978)). These

518   5   C ONCLUSIONS AND D ISCUSSIONS
519
520   Learning Gaussian mixture models is a challenging task, particularly in high dimensions or when the
521   number of components is large or unknown. The performance of the learning algorithms depends
522
      on the separation distance and minimal weight of the components. In this paper, we proposed an
      efficient algorithm for estimating the model order and mixing distribution of the high-dimensional
523
      GMMs. Our algorithm leverages the Fourier measurement of the samples, drawing a natural connec-
524
      tion to line spectral estimation and super-resolution techniques. We have established the sampling
525   complexities for estimating the model order and mixing distributions in relation to the separation
526   distance and minimal weight. Additionally, we demonstrated that the computational complexity for
527   learning high-dimensional mixtures can be further reduced using dimension reduction techniques
528   such as PCA. Empirical results confirmed that our algorithm achieves efficiency and accuracy com-
529   parable to, or better than, the EM algorithm.
530
      We also acknowledge some aspects of our approach that present opportunities for future improve-
531   ment. While our algorithm assumes that the unified covariance matrix Σ is known a priori, there are
532   scenarios where this may not be the case. To enhance the versatility of our method, estimating the
533   covariance matrix using Fourier measurements, as explored in the 1-D algorithm in Liu & Zhang
534   (2024), could be a promising direction. Additionally, while the time complexity remains quadratic
535   with respect to dimensionality, this opens avenues for further research. Employing random pro-
536   jections that preserve pairwise distances between components, such as the Johnson–Lindenstrauss
537   embedding (see Sanjeev & Kannan (2001)), could be an effective way to address this challenge. We
538   will exploring these possibilities in future work.
