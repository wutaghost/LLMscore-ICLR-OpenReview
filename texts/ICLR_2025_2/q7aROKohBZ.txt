008                                                A BSTRACT
009
010            Event sequences (ESs) arise in many practical domains, including finance, retail,
011            social networks, and healthcare. In machine learning, event sequences are a spe-
012
               cial type of tabular data with annotated timestamps. Despite the importance of ESs
               modeling and analysis, little effort was made in adapting large language models
013
               (LLMs) to the ESs domain. In this paper, we highlight the common difficulties of
014
               ESs processing and propose a novel solution capable of solving multiple down-
015            stream tasks with little or no finetuning. In particular, we solve the problem of
016            working with long sequences and improve time and numeric feature processing.
017            The resulting method, called ESQA, effectively utilizes the power of LLMs and,
018            according to extensive experiments, achieves state-of-the-art results in the ESs
019            domain.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Temporal data often comes in the form of event sequences, where each event is characterized by
025   the arrival time and additional structured data. This type of data is widely spread in domains like
026   geoscience Bergen et al. (2019), healthcare Esteva et al. (2019), sociology Hossain et al. (2020),
027
      industry Choi et al. (2021), e-commerce Ni et al. (2018) and finance Babaev et al. (2022). Event
      sequences combine properties of time series and tabular data while having major differences. Unlike
028
      time series, events can arrive with irregular time steps and have structured annotations, similar to tab-
029
      ular datasets. Unlike tabular data, events have timestamps and associated orders. These differences
030   require special data processing, modeling, and inference approaches.
031
032   The new frontier in machine learning, especially deep learning, focuses on adapting large language
033
      models (LLMs) to domains beyond language. The reasons behind this adaptation are that LLMs
      can use additional information not found in domain-specific data, process the textual context of
034
      the underlying task, generate answers in a free natural form, argue their decisions, and support
035
      dialog with the user. The potential benefits of using LLMs include improved modeling quality
036   and generalization. The latter means that the hybrid model can solve new problems with little or
037   no finetuning, which largely increases the model’s applicability and reduces development costs.
038   Successful applications of LLMs were demonstrated in both time series Cai et al. (2023) and tabular
039   datasets Dinh et al. (2022), but no effort was made to adapt LLMs to event sequences: financial
040   transactions, electronic health records, activity on different devices, etc. These data characterize
041   human life and are used to personalize many AI services across different domains.
042   Event sequence processing with LLM encounters several difficulties. First, structured data must be
043   effectively encoded at the LLM’s input. Textual representation considerably increases the sequence
044   length and can’t be effectively processed by modern Transformer models due to the quadratic com-
045   plexity. Second, the desired method must be capable of processing long input sequences, even when
046   the downstream tasks require historical data analysis. The problem is similar to the first but focuses
047   on the model architecture rather than input processing. Finally, time features and the order must
048   be properly provided to the model, as they constitute the essence of event sequences and include
049   important information for solving downstream tasks.
050   In this paper, we propose a new neural architecture called ESQA that exploits the power of LLMs
051   to model event sequences and solve associated practical tasks. In particular, we, for the first time,
052   develop a question-answering approach with LLM backbone in the event sequences domain. We
053   show the proposed model can solve multiple downstream tasks without finetuning. When finetuned,
      ESQA outperforms other methods and achieves a new state-of-the-art.

070
      Temporal Point Processes (TPPs) and their marked variants (MTPPs) are the simplest forms of
071   event sequences. Previous research was focused on accurate next-event prediction with or without
072   neural networks Liniger (2009); Mei & Eisner (2017); Xue et al. (2023). Another branch of research
073   addressed event streams of the general form Padhi et al. (2021); Babaev et al. (2022); McDermott
074   et al. (2024). To the best of our knowledge, question answering with LLMs was not previously
075   applied to TPPs or event sequence modeling.
076
      LLMs for Tabular Data. Large Language Models (LLMs) are a family of neural architectures
077   pretrained on a large corpus of texts. LLMs accept inputs in the form of text and generate textual
078   output. In practice, LLM architecture is composed of three main blocks. The first is an embedding
079   layer that converts input text to a sequence of numeric vectors called embeddings. The second
080   block, the backbone, transforms input embeddings to the output embeddings sequence with possibly
081   different lengths. The final part of the model maps embeddings to the output text.
082
      There are two main approaches for encoding tabular data at the input of LLM. The first one is
083   to provide a description of each table field in the textual form Dinh et al. (2022). This approach
084   suffers from little flexibility and extremely long input sequences. The second approach is to replace
085   the embedding layer, with a newly designed module capable of directly encoding table fields to
086   embeddings with the required number of features. The latter approach is also known as embedding
087   injection and usually achieves better results Koh et al. (2023); Huang et al. (2023).
088
      In our approach, we endeavor to rethink the best practices for making embeddings from categorical
089   and numeric features from tabular neural networks Yin et al. (2020); Iida et al. (2021); Padhi et al.
090   (2021); Hegselmann et al. (2022); Yang et al. (2022); Dinh et al. (2022). At the same time, event
091   sequences require analysis of multiple events simultaneously, while tabular datasets can be processed
092   one row at a time. To this end, ESQA applies the encoding method and adapts Q-Former, which is
093   not seen in tabular neural networks.
094
      LLMs for time series. Previous works used LLMs in the context of time series analysis Gruver
095   et al. (2023); Cai et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024). ESQA implements a novel context encoding,
096   unlike time series models, and can process complex data structures.
097
098
      Question Answering with LLMs. The popular way to solve problems with LLMs is to design
      a question such that a valid answer to this question solves the problem Dinh et al. (2022). The
099
      question must include the context, i.e. all necessary data for reasoning, and the task definition.
100
      This way, LLM input is usually composed of the context, task, and connecting words indicating the
101   boundaries of each part.

130   3.1   Q UESTIONS AND ANSWERS CONSTRUCTION
131
132   The concept behind this method is to frame all tasks involving temporally structured data as natural
                                                                   M
133   language questions and answers. Each task from {taskm }m=1 takes the form {Qm , Xm , Am },
134   where Qm is the question that defines the problem, X represents the input data, and Am is the
135   answer sought based on the target variable Ym .
136   A question Qm consists of two components: the prefix and the question body. The prefix initiates
137   the query token sequence and is placed before embeddings of other modalities. The question body
138   then describes the task in textual form. For example, determining the most frequent value of feature
139   cm is represented as: “What is the most frequent value of cm in the entire dataset?”.
140
      Given the nature of time-structured data, we classify questions into extractive and predictive. Ex-
141   tractive questions focus on tasks involving existing event sequences, such as computing statistics
142   or identifying trends and characteristics. On the other hand, predictive questions pertain to tasks
143   concerning predicting future events or attributes based on available data.
144
      Tasks and their corresponding questions can also be categorized based on the type of response
145
      sought: binary, multiple-choice, or open-ended. Binary questions seek a straightforward answer,
146
      either as Am ∈ {0, 1} or in the form of “Yes” or “No”. For instance, a question like “Is drinking
147   water the most frequently purchased product?” can be answered with a simple “Yes” or “No”.
148
149   In contrast to binary questions, multiple-choice and open-ended questions assume a specific answer
150
      corresponding to the required feature, whether numerical Am ∈ R or categorical Am ∈ |cj | =
      {cat1 , . . . , catK }. Multiple-choice questions provide a list of possible answer choices. For example,
151
      one might ask “What is the most frequently purchased product? Options: black tea; bread; drinking
152
      water; grapes.”. On the other hand, open-ended questions prompt a direct response, such as “What
153   is the name of the most frequently purchased product? Please provide the name in your response.”.

253   The output representation of the event sequence encoder grows in dimensionality as the number of
254   events in each sequence increases. This size is crucial, as it must fit within a common multimodal
255   embedding sequence, impacting the language model’s context length extension. Our goals are to
256   shorten the event sequence length without significant information loss and to adapt each event’s
257   vectorized representation to match the language model’s embedding dimension. We propose an
258   intermediate connection layer between the event sequence encoder and the LLM to achieve this. We
259   suggest using the Query Transformer model, or Q-Former Li et al. (2023), to extract features from
260
      the encoder output efficiently.
261   The Q-Former architecture (Fig. 3) includes two transformer submodules: a novel modality trans-
262   former (originally an image transformer) that works with a fixed image encoder for feature extrac-
263   tion, and a text transformer that functions as both an encoder and a decoder. A set of trainable
264   query embeddings q is the input for the novel modality transformer. These queries engage in self-
265   attention, interacting with each other and with the fixed modality features through cross-attention
266
      layers in every other transformer block.
267   In our approach, Q-Former produces q query vectors for each event sequence, which are then passed
268   to the LLM. We use a single fully connected layer to project the output query vectors into the
269   language model’s text embedding dimension. In this study, we initialize Q-Former with the weights
      from the BLIP-2 approach, derived from BLIP-2 with the FLAN-T5-xl model Li et al. (2023). The

      3.5   T RAINING PROTOCOL
274
275   As the backbone for the pre-trained LLM, our approach utilizes the FLAN-T5 family of encoder-
276   decoder models Wei et al. (2021). Any process of fine-tuning model parameters influences the
277   model’s proficiency in a specific domain and causes it to ”forget” essential general and linguistic
278   knowledge. We have frozen most of the LLM parameters to preserve this knowledge and save
279   computational resources. Studies Lu et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2023) indicate that freezing most of
280   the model’s weights often yields better results than fully fine-tuning a pre-trained LLM.
281   To efficiently select a limited set of trainable parameters, we propose using Parameter-Efficient
282   Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods. Specifically, we employed the Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) ap-
283   proach Hu et al. (2021), which keeps most of the model weights frozen while adding trainable rank
284   decomposition matrices to a subset of the parameters.
285   Similar to the conventional language model training process, the ESQA approach was trained to
286   minimise the cross-entropy loss function between the predicted probability distribution of text tokens
287   and the predefined tokens of ground-truth answers.
288
      The training of the ESQA approach to solve multiple problems is conducted in a single stage, during
289
      which the LLM weights are maintained frozen and the following components are optimised:
290
291          • categorical, numeric and temporal features embeddings;
292
             • an event sequences encoder;
293
294          • a connector as a Q-Former model;
295          • a linear layer, that is responsible for mapping queries vectors from the connector to the
296            input embedding space of LLM;
297          • decomposition matrices W A ∈ Rr×hidden size and W B ∈ Rhidden size×r for Wq and Wv
298            matrices of the self-attention and encoder-decoder attention layers.

      LLMs possess an extraordinary capacity to generalize to novel, previously unseen tasks. Our method
473
      maintains the integrity of the language model’s weights, thereby preserving its inherent capabilities.
474   Moreover, by training adaptors within the attention layers, we expand the domain of zero-shot tasks
475   from exclusively text-based tasks to those based on event sequences. Following comprehensive pre-
476   training on contextual tasks, we evaluated the ESQA approach’s adaptability to new predictive tasks.
477   The model was trained in a multi-task setting on all event features of the AlfaBattle dataset and was
478   subsequently tested in a zero-shot setting across various predictive tasks within the same dataset.
479   Table 5 compares our experimental results against statistical baselines, a text baseline, and an ESQA
480   model specifically trained on those predictive tasks.
481   For the MCC code and MCC category attributes, ESQA multi-task outperforms all baselines, indi-
482   cating its strength in handling categorical predictions. However, in the zero-shot setting, ESQA’s
483   performance is comparable to the statistical baseline, suggesting room for improvement in scenarios
484   without task-specific training. In predicting the Amount attribute, the text-only approach achieves
485   the best MAE/MSE, while ESQA multi-task shows competitive performance, demonstrating its ro-
      bustness in handling regression tasks despite not being the top performer. However, the regression

      problem on real numbers with many decimals is still a challenging task for zero-shot ESQA, which
487   performed poorly. For temporal predictions like Days before and Hour diff, ESQA multi-task sig-

489   patterns. Overall, ESQA zero-shot performance, while not leading, still provides valuable insights

      5   C ONCLUSION
494
      In this paper, we introduced Event Sequences Question Answering (ESQA), a novel approach for
495
      modelling event sequences with LLMs. Our empirical results demonstrate that our approach per-
496
      forms robustly across various datasets. For several downstream problems, ESQA performs at least as
497   well as specialized baselines (Table 3), and for the task of predicting the attributes of the next event,
498   it significantly surpasses baseline methods (Table 4). Furthermore, we have shown that ESQA can
499   handle multiple tasks simultaneously without any special fine-tuning (Table 5), highlighting its re-
500   markable ability to adapt swiftly to new tasks without the need for complex and time-consuming
501   training. These findings position ESQA as an exceptionally promising approach leveraging the
502   strong generalisation capabilities of LLM backbones for the field of event sequences. The source
503   code will be made publicly available at: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/ESQA-AD2A.
504   Limitations. Our research has certain limitations. In processing numerical features, ESQA employs
505   value discretization, which introduces an inherent discretization error. This error is significantly
506   influenced by the number of discretization buckets and the ranges of the actual feature values. To
507   mitigate this error, we conducted several additional experiments to refine the pre-processing method.
508   Furthermore, handling time features in event sequences requires special attention. We are actively
509   exploring ways to enhance temporal feature processing within ESQA. In future work, we will fo-
510   cus on implementing these improvements and addressing the challenge of dealing with unbalanced
511
      classes.
512   Ethical Statement. The purpose of this paper is to advance the field of event sequence analysis
513   by leveraging the extensive capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs). We propose that inte-
514   grating classical approaches to event sequence analysis with LLMs represents a promising research
515   direction. Such integration has the potential to significantly enhance both practical decision-making
516   and analytical intelligence in the domain.
517   While our primary focus is on contributing to the academic landscape, our research also has potential
518   societal implications, particularly in decision-making processes and user analytics across various
519   error-sensitive industries. From an ethical standpoint, the responsible and transparent application
520   of LLMs requires a thorough understanding of their strengths and limitations, acknowledging the
521   possibility of errors without intent to cause harm. At this stage of the research, we do not foresee
522   immediate social risks that warrant special attention. However, we emphasize the need to continually
523
      assess ethical considerations as this interdisciplinary field evolves and its impact on society becomes
      clearer.

689   Training a language model to understand a different modality is not a novel challenge and has been

692
      for three highly developed modalities: text, images, and audio. For text architectures, we examined
      several models including encoder-only models like BERT (base, large), encoder-decoder models
693

694

      A.2    C ONNECTOR ARCHITECTURE SELECTION
776
777
778
      Integrating multiple modalities within a single approach centered around an LLM requires mapping
779
      new modalities into a textual model. Employing a separate encoder for each modality simplifies the
780   task to finding an efficient architecture for mapping each modality’s vector space to the LLM em-
781   bedding text space. When analyzing event sequences, processing extended data sequences presents
782   challenges due to increased context length, which leads to higher computational complexity. In some
783   instances, the sequence length may surpass the maximum context length of the language model.
784
      To address these challenges, we conducted experiments to determine the optimal architecture for the
785
      connection layer between the event sequence encoder and the LLM. We evaluated several potential
786   implementations: a single linear layer, a transformer layer, and two model sizes of the Q-Former
787   architecture. Additionally, we investigated the impact of initialization on problem-solving quality
788   and training speed by initializing the Q-Former with weights from the pre-trained visual-text model
789   BLIP-2, based on FLAN-T5. In all experiments, we tackled three tasks in a multi-task mode us-
790   ing the AlfaBattle dataset. The components used in all experiments included Whisper-tiny as the
791   transaction encoder and FLAN-T5-small as the language model. Performance was measured at
792   20 epochs, with fixed batch size, learning rate, and optimization parameters. We used multi-class
793   accuracy for classification tasks and MSE for numerical response prediction tasks as target metrics.
794   The results revealed that simply increasing the number of trainable parameters does not necessarily
795   enhance task solution quality. A linear layer with a small number of parameters performed worse
796   than Q-Former-small, which also trained much faster. However, adding more simple identical blocks
797   within a single connector, such as ′ 2xLinear′ , did not significantly improve performance. On the
798   other hand, more complex blocks, such as ′ 2xT ransf ormer′ , showed substantial quality gains.
799   Increasing the model size to Q-Former-base yielded mixed results: while MCC code prediction
800
      quality improved by 2%, the metrics for MCC category prediction and numerical attribute Amount
      declined.
801
802   Additional initialization with weights from visual-text pre-training marginally improved the MCC
803   code prediction task but slightly degraded the metrics for the other two tasks. The overall impact of
804   initialization was minimal, indicating few common patterns between extracting salient information
805   from images and deriving dependencies from event sequences. This discrepancy is expected due to
806
      the lack of temporal dependence within a single image, in contrast to the strong temporal dependence
      between events in a sequence.
807
808   Therefore, we selected the Q-Former-base model without initialization, anticipating an increase in
809   the number of tasks our approach can handle simultaneously. This model offers a sufficient margin
      for increasing the complexity of future experiments.

       B.3   BASELINES IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS
994
995    Below, we provided details about the architectures and hyperparameters of the baseline approaches
996    used in our study. While selecting baselines we tried to cover wide range of approaches for handling
997    sequential data including contrastive, autoregressive, token masking and manual feature engineering
998    and cover variety in architectures such as transformer or RNN.
999
       Handcrafted features with LightGBM: This baseline aggregates numerical feature values across
1000
       buckets of categorical features and includes statistics such as count, mean, variance, minimum, and
1001   maximum. The LightGBM classifier Ke et al. (2017) is then used for prediction. Such approach is
1002   following widely used practice of hand designing features.
1003
1004
       Randomly initialised RNN encoder: This approach utilizes a randomly initialized and untrained
       RNN sequence encoder based on a unidirectional Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) with a single hidden
1005
       layer of size 1024. The resulting 1024-dimensional event sequence representations are used with
1006
       LightGBM to solve the downstream task. This is a very simple baseline which main goal is to show
1007   whether any training is helpful for this task.
1008
1009   NPPR (Next Event Prediction and Past Reconstruction): The method proposed in Skalski et al.
1010
       (2023) is based on two underlying pretraining tasks solving simultaniously 1) auto-regressive pre-
       diction of next events and 2) reconstruction of past of events with some predefined depth in time.
1011
       This method demonstrated the best result on AlfaBattle and Age datasets so we included to our
1012
       scope as a strong baseline.
1013
1014   CoLES (Contrastive Learning for Event Sequences): This method employs a self-supervised con-
1015   trastive pretraining approach called CoLES Babaev et al. (2022) to generate vector representations
1016
       of event sequences. The encoder is a recurrent neural network (RNN) GRU with one hidden layer of
       size 1024, producing final embeddings of the same size. A supervised classifier based on LightGBM
1017
       is then trained using the pretrained embeddings. This is the one the strongest baseline demonstrated
1018
       competitive quality on most task according to Babaev et al. (2022).
1019
1020   CPC (Contrastive Predictive Coding): This approach uses a similar sequence encoder architecture to
1021   CoLES but applies the Contrastive Predictive Coding (CPC) method van den Oord et al. (2018) for
1022
       pretraining. CPC is a self-supervised technique for learning vector representations using an autore-
       gressive model for non-discrete data sequences. This baseline represents common autoregressive
1023
       type of pretraining and can be challenging basiline for next item prediction tasks.
1024
1025   The next series of baselines Barlow Twins, NSP, RTD, SOP are architecturally identical to COLES
       but differ in underlying pretraining task.
