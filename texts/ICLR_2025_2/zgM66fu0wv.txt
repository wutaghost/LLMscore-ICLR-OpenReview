009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011               Causal discovery is fundamental to scientific research, yet traditional statistical
012               algorithms face significant challenges, including expensive data collection, redun-
013               dant examination of known relations, and unrealistic assumptions. Additionally,
014               while recent LLM-based methods excel at identifying commonly known causal re-
015               lations, they fall short in uncovering novel relations. We introduce IRIS (Iterative
016
                  Retrieval and Integrated System for Real-Time Causal Discovery), a novel frame-
                  work that addresses these limitations. Starting with a set of initial variables, IRIS
017
                  automatically retrieves relevant documents, extracts variable values, and organizes
018
                  data for statistical algorithms in real-time. Our hybrid causal discovery method
019               combines statistical algorithms and LLM-based methods to discover existing and
020               novel causal relations. The missing variable proposal component identifies miss-
021               ing variables, and subsequently, IRIS expands the causal graphs by including both
022               the initial and the newly suggested variables. Our approach offers a scalable and
023               adaptable solution for causal discovery, enabling the exploration of causal rela-
024               tions from a set of initial variables without requiring pre-existing datasets.1

027
028
      A fundamental task in various disciplines of science, including biology, economics and healthcare,
029   is to find underlying causal relations and make use of them Kuhn (1962). Although interventional
030   experiments are ideal for discovering causality, they are often impractical or impossible due to eth-
031   ical, financial, or logistical constraints. Consequently, researchers have developed methods to infer
032   causal relations from purely observational data Pearl (2009); Spirtes et al. (2000).
033
      Both statistical and large language model (LLM) -based causal discovery algorithms face several
034
      significant challenges that limit their applicability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios. First,
035   statistical algorithms typically require extensive sample collection, a process that can be both time-
036   consuming and expensive. As a result, many studies rely on synthetic data, potentially limiting the
037   generalizability of their findings Dong et al. (2023); Gasse et al. (2021); Korb & Nicholson (2010);
038   Binder et al. (1997). Second, statistical algorithms often redundantly examine well-established
039   causal relations, leading to inefficient use of computational resources, especially given the high
040   complexity of many causal discovery algorithms Zhang et al. (2011); Glymour et al. (2019). Third,
041   while LLM-based methods may identify well-established causal relations, they struggle to uncover
042   novel causal relations not previously documented Feng et al. (2024); Zečević et al. (2023). Lastly,
043
      most statistical algorithms operate under assumptions that rarely hold in real-world scenarios, such
      as the causal sufficiency assumption (i.e., the absence of unobservable variables in the causal graph)
044
      and acyclicity assumption (i.e., the absence of cycles in the causal graph) Pearl (2009); Neal (2020).
045
046   To address these limitations, we propose a framework, called Iterative Retrieval and Integrated
047   System (IRIS) for real-time causal discovery. IRIS begins with a set of initial variables and em-
048   ploys an automated process to collect and analyze unstructured text data in real-time, eliminating
049
      the need for pre-existing sample collection. This data is then transformed into structured format suit-
      able for statistical causal discovery methods. Our framework utilizes a hybrid approach to causal
050
      discovery, merging statistical methods with LLM-based causal relation extraction and verification
051
      techniques. This hybrid strategy allows us to leverage existing knowledge while simultaneously un-
052   covering novel causal relations. Specifically, this hybrid approach allows cycles in causal graphs,

097   2    BACKGROUND
098
099   Causal discovery focuses on uncovering causal structures within a set of variables. Given a pair of
100   variables (X, Y ), the objective is to determine whether X ← Y , Y ← X, or no causal influence
101   between them, where ← denotes causal direction. A key distinction between causal discovery and
102   relation extraction in NLP is that causal discovery reveals previously unknown causal relations,
103   whereas relation extraction focuses on transforming relations in free text into structured relational
104
      tuples.
105   Although randomized controlled trials and A/B testing are the gold standard for causal discovery
106   Fisher (1935), these experimental approaches are often impractical due to ethical or financial lim-
107   itations. Thus, researchers turn to alternative methods that rely solely on statistical analysis of
      observational data to infer causal structures.


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Statistical approaches to causal discovery can be broadly classified into two categories: constraint-
109   based methods, exemplified by Peter and Clark (PC) Spirtes et al. (2000) and inductive causation
110   (IC) Pearl (2009), and score-based methods Heckerman et al. (1995); Chickering (2002); Koivisto
111   & Sood (2004); Mooij et al. (2016). These methodologies employ statistical measures derived from
112   observational data to construct causal graphs. However, these approaches face significant challenges.
113   Firstly, they require extensive data collection, which can be resource-intensive and time-consuming.
114   Secondly, they are subject to theoretical limitations. These statistical methods cannot guarantee the
115   precise identification of ground-truth causal graphs. Instead, they typically produce an equivalence
116   class of true causal graphs Spirtes et al. (2000); Pearl (2009).
117   Furthermore, many causal discovery algorithms, such as PC and Greedy Equivalence Search (GES),
118   operate under assumptions that can limit their applicability to real-world scenarios. Causal suf-
119   ficiency assumption posits that all variables are observed and included, neglecting the potential
120   unobserved variables Neal (2020). Some algorithms, such as Tetrad condition-based Silva et al.
121   (2006); Kummerfeld & Ramsey (2016) and high-order moments-based approaches Adams et al.
122   (2021); Chen et al. (2022) focus on uncover specific types of unobserved variables, such as latent
123
      confounders (i.e., common causes). However, our work aims to identify more general unobserved
      variables, including confounders, mediators, cause or effect of observed variables. Acyclicity as-
124
      sumption states that causal graphs contain no cycles, which allows causal discovery to align with
125
      Bayesian network and simplifies mathematical challenge. However, this assumption often contra-
126   dicts real-world phenomena. Many causal processes are known to contain feedback loops, such as
127   the poverty cycle: poverty → limited access to education → low-paying jobs → poverty, Banerjee
128   & Duflo (2012); De Weiss & Sirkin (2010) and the predator-prey cycle: increase in predator pop-
129   ulation → decrease in prey population → decrease in predator population Schmitz (2017); Abrams
130   (2001). In contrast to prior work, our proposed causal discovery method relaxes these assumptions.
131   By allowing for the inclusion of unobserved variables and permitting cycles within causal graphs,
132   our approach aims to model causal relations more closely aligned with real-world scenarios.
133   The advent of LLMs provides new opportunities to address causal discovery Kıcıman et al. (2023);
134   Zečević et al. (2023); Long et al. (2022). In this approach, LLMs are prompted to determine the
135   causal direction between a given pair of variable names. However, the reliability of such methods is
136   under scrutiny. Zečević et al. (2023) argue that LLMs may function as ”causal parrots”, which de-
137   pend on memorization to recall the causal relations present in their training data rather than genuine
138   causal reasoning. This raises concerns about the models’ ability to generalize and identify causal
139   relations that are rare or absent in pre-training data. Feng et al. (2024) presents empirical evidence to
140
      support this argument, suggesting that while LLMs may excel at reproducing frequently presented
      causal relations, they may struggle to uncover novel causal relations.
141
142   In contrast to approaches that directly employ LLMs for causal discovery, Liu et al. (2024) utilize
143   LLMs as variable proposers to abstract causal variables and extract their values from collected docu-
144   ments, subsequently applying statistical methods to uncover causal relations among these variables.
145   Our work diverges from this approach by taking a set of initial variables as input and employing an
146
      automated process to collect relevant documents. Following variable value extraction, our methodol-
      ogy implements a hybrid causal discovery approach, integrating both statistical methods and relation
147
      extraction methods. Furthermore, our approach is capable of proposing additional variables that ex-
148
      hibit causal relations with the initial set, thereby enabling an iterative process of data collection
149   and causal discovery of a expanded set of variables. This iterative methodology allows for a more
150   comprehensive exploration of the causal relations surrounding the variables of interest.

153   3   M ETHODOLOGY
154
155
      In this work, we introduce a novel causal discovery framework, IRIS. Our method differs from
156   prior causal discovery algorithms in three key aspects. First, IRIS does not rely on pre-existing
157   observational data; instead, it automatically collects and extracts observational data related to the
158   initial variables. Second, our approach does not require the acyclicity assumption; we allow cycles
159   in the causal graph. Third, we do not assume causal sufficiency; our missing variable proposal
160   component is designed to suggest new variables that may have causal relations with initial variables.
161   IRIS consists of three principal components: Data Collection and Value Extraction, Hybrid Causal
      Discovery, and Missing Variable Proposal. We detail each component in the following sections.

      3.1     P ROBLEM D EFINITION
163
164   IRIS begins by taking a set of initial variables, denoted as Z = (z1 , z2 , ..., zN ), where zi represents
165   one variable. Our method automatically collects a set of unstructured textual data D relevant to these
166   initial variables. From D, our method extracts the values of these variables to assemble structured
167   data X, which serves as the input for statistical causal discovery algorithms. Then our missing vari-
168   able proposal component suggests new variables related to initial variables, resulting in an expanded
169   set of variables Zm . The final output of our method is an expanded causal graph G = (Zm , R),
170
      where R = (r1 , r2 , ..., rl ) represents the set of causal relations (edges in the graph).
171
172   3.2     DATA C OLLECTION AND VALUE E XTRACTION
173
      The initial phrase of IRIS comprises two main steps: retrieval of relevant documents and extraction
174
      of variable value. The detailed procedure is outlined in Algorithm 1 in Appendix A.4.
175
176
      Retrieval of Relevant Documents We employ the official Google API 2 to retrieve documents
177
      related to the initial variables Z. To maximize the relevance to all initial variables, we construct
178   search queries using a stepwise removal approach: 1) Begin with queries containing all variable
179   names (e.g., ”smoking” AND ”cancer” AND ”pollution”). 2) Progressively remove one variable at
180   a time (e.g., ”smoking” AND ”cancer”). 3) Conclude with single-variable queries (e.g., ”smoking”).
181   We also incorporate synonyms of variable names to enhance coverage (e.g., ”smoker” and ”tobacco”
182   for ”smoking”). For each query, we retrieve the top-k documents to ensure data diversity. To ensure
183   relevance to most variables, we require queries containing more variables yield more documents.
184   The retrieval process continues until a predefined threshold of the number of collected documents is
185   reached. The resulting document set is denoted as D = (d1 , d2 , .., dT ), where di represents a single
186
      document.
187
188
      Extraction of Variable Value The next step involves extracting variable values from the collected
      documents D to construct structured data X. We leverage LLMs for this task. Given an LLM
189
      M , we design a prompt l that incorporates one document di and the description of one variable
190
      zj . The variable description includes its name and the meaning of each value. We guide the LLM
191   to generate responses following multiple thinking steps, mimicking human expert reasoning, and
192   provide the final answer in a specific format Lin et al. (2024). This generation process can be denoted
193   as oij = M (l(di , zj )), where oij is LLM’s response regarding the value of variable zj in document
194   di . We then extract the exact value vij from this formatted response oij . By iterating through
195   all variables and documents, we populate the structured data X where each column represents a
196   variable and each row corresponds to a document. The prompt template for value extraction is
197   presented in Table 9 in Appendix A.5.
198
199   3.3     H YBRID C AUSAL D ISCOVERY
200
201   With the collected unstructured data D and structured data X, we employ a hybrid approach to
202
      causal discovery, leveraging both statistical methods and relation extraction techniques. This hybrid
      strategy allow us to leverage existing causal relation and uncover novel causal relations.
203
204
      Statistical Causal Discovery For structured data X, we employ statistical causal discovery al-
205
      gorithms such as the PC algorithm Spirtes et al. (2000), GES Chickering (2003), and NOTEARS
206
      Zheng et al. (2018). These methods determine causal structures by analyzing statistical relations
207   between variables. For instance, the PC algorithm performs conditional independence tests between
208   variable pairs, progressively expanding the conditioning sets to determine the presence of causal
209   relations. These algorithms process structured data X, which contains variables and samples, and
210   use predefined hyperparameters to produce a causal graph Gˆs as the output.
211
212   Causal Relation Extraction and Verification We introduce a complementary method inspired
213   by causal relation verification Si et al. (2024); Wadden et al. (2022). In this approach, we treat each
214   potential causal relation as a claim (e.g., ”smoking causes lung cancer”) and retrieve documents
215
         2
             https://developers.google.com/custom-search/docs/overview

216
      containing both the cause and effect terms (e.g., ”smoking” AND ”lung cancer”). To ensure the
217   trustworthiness of retrieved documents, we restrict the search domain to reputable academic reposi-
218   tories 3 . We then employ LLMs to assess whether each document supports or refutes or not relates
219   with the causal relation using a carefully designed prompt (see Table 10 in Appendix A.5). If a ma-
220   jority of documents support the causal relation, we incorporate it into a causal graph Gˆv . Otherwise,
221   this causal relation should not exist in Gˆv .
222
223   Graph Merging The two branches of our hybrid method produce two causal graphs: Gˆs from the
224
      statistical algorithms and Gˆv from the causal relation verification process. To merge these into a
225
      final causal graph Ĝ, we post-process the causal graph Gˆs by adding high-confidence verified causal
226
      relations from Gˆv and removing relations that are strongly refuted by the verification process. This
227
      merging strategy is adopted for two main reasons: firstly, the structured data X used in the statistical
228   analysis might contain noise introduced during the value extraction phase; secondly, causal relations
229   that are widely supported or refuted by the majority of documents are considered well-established
230   knowledge and are deemed trustworthy. The detailed process of our hybrid causal discovery method
231   is outlined in Algorithm 2 in Appendix A.4.

233   3.4   M ISSING VARIABLE P ROPOSAL
234
235   In this step, our goal is to identify missing variables that are not included in the initial variables but
236   may serve as confounders, mediators, causes, or effects of initial variables.
237
238   Variable Abstraction We leverage LLMs to abstract missing variables from the retrieved docu-
239   ments D. For each document, the LLMs is instructed to abstract variables that may potentially have
240   causal relations with the initial variables. The instruction involves analyzing the content of each
241   document, identifying variables that could influence or be influenced by the initial variables, and
242
      then providing the most reliable variable in a specified format. The prompt for variable abstraction
      is provided in Table 11 in Appendix A.5.
243
244
245
      Variable Selection To select the most promising variables from the abstracted variables, we em-
      ploy a dual approach combining relation extraction and statistical methods:
246
247          • Verified Relation Extraction Approach: We verify whether each abstracted variable has a
248            confirmed causal relation with any initial variable using our causal relation extraction and
249            verification method, as stated in Section 3.3. Variables that are supported by the majority
250            of the documents are subsequently added to the expanded set of variables Zm .
251
             • Statistical Approach: We compute the Pointwise Mutual Information (PMI) between each
252            new variable and the initial variables. PMI quantifies the dependence between two vari-
253            ables, with higher scores indicating a greater likelihood of potential causal association.

483   7.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
484
485   Datasets. Evaluating the missing variable proposal component presents a unique challenge: the
      ground-truth missing variables are inherently unknown in real-world scenarios. To address this, we


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      simulate missing variables and assess our method’s ability to identify them. We start with complete,
487   ground-truth causal graphs and systematically remove variables to create incomplete graphs. We
488   employ five causal graphs: Cancer, Respiratory Disease, Diabetes, Obesity, and ADNI. For each
489   causal graph, we iteratively remove one variable at a time, creating multiple test cases per graph. We
490   then apply our missing variable proposal component to these incomplete graphs, aiming to identify
491   the removed variables.
492
      Our Method and Baselines. For our missing variable proposal component, we employ GPT-4o and
493   Llama-3.1-8b-instruct as the LLM. To ensure a thorough evaluation, we have introduced a baseline
494   method that employs LLM to directly suggest new variables through a prompt-based approach.
495
496
      Metrics. We evaluate the performance using a success rate metric, calculated as follows:
      1) For each incomplete causal graph, we check if our method successfully proposes the re-
497
      moved variable in its final set of proposed variables Zm . 2) We count a ”success” for
498
      each correctly proposed variable. 3) The success rate is computed as: Success Rate =
499   Number of Successes/Total Number of Incomplete Graphs. For instance, in a causal graph with
500   five variables, we create five different incomplete graphs by removing one variable at a time. If our
501   method correctly proposes the removed variable in three of these five graphs, the success rate would
502   be 0.6. For the statistical approach, we select the top-5 variables based on their PMI scores.

526   8     C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we introduce IRIS, a novel framework that addresses several longstanding challenges
529   in the field of causal discovery. By integrating automated data collection, hybrid causal discov-
530   ery methods, and missing variable proposal components, IRIS significantly advances our ability to
531   uncover causal relations in real-world scenarios. Our approach not only reduces the reliance on
532   extensive manual data collection but also leverages existing knowledge while facilitating the dis-
533   covery of novel causal relations. The ability to propose potentially missing variables allows for the
534   development of comprehensive causal graphs. Our experimental results show that IRIS consistently
535   outperforms existing baselines. Future work could aim to enhancing the scalability of IRIS for larger
536
      and more complex causal relations and to explore innovative methods for integrating causal relations
      extracted from texts with those identified through statistical algorithms.

      A.1   R ELATED W ORK
760
      Causal Discovery Causal discovery aims to uncover causal structures among variables, distin-
761
      guishing itself from relation extraction in NLP by revealing novel causal relations rather than merely
762   extracting known relations. While experimental approaches such as randomized controlled trials are
763   gold standard methodsFisher (1935), practical limitations often necessitate statistical methods using
764   observational data. These include constraint-based and score-based approaches Spirtes et al. (2000);
765   Pearl (2009); Heckerman et al. (1995). However, statistical methods face challenges in data collec-
766   tion and theoretical limitations. Recent advancements in LLMs have introduced new possibilities
767   for causal discovery without direct data access Kıcıman et al. (2023); Zečević et al. (2023); Long
768   et al. (2022). However, concerns about LLMs functioning as ”causal parrots” and their ability to
769   generalize to novel relations have been raised Zečević et al. (2023); Feng et al. (2024). Alternative
770
      approaches, such as using LLMs for variable proposer and combining them with statistical meth-
      ods Liu et al. (2024), have emerged. Our work builds upon these ideas, introducing an automated
771
      document collection process, a hybrid causal discovery method integrating statistical and relation
772
      extraction techniques, and a hybrid approach for new variable proposal.
773
774
775   Relation Extraction Relation extraction aims to transform unstructured textual relations into
776   structured relation tuples of the form < e1 , r, e2 >, where e1 and e2 represent entities and r denotes
777
      the relation between them Yang et al. (2022); Dasgupta et al. (2018). While relation extraction can
      identify cause-effect relationships from documents, it fundamentally differs from causal discovery in
778
      that it relies on explicitly stated relations in texts, whereas causal discovery can uncover novel causal
779
      relationships from observational data even in the absence of explicit textual mentions. Nevertheless,
780   relation extraction can serve as a complementary method for identifying commonly known causal
781   relations in textual data. Several studies have focused on extracting causal relations from natural
782   language texts Balashankar et al. (2019); Bui et al. (2010); Chang & Choi (2006). The methods for
783   causality extraction can be divided into pattern-based and deep learning-based approaches. Pattern-
784   based methods utilize predefined linguistic patterns to extract relevant text segments, which are then
785   converted into tuples using hand-crafted algorithms Garcia (1997); Khoo et al. (2000). However,
786   these methods often suffer from limited coverage of causal relations and require significant effort in
787   pattern design. Deep learning-based methods employ neural networks to learn high-level abstract
788
      features and representations from sentences, framing relation extraction as a sequence-to-sequence
      task Zhao et al. (2023; 2024). While these approaches offer improved performance, they typically
789
      require large fine-tuning datasets and may not consistently produce structurally correct output tuples.
790
791   A notable limitation of many relation extraction systems is the lack of verification for extracted rela-
792   tions, potentially leading to the extraction of false or unreliable relations from untrustworthy sources
793   Si et al. (2024); Wadhwa et al. (2023). Our work addresses this issue by adopting a novel approach:
794
      instead of directly extracting causal relations from documents, we pre-create textual mentions of
      causal relations (e.g., ”smoking causes lung cancer”) and employ LLMs to verify the veracity of
795
      these relations based on relevant documents. We consider a causal relation to hold if the majority of
796
      documents support its veracity, thereby enhancing the reliability of our extracted causal relations.
797
798
799   Claim Verification Claim verification aims to assess the veracity of claims based on relevant doc-
800   uments Bekoulis et al. (2021). This process typically encompasses several key components: claim
801
      detection, document retrieval, veracity prediction, and explanation generation. Research in this
      field often focuses on specific aspects of the verification pipeline. For instance, Panchendrarajan
802
      & Zubiaga (2024) and Li et al. (2024) concentrate on identifying check-worthy statements from
803
      large text corpora. Others, such as Wadden et al. (2022) and Mohr et al. (2022), prioritize veracity
804   prediction, while Wang & Shu (2023) emphasize the importance of generating explanations for ver-
805   ification outcomes. The emergence of LLMs has significantly influenced the field, with numerous
806   studies leveraging LLMs for claim verification through carefully crafted prompts Kim et al. (2024);
807   Bazaga et al. (2024); Asai et al. (2024). Building on these advancements, one branch of our hy-
808   brid causal discovery approach reframes causal discovery as a causal relation verification task. We
809   employ LLMs to assess the veracity of causal relations based on retrieved documents, subsequently
      incorporating verified relations into a causal graph. This methodology bridges the gap between tra-

810
      ditional claim verification techniques and causal discovery, offering a novel approach to uncovering
811   and validating causal relations.

996    Causal Relation Annotation Task

997    Task overview:
       Your task is to identify and annotate causal relations among a set of variables. A causal relation exists when one variable directly influences another.
998
       Instructions:
999    1. Consider each pair of variables and determine if there is a direct causal relationship between them.
1000   2. If you believe variable A causes variable B, indicate this as: A → B
       3. Be cautious of confusing correlation with causation. Only mark a relationship if you believe there is a direct causal link.

       5. It’s okay to have multiple causes for a single effect, or multiple effects from a single cause.
1002   6. Not all variables will necessarily have causal relationships with others.
