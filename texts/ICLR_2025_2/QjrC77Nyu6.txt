009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Wearable sensing devices, such as electrocardiogram (ECG) heart-rate monitors,
012            will play a crucial role in the future of digital health. This continuous monitor-
013            ing leads to massive unlabeled datasets, making the development of unsupervised
014            learning frameworks essential to associate these single-lead ECG signals to their
015            anticipated clinical outcomes. While the Masked Data Modelling (MDM) meth-
016            ods have enjoyed wide use, the idiosyncrasies of single-lead ECG data make its
017            direct application impractical. In this paper, we present Cueing the Predictor In-
018
               crements the Detailing (CuPID), a novel Self-Supervised Learning (SSL) method
               that adapts MDM methods for use on single-lead ECG data. CuPID accomplishes
019
               this via cueing spectrogram-derived context to the predictors, thus incentivizing
020
               the encoder to produce more detailed representations. This leads the class token
021            to accommodate fine-grained information. We demonstrate that CuPID outper-
022            forms state-of-the-art methods in a variety of downstream tasks and databases,
023            increasing the accuracy for each task from 3.6 % to 9.7%.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The wearable sensing field has seen remarkable advancements in recent years, and is expected to
029   play a crucial role in the future of digital health. One widely used type of wearable health sensor is
030   the heart monitor that captures cardiac activity as single-lead ECG signals during free-living condi-
031   tions, such as in the patient’s home. Mapping these signals with significant clinical outcomes has
032   the potential to provide outstanding benefits such as simplifying the diagnostic process (Himmel-
033
      reich et al., 2019) or enabling users to engage proactively in tracking their heart health (Abdou &
      Krishnan, 2022). In this context, models that extract information from single-lead ECG into gen-
034
      eralizable representations are mandated to address distinct downstream tasks. These models should
035
      be optimized using large volumes of unlabelled data. This makes Self-Supervised Learning (SSL)
036   framework particularly well-suited for addressing this clinical challenge.
037
038   Recently, Masked Data Modelling (MDM) methods have been gaining attention in the SSL field (He
039
      et al., 2021; Gupta et al., 2023; Assran et al., 2023). They rely on masking a portion of the input and
      driving a transformer-based encoder, typically a Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021)
040
      to compute detailed patch representations that enable a predictor to infer the information accommo-
041
      dated within the unseen patches. This approach is especially effective in fields like computer vision,
042   where the predictor can associate unseen tokens with the object they represent by simply perceiving
043   a portion of the whole picture and having the spatial information of unseen patches.
044
045
      However, it is impractical to directly apply these methods to single-lead ECG data. These signals
      capture the sequence of activities that are executed in each beat by the heart’s different chambers
046
      to ensure the blood reaches the entire body. Figure 1a illustrates how various cardiac activities are
047
      represented by distinct wave morphologies in the ECG. Even though the sequence of activities
048   occurs periodically over time, the distance between consecutive periods varies moderately as shown
049   in Figure 1b and 1c, respectively. This combination of ECG idiosyncrasies leads to the following
050   dilemma: it is challenging for the predictor to accurately model the position of each wave for masked
051   inputs because of the varying distances between periods, and not inferring exactly this position has a
052   big impact on the loss since consecutive strips accommodate distinct waves. This dilemma leads the
053   predictor to be cautious when reconstructing the masked patches. As shown in Figure 2b, it prefers
      to estimate a value near the average rather than trying to match precisely the signal’s morphology.

075   information provided by the spectrogram of the input signal. This information is fed into the
076   attention mechanism of the transformer-based predictor as the Key (K) to ensure that its role is
077   merely informative and its value can not be used directly to reconstruct the representations. It leads
078   to the loss function reaching significantly lower values, as shown in Figure 2a. Therefore, the
079   reconstructions are more adjusted to the morphology of the original signal, as captured in Figure

      nal signal due to the unpredictability of the distance between periods limits the encoder’s learning
      potential. (ii) By cueing the predictor with the spectrogram, we enable it to manage this delay and
083

084
      original input with high precision. (iii) The more informative the patch representations are, the more
085   informative the class token will be, thereby enhancing the model’s performance in downstream tasks.

      To assess our hypothesis, we have conducted an extensive evaluation where CuPID is com-
109   pared against the existing state-of-the-art (SOTA) SSL methods tailored for single-lead ECG
110   analysis. In the proposed evaluation, up to three distinct noisy databases; MIT-BIH Atrial Fibril-

121   In summary, the contributions of this paper are:
122
             • We have discussed the limitations of applying MDM techniques directly to single-lead ECG
123
               signals due to the idiosyncrasy of this kind of data.
124
125
             • We introduce CuPID, a novel SSL method that addresses these limitations by helping the

127

             • We provide a model that achieves markedly enhanced results in a variety of downstream

133   2.1   M ASKED DATA M ODELLING (MDM)
134
      Masked Data Modelling (MDM) has been a commonly used technique in the Natural Language
135
      Processing (NLP) field. Methods such as Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transform-
136
      ers (BERT) (Devlin et al., 2019) that rely on hiding a series of words within a sentence and opti-
137   mizing a predictor to infer these words have proven to be the most effective pre-training method
138   in the field. In recent times, this pre-training mechanism has been adapted in the field of com-
139   puter vision. Existing methods, such as, Masked Autoencoders (MAE) (He et al., 2021) or Siamese
140   Masked Autoencoders (SiamMAE) (Gupta et al., 2023) incorporate a predictor trained to reconstruct
141   masked patches from the original input. Alternatively, Image-based Joint-Embedding Predictive Ar-
142   chitecture (I-JEPA) (Assran et al., 2023) reconstructs the representations computed by a teacher
143   network instead of the input itself. The weights of this teacher network are not optimized using
144   the gradients but by an exponential moving average (EMA) of the weights of the student network.
145
      Both approaches have shown promising results in the field of computer vision, outperforming gold-
      standard Energy-Based Modelling (EBM) methods such as Variance-Invariance-Covariance Regu-
146
      larization (VIC-REG) (Bardes et al., 2022), Self-Distillation with no Labels (DINO) (Caron et al.,
147
      2021), or Bootstrap Your Own Latent (BYOL) (Grill et al., 2020).
148
149   Given the idiosyncrasies of ECG data, we consider a more suitable to reconstruct the original input
150   rather than the teacher representations. This is due to the fact that the critical information in ECG
151
      data resides in the morphology of each heartbeat. Reconstructing the original input ensures that
      these waves are given greater importance since the amplitude values are greater than strips with
152
      no waves. This interesting property does not occur when reconstructing the representations from
153
      the teacher network, since they are expected to lie with the same range of values. This is reflected
154   in better-performing models by optimizing them to reconstruct the original input (See Section A).
155   However, the effect of incorporating the spectrogram into the predictor has also been studied for the
156   two approaches (See Section 5).
157
158   2.2   SSL IN S INGLE -L EAD ECG S IGNAL P ROCESSING
159
160   Most-widely used single-lead ECG SSL methods follows a EBM approach; (i) Contrastive Learning
161   of Cardiac Signals Across Space (CLOCS) (Kiyasseh et al., 2021) utilizes two consecutive ECG
      time strips as positive pairs, (ii) Mixing-Up (Wickstrøm et al., 2022) introduces a more tailored


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      data augmentation product of two time series from the same recording, (iii) Patient Contrastive
163   Learning (PCLR) (Diamant et al., 2022) which considers two time strips from the same subject but
164   different recordings. While all these methods utilize the Contrastive Learning (Chen et al., 2020)
165   as a common framework for learning the invariant attributes considering non-overlapping inputs as
166   positive pairs, (iv) Distilled Embedding for Almost-Periodic Time Series (DEAPS) (Atienza et al.,
167   2024) drives the model to capture the also dynamic patterns of the single-lead ECGs. It follows a
168   non-contrastive learning approach, being built on top of BYOL (Grill et al., 2020).
169   All of these SSL methods will compose the set of baselines for the CuPID’s evaluation, where
170   the representations computed by each pre-trained model will be employed for addressing several
171   downstream tasks.
172
173
      2.3   SSL IN 12-L EAD ECG S IGNAL P ROCESSING
174
175   In the realm of 12-lead ECG signals, research has effectively utilized MDM techniques. The in-
176   troduction of a new spatial dimension broadens the scope for input masking, thereby aiding the
177   predictor in identifying the locations of various waves within the masked tokens. Techniques like
178   MTAE, MLAE, and MLTAE, all introduced by MAE family of ECG (MaeFE) (Zhang et al., 2023),
179   suggest three masking strategies: temporal masking, spatial masking across different leads, or a
180   combination of both. More recent approaches, such as Spatio-Temporal Masked Electrocardiogram
181
      Modeling (ST-MEM) (Na et al., 2024), adopt this combined strategy by employing a joint predictor
      that reconstructs the original input attending to each lead independently.
182
183   Among the four listed methods, only MTAE is suitable for single-lead ECG signals, as the other
184   three methods require multiple leads. Consequently, only MTAE has been trained to handle single-
185   lead signals and has been included in the main evaluation. Nevertheless, CuPID is also benchmarked
186   against these methods when 12-lead data is available, despite the fact that CuPID only utilizes one
187
      lead for inference.
188
189   3     C UEING THE P REDICTOR I NCREMENTS THE D ETAILING (C U PID)
190
191   The core idea behind CuPID is cueing the predictor with the contextual information provided by the
192   spectrogram. Its workflow is illustrated in Figure 3. From left to right the original signal input is
193   patched and embedded using a linear layer. A portion of these tokens (Represented as gray blocks in
194   the figure) is randomly masked with a fixed ratio. Only the unmasked tokens are passed through the
195   encoder. Learnable mask tokens with their respective positional encoding are placed in the original
196   position of the masked segments. What sets CuPID apart is that it uses the spectrogram as the Key for
197   the attention mechanism, as represented in Figure 3. This predictor reconstructs the original input.
198   The L1 metric is computed between this reconstruction and the original input. This loss function is
199
      only calculated on the masked patches. It is important to note that the predictor is discarded after
      training, with the encoder being used for downstream tasks. Therefore, the spectrogram is only
200
      utilized during pre-training and not during inference.
201
202
203   3.1   ROLE OF S PECTROGRAM IN THE P REDICTOR
204
      The core idea behind CuPID is providing the predictor with more contextual information than the
205
      regular positional encodings. We identify the spectrogram as a tool that has the potential to do it.
206   A spectrogram is a visual representation of the spectrum of frequencies in a signal as they vary
207   over time. They are commonly generated using the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), which converts
208   a time-domain signal into its frequency components. The spectrogram is expected to provide de-
209   tailed contextual information to the predictor since the waves composing the R-R interval operate in
210   distinct frequencies. This feature is leveraged by traditional signal processing methods to perform
211   ECG signal delineation (Martinez et al., 2004).
212
213   Limiting the Information Provided by the Spectrogram: Just as the time domain input is trans-
214   formed into the frequency domain when computing the spectrogram, it can also be converted back
215   to the time domain. It means that the predictor could potentially reconstruct the original input with-
      out using the encoder’s representations. To prevent this, the spectrogram is used just as the K in the

      Challenges of Using the Spectrogram as the Key: A primary issue arises when using the spec-
256
      trogram as the key instead of the standard concatenation of encoder representations and mask tokens.
257
      The predictor cannot distinguish between informative tokens and mask tokens, as this distinction is
258   not present in the spectrogram. It is important to note that CuPID, in accordance with standard prac-
259   tices, permits mask tokens to interact with each other, ensuring the spectrogram remains unmasked.
260   Consequently, a token might incorrectly focus on another due to its spectrogram key, even if that
261   token is merely a mask and contains no actual information. To overcome this issue, CuPID delays
262   incorporating the spectrogram into the predictor’s second block. The regular concatenation of en-
263   coder representations and mask tokens is used as the K in the first block. This approach ensures
264   that each mask token retains some information after the initial block, which can then be distilled in
265   subsequent blocks with the context information provided by the spectrogram as K.

304   3.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
305
306   To ensure the replication of the method, we meticulously outline the hyperparameter settings and
307   the model architecture.
308
309   Model Architecture: The ViT model proposed by CuPID for processing the single-lead ECG
310   signals counts with four regular transformer blocks with four heads each and a dimension of 128.
311   The input consists of a one-dimensional 10-second signal sampled at 100 Hz. This signal is split
312   into patches with a length of 10 samples.
313
314   CuPID Implementation and Optimization: The predictor consists of a ViT model with two blocks
315   and a dimension of 128. The training procedure consists of 40,000 iterations. We use a batch size
316   of 256, AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) optimizer with a learning rate of 1e − 3. The masking
317   ratio is set to 0.5. To achieve the patch-wise spectrogram consistent with the dimensions of the
318   predictor, the number of coefficient bins is set to 255, and the window length to 20. CuPID has been
319   trained on publicly available Sleep Heart Health Study (SHHS) database (Zhang et al., 2018; Quan
320
      et al., 1998). The training procedure and the evaluations are performed on a desktop computer, with
      a Nvidia GeForce RTX 3070 GPU.
321
322   The influence of the masking ratio parameter as well as the influence of incorporation of the spec-
323   trogram for different values of it has been evaluated (See Section 5). In addition, the effect of
      incorporating the spectrogram has also been studied in the Icentia (Tan et al., 2019) database.

422   4.2.1    D ISCUSSION OF THE R ESULTS
423
      Throughout this comprehensive evaluation, it has been established that by cueing the predictor,
424
      CuPID drives the learning encoder to compute more detailed patch representations. This results in
425
      the model achieving markedly enhanced results in a variety of downstream tasks, as detailed in Table
426   1. These findings provide robust evidence in favor of the hypotheses posited by this study: (i) The
427   predictor’s inability to reconstruct the original signal due to the unpredictability of the delay limits
428   the learning potential of the encoder. (ii) By cueing the predictor with the spectrogram, we enable
429   it to deal with this delay and drive the encoder to compute detailed token representations that can
430   be used to reconstruct the original input with a great level of detail. (iii) The more informative the
431   patch representations are, the more informative the class token will be, improving the performance
      of the model when addressing downstream tasks.

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      This research provides strong evidence that directly applying the Masked Data Modelling (MDM)
489   framework to single-lead ECG signals is insufficient. This is due to the idiosyncrasies of ECG
490   data, where consecutive data chunks represent a distinct wave, and the distance between consecutive
491   heartbeats varies moderately. This leads the predictor to be cautious when reconstructing the masked
492   patches and to not drive the encoder to compute detailed patch representations that can be used for
493   addressing downstream tasks. To overcome this issue, we introduce CuPID, a novel SSL technique
494   for ECG analysis. By cueing the predictor with the contextual information given by the spectrogram
495   of the input signal, CuPID enforces the encoder to compute more informative representations. It
496   results in a significant performance improvement when addressing downstream tasks.
497
498   Limitations: CuPID has only been evaluated on a single architecture configuration. However,
499
      the incorporation of the spectrogram in the predictor is agnostic to the ViT configuration and similar
      performance improvements should be obtained .
500
501
      Future Work:        We were surprised to observe a decline in performance when pre-training the
502
      model on the Icentia 11K database, despite it being theoretically more comprehensive than SHHS.
503   We believe this issue stems from the high level of noise present in the Icentia 11K database. Moving
504   forward, we aim to explore potential integrations with CuPID to address this problem and fully
505   leverage the database’s potential.

      C.1   SHHS DATA S ELECTION
757
758   Only the subjects that appear in both recording cycles are used during the training procedure. This
759   leads to 2643 subjects. ECG signals are extracted from the Polysomnography (PSG) recordings.
760   The quality of every 10 seconds-data strips has been evaluated with the algorithm proposed by Zhao
761   and Zhang Zhao & Zhang (2018). We use SHHS since it contains two records belonging to the same
762   subject. This makes this specific database special, and this is the reason that it has been the only
763   database used during the optimization.

768   any DC-offset and baseline wander. Finally, each dataset underwent normalization to achieve unit
769   variance, ensuring that the signal samples belong to a N (0, 1) distribution. This normalization
770   process aimed to mitigate variations in device amplifications.
