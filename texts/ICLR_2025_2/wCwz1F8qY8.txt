011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Accurate prediction of the interface residue-residue contacts between interacting
014            proteins is valuable for determining the structure and function of protein com-
015
               plexes. Recent deep learning methods have drastically improved the accuracy of
               predicting the interface contacts of protein complexes. However, existing meth-
016
               ods rely on Multiple Sequence Alignments (MSA) features which pose limitations
017
               on prediction accuracy, speed, and computational efficiency. Here, we propose a
018            transformer-powered deep learning method to predict the inter-protein residue-
019            residue contacts based on both single-sequence and structure-aware protein lan-
020            guage models (PLM), called DeepSSInter. Utilizing the intra-protein distance
021            and graph representations and the ESM2 and SaProt protein language models, we
022            are able to generate the structure-aware features for the protein receptor, ligand,
023            and complex. These structure-aware features are passed into the Resnet Inception
024            module and the Triangle-aware module to effectively produce the predicted inter-
025            protein contact map. Extensive experiments on both homo- and hetero-dimeric
026
               complexes show that our DeepSSInter model significantly improves the perfor-
               mance compared to previous state-of-the-art methods.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Understanding the interactions between proteins is fundamental to deciphering the molecular mech-
032   anisms underlying cellular processes (Hu et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2024b). Accurate prediction of the
033   interface residue-residue contacts of protein-protein interactions (PPI) allows for the determination
034   of the resulting protein complex structure (see Figure 1 for an example) (Gao et al, 2024), hav-
035   ing significant implications for understanding the protein complex’s biological function, increasing
036   the efficiency for drug discovery, and saving time and resources for experimental methods (Lin et
037   al., 2024a). However, current methods still lack accuracy and efficiency when predicting the inter-
038   face contacts of protein complexes. Computational methods include more traditional methods such
039
      as docking simulation (Yan et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024a; Honorato et al., 2024)
      and coevolutionary analysis (Ovchinnikov et al., 2014), as well as more recent deep learning meth-
040
      ods (Liu & Gong, 2019; Zeng et al., 2018; Adhikari et al., 2018; Quadir et al., 2021b; Roy et al.,
041
      2022; Quadir et al., 2021a; Yan and Huang, 2021) that can capture the complex patterns in protein
042   sequences and structures. However, the former lacks in scalability and generalizability across dif-
043   ferent types of protein complexes, especially heterodimers; while the latter still lacks in accuracy
044   and ability to fully leverage the structural context of proteins, which is crucial for accurate contact
045   prediction (Lin et al., 2024a).
046
      Recent advances in deep learning have led to more sophisticated models for inter-protein contact
047
      predictions such as DeepInter(Lin et al., 2023), DeepHomo2.0(Lin et al., 2022), GLINTER(Xie &
048   Xu, 2022), and CDPred (Guo et al., 2022), or direct prediction of protein complex structures like
049   RosettaFold (Baek et al., 2021) and AlphaFold-Multimer (AFM) (Evans et al., 2021). However,
050   all current methods rely on input of the multiple sequence alignment information of proteins. Use
051   of MSA information can be beneficial, but there exist several drawbacks and challenges associated
052   with integrating MSA data into deep learning models. First, usage of MSA information brings high
053   computational complexity. MSA data can be very large, especially for long sequences and large
      sequence databases. Preprocessing of MSA data, including generating the alignment and converting

079   (Bitbol et al., 2016; Szurmant et al., 2018; Gueudré et al., 2016; Ovchinnikov et al., 2014; Zeng et
080   al., 2018; Bryant et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023). Thus, improved methods are needed to overcome
081

082   In contrast to MSA-reliant methods, single sequence-based methods have also been developed to
083   predict protein monomer structures (Wang et al., 2022; Chowdhury et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2023;
084   Jing et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024b). Single sequence-based methods utilize evolutionary informa-
085   tion extracted from protein language models (PLM) to effectively capture features. Unlike MSA
086   which requires high computational resources, single sequence-based methods are less computation-
087
      ally intensive and are faster in speed. In addition, single sequence-based methods do not rely on
      the availability of homologous sequences. This allows for prediction and design of novel or engi-
088
      neered protein where MSA information is not applicable (Chowdhury et al., 2022; Watson et al.,
089
      2023; Ren et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2022). Furthermore, single sequence-based methods may be bet-
090   ter at capturing the structural and interaction properties of dynamic or disordered regions (Jing et
091   al., 2024). These regions are often poorly aligned in MSAs, making single sequence-based meth-
092   ods favorable. The advantages of single sequence-based methods over MSA-reliant methods make
093   them more reliable, scalable, and robust. However, there still exists a lack of well-performing single
094   sequence-based methods for the interface contact prediction of protein complexes.
095
      To address this need, we propose DeepSSInter, a single sequence-based deep learning model for the
096   interface contact prediction of protein complexes. We utilize two protein language models (PLM) to
097   effectively capture evolutionary and structural patterns of input monomer proteins. Specifically, one
098   of the PLMs is ESM2, which takes in the monomer sequences of the proteins (Lin et al., 2024b), and
099   the other is SaProt, which takes in the structure-aware sequence of the proteins generated with their
100   3D structure information (Su et al., 2023). Due to the input of structure-aware sequences into the
101   latter PLM, the resulting representations and attentions contain structural information of individual
102   proteins. Our model leverages this information to provide faster and more accurate predictions of
103   interface contacts. To validate the effectiveness of our method, we comprehensively evaluated our
104
      model on diverse data sets of homodimeric and heterodimeric protein complexes. It is shown that
      our model outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods, especially when predicting challenging
105
      heterodimer complexes, establishing the effectiveness of our single-sequence and structure-aware
106
      protein language model.

      Currently, state-of-the-art methods for predicting the inter-protein residue-residue contacts of protein
128
      complexes all require the MSA as input.

131   2.1   D EEP H OMO 2.0
132
133   DeepHomo2.0 (Lin et al., 2022) predicts the inter-protein contact probabilities of homodimeric com-
134   plexes by combining sequential 1D features and pairwise 2D features, passing them through convo-
135   lutional neural networks. The model achieves high accuracy by integrating evolutionary information,
136   residue-residue distance maps, and transformer-derived context. However, DeepHomo2.0 has lim-
137   ited generalizability to other types of protein-protein interactions such as heterodimers because the
138
      model is specifically designed for homodimeric protein complexes.
139
140
      2.2   GLINTER
141
142   GLINTER (Xie & Xu, 2022) predicts the interface contact probabilities by using graph represen-
143   tations and MSA features through a graph convolutional network with an attention mechanism and
144   ResNet layers. GLINTER improves the accuracy and robustness in interface contact prediction of
145   protein complexes, but still faces challenges with computational complexity and protein dimer pre-
146   cision.
147
148
149   2.3   CDP RED
150
151
      CDPred (Guo et al., 2022) predicts inter-chain distance maps of protein complexes by passing fea-
      tures into a model consisting of a deep residual network, a channel-wise attention mechanism, and
152
      a spatial-wise attention mechanism. Despite its effectiveness, especially for homodimers, it faces
153
      challenges on heterodimeric complexes or the cases with shallow MSAs.
154
155
156   2.4   D EEP I NTER
157
158   DeepInter (Lin et al., 2023) predicts interface contact probabilities of protein complexes by in-
159   corporating a ResNet-Inception module and triangle-aware mechanism that can capture geometric
160   consistency and long-range interactions. This allows DeepInter to provide more accurate and robust
161   contact predictions compared to existing methods. However, it lacks in still lacks in accuracy for
      heterodimers and relies on high-quality MSA information.

      3.1       OVERVIEW OF D EEP SSI NTER
165
166   Figure 2 shows the overall architecture of the DeepSSInter network and the flow of data through
167   the network. The architecture consists of four main components: 1) a geometric graph transformer
168   module (Morehead et al., 2021) consisting of a graph neural network that generates protein sequence
169   representations, 2) two protein-language models (ESM2 (Lin et al., 2024b) and SaProt (Su et al.,
170   2023)) to generate protein sequence-aware and structure-aware sequence and attention representa-
171   tions, 3) a ResNet-Inception module (Lin et al., 2023), and 4) a triangle-aware module consisting
172
      of the triangle update, triangle self-attention, and transition layers (Lin et al., 2023). Graph repre-
      sentations of the individual proteins are passed into the geometric transformer module to generate
173
      sequence representations of the proteins. The sequences of the individual proteins are passed into
174
      the ESM2 and SaProt models to generate sequence and attention representations, for which those of
175   SaProt are structure-aware. In addition, the linked sequences of individual proteins are also passed
176   into ESM2 and SaProt to generate attention representations associated with the interface. Then, the
177   sequence representations, attention representations, and distance features, obtained by applying a
178   radial basis function on the locations of the residues within each protein, are respectively concate-
179   nated into sequence-aware features and passed into the ResNet-Inception module. The data is finally
180   passed into the triangle-aware module from the ResNet-Inception module. At the prediction time,
181   only one (for a homodimer case) or two (for a heterodimer case) monomer structures are needed as
182   input for the model. By default, DeepSSInter does crop the monomer structure during the inference.
183
      However, for a very long protein, users may use a sliding window strategy (Appendix F). The output
      is a contact map consisting of the pairwise probabilities between the amino acids of two proteins.

208   3.2       N ETWORK A RCHITECTURE AND PARAMETERS
209
210   The model takes in the graph representations, intra-protein distance matrices, sequences, and
211   structure-aware sequences (generated by foldseek (van Kempen et al., 2024) for SaProt (Su et al.,
212
      2023)) of two proteins as input (Figure 2). The graph representations of two individual proteins are
      passed through the geometric transformer to obtain two representations of dimensions LA × 128 and
213
      LB × 128 respectively, where LA is the length of protein A and LB is the length of protein B.
214
215   The intra-protein distance feature of individual proteins is represented by the Gaussian radial basis
      function (RBF), which is calculated as follows.

      where d is the intra-protein distance. dµ is a hyperparameter representing the centers of 64 evenly
220
      spaced Gaussian RBFs between 2 to 22 Å with standard deviation σ = 0.3125. The results are two
221
      distance matrices of dimensions LA × LA × 64 and LB × LB × 64 for protein A and protein B
222
      respectively.
223
224   The amino acid sequences of the two proteins are passed into the ESM2 protein language model
225   (specifically the esm2 t33 650M UR50D model with 33 layers and 650 million parameters) to gen-
226
      erate representation matrices of dimensions LA × 1280 and LB × 1280, respectively, and attention
      matrices of dimensions LA × LA × 660 and LB × LB × 660, respectively, for protein A and protein
227
      B. We also concatenate sequence A and sequence B and pass the resulting complex sequence into
228
      the ESM2 model to get the representation and attention matrices of dimensions (LA + LB ) × 1280
229   and (LA + LB ) × (LA + LB ) × 660 for the protein complex.
230
231   The structure-aware sequences generated with the foldseek algorithm of two proteins are passed into
232
      the SaProt protein language model (specifically the SaProt 650M AF2 model trained with a dataset
      of 40 million AlphaFold2 structures (Varadi et al., 2024)) to generate sequence-aware representation
233
      and attention matrices. For protein A and protein B, the dimensions of the representation matrices
234
      are LA × 1280 and LB × 1280, respectively, and the attention matrices are of dimensions LA ×
235   LA × 660 and LB × LB × 660, respectively. Similar to before, we also concatenate structure-aware
236   sequence A and structure-aware sequence B and pass the resulting complex sequence into the SaProt
237   model to get the structure-aware representation and attention matrix of dimensions (LA +LB )×1280
238   and (LA + LB ) × (LA + LB ) × 660 respectively for the protein complex.
239
      We then concatenate the geometric transformer representations, ESM2 representations, and SaProt
240
      representations of protein A and protein B to obtain 1D features for the two proteins. We also
241   concatenate the ESM2 attention matrices, SaProt attention matrices, and RBF distance matrices of
242   protein A and protein B to obtain the 2D features for the two proteins. Finally, we concatenate the
243   ESM2 attention matrix and the SaProt attention matrix of the protein complex (protein A + protein
244   B) to obtain 2D features for the protein complex. The two 1D features of protein A and protein B, the
245   two 2D features of protein A and protein B, and the 2D features of the protein complex are passed
246   through linear layers performing dimensionality reduction to prevent overflow of GPU memory.
247   These features are then passed into the Resnet-Inception module. The ResNet-Inception module
248   outputs the receptor (protein A), ligand (protein B), and complex features (protein complex) of
249
      dimensions LA ×LA ×d, LB ×LB ×d, and LA ×LB ×d, respectively, where d is a hyperparameter set
      as 64. The receptor, ligand, and complex 2D structures are passed into the Triangle-aware module.
250
      The model finally outputs the pairwise probabilities that the residue-residue contacts exist between
251
      protein A and protein B.

      3.3   I MPLEMENTATION OF T RAINING
254
255   DeepSSInter uses Focal Loss as the loss function for training, the same loss function as that used
256   for DeepInter (Lin et al., 2023). Compared to other classification loss functions such as standard
257   Cross-Entropy, Focal Loss is able to address the issue of class imbalance. In the case of interface
258   contact prediction, there is an extreme class imbalance between non-contacts and contacts, with the
259   number of non-contacts greatly outnumbering the number of contacts. Focal loss helps the model
260   focus on the minority class, the true contacts in this case. Focal loss also makes the model focus
261   more on difficult, misclassified examples, improving the model’s ability to generalize on challenging
262   test cases. Focal loss is defined by:
263
264                                F ocalLoss(pt ) = −αt (1 − pt )γ log(pt )
265
266                                               p   if y = 1
                                          pt =
267                                               1–p otherwise,
268
269   where y ∈ 0, 1 is the ground truth label. We use parameters of αt = 0.25 and γ = 1.5 (Lin et al.,
      2017).

270
      Our model is trained using PyTorch Lightning on one A100 GPU with 40G memory. We trained the
271   model with a learning rate of 0.001 and a weight decay of 0.01. Due to GPU memory limitations
272   of each module, especially the triangle self-attention module, we set a maximum sequence length
273   of 320 for each input protein. If the input protein has a length greater than 320, we use a window
274   of size 320 to scan the labels and find the windows that have the maximum number of inter-protein
275   contacts. From the windows with the most contacts, we randomly select a window and crop the
276   protein sequence and input features to match the window. For the ground truth labels of the interface
277   contacts, we consider two amino acids with a distance of < 8.0 Å among their heavy atom pairs from
278   two proteins in the complex to be an interface contact.

517   more robust against structural errors. As such, geometric transformer may not help the model for
518   high-accuracy homodimer cases that are determined by co-evolutions, but would play a significant
519   role for medium or low-accurate heterodimer cases that are largely determined by structural features.
520   How to balance the structure representations from graph transformer and SaProt protein language
521   model remains an important topic in the future development of DeepSSInter.

524   5   C ONCLUSION
525
526   We have proposed a sequence and structure-aware protein language-based deep learning model to
527   effectively predict the interface contacts for protein-protein interactions, named DeepSSInter. Com-
528   pared with state-of-the-other methods such as DeepInter, DeepHomo2.0, GLINTER, CDPred, and
529   DeepHomo, our DeepSSInter model achieves the best performance for all precision metrics on di-
530   verse test sets of homodimeric and heterodimeric protein complexes, respectively, when utilizing
531
      experimental protein structures as input. On average, our DeepSSInter method achieves a top L/5
      prediction of 79.8% on the homodimeric complexes, compared with 77.1% for DeepInter, 67.9%
532
      for CDPred, 67.2% for DeepHome2.0, 57.4% for GLINTER, and 52.0% for DeepHomo, respec-
533
      tively. On the heterodimeric complexes, DeepSSInter obtains a top L/5 precision of 48.8%, which is
534   significantly higher than 44.4% for DeepInter, 33.3% for CDPred, and 29.0% for GLINTER, respec-
535   tively. In addition, our model also performs well on AlphaFold2-predicted structures, showing its
536   robustness on predicted structures. Despite DeepSSInter’s high precision and robustness, there still
537   exist some limitations in the model, such as the difficulty in predicting the inter-protein contacts of
538   heterodimers with AlphaFold2-predicted structure. However, our model shows the effectiveness of
539   using protein language models and structure-aware features in improving the accuracy of predicting
      the interface contacts of protein complexes.

       F    H OW TO USE D EEP SSI NTER AT THE PREDICTION TIME .
973
974
       At the prediction time, only one (for a homodimer case) or two (for a heterodimer case) monomer
975    structures are needed as input for DeepSSInter. By default, DeepSSInter does not crop the protein
976    structure and use the full sequence to predict residue-residue contacts during the inference. For the
977    best performance of DeepSSInter, it is not recommended that users crop the protein structures at
978    the prediction time. If a very long protein (e.g. > 5000aa) causes an overflow of GPU memory
979    at the prediction time, users may run DeepSSInter on CPU. Nevertheless, if users really need to
980    crop a protein due to memory and/or speed reason at the prediction time, they may use a sliding
981    window strategy. In such cases, it is recommended to use a window size as long as allowed by
982    users’ computer or choose a window size that have an opportunity to cover more interface residues
983
       during sliding. As shown in Figure 6, if a cropped structure can cover more interface residues, the top
       predicted contact tends to have a higher contact probability, which may be used to guide the choice
984
       of a window size. Moreover, if a cropped structure can cover more interface residues, DeepSSInter
985
       also tends to have a higher precision in contact prediction (Figure 7). There results demonstrate the
986    feasibility of using a sliding window strategy for very long proteins at the prediction time.
