009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Many important phenomenon in scientific fields such as climate, neuroscience
012            and epidemiology are naturally represented as spatiotemporal gridded data with
013            complex interactions. Inferring causal relationships from these data is a difficult
014            problem compounded by the high dimensionality of such data and the correlations
015            between spatially proximate points. We present SPACY (SPAtiotemporal Causal
016            discoverY), a novel framework based on variational inference, designed to explic-
017            itly model latent time-series and their causal relationships from spatially confined
018
               modes in the data. Our method uses an end-to-end training process that maxi-
               mizes an evidence-lower bound (ELBO) for the data likelihood. Theoretically, we
019
               show that, under some conditions, the latent variables are identifiable up to trans-
020
               formation by an invertible matrix. Empirically, we show that SPACY outperforms
021            state-of-the-art baselines on synthetic data, remains scalable for large grids, and
022            identifies key known phenomena from real-world climate data.

      Several methods have been developed for causal structure learning from time-series data (Granger,
042   1969; Hyvärinen et al., 2010; Runge, 2020a; Tank et al., 2021; Gong Wenbo & Nick, 2022; Cheng
043   et al., 2023). However, applying these methods to spatiotemporal data presents significant chal-
044   lenges. The high dimensionality of large gridded data makes it difficult for many of these tech-
045   niques, especially those relying on conditional independence tests, to scale effectively (Glymour
046   et al., 2019). Additionally, spatially proximate points often exhibit highly correlated, redundant
047   time series. Conditioning on nearby correlated points can obscure true causal relationships between
048   distant locations, reducing statistical power and leading to inaccurate results (Tibau, 2022).
049   Recent advances in spatiotemporal causal discovery have sought to address these challenges. One
050   common approach is a two-stage process: first, dimensionality reduction is applied to extract a
051   small number of latent time series from the original grid of time series; then, causal discovery is
052   performed on these reduced-dimensional representations. Examples of this approach include Tibau
053   (2022) and Falasca et al. (2024). However, these methods perform dimensionality reduction inde-
      pendent of the causal structure, potentially leading to low-dimensional representations that obscure

054
      the relationships among causally relevant entities. Another important line of research is causal rep-
055   resentation learning from time series data (Schölkopf et al., 2021). While approaches like those in
056   Yao et al. (2022b;a); Chen et al. (2024) model latent time series from high-dimensional data, they
057   do not incorporate spatial priors, making them less suitable for spatiotemporal causal discovery.
058   Causal Discovery with Single-parent Decoding (CDSD) (Brouillard et al., 2024; Boussard et al.,
059   2023) learns a mapping from the observational time series to latent variables to infer the latent time
060   series. However, it assumes that each observed variable is influenced by only one latent variable.
061   We present a novel variational inference-based framework for spatial-temporal causal discovery
062   called SPAtio-temporal Causal DiscoverY (SPACY) to address these limitations (Figure 1). Our
063   approach jointly infers both the latent time series and the underlying causal graph in an end-to-
064   end process. The key idea of our approach is to learn the location and scale parameters of spatial
065   factors on the grid, which we model using Radial Basis Functions (RBFs). These spatial factors
066   determine the grid locations corresponding to each inferred latent time series. Additionally, we
067   analyze the identifiability of our framework. We demonstrate that when the grid is infinitely fine, we
068   can uniquely recover the spatial factors and latent time series (up to permutation) that generate the
069
      observed data distribution. Notably, compared to previous works, our framework can handle both
      instantaneous edges and overlapping spatial factors, allowing observed variables to be associated
070
      with multiple latent factors.

087   In this section, we provide an overview of the literature on causal discovery from time-series, causal
088   representation learning and spatiotemporal causal discovery.
089
090   Causal Discovery from time series data. A prominent line of research in time series causal dis-
091   covery is based on Granger causality, as introduced by Granger (1969). For example, Tank et al.
092   (2021) use component-wise MLPs and LSTMs with sparsity constraints to infer non-linear Granger
093
      causality. In contrast, Khanna & Tan (2020) apply Statistical Recurrent Units (SRUs) to detect causal
      relationships across multiple scales. Löwe et al. (2022) propose Amortized Causal Discovery using
094
      a variational autoencoder and Graph Neural Networks. Cheng et al. (2023; 2024) infers Granger
095
      causal links from irregularly sampled or incomplete data by simultaneously imputing missing val-
096   ues. However, Granger causality only captures predictive relationships and ignores instantaneous
097   effects, latent confounders, and history-dependent noise (Peters et al., 2017).
098
099
      The Structural Causal Model (SCM) framework can theoretically overcome these limitations by
      explicitly modeling the causal relationships between variables. Hyvärinen et al. (2010) extend
100
      LiNGAM (Shimizu et al., 2006) to develop VARLiNGAM, incorporating vector autoregressive
101
      models for time series data. DYNOTEARS (Pamfil et al., 2020) adapts NOTEARS (Zheng et al.,
102   2018) to dynamic Bayesian networks. Both methods, however, are restricted to linear relationships.
103   PCMCI and PCMCI+ (Runge et al., 2019; Runge, 2020a) extend the PC (Spirtes et al., 2000) algo-
104   rithm to handle instantaneous effects. Rhino (Gong Wenbo & Nick, 2022) uses neural networks to
105   model the functional relationships and estimates the temporal adjacency matrix from observational
106   data while accounting for exogenous history-dependent noise distributions. Wang et al. (2024) use
107   stochastic differential equations for causal structure learning from continuous-time temporal pro-
      cesses with potentially irregular sampling.

108
      However, applying these methods directly to spatiotemporal data presents significant challenges.
109   The high dimensionality of large gridded datasets makes it difficult for many techniques—especially
110   those that rely on conditional independence tests—to scale effectively (Glymour et al., 2019). Fur-
111   thermore, spatially proximate points often exhibit highly correlated and redundant time series. Con-
112   ditioning on these nearby correlated points can obscure true causal relationships between distant
113   locations, reducing statistical power and leading to inaccurate results (Tibau, 2022).
114
115
116   Spatiotemporal Causal Discovery/Causal Representation Learning Numerous studies have
117
      extended Granger causality to spatiotemporal settings, particularly in climate science (Mosedale
      et al., 2006; Kodra et al., 2011; Ali et al., 2024). Lozano et al. (2009) proposed a method combining
118
      Granger causality with a Group Elastic Net to capture spatial and temporal dependencies, enabling
119
      the identification of causal relationships among climate variables. However, the model assumes that
120   the causal relationships are linear, and only infers a summary graph.
121
122   One approach to spatiotemporal causal discovery is to perform dimensionality reduction to obtain
123
      a smaller number of latent time series and then infer a causal graph among the latent variables.
      For example, Tibau (2022) use Varimax for dimensionality reduction and PCMCI+ (Runge, 2018;
124
      2020b) for causal discovery. Falasca et al. (2024) infer regional modes based on correlation and
125
      spatial proximity, applying linear-response theory to uncover causal links. A key limitation of these
126   methods is that dimensionality reduction occurs independently of the causal structure in the data.
127   Consequently, the latent variables may not correspond to causally relevant entities. Additionally,
128   conditional independence-based methods are computationally intensive as they may require an ex-
129   ponential number of conditional independence tests.
130
      Other approaches use neural networks to model nonlinear interactions. The Spatial-Temporal Causal
131
      Discovery Framework (STCD) (Sheth et al., 2022) utilizes attention-based convolutional neural net-
132   works to identify causal relationships from gridded time-series data. However, it encodes an explicit
133   form of spatial dependence specific to the problem of hydrological systems (i.e. reduce attention
134   scores based on geographic height) rather than inferring it from data.
135
      Causal representation learning from time series involves inferring abstract, high-level causal vari-
136
      ables and their relationships from temporal data. Lippe et al. (2022; 2023) focus on causal repre-
137
      sentation learning from interventional time-series data. Yao et al. (2022b;a); Chen et al. (2024) in-
138   troduce frameworks to recover latent causal variables and identify their relations from observational
139   sequential data. However, these methods do not model instantaneous edges in the causal graph.
140   Morioka & Hyvarinen (2024) prove the identifiability of causal relations, even in the presence of in-
141   stantaneous edges, by assuming that the observational variables can be appropriately grouped. How-
142   ever, this grouping is rarely known in practice apriori. Moreover, none of these methods consider
143   the spatial structure present in the data. The work most closely related to ours is Causal Discovery
144   with Single Parent Decoding (CDSD) (Brouillard et al., 2024; Boussard et al., 2023), which learns
145   a mapping from the observational time series to latent variables. However, CDSD operates under
146
      the assumption that each observed variable is influenced by only one latent variable, and the causal
      graph has no instantaneous edges. In contrast, SPACY allows both instantaneous edges and over-
147
      lapping modes, i.e., an observational variable can be influenced by more than one latent variable.
148
149   Preliminaries. A Structural Causal Model (Pearl, 2009) (SCM) explicitly defines the causal rela-
150   tionships between variables in the form of functional equations. Formally, an SCM over D variables
151   consists of a 5-tuple ⟨X , ε, F, G, P (ε)⟩:
152
153        1. Endogenous (observed) variables X = {X 1 , X 2 , . . . , X D };
154
155        2. Exogenous (noise) variables ε = {ε1 , ε2 , . . . , εD } influencing the endogenous variables.
156
157        3. A Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) G, denoting the causal links amongst the members of X ;
158
159        4. A set of D functions F = {f 1 , f 2 , . . . , f D } determining X through the equations X i =
160           f i (PaiG , εi ), where PaiG ⊂ X denotes the parents of node i in graph G and εi ⊂ ε;
161
           5. P (ε), which describes a distribution over noise ε.

309   4    I DENTIFIABILITY A NALYSIS
310
311   In this section, we examine the identifiability of the generative model introduced in Section 3.1.
312   Roughly speaking, a model is said to be identifiable if the latent variables can be uniquely recovered
313   from observational data. Several prior works have investigated the identifiability of latent parameters
314
      in various deep generative models (Khemakhem et al., 2020; Zheng et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2022b).
315   We focus on the specific case where no non-linearity maps the latents to the observable space,
316   meaning gℓ in equation 6 is the identity map. To analyze identifiability, we extend the notion of
317   a gridded time series to infinite resolution. Instead of observing the time series at a finite set of
318   grid points, we assume it can be observed at every point within the bounded K-dimensional grid
319   G = [0, 1]K . In this framework, X(x) represents a T -dimensional random variable describing the
320
      observational time series at location x on the grid.
321   We also generalize our assumptions about how the spatial factors are generated, and assume that they
322   are function evaluations at the grid points of a family of linearly independent functions. Notably, the
323   family of RBF functions are one such family of functions (Smola & Schölkopf, 1998). To formalize
      this, we introduce the following definition.

331   The first result demonstrates that if two SFPs are equal at all grid points, they must share the same
332   spatial factors and latent time series, up to a permutation. This implies that the spatial factors and
333   latent time series are identifiable from the conditional likelihood log pθ (X(x)|Z, Fx ).
334   Theorem 1 (Identifiability of SFPs). Given two SFPs X = SFP(Z, F, pε ) and Y = SFP(Z̃, F̃, qε )
335   where none of the rows of Z or Z̃ are all zero, such that p(X(x)) = p(Y(x)) for every x ∈ G, then
336   Z = P Z̃ and F = F̃ for some permutation matrix P .

338   We now turn to the identifiability of the latent time series from the observational distribution. The
339   following result shows that the latent variable distribution can be recovered up to a transformation
340   by an invertible matrix. Although not as precise as Theorem 1, it still guarantees that the latents are
341   partially identifiable.
342   Theorem 2 (Identifiability of the latents). Suppose two spatial factor processes X(x) and X̃(x)
343   with spatial factors Fx and F̃x have the same observational distributions for all x ∈ G. Then the
344   latent variable distribution is identifiable up to transformation by an invertible matrix.

      Setup. Since real-world datasets lack ground truth causal graphs, we generate synthetic datasets

      with known causal relationships to benchmark SPACY’s causal discovery performance. These are

      generated from randomly constructed ground-truth graphs, following the forward model described

366   generated using either (1) a linear structural causal model (SCM) with randomly initialized weights

368   randomly initialized MLPs, combined with additive history-dependent conditional-spline noise.
369
      The mapping function gℓ is set as (1) linear, where the identity function is used, or (2) nonlinear,

      where an MLP is used. For each configuration, we generate N = 100 samples, each with time

372   and 30 nodes in each setting. For more details on dataset generation, refer to Appendix A.3.1.
373
374

527   6     C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work, we examined the problem of inferring causal relationships from spatiotemporal data.
530   This problem has significant applications in climate, neuroscience, and biomedical science, among
531   other fields. We proposed an end-to-end variational inference method to learn the latent causal
532   representations and the underlying SCM, while producing an interpretable output. We discussed the
533   structural identifiability of our model, and demonstrated the empirical efficacy of our method on
534   both synthetic and simulated climate datasets. SPACY successfully recovers spatial patterns linked
535   to known events like the Northern Atlantic Oscillation and Antarctic Oscillation.
536   As a direction for future work, our method can be extended to multivariate settings. Performing
537   latent causal representation learning and causal discovery between multiple variables could further
538   enhance the capability of our approach in handling complex real-world datasets. Such an extension
539   would be particularly valuable in domains like climate science (Tibau, 2022; Brouillard et al., 2024),
      where interactions between multiple variables (e.g. temperature and pressure) are critical.

1088
1089   Freezing Latent Causal Modules. To stabilize the training and ensure accurate causal discovery,
1090   we freeze the parameters of the latent SCM and causal graph, and only train the spatial factors
1091   and encoder for the first 200 epochs. This allows the spatial factor parameters to be learned without
1092   interference from incorrect causal relationships in the latent space. Once these modules are unfrozen

       A.2.4    E VALUATION D ETAILS
1099
1100   The mean correlation coefficient (MCC) is adapted as a measure of alignment between the inferred
1101   and true latent variables, widely used in causal representation learning works (Yao et al., 2022b;a).
1102   Here, MCC is computed as the mean of the correlation coefficients between each pair of true and
1103   inferred latent variables, providing a balanced metric that captures how well the inferred variables
1104   match the true underlying causal structure.
1105   To evaluate the accuracy of inferred causal graphs and representations, we match the nodes of the
1106   inferred graph to the ground truth using a permutation-invariant approach. Specifically, we apply
1107   the Hungarian algorithm to find the optimal permutation of nodes that aligns the inferred graph’s
1108   adjacency matrix with the ground truth, minimizing the discrepancies between them. This optimal
1109   permutation is then used to calculate both the F1 Score and the Mean Correlation Coefficient (MCC),
1110   providing consistent node alignment across these metrics.

1338
1339   The Global Temperature Dataset is a comprehensive, mixed real-simulated dataset encompassing
1340   monthly global temperature data spanning the years 1999 to 2001. It contains 7531 simulated sam-
1341   ples, each with a time sequence of 24 months, covering the entire globe at a fine spatial resolution.
1342   The grid size is 145 × 192, which corresponds to a spatial division of approximately 1.24◦ latitude
1343   and 1.875◦ longitude. This spatial resolution allows the dataset to provide detailed global coverage,
1344   capturing temperature variations across diverse geographical regions. The resulting data dimensions
1345   are 7531 × 24 × 145 × 192, representing the total number of samples, the temporal sequence, and
1346
       the spatial grid, respectively.
1347   To facilitate causal analysis of complex climate phenomena beyond seasonal patterns, a de-
1348   seasonality procedure was applied. This normalization process involved computing the monthly
1349   mean for each month across all years and then adjusting the data accordingly (for example, normal-
       izing all January data by the mean of all January values). This approach aims to remove regular

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

       seasonal influences, thereby emphasizing more intricate climate events and enabling deeper causal
1351   learning and understanding of global temperature dynamics.

       These representations capture regions of similar weather properties and help identify causal links
       between these regions and weather phenomena occurring elsewhere. The methodology uses a linear
1355
       functional relationship paired with multi-layer perceptron (MLP) spatial decoding. Specifically, we
