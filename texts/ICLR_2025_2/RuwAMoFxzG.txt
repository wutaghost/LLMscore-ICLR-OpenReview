011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Expanding the application of large language models (LLMs) to societal life, in-
014            stead of primary function only as auxiliary assistants to communicate with only
015
               one person at a time, necessitates LLMs’ capabilities to independently play roles
               in multi-user, multi-turn social agent tasks within complex social settings. How-
016
               ever, currently the capability has not been systematically measured with avail-
017
               able benchmarks. To address this gap, we first introduce an agent task leveling
018            framework grounded in sociological principles. Concurrently, we propose a novel
019            benchmark, How Social Is It (we call it HSII below), designed to assess LLM’s
020            social capabilities in comprehensive social agents tasks and benchmark represen-
021            tative models. HSII comprises four stages: format parsing, target selection, tar-
022            get switching conversation, and stable conversation, which collectively evaluate
023            the communication and task completion capabilities of LLMs within realistic so-
024            cial interaction scenarios dataset, HSII-Dataset. The dataset is derived step by
025            step from news dataset. We perform an ablation study by doing clustering to
026
               the dataset. Additionally, we investigate the impact of chain of thought (COT)
               method on enhancing LLMs’ social performance. Since COT cost more compu-
027
               tation, we further introduce a new statistical metric, COT-complexity, to quantify
028
               the efficiency of certain LLMs with COTs for specific social tasks and strike a bet-
029            ter trade-off between measurement of correctness and efficiency. Various results
030            of our experiments demonstrate that our benchmark is well-suited for evaluating
031            social skills in LLMs.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large language models (LLMs) enhance their expressive and reasoning capabilities through an in-
037   crease in model parameters, depth, and breadth. They exhibit robust knowledge retention and rea-
038   soning abilities, and are continuously evolving. Recent surveys (Zhao et al., 2024), (Minaee et al.,
039
      2024), and (Gao et al., 2023b) provide comprehensive and detailed insights into this evolution. In
      practical applications, LLMs have shown significant potential across various domains, contributing
040
      notably to multi-agent systems (Han et al., 2024), (Guo et al., 2024a), (He et al., 2024), digital hu-
041
      mans (Yang et al., 2024), (Zhang et al., 2023), embodied intelligence (Li et al., 2024b), (Song et al.,
042   2023), education, intelligent customer service (Xu et al., 2024), (Shi et al., 2024), and code genera-
043   tion (Jiang et al., 2024), (Hassid et al., 2024), bringing artificial intelligence closer to everyday life.
044   However, when compared to internet technology, which has become ubiquitous in social interactions
045   through iterative development, there remains a more discernible gap between LLMs developer and
046   non-developer accessibility. For instance, LLMs usually struggle to communicate independently
047   with customers without supervision and do not excel in roles such as daily butler services or manag-
048   ing comprehensive company operations beyond simple tasks. Beyond the underutilization of current
049   computational power, a significant reason may be the lack of capabilities in LLMs to independently
050
      and skillfully interact in complex social scenarios. To examine those possibilities we need to do
      precise evaluation to social capabilities.
051
052   To study LLMs’ capabilities in complex social tasks is also essential for enhancing LLM sociology
053   analysis. Recent exploration of the rationality and biological traits of LLMs, as discussed in Chen
      et al. (2023c) and Lyu et al. (2024), has been complemented by research simulating virtual societies

054
      and systems through dialogues among multiple LLMs. This research aims to analyze their social
055   attributes and perform a social division of labor, as exemplified in works such as Gurcan (2024),
056   Dai et al. (2024), and Gao et al. (2023a). These endeavors, however, may be constrained by their
057   idealized scenario settings, which limit the degree of realism (Zhou et al., 2024a). By building
058   more complex and closer-to-reality agent tasks in social scenes based on sociology theory, and then
059   benchmarking above them we may get sociology about LLM more solid.
060
      Up to date the significance of LLMs’ interpersonal communication skills has become gradually rec-
061   ognized, but current benchmarks have not fully covered this. To evaluate these skills, some works
062   such as SOTOPIA-EVAL (Zhou et al., 2024b) and MUCA (Mao et al., 2024) have been made.
063   SOTOPIA-EVAL focuses on designing scenarios to assess social intelligence through role-playing,
064   comparing models against human performance. MUCA, on the other hand, simulates group inter-
065   actions to establish a framework for determining chat targets’ interactions with specified objects.
066   Both works highlight the importance of multi-user dialogue in social relationships and the need for
067   evaluation. Yet, currently there has been no work to bridge social dialogue scenarios with traditional
068   dyadic dialogue assessments, explore their interrelation from a sociological perspective, and build a
069
      systematical benchmark for overall evaluation in all social capability dimensions.Moreover, main-
      stream evaluation frameworks, including thep (Chen et al., 2024) arena (Chiang et al., 2024), GPQA
070
      (Rein et al., 2023), and security assessment frameworks (Zhang et al., 2024c) (Li et al., 2024a),
071
      also do not explicitly assess social communication capabilities as a distinct dimension alongside
072   mathematical, coding, and other critical thinking skills.
073
074   As our first approach to assess social competencies effectively, it is imperative to dissect the foun-
075
      dational elements of social interaction through a sociological lens and reinterpret them within the
      framework of LLMs. Our approach is bolstered by seminal sociological works that analyze social
076
      dynamics (Goffman, 1959; Duncan, 1972; Clark & Brennan, 1991), alongside contemporary inves-
077
      tigations into LLMs from a sociological standpoint (Dai et al., 2024; Lan et al., 2024). Furthermore,
078   the field of artificial intelligence, especially multi-agent systems, has provided valuable insights
079   into the analysis and reconstruction of hierarchical systems (Li et al., 2019). By integrating these
080   perspectives, we introduce a tiered division of tasks for social agents, categorizing them into four
081   distinct levels: the first being fundamental and well-explored, the subsequent two being relatively
082   autonomous, and the final level representing an integration of the former two.
083
      With novel LLM sociology framework We endeavor to develop high-caliber evaluation datasets
084   akin to established benchmarks. Deviating from the common practice of repurposing existing LLM
085   datasets, we opt to initiate our dataset construction with manually and fairly reviewed-filtered real
086   news data. The news data is algorithmically clustered and detoxified to capture a more authen-
087   tic and representative cross-section of real-world social scenarios. Leveraging the comprehension
088   and summarization prowess of the GPT4 model (OpenAI et al., 2024), we refine this data further.
089   Subsequently, human evaluators curate and amend the data based on predefined criteria, yielding
090   a collection of social scenarios featuring multiple participants and a spectrum of conflicts. The
091   presence of heightened conflict is instrumental in rigorously testing the models’ capabilities within
092
      intricate social contexts. This process culminates in the creation of a multi-user multi-turn dialogue
      dataset that is intrinsically linked to these scenarios.
093
094   In this study, we delve into the nature of evaluation methodologies for models in multi-user multi-
095   turn social tasks and propose a novel metric HSII score. A recent study (Ren et al., 2024) introduces
096   a framework aimed at enhancing social norms. Furthermore, Mao et al. (Mao et al., 2024) emphasize
097   the importance of selecting an interlocutor and crafting dialogue content in multi-turn conversations.
098
      This involves determining ”who to converse with and what to convey,” while also establishing an
      interaction pattern across multiple users through extended dialogues. Building on these insights, we
099
      have designed a four-tiered evaluation protocol: prompting the model to respond with certain format,
100
      enabling the model to select from a broader array of potential interlocutors within our curated multi-
101   turn dialogue dataset, articulating transitions between turns, and ensuring the continuity and stability
102   of the dialogue post-switch, all of whose results was compiled up to compute the final HSII score.
103   In this track we carry out our experiments on LLMs and propose our discoveries.
104

127   Social Relationship and Social Scene. Crafting social scenarios and interactions involves several
128   critical elements and stages. Social scenarios are intricately woven from components such as set-
129   ting, participants, and behavioral norms(Goffman, 1959). The process of social interaction generally
130   unfolds through stages including initiation, development, and termination(Duncan, 1972). Dialogue
131   acts involve the selection of conversational targets and the management of transitions between differ-
132   ent speakers(Clark & Brennan, 1991). Navigating multi-turn dialogues with a single conversational
133
      target necessitates the application of adjacency pairs and the preservation of topical coherence(Clark
      & Schaefer, 1987).
134
135   Agent Task Stratification. Within the academic community, significant progress has been made
136   in the field of agent task stratification. This process involves the systematic breakdown of com-
137   plex missions into smaller, more tractable subtasks, which is essential for the effective operation of
138   Multi-Agent Systems (MAS). For instance, Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (HRL)(Li et al.,
139
      2019) tackles the challenges posed by sparse rewards and complex environments by decomposing
      intricate tasks into simpler subtasks. Meta-Task Planning (MTP)(Zhang et al., 2024a), a strategy for
140
      simplifying complex task planning in collaborative, LLM-based MAS, furthers this approach by de-
141
      composing tasks into a sequence of subordinate tasks, or meta-tasks, which are then translated into
142   actionable steps. AI Agents can be classified into a spectrum of levels, ranging from Level 0 (non-
143   AI, basic tools) to Level 5 (highly advanced agents exhibiting personality and cooperative interac-
144   tion)(Huang, 2024). Each ascending level integrates additional modules and functionalities, thereby
145   augmenting the AI capabilities and utility of the agents. The SMART-LLM framework(Kannan
146   et al., 2023) exemplifies this progression, applying LLMs to multi-robot task planning. It translates
147   high-level directives into actionable multi-robot plans through a systematic process that includes
148   task decomposition, coalition formation, and task allocation.
149   LLM Intelligent Agent Application Evaluation and Benchmarking. Evaluating large AI models
150   necessitates a rigorous methodology to assess their performance, robustness, and reliability(NIST,
151   2022). This evaluation is essential for guaranteeing that models adhere to benchmarks of safety,
152   efficacy, and ethical standards prior to their operational deployment(Committee, 2024). Prominent
153   benchmarks for LLM assessment include ImageNet(Russakovsky et al., 2015), a seminal bench-
154   mark for computer vision models, GLUE(Wang et al., 2019a), which evaluates natural language
155   understanding, and ArenaBench(Kastner et al., 2022), a comprehensive benchmark designed to as-
156
      sess AI systems across a spectrum of tasks and environments(MLSys, 2020). These benchmarks are
      instrumental in promoting transparency and propelling the evolution of AI technology.

      In practical applications, dialogue systems often encounter scenarios where a single agent must en-
232
      gage with multiple distinct users, each requiring tailored responses. This dynamic unfolds across
233   multiple dialogue turns, with target transitions facilitating the switch between interlocutors. For
234   instance, in a school setting like a parent-teacher conference, various targets like parents, students,
235   teachers, principals are involved. Certain information, such as a student’s report card, is restricted
236   to specific parties. A straightforward solution is to have a single intelligent assistant handle these
237   diverse needs simultaneously, representing the temporal relationships through a unified target. How-
238   ever, challenges arise in complex social scenarios where the needs of multiple parties are interdepen-
239   dent. For example, an intelligent assistant may need to interact with the parents of high-achieving
240   students and teachers to glean effective learning strategies first, which can then inform advice for
241
      parents of struggling students. In such cases, the assistant must assess the priority of responding to
      different targets, drawing on the social dynamics of real-world conversations (Choi et al., 2020)(Per-
242
      alta et al., 2022)(Adams et al., 2022), and generate utterances based on the unique information per-
243
      tinent to the selected target. In our approach we focus on the evaluation of LLMs’ performance in
244   those complex scenes, separately in single-turn and with COT form, under different requirements.

      5.2.2    E VALUATION PIPELINE
325
326   In our proposed evaluation framework HSII, the multi-user dialogue capabilities of a LLM agent
327   are rigorously assessed through both objective and subjective measures. Main evaluation pipeline is
328   displayed in 2b.
329   The objective evaluation focuses on accuracy of target selection, which is quantified by calculating
330   the proportion of correct next-target selections made by test LLM across all test cases. The subjective
331   evaluation assesses the quality of the first-utterance and long-run statements generated by the model.
332   Here we adopt the win rate metric introduced in ToolBench (Qin et al., 2023) to gauge the overall
333   performance. There is one difference that we adopt both GPT4 and human-eval to get final win
334   rate. Incorrect selections result in no score, as they lead to an invalid dialogue sequence by the LLM
335   agent. But if in later phase the response is unfavorable, some score may still be awarded for correct
336   selection. In appendix section we provide a rough theoretical foundation and sociology meaning for
337
      this approach.

503   7   C ONCLUSION
504
505   In this research, we focus on evaluating the social communication capabilities of Large Language
506   Models (LLMs) within multi-user, multi-turn real-world social contexts. To enhance our assessment
507   of model adaptation to social scenarios and to potentially facilitate the integration of LLMs into real-
508   life applications, we develop a novel framework HSII. This framework is grounded in traditional
509   sociological theory and designed for overall social scenes. It complements the basic single-turn so-
510   cial evaluations with the complex scenarios. We harness the untapped potential of news source data
511   to create the first multi-user multi-turn dataset that extensively covers real-life dialogue scenarios
512
      characterized by complexity and conflicts among various personas. Furthermore, we introduce a
      new statistical metrics, termed How Social Is It (HSII) overall score, to quantify LLMs’ capability
513
      in navigating the challenging social scenes. This metric is derived from the discrimination bound of
514
      grading models at different stages. Then our focus also extends to the approach of COT to enhance
515   model performance, an methodology that has been neglected in some previous benchmarks. To this
516   end, we define a second novel metric, COT complexity, to measure the efficiency of LLMs when
517   prompted and reflecting on certain social scenarios under a set of COTs. Based on the construction
518   above, We detail the construction pipeline of our dataset and elucidate the workings of the entire
519   evaluation process. Subsequently, we conduct evaluations on our benchmark using several repre-
520   sentative LLMs and compare their performance with human beings, yielding novel and fresh results
521   from these experiments. Looking forward, a compelling direction for future work is to expand the
522   scale of our dataset and to test LLMs with a more diverse range. Additionally, probing the cur-
523
      rent capabilities of LLMs in social contexts presents a promising path for gaining insights into how
      LLMs perceive different characters, the roles they should assume in society, and how these roles
524
      might evolve.

      A.3    D ETAILED DEFINITION OF C OMPLEX TASK AGENT TASK LEVELS .
811
812   Building upon the single-user foundational task, composite tasks are defined as encompassing three
813   categories of agent enhancement tasks that may intercombine and nest within each other: multi-
814   agents, multi-users, and multi-tasks. Multi-agents: Multiple LLM agents collaborate to jointly
815   accomplish a single task (Han et al., 2024)(Hong et al., 2023)(Chen et al., 2023a). Multi-users: A
816   single LLM agent serves and caters to multiple users, where each conversation requires determining
817   which user to engage with currently, what to say to facilitate the transition between conversational
818   roles, and how to better achieve a Pareto optimality in the interests of all roles after the transition is
819
      completed. Multi-tasks: A single LLM agent performs a variety of tasks, for example, generaliz-
      ability Tan et al. (2023)(Chen et al., 2023b).
820

822   A.4    F URTHER DIVISION OF MULTI - USER TASKS
823
      Following discussions in main-text, multi-user tasks can be further divided into two main types. The
824
      multi-user scenario is characterized as a multi-turn dialogue scenario where an intelligent agent,
825
      based on a Large Language Model (LLM), addresses two or more distinct users within a unified task.
826   Specifically, there are two types: Multi-stage multi-user scenarios, where each stage is dedicated to
827   a single user. In such scenarios, synchronous communication with multiple users is effectively re-
828   duced to a single-user communication process with an embedded memory component. e.g., Grocery
829   shopping task: Owner-Buy groceries-Owner, Room reservation task: Owner-Administrator-Owner.
830   Single-stage multi-user scenarios, where the agent engages in simultaneous conversation with mul-
831   tiple users within the same stage. These scenarios require adherence to a structured (potentially
832   interdependent) decision-making sequence, which includes determining the addressee, the timing of
833   speech, the content of the dialogue, and the subsequent actions.

892   B.4     DATA S TRUCTURE
893
894   Crafting scenarios for social agent dialogues necessitates a rich social context to capture multi-user
895   interactions and the dynamics of multi-turn conversations. To enhance the authenticity and diversity
896   of our scenarios, we amalgamated data from three distinct sources:
897
898
               • Native Dialogues We leverage existing datasets known for their multi-turn dialogues and
                 target-switching features. Specifically, we meticulously curate and refine data from sources
899
                 such as the Doc2Dial dataset(Feng et al., 2020) and multi-person chat forums to extract
900
                 high-quality multi-turn dialogues.
901
902            • Dataset Adaptation This strategy involves augmenting existing datasets to enrich our
903              training material. The process begins with scenario generation using GPT to simulate
904              a spectrum of personalities. Subsequently, we orchestrate dialogue turns from various
905
                 datasets, delineating user transitions and personality shifts through a meticulous tagging
                 and alignment protocol. But currently this part of data has not been added to HSII dataset.
906
907            • Thematic Dialogues Recognizing the paucity of multi-user, multi-turn dialogues in current
908              Document Based Dialog (DBD) datasets, which predominantly feature two-party interac-
909              tions, we introduce a novel framework. This framework facilitates the creation of thematic,
910              multi-object, multi-turn dialogues derived from real-world scenarios and news narratives.

1245   In addressing the intricacies of social issues, we posit two foundational assumptions for our analysis:
1246
       1. In a scenario encompassing N potential dialogue subjects, the selection process of a LLM agent
1247
       across various dialogue targets is structured into distinct rounds. Each dialogue round is conducted
1248   sequentially and in its entirety with each interaction target. For those deemed non-essential for
1249   the current round, we employ placeholder dialogues and return statements. Consequently, the
1250   initial selection of specific dialogue targets, denoted as i1 , i2 , i3 , . . . , ik , is expanded to include
1251   i1 , i2 , i3 , . . . , ik , ik+1 , . . . , in , where ik+1 to in represent empty dialogues. This modification en-
1252   sures an unbiased selection process.
1253
       2. We acknowledge the existence of a benchmark model π, which serves as a standard for object
1254   selection and dialogue generation in practical applications. Additionally, we introduce an evaluation
1255   model π ′ , which is assumed to align closely with π in most contexts. However, π ′ exhibits a minor
1256   deviation δ when applied to social agent scenarios, relative to the overall capability of the model.
1257   Formally, this relationship is expressed as π ′ = πδ , where the discrepancy between the two models
1258   is quantified as |π − πδ | ≈ δ.
1259
       Within this framework, we meticulously analyze the three distinct phases of social communication
1260   behavior exhibited by the aforementioned large model agent.
1261
1262   Stage One: Dialogue Order Selection. Initially, the model discerns the dialogue sequence
1263
       o1 , o2 , o3 , . . . , on for the current round from a pool of N potential dialogue subjects, represented
       as x1 , x2 , . . . , xn .
1264
1265   Stage Two: Sequential Dialogue Engagement. Subsequently, in each iteration of the round, the
1266   model initiates dialogue with the selected subjects in accordance with the order established in Stage
1267   One. These dialogues are represented as s1 , s2 , s3 , . . . , sn .
1268   Stage Three: Multi-Round Dialogue Interaction. Finally, the model partakes in multi-round
1269   dialogues with a particular subject, with the outcomes of these interactions across m rounds denoted
1270   as s′1 , p1 , s′2 , p2 , . . . , s′m , pm .

1272

1273   ⃗oδ = πδ (⃗s, ⃗x). The set of feasible dialogue orders, ⃗o and ⃗oδ , encompasses all conceivable permuta-
1274    tions, which is left to be defined in the subsequent sections.

       Proceeding to the second stage, we draw upon the straight-forward theoretical proof below to infer
       that any two distinct orders within the set of all possible permutations can be interconverted through
1277
       dialogues involving multiple pairs of dialogue subjects, denoted as i and j.
1278
1279   Theorem 1. For any two permutations σ and τ on the set {1, 2, . . . , n}, there exists a sequence of
1280   pairwise swaps that transforms σ into τ .
1281
1282   Proof. 1. Base Case When n = 1, there is only one element, and the two permutations are identical,
1283   requiring no swaps. 2. Inductive Step Assume that for all permutations with fewer than n elements,
1284   the theorem holds. We need to show that it also holds for permutations of n elements. We consider
1285   permutations σ and τ . Then we find an element a in σ that is in a different position from τ . Suppose
1286
       a is at position i in σ and at position j in τ . By a series of swaps, move a to the j-th position in σ.
       This can be achieved by swapping a with the elements in front of it until it reaches the j-th position.
1287
       Now, a is in the same position in both σ and τ . We can ignore a and consider the remaining n − 1
1288
       elements. By the inductive hypothesis, the permutation of the remaining n − 1 elements can be
1289   transformed into the corresponding permutation in τ through a series of pairwise swaps. Therefore,
1290   the entire permutation σ can be transformed into τ through a series of pairwise swaps.
