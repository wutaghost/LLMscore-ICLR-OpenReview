011                                                       A BSTRACT
012
013                Despite recent advancements in large language models (LLMs), their performance
014                on complex reasoning problems requiring multi-step thinking and combining var-
015                ious skills is still limited. To address this, we propose a novel framework HDFlow
016
                   for complex reasoning with LLMs that combines fast and slow thinking modes
                   in an adaptive manner. Our approach consists of two key components: 1) a new
017
                   approach for slow, deliberate reasoning called Dynamic Workflow, which auto-
018
                   matically decomposes complex problems into more manageable sub-tasks and
019                dynamically designs a workflow to assemble specialized LLM or symbolic rea-
020                soning tools to solve sub-tasks; 2) Hybrid Thinking, a general framework that dy-
021                namically combines fast and slow thinking based on problem complexity. Finally,
022                we propose an easy-to-scale method for automatically synthesizing a large-scale
023                dataset of 27K challenging reasoning problems for complex reasoning and a hy-
024                brid thinking tuning method that trains smaller LLMs on this dataset to internalize
025                the fast/slow hybrid reasoning strategies. Experiments on four reasoning bench-
026                mark datasets demonstrate that our slow thinking with dynamic workflows signif-
027
                   icantly outperforms Chain-of-Thought, and hybrid thinking achieves the highest
                   accuracy while providing an effective balance between computational efficiency
028
                   and performance. Fine-tuning using our hybrid thinking approach also signifi-
029
                   cantly boosts the complex reasoning capabilities of open-source language models.
030                The results showcase the promise of slow thinking, dynamic workflows, and hy-
031                brid thinking in expanding the frontier of complex problem-solving with LLMs1 .

034   1       I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a wide range of
037   tasks, from code generation and mathematical reasoning to natural language understanding and gen-
038   eration. Recent advancements in symbolic reasoning and tool usage, such as AlphaGeometry (Trinh
039   et al., 2024) and AlphaProof (AlphaProof/AlphaGeometry teams), have shown significant improve-
040   ments in specific domains by integrating LLMs with specialized procedures and symbolic reasoning
041
      engines. Various prompting strategies, such as Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022), Tree
      of Thoughts (ToT) (Yao et al., 2024), and Graph of Thoughts (GoT) (Besta et al., 2024a), have
042
      been developed to enable different reasoning topologies to enhance LLM problem-solving capabili-
043
      ties. However, enhancing the reasoning abilities of LLMs to solve complex problems across various
044   domains in a unified framework remains a challenge for expanding their real-world applicability.
045
046   First, complex problem-solving often requires combining various knowledge domains, skills, and
047
      tool usage. While previous approaches such as AlphaCodium (Ridnik et al., 2024) and Alpha-
      geometry (Trinh et al., 2024) have demonstrated the potential of combining language models and
048
      symbolic reasoning to solve complex problems, they rely on manually designed workflows tailored
049
      to specific domains (i.e., competitive programming or geometry theorem proving). The language
050   model and symbolic engine take predefined turns in a rigid problem-solving process. This limits the
051   applicability and adaptability of these systems to broader domains. Thus, we aim to enhance the
052   generic problem-solving capabilities of LLMs by dynamically alternating between natural language

055   lem at hand. This dynamic integration of the two reasoning modes enables the system to address
056   a much broader range of problems and adapt the problem-solving process to the unique require-
057   ments of each task. Second, traditional approaches to complex problem-solving with LLMs often
058   rely on a single mode of thinking, which may struggle with more intricate tasks that demand a de-
059   liberate, analytical approach. For example, many approaches employ a fixed reasoning strategy,
060   such as CoT prompting, regardless of the problem’s complexity. For instance, OpenAI’s most re-
061   cent o1 model2 only engages in a singular deep thinking mode despite the complexity of the user’s
062   query. This can lead to suboptimal performance on tasks requiring varying reasoning levels. The

      While multi-agent frameworks such as AutoGPT (Significant Gravitas), ReAct Yao et al. (2022),
064
      and AutoGen (Wu et al., 2023) have addressed some aspects of this challenge by enabling recursive
065
      goal decomposition, interleaving reasoning and acting, and state-driven workflows, they do not fully
066   exploit the potential of thinking approaches that can switch between intuitive thinking and more an-
067   alytical thinking modes based on problem complexity. Finally, as problem complexity increases, the
068   performance of existing approaches tends to degrade significantly, highlighting the need for frame-
069   works that can scale to handle even the most challenging reasoning problems. Recently, OpenAI
070   o1 model (OpenAI, 2024) demonstrates the potential to consistently improve LLM performance of
071   complex reasoning with compute scaling in inference-time through deep thinking.
072   To address these limitations, we propose a novel framework for complex reasoning with LLMs
073   that combines fast (system I) and more analytical slow thinking (system II) adaptively, inspired
074   by the dual process theory of human cognition (Daniel, 2017). Our approach consists of two key
075   components. First, we introduce a new approach for slow, deliberate reasoning called Dynamic
076   Workflow, which automatically decomposes complex problems into more manageable sub-tasks. It
077   then dynamically designs a workflow to assemble specialized LLM or symbolic tools to solve each
078   sub-task. To achieve this, the dynamic workflow orchestrates a team of specialized LLM experts,
079   each contributing unique domain knowledge or tool usage, to solve the sub-tasks in a structured
080
      manner. Second, we propose Hybrid Thinking, a general framework that dynamically combines
      fast and slow thinking based on problem complexity. For simpler tasks, the model defaults to a
081
      fast-thinking mode using CoT strategy. When the model’s confidence in the fast thinking output is
082
      low, it automatically switches to slow thinking with dynamic workflow, allowing for more efficient
083   and more accurate problem-solving. Finally, to train local LLMs for complex reasoning, we present
084   an easy-to-scale method for automatically synthesizing a large-scale dataset of 27K challenging
085   reasoning problems and propose a hybrid thinking tuning approach that finetunes open-source LLMs
086   on this dataset, enabling them to internalize the fast/slow hybrid reasoning strategies.
087
      We conduct experiments on four reasoning benchmark datasets (i.e., BBH (Suzgun et al., 2022),
088
      MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), Game of 24 Yao et al. (2024), DeepMind Math (Saxton et al.,
089   2019). Experiments using GPT-4-Turbo reveal that slow thinking with dynamic workflows sig-
090   nificantly outperformed CoT, with an average accuracy improvement of 22.4%. Hybrid thinking,
091   which combines fast and slow thinking, achieved the highest accuracy on three of the four datasets
092   and struck an effective balance between computational efficiency and performance. Furthermore,
093   fine-tuning Llama-3-8B-Instruct using hybrid thinking significantly boosted performance across all
094   datasets compared to the original model. Overall, the results demonstrate the promise of slow think-
095   ing with dynamic workflows and hybrid thinking in enhancing the complex problem-solving abilities
096   of LLMs.

191   4       S LOW T HINKING WITH DYNAMIC W ORKFLOW
192
193   In contrast to the rapid responses of fast thinking (e.g., CoT), our new slow-thinking mechanism
194   applies dynamic workflow to enable a more deliberate, analytical approach to complex problem-
195   solving (see Figure 2). It allows an LLM to dynamically transition between reasoning in the text
196
      space (natural language reasoning) and the symbolic space (symbolic reasoning). The high-level
      idea is we first let the LLM decompose the original reasoning problem into several more manageable
197
      sub-tasks and solve each sub-task to form the final solution. When necessary, the LLM Engine will
198
      translate the sub-problem from the text space into the symbolic space, enabling the symbolic engine3
199   to perform precise symbolic reasoning. The results are then mapped back into natural language using
200   the LLM Engine. By decomposing the problem, combining the strengths of both natural language
201   and symbolic reasoning in a tailored workflow, and executing it from start to finish, LLMs can
202   tackle very hard problems that require multiple steps of accurate reasoning. Appendix B presents a
203   complete example solution using our dynamic workflow approach and compares with the solution
204   using OpenAI o1-preview. Prompts used are listed in Appendix C.
205
206   4.1       B REAKING D OWN C OMPLEXITY: P ROBLEM A NALYSIS AND D ECOMPOSITION (S TAGE 1)
207
208   The first step in our slow thinking is problem analysis and planning. We aim to break down the
209   original problem statement into more manageable sub-tasks. Specifically, the LLM is asked to
210   analyze the key elements of the query, such as available information, constraints, and the desired
211   output. It then identifies logical sub-goals needed to progress from the initial state to the solution.
212   This decomposition allows the LLM to approach the problem in a structured manner, focusing on
213   one part at a time. Therefore, the LLM can catch gaps in reasoning and handle complex problems
214
      that the fast thinking of CoT alone would struggle with.

      Problem Reflection. The first step in tackling complex problems is conducting a thorough problem
217   reflection. This involves the LLM analyzing the original problem and restating it in its own words
218   to demonstrate understanding. Our problem reflection includes two parts: 1) Identifying the core
219   objective or question posed by the problem. 2) Recognizing any constraints, assumptions, or special
220   conditions mentioned. By internalizing the problem through reflection, the LLM can gain a solid
221   understanding of what needs to be accomplished before proceeding to decomposition.
222
      Subtask Decomposition. Once the problem is well understood, the LLM is instructed to perform
223   a multi-level decomposition to break it down into some tractable sub-problems. The LLM is asked
224   to follow four principles to achieve an optimal decomposition. Sequential dependency. The sub-
225   problems are organized in a logical sequence, such that the outputs of earlier steps feed into subse-
226   quent ones, creating a structured workflow from start to finish. Non-overlapping. Each sub-problem
227   represents a distinct portion of the original problem, with no duplication of work between sub-
228   problems. This keeps the overall solution efficient. Proper Decomposition. The sub-problems are
229   decomposed to the optimal level of granularity - not so small that there are too many to track and
230   coordinate, but not so large that they are still struggling to solve. Modular. Where appropriate,
231
      sub-problems are defined in a generalizable, modular way, such that the logic and code used to solve
      them can potentially be reused to solve similar problems in other contexts.
232
233   Integrating Symbolic Reasoning. Another key aspect of our approach is leveraging the symbolic
234   engines to modularize the solution and handle well-defined sub-tasks more accurately. For example,
235   some sub-tasks in the decomposition can often be addressed by writing code functions. Therefore,
236   we explicitly instruct the LLM to consider sub-tasks that can be well handled by writing and exe-
237
      cuting modular code in subtask decomposition.

239   4.2    O RCHESTRATING E XPERTISE : W ORKFLOW D ESIGN (S TAGE 2)
240
241
      With the problem decomposed into sub-tasks, our approach next proposes a team of specialized
      experts, each contributing unique skills and tools, arranged in a dynamic workflow. The central
242
      component is a Meta-Expert, initialized from the foundation LLM, designs the expert team, and
243
      coordinates their efforts. The orchestration process consists of four steps.
244
245          1. Design of Experts. Based on the identified sub-tasks, the Meta-Expert designs a team of
246             specialized experts with one expert solving one sub-task. Each expert is assigned a unique
247             name and a clear description of their specific skills, knowledge, and responsibilities4 . The
248             dynamic workflow leverages two types of experts to handle each sub-task, enabling a seam-
249             less integration of verbal and symbolic reasoning. The first type are specialized experts
250             initiated from LLMs, such as linguists, mathematicians, and data scientists. These experts
251             bring domain-specific knowledge and skills to the workflow, allowing for sophisticated
252
                verbal reasoning and analysis within their fields. The second type of expert focuses on
                symbolic reasoning, particularly using programming or other symbolic engines5 . For ex-
253
                ample, some sub-tasks can often be addressed by writing compact, targeted code functions.
254
                This allows the LLM to handle common operations such as mathematical calculations, data
255             parsing and manipulation, and so on without bringing errors.
256
257
             2. Workflow Arrangement. The Meta-Expert arranges the experts into an efficient workflow
                sequence. Each expert’s output serves as the input for the next, progressively moving
258
                towards the final solution. The Meta-Expert ensures there is no redundancy of functions
259
                across experts.
260
261          3. Collaboration and Iteration. As the experts work through the problem, the Meta-Expert
262             facilitates collaboration and puts together their inputs and outputs. For sub-tasks involving
263
                logical reasoning, mathematical operations, data structures, or programming, the Meta-
                Expert provides strategic guidance and sends the implementation details to the correspond-
264
                ing symbolic reasoning experts. These experts utilize LLMs to generate code, which is
265
                then executed to perform symbolic reasoning in Stage 3.

288   4.3     F LOW E XECUTION : C ONSTRUCTING AND RUNNING W ORKFLOWS (S TAGE 3)
289
290   With the workflow graph generated, our approach finally proceeds to execute the graph to get the
291   final result. The execution follows the dependency order, ensuring the correct flow of data between
292   experts. To ensure robust execution, if any of the generated code encounters errors, the correspond-
293
      ing symbolic reasoning experts will trace the issue, use the error message to repair the code, and
      rerun it. As the workflow progresses, the downstream experts continually update their memory with
294
      the intermediate results and insights generated by previous experts. Upon completion of the work-
295
      flow execution, the last LLM expert analyzes the results, identifies key findings, and summarizes
296   them into a final answer to the original problem. The workflow execution is not a one-time process.
297   The LLM continually assesses the quality and correctness of the final generated solutions and iden-
298   tifies potential errors. It engages in iterative rerun by applying a different problem decomposition,
299   expert assignments, or adjusting the workflow structure.

      5      M ODEL T UNING OF H YBRID T HINKING
302
303
      In our experiments, we observed that open-source language models (typically those with around
304
      7B parameters) often struggle with advanced meta-planning and problem-solving skills required for
305   solving difficult reasoning tasks. To address this limitation and develop local smaller models with
306   hybrid thinking abilities comparable to the large models, we construct a comprehensive training
307   dataset and propose hybrid thinking tuning to improve the complex reasoning abilities of local mod-
308   els. We define “local” models as models that can be trained and deployed on local hardware with
309   limited computational resources, such as the Llama-3 model (Meta, 2024). The primary challenge
310   lies in constructing a large-scale dataset of reasoning problems that are sufficiently diverse, high-
311   quality, and difficult. Such a dataset is crucial for teaching smaller local models to perform complex
312   reasoning tasks. However, manually curating such a dataset presents significant difficulties in ensur-
313
      ing a wide range of problem domains and maintaining high standards in problem formulation. As a
      result, it is extremely time-consuming and expensive to ask human experts to consistently generate
314
      problems meeting all criteria. Therefore, we propose a novel approach for automatically generate
315
      a variety of reasoning problems and collect solutions of hybrid thinking, which can then be used to
316   train our local LLMs.

      Task Generation Inspired by Seed Tasks. The first step of our reasoning data synthesis pipeline is
      generating an expanded set of reasoning tasks. We augment the few-shot prompts with 10 high-level
348
      task descriptions randomly sampled from the 214 BigBench tasks (Srivastava et al., 2022). Next,
349
      we employ the 10 seed tasks as in-context examples to prompt LLMs6 to generate 10 new tasks. To
350   encourage additional diversity in the generated tasks, we also let the LLM to brainstorm different
351   genres of puzzles, such as crossword puzzles, math puzzles, number puzzles, relational puzzles,
352   logic puzzles, etc. By repeating two strategies, we produce an expanded pool of 45K candidate
353   reasoning tasks that creatively cover diverse reasoning types and scenarios.
354
      Data Filtering and Deduplication. The previous task generation step produces a sizable pool
355
      of candidate reasoning tasks. However, the generated data is likely to contain duplicate or highly
356   similar entries. To address this, we employ a comprehensive data filtering and deduplication process.
357   First, we apply n-gram to identify nearly identical tasks. Next, we filter out any tasks or problems
358   that fail to meet our quality criteria by prompting GPT-4-Turbo, such as insufficient complexity (e.g.,
359   trivial one-step questions), or ambiguity in the description. This helps ensure that only high-quality,
360   unambiguous reasoning tasks are retained in the final dataset. Through this rigorous deduplication
361   and filtering process, we condense the pool of 45K generated tasks down to 18K deduplicated tasks.
362   Reasoning Problem Synthesis. In the last step, we aim to synthesize multiple concrete reasoning
363   problems for each of the 18K tasks. Taking each task’s description as input, we prompt an LLM to
364   generate 3 distinct questions or problems that test the specified reasoning skill. This enables us to
365   turn each high-level task into a set of actual solvable questions, resulting in a pool of 54k reasoning
366   problems. To ensure the generated problems are well-posed and solvable, we employ a chain-of-
367   thought (CoT) based validation step. We prompt GPT-4-Turbo to apply CoT to each synthesized
368   problem and analyze if the resulting reasoning steps coherently lead to a definite answer. Problems
369   for which the model fails to converge to a clear solution or exhibits inconsistent reasoning are filtered
370
      out. This results in the final 27K reasoning problems. Figure 4 provides three examples of reasoning

516
      Figure 5 illustrates the proportion of fast thinking and slow thinking (orange) approaches applied by

518   strates a higher reliance on fast thinking for BBH, DeepMind MATH, and Game of 24 tasks com-
519   pared with Llama-3-8B-Instruct model. This observation can be attributed to the fact that GPT-4-
520   Turbo’s fast thinking (in the form of CoT) is more reliable and effective compared to Llama-3-8B-
521   Instruct. As a result, hybrid thinking in GPT-4-Turbo tends to apply more fast thinking since it is
522   sufficient to achieve a correct solution in many cases. In contrast, Llama-3-8B-Instruct after tun-
523   ing exhibits a greater reliance on slow thinking strategies, particularly in complex tasks, where fast
524   thinking alone may not yield the desired results. This highlights the importance of hybrid thinking to
525
      improve problem-solving efficiency, suggesting that our method can dynamically adjust the optimal
      balance between fast and slow thinking based on the model’s downstream reasoning capabilities.
526

528   problem-solving capabilities. Our results showcase the potential of hybrid thinking approaches to
529   expand the frontier of what LLMs can achieve on challenging tasks.

      This paper introduces a novel framework HDFlow for enhancing the complex problem-solving
534   capabilities of LLMs through hybrid thinking and dynamic workflows. The dynamic workflow
535   mechanism enables LLMs to decompose complex problems into manageable sub-tasks and inte-
536   grate specialized language models and symbolic reasoning tools, while hybrid thinking strategically
537   engages deeper, multi-step reasoning for challenging problems that exceed the capabilities of fast
538   thinking alone. Extensive experiments demonstrate the significant advantages of our approach, with
539   slow thinking with dynamic workflow greatly outperforming CoT and hybrid thinking achieving the
      highest overall accuracy by balancing efficiency and performance.
