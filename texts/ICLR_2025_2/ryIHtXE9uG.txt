009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Motivated by the recent success of time-series foundation models for zero-shot
012            forecasting, we present a methodology for in-context fine-tuning of a time-series
013            foundation model. In particular, we design a pretrained foundation model that can
014            be prompted (at inference time) with multiple time-series examples, in order to
015            forecast a target time-series into the future. Our foundation model is specifically
016
               trained to utilize examples from multiple related time-series in its context window
               (in addition to the history of the target time-series) to help it adapt to the specific
017
               distribution of the target domain at inference time. We show that such a founda-
018
               tion model that uses in-context examples at inference time can obtain much bet-
019            ter performance on popular forecasting benchmarks compared to supervised deep
020            learning methods, statistical models as well as other time-series foundation mod-
021            els. Interestingly, our in-context fine-tuning approach even rivals the performance
022            of a foundation model that is explicitly fine-tuned on the target domain.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Time-series data is ubiquitous in several domains such as retail, finance, manufacturing, healthcare,
      and natural sciences. In many of these domains, time-series forecasting, i.e. predicting time-series
028
      into the future, is a critical problem - for example, in applications like retail forecasting, climate
029
      and weather predictions, traffic forecasting. In the last decade deep learning approaches (Salinas
030   et al., 2020; Oreshkin et al., 2019; Sen et al., 2019) have become popular in forecasting, often
031   outperforming statistical approaches like ARIMA (Box & Jenkins, 1968). However, until recently,
032   deep learning approaches for forecasting have adhered to the traditional supervised machine learning
033   framework of having to train a forecasting model on task-specific training data, before being able to
034   perform forecasting for that task. On the other hand, in Natural Language Processing (NLP), Large
035   Language Models (LLMs) (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020) have shown the promise of
036   foundation models i.e. a single pretrained model can perform well and adapt to tasks like translation,
037   code generation, text summarization during inference time in a zero-shot or few-shot manner.
038   Motivated by the success in NLP, there has been significant work in recent years on time-series
039   foundation models for forecasting, ranging from re-purposing LLMs directly for forecasting (Gru-
040   ver et al., 2023) to fine-tuning pretrained LLMs on time-series data (Zhou et al., 2023; Chang et al.,
041   2023) to pretraining time-series foundation models from scratch (Das et al., 2024; Goswami et al.,
042   2024; Woo et al., 2024; Ansari et al., 2024; Garza & Mergenthaler-Canseco, 2023). The last ap-
043   proach in particular has been shown to obtain strong zero-shot accuracy, rivaling the best supervised
044
      models trained specifically for the target datasets.
045   Several of these papers (Zhou et al., 2023; Ansari et al., 2024; Goswami et al., 2024) have shown
046   an opportunity for further accuracy improvement by fine-tuning of their pretrained models on target
047   datasets. However this breaks the zero-shot paradigm that precisely makes these time-series foun-
048   dation models so appealing to practitioners who do not want to build training pipelines. This raises
049   a natural question: Can we recover the benefits of fine-tuning a time-series foundation-model, by
050
      providing examples from a target dataset at inference time?
051   At the same time, the first iterations of these foundation models lack some of the desirable features
052   of LLMs with respect to in-context learning: the zero-shot performance of an LLM can be greatly
053   improved at inference time by using its context window for prompting techniques such as few-
      shot (Brown et al., 2020), chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2022b) or instruction tuning (Wei et al.,

076   In particular, we train a foundation model that lets us forecast a time-series by providing in its
077
      context window not just the historical values of the time-series, but also examples from other related
      time-series that could help the model adapt, at inference time, to the distribution of the target time-
078
      series. For example, consider a highway traffic prediction system that stores hourly data from the
079
      last week, in order to predict the future hourly traffic for a particular highway. Consider a time-
080   series foundation model that has not seen data in pretraining that captures the temporal patterns in
081   this traffic data. Then, simply prompting the model with the previous week’s traffic time-series for
082   that highway might not be enough to obtain accurate zero-shot performance. However, adding to
083   the prompt historical traffic data from other highways and weeks, might help the model better adapt
084   to the traffic data distribution, and improve the target accuracy significantly.
085
      To summarize, the main contributions of our paper are as follows:
086
087   (i) We introduce the study of in-context fine-tuning for time-series foundation models, and propose
088   the use of prompts that not only include the usual history of the target time-series for forecasting,
089
      but also include related time-series examples in-context.
090   (ii) We pretrain a time-series foundation model to be able to effectively utilize these in-context
091   time-series examples mentioned above. Our training is decoder-only (Liu et al., 2018) and can adapt
092   not only to any context, horizon pair (up to a certain maximum context) but also to any number of
093   supplementary time-series examples (again up to a certain maximum number of examples). Appro-
094   priately trained models can then learn to borrow patterns from these related examples to do better
095
      on the original forecasting task.
096   (iii) We empirically evaluate the benefits of in-context fine-tuning using our foundation model.
097   Using evaluations on popular forecasting benchmarks, we show that in-context fine-tuning can lead
098   to better zero-shot performance on popular forecasting benchmarks as compared to supervised deep
099   learning methods, statistical models as well as other foundation models. In particular, it obtains up
100   to 25% better performance than a state-of-the-art time-series foundation model and other supervised
101
      deep learning and statistical baselines. Surprisingly, it even slightly improves upon the performance
      of a time-series foundation model that is specifically fine-tuned to the target datasets.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
109
110   As mentioned previously, there has been a spurt of recent work on time-series foundation models
111   for forecasting. These approaches can be broadly divided into three categories. (i) Prompting LLMs
112   like GPT-4 to directly predict the future of a numerical series encoded as text. This was investigated
113   in LLMTime (Gruver et al., 2023); despite the initial promise subsequent works have shown that
114   such approaches can be lacking in accuracy (Woo et al., 2024; Das et al., 2024). (ii) fine-tuning
115   pretrained LLMs like GPT2 on time-series data with adapter layers (Zhou et al., 2023; Chang et al.,
116   2023). These approaches have mostly been shown to work well in the dataset-to-dataset transfer
117   learning setting (rather than in the zero-shot setting) and they are also disadvantaged from having to
118
      use excessively large models due to their LLM backbones. (iii) Pretraining transformer based models
      from scratch on huge volumes of time-series data, which seems to be the most promising approach
119
      towards times-series foundation models (Das et al., 2024; Garza & Mergenthaler-Canseco, 2023;
120
      Ansari et al., 2024; Woo et al., 2024; Goswami et al., 2024). Indeed some of these models have
121   shown superior zero-shot accuracy when compared to supervised deep forecasters and statistical
122   methods even on datasets that are outside of their pretraining set.
123
124
      Some of the above papers have additionally shown (Ansari et al., 2024; Goswami et al., 2024) that
      their pretrained models’ performance can be further improved by fine-tuning the model on examples
125
      from the target dataset. While this supervised fine-tuning results in improved per-task accuracy, this
126
      practice breaks the zero-shot paradigm in terms of requiring extra training on the target dataset.
127
128   In the NLP domain, a defining property of a foundation LLM is its ability to be further adapted
129   to domain-specific tasks through either fine-tuning or prompting. In particular, LLMs have been
130
      shown to perform in-context learning on a variety of downstream NLP tasks by prompting them
      with a natural language instruction (Radford et al., 2019) and a few demonstrations or examples of
131
      the task. This phenomenon is also referred to as few-shot learning (Brown et al., 2020). Subsequent
132
      works (Min et al., 2022a; Chen et al., 2022) have proposed fine-tuning a pretrained LLM to obtain
133   better performance on few-shot learning prompts. Other papers (Min et al., 2022b; Wei et al., 2023)
134   have empirically investigated how few-shot learning works in LLMs. More recently, Shi et al.
135   (2023) explored a similar idea for in-context pretraining, where they pretrain an LLM on sequences
136   of related documents. This in-context learning ability is widely recognized as being associated with
137   the stacked transformers used in the LLMs, and their theoretical properties are studied in a more
138   precise sense (Garg et al., 2022; Von Oswald et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2024) for simpler function
139   classes such as linear regression.
140   Despite the commonality between time-series foundation models and LLMs, it is not obvious how
141   (or even if) the phenomenon of few-shot learning for NLP tasks carry over to the time-series setting.
142   There is no clear definition of few-shot learning in terms of a time-series foundation model. In fact
143   prior pretrained time-series foundation models like (Ansari et al., 2024; Das et al., 2024; Garza &
144   Mergenthaler-Canseco, 2023) do not provide a clear opportunity to be prompted with anything apart
145   from the past values of a time-series in the context window.

215   Motivated by the strong zero-shot performance achieved by stacked transformer models in decoder-
      only mode for time-series forecasting, we propose to adapt a base TimesFM model (Das et al., 2024)

      to leverage the additional information available via in-context examples. In particular, we pretrain
217   TimesFM in its original fashion to obtain a base checkpoint. We then modify the model architecture
218   and continue pretraining from the base checkpoint using training data with in-context examples (we
219   call this phase continued pretraining) to obtain a new pretrained foundation model TimesFM-ICF.
220   The base TimesFM checkpoint that we start from will be referred to as TimesFM (base).
221
      Adapting their model architecture to make use of the in-context examples is somewhat delicate, and
222
      requires modifications to the original model. A depiction of our proposed model architecture is
223   given in Figure 3. As in their model, our model partitions each example into non-overlapping input
224   patches, and uses a shared input residual block (a one-hidden layer perceptron with skip connection,
225   see Das et al. (2023)), to embed each patch as a token before feeding the tokens into the stacked
226   transformers in a decoder-only fashion. The output embeddings are mapped to the next output
227   patches via another shared output residual block.
228
      To teach the model to use the new in-context examples, we adapt the original TimesFM architec-
229   ture to better handle (1) the in-context example separators, (2) the cross-example attention, and (3)
230   the positional encoding. Despite these changes, we are still able to leverage the TimesFM (base)
231   checkpoint, which was pretrained for forecasting given just the history of the target time-series. We
232   describe the key details of our architecture design below.
233
234   4.1   S EPARATORS FOR I N - CONTEXT EXAMPLES
235
236   Our context window contains in-context examples from different time-series. Hence the model
237   needs to be able to separate these, since naı̈ve concatenation can confuse the model. Consider the
238   example in Figure 2. If we naı̈vely concatenate multiple in-context examples (e.g., linear trends,
239   Figure 2c) together, then the combination of these trends may appear to the model as an entirely
240   different time-series (e.g., a triangle wave, Figure 2b). Therefore, we choose to insert a common
241
      learnable separator token after each in-context example. We visually depict these separators as
      the dashed lines in Figure 2c. When feeding examples to the decoder, we sequentially pass each
242
      tokenized patch of each time-series example to the model, followed by the separator token at the
243
      end of an example. This process is depicted in Figure 3.
244
245
246
      4.2   C ROSS - EXAMPLE ATTENTION
247
      In order to allow our model to distinguish between different in-context examples, we allow the
248   transformer to attend (causally) to all previous patches including the separator tokens. Note that, if
249   the model did not attend to the separator tokens, then we could never hope to distinguish between
250   the two scenarios from Figure 2b and Figure 2c. By attending to the previous separator tokens, the
251   model can potentially distinguish how many in-context examples have been processed so far.
252
      Although at the input to the stacked transformer we use a common separator token to separate the
253
      examples, the output tokens corresponding to the positions of these separator tokens can play a
254   much more nuanced role as we proceed through the subsequent transformer layers. As the output
255   tokens corresponding to these separator tokens causally attend to all previous tokens, after several
256   transformer layers these tokens can, for instance, potentially summarize the information in all the
257   patches corresponding to their example and/or convey the separation boundaries of the different
258   in-context examples to the model.
259
260   4.3   P OSITIONAL E NCODING
261
262   Based on the findings in Haviv et al. (2022), we create the pretrained TimesFM (base) checkpoint
263   with No Positional Encodings (NoPE), in contrast to the absolute positional encodings (Vaswani
264   et al., 2017) used in the original TimesFM model. We note that we can achieve the same accuracy
265   reported in the original TimesFM paper without using any positional encodings. Indeed it has been
266
      hypothesized in Haviv et al. (2022) that the presence of causal attention itself can encode positional
      information when there are more than one stacked transformer layers.
267
268   The advantages of NoPE for our continued pretraining are two fold: (i) NoPE models usually have
269   better length generalization, which is particularly important here since we increase the prompt length
      by adding in-context examples to the context (ii) If we use the original absolute positional encodings


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      used in (Das et al., 2024), the meaning of these positional encodings in the base model would be
271   different from their meaning during the continued pretraining with in-context examples. This could
272   cause problems for the continued pretraining phase.

      6.1   O UT- OF - DOMAIN F ORECASTING ON M ONASH
365
366   Monash archive (Godahewa et al., 2021) is a collection of 30 datasets of different training and predic-
367   tion lengths that covers granularities ranging from minutes to years and domains including finance,
368   demand forecasting, weather and traffic. The archive reports four official metrics for several statisti-
369   cal baselines such as Exponential Smoothing(ETS) and ARIMA, as well as supervised ML baselines
370   like CatBoost (Prokhorenkova et al., 2018), DeepAR (Salinas et al., 2020) and WaveNet (Oord et al.,
371   2016). We report our results on the 18 datasets that were also considered for zero-shot forecasting

      6.3                C OMPARISON WITH F INE - TUNING PER DATASET
455
      One of the main motivations of this work was to see whether we can recover the gains from fine-
456   tuning foundation models on the target domain without doing any gradient updates. Therefore, in
457   this section, we compare against a very strong baseline: for every dataset in our Monash benchmark
458   from Section 6.1 we fine-tune the TimesFM (base) model on the training set and evaluate it on the
459   test set. We do two kinds of fine-tuning (1) we update all the model weights which we will refer to
460   as FT-TimesFM (Full) (2) we hold all the transformer layer fixed while only the input and output
461   residual blocks are fine-tuned, which we will refer to as FT-TimesFM (LP) 3 .
462   The aggregated scaled MAE numbers are presented in Figure 5a. TimesFM-ICF actually yields close
463   to 3% improvement over FT-TimesFM (Full) which is already a 4% improvement over TimesFM
464   (base). This shows that in-context fine-tuning can sometimes be better than per-dataset fine-tuning,
465   even though we do not perform any gradient updates! The advantages of our method are further
466   highlighted by the fact the total time required for fine-tuning on all datasets is 115 minutes (not
467   including job scheduling times) for the cheaper FT-TimesFM (LP) method while the total inference
468   time for TimesFM-ICF is merely 4 minutes 4 .
469   While this is surprising, we believe that one reason could be that in many of the smaller datasets in
470   Monash, fine-tuning the weights of a foundation model can actually lead to catastrophic forgetting
471   of the learnt patterns which is also observed in LLMs (Luo et al., 2023). Indeed on the smaller
472   datasets like tourism yearly, bitcoin and us births, TimesFM-ICF is better than FT-TimesFM and
473   vice versa on larger datasets like Australian electricity demand. We provide per dataset metrics and
474   more details about the fine-tuning in Section A.5.

507
508   In this section, we compare the performance of TimesFM-ICF with a version of TimesFM
509   (base) trained with a longer history L = 2048 which we will refer to as TimesFM (LH). We pro-
510   vide the aggregate scaled MAE on Monash datasets in Figure 5b where we include two versions
511   of TimesFM-ICF, one with 4 in-context examples (TimesFM-ICF-4ex) and one with 50 in-context
512   examples (TimesFM-ICF-50ex). We can see that TimesFM (LH) yields a modest 1% improvement

519   inside the context, while for the case of usual long history training such time-series will just be
520
      padded and most of the context is wasted. As shown in the detailed results in Appendix A.2, the
      long history model performs better on longer datasets like australian electricity demand, but degrades
521
      on shorter datasets like cif and tourism yearly.

524   7   C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this paper, we introduce and study a methodology for in-context fine-tuning of a time-series
527   foundation model for forecasting. In particular, we start with a base foundation model and adapt it
528   to be able to effectively utilize, at inference time, not just the history of the target time-series for
529
      forecasting, but also in-context examples from related time-series. Our results show that in-context
      fine-tuning can lead to significantly better zero-shot performance on popular forecasting benchmarks
530
      compared to the base foundation model and state-of-the-art supervised models. Furthermore, it even
531
      outperforms a version of the base foundation model that is explicitly fine-tuned on the target domain.
532
533   While we have chosen a specific base time-series foundation model (TimesFM) for our in-context
534   fine-tuning approach, it would be an interesting direction of future work to study these adaptations
535
      for other base foundation models. It would also be interesting to study better forms of relative posi-
      tional encodings specifically designed for handling in-context examples and length generalization.

      A.1   M ORE D ETAILS ABOUT OUR M ODEL AND BASELINES
705
706   Monash Baselines. For the results on Monash datasets, we borrow the official numbers from (Go-
707   dahewa et al., 2021). For LLMTime (Gruver et al., 2023) we use the pre-computed outputs supplied
708   by the original authors.
709   We also add the PatchTST (Nie et al., 2022) as a baseline for this benchmark because it is the best
710   performing baseline (only worse than our models) in the ETT datasets. For this model we use the
711   hyperparameters used by original paper for the ETTh datasets 5 .
712
      ETT Baselines. On the ETT datasets, the baseline numbers (except TimesFM (base)) are borrowed
713
      from the official numbers reported in Table 2 of (Das et al., 2023). We evaluate the base model,
714
      TimesFM (base) as well as our method in a rolling validation manner on the test splits to obtain the
715   corresponding metrics.
716
717   TimesFM (base). Following Das et al. (2024), we train a 200M model with 16 attention heads, 20
718
      layers, a input patch length of 32 and output patch length of 128. The model dimension is set to
      1280. We use the learning rate schedule in (Vaswani et al., 2017) with peak learning rate of 5e − 4.
719
      The hidden dims of both the residual block and the FFN in the transformer layers are set as the same
720
      as model dimensions. We keep layer norm in transformer layers but not in the residual blocks. The
721   only difference between the model in Das et al. (2024) and our base model is that we use NoPE
722   instead of teh absolute positional encoding (Vaswani et al., 2017). As we have mentioned before,
723   this leads to no loss in accuracy while being easier to extend to our in-context fine-tuning setting.
724
      Fine-tuning Per Dataset. On the Monash benchmark, we also compare with TimesFM (base) fine-
725
      tuned on the train set for every dataset and the forecasting on the corresponding test set. For all our
726
      fine-tuning runs, we use a batch size of 16 and a maximum of 10k iterations. Note that this means
727   that the fine-tuned model will see many more training examples than the in-context examples given
728   to our model. For the fine-tuning runs, we use the same decoder only loss function that was used in
729   the original pretraining of TimesFM (base), the only difference is that the training is not restricted
730   to the training set of one dataset. We do two kinds of fine tuning:
731
732          • Full: All weights in the model are updated during fine-tuning.
733          • Linear Probing (LP): We hold the transformer weights fixed and only update the parameters
734            in the input and output residual blocks.
735
736   TimesFM-ICF. We continue to train TimesFM-ICF model from TimesFM (base). Therefore, most
737   of the parameters in the model remain the same. Here, are the key training details that are unique to
738
      TimesFM-ICF:
739
             • Separator Token: We have a trainable separator token that is also updated during the con-
740
               tinued pretraining. The token is nothing but a learnt embedding whose dimension is equal
741            to the model dimension i.e. 1280 in our case.
742
             • Number of Examples: We use a maximum of n = 50 in-context examples for each context
743
               during training.
744
745
             • Padding: In short datasets like M4 yearly and quarterly, each time-series might have num-
               ber of time-points much less than T = 640. Sometimes the number of time-points are even
746
               less than our input patch length p = 32. For such cases, a whole time-series can fit into one
747
               of the n examples and they are preprocessed in the following manner:
748
749
                  – If the length of the time-series l is less than p, we left pad with k padding time-points
                     such that p < k + l < 2p. This is because we want the decoder only model to predict
750
                     something meaningful for the second patch after seeing the first patch and if not, is
751
                     penalized by the loss on the second patch. If the l > p, we do not need to perform this
752                  left padding.
753
                  – Lastly, we right pad such that the length of the total padded example is T = 640.

      scripts/PatchTST/etth1.sh

                                      – Note that the last patch in such examples would be padded from the right i.e., they
757                                     will have real time-series values for the first few points and padding for the rest. We
758                                     make sure that such incomplete from the right patches are not attended by subsequent
759                                     tokens belonging to examples coming after.
