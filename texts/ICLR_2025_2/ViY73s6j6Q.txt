009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Self-supervised learning of graph-structured data aims to produce transferable and
012            robust representations that could be transferred to the downstream tasks. Among
013            many, graph contrastive learning (GCL) based on data augmentation has emerged
014            with promising performance in learning graph representation. However, it is ob-
015            served that some augmentations might change the graph semantics due to the
016
               perturbations in the graph structure such as perturbing some nodes/edges. In such
               cases, existing GCL methods may suffer from performance limitations due to the
017
               introduction of noise augmentations. To address this issue, we propose to train a
018
               discriminative model to enhance GCL for graph-structured data, called Perturbation
019            Discrimination-Enhanced GCL (PerEG). Specifically, for each perturbed graph, the
020            discriminative model is trained to predict whether each node in the augmentation
021            was perturbed by the perturbation compared to the original graph or not. Based
022            on this, the results of perturbation discrimination are exploited to refine the GCL,
023            enabling its controllable use of augmentation, thereby preferably utilizing augmen-
024            tation and effectively avoiding the introduction of noise augmentation. Extensive
025            experiments in unsupervised, semi-supervised, and transfer learning scenarios show
026            that our PerEG outperforms the state-of-the-art methods on eight datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Graph neural networks (GNNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Velickovic et al., 2018; Xu et al., 2019) are
031   becoming increasingly popular in the realm of learning graph representation for graph-structured data,
032   where the structural information of graphs could be modeled with a neighborhood aggregation scheme.
033   By inheriting the power of neural networks, GNNs have reached great progress in learning information
034   from real-world graphs such as knowledge graphs (Baek et al., 2020), social networks (Fan et al.,
035   2019), point clouds (Shi & Rajkumar, 2020), and chemical analysis (De Cao & Kipf, 2018). Most
036   existing GNN-based models are trained in a supervised manner with end-to-end form when solving
037   graph-based tasks. However, annotating a high volume of fine-annotated data requires time-consuming
038   laboratory annotations based on professional knowledge (Hu et al., 2020a; Tan et al., 2021).
039   To alleviate this issue, various self-supervised learning methods are explored on graph-structured
040   data for learning transferable and robust representations from unlabeled graphs (Hu et al., 2020a;
041   You et al., 2020). Among them, graph predictive learning and graph contrastive learning (GCL)
042   have received a lot of attention in learning graph representations. Here graph predictive learning can
043   learn the contextual relationships between neighboring nodes (Hu et al., 2020a; Kim & Oh, 2021)
044   or discriminating original graphs from perturbed graphs (Kim et al., 2022), and graph contrastive
045
      learning (GCL) (You et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2021; Xia et al., 2022b) can learn the mutual information
      between augmentations by maximizing the similarity between augmented views. While promising
046
      progress is made, existing methods generally attempt to learn the differences or similarities from
047
      the graph augmentations obtained by augmentation strategy link perturbations on nodes or edges.
048   However, as many studies have pointed out, the perturbation of nodes/edges of the graph might
049   change the nature of the original graphs (Kim et al., 2022; Yue et al., 2022). In addition, another
050   study attempts to perturb the encoder to obtain contrastive objectives without any change in the graph
051   structure, to preserve the graph semantics well (Xia et al., 2022a). However, this work is more like
052   a general method at the representation level, ignoring the learning of rich structured information
053   when performing GCL. We thus argue that to explore rich structural information for improving graph
      representation, we should use graph augmentation with structural perturbations, but it is necessary to


                                                        1
054
      control the application of augmentation to graph contrastive learning by identifying the properties of
055   perturbations to prevent the introduction of noise.
056
057   To reach this goal, we propose a discriminative model to identify the fine-grained perturbation in
058
      a perturbed augmentation compared to the original graph, and then enhance GCL based on the
      discriminative results, called Perturbation Discrimination-Enhanced Graph Contrastive Learning
059
      (PerEG). We observe that for an augmentation of an original graph, its structural perturbations should
060
      be effective and tolerable, that is, the augmentation is different from but associated with the original
061   graph. Therefore, for a qualified augmented graph, the model should be able to identify where it
062   has been disturbed compared to the original graph. Otherwise, if a perturbed graph is completely
063   different from the original graph, it probably changes the semantic information of the original graph
064   and cannot be used as an augmentation of the original graph for learning graph representation.
065
      In light of this, we get inspiration from ELECTRA (Clark et al., 2019) and train a discriminative
066
      model that predicts whether each node in the augmented graph was affected by perturbations or not,
067   aiming to perceive the fine-grained perturbation of a perturbed augmentation compared to the original
068   graph. The motivation for this operation comes from the fact that a vertex/node is the fundamental
069   unit of which graphs are formed, and judging changes in node representation can identify where the
070   augmentation has been disturbed compared to the original graph. Further, the perturbation of a node
071   is usually caused by two situations: one is directly perturbing the node, and the other is caused by
072   the perturbation of the edge. To this end, we devise our discriminative model from two perspectives:
073   node-oriented discriminator and edge-oriented discriminator. In this way, both node representation
074   changes caused by node perturbation and by edge perturbation will be taken into account. It should be
075
      noted that if perturbed nodes in an augmentation cannot be accurately identified, their representations
      are either very similar to the original ones or have deviated significantly in the latent space. In such
076
      cases, those augmentations cannot be considered as qualified perturbed augmentations and should be
077
      limited in GCL, because they are neither effective nor plausible. Subsequently, the discriminative
078   accuracy of perturbed nodes is exploited in the GCL, which essentially enables the model to use graph
079   augmentation in a controlled manner, effectively avoiding the introduction of noise augmentation.
080   Our main contributions are as follows:
081
082          • We propose Perturbation Discrimination-Enhanced Graph Contrastive Learning (PerEG),
083            the first to enhance GCL by training a discriminative model for perturbation identification,
084            which enables the model to use graph augmentations for GCL in a controlled manner,
085            effectively avoiding the introduction of noise augmentation.
086          • Instead of laboriously entangling about how to prevent the introduction of noise structures
087            during graph augmentation, this paper aims to find the potential noise augmentations that
088            might change the graph semantics and limit them in GCL according to the results of
089            perturbation discrimination.
090          • Node-oriented and edge-oriented discriminators are designed to identify whether each node
091            in the perturbed graph is affected by the perturbations or not, empowering our PerEG to
092            discriminate the effective and plausible augmented graphs for contrastive learning.
093          • We conduct a series of experiments on eight graph-structured benchmark datasets to eval-
094            uate the effectiveness of our PerEG in both unsupervised and semi-supervised scenarios.
095            Experimental results show that our PerEG significantly outperforms baselines.

      2.2     S ELF - SUPERVISED L EARNING FOR G RAPH R EPRESENTATION

      Many studies have been devoted to graph representation learning based on self-supervised learn-
138
      ing (Hu et al., 2020a). Recently, some studies have attempted to perform prediction in learning
139
      contextual properties of graph-structured data (Kim & Oh, 2021; Hwang et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2020b;
140   Rong et al., 2020). Further, D-SLA (Kim et al., 2022) proposes to discriminate the original graphs
141   from the perturbed graphs. In addition, many recent studies are dedicated to exploring contrastive
142   learning to achieve better graph representations based on graph augmentations. GraphCL (You et al.,
143   2020) designs four types of graph augmentations to learn the similarly metrics between augmentations
144   with contrastive learning. JOAO (You et al., 2021) and AutoGCL (Yin et al., 2022) propose an auto
145   augmentation strategy to enhance the performance of graph contrastive learning. MVGRL (Hassani &
146   Khasahmadi, 2020) performs contrastive learning on different structural views of graphs for both node
147   and graph levels to enrich the feature learning. In addition, some recent studies attempt to alleviate
148
      the issue that some augmentations might change the graph semantics in graph contrastive learning.
      SimGRACE (Xia et al., 2022a) generates contrastive objects of graphs for contrast by perturbing
149
      the weights of the model rather than structure perturbations. However, this work overlooks the
150
      exploration of graph structures and thus cannot produce robust graph representations by introducing
151   richer graph structures. Further, GLA (Yue et al., 2022) utilizes label information to keep the label of
152   the augmented sample the same as the original graph for contrast. However, this work requires the
153   introduction of annotations and cannot be used in unsupervised scenarios.

190
      modules: 1) Node-oriented Discriminator, which predicts whether each node in the augmented
191   graph was perturbed or not by the perturbation acting on nodes; 2) Edge-oriented Discriminator,

193   3) Reweighted Contrastive Learning, which performs graph contrastive learning reweighted by the
194   perturbation discrimination results.

797   E    A NALYSIS OF AUGMENTATION S TRENGTH
798
799   To examine whether the strength of augmentations can affect the performance of our PerEG, we
800   conduct experiments with different strengths in producing augmentations and show the results in
801   Figure 6. The structure-perturbed graph augmentation results of Figure 6(a)-(c) show that ratios near
802   0.5 worsen the performance, denoting that disturbing around half of the nodes in a graph will increase
803   the uncertainty of the graph structure, thereby increasing the difficulty of prediction. Figure 6(d)
804   shows that, for edge perturbation, the fluctuation in performance is relatively slight at different
805   ratios, implying that an edge perturbation affects multiple nodes, and a smaller proportion of edge
806
      perturbations may cause significant structural changes.
807   Further, Figure 7 shows the comparison results of using different perturbation strategies to produce
808   perturbed augmentations. Our PerEG achieves better performance than GraphCL in different aug-
809   mentation strategies. Meanwhile, the improvement of different strategies varies. Therefore, we need
      to explore different augmentation strategies to learn richer graph representations.

903   B ROADER I MPACTS
904
905   Learning on graph-structured data has a wide range of interests and applications, such as recommen-
906   dation systems, social media, neural structure search, and drug discovery. Our PerEG contributes
907   a general framework for processing different kinds of graph augmentations. Further, the proposed
908   node-level predictive learning can perceive the fine-grained structural differences and identify the
909   topological information of the graph from node-oriented and edge-oriented perspectives, providing a
910   new idea for learning graph-structured information.
911
912   L IMITATIONS
913
914   In order to mitigate the generation of isolated outlier nodes, we produce four perturbed augmentations
915   for each graph. However, there is still a situation where a node is isolated in different augmentations,
916   which will hinder the performance of perturbation discrimination in our PerEG. Therefore, in future
917   work, we will consider trying more reasonable strategies for producing perturbed augmentations to
      avoid the generation of isolated nodes, aiming to further improve the performance of our PerEG.
