010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            The Hierarchical Navigable Small World (HNSW) algorithm is widely used for
013            approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search, leveraging the principles of naviga-
014            ble small-world graphs. However, it faces some limitations. The first is the local
015            optima problem, which arises from the algorithm’s greedy search strategy, select-
016
               ing neighbors based solely on proximity at each step. This often leads to cluster
               disconnections. The second limitation is that HNSW frequently fails to achieve
017
               logarithmic complexity, particularly in high-dimensional datasets, due to the ex-
018
               haustive traversal through each layer. To address these limitations, we propose
019            a novel algorithm that mitigates local optima and cluster disconnections while
020            enhancing the construction speed, maintaining inference speed. The first com-
021            ponent is a dual-branch HNSW structure with LID-based insertion mechanisms,
022            enabling traversal from multiple directions. This improves outlier node capture,
023            enhances cluster connectivity, accelerates construction speed and reduces the risk
024            of local minima. The second component incorporates a bridge-building technique
025            that bypasses redundant intermediate layers, maintaining inference and making
026            up the additional computational overhead introduced by the dual-branch struc-
027
               ture. Experiments on various benchmarks and datasets showed that our algorithm
               outperforms the original HNSW in both accuracy and speed. We evaluated six
028
               datasets across Computer Vision (CV), and Natural Language Processing (NLP),
029
               showing recall improvements of 18% in NLP, and up to 30% in CV tasks while
030            reducing the construction time by up to 20% and maintaining the inference speed.
031            We did not observe any trade-offs in our algorithm. Ablation studies revealed
032            that LID-based insertion had the greatest impact on performance, followed by the
033            dual-branch structure and bridge-building components.

038   Hierarchical Navigable Small World (HNSW) graphs have become a state-of-the-art method for
039   approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search due to their efficiency and effectiveness in handling
040   large-scale datasets (Malkov & Yashunin, 2020). HNSW constructs a multi-layer graph, where each
041   layer provides a different level of abstraction of the data. The search process navigates these layers,
042   starting from the top, to efficiently approximate the nearest neighbors of a query point.
043   Despite its success, HNSW faces several limitations. The first drawback relates to local optimum
044   during the search process. This issue arises from the node insertion mechanism, which inserts nodes
045   into the HNSW graph randomly. Random insertion can result in disconnected regions and weaker
046   inter-cluster connectivity, increasing the likelihood of the search process becoming trapped in local
047   optimum. As shown in Figure 1, random insertion in the HNSW graph makes the greedy search pro-
048   cess traverse to a local optimum (denoted by the red node), instead of reaching the global optimum
049   (denoted by the green node). The second drawback is that the logarithmic complexity O(n log n)
050
      proposed in the original HNSW paper (Malkov & Yashunin, 2020) is difficult to achieve consistently,
      particularly in high-dimensional spaces (Lin & Zhao, 2019). The exhaustive traversal through each
051
      layer introduces significant overhead, slowing both the construction and query processes.
052

      graphs by inserting nodes based on their Local Intrinsic Dimensionality (LID) values instead of

      2.2   H IGH L OCAL I NTRINSIC D IMENSIONALITY IN HNSW
112
113   One of the key challenges in Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN) search is dealing with local
114   minima, where the search process gets stuck in suboptimal regions of the data space. High Local
115   Intrinsic Dimensionality (LID) values, as demonstrated by (Houle et al., 2018), indicates its role
116   in capturing the density of data points around the query, serving as a measure of local intrinsic
117   dimensionality. High-LID values often represent outliers or points in sparse, locally complex re-
118
      gions, which can disrupt the search process by anchoring the graph structure in unfavorable ways,
      mitigating the risk of local minima.
119
120   The importance of handling high-LID outliers in graph construction was also explored by (Amsaleg
121   et al., 2015), who proposed several methods for estimating LID and demonstrated its usefulness
122   in ANN search, classification, and outlier detection. Their findings support the idea that high-LID
123   points should be treated differently during graph construction.
124   Recent work by (Elliott & Clark, 2024) has highlighted the significant impact of data insertion order
125   on the recall performance of HNSW graphs. Their study showed that inserting nodes with higher
126   LID values into the upper layers of the HNSW graph can improve recall by up to 12.8 percentage
127   points.
128   While these works offer valuable insights into handling high-dimensional data and mitigating local
129   minima, they leave open questions regarding the best methods for integrating LID considerations
130   into HNSW graph construction. Furthermore, the impact of LID-based strategies on the broader
131   structure of HNSW graphs remains an area for further exploration.

      3.1   M OTIVATION
138
      In HNSW, nodes are assigned to layers randomly, with the probability of a node being placed in a
139   higher layer decreasing exponentially. In (Baranchuk et al., 2019), the authors pointed out that simi-
140   larity graphs are vulnerable to local minima when the query is unable to escape suboptimal vertices.
141   The combination of random insertion and greedy search in HNSW worsens this issue, as random
142   factors can cause the search to deviate from the optimal path. Another challenge arising from the
143   random insertion is the low inter-cluster connectivity. This problem can place different clusters on
144   separate layers, limiting their connectivity. Consequently, the search process may terminate in a
145   cluster different from the one containing the true nearest neighbors. (Lin & Zhao, 2019) also ob-
146   served that the hierarchical structure of HNSW fails to achieve the expected logarithmic complexity.
147
      Instead, the exhaustive traversal of each layer becomes a bottleneck. As a result, HNSW encounters
      several inherent problems: (1) a high likelihood of local minima, which grows with the size of the
148
      data; (2) weak connectivity between clusters; and (3) slower search times, construction time, making
149
      it difficult to achieve logarithmic complexity in practice.
150
151

152
153   To address these issues, we propose HNSW++, which partitions the dataset into two branches based
154   on the index of the inserted nodes. By doing so, spatial regions are divided into different branches,
155   allowing the algorithm to search in both simultaneously. The search process begins in the upper
156   layers of both branches, navigating greedily until a local minimum is encountered. The algorithm
157   then descends to the lower layers, where the two branches merge at layer 0, combining their results
158   for the final output (see Fig. 3). This approach not only minimizes the influence of local minima and
159
      ensures a more accurate search for the true nearest neighbors, but it also reduces construction time,
      as each new node only needs to search through half the nodes already inserted. This dual-branch
160
      approach can be expressed as follows:

      3.3   A LGORITHM D ESIGN
259
260   The network construction algorithm incrementally inserts nodes into the hierarchical structure based
261   on their assigned layer and order. Starting at the top layer, the algorithm searches for the closest
262   nodes to the query, then progresses to the next lower layer, using the previous layer’s result as
263   the entry point. This continues iteratively until reaching the query node’s designated layer. From
264   there, connections between the query node q and surrounding nodes are established, with further
265   refinement through adding or dropping connections as limits are exceeded.
266   Given an array of original LID values, the layer assignment for new nodes is guided by their nor-
267   malized Local Intrinsic Dimensionality (LID), as detailed in Algorithm 4. Nodes are divided be-
268   tween two branches, branch0 and branch1 , ranked by descending normalized LID calculated by
269   Algorithm 5. The number of nodes per layer is determined by a random scaling factor based on a
      normalization constant mL , ensuring a balanced distribution. If the total node count is odd, branch0

270
      receives an extra node. The algorithm alternates between branches during assignment, and once a
271   branch reaches capacity, remaining nodes are assigned to the other branch.
272
273   In the construction phase (Algorithm 1), the insertion process alternates between the two branches
274
      to ensure an even node distribution, with each branch handling approximately half of the dataset.
275   For inference (Algorithm 2), a modified greedy search algorithm locates the nearest neighbors for
276   query node q, starting from the top layer. Separate search paths are initialized for each branch,
277   with W1 and W2 as entry points. Both branches search concurrently at layer 0 (Algorithm ??). The
278   inference phase also employs a layer-skipping mechanism—if the distance between the current node
279   and the query is below the dataset’s average distance and has a high normalized LID, the search can
280
      directly skip to layer 0, bypassing intermediate layers. This reduces the search time, while the other
      branch continues unless also skipped.
281
282   When one branch finishes first, it passes its nearest neighbor set to the other branch to avoid duplicate
283   results. If both branches finish simultaneously, one branch’s results are passed to the other to ensure
284   consistency (Algorithm ??). Finally, the neighbor sets from W1 and W2 are combined. This dual-
285   path approach mitigates local minima by exploring two distinct search regions.

306   3.5     C OMPLEXITY ANALYSIS
307
308   3.5.1    S EARCH C OMPLEXITY
309
310   The search complexity of HNSW++ builds upon the original HNSW framework, integrating dual-
311   branch navigation and LID-driven optimizations. By employing a dual-branch structure, the search
312   process is divided between two branches, each exploring distinct graph regions. Starting at the top
313   layers of both branches, the algorithm ensures robust exploration and minimizes the risk of search
314
      stagnation in local minima.
315   Incorporating layer-skipping bridges, the complexity is further reduced by allowing direct transitions
316   to lower layers when LID conditions are met. Let Pskip represent the probability of a skip occurring
317   based on a node’s LID exceeding the threshold T , and Ltotal represent the total number of layers.
318   The expected number of layers traversed is reduced to Ltotal · (1 − Pskip ), making the effective layer
319   exploration more efficient than the standard HNSW.
320   Each branch independently executes the search with complexity scaling as O(log(N )) due to the
321   hierarchical structure. The merging of results at the base layer introduces a constant overhead,
322   maintaining the overall search complexity at O(log(N )). Experimental results confirm that dual-
323   branch navigation with selective layer skipping does not compromise logarithmic scaling, even in
      higher-dimensional datasets.

      3.5.2    C ONSTRUCTION C OMPLEXITY
325
326   Construction in HNSW++ follows a layered insertion protocol, where nodes are assigned layers
327   based on normalized LID values. The dual-branch structure halves the effective dataset size per
328   branch, reducing the computational load for insertion operations. Each node is placed after executing
329   nearest neighbor searches at each layer, with complexity per insertion scaling logarithmically as
330   O(log(N )).
331   The layer-skipping mechanism also optimizes insertion by allowing high-LID nodes to directly es-
332   tablish connections in deeper layers, bypassing intermediate ones. With Mmax as the maximum
333   number of connections per node and k as the neighbor set size, the average insertion time is propor-
334   tional to O(Mmax · log(N )) per node. Thus, the overall construction complexity for a dataset of size
335   N is O(N · log(N )), consistent with the standard HNSW scaling.
336   Assuming the LID values are provided beforehand, HNSW++ does not add significant computa-
337   tional overhead. The LID-based assignment process scales efficiently, as the majority of the com-
338   putational load is absorbed during initial LID calculations. Consequently, HNSW++ retains the
339   scalability of the original HNSW construction process.

      In this work, we introduce HNSW++, a novel algorithm that addresses several key challenges in
543
      nearest neighbor search. HNSW++ is designed to reduce the probability of falling into local minima,
544   improve the detection of outlier nodes, and enhance the connectivity of clusters. It also significantly
545   accelerates the construction process while maintaining stable inference time. To achieve these ad-
546   vancements, we integrate innovative mechanisms such as a multi-branch structure, insertion based
547   on Local Intrinsic Dimensionality (LID) values, and a skip-layer approach, ensuring the algorithm’s
548   efficiency and scalability.
549
      Furthermore, we perform an in-depth analysis of how each of these factors contributes to the overall
550   performance of HNSW++, providing insights into their individual and combined effects. Through
551   extensive experimentation on a wide range of tasks, datasets, and benchmarks, HNSW++ consis-
552   tently achieves state-of-the-art performance, demonstrating both superior accuracy and faster exe-
553   cution times compared to existing methods. This highlights the potential of HNSW++ as a robust
554   solution for large-scale nearest neighbor search problems.

      The Search algorithm is complex due to the integration of Multi-branch and Skipping methods.
694
      Initially, for layers greater than 0, the search identifies one nearest neighbor for each branch, setting
695
      it as the entry point ep for the next layer of that branch. If a branch triggers a skip, the search for
696   that branch will continue at layer 0, while it waits for the other branch to complete its search. If
697   both branches reach layer 0 simultaneously, or if branch0 reaches layer 0 first, branch0 will initiate
698   the search with ef search, resulting in W1 , the set of k nearest neighbors from branch0 . When
699   branch1 starts its search, it will use W1 as an exclude set to avoid returning the same neighbors
700   as branch0 (Fig.19) . Conversely, if branch1 reaches layer 0 first, the roles are reversed. Once the
701   searches from both branches are completed, W1 and W2 are combined to retrieve the final set of k
      nearest neighbors.

      The Search Layer algorithm is designed to operate within the HNSW++ hierarchical graph structure.
727
      It takes as input the hierarchical graph HN SW , the current layer index layer i, the query element
728   index q, the starting entry point ep, the number of neighbors to return ef , the branch index world,
729   an optional LID threshold lid threshold, and an optional set of nodes to exclude exclude set.
730
731
      Initially, the algorithm checks whether the entry point ep is part of the exclude set. If so, it searches
      for a new entry point that is not in the exclude set by exploring neighboring nodes.
732
733   Next, the search begins by identifying the nearest neighbors for the query node q within the specified
734   layer. The entry point ep is initialized as both the first candidate and the initial nearest neighbor.
735   The algorithm iteratively selects the closest candidate c, compares its distance to q with that of the
736   furthest neighbor f in the current nearest neighbors set, and updates the set if c is closer. This
737
      process continues until the desired ef nearest neighbors are identified, gradually refining the search
      by exploring neighboring nodes.
738
739   Before returning the final set of ef neighbors, the algorithm performs an additional check. If the
740   distance from the nearest node in the ef neighbors set to the query point is less than the dataset’s
741   average distance, and the node’s normalized LID value exceeds the threshold, the algorithm will
742   return a skip signal. In this case, the neighbor set W and the skip signal are passed to Algorithm 2
743
      for further processing.
