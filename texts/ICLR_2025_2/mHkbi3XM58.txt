009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Temporal prediction is inherently uncertain, but representing the ambiguity in nat-
012                ural image sequences is a challenging high-dimensional probabilistic inference
013                problem. For natural scenes, the curse of dimensionality renders explicit den-
014                sity estimation statistically and computationally intractable. Here, we describe
015                an implicit regression-based framework for learning and sampling the conditional
016                density of the next frame in a video given previous observed frames. We show that
017
                   sequence-to-image deep networks trained on a simple resilience-to-noise objective
                   function extract adaptive representations for temporal prediction. Synthetic exper-
018
                   iments demonstrate that this score-based framework can handle occlusion bound-
019
                   aries: unlike classical methods that average over bifurcating temporal trajectories,
020                it chooses among likely trajectories, selecting more probable options with higher
021                frequency. Furthermore, analysis of networks trained on natural image sequences
022                reveals that the representation automatically weights predictive evidence by its
023                reliability, which is a hallmark of statistical inference1 .

026   1       I NTRODUCTION
027
028   All organisms make temporal predictions, and more complex organisms make prediction about more
029   complex aspects of their environment. Even in the simplest cases and at very short timescales, tem-
030   poral prediction is difficult: sensory measurements are incomplete and insufficient to fully specify
031
      the future, making prediction uncertain. Consider for example observing an image sequence: the
      next frame in that sequence is only incompletely determined by past frames, and as a result there
032
      is a whole distribution of possible next-frames. Typically, the distribution of the next-frame has
033
      more than one mode, making video prediction challenging. In particular, the classical Minimum
034   Mean Squared Error approach to prediction (MMSE, Wiener, 1942) produces inaccurate and blurry
035   solutions. This failure is well understood: the MMSE solution is given by the expectation of the
036   next-frame, but since the distribution of the next-frame is multimodal, its expectation tends to have
037   a low probability. To avoid blurry predictions, video prediction methods based on optic flow have
038   been developed in image processing, and form the basis of most commonly used video codecs.
039   Unfortunately, these flow-based methods are prone to errors when motion is discontinuous, e.g.,
040   at occlusion boundaries, or non-rigid, e.g., on deformable objects. These failures highlight the
041   limitations of deterministic approaches: point estimates are inadequate for handling ambiguous sit-
042
      uations. We will consider a probabilistic formulation of video prediction: estimating and sampling
      from the conditional density of the next frame given the previous τ frames in the image sequence,
043
      p(xt+1 |xt , . . . , xt−τ +1 ). Abstractly, temporal prediction can be thought of as an inverse problem
044
      that requires prior information in order to recover the part of the image sequence obfuscated by the
045   arrow of time.
046
047   Learning a density from data is generally considered intractable for high-dimensional signals such
048
      as videos: the sample complexity of density estimation is exponential in the signal dimensionality
      (the so-called “curse of dimensionality”). As a result, the statistical difficulty of high-dimensional
049
      density modeling can only be approached by making strong assumptions, and many methods have
050
      been proposed to tackle the case of video prediction (Oprea et al., 2020). Each of these methods
051   imposes inductive biases through choices of objective function and/or prediction architecture. But,
052   overall, video prediction remained largely out of reach for statistical machine learning methods until

081                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    learning generative models (Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021b). They have
082                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    also been applied to video generation where they achieve impressive empirical results (Ho et al.,
083                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    2022; Blattmann et al., 2023; Bar-Tal et al., 2024; Brooks et al., 2024). These results typically
084                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    rely on huge datasets, opaque networks, and sophisticated text conditioning. In this paper, we will
085                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    consider a simplified diffusion-like framework where the learned representation can be analyzed.
086                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    Diffusion models combine two important ideas: score-matching estimation and reversing a diffusion
087                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    process. Score-matching bypasses the normalization constant that plagues maximum likelihood esti-
088                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    mation (Hyvärinen, 2005). This simplification arises from considering the gradient of the logarithm
089                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    of the data distribution—known as the score function—which does not depend on the problematic
090                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    normalization constant. Score-based estimation has its roots in the “empirical Bayes” subfield of
091                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    statistical inference, and the central role of the score of the noisy data distribution can be gener-
092                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    alized to many other types of estimation (Raphan & Simoncelli, 2011).       2     S CORE - BASED ESTIMATION AND SAMPLING OF CONDITIONAL DENSITIES
109
110
      Given access to a video dataset, i.e., image sequences {xt ∈ Rd , t = 1, . . . , T }, we want to predict
111   future frames on new videos, i.e., generate probable next-frames on test image sequences. To achieve
112   this, we describe a scalable framework for learning to sample from the distribution of the next
113   frame conditioned on the recent past. First, we assume that the temporal dependencies are local
114   and define a corresponding network architecture. Then, we introduce a useful intermediate object,
115   the score of the noisy observation distribution, and propose a regression objective for training a
116   network to approximate the score. Finally, we express an iterative procedure that utilizes the trained
117   network for sampling probable next-frames. These methods extend previous work on unconditional
118   density modeling (Kadkhodaie & Simoncelli, 2021) to the conditional setting of video prediction.
119
      An intuitive example with low-dimensional visualizations is given in Appendix B.
120
121   2.1   C ONDITIONING WITH A SEQUENCE - TO - IMAGE NETWORK ARCHITECTURE
122
      We assume that temporal dependencies are local and well approximated by the Markov condition:
123
124                                             p(xt+1 |x≤t ) = p(xt+1 |ct ),                               (1)
125
      where x≤t denotes all the frames until time t, and ct = [xt , . . . , xt−τ +1 ] denotes the past τ frames.
126
      We enforce this conditional independence assumption by considering networks with a finite memory
127   length. Specifically, we use deep convolutional networks that map sequences of images to estimates
128   of the next frame. The length of the temporal dependencies captured by this sequence-to-image
129   network can be varied by modifying the number of input frames. More details on the architecture
130   are provided in Appendix C. In the remainder of the text, we drop time subscripts for clarity and
131   write x for the target next frame, and c for the corresponding previous frames. We refer to the
132   conditional distribution of the next frame given past conditioning, p(x|c), as the data distribution.
133
134   2.2   L EARNING SCORE FUNCTIONS VIA DENOISING
135
136   We break down the problem of estimating the conditional distribution of the next frame into a family
137
      of easier sub-problems: given clean past conditioning frames, remove undesired noise from an ob-
      servation of the next frame. Training a network on this simple resilience-to-noise objective function
138
      across noise levels yields an adaptive estimator of the score of the noisy observation distribution.
139
140   Specifically, we sample noisy observations of the next frame conditioned on the previous frames:
141   y|c ∼ pσ (y|c), where the noise is additive, white, and Gaussian, i.e., y = x + σz, with z ∼ N (0, I).
142   We refer to the distribution of the noisy signal given conditioning, pσ (y|c), as the observation dis-
143
      tribution. It is well-known in statistical estimation that the Minimum Mean Squared Error (MMSE)
      denoising function is given by the posterior expectation:
144
                                                 h                i
145
                                     arg min E ||x − x̂(y, c)||2 = E[x|y, c].                            (2)
146                                        x̂
147   Remarkably, this posterior expectation can be expressed as a step in the direction of the score of
148   the observation distribution scaled by the variance of the noise, an identity attributed to Tweedie or
149   Miyasawa (Robbins, 1956; Miyasawa et al., 1961):
150
                                        E[x|y, c] = y + σ 2 ∇y log pσ (y|c).                                (3)
151
152   This important identity links MMSE denoising with the score function, i.e., the gradient of the
153   logarithm of the observation distribution (a detailed derivation is provided in Appendix A). The key
154   idea of the score-based framework is to exploit this link in the reverse direction: first train a denoiser
155   to minimize MSE, and then treat the denoising residual as an approximation of the scaled score
156   function. In Section 2.3, we will describe how to use this approximate score function to ascend the
157   probability gradient and draw samples from the data distribution.
158   We consider networks that jointly process sequences of τ + 1 frames and produce estimates, x̂,
159   of the target frame, x. The input sequences contain a noisy observation of the target frame, y,
160   and τ past conditioning frames, c. Optimizing such a network on the video next-frame denoising
161   objective, eq. (2), yields a denoising residual, f (y, c) = x̂(y, c) − y, that approximates the scaled
      score functions, σ 2 ∇y log pσ (y|c), and therefore depends on the noise level, σ. In the remainder


                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      of this paper, we will integrate across noise levels. Specifically, we train networks to remove noise
163   of arbitrary magnitude, i.e., the expectation in eq. (2) is taken over the signal x, the noise z, and
164   the noise level σ. Such networks must (at least implicitly) infer the distortion level, σ, and are
165   called blind universal denoisers (Mohan et al., 2020). As a result, a trained universal blind denoiser
166   contains information about a whole family of scores: {∇y log pσ (y|c)}σ . Recall that adding noise
167   to the target next frame is equivalent to smoothing the data distribution with a Gaussian kernel, the
168   more noise is added, the larger the extent of the smoothing. This family therefore contains scores
169   at all levels of smoothness, which corresponds to a scale-space representation (Witkin, 1983) of
170   the score of the data distribution, ∇x log p(x|c). In summary, we train universal blind denoisers
171
      to adaptively process signals across distortion levels and estimate a family of score functions—a
      property that will be exploited in the sampling algorithm described next.

173

      2.3   G ENERATING PREDICTED FRAMES VIA SCORE ASCENT
175
      Assuming that we have trained a denoiser, we now turn to the question of sampling from the distri-
176   bution implicit in the network, i.e., predicting probable next frames conditioned on past frames. In
177   the previous section, we showed that the residual of the optimal denoising function is proportional
178   to the gradient of the logarithm of the observation distribution, eq. (3). We now describe an iterative
179   procedure that starts from an arbitrary initialization and uses the denoiser to climb the gradient of
180   the logarithm of the observation distribution towards more probable images.
181
      Specifically, we define a conditional sampling algorithm that takes partial iterative denoising steps
182   until reaching a sample from the data distribution, p(x|c). Starting from an arbitrary image, y0 , this
183   algorithm moves the candidate frame, yk , in the direction of the denoising residual, and optionally
184   adds a small amount of fresh noise, zk ∼ N (0, I). This algorithm iterates the map:
185
                                       yk = yk−1 + αk f (yk−1 , c) + γk zk ,                              (4)
186
187   where the parameter αk controls the step-size and the parameter γk controls the amplitude of the
188   additive noise. Importantly, we choose γk so as to reduce the effective noise level on each itera-
189   tion. Adding noise along the sampling iterations avoids getting stuck in local maxima and promotes
190   exploration. A more detailed description of these sampling parameters is provided in Appendix E.
191   This procedure follows the estimated scores given by a trained denoiser and gradually reduces the
192   effective noise level of a candidate frame until it reaches a sample of the next frame. As this al-
193   gorithm progresses, the candidate frame effectively traverses the scale-space family of observation
194   distributions from large to small noise level. Such a coarse-to-fine iterative refinement approach en-
195   ables local search in complex high-dimensional landscapes. Importantly, this annealing schedule is
196   set by the network itself: at each iteration, the step size depends on the magnitude of the denoising
197   residual, this magnitude is set by the blind universal denoiser, i.e., it automatically adapts to the
198
      effective noise level of the candidate frame.

349   Conditional denoising performance. Several U-nets with varying conditioning lengths were
350   trained for next frame prediction on a generic dataset of small natural image sequences (see de-
351   scription in Appendix D). Estimated next frames for denoisers with varying number of conditioning
352   frames are shown for an example image sequences in Figure 6. This result holds in general over
353   the whole test set, as evidenced by the summarized performance plot of these networks. We quan-
354
      tify performance according to Peak Signal to Noise Ratio2 . In the unconditional case (i.e., τ = 0),
      the network only exploits static image structure and performance is linear in terms of input-output
355
      PSNR, with slope of approximately one-half, and meets the identity line around 40 dB. For condi-
356
      tional image denoising, there is a gradual performance improvement that is particularly salient at
357   low input PSNR values. The largest increases in output PSNR is obtained from conditioning on
358   one baseline image (i.e., τ = 1). Adding a second conditioning frame (i.e., τ = 2) improves per-
359   formance further, which is indirect evidence that the network is making use of motion information.
360   The benefits of conditioning reach a plateau with three past frames. Notice that trained denoiser
361   networks with two and three conditioning frames match the performance of networks trained just
362   for prediction (i.e., two input frames xt−1 , xt , no noisy observation yt+1 ), indicated by a horizontal
363   dashed line at 28dB. Remarkably, at very low input PSNR values the network automatically revert
364   to pure temporal prediction, ignoring the uninformative observation.

      4    R ELATED WORK
487
488
      Video prediction. Estimating optic flow is an essential component of standard video codecs and
489
      deep network solutions have been proposed (Wu et al., 2022). But explicit motion warping fails
490
      around occlusion boundaries and non-rigid objects (Baker et al., 2011). Direct next-frame predic-
491   tion has been explored with generative adversarial networks (Mathieu et al., 2016). But the saddle
492   point objective function is difficult to train and suffers from mode collapse (Goodfellow, 2016).
493   Methods based on variational autoencoders (Kingma & Welling, 2014) tend to suffer from posterior
494   collapse (Wang et al., 2021). Normalizing flows (Dinh et al., 2014) are limited to architectures that
495   admit tractable Jacobians at each layer (Papamakarios et al., 2021).
496
      Diffusion based video generation. Recent work on video generation uses diffusion models (Har-
497   vey et al., 2022; Höppe et al., 2022) and has been applied to video prediction Voleti et al. (2022).
498   These studies obtain impressive empirical results but rely on complex architectures and sampling al-
499   gorithms that make their results difficult to interpret. Mathematically principled approaches to video
500   prediction have been proposed Chen et al. (2024). Here we describe a simple score-based frame-
501   work that facilitates visualization of the learned representation, casting some light on the adaptivity
502   of trained networks and their ability to exploit spatio-temporal structure in image sequences.
503

      5    D ISCUSSION
506
507   We describe a simple framework for estimating and sampling from the distribution of probable
508
      next-frames in image sequences and use this framework to visualize and study the adaptivity of
      deep network representations. We show how deep denoising networks trained on a simple regres-
509
      sion task—minimizing mean squared error—implicitly represent the score of the data distribution.
510
      We put this implicit density model to use by generating probable next-frames. Experiments on syn-
511   thetic data show that the sampled next-frames correctly handle ambiguous situations, choosing a
512   depth order for objects that occlude each other, which contrast with traditional methods that blur
513   such ambiguous cases. Furthermore, we showed that denoisers display hallmarks of probabilistic
514   computation: the network adaptively combines information from past conditioning and noisy obser-
515   vations, appropriately weighting them according to their reliability. The adaptability to the amount
516   of predictive information is remarkable: the network evaluates its own ability to extract evidence
517   from past frames. The simplicity of the score-based framework suggests that density estimation and
518   sampling—although very challenging in high-dimensions—may be tackled with elementary tools:
519
      (non-linear) least squares regression and (coarse-to-fine) gradient ascent. This is only possible be-
      cause i) noise offers a local learning signal and enables sampling, and ii) the network architecture
520
      has inductive biases that are appropriate for natural image sequences.
521
522   What is noise good for? The functional role played by noise plays in both estimation and sampling
523   and can be summarized by three properties: regularity, diversity, and locality. First, adding noise
524   smooths the energy landscape, making it easier to learn and to climb with local search methods
525
      (both estimation and sampling are gradient based). Second, adding noise during sampling drives
      exploration, allowing the samples to escape from local maxima, and promoting diversity. Third,
526
      resilience to noise is a local objective in the sense that it brings random Gaussian samples towards
527
      the data distribution incrementally. Such locality in the space of densities offers a powerful simpli-
528   fication: the transformation from data to Gaussian noise and back to data is broken down into small
529   steps. Denoising provides a learning signal for each of these intermediate steps and allows learning
530   to proceed in parallel. We conjecture that this locality of denoising underlies the statistical efficiency
531   of score estimation compared to traditional approaches based on end-to-end objective functions.
532
      Implicit bias of the architecture. The performance achieved by deep networks on (conditional)
533
      image denoising is remarkable. Accurate denoisers provide good approximations of score functions
534   and fuel the success of diffusion models. Understanding how the architecture of deep denoising net-
535   works enables such denoising performance is of great interest. Local linear analysis affords partial
536   access to the adaptive network representation, but it does not elucidate the formation of these rep-
537   resentations. Further study of deep network implicit biases is necessary, and the simple framework
538   and visualization methods described here offer a starting point. Finally, it would be interesting to
539   leverage video prediction for extracting abstract representations. Considering an encoder-decoder
      architecture instead of a direct sequence-to-image mapping is a promising direction.

689
690   Estimation. A simple example consisting of a one dimensional distribution comprising two point
691   masses is displayed in Figure 9. At very high noise levels the score points in the direction of the

694
      consider universal blind denoisers, i.e., mappings that automatically adjust to the noise level. The

      C    D ESCRIPTION OF ARCHITECTURES
757
758
      For all numerical experiments, we use U-nets composed of convolutional layers and rectifying non-
759
      linearities at three spatial scales (Ronneberger et al., 2015). We consider sequence-to-image net-
760   works, i.e., mappings from τ past frames and one noisy observation to an estimated next frame:
761   x̂θ : Rd×τ × Rd → Rd .
762
763   Conditioning. The networks take as input the past conditioning frames concatenated with the
764   noisy observation, i.e., five dimensional tensors of shape: [batch, channel, time, height, width],
765   where time = τ + 1. The size of the first layer spatio-temporal convolution kernel is adjusted to
766   match the number of input frames: (τ + 1) × 3 × 3. Concatenating past frames as additional input
767   is a simple approach to conditioning and it has the advantage of being well suited to temporal pre-
768   diction: conditioning frames have the same shape as the noisy observation, and local convolutional
769   processing can make use of these two sources of evidence for the denoising task.
770
771   Details. At each scale, the networks cascade two blocks, each containing a layer of spatial con-
772   volution, then layer-normalization, and finally rectification. Specifically, the convolutional filters
773   are of size 3 × 3 in space. The first stage contains 64 filters. Each successive stage of the encoder
774
      doubles the number of channels and downsamples the image by a factor 2 in each spatial dimension.
      Each stage of the decoder reverses this, upsampling spatially, and halving the number of channels.
775
      Each stage of the decoder also combines information from the upsampled coarse stage with the
776
      output of the encoding at the corresponding stage. The network directly outputs an estimated next
777   frame, i.e., there is no input-output skip connection. In total these networks contain almost 2 million
778   parameters.
779
780   Homogeneity. Importantly, we remove all bias terms from the networks, i.e., there are no additive
781   constants in the convolutional layers or in the normalization layers. Such bias-free networks are
782   positively homogeneous of order 1, i.e., scaling the input by a positive number also scales the output
783   by the same amount. This simplification of the network architecture was observed (Mohan et al.,
784   2020) to facilitate generalization across noise levels, moreover, it enables the local linear analysis
785   presented in section 3.2.

      D    D ESCRIPTION OF DATASETS
788
789
      Moving Leaves. Each image sequence contains two disks on a blank background that move and
790
      occlude each other. In each sequence, both disks are assumed to have the same 3D physical size
791
      but their distance to the imaging plane is randomized. As a result, each disk’s projected 2D size is
792   a reliable, although indirect, cue to its distance in the scene. The disk with larger projected 2D size
793   always occlude the smaller one. The trajectories of each disk are sampled from Gaussian processes,
794   and their average speed of motion scales inversely with distance. Thus, speed in the image plane
795   provides an additional (albeit weaker) cue to a disk’s distance.
796
      This synthetic dataset contains 105 image sequences, and is split into train and test set with at a
797
      9:1 ratio. Each sequence is composed of 11 frames and is of size 32 × 32 pixels. For each image
798   sequence, luminance and depth are sampled uniformly at random. The spatial positions (x and y) are
799   sampled from a Gaussian process, and the bandwidth of the GP scales with depth (smoother slower
800   trajectories for objects further away). In each image sequence, there is at least one frame where half
801   of smaller disk is occluded by the larger disk.
802
      There are several benefits to using such a procedural dataset: it enables sampling a distribution along
803
      continuous features such as size, speed, texture, etc.; and it is easy to change the rules and control
804   the difficulty of the task. For example allowing the disks to move along the depth axis, thereby
805   decoupling size from distance; or changing the occlusion rule (e.g., assuming the brighter disk
806   always occludes the darker, or making the occlusion rule stochastic; or changing the number of disks,
807   etc.). This dataset is also useful to study the representation of fast small objects, which are subject to
808   temporal aliasing. The moving leaves procedural dataset was designed to illustrate the fact that, even
809   in elementary mechanical scenarios, probabilistic modeling is appropriate. Indeed, measurements
      are always incomplete and the probabilistic framework can readily handle this ambiguity.

      DAVIS32. We use the DAVIS dataset (Pont-Tuset et al., 2017), which was originally designed
811   as a benchmark for video object segmentation. Image sequences in this dataset contain diverse
812   motion of scenes and objects (eg., with fixed or moving camera, and objects moving at different
813   speeds and directions), which make next frame prediction challenging. Small image sequences were
814   cropped out of the original DAVIS dataset, each sequence is composed of 11 frames of size 32 × 32
815   pixels. The spatial crops were taken at 21 spatial locations and 3 different spatial scales. The frames
816   were mapped to grayscale and their intensity rescaled to lies in [0, 1]. These approximately 5 · 104
817   sequences were divided into about 36 thousands training sequences (or about 400 thousand frames),
818   and about 5 thousands test sequences (or about 55 thousand frames).

821
822   Training procedure. The networks are trained to minimize mean squared error weighted by in-
823   verse variance of the noise. For each frame in a minibatch, the logarithm of the variance of the noise
824   is sampled uniformly in the range [10−5 , 102 ] (corresponding to input PSNRs in [-20dB, 50dB]).
825   These choices of MSE weighting and of variance distribution is equivalent, up to reparameteriza-
826   tion, to the commonly used likelihood weighting (Song et al., 2021a). It can be shown that this
827   training procedure minimizes an upper bound on the conditional entropy of the future frame x given
828
      the past c (up to constants). Notice that these choices emphasize the samples with low noise by
      sampling them more frequently and by amplifying their contribution to the loss. In practice, small
829
      gains in denoising performance, especially in the low noise regime, can have a large impact on the
830
      quality of generated samples. The models are trained for 1000 epochs on either the moving leaves,
831   or the DAVIS32 datasets using the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2015) with default parameters
832   and a learning rate η = 1 · 10−3 . The learning rate is halved on epoch 500, and then every 100
833   epochs. A detailed description is presented in Algorithm 1.

      Sampling algorithm. New samples are generated by stochastic score ascent, using a family of
849   learned approximate scores. The procedure is initialized on pure noise of large variance, σ∞ =
850   3, The algorithm proceeds by taking steps in the direction of the denoiser residual and gradually
851   reducing the effective noise level. This effective noise√    level at iteration k is defined as the root-

853   signal. The sampling algorithm terminates when the effective noise level falls below a threshold,
854   we used σ0 = 0.01 in our experiments. The amount of noise added at each step of the sampling

857   the additive noise is set to: γk2 = ((1 − βαk )2 − (1 − αk )2 )σk2 . This choice ensures that the effective
