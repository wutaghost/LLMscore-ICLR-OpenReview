009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               State estimation for nonlinear state space models (SSMs) is a challenging task.
012            Existing assimilation methodologies predominantly assume Gaussian posteriors
013            on physical space, where true posteriors become inevitably non-Gaussian. We
014            propose Deep Bayesian Filtering (DBF) for data assimilation on nonlinear SSMs.
015            DBF constructs new latent variables ht in addition to the original physical vari-
016            ables zt and assimilates observations ot . By (i) constraining the state transition
017            on the new latent space to be linear and (ii) learning a Gaussian inverse obser-
018            vation operator r(ht |ot ), posteriors remain Gaussian. Notably, the structured de-
019            sign of test distributions enables an analytical formula for the recursive compu-
020
               tation, eliminating the accumulation of Monte Carlo sampling errors across time
               steps. DBF trains the Gaussian inverse observation operators r(ht |ot ) and other
021
               latent SSM parameters (e.g., dynamics matrix) by maximizing the evidence lower
022
               bound. Experiments demonstrate that DBF outperforms model-based approaches
023            and latent assimilation methods in tasks where the true posterior distribution on
024            physical space is significantly non-Gaussian.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Data assimilation (DA) is a crucial technique across various scientific domains. Its primary objective
031   is to estimate the trajectory and current state of a system by integrating an imperfect model with
032   partially informative observations. Specifically, given a series of observations T time steps o1:T ,
033
      the goal is to infer the posterior distribution of the system’s physical variables zt : p(zt |o1:t ). DA
      has been widely applied in fields such as weather forecasting (Hunt et al., 2007; Lorenc, 2003;
034
      Andrychowicz et al., 2023), ocean research analysis (Ohishi et al., 2024), sea surface temperature
035
      prediction (Larsen et al., 2007), seismic wave analysis (Alfonzo & Oliver, 2020), multi-sensor fusion
036   localization (Bach & Ghil, 2023), and visual object tracking (Awal et al., 2023).
037
038   A key challenge in DA arises from the non-Gaussian nature of the posterior distributions p(zt |o1:t ),
039
      which results from the inherent nonlinearity in both the system dynamics and observation models.
      Despite this, many operational DA systems, such as those used in weather forecasting, rely on meth-
040
      ods like the ensemble Kalman Filter (EnKF) (Evensen, 1994; Bishop et al., 2001) for sequential
041
      state filtering (i.e., p(zt |o1:t )) and the four-dimensional variational method (4D-Var) for retrospec-
042   tive state analysis (i.e., p(zt |o1:T ), t < T ). These approaches assume Gaussianity in their test
043   distributions q(zt |o1:t ) or q(zt |o1:T ), a simplification driven by computational constraints. While
044   exact methods such as bootstrap Particle Filters (PF) or sequential Monte Carlo (SMC) (Chopin
045   & Papaspiliopoulos, 2020; Daum & Huang, 2007; Hu & van Leeuwen, 2021) could compute the
046   true posterior, their performance degrades significantly when the number of particles is insuffi-
047   cient (Beskos et al., 2014). This issue is exacerbated in high-dimensional systems, making SMC
048   approaches impractical for many physical problems.
049   To address these limitations, we propose a novel variational inference approach called Deep
050   Bayesian Filtering (DBF) for posterior estimation. Our strategy consists of two main components:
051   (i) constraining the test distribution to remain Gaussian to ensure computational tractability, and,
052   in cases where the original dynamics are nonlinear, (ii) leveraging a nonlinear mapping to enhance
053   the expressive capability of the test distribution. The DBF methodology diverges into two paths
      depending on the nature of the system dynamics, whether linear Gaussian or nonlinear:

054
      Linear dynamics When the system’s dynamics p(zt+1 |zt ) are linear, DBF assumes Gaussianity
055   in the original space, similar to traditional methods. However, DBF introduces the concept of the
056   inverse observation operator (IOO; see also Frerix et al. 2021) to construct Gaussian test distribu-
057   tions q(zt |o1:t ). The IOO, along with any unknown system parameters, are trained to minimize the
058   Kullback-Leibler divergence between the test distribution q(zt |o1:t ) and the true posterior p(zt |o1:t ).
059   The IOO and the system parameters are trained without teacher signals zt .
060
061   Nonlinear dynamics In the more common case of nonlinear dynamics, DBF operates in a latent
062   space, assuming Gaussianity in the latent variables ht . The original physical variables are recovered
063   through a nonlinear mapping function ϕ, implemented via neural networks (NNs). This nonlinear
064
      mapping allows for a more flexible representation of the test distribution q(zt |o1:t ). The IOO and
      other parameters are trained in a supervised manner (i.e., zt is used during training).
065
066   For state space models (SSMs) with nonlinear dynamics, DBF functions as a variational autoen-
067   coder (VAE) that adheres to the Markov property. Posterior distributions of the latent variables
068   ht are expressed in a Bayesian framework. This approach is closely related to dynamical VAEs
069   (DVAEs, Girin et al. 2021 for a review), which use VAEs to model time-series data. However, DBF
070
      distinguishes itself by its posterior design. Unlike DVAEs, where Monte Carlo sampling is required
      for inference (see Sec. 2.6.1), DBF allows for the analytical computation of the prediction step,
071
      recursively computing posteriors through closed-form expressions.
072
073   When applied to problems with nonlinear or unknown dynamics, DBF can be interpreted as learn-
074   ing the Koopman operator (Koopman, 1931) using NNs. The discovery of such latent spaces and
075   operators through machine learning has been extensively studied (Takeishi et al., 2017; Lusch et al.,
076
      2018; Azencot et al., 2020) and will be experimentally validated through the handling of nonlinear
      filtering tasks involving chaotic dynamics.
077
078   Key contributions of the proposed DBF methodology include:
079
080
             • DBF is the first VAE-based model for time-series data that maintains a posterior structure
               faithful to the Markov property in SSMs.
081
082          • For linear dynamics, DBF extends the Kalman Filter (KF) with a more flexible observation
083            update. A simple object-tracking experiment is presented in Sec. 3.1. Additional experi-
084
               ments in Sec. B demonstrates that DBF can infer unknown system parameters via training.
085          • For nonlinear dynamics, DBF constructs a new latent space for data assimilation, allowing
086            for the analytical integration of time steps and preventing the accumulation of Monte Carlo
087
               sampling errors. This is accomplished through the application of Koopman operator theory,
               which ensures that the model’s representational power is maintained, as long as the latent
088
               space is sufficiently high-dimensional (see Sec. 3.2 and 3.3).
089
090          • As a generative model, DBF estimates the uncertainty of the physical variables zt , in con-
091
               trast to 3D- and 4D-Var, which yield only point estimates (see Sec. 3.2 and Fig. 3).
092          • The linear constraint on dynamics stabilizes the training process, which is known to be
093            unstable in standard recurrent NNs (see Sec. 3.3 and Fig. 6).
094
      DBF has demonstrated superior performance over classical DA algorithms and latent assimilation
095   methods in scenarios with highly non-Gaussian posteriors, particularly in the presence of strongly
096   nonlinear observation operators or large observation noise.

      2.4   DBF FOR NONLINEAR DYNAMICS , LINEAR / NONLINEAR OBSERVATIONS
163
164   In this scenario, the Gaussianity of the test distribution is lost during the predict step, making it
165   impossible to apply the original dynamics over the physical variables zt . Therefore, we introduce a
166   new set of latent variables ht and assume a dynamics model over ht : p(ht+1 |ht ) = N (ht+1 |Aht , Q)
167   (see panel (b) in Fig. 1). The IOO maps observations into the latent variables ht : r(ht |ot ). The
168   recursive formula follows Equations 4 and 5. To retrieve the distribution of the original physical
169   variables zt , we introduce an emission model p(zt |ht ) = N (zt ; ϕ(ht ), R), where ϕ is represented
170
      by a NN. By marginalizing over ht with this emission model, a trained DBF can generate samples
      of zt that follow the test distribution q(zt |o1:t ) given observations o1:t .
171
172   Although the dynamics operator A for the latent variables ht is linear, it can express any nonlinear
173   dynamics if the latent space is sufficiently high-dimensional. The Koopman operator (Koopman,
174   1931) provides a framework for representing nonlinear systems by mapping observables—functions
175   of the system’s state—into a higher-dimensional space where the dynamics are linear. For a system
176
      zt+1 = f (zt ), the Koopman operator K is a linear operator acting on a set of observables g(z), such
      that Kg(zt ) = g(f (zt )). This reformulates the system as ht+1 = Aht in the latent space, where A
177
      is the dynamics matrix learned by DBF. While the physical dynamics f (z) are nonlinear, the Koop-
178
      man operator ensures the existence of an embedding that linearizes the dynamics, enabling recursive
179   computation of test distributions. Discovering such embeddings in finite dimensions has been widely
180   studied (Takeishi et al., 2017; Lusch et al., 2018; Azencot et al., 2020). In high-dimensional simula-
181   tions, the true degrees of freedom are often far fewer than the simulated variables, making surrogate
182   modeling with the Koopman operator a promising approach to reducing computational costs.

221   2.6.1    DYNAMICAL VARIATIONAL AUTOENCODERS
222
223   DVAEs (see Girin et al. 2021 for a review) are a broad class of models incorporating time-series
224
      architectures into VAEs, with DBF as a specialized subcategory. Key differences include (i) the
      posterior design and realization of the dynamics step, and (ii) the loss function.
225
226
      posterior design Our strategy for the test distribution is to incorporate an appropriate architecture
227
      that reflects the Markov property in the time dimension of the test distribution. The IOO, r(ht |ot ),
228
      and the linear dynamics model serve as key instruments in constructing the test posterior distribu-
229   tions. A distinguishing feature of our methodology is that each component’s role is defined with
230   respect to the Markov property of the state-space model (SSM) and is clearly differentiated from
231   other components involved in posterior construction. For example, the IOO influences only the up-
232   date step and does not affect the prediction step. We refer to this methodology as ”Bayes-Faithful”
233   due to its tailored design for SSMs that exhibit the Markov property.
234
      In contrast, the test posterior distributions in DVAEs are constructed using RNNs. The complexity
235   of the transition model prevents the analytical computation of latent variables across time steps. As
236   a result, these values can only be estimated via Monte Carlo sampling. Consequently, during infer-
237   ence, successive Monte Carlo sampling (“cascade trick”; Girin et al. 2021) becomes unavoidable.

262
      S4 by introducing stochasticity through a VAE-like structure. Both LS4 and DBF employ linear
      SSMs and Gaussian posterior approximations, but DBF updates the mean and covariance using a
263
      recursive formula based on Bayes’ rule, while the construction of posteriors in LS4 is not recursive.

266
267   Various approaches have been explored to address LGSS limitations, including linearizing the model
268   via first-order approximations like the extended Kalman Filter (EKF), approximating populations
269   with a Gaussian distribution in the ensemble Kalman Filter (EnKF; Evensen 1994), and using NNs
      to approximate the Kalman gain (Revach et al., 2022). EKFNet (Xu & Niu, 2024) assumes EKF for

270
      the construction of test distribution and train the SSM parameters. Auto-EnKF (Chen et al., 2022;
271   2023) leverages EnKF and train the model by optimizing ELBO. The EnKF and its variants (e.g.,
272   ETKF; Bishop et al. 2001) are commonly used in real-time data assimilation for weather forecasting.
273   However, these methods rely on the KF’s posterior update equations, limiting the expressivity of
274   the distributions. Additionally, computations for covariance matrices become challenging in high-
275   dimensional spaces, requiring specialized techniques for computational efficiency.

277   2.6.3   S AMPLING - BASED METHODS
278
279
      The Particle Filter is a popular method for assimilating any posterior. However, achieving adequate
      particle density in high-dimensional state spaces poses significant challenges. Insufficient density of
280
      particles leads to particle degeneracy, where few particles explain the observed data (Beskos et al.,
281
      2014). In contrast, DBF directly learns to position density through the IOO, offering advantages for
282   high-dimensional tasks. The Particle Flow Filter (PFF; Daum & Huang 2007; Hu & van Leeuwen
283   2021) addresses particle degeneracy by moving particles according to gradient flow and effectively
284   scales to nonlinear SSMs with hidden state dimensions up to 1000 (Hu & van Leeuwen, 2021).
285

      3.1              L INEAR DYNAMICS : OBJECT TRACKING
330
331   In a single-object tracking problem, a detector identifies a bounding box for the object in each
332   frame, and these boxes are then connected across frames. When the object is not fully visible or
333   is obscured, the detector often fails to accurately determine its position. In such scenarios, the KF
334   aids by predicting and assimilating the object’s true position. However, a key limitation of the KF
335   is its reliance on a fixed observation model throughout the tracking process. While low-confidence
336   observations can provide valuable approximate position information, they may also mislead the
337
      tracker with inaccurate data, potentially degrading overall tracking performance.
338   We demonstrate that DBF can enhance tracking stability without requiring additional training. Dur-
339   ing the computation of the posterior p(zt |o1:t ) from p(zt |o1:t−1 ), the importance of the observation
340   ot is regulated via Gθ (ot ). This allows the observational confidence to be effectively incorporated
341   into the posterior estimation. We evaluate the tracking performance using the “airplane” category
342   from the LaSOT dataset (Fan et al., 2019; 2021).
343   We use the first 1,000 frames from 20 videos for evaluation. The first 10 videos serve as a validation
344   set for determining filter parameters (see Sec. C.1), while the performance is assessed using videos
345   11–20. Each set of 1,000 frames is divided into 20 subsets of 50 frames. Filters are initialized at the
346   ground truth coordinates of the bounding box in the first frame, after which each filter is responsible
347   for tracking the bounding box throughout the subset. We employ the YOLOv8n model (Jocher
348   et al., 2023) as the object detector. The detector outputs the bounding box position, X, along with
349
      a confidence score, c. A detection threshold of 0.01 is applied. When multiple bounding boxes are
      detected, the one with the highest posterior probability is selected.
350
351   The bounding box coordinates are used as fθ (ot ) = X. We experiment with linear confidence
352   Gθ (ot ) ∝ c and squared confidence Gθ (ot ) ∝ c2 and find that the squared confidence Gθ (ot ) ∝ c2
353   perform better. For further settings, see Sec. C.1.

409   This section presents our experiments with a double pendulum system, selected for its nonlinear and
410   chaotic behavior. The pendulum consists of two 1 kg masses, P1 and P2, connected by two 1 meter
411   bars, B1 and B2. One end of the bar B1 is fixed at the origin (“O”), with the other end attached to
412   P1. Mass P2 is connected to P1 via bar B2. A schematic of the setup is shown in panel (a) of Fig. 3.
413
      We use the angles θ1 and θ2 , and the two angular velocities, ω1 and ω2 , as target physical variables.
414   The latent dimension for DBF, VRNN, SRNN, and DKF is set to 50. For the choice of the latent
415   dimensions, refer to Sec. E.1. Observation data consists of the two-dimensional spatial positions of
416   masses P1 and P2 , corrupted by Gaussian noise. The observation operator combines trigonometric
417   functions for θ1 and θ2 which are highly nonlinear. Experiments are conducted with noise levels of
418   σ = 0.1, 0.3, and 0.5 [m], with a time step of 0.03 [s] between observations. In the emission model
419   p(zt |ht ), we assume von Mises distributions for θ1 and θ2 , while ω1 and ω2 follow Gaussians.
420   Table 1 presents the RMSE between the physical variables and the mean of the filtered distribution.
421   For both the angles θ and angle velocities ω, we compute the averages of the two variables across
422   two pendulums. Training for KalmanNet was unsuccessful under all conditions. For the DVAEs,
423   we exclude failed initial conditions (2/15 for VRNN and DKF, and 3/15 for SRNN) when calcu-
424   lating the RMSE. DBF outperforms both model-based and latent assimilation methods across all
425   settings, showing significant improvements in estimating ω, which cannot be inferred from a single
426   observation. Fig. 3 (b) illustrates an example of RMSE evolution during assimilation, where DBF
427   consistently outperforms the other methods. The assimilation of ω occurs within the first ∼ 20 steps,
428
      maintaining an excellent estimation accuracy throughout the experiment.
429   A key feature of DBF is its ability to generate samples of zt and assess the uncertainty in state
430   estimates. To evaluate this capability, we analyze the distributions of normalized errors defined as
431   ϵnorm,t,i = (zt,sample,i − zt,i )/δi , where zt,i represents the true value of dimension i at time t,
      and δi is the standard deviation of zt,sample,i . We collect ϵnorm,t,i across all time steps, focusing


                                                                      8

432
      on i = ω1 and i = ω2 , since θ1 and θ2 follow von Mises distributions. If the uncertainty esti-
433   mates are accurate, ϵnorm,t,i should approximate a Gaussian distribution with a standard deviation
434   of one. To quantify the accuracy, we compute the symmetric KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence)
435   KLsym [p, q] = (KL[p||q] + KL[q||p])/2 between the histogram of ϵnorm,t,i and a unit Gaussian.

517   4        L IMITATION
518
519   DBF’s learning of IOO requires a training phase, unlike classical model-based data assimilation
520   methods. Specifically, when dealing with nonlinear dynamics, DBF requires either: (i) a pair of
521   (zt , ot ) generated from the original SSM, (ii) a pair of (zt , ot ) obtained via, e.g., retrospective re-
522   analysis (ERA5; Hersbach et al. 2020 in weather forecasting), or (iii) a pretrained Koopman operator
523
      and observed data ot .
524   In the Lorenz96 experiment, DBF’s performance with direct observation with σ = 1 falls short
525   compared to EnKF and ETKF. In this setting, the non-Gaussianity of posteriors is weak, resulting in
526   minor approximation errors due to Gaussian assumptions. Consequently, a model-based approach
527   may be more advantageous in such situations, as it leverages complete SSM knowledge without
528   introducing training biases.
529
530   5        C ONCLUSION
531
532   We propose DBF, a novel DA method. DBF is a NN-based extension of the KF designed to handle
533   nonlinear observations. While constraining the test distributions to remain Gaussian, DBF enhances
534   their representational capacity by leveraging nonlinear transform expressed by a NN. DBF is the first
535   “Bayes-Faithful” amortized variational inference methodology, constructing test distributions that
536   mirror the inference structure of a SSM with the Markov property. This structured inference enables
537
      analytical computation of test distributions, preventing the accumulation of Monte Carlo sampling
      errors over time steps. DBF exhibits superior performance over existing methods in scenarios where
538
      posterior distributions become highly non-Gaussian, such as in the presence of nonlinear observation
539
      operators or significant observation noise.

1147   solve ivp function in SciPy, with a relative tolerance rtol = 10−2 and an absolute tolerance of
1148   atol = 10−2 . Gaussian noise with standard deviations of σ = 1, 3, or 5 is added to all measure-

1157
