011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Blind Image Quality Assessment (BIQA) has advanced significantly through deep
014            learning, but the scarcity of large-scale labeled datasets remains a challenge.
015            While synthetic data offers a promising solution, models trained on existing syn-
016
               thetic datasets often show limited generalization ability. In this work, we make a
               key observation that representations learned from synthetic datasets often exhibit
017
               a discrete and clustered pattern that hinders regression performance: features of
018
               high-quality images cluster around reference images, while those of low-quality
019            images cluster based on distortion types. Our analysis reveals that this issue stems
020            from the distribution of synthetic data rather than model architecture. Conse-
021            quently, we introduce a novel framework SynDR-IQA, which reshapes synthetic
022            data distribution to enhance BIQA generalization. Based on theoretical derivations
023            of sample diversity and redundancy’s impact on generalization error, SynDR-IQA
024            employs two strategies: distribution-aware diverse content upsampling, which en-
025            hances visual diversity while preserving content distribution, and density-aware
026            redundant cluster downsampling, which balances samples by reducing the density
027
               of densely clustered areas. Extensive experiments across three cross-dataset set-
               tings (synthetic-to-authentic, synthetic-to-algorithmic, and synthetic-to-synthetic)
028
               demonstrate the effectiveness of our method. Additionally, as a data-based ap-
029
               proach, SynDR-IQA can be coupled with model-based methods without increas-
030            ing inference costs. The source code will be publicly available.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Blind Image Quality Assessment (BIQA) aims to automatically and accurately evaluate image qual-
036   ity without relying on reference images Li et al. (2016); Yan et al. (2019). It plays a crucial role in
037   enhancing user experience in multimedia applications, improving the robustness of downstream im-
038   age processing algorithms, and guiding the optimization of image enhancement methods. However,
039   the BIQA task is challenging due to its complexity and high association with human perception.
040
      In recent years, mainstream BIQA methods have greatly surpassed traditional methods owing to the
041
      powerful representational capabilities of deep learning models. However, the success of deep learn-
042   ing largely relies on large-scale annotated datasets. The high cost of acquiring subjective quality
043   labels limits the growth of existing datasets. The availability of reference images and the controlla-
044   bility of quality degradation in synthetic distortions suggest that low-cost data augmentation through
045   artificially synthesized data appears to be a feasible solution. In practice, training directly on existing
046   synthetic distortion datasets results in suboptimal quality representations with limited generalization
047   capabilities.
048   We observe a key phenomenon: models trained on synthetic data tend to produce a discrete and
049   clustered feature distribution. Specifically, as shown in Fig. 1 high-quality image features form dis-
050   tinct clusters based on reference images, while low-quality image features gather based on distortion
051   types. Medium-quality image features lack smooth transitions and tend to attach to high/low-quality
052   clusters. This discontinuous representation is detrimental to the performance of regression tasks like
053   BIQA Zha et al. (2022); Li et al. (2024b). We believe that this phenomenon is primarily caused by
      two core problems in synthetic distortion datasets:

074     distorted datasets. This leads to the model’s tendency to overfit, hindering the formation of a
075     globally consistent quality representation.
076   • 2) Excessive redundant samples, which stem from the distorted images in synthetic distorted

080
081   To understand these issues thoroughly, we theoretically derive the impact of sample diversity and
082
      redundancy on generalization error. Based on this theoretical foundation, we design a framework
      called SynDR-IQA from a novel perspective, which reshapes the synthetic data distribution to im-
083
      prove the generalization ability of BIQA. Specifically, to address the issue of insufficient content
084
      diversity, we propose a Distribution-aware Diverse Content Upsampling (DDCUp) strategy. By
085   sampling reference images from an unlabeled candidate reference set based on the content dis-
086   tribution of existing training set to generate distorted images, we increase the diversity of visual
087   instances, helping the model learn consistent representations across different content. To label the
088   newly generated distorted images, we employ a key assumption: similar content under the same
089   distortion conditions should result in similar quality degradation. Based on this assumption, we
090   generate pseudo-labels for the newly generated images referencing given labeled data correspond-
091   ing to similar reference images in the training set, ensuring the reasonableness and consistency of
092   the generated pseudo-labels. To address the issue of excessive redundant samples, we design a
093
      Density-aware Redundant Cluster Downsampling (DRCDown) strategy. It identifies high-density
      redundant clusters in the training dataset and selectively removes samples from these clusters while
094
      retaining samples from low-density regions. This mitigates the negative impact of redundant sam-
095
      ples while alleviating data distribution imbalance, thus helping the model learn more generalizable
096   representations. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
097
098   • We observed the key phenomenon that models trained on synthetic data exhibit discrete and clus-
099     tered feature distributions, and provide an in-depth analysis of the underlying causes. Through the-
100     oretical derivation, we demonstrate the impact of sample diversity and redundancy on the model’s
101     generalization error.
102   • From a novel perspective of reshaping synthetic data distribution, we proposed the SynDR-IQA
103     framework, which includes a DDCUp strategy and a DRCDown strategy, to enhance the general-
104     ization capability of BIQA models.
105   • Extensive experiments across various cross-dataset settings, including synthetic-to-authentic,
106     synthetic-to-synthetic, and synthetic-to-algorithmic, validated the effectiveness of the SynDR-
107     IQA framework. Additionally, as a data-based approach, SynDR-IQA can be integrated with
        existing model-based methods without adding inference costs.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
111   Deep Learning-based BIQA Methods. Deep learning has revolutionized BIQA, leading to signifi-
112   cant advancements in accuracy and robustness Kang et al. (2014); Kim & Lee (2016). Recent works
113   have explored various innovative approaches to address the challenges in this field. Zhu et al. Zhu
114
      et al. (2020) proposed MetaIQA, employing meta-learning to enhance generalization across diverse
      distortion types. Su et al. Su et al. (2020) introduced a self-adaptive hyper network architecture for
115
      adaptive quality estimation in real-world scenarios. Ke et al. Ke et al. (2021) developed MUSIQ, a
116
      multi-scale image quality transformer processing native resolution images with varying sizes. Shin
117   et al. Shin et al. (2024) proposed QCN, utilizing comparison transformers and score pivots for im-
118   proving cross-dataset generalization. Xu et al. Xu et al. (2024) demonstrated the effectiveness of
119   injecting local distortion features into large pretrained vision transformers for IQA tasks. Despite
120   these advancements, the success of deep learning-based BIQA methods heavily relies on large-scale
121   annotated datasets. The high cost and time-consuming nature of acquiring subjective quality la-
122   bels for real-world images significantly limit the growth of existing datasets. This limitation has
123   prompted researchers to explore the potential of leveraging synthetic distortions to generalize to
124   real-world distortions.
125   Synthetic-to-Real Generalization in BIQA. Due to the significant domain differences between
126   synthetic distortions and real-world distortions, models trained on synthetic distortion data often
127   perform poorly when facing real-world images. To bridge this gap, several studies have explored
128   Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA) techniques. Chen et al. Chen et al. (2021b) proposed
129   a curriculum-style UDA approach for video quality assessment, adapting models from source to
130   target domains progressively. Lu et al. Lu et al. (2022) introduced StyleAM, aligning source and
131
      target domains in the feature style space, which is more closely associated with image quality. Li et
      al. Li et al. (2023) developed FreqAlign, which excavates perception-oriented transferability from
132
      a frequency perspective, selecting optimal frequency components for alignment. Most recently,
133
      Li et al. Li et al. (2024a) proposed DGQA, a distortion-guided UDA framework that leverages
134   adaptive multi-domain selection to match data distributions between source and target domains,
135   reducing negative transfer from outlier source domains. However, previous work has neglected the
136   distributional issues of synthetic distortion datasets. In this work, we introduce a novel framework
137   SynDR-IQA to enhance the syn-to-real generalization ability of BIQA by reshaping the distribution
138   of synthetic data.

      In this section, we introduce the SynDR-IQA framework, which aims to enhance the generalization
      capability of BIQA models by reshaping the synthetic data distribution. We begin with a problem
145
      formalization for BIQA, establishing the foundational context for our work. Then, we conduct a
146
      theoretical analysis exploring the impact of sample diversity and redundancy on the generalization
147   error. Building upon this theoretical foundation, we detail the two core components of SynDR-
148   IQA: Distribution-aware Diverse Content Upsampling (DDCUp), which addresses the challenge of
149   limited diversity in synthetic datasets and Density-aware Redundant Cluster Downsampling (DRC-
150   Down), which mitigates the issue of data redundancy.

191   3.2   T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS
192
193   The construction of synthetic distortion datasets follows a systematic process: applying predefined
194
      distortion types at various intensity levels to a set of reference images Lin et al. (2019). This gen-
      eration mechanism exhibits two key characteristics: First, low-intensity distortions produce images
195
      nearly identical to their references, while high-intensity distortions generate images predominantly
196
      characterized by distortion-specific patterns. Second, the dataset generation typically employs uni-
197   form sampling across reference images, distortion types, and intensity levels. These two charac-
198   teristics jointly lead to a fundamental issue: samples are drawn from different local distributions
199   rather than following identical sampling from the true distribution, resulting in a discretely clustered
200   structure in the distribution space.
201
      To understand how this clustered distribution affects model generalization, we need to extend the
202
      classical generalization error analysis to account for samples being drawn from different local dis-
203   tributions rather than the true underlying distribution. To formalize this extension, we model the
204
      synthetic dataset D̂ = {(xi , yi )}ni=1 as comprising m clusters, where each cluster consists of one
205   i.i.d. sample from the true distribution D, along with its associated ki − 1 samples drawn indepen-
206   dently from the corresponding local distribution Di ⊂ D. This formal characterization leads to the
207   following generalization bound:

      According to Theorem 1, we can observe that the upper bound of the generalization error is in-
225
      fluenced by the Rademacher complexity Radm (F), total sample size n, number of i.i.d. samples
226   m (also referred to as diverse samples), and redundancy heterogeneity η. Excluding model-related
227   factors (captured by Radm (F)), the bound reveals that the number of distinct samples m plays
228   a primary role in determining the upper bound. Enhancing the sampling of diverse samples can
229   effectively lower this upper bound. Balancing samples to reduce redundancy heterogeneity η can
230   also effectively decrease the upper bound of the generalization error. While increasing redundant
231   samples from local distributions can enlarge n and reduce the second term, it may lead to higher
232   redundancy heterogeneity η, potentially amplifying the third term degrading overall generalization
233   performance.
234   These theoretical insights motivate us to reshape the sample distribution from the perspectives of in-
235   creasing content diversity (increasing m) and balancing sample density (decreasing η) to improve
236   the generalization performance of BIQA.

526   5   C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this work, we aim to address the critical challenge of limited generalization ability in BIQA mod-
529   els trained on synthetic datasets. Our investigation reveals a key pattern: representations learned
530   from synthetic datasets often exhibit discrete and clustered distributions, with high-quality image
531
      features clustering around reference images and low-quality features clustering based on distortion
      types, which significantly hinders regression performance in BIQA tasks. Guided by this observa-
532
      tion, we conduct theoretical derivations to understand the impact of sample diversity and redundancy
533
      on generalization error. This theoretical foundation leads to the development of our novel frame-
534   work, SynDR-IQA, designed to reshape synthetic data distribution for enhancing BIQA generaliza-
535   tion. The proposed SynDR-IQA consists of two key strategies: 1) DDCUp, which enhances content
536   diversity while preserving the content distribution of the training dataset; 2) DRCDown, which
537   optimizes sample distribution by reducing the density of dense clusters. We validate the effective-
538   ness of SynDR-IQA through extensive experiments across three cross-dataset settings (synthetic-
539   to-authentic, synthetic-to-algorithmic, and synthetic-to-synthetic). The results consistently demon-
      strate improved generalization ability of BIQA models trained with our framework.
