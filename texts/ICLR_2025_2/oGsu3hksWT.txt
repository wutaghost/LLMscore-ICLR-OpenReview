010                                              A BSTRACT
011
012            Motivated by deep neural networks, the deep Gaussian process (DGP) general-
013            izes the standard GP by stacking multiple layers of GPs. Despite the enhanced
014            expressiveness, GP, as an L2 regularization prior, tends to be over-smooth and
015
               sub-optimal for inhomogeneous subjects, such as images with edges. Recently,
               Q-exponential process (Q-EP) has been proposed as an Lq relaxation to GP and
016
               demonstrated with more desirable regularization properties through a parameter
017
               q > 0 with q = 2 corresponding to GP. Sharing the similar tractability of pos-
018            terior and predictive distributions with GP, Q-EP can also be stacked to improve
019            its modeling flexibility. In this paper, we generalize Q-EP to deep Q-EP to enjoy
020            both proper regularization and improved expressiveness. The generalization is re-
021            alized by introducing shallow Q-EP as a latent variable model and then building
022            a hierarchy of the shallow Q-EP layers. Sparse approximation by inducing points
023            and scalable variational strategy are applied to facilitate the inference. We demon-
024            strate the numerical advantages of the proposed deep Q-EP model by comparing
025            with multiple state-of-the-art deep probabilistic models.
026
027   Keywords: Deep Models, Inhomogeneous Subjects, Regularization, Latent Representation, Model
028   Expressiveness
029
030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Gaussian process (GP Rasmussen & Williams, 2005; J. M. Bernardo & Smith, 1998) has gained
033   enormous successes and been widely used in statistics and machine learning community. With its
034   flexibility in learning functional relationships (Rasmussen & Williams, 2005) and latent represen-
035   tations (Titsias & Lawrence, 2010), and capability in tractable uncertainty quantification, GP has
036   become one of the most popular non-parametric modeling tools. Facilitated by the sparse approxi-
037   mation (Titsias, 2009) and scalable variational inferences (SVGP Hensman et al., 2015; Salimbeni &
038   Deisenroth, 2017), GP has been popularized for a variety of high-dimensional learning tasks. Neal
039
      (1996) in his seminal work discovered that Bayesian neural networks with infinite width converged
      to GP with certain kernel function. Inspired by the advancement of deep learning (Goodfellow
040
      et al., 2016), Damianou & Lawrence (2013) pioneered in generalizing GP with deep structures,
041
      hence named deep GP. Ever since then, there has been a large volume of follow-up works including
042   deep convolutional GP (Blomqvist et al., 2020), deep sigma point process (DSPP Jankowiak et al.,
043   2020b), deep image prior (Ulyanov et al., 2020), deep kernel process (Aitchison et al., 2021), deep
044   variational implicit process (Ortega et al., 2023), deep horseshoe GP (Castillo & Randrianarisoa,
045   2024), and various applications (Dutordoir et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Jones et al., 2023).
046
      Despite its flexibility, GP, as an L2 regularization method, tends to produce random candidate func-
047
      tions that are over-smooth and thus sub-optimal for modeling inhomogeneous objects with abrupt
048   changes or sharp contrast. To address this issue, an Lq based stochastic process, Q-exponential
049   process (Q-EP Li et al., 2023), has recently been proposed to impose flexible regularization through
050   a parameter q > 0, which includes GP as a special case when q = 2. Similarly as Lasso induc-
051   ing sparsity for regression, q = 1 is often adopted for Q-EP to impose stronger regularization than
052   GP to properly capture dramatic changes in certain portions of inhomogeneous data, e.g., edges in
053   an image. Different from other L1 based priors such as Laplace random field (Podgórski & We-
      gener, 2011; Kozubowski et al., 2013) and Besov process (Lassas et al., 2009; Dashti et al., 2012),

054
      Q-EP shares with GP the unique tractability of posterior and predictive distributions (Theorem 3.5
055   of Li et al., 2023), which essentially permits a deep generalization by stacking multiple stochastic
056   mappings (Damianou & Lawrence, 2013).
057
058
      Motivated by the improved expressiveness of deep GP and the flexible regularization of Q-EP, in
      this work we generalize Q-EP to deep Q-EP to enhance the capability of Q-EP in modeling inho-
059
      mogeneous data. On one hand, by stacking multiple layers of Q-EP mappings, deep Q-EP becomes
060
      more capable of characterizing complex latent representations than the standard Q-EP. On the other
061   hand, inherited from Q-EP, deep Q-EP maintains the control of regularization through the parameter
062   q > 0, whose smaller values impose stronger regularization, more amenable than (deep) GP to pre-
063   serve inhomogenous traits such as edges in an image. First, we introduce the building block, shallow
064   Q-EP model, which can be regarded as a kernelized latent variable model (LVM) (Lawrence, 2003;
065   Titsias & Lawrence, 2010). Such shallow model is also viewed as a stochastic mapping F from in-
066   put (or latent) variables X to output variables Y defined by a kernel. Then as in Lawrence & Moore
067   (2007); Damianou & Lawrence (2013), we extend such mapping by stacking multiple shallow Q-
068   EP layers to form a hierarchy for the deep Q-EP. Sparse approximation by inducing points (Titsias,
069
      2009) is adopted for the variational inference of deep Q-EP. A theoretic barricade for developing
      the evidence lower bound (ELBO) in the setting of Q-EP is that the power in the exponent of its
070
      density makes many involved expectations intractable. We solve this challenge by taking advantage
071
      of Jensen’s inequality. The inference procedure, as in deep GP, can be efficiently implemented in
072   GPyTorch (Gardner et al., 2018).
073
074   Connection to existing works Our proposed deep Q-EP is closely related to deep GP (Damianou
075   & Lawrence, 2013) and two other works, deep kernel learning (DKL-GP Wilson et al., 2016) and
076   DSPP (Jankowiak et al., 2020b). Deep Q-EP generalizes deep GP with a parameter q > 0 to control
077   the regularization (See Figure 1 for its effect on learning representations) and includes deep GP as
078   a special case for q = 2. DKL-GP combines the deep learning architectures (neural networks) with
079   the non-parametric flexibility of kernel methods (GP). The GP part can also be replaced by Q-EP
080   to generate new methods like DKL-QEP (See Section 5.4.) DSPP is motivated by parametric GP
081
      models (PPGPR Jankowiak et al., 2020a) and applies sigma point approximation or quadrature-like
      integration to the predictive distribution. The majority of popular deep probabilistic models rely on
082
      GP. This is one of the few developed out of a non-Gaussian stochastic process. Our proposed work
083
      on deep Q-EP has multi-fold contributions to deep probabilistic models:
084
085         1. We propose a novel deep probabilistic model based on Q-EP that generalizes deep GP with
086            flexibility of regularization for handling data inhomogeneity.
087         2. We develop the variational inference for deep Q-EP and efficiently implement it.
088         3. We demonstrate numerical advantages of deep Q-EP in modeling inhomogeneous data by
089            comparing with state-of-the-art deep probabilistic models.
090
091   The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces the background of Q-EP. We then
092
      develop shallow Q-EP in Section 3 as the building block for deep Q-EP in Section 4. In these two
      sections, we highlight the importance of posterior tractability in the development and some obstacles
093
      in deriving the variational lower bounds. In Section 5 we demonstrate the numerical advantages by
094
      comparing with multiple deep probabilistic models in various learning tasks. Finally, we conclude
095   with some discussion on the limitation and potential improvement in Section 6.

148

149
      than GP. In practice, q = 1 is often adopted for faster posterior convergence (Agapiou et al., 2021;
150   Lan et al., 2023) and the capability of preserving inhomogeneous features (rough functional data,

154   for the special Gaussian case (q = 2). For multiple Q-EPs, (u1 (x), · · · , uD (x)), we usually do not

156

524   6     C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this paper, we generalize Q-EP to deep Q-EP, which includes deep GP as a special case. Moreover,
527   deep Q-EP inherits the flexible regularization controlled a parameter q > 0, which is advantageous
528   in learning latent representations and modeling data inhomogeneity. We first generalize Bayesian
529   GP-LVM to Bayesian QEP-LVM (as shallow Q-EP layer) and develop the variational inference for
530   it. Then we stack multiple shallow Q-EP layer to build the deep Q-EP model. The novel deep model
531   demonstrates numerical benefits in various learning tasks and can be combined with neural network
532
      for better characterizing complex latent representations in different data applications.
533   As common in GP and NN models, we do observe multi-modality of the posterior distributions,
534   especially in the hyper-parameter spaces. Sub-optimal solutions can appear in the stochastic training
535   process. These issues can be alleviated by dispersed or diversified initialization, or with adaptive
536   training schedulers. One potential application of deep Q-EP is the inverse learning, similarly as
537   done by deep GP (Jin et al., 2017; Abraham & Deo, 2023). Theory of the contraction properties
538
      (Finocchio & Schmidt-Hieber, 2023) is also an interesting research direction.
