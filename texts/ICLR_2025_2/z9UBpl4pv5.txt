009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The application of Vision Transformers (ViTs) to new domains where an inductive
012            bias is known but only small datasets are available to train upon is a growing
013            area of interest. However, training ViT networks on small-scale datasets poses a
014            significant challenge. In contrast, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have an
015            architectural inductive bias enabling them to perform well on such problems. In this
016
               paper, we propose that the architectural bias inherent to CNNs can be reinterpreted
               as an initialization bias within ViT. Specifically, based on our theoretical findings
017
               that the convolutional structures of CNNs allow random impulse filters to achieve
018
               performance comparable to their learned counterparts, we design a “structured ini-
019            tialization” for ViT with optimization. Unlike conventional initialization methods
020            for ViTs, which typically (1) rely on empirical results such as attention weights in
021            pretrained models, (2) focus on the distribution of the attention weights, resulting
022            in unstructured attention maps, our approach is grounded in a solid theoretical
023            analysis, and builds structured attention maps. This key difference in the attention
024            map empowers ViTs to perform equally well on small-scale problems while pre-
025            serving their structural flexibility for large-scale applications. We show that our
026            method achieves significant performance improvements over conventional ViT ini-
027
               tialization methods across numerous small-scale benchmarks including CIFAR-10,
               CIFAR-100, and SVHN, while maintaining on-par if not better performance on
028
               large-scale datasets such as ImageNet-1K.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Vision Transformers (ViTs) have shown remarkable performance with large-scale training data.
034   However, their performance significantly drops when applied to small-scale datasets. To close this
035   performance gap, various methods have been proposed, including self-supervised pre-training in
036   large-scale datasets (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Touvron et al., 2021), advanced data augmentation
037   techniques (Yun et al., 2019; Cubuk et al., 2020), and incorporation of convolutional layers (Wu et al.,
038   2021; Liu et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2021). Recently, Zhang et al.
039
      (2022) explored leveraging pretrained weights to initialize ViTs. Building on this pioneering idea,
      researchers found that a simple network weight initialization can improve ViT training on small-scale
040
      datasets without altering ViT architectures. In particular, Trockman & Kolter (2023) developed
041
      a “mimetic” method that mimics the distribution of pretrained ViT weights during initialization.
042   Nevertheless, this distribution relies on the observations from pretrained models with the same
043   architectures. Similarly, Xu et al. (2024) proposed to directly sample weights from pretrained large-
044   scale models, while it is not practical to pretrain a large model before deploying a small-scale one. In
045   conclusion, these initialization strategies (1) focus more on replicating the distribution of attention
046   weights rather than the structure of attention maps, and (2) rely on pretraining with large-scale
047   datasets, which are often not readily available for many domain-specific applications.
048   In this work, we introduce structured initialization for ViTs, which focuses on structuring attention
049   maps and does not rely on any form of pretraining with large-scale models. This initialization strategy
050   is grounded in our theoretical findings that the structural bias within randomly initialized depth-wise
051   (spatial mixing) convolutions is the key factor enabling comparable performance (Cazenavette et al.,
052   2023) to their learned counterparts in ConvMixer (Trockman & Kolter, 2022) and ResNet (He et al.,
053   2016) frameworks. We further propose to use random impulse convolution kernels as the initialization
      structure for attention maps, as shown in Fig. 1. In detail, we use a simple yet efficient optimization

098   Introducing inductive bias of CNN to ViT through architecture. Many efforts have aimed to
099
      incorporate convolutional inductive bias into ViTs through architectural modifications. Dai et al.
      (2021) proposed to combine convolution and self-attention by mixing the convolutional self-attention
100
      layers. Pan et al. (2021) and Li et al. (2023) introduced hybrid models wherein the output of each
101
      layer is a summation of convolution and self-attention. Wu et al. (2021) explored using convolution
102   for token projections within self-attention, while Yuan et al. (2021) demonstrated promising results by
103   inserting a depthwise convolution before the self-attention map as an alternate strategy for injecting
104   inductive bias. d’Ascoli et al. (2021) introduced gated positional self-attention (GPSA) to imply a
105   soft convolution inductive bias. All these efforts have been proven to be effective. However, these
106   techniques have a fundamental limitation—they aim to introduce the inductive bias of convolution
107   through architectural choices. Our approach, on the other hand, stands out by not requiring any
      modifications of architecture. Such an approach offers several advantages as it: (i) exhibits data

      efficiency on small-scale datasets, (ii) retains the architectural freedom to be seamlessly applied to
109   larger-scale datasets, and (iii) gives an alternate theoretical perspective on how the inductive bias of
110   convolution can be applied within transformers.

      Initializating ViT from pretrained weights. To date, the exploration of applying inductive bias
      through initialization within a transformer is limited. Zhang et al. (2022) posited that the benefit of
113
      pretrained models in ViTs can be interpreted as a more effective strategy for initialization. Trockman
114
      & Kolter (2023); Trockman et al. (2022) recently investigated the empirical distributions of self-
115   attention weights, learned from large-scale datasets, and proposed a mimetic initialization strategy.
116   While this approach lies between structured and generative initialization, it relies on the pretraining
117   results of large models. Xu et al. (2024) directly sampled weights from pretrained large-scale models
118   as initialization for smaller models. This may be astonishing at first glance, but the sampled weights
119   must follow the distribution of these pretrained weights, which means this method is a special case of
120   mimetic initialization. A key difference in our approach is that our method does not require offline
121   knowledge of pretrained networks (mimetic or empirical). Instead, our initialized structure is derived
122   from theoretical analysis of convolution layers.
123   Convolution as attention. Since their introduction (Vaswani et al., 2017; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021),
124   the relationship between transformers and CNNs has been a topic of immense interest to the vision
125   community. Andreoli (2019) studied the structural similarities between attention and convolution,
126   bridging them into a unified framework. Building on this, Cordonnier et al. (2020) demonstrated that
127   self-attention layers can express any convolutional layers through a careful theoretical construction.
128   While these studies highlighted the functional equivalence between self-attention in ViTs and con-
129
      volutional spatial mixing in CNNs, they did not delve into how the inductive bias of ViTs could be
      adapted through this theoretical connection. In contrast, our work offers a simple insight: random
130
      convolutional impulse filter can be effectively approximated by softmax self-attention.

231   Why using impulse filters? Usually, random convolution filters contain both positive and negative
232
      values, while the output of the softmax function is always positive. One straightforward option is
      to use random positive convolution filters with a normalized sum of one, following the property of
233
      softmax. However, this approach often proves inefficient as the patterns may be too complicated for a
234
      softmax function to handle. Tarzanagh et al. (2023) found that the softmax attention map functions as
235   a feature selection mechanism, and typically tends to select a single related feature. In convolution
236   filters, this softmax attention map can be viewed as an impulse filter. According to Proposition 1,
237   random impulse filters are also f 2 − k spanned. In conclusion, when initializing a softmax attention
238   map, the most straightforward and suitable choice is random impulse convolution filters.

      Pseudo input. The advantage of self-attention is that its spatial mixing map is learned from data.
240
      The real input to an attention layer is P + X for the first layer and X (the intermidiate output from
241   previous layer) for the following layers. However, during initialization, there is no prior information
242   about the input. To address this problem, we simply treat all the layers identically and use absolute
243   sinusoidal positional encoding P (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) as pseudo input, replacing the actual
244   input data P + X or intermidiate outputs. Remember that this only happens when we solve the
245   initialization to avoid data-dependent initialization, while in the training stage the input is not changed.
246   The ablation study of different pseudo inputs is presented in Appendix C.1.

253
254

      certain distribution. Singular value decomposition (SVD) is utilized to solve Qinit and Kinit . While a
255
      similar SVD-based approach could be employed in our scenario—despite we intend to initialize the
256

257   inverse of P and low-rank approximation. Consequently, we opt not to pursue an analytical solution
258   but rather employ a simple optimization to obtain Qinit and Kinit . This approach also addresses
259   concerns regarding scale and layer normalization in the attention mechanism.

351
      Although our method demonstrates impressive performance when training ViT-T on small-scale
352
      datasets, the model ViT-T only has 3 heads, which falls short of the requirements defined in Proposi-
353
      tion 1. To better showcase the advantage of our method, we increased the number of heads to 8 in
354   ViT-T, denoted as ViT-T/h8. In addition to the experiments with ViT-T, we also tested our method on
355   the small ViT model (ViT-S). The configuration of ViT-S includes an embedding dimension of 384, a
356   depth of 12, and 6 heads, denoted as ViT-S/h6. Furthermore, we increasde the number of heads to 16
357   and denoted this model as ViT-S/h16. The results on CIFAR-100 are shown in Tab. 3.
358
      As the model size increases, particularly with a higher number of heads, our initialization method
359
      demonstrates improved and more stable performance, bringing a larger gap between other initial-
360   ization methods. This performance increase proves our theory (see Proposition 1) regarding the
361   expressibility of spatial mixing matrix: more heads provide more linearly independent filters. For
362   instance, when the number of heads is 3, as in ViT-T/h3, each layer contains only 3 unique “filters”
363   with each filter having 192 / 3 = 64 copies. While the number of copies is sufficient, having only 3
364   unique “filters” is inadequate for forming the filter basis, even for a 3 × 3 random impulse filter.
365
      As we increase the number of heads, we observe an adequate improvement in the performance of
366   our method. However, maintaining a constant embedding dimension while increasing the number of
367   heads leads to fewer copies per head. While this may not present a significant issue in ConvMxier as
368   long as the number of copies exceeds the rank of the inputs, a notable challenge arises with multi-head
369   attention: the dimensionality of Q and K will decrease to D / h as the number of heads h increases.
370   Consequently, the rank of Q and K diminishes considerably, making it more challenging for the
371   low-rank approximation QKT to learn an effective attention map.
372   This phenomenon may explain why the Kaiming Uniform and Trunc Normal methods occasionally
373   exhibit inferior performance as the number of heads increases. For the mimetic initialization, the
374   situation is potentially more problematic, as it utilizes SVD to solve for a low-rank Q and K. As
375   the number of heads increases, resulting in a lower rank, the approximation error grows, further
376   deviating the actual QKT from the anticipated value. In contrast, our initialization strategy employs
377   an iterative optimization method, which helps mitigate errors arising from low-rank approximations.
      Consequently, our method benefits more when applied with a larger number of heads.

451
      initialization methods only initialize the spatial convolution filters without training. Specifically, the
452
      box filters use all ones, serving as an average pooling function.
453
454   In general, random and impulse initialization achieve comparable accuracy compared to the end-
455   to-end trained model, while box initialization exhibits inferior performance. This discrepancy can
456
      be attributed to the deficient rank of box filters, as they lack f 2 linearly independent filters, unlike
      random and impulse initialization, which can form the basis of the filter space.
457
458   ViT and ConvMixer of same embedding dimension and depth. For ConvMixers with the same
459   embedding dimension, the performance gap between trained filters and random or impulse widens as
460   the kernel size increases. As we discussed in Sec. 5.3, as the kernel size increases, more unique filters
461   are needed to form the basis of filter space. Consequently, each head (unique filter) has fewer copies,
462
      making it difficult to match the rank of inputs, thus failing to meet the condition in Proposition 1.
      When the embedding dimension doubles, the performance gap between trained filters and random or
463
      impulse filters diminishes with the same kernel size. However, models with larger kernel sizes still
464
      tend to have a bigger gap due to an insufficient number of copies for each unique filter.
465
466   Our method is motivated by the similarity between ViT and ConvMixer. To show this connection, we
467   train ViTs with similar configurations to ConvMixer as described in Sec. 5.5. Specifically, we train
468
      ViTs of a depth of 8 with embedding dimensions of 256 and 512. The number of heads is from 4 to
      32. Results of different initialization methods are shown in Tab. 5. We also provide additional results
469
      with embedding dimensions of 64 and 512 in Appendix C.2
470
471   Our impulse initialization methods demonstrate superior performance across nearly all configurations.
472   Especially, our method achieves a top accuracy of 90.67% and 91.87% with an embedding dimension
473   of 256 and 512, respectively, significantly outperforming other initialization methods. Moreover, our
474
      method achieves results on par with end-to-end trained ConvMixers (91.76% and 92.82%) of equal
      depth and embedding dimensions. In addition, these results with different numbers of heads validate
475
      our discussions in Sec. 5.3 regarding the impact of the number of heads on the performance.

478   6      C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this paper, we propose a structured initialization method with convolutional impulse filters for
481   attention maps in ViTs. Our method preserves both the advantage of locality within CNNs and
482
      the capacity to learn long-range dependencies inherited from ViTs. We also provide a thorough
      theoretical explanation of the spatial and channel mixing in ConvMixer and ViT, building connections
483
      between the structural bias in CNNs and the initialization of ViTs. Our results on small-scale datasets
484
      validate the effectiveness of the convolutional structural bias, while on-par performance on large-scale
485   datasets indicates the preservation of architectural flexibility.
