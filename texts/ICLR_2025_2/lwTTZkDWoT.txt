011            We introduce a low-resource safety enhancement method for aligning large lan-
012            guage models (LLMs) without the need for supervised fine-tuning (SFT) or re-
013            inforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF). Our main idea is to exploit
014            knowledge distillation to extract the alignment information from existing well-
015            aligned LLMs and integrate it into unaligned LLMs in a plug-and-play fashion.
016            Methodology, we employ delta debugging to identify the critical components of
017            knowledge necessary for effective distillation. On the harmful question dataset, our
018
               method significantly enhances the average defense success rate by approximately
               14.41%, reaching as high as 51.39%, in 17 unaligned pre-trained LLMs, without
019
               compromising performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
023   We introduce Decoupled Alignment for Robust Plug-and-Play Adaptation (termed DAPA), a low-
024   resource safety enhancement method for aligning large language models (LLMs). DAPA aligns
025   unaligned Large language models (LLMs) with ethical guidelines even without supervised fine-tuning
026   (SFT) or reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF).
027   This innovation is practically urgent and important. LLMs have been widely adopted in various
028   applications recently, demonstrating their ability to generate high-quality human-like texts (Team
029   et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023; Ivison et al., 2023). However, the security of these models has
030   become a significant concern due to the potential risks of generating harmful content (Wu et al.,
031   2024a; Anonymous, 2024; Yu et al., 2023a; Chao et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2023). To align the
032   LLMs with ethical guidelines, researchers have developed various methods to enhance their safety.
033
      For example, the Llama-2-Chat (Touvron et al., 2023) and Gemma-it (Team et al., 2024) models
      have been extensively fine-tuned to improve their alignment performance. However, these methods
034
      often require extensive computational resources or manual red-teaming, which can be costly and
035
      time-consuming (Team et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2024; Bai et al., 2022; Ganguli et al., 2022). Thus, most
036   of the LLMs finetuned from the pre-trained models by third-party developers do not undergo the
037   alignment process (Xu et al., 2024a; Chiang et al., 2023; Ivison et al., 2023), leaving them vulnerable
038   to generating harmful content by users with malicious intent. To combat these issues, we seek
039   motivations from knowledge distillation technologies (Xu et al., 2024b; Hahn & Choi, 2019), where
040   a teacher model’s knowledge is transferred to a student model.
041
      Specifically, through numerical experiments Figure 3 and Figure 4, we make two key detections:
042
043   • MLP Alignment. The alignment knowledge is stored in the Feed-Forward Network (FFN) layers,
044
        also referred to as MLP layers,
045   • Gate Alignment. Further, the gate layer within MLP layers is the key component that determines
046     whether the model’s output is aligned with ethical guidelines.
047   Focusing on these two key components, we propose to use memory editing technology (Meng et al.,
048   2022a;b) to transfer alignment knowledge from one aligned LLM to another unaligned LLM within
049   the same family. Surprisingly, by incorporating the aligned gate layers into unaligned LLMs, we
050   dramatically improve their alignment performance. This technique differs from other alignment
051   strategies in two aspects: computational and manual resources for model alignment through fine-
052
      tuning or reinforcement learning (Dai et al., 2024). This work provides a timely, robust and economic
      solution to LLM safety enhancement, aiming for more efficient and low-cost alignment.
053
      Contribution. We present a low-resource safety enhancement method for aligning LLMs.

      R ELATED W ORKS
089   Model Alignment. With the developments of the LLMs (Team et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023;
090   Bai et al., 2023a), concerns about their security have become significant (Weng & Wu, 2024; Yu
091   et al., 2023b). Among these concerns, the potential risks of generating harmful content have attracted
092   the most attention. To counteract the potential risks of harmful outputs, developers often engage
093
      in fine-tuning during safety training to decrease the likelihood of such outputs (Wu et al., 2024a;
      Touvron et al., 2023; Ganguli et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022). Current methods for safeguarding LLMs
094
      against jailbreak attacks include Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), Direct
095
      Preference Optimization (DPO), and Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) (Rafailov et al., 2023; Peng et al.,
096   2023; Ouyang et al., 2022). However, these fine-tuning methods are both slow and costly. Many
097   practitioners are exploring ways to lower the expenses associated with alignment fine-tuning (Wang
098   et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2023b), yet costs remain substantial.
099
      Memory Editing. Knowledge editing focuses on altering specific behaviors of LLMs (Huang et al.,
100
      2023; Meng et al., 2022a;b), and can be divided into three primary paradigms (Yao et al., 2023a).
101
      The first paradigm edits the memory during the inference stage (Wei et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023;
102   Mitchell et al., 2022), employing memory retrieval or in-context learning for modifications. The
103   second paradigm adjusts model parameters and structures during the training stage (Meng et al.,
104   2022a;b). The third paradigm utilizes associative memory models such as the Modern Hopfield
105   Network (Hu et al., 2024a;b;c; Wu et al., 2024b;c; Hu et al., 2023; Ramsauer et al., 2020) to edit
106   model memory effectively. These networks feature fast convergence and significant memory capacity,
107   facilitating plug-and-play methods in model editing. Subsequent efforts utilize knowledge editing
      to detoxify LLMs. Wang et al. (2024) explore the use of contextual semantics to allocate memory

108
      space, employing memory editing techniques to adjust the relevant memory areas. They achieve this
109   by training new parameters specifically within the attention and MLP layers of relevant LLM layers.
110   However, these knowledge editing methods either need to modify the hidden representation each
111   time when generating the outputs or require fine-tuning the model to edit the knowledge stored in
112   the attention and MLP layers. Our method does not require fine-tuning the model nor modifying the
113   hidden representation each time during inference, which is more efficient and cost-effective.

      3   D ELTA D EBUGGING
209
210   Although the gate layer within MLP layers is crucial for ensuring model responses adhere to ethical
211   guidelines from §2, modifying all gate layers could degrade the original performance due to a large
212
      number of parameter changes. We propose a strategy to efficiently identify the optimal memory space
      for targeted modifications, enhancing alignment while preserving performance.
213
214   We incorporate delta debugging (Zeller & Hildebrandt, 2002) in our strategy. Delta debugging is a
215   systematic approach that automates the debugging process by identifying the smallest set of changes
      responsible for a program’s failure. It methodically reduces the set of changes, testing progressively


                                                                       4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      smaller subsets until pinpointing the precise cause of the failure. In DAPA, we consider it a program
217   failure when LLMs provide an unethical response to an unethical question. To demonstrate how delta
218   debugging works in DAPA, let S ∈ S be a memory space where S is the universe memory of all MLP
219   modules. A policy is defined by the function π : S → {0, 1}, where if π(S) = 1, it indicates that the
220   memory space S is beneficial for enhancing alignment, and if π(S) = 0, it indicates that the memory
221   space S does not contribute to improving alignment. Given an aligned model memory space S and
222   policy π, we aim to find the smallest memory space S∗ ∈ S in the aligned model which can most
223   efficiently improve the unaligned ability to defend the jailbreak. In our case, we define π(S) as the
224   evaluation on a small set of additional unethical questions (e.g., 5% of preserved data). If the model
225
      provides ethical responses to all these questions, we set π(S) = 1; otherwise, π(S) = 0.

322   4.1 A LIGNMENT P ERFORMANCE
323   To evaluate DAPA’s effectiveness in aligning unaligned models, we substitute the relative memory in
      17 models with DAPA’s configuration and assess their performance in defending against jailbreak


                                                                      6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      attacks. Because we set the response generation with deterministic, the variance of the evaluation is
325   0. As a result, we only need to run one evaluation for each model.
326
327   Dataset. In our experiment, we use the AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023b) to validate the performance
328
      of DAPA. It is a benchmark dataset that contains various unethical prompts to evaluate the align-
      ment of language models, encompassing different categories such as violence, hate speech, and
329
      misinformation. We sample 128 prompts from the AdvBench dataset to form our evaluation dataset.
330
331   Metrics. We employ the DSR as the primary metric to evaluate the alignment performance. For each
332   unethical prompt, if the model provides a refusal or an ethical response, it is considered aligned. To
333   automatically evaluate the model’s responses, we combine LLM judgments with gpt-3.5 Turbo and
334

      5     D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
522   We introduce the Decoupled Alignment for Robust Plug-and-Play Adaptation, DAPA, which edits the
523   unaligned model memory to enhance the model’s defenses against jailbreak attacks. This method
524
      improves model alignment without the substantial computational expense typically associated with
      fine-tuning. It also efficiently identifies the optimal memory space for alignment. Visualizations
525
      confirm that the ethical boundary of model alignment is predominantly situated within the middle
526
      MLP’s gate layers. Empirically, DAPA achieves a 14.41% improvement in model alignment, reaching
527   up to 51.39% in one of the Gemma family models, with an average parameter change of only 6.26%.
528   Moreover, DAPA minimally impacts the model’s performance in generation and reasoning tasks.
529
530
      Limitation and Future Work. One limitation of our approach is the extent of memory space editing
      required. Although the average memory modification across three family models is 6.26%, popular
531
      model adapters like Lora (Hu et al., 2021) and QLora (Dettmers et al., 2023) typically require only
532
      about 1% of parameter changes. In future work, we aim to explore strategies to reduce the percentage
533   of memory space editing necessary for effective model alignment. Another limitation of DAPA is that
534   it cannot overcome superficial alignment issues (Zhou et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2024) caused by most
535   alignment methods. Because DAPA is a memory editing technique derived from current powerful
536   alignment methods. In future work, we aim to explore alternative alignment methods that do not
537   require training, such as model unlearning (Zhang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024d), for model alignment.
538   Additionally, DAPA relies on the existence of a pre-aligned teacher model to transfer alignment
539   knowledge, as DAPA cannot independently achieve alignment without this prerequisite.

812   Our proposal improves LLMs’ defenses against jailbreak attacks. It enables third-party supervised
813   fine-tuning of LLMs to acquire alignment capabilities. However, there is a risk that malicious actors
814   could use this research to strengthen their attacks on LLMs. Nonetheless, we consider it crucial to
815   expose this vulnerability to the public, despite the potential dangers.

991    F     DAPA I NTERPRETABILITY A NALYSIS WITH ROME
992
993    To analyze the interpretability of DAPA, we employ ROME (Rank-One Model Editing) (Meng et al.,
994    2022a), a tool designed to identify and edit specific behaviors in language models. Using ROME,
995    we investigate how DAPA handles ethically aligned prompts versus misaligned ones by probing the
996    internal representations and decision-making pathways. This analysis helps us uncover the latent
997    mechanisms by which DAPA classifies prompts and generates responses, offering deeper insights into
998    its robustness and alignment performance. In Figure 13, we present the visualization results obtained
999    through ROME analysis. We could observe that the hidden states in the begin and middle layers of
1000
       the model have the most significant impact on the model’s output, and the MLP layers have a higher
       indirect effect than the attention layers. This aligns with the findings in Section 2.
1001
1002   Our findings on the role of MLP layers in storing alignment-related knowledge are closely aligned
1003   with insights from prior work (Geva et al., 2020; Dai et al., 2021) on the interpretability of transformer
1004   models. Specifically, Geva et al. (2020) demonstrates that feed-forward layers in transformers function
1005   as key-value memory systems, with input tokens serving as keys and output activations acting as
1006
       values. This supports our observation that alignment knowledge is primarily stored in the MLP
       layers. Similarly, Dai et al. (2021) identifies specific neurons in MLP layers responsible for encoding
1007
       factual or domain-specific knowledge. This concept resonates with our methodology of isolating
1008
       alignment-critical components using delta debugging and transferring them through knowledge
1009   distillation. Additionally, our interpretability analysis using ROME aligns with the methodologies
1010   employed to identify and modify knowledge neurons. Together, these works reinforce the theoretical
1011   foundation of our study and highlight the broader significance of understanding and leveraging the
1012   role of MLP layers in transformers for tasks such as alignment and safety enhancement.
1013
       Additionally, numerous related works (Geva et al., 2020; Dai et al., 2021) have discussed the role of
1014
       MLP layers in storing knowledge within LLMs. Both papers strongly support the underlying premise
1015   of our work that MLP layers store specific and critical information in transformers.
