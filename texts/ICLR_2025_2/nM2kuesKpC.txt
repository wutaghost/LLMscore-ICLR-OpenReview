009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Stochastic optimization is a key enabler in modern machine learning, producing
012            effective models for various tasks. However, several researchers have shown that
013            model parameters and gradient information are susceptible to privacy leakage.
014            Although, Differentially Private SGD (DPSGD) addresses privacy concerns, its
015            static noise mechanism impacts the error bounds for model performance. Addition-
016            ally, with the exponential increase in model parameters, efficient learning of these
017            models using stochastic optimizers has become more challenging. To address these
018
               concerns, we introduce the Dynamically Differentially Private Projected Stochas-
               tic Gradient Descent (D2P2-SGD) optimizer. In D2P2-SGD, we combine two
019
               important ideas: (i) dynamic differential privacy (DDP) with automatic gradient
020
               clipping and (ii) random projection with SGD, allowing dynamic adjustment of
021            the tradeoff between utility and privacy of the model. It demonstrates provably
022            tighter error bounds compared to DPSGD across different behavior (i.e. convex and
023            non-convex) of the objective function. The theoretical analysis further suggests that
024            DDP leads to better utility at the cost of privacy, while random projection enables
025            more efficient model learning. Extensive experiments across diverse datasets show
026            that D2P2-SGD significantly enhances accuracy while maintaining privacy. Our
027            code is available here.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Deep learning models Kasneci et al. (2023); Chang et al. (2023); Thirunavukarasu et al. (2023);
033
      Zhao et al. (2023); Menghani (2023), enabled by stochastic optimization techniques have achieved
      remarkable success in many fundamental machine learning tasks. Though these models empirically
034
      show incredibly appealing capabilities, there are critical concerns regarding the privacy of these
035
      models. In numerous applications, such as healthcare Chen et al. (2021) and finance Goodell et al.
036   (2021), training datasets often contain highly sensitive information that must remain confidential.
037   However, due to the wide use of deep learning models, their rich representations can possibly disclose
038   private information under privacy attacks, as demonstrated in the prior works Zhu et al. (2019); Zhao
039   et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2022).
040
      To mitigate these privacy concerns, differential privacy (DP) Dwork (2006; 2008); Li et al. (2011)
041
      was introduced, and it has gained considerable attention Ji et al. (2014); Blanco-Justicia et al. (2022)
042   to provide principled and rigorous privacy guarantees. Intuitively speaking, DP is a mechanism to
043   ensure that all data samples have no significant impact on the ultimate trained model. Differentially
044   private SGD (DPSGD) Song et al. (2013); Jagielski et al. (2020); Abadi et al. (2016); Bassily et al.
045   (2014) is one of the most acknowledged methods to solve the private empirical risk minimization
046   (ERM) problems. Specifically, it perturbs each gradient update with a static random noise vector
047   (with the same dimension as that of the gradient) sampled from a distribution. Using the perturbed
048   gradient updates, we can compute the tradeoff between the utility and privacy of the model. This
049   tradeoff can be adjusted via a noise mechanism, typically sampled from a static distribution with
050
      properly chosen yet fixed variance. While this approach is technically simple and provably effective,
      the noise variance significantly impacts the ultimate error bound.
051
052   A challenging issue that remains in various private machine learning tasks is how to achieve a
053   desirable tradeoff between privacy and utility, which becomes particularly important in large-scale
      deep learning models Li et al. (2021a); Flemings et al. (2024); Mattern et al. (2022).

054
      Recent work Du et al. (2021) proposed a dynamic DP mechanism to adjust the tradeoff on the fly,
055   reducing the model performance loss gap but at the cost of increased privacy loss. Further, the
056   dimension of the noise vector is typically the same as that of the gradient, which could be extremely
057   large to cause intensive computational complexity. This issue motivates us to seek out an approach
058   that assists in reducing the complexity while maintaining privacy. Inspired by the work Blocki et al.
059   (2012), we pay attention to model compression techniques.
060
      Model compression Buciluǎ et al. (2006); Choudhary et al. (2020) has been utilized to reduce
061   the computational complexity, including quantization Zhou et al. (2018); Chmiel et al. (2020),
062   regularization Moradi et al. (2020); Orvieto et al. (2023); Tao et al. (2015), and projection Gu
063   et al. (2023); Tsfadia (2024). Though these methods aim to reduce computational complexity, their
064   technical details can differ significantly depending on the specific focus. For example, in Gu et al.
065   (2023), the authors used projection to identify the dominating gradient subspace, which assisted in
066   improving model accuracy, without changing the dimension of model parameters. This is analogous
067   to L1-norm regularization Xu et al. (2008), which enforces some model parameters to be exactly 0.
068   Albeit model compression enables enticing performance, it comes at the expense of possible accuracy
069
      reduction and sophisticated compression techniques, necessitating an effective optimizer that can
      balance the dynamic trade-off between privacy and utility. Thus, this naturally leads to a synthesis
070
      among complexity, privacy and utility and the following question:
071
072       Can we design an efficient differentially private optimizer to allow small model performance
073                                 reduction gap and maintaining privacy?
074
075   Contributions. In this work, we answer this question affirmatively. Specifically, we propose a novel
076   stochastic optimizer termed Dynamically Differentially Private Projected Stochastic Gradient Descent
077
      (D2P2-SGD), which, for the first time, integrates the dynamic DP mechanism with automatic gradient
      clipping and random projection for optimization. The dynamic DP mechanism involves an isotropic
078
      Gaussian distribution with a properly chosen time-varying variance that decreases along with the
079
      iterations, reducing noise effects as privacy loss increases. To further warrant differential privacy and
080   bound the influence of each individual example on the stochastic gradient, we resort to a recently
081   developed automatic gradient clipping mechanism Bu et al. (2024). This is different from traditional
082   clipping method Abadi et al. (2016) that there is an upper bound imposed for gradients. Additionally,
083   the stochastic gradient is projected into a lower-dimensional space, reducing the dimension of additive
084   noise and mitigating the increase in privacy loss. Concretely, the main contributions are as follows:
085
086   1. We propose a novel DP optimizer, D2P2-SGD, by defining a dynamic DP mechanism with
087
         time-varying noise variance and using random projection to reduce the model performance loss
         gap and the dimension of noise vectors added to stochastic gradients. The per-sample gradient
088
         normalization is utilized as the automatic gradient clipping mechanism to control the influence of
089
         each individual example on the stochastic gradient.
090
      2. Theoretically, we show that with D2P2-SGD, the error rates for generally convex and non-convex
091
         functions are tighter than those for regular DPSGD. The results for the dynamic DP mechanism
092      can immediately degenerate to those for the static scenario, revealing the consolidation among
093      complexity, utility, and privacy.
094   3. We validate the proposed D2P2-SGD on a wide spectrum of datasets, demonstrating that the
095      model accuracy can substantially improve compared to the state-of-the-art.
096
097   It is noteworthy that our approach in this paper prioritizes theoretical exploration over scalability to
098   larger datasets and models, such as transformers and large language models Kasneci et al. (2023);
099   Chang et al. (2023). While some work has been done in this area Anil et al. (2021); Yu et al. (2021a),
100   developing a differentially private optimizer for these models remains a significant challenge and is
101
      left for future work.

134   is mathematically simple, calculating eigenvectors from structure tensors could become problematic

144

145   utility analysis only applies to convex case. A more recent work Feng & Venkitasubramaniam (2024)
146   employed randomized gradient quantization to compress the dimension of the gradient vector for
147   reducing the computational complexity, facing the same issues including large dimension and static
148   noise power. Different from these existing works, our work aims to reduce the performance loss gap
149   by attenuating the negative noise impact without significantly sacrificing the privacy. Please refer to

176
      standard definition of differential privacy.
177   Definition 1. (ε-Differential Privacy Dwork (2006)) A randomized algorithm M is ε-differentially
178   private if for any pair of datasets D, D′ differ in exactly one data point and for all events Y ⊆
179   Range(M) in the output range of M, we have P r{M(D ∈ Y)} ≤ exp(ε)P r{M(D′ ∈ Y)},
180
      where the probability is taken over the randomness of M.
181
      Range(M) refers to the set of all possible outcomes of M. Technically speaking, the set Y in
182   Definition 1 must be measurable. This definition implies that the probability of observing a specific
183   output on any two neighboring datasets can differ by at most a multiplicative factor of exp(ε).
184   Intuitively, a sufficiently small ε value suggests that either including or excluding a single data point
185   from the dataset does not likely affect the output. Hence, an adversary only accessing the output of M
186   is difficult to infer whether any data point is present in the dataset. The parameter ε is called privacy
187   budget and its practical selection varies significantly, depending on different scenarios Ponomareva
188   et al. (2023). However, a relaxation of ε-DP in Definition 1 has been used more commonly instead,
189   which is primarily due to attaining better utility and easier privacy accounting for composing multiple
190
      DP mechanisms. Consequently, an Approximate DP mechanism is defined as follows.
191   Definition 2. ((ε, δ)-differential privacy Dwork (2006)) A randomized algorithm M is (ε, δ)-
192   differentially private if for any two neighboring datasets D, D′ and for all events Y ⊆ Range(M)
193   in the output range of M, we have P r{M(D ∈ Y)} ≤ exp(ε)P r{M(D′ ∈ Y)} + δ, where the
194
      probability is taken over the randomness of M.
195
      Here, it can be observed that δ controls the strength of the relaxation, compared to Definition 1,
196   with smaller values leading to stronger privacy guarantees. A generally recommended δ value in
197   the literature is to choose δ ≪ n1 Ponomareva et al. (2023). In our analysis, we will establish the
198   privacy guarantee for the proposed algorithm presented in the next section. In the sequel, we present
199   preliminaries on random projection and define formally the projection matrix for our algorithm.
200
      Random projection (RP) Achlioptas (2001) is an effectively fundamental tool that has been used
201
      in numerous applications to analyze datasets and then characterize their major features. It projects
202   data points to random directions that are independent on the dataset, which renders simpler and
203   computationally faster trend than classical methods such as singular value decomposition (SVD). RP
204   is based upon the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma Larsen & Nelson (2017), described as follows.

519   Limitation. Though D2P2-SGD has shown good performance compared to the existing baselines,
520   some potential limitations exist, which can also help us close such gaps in future work. First, D2P2-

      SGD may not work well in the scenarios with high privacy restrictions. As we have the decaying

522

      challenging to scale. One way to get rid of this is to develop more effective dynamic DP mechanisms
524   such that the tradeoff between utility and privacy can be handled better. Second, getting an optimal
525   p value for random projection may be difficult as well. Though based on Johnson-Lindenstrauss
526   Lemma, p can be analytically obtained, its practical values for different scenarios have not yet been
527   accessible in a principled manner.

      6 C ONCLUSIONS AND B ROADER I MPACTS
530   This work presents a novel differentially private optimizer termed D2P2-SGD with dynamic DP
531
      mechanism, automatic gradient clipping, and model compression, which reveals the synthesis among
      privacy, utility, and complexity. Specifically, we establish the dynamic privacy guarantee such that a
532
      relatively larger variance is required in the early phase of optimization to compensate the privacy loss
533
      in the latter phase. Given a pre-defined dynamic variance, D2P2-SGD enables a tighter error bound
534   compared to vanilla DPSGD with static DP mechanism. Empirical results are shown to first validate
535   the theory and then compare with baselines, by using a popular model and benchmark datasets.
536   Compared to vanilla DPSGD, our D2P2-SGD is more robust against larger noise variance, but with
537   a slightly larger privacy loss. However, the accuracy is significantly improved without dependence
538   on the large dimension. Broader vision of this work is to advance the field of differentially private
539   machine learning with potential impact in building privacy-aware deep learning models with highly
      sensitive information for critical sectors such as healthcare and national security.
