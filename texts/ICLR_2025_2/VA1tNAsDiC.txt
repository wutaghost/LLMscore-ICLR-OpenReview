008                                                A BSTRACT
009
010            Fully decentralized learning, where the global information, i.e., the actions of
011            other agents, is inaccessible, is a fundamental challenge in cooperative multi-agent
012            reinforcement learning. However, the convergence and optimality of most decen-
013
               tralized algorithms are not theoretically guaranteed, since the transition proba-
               bilities are non-stationary as all agents are updating policies simultaneously. To
014
               tackle this challenge, we propose best possible operator, a novel decentralized
015
               operator, and prove that the policies of cooperative agents will converge to the
016            optimal joint policy if each agent independently updates its individual state-action
017            value by the operator when there is only one optimal joint policy. Further, to make
018            the update more efficient and practical, we simplify the operator and prove that the
019            convergence and optimality still hold with the simplified one. By instantiating the
020            simplified operator, the derived fully decentralized algorithm, best possible Q-
021            learning (BQL), does not suffer from non-stationarity. Empirically, we show that
022            BQL achieves remarkable improvement over baselines in a variety of cooperative
023            multi-agent tasks.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) trains a group of agents to maximize the
029   cumulative shared reward, which has great significance for real-world applications, including logis-
030   tics (Li et al., 2019), traffic signal control (Xu et al., 2021), power dispatch (Wang et al., 2021), and
031   games (Vinyals et al., 2019). Although most existing methods follow the paradigm of centralized
032   training and decentralized execution (CTDE), in many scenarios where the information of all agents
033
      is unavailable in the training period, each agent has to learn independently without centralized infor-
      mation. Thus, fully decentralized learning, where the agents can only use local experiences without
034
      the actions of other agents, is highly desirable (Jiang & Lu, 2022).
035
036   However, in fully decentralized learning, as other agents are treated as a part of the environment and
037   are updating their policies simultaneously, the transition probabilities from the perspective of indi-
038   vidual agents will be non-stationary. Thus, the convergence of most decentralized algorithms, e.g.,
039
      independent Q-learning (IQL) (Tan, 1993), is not theoretically guaranteed. Multi-agent alternate
      Q-learning (MA2QL) (Su et al., 2022) guarantees the convergence to a Nash equilibrium, but the
040
      converged equilibrium may not be the optimal one when there are multiple equilibria (Zhang et al.,
041
      2021a). Distributed IQL (Lauer & Riedmiller, 2000) and I2Q (Jiang & Lu, 2022) can learn the op-
042   timal joint policy, yet are limited to deterministic environments. How to guarantee the convergence
043   of the optimal joint policy in stochastic environments remains open.
044
045
      To tackle this challenge, we propose best possible operator, a novel decentralized operator to update
      the individual state-action value of each agent, and prove that the policies of agents converge to the
046
      optimal joint policy under this operator when there is only one optimal joint policy. However, it is
047
      inefficient and thus impractical to perform best possible operator, because at each update it needs to
048   compute the expected values of all possible transition probabilities and update the state-action value
049   to be the maximal one. Therefore, we further propose simplified best possible operator. At each
050   update, the simplified operator only computes the expected value of one of the possible transition
051   probabilities and monotonically updates the state-action value. We prove that the policies of agents
052   also converge to the optimal joint policy under the simplified operator. We respectively instantiate
053   the simplified operator with Q-table for tabular cases and with neural networks for complex envi-
      ronments. In the Q-table instantiation, non-stationarity is instinctively avoided, and in the neural


                                                         1

054
      network instantiation, non-stationarity in the replay buffer is no longer a drawback, but a necessary
055   condition for convergence.
056
057   The proposed algorithm, best possible Q-learning (BQL), is fully decentralized, without using the
058
      information of other agents. We evaluate BQL on a variety of multi-agent cooperative tasks, i.e.,
      stochastic games, MPE-based differential games (Lowe et al., 2017), Multi-Agent MuJoCo (de Witt
059
      et al., 2020b), SMAC (Samvelyan et al., 2019), and GRF (Kurach et al., 2020), covering fully and
060
      partially observable, deterministic and stochastic, discrete and continuous environments. Empir-
061   ically, BQL substantially outperforms baselines. To the best of our knowledge, BQL is the first
062   decentralized algorithm that guarantees the convergence to the global optimum in stochastic envi-
063   ronments. More simplifications and instantiations of best possible operator can be further explored.
064   We believe BQL can be a new paradigm for fully decentralized learning.

      2018; Son et al., 2019) follow centralized training and decentralized execution (CTDE), where the
      information of all agents can be accessed in a centralized way during training. Unlike these meth-
316
      ods, we focus on fully decentralized learning where global information is not available. The most
317
      straightforward decentralized methods, i.e., independent Q-learning (Tan, 1993) and independent
318   PPO (IPPO) (de Witt et al., 2020a), cannot guarantee the convergence of the learned policy, because
319   the transition probabilities are non-stationary from the perspective of each agent as all agents are
320   learning policies simultaneously. Multi-agent alternate Q-learning (MA2QL) (Su et al., 2022) guar-
321   antees the convergence to a Nash equilibrium, but the converged equilibrium may not be the optimal
322   one when there are multiple Nash equilibria. Moreover, to obtain the theoretical guarantee, it has to
323   be trained in an on-policy manner and cannot use replay buffers, which leads to poor sample effi-
      ciency. Following the principle of optimistic estimation, Hysteretic IQL (Matignon et al., 2007) sets


                                                               6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      a slow learning rate to the value punishment. Distributed IQL (Lauer & Riedmiller, 2000), a special
325   case of Hysteretic IQL with the slow learning rate being zero, guarantees the convergence to the
326   optimum but only in deterministic environments. I2Q (Jiang & Lu, 2022) lets each agent perform
327   independent Q-learning on ideal transition probabilities and could learn the optimal policy only in
328   deterministic environments. Our BQL is the first fully decentralized algorithm that converges to the
329   optimal joint policy in stochastic environments.
330
      In the next section, we compare BQL against these Q-learning variants (Distributed IQL is included
331   in Hysteretic IQL). Comparing with on-policy algorithms, e.g., IPPO, that are not sample-efficient
332   especially in fully decentralized settings, is out of focus and thus deferred to Appendix. Decentral-
333   ized methods with communication (Zhang et al., 2018; Konan et al., 2021; Li & He, 2020) allow
334   information sharing with neighboring agents according to a communication channel. However, they
335   do not follow the fully decentralized setting and thus are beyond the scope of this paper.

350   To support the theoretical analysis of BQL, we test the Q-table instantiation on stochastic games
351   with 4 agents, 30 states, and infinite horizon. The action space of each agent is 4, so the joint action
352   space |A| = 256. The distribution of initial states is uniform. Each state will transition to any state
353   given a joint action according to transition probabilities. The transition probabilities and reward
354   function are randomly generated and fixed in each game. We randomly generate 20 games and train
355   the agents for four different seeds in each game.
356   The mean normalized return and std over the 20 games are shown in Figure 1a. IQL cannot learn
357   the optimal policies due to non-stationarity. Although using the optimistic update to remedy the
358   non-stationarity, Hysteretic IQL (H-IQL) still cannot solve this problem in stochastic environments
359   and shows similar performance to IQL. In Appendix A, we thoroughly analyze the difference and
360   relationship between H-IQL and BQL. I2Q performs Q-learning on the ideal transition function
361   where the next state is deterministically the one with the highest value, which however is impossible
362   in stochastic tasks. So I2Q cannot guarantee the optimal joint policy in stochastic environments.
363
      MA2QL guarantees the convergence to a Nash equilibrium, but the converged one may not be the
      optimal one, thus there is a performance gap between MA2QL and optimal policies. BQL could
364
      converge to the optimum, and the tiny gap is caused by the fitting error of the Q-table update. This
365
      verifies our theoretical analysis. Note that, in Q-table instantiations, MA2QL and BQL use different
366   experience collection from IQL, i.e., exploration strategy and replay buffer. MA2QL only uses on-
367   policy experiences and BQL collects a series of small buffers. However, for sample efficiency, the
368   two methods have to use the same experience collection as IQL in complex tasks with neural net-
369   works. MA2QL- and BQL- respectively denote the two methods with the same experience collection
370   as IQL. Trained on off-policy experiences, MA2QL- suffers from non-stationarity and achieves sim-
371   ilar performance to IQL. Even if using only one buffer, as we have analyzed in Section 2.4, if the
372   non-stationary buffer sufficiently goes through all possible transition probabilities, BQL agents can
373   also converge to the optimum. Although going through all possible transition probabilities by one
374
      buffer is inefficient, BQL- significantly outperforms IQL, which implies the potential of BQL with
      one buffer in complex tasks.

      5     C ONCLUSION
514
515
      We propose best possible operator and theoretically prove that the policies of agents will converge
      to the optimal joint policy if each agent independently updates its individual state-action value by the
516
      operator. We then simplify the operator and derive BQL, the first decentralized MARL algorithm
517
      that guarantees the convergence to the global optimum in stochastic environments. Empirically,
518   BQL outperforms baselines in a variety of multi-agent tasks. We also discuss the limitation of
519   unique optimal joint policy and sample efficiency, and provide corresponding solutions for BQL.

734   One may ask “since you obtain all possible transition probabilities, why not perform IQL on each

739
      |A−i ||S| times is also formidable. BQL only requires that any one of possible transition probability
      of any state-action pair could be found in one buffer, which is much more efficient.

743   age of other agents’ historical policies, which is stochastic. Therefore, to guarantee the optimality,
744   in theory, BQL with one buffer has to go through almost the whole π−i space, which is costly. As
745

746
      network instantiation, we show the results of BQL with the buffer series in Figure 9. Due to sample
747
      efficiency, the buffer series cannot achieve strong performance, and maintaining one buffer like IQL
748   is a better choice in complex environments.
