009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Neural operators (NOs) have demonstrated remarkable success in learning map-
012            pings between function spaces, serving as efficient approximators for the forward
013            solutions of complex physical systems governed by partial differential equations
014            (PDEs). However, while effective as black-box solvers, they offer limited in-
015            sight into the underlying physical mechanism, due to the lack of interpretable
016            representations of the physical parameters that drive the system. To tackle this
017            challenge, we propose a new paradigm for learning disentangled representations
018            from neural operator parameters, thereby effectively solving an inverse problem.
019            Specifically, we introduce DisentangO, a novel hyper-neural operator architecture
020
               designed to unveil and disentangle the latent physical factors of variation embed-
               ded within the black-box neural operator parameters. At the core of DisentangO
021
               is a multi-task neural operator architecture that distills the varying parameters of
022
               the governing PDE through a task-wise adaptive layer, coupled with a variational
023            autoencoder that disentangles these variations into identifiable latent factors. By
024            learning these disentangled representations, our model not only enhances physical
025            interpretability but also enables more robust generalization across diverse physical
026            systems. Empirical evaluations across supervised, semi-supervised, and unsuper-
027            vised learning contexts show that DisentangO effectively extracts meaningful and
028            interpretable latent features, bridging the gap between predictive performance and
029            physical understanding in neural operator frameworks.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033   Interpretability in machine learning (ML) refers to the ability to understand and explain how models
034   make decisions (Rudin et al., 2022; Molnar, 2020). As ML systems grow more complex, especially
035   with the use of deep learning and ensemble methods (Sagi & Rokach, 2018), the reasoning behind
036   their predictions can become opaque. Interpretability addresses this challenge by making the models
037   more transparent, enabling users to trust the outcomes, detect biases, and identify potential flaws. It
038   is a critical factor in applying AI responsibly, especially in fields where accountability and fairness
039
      are essential (Cooper et al., 2022). In physics, where models endeavor to capture the governing laws
      and physical principles, understanding how a model arrives at its predictions is vital for verifying
040
      that it aligns with known scientific theories. Interpretable models also allow researchers to gain
041
      insights into the learned relationships, ensuring that the predictions are physically meaningful and
042   consistent with real-world behaviors. This transparency is key for advancing scientific discovery,
043   validating results, and enhancing trust in models used for complex physical systems.
044
045
      Discovering interpretable representations of physical parameters in learning physical systems is
      challenging due to the intricate nature of real-world phenomena and the often implicit relationships
046
      between variables. In physics, parameters like force, energy, and velocity are governed by well-
047
      established laws, and extracting these parameters in a way that aligns with physical intuition requires
048   models that go beyond mere pattern recognition. Traditional ML models may fit the data but fail to
049   represent these parameters in a physically interpretable way. To address this, recent developments
050   include the integration of physical constraints into the learning process (Raissi et al., 2019), such
051   as embedding conservation laws (Liu et al., 2023; 2024a), symmetries (Mattheakis et al., 2019), or
052   invariances (Cohen & Welling, 2016) directly into model architectures. Additionally, methods like
053   symbolic regression (Biggio et al., 2021) and sparse modeling (Carroll et al., 2009) aim to discover
      simple, interpretable expressions that capture the underlying dynamics. However, balancing model

054
      complexity with interpretability remains a significant challenge, as overly complex models may ob-
055   scure the true physical relationships, while oversimplified models risk losing critical details of the
056   system’s behavior.

058
      In this work, we introduce DisentangO, a novel variational hyper-neural operator architecture to
      disentangle physical factors of variation from black-box neural operator parameters for solving
059
      parametric PDEs. Neural operators (NOs) (Li et al., 2020a;c) learn mappings between infinite-
060
      dimensional function spaces in the form of integral operators, making them powerful tools for dis-
061   covering continuum physical laws by manifesting the mappings between spatial and/or spatiotem-
062   poral data; see You et al. (2022a); Liu et al. (2024a;b; 2023); Ong et al. (2022); Cao (2021); Lu
063   et al. (2019; 2021); Goswami et al. (2022); Gupta et al. (2021) and references therein. However,
064   most NOs play the role of efficient forward surrogates for the underlying physical system under a
065   supervised learning setting. As a result, they act as black-box universal approximators for a single
066   physical system governed by a fixed set of PDE parameters, and lack interpretability with respect
067   to the underlying physical laws. In contrast, the key innovation of DisentangO lies in its use of a
068   hypernetwork architecture that distills the varying physical parameters of the governing PDE from
069
      multiple physical systems through an unsupervised learning setting. The distilled variations are
      further disentangled into distinct physical factors to enhance physical understanding and promote
070
      robust generalization. Consequently, DisentangO effectively extracts meaningful and interpretable
071
      physical features, thereby simultaneously solving both the forward and inverse problems. Our key
072   contributions are:
073
              • We bridge the divide between predictive accuracy and physical interpretability, and intro-
074
                duce a new paradigm that simultaneously performs physics modeling (i.e., as a forward
075             PDE solver) and governing physical mechanism discovery (i.e., as an inverse PDE solver).
076
              • We propose a novel variational hyper-neural operator architecture, which we coin Disen-
077             tangO. DisentangO extracts the key physical factors of variation from black-box neural
078             operator parameters of multiple physical systems. These factors are then disentangled into
079             distinct latent factors that enhance physical interpretation and robust generalization.
080           • We provide theoretical analysis on the component-wise identifiability of the true generative
081             factors in physics modeling: by learning from multiple physical systems, the variability of
082             hidden physical states in these systems promotes identifiability.
083           • We explore the practical utility of disentanglement rather than pursuing it as an end goal,
084             and perform experiments across a broad range of settings including supervised, semi-
085             supervised, and unsupervised learning. Our results show that DisentangO effectively ex-
086             tracts meaningful and interpretable physical features for enhanced interpretability.

088   2   BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK
089   Neural operators. Learning complex physical systems directly from data is crucial in many scien-
090   tific and engineering applications (Ghaboussi et al., 1998; Liu et al., 2024c; Ghaboussi et al., 1991;
091   Carleo et al., 2019; Karniadakis et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2018; Cai et al., 2022; Pfau et al., 2020;
092   He et al., 2021; Jafarzadeh et al., 2024). Often, the governing laws of these systems are unknown
093
      and must be inferred from data through learning models that are both resolution-invariant to ensure
      consistent performance across various levels of discretizations and interpretable to domain experts.
094
      Neural operators (NOs) address the first aim by learning mappings between infinite-dimensional
095
      function spaces (Li et al., 2020a;b;c; You et al., 2022a; Ong et al., 2022; Cao, 2021; Lu et al., 2019;
096   2021; Goswami et al., 2022; Gupta et al., 2021). By capturing the relationship between spatial and
097   spatio-temporal data, NOs offer a promising approach to discovering continuum physical surrogates,
098   enabling more accurate and consistent predictions of the underlying responses of complex systems.
099   Nevertheless, NOs lack an interpretable representation of the physical states driving the system, and
100   as a result offer limited insight into the underlying physical mechanisms.
101
      Hypernetworks. Hypernetworks (Ha et al., 2016; Schmidhuber, 1992; Stanley et al., 2009;
102   Chauhan et al., 2024) are a class of neural network architectures that use one network to generate
103   weights for another neural network. Then, both networks can be trained in an end-to-end differ-
104   entiable manner. As such, hypernetworks allow soft weight sharing across different tasks, which
105   makes them useful for transfer learning and dynamic information sharing (Chauhan et al., 2023).
106   Hypernetworks can also be employed as a versatile technique in existing neural network architec-
107   tures. For instance, in Nguyen et al.; Oh & Peng (2022), a hypernetwork is employed to generate
      parameters for a VAE model and enable multi-task learning. Similarly, Lee et al. (2023) integrates


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the hypernetwork architecture with neural operators. However, none of the existing work discusses
109   the capability of hypernetworks in hidden physics discovery from data.
110
111   Forward and inverse PDE learning. Existing NOs focus on providing efficient surrogates for
112
      the underlying physical systems as forward PDE solvers. They are often employed as black-box
      universal approximators but lack interpretability of the underlying physical principles. On the other
113
      hand, several deep learning methods have been developed as inverse PDE solvers (Fan & Ying,
114
      2023; Molinaro et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023), aiming to reconstruct the PDE
115   parameters from solution data. However, the inverse problem is typically more challenging due to its
116   ill-posedness. To tackle this pathology, many NOs incorporate prior information via governing PDEs
117   (Yang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021), regularizers (Dittmer et al., 2020; Obmann et al., 2020; Ding et al.,
118   2022; Chen et al., 2023), or additional operator structures (Uhlmann, 2009; Lai et al., 2019; Yilmaz,
119   2001). However, all these work rely on prior information on the operator form, which is not realistic
120   in many real-world problems where neither the parameter field nor the model form are available.
121   Worth noting is a recent work that leverages the attention mechanism to build a nonlinear kernel map
122   in NO (Yu et al., 2024), with which a data-dependent kernel can be constructed to characterize the
123
      inverse mapping from data to the hidden governing mechanism. While effective, this approach does
      not disentangle the learned kernel or extract physically interpretable parameters. To the best of our
124
      knowledge, our proposed DisentangO is the first that tackles both forward (i.e., physics prediction)
125
      and inverse (i.e., physics discovery) PDE learning problems, while simultaneously inferring the
126   governing distinct physical parameters from the learned NO.
127
128   Disentangled representation learning. Disentangled representation learning seeks to separate data
129
      into distinct, interpretable factors, where each factor captures an independent aspect of variation in
      the input. This area has critical implications for transfer learning, generative modeling, and fairness
130
      in AI, as it allows models to generalize across tasks by leveraging isolated features, making them
131
      more interpretable and modular. Disentangled representation learning has seen significant advances,
132   primarily driven by models like β-VAE (Higgins et al., 2017), FactorVAE (Kim & Mnih, 2018), and
133   InfoGAN (Chen et al., 2016), who encourage disentanglement by controlling the trade-off between
134   reconstruction accuracy and latent regularization, or introduce mutual information constraints to en-
135   courage the discovery of interpretable latent factors. Early work in disentanglement largely focus
136   on supervised or semi-supervised approaches (Ridgeway & Mozer, 2018; Shu et al., 2019; Mathieu
137   et al., 2019), requiring labeled data for factors of variation. However, more recent advancements
138   have concentrated on unsupervised learning methods (Duan et al., 2019), where disentanglement
139   is achieved without explicit labels. Despite these efforts, fully unsupervised disentanglement re-
140
      mains challenging (Locatello et al., 2019), with ongoing research focusing on improving metrics,
      robustness, and applicability to more complex and high-dimensional data. In this context, although
141
      a large body of research has been done in disentanglement, its applications remain largely confined
142
      to fields like computer vision and robotics, where the latent dimensions typically correspond to
143   human-interpretable (often visually recognizable) features, with very limited exploration in areas
144   such as physical system learning (cf. Lingsch et al. (2024); Tong et al. (2024); Fotiadis et al. (2023)
145   in supervised learning). Moreover, existing work focuses on disentangling from data, whereas our
146   work is the first attempt that explores disentanglement from black-box neural network parameters.

      5     C ONCLUSION
531   We present DisentangO, a novel hyper-neural operator architecture designed to disentangle latent
532   physical factors embedded within black-box neural operator parameters. DisentangO leverages a
533   hypernetwork-type neural operator architecture that extracts varying parameters of the governing
534   PDE through a task-wise adaptive layer, then further disentangles these variations into distinct la-
535   tent factors. By learning these disentangled representations, DisentangO not only enhances physical
536
      interpretability but also enables more robust generalization across diverse physical systems. We per-
      form empirical evaluations across supervised, semi-supervised, and unsupervised learning contexts.
537
538   Limitations: Due to limited computational resource, our experiments are focused on learning a
539   small-to-medium number (< 500). Expanding the coverage to encompass a broader range of physi-
      cal systems and enabling learning across more diverse parameters would be beneficial.

875   A.2    F URTHER DISCUSSION ON THE ASSUMPTIONS
876
877   Herein, we provide additional discussion on the validity and emprical validation for Assumptions
878   1-4.
879   As seen in the proof above, Assumptions 1 (Density Smoothness and Positivity) and 2 (Invertibil-
880   ity) are required to guarantee that there exists a smooth and injective mapping h := H ◦ r ◦ Ĥ−1 ,
881   from the ground-truth latent embedding z to the learned embedding ẑ. Furthermore, the smoothness
882   assumption further makes it feasible to take derivatives of z with respect to ẑ, which supports the
883   permutation-wise identifiability proof for Theorem 2. Here, we note that the smoothness assumption
884   may possibly be relaxed to C 2 . Assumptions 3 (Conditional Independence) and 4 (Linear Indepen-

      column. Without these assumptions, it is possible that the data from different b lack variability.
