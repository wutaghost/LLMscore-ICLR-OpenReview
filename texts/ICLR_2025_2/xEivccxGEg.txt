009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Zero-shot coordination has recently become a hot topic in reinforcement learn-
012            ing research recently. It focuses on the generalization ability of agents, requiring
013            them to coordinate well with collaborators that are not seen before without any
014            fine-tuning. Population-based training has been proven to provide good zero-shot
015            coordination performance; nevertheless, existing algorithms exhibit inefficiency,
016            as the training cost scales linearly with the population size. To address this issue,
017            this paper proposes the Conditional Mutual Information Maximized Population
018
               (CMIMP), an efficient training framework comprising two key components: a
               meta-agent that efficiently realizes a population by selectively sharing parameters
019
               across agents, and a mutual information regularizer that guarantees population di-
020
               versity. To empirically validate the effectiveness of CMIMP, this paper evaluates
021            it along with representational frameworks in Hanabi and confirms its superiority.
022

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Over these years, Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) has achieved remarkable success
027   in various tasks, such as UAV navigation (Han et al., 2020), traffic signal control (Calvo & Dus-
028   paric, 2018) and resource allocation (Lin et al., 2018). To overcome the instability of reinforcement
029   learning in multi-agent scenarios, researchers commonly adopt the strategy of self-play (Lowe et al.,
030   2017), where a fixed group of agents are trained and tested together. This training paradigm endows
031   agents with the capability to rapidly learn cooperative strategies, while posing the risk of overfitting
032   to the training partners.
033   In order to improve generalization performance of cooperative agents, Hu et al. (2020) propose
034   the problem of Zero-Shot Coordination (ZSC), which requires agents to coordinate with unknown
035   agents without prior knowledge. One solution to this problem is reasoning about the task or partners
036   (Shih et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023). Such solutions help agents learn consensus at the algorithm level,
037   i.e. agents trained by the same framework can zero-shot coordinate well, but the agents still cannot
038   coordinate well with agents trained by other types of algorithms (Lucas & Allen, 2022). Population-
039
      based training is another popular solution, which allows for training a best-response agent against
      a population of agents (Charakorn et al., 2022). One crucial advantage of these methods is that
040
      they can directly improve agents’ zero-shot coordination performance with a population filled with
041
      diverse agents striving toward the same objective but exhibiting different behaviors. However, train-
042   ing a diverse population of agents significantly increases the computational cost. Simultaneously,
043   there lack a robust and direct constraint to ensure that different agents act in distinct styles, while
044   existing representative methods (Zhao et al., 2023) prefer to incorporate the average entropy of pop-
045   ulation actions carrying the risk of being misled by an agent exhibiting a random style. Besides,
046   some population-training frameworks can only accommodate policies that output differentiable ac-
047   tion distributions (Guo et al., 2024), hindering their practicality
048   Consequently, a new paradigm of population training for ZSC is needed that reduces computational
049   costs while maintaining population diversity to achieve an efficient and diverse population. Meta-
050   learning is an approach that enhances network’s generalization ability through multi-task training.
051   Inspired by that traditional meta-learning and multi-task learning (Tang et al., 2020; Kim & Sung,
052   2023) manage to train a single network with the ability of quickly adapting to various tasks while
053   population training can also be treated as a multi task learning process, agent should also be able to
      perform various policies through a meta-policy and different task related adapters, thereby simulat-

      agents while still providing various actions from diverse policies within the meta-agent.

070   Motivated by the aforementioned issues and studies, this paper introduces an efficient population-
071   based training framework called Conditional Mutual Information Maximized Population (CMIMP).
072   Within this framework, CMIMP leverages a meta-agent with hierarchical architecture, allowing
073   multiple agents to share parameters for processing observations and history information, while us-
074
      ing distinct sub-decision modules for action generation. This reduces the number of parameters and
      computational complexity by enabling N agents to generate actions in one single forward calcu-
075
      lation. To ensure distinct behaviors among sub-decision modules, CMIMP maximizes the mutual
076
      information between actions and the sub-decision modules’ index, conditioned on observations.
077   Since some reinforcement learning (RL) frameworks cannot output differentiable action distribu-
078   tions, CMIMP optimizes an alternative objective: maximizing differences in preference values (e.g.,
079   Q-values or action selection probabilities) between sub-decision modules, which theoretically in-
080   creases mutual information.
081
      To empirically illustrate the effectiveness of CMIMP, we conduct experiments on Hanabi, a coop-
082
      erative card game commonly used for zero-shot coordination research. For more comprehensive
083   evaluation, we present two metrics: intra-algorithm cross-play scores (Hu et al., 2020) and one-
084   sided zero-shot coordination cross-play scores (Lucas & Allen, 2022). CMIMP is evaluated along
085   with several representative frameworks designed for zero-shot coordination and demonstrates supe-
086   riority. Furthermore, we conduct additional experiments to explore the impact of specific settings
087   on population training, including the pairing mode and population size.
088
      Our contributions are summarized below:
089
090           • We analyze the necessity of population-based methods for zero-shot coordination and the
091             limitations of existing methods in terms of computational complexity and population di-
092             versity. By comparing the similarities between population frameworks and meta-RL, we
093             suggest that meta-RL can be employed to achieve an efficient population framework. In
094             line with our motivation, we explore the application of meta-RL within this domain.
095           • We propose CMIMP, a novel zero-shot coordination framework consisting of a meta-agent
096             with hierarchical architecture to realize a population and a conditional mutual informa-
097             tion maximized scheme that guarantees population diversity by maximize the conditional
098
                mutual information of different action modules in meta-agent.
099           • We empirically validate the superiority of the proposed CMIMP over existing approaches
100             in Hanabi. In comparison, our method achieves better zero-shot coordination performance,
101             significantly enhances training efficiency, and reduces resource consumption. Moreover,
102
                we conduct ablation studies to investigate how different training modes affect the perfor-
                mance of population training.

      the diversity of which is innate and invariable. In contrast, CMIMP’s meta-agent is designed to

142         F RAMEWORK
143
144
      In order to achieve efficient and versatile population training for zero-shot coordination, we propose
145   a novel CMIMP framework consisting of a hierarchical meta-agent that efficiently realizes popula-
146   tion and a conditional mutual information maximization term that guarantees population diversity.
147   Notably, the term is versatile since it does not require agents to output differentiable action distribu-
148   tions like some related works (Lupu et al., 2021) do. The details of CMIMP are presented below.
149
150   3.1    H IERARCHICAL M ETA -AGENT F OR E FFICIENT P OPULATION
151
152   One major drawback of population training is that it has to train multiple agents, which is time-
153   consuming. Considering that meta-RL techniques manage to train meta-agents with the ability of
154   quickly adapting to various tasks, selectively sharing parameters across different agents in a popula-
155   tion is feasible: those task-related parameters (correspond to modules that process observation and
156   keep history memory) can be shared, and those behavior-related parameters (correspond to modules
157   used for decision-making) can be individually optimized.
158   Based on the aforementioned idea, we design a meta-agent with hierarchical architectures to real-
159   ize a population. It consists of several neural-network-based modules, including an observation
160   encoder f o that processes observations, an LSTM f l that keeps historical information and up-
161   dates hidden states, a value head f v that outputs state value v t , and several sub-decision modules

      4.1    E XPERIMENTAL E NVIRONMENT
298
299
      We conduct experiments on Hanabi (Bard et al., 2020), a card game which requires players to co-
300   operatively play cards of different colors and ranks in order. Playing cards wrongly leads to the
301   loss of life tokens, and the shared team score is the number of cards that have been correctly played
302   at the end of the game. Notably, players can only view the cards of the collaborators, yet lack the
303   capacity to observe the cards in their own hands. Due to this setting, players have to reason others’
304   intention as well as convey information through actions. Therefore, self-play agents can easily get
305   high scores for they are familiar with each other, while cooperating with strangers gets quite hard,
306   making Hanabi a popular benchmark for zero-shot coordination research.
307
308   4.2    E VALUATION C RITERIA FOR Z ERO -S HOT C OORDINATION
309
310   Zero-shot coordination requires agents to cooperate well with collaborators that are not seen before,
311   namely “strangers”. Since “strangers” does not refer to specific datasets or agents, the corresponding
312   evaluation criteria are slightly different from those of normal MARL. Below we give a formulaic
313
      representation in two-agent scenarios.
314   Use J(π1 , π2 ) to denote the expected cumulative discounted return obtained by the collaboration
315   of π1 and π2 . Use πiM to denote the policy obtained with training framework M and random seed
316   i. The earliest metric to evaluate zero-shot cooperation performance is intra-algorithm cross-play
317   (abbreviated as Intra-XP) score (Hu et al., 2020):
318
                                    Sintra−XP (M ) = E[J(πiM , πjM )|i ̸= j]                             (9)
319
320   Cross-play score represents how well agents cooperate with partners that are trained by the same
321   framework but different seeds. This metric is easily accessible and relatively objective, but has a
322   strong assumption on the “strangers”. Besides, considering that testing partners are trained with the
323   same algorithm, the main test agent has a little prior information about them, therefore, it is not
      rigorous to judge zero-shot coordination performance based on this metric alone.

      4.3      C OMPARATIVE E XPERIMENTS
348
349   To empirically validate the superiority of CMIMP, we test it along with the following frameworks:
350
351   SP(Self-Play): The baseline self-play training with parameter sharing which acts as a baseline.
352   OP (Hu et al., 2020): A classical framework that improves zero-shot coordination by breaking
353   symmetries in self-play training.
354
      OBL (Hu et al., 2021): A representational framework that trains policies with multi-level cognitive
355
      reasoning and thus avoids over-fitting to certain training partners.
356
357   TrajeDi (Lupu et al., 2021): A population-based training framework that improves the trajectory
358   diversity of agents in the population.
359   MEP (Zhao et al., 2023): A population-based framework that improves diversity by maximizing the
360   average policy entropy of the population.
361

362

364   Besides, TrajeDi and MEP require agents to output differentiable action distribution and thus act in
365   a Boltzmann way1 instead of the conventional ϵ-greedy way.

      4.5                  C OMPARISON OF D IFFERENT T RAINING M ODES
514
515
516
      Table 2 introduces six feasible training modes for population-based training. Then which mode
      is the best? We present training curves of different modes in Fig. 5. It can be seen that Diff Prob
517
      and MP Score exhibit consistent trends across all training modes, indicating that different modes are
518
      consistent in optimizing the primary objective (J(πm , πp )) and enhancing diversity. However, Tab. 5
519   confirms that zero-shot coordination performance of different training modes varies a lot, and such
520   differences are brought by the settings of secondary objectives. Mode-I is the worst, indicating that
521   only optimizing the primary objective (J(πm , πp )) is not enough. Mode-II is the best, confirming
522   the necessity of adding the self-play objective for the main agent. Notably, Mode-IV and Mode-VI
523   additionally require increasing self-play scores for partner agents on the basis of Mode-II, and this
524   operation is of no benefit judging from the results.

527   5                C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   In this paper, we discuss the necessity of population training for the zero-shot coordination and high-
531
      light the logical commonalities between population training and meta-RL or multi-task learning,
      which can address the inefficiencies of existing population-based zero-shot coordination methods
532
      due to outdated training frameworks. Driven by this motivation, we propose an efficient population-
533
      based zero-shot coordination framework, called CMIMP, to achieve a simulation of diverse pop-
534   ulations of any population size through a single parameter adjustment, while incurring almost no
535   additional training costs compared to training a single agent. Experiments conducted in Hanabi
536   validate the outstanding performance of our proposed method in zero-shot coordination capabil-
537   ities, efficiency, and low resource requirements. Additionally, our proposed training framework
538   demonstrates promising potential for large-scale population training, as its training is unaffected by
539   population size, allowing for the implementation of extremely large-scale population training, which
      could further enhance zero-shot coordination capabilities.
