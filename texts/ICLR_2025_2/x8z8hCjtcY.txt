010                                                   A BSTRACT
011
012             The demand for regulating the behavior of large language models (LLMs) has
013             ignited research on alignment algorithms, the essence of which is to align LLMs’
014             generations with human preferences. Due to infeasibility of humans directly
015             participating in the training or generation of LLMs, existing alignment algorithms
016             choose to align with human preferences carried by proxies, i.e., preference data or
017             reward models. However, whether these human proxies faithfully represent human
018             preferences remain under-explored. We categorize human proxies into two levels
019
                based on the degree to which they directly embody human preferences: Level-
                1 Proxy (preference data) and Level-2 Proxy (reward models). We empirically
020
                examine the faithfulness of both levels of proxies and its impacts on alignment
021
                performance. We notice that current algorithms tend to overlook the faithfulness
022             of these proxies in reflecting human preferences; many works even directly use
023             reward models as their automatic evaluators without any correlation verification.
024             Current literature of alignment overly focuses on optimizing algorithms, rendering
025             the faithfulness of human proxies an "elephant in the room"—something extremely
026             important yet largely overlooked. According to experimental results, we unveil
027             potential risks of using inferior “human proxies”, aiming to arouse attention to
028             this huge “elephant” in alignment research. We summarize existing pitfalls from
029             different angles and provide a re-labeled preference dataset and insights about
030
                reward model usage to facilitate the healthy development of alignment1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated formidable capabilities across diverse applica-
036
      tions, necessitating their alignment (Ouyang et al., 2022) with human values and preferences for
      responsible use. Since it is infeasible for humans to directly participate in the training or inference
037
      of LLMs, existing alignment methods turn to indirect alignment with various human proxies, such
038
      as preference datasets (Bai et al., 2022; Köpf et al., 2023) or reward models. For example, the
039   pioneering alignment method, Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang
040   et al., 2022), uses a reinforcement learning algorithm where the reward model provides preference
041   signals (Schulman et al., 2017) for the alignment of the target LLM. Following RLHF, various
042   novel alignment methods (Yuan et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023) have emerged,
043   aiming to address the inherent limitations of RLHF, e.g., instability, difficulty in convergence, and
044   sensitivity to hyperparameters (Wolf et al., 2023; Casper et al., 2023), while achieving comparable
045   alignment performance. For almost all the alignment algorithms, the consensus that “human proxies
046   faithfully reflect human preferences” is taken for granted. Otherwise, aligning with something that are
047
      unrelated or even opposite to real human preferences may seem too scary. Since participants (reward
      models for providing alignment signals) are also evaluators (reward models for evaluating alignment
048
      performance)2 , even if there is indeed a misalignment, this secret seems to be deeply buried in this
049
      closed loop. The reliability of human proxies are gradually becoming an “elephant in the room”.

069   In this work, we try to make this “elephant” visible by unveiling pitfalls and risks from different
070   angles through empirical analysis. According to the direct degree to which different proxies reflect
071   human preferences, we first categorize them into two levels: Level-1 Proxy (preference data) and
072   Level-2 Proxy (reward models trained with Level-1 Proxy). Level-1 Proxy, preference data, is
073
      normally expected to be the most direct carrier of genuine human preferences, which is usually
      used in the following two ways: (1) directly used to train LLMs to fit human preferences through
074
      alignment algorithms like DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023); (2) used to train Level-2 Proxy, i.e., reward
075
      models, which are further used to guide the alignment of LLMs using alignment algorithms like
076   PPO (Schulman et al., 2017). As for reward models, the Level-2 Proxy, there are generally two uses
077   in alignment: (1) providing alignment signals for alignment optimization (Ouyang et al., 2022; Yuan
078   et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024); (2) serving as an automatic evaluator for alignment performance (Song
079   et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2023). Due to the heavy reliance of alignment on various human proxies,
080   realizing and studying the reliability of them is important. As shown by the illustrative example in
081   Figure 1 in the widely-used HH-RLHF preference dataset, the Chosen Reponse for LLMs to align
082   with expresses severe toxicity by suggesting the user to “get a new mom”. In considering how this
083   preference data might guide LLMs to align with distorted values, we observe flaws that trigger doubts
084
      about the reliability of these human proxies: Do these so-called human proxies indeed faithfully
      reflect genuine human values? If not, what impacts they may have on alignment performance?
085
086   To answer these questions, we first study the quality of the widely-used preference dataset, HH-
087   RLHF (Bai et al., 2022). We re-label the original dataset and present our cleaned version, CHH-RLHF,
088   as a more reliable testbed for alignment research. Using CHH-RLHF, we compute the correlation
089   between human evaluation and reward model evaluation, which unveils the pitfalls of using inferior
090
      reward models for evaluation of alignment performance. We also examine the accuracy of various
      reward models used in previous alignment research, uncovering potential risks of misalignment.
091
      Moreover, we conduct systematic experiments on various alignment algorithms to study the impacts
092
      of Level-1 and Level-2 Proxy with different quality on alignment performance. This work aims to
093   ring the bell for researchers to attach importance to verification of human proxies before using them
094   whether for alignment optimization or evaluation. Our main contributions are summarized below:
095
096     • We first categorize different human proxies into two levels: Level-1 Proxy and Level-2 Proxy.
097       We then analyze the HH-RLHF preference dataset and present our cleaned version, CHH-RLHF,
098       to facilitate reliable alignment research in the future3 .
099     • Using CHH-RLHF, we conduct empirical analysis to systematically unveil pitfalls and potential
100       risks of using unverified human proxies for alignment optimization and evaluation. Specially, we
101       examine the accuracy of various reward models and the correlation between human evaluation
102       and reward model evaluation. We also conduct extensive experiments to study the impacts of 2
103       levels of human proxies with different quality on the alignment performance.
104     • Through systematic analysis and experiments of 2 levels of human proxies from different angles,
105       we aim to ring the bell for researchers to pay attention to this “huge elephant” in the alignment
106       research.

125   2       TAXONOMY OF H UMAN P ROXIES
126
127   As illustrated in Figure 2, we categorize human proxies into two levels according to the direct level to
128
      which they reflect human preferences.
129
130   2.1      L EVEL -1 P ROXY: P REFERENCE DATA
131
      Preference data is the most direct information carrier of human preferences, which usually takes
132
      the form of pairwise data with labels annotated by humans: “chosen” for the human preferred
133   response, which “rejected” for the dispreferred one. Preference data is expected to faithfully reflect
134   human preferences, which is usually used to train LLMs to generate responses aligned with the
135   chosen responses. Since preference data is directly annotated by humans, making it the most direct
136   information carrier of human preferences, we thus define it as the Level-1 Proxy. In alignment
137   research, preference data is usually used for two purposes: (1) used to train LLMs to align with
138   human preferences contained in the preference data using algorithms like DPO; (2) used to train
139   reward models. However, pitfalls in preference data may hinder the achievement of both purposes.
140
141   2.2      L EVEL -2 P ROXY: R EWARD M ODEL
142
143   Reward models used in alignment are models that are typically trained with the Level-1 Proxy
144
      (preference data) to automatically score any given responses: generate higher scores for human-
      preferred responses and lower scores for human-dispreferred ones. Reward models are expected to
145
      infer human intentions and preferences from Level-1 Proxy, not directly labeled or guided by humans,
146
      which is thus defined as Level-2 Proxy. In alignment research, reward models are usually utilized
147   for two purposes: (1) providing signals to LLMs for alignment optimization; (2) providing scores
148   to LLMs’ generations as an evaluation metric for the alignment performance. However, pitfalls of
149   reward models may pose great obstacles to achieving these two purposes.

      We analyze the correlation between human evaluation and reward model evaluation to assess whether

304   not qualified to act as a human proxy for automatic evaluation of alignment performance. This finding

       5    D ISCUSSION
505
506   5.1    S TUDIES R ELATED TO P ROXIES
507
      Research on the reliability of human proxies is limited. (Wang et al., 2024) mainly analyzes the
508
      impact of preference data quality on reward model performance, and also proposes to use contrastive
509
      learning to enhance the abilities of reward models. Nevertheless, this work also fails to analyze the
510   impact of preference data or reward model on the alignment performance, which is a key gap we aim
511   to fill in this work: validating the reliability of human proxies is vital for alignment development.
512   REWARDBENCH (Lambert et al., 2024) benchmarks existing open-source reward models. However,
513   they only establish a leaderboard for reward models, failing to unveil the relationship between the
514   alignment performance and reward models quality. In this situation, even with a leaderboard clearly
515   reflecting the performance of various reward models, it remains uncertain which reward model should
516   be chosen to achieve optimal alignment performance and reliable alignment evaluation.
517
518   5.2    G OODHART ’ S L AW
519
520   “When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.”                         —     Charles Goodhart
521   Although our experiments have demonstrated that using a better reward model in existing alignment
522   algorithms can improve alignment performance, this finding is constrained by a limitation known as
523   Goodhart’s Law (Hennessy & Goodhart, 2023; Manheim & Garrabrant, 2019). Originally articulated
524   by economist Charles Goodhart in the context of economic policy (Goodhart, 1984), this principle
525   posits that when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. Applying Goodhart’s Law,
526   we recognize that over-optimizing the reward model—an imperfect proxy of human intentions—may
527   impair the effectiveness of the policy model. While this phenomenon is frequently discussed in the
528   field of reinforcement learning, it has been somewhat overlooked in alignment research. We aim to
529
      highlight the limitations of reward model optimization and call for future research to adopt a balanced
      approach, carefully navigating the risks of both under-optimization and over-optimization.
530
531
532    6    C ONCLUSION
533
534   This paper highlights the overlooked yet critical role of various human proxies in alignment research.
535   We curate a cleaned dataset, CHH-RLHF, derived from a widely-used preference dataset, HH-RLHF,
536   to facilitate reliable alignment research. We categorize human proxies into two levels (Level-1
537   Proxy and Level-2 Proxy), and conduct empirical analysis from different angles to systematically
538   unveil potential pitfalls and risks in current alignment research. We call on alignment researchers
539   to recalibrate their future research objectives by not only focusing on alignment algorithms but also
      considering other influential elements such as reliability of various human proxies.

      B.1.1    A NNOTATOR I NFORMATION
730
731   We hire three well-educated postgraduates to distinguish and score responses. Here, by “well-
732   educated”, we mean that they have the ability to distinguish between good and bad responses and
733   can fully understand the predetermined scoring criteria. Based on the criteria, they can accurately
734   and reasonably score responses. These annotators can complete the annotation task well. We pay
735   annotators $0.05 per annotated sample and expect each annotation to take an average of 20 seconds.
736
737   B.1.2    A NNOTATION TASK
738   We ask annotators to complete the following two annotation tasks. The first annotation task is to
739   evaluate the alignment performance by selecting a better response between the response generated
740   by the aligned LLM and the chosen response in the dataset. The second annotation task is to score
741   responses. We then use the average scores labeled by all annotators to conduct a correlation analysis
742   with the scores generated by the reward model.
743
744     • Alignment Evaluation: Annotators need to use harmlessness and helpfulness as the basis for
745       their annotation. The criteria for selecting a better response are as below:
746       (1) A harmless response is better than a harmful one;
747       (2) For two harmful responses, the less harmful response is better than the more harmful one;
748       (3) For two harmless responses, the more helpful response is better than the less helpful one.
749       In rare cases where the levels of harmlessness and helpfulness of two responses are roughly
750       equivalent, annotators should further compare aspects such as accuracy, completeness, and fluency
751       to determine which response is of higher quality.
752     • Correlation Analysis: To quantitatively measure the correlation between reward model evalu-
753       ation and human evaluation, we ask annotators to score responses. However, there are several
754       challenges here. Firstly, because different individuals have varying standards for scoring the
755       same response, significant discrepancies can arise even if the response is generally considered
          good. To address this, we first standardize the scoring criteria and provide uniform pre-training to

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
            the annotators. This ensures as much consistency in scoring as possible. Additionally, the same
757         individual might exhibit inconsistency in scoring the whole set of responses. To address this, we
758         require annotators to review all their scores after completing the whole annotation process to
759         ensure the overall scoring standards are stable. Annotators are also asked to sign a confirmation
760         statement to affirm the consistency and accuracy of their annotations. The main criteria for
761         annotators to score responses are as follows:
762         (1) Harmlessness: Assess whether the response contains inappropriate content such as malicious,
763         offensive, discriminatory, or violent information;
764         (2) Helpfulness: Evaluate whether the response accurately answers the user’s question and
765         whether the information provided is practically useful and valuable;
766         (3) Accuracy: Evaluate whether the information provided in the response is correct and reliable;
767         (4) Completeness: Assess whether the response provides a thorough and comprehensive answer,
768         covering all aspects of the user’s query;
769         (5) Fluency: Evaluate whether the response is grammatically correct, has proper spelling, and if
770         the sentence structure is natural and coherent, ensuring smooth readability.

780   harmless responses, you should choose the more helpful one as the better response.”
781   Here, {generated} and {chosen} are placeholders, which are replaced with the response generated

      D      L IMITATIONS
793
794
      Despite the contributions of our study in highlighting the critical role of various human proxies in
795   LLM alignment, there are several limitations to consider. One notable limitation is the absence of a
796   systematic discussion and investigation of Goodhart’s Law (Goodhart, 1984; Manheim & Garrabrant,
797   2019), which states that when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. This
798   principle is relevant to our work, as overly optimizing reward models without considering potential
799   upper bounds may lead to suboptimal or even harmful outcomes. Moreover, although using a cleaned
800   dataset yields better alignment performance, past research suggests that training models with clean
801   datasets may affect the robustness of the models (Shi et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2021). Our work lacks
802   research and discussion on this aspect. We plan to conduct systematic analysis and research on the
803
      robustness of aligned models in future work, where our CHH-RLHF dataset can serve as a testbed.
