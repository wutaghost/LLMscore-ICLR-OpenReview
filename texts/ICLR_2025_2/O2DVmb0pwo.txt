009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Large language models (LLMs) have showcased remarkable capabilities on a va-
012                riety of natural language processing (NLP) tasks, powering various real-world ap-
013                plications. Ensuring the safe and effective deployment of LLMs requires careful
014                alignment to mitigate risks associated with malicious inputs, which now mainly
015                involve toxic content and misinformation. In this study, we expand this focus by
016                identifying and exploring a novel category of energy-oriented malicious instruc-
017                tions, akin to Denial-of-Service (DoS) attacks. These instructions provoke LLMs
018
                   to generate excessively lengthy responses through impractical tasks, resulting in
                   high energy and computational resource consumption, and even risking system
019
                   overload. To address this gap, we curate EnergyAlign, the first energy-oriented
020
                   malicious instruction dataset with 8 diverse categories. Then, we conduct a com-
021                prehensive evaluation of 5 advanced proprietary LLMs and 24 open-source LLMs.
022                The results reveal a notable disparity: while proprietary LLMs can refuse such
023                malicious inputs, most open-source LLMs are extremely vulnerable with a fail-
024                ure rate of up to 96.8%. Additionally, we assess the effectiveness of jailbreak
025                techniques in bypassing the energy-related safety measures of proprietary models.
026                Lastly, we highlight the inadequacies of existing defense mechanisms and propose
027                energy-oriented alignment data against EnergyAlign for future research.1

030   1       I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have unlocked unprecedented capabilities
033   across diverse natural language processing (NLP) domains (OpenAI, 2023a; Gemini Team et al.,
034
      2023; Touvron et al., 2023). Numerous commercial applications like 24-hour customer support
      systems are now powered LLMs, enhancing service availability and responsiveness. Despite their
035
      impressive capabilities, LLMs can still exhibit undesirable behaviors that, without careful align-
036
      ment, may result in harmful outcomes. Consequently, many research has focused on aligning LLMs
037   to mitigate these behaviors, particularly in handling malicious inputs (Wang et al., 2023; Sun et al.,
038   2024), with a primary focus on reducing toxicity (Ji et al., 2023; Radharapu et al., 2023) and misin-
039   formation (Buszydlik et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2023).
040
      In contrast to prior research, we explore a novel and under-explored category of energy-oriented
041
      threats to LLM-powered services, analogous to denial-of-service (DoS) attacks (Lau et al., 2000;
042
      Mirkovic & Reiher, 2004; Needham, 1993). Instead of provoking toxic responses, energy-oriented
043   threats aim to overload systems by inducing excessive energy consumption and resource waste in
044   neural models (Hong et al., 2021; Krithivasan et al., 2022; Tao et al., 2024). This is particularly
045   problematic in time-sensitive applications where such attacks can severely degrade performance.
046   Given that LLM service providers typically offer API access and host public web applications, this
047   type of threat is a viable risk. Attackers may exploit credit card fraud or reverse engineering (Rekoff,
048   1985) to send malicious queries, imposing significant computational burdens on service providers
049   at minimal costs. The higher inference costs associated with LLMs, compared to smaller models,
050   amplify the severity of these threats.
051   Complementary to traditional DoS attacks, which flood a system with a large number of requests,
052   we aim to maximize energy consumption and resource waste with a single query by crafting energy-

087   reveal a notable discrepancy between the two groups. While proprietary LLMs exhibit high ro-
088   bustness, most open-source LLMs struggle, often either fully complying with malicious instructions
089   to generate lengthy outputs or failing to justify their refusal effectively. The failure rate for these
090   LLMs reaches as high as 96.8%. To further challenge the resilience of proprietary LLMs, we intro-
091   duce more complex EnergyAlign instructions with various jailbreak techniques, which successfully
092   bypass the safety measures of GPT-3.5 in 63.2% of cases. Finally, we find that existing defense tech-
093   niques achieve unsatisfactory performance against energy threats, underscoring the need for further
094

095
096   In summary, this study introduces several key contributions to lay the foundation for future research
097   on energy-oriented threats to LLMs: (1) Novel EnergyAlign dataset: We introduce EnergyAlign,
098   the first dataset designed to study and address energy-oriented malicious instructions. (2) Compre-
099   hensive evaluation of state-of-the-art LLMs: We provide valuable insights through a comprehen-
100
      sive assessment of both proprietary and open-source LLMs. (3) Extensive benchmark of jailbreak
      and defense strategies: We explore jailbreak and defense strategies against energy threats, offering
101
      a solid foundation for future work in this domain.

104   2   BACKGROUND
105
106   Alignment and red-teaming: The integration of LLMs into real-world applications has brought
107   up concerns over their misuse and overall safety (Bender et al., 2021; Weidinger et al., 2022;
      Bommasani et al., 2021). To mitigate these risks, various techniques such as Supervised Fine-


                                                                                2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Tuning (Zheng et al., 2023; Ouyang et al., 2022) and Reinforcement Learning from Human/AI
109   Feedback (Bai et al., 2022a;b) are employed to mitigate undesired responses to adversarial prompts.
110   Meanwhile, red-teaming and jailbreaking efforts aims to discover inputs that elicit undesired re-
111   sponses from a target LLM (Perez et al., 2022; Ganguli et al., 2022) or to augment malicious queries
112   to bypass the safety measures of aligned LLMs (Zou et al., 2023; Chao et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023;
113   Shen et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b; Lapid et al., 2023). Extensive research has studied issues like
114   misinformation (Yin et al., 2023; Vu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Fernandes et al., 2023), toxic-
115   ity (Rao et al., 2024; Casper et al., 2023), bias (Gallegos et al., 2023; Xue et al., 2023; Simmons,
116   2023), and privacy leakage (Staab et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2023b) within LLMs.
117
      In contrast to prior work, we focus on energy-oriented malicious instructions, which present a new
      threat to the reliability of LLM-powered applications. Our study also evaluates jailbreak and defense
118
      strategies in energy-oriented contexts, offering novel insights into this emerging challenge.
119
120   Energy-oriented threats: Like other systems, applications powered by neural models are vulner-
121   able to Denial-of-Service (DoS) attacks (OpenAI, 2023b). Beyond conventional DoS techniques,
122   some methods are designed specifically to exploit neural models, often by adding adversarial per-
123
      turbations to benign inputs, thus increasing the computational cost of task-specific models (Hong
      et al., 2021; Krithivasan et al., 2022; Tao et al., 2024). These attacks, including those on multi-exit
124
      classification models and small generative models, typically increase the number of computed lay-
125
      ers (Zhang et al., 2023; Coalson et al., 2023) or the length of output sequences (Li et al., 2023b; Chen
126   et al., 2022). Recent research has extended energy attacks to LLMs (Feng et al., 2024; Geiping et al.,
127   2024) and multimodal LLMs (Gao et al., 2024), optimizing adversarial perturbations using white-
128   box attack algorithms. In contrast, we introduce the first dataset of natural malicious instructions,
129   specifically designed to evaluate black-box LLMs in realistic operational settings. Specifically, our
130   approach harms system performance and security with lengthy output response. Several studies
131   support our motivation by highlighting the impact of output length on system performance, such as
132   throughput, energy consumption, and workload during LLM inference (Samsi et al., 2023; Stojkovic
133   et al., 2024; Wilkins et al., 2024).2 Yet, they have not addressed the deliberate manipulation of out-
134
      put length, underscoring the unique contribution of our EnergyAlign dataset. Our study fills this
      gap by being the first to conduct a comprehensive evaluation of various proprietary and open-source
135
      LLMs, providing a holistic view of their resilience and vulnerabilities to energy-oriented threats.

140   Instruction categories: Our goal is to design instructions that present impractical and meaning-
141   less tasks for LLMs due to their high computational demands or the generation of outputs with
142   uninformative contents. To this end, we identify 8 distinct categories of impractical instructions that
143
      may elicit extremely lengthy responses from LLMs, testing their capability to recognize and manage
      novel energy-oriented threats. Representative examples of each category are shown in Tab. 1.
144
145   • Repeat: The model is prompted to duplicate a short text multiple times, creating a voluminous
146     output. Possible objects for repetition include words, lyrics, etc.
147   • Random: This category requires the model to generate a substantial quantity of random entities,
148     such as IDs or hashtags.
149
      • Recitation: The model is asked to recite lengthy texts, like constants or literature.
      • Enumeration: This category involves listing a detailed and extensive collection of items, such as
150
        synonyms or names.
151
      • Expansion: The model is asked to expand a concise representation into a detailed, extensive for-
152     mat, such as converting a fraction to a decimal or a base 36 value to binary form.
153   • Recursion: This category requires the model to output a long sequence defined by recurrence
154     relation, like geometric progression.
155   • Nest: The model is asked to outline deeply nested structures, such as formulas or trees.
156   • Count: The model is required to count over a long series, involving objects like numbers or
157     timestamps.
158   These categories vary significantly in output characteristics such as repetitiveness, randomness, pres-
159   ence or absence of information, etc. The level of expertise required to address these tasks also differs
160   widely: some can be solved using simple programming techniques, while others demand complex

188   Instruction generation: Following previous works (Shaikh et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024c), we
189   use GPT-4 to generate malicious instructions. The process begins with the manual creation of a seed
190
      set of 20 instructions for each category, which serves as the initial few-shot examples for GPT-4.
      As new instructions are generated, these initial examples, along with newly produced samples, are
191
      used as few-shot demonstrations to enhance the diversity of the instructions. During the generation
192
      process, we observe that GPT-4 may produce outputs of low quality or misaligned with the intended
193   category. Therefore, we manually exclude data samples that are overly similar or do not align with
194   the category definitions after each generation round. We also filter out any samples that could be
195   considered meaningful or practical, as our dataset focuses solely on meaningless and impractical
196   tasks. The manual filtering is conducted by three experts, with decisions made by majority vote.
197
198   Instruction filtering: A key factor in deciding whether an input instruction should be refused
199   is the length of the requested output sequence. For instance, ”repeat “A” for 1000 times” might be
200   deemed impractical, whereas a repetition of 10 times could be considered feasible for LLMs. Instead
201   of defining a new refusal threshold, we opt to use the established criteria of advanced proprietary
202   LLMs. Following previous safety alignment literature (Wang et al., 2024c; Huang et al., 2024),
203   we keep the data points refused by at least one of the three proprietary LLMs: GPT-4, GPT-3.5,
204
      and Claude-2.1 as a proxy of their well-established safety standards. This filtering process helps
      differentiate malicious instructions from normal user queries that naturally produce lengthy outputs,
205
      like story generation. Finally, in line with the scale of popular malicious instruction datasets (Röttger
206
      et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024; Shaikh et al., 2023), we maintain a total of 400
207   malicious instructions, with 50 per category.

242   Victim models: We examine 24 open-source LLMs from 11 cutting-edge LLM series, including
243   Llama (Touvron et al., 2023), TÜLU (Ivison et al., 2023), Vicuna (Zheng et al., 2023), Orca (Mitra

      sampling to ensure reproducibility.

      Evaluation metrics: We use attack success rate (ASR) as our evaluation metric. Ideally, victim
248
      LLMs should refuse unreasonable requests and provide valid justifications. Accordingly, we define
249
      two types of ASR metrics. The first, ASRl , is based on output token length to assess the energy-
250   oriented robustness of victim models. As shown in Fig. 2, normal user queries can be handled within
251   1000 tokens, while EnergyAlign queries lead to responses exceeding 1000 tokens. Given this, we
252   consider prompts that elicit more than 1000 output tokens as successful ones, as they robustly capture
253   instances leading to excessive generation and effectively reflect the model’s vulnerability to energy
254   threats. The second, ASRh , evaluates the helpfulness of the output text. Previous works (Liu et al.,
255   2023; Bai et al., 2023b) have shown that GPT-4 achieves exceptionally high agreement with human
256   judgment on text evaluation. Therefore, we utilize GPT-4 to assign binary helpfulness labels to the
257   responses with the evaluation prompt listed in Appx. G. The ASRh represents the ratio of samples
258
      where the LLM refuses the instruction but provides wrong justifications. Finally, we define a joint
      ASRj , which shows the ratio of samples that meet either ASRl or ASRh criteria. We assess the
259
      reliability of both ASRl and ASRh in Appx. B.

404

405

407   tive than those with high-resource languages (Deng et al., 2024; Yong et al., 2023). This discrepancy
408   arises from the distinct working mechanisms underlying translation attacks in scenarios of toxicity
409   versus energy. In the context of toxicity, translation attacks leverage the lack of alignment data for
410   low-resource languages. Conversely, in energy scenarios, the success hinges on the intrinsic differ-
411   ences in token counts when expressing the same sentence in two different languages. To elucidate
412   this observation, we report two variants of translation attacks in Tab. 4. For translation, we calculate
413   the ASRl using the output sequence directly from the LLM. For translation∗ , we first convert the
414
      output sequence to English before computing the ASRl based on this translated English sequence.
      Converting the output to English dramatically lowers the ASRl to approximately 0, indicating that
415
      most malicious instructions are effectively defended by GPT-4. Given GPT-4’s superior multilin-
416
      gual capabilities, it can generate detailed explanations in the target language, resulting in lengthy
417   responses. In contrast, the other two LLMs, with their limited multilingual proficiency, often fail
418   to comprehend the instructions, typically yielding brief responses such as ”I cannot complete this
419   task.” Consequently, translation attacks lead to lower ASRl . Overall, translation attacks pose unique
420   threats to advanced multilingual LLMs.

      In Sec. 4, we find that even extensively aligned open-source LLMs remain vulnerable to the vanilla
435   EnergyAlign instructions. While proprietary LLMs show better resistance to the energy threat, the
436   proprietary nature of these models means their mitigation techniques remain undisclosed, leading to
437   a knowledge gap for the open-source community. Therefore, in this section, we explore strategies
438   to improve LLM resilience with defense strategies and direct energy-oriented alignment.
439
440   Experiment setup: We benchmark a diverse range of widely recognized jailbreak defense tech-
441   niques with distinct working mechanisms on EnergyAlign. According to recent literature (Yi et al.,
442   2024; Dong et al., 2024), defense methods can be categorized into input/output filtering, infer-
443   ence guidance, SFT, and RLHF. For input/output filtering, we evaluate SmoothLLM (Robey et al.,
444   2023), LLaMAguard (Inan et al., 2023), PPL (Alon & Kamfonas, 2023), ReTokenization, and Para-
445
      phrase (Jain et al., 2023). For inference guidance, we evaluate Self-Reminder (Xie et al., 2023),
      ICD (Wei et al., 2023b), SelfDefend (Wang et al., 2024b), IntentionAnalysis (Zhang et al., 2024),
446
      and Self-Examination (Phute et al., 2024). We also examine the possibility of augmenting the ro-
447
      bustness of LLMs by direct energy-oriented alignment on EnergyAlign. We consider two evaluation
448   scenarios: 5-fold cross-validation and transfer evaluation. We apply these defense strategies on
449   3 open-source LLMs with different sizes and capabilities: Vicuna-7B-v1.5, Vicuna-13B-v1.5, and
450   Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2. Due to space limitations, we list the transfer evaluation and Vicuna-13B
451   results in the Appx. C, illustrating improved robustness against unseen malicious instructions.
452
453   Alignment data construction: Following previous works (Peng et al., 2023), we use GPT-4’s
454   response to construct the training set from EnergyAlign. After inspecting the response pattern of
455   GPT-4 to these malicious instructions, we observe that GPT-4’s responses mainly follow three dis-
456   tinct patterns: (1) Refusal with explanation: GPT-4 refuses the request and elucidates the reasons for
457   its inability to fulfill the task; (2) Refusal with solution: GPT-4 rejects the request but suggests an
458
      alternative, more efficient method to achieve the goal, such as providing python code or recommend-
      ing a web search; (3) Refusal with demonstration: GPT-4 denies the request but offers preliminary
459
      examples for illustrative purposes. Therefore, we modify the system prompt to explicitly notify
460
      GPT-4 of the malicious nature of the incoming requests. Subsequently, we prompt GPT-4 to gener-
461   ate its response according to these three identified patterns, which formulate three separate training
462   datasets, each characterized by a different style of response. Finally, we are able to train LLMs with
463   different styles on the obtained training data. Due to space limitations, detailed system prompts and
464   data statistics are listed in Appx. I.
465
466   Experiment results: The results are summarized in Tab. 5. Despite the strong performance of
467   existing defense techniques in toxicity scenarios, they are usually unsuitable for defending novel
468   energy-oriented threats. All output filtering methods are ineffective against energy threats since
469   they do not prevent the generation of lengthy outputs, merely blocking them post-factum. Since
470   the adversary’s goal of generating lengthy outputs has been reached, blocking the output would
471
      be meaningless. For both input filtering and inference guidance methods, most of them exhibit
      unsatisfactory effectiveness against energy threats. Notably, methods requiring multiple inference
472
      paths, e.g., SmoothLLM, not only fail to filter the malicious instructions with a high ASRl around
473
      100 but also significantly increase computational costs. For example, deploying SmoothLLM leads
474   to over 6 times more inference time for Vicuna-7B due to multiple inference paths, which extensively
475   intensifies the energy threat. Additionally, the results reveal significant performance variations for
476   the same defense methods across different models, highlighting their sensitivity to the choice of
477   backbone LLM.
478
      On the contrary, simple instruction tuning on EnergyAlign can effectively defend the malicious
479
      instructions, which significantly outperforms existing methods on both ASRl and ASRh across
480   different LLMs. Yet, these LLMs, particularly those with lesser capabilities like Vicuna-7B, still
481   occasionally provide incorrect justifications for their refusals. This contributes to a relatively high
482   ASRh , with the incorrect rationale largely stemming from a deficiency in knowledge regarding the
483   requested sequence. In addition, we observe a lower ASR when models are finetuned to follow the
484   “Refusal with explanation” pattern compared to the other two patterns. The discrepancy is reason-
485   able, as giving efficient solutions or starting examples demands a more profound comprehension
      of the request. Therefore, the performance is largely limited by the foundational capabilities of

487   lored to combat energy threats. Additionally, the proposed EnergyAlign demonstrates high efficacy

533
      In this work, we introduce EnergyAlign, the first dataset tailored to explore the domain of energy-
534   oriented malicious instructions targeting LLMs. Through extensive experiments on a comprehensive
535   selection of both open-source and proprietary LLMs, we validate the presence of energy-oriented
536   threats, a novel challenge of LLM deployment. Our findings not only underscore the susceptibility
537   of LLMs to such malicious instructions but also establish an effective baseline for aligning LLMs
538   against these instructions. Future research directions include curating a more diverse dataset, devel-
539   oping more sophisticated defense methods, extending to multi-modal scenarios, and investigating
      potential over-alignment issue (Röttger et al., 2023; Varshney et al., 2023).

951
      include input prompt length and output response length (Li et al., 2023c;a; Liu et al., 2024c). In this
952
      section, we in-depth study the impact of input/output length on model efficiency which is typically
953   measured by inference time and energy consumption You et al. (2023). Specifically, we measure
954   the end-to-end inference time for three proprietary large language models (LLMs), alongside both
955   the end-to-end inference time and GPU energy consumption for three open-source LLMs of varying
956   sizes.3 For these open-source LLMs, end-to-end inference time and GPU energy consumption are
957   assessed using Zeus (You et al., 2023), with all experiments conducted on NVIDIA A100 GPUs.
958   Fig. 3 shows the correlations between input/output token length and inference time, while Fig. 4
959   visualizes the correlations between input/output token length and consumed energy.
960   Across all LLMs examined, we observe an approximated positive linear correlation linking infer-
961   ence time and energy consumption to the input/output token length. In addition, the inference time
962   of proprietary LLMs exhibits considerable variability, which may be attributed to unstable network
963   connections. Notably, we observe that the input query length has very limited impact on the model
964   efficiency compared to the output response length. Regarding inference time, encoding 1000 input

      around 620 J, while generating 1000 output tokens takes 52600 J. The large gap between the impact
      of input and output length on the model efficiency shows that the most effective way to reduce model

      efficiency and cause system overload is maximizing output response length. Under a DoS attack sce-

1012   We define two evaluation metrics: ASRl and ASRh to measure the energy robustness of LLMs. In

1015   Length metric: We define ASRl with a threshold 1000 to measure whether the examined LLM
1016   follows the malicious instruction. Considering that a longer response indicates a more successful
1017

       through a comprehensive analysis, ensuring that it appropriately reflects the behavior of LLMs in
1018

1019

1022   extend far beyond this limit. Secondly, our collected reference refusal answers never exceeded 623
1023   tokens, underscoring the sufficiency of the 1000-token threshold for distinguishing between compli-
1024   ance and refusal. Thirdly, for the same instruction, the length of refusal responses can vary, making
1025   it impractical to set a universally precise threshold. Thus, using 1000 tokens as a threshold serves
       as an effective, generalized indicator. Lastly, our analysis shows a strong correlation (0.99) between

1516        [Explanation]       I’m sorry, but I’m unable to fulfill this request. Writing out the full numerical value of 1.879789 ×
                                101000 without using scientific notation would result in a number that is impractically long to display
1517                            or read in this format. To give you an idea, this number would have 1,001 digits, starting with 1879789
1518                            followed by 994 zeros.
                                There are several reasons why this is impractical:
1519                            1. **Length and Readability**: A number with over a thousand digits would be extremely difficult
1520                            to read and comprehend. In any practical scenario, such as in mathematics, science, or engineering,
                                numbers of this magnitude are always expressed in scientific notation for clarity and simplicity.
1521                            2. **Display Limitations**: Most platforms, including this one, have limitations on the number of char-
1522                            acters that can be included in a single response. Writing out a number with 1,001 digits would likely
                                exceed these limits, making it impossible to display the full value as requested.
