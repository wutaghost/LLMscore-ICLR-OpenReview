009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite the promising results of large multimodal models (LMMs) in various
012            vision-language tasks, recent benchmarks reveal that these models can struggle
013            with low-level chart perception tasks that require precision. However, since ex-
014            isting benchmarks primarily focus on end tasks that evaluate models’ knowledge
015            and reasoning abilities all together, they provide limited fine-grained insights into
016            how the models’ perception abilities affect their performance in chart tasks. To ad-
017            dress this gap, we leverage the theory of graphical perception, an approach used
018
               to study how humans decode visual information encoded on charts and graphs, to
               develop an evaluation framework for analyzing gaps in LLMs’ perception abili-
019
               ties in charts. With automated task generation and response evaluation designs,
020
               our framework enables comprehensive and controlled testing of LMMs’ graphical
021            perception across diverse chart types, visual elements, and task types. We apply
022            our framework to evaluate the perception capabilities of state-of-the-art LMMs at
023            three granularity levels (chart, visual element, and pixel). Our findings underscore
024            several critical limitations of current state-of-the-art LMMs, including GPT-4o:
025            their inability to (1) generalize across chart types, (2) understand fundamental vi-
026            sual elements, and (3) cross reference values within a chart. These insights provide
027            guidance for future improvements in perception abilities of LMMs. The evalua-
028            tion framework and labeled data will be publicly available upon acceptance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large multimodal models (LMMs; OpenAI (2024); Gemini Team (2023)) have shown impressive
      results in a range of visual-language tasks (Yue et al., 2024a; Lu et al., 2024), including complex
034
      knowledge- and reasoning-intensive tasks over infographics in scientific documents. However, re-
035
      cent studies (Wang et al., 2024), along with our findings, have revealed limitations in their ability
036   to perform low-level perception tasks, such as retrieving specific values or finding the extremum in
037   given charts, as evidenced by Figure 1 which shows an example used in our framework.
038
039
      Such an inconsistency indicates that existing LMMs may have potential limitations in their percep-
      tion abilities. Yet, there is a lack of systematic exploration of these limitations. Existing benchmarks
040
      primarily focus on overall task performance, often combining perception, knowledge, and high-level
041
      reasoning into a single accuracy score. This metric serves as an indirect proxy for measuring LMMs’
042   perception of charts—a high score may suggest good perception abilities, but poor performance
043   makes it unclear whether the failure stems from perception errors, lack of knowledge, reasoning
044   flaws, or a combination of these factors. Additionally, models may not even need to perceive and
045   reason about charts to answer questions: as reported in recent studies, models can generate correct
046   answers without the visual input (Yue et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024a). Finally, the way current
047   models perceive charts—especially their understanding of fundamental visual elements—remains
048   unexplored in existing evaluations. Thus, it is desirable to systematically study models’ perception
049   capabilities to understand factors limiting their performance in low-level chart perception tasks.
050   In this paper, we leverage graphical perception (Cleveland & McGill, 1984), a theory originally
051   developed to study human interpretation of visual data in charts and graphs, to evaluate models’ per-
052   ception capabilities. For example, prior studies on human graphical perception show that, because
053   humans can better perceive the length of lines than area sizes when comparing values, we can more
      efficiently and accurately read bar charts than pie charts to answer questions about calculating the

080   RQ1: Can SOTA LMMs Generalize Across Diverse Chart Types? Our analysis reveals that
081   LMMs exhibit significant performance fluctuations depending on the chart type and rely heavily on

092
      data points, resulting in significant errors due to error propagation.

127   The datasets we use include three major types of data attributes: (1) Nominal Attributes: Categorical
128   variables that represent distinct labels without an inherent order (e.g., country, movie genres). (2)
129   Ordinal Attributes: Variables that have a meaningful order or ranking but no fixed intervals between
130   values (e.g., movie ratings, years). (3) Numerical Attributes: Continuous variables that allow for
131
      the calculation of differences and other mathematical operations (e.g., exhibition space in Figure 2).

132
      with a numerical attribute or an ordinal attribute paired with a numerical attribute). In addition, the
133
      number of data points is limited to 5. These constraints allow us to evaluate the models’ graphical
134   perception capabilities without overwhelming them with complex, multidimensional data.

      2.3     M ODEL S ELECTION
163
164   Given the extensive variety of LMMs with different vision and language backbones, a comprehen-
165   sive evaluation and fair comparison of all models may not be feasible. Therefore, we focus on four
166   categories of models: proprietary, open-source, lightweight, and chart-specialized LMMs. We se-
167   lect the most representative model from each category for evaluation, based on the averaged results
168   reported on prior chart-included benchmarks (Wang et al., 2024; Yue et al., 2024a; Lu et al., 2024).
169   • GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), the strongest proprietary general-purpose model, represents the SOTA
170     in LMMs. Benchmarking GPT-4o allows us to evaluate the performance of the latest model in
171     chart tasks, providing a reference point for comparison with other models in this domain.
172   • InternVL2 (Chen et al., 2024b) is one of the best open-source general-purpose LMMs. It is built
173     upon Llama3.1 (Dubey et al., 2024) and has a total of 76B parameters. Evaluating InternVL2 can
174     show the gap between open-source models and GPT-4o in graphical perception.
175   • Phi-3.5-Vision (Abdin et al., 2024) is selected as a strong lightweight general-purpose LMM,
176     with only 4.2B parameters. With Phi-3.5, we can evaluate whether models with smaller vision
177     backbones can still maintain decent levels of graphical perception.
178   • ChartAssistant (Meng et al., 2024) is the best chart-specialist model. It is continually trained with
179     the LLaVa-13B (Liu et al., 2023) on a massive amount of chart datasets, including the original
180     VisText dataset. With this specialist model, we can measure the benefits of in-domain training in
181     enhancing perception and generalization abilities.

      2.4     E VALUATION
184
185   We employ GPT-4o as an automated text evaluator, which is particularly useful when models being
186   evaluated output varied answer formats, such as Chain-of-Thought reasoning format (Wei et al.,
187   2022), or when dealing with open-ended tasks. GPT-4o evaluates responses by comparing the textual
188   responses of models against the predefined answer, which is generated automatically by GPT-4o
189   based on textual representation of the data and chart program that do not need visual perception. The
190   evaluation process is guided by a detailed rubric designed for different task types. For example, in
191   Retrieve Value tasks, answers are considered accurate if they are within a 5% margin of the correct
192   value. For order-based tasks, such as ranking items, the model must return the exact sequence
193
      expected, while other list-based tasks do not require specific ordering. Evaluation outcomes are
      categorized into accurate, fair, skipped, inaccurate, and n/a. See Appendix B for details.
194
195   To calibrate GPT-4o’s evaluation process, we use a 10-shot demonstration (Brown et al., 2020) that
196   includes examples of textual data tables, tasks, reference answers, model responses, and expected
197   evaluations. This calibration helps ensure consistency and accuracy in evaluation. We validate the
198   reliability of GPT-4o as an evaluator by manually checking 200 evaluation results of GPT-4o and
199
      InternVL2, achieving an accuracy rate of 99.5% without noticeable bias across different models.
      This establishes a reliable foundation for our evaluation framework.

243   3.1   A NALYSIS ON C HARTS WITH N UMERICAL A NNOTATIONS
244
245   Figure 3 presents the performance of models on different types of charts with numerical annotations
246   given the same tasks, from which we can make the following observations:
247   (1) A significant performance gap exists for the same model when interpreting different types of
248   charts containing the same information, suggesting that these models lack generalization across
249   chart types. For example, GPT-4o consistently performs the best overall, yet shows a clear prefer-
250   ence for Bar (Anno) over Pie charts (85.0% vs. 69.1%). This indicates that while GPT-4o excels
251   in understanding some chart types, it still relies on specific visual structures to achieve its highest
252   performance. Meanwhile, InternVL2 and Phi-3.5, the open-source general-purpose models, per-
253   form best when presented with table images. This observation suggests that these models might
254
      be specifically optimized for structured data. However, the performance gaps of these models be-
      tween different chart types are even larger, with up to 21.7% for InternVL2 and 28.9% for Phi-3.5,
255
      highlighting a stronger dependency on specific visual structures.
256
257   (2) Despite being trained on an extensive range of chart-related datasets and tested on simplified
258   versions of its training data, ChartAssistant underperforms general-purpose models and struggles to
259   generalize effectively across different chart types. Its relatively better performance on Bar (Anno)
260
      can be attributed to the fact that bar charts make up 44.3% of its training data. This raises concerns
      about the effectiveness of chart-specific training for generalization.
261
262   (3) Models demonstrate significant performance variations across the ten tasks (T1 to T10), and
263   task complexity amplifies the inconsistencies across chart types. For example, GPT-4o performs
264   relatively consistently across all tasks when given Bar (Anno) charts. However, when interpreting
265   Pie charts, its performance varies dramatically, with a gap of up to 48% between simpler tasks like
266
      Determine Range (T4, 93%) and more complex tasks like Order (T8, 46.3%). A similar trend can
      also be observed in the other two general-purpose open-source models.
267
268   These observations highlight the importance of improving graphical perception across a broader
269   range of chart types to enhance LMMs’ generalization in real-world applications where diverse
      charts appear in various forms. Please refer to Appendix D for more detailed results.

      Table 3 presents the performance of models when interpreting charts rendered with single or multiple
325   basic visual elements. We make three observations:
326
327   (1) LMMs suffer from basic visual element understanding. Across the board, models show relatively
328
      poor performance when interpreting charts that rely on a single visual element, such as length,
      color, or size. For example, GPT-4o achieves only 17.6% accuracy on charts using length alone,
329
      despite its otherwise strong performance. This indicates a fundamental challenge for LMMs in
330
      extracting quantitative values from basic visual elements, potentially limiting their abilities when
331   comprehending complex charts where such basic visual elements are used.
332
333   (2) Surprisingly, the addition of redundant visual elements often hurt model performance. For ex-
334
      ample, while using position only results in decent performance (e.g., GPT-4o scores with 41.2%
      accuracy), rendering values via size at the same time ( , ⋆) hurts the performance dramatically
335
      across all models. Although the size can be more straightforward than position for tasks like order-
336
      ing, LMMs clearly fail to leverage the advantages of various visual elements in most of the times.
337   This suggests that the presence of multiple visual elements may overwhelm the models’ capacity to
338   prioritize relevant visual cues, leading to confusion and misinterpretation of the data.
339
340
      (3) LMMs often fail to generalize effectively across charts that use similar visual elements. For
      instance, models show strong performance on bar charts that combine position, length, and size (↔,
341
        , ⋆), but struggle with similar charts that only use position and length (↔, ⋆). This suggests that
342
      models excel only with specific combinations of visual elements and lack the robustness needed to
343   transfer this understanding to simpler or slightly altered visualizations.
344
345   Overall, these results demonstrate that current LMMs merely follow specific and superficial percep-

      7     R ELATED W ORK
509
510
      Graphical Perception Cleveland & McGill (1984) introduce graphical perception as the visual
      interpretation of data through basic visual elements, such as position, length, area, and color. Ex-
511
      tensive research has since expanded on their work, evaluating human perception across diverse data
512
      types (Heer et al., 2009; Javed et al., 2010; Whitlock et al., 2020; Borkin et al., 2013), increas-
513   ingly complex tasks (Saket et al., 2019; Xiong et al., 2023; Bearfield et al., 2024), and complex
514   charts (Heer & Bostock, 2010). In the context of neural networks, prior work tests the graphical per-
515   ception abilities of vision-only models (Haehn et al., 2019) following a similar protocol. However,
516   evaluating and understanding the graphical perception of LMMs remains under-explored.
517
518   Large Multimodal Models and Their Benchmarks Multimodal, especially vision-and-
519
      language, modeling has evolved significantly, beginning with early models (Tan & Bansal, 2019;
      Chen et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2019) that inject vision features into language understanding models,
520
      to those using contrastive learning for cross-modality representation (Radford et al., 2021; Yu et al.,
521
      2022; Zhang et al., 2024), and to the recent unified LMM frameworks (OpenAI, 2024; 2023; Gem-
522   ini Team, 2023; 2024; Liu et al., 2024a; Cai et al., 2024) for various downstream tasks. Graphical
523   perception is widely yet implicitly considered in benchmarks for evaluating these LMMs, including
524   task-specific ones (Masry et al., 2022; Mathew et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024) and the recent holistic
525   ones (Yue et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2024b; Lu et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023). Despite its ubiquity, all ex-
526   isting benchmarks assess graphical perception indirectly, often evaluating it alongside other abilities
527   like reasoning or by introducing increasingly complex charts. Our work, instead, uniquely isolates
528   and directly evaluates the graphical perception abilities of LMMs in a comprehensive fashion.
529
530
      8     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      This work introduces a comprehensive and configurable evaluation framework for automatically
533
      measuring the graphical perception abilities of LMMs, offering fine-grained insights into current
534   state-of-the-art LMMs. Our findings reveal that these models struggle to generalize across diverse
535   chart types, understand fundamental visual elements, and cross reference values within charts. In
536   addition, our framework serves as a flexible test suite that can be easily adapted to support the
537   development of future LMMs. Future work may leverage this framework to synthesize diverse data
538   for training and testing on a wider range of tasks, potentially enabling improved graphical perception
539   and general low-level visual reasoning. We hope these findings and the framework can guide the
      development of LMMs with more generalizable perception abilities in the future.

      A    TASK G ENERATION
811

815       Task Generation Prompt (with text input only)
816
817       You are a teacher to provide problems for students to solve. The problems are about understanding
818       data and visualizations. We will provide you with an input data, a Vega-Lite program, and a task
          type that the understanding task should base on. You will need to design a chart understanding task
819
          contextualized in the given data and chart.
820
821       Design the task based off one of the following idioms:
822       • Retrieve Value. For this task, ask students to identify values of attributes for given data points. For
823       example, what is the value of horsepower for Mazda CX50?
824       • Find Extremum: For given concrete conditions on data attribute values, ask students to find data
          points satisfying those conditions. For example, which car types have the most city miles per gallon?
825
          • Find Anomalies: ask students to identify any anomalies within a given set of data points with respect
826       to a given relationship or expectation. For example, which car types have abnormally low MPG?
827       • Determine Range: For a given set of data points and an attribute of interest, ask students to find the
828       span of values within the set. For example, what is the range of car prices?
829       • Find Correlation: for a given set of two data attributes, ask students to determine if there is a corre-
          lation between them. For example, is there a strong correlation between car price and MPG?
830
          • Compute Derived Value: for a given set of data points, ask students to compute an aggregate value
831       of those data points. For example, what is the sum of the budget for the action and the sci-fi movies?
832       • Filter: For given concrete conditions on data attribute values, ask students to find data points satisfy-
833       ing those conditions. For example, which car types have miles per gallon ranging from 20 to 40?
834       • Order: For a given set of data points, ask students to rank them according to a specific ordinal metric.
          For example, list the car types based on their MPG from low to high.
835
          • Find Clusters: for a given set of data points, ask students to count the number of groups of similar
836       data attribute values. For example, how many different car brands are shown in the chart below?
837       • Characterize Distribution: for a given set of data points, ask students to identify the distribution of
838       that attribute’s values over the set. For example, what percentage of the cars with MPG higher than
839       30?
840
          You need to match the following requirements:
841
            1. The task should be reasonable, and it should not exceed one sentence, and it should be contexual-
842
          ized in the given data.
843         2. The task should be achievable by reading the visualization without referring other tools.
844         3. The task should be self-contained with the given dataset, it should not require student to look up
845       external information.
846         4. Each task should have a standard answer, avoid generating questions like “compare two values of
          your choice.”
847
            5. Try not to repeat the verb for each task to maximize diversity.
848
849       Create a [Task] based off the [Data Summary] and [VegaLite Script] provided.
850       The response should be in a json format:
851       {"reason":...,"tasks":[{"description":...,"type":...},...]},               including
852       how you design the task and the actual task description.
          Generate 10 tasks at once.
853
854
          For example:
855
          [Data Summary]
856
857       |Date      |Location
          0|5/12/2009|Houston, TX
858

859       2|7/11/2009|Indianapolis, IN
860       3|11/14/2009|Kansas City, MO|MO
861       4|3/12/2010|Chicago, IL|IL
862       ...

          Evaluation Prompt (with text input only)
867
868       You are a teacher to grade students’ answers. We will provide you a dataset, a list of tasks and student
869       answers. Your goal is to use the dataset to evaluate if the student’s answer is correct. In order to form
870       a good judgement, you should first use the dataset to derive your answer, and then compare it with the
871       students asnwer.
872
          When you generate the referenece answer:
873
          * If the task asks for a value, provide the value directly.
874       * If the task asks for trend or correlation, answer it with one of “increasing”, “decreasing” if the general
875       trend point to the direcrtion, otherwise provide ”unclear”.
876       * Provide a brief reasoning of how you come up with your answer in “reasoning” part.
877       * If you cannot answer a question, provide “I don’t know” as the answer, try not to provide a wrong
          answer.
878
879
          When evaluating student’s answer:
880       The student answer correctness should include the grading results of the student’s answer and must be
881       one of the following options:
882         - correct
883         - fair (somewhat close but not precise)
            - incorrect
884
            - skipped (if the student skipped the answer)
885         - n/a (if the task does not make sense or is not answerable with the given dataset)
886
887       Note that if the student’s answer (value) is an approximation within 5% of your reference answer, it is
888       considered as correct. If is is an approximation within 20% of your reference answer, it is fair.
889       For order-based tasks, such as ranking items, the student answer must match the expected orders you
          found. However, for list-based tasks where order is not important, the specific sequence does not need
890
          to match as long as all relevant items are included.
891
892       Grade student questions based on [Data], [Tasks & Student Answers].
893       The output json should have the format:
894       [{“reasoning”: ...,
895          “reference answer”: ...,
             “comparison with student answer”: ...,
896
             “student answer correctness”: ...},
897       ...]
