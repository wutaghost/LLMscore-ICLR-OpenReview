009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Feature-based methods are commonly used to explain model predictions, but these
012            methods often implicitly assume that interpretable features are readily available.
013            However, this is often not the case for high-dimensional data, and it can be hard even
014            for domain experts to mathematically specify which features are important. Can we
015            instead automatically extract collections or groups of features that are aligned with
016
               expert knowledge? To address this gap, we present FIX (Features Interpretable to
               eXperts), a benchmark for measuring how well a collection of features aligns with
017
               expert knowledge. In collaboration with domain experts, we propose FIXScore, a
018
               unified expert alignment measure applicable to diverse real-world settings across
019            cosmology, psychology, and medicine domains in vision, language and time series
020            data modalities. With FIXScore, we find that popular feature-based explanation
021            methods have poor alignment with expert-specified knowledge, highlighting the
022            need for new methods that can better identify features interpretable to experts.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Machine learning is increasingly used in domains like healthcare (Tjoa & Guan, 2019), law (Atkin-
      son et al., 2020), governance (Meijer & Wessels, 2019), science (de la Torre-López et al., 2023),
028
      education (Holstein et al., 2018) and finance (Modarres et al., 2018). However, modern models
029
      are often black-box, which makes it hard for practitioners to understand their decision-making and
030   safely use model outputs (Rai, 2019). For example, surgeons are concerned that blind trust in model
031   predictions will lead to poorer patient outcomes (Hameed et al., 2023); in law, there are known
032   instances of wrongful incarcerations due to over-reliance on faulty model predictions (Zeng et al.,
033   2016; Wexler, 2017). Although such models have promising applications, their opaque nature is a
034   liability in domains where transparency is crucial (Jacovi et al., 2021; Hong et al., 2020).
035
      To address the pertinent need for transparency and explainability of their decision-making, the
036   interpretability of machine learning models has emerged as a central focus of recent research (Arrieta
037   et al., 2019; Saeed & Omlin, 2023; Räuker et al., 2023). A popular and well-studied class of
038   interpretability methods is known as feature attributions (Ribeiro et al., 2016; Lundberg & Lee,
039   2017; Sundararajan et al., 2017). Given a model and an input, a feature attribution method assigns
040   scores to input features that reflect their respective importance toward the model’s prediction. A key
041   limitation, however, is that the attribution scores are only as interpretable as the underlying features
042   themselves (Zytek et al., 2022).
043   Feature-based explanation methods commonly assume that the given features are already interpretable
044   to the user, but this typically only holds for low-dimensional data. With high-dimensional data like
045   images and text documents, where the readily available features are individual pixels or tokens, feature
046   attributions are often difficult to interpret (Nauta et al., 2023). The main problem is that features
047   at the individual pixel or token level are often too granular and thus lack clear semantic meaning
048   in relation to the entire input. Moreover, the important features are also domain-dependent, which
049   means that different attributions are needed for different users. These factors limit the usefulness of
050
      popular feature attribution methods on high-dimensional data.
051   Instead of individual features, people understand high dimensional data in terms of semantic col-
052   lections of low level features, such as regions in an image or phrases in a document. Moreover,
053   for a feature to be useful, it should align with the intuition of domain experts in the field. To this
      end, an interpretable feature for high-dimensional data should have the following properties. First,

      But how can we obtain such features? In practice, it is left to domain experts to identify and provide
083
      such features for individual tasks. Although experts often have a sense of what the expert features
084

085   features can also be expensive and labor-intensive. These challenges raise the critical question:
086
087                 Can we automatically discover expert features that align with domain knowledge?
088
089   To this end, we present the FIX benchmark, a unified evaluation measuring feature interpretability that
090   can capture each individual domain’s expert knowledge. Our goal is to guide the development of new
091
      methods that produce interpretable features by building a unified metric to measure how interpretable
      a proposed feature group is. The FIX datasets (summarized in Figure 1) collectively encompass
092

093

094   2022), chest X-ray classification (Lian et al., 2021), mass maps regression (Kacprzak et al., 2023),

096   et al., 2023a), and emotion classification (Demszky et al., 2020; Havaldar et al., 2023b). The challenge
097   here lies in unifying all 6 different real-world settings and 3 different data modalities into a single
098   framework, which our proposed expert alignment measure FIXS CORE achieves. This allows us to
099   have a benchmark that does not overfit to any particular domain. To our knowledge, while previous
100   work has identified the need for interpretable features (Zytek et al., 2022; Doshi-Velez & Kim,
101   2017), there does not exist yet a benchmark that measures the interpretability of features for real-
102   world experts. The FIX benchmark accomplishes this while also serving as a basis for studying,
103
      constructing, and extracting expert features. In summary, our contributions are as follows:

      2   R ELATED W ORK
117
118
      Interpretability. Interpretability in machine learning is often viewed as a multifaceted concept that
119   encompasses algorithmic transparency (Shin & Park, 2019; Rader et al., 2018; Grimmelikhuijsen,
120   2023), explanation methods (Marcinkevičs & Vogt, 2023; Havaldar et al., 2023c), and visualization
121   techniques (Choo & Liu, 2018; Spinner et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2023), among other aspects. In
122   this work, we focus on feature-level interpretability, a central topic in interpretability research (Hong
123   et al., 2020; Nauta et al., 2023). Feature-based methods are popular because they are believed to
124   offer simple, adaptable, and intuitive settings in which to analyze and develop interpretable machine
125   learning workflows (Molnar et al., 2020). We refer to (Nauta et al., 2023; Dwivedi et al., 2023; Weber
126   et al., 2023) and the references therein for extensive reviews on feature-based explanations.
127   Application-grounded Evaluation. Chaleshtori et al. (2024) extend the work of Doshi-Velez &
128   Kim (2017) to propose a comprehensive taxonomy of evaluating explanations. Notably, this includes
129   application-grounded evaluations, which broadly seek to measure the efficacy of feature-based
130   methods in settings with human users and realistic tasks, such as AI-assisted decision-making.
131   However, the available literature on application-grounded evaluations is sparse: Chaleshtori et al.
132   (2024) reviewed over 50 existing NLP datasets and found that only four were suitable for application-
133
      grounded evaluations (DeYoung et al., 2019; Wadden et al., 2020; Koreeda & Manning, 2021; Malik
      et al., 2021). A principal objective of the FIX benchmark is to provide an application-grounded
134
      evaluation of feature-based explanations in real-world settings.
135
136   Feature Generation. Because high-quality and interpretable features may not always be available,
137   there is interest in automatically generating them by combining low-level features (Nargesian et al.,
138   2017; Erickson et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2023a). Notably, Zhang et al. (2023a) propose a method for
139
      tabular data using the expand-and-reduce framework (Kanter & Veeramachaneni, 2015). However,
      existing generation methods do not necessarily produce interpretable features, and most works focus
140
      on tabular data. The FIX benchmark aims to address these limitations by providing a setting in which
141
      to study and develop methods for interpretable feature generation across diverse problem domains.
142
143   XAI Benchmarks. There exists a suite of benchmarks for explanations that cover the properties
144   of faithfulness (or fidelity) (Zhou et al., 2021; Agarwal et al., 2022), robustness (Alvarez-Melis &
145
      Jaakkola, 2018; Agarwal et al., 2022), simulatability (Mills et al., 2023), fairness (Fel et al., 2021;
      Agarwal et al., 2022), among others. Quantus (Hedström et al., 2023), XAI-Bench (Liu et al., 2021),
146
      OpenXAI (Agarwal et al., 2022), GraphXAI (Agarwal et al., 2023), and ROAR (Hooker et al., 2019)
147
      are notable open-source implementations that evaluate for such properties. CLEVR-XAI (Arras et al.,
148   2022) and Zhang et al. (2023b) provide benchmarks that combine vision and text. ERASER (DeYoung
149   et al., 2019) is a popular NLP benchmark that unifies diverse NLP datasets of human rationales and
150   decisions. In general, however, there is a lack of interpretability benchmarks that evaluate feature
151   interpretability in real-world settings — a gap we aim to address with the FIX benchmark.

      3   E XPERT F EATURE E XTRACTION
154
155
      Feature-based explanation methods require interpretable features to be effective. For example,
156   surgeons communicate safety in surgery with respect to key anatomical structures and organs, which
157   are interpretable features for surgeons (Strasberg & Brunt, 2010; Hashimoto et al., 2019). These
158   interpretable features are a key bridge that can help surgical AI assistants communicate effectively
159   with surgeons. However, ground-truth annotations for such interpretable features are often expensive
160   and hard to obtain, as they typically require trained experts to manually annotate large amounts of
161   data. This bottleneck is not unique to surgery, and such challenges motivate us to study the problem
      of extracting features interpretable to experts, or expert features.

178   Consider a task with inputs from X ⊆ Rd and outputs in Y. In the example of surgery, X may be
179   the set of surgery images, and Y is the target of where it is safe or unsafe to operate. We model a
180   higher-level expert feature of input x ∈ X as a subset of features represented with a binary mask
181   g ∈ {0, 1}d , where gi = 1 if the ith feature is included and 0 otherwise. In surgery, for example,
182   a good mask β is one that accurately selects a key anatomical structure or organ from an input x.
183   The objective of interpretable feature extraction is to find a set of masks Ĝ ⊆ {0, 1}d that effectively
184   approximates the expert features of x. That is, each binary mask ĝ ∈ Ĝ aims to identify some subset
185   of features meaningful to experts.

187   3.1   M EASURING A LIGNMENT OF E XTRACTED F EATURES WITH E XPERT F EATURES
188
189   At the core of FIX is a general framework for measuring the quality of extracted features with respect
190   to expert knowledge. Let Ĝ be a proposed set of expert features for an input x ∈ Rd , and suppose
191   there exists a function E XPERTA LIGN(ĝ, x) ∈ [0, 1] that captures how well a single extracted feature
192   ĝ is expert-interpretable for x. Here, a score of 1 means that a domain expert considers ĝ highly
193   interpretable, whereas a score of 0 means that ĝ is a highly uninterpretable feature. Then, given a set
194   of proposed groups Ĝ and input x, we measure the quality of Ĝ for x as:

199   where let Ĝ[i] = {ĝ ∈ Ĝ : i ∈ ĝ} be the subset Ĝ that cover feature i. Intuitively, FIXS CORE is an

201   all covers Ĝ[i]. This metric has two key strengths:
202
203         1. Duplication Invariance at Optimality. If one extracts perfect expert features (i.e., with
204            an alignment score of 1), the FIXS CORE cannot be increased further by duplicating expert
205            features. This property ensures that the score cannot be trivially inflated with repeats.
206         2. Encourages Diversity of Expert Features. Since the score aggregates a value for each
207            feature from i = 1, . . . , d, adding a new expert feature that does not yet overlap with already
208            extracted features is always beneficial.

      4.1   M ASS M APS DATASET
244
245   Motivation. A major focus of cosmology is the initial state of the universe, which can be characterized
246   by various cosmological parameters such as Ωm , which relates to energy density, and σ8 , which
247   pertains to matter fluctuations (Abbott et al., 2022). These parameters influence what is observable
248   by mass maps, also known as weak lensing maps, which capture the spatial distribution of matter
249   density in the universe. Although mass maps can be obtained through the precise measurement
250
      of galaxies (Jeffrey et al., 2021; Gatti et al., 2021), it is not known how to directly measure Ωm
      and σ8 . This has inspired machine learning efforts to predict the two cosmological parameters
251
      from simulations (Ribli et al., 2019; Matilla et al., 2020; Fluri et al., 2022). However, it is hard for
252
      cosmologists to gain insights into how to predict Ωm and σ8 from black-box ML models.
253
254   Problem Setup. Our dataset contains clean simulations from CosmoGridV1 (Kacprzak et al., 2023).
255   Each input is a one-channel image of size (66, 66), where the task is to predict Ωm and σ8 . Here,
256
      Ωm is the average energy density of all matter relative to the total energy density, including radiation
      and dark energy, while σ8 describes fluctuations in the distribution of matter (Abbott et al., 2022).
257
      The dataset has 90,000/10,000/10,000 mass maps in train/validation/test splits.
258
259   Expert Features. When inferring Ωm and σ8 from the mass maps, we aim to discover which
260   cosmological structures most influence these parameters. Two types of cosmological structures
261   in mass maps known to cosmologists are voids and clusters (Matilla et al., 2020). An example is
262
      illustrated in Figure 3, where voids are large regions that are under-dense relative to the mean density
      and appear as dark, while clusters are over-dense and appear as bright dots.
263
264   To quantify the interpretability of an expert feature in the mass maps, we develop an implicit
265   expert alignment scoring function. Intuitively, a group that is purely void or purely cluster is more
266   interpretable in cosmology, while a group that is a mixture is less interpretable. We thus develop the
267   purity metric based on the entropy among void/cluster pixels (Zhang et al., 2003) weighted by the
268
      ratio of interpretable pixels in the expert feature. We give additional details in Appendix A.1.

      4.2   S UPERNOVA DATASET
297
298   Motivation. The astronomical time-series classification, as mentioned in (Team et al., 2018), involves
299   categorizing astronomical sources that change over time. Astronomical sources include transient
300   phenomena (e.g. supernovae, kilonovae) and variable objects (e.g. active galactic nuclei, Mira
301   variables). This task analyzes simulation datasets that emulate future telescope observations from
302   the Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST) (Željko Ivezić et al., 2019). Given the vastness of
303   the universe, it is essential to identify the time periods that have the most significant impact on
304   classification of astronomical sources to optimize telescope observations. Time periods with no
305
      observed data are less useful. To avoid costly searching over all timestamps for high-influence time
      periods, we aim to identify significant timestamps that are linearly consistent in specific wavelengths.
306
307   Problem Setup. We take parts of the dataset from the original PLAsTiCC challenge (Team et al.,
308   2018). The input data are simulated LSST observations comprising four columns: observation times
309   (modified Julian days), wavelength (filter), flux values, and flux error. The dataset encompasses 7
310   distinct wavelengths that work as filters, and the flux values and errors are recorded at specific time
311
      intervals for each wavelength. The classification task is to predict whether or not each of 14 different
      astronomical objects exists. The supernova dataset contains 6274/728/792 train/valid/test examples.
312
313   Expert Features. A feature with linearly consistent flux for each wavelength is considered more
314   interpretable in astrophysics. An illustration of expert features used for supernova classification is
315   presented in Figure 4. This example showcases the flux value and error for various wavelengths, each
316   represented by a different color. We colored the timestamp of expert features with the wavelength
317

318

339   4.3   M ULTILINGUAL P OLITENESS DATASET
340
341   Motivation. Different cultures express politeness differently (Leech, 2007; Pishghadam & Navari,
342   2012). For instance, politeness in Japan often involves acknowledging the place of others (Spencer-
343
      Oatey & Kádár, 2016), whereas politeness in Spanish-speaking countries focuses on establishing
      mutual respect (Placencia & Garcia-Fernandez, 2017). Therefore, grounding interpretable features
344
      that indicate politeness is language-dependent. Previous work from Danescu-Niculescu-Mizil et al.
345
      (2013) and Li et al. (2020) use past politeness research to create lexica that indicate politeness/rudeness
346   in English and Chinese, respectively. A lexicon is a set of categories where each category contains a
347   curated list of words. For instance, the English politeness lexicon contains categories like Gratitude:
348   “appreciate”, “thank you”, et cetera, and Apologizing: “sorry”, “apologies”, etc. Havaldar et al.
349   (2023a) expand on these theory-grounded lexica to include Spanish and Japanese.
350
351   Problem Setup. The multilingual politeness dataset from (Havaldar et al., 2023a) contains 22,800
352   conversation snippets from Wikipedia’s editor talk pages. The dataset spans English, Spanish,
353   Chinese, and Japanese, and native speakers of these languages have annotated each conversation
354   snippet for politeness level, ranging from -2 (very rude) to 0 (neutral) to 2 (very polite).
355
356
      Expert Features. When extracting interpretable features for a task like politeness classification
      across multiple languages, it is useful to ground these features using prior research from com-
357
      munication and psychology. If extracted politeness features from an LLM are interpretable and
358
      domain-aligned, they should match what psychologists have determined to be key politeness indica-
359   tors. Examples of expert-aligned features are shown in Table 1. Concretely, for each lexical category,

370   Motivation. Emotion classification involves inferring the emotion (e.g., Joy, Anger, etc.) reflected in
371   a piece of text. Researchers study emotion to build systems that can understand emotion and thus

374

375
      not necessarily tied to a subject, and comments containing “puppy” may also be angry or sad.

395
      that all emotions can be projected onto the 2D unit circle with respect to two independent dimensions
396   – arousal (the magnitude of intensity or activation) and valence (how negative or positive). By
397   projecting features onto the unit circle, we can quantify emotional relations. In particular, we
398   calculate the following two attributes of the features with a group: (1) their emotional signal, i.e.,
399   mean distance to the circumplex and (2) their emotional relatedness, i.e., mean pairwise distance
400   within the circumplex. We then calculate the following: Signal(ĝ, x), which measures the average
401   Euclidean distance to the circumplex for every projected feature in ĝ, and Relatedness(ĝ, x), which
402   measures the average pairwise distance between every projected feature in ĝ (details in Appendix A.4).

      4.5   C HEST X-R AY DATASET
410
411
      Motivation. Chest X-ray imaging is a common procedure for diagnosing conditions such as at-
      electasis, cardiomegaly, and effusion, among others. Although radiologists are skilled at analyzing
412
      such images, modern machine learning models are increasingly competitive in diagnostic perfor-
413
      mance (Ahmad, 2021). Therefore, ML models may prove useful in assisting radiologists in making
414   diagnoses. However, in the absence of an explanation, radiologists may only trust the model output if
415   it matches their own predictions. Moreover, inaccurate AI assistants are shown to negatively affect
416   diagnostic performance (Yu et al., 2024). To address this problem, explainability could be employed
417   as a safeguard to help radiologists decide whether or not to trust the model. As such, it is important
418   for machine learning models to provide explanations for their diagnoses.
419
      Problem Setup. We use the NIH-Google dataset (Majkowska et al., 2020) available from the
420   TorchXRayVision library (Cohen et al., 2022). This is a relabeling of the NIH ChestX-ray14
421   dataset (Wang et al., 2017) which improved the quality of the original labels. It contains 28,868 chest
422   X-ray images labeled for 14 common pathology categories: atelectasis, calcification, cardiomegaly,
423   etc. We randomly partition the dataset into train/test splits of 23,094 and 5,774, respectively. The
424   task is a multi-label classification problem for identifying the presence of each pathology.
425
      Expert Features. Radiology reports commonly refer to anatomical structures (e.g., spine, lungs),
426   which allows radiologists to perform and communicate accurate diagnoses to patients. We provide
427   these expert-interpretable features in the form of anatomical structure segmentations. However,
428   because we could not find datasets with both pathology labels and anatomical segmentations, we
429   used a pre-trained model from TorchXRayVision to generate the structure labelings for each image.
430   We use explicit expert alignment as described in Equation 2 to compute alignment of an extracted
431   feature ĝ and the 14 predicted anatomical structure segments, including the left clavicle, heart, etc.
      Details of the Chest X-Ray dataset can be found in Appendix A.5.

445   4.6   L APAROSCOPIC C HOLECYSTECTOMY S URGERY DATASET
446
447   Motivation. Laparoscopic cholecystectomy (gallbladder removal) is one of the most common elective
448   abdominal surgeries performed in the US, with over 750,000 operations annually (Stinton & Shaffer,
449   2012). A common complication of laparoscopic surgery is bile duct injury, which is associated with
450   an 8-fold increase in mortality (Michael Brunt et al., 2020) and accounts for more than $1B in US
451   healthcare annual spending (Berci et al., 2013). Notably, 97% of such complications result from
452
      human visualization errors (Way et al., 2003). The surgery site commonly contains obstructing tissues,
      inflammation, and other patient-specific artifacts — all of which may prevent the surgeon from getting
453
      a perfect view. Consequently, there is growing interest in harnessing advanced vision models to
454
      help surgeons distinguish safe and risky areas for operation. However, experienced surgeons rarely
455   trust model outputs due to their opaque nature, while inexperienced surgeons might overly rely on
456   model predictions. Therefore, any safe and useful machine learning model must be able to provide
457   explanations that align with surgeons’ expectations.
458
      Problem Setup. The task is to identify the safe and unsafe regions for incision. We use the
459
      open-source subset of the data from (Madani et al., 2022), wherein the authors enlist surgeons
460
      to annotate surgery video data from the M2CAI16 workflow challenge (Stauder et al., 2016) and
461   Cholec80 (Twinanda et al., 2016) datasets. This dataset consists of 1015 annotated images with a
462   random train/test split of 812 and 203, respectively.
463
464
      Expert Features. In cholecystectomy, it is a common practice for surgeons to identify the critical
      view of safety before performing any irreversible operations (Strasberg & Brunt, 2010; Hashimoto
465
      et al., 2019). This view identifies the location of vital organs and structures that inform the safe region
466
      of operation and is incidentally what surgeons often need as part of an explanation. We provide
467   these expert-interpretable labels in the form of organ segmentations (liver, gallbladder, hepatocystic
468   triangle). We use explicit expert alignment as described in Equation 2 to compute alignment of an
469   extracted feature ĝ and the surgeon-annotated organ labels taken from Madani et al. (2022). Details
470   of the Cholecystectomy dataset can be found in Appendix A.6.

474
475   We evaluate standard techniques widely used within the vision, text, and time series domains to create
476   higher-level features. We provide a brief summary below, with additional details in Appendix C.
477
      Domain-specific Baselines. We consider the following domain-centric baselines. (Image) For image
478
      data, we consider three segmentation methods (Kim et al., 2024). Patches (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021)
479
      divides the image into grids where each cell is the same size. Quickshift (Grady, 2006) connects
480   similar neighboring pixels into a common superpixel. Watershed (Levner & Zhang, 2007) simulates
481   flooding on a topographic surface. CRAFT (Fel et al., 2023) generates concept attribution maps.
482   (Time-series) For time series data, we take equal size slices of the data across time as patches (Schlegel
483   et al., 2021). We use different slice sizes to see how they impact multiple baselines. We take various
484   slice sizes, such as 5, 10, and 15, separately to evaluate the results of multiple baselines. (Text) For
485   text data, we present three baselines for extracting features (Rychener et al., 2022). At the finest
      granularity, we treat each word as a feature. The second baseline considers each phrase as a feature.

      Results and Discussions. We show results on the baselines in Table 2. For image datasets, Quickshift
515
      has the best performance compared to Patch and Watershed on both the Cholecystectomy dataset
516   and the Chest X-ray dataset, since they have natural images. All baselines perform similarly for the
517   Mass Maps dataset. That the range of mass maps is different from other tasks is potentially because
518   they are not natural images, but rather similar to topographic surfaces. For the Supernova time-series
519   dataset, larger slices score yield higher expert alignment scores. For both Multilingual Politeness
520   and Emotion datasets, individual words appear to be the most expert-aligned features. Generally,
521   however, we see that the domain-agnostic neural baselines tend to also perform better than or close
522   to the best domain-centric baseline. The main benefit of using a neural approach is that it can more
523   easily automatically discover relevant features.

      6    C ONCLUSION
527
528   We propose FIX, a curated benchmark of datasets with evaluation metrics for extracting expert
529   features in diverse real-world settings. Our benchmark addresses a gap in the literature by providing
530
      researchers with an environment to study and automatically extract interpretable features for experts.
531   Limitations and Future Work. The FIX benchmark is not an exhaustive specification of all expert
532   features, and may fail to capture others types. The ones we included are generally non-controversial
533   and well-accepted by the domain’s expert community, but we can foresee that there are cases where
534   this may not be true. Dealing with potential conflicting expert opinions may need a more nuanced
535   approach, which is left for future work to address. Furthermore, although we cover cosmology,
536
      psychology, and medicine domains in this work, the metrics for these domains may not be appropriate
      for all settings. We encourage prospective users to consider and implement metrics most appropriate
537
      to their particular settings. Future work includes the development of new, general purpose techniques
538
      that can extract expert features from data and models without supervision.

       Problem Setup. We extracted data from the PLAsTiCC Astronomical Classification challenge (Team
       et al., 2018). 2 PLAsTiCC dataset was designed to replicate a selection of observed objects with type
1090
       information typically used to train a machine learning classifier. The challenge aims to categorize a
1091
       realistic simulation of all LSST observations that are dimmer and more distorted than those in the
1092   training set. The dataset contains 15 classes, with 14 of them present in the training sample. The
1093   remaining class is intended to encompass intriguing objects that are theorized to exist but have not
1094   yet been observed.
1095
       In our dataset, we split the original training set into 90/10 training/validation, and the original test set
1096
       was uploaded unchanged. We made these sets balanced for each class. The class includes objects
1097
       such as tidal disruption event (TDE), peculiar type Ia supernova (SNIax), type Ibc supernova (SNIbc),
1098   and kilonova (KN). The dataset contains four columns: observation times (modified Julian days,
1099   MJD), wavelength (filter), flux values, and flux error. Spectroscopy measures the flux with respect to
1100   wavelength, similar to using a prism to split light into different colors.
1101
       Due to the expected high volume of data from upcoming sky surveys, it is not possible to obtain
1102
       spectroscopic observations for every object. However, these observations are crucial for us. Therefore,
1103
       we use an approach to capture images of objects through different filters, where each filter selects light
1104   within a specific broad wavelength range. The supernova dataset includes 7 different wavelengths
1105   that are used. The flux values and errors are recorded at specific time intervals for each wavelength.
1106   These values are utilized to predict the class that this data should be classified into.
