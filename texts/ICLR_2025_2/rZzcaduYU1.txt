                                                      A BSTRACT
009
010
               Neural processes (NP) are a flexible class of models that generate stochastic processes
               by operating on finite-dimensional marginal distributions. NPs are designed to maintain
011
               exchangeability and marginal consistency, which are necessary to define a valid stochastic
012
               process. However, NP variants can come with drawbacks such as limited expressivity, un-
013            correlated samples, and consistency sacrifices. To address the issues of previous NPs, we
014            introduce score-based neural processes, scoreNP, which incorporate a score-based gen-
015            erative model within the neural process paradigm. This score-based approach enhances
016            expressivity, allowing the model to capture complex non-Gaussian distributions of func-
017            tions, generate correlated samples, and maintain marginal consistency. Previously, no NP
018            variant has been able to maintain conditional consistency. We show that using guidance
019            methods from conditional diffusion sampling, scoreNP is the first NP is able to satisfy con-
020            ditional consistency. Empirically, scoreNP performs well qualitatively and quantitatively
021
               well across a range of unconditional and conditional functional generation tasks.
022

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Neural processes (NP) (Garnelo et al., 2018) are a class of generative models on function spaces, that is
027
      they model stochastic processes. Rather than operating directly in the function space NPs leverage the Kolo-
      mogorov Extension theorem (KET) to operate on the finite dimensional marginal distributions. They can
028
      be used for a range of tasks including function regression and Bayesian optimisation. In practical settings,
029
      such as weather monitoring (Andersson et al., 2023), noisy and irregularly gathered data are often the norm.
030   Moreover, the functional nature of these settings motivates the use of NPs to provide a functional probabilis-
031   tic perspective. However, the original NP (Garnelo et al., 2018) lacks expressivity, limiting its applicability.
032   Follow-up works, including Attentive NPs (ANPs) (Kim et al., 2019), Convolutional NPs (ConvNPs) (Gor-
033   don et al., 2020) and Gaussian NPs (GNPs) (Bruinsma et al., 2021) aim to improve expressivity. However,
034   these approaches fall into the setting of predictive NPs, meaning the generative process no longer follows an
035   underlying stochastic process.
036   Recently, Markov NPs (MNPs) (Xu et al., 2023) were proposed as a generalisation of the original NP,
037   offering greater expressivity while maintaining the generative nature through a finite hierarchical model of
038   NPs. Score-based generative models are an expressive class generative models and can be interpreted as
039   an infinitely deep hierarchical model which motivates further generalisation to incorporate them within the
040   NP framework. Recently, (Geometric) Neural Diffusion Processes (NDPs) (Mathieu et al., 2023; Dutordoir
041   et al., 2023) adopted the NP setting for diffusion models. However, due to the architectural constraints, they
042   disregard the marginal consistency element of the KET, (Eq.2), meaning they do not model valid stochastic
043   processes.
044   Score-based generative models (Song et al., 2021b) have become one of the most commonly used class of
045   diffusion models. Typically, diffusion models are applied in the finite dimensional domain such as audio or
046   images. Recently, they have been extended to work with the infinite-dimensional function space (Pidstrigach

057   et al., 2023; Bond-Taylor & Willcocks, 2023; Phillips et al., 2022; Biloš et al., 2023; Franzese et al., 2023).
058   While these works extend diffusion models to function spaces, operating directly on the function space
059   means practical implementations sacrifice consistency due to discretisation. Moreover, they require full

061
      Our main contribution is the introduction of a novel NP framework, score-based neural processes (scoreNP).
062
      This extends generative neural processes by incorporating score-based generative models through a joint
063   diffusion process in a latent-data space, which satisfies the KET consistency conditions. The score-based
064   approach enables us to model expressive distributions and maintain strong performance even in low or
065   zero-context settings. Additionally, it allows us to train the model using a variational adaptation of the
066   score-matching objective, which we demonstrate can be interpreted as a variational lower bound on the
067   log-likelihood.
068
      Additionally, unlike all existing NP models we show how utilising a form of guidance on an unconditional
069   diffusion model to produces posterior distributions which are consistent w.r.t to both the context and tar-

071   unconditional (see Fig.1) and conditional samples from a single objective and training run.

300
      ∇zτ log q τ (z) = −z τ initially for a pretraining stage, which regularises qϕ (z) and allows the encoder to

314   4       C ONDITIONAL G ENERATION
315
316   In many generative modelling scenarios we want to be to generate samples from a posterior distribution
317   conditioning on some input, e.g inpainting, class-conditional generation. This is the case for the Neural
318
      Process Family, where we want to generate a posterior distribution over a function space given a (finite)
      context set C = {xC , yC }, and this is achieved by modelling a posterior distribution over target sets, T =
319
      {xT , yT }. Neural Process variants achieve by maximising the marginal log-likelihood over the targets given
320
      a context set during training. Then, at test time, only the contexts are known and are used to generate samples
321   at target points given the context and the conditional model.
322
323   This comes with two main issues:
324
325
      (a) Firstly, inferring from a conditional generative model sacrifices of consistent marginal distributions
      with respect to the context set C, as it breaks conditional consistency (Xu et al., 2023). Hence, all previous
326
      NPs are at most (marginally) consistent w.r.t to the target set.

      Infinite Dimensional Diffusion Models Diffusion Models have recently been extended to function spaces
426   (Pidstrigach et al., 2023; Bond-Taylor & Willcocks, 2023; Biloš et al., 2023), theory of diffusion models to
427   work in Hilbert spaces, which makes them theoretically well-defined. However, even with Neural archi-
428   tectures which heavily reduce inconsistency under discresation, such as Implicit Neural Representations
429   (Sitzmann et al., 2020) or Neural Operators (Kovachki et al., 2021). This leaves a gap between practical and
430   theoretical consistency (Phillips et al., 2022) decomposes the function space remaining marginally consis-
431   tent to the target points but has limited representational power. (Mathieu et al., 2023; Dutordoir et al., 2023)
432   both fully ignore consistency and work directly on finite dimensions, empirically showing that consistency
433   is often approximated in the learning process. However, without the guarantees in place these models are
434
      still vulnerable to producing miscalibrated uncertainties and inconsistent predictions.
435   Gaussian Processes and the Neural Process Family Gaussian Processes (GP) (Rasmussen & Williams,
436   2006) are perhaps the most important class of models for stochastic processes due to closed-form solutions
437   and exact posteriors. However, they are extremely limited by the Gaussian assumption. The Neural Process
438   Family is a neural approximate alternative to GPs, but also come with their own problems with consistencies
439   and similar expressivity issues. Diffusion models provide a neat solution to these problems. (Bonito et al.,
440   2023) use a the diffusion noising process to induce correlations for predictive NPs.
441   Latent diffusion models have become a staple in image synthesis (Rombach et al., 2022) The benefit of
442   the a latent prior learnt through a diffusion means you to sample from well supported regions of the latent
443
      space. This brings improved results in zero and low context settings where previous NPs have struggled.
      We followed a lot of (Vahdat et al., 2021) practical implementation for scoreNP. The training procedure of
444
      scoreNP is not dissimilar to that recently proposed in, DisCo-Diff (Xu et al., 2024),where they augment a
445
      diffusion model with discrete latents to induce smoother score networks.

450   Limitations Sampling time of diffusion models is a drawback meaning the application high-speed sequen-
451
      tial decision making isn’t possible in the current scoreNP framework. Hence, improving synthesis speed is a
      necessary extension. The variational inference makes the learnability a difficult task with a couple of difficult
452
      balancing hyperparameters.
453
454   Future work ScoreNP provides new avenues neural processes. Further in-depth empricial analysis on the
455   training procedure to fully extract the power of diffusion models. The flexibility of non-strictly permutation
456   invariant encoder may further improve the expressivity of the latent space orthogonal extensions to include
457   inductive biases such as stationarity or equivariance is an exciting direction. The proposed conditional
458
      sampling opens up some exciting areas for future NP research.
459   Conclusion We introduce score-based Neural Process (scoreNP), the first diffusion model which correctly
460   adopts the NP framework satisfying the marginal consistency and exchangeability conditions of KET. We
461   demonstrate how to train our model with a VLB based on a variational score-matching objective. We also
462   show that leveraging reconstruction guidance for conditional sampling makes scoreNP the first NP variant to
463   achieve conditional consistency. Our experiments on both unconditional and conditional function regression
464   tasks quantitatively and qualitatively validates our framework.

      D.1   L IKELIHOOD E VALUATION
781
      We want to evaluate our model through log-likelihoods. Due to the variational aspect of our model we will
782
      only be able to approximate using NELBO. As mentioned in (Vahdat et al., 2021) IWAE objective often used
783   in variational models can be biased due to variance when computing the likelihoods through the probability
784   flow ODE. Hence, we resort to NELBO, which is:
785
786                    log p(yT |xT , yC ) ≥ log pθ,ψ (yT , z|yC ) − log qϕ (z|yT , xT )                          (39)
787                                        = log pθ (yT |z) + log pψ,θ (z|xC , yC ) − log qϕ (z|yT , xT )         (40)
788
789   Where the the context set is a subset of the target set C ⊂ T and pψ,θ (z|xC , yC ) denotes the latent density
790   under conditional sampling given the context. If C = ∅, pψ,θ (z|xC , yC ) simply becomes the unconditional
791   model pψ (z). The first two terms in 40 corresponding to the outputs of the under the joint diffusion are
792   computed by solving the corresponding joint probability flow ODE (Song et al., 2021b; Grathwohl et al.,
793   2018).
794   No guidance scale Although the guidance scale is useful for generating more accurate generations on the
795   context points it is problematic for likelihood evaluation. Due to the gradients of the approximated likelihood
796   p̂ being untrustworthy, especially at times close to 0. This is due to the fact that the parametrised score model
797   cares primarily about the value of y. This causes the latents to move outside of their true range and means
798   that solving the probability ODE becomes infeasible with ODE solvers.

      D.4   M ONOTONIC F UNCTIONS
833
      The generation of monotonic functions starts by sampling N ∼ Poisson(5.0) to determine the number of
834
      interpolation nodes. We then sample N + 1 increments Xincrements sampled from a Dirichlet distribution.
835   Increments are increased by 0.01 to avoid excessively small values, and are then normalized such that their
836   sum is 4.0. The final X values for interpolation nodes are obtained by adding −2.0 to the cumulative sum
837   of these increments so that these X values are within the range [−2.0, 2.0].
838
      For each X value, a corresponding Y value is sampled from a Gamma distribution Y ∼ Gamma(2, 1). The
839
      cumulative sum of Y values ensures monotonicity. A PCHIP interpolator (Fritsch & Butland, 1984) is then
840
      created using these interpolation nodes (X and Y values) to generate function outputs. Given the functions,
841   we randomly sample 128 X values and compute their corresponding function values. Note that these X
842   values are now used to evaluate the functions, rather than serving as interpolation nodes. The function
843   values are normalized to the range [−1.0, 1.0]. Finally, Gaussian observation noise with a standard deviation
844   of 0.01 is added to these function values. For this dataset, evaluation context size varies randomly from 2 to
845   20.

      D.5      C ONVEX F UNCTIONS
847
848   To create a dataset of convex functions, we compute integrals of the monotonic functions previously created.
849   These convex functions are then randomly shifted and rescaled to increase diversity. The function values are
850   normalized to the range [−1.0, 1.0]. Finally, Gaussian observation noise with a standard deviation of 0.01 is
851   added to these function values. Evaluation Context sizes varied randomly from 2 to 20.

855   We create a dataset of 1D functions, each of which represents a solution to a Stochastic Differential Equation
856   (SDE). This SDE is defined by the drift function not to be confused with the SDE used for the diffusion
857   model:
858                                        f (x, t) = −(a + x · b2 ) · (1 − x2 )
859   and the diffusion function:
860
                                                         g(x, t) = b · (1 − x2 ),

862   with constants a and b both set to 0.1. The function sets up a time span that includes 128 uniformly dis-
863
      tributed points within the range of [−5.0, 5.0]. We then uniformly sample an initial condition, x0 , between
      0.2 and 0.6. We use the sdeint.stratKP2iS function from the sdeint library to generate a solution
864
      to the SDE. This solution forms a 1D function that depicts a trajectory of the SDE across the defined time
865
      span, originating from the initial condition x0 . Lastly, we randomly alter the eval context sizes between 2

       E.2    A RCHITECTURES
1024
1025
       Our model is made up of 3 main components during training,
1026
1027          1. Encoder: qϕ (·|X, Y )
1028
1029          2. Time-dependent Data Score: Sθ (y, t, x)
1030          3. Time-dependent Latent Score: Sψ (z, t)
1031
1032   Once training is complete the Encoder is dropped and only the data-score and latent-score models are used
1033   during generation.

                                                            22
1034
       Encoder The encoder is a SetTransformer (Lee et al., 2019), DeepSet(Zaheer et al., 2018), or a Convolu-
1035
       tional Encoder based on architectures in the Hugging Face diffusers library (von Platen et al., 2022) for grid
1036   based experiments (Images). The encoders take a collection of function input and output pairs (X, Y ) and
1037   output a mean and log-variance for a Normal distribution as in VAEs Kingma & Welling (2022). The mean
1038   is computed through the main encoder with a small DeepSet used to produce the log-variance. Additionally,
1039   the input locations X are encoded with fourier features (Tancik et al., 2020).
1040
1041   Time-dependent data score The time-dependent score model conditions on the time, the latent represen-
1042   tation at that time and pointwisely on the input location xi and the corresponding noised function output yi .
1043   Initially, z τ and τ are passed through a 1D Unet (von Platen et al., 2022), before employing a pointwise MLP
1044   conditioning on the Unet output and (xi , yi ). Each hidden layer of the MLP is appended with a layernorm
1045   (Ba et al., 2016).
1046
1047
       Time-dependent Latent Score The latent variables are modelled as large 1D vectors. In the 1D regression
       tasks z ∈ R128 . We use a 1D Unet (von Platen et al., 2022) from the diffusers library to model the scores.
