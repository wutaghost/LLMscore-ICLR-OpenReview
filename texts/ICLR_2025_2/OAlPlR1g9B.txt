009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This work introduces a novel class of Data Augmentation (DA) techniques in
012            the context of graph learning. In general, DA refers to techniques that enlarge
013            the training set using label-preserving transformations. Such techniques enable
014            increased robustness and generalization, especially when the size of the original
015            training set is limited. A fundamental idea in DA is that labels are invariant to
016
               domain-specific transformations of the input samples. However, it is challenging
               to identify such transformations in learning over graphical input domains due
017
               to the complex nature of graphs and the need to preserve their structural and
018
               semantic properties. In this work, we propose explanation-assisted DA (EA-DA)
019            for Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). A graph explanation is a subgraph which is
020            an ‘almost sufficient’ statistic of the input graph with respect to its classification
021            label. Consequently, the classification label is invariant, with high probability,
022            to perturbations of graph edges not belonging to its explanation subgraph. We
023            develop EA-DA techniques leveraging such perturbation invariances. First, we
024            show analytically that the sample complexity of explanation-assisted learning can
025            be arbitrarily smaller than explanation-agnostic learning. On the other hand, we
026            show that if the training set is enlarged using EA-DA techniques and the learning
027
               rule does not distinguish between the augmented data and the original data, then
               the sample complexity can be worse than that of explanation-agnostic learning.
028
               We identify the main reason for the potential increase in sample complexity as the
029
               out-of-distribution nature of graph perturbations. We conclude that theoretically
030            EA-DA may improve sample complexity, and that the learning rule must distinguish
031            between the augmented data and the original data. Subsequently, we build upon
032            these theoretical insights, introduce practically implementable EA-DA techniques
033            and associated learning mechanisms, and perform extensive empirical evaluations.

036   1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Graphs are used to represent relationships between entities in a wide range of applications including
039
      social networks, biology, and finance (Koller & Friedman, 2009; Barabási & Albert, 1999; de Dios Or-
      túzar & Willumsen, 2011; Barabási et al., 2011; Newman, 2018). In order to effectively leverage the
040
      rich relational information encoded in graphs, and inspired by conventional deep learning methods,
041
      various graph neural network (GNN) architectures have been developed, such as methods based
042   on convolutional neural networks (Defferrard et al., 2016; Kipf & Welling, 2017), recurrent neural
043   networks (Li et al., 2016; Ruiz et al., 2020), and transformers (Yun et al., 2019; Rong et al., 2020).
044   Given the vast potential applications and use cases of GNNs, there is significant interest in developing
045   data augmentation (DA) techniques to enhance their generalization capabilities and avoid overfitting
046   during training (Kong et al., 2020; Han et al., 2022; Ling et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2021; Rong et al.,
047   2019).
048   In general, DA refers to techniques that enlarge the training set through label-preserving transforma-
049   tions. These techniques enhance generalization, especially when the size of the original training set is
050   limited (Ding et al., 2022). A fundamental idea in DA is that labels are invariant to domain-specific
051   transformations. For instance, in many image classification tasks, it is expected that the output label
052   remains invariant to specific affine transformations of the original image, such as rotation and scaling.
053   Thus, the training set can be enlarged using artificially generated samples created through these
      transformations. Building on the DA techniques used in non-graphical domains, techniques such


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      as Mixup (Han et al., 2022) and DropEdge (Rong et al., 2019) have been proposed for learning
055   over graphs. However, in contrast to DA in non-graphical domains, in graphs even slight edge
056   perturbations often lead to out-of-distribution samples. For instance, in molecular structures which
057   are modeled as graphs, any edge perturbation that connects a carbon atom to more than four other
058   atoms yields an out-of-distribution sample. Furthermore, classification labels are highly sensitive to
059   edge modifications, and a single edge removal or addition may significantly change the properties of
060   the molecular structure. As a result, it is challenging to identify label-preserving transformations in
061   learning over graphs due to the complex nature of graphs and the need to preserve their structural
062   and semantic properties and to ensure in-distribution augmentations. Moreover, it has been shown in
063
      learning over non-graphical domains that out-of-distribution augmentations can even lead to increased
      sample complexity (Shao et al., 2022).
064
065   In this work, we propose explanation-assisted data augmentation (EA-DA) for learning over graph-
066   structured inputs. We introduce DA techniques that leverage the notion of subgraph explainability
067   to enlarge the training set via label-preserving graph perturbations. This is based on the intuitive
068   assumption that the presence of certain structural patterns or motifs within the input graph plays a
069
      critical role in the model’s decision-making process (Ying et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2020; Yuan et al.,
      2021; Shan et al., 2021). Consequently, slight perturbations of the edges in the ‘non-explanation’
070
      subgraph must be label-preserving. This assumption has been widely adopted in the literature of
071
      explainable GNNs (Ying et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2020; Yuan et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023). The
072   label invariance to perturbations of non-explanation edges resembles the transformation invariances
073   observed in various learning tasks on non-graphical data, such as invariance to scaling and rotation
074   in image classification tasks (Cohen & Welling, 2016; Bloem-Reddy et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020;
075   Shao et al., 2022). To leverage this, we consider learning scenarios where each training sample,
076   in addition to its associated label, is accompanied by its ground-truth explanation subgraph. Such
077   ground-truth explanations may be produced at the time the training data is compiled. For example, in
078   a dataset of labeled radiology scans, the most informative sections of each scan could be identified
079   by the contributing physicians during the compilation phase of the training dataset. Alternatively,
080
      an estimate of the explanation can be produced by joint training of the classifier and its explainer
      on the original (unexplained) training data, as shown in the sequel. Consequently, we introduce
081
      explanation-assisted learning rules and data augmentation methods.
082
083   Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
084   • To provide a rigorous theoretical formulation of EA-DA mechanisms, the explanation-assisted
085     graph learning problem, and the associated sample complexity.
086   • To introduce the explanation-assisted empirical risk minimization (EA-ERM) learning rule and to
087     derive an upper-bound to its sample complexity. (Theorem 5.4)
088   • To show that the EA-ERM sample complexity can be arbitrarily smaller than the (explanation-
089     agnostic) ERM sample complexity. (Example 5.3)
090   • To provide a theoretical justification, along with an example, showing that if EA-DA is used without
091     distinguishing between original and augmented samples, then the sample complexity may be worse
092     compared to that of the explanation-agnostic learners. (Example 6.2)
093   • To provide an implementable class of EA-DA mechanisms by building on the insights gained from
094     our theoretical analysis. (Section 7)
095   • To provide empirical simulations verifying the improved performance of the GNNs trained using
096     the EA-DA mechanisms when the necessary conditions in our theoretical derivations are satisfied,
097     and to provide empirical simulations illustrating potentially worse performance in scenarios not
098     satisfying the necessary conditions. (Section 8)

102   Explainable Graph Neural Networks. Prior works have introduced various methods for extracting
103   subgraph explanations using GNNs (Ying et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2020; Yuan et al., 2020; 2022; 2021;

105   2022). Traditional methods, such as SA (Baldassarre & Azizpour, 2019) and Grad-CAM (Pope et al.,
106   2019), use gradients to extract explanations. Model-agnostic methods include perturbation-based
107   methods, surrogate methods, and generation-based methods. Perturbation-based methods, including
      GNNExplainer (Ying et al., 2019), PGExplainer (Luo et al., 2020), and ReFine (Wang et al., 2021a),

      generate perturbations to determine which features and subgraph structures are important. Surrogate
109   methods (Vu & Thai, 2020; Duval & Malliaros, 2021) use a surrogate model to approximate the local
110   prediction and use this surrogate model to generate explanations. Generation-based methods (Yuan
111   et al., 2020; Shan et al., 2021; Wang & Shen, 2023) adopt generative models to derive instance-level
112   and model-level explanations.
113
      Data Augmentation. Data augmentation is widely used in self-supervised learning (You et al.,
114
      2020; Zhu et al., 2020). A large class of graph augmentation methods can be categorized as
115   rule-based (Wang et al., 2021b; Rong et al., 2019; Gasteiger et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2022a), learning-
116   based methods (Zhao et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022b), and explanation-assisted data
117   augmentation methods (Gu et al., 2023; Kwon & Lee, 2023; Wickramanayake et al., 2021; Tětková
118   & Hansen, 2023; Shi et al., 2023).
119
      Rule-based methods include NodeDrop (Rong et al., 2019), EdgeDrop (Feng et al., 2020), and
120
      MessageDrop (Fang et al., 2023b), which randomly drop a subset of features in the original graph.
121   GraphCrop (Wang et al., 2020) and MoCL (Sun et al., 2021) randomly crop and substitute the graphs.
122   Learning-based methods use GNNs to learn edge importance. For instance, ProGNN (Jin et al.,
123   2020) learns a structural graph from a poisoned graph. GraphAug (Luo et al., 2022) introduces a
124   reinforcement learning method to produce the label-invariant augmentations. Half-Hop (Azabou et al.,
125   2023) proposes a novel graph augmentations by inserting a slow node. In (Liu et al., 2022), a local
126   augmentation is proposed by learning the conditional distribution of the node under its neighbors.
127   EA-DA methods construct label-preserving transformations based on explainations. For instance,
128   in (Gu et al., 2023), given the ground-truth explanation, a generative adversarial network (GAN) is
129   used to generate image augmentations conditioned on the explanation sub-image. Other EA-DA
130   methods (also called explanation-guided DA) have been studied recently (Gao et al., 2024), including
131   in contrastive learning for sequential recommendation (Wang et al., 2022), image classification (Wick-
132   ramanayake et al., 2021), and security analysis and risk detection (He et al., 2023). Mixup (Zhang
133   et al., 2017) is a common strategy to generate explanation-assisted augmentations. In (Kwon & Lee,
134   2023), it is claimed that Mixup doesn’t reflect the importance of each token in natural language
135
      processing, and a soft label assignment method is proposed. In the graph learning domain, (Shi
      et al., 2023) proposed a framework, ENGAGE, to use explanations to enhance contrastive learning
136
      representations.

141   3.1       T HE G RAPH C LASSIFICATION P ROBLEM
142
      A graph G is parametrized by i) a vertex1 set V = {v1 , v2 , · · · , vn }, where n ∈ N, ii) an edge set
143
      E ⊆ V × V, iii) a feature matrix X ∈ Rn×d , where the ith row Xi is associated with vi and d is the
144
      feature dimension, and iv) an adjacency matrix A ∈ {0, 1}n×n , where Ai,j = 1((vi , vj ) ∈ E). The
145   graph is associated with a label Y ∈ Y, where Y is a finite set. The graph parameters (A, X) and
146   label Y are generated based on the joint distribution PY,A,X . The notation PY,G and PY,A,X are
147   used interchangeably. A classification scenario is completely characterized by PY,G ; consequently,
148   we refer to PY,G as the classification problem. A graph classifier is a function f : G → Y, where G is
149   the support of PG . Given ϵ ∈ [0, 1], the classifier is called ϵ-accurate if P (f (G) ̸= Y ) ≤ ϵ.
150
      A training set T is a collection of labeled graphs. The elements of the training set are generated
151   independently and according to PY,G . A learning rule is a procedure that takes the training set T as
152   input, and outputs a graph classifier f (·) belonging to an underlying hypothesis class H.
153
154   3.2       S UBGRAPH E XPLANATIONS
155
156   At a high level, for a given task, an explanation function (explainer) Ψ(·) map the input graph G to an
157   explanation subgraph Gexp . The subgraph is a good explanation if it is minimal and sufficient with
158   respect to G. The notions of minimality and sufficiency are rigorously quantified in the following.
159   The minimality of the subgraph is measured in terms of its number of edges (size). That is, Ψ(G) is
160   minimal if E(|Ψ(G)|) is as small as possible. Sufficiency means that the posterior distribution of the

      transformations that can be applied to enlarge the training set and facilitate generalization. We
201

202

223   The notion of perturbation invariance is analogous to transformation invariances, such as rotation and
224   scaling invariances, observed in image classification. Prior works on sequential data have shown that
225   invariance-aware learning rules can achieve improved sample complexity, e.g., (Shao et al., 2022).
226   Building on this, we introduce the explanation-assisted ERM (EA-ERM) and derive an upper-bound
227   on its sample complexity. We provide an example where this sample complexity can be arbitrarily
228   smaller than that of (explanation-agnostic) ERM. We conclude that for explainable classification
229   tasks, there may be significant benefits in using explanation-assisted learning rules, in terms of sample
230   complexity. This is further verified via empirical analysis in the subsequent sections. It should be
231
      noted that while our observations in this section regarding the improved sample complexity of EA-DA
      methods align with those of prior works including (Shao et al., 2022), and the proof techniques build

333   observing at least one sample per label since all graphs of size n have label 0 and all graphs of size
334   n + 1 have label 1, and the hypothesis class decides based only on the number of edges. On the other
335   hand, for DA-ERM to achieve zero error it needs to observes all possible explanation outputs, as it
336   cannot distinguish between the augmented elements of B0 and the original elements of B1 and vice
337   versa since they may have the same number of edges. Thus, data augmentation has sample complexity
338   that can grow arbitrarily large, whereas ERM and EA-ERM have sample complexity equal to two.
339
      The issue illustrated in the previous example appears to be a fundamental issue. To explain further,
340   note that DA-ERM empirically minimizes the risk over the augmented dataset. If the elements of
341   the augmented dataset are in-distribution with respect to PY,G , this also guarantees that the risk is
342   minimized with respect to the original dataset, hence achieving similar performance as that of EA-
343   ERM. However, if the elements of the augmented dataset are out-of-distribution with respect to PG ,
344   then it may be the case that the output of DA-ERM performs well on the out-of-distribution elements
345   but has high error on the in-distribution elements (which are dominated by the out-of-distribution
346   elements). Hence, DA-ERM may achieve high error probability on the original data distribution. This
347   is exactly the phenomenon observed in the previous example. We show this phenomenon empirically
348
      and further explain it in our empirical evaluations in the subsequent sections.

428   accuracy—2.40% and 2.75%, on average—when compared to the best-performing baselines across

      our methods can still beat the backbone significantly.

478   9                 C ONCLUSION
479
480   Explanation-assisted learning rules were considered, where in additional to labeled training samples,
481   the learning rule has access to explanation subgraphs. The sample complexity was characterized and
482   was shown to be arbitrarily smaller than the explanation-agnostic sample complexity. Subsequently,
483   explanation-assisted data augmentation methods were considered, followed by a generic learning
484   rule applied to the augmented dataset. It was shown both theoretically and empirically that this may
485   sometimes lead to better and sometimes to worse performance in terms of sample complexity, where
      gains are contingent on producing in-distribution augmented samples.

       B.1   DATASETS
1081
1082   In our empirical experiments, we use a benchmark synthetic dataset and 6 real-life datasets.
1083
1084   • BA-2motifs (Luo et al., 2020) dataset includes 1,000 synthetic graphs created from the basic
1085     Barabasi-Albert (BA) model. This dataset is divided into two different categories: half of the
1086     graphs are associated with ‘house ’motifs, while the other half are integrated with five-node circular
1087     motifs. The labels of these graphs depends on the specific motif they incorporate.
1088   • MUTAG (Debnath et al., 1991) dataset comprises 2,951 molecular graphs, divided into two classes
1089     according to their mutagenic effects on the Gram-negative bacterium S. Typhimurium. Functional
1090     groups N O2 and N H2 are considered as ground truth explanations for positive samples (Luo et al.,
1091     2020).
1092   • Benzene (Sánchez-Lengeling et al., 2020) is a dataset of 12,000 molecular graphs from the
1093     ZINC15 database(Sterling & Irwin, 2015). The graphs are divided into two classes based on
1094     whether they have a benzene ring or not. If a molecule has more than one benzene ring, each ring
1095     is a separate explanation.
1096
1097   • Fluoride (Sánchez-Lengeling et al., 2020) dataset contains 8,671 molecular graphs, divided into
1098     two classes based on whether they have both a fluoride and a carbonyl group or not. The ground
1099     truth explanations are based on the specific combinations of fluoride atoms and carbonyl functional
1100     groups found in each molecule.
1101
1102   • Alkane (Sánchez-Lengeling et al., 2020) is a dataset of 4,326 molecular graphs, divided into two
1103     classes. A positive sample is a molecule with an unbranched alkane and a carbonyl group.
1104
       • D&D (Dobson & Doig, 2003) comprises 1,178 protein structures. proteins are depicted as graphs
1105     where each node represents an amino acid. Nodes are interconnected by an edge if the amino
1106     acids are within 6 Angstroms of each other. Protein structures into binary classes: enzymes and
1107     non-enzymes.
1108
       • PROTEINS (Dobson & Doig, 2003; Borgwardt et al., 2005) consists of 1,113 protein graphs which
1109
         are generated in the same way as D&D.

1126

       We use the same GCN model architectures and hyperparameters as (Luo et al., 2020). Specifically,
1128   For the GCN model, we embed the nodes with two GCN-Relu-BatchNorm blocks and one GCN-Relu
1129   block to learn node embeddings. Then, we adopt readout operations (Xu et al., 2019) to get graph
1130   embeddings, followed by a linear layer for graph classification. The number of neurons is set to 20
1131   for hidden layers. For the GIN model, we replace the GCN layer with a Linear-Relu-Linear-Relu GIN
1132   layer. For the PNA model, we adopt a similar architecture in (Miao et al., 2022). We initialize the
1133   variables with the Pytorch default setting and train the models with Adam optimizer with a learning
       rate of 1.0 × 10−3 .
