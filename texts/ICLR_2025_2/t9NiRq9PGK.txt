               Traditional Federated Learning (FL) approaches assume collaborative clients with
012            aligned objectives working towards a shared global model. However, in many
013            real-world scenarios, clients act as rational players with individual objectives and
014            strategic behaviors, a concept that existing FL frameworks are not equipped to ad-
015            equately address. To bridge this gap, we introduce Multiplayer Federated Learn-
016            ing (MpFL), a novel framework that models the clients in the FL environment as
017            players in a game-theoretic context, aiming to reach an equilibrium. In this sce-
018            nario, each player tries to optimize their own utility function, which may not align
019            with the collective goal. Within MpFL, we propose Per-Player Local Stochas-
020
               tic Gradient Descent (PEARL-SGD), an algorithm in which each player/client
               performs local updates independently and periodically communicates with other
021
               players. We theoretically analyze PEARL-SGD and prove that it reaches a neigh-
022
               borhood of equilibrium with less communication in the stochastic setup than its
023            non-local counterpart. Finally, we experimentally verify our theoretical findings.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a powerful collaborative learning paradigm where multiple
029   clients jointly train a machine learning model without sharing their local data. In the classical FL
030   setting, a central server coordinates multiple clients (e.g., mobile devices, edge devices) to collab-
031   oratively learn a shared global model without exchanging their own training data (Kairouz et al.,
032   2021; Konečnỳ et al., 2016; McMahan et al., 2017b; Li et al., 2020a). In this scenario, each client
033
      performs local computations on its private data and periodically communicates model updates to the
      server, which aggregates them to update the global model. This collaborative approach has been
034
      successfully applied in various domains, including natural language processing (Liu et al., 2021;
035
      Hard et al., 2018), computer vision (Liu et al., 2020a; Li et al., 2021), and healthcare (Antunes et al.,
036   2022; Xu et al., 2021).
037
038   Despite their success, traditional FL frameworks rely on the key assumption that all participants are
039
      fully cooperative and share aligned objectives, collectively working towards optimizing the perfor-
      mance of a shared global model (e.g., minimizing the average of individual loss functions). This
040
      assumption overlooks situations where participants have individual objectives, or competitive in-
041
      terests that may not align with the collective goal. Diverse examples of such scenarios have been
042   considered in the game theory literature, including Cournot competition in economics (Ahmed &
043   Agiza, 1998), optical networks (Pan & Pavel, 2007), electricity markets (Saad et al., 2012), energy
044   consumption control in smart grid (Ye & Hu, 2017), or mobile robot control (Kalyva & Psillakis,
045   2024). In the current era dominated by large-scale machine learning, relevant game-theoretic learn-
046   ing applications involving a large network of players could emerge.
047
      To address these limitations of classical FL approaches, we propose a novel framework called Multi-
048   player Federated Learning (MpFL), which models the FL process as a game among rational players
049   with individual utility functions. In MpFL, each participant is considered a player who aims to
050   optimize their own objective while interacting strategically with other players in the network via a
051   central server. This game-theoretic perspective acknowledges that participants may act in their self-
052   interest, have conflicting goals, or be unwilling to fully cooperate. By incorporating these dynamics,
053   MpFL provides a more realistic and flexible foundation for FL in competitive and heterogeneous
      environments.

      In the literature, there are multiple strategies that aim to incorporate a personalization approach
055   into classical FL, including multi-task learning (Smith et al., 2017; Mills et al., 2021), transfer
056   learning (Khodak et al., 2019), and mixing of the local and global models (Hanzely & Richtárik,
057   2020; Hanzely et al., 2020), to name a few. However, to the best of our knowledge, none of them is
058   able to formulate the behaviour of the clients/players in a non-cooperative environment. This gap is
059   precisely what MpFL aims to address.
060
      In this work, we make the following main contributions:
061
062   • Introducing Multiplayer Federated Learning (MpFL). We develop a novel MpFL framework,
063     which models the FL process as a game among rational players with individual utility functions.
064
        In MpFL, each client within the FL environment is viewed as a player of the game, and their local
        models are viewed as their actions. Each player constantly adjusts their model (action) to optimize
065
        their own objective function, and the MpFL framework aims for each player to reach to a Nash
066
        equilibrium by collaboratively training their model under the orchestration of a central server (e.g.,
067     service provider), while keeping the training data decentralized. That is, MpFL extends the scope
068     of FL to scenarios where clients are allowed to have more general, diversified, possibly competing
069     objectives.
070   • Design and analysis of Per-Player Local SGD. To handle the Multiplayer Federated Learning
071     framework, we introduce Per-Player Local SGD (PEARL-SGD), a new algorithm inspired by
072     the stochastic gradient descent ascent method in minimax optimization, that is able to handle
073     the competitive nature of the players/clients. In PEARL-SGD, each player performs local SGD
074     steps independently on their own actions/strategies (keeping the strategies of the other players
075     fixed), and the udpated actions/models are periodically communicated with the other players of
076     the network via a central server.
077   • Convergence guarantees for PEARL-SGD on heterogeneous data. We provide tight conver-
078     gence guarantees for PEARL-SGD, in both deterministic and stochastic regimes with heteroge-
079
        neous data (see Table 1 for a summary of our results).
080       – Deterministic setting: For the full-batch (deterministic) variant of PEARL-SGD, we prove
081         that under suitable assumptions, PEARL-SGD converges linearly to an equilibrium for any
082         communication period τ > 1, provided that the constant step-size γ is sufficiently small
083         (see Theorem 3.3). In this setting, no communication gain is achieved via our analysis.
084       – Stochastic setting: In its more general version, PEARL-SGD assumes that each player uses an
085         unbiased estimator of its gradient in the update rule. For this setting, we provide two Theorems
086
            based on two different step-size choices:
087          * Constant step-size: We show that under the same assumptions as in the deterministic case,
088
                PEARL-SGD converges linearly to a neighborhood of equilibrium (see Theorem 3.4). In
                Corollary 3.5, we show that with appropriate step-size depending on the total number of
089
                local SGD iterations T , PEARL-SGD achieves Õ(1/T ) convergence rate with improved
090
                communication complexity when T is sufficiently large.
091
092          * Decreasing step-size rule: We prove that PEARL-SGD converges to an exact equilibrium
                (without neighborhood of convergence) with sublinear convergence (see Theorem 3.6). In
093             this scenario, the asymptotic rate and communication complexity are essentially the same
094             as in Corollary 3.5, but this result does not require the step-sizes to depend on T .
095
096
      • Numerical Evaluation: We provide extensive numerical experiments verifying our theoretical
        results and show the benefits in terms of communications of PEARL-SGD over its non-local
097
        counterpart in the MpFL settings.

127   straightforward, mainly because all players were in close proximity and had direct access to one

131   especially in distributed systems where the clients/players are geographically dispersed or operate
132   under communication constraints. Addressing communication costs and designing communication-
133   efficient algorithms for n-player games have become increasingly important, and this is precisely
134   the challenge that Multiplayer Federated Learning aims to address.

236   3    PEARL-SGD: A LGORITHM AND C ONVERGENCE G UARANTEES

238   SGD), which is suitable for the MpFL setting we described in Section 2.
239
      PEARL-SGD works by having the clients/players of the game run SGD independently in parallel
240
      for updating their strategy (keeping the strategies x−i of the other players fixed) and communicates
241   the strategies of players only once in a while (via a central server). In more detail, in every round of
242   PEARL-SGD, each player i ∈ [n] runs τ iterations of SGD with respect to fi (·, x−i ), with x−i fixed
243   to the information of the other players’ actions obtained from the previous synchronization. Once
244   each player completes τ SGD iterations (local updates), the server collects actions of all players,
245   and distributes the concatenation of all updated strategies to all players (synchronization step).
246
      Note that the synchronization step involves transferring D = (d1 + · · · + dn )-dimensional vector
247   (different from communication from classical FL where the dimension does not scale with n). This
248   is a significant computational overhead, and it is required at every iteration for the non-local version
249   of PEARL-SGD. We aim to reduce this overhead by communicating less frequently (with τ > 1).
250
      We emphasize that PEARL-SGD and its convergence hold without any assumption on players’ data
251
      distributions Di , i.e., fi ’s can be very different among players and the setting is fully heterogeneous.

528   5                      C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we introduce Multiplayer Federated Learning (MpFL), a FL framework under setups
531   where clients, strategically acting in their own interests, collaborate through a central server to train
532   models (actions) with the goal of reaching an equilibrium. We propose the PEARL-SGD algorithm
533   handling MpFL, and provide its tight convergence guarantees under heterogeneous setups where
534   each player has distinct objectives and data distributions. We show that PEARL-SGD provides
535   improved communication complexity, reducing the primary overhead in large-scale applications.
536   Our work offers a number of potential extensions by incorporating the ideas such as Extragradient
537   (Korpelevich, 1976), asynchronous updates (Dean et al., 2012; Stich, 2019), gradient compression
538   (Alistarh et al., 2017), gradient tracking (Nedic et al., 2017) and algorithmic correction for drifts
539   (Karimireddy et al., 2020; Mishchenko et al., 2022). We anticipate that our initiation of the study of
      MpFL will lead to interesting future work including but not limited to these topics.

      A    A DDITIONAL RELATED WORK AND DISCUSSION
919
920
      Heterogeneity and client drift. One fundamental challenge for theory of Local SGD (FedAvg)
921   is heterogeneity, i.e., varying fi ’s due to differences in local data distributions (Konečný et al.,
922   2016; Li et al., 2020b). Under such setup, Local SGD is prone to client drift (Zhao et al., 2018;
923   Karimireddy et al., 2020) where local descent trajectories head toward distinct minima (of local
924   objectives), and convergence theories require either additional assumptions (Wang et al., 2019; Yu
925   et al., 2019; Haddadpour & Mahdavi, 2019; Li et al., 2020b) or technical analyses (Khaled et al.,
926   2020; Koloskova et al., 2020) to control this drift. Some papers, based on theoretical insights,
927   introduced/analyzed correction mechanisms for Local SGD to mitigate client drift (Karimireddy
928   et al., 2020; Gorbunov et al., 2021; Mitra et al., 2021; Mishchenko et al., 2022; Hu & Huang, 2023;
929
      Grudzień et al., 2023). We note that the n-player game setup of MpFL is also fully heterogeneous as
      each player has distinct (possibly even conflicting) objective functions, and consequently, we have
930
      the analogous concept of player drift.
931
932
      Client drift vs. Player drift. Two two concepts of drifts are seemingly similar, but they are distinct
933
      concepts. Here we highlight the key differences between them. The client drift occurs in the classical
934   FL (minimization) setup

938   and indicates the phenomenon where each client i converges to xi⋆ = argminx∈Rd fi (x) (if exces-
939   sive number of local steps are performed using large step-sizes). Usually, this leads Local SGD to
940   converge to the mean of xi⋆ ’s, instead of x⋆ = argminx∈Rd f (x) (so the problem is the convergence
941   to a biased—incorrect—solution). On the other hand, player drift occurs in the MpFL setup, and

950   FL frameworks with individual models. There are several distinct contexts for FL frameworks
951   where each client learns an individual model. In Personalized FL (Fallah et al., 2020; T. Dinh et al.,
952   2020; Hanzely et al., 2020; Hanzely & Richtárik, 2020; Deng et al., 2020; Tan et al., 2023), clients
953
      aim to learn models tailored to each local distributions, while benefiting from collaborative learning.
      In Vertical FL (Yang et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022; 2024) scenarios, multiple organizations hold dis-
954
      tinct features from the common set of samples and they collaborate to train their each local model.
955
      In Federated Transfer Learning (Sharma et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2020b; Feng et al., 2022), the par-
956   ticipating organizations similarly keep and train local models, but their datasets have heterogeneity
957   over both sample and feature spaces with limited overlaps. Federated Multi-Task Learning (Smith
958   et al., 2017; Marfoq et al., 2021; Mills et al., 2021) extends FL to cases where each client solves
959   different, but related tasks.
960
961   Distributed coordinate descent methods. In the “homogeneous” case of MpFL, where all play-
962   ers share the same objective f , our PEARL-SGD seems related to distributed coordinate descent
963   methods (Richtárik & Takáč, 2016), where coordinates of the optimization variable are partitioned
964   and distributed to multiple computers, working in parallel to minimize f . However, the main mo-
965   tivation of coordinate descent is to gain speedup via parallelization of gradient computation over
966
      nodes, and hence they focus on how the number of workers or the number of random coordinates
      chosen per iteration affects the convergence rate. On the other hand, PEARL-SGD aims to reduce
967
      the cost of communication among players, and we focus on how large τ (the communication period)
968
      can become without compromising the convergence rate.
