011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) have proven to be important tools
014            for solving both forward and inverse problems of partial differential equations
015
               (PDEs). However, PINNs face the retraining challenge in which neural networks
               need to be retrained once the parameters, or boundary/initial conditions change.
016
               To address this challenge, meta-learning PINNs train a meta-model across a range
017
               of PDE configurations, and the PINN models for new PDE configurations are then
018            generated directly or fine-tuned from the meta-model. Meta-learning PINNs are
019            confronted with either the issue of generalizing to significantly new PDE configu-
020            rations or the time-consuming process of fine-tuning. By analyzing the mathemat-
021            ical structure of various PDEs, in this paper we establish the direct and mathemat-
022            ically sound connections between PDE solutions and boundary/initial conditions,
023            sources and parameters. The learnable functions in these connections are trained
024            offline in less than 1 hour in most cases. With these connections, the solutions
025            for new PDE configurations can be obtained directly and vice versa, without re-
026
               training and fine-tuning at all. Our experimental results indicate that our methods
               are comparable to vanilla PINNs in terms of accuracy in forward problems, yet
027
               at least 400 times faster than them (even over 800 times faster for variable ini-
028
               tial/source problems). In inverse problems, our methods are much more accurate
029            than vanilla PINNs while being 80 times faster. Compared with meta-learning
030            PINNs, our methods are much more accurate and about 20 times faster than fine-
031            tuning. Our inference time is less than half a second in forward problems, and
032            at most 3 seconds in inverse problems (less than half a second for variable ini-
033            tial/source problems of linear PDEs). Our code will be made publicly available
034            upon acceptance.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      PDEs are crucial mathematical tools used to describe various phenomena in fields such as physics,
      chemistry, and biology. They provide precise descriptions of complex systems’ dynamic behaviors
040
      and offer a theoretical foundation for system analysis, prediction, and control. In practice, PDEs are
041
      often required to be solved repetitively in forward problems under different configurations of param-
042   eters, boundary/initial conditions or sources, and it is also often necessary to repetitively find the op-
043   timal values of them in inverse problems given different constraints on solutions. Such many query
044   type of applications includes optimal design/control, data assimilation and uncertainty quantifica-
045   tion. Obtaining the results rapidly in each query is important for these applications. For example, it
046   is crucial in interactive design to immediately see the PDEs solutions or optimal configurations once
047   users change their design options.
048   Traditional numerical methods to solve PDEs, including finite difference and finite element methods,
049   face inefficiencies when dealing with high-dimensional, large-scale and inverse problems. Physics-
050   Informed Neural Networks (Raissi et al. (2019)), which utilize deep learning to solve PDEs, have
051   gained significant attention in recent years. PINNs approximate the solutions with the predictions of
052   neural networks, which are trained by embedding the PDE equations and boundary/initial conditions
053   into the loss function. However, this leads to one of the fundamental limitations of PINNs: they need
      to be retrained when the parameters or boundary/initial conditions change, which is time-consuming


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      and limits their applications in many query scenarios. Current approaches to solve this retraining
055   problem of PINNs are based on meta-learning (see section 2), in which a meta-model is trained
056   across a range of PDE configurations and the PINNs for new PDE configurations are generated
057   directly or fine-tuned from this meta-model. The accuracy of meta-learning PINNs is not satisfactory
058   yet, and the fine-tuning still consumes some time and does not meet the real-time requirement.
059
      In this paper, through in-depth investigating the mathematical structure of various PDEs, we pro-
060
      pose mathematically sound methods to the many query problem of PINNs by establishing the direct
061   analytic connections between PDE solutions and boundary/initial conditions, sources and param-
062   eters. The unknown parameters in these connections are learned through offline training. With
063   these connections, the solutions for new PDE configurations can be obtained directly and vice versa,
064   without retraining and fine-tuning at all, making the real-time inference in both forward and inverse
065   problems practical. In contrast, vanilla and meta-learning PINNs are general but agnostic to the
066   mathematical structure of PDEs and thus did not fully leverage the potential of PINNs. They either
067   need time-consuming retraining or fine-tuning, or face the issue of generalizing to significantly dif-
068   ferent configurations. Also, inverse problems are largely neglected by current meta-learning PINNs
069
      researches.
070   We first consider linear PDEs with variable boundary/initial conditions or sources. For linear PDEs,
071   a solution can be expressed as a linear combination of basis solutions. We train multiple PINNs
072   offline to solve PDEs under various sine and cosine bases, thereby obtaining basis solutions. The
073   solution corresponding to an arbitrary boundary/initial/source g(x) is then obtained by the linear
074   combination of such basis solutions using discrete Fourier transformation (DFT) of g(x). This basis
075
      solution method is accurate and fast since no fine-tuning is required.
076   For PDEs with variable parameters, we directly model the solutions as polynomials of PDE pa-
077   rameters with learnable coefficient functions. We derive the differential equations for coefficient
078   functions and train them offline with theoretical guarantees. With this polynomial model, the solu-
079   tions to PDEs with new parameters can be obtained immediately and no fine-tuning is needed. We
080   also use this polynomial model to establish the connections between solutions and variable initial
081
      conditions for nonlinear PDEs. Finally, a simpler scaling method is proposed for some PDEs which
      directly scales the solution of a canonical PINN to obtain the solutions for new parameter values.

084   2   R ELATED W ORK
085
086   Physics-Informed Neural Networks. PINNs have been successfully applied to a wide range of
087   scientific problems, such as fluid dynamics (Rao et al. (2020); Zhu et al. (2021)), medical imaging
088   (Sahli Costabal et al. (2020); van Herten et al. (2022)) and climate modeling (Lütjens et al. (2021)).
089   Many works have been devoted to the training of PINNs, such as loss reweighting (Wang et al.
090   (2021a; 2022); Yao et al. (2023); Hao et al. (2023)), resampling (Nabian et al. (2021); Zapf et al.
091   (2022); Hanna et al. (2022); Zeng et al. (2022); Peng et al. (2022); Tang et al. (2023); Gao & Wang
092
      (2023); Lu et al. (2021); Daw et al. (2022); Lau et al. (2024)), and ill-conditioning of differential
      operators (Krishnapriyan et al. (2021); De Ryck et al. (2023); Rohrhofer et al. (2022); Liu et al.
093
      (2024); Rathore et al. (2024)).
094
095   Wang & Wang (2021) propose architectures that use Fourier features (Tancik et al. (2020); Ng et al.
096   (2024)) to effectively mitigate the spectral bias of PINNs, which is not our focus in this paper.
097   Many Query Problem and Meta-Learning PINNs. The reduced basis method (RBM) (Haasdonk
098   (2016)) is a popular numerical method for efficiently simulating parametric PDEs. It includes an
099   offline training stage and an online stage. The offline stage selects a number of representative pa-
100   rameter values via a greedy algorithm and then in the online stage a rapid reduced solution is sought
101   for each unseen parameter value. In inverse problems, numerical methods (Hasanoğlu & Romanov
102   (2021); Isakov (2017)) usually search the unknown parameters of PDEs in an iterative manner and
103   require to solve forward problems in each iteration, leading to high computational cost.
104   The conditioned PINNs method (Moseley & Markham (2020)) takes PDE parameters or boundary
105   conditions as additional network input and trains over many different PDE configurations, allowing
106   it to generalize without needing to be retrained. Recently, there has been increasing interest in us-
107   ing meta-learning to solve parametric PDEs. Representative methods include HyperPINN (de Avila
      Belbute-Peres et al. (2021)), MAD-PINN (Huang et al. (2022)), NRPINN (Liu et al. (2022)), Meta-


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      MgNet (Chen et al. (2022)) and Hyper-LR-PINNs (Cho et al. (2023)). The implementation strate-
109   gies of these methods can be divided into two main types: the first type (Chen et al. (2022); de Avila
110   Belbute-Peres et al. (2021)) involves training a meta-network to map from PDE configurations to
111   the parameters of the main PINN network, which generally does not require fine-tuning but of-
112   ten necessitates multiple networks. The second type (Huang et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2022)) in-
113   volves learning an effective initialization of network parameters using multiple tasks and requires
114   fine-tuning when the PDE configuration changes, leading to higher time cost. Additionally, since
115   meta-learning involves multi-task training, the difficulty of different tasks can affect training results.
116   Consequently, Toloubidokhti et al. (2024) proposes the difficulty-aware task sampler (DATS), and
117
      GPT-PINN (Chen & Koohy (2024)) employs the reduced basis method for task selection. P2 INNs
      (Cho et al. (2024)) resolve the retraining issue by modeling the solutions of parameterized PDEs via
118
      explicitly encoding a latent representation of PDE parameters.
119
120   The main differences between our methods and the above ones lie in that our methods neither require
121   a large number of training tasks nor fine-tuning, and can solve inverse problems efficiently due to
122   the explicitly established analytic connections between solutions and conditions/parameters.
123   Operator Learning. Operator learning is another approach to solve parametric PDEs. Representa-
124   tive methods include DeepONet (Lu et al. (2019)) and FNO (Li et al. (2020)), which rely on supervi-
125   sion from explicit solutions of different configurations to train neural networks. In comparison, our
126   methods are unsupervised and incorporate prior knowledge of physics laws. The physics-informed
127   DeepONet (PI-DeepONet) method (Wang et al. (2021b)) integrates physical laws into the operator
128   learning framework to reduce the data collection burden.

355   Settings in Our Methods. In this section, we experimentally verify the performance of our
356   methods. The PDEs used in our experiments and their configurations of parameters and bound-
357   ary/initial/sources are given in Table 5 in Appendix A. In our basis solution method, we set
358   M, N = 512. The Convection and Heat equations are trained offline using 10 low frequency sine
359   and cosine bases corresponding to i = 0, 1, . . . , 9 in equation 3, and the Poisson equation is trained
360   offline with 100 low frequency bases corresponding to i, j = 0, 1, . . . , 9 in equation 15. In our
361   polynomial model, the maximal power Np is set to 29 for the Convection and Heat equations and
362
      6 for the Reaction equation, based on our theoretical analysis in Theorem 1 and Theorem 2. Np is
      empirically set to 40 for the Burgers’ equation.
363
364   Methods Compared. We compare our methods with DATS (including DATS+HyperPINN
365   and DATS+MAD-PINN) (Toloubidokhti et al. (2024)), GPT-PINN (Chen & Koohy (2024))
366   and vanilla PINNs using L2 relative error, training and inference times as evaluation metrics.
367   DATS+HyperPINN and GPT-PINN are only applicable to PDEs with variable parameters, and
368
      DATS+MAD-PINN is applicable to PDEs with both variable parameters and variable boundary
      conditions. The settings of DATS and GPT-PINN are consistent with the original papers. We also
369
      compare with PI-DeepONet (Wang et al. (2021b)) and P2 INNs (Cho et al. (2024)).

      ples of initial conditions in (Wang et al. 2021)). During testing, we use testing initial conditions
      (see table 6 and table 7 in Appendix D.1) that are apparently different from those used in training.

      Therefore, PI-DeepONet obtained a higher relative error.

394   DATS+MAD-PINN has large errors for the considered equations due to the difficulty of simulta-
395   neous training of multiple distinct tasks. Table 2 shows that our inference time is less than half a
396   second, on average over 800 times faster than vanilla PINNs which require retraining. Our basis
397   solution method significantly outperforms PI-DeepOnet when training with the same set of sine and
398   cosine initial conditions. This is due to the fact that our basis solution method accurately reconstructs
399
      arbitrary boundary/initial conditions using only a limited number of low frequency Fourier bases,
      while PI-DeepOnet requires large number of diverse training samples of boundary/initial conditions
400
      to generalize. Our method is also faster than PI-DeepONet in both training and inference.
401
402   Tables 1 and 2 also report the performance of our methods and vanilla PINNs on inverse problems.
403   Our method is better than vanilla PINNs in terms of L2 error. Vanilla PINN has a large error for
404   the Poisson equation. This is due to the fact that the unknown sources at all internal collocation
405
      points need to be recovered in vanilla PINNs. In contrast, our basis solution method only needs to
      optimize a few coefficients associated with low frequency bases. In terms of inference time, our
406
      basis solution method usually can solve the inverse problems within half a second, on average over
407
      1100 times faster than vanilla PINNs.

483   6      C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
484
485   By establishing the analytic connections between PDE solutions and boundary/initial conditions,
      sources or parameters, we propose methods in this work to solve the retraining problem of PINNs in


                                                                                  9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      which neural networks need to be retrained once the PDE configurations change. The basis solution
487   method applies to linear PDEs with variable boundary/initial conditions or sources, the polynomial
488   model mainly applies to linear or nonlinear PDEs with variable parameters. Our methods are very
489   fast as well as accurate, making the applications of PINNs to interactive engineering design possible.
490
      A limitation of our methods is that we have considered general but fixed boundary shapes, and
491
      solving PDEs with varying geometry in real-time is one of our future work. We also want to explore
492
      the problem of varying boundary/initial conditions and parameters simultaneously. Finally, we will
493   investigate more nonlinear PDEs in our future work.
