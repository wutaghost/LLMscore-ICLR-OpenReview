009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Optimizing control parameters is crucial to estimate reliable tissue characteristics
012            in quantitative MRI. Basically, multiple hardware parameters are simultaneously
013            controlled to generate a signal from MRI system. Repetitive acquisitions with dif-
014            ferent control parameter combinations create distinct signal modulations and then
015            tissue characteristics are deduced from prior knowledge of physics-based relation-
016
               ship among modulated signals, control parameters, and tissue characteristics. The
               choice of control parameters, which determines the attribute of signal modulation,
017
               directly impacts the inverse problem in tissue characteristic estimation. Thus, the
018
               multidimensional control parameter optimization remains an open research topic
019            in MRI field for accurate analysis of tissue characteristics. Typically, optimal
020            parameters are determined by iteratively updating sets of control parameters to
021            maximize the estimation accuracy of the tissue characteristics. However, the con-
022            ventional optimization process is restricted to explore only the vicinity of control
023            parameters at the current iteration. Therefore, it could highly depend on initializa-
024            tion and current parameters, which might lead to inefficient search especially when
025            noise is present in the system. In this work, to mitigate this limitation, we propose
026            a novel Gumbel-Softmax-based optimization scheme that enables a probabilis-
027
               tic search across an expanding set of all candidates for each control parameter
               using categorical reparameterization. As a case study, the proposed method is em-
028
               ployed to find optimal control parameters for quantitative MRI. We demonstrate
029
               that our Gumbel-Softmax-based optimization simultaneously explores the entire
030            range of control parameters from early iterations and outperforms the conven-
031            tional optimization approach on accuracy of MR tissue characteristic estimation
032            and repeatability of optimization, especially under noisy environments.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Optimization of control parameters is vital to find accurate tissue characteristics in quantitative MRI.
038   Basically, multiple control parameters are simultaneously adjusted to produce a signal from MRI
039
      system. Repetitive acquisitions using various combinations of control parameters yield signal mod-
      ulations, and tissue characteristics are inferred based on the prior knowledge of the MRI system
040
      (Figure 1). Since the observed signal modulations are dependent on control parameters, optimizing
041
      control parameters is significant to accurately analyze the tissue characteristics. Various quantitative
042   MRI techniques are designed using their specific physics models, e.g. intravoxel incoherent motion
043   (IVIM), Arterial spin labeling (ASL), Magnetization transfer (MT), etc (Le Bihan (2019); Hilbert
044   et al. (2020); Hernandez-Garcia et al. (2022)). Thus physics model enables simulation of the tissue
045   characteristics through control parameters. Although the physics model could provide an accurate
046   signal, it was challenging to optimize the control parameters due to the high degree of freedom in
047   the physics model and lack of objective function that can capture all aspects of tissue characteristics
048   estimations (Perlman et al. (2023)). Especially, the simulation of physics models in MRI system
049   often includes solving complicated differential equations which does not provide an analytical so-
050
      lution for complex, nonlinear, inverse mapping problem. Therefore, evaluating estimation error of
      inverse problem for tissue characteristics becomes even more important to ensure an accurate update
051
      of control parameters towards optimal solutions.
052
053   Recently, the neural network was proposed to solve nonlinear inverse problems of estimating tissue
      characteristics in quantitative MRI (Hoppe et al. (2017); Yoon et al. (2018); Scannell et al. (2020);

098

101   our knowledge, this is the first work that applies categorical reparameterization of control parameters

120   2.1   P HYSICS MODEL
121
122   For tissue characteristic estimation, the signal modulations obtained from various combinations of
123
      control (scan) parameters are fitted into physics model. The physics model is based on the principles
      of physics that explain the underlying mechanisms of MRI system. Therefore, the physics model
124
      can accurately simulate the signal with given tissue characteristics and control parameters. Solving
125
      inverse problem of physics model with the known control parameters could provide estimations of
126   tissue characteristic from experimentally observed signal modulations. Basically, the signal (S) is
127   defined with two sets of parameters:
128
129
                                                  S = P M (tc, cp)                                          (1)
130
131
      where tc is a set of tissue characteristics and cp is a set of control parameters. Multiple acquisitions
132   are acquired with different combinations of control parameters to generate unique signal modula-
133   tions which encode the tissue characteristics. Thus, each set of tissue characteristics result in distinct
134   signal modulations, which can be considered as a unique fingerprint for those tissue characteristics.
135   The unique signal modulations with respect to tissue characteristics can be described as follows (Ma
136   et al. (2013); Cohen-Adad et al. (2021); Jordan et al. (2021); Kang et al. (2023)):
137
138                                  S(tc, cp) = [S(tc, cp1 ), ..., S(tc, cpN )]                            (2)
139
140
                                           cpi = [cpi,1 , cpi,2 , ..., cpi,M ]                              (3)
141
      where N represents the number of acquisitions and M is the number of control parameters for single
142   acquisition. Different combinations of M control parameters would result in different signal modu-
143   lations with the same set of tissue characteristics. Therefore, the choice of control parameters is of
144   importance to discriminate numerous signal modulations which would lead to an accurate estimation
145   of tissue characteristics. For example, linearly-increasing control parameters within the pre-defined
146   range would generate similar signal modulations, regardless of tissue characteristics. This could
147   result in poor discrimination of signal modulations, leading to an inaccurate estimation of tissue
148   characteristics. The control parameters were often chosen to reduce the redundancy between acqui-
149   sitions (Cohen & Rosen (2017); Kim et al. (2020)).
150   The physics model is used to understand the complex relation of signal modulations, control pa-
151   rameters, and tissue characteristics. Although the inverse problem of physics-based model is often
152   ill-posed due to its intricacy, many studies have addressed it with neural networks (Cohen et al.
153   (2018); Aggarwal et al. (2018); Jun et al. (2021)):
154

      3.1   C ATEGORICAL REPARAMETERIZATION WITH G UMBEL -S OFTMAX
246
247   We use a categorical representation (z) for each control parameter (cp) to constrain the discrete min-
248   max range, based on the prior knowledge of the hardware, along with their associated probabilities
249   (Figure 3). Each bin corresponds to a respective candidate of control parameter and the number of
250   bin determines the step size of control parameters. The Softmax function converts the representation
251   into probability:
252
253                                                  exp(zi )

256   where πi is a class probability for ith bin of discrete values. To obtain the value of max probability,
257   a Gumbel-Max trick is often used to efficiently sample y from a categorical distribution with class
258   probabilities πl (Gumbel (1954); Maddison et al. (2014)).
259
260                                          y = argmax [logπl + gl ]                                   (10)

265   calculation of sampling from the probabilities (π). Thus, the Gumbel-Softmax trick provides soft-

350   4.1   TISSUE CHARACTERISTIC ESTIMATION WITH NEURAL NETWORK
351
352   The goal of optimization of control parameters is to minimize the estimation error of tissue charac-
353   teristics. Therefore, the estimation errors from the proposed GLOAS was compared to those from
354   the conventional LOAS. As a case study, we adopted a two-pool proton exchange model as a physics
355   model (PM), which is described with the modified Bloch-McConnell equations, to simulate the MR
356   signal (See Appendix A).
357   We used a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) with seven hidden layers of 256 units each with ReLU acti-
358   vation for estimation network. The normalized tissue characteristics were obtained with the sigmoid
359   activation function at the final layer, which was then re-normalized to each range. We performed
360   optimizations on a dataset comprising of one million sets of four tissue characteristics, each of
361   which was uniformly sampled from a pre-defined range (Table 2). The adaptive moment estimation
362   (ADAM) optimizer was used to update the network and control parameters via backpropagation.
363
      Learning rates of estimation network (θ) and control parameters (cp) were heuristically determined
      as follows: 10−4 and 10−4 for the LOAS method and 10−4 and 10−2 for the GLOAS method. The
364
      temperature for the soft-labeled control parameters is annealed using the schedule τ = max (1.0,
365
      10exp(−10−3t )) of the iteration step t.
366
367   The estimation errors of tissue characteristics were evaluated with respect to iteration for various
368   conditions (Figure 4). The noise level and the number of acquisitions were changed. Basically, the
369
      difficulty of estimation for tissue characteristics increases as the number of acquisition decreases
      and the level of noise increases. Therefore, the most challenging condition is at the highest noise
370
      level (σ = 0.02) and the fewest acquisitions (N=10), whereas the easiest condition is without noise
371
      (σ = 0) and the largest acquisitions (N=30). This is well demonstrated in the Figure 4. The pro-
372   posed GLOAS is on par with the LOAS optimization if the signal is free from noise. However, the
373   GLOAS method outperforms the LOAS when noise are included in the physics-based simulation.
374   The accuracy gap between the LOAS and GLOAS methods becomes larger as the complexity of
375   inverse problem increases, i.e. reduction of the number of acquisitions and increase of the noise
376   level. This results underscore the capability of the soft-labeled control parameters in calculating ac-
377   curate gradients in the presence of noise (equation 16). In addition, the LOAS optimization resulted
      in high variance of estimation loss showing the instability of optimization and the dependency on

467   4.2      P ROBABILISTIC REPRESENTATION OF CONTROL PARAMETERS
468
469   We assessed the probability of the soft-labeled control parameters during the optimization process.
470   The probabilities of all candidates for each control parameter are monitored. As shown in Figure
471   5, at early iteration, the probabilities of all candidates are similar indicating that all possible control
472   parameters are being equally considered. As iteration progresses, the probability of one candidate
473
      converges to one, while the probabilities of other candidates approach zero. This trend holds for
      every control parameter although the rates of convergence are may vary slightly. It is worth noting
474
      that optimized values often appeared at either the lower bound or the upper bound of the predefined
475
      control parameter ranges, which is consistent with previous findings (Zhao et al. (2018); Kang et al.
476   (2022)). This is a commonly observed behavior in bang-bang control problems and mitigating this
477   may be an important direction for future studies (Seyde et al. (2021)).

525   5                              D ISCUSSION
526
527
528   The main contribution of this work is the categorical reparameterization of control parameters with
529   the Gumbel-Softmax trick which allows a probabilistic search over complex multidimensional space
530   of physics model. The Gumbel-Softmax function enabled a selection of control parameters with the
531
      highest probability from the probabilistic representation so that the probability of control parameters
      is updated for optimization. Unlike the conventional LOAS optimization which uses a single set of
532
      control parameters for update, the proposed GLOAS calculates the gradients of numerous combi-
533
      nations of control parameters simultaneously. We demonstrated that the probabilistic representation
534   was effective on optimization of control parameter, outperforming the LOAS framework in terms
535   of the estimation accuracy of tissue characteristics and repeatability of optimization. In specific, we
536   validated that the proposed GLOAS provides accurate stochastic gradient in the presence of noise.
537   In addition, we showed that the proposed probabilistic optimization explores the numerous com-
538   binations of control parameters equally from early iteration which allows a comprehensive search
539   over complex multidimensional space. Therefore, the proposed Gumbel-Softmax-based optimiza-
      tion could be an efficient tool for optimizing control parameters in various physical systems.
