009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent studies have delved into constructing generalist agents for open-world
012            environments like Minecraft. Despite the encouraging results, existing efforts
013            mainly focus on solving basic programmatic tasks, e.g., material collection and
014            tool-crafting following the Minecraft tech-tree, treating the ObtainDiamond
015            task as the ultimate goal. This limitation stems from the narrowly defined set of
016
               actions available to agents, requiring them to learn effective long-horizon strate-
               gies from scratch. Consequently, discovering diverse gameplay opportunities in
017
               the open world becomes challenging. In this work, we introduce O DYSSEY, a
018
               new framework that empowers Large Language Model (LLM)-based agents with
019            open-world skills to explore the vast Minecraft world. O DYSSEY comprises three
020            key parts: (1) An interactive agent with an open-world skill library that consists of
021            40 primitive skills and 183 compositional skills. (2) A fine-tuned LLaMA-3 model
022            trained on a large question-answering dataset with 390k+ instruction entries de-
023            rived from the Minecraft Wiki. (3) A new agent capability benchmark includes
024            the long-term planning task, the dynamic-immediate planning task, and the au-
025            tonomous exploration task. Extensive experiments demonstrate that the proposed
026            O DYSSEY framework can effectively evaluate different capabilities of LLM-based
027
               agents. All datasets, model weights, and code are publicly available to motivate
               future research on more advanced autonomous agent solutions.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Developing autonomous agents capable of performing open-world tasks represents a significant
033   milestone towards achieving artificial general intelligence (Savva et al., 2019; Reed et al., 2022;
034   Driess et al., 2023). These open-world tasks necessitate that agents interact with complex and dy-
035   namic environments, make decisions based on incomplete information, and adapt to unexpected
036   events. Early reinforcement learning agents (Tessler et al., 2017; Oh et al., 2017; Guss et al., 2019)
037   have demonstrated limited knowledge in such open-world setting. Furthermore, these agents of-
038   ten struggle with long-term planning, which is crucial for the fulfillment of intricate goals. Recent
039
      breakthrough of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Hu et al., 2021; Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron
      et al., 2023) have shown the potential to revolutionize various fields such as healthcare (Zhang et al.,
040
      2023b; Yang et al., 2024b), robotics (Huang et al., 2022; Ahn et al., 2022; Singh et al., 2023), and
041
      web services (Nakano et al., 2021; Deng et al., 2023; Iong et al., 2024), attributed to its capability
042   on endowing agents with expansive knowledge and sophisticated planning akin to human reason-
043   ing (Wei et al., 2022a; Wang et al., 2024a; Liang et al., 2023). However, the development of LLMs in
044   open-world tasks remains challenging due to the need for well-defined environments and measurable
045   benchmarks (Zhu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a; Qin et al., 2023).
046
      The popular Minecraft game features a vast and diverse world with various biomes, terrains, and
047
      resources, making it an ideal testbed for evaluating the capabilities of autonomous agents in the
048   open-world setting (Guss et al., 2019). To facilitate the development of generalist agents in this
049   setting, MineRL (Guss et al., 2019) and MineDojo (Fan et al., 2022) introduced simulation bench-
050   marks built upon the sandbox Minecraft environment. The seminal work, Voyager (Wang et al.,
051   2023a), proposed an LLM-based agent to drive exploration in Minecraft. Subsequently, there has
052   been a surge of efforts to leverage the superior performance of LLMs to extend the capabilities of
053   such Minecraft agents (Zhu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Zhou et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2023c;
      Qin et al., 2023). Despite recent advancements, existing works mainly focus on solving basic pro-

077   tech-tree, such as collecting materials and crafting tools. Such tasks inherently only assess the abil-

085   In this work, we introduce O DYSSEY1 , a novel framework that equips LLM-based agents with ad-
086
      vanced open-world skills, enabling efficient interaction and exploration within the Minecraft envi-
      ronment. O DYSSEY allows agents to move beyond basic programmatic tasks and focus more on
087
      complex open-world challenges. As shown in Fig. 1, O DYSSEY comprises three key contributions:
088
089   1. We develop an LLM-based interactive agent with an open-world skill library, encompassing
090      40 primitive skills that serve as underlying interfaces and 183 compositional skills tailored for
091      complex and diverse tasks in an open-world setting. A recursive method improves skill execution
092      by checking prerequisites. The O DYSSEY agent consists of a planner for goal decomposition, an
093      actor for skill retrieval and subgoal execution, and a critic for feedback and strategy refinement.
094   2. We fine-tune the LLaMA-3 model (Touvron et al., 2023) for Minecraft agents using a compre-
095      hensive question-answering dataset. This involves generating a large-scale training dataset with
096      390k+ instruction entries from Minecraft Wikis, fine-tuning various sizes of the LLaMA-3 mod-
097      els using LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), and evaluating them with a custom multiple-choice dataset.
098   3. We introduce a new agent capability benchmark to evaluate different aspects of agent perfor-
099      mance in Minecraft, including the long-term planning task, the dynamic-immediate planning
100      task, and the autonomous exploration task. Extensive experiments demonstrate that the proposed
101      O DYSSEY framework provides a robust measure of agent effectiveness, showcasing the practical
102      advantages of our framework using the open-source models.
103
      It is worth noting that our focus is not to design a new LLM-based agent architecture. Instead,
104
      this work aims to provide a comprehensive framework for developing and evaluating autonomous
105
      agents in open-world environments, enabling them to explore the vast and diverse Minecraft world.

131   2       O PEN -W ORLD S KILL - BASED I NTERACTIVE AGENT
132
133   O DYSSEY develops an LLM-based interactive agent with an open-world skill library, aiming to en-
134   hance the efficiency and adaptability of agents in complex Minecraft environments. The skill library
135   comprises 40 primitive skills and 183 compositional skills, while the LLM-based agent employs a
136   planner-actor-critic architecture to facilitate task decomposition, skill execution, and performance
137
      feedback. The architecture of the interactive agent is depicted in Fig. 2. Full skill and prompt details
      used in the LLM-based interactive agent are given in Appendix C.
138
139
140   2.1       O PEN -W ORLD S KILL L IBRARY
141
      Primitive skills encompass a series of underlying interfaces on top of Mineflayer JavaScript
142
      APIs (PrismarineJS, 2023), divided into two main categories: 32 operational skills and 8 spatial
143
      skills. This suite of skills exceeds the 18 primitive skills (all are operational skills) delineated in
144   Voyager (Wang et al., 2023a). Operational skills serve as foundational interfaces with parameter-
145   ized input, such as mine(·) for material collection and craft(·) for tool crafting. Additionally,
146   we pioneer 8 spatial skills that Voyager (Wang et al., 2023a) lacks, allowing for environmental in-
147   teractions based on the agent coordinates. Given that our work is conducted within a text-based
148   Minecraft environment (Wang et al., 2023a; Fan et al., 2022), spatial skills are crucial for handling
149   tasks that require precise positioning and orientation, especially in the absence of visual input.
150   Compositional skills encapsulate primitive skills into higher-level ones, functioning to address a va-
151   riety of basic programmatic tasks, such as mineDiamond and craftIronPickaxe. O DYSSEY
152   classifies 183 compositional skills into types like mineX, craftX, plantX, breedX, cookX,
153   etc. We use a recursive method to construct the skill library, simplifying complex task decomposi-
154   tion by ensuring prerequisites are met before skill execution. Taking mineDiamond as an example,
155   if the agent lacks an iron pickaxe, it will recursively execute craftIronPickaxe. This indicates
156   that our program internally manages the construction and execution order of skills through its recur-
157   sive method, thereby avoiding the need for the agent to engage in additional planning.
158   To facilitate efficient retrieval of skills in the skill library, we first generate a description for each skill
159   by calling the LLM and using the complete program code as a prompt. We then employ Sentence
160   Transformer (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) to encode the skill description. This method transforms
161   text information into vector representations, facilitating semantic retrieval and enabling the agent to
      find the most relevant skill description based on the context provided.

      2.2   P LANNER -ACTOR -C RITIC A RCHITECTURE
163
164   LLM Planner. The LLM Planner is responsible for developing a comprehensive plan, facilitat-
165   ing efficient exploration through long-term goal decomposition. The LLM Planner breaks down
166   high-level goals into specific low-level subgoals, each corresponding to a particular skill outlined in
167   Sec. 2.1. By addressing each subgoal in the plan, the ultimate goal can be progressively achieved.
168   The input prompt to the planner consists of several components: (1) Ultimate goals and behavioral
169   constraints. For example, “My ultimate goal is to ... Propose the current task only when you ensure
170   that you have all the necessary dependent items in inventory”. (2) States of the agent. This re-
171
      flects the interaction between the agent and environment, such as hunger and health values, position
      and nearby entities, etc. (3) Achievements of the agent. This includes the current inventory and
172
      unlocked equipment, as well as previously successful and failed tasks.
173
174   LLM Actor. In the execution phase, the LLM actor is invoked to sequentially execute the subgoals
175   generated by the LLM planner within the Minecraft environment. This process utilizes the open-
176   world skill library to achieve these subgoals. The mapping from high-level subgoals to executable
177
      skill code is accomplished through query context encoding and skill similarity retrieval. This process
      includes: (1) Query context. The text-based subgoals generated by the LLM planner are encoded by
178
      Sentence Transformer (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) to vector representations as the query context.
179
      (2) Similarity matching. The vector similarity between the query context and the skill descriptions
180   in the skill library is computed to determine semantic closeness. (3) Skill selection. The top-5
181   relevant skills with the highest scores are identified, and the actor agent selects the most appropriate
182   code for execution within the environment based on their descriptions.
183
      LLM Critic. During action execution, it is critical for an agent to document its experiences, es-
184
      pecially noting successful outcomes and failure points. This is crucial in open-world planning to
185
      establish a feedback-informed system, which corrects initial plan discrepancies that can cause exe-
186   cution errors. For instance, achieving the animal breeding goal requires prerequisite crops for feed.
187   The LLM critic can assess action effectiveness by comparing expected and actual outcomes, provid-
188   ing insights for refining future strategies. We categorize feedback into three types: (1) Execution
189   feedback. This captures the progress of skill execution. For example, “No hoe in inventory. Craft a
190   hoe first!” not only highlights the reason for failure in hoeing farmland but also provides a guideline
191   to address this problem. (2) Self-validation. By presenting inventory changes post-action to the
192   LLM critic, we empower it to validate whether the skill has achieved its subgoal, eliminating the
193   need for manual checks. (3) Self-reflection. Simply confirming the completion of a subgoal is often
194
      inadequate for correcting planning errors. The LLM critic also serves as an analyst, deducing the
      cause of task failure by evaluating the current state of the agent and its environment. It then offers a
195
      critique, suggesting a more efficient strategy for task completion.

198   3     F INE - TUNE M INECRAFT LLM
199
200   To improve agent performance in Minecraft, we fine-tune the LLaMA-3 model (Touvron et al., 2023)
201   using a large-scale Question-Answering (Q&A) dataset with 390k+ instruction entries sourced from
202   the Minecraft Wiki. O DYSSEY presents an effective procedure for converting a foundation model
203   into a domain-specific model, which involves dataset generation, model fine-tuning, and model eval-
204   uation. The detailed descriptions can be found in Appendix D.
205   Dataset Generation. We develop a GPT-assisted method to generate an instruction dataset for
206   Minecraft. First, we crawl relevant content from the Minecraft Wiki, excluding non-essential sec-
207   tions like history. The collected data is then categorized and separated into different files based on
208   their content type. Then we use GPT-3.5-Turbo (OpenAI, 2023) with different customized prompts
209   to automatically generate diverse Q&A pairs. Note that both the questions and answers were gener-
210   ated by GPT. These Q&A pairs are categorized into four types based on the nature of the answers:
211   short, normal, long, and boolean, yielding 390k+ entries. In contrast, the Wiki dataset released
212
      by MineDojo (Fan et al., 2022) only collects Minecraft Wiki pages, without refining the content
      and generating Q&A pairs for model training. STEVE (Zhao et al., 2023) introduces a non-public
213
      dataset with 20k+ Q&A pairs, which is smaller than our dataset in terms of scale and diversity.
214
215   Model Fine-tuning. We employ LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) for model fine-tuning, which is a parameter-
      efficient training technique. LoRA introduces small, trainable low-rank matrices to adapt a pre-


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      trained neural network, enabling targeted updates without the need to retrain the entire model. Us-
217   ing LoRA, we fine-tune the LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct and LLaMA-3-70B-Instruct models with our
218   Minecraft dataset, resulting in the new models termed MineMA-8B and MineMA-70B, respectively.
219
220
      Model Evaluation. In Minecraft, questions are often open-ended and can yield diverse answers;
      therefore, conventional evaluation metrics (Papineni et al., 2002; Lin, 2004) may fall short. Mean-
221
      while, common benchmarks (Wang et al., 2018; 2019; Hendrycks et al., 2021) are not suitable for
222
      assessing the capabilities of expert models. Thus, we employed GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) to gen-
223   erate two Multiple-Choice Question (MCQ) datasets based on different themes and keywords related
224   to Minecraft. These datasets can quantitatively evaluate the domain-specific expertise of models.

246   Long-term Planning Task. We design a suite of combat scenarios to assess the long-term planning
247   capability of agents, requiring them to craft appropriate weapons and equipment to defeat various
248   monsters. These combat scenarios can be divided into single-type and multi-type monster scenarios.
249
      For the single-type scenarios, we choose various unique monsters, each with its own attack styles,
      movement patterns, and hostility levels. For the multi-type scenarios, we focus on typical monster
250
      groupings encountered in the game. Agents must generate a comprehensive long-term plan, detail-
251
      ing the sequence of crafting the necessary weapons and equipment for the assigned combat task.
252   Performance is measured by remaining health and time consumed during combat. After each battle,
253   agents can iteratively optimize their plan, learning from previous outcomes to improve performance
254   in subsequent rounds. To extend the scope of the long-term planning task beyond combat, we also
255   adopt animal husbandry and cooking scenarios, where agents are required to formulate detailed
256   plans for completing tasks related to specific animals.
257
      Dynamic-immediate Planning Task. The dynamic-immediate planning task requires agents to
258   dynamically generate and execute plans based on immediate environmental feedback. Thus, we
259   design a suite of farming scenarios, where agents engage in activities like planting, cooking, and
260   animal husbandry. Although some scenarios are similar to the long-term planning task, the dynamic-
261   immediate planning task emphasizes reacting to real-time feedback like available resources and
262   nearby animals. Performance is evaluated through task completion time and success rates.
263
      Autonomous Exploration Task. To test the exploratory capability of agents within open-world
264   settings, we design an autonomous exploration task in Minecraft. In this task, agents are required
265   to determine their subsequent objectives and execute the appropriate skills based on the game con-
266   text. The exploration task involves discovering and utilizing resources, while adapting to unexpected
267   events such as encounters with hostile monsters. Agents must adapt to these challenges by devel-
268   oping strategies for resource management and task prioritization. The performance metrics include
269   the number of distinct items obtained, the total items crafted, the recipes and advancements (R&A)
      unlocked, and the distance traveled.

294   on basic programmatic tasks and the agent capability benchmark. Our simulation environment is

      conducted with the open-source LLaMA-3 model, significantly reducing costs compared to GPT-4-

520   programmatic tasks and focus on complex open-world challenges.

523   7                                                       C ONCLUSION
524
525
      This work proposes O DYSSEY to empower agents with open-world skills in the Minecraft environ-
526
      ment. We introduce (1) an interactive agent endowed with an extensive open-world skill library com-
527
      prising various primitive skills and compositional skills; (2) a fine-tuned LLaMA-3 model, trained on
528   a large-scale question-answering dataset sourced from the Minecraft Wiki; (3) a new agent capabil-
529   ity benchmark that encompasses tasks requiring long-term planning, dynamic-immediate planning,
530   and autonomous exploration. The public availability of all datasets, model weights, and code will
531   facilitate future research in the development of autonomous agents. We hope that O DYSSEY will
532   inspire further innovation and progress in the field of autonomous agent development.
533
      Limitations and Future Works. The proposed open-world skill library enables the use of open-
534   source LLMs as the foundation for agents to call upon skills, avoiding the high costs associated with
535   previous work using GPT-4 (Wang et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2023). However, the open-
536   source LLMs are prone to generating hallucinations, leading to a decrease in agent performance.
537   Thus, our future research will focus on employing retrieval-augmented generation to improve LLMs
538   in Minecraft. Additionally, this work focuses on developing and evaluating text-based LLMs in the
539   context of Minecraft, with visual aspects currently out of scope. Looking ahead, we plan to integrate
      visual understanding into the skill library to enhance the agent capabilities.

      A     D ISCUSSION ON S OCIETAL I MPACTS
865
866
      When developing autonomous embodied agents within Minecraft, the negative impacts are rela-
867   tively minimal. Minecraft provides a controlled environment to test these technologies. Concerns
868   include potential over-reliance by players, reducing their exploratory and creative thinking, minor
869   data privacy issues due to the collection of anonymized player data, and possible impacts on game
870   balance, particularly in multiplayer settings. Overall, Minecraft is an ideal experimental platform
871   where these mild negative impacts can be effectively managed.
872
873
      B     D ISCUSSION ON M IGRATING O DYSSEY TO OTHER D OMAINS
874
875   The skill library designed for Minecraft is built with modularity and generalizability in mind, al-
876   lowing for potential adaptation to other domains such as web navigation (Lai et al., 2024; Lu et al.,
877   2024), robot manipulation (Mosemann & Wahl, 2001; Pedersen et al., 2016; Liang et al., 2023;
878   Singh et al., 2023), robot navigation (Zhou et al., 2024b; Shah et al., 2023), and other game-playing
879   environments (Xu et al., 2024). These skills abstract underlying actions and focus on high-level
880   interactions, allowing them to be adapted to different environments by redefining low-level actions
881   without changing the overall structure of the skill library. Even without direct API access, basic
882   action spaces (e.g., keyboard and mouse operations in games, or movement operations in robotics)
883
      can be employed to construct primitive skills. Prior research in robotic manipulation, including
      CaP (Liang et al., 2023) and ProgPrompt (Singh et al., 2023), demonstrates how primitive skills
884
      such as picking and placing objects or opening containers can be built from basic actions. More-
885
      over, we believe that the concept of "skills" should extend beyond code APIs to include knowledge
886   from various sources. For example, handbooks can provide informational segments treated as skills,
887   retrievable by LLMs using techniques like retrieval-augmented generation (Lewis et al., 2020), en-
888   hancing decision-making.
889
      To fine-tune the LLaMA-3 model for the Minecraft agent, we crawled the Minecraft Wiki and used
890
      a GPT-assisted approach to generate an instruction dataset. Researchers in other domains can repli-
891
      cate this process to create their own instruction datasets. To facilitate this, we have open-sourced our
892   Minecraft Wiki crawler on Github, which can be easily modified to crawl similar Wiki websites for
893   other domains. Additionally, our benchmark tasks evaluate agent performance from three perspec-
894   tives: long-term planning, dynamic-immediate planning, and autonomous exploration. These di-
895   mensions effectively assess the capabilities of open-world autonomous agents. Researchers in other
896   domains can adopt these perspectives to design comprehensive evaluation tasks for their needs.

990    C.2     LLM P LANNER
991
992    O DYSSEY relies on LLMs to generate language-based plans. In our Minecraft experiment, we pro-
993
       pose three novel tasks (long-term planning task, dynamic-immediate planning task and autonomous
       exploration task) for agents to explore. Therefore we designate three types of prompt messages for
994
       them respectively, offering LLM Planner the ability to generate different routines on different tasks.
995
       The format of the prompt is presented thus:
996
997            • "SYSTEM" role: A high-level instruction that gives directions to the model behavior. It
998              sets an overall goal for the interaction and provides external information.
999            • "USER" role: Detailed information like environment, states and achievements of the agent
1000             will be provided to the planner for the next immediate subgoals.
1001
               • "ASSISTANT" role: A guideline generated by the planner.
1002
1003
       C.2.1     L ONG - TERM P LANNING
1004
1005   We design a suite of combat tasks to assess the long-term planning capabilities of agents, where the
1006   LLM Planner should plan to craft appropriate weapons and equipment to defeat monsters.
1007   The input prompt to LLM consists of several components:
1008
1009           • Ultimate goals: The monsters that need to be defeated.
1010           • Directives and behavior constraints that guarantee the proposed task is achievable and ver-
1011             ifiable.
1012
               • Information of last combat: This ensures that the prompt is exposed to increasing amounts
1013
                 of information over the combat and thus progressively advances towards more efficient
1014             plans.
1015

1017

1019
1020         Your goal is to generate the plan that can defeat all monsters while using the shortest
1021         time. So, more is not always better when proposing your plan list.
1022
1023         —External information—
1024

       For the Minecraft Fandom Wiki, we first crawl the content of all its pages and perform a preliminary
1351   filtering on the resulting pages, removing pages that were obviously useless for our fine-tuning task,
1352   such as game version information, and obtaining a series of JSON files. These files still contain a

1363   word count of the segmented file did not exceed the set limit without cutting the section block, thus

1788   E.4   S PECIFIC AGENT C APABILITY R EQUIREMENTS FOR D IFFERENT TASKS
1789
1790   This section provides an overview of the specific agent capabilities required for each task, laying the
1791   foundation for a deeper understanding of how our benchmark evaluates different aspects of agent
1792   performance. Different agent capabilities are detailed as follows:
1793
       • Goal-based Planning: This capability refers to the agent’s ability to formulate and execute com-
1794
         prehensive plans based on predefined goals. It involves understanding the given goals and devising
1795     a step-by-step plan to achieve them over extended periods. This is critical for tasks such as the
1796     long-term planning task, where agents need to craft weapons and equipment to defeat specific
1797     monsters.
1798
       • Feedback-based Planning: This capability involves the agent’s ability to adapt its plans dynam-
1799     ically based on environmental feedback. It is essential for tasks where environmental feedback is
1800     crucial, such as in the dynamic-immediate planning task and the multi-round long-term planning
1801     task, where agents must adjust their strategies in response to the outcomes of previous actions or
1802     environmental changes.
1803   • Exploratory Planning: This capability evaluates the agent’s ability to set its own goals and make
1804     decisions independently in a complex environment. Agents must navigate, gather information, and
1805     decide on objectives without predefined goals. This is central to the autonomous exploration task,
1806     where agents explore the Minecraft world, discover resources, and adapt to unforeseen events.
1807   • Task Decomposition: This capability refers to the agent’s ability to break down complex tasks
1808     into specific, manageable sub-tasks. It is vital for the long-term planning task where agents need
1809     to craft a sequence of items, requiring the breakdown of the end goal into a series of intermediate
1810     steps.
1811   • Resource Management: This capability involves the efficient allocation and utilization of avail-
1812     able resources. Agents must maintain awareness of their inventory, manage assets effectively, and
1813     identify which resources need to be gathered. This is particularly important in farming tasks and
1814     autonomous exploration, where resource availability and management are crucial for subsequent
1815     behavior.
1816   • Skill Retrieval: This capability pertains to the agent’s ability to identify and choose the most
1817     suitable skill from a set of options. Agents evaluate a list of relevant skills and select the one
1818     that best fits the current environmental context and task requirements. All tasks require agents to
1819     retrieve and apply relevant skills based on situational demands.
1820   • Self-Reflection: This capability involves the agent’s ability to analyze and learn from the out-
1821     comes of its actions. Simply confirming the completion of a subgoal is often inadequate for cor-
1822     recting planning errors. The agent evaluates its performance, deduces the cause of task failures,
1823     and suggests more efficient strategies for future tasks. This is particularly important in multi-round
1824     tasks.
1825   • Self-Validation: This capability enables the agent to autonomously confirm the success of its
1826     actions against intended outcomes. By assessing inventory changes after actions, the agent ensures
1827     that each step contributes towards the overarching objectives without external verification. This
1828     capability is crucial for all tasks, as agents need to continuously ensure their actions align with the
1829     objectives.
