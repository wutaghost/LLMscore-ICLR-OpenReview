009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The performance of masked autoencoders hinges significantly on masking, prompt-
012            ing considerable efforts towards devising superior masking strategies. However,
013            these strategies mask only once and employ masking directly on the input image.
014            Afterward, inspired by the flexibility of masking, subsequent works introduce two
015            rounds of masking. Unfortunately, all initiatives primarily focus on enhancing
016            model performance, lacking an in-depth and systematical understanding of mul-
017            tiple masking for masked autoencoder. To bridge this gap, this work introduce
018
               a masked framework with multiple masking stages, termed Conditional MAE,
               where subsequent maskings are conditioned on previous unmasked representa-
019
               tions, enabling a more flexible masking process in masked image modeling. By
020
               doing so, our study sheds light on how multiple masking affects the optimiza-
021            tion in training and performance of pretrained models, e.g., introducing more
022            locality to models, and summarizes several takeaways from our findings. Fi-
023            nally, we empirically evaluate the performance of our best-performing model
024            (Conditional-MAE) with that of MAE in three folds including transfer learning,
025            robustness, and scalability, demonstrating the effectiveness of our multiple masking
026            strategy. We also follow our takeaways and show the generalizability to other
027            heterogeneous networks including SimMIM and ConvNeXt V2. We hope our
028            findings will inspire further work in the field and release the code at https:
029
               //anonymous.4open.science/r/conditional-mae-512C.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      Masked autoencoder (MAE) (He et al., 2021) has recently emerged prominently in the field of
034
      self-supervised learning (Bao et al., 2021; He et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021). One of the most
035
      representative work, MAE, which partitions an image into visible patches and masked ones, and
036   predict the masked patches from visible ones in RGB space, has gained vast attention from community.
037
038   A crucial element of the masked autoencoder is masking, e.g., how (where) to mask and how much to
039
      mask, which directly impacts model’s performance. Thus, follow-up work develops various masking
      strategies categorized into four different types by a recent survey (Li et al., 2023b) including Hard
040
      Sampling (Kakogeorgiou et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022a; Wang et al., 2023a; Hou et al., 2022; Wu & Mo,
041
      2022), e.g., guided by attention (Kakogeorgiou et al., 2022), Mixture (Chen et al., 2023b; Liu et al.,
042   2022a; Zhang & Shen, 2022), e.g., by mixing different images (Chen et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2022a),
043   Adversarial (Shi et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023a), e.g., by introducing adversarial learning (Shi et al.,
044   2022), and Contextual Masking (Li et al., 2022b; Chen et al., 2022a) e.g., using local window (Chen
045   et al., 2022a). Basically, these works mostly mask once and mask only on the input image and focus
046   on how to further improve the performance.
047
      Intuitively, masking is a flexible operation that can be performed at different stages (e.g., the input im-
048   age and different levels of representations) and with different ratios. Following this line, UnMAE (Li
049   et al., 2022b) introduces two rounds of masking but still perform on the input image. VideoMAE
050   v2 (Wang et al., 2023b) introduces dual masking but primarily focuses on reducing computational
051   costs. A2 MIM (Li et al., 2023a) proposes to mask intermediate features from PatchEmbed layer
052   following MAE (He et al., 2021). Though these initiatives have effectively reduced computational
053   costs or enhanced model performance, these efforts have gone only so far, lacking an in-depth and
      systematical analysis of multiple rounds of masking for masked autoencoder. Hence, a question


                                                          1

054
      naturally arises: How does multiple masking impact the optimization of the masked autoencoder in
055   both training and performance?
056
057   To answer this question, this work presents a framework called Conditional MAE, which aims
058
      to explore the impact of multiple rounds of masking in the training process and performance. In
      Conditional MAE, subsequent maskings are conditioned on previous unmasked representations,
059
      enabling more flexible masking on different granularities of inputs. Based on it, we progressively
060
      conduct a thorough empirical study about multiple masking to address three critical questions: 1)
061   where to mask, 2) how much to mask, and 3) what’s the impact? In our experiments, we investigate
062   one, two, and three-shot masking 1 , where each round of masking is considered a shot. Our results
063   highlight several key takeaways from each shot:
064
      • In the one-shot case, we find that masking at the beginning is always beneficial for task performance.
065
      Moreover, it is critical to find a suitable mask ratio. Generally, though the model size is different, e.g.,
066
      ViT-S and ViT-B, 75% mask ratio is firstly recommended.
067
068   • In the two-shot case, building on the best one-shot setting, increasing the interval of two-shot
069   masking with a large ratio followed by a small ratio is helpful for fine-tuning. Additionally, our
070
      experiments strongly suggest that there may not exist a positive relationship between linear probing
      and fine-tuning. Finally, the second masking brings locality bias into the model and helps capture
071
      low-level features, especially for finer-grained classification
072
073   • In the three-shot case, we find that using a greedy-like masking selection strategy, which uses the
074   best two-shot setting as a starting point, is superior to other three-shot strategies. Simultaneously, the
075   third masking brings more locality into models than two-shot case.
076   Based on the above results of our empirical experiments, we select the best-performing model
077   (Conditional-MAE) and evaluate its transferability to downstream tasks, including image classifica-
078   tion, object detection, and semantic segmentation. We also verify its robustness to noisy inputs, e.g.,
079   random occlusion and shuffling, and empirically demonstrate its scalability. Besides, we follow our
080   takeaways and evaluate the generalizability to other network architectures including SimMIM (Xie
081   et al., 2022) and ConvNeXt V2 (Woo et al., 2023).
082   Note that in this work, we are not to propose a state-of-the-art method, but to enhance both the
083   understanding and performance of MAE by exploring the potential of masking and to inspire future
084   work. Our contributions are three-fold:
085
      • Building on our proposed flexible framework, i.e., Conditional MAE, our primary contribution lies in
086   the first in-depth and comprehensive analysis of how multiple masking influences model optimization
087   from the various aspects including performance comparison, loss, representation, attention map, etc.
088
089
      • Through extensive empirical experiments on multiple masking, we provide several key takeaways
      from each shot as shown above. More importantly, we observe a key phenomenon that multiple
090
      masking is capable of introducing locality bias to models.
091
092   • We demonstrate the superiority of our Conditional-MAE over MAE in downstream transfer,
093   robustness against occlusion and shuffling, and scalability. We also show the generalizability to other
094   network architectures.

178   3.1 M ULTIPLE S HOTS M ASKING
179   In our study, we investigate the Conditional MAE in three different settings by pretraining on
180   ImageNet100: one-shot masking, two-shot masking, and three-shot masking. We do not explore
181   settings with more shots, as preliminary experiments have shown them to be inferior to three-shot. For
182   ease of description, we denote the three mask ratios as η1 , η2 , η3 , and the corresponding layer indexes
183
      as i, j, k, respectively, where masking is applied before inputting. Considering our Conditional MAE
      is derived from MAE, we fix i = 0 to match with MAE. Through exhaustive experiments conducted
184
      below, we aim to address three key questions: where to mask, how much to mask, and what is the
185
      impact? For training details, please refer to the Appendix A.1.
186
187
      3.1.1 O NE - SHOT M ASKING
188
      In the one-shot setting, we only mask patch tokens in the encoder once, allowing us to examine
189
      the impact of different mask positions and mask ratios on encoder performance. Specifically,
190   for mask positions, we consider four positions at equal intervals: the 0-th, 3-th, 6-th, and 9-th
191   layer of encoder blocks, denoted as (i, j, k) = (0, 0/3/6/9, 0). We exclude the 12-th layer as it
192   cause a denoise autoencoder to degenerate into a vanilla one. Regarding mask ratios, we carefully
193   select two representative ratios used in MAE (He et al., 2021), namely 0.75 and 0.9, denoted as
194   (η1 , η2 , η3 ) = (0, 0.75/0.9, 0) 3 . The reasons are two-fold: 0.75 is widely used in MAE; For 0.9,
195   previous work (Riquelme et al., 2021) has shown that even using 10% patch features can still yield
196   competitive performance in visual recognition. The results on ViT-S/16 are illustrated in Fig 2.
197   It has been observed that masking at the beginning position (j = 0) is beneficial for both linear
198   probing and fine-tuning. Conversely, we also notice a significant drop in performance for linear
199   probing when masking is applied at the other positions. This implies that the representations
200   encoded by the fixed encoder at j = 0 are relatively more distinguishable while other encoders learn
201   comparatively less knowledge compared to the encoder at j = 0, which could be attributed to the
202   information leakage from attention interaction. To support this speculation, we visualize the training
203   loss curves of pretraining and linear probing and t-SNE of output representation in Appendix A.2.1.

      3.1.2          T WO - SHOT M ASKING

338   Information Leakage and Locality. In the two-shot setting, the second masked patches have been
339   seen by previous layers, potentially resulting in information leakage. However, it’s important to note
340
      that this leakage does not cause a trivial solution as the presence of η1 and its substantial gap in
      magnitude compared to η2 necessitates the model to acquire the ability to infer the masked patch in the
341
      first masking. In contrast, the presence of the second masking necessitates that patches that interacted
342
      in previous layers must recover their corresponding masked neighbors in the forward pass. As a result,
343   the model needs to dedicate a portion of its capacity to learn how to infer local neighbors. This would
344   introduce a certain degree of locality bias. To illustrate this, we visualize the reversed attention (Ding
345   et al., 2023) of pretrained model L(0, 10; 0.75, 0.1) as shown in Fig 6 (bottom), containing the
346   information flow of second masking, i.e., how much information a second-masked patch sends to
347   other. It clearly demonstrates that the attention head retains object-related local information. In this
348   way, the information leakage is controllable, and information of the second-masked patch flows and is
349   stored in the neighboring patches, to be reconstructed after the second masking. Also, compared with
350   single masking in Fig 6 (top), the locality of the attention head is enhanced, potentially benefiting
351
      some downstream tasks that require low-level or local representations (Jiang et al., 2022).

             Takeaways. For two-shot masking, we summarize four useful findings: ① building on one-shot,
391
             increasing the interval of two-shot masking with a large η1 and a small η2 is helpful for fine-tuning in

472   Masked image modeling. Masked image modeling is the task of predicting the masked part of an
473   image from the visible part. Inspired by masked language modeling in natural language processing,
474   BEiT (Bao et al., 2021) is the first to employ this paradigm in computer vision. PeCo (Dong et al.,
475   2021) further improves the performance of BEiT by involving more semantics in visual tokens.
476   MAE (He et al., 2021) removes the need for a tokenizer (e.g., d-vae (Ramesh et al., 2021) in BEiT)
477
      by directly predicting the masked part in RGB space. This greatly simplifies the whole pipeline and
      improves the model performance simultaneously. CAE (Chen et al., 2022b) adds a regressor between
478
      the encoder and decoder to align masked and visible representations in the same representation space.
479
      iBOT (Zhou et al., 2021) combines masked image modeling with contrastive learning, showing great
480   potential. (Shi et al., 2022) uses an adversarial objective to consistently improve on state-of-the-art
481   self-supervised learning (SSL) methods. MaskFeat (Wei et al., 2022a) uses Histograms of Oriented
482   Gradients (HOG), a hand-crafted feature descriptor, as reconstruction target. Recently, with more
483   effort devoted to this field, numerous works (Dong et al., 2022a; Gao et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022b;
484   Chen et al., 2022c; Kakogeorgiou et al., 2022; Li et al., 2021; El-Nouby et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022b;
485   Tao et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2022a; Yu et al., 2022; Assran et al., 2022; Fang et al.,
      2022; Bachmann et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022b; Huang et al., 2022a;b; Dong et al.,


                                                               9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      2022b) are proposed including BootMAE (Dong et al., 2022a), SdAE (Chen et al., 2022c), MST (Li
487   et al., 2021), SplitMask (El-Nouby et al., 2021), SIM (Tao et al., 2022), etc.
488
489   Understanding masked image modeling. Xie et al. shows that masked image modeling brings
490
      rich diversity to the self-attention head and pays more attention to locality compared to supervised
      one (Xie et al., 2021b). Additionally, Xie et al. also demonstrates that larger models, more data, and
491
      longer training times are beneficial for masked image modeling (Xie et al., 2021a). CAE (Chen et al.,
492
      2022b) illustrates its attention map and speculates that masked image modeling cares more about the
493   global including both foreground and background. Kong & Zhang (Kong & Zhang, 2022) point out
494   that masked image modeling brings occlusion invariant to the model representation. Cao et al. (Cao
495   et al., 2022) deliver a mathematical understanding of masked image modeling. More recently, Zhu et
496   al. (Zhu et al., 2023) speculate that masked image modeling is a part-to-part process: the masked
497   representations are hallucinated from the visible part.
498
      Masking. Masking is a key operation in masked image modeling. Trandional masked strategies
499   include random masking used in MAE (He et al., 2021), and block masking used in BEiT (Bao et al.,
500   2021) and CAE (Chen et al., 2022b). Besides, previous works also explore extra masking strategies.
501   MST (Li et al., 2021) masks low-attended patches to enhance the performance without additional
502   cost. AttMask (Kakogeorgiou et al., 2022) further proves the usefulness of masking highly attended
503   portions. AMT (Gui et al., 2022) uses the attention map in the last layer of the vision transformer to
504   guide the masking. SemMAE (Li et al., 2022a) leverages a masking with semantics provided by an
505   additional pretrained model. However, it is worth noticing that almost all of them mask an image just
506   at the beginning and primarily focus on how to further improve the performance. UnMAE (Li et al.,
507
      2022b), VideoMAE v2 (Wang et al., 2023b), and A2 MIM (Li et al., 2023a) introduces two rounds
      of masking but these efforts have gone only so far, lacking an in-depth and systematical analysis
508
      of multiple rounds of masking for masked autoencoder. In contrast, our work fills this gap and
509
      reveals the secret of multiple masking on masked autoencoder’s optimization with different masking
510   positions and ratios. We also discuss masking in generation modeling in Appendix A.6.

513   5   C ONCLUSION
514   In this paper, we reveal how multiple masking affects masked autoencoder’s optimization in training
515   and performance by using a flexible framework called Conditional MAE. Based on our findings, we
516   summarize several takeaways from each shot and find that multiple masking can bring locality bias
517   to models. We also show the superiority of our best two-shot model Conditional-MAE over MAE
518   in downstream tasks, robustness again occlusion and shuffling, masking generalizability to other
519   heterogeneous architectures, and model scalability, providing sufficient insight for future work.
520   Limitation and Broader Impact. Our study is constrained by limited computational resources.
521   We conducted our experiments using small, base, and large ViT. Therefore, it would be interesting
522   to extend this study to larger models e.g., Huge ViT. Our empirical study primarily focuses on the
523   masked autoencoder. There may not exist any negative effects on itself but on how it is used.

1063   A.6                                               M ORE R ELATED W ORK
1064
1065   Masking in generation modeling. Chang et al. introduce MaskGIT (Chang et al., 2022), which
1066
       employs a bidirectional transformer decoder and is capable of learning to predict randomly masked
       tokens via attending to tokens in all directions during training. When inference, MaskGIT first
1067
       generates all tokens of an image and then refines the generated image iteratively based on the previous
1068
       generation. Recently, Chang et al. propose Muse (Chang et al., 2023) and train it to predict randomly
1069   masked image tokens given the text embedding extracted from a pre-trained large language model
1070   (LLM). Leveraging LLM enables Muse to understand fine-grained language, translate to high-fidelity
1071   image generation, etc. Moreover, Muse directly enables inpainting, outpainting, and mask-free
1072   editing without the need to fine-tune or invert the model. Li et al. (Li et al., 2023c) propose to
1073   use semantic tokens learned by a vector-quantized GAN at inputs and outputs and combine this
1074   with masking to unify representation learning and image generation. Bandara et al. propose an
1075   adaptive masking strategy called AdaMAE (Bandara et al., 2023). AdaMAE samples visible tokens
1076   based on the semantic context using an auxiliary sampling network and empirically demonstrates the
1077
       efficacy. Xiao et al. introduce a simple yet effective adaptive masking over masking strategy called
       AMOM (Xiao et al., 2023) to enhance the refinement capability of the decoder and make the encoder
1078
       optimization easier.
