                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014            Recent advancements in machine learning have significantly improved the iden-
015
               tification of disease-associated genes from gene expression datasets. However,
               these processes often require extensive expertise and manual effort, limiting their
016
               scalability. Large Language Model (LLM)-based agents have shown promise in au-
017
               tomating these tasks due to their increasing problem-solving abilities. To leverage
018            the potential of agentic system, we introduce GenoAgent, a team of LLM-based
019            agents designed with context-aware planning, iterative correction, and domain
020            expert consultation to collaboratively explore gene datasets. GenoAgent provides
021            generalized approach for addressing a wide range of gene identification problems,
022            in a completely automated analysis pipeline that follows the standard of computa-
023            tional genomics. Our experiments with GenoAgent demonstrate the potential of
024            LLM-based approaches in genomics data analysis, while error analysis highlights
025            the challenges and areas for future improvement. We also propose GenoTEX, a
026
               benchmark dataset for automatic exploration of gene expression data, and also a
               promising resource for evaluating and enhancing AI-driven methods for genomics
027
               data analysis.

      In biomedical research, gene analysis is crucial for understanding biological mechanisms and advanc-
      ing clinical applications such as disease marker identification and personalized medicine. Advances in
034
      next-generation sequencing and other technologies have led to a surge in the volume of transcriptomic
035
      data. Genomics research is expected to produce between 2 and 40 exabytes of data in the next decade
036   Institute (2024), greatly facilitating research and discoveries in genomics.
037
038   Despite the scientific value of gene data analysis, these tasks are often repetitive, labor-intensive,
039
      and prone to errors BPC (2023). The rapid increase in transcriptomic data and potentially inefficient
      workflows lead to considerable financial burden Intelligence (2023). The genetics research industry
040
      incurs an annual expense of around $848.3 million on manual data analysis tasks Research and
041
      Markets (2024), with costs expected to increase at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 12%
042   Research and Markets (2024) to 16% Research (2024) by 2030. Bioinformaticians spend significant
043   effort on these repetitive tasks, valued at around $29 per hour Payscale. This high volume of routine
044   tasks greatly impacts job satisfaction among bioinformatics professionals, as surveys show that
045   data scientists, including bioinformaticians, prefer engaging in advanced analytical tasks rather than
046   routine data processing. Currently, up to 45% of their work hours are spent on tasks that could be
047   automated Woodie (2020). These financial and workforce challenges highlight the urgent need for
048   more efficient and cost-effective data analysis solutions in genetics research Bartley (2023).
049   Meanwhile, the increasing abilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) OpenAI (2024) have enabled
050   methods for automating certain data analysis tasks Ma et al. (2023); Arasteh et al. (2024), and
051   relevant benchmarks have been proposed Stühler et al. (2023); Eldeeb et al. (2024). However, these
052   studies have mostly focused on simplified synthetic datasets, or specific steps in the analyze pipeline
053   such as missing data imputation or hyper-parameter tuning. In contrast, analysis on real-world
      gene expression data involves complex domain-specific procedures, and inherently requires the

      flexible planning, troubleshooting, and domain knowledge inference typically performed by a human
055   bioinformatician, posing higher demands on automatic methods.

      3.1   M OTIVATION AND ROLE DESIGN
125
126   When human experts engage in complex genomic analysis tasks, they demonstrate several key abilities,
127   including procedural memory, context-aware planning, tool utilization, and domain knowledge
128   inference. We believe that integrating these components is essential for enabling agent systems to
129   navigate the complexities of gene data analysis.
130
      Inspired by the workflows of human bioinformaticians, we propose GenoAgent, a team of LLM-based
131   agents, each equipped with several fundamental features to effectively tackle the challenges of data
132   preprocessing and gene expression analysis.
133
134   Procedural Memory Our agent will develop a comprehensive set of guidelines and action se-
135   quences for genomic analysis tasks, including optimal parameter selection for data normalization
136   and variant calling. These procedures will be dynamically refined through experience, mirroring the
137   expertise development seen in bioinformaticians. Formally, let P represent the set of procedures and
138   E denote the experience gained. The refinement process can be expressed as P ′ = f (P, E), where
139   P ′ is the updated set of procedures and f adjusts them based on accumulated experience.
140
141   Context-aware planning and error corrections Before initiating any task, the agent reviews
142
      its historical actions and the current genomic analysis context. This review can be formalized as
      D(Ht , Ct ), where Ht = {a1 , a2 , . . . , at−1 } represents the history of actions and Ct represents the
143
      current analysis context. This function helps the agent make informed decisions about the next
144
      steps, such as adjusting analysis parameters or revising data filters, and to correct any prior errors or
145   inaccuracies. This capability is crucial for ensuring the adaptability and reliability of genomic data
146   analyses.
147
148   Tool Utilization Upon deciding on an action, the agent utilizes a curated library of bioinformatics
149   code snippets to perform tasks efficiently. This method is akin to a bioinformatician using well-
150   established bioinformatics libraries. The agent selects the optimal tool by minimizing both time
151   and error, which can be modeled as T = argminTi ∈T (Time(Ti , task) + Error(Ti , task)), where
152   T represents the available tools. If a novel task arises, the agent develops new scripts T ′ =
153   GenerateNewTool(task), ensuring both speed and precision in handling complex genomic data.
154
155   Domain Knowledge Inference The agent observes the metadata of the dataset and intermediate
156   processing results, using domain knowledge to infer the desired information. This inference process
157   is modeled as I(K, D) → True or False, where K is the domain knowledge and D represents the
158
      dataset. This allows the agent to check whether their code works as expected, ensuring the accuracy
      and reliability of their genomic analyses.
159
160   The GenoAgent team consists of various specialized roles, each contributing unique expertise to
161   the analysis process. A Project Manager coordinates the analysis process for solving each gene
      identification problem, assigning tasks to agents according to the standardized pipeline from our

192   3.2   C OLLABORATION AMONG LLM AGENTS
193
194   This subsection introduces the two main patterns of collaboration between agents.
195
196
      Code review and iterative debugging This process involves the interaction between the Code
197
      Reviewer and a programming agent (Statistician or Data Engineer). Let R(v) represent the review
198
      function performed by the Code Reviewer, where v is the code version. If the execution of v fails,
199   the reviewer evaluates it based on its execution result, error-free status, and compliance with the
200   instructions. Then the reviewer either approves the code, or rejects it with feedback. Based on
201   the feedback, the programming agent refines the code, representing with P (v, f ), where f is the
202   feedback from the reviewer, generating new versions vi+1 = P (vi , f ). This process iterates, with the
203   agent generating vi for i = 1, 2, . . . , n, until either R(vn ) = approved or the maximum debugging
204   rounds nmax are reached. This mechanism facilitates troubleshooting and improves adherence to
205   task instructions, as shown in Figure 4 in Appendix.
206
207
      Domain-guided programming The second collaboration pattern involves a Data Engineer con-
208
      sulting a Domain Expert for data preprocessing tasks that require specialized knowledge. The Data
209
      Engineer sends questions to the Domain Expert, providing the necessary context such as metadata,
210   summary information about a dataset, or other intermediate results in data processing. Let D represent
211   the dataset and P denote preprocessing functions. The Data Engineer may formulate queries of the
212   form Q(D), seeking P (D). The Domain Expert then provides answers in the form of executable code,
213   as shown in 5 in Appendix. This type of programming also undergoes a debugging process, where
214   execution results R = P (D) are sent back to the same Domain Expert. Some questions are complex
215   enough that the Domain Expert may not provide the correct answer immediately, necessitating further
      refinement based on the execution results R and adjustments to the preprocessing functions P .

227   3.3      S TANDARDIZED PIPELINE FOR GENE EXPRESSION DATA ANALYSIS
228
      Our study aims to automate the gene expression data analysis process to address a class of important
229
      problems: What are the significant genes associated with a specific trait, given the influence of some
230
      condition? Here, a “trait” refers to a characteristic such as a disease (e.g., diabetes), and a “condition”
231   refers to a factor like age, gender, or a co-existing trait (e.g., hypertension). By incorporating
232   these factors into our analysis, we aim to gain a more comprehensive understanding of the genetic
233   underpinnings of these traits.
234
      Thus, to enhance the reliability of our GenoAgent, we have developed a standardized pipeline, serving
235
      as an instructive guideline for data preprocessing and statistical analysis tasks, detailed in Appendix A.
236
      This pipeline mirrors the steps a skilled bioinformatician would follow, enabling systematic evaluation
237   of the automated methods against established human expertise. In the following subsection, we
238   introduce this pipeline in detail and provide the necessary background knowledge to understand its
239   significance and application in our research.
240
241   3.3.1           DATA PREPROCESSING
242
243   The preprocessing of gene expression data involves a comprehensive pipeline with several main steps
244   such as dataset filtering and selection, gene data preprocessing, trait data extraction, and data linking.
245   Below we introduce the preprocessing steps for gene expression data within our pipeline. Please refer
246   to our guidelines file in Appendix A for more details. Fig. 2 shows the pipeline of preprocessing a
247
      series dataset from the GEO database.
248
249   Dataset filtering and selection When selecting datasets for gene expression data analysis, the
250   process involves the following steps: (i) Initial filtering. We assess each dataset’s relevance by
251
      reviewing its metadata, ensuring the availability of gene expression data and confirming the traits
      of interest; (ii) Quality verification. Datasets with abnormalities unresolved during preprocessing
252
      are discarded to maintain quality; (iii) Dataset selection. Given the high dimensionality of gene
253
      expression data, we prioritize datasets with the largest sample sizes for single-trait analyses. For
254   two-trait analyses, we select the dataset pair with the highest product of their sample sizes.
255
256
      Gene data preprocessing In this step, we prepare a data table where each attribute represents the
257
      expression level of a specific gene within a sample. We map the initial identifiers to gene symbols
258   using platform-specific gene annotation data, then normalize and deduplicate these gene symbols
259   by querying gene databases via APIs to prevent potential inaccuracies due to different gene naming
260   conventions. This process requires flexible planning and proficient use of bioinformatics tools to
261   ensure accuracy and consistency.
262
263   Data linking In this step, we merge the preprocessed gene data with the extracted trait data based
264   on the sample IDs. This integration creates a data table containing both genetic and clinical features
265   for the same samples, ready for association studies to identify significant genes.
266
267   3.3.2           S TATISTICAL ANALYSIS
268
269   After preprocessing, one can perform basic regression analysis to identify the genes that are predictive
      of the disease (or trait) Ghosh and Chinnaiyan (2005); Wu et al. (2009). Lasso Tibshirani (1996) is


                                                                                                                      5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      often chosen as the model due to its ability to identify a sparse set of genes. In addition to directly
271   using regression model, some other steps are often taken.
272
273
274
      Confounding factor correction To ensure reliable identification of genes, the pipeline often
      involves steps to correct potential confounding factors Leek et al. (2010); Bruning et al. (2016). One
275
      type of confounding factor arises when the distribution of gene expressions varies across subgroups
276
      within the data due to different background distributions rather than the disease itself Yu et al. (2006).
277   This variation can introduce significant bias, leading to incorrect conclusions where the association
278   between certain genes and the disease might be mistakenly attributed to differences in gene expression
279   distributions across groups, rather than a true link to the disease Wang et al. (2022a).
280
281
      Incorporating conditions in regression Additionally, one can include additional covariates in the
282
      regression model, such as patient demographics and co-occurrence of other diseases Kyalwazi et al.
283   (2023). Including these conditions allows for identifying gene expression patterns that are not only
284   associated with the disease status but also modulated by these conditions. This nuanced analysis
285   supports the development of more personalized treatment strategies by identifying how different
286   conditions affect gene-disease relationships Rosenquist et al. (2023). This practice is encouraged due
287   to the need for “precision medicine” Hamburg and Collins (2010); Chan and Ginsburg (2011).

359   4.2     TASKS AND METRICS
360
361
      Dataset selection and filtering We evaluate the performance of Dataset Filtering and Dataset
      Selection seperately. The former is a binary classification task, and we use F1 as the primary metric;
362
      For the latter, we use accuracy to measure the percentage of problems for which the method chooses
363
      the same dataset (or pairs of datasets) as the bioinformations did in our benchmark.
364
365
      Preprocessing To evaluate the performance of different methods, we adopted the following
366
      metrics: (i) Attribute Jaccard (AJ) is the Jaccard similarity between sets of attributes of two datasets.
367
      It evaluates how well the method extracts attributes from the dataset by encoding clinical features and
368   normalizing gene symbols. (ii) Sample Jaccard (SJ) is the Jaccard similarity between sets of sample
369   IDs of two datasets. It measures how well the method integrates features of the same samples and
370   handles missing values. Based on these metrics, we define (iii) Composite Similarity Correlation
371   (CSC) as the product of the Attribute Jaccard, Sample Jaccard, and the Pearson correlation of the
372   common feature vectors (common rows and columns) between the datasets. This metric captures
373   both the structural and content similarity of the resulting datasets, so we consider it as the primary
374   metric for evaluation preprocessing alignment.
375
376   Statistical analysis The goal of statistical analysis is to identify sigificant genes related to traits.
377   To evaluate this process, we adopt multiple metrics such as precision, recall, and Jaccard index. The
      Jaccard index evaluates the similarity between the sets of genes identified by our method and the

378
      gold standard. We also consider gene identification as a binary classification problem of predicting
379   whether a gene is related to the trait, and use Precision, Recall, and F1 to measure the performance.

474   6    C ONCLUSION
475
476   In this work, we introduced GenoAgent, a team of LLM-based agents demonstrating the potential of
477   large language models in facilitating the automatic exploration of gene expression data for identifying
478   disease-associated genes. By incorporating mechanism of iterative code review and domain experts
479
      programming into standard pipeline, we provide a robust framework for developing and enhancing
      automated methods. Our experiments highlight both the strengths and limitations of these agents,
480
      underscoring the need for further research to address challenges in nuanced human judgment and
481
      data anomalies. We also proposed GenoTEX, which is poised to be a useful resource in evaluating
482   and advancing AI-driven genomics data analysis, promoting efficiency, accuracy, and scalability in
483   biomedical research.

      A    G UIDELINES FOR GENE EXPRESSION DATA ANALYSIS
770
      To tackle the complexities of gene expression data analysis, we have established a set of compre-
771
      hensive guidelines shown below. These guidelines try to replicate the detailed processes of a skilled
772
      bioinformatician, covering dataset preprocessing, selection, and statistical analysis. By follow-
773   ing these standardized procedures, we seek to improve consistency and reliability in our manual
774   benchmark curation.
775
776
        This document describes the standardized pipeline for analyzing gene
        expression data for identifying disease-associated genes, involving
777     dataset preprocessing, selection, and statistical analysis. These
778     steps follow the practices of computational genomics and ensure the
779     reproducibility and reliability of the analysis.
780
781
        Data Sources and Organization:
        - Gene expression data are sourced from two public databases,
782     organized by trait in specific subdirectories:
783         - Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO): Data are downloaded under certain
784      criteria and saved under the path "{data_root}/GEO". Within this
785     directory, datasets related to each trait are organized in
786
        subdirectories named after the trait.
            - The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) data via the Xena platform: Data
787     are saved under the path "{data_root}/TCGA". Similar to GEO, datasets
788     related to each cancer type are organized in subdirectories named
789     after the specific cancer trait.
790
791
        Problem Setting Differentiation:
        - If the problem is to identify significant genes predictive of a
792     trait (optionally conditioning on age or gender, but not involving
793     another trait), prepare the data related to this trait.
794     - If the problem is to identify significant genes predictive of a
795     trait while conditioning on another trait, prepare data for both
796
        traits. These datasets will be integrated in a two-step regression
        process.
797

799     PART I. GEO Data Preprocessing
800
801
        Step 1: Initial Data Loading
        1. Identify the names of the SOFT file and Matrix file of the Series
802     data.
803     2. Read the Matrix file to obtain background information and clinical
804     trait data. This involves extracting the text data of series titles,
805     summaries, and overall designs, as well as the tabular data of sample
806
        characteristics.
        3. Get the unique values of all attributes in the sample
807     characteristics table into a Python dictionary.
808     4. Print the background information and the sample characteristics
809     dictionary for later observation.



                                                      15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
       Step 2: Dataset Analysis and Clinical Feature Extraction
811    1. Read the metadata to determine if the dataset is likely to contain
812    gene expression data (which does not include miRNA data or methylation
813     data).
814    2. Based on the metadata and the sample characteristics dictionary,
       for each of the variables of interest (e.g., a specific trait, age,
815
       gender):
816        a. Assess the availability of data.
817        b. If available, identify the key in the sample characteristics
818    dictionary where unique values of this variable are recorded.
819        c. Choose the appropriate data type (continuous, binary, or
       categorical) and design conversion functions to encode the features
820
       into that type.
821    3. Conduct initial filtering. If either the gene data or trait data is
822     not available, discard this dataset; otherwise, continue with the
823    following steps.
824
       Step 3: Gene Data Extraction
825
       1. Read the Matrix file to extract the tabular gene expression data
826    into a dataframe.
827    2. Print the first few row identifiers in the dataframe for later
828    observation.
829    3. Determine if the row identifiers are human gene symbols or other
       types that require mapping.
830
831    Step 4: Gene Annotation (Conditional)
832    1. If gene mapping is required, extract the gene annotation table from
833     the SOFT file.
834    2. Preview the gene annotation table for later observation.
835
       Step 5: Gene Identifier Mapping
836    1. If gene mapping is required, identify the columns for the
837    identifiers and gene symbols from the gene annotation table.
838    2. Create a mapping dataframe and apply it to the gene expression data.
839     Handle many-to-many relationships between probe IDs and gene symbols
       by splitting concatenated strings of symbols using separators such as
840
       semicolons (;), vertical bars (|), double slashes (//), and commas (,).
841     Assign the corresponding expression values to each gene symbol linked
842     to an identifier. Finally, aggregate the expression values for each
843    gene symbol by averaging the values from multiple probes, with the aim
844     of accurately representing the expression level of each gene symbol.
845
       Step 6: Data Normalization and Merging
846    1. Normalize the gene symbols in the gene data by querying databases
847    with the Python MyGene library, setting the ‘scopes’ parameter
848    properly. Remove data corresponding to genes that cannot be normalized.
849     For genes that normalize to the same symbol, deduplicate by averaging
        their expression values.
850
       2. Merge the clinical data with the normalized gene data on sample IDs.
851
852    3. Handle missing values. Drop records with the clinical trait missing
853     or with more than 20% of the gene features missing. Use mean
854    imputation for other missing values in the gene expression data.
       4. Observe the resulting dataset for quality verification. If the
855
       dataset is successfully preprocessed, save the merged data to a CSV
856    file.

860
       Step 1: Initial Data Loading
861    1. Identify the names of the clinical data file and the genetic data
862    file, and load them into two separate dataframes. For gene expression,
863     we choose the ‘gene expression RNAseq’ dataset instead of its PANCAN
       normalized or percentile versions.

866    1. Print and observe the column names of the clinical data file.
867    Identify all columns that might hold relevant data for age and gender
868    from the list of column names.
       2. Inspect the first few values of all candidate columns. Select a
869
       single column from the candidate columns that accurately records age
870    and gender information, respectively, considering meaningful values
871    and minimal missing data.
872    3. Based on metadata of the TCGA database, use a simple rule to
873    convert the trait (whether the sample has the particular type of
       cancer) to binary values.
874
       4. Conduct initial filtering. If all samples have the same target
875    values, or if the clinical dataset shows other abnormalities, discard
876    the dataset. Otherwise, continue with the next step.

       1. Normalize the gene symbols in the gene data by querying databases
879
       with the Python MyGene library, setting the ‘scopes’ parameter
880    properly. Remove data corresponding to genes that cannot be normalized.
881     For genes that normalize to the same symbol, deduplicate by averaging
882     their expression values.
883    2. Merge the clinical and genetic datasets on sample IDs.
       3. Handle missing values. Drop records with the clinical trait missing
884
        or with more than 20% of the gene features missing. Use mean

890    PART III. Statistical Analysis
891
892    Step 1: Data Selection and Loading
893    1. Select the best input data relevant to the gene identification
       problem, and load the data into a dataframe. If multiple preprocessed
894
       datasets are available for statistical analysis about a trait, we
895    select the one with the largest sample size.
896    2. If the analysis requires integrating datasets about two traits, we
897    sort the possible pairs of datasets for both traits by the product of
898    their sample sizes, and select the pair with the largest product. Load
        data for the trait and condition into separate dataframes and select
899
       common gene regressors.
900
901    Step 2: Data Wrangling
902    1. Extract the relevant data columns and convert into numpy arrays for
903     analysis. Get the data matrices of features, the target variable, and
        also the condition when applicable.
904
       2. For two-step regression, this needs to be done twice. In the first
905    step, the features are the common gene regressors, and the target is
906    the condition, and we need to extract these matrices from the
907    condition dataset. The second step follows other cases for extracting
908    relevant data.
909
       Step 3: Condition Prediction (Only for Two-Step Regression)
910    1. Determine the variable type (binary, continuous, or categorical) of
911     the condition.
912    2. Select a simple regression model based on the type of the target
913    variable, and train it to regress the condition on the common gene
       regressors in the condition dataset.
914
       3. Use the trained model to predict the condition values in the trait
915    dataset using the common gene regressors. Remove the columns in the
916    trait dataset corresponding to the common regressors, and add the
917    predicted condition values to it as a new column.



                                                   17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
        Step 4: Model Selection Based on Batch Effect
919     1. Assess whether the dataset shows batch effects by observing gaps in
920      eigenvalues. Choose the appropriate model based on the presence of
921     batch effects. Use a Linear Mixed Model (LMM) if batch effects are
922     detected. Otherwise, use a Lasso model.
923
        Step 5: Data Normalization
924     1. For the feature matrix, and the condition matrix (if applicable),
925     apply Z-score normalization so that each feature has a mean of 0 and
926     standard deviation of 1. Make sure this is done every time before
927     training the model.
928
        Step 6: Hyperparameter Tuning
929     1. Do 5-fold cross-validation, and perform hyperparameter search on
930     the logarithm scale with base of 10. Record the best hyperparameter
931     settings.
932
        Step 7: Model Training
933
        1. Train the model on the entire dataset, with the best
934     hyperparameters found during cross-validation. For conditional
935     analyses, incorporate the condition matrix into the model.
936
937     Step 8: Model Interpretation
        1. Interpret the trained model to identify significant factors and
938
        effects. For Lasso, choose gene variables with non-zero coefficients.
939     For LMM, apply the Benjamini-Hochberg correction for multiple
940     hypothesis testing, and select variables whose corrected p-value is
941     less than 0.05.
942     2. Save the regression output to a JSON file, with the identified
        genes and the corresponding coefficient or p-values.

1099   C     C RITERIA FOR MANUAL CORRECTION OF TRAIT- CONDITION PAIRS
1100
1101   To ensure the scientific validity of our benchmark questions, we apply specific rules for including and
1102   excluding certain trait-condition pairs. Each biomedical entity in our list can be considered a trait and
1103   paired with a condition, where the condition is either another entity from the list or a demographic
1104   attribute like "age" or "gender." The following criteria are designed to maintain scientific relevance
1105
       and robustness:
1106
              • Trait-Condition Role Assignment: Entities such as language abilities, Vitamin D levels,
1107
                and bone density are included only as conditions and not as traits. This distinction ensures
1108            that the primary focus remains on traits with more direct clinical implications, while these
1109            entities serve as influential factors that could affect those traits.
1110
              • Universal Conditions: Entities such as obesity, hypertension, and mental disorders like
1111
                anxiety disorder and bipolar disorder are designated as conditions to be paired with all other
1112            traits. This is because these conditions are widespread and significantly impact various
1113            health outcomes, making them critical factors to consider in any genetic analysis.
1114
              • Gender-Specific Considerations: Gender-specific entities such as prostate cancer, en-
1115            dometriosis, and breast cancer are not conditioned on gender. Furthermore, entities from
1116            different genders are not paired. This approach respects the biological distinctions between
1117            genders and ensures that the resulting questions remain relevant and meaningful.
1118
              • Cancer Category Exclusion: Pairs where both the trait and the condition belong to the
1119            cancer category are excluded. This is because investigating genetic factors behind one type
1120            of cancer conditioned on another type of cancer is often less scientifically important. The
1121            focus is placed on broader, more impactful genetic relationships that offer greater insight
1122            into cancer biology.
1123
1124   These criteria are used in combination with the Jaccard similarity of related genes (Section 3.2), to
1125   uphold the scientific integrity and relevance of the benchmark questions, facilitating meaningful and
1126   insightful gene expression analysis.

1151
             BENCHMARK
1152
1153
       Gene expression data analysis is a complex and specialized task. Despite their problem-solving
1154   abilities, state-of-the-art LLMs and agent-based methods struggle with gene expression data. Our
1155   evaluations of methods such as GPT-4o OpenAI (2024), MetaGPT Hong et al. (2023), and CodeAct
1156   Wang et al. (2024) revealed consistent failures across various settings.
1157
       We tested these methods under three different settings: (i) providing general task instructions, (ii)
1158
       providing detailed task instructions used by GenoAgent, and (iii) providing detailed task instructions
1159
       and all necessary library functions as in GenoAgent. Each setting was tested on a subset of 50 gene
1160   identification problems. Our results show that none of the methods generated runnable code for
1161   preprocessing datasets downloaded from GEO. Persistent errors in the generated code prevented
1162   testable outputs, regardless of the level of detail provided.
1163
       First, we find that when preprocessing GEO data, these methods often fail at dataset loading in the
1164
       initial steps. The gene expression data files follow special formats. The agent struggles to extract
1165
       tabular data embedded in the text file by identifying special markers, skipping metadata rows, and

       Discussion The randomness observed may stem from the LLM itself, suggesting a need to prevent
1366
       one agent from misleading another. During the development of our baseline methods, we implemented
1367   several prompt engineering techniques to mitigate this issue: (i) Limiting the Reviewer’s feedback
1368   to three main suggestions to focus on problem-solving rather than providing numerous distracting
1369   comments about code quality, and (ii) Encouraging the agent receiving the review to critically
1370   evaluate the feedback and possibly retain its original code. While these measures have alleviated
1371   some issues, they persist to some extent in our GenoAgent baseline. A promising future direction
1372   involves designing collaborative modes that foster iterative discussions among agents to reconcile
1373   differing opinions and enhance their task performance abilities.
1374   We hope this discussion highlights the challenges of our benchmark tasks and encourages future work
1375   to address these issues.
