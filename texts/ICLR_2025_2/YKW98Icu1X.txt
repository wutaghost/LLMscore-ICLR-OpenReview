011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013
               Learning and skill mastery requires extensive and deliberate practice. In many
               learning settings, producing high-quality pedagogical materials can require a high
014
               level of domain expertise and be very time-consuming. Pedagogical materials
015
               often need to train students to engage in different thinking patterns. In some do-
016            mains, such as chess, puzzles are used to help students practice their skills in
017            calculating the next moves and recognizing known patterns on a board. Giving
018            students a practice set of puzzles to help them learn different modes of thinking
019            is challenging because the teacher needs to carefully balance between different
020            motifs and how many look-ahead steps a student needs to perform. Popular online
021            platforms like Chess.com and Lichess offer players millions of puzzles. Unlike
022            chess tactics puzzles procured by human experts, where chess beginners can learn
023            valuable insights, these puzzles are automatically generated and often regarded as
024
               having low pedagogical values. These platforms also rely on a heuristic to rec-
               ommend puzzles to users for practice. Using the user history data over an entire
025
               year, a total of 1.6 billion puzzle-solving histories, we learn the pedagogical value
026
               of a puzzle and how to automatically choose a set of puzzles to better support
027            chess learners in a completely unstructured way using insights from offline re-
028            inforcement learning. We validate the quality of the puzzles discovered by our
029            model by collecting annotation ratings from titled chess players. The success of
030            our pipeline shows promise for a future where we can understand the pedagogical
031            values of practice items in other domains like math or coding problems.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Practice makes perfect. The foundation of gaining knowledge or mastering a new skill relies on
036
      countless hours of mindful and delibrate practice (Ericsson et al., 1993; Anders Ericsson, 2008).
      Koedinger et al. (2023) suggests that across many different laerning settings, when students are
037
      provided with high-quality, curated learning materials, they can all learn at a similar rate and achieve
038
      success with extensive practice. However, the creation of high-quality learning materials is often a
039   key bottleneck. Though lectures can be recorded in video and knowledge can be transcribed in text,
040   students need to be able to practice what they have learned through forced retrieval and synthesis,
041   which has been shown to greatly enhance learning (Roediger III et al., 2011). Producing a large
042   amount of practice materials often requires high human expertise and heavy time investment. It is
043   also unclear how effective some of the learning materials are in terms of helping students achieve
044   mastery of certain skills.
045
      In chess, players often learn by playing against each other directly. However, they also learn impor-
046
      tant skills through tactics books, which are comprised of puzzles – subgames limited to a few moves
047
      to teach important concepts (called motifs) or to train players to plan multiple moves in a sequence
048   in order to gain more advantage over their opponent. These puzzles are very common for beginners
049   and are considered good learning materials because they isolate and highlight difficult concepts into
050   a small subgame and prime beginners into a habit of thinking strategically (Henkin, 2021). Online
051   chess platforms such as Chess.com and Lichess provide puzzles for players to practice. In order to
052   keep their players engaged, these online platforms aim to serve fresh puzzles on a weekly basis so
053   that players will always have new materials for learning and fun. Chess.com serves roughly 0.5M
      puzzles to their players and Lichess hosts over 3.8M puzzles.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      In order to produce a large number of new puzzles quickly, unlike classic tactics books where puz-
055   zles are curated by human experts through careful deliberation, review, and editing, online chess
056   platforms use an algorithm to automatically generate puzzles from the actual games played by play-
057   ers during that week. On a high level, this automatic algorithm captures some human intuition that
058   each puzzle needs to have a sequence of best moves, where deviating from the best move will result
059   in a large decrease in the player’s win rate in that game. The puzzles are assigned with an Elo rating
060   and then served to players randomly if the difficulty is somewhat within the range of the player. We
061   do not know whether these puzzles are high-quality teaching materials – whether they are effective
062   at increasing the long-term chess knowledge of the player. In a system where learning materials
063
      can be automatically generated, it is crucial to filter out materials that do not lead to an effective
      long-term increase in knowledge or skill.
064
065   Assessing the quality of a learning material requires two steps. First, we model the dynamics of a
066   player’s knowledge growth: a puzzle that increases the user’s internal knowledge is more valuable
067   than a puzzle that does not. We then need to know which puzzles are most effective at increasing
068   a player’s knowledge gain, where a player can gain knowledge faster by solving the right sequence
069   of puzzles. Offline reinforcement learning (RL) (Levine et al., 2020) has proposed a suite of tools
070   to learn and evaluate a policy through past data. Recommending chess puzzles to players can be
071
      modeled as a policy that decides on which puzzle to serve. Combined with a deep knowledge tracing
      model, we show that we can discover high-quality chess puzzles using historical data alone.
072
073   In the era of generative models, where large foundation models can automatically produce a massive
074   amount of educational materials at a moment’s notice, it becomes increasingly crucial to understand
075   which materials are more valuable and effective for learning than others. Using a large dataset from
076   Chess.com, which contains 2.9M users and their 1.6 Billion puzzle interactions over a year, we are
077   able to propose a learning material assessment model that combines deep knowledge tracing and
078   offline reinforcement learning. We designed a chess puzzle quality annotation schema and recruited
079   United States Chess Federation (USCF) titled players to validate our model. We show that our model
080
      can serve valuable and effective puzzles for players with some history of interactions and a known
      rating, a valuable contribution to the chess community. Even though we only demonstrated success
081
      in a single domain of chess puzzles, we expect the possibility of building such a system in other
082
      domains of learning, such as language, math, and coding.

      2   DATA
085
086   Our dataset consists of players’ puzzle history data from 3,132,428 unique, active users of a popular
087   chess website, Chess.com, playing a total of 1,536,254,297 puzzles (441,113 unique puzzles) over
088   the course of one year from March 2021 to March 2022. On average, a user played 490.4 puzzles
089   over this one year. Of those 490.4 puzzles, 96.9% are played within three minutes of another puzzle,
090   which indicates that players tend to play puzzles in short bursts of time. An average burst comprises
091
      5.1 games. Playing in bursts naturally suggests that users do not continuously play puzzles through-
      out the day. In fact, the average time between these bursts is approximately two and a half days. In
092
      general, our dataset reflects the engaged and extensive user base of online chess players.
093
094   Puzzle Serving According to Chess.com administrators, the site serves puzzles to users using
095   a bucketed uniform policy. This policy serves a player’s next puzzle by first bucketing all chess
096   puzzles that have puzzle ratings within ±200 points of a player’s ELO and then sampling uniformly
097   from that bucket. As a player begins to fail puzzles, that ±200 bucket eases to −300/+100 after one
098   incorrect puzzle, −400/+0 after two, −500/−100 after three, and −600/−200 after four incorrect
099   puzzles. In this way, the Chess.com policy adapts to player performance by serving progressively
100
      easier puzzles in response to a player’s difficulty in solving previously served puzzles. Overall,
      Chess.com’s bucketed uniform policy integrates a player’s performance on their past four puzzles,
101
      their ELO rating, and puzzle rating to determine the next puzzle served.
102
103   Chess Puzzle Chess puzzles are core units of learning. In a chess puzzle, the player is presented
104   with an initial position and tasked with finding the correct solution in one or more moves, adhering
105   to the puzzle’s main goal or task, described in part through a puzzle’s motifs. The intial position is
106   shown to the player as a board, and it can also be conveniently encoded as a FEN (Forsyth-Edwards
107   Notation) string. Move counts for puzzles in our dataset range from 1 to 16, averaging at 2.6. Harder
      puzzles tend to demand more moves, with the puzzle’s difficulty reflected in its puzzle rating. Our

      puzzles’ ratings range from 100 to 4000. In contrast to move count and puzzle rating, which are
109   both numerical, motifs provide more qualitative insights into the diversity of puzzles.

111   A puzzle’s motifs describe the primary strategy or skill required to solve the puzzle. Examples of
112   such motifs include “Exchange Sacrifice” and “Promotion,” describing a notable event that takes

      place in a puzzle. A puzzle can have more than one motif; in fact, the puzzles in our dataset have, on

114

160   2002; Silver et al., 2017; 2018). Although these models have developed superhuman abilities to
161   master the game, few investigations have focused on leveraging their knowledge to teach humans.
      Schut et al. (2023) and McGrath et al. (2022) did pioneering work on uncovering chess knowledge

      learned in AlphaGo and AlphaZero. McIlroy-Young et al. (2021) built models to identify the chess
163   playing styles of each user and then later released models that imitate chess players of different
164   levels (McIlroy-Young et al., 2022). But largely, there have not been end-to-end systems that can
165   successfully transfer knowledge learned in models to teach humans.
166
      On the building of automated systems to teach front, reinforcement learning has been used to create
167
      systems that can adaptively select teaching materials for students. Ruan et al. (2024) built a math
168
      tutoring tool that decides when to provide hints. Mandel et al. (2014) uses offline RL to augment

170   small in scale, and offline RL has not demonstrated usefulness for large-scale educational settings.
171   In our work, we follow the lead of Kumar et al. (2022) to scale offline RL models to large datasets
172   with billions of interactions and use offline RL to discover high-quality puzzles that can improve a
173   chess player’s learning experience.

255   4.3 A RCHITECTURE FOR C HESS P UZZLE R ECOMMENDATION
256   Since we formulate the recommendation problem as non-Markovian offline policy learning, we use
257   a transformer decoder architecture (i.e., casual transformer) to power our decision making policy,
258   denoted by TθT . At each sequence element of the transformer, we input a concatenation of a user
259
      representation ut and puzzle representation pt at the current time, constructed through applying a
      neural network on user and puzzle features respectively. The corresponding outputs at each se-
260
      quence element are (a) the prediction of the next puzzle π(a | s) and (b) the value function estimate
261
      V (s).
262
263   During training, we optimize Lπ , LQ LV on predictions at each sequence element, with training
264   sequences sampled uniformly from the chess puzzle interaction data. Importantly, we apply teacher
265
      forcing during the forward pass of training, which is standard in training autoregressive decoder-only
      transformers.

269   (MLP), denoted as Uθu . We normalize the output user embedding ut = Uθu (fu ) to unit norm (e.g.,
      L-2 normalization).

      For context, the chess legend Magnus Carlsen has an FIDE rating of 2832. The 100th highest-rated
363
      player has a FIDE rating of 2637 as of Fall 2024.
364
365   The puzzles are randomly sampled from a bucketed uniform policy based on the player’s rating
366   range, and we have 6 players with different Elo scores. We chose 12 puzzles as preference learning
367
      puzzles that are annotated by all experts. This means that out of the 30 puzzles each expert annotated,
      only 18 puzzles are unique between them. The annotation is performed through an anonymized
368
      spreadsheet. Each annotator is asked to spend 2 hours on the annotation and is later compensated
369
      with a gift card.

371   5.2 S CALING A NNOTATIONS WITH LLM
372   Judging whether a chess puzzle has high quality or not requires annotators with significant expertise,
373   who are distinctly different from standard crowd workers. This also means we are not able to
374   perform massive annotations that can take up to hundreds of hours. However, sometimes, two
375   policies that learn to recommend puzzles can behave very similarly and, therefore, require a lot of
376   annotations to understand if they are statistically significantly different. To help with scaling up the
377   annotation effort, inspired by Zheng et al. (2023); He et al. (2023), we use the expert annotations
      to calibrate an LLM that can imitate expert chess annotator’s judgments. Anecdotally, LLMs seem


                                                         7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      to be able to understand FEN strings and can play chess to some extent12 . These evidence gave us
379   belief that LLM has some preliminary understanding of chess.
380
381   With some example expert annotations, we choose to use DSPy (Khattab et al., 2023) to create our
382   LLM chess puzzle judge. DSPy is an LLM library that allows us to specify a set of initial prompts,
383   training data, and a metric. It then automatically selects the best expert examples to include in
384
      the LLM prompt that can maximize the metric. Internally, DSPy performs cross-validation with a
      random search to find the best subset of expert annotations.
385
386   Training Data We learn 8 LLM annotators using the data from 8 chess annotators. We separately
387   learn 8 annotators is because each annotator’s preference might be different and imitating the be-
388   havior of 8 individual seems more difficult than imitating the behavior of one individual with their
389   own data. Since each annotator produced 30 puzzle annotations, we split this into a training set of
390   12 puzzles, a validation set of 9 puzzles, and a test set of 9 puzzles.
391
      Prompt Design We use the DSPy Signature module to design the prompt. We specify three fields:
392   annotation guideline, the persona of the annotator (USCF ELO rating and title), user profile, and
393   puzzle board. We provide a complete description of each field in the appendix. For puzzle board
394   representation, we experimented with FEN string and a grid-like representation of the entire board
395   (with piece names and positions). We found the grid-like representation to work better. Along with
396   the board position, we also describe what the first move of the puzzle is in text.
397
      Optimization Metric Our task is a rating task, where experts rate each puzzle for categories in Ta-
398
      ble 4. Therefore, the metric we define is the average over mean absolute error (MAE) of each rating
399
      category. DSPy can only maximize a metric; therefore, we maximize the negative MAE.
400
401
      6       E XPERIMENT
402
403   To train our chess puzzle recommendation policy, the dataset is partitioned randomly by users, with
404   90% used for training and 10% for evaluation. During training, each batch consists of 256 examples
405   of users’ puzzle histories, with a sampled sequence length of 256 to accommodate computational
406   constraints. Consequently, we use roughly 65K puzzles to estimate our policy’s probabilities for
407   training (approximately 10% of all puzzles). We train the policy for 25K, alongside the learned
408
      action-value and value functions.
409   Evaluation using Importance Sampling To evaluate our trained policy with respect to the be-
410   havioural policy, we can leverage importance sampling, using the known probability distributions
411   of the behavioural policy (e.g., as deployed by chess.com) and our trained policy. Importantly, tra-
412   ditional importance sampling with sequences of size 256 (or even a fraction of that) is impractical
413   due to the significant induced variance in the estimator over large sequences. Instead, we employ a
414   one-step approximation (e.g., removing past weights) across 32-steps, which significantly reduces
415   variance at the cost of being biased (Chen et al., 2019). To further reduce variance, we clip the
416
      importance weight for each step πθ (a|s)/πβ (a|s) between ϵs = 0.1 and ϵ1s = 10.0.
417   To quantify our improvements over the behavioural policy for different users, we break down our
418   results into different ELO groups. Additionally, we examine groups of stagnant growth and different
419   growth groups, similarly to Figure 1; for this analysis, we use a simple definition of stagnant growth,
420   which is growth of no more than 50 ELO points across the sequence. Importantly, this allows for ex-
421   amination of our improvements on different types of users and learning patterns, e.g., improvements
422   on stagnant or new users may be more critical to retention on the chess.com platform.
