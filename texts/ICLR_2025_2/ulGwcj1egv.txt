011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Auto-regressive Large Language Models (LLMs) demonstrate remarkable perfor-
014            mance across domanins such as vision and language processing. However, due
015
               to sequential processing through a stack of transformer layers, autoregressive de-
               coding faces significant computation/latency challenges, particularly in resource-
016
               constrained environments like mobile and edge devices. Existing approaches in
017
               literature that aim to improve latency via skipping layers have two distinct flavors
018            - 1) Early exit 2) Input-agnostic heuristics where tokens exit at pre-determined
019            layers irrespective of input sequence. Both the above strategies have limitations
020            - the former cannot be applied to handle KV Caching necessary for speed-ups in
021            modern framework and the latter does not capture the variation in layer impor-
022            tance across tasks or more generally, across input sequences. To address both
023            limitations, we propose F I RST, an algorithm that reduces inference latency by
024            using layer-specific routers to select a subset of transformer layers adaptively for
025            each input sequence - the prompt (during prefill stage) decides which layers will
026
               be skipped during decoding. F I RST preserves compatibility with KV caching en-
               abling faster inference while being quality-aware. F I RST is model-agnostic and
027
               can be easily enabled on any pre-trained LLM. We further improve performance
028
               by incorporating LoRA adapters for fine-tuning on external datasets, enhancing
029            task-specific accuracy while maintaining latency benefits. Our approach reveals
030            that input adaptivity is critical - indeed, different task-specific middle layers play
031            a crucial role in evolving hidden representations depending on task. Extensive
032            experiments show that F I RST significantly reduces latency while retaining com-
033            petitive performance (as compared to baselines), making our approach an efficient
034            solution for LLM deployment in low-resource environments.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Large Language Models (LLM’s) have revolutionized the fields of Natural Language Processing and
      Computer Vision achieving incredible performance on a diverse set of benchmark tasks. However,
040
      the scale of these LLM’s characterized by billions of parameters hinder their adoption in resource-
041
      constrained environments with memory, latency and compute serving as the main challenges. In this
042   work, we focus on the latency aspect which becomes the most significant bottleneck for tasks such as
043   machine translation, question answering, summarization particularly on devices, such as laptops and
044   mobile phones. As mentioned in (Schuster et al., 2022), the auto-regressive nature of decoding in
045   LLM’s further pronounces the latency bottleneck. Our main interest lies in the resource-constrained
046   on-device setting where resolving this bottleneck is of particular importance.
047
      Transformer based LLMs have several stacks of layers (including attention and FFN layers) leading
048   to high latency and compute requirements, making inference very slow or even infeasible in resource
049   constrained settings. This is because of the sequential processing of tokens through all the layers
050   for every input sequence and task. However, it is important to note that in the real world, there is
051   a lot of heterogeneity in input sequences and tasks. (Schuster et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2022) noted
052   that the generations made by LLMs can have varying levels of difficulty and certain generations can
053   be solved with reduced compute, by exiting the transformer stack early. At the same time, it has
      been noted in recent works (Wendler et al., 2024) that inference forward pass proceeds in phases

054
      through the layers of transformer based models, with different types of information being extracted
055   or mapped at different phases (sequences of layers) for certain tasks such as translation. Motivated
056   by these and other related works, we hypothesize that different sequential combinations of layers
057   are important for different input sequences and tasks. Learning the right sequential combination
058   of layers can help reduce inference latency and compute for on-device scenarios. However, there
059   are several challenges. Any algorithm for determining the “right” combination of layers should
060   minimize any quality loss, be compatible with other latency reduction strategies such as KV cache
061   handling and batch inference, should not introduce any additional latency or compute and and be
062   learnable with minimal compute and training overhead.
063   In the last few years, several promising approaches have been proposed in literature that adaptively
064   prune layers at each decoding step. Token-level early exit proposed in (Schuster et al., 2022; Sun
065   et al., 2022) allow tokens to exit the transformer layer stack early based on different strategies to
066   compute the confidence or saturation level. (Elhoushi et al., 2024; Elbayad et al., 2020; Zhang et al.,
067   2019) extended this idea to incorporate layer skipping at a token level during training. While token
068   level early exit is a useful idea in theory, it suffers from a major limitation of incompatible KV
069
      Caching in practice (Del Corro et al., 2023). The incompatibility stems from having to recompute
      KV caches for preceding tokens if we have a delayed exit point for latter tokens often resulting in
070
      loss of early exit advantages. Since KV cache is crucial in significantly speeding up auto-regressive
071
      decoding, inappropriate handling of KV cache limits practical adoption.
072
073   Recently, (Liu et al., 2024; Del Corro et al., 2023) have proposed input-agnostic layer skipping at
074   token level, that handle KV cache appropriately as well as retain the advantage of adaptive partial
075
      computation. In these solutions, tokens exit at pre-determined layers irrespective of the input se-
      quence, and for all sequences in a batch, tokens at the same position in a sequence exit at the same
076
      layer. Furthermore, tokens at latter parts of the sequence are constrained to exit earlier than the
077
      previous tokens to ensure that there is no redundant KV cache re-computation. These solutions are
078   heuristic based and impose hard rules and constraints irrespective of input sequences, which can
079   lead to drop in output quality. Others (Jaiswal et al., 2024) have proposed circumventing the KV
080   cache issue entirely by skipping only FFN layers, but such a strategy cannot reduce redundancy in
081   transformer layer computations. Moreover, they propose an input adaptive skipping heuristic based
082   on cosine similarity of outputs: if two adjacent layers have a similarity greater than a threshold, then
083   all subsequent layers except the last few are skipped. However, such a strategy does not take into ac-
084   count that several middle layers are crucial (see (Liu et al., 2024)) and furthermore, final prediction
085   capability of full model is not taken into account while deciding which layers to skip.
086   Our goal is to design an input-adaptive learnable layer selection strategy with quality aware latency
087   gains that is also able to handle the KV Cache appropriately. Ideally, for every input sequence
088   and task, we want to predict the optimal (sequential) combination of layers at inference time, such
089   that quality loss is minimum and the latency gains are as high as possible. We want to do this
090   with expending very little compute/additional training, with no or minimal additional latency (for
091   inference) and handle KV cache appropriately. Since there are exponential number of possible
092
      layer sequences, this seems like a hard goal computationally and otherwise - however, we have
      addressed this layer selection challenge partially in this work. We propose an approach for learning
093
      and predicting the layer selections based on the input sequence and task, via training routers. Based
094
      on the output of each layer for a sequence, a router will decide whether or not to skip the subsequent
095   layer in the transformer architecture. Since the decision is at a sequence level, KV cache issues
096   do not arise, as all tokens in a sequence would pass through the same set of layers. Moreover, we
097   further generalize our approach to handle batch inference by making the layer selection unified for
098   all sequences in a batch. Finally, we fine-tune the model combined with trained routers using LoRA
099   adapters to improve the quality significantly while retaining the latency gains. As an added bonus,
100   LoRA finetuning smoothens the layer skipping 1 , reduces the amount of performance degradation
101   and further highlights the varied importance of layers based on input sequence.
102   We summarize our contributions below:

         selecting layers in an input-adaptive manner. The layer selection is uniform for all tokens in a

119   2   R ELATED W ORK
120
121   Early Exit: Several works have been proposed in the early exit theme (Zhu, 2021; Zhou et al.,
122   2020; Xin et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Hou et al., 2020; Schuster et al., 2022)
123   where adaptive compute is used for different parts of the token sequence. While these approaches
124   have been popular for encoder-only models which processes the entire sequence as a whole, they
125   have faced challenges in generation tasks. The main limitation of these set of techniques are their
126   inability to handle KV caching appropriately which is crucial for multi-fold speed-ups in current
127   LLM architectures. We emphasize that in our work, we assign varying compute to sequences in
128   different batches but within the same sequence, we assign the same compute to every token.
129   Input Agnostic Heuristics: In Skip Decoding (Del Corro et al., 2023), initial tokens pass through
130   more layers than later ones, contradicting the observation that later tokens are harder to decode (Liu
131   et al., 2024). Additionally, Skip Decoding skips several bottom layers for most tokens, causing
132   undesirable sub-network imbalance. To address this, Unified Layer Skipping (Liu et al., 2024) pro-
133   poses a discrete skipping strategy that is uniform for all tokens in a sequence. Based on a latency
134   budget, retained layer ids are passed through by all tokens, ensuring KV Cache handling and retain-
135
      ing key layers. However, the limitation of this approach is that skipping is independent of the input
      sequence. In contrast, early exit strategies adapt layer skipping to the input sequence, offering more
136
      flexibility. In (Fan et al., 2019), a method akin to dropout randomly skips layers during training,
137
      but this leads to performance decline during the pre-fill stage. FFN-SkipLLM (Jaiswal et al., 2024)
138   constrains skipping to FFN layers to avoid KV Cache issues but fails to fully exploit redundancy as
139   discussed already.
140
141
      Model Compression and Quantization Aware Training: Orthogonal approaches to explore the
      latency/memory-performance trade-off in Large Language Models aim to build smaller models that
142
      approximate the performance of larger ones with reduced memory and latency costs. Key techniques
143
      include: 1) compressing model parameters into fewer bits (Frantar et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2024; Lee
144   et al., 2024; Saha et al., 2023); 2) pruning the network by removing components like attention heads
145   or neurons based on heuristics (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Ma et al., 2023); and 3) distilling the large
146   model into a smaller, faster counterpart (Agarwal et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2024). For further details,
147   we refer to the survey by (Zhu et al., 2023). A significant body of work (Dettmers et al., 2024; Liu
148   et al., 2023b; Peri et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023) has focused on quantization-aware training to reduce
149   memory footprints and mitigate performance loss, starting with QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2024). In a
150   similar vein, our work proposes fine-tuning router-augmented models to improve layer skipping and
151   reduce performance degradation, as pre-trained models do not account for layer skipping, leading to
152
      higher degradation with vanilla skipping.

156   Our goal is to exploit the heterogeneity in inputs and tasks to selectively use LLM layers in a quality
157   aware manner for reducing inference latency and compute for on-device constraints. Ideally, we

159   and task, such that the overall latency as well as expended computation are both low, while quality
160   is comparable to the un-modified case where every input sequence passes through every layer. For
161   ease of explanation, without loss of generality, we assume the task is same and simply consider an
      input sequence for describing the problem.

256   In this section we describe the training and inference frameworks and procedure for F I RST in de-
257   tails. We first describe how to train Routers to be adaptive to input sequences. Given an off-the-shelf
258
      pre-trained LLM, we propose two training phases (Figure: 2). In the first phase, we train a router
      for each layer that decides whether the tokens in the input sequence should skip the layer or not. In
259
      the second phase, to tackle the issue of unseen skipping during pre-training, we fine-tune the router-
260
      augmented LLM keeping router weights fixed using LoRA (low rank adapters) to ensure the model
261   learns to perform well on the target dataset without reducing the skipping level. In other words,
262   the LoRA fine-tuning ensures that the gap in performance with and without skipping is significantly
263   reduced when compared to the base model. Below, we provide the details of each phase.

      5.3   I NFERENCE FOR F I RST
351
352   During inference, given an input sequence, the decision to skip or pass through a layer is determined
353   by a threshold. For the input sequence, each router (corresponding to a layer) outputs a number
354   in the interval [0, 1]. If this number (corresponding to the probability of not skipping the layer) is
355   greater than or equal to 0.5, the sequence passes through the layer. On the other hand, if the output
356   from the router is less than 0.5, the sequence skips the layer (Figure: 3). Below, we describe some
357   salient points about the functioning of the router during inference to handle KV Cache appropriately
358   to retain the modern latency speed-ups:
359   1. Prefill phase handling: Skipping is not allowed during prefill phase. This ensures the first token
360      is generated correctly, which is crucial for WMT tasks, as they are highly sensitive to the correct
361      generation of the first token in the target language. It has been observed in prior works (Liu et al.,
362      2024) that skipping during prefill phase is detrimental to performance during inference.
363   2. Fixed router decisions during decoding and handling KV Cache: During the prefill phase,
364      the decisions made by the routers are cached. During the decoding phase, every token adheres
365
         to the cached decision made during prefill. In other words, for a particular layer, if a router
         outputs a number less than 0.5 during prefill, the number is fixed for the decoding steps and
366
         therefore the same layer will be skipped by all tokens during decoding. Similarly, if the router
367
         outputs a number more than 0.5 during prefill, the same layer will be processing all tokens during
368      decoding. Such a step ensures that for each decoding step and each layer that is not skipped,
369      the KV cache for all previous tokens is available for that layer - this is because a fixed set of
370      layers (decided during the prefill phase) will be skipped for all tokens during the decoding phase
371      of inference. This approach effectively addresses the caching issues encountered in early exit
372      strategies, ensuring consistent decisions across the decoding process.

382   methodology outlined in previous studies (Liu et al., 2023a; Jiao et al., 2023). Translation perfor-
383   mance is evaluated using the test set from the WMT 2022 dataset (Kocmi et al., 2022) which was
384   developed using recent content from diverse domains. These domains include news, social media,
385   e-commerce, and conversational contexts. (Details in Appendix: A.1, Table: 4).
386   Summarization: We use the popular CNN-DailyMail (CNN-DM) (Hermann et al., 2015) dataset
387   which is a large collection (over 300k) of text summarization pairs, created from CNN and Daily
388   Mail news articles. Each datapoint in this dataset comprises of an article (the body of the news arti-
389   cle with 683 words on average) and the corresponding highlights (article summary as written by the
390   article author). While the training set contains more than 287k samples, we have randomly chosen
391   4k samples for training both routers and LoRA. During training in our framework, the number of
392   trainable parameters is small in both phases - therefore a small subset of data points is sufficient for
393
      training. Inference is performed on the standard test set with 11,490 samples.
394
395   6.2     E VALUATION M ETRICS
396
397
      Quality-Based Metrics for Translation task:
398   • BLEU Score: BLEU (Bilingual Evaluation Understudy) scores are used to measure the quality
399     of translations. BLEU compares n-grams of the candidate translation to n-grams of the reference
400     translation, providing a score between 0 and 1, with higher scores indicating better translations.
401     In this evaluation, NLTK BLEU is employed, focusing on BLEU-1 and BLEU-2 scores.
402   • COMET: COMET (Cross-lingual Optimized Metric for Evaluation of Translation) is used to
403
        assess translation quality further. COMET evaluates translations using a model trained to correlate
        well with human judgments. Specifically, Unbabel/XCOMET-XL 2 is used in this evaluation.
404
        COMET provides a more nuanced assessment of translation quality by considering the intricacies
405
        of both source and target languages, beyond the n-gram matching used in BLEU.
406
407   Quality based Metrics for Summarization Task:
408   • BERTScore: This metric quantifies semantic similarity between texts by leveraging contextual
409     word embeddings. BERTScore captures meaning-based similarity rather than relying on exact
410     word matches, providing a nuanced evaluation of text generation quality.
411   • ROUGE: (Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation) is a common metric - ROUGE-1
412     refers to overlap of unigrams between the system summary and reference summary. Similarly,
413     ROUGE-L measures longest matching sequence of words.
414   Finally, for benchmarking latency, we look at the TPOT (Time Per Output Token): This metric
415   evaluates the average time taken to produce each output token and is calculated for GPU to gauge
416   overall decoding performance.

      6.3     T RAINING AND I NFERENCE S ETUP
419
420   • Training settings: For our experiments, we use Llama-3-8B base model from Meta, which com-
421     prises of 32 layers. Training of routers and LoRA adapters is conducted on A100 80GB GPUs,
422
        with training/inference is performed in full precision to avoid performance degradation due to
        quantization. The training process employs our custom loss function and continues for a fixed
423
        number of epochs, terminating when the validation loss fails to improve over 4 consecutive steps.
424
        The learning rate is set between 1e−4 and 3e−4 - a cosine scheduler is used to adjust the learn-
425     ing rate. Gradients are accumulated after 5 steps and the regularization coefficient λ is fixed at
426     0.01. For LoRA fine-tuning, we employ a rank of 8, a dropout rate of 0.1, and a scaling factor
427     (lora alpha) of 32. For translation, the maximum sequence length is set to 128 for router training
428     and 256 for LoRA training. Similarly, for summarization, the maximum sequence length is set to
429     500 and 700 respectively. Prompts for the different tasks regarding training/inference are shown
430     in Appendix A.2.

532   7      C ONCLUSION
533
534   We provide a new algorithm and framework F I RST for layer selection corresponding to input se-
535   quence and task towards reducing latency in a quality aware manner. This operates in a KV cache
536   compatible manner and handles batches of sequences, which are drawbacks in many existing work
537   on early exit. We show significant reduction in latency with low degradation of quality on multiple
538   tasks on well known open source datasets and demonstrate superior quality and latency over input
539   agnostic baselines. In the future, we would like to extend our method to 1) improve the optimality
      of selection of layers, and 2) learn dynamically as new input sequences and tasks arrive.
