011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Neural combinatorial optimization (NCO) leverages machine learning models to
014            tackle complex combinatorial problems by learning heuristics or direct solution
015            construction. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are particularly effective for NCO
016
               due to their ability to capture the relational structure inherent in many such prob-
               lems. In this work, we examine the supervised non-autoregressive (NAR) solution
017
               construction framework, revealing a misalignment between training objective and
018
               solution quality. Specifically, through experiments on six GNN architectures across
019            three problems—Traveling Salesperson Problem (TSP), Maximum Independent
020            Set (MIS), and Minimum Vertex Cover (MVC)—we show that lower training
021            loss does not correlate with lower optimality gap. To address this, we propose a
022            supervised autoregressive (AR) framework that leverages the conditional dependen-
023            cies between variables by training to complete partial solutions. Empirical results
024            show that the proposed AR framework does not exhibit the same misalignment
025            and consistently improves performance. We further compare the proposed AR
026            framework against existing supervised GNN-based methods and achieve superior
027
               performance, especially in terms of generalizing to larger problem instances.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Combinatorial optimization problems are fundamental to a wide range of industries, including
033
      vehicle routing (Tassone & Choudhury, 2020), machine scheduling (Brucker, 2007), and resource
      allocation (Xiao et al., 2012). As these problems scale, they become increasingly difficult to solve
034
      optimally, presenting significant challenges for exact methods. Despite decades of research, traditional
035
      approaches often fall short of addressing the large-scale demands posed by real-world applications,
036   especially given the complexity driven by recent globalization and technological advancements
037   (Bertsimas & Dunn, 2019).
038
039
      The rise of modern deep learning techniques has opened new avenues for tackling these problems,
      leading to the emergence of Neural Combinatorial Optimization (NCO) (Khalil et al., 2022; Gasse
040
      et al., 2019; Joshi et al., 2019; Li et al., 2018; Khalil et al., 2017; Sun & Yang, 2024; Luo et al.,
041
      2024; Qiu et al., 2022). One dominant NCO approach is the application of Graph Neural Networks
042   (GNNs) to construct primal solutions. GNNs, which are specialized deep learning architectures
043   for graph-structured data, have gained prominence in NCO due to their inherent suitability for
044   combinatorial optimization as many such problems (e.g. vehicle routing problems) are naturally
045   represented as graphs. Furthermore, many combinatorial optimization problems can be modeled as
046   constraint-variable graphs (Gasse et al., 2019; Khalil et al., 2022), further emphasizing the alignment
047   between GNNs and the NCO domain.
048   GNN-based approaches for constructing primal solutions in combinatorial optimization can be broadly
049   categorized based on their underlying learning problem: deep reinforcement learning (Khalil et al.,
050   2017; Qiu et al., 2022; Ahn et al., 2020; Kool et al., 2019; Xing & Tu, 2020; Peng et al., 2020; Zhou
051   et al., 2023), unsupervised learning (Min et al., 2024; Min & Gomes, 2024; Drori et al., 2020; Karalias
052   & Loukas, 2020; Toenshoff et al., 2021; Amizadeh et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2022), and supervised
053   learning (Joshi et al., 2019; Kool et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2021; Sun & Yang, 2024; Li et al., 2024;
      Huang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2018). This paper primarily focuses on GNN-based supervised learning

054
      methods. These methods are dominated by non-autoregressive (NAR) algorithms where a GNN
055   model, that is trained using optimal solutions as ground-truth labels, assigns independent probabilities
056   to each variable, treating the task as a node or edge classification task. These probabilities, known
057   as a probability map or a heat map, indicate the likelihood of each node or edge being part of the
058   optimal solution. A search algorithm, such as greedy search (Joshi et al., 2019; Sun & Yang, 2024),
059   beam search (Joshi et al., 2019), or Monte-Carlo tree search (MCTS) (Sun & Yang, 2024; Fu et al.,
060   2021), is then applied based on the GNN-generated probability map to construct a valid solution.
061   While recent supervised approaches have shown state-of-the-art empirical results (Sun & Yang, 2024;
062   Fu et al., 2021), key limitations have been identified in some of these methods, particularly regarding
063   the practicality of the generated probability maps (Li et al., 2018; Xia et al., 2024) and their ability
064   to scale to larger problem instances (Fu et al., 2021; Joshi et al., 2022). To better understand these
065   limitations, we conduct an extensive experimental evaluation to analze the connection between the
066   quality of the generated probability maps and the quality of the solutions they produced. Our analysis
067   revealed a notable misalignment: improvements in the quality of probability maps do not correlate
068   with higher quality of constructed solutions. We hypothesize that the non-autoregressive nature of
069
      these approaches is the source of the misalignment and propose to use autoregressive models to
      resolve the misalignment. Extensive experiments provide empirical support to our hypothesis and
070
      demonstrate how the use of autoregressive models mitigate the observed misalignment and improve
071
      the performance of these models.
072
073   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
074
            1. We examine the supervised NAR solution construction framework, that encompass sev-
075
               eral notable approaches, and identify a clear misalignment between the accuracy of the
076
               probability maps (i.e., the training objective) and the quality of the constructed solutions.
077            Specifically, we run experiments for six different GNN architectures across three different
078            graph-based combinatorial optimization problems— Traveling Salesperson Problem (TSP),
079            Maximum Independent Set (MIS), and Minimum Vertex Cover (MVC)— and show that
080            improvements in training loss do not correlate with improvements in optimality gap.
081         2. We introduce a general framework for supervised AR solution construction leveraging
082            conditional generation by training to complete randomly sampled partial solutions. Our
083            results show that this framework, tested on the same three combinatorial optimization
084            problems and six GNN architectures, does not exhibit the previously observed misalignment
085            and consistently leads to higher quality primal solutions.
086         3. We compare the proposed AR framework against existing supervised GNN-based methods
087            on TSP instances of various sizes. Our experimental results show that our AR framework
088            achieves superior performance, especially in terms of generalizing to larger instances.

      2    BACKGROUND
091
092
      In this section, we give some background on the general framework of GNN-based supervised
093
      methodologies. Formally, given an instance g of combinatorial optimization problem G with binary
094   variables Dg , we denote Xg ⊆ 2Dg as the set of feasible solutions of g and cG : Xg → R as the
095   objective function. To goal is to find the optimal solution defined as:
096
097                                          x̂g = arg min cG (xg )                                        (1)
                                                        xg ∈Xg
098
099   Instead of searching through the large discrete solution space Xg , existing methods define a continuous
100   solution space Ωg ⊆ [0, 1]|Dg | and a model M with parameters θ:
101
102                                               Mθ : G → Ωg                                              (2)
103   is tasked with predicting a |Dg |-dimensional vector ω ∈ Ωg , representing a probability map where
104   each entry ωi estimates the probability of variable i being true in optimal solution x̂g . Then, a
105   search algorithm SG : Ωg → Xg constructs a feasible solution xg by searching through the predicted
106   probability map ω. Therefore, the objective of the model is defined as:
107
                                         L(θ) = Eg∼G [cG (SG (Mθ (g)))]                                    (3)


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      However, since the objective functions cG of combinatorial optimization problems and the search
109   algorithms SG are generally non-differentiable (Qiu et al., 2022), this objective cannot be directly
110   optimized. Instead, existing methods adopt some surrogate loss function lsurrogate to approximate
111   cG (S(·)). Then, the optimization objective becomes:
112
113                                     L′ (θ) = Eg∼G [lsurrogate (Mθ (g))]                               (4)
114
115   Most commonly, a supervised classification loss (e.g. cross-entropy loss) is employed to treat this
116   as a classification task (Joshi et al., 2019; Sun & Yang, 2024; Luo et al., 2024; Vinyals et al., 2015).
117   That is, we can construct a training set by finding the optimal solution x̂g for problem instance g and
118   using x̂g as the ground-truth labels for the variables (1 for true in x̂g and 0 otherwise). Then, we
119   can train M as a binary classification problem. A GNN model is a common choice for M as many
120   combinatorial optimization problems are naturally defined on graphs (Joshi et al., 2019; Sun & Yang,
121   2024; Fu et al., 2021; Kool et al., 2022). For example, the TSP and many related vehicle-routing
122
      problems can be defined on a graph where nodes represent locations, edges represent routes, and
      edge weights represent costs. Following this setup, the surrogate objective in Eq. 4 becomes a binary
123
      node/edge-level classification task.
124
125   This general framework is non-autoregressive (NAR) in nature. That is, the probability outputs of
126   each variable are generated independent of each other (Sun & Yang, 2024). On the other hand, in
127   autoregressive (AR) approaches, the model would generate predictions conditioned on its previous
128
      predictions. In this context, models would generate probabilities iteratively, where each output is
      conditioned on previous outputs (Luo et al., 2024). However, to the best of our knowledge, there is
129
      no existing supervised GNN-based AR method.
130
131
132   2.1   R ELATED W ORK
133
134   NAR Methods. Following the general NAR framework, notable works include Joshi et al. (2019)
135
      who approach the TSP as a binary edge classification task, using the optimal TSP tour as labels (in this
      work, we term this approach E FFICIENT T SP for brevity). The model’s backbone is a Residual Gated
136
      Graph Convolutional Network (GatedGCN) (Bresson & Laurent, 2017), trained using cross-entropy
137
      loss. To construct a valid TSP tour based on the generated probability map, E FFICIENT T SP employs
138   a greedy search algorithm, which can also be generalized to a beam search. Another notable work is
139   Li et al. (2018) who target the MIS problem and, using optimal solutions as binary node labels, train
140   a GNN model to generate multiple probability maps per instance. A tree search method is deployed
141   to search through these probability maps to construct a solution.
142
      Subsequent works have expanded on this general framework by incorporating novel architectures
143
      or complex search algorithms. For instance, Fu et al. (2021) train a GNN model to generate
144   probability maps for subgraphs of a TSP instance, which are then merged into a comprehensive
145   probability map used by a Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithm to construct a valid tour.
146   Additionally, Kool et al. (2022) introduced Deep Policy Dynamic Programming (DPDP) which uses
147   dynamic programming as the search algorithm to construct valid solutions for TSP and related routing
148   problems. Recently, Sun & Yang (2024) proposed D IFUSCO, a method that integrates diffusion-based
149   GNN models within the existing framework, achieving state-of-the-art results on TSP, with further
150   post-processing techniques presented by Li et al. (2024) and Huang et al. (2023).
151   Recent research has highlighted limitations in the existing NAR methods. Notably, Joshi et al.
152   (2022) found that these methods exhibit poor generalization capabilities. Additionally, concerns
153   have been raised regarding the practicality of these generated probability maps when decoded by
154   complex post-hoc search algorithms. In particular, Xia et al. (2024) demonstrated that a simple
155   softmax-based heuristic could produce probability maps yielding solutions of comparable quality to
156   those produced by trained GNNs when decoded with MCTS. Similarly, Böther et al. (2022) showed
157   that the guided tree search method in (Li et al., 2018) can construct near-optimal solutions even from
158
      random probability maps.
159   However, these studies are limited to tree search methods and do not investigate the link between
160   the quality of the probability maps and the quality of the resulting solutions. To our knowledge, our
161   study is the first to identify the misalignment between probability map quality and solution quality in
      supervised NAR approaches.

      AR Methods. Despite its absence within the supervised GNN domain, we note that AR methods
163   have been proposed in NCO through other learning paradigms, such as deep reinforcement learning
164   (Kool et al., 2019; Qiu et al., 2022), and in other architectures like pointer networks (Vinyals et al.,
165   2015) and transformers (Bresson & Laurent, 2021; Luo et al., 2024). We also note that Li et al.
166   (2018)’s MIS-specific graph reduction technique behaves similarly in concept to an AR approach.
167   Though, it is specific to the MIS problem and is not compatible with other types of problems. We
168   further discuss this in Appendix C.

171   3     T HE M ISALIGNMENT IN THE G ENERAL S UPERVISED NAR F RAMEWORK
172
173   In this section, we conduct experimental analysis of the NAR framework to understand the observed
174   limitations. Specifically, we implement and evaluate the existing NAR framework by continu-
175   ously tracking and comparing the quality of the generated probability maps with the quality of the
176   constructed solutions after each training epoch. This is performed across three NP-complete combina-
177   torial optimization problems (Hartmanis, 1982): TSP, MIS, and MVC. While all three problems are
178   well studied in NCO literature, TSP has been a focal point in NCO research (Sun & Yang, 2024; Joshi
179
      et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2021), while MIS (Li et al., 2018; Sun & Yang, 2024) and MVC (Khalil et al.,
      2022) complement it by representing node-based decision variables, as opposed to the edge-based
180
      decision variables in TSP.
181
182   TSP involves finding the minimum-cost cycle that visits each node exactly once, MIS involves finding
183   the largest set of non-adjacent nodes in a graph, and MVC involves finding the smallest set of nodes
184   such that at least one endpoint of each edge is included. Formal definitions of these problems are
185
      included in Appendix A.
186   The implemented method follows the workflow outlined in Section 2. We define a training instance
187   as the input graph with labels for the decision variables extracted from the optimal solution (1 if
188   included in the optimal solution, 0 otherwise). A GNN model is trained to predict these labels using
189   cross-entropy as the surrogate loss. This approach mirrors the probability map generation process
190   employed in all aforementioned NAR methods (Joshi et al., 2019; Kool et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2021;
191
      Sun & Yang, 2024).

      2024). Details on the exact decoding process for each problem are provided in Appendix E.

      4    AUTOREGRESSIVE FRAMEWORK FOR GNN- BASED NCO
298
299   In this section, we present a supervised GNN-based AR solution construction framework. The
300
      motivation for the proposed approach stems from the misalignment identified in Section 3 between
      the desired optimization objective defined in Eq. 3 and the surrogate loss function defined in Eq. 4.
301
      The proposed method addresses this issue by taking an AR approach and changing the optimization
302
      objective of the model accordingly. Specifically, we propose a novel optimization objective from an
303   AR perspective that aims to construct a valid solution by iteratively generating conditional probability
304   maps after each variable selection.
305
306
      Formally, given a binary combinatorial optimization problem instance g ∈ G with variable set Dg ,
307
      feasible solution set Xg ⊆ 2Dg , and objective function cG : Xg → R; we aim to find optimal solution
      set x̂g ∈ Xg as defined in Eq. 1. However, due to its size, it is infeasible to directly search through the
308
      discrete solution space Xg . As such, the proposed method defines a GNN model M with parameters
309
      θ:
310
311                                           Mθ : (g, x̃g ) 7→ ω ∈ Ωg                                       (6)
312
313   where Ωg ⊆ [0, 1]|Dg | represent probability maps over Dg and x̃g ∈ X̃g ⊆ 2Dg represents a partial
314   solution of g. Unlike in the NAR framework (Eq. 2), here the generation of probability maps is
315   conditioned on some partial solution x̃g ∈ X̃g . To construct a feasible solution, we initialize a partial
316
      solution x̃g = ∅. Then, a search algorithm SG : Ωg × X̃g → X̃g updates a given partial solution x̃g by
317   selecting one variable to be true based on a probablity map ω. This process iterates by generating a
318   new probability map ω after every update to x̃g until some problem-specific termination criteria. This
319   process is autoregressive as each model output ω = Mθ (g, x̃g ) is conditioned on previous model
320   outputs represented by x̃g . Following this method, the objective of the model is to predict probability
321   maps that will construct partial solutions with the lowest expected objective value, which is defined
322   as:
323
                               L(θ) = Eg∼G Exg ∼Xg |SG (Mθ (g,x̃g ))⊆xg [cG (xg )]                           (7)


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Again, as the objective functions cG of combinatorial optimizations and search algorithms SG are
325   generally non-differentiable (Qiu et al., 2022), we approximate Exg ∼Xg |SG (Mθ (g,x̃g ))⊆xg [cG (xg )]
326   using a classification loss function, lclassification , as a surrogate:
327
328
                                     L′ (θ) = Eg∼G [lclassification (Mθ (g, x̃g ), x̂g )]                      (8)
329   Specifically, we use optimal solution x̂g ∈ X̂g as the ground-truth label and train the model to com-
330   plete x̂g from partial solutions x̃g ⊂ x̂g . That is, to approximate Exg ∼Xg |SG (Mθ (g,x̃g ))⊆xg [cG (xg )],
331   we use the following supervised classification loss:
332
                               lclassification (θ | g, x̃g ) = Ex̂g ∼X̂g [lCE (Mθ (g, x̃g ), x̂g )]            (9)
333
334   where lCE is cross-entropy loss. This approach aims to achieve alignment between the surrogate
335   loss function (Eq. 8) with the desired optimization objective (Eq. 7) by significantly simplifying
336   the prediction task. Intuitively, instead of generating a single probability map that would dictate
337   the selection of the entire solution set (as in the NAR framework), the proposed approach is to
338
      generate a probability map that is used to select only the next variable to be added to the solution
      set. This process can then be repeated to construct a valid solution in an autoregressive manner. Not
339
      only does the proposed method significantly simplify the desired model objective, it also enables
340
      the model to perform conditional generation. That is, the generation of each probability map is
341   conditioned on the current partial solution. Unlike existing NAR methods, the conditional generation
342   in our method allows the model to adjust for any suboptimal choices made earlier in the solution
343   construction process. It also better captures the multimodal nature of combinatorial optimization
344   problems, avoiding the pitfall of getting caught between predicting multiple equally optimal solutions.
345
346   4.1   T RAINING VIA PARTIAL S OLUTION S AMPLING
347
348   To train the model to make predictions conditioned on a partial solution, our method treats the task
349   as a node or edge-level classification task, trained using cross-entropy loss. We construct partial
350
      solutions from the ground-truth (i.e., optimal solution) of each training instance and encode them as
      part of the model input and ask the model to complete each partial solution. In this way, the model is
351
      trained to predict the next variable to be included conditioned on a partial solution.
352
353   Formally, given problem instance g ∈ G and the optimal solution set x̂g , we can construct a partial
354   solution x̃g ⊂ x̂g and task the model with predicting the remainder x̃′g = x̂g \x̃g . To explicitly encode
355   the condition on a partial solution x̃g , each variable in the graph is given a binary feature indicating
356
      its inclusion in x̃g (1 for included, 0 otherwise). Correspondingly, a binary label is assigned to each
      variable to indicate whether it should be selected next, with those in x̃′g labeled as 1 and others as 0.
357
      This setup provides explicit information about the current partial solution and the correct subsequent
358
      inclusions, enabling the model to make conditional predictions. We can apply this process many
359   times per problem instance allowing us to significantly increase the diversity and volume of the
360   training set, resulting in a more robust framework, especially in cases where labeled data is scarce.
361
362
      Ideally, for each labeled problem instance g in the training set and each of its optimal solutions
363   x̂g ∈ X̂g , we construct training data from all possible subsets of x̂g which would result in training
364   data in the magnitude of O(2|x̂g | ) for each optimal solution. For tractability, we instead opt for
365   sampling k partial solutions (and their corresponding labels) from one optimal solution for each
366
      problem instance, by first sampling the length of the partial solution uniformly at random and then
      randomly sampling a subset of the optimal solution of the chosen length. The pseudocode for the
367
      proposed framework is provided in Appendix H.

376
377   Solution Construction. To construct valid solutions after training, we employed greedy search,
      as was done previously for the NAR framework. We followed the same greedy process, where

      the highest-scored variable is iteratively added, subject to problem-specific constraints. The key
379   difference is that we now generate a new probability map after each iteration. Additionally, for TSP,
380   we no longer enforce that the partial solution be a connected path, ensuring better alignment between
381   training and deployment. During training, the model predicts over all edges, not just those incident to
382   the current partial tour. Therefore, during deployment, we do not require the tour to be constructed
383   sequentially. The decoding process for each problem is described in detail in Appendix E.

521   6     C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this paper, we examined the general supervised NAR solution construction framework across
524   three well-known combinatorial optimization problems and six GNN architectures. We identified
525   a misalignment between the desired optimization objective (optimality gap) and the surrogate opti-
526   mization objective (training loss). To address this, we proposed a general supervised AR framework
527   that leverages conditional generation. Our training process involves sampling partial solutions from
528
      optimal solutions and training the model to complete them. Empirical results show that our proposed
      framework does not exhibit the previously observed misalignment and leads to improved performance.
529
      Notably, when compared against existing GNN-based supervised methods on TSP datasets of various
530
      sizes, our AR framework displays superior performance, especially in terms of generalizing to larger
531   instances.
532
533
      6.1   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
534
535   One promising extension of our work is the development of more sophisticated encoding mechanisms
536   for the current partial solutions, possibly tailored to specific combinatorial optimization problems.
537   The iterative nature of the AR methods in general, while beneficial for solution quality, incurs
538   computational costs. Future research could focus on exploring AR approaches that balance solution
539   quality with computational efficiency. Lastly, our approach only uses one optimal solution per
      problem instance for training while combinatorial optimizations can have several different optimal

791   Recent works within unsupervised learning for NCO predominantly employ NAR methods. The
792   goal is to develop models that can learn combinatorial optimization solutions without labeled data,
793   utilizing techniques like probabilistic modeling and objective relaxation.
794
      Methods for Routing Problems. Min et al. (2024); Min & Gomes (2024) propose unsupervised
795
      methods for the TSP, introducing approaches that generate edge probability maps and leverage the
796   Gumbel-Sinkhorn operator for permutation representation.
797
798   Methods for Other Graph-Based Problems. Toenshoff et al. (2021) introduce a generic GNN
799
      architecture for maximum constraint satisfaction problems, training unsupervised on small instances
      to effectively solve larger ones. Karalias & Loukas (2020) employ a probabilistic method for CO,
800
      creating a framework that finds integral solutions via neural network parametrization over sets.
801
      Amizadeh et al. (2019) propose a neural framework for solving the Circuit-SAT problem through an
802   unsupervised differentiable approach. Wang et al. (2022) present a relaxation-based approach for
803   CO with neural networks, particularly effective for applications without explicitly defined objectives.
804   Schuetz et al. (2022) use a physics-inspired GNN model to solve CO problems framed as quadratic
805   unconstrained binary optimizations, achieving scalability and strong performance.
806

      C    R ELATION TO G UIDED T REE S EARCH
843
844   Guided Tree Search (Li et al., 2018) is a supervised method for constructing solutions to the MIS
845
      problem. It employs a tree search algorithm that leverages probability maps generated by a Graph
      Convolutional Network (GCN) (Kipf & Welling, 2017). The training process is the same to that of
846
      the NAR framework we examine in Section 3, with a a slight difference: the GCN produces multiple
847
      probability maps for each graph instance in a single forward pass. Specifically, each node is assigned
848   scores m times (with m = m in their configuration), representing its likelihood of inclusion in 32
849   different potential solutions. This approach acknowledges the presence of multiple optimal solutions
850   for each MIS instance, allowing the model to generate a diverse set of focused probability maps.
851
      The GCN is trained using a hindsight cross-entropy loss:
852
853                                       Lhindsight =     min LCE (ytrue , fi )                              (15)
854                                                      i=1,...,m

855
856   where LCE is the standard cross-entropy loss, ytrue are the true labels, and f1 , ..., fm are the m different
857   probability maps produced by the GCN. The training aims to minimize the lowest cross-entropy loss
858   among all generated maps. A tree search algorithm is then employed to construct a feasible solution
859   from these maps, switching between different maps as needed.
860   During the traversal of a single probability map, the method iteratively includes the node with the
861   highest probability into the solution and marks all its neighbors as excluded. After this step, all
862   marked nodes, both included and excluded, are removed from the graph. The GCN then generates
863   a new probability map for the remaining subgraph. This process repeats until all nodes have been
      marked.


                                                             16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      In Section 2, we classify this method as non-autoregressive. However, it differs from other NAR
865   methods because it conceptually resembles conditional generation in autoregressive methods. To
866   condition the probabilities on the existing partial solution, this method does not explicitly train the
867   GCN to generate conditioned outputs. Instead, it reduces the original graph after each selection by
868   removing the selected node and its neighbors, and feeds this reduced subgraph back into the GCN to
869   produce a new probability map. This is a unique property of the MIS problem, where the state of the
870   current partial solution can be implicitly represented by the modified input graph.
871   Formally,  given a graph G = (V, E) and an independent set of nodes K ⊆ V , let K ′ = K ∪
872                                                                         ′         ′
      S
        v∈K N (v), where N (v) denotes the set of neighbors of node v. Let G = G[V \ K ] be the induced
873   subgraph obtained by removing K from G. If L is a maximum independent set on G ′ , then K ∪ L
                                          ′
874   constitutes the largest independent set on G that includes K.
875
      In other words, finding the largest independent set on G conditioned on the partial solution K is
876
      equivalent to finding the maximum independent set on the reduced subgraph G ′ . In G ′ , all nodes in
877
      K and their neighbors, along with all edges incident to them, have been removed. This reduction
878   effectively encodes the condition into the graph structure.
879
880   This property suggests that while the model does not explicitly perform conditional generation, it
881
      achieves a similar outcome by manipulating the input graph to reflect the current partial solution.
      Moreover, this approach of implicit conditional generation through input manipulation does not
882
      readily extend to other combinatorial optimization problems. As such, we classify this method as non-
883
      autoregressive because the GCN model itself is not trained to generate predictions autoregressively.

886   D     DATASET G ENERATION
887
888   To generate the MVC and MIS instances used in this study, we generate Erdős–Rényi (ER) graphs
889   (Erdos et al., 1960) using the NetworkX library (Hagberg et al., 2008). Each n node ER graph is
890
      generated with edges established between node pairs with probability p = 0.05 ± 0.02.
891   For TSP, we generate Waxman graphs (Waxman, 1988) using NetworkX Hagberg et al. (2008) with
892   the parameter β = 1 and domain coordinates ranging from (0, 0) to (1, 1). In this setup, each node is
893   assigned a coordinate within the unit square, resulting in a complete graph where every pair of nodes
894   is connected. Edge costs are calculated based on the Euclidean distance between node coordinates.
895   To make the TSP graphs more computationally tractable, we sparsify them by connecting each node
896
      only to its k = 20 nearest neighbors. This graph generation process, including sparsification, follows
      the conventions established in prior TSP studies (Joshi et al., 2019; Sun & Yang, 2024; Fu et al.,
897
      2021).
898
899   To generate the ground-truth labels, we obtain an optimal solution for each problem instance using
900   Gurobi (Gurobi Optimization, LLC, 2023).

902   E     D ECODING A LGORITHMS
903
904   In this section, we detail the greedy search algorithms for each of the three problems (TSP, MIS,
905   MVC) within both the existing NAR frameworks and the proposed AR framework.
906
907   E.1   T RAVELING S ALESPERSON P ROBLEM
908
909   In the NAR framework, given a probability map over all edges, we start by randomly selecting a node
910   as the current position. We then iteratively construct the tour by greedily choosing the incident edge
911   with the highest probability from the current node. After selecting an edge, we update the current
912   node to the adjacent node connected by that edge. This process repeats, with already visited nodes
913   being masked to prevent revisiting, until a valid TSP tour is completed. This is the greedy decoding
914
      method used in E FFICIENT T SP (Joshi et al., 2019) and is similar to the variant in D IFUSCO (Sun &
      Yang, 2024), where edge probabilities are adjusted by dividing them by their corresponding edge
915
      costs.
916
917   In our proposed AR framework, we remove the constraint of sequential tour construction. Starting
      with an empty solution set and a probability distribution over all edges, we greedily add the edge with


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      the highest probability to the solution set, provided it does not violate any TSP constraints and is not
919   already included. After each addition, we use the trained GNN model to generate a new probability
920   distribution based on the updated solution set. This iterative process continues until a valid TSP tour
921   is formed. We found that the non-sequential variant of the search algorithm yields slightly better
922   performance.
923
924   E.2   M AXIMUM I NDEPENDENT S ET
925
926   In the NAR framework, we begin with a probability map over all nodes. We iteratively select the node
927   with the highest probability that is neither already selected nor adjacent to any previously selected
928   nodes. This process repeats until every node is either selected or adjacent to a selected node.
929   In the AR framework, we follow the same process but generate a new probability map after each
930   node selection.
931
932   E.3   M INIMUM V ERTEX C OVER
933
934   In the NAR framework, we start with a probability map over all nodes and iteratively select the
935   highest-probability node that has not yet been chosen. This continues until all edges are covered—that
936   is, every edge has at least one endpoint in the solution set.
937   In the AR framework, we follow the same process but generate a new probability map after each
938   node selection.
