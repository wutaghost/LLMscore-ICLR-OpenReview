011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Depth estimation is a key topic in the field of computer vision. Self-supervised
014            monocular depth estimation offers a powerful method to extract 3D scene informa-
015            tion from a single camera image, allowing training on arbitrary image sequences
016
               without the need for depth labels. However, monocular unsupervised depth esti-
               mation still cannot address the issue of scale and often requires ground truth for
017
               calibration. In the deep learning era, existing methods primarily rely on relation-
018
               ships between images to train unsupervised neural networks, often overlooking
019            the fundamental information provided by the camera itself. In fact, the intrinsic
020            and extrinsic parameters of the camera can be used to compute depth information
021            for the ground and its related areas based on physical principles. This informa-
022            tion can offer rich supervisory signals at no additional cost. Additionally, by as-
023            suming that objects like people, cars, and buildings share the same depth as the
024            corresponding ground, the physical depth of the entire scene can be inferred, and
025            gaps in the depth map can be filled. Since some areas may have depth estima-
026            tion errors, to make full use of these regions, we introduce a contrastive learning
027
               self-supervised framework. This framework consists of two networks with the
               same structure: the Anchor network and the Target network. While calculating
028
               depth, the network also outputs semantic segmentation results to assist in com-
029
               puting the physics depth, which is then used as the label for the model. Semantic
030            segmentation can identify dynamic objects, reducing photometric reprojection er-
031            rors caused by moving objects. The predictions from the Anchor network are used
032            as pseudo-labels for training the Target network. Reliability is determined by en-
033            tropy, dividing the predicted depth into positive and negative samples to maximize
034            the use of physics depth information.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038   Monocular depth estimation plays a critical role in fields such as computer vision Newcombe et al.
039   (2011); Luo et al. (2021); Tateno et al. (2017), scene understanding Hazirbas et al. (2017), and 3D
040   mapping Li et al. (2023). Its goal is to infer depth from a single RGB image, but this is inherently
041   an ill-posed problem due to scale ambiguity, as the same 2D image can be projected from infinitely
042   many 3D scenes. The advent of convolutional neural networks has significantly advanced monoc-
043   ular depth estimation Simonyan & Zisserman (2014); Szegedy et al. (2015); He et al. (2016), with
044   the most accurate results being achieved through supervised learning Eigen et al. (2014); Fu et al.
045
      (2018); Ranftl et al. (2020); Bhat et al. (2021), which requires sparse depth data collected by sen-
      sors like LiDAR as labels. The high cost of data collection and labeling has driven researchers to
046
      explore self-supervised depth estimation frameworks. Early self-supervised methods used regres-
047
      sion modules to estimate per-pixel depth and infer 3D structures Godard et al. (2019); Gordon et al.
048   (2019); Peng et al. (2021); Watson et al. (2019), relying on photometric consistency loss for model
049   training. However, the accuracy of self-supervised monocular depth estimation still falls short when
050   compared to supervised learning methods. In deep learning-driven depth estimation, the rich infor-
051   mation provided by sensors is often overlooked. This paper proposes a camera model that combines
052   image semantics with the camera’s physical model (including intrinsic and extrinsic parameters) to
053   calculate the depth information of road surfaces. Based on this, we can directly infer the depth of
      objects on the ground, such as buildings and vehicles, and generate a dense depth map by filling in

      2.1   D EPTH E STIMATION
105
106   Monocular depth estimation has seen significant advancements since the pioneering work by Eigen
107   et al. (2014),. Since then, the field has evolved with improvements in both network architectures
      and loss functions Laina et al. (2016); Lee et al. (2018); Liu et al. (2015); Miangoleh et al. (2021).


                                                                                     2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Approaches in supervised monocular depth estimation typically revolve around either pixel-wise
109   regression Eigen et al. (2014); Zhao et al. (2021); Ranftl et al. (2021); Huynh et al. (2020) or pixel-
110   wise classification Fu et al. (2018); Diaz & Marathe (2019). While regression predicts continuous
111   depths, it can pose optimization challenges, whereas classification, though easier to optimize, re-
112   sults in discrete depth predictions. Self-supervised depth estimation has gained prominence due to
113   the difficulty in acquiring accurate ground truth data. The seminal work by Zhou et al. (2017) in-
114   troduced a framework for jointly training depth and pose networks using image reconstruction loss.
115   Subsequent innovations, such as minimum re-projection loss and auto-masking loss by Godard et al.
116   (2019), further advanced the state of the art. Scale ambiguity in monocular Structure-from-Motion
117
      (SfM) models, a common challenge, has been addressed by incorporating real-time data like GPS
      or camera velocity in works such as Guizilini et al. (2020) and Chawla et al. (2021). These methods
118
      rely on photometric consistency for re-projection Wang et al. (2004). In stereo depth estimation,
119
      disparity prediction, which is inversely related to depth, plays a crucial role. Garg et al. (2016)
120   introduced self-supervised training of monodepth models with stereo pairs, which was refined by
121   Godard et al. (2017) using left-right consistency and later extended to continuous disparity predic-
122   tion by Garg et al. (2020). Stereo models predict absolute depth scales, while monocular models
123   typically predict relative depth, requiring calibration with ground truth. Integrating physics-based
124   depth data improves the accuracy of absolute depth predictions, particularly for datasets like KITTI.
125
126
127   2.2   G EOMETRIC P RIORIS
128
      Geometric priors have become increasingly important in monocular depth estimation. Among them,
129
      the normal constraint Long et al. (2021); Qi et al. (2018) is widely applied, ensuring that the normal
130
      vectors of the predicted depths align with those of the ground truth. The piecewise planarity prior
131   Gallup et al. (2010) provides a practical approximation for real-world scenes. Although monocular
132   depth estimation inherently suffers from ambiguity, and while Transformers have improved predic-
133   tion accuracy, they do not fundamentally address the core error issues in monocular depth estimation.
134   Geometric priors help alleviate some uncertainty, but their overall contribution to solving the prob-
135   lem remains limited. We utilize camera model parameters to compute scene depth directly. The
136   surface normal method Xue et al. (2020); Wagstaff & Kelly (2021) calculates surface normals and
137   estimates camera height through camera parameters, thereby determining the scale factor. However,
138   while these methods focus on using camera parameters to compute scale, they do not consider how
139
      to use the camera model as a prior for depth estimation. Our approach offers more accurate and
      generalizable depth predictions, further improving model performance.

147

148
      ing a physics-based depth map under the assumption that all surfaces are ideal planes. Semantic
149

187   3.2   E XTENSION OF P HYSICS D EPTH
188
189   Our physics-based depth method closely aligns with LiDAR data for flat surfaces but may overfit
190   to road regions. To improve effectiveness in diverse scenes, we extended the method to cover the
191   entire image. By assuming flat surfaces at camera level and incorporating vertical elements like
192   vehicles and buildings, we create a more comprehensive depth map, termed Edge Extended Physics
193   depth. We extend the physics depth to vertical entities in contact with flat surfaces, like vehicles and
194
      buildings, by propagating depth values from intersection points, forming the Edge Extended Physics
      depth. Missing depth for partially connected objects is filled using the Telea inpainting technique
195
      Telea (2004). For objects not touching the ground, depth is extrapolated from nearby objects. The
196
      sky is filled with 1.5 times the maximum inpainted depth, creating a seamless Dense Physics depth
197   label for subsequent networks. The effectiveness of Our method has been validated on the KITTI
198   Geiger et al. (2013) and Cityscapes Cordts et al. (2016) datasets, showing accuracy closely aligned
199   with LiDAR-derived depth measurements, particularly for ground surfaces.

203   4.1   N ETWORK A RCHITECTURE
204
205   In our study, selecting the physics depth of ground regions as labels based on accuracy may result
206   in many pixels being unused due to errors. We believe every pixel is crucial for model training,
207
      even if its prediction is uncertain. While interpolated depth may cause confusion in similar ranges,
      it should maintain high confidence for pixels in larger disparity ranges, allowing those pixels to be
208
      convincingly treated as negative samples. To fully leverage this data, we developed a self-supervised
209
      contrastive learning framework. We discretize depth values and linearly combine the predicted clas-
210   sifications to obtain accurate estimates. Our framework uses physics depth as labels to train an
211   anchor network, and the anchor network’s predictions for accurate regions are retained as pseudo-
212   labels for self-supervised training of the target network, progressively increasing the proportion of
213   accurate regions in each iteration. Our network also outputs semantic segmentation, which aids in
214   computing physics depth and identifies dynamic objects. This helps reduce errors in photometric re-
215   projection loss caused by moving objects. We propose a semantic segmentation-guided photometric
      reprojection loss that improves accuracy by excluding dynamic objects from calculation.

532   6      C ONCLUSION
533
      This paper proposes a self-supervised monocular depth estimation model based on calculating
534   physics depth using the camera model. Existing self-supervised techniques still lag behind super-
535   vised methods in accuracy and often require ground truth to resolve scale issues. Our network also
536   outputs semantic segmentation, used for calculating physics depth and identifying dynamic objects.
537   We introduce a segmentation-guided photometric reprojection loss, which improves accuracy by
538   excluding dynamic objects. For physics depth, we designed an Anchor-Target network that fully
539   utilizes both correct and erroneous depth information, enhancing the performance of self-supervised
      models. By leveraging physics depth, we resolve the scale problem in monocular depth estimation.
