               Fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) on additional datasets is often necessary
012
               to optimize them for specific downstream tasks. However, existing safety align-
013
               ment measures, which restrict harmful behavior during inference, are insufficient
014            to mitigate safety risks during fine-tuning. Alarmingly, fine-tuning with just 10
015            toxic sentences can make models comply with harmful instructions. We introduce
016            SafetyLock, a novel alignment intervention method that maintains robust safety
017            post-fine-tuning through efficient and transferable mechanisms. SafetyLock lever-
018            ages our discovery that fine-tuned models retain similar safety-related activation
019            representations to their base models. This insight enables us to extract what we
020            term the Meta-SafetyLock, a set of safety bias directions representing key activation
021            patterns associated with safe responses in the original model. We can then apply
022
               these directions universally to fine-tuned models to enhance their safety. By search-
               ing for activation directions across multiple token dimensions, SafetyLock achieves
023
               enhanced robustness and transferability. SafetyLock re-aligns fine-tuned models
024
               in under 0.01 seconds without additional computational cost. Our experiments
025            demonstrate that SafetyLock can reduce the harmful instruction response rate from
026            60% to below 1% in toxic fine-tuned models. It surpasses traditional methods
027            in both performance and efficiency, offering a scalable, non-invasive solution for
028            ensuring the safety of customized LLMs. Our analysis across various fine-tuning
029            scenarios confirms SafetyLock’s robustness, advocating its integration into safety
030            protocols for aligned LLMs.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated increasing utility across various domains (Wei
037   et al., 2022b;a; Weng et al., 2023; Hadar-Shoval et al., 2024), yet their potential to handle harmful
038   queries has raised significant concerns (Carroll et al., 2023; Hendrycks et al., 2023). In response,
039
      researchers have developed various post-training alignment methods (Anwar et al., 2024), including
      post-training adjustments to the models (Bianchi et al., 2024), knowledge editing (Wang et al., 2024c),
040
      and vector steering methods (Lee et al., 2024b; Zheng et al., 2024), aiming to ensure LLMs generate
041
      helpful, honest, and harmless (Rosati et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024d; Yi et al., 2024) responses.
042   These measures are expected to teach models to refuse harmful queries during inference (Huang et al.,
043   2024b; Wang et al., 2024b; Raza et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2024).
044
045
      However, recent work has revealed significant safety risks in fine-tuned models when using explicitly
      harmful, implicitly harmful, or even benign datasets (e.g. Alpaca (Wang et al., 2023b) dataset)
046
      (Kumar et al., 2024; Leong et al., 2024). Qi et al. (2023b) observes that even if a model’s initial safety
047
      alignment is impeccable, this alignment will not be preserved after a customized fine-tuning. The
048   safety alignment of LLMs can be compromised by fine-tuning with only a few adversarially designed
049   training examples. For instance, jailbreaking GPT-3.5 Turbo’s safety guardrails by fine-tuning it
050   on only 10 such examples at a cost of less than $0.20 via OpenAI’s APIs (Qi et al., 2023b). This
051   vulnerability extends to open-source models such as Meta’s Llama series and proprietary models like
052   GPT-4 (Gade et al., 2023; Zhan et al., 2023). These findings suggest that fine-tuning aligned LLMs
053   introduces new safety risks that current safety infrastructures fall short of addressing, how can it be
      maintained after fine-tuning?

      Existing safety alignment techniques can be categorized into three mainstream methods (see Figure
055   1b). The first and most intuitive approach is the post-training method, which involves retraining
056   the model using aligned data. While this method is effective, it is computationally expensive and
057   time-consuming (Zhang et al., 2024b). Second, model-editing approaches (Mitchell et al., 2021; 2022;
058   Wang et al., 2023a) aim to modify specific parts of the model to prevent harmful outputs. However,
059   they often degrade the overall performance of the model, negatively impacting generation plausibility
060   and reasoning abilities (Zhang et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024a). Third, an alternative approach
061   involves adding extra prompts or detectors during inference to avoid unsafe content generation.
062   However, these methods are susceptible to adversarial attacks. Activation steering methods (Zou
063
      et al., 2023a; Wu et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024d) offer another promising direction, as they intervene
      directly in the model’s inference process by steering internal representations. Nevertheless, they often
064
      treat these representations as a whole, which can result in a high refusal rate, even for benign queries,
065
      thereby limiting the model’s utility. The number of fine-tuned models may be tens of thousands of
066   times that of the original model, making it difficult for all existing work to restore safety one by one
067   at a low cost. This leads to our key research question: How can we locate safety-relevant attention
068   heads in such a large scale of fine-tuned models and effectively obtain the safety vector for
069   fine-tuned large language models (LLMs) without negative transfer to other general tasks?
070
      Our research aims to address this gap by developing a novel approach that strikes the right balance
071
      between safety and generation quality. To achieve this, we propose SafetyLock, which further refines
072   existing methods. The main characteristics of SafetyLock can be summarized in two aspects: 1)
073   Precise Safety Alignment with Minimal Degration of General Abilities: By employing safety
074   probes (Li et al., 2024a), we identified the attention heads most closely associated with harmfulness,
075   and determining a safety direction for each. By applying intervention vectors to these heads, we
076   modify the model’s internal activations towards harmlessness during inference, achieving precise
077   safety alignment with minimal impact on response. 2) Transferable and Robust Meta-SafetyLock:
078   Assuming that safe intervention directions are similar between the original and fine-tuned models,
079   we derive safety vectors (Meta-SafetyLock) from the original model (e.g., Llama-3-Instruct) and
080
      efficiently distribute them to a series of fine-tuned models (e.g., Alpaca-Llama-3-Instruct).
081   Experimental results show that our approach is highly transferable and robust, requiring minimal
082   time cost and minimally impacting the generation quality compared to traditional methods. First,
083   we facilitate the efficient transfer of safety measures from base models to their fine-tuned variants,
084   including Llama-3-8B Instruct, Llama-3-70B Instruct, and Mistral-Large-2 123B (Section 3.3).
085   Second, SafetyLock can be deployed without GPU resources in less than 0.01 seconds (Sections 3.2
086
      and 4.3), highlighting our method’s universality. Secondly, SafetyLock significantly reduces the ASR
      from 54.24% to 0.03% in fine-tuned language models and demonstrates robust resistance to both
087
      typical safety attacks and dual attacks with prompt-based methods. With the help of SafetyLock,
088
      we decrease ASR from 98% to 2% for DeepInception attacks (Sections 4.2 and 4.4). Finally, we
089   conducted experiments on eight general tasks, demonstrating minimal performance decay. We
090   show that SafetyLock maintains a high response rate, with a slight decrease from 99.4% to 98.1%
091   (Sections 4.3 and 4.5). Our work advances the field of LLM safety alignment by introducing
092   Meta-SafetyLock, a framework that fundamentally reimagines how safety measures can be efficiently
093   distributed across fine-tuned models. While previous works established important foundations
094   through safety vectors (Bhardwaj et al., 2024) and various safety intervention methods (Zhao et al.,
095   2024; Hazra et al., 2024; Yi et al., 2024), our approach uniquely operates at the attention-head
096   level, supported by our discovery that safety-relevant attention heads maintain consistency even after
097
      fine-tuning. This insight enables us to extract a single Meta-SafetyLock from the base model that
      can be rapidly deployed across multiple fine-tuned variants without requiring repeated safety pattern
098
      searches, achieving remarkable efficiency without GPU resources.

168   As illustrated in Figure 1b, SafetyLock comprises two main phases: manufacturing Meta-SafetyLock
169

171

173   the transferability of SafetyLock during distribution (Section 3.4).

177   Transformer-based language models consist of layers comprising multi-head attention (MHA)
178   mechanisms and multi-layer perceptrons. During inference, tokens are processed into high-
179   dimensional embeddings and passed through each layer. The MHA mechanism can be described as:

182   specific headspace, and Qhl ∈ RDH×D reprojects them back, facilitating interaction among input
183   tokens. We add preference vectors to this residual stream, after Atthl and before Qhl .

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL D ETAILS
282
283   Threat Model Selections. Following previous red teaming and safeguarding studies on aligned
284   LLMs (Yuan et al., 2024), we consider a threat model where attackers can fine-tune aligned LLMs,
285   typically through API access to closed-source models. The primary objective is jailbreaking these
286   models and removing safety constraints (Wei et al., 2023; Carlini et al., 2023) while SafetyLock aims
287   to rebuild the safety guard. We use Llama-3-8B Chat, Llama-3-70B Chat, and Mistral-Large-2 123B
288   as our base models, fine-tuning them on datasets representing each risk level to simulate real-world
289
      scenarios. Please refer to Appendix C for detailed baseline experimental setups.
290   Fine-tuning Datasets. We conducted experiments on three risks: (1) explicitly harmful datasets,
291   where attackers intentionally fine-tune models on malicious content (Ganguli et al., 2022; Qi et al.,
292   2023a); (2) implicitly harmful datasets, which may appear benign but lead to compromised safety
293   guardrails (Qi et al., 2023b); and (3) benign datasets, where even well-intentioned fine-tuning can
294   inadvertently degrade model safety (Wang et al., 2023b). For Risk-1, we use negative samples from
295
      the HH-RLHF preference dataset (Bai et al., 2022). We select 10, 100, 1000, and 10000 samples
      respectively and trained for 5 epochs with a learning rate of 2 × 10−5 . For Risk-2, we use 10 samples
296
      from Qi et al. (2023b) and train for 5 epochs with a learning rate of 2 × 10−5 . For Risk-3, we used
297
      the first 50,000 samples from the Alpaca dataset (Wang et al., 2023b) and trained for 5 epochs with a
298   learning rate of 2 × 10−5 .
299
300   Safety Evaluation and Metrics. Two datasets are used to investigate these risks and evaluate
301
      potential mitigation strategies. HEx-PHI (Qi et al., 2023b) is based on 11 categories of prohibited use
      cases merged from Meta’s Llama-3 acceptable use policy and OpenAI’s usage policies. The dataset
302
      includes 30 examples per category, totalling 330 examples. This ensures a comprehensive safety
303
      evaluation aligned with industry-standard usage policies. The HEx-PHI utilizes GPT-4 for automated
304   assessment, providing harmfulness scores from 1 to 5. We calculated the Harmfulness Rate as the
305   proportion of scores equal to 5. AdvBench is released by Zou et al. (2023b), we adhere to the original
306   paper’s setup and calculate the ASR through string matching.
307
      Baselines. The baseline methods encompass a diverse range of approaches, each with its unique
308
      characteristics. Inference-time methods include in-context demonstration (ICD) (Wei et al., 2024),
309
      PPL (Alon & Kamfonas, 2023), Paraphrase (Jain et al., 2023), Retokenization (Jain et al., 2023),
310   Self-Reminder (Xie et al., 2023), and Self-Examination (Phute et al., 2024), which operate without
311   modifying the underlying model. Training-based methods, such as PPO, DPO, SFT with safety data
312   mixing, and Model-Edited (DINM) (Wang et al., 2023a), involve altering the model’s parameters
313   to enhance safety. These baselines represent the current state-of-the-art in mitigating safety risks in
314   language models, providing a robust benchmark for our evaluation.

476   challenging attacks to defend against, SafetyLock achieves a remarkably low 2.0% ASR, while all
477   other methods fail to provide any meaningful defense (98.0% ASR across the board). For GCG
478   attacks, SafetyLock maintains strong performance with only a 10.0% ASR, second only to PPL’s
479   0.0% but considerably better than most other methods, including Vanilla (74.0%) and Retokenization

482
      benchmark, SafetyLock achieves a state-of-the-art 4.0% ASR, substantially outperforming other
      approaches such as ICD (7.0%) and Self-Reminder (8.0%), while methods like Paraphrase and
483
      Retokenization show significant vulnerabilities with 40.0% and 57.5% ASR respectively.

1008   A.1   L IMITATIONS
1009
       While SafetyLock demonstrates promising results in maintaining the safety of fine-tuned language
1010
       models, it is important to acknowledge several limitations. Primarily, SafetyLock requires access to
1011
       both model weights and intermediate activations for implementation, which may limit its applicability
1012   in scenarios where such access is restricted or unavailable. Additionally, the method employs a
1013   symmetric locking mechanism; consequently, if an unauthorized party gains access to the model
1014   weights or activation values, they could potentially reverse-engineer the process to unlock and bypass
1015   SafetyLock’s protections. Lastly, while SafetyLock shows strong performance against current attack
1016   methods, its long-term robustness against evolving adversarial techniques remains to be studied.
1017   These limitations present opportunities for future work to enhance and expand the capabilities of
1018   SafetyLock, ensuring its continued effectiveness in maintaining AI safety.

1166   dimensions relevant to safety, is preserved during fine-tuning.

1171
       directions, we effectively suppress harmful responses without requiring retraining or fine-tuning of
1172
       the model. The minimal changes to the activation distributions during fine-tuning ensure that the
1173   safety directions remain applicable, allowing for efficient and transferable safety interventions across
1174   different models and fine-tuning scenarios.

       This theoretical explanation provides a foundation for understanding the effectiveness of SafetyLock
       in suppressing harmful outputs while maintaining the model’s overall performance on benign tasks.

       E    R ECOMMENDATIONS FOR D EPLOYING S AFETY L OCK
1513
1514
       Understanding the diverse landscape of model deployment scenarios is crucial for effectively imple-
1515   menting SafetyLock to maintain safety while enabling customization. The method’s effectiveness
1516   and implementation strategy vary significantly depending on the model’s distribution approach and
1517   user priorities, leading to distinct considerations for different deployment contexts.
1518
       For closed-source models served through APIs (e.g., GPT-4), SafetyLock offers an optimal solution
1519
       through seamless integration into the service provider’s infrastructure. Model providers can automati-
1520
       cally apply SafetyLock after each fine-tuning operation, ensuring consistent safety standards while
1521   maintaining customization capabilities. This approach particularly benefits enterprises in regulated
1522   industries that require both task-specific optimization and strict safety controls, as it preserves the
1523   ability to customize models for specific use cases without compromising safety standards. The
1524   automated application of SafetyLock in this context ensures that all model variants maintain robust
1525   safety guardrails, regardless of the extent of customization.
1526
       In scenarios involving open-source models with safety-conscious users, SafetyLock can be effectively
1527   implemented as part of the standard deployment pipeline. Organizations using open-source models
1528   can apply SafetyLock during their model serving phase, maintaining safety controls while preserving
1529   the benefits of customization. This implementation strategy allows organizations to balance the
1530   flexibility inherent in open-source models with the need for robust safety guarantees, ensuring that
1531   fine-tuned models remain both useful and safe. Safety-conscious users can leverage SafetyLock
1532   to maintain consistent safety standards across their deployments while still benefiting from the
1533   customization capabilities that open-source models provide.
1534   To address the fundamental challenge of malicious users with full access to open-source weights,
1535   we propose a hybrid deployment strategy that combines transparency with controlled access to
1536   safety-critical components. This approach involves open-sourcing the majority of model weights
1537   while retaining control of a small subset of safety-critical weights using methods like Taylor Unswift
1538   (Wang et al., 2024a). By providing efficient access to these controlled weights through a service API
1539   and applying SafetyLock during the serving phase, organizations can maintain crucial safety controls
1540   while preserving the benefits of open-source accessibility. This balanced solution ensures that users
1541
       can customize models for their specific needs without easily circumventing safety measures, as the
       critical safety-related parameters remain protected under controlled access.
1542
1543   For successful implementation, organizations should establish comprehensive monitoring systems to
1544   regularly update safety vectors, implement automatic safety checks post-fine-tuning, and develop
1545   clear protocols for handling potential conflicts between safety measures and legitimate use cases.
1546   Regular assessment and updating of safety mechanisms ensure that SafetyLock remains effective
1547
       against evolving harmful behaviors, while clear documentation and guidelines help users understand
       the implications and importance of these safety measures. Through these carefully considered
1548
       deployment strategies and best practices, SafetyLock provides a robust framework for maintaining
1549
       model safety across various deployment scenarios, acknowledging and addressing the inherent
1550   challenges in protecting open-source models while enabling their beneficial applications.
