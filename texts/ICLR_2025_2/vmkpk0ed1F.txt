009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Spuriousness arises when there is an association between two or more variables in
012            a dataset that are not causally related. Left unchecked, they can mislead a machine
013            learning model into using the undesirable “spurious” features in decision-making
014            over the “core” features, hindering generalization. In this work, we propose a novel
015            explainability framework to disentangle the nature of such spurious associations,
016
               i.e., how the information about a target variable is distributed among the spuri-
               ous and core features. Our framework leverages a body of work in information
017
               theory called Partial Information Decomposition (PID) to first decompose the
018
               total information about the target into four non-negative quantities namely unique
019            information (in core and spurious features respectively), redundant information,
020            and synergistic information. Next, we leverage this decomposition to propose a
021            novel measure of the spuriousness of a dataset that steers models into choosing
022            the spurious features over the core. We arrive at this measure systematically by
023            examining several candidate measures, and demonstrating what they capture and
024            miss through intuitive canonical examples and counterexamples. Our proposed
025            explainability framework Spurious Disentangler consists of segmentation, dimen-
026            sionality reduction, and estimation modules, with capabilities to specifically handle
027
               high dimensional image data efficiently. Finally, we also conduct empirical evalua-
               tion to demonstrate the trends of unique, redundant, and synergistic information,
028
               as well as our proposed spuriousness measure across several benchmark datasets
029
               under various settings. Interestingly, we observe a novel tradeoff between our
030            measure of dataset spuriousness and empirical model generalization metrics such
031            as worst-group accuracy, further supporting our proposition.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   While machine learning is rapidly percolating into almost every aspect of our lives, its success
037   is heavily determined by the datasets used for training or fine-tuning. Spurious patterns (Haig,
038   2003) arise when two or more variables are associated in a dataset even though they do not have
039
      a causal relation. For example, image classifiers trained on the Waterbird
      dataset (Wah et al., 2011) learn to use the background rather than the
040
      foreground (actual characteristics of the bird) for classification, because
041
      most waterbirds are photographed on a water background (see Fig. 1).
042   This pattern in the dataset misleads a machine learning classifier into
043   learning an undesirable spurious link between the target label (bird type)
044   and background (“spurious” feature) as opposed to the foreground (core
045   feature). Spuriousness in datasets may result in deceptively high perfor-
046   mance on in-distribution datasets but significantly hinders generalization Figure 1: Spurious pat-
047   on out-of-distribution datasets, e.g., accuracy on minority groups like terns due to sampling bias.
048   waterbirds with land background is low (Lynch et al., 2023; Sagawa et al., 2019; Puli et al., 2023).
049   Despite advances in dataset-based and model-training-based approaches to mitigate such spurious
050   patterns (Kirichenko et al., 2022; Izmailov et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
051   2023), this notion of spuriousness in any given dataset has classically lacked a formal definition. To
052   address this gap, in this work, we ask the following question: Given a dataset and a split of core
053   and spurious features, how do we quantify the undesirable spuriousness of the dataset which steers
      machine learning models into choosing the spurious features over the core features?

      Towards answering this question, we present an information-theoretic explainability framework
055   to disentangle the nature of such spurious associations, i.e., how the information about the target
056   variable is distributed among the spurious and core features. To this end, we leverage a body of
057   work in information theory called Partial Information Decomposition (PID) (Bertschinger et al.,
058   2014; Banerjee et al., 2018), which has its roots in statistical decision theory. We note that classical
059   information-theoretic measures such as mutual information (Cover & Thomas, 2012) captures the
060   entire statistical dependency between two random variables but fail to capture how this dependency is
061   distributed among those variables, i.e., the structure of the multivariate information. Partial Informa-
062   tion Decomposition (PID) addresses this nuanced issue by providing a formal way of disentangling
063
      the joint information content between the core and spurious features into non-negative terms, namely,
      unique, redundant, or synergistic information (see in Section 2). We leverage this decomposition
064
      to systematically arrive at a novel measure of dataset spuriousness with empirical evaluation on
065
      high-dimensional image datasets. This work provides a more nuanced understanding of the interplay
066   between spurious and core features in a dataset that can better inform dataset quality assessment.
067
068   Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
069   Unraveling nature of spurious associations leveraging Partial Information Decomposition:
070   Novel to this work, we investigate the problem of learning spurious patterns from a dataset through
071   the lens of partial information decomposition (PID). We leverage PID to disentangle the total
072   information about a target (Y ) in the core (F ) and spurious (B) features into four non-negative
073   terms: unique information (in core and spurious features respectively), redundant information, and
074   synergistic information (see Proposition 1). We elucidate four types of statistical dependencies
075
      captured by these PID terms (see Fig. 3), providing pre-emptive insights on when an optimal classifier
      might find a spurious feature more informative or useful than the core features. We establish how
076
      unique information quantifies the informativeness of a random variable over another for predicting Y
077
      (see Theorem 1 for interpretability insights, also leveraging Blackwell Sufficiency). Then, redundant
078   information turns out to be the common information that can be obtained from either the spurious or
079   core features, allowing a predictor to potentially choose either without a preference. An interesting
080   term is the synergistic information that captures scenarios when both spurious and core features are
081   jointly informative about the target Y but not individually.
082
      Novel information-theoretic measure of spuriousness: Though many works attempt to prevent a
083
      model from learning spurious patterns, there is limited theoretical understanding of how to quantify
084   the spuriousness of a dataset, given a choice of core and spurious features. In this work, we leverage
085   PID to propose a novel measure of the undesirable spuriousness of a dataset (Msp ) that steers
086   predictors into choosing the spurious features over the core (see Proposition 2). We arrive at this
087   measure systematically by examining several candidate measures, and demonstrating what they
088   capture and miss through intuitive canonical examples and counterexamples. Our measure provides a
089   fundamental understanding of which features can be more informative for a classification task, paving
090   a pathway for dataset quality assessment and interpretability.
091   Spuriousness Disentangler: An autoencoder-based explainability framework: We propose an
092   autoencoder-based explainability framework that we call – Spuriousness Disentangler – to compute
093   the PID values and our spuriousness measure for high dimensional image data. The framework con-
094   sists of three modules: (i) Segmentation: If desired, our framework performs segmentation to separate
095   the foreground (core features F ) and background (spurious features B) for every image; (ii) Di-
096   mensionality Reduction: An autoencoder converts high-dimensional images into lower-dimensional,
097   discrete feature representations. Along the lines of Guo et al. (2017), the dimensionality reduction
098
      and clustering are efficiently performed through minimization of a joint loss function. We also
      incorporate a bottleneck structure from Sadeghi & Armanfard (2023) to have a more informative
099
      lower dimensional representation; (iii) Estimation: The final step includes the estimation of the joint
100
      probability distribution of the acquired lower-dimensional representation followed by computing
101   PID values and our measure Msp . The computation is performed by solving a convex optimization
102   problem using the Discrete Information Theory (DIT) package (James et al., 2018).
103

      the goal of our experiments is to show broad agreement between our anticipations from the dataset
105
      before training and the post-training behavior of models for various experimental setups. We observe

      a negative correlation between our proposed measure of dataset spuriousness Msp and post-training

111   Related Works: There are several perspectives on spurious correlation (see Haig (2003); Kirichenko
112
      et al. (2022); Izmailov et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2023); Ye et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023); Stromberg
      et al. (2024); Singla & Feizi (2021); Moayeri et al. (2023); Lynch et al. (2023) and the references
113
      therein; also see surveys Ye et al. (2024); Srivastava (2023); Ghouse et al. (2024)). Spuriousness
114
      mitigation techniques are broadly divided into two groups: (i) Dataset-based techniques (Goel et al.,
115   2020; Kirichenko et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023; Moayeri et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2021) and (ii) Learning-
116   based techniques (Liu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022). Among
117   dataset-based techniques, Kirichenko et al. (2022) shows that last-layer fine-tuning of a pre-trained
118   model with a group-balanced subset of data is sufficient to mitigate spurious correlation. Wu et al.
119   (2023) proposes a concept-aware spurious correlation mitigation technique. A recent work (Wang
120   & Wang, 2024) looks into the problem of spurious correlations through the mathematical lens of
121   separability of the spurious and core features under mixture of Gaussian assumptions (also assuming
122   a split between core and spurious). Ye et al. (2023) discusses how the noise in the core feature
123
      plays a role in a model’s reliance on it. Our novelty lies in investigating the problem of spurious
      patterns through the lens of Partial Information Decomposition, rooted in statistical decision theory,
124
      focusing on quantifying the spuriousness of a dataset for interpretability and quality assessment. Our
125
      work isolates four specific types of statistical dependencies in the dataset, providing a more nuanced
126   understanding (see Fig. 3) going beyond identifying a model’s reliance on a specific feature.
127
128   Partial Information Decomposition (Williams & Beer, 2010; Bertschinger et al., 2014) is an active
129
      area of research. PID measures are beginning to be used in different domains of neuroscience
      and machine learning (Tax et al., 2017; Dutta et al., 2021; Hamman & Dutta, 2024; Ehrlich et al.,
130
      2022; Liang et al., 2024; Wollstadt et al., 2023; Mohamadi et al., 2023; Venkatesh et al., 2024).
131
      However, interpreting spuriousness in datasets through the lens of PID and observing novel empirical
132   tradeoffs between spuriousness and worst-group accuracy is unexplored. Additionally, there is limited
133   work on calculating PID values for high dimensional multivariate continuous data. Some existing
134   works (Dutta et al., 2021; Venkatesh et al., 2024) handle continuous data with Gaussian assumptions
135   while (Pakman et al., 2021) considers one-dimensional multivariate case. Hence, estimating PID
136   for high-dimensional data through proper dimensionality reduction and discretization is also fairly
137   open. For dimensionality reduction, different learning based methods exist (Hotelling, 1933; Law &
138   Jain, 2006; Lee & Verleysen, 2005; Wang et al., 2015; 2014; Sadeghi & Armanfard, 2023). Similarly,
139   for discretization, different clustering algorithms exist, e.g., k-means clustering (MacQueen et al.,
140
      1967; Bradley et al., 2000), deep embedded clustering (Xie et al., 2016). There are also some works
      that try to separate spurious and core features in the feature space of deep neural networks using
141
      external feedback (Sohoni et al., 2020; Kattakinda et al., 2022). In this work, along the lines of an
142
      autoencoder-based clustering setup in Guo et al. (2017), we train an autoencoder to jointly learn a good
143   lower-dimensional representation of the input image data in a self-supervised manner (with additional
144   bottleneck structure from Sadeghi & Armanfard (2023)) while also clustering simultaneously to deal
145   with the challenge of high dimensional and continuous image data.

      Background on Partial Information Decomposition: We provide a brief background on PID that
163   would be relevant for the rest of the paper. The classical information-theoretic quantification of the
164   total information that two random variables A and B together hold about Y is given by the mutual
165   information I(Y ; A, B) (see (Cover & Thomas, 2012) for a background on mutual information).
166   Mutual information I(Y ; A, B) is defined as the KL divergence (Cover & Thomas, 2012) between
167   the joint distribution PY AB and the product of the marginal distributions PY ⊗ PAB and would go
168   to zero if and only if (A, B) is independent of Y . Intuitively, this mutual information captures the
169   total predictive power about Y that is present jointly in (A, B) together, i.e., how well can one learn
170   Y from (A, B) together. However, I(Y ; A, B) only captures the total information content about Y
171
      jointly in (A, B) and does not unravel anything about what is unique or shared between A and B.
172   PID (Bertschinger et al., 2014; Banerjee et al., 2018) provides a mathematical framework that
173   decomposes the total information content I(Y ; A, B) into four non-negative terms (also see Fig. 2):
174              I(Y ; A, B) = Uni(Y :B|A) + Uni(Y :A|B) + Red(Y :A, B) + Syn(Y :A, B).                     (1)
175
176
      Here, Uni(Y :A|B) denotes the unique information about Y that is
      only in A but not in B and Uni(Y :B|A) denotes the unique infor-
177
      mation about Y that is only in B but not in A. Next, Red(Y :A, B)
178
      denotes redundant information (common knowledge) about Y in
179   both A and B. Lastly, Syn(Y :A, B) is an interesting term that de-
180   notes the synergistic information that is present only jointly in A, B
181   but not in any one of them individually, e.g., a public and private key
182   can jointly reveal information not in any one of them alone.

231   Interpreting Unique Information Uni(Y :B|F ) and Uni(Y :F |B): Unique information captures in-
232   formation that is unique in one feature and cannot be obtained from another. To explain the role of
233
      unique information in interpreting spuriousness, we draw upon a concept in statistical decision theory
      called Blackwell Sufficiency (Blackwell, 1953) which investigates when a random variable is “more
234
      informative” (or “less noisy”) than another for inference (also relates to stochastic degradation of chan-
235
      nels (Venkatesh et al., 2023; Raginsky, 2011)). Let us first discuss this notion intuitively when trying
236   to infer Y using two random variables F and B. Suppose, there exists a trans-
237   formation on F to give a new random variable B ′ which is always equivalent
238   to B for predicting Y (similar predictive power). We note that B ′ and B do
239   not necessarily have to be the same since we only care about inferring Y . In
240   fact, B and B ′ can have additional irrelevant information that do not pertain to
241   Y , but solely for the purpose of inferring Y , they need to be equivalent. Then, Figure 4: Blackwell
242   F will be regarded as “sufficient” with respect to B for predicting Y since F Sufficiency
243   can itself provide all the information that B has about Y (see Fig. 4 and first two cases of Fig. 3).
244   Definition 2 (Blackwell Sufficiency (Blackwell, 1953)). A conditional distribution PF |Y is Blackwell
245   sufficient with respect to another conditional distribution PB|Y if and only if there exists a stochastic
246   transformation (equivalently another conditional distribution PB ′ |F with both B and B ′ ∈ B) such
247   that PB ′ |F ◦ PF |Y = PB|Y .
248
249   In fact, the unique information Uni(Y :B|F ) is 0 if and only if PF |Y is Blackwell sufficient with
250   respect to PB|Y (see Theorem 1, the proof is given in the Appendix F).
251   Theorem 1 (Interpretability Insights from Unique Information). The following properties hold:
252
      • Uni(Y :B|F ) ≤ I(Y ; B) and goes to 0 if the spurious feature B is independent of the target Y .
253     However, Uni(Y :B|F ) may be 0 even if I(Y ; B) > 0.
254
255   • Uni(Y :B|F ) = 0 if and only if PF |Y is Blackwell sufficient with respect to PB|Y .
256   • Uni(Y :B|F ) ≤ Uni(Y :B ′ |F ′ ), i.e., it is non-decreasing if some features from the core set are
257     moved to the spurious set, i.e., B ′ = B ∪ W and F ′ = F \W .
258
259   Since unique information Uni(Y :B|F ) = 0 if and only if PF |Y is Blackwell Sufficient with respect to
260   PB|Y , we note that Uni(Y :B|F ) > 0 captures the “departure” from Blackwell Sufficiency, and thus
261   quantifies relative informativeness. Intuitively, what this means is that for a data distribution, there is
262   no such transformation on core feature F that is equivalent to the spurious feature B for the purpose
263   of predicting Y . This essentially makes spurious feature B indispensable for predicting Y , forcing
264
      a model to emphasize it in decision-making. A similar argument can be made for Uni(Y :F |B).
      Furthermore, Uni(Y :B|F ) also satisfies an intuitive property that as more features get categorized as
265
      spurious instead of core, the unique information in the spurious set would keep increasing.

      3.2   N OVEL INFORMATION - THEORETIC MEASURE OF SPURIOUSNESS
293   Our objective is to quantify a dataset’s spuriousness which steers machine learning models towards
294   the spurious features over the core features. To this end, we will examine some candidate measures
295   (Msp ) of spuriousness through examples and counterexamples and systematically arrive at a measure
296   that meets our requirements. Since we are trying to capture spuriousness which arises when the target
297   variable Y is associated with the spurious features B, we might first consider the mutual information
298   I(Y ; B) as a candidate measure for spuriousness since it captures the dependence between Y and B.
299   Candidate Measure 1. Msp = I(Y ; B).
300
301   Counterexample 1. We refer to the example in Lemma 1 where Uni(Y :B|F ) = 0. Hence,
302   I(Y ; B) = Uni(Y :B|F ) + Red(Y :F, B) = Red(Y :F, B). Here, our candidate measure Msp =
303   I(Y ; B) is positive which would indicate “spuriousness,” i.e., undesirable steering towards B. How-
304
      ever, in this case the model can use either spurious features B or core features F (see Lemma 1)
      without any preference. Thus, I(Y ; B) is not well suited to be a measure of undesirable spuriousness.

      A    A DDITIONAL E XPERIMENT 1: AUTOMATIC S EGMENTATION OF F EATURES
757
758
759   Segmentation, a component of our Spurious Disentangler, plays a pivotal role in identifying core
760   features from spurious ones. Identifying spurious features (pixels) without any additional information
761   is challenging in image datasets, particularly if they lack group labels. However, in supervised classi-
762
      fication tasks, the availability of target labels corresponding directly to the goal of the classification
      task (and hence some partial knowledge of what the core features should be if not the exact pixels)
763
      often offers a practical workaround. Specifically, one can leverage automatic segmentation to at least
764
      perform object detection and choose the most relevant objects as the “core”. Then, the regions of an
765   image not associated with the “core” objects can often be considered a subset of spurious features.
766
767   Advances in Open-Vocabulary Semantic Segmentation (OVSS) have significantly reduced the de-
768
      pendence on task-specific training by enabling generalization to unseen categories without requiring
      labeled data. To leverage these advancements, we employ CLIPSeg (Lüddecke & Ecker, 2022),
769
      a state-of-the-art OVSS model, to generate masks for various objects in a zero-shot manner using
770
      partial knowledge of the classification task in mind. For instance, in the Waterbird dataset, we specify
771   the prompt "bird" to obtain a mask for the bird object. This approach utilizes publicly available
772   fine-grained weights, enabling efficient and accurate segmentation without additional labeled data.
773
774
      The generated mask is applied to the original image to extract the foreground, while the background
      is obtained by multiplying the original image with 1 − mask, as illustrated in Fig. 13. Fig. 14 reveals
775
      a negative correlation between the worst-group accuracy and increasing values of Msp , calculated
776
      using the obtained background and foreground.

1213   Formalizing and analyzing the information distribution among spurious and core features can provide
1214   a theoretical understanding of the biases or spuriousness of any dataset. Calculating the measure
1215   of spuriousness introduces an efficient way to assess dataset quality before performing the actual
1216   training or fine-tuning which can be computationally intensive, particularly in the era of foundational
1217   models. In this work, we theoretically justify the importance of each component of partial information
1218
       decomposition (PID) for understanding the nature of dataset and the prediction. We also justify the
       validation of the proposed measure of spuriousness with examples and counterexamples along with
1219
       experimental findings that proposed measure has a relationship with worst-group accuracy (and hence,
1220
       dataset quality). We also introduce the use of Spurious Disentangler for handling high dimensional
1221   image data and estimating the PIDs (Broader Impacts in Appendix E).
1222
1223   Limitations: (i) Identifying spurious features and core features of a given dataset automatically
1224
       is not always straightforward. Future work will look into alternate techniques, such as causal
       discovery (Zanga et al., 2022) (recently using LLMs (Liu et al., 2024)) as well as validation on NLP
1225
       datasets. (ii) The estimation is highly data-dependent. A small change in the dataset can greatly
1226
       affect the PID values. Future work will look into sensitivity and estimation error analysis. (iii) The
1227   efficiency and robustness of the Spurious Disentangler can also be improved. (iv) Additionally, there
1228   can be groups of spurious features rather than just one which can have nuanced interplay among
1229   them, which is another interesting direction.

1246   PID (Bertschinger et al., 2014; Banerjee et al., 2018) provides a mathematical framework that
1247   decomposes the total information content I(Y ; A, B) into four non-negative terms:
1248            I(Y ; A, B) = Uni(Y :B|A) + Uni(Y :A|B) + Red(Y :A, B) + Syn(Y :A, B).           (5)

1251   2014; Banerjee et al., 2018):
1252                                  I(Y ; A) = Uni(Y :A|B) + Red(Y :A, B).                                 (6)
