009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Learning Conditional average treatment effect estimation from observational data
012            is a challenging task due to the existence of unobserved confounders. Previous
013            methods mostly focus on assuming the Ignorability assumption ignoring the un-
014            observed confounders or overlooking the impact of an apriori knowledge on the
015            generation process of the latent variable, which can be quite impractical in real-
016            world scenarios. Motivated by the recent advances in the latent variable modeling,
017            we propose to capture the unobserved latent space using diffusion model, and
018
               accordingly to estimate the causal effect. More concretely, we build on the reverse
               diffusion process for the unobserved confounders as a Markov chain conditioned
019
               on an apriori knowledge. In order to implement our model in a feasible way, we
020
               derive the variational bound in closed form. In the experiments, we compare our
021            model with the state-of-the-art methods based on both synthetic and real-world
022            datasets, demonstrating consistent improvements of our model.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Estimating the Conditional average treatment effect estimation (CATE) or Heterogeneous Treatment
028   Effect (HTE) from observational data is a fundamental problem across a wide variety of domains. For
029   example, re-weighting the training instances with the inverse propensity score (IPS) in recommender
030   system (Wang et al., 2021; 2022), measuring the effect of a certain medicine against a disease in
031   healthcare (Shalit, 2020) and providing counterfactual visual explanations in computer vision (Goyal
032   et al., 2019). In this paper, we focus on these measure problems from confounders perspective.
033   How to measure the confounder is an essential problem in estimating CATE of an treatment A
034   (e.g.,medicine) on an individual with features X (e.g., demographic characteristics ). A confounder
035   is a variable which affects both the treatment and the outcome. On the one hand, one can account
036   for CATE by controlling it with the Ignorability assumption in mind, i.e., there does not exists the
037   unobserved confounder. The most crucial mechanism lie in balance the distribution among groups,
038   usually through inverse propensity weighting (IPW) or covariate adjustment (Yao et al., 2021; Louizos
039
      et al., 2017). While quite a lot of promising models have been proposed and achieved impressive
      performance, such as, the representative CFR (Shalit et al., 2017), the augmented IPW estimator
040
      DR (Funk et al., 2011) and so on, these methods build on the Ignorability assumption, which can be
041
      impractical in real-world scenarios. On the other hand, exactly collecting all of valid confounders
042   is impossible in the general case. For example, demographic characteristics and genetic factor can
043   both affect the choice of medication to a patient, and the patient’s health. However we can only have
044   access to the former in the observational data. As illustrated in Figure 1, the genetic factor acts as
045   an unobserved confounder Z both affecting the treatment A and health outcomes Y , and without
046   controlling it we can not block the backdoor path: A ← Z → Y as of estimating the causal effect of
047   treatments on health measures.
048   In the past few years, some prominent generative models have been proposed to generate such
049   unobserved confounder that we could utilize it to isolate the causal effect of treatment on outcome.
050   For instance, VAE-based method CEVAE (Louizos et al., 2017) assume that there exists a proxy
051   variable in causal graph, and then generates the hidden confounder Z by optimizing the variational
052   lower bound of this graphical model, GANITE (Yoon et al., 2018) aims to generate the counterfactual
053   distributions using GAN, and accordingly to infer the CATE in an unbiased settings. Additionally,
      some advanced techniques are also applied to reconstruct or generate the hidden confounder, like

055   models (Josse et al., 2020) and more (Li & Zhu, 2022; Yao et al., 2021).

095   In the context of estimating CATE, understanding the underlying mechanisms of data generation
096   and transformation is crucial. The Diffusion Denoising Probabilistic Model (DDPM) framework,
097   which simulates the conversion of real data into Gaussian noise and its subsequent reversal, provides
098   a powerful tool for modeling complex data distributions. This background is essential for our
099   application of diffusion models to identify unobserved confounders—a critical step in accurately
100   estimating CATE under the presence of hidden biases.

      The process of conditional image generation using diffusion models has been extensively explored
188
      (Luo & Hu, 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Ni et al., 2023). Unlike the well-documented generation
189   of images where generated outputs can be directly compared with training data, the generation of
190   unobserved confounders Z presents unique challenges due to the absence of observable data for Z.
191   This issue necessitates the development of effective representations for unobserved variables. We
192   address the challenge of learning these representations in Section 3.2. The formulation begins with
193   using observed data X to infer unobserved confounders Z through a diffusion model.
194

195   introducing a latent variable η to capture the shared parental information of the observed variable X

347   unobserved confounders, which includes detailing both the forward and reverse diffusion processes.
348   We introduce a variational lower bound formulation as our training objective, which facilitates
349   efficient model optimization by approximating the intractable log-likelihood. Lastly, we demonstrate
350   how these synthesized unobserved confounders are integrated into regression models to enhance the
351   accuracy of CATE estimation.

660
661
662   Treatment Effect Estimation. Accurate and correct estimation of Conditional average treatment
663   effect estimation is an challenging task in real-world scenarios, since the lack of counterfactuals
664   can lead to an biased estimation from observational study. To alleviate this problem, early methods,
665
      like re-weighting models (Austin, 2011; Imai & Ratkovic, 2014; Fong et al., 2018), use the Inverse
      propensity weighting (IPW) mechanism to reduce selection bias based on covariates. Another active
666
      top line of research is to incorporate traditional machine learning into the study of estimating CATE,
667
      like Bayesian Additive Regression Trees (BART) (Hill, 2011), Random Forests (RF) (Breiman,
668   2001), Causal Forests (CF) (Wager & Athey, 2018), etc. In order to balance the distribution among
669   groups in representation space, some advanced models are designed, like DragonNet (Shi et al.,
670   2019) , CFR (Shalit et al., 2017), QHTE (Qin et al., 2021), etc. There models use more flexibility
671   and sophisticated technique, like Integral Probability Metric (IPM), to pull in that distributions and
672   while minimize generalization bound for CATE estimation. While remarkable progresses have made
673   by these models, the premise that they need to get the Ignorability assumption hold. However,
674   the Ignorability assumption is untestable in practice. To this end, some promising deep generative
675   models are proposed to generate latent variables. For example, Causal Effect Variational Autoen-
676
      coder (CEVAE) (Louizos et al., 2017) leverage Variational Autoencoders to obtain the unobserved
      confounders and simultaneously infer causal effects, GAITE (Yoon et al., 2018) use Generative
677
      Adversarial Nets (GANs) framework to capture the uncertainty in the counterfactual distributions.
678
      While remarkable progresses have made by these models, here are some intrinsic limitations for
679   modeling latent variables. For examples, GAN-based methods could be unstable in modeling CATE
680   due to the adversarial losses. VAEs make substantially weaker assumptions in generating the structure
681   of the hidden confounders (Louizos et al., 2017), which could restrict the model’s flexibility. In
682   this paper, we build on diffusion model to generate the unobserved confounders and accordingly to
683   measure CATE. The benefits are presented in two aspects; (1) Comparing to CEVAE, diffusion model
684   has less assumptions in our settings, which is great of importance for estimating CATE; (2) Diffusion
685   model has a comparative stable loss function, which indeed contribute to the generation process of
686   unobserved confounders.
687   Diffusion Model. Diffusion Model is a concept describing the study of the deep generative process. It
688   basically involves two types of Markov chains, called forward diffusion process and reverse diffusion
689   process respectively. The former is capable of converting any data distribution into a simple or
690   noise prior distribution, while the latter aims to reconstruct the original data distribution by a reverse
691   Markov chain. In that process, the goal is to learn a transition kernels parameterized by deep neural
692   networks (Yang et al., 2022) and accordingly to generate the desired data. Due to its flexibility and
693
      strength, recent years have witnessed many studies on incorporating diffusion model into a variety of
      challenging domains (Yang et al., 2022; Luo & Hu, 2021; Ho et al., 2020) and achieved impressive
694
      results. For example, inspired by the diffusion model in computer vision, Luo & Hu (2021) proposes
695
      to generate 3D point cloud by a Markov chain conditioned on certain shape latent. In natural language
696   processing, in order to handle more complex controls in generating text, Diffusion-LM (Li et al.,
697   2022) is proposed as a new language model based on continuous diffusion. Additionally, Adaptive
698   Denoising Purification (Yoon et al., 2021) proposes an effective randomized purification scheme to
699   purify attacked images in robust learning. Similar to these applications, in this paper, we proposed
700   to generate the unobserved confounders by a Markov chain conditioned on the generate factor that
701   is derived from the observed confounders. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work on
      estimating conditional average treatment effect estimation.

      C     BACKGROUND : H ETEROGENEOUS T REATMENT E FFECT
703
704
      Under the Neyman-Rubin potential outcomes framework (Rubin, 2005), CATE estimation aims to
705
      measure the causal effect of a treatment or intervention a ∈ A on the outcome y ∈ Y for given
706   the unit’s confounders or descriptions x ∈ X . Throughout this paper, we only focus on the binary
707   treatment case, where A = {0, 1}, y represents the factual outcome. We treat units which received
708   treatment, i.e., a = 1 as treated units and the other units with a = 0 as control units. The Conditional
709   Average Treatment Effect (CATE) for unit x is (Shalit et al., 2017):
710                                             τ (x) := E[Y1 − Y0 |x]                                    (11)
711
       Where Ya denotes the potential outcome for treatment a. In practice, we can only observe the factual
712   outcome with respect to treatment assignment, i.e., y = Y0 if a = 0, otherwise y = Y1 . Usually, we
713   build on three significant assumptions to guarantee that the potential outcomes are identifiable from
714   observational study.
715
      Assumption 1. Consistency. For a given patient with treatment assignment a, then the potential
716
      outcome for the treatment a is the same as the observed (factual) outcome: Ya = y
717
718   Assumption 2. Positivity (Overlap) . if P (X = x) ̸= 0, then P (A = a|X = x) > 0, ∀ a and x.
719   Assumption 3. Strong ignorability. For a given patient (i), the treatment are independent of the
720   potential outcomes if given the confounders X : A ⊥⊥ Y1 , Y0 |X.
721
722   With these assumptions in mind, the estimation on potential outcomes could be transformed into
723
      identifiable estimation from a statistical point of view. In other words, we call that the counterfactual
      outcomes can be identified under these assumptions, i.e, τ (x) = E[Y |X = x, A = 1] − E[Y |X =
724
      x, A = 0]. From machine learning perspective, these observational dataset can be modeled via
725
      a standard supervised learning model, such as SVM, for estimating τ (x). However, this model
726   could be unreliable and unviable employed to estimate the future counterfactual outcomes under the
727   fact that without adjusting for the bias introduced by the unobserved confounders and imbalanced
728   distribution between treated groups and control groups. The existing generative-based models
729   can achieve promising results in generating unobserved confounders (Louizos et al., 2017) and
730   counterfactuals (Yoon et al., 2018), which indeed eliminate the influence from backdoor between
731   treatment and outcome. However, they have some inherent limitations, which would hinder the
732   model’s flexibility and performance. In this paper, we build on the prominent diffusion model to
733   generate the unobserved confounders, and accordingly align the distribution between treated groups
734
      and control groups and measure the CATE. We proceed in two steps: (1) Generate the unobserved
      confounders conditioned on generation factor; (2) Balance the confounder’s representation in latent
735
      space and measuring the CATE based on the observed and unobserved confounders.
