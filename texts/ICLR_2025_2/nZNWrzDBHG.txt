011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Video editing task has gained widespread attention in recent years due to their
014            practical applications and rapid advancements, driven by the emergence of dif-
015            fusion techniques and Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs). How-
016            ever, current automatic evaluation metrics for video editing are mostly unreli-
017            able and poorly aligned with human judgments. As a result, researchers heavily
018
               rely on human annotation for evaluation, which is not only time-consuming and
               labor-intensive but also difficult to ensure consistency and objectivity. To address
019
               this issue, we introduce VEBench, the largest-ever video editing meta-evaluation
020
               benchmark to evaluate the reliability of automatic metrics. It includes 152 video
021            clips and 962 text prompts, from which 160 instances are sampled to generate
022            1,280 edited videos using 8 open-source video editing models, accompanied by
023            human annotations. Especially, the text prompts are first crafted using GPT-4,
024            followed by manual review and careful categorization based on editing types for
025            a systematic evaluation. Our human annotations cover 3 criteria: Textual Faith-
026            fulness, Frame Consistency, and Video Fidelity, ensuring the comprehensiveness
027            of evaluation. Since human evaluation is costly, we also propose VEScore, em-
028            ploying MLLMs as evaluators to assess edited videos from the criteria above.
029
               Experiments show that the best-performing video editing model only reaches an
               average score of 3.18 (out of a perfect 5), highlighting the challenge of VEBench.
030
               Besides, results from more than 10 MLLMs demonstrate the great potential of
031
               utilizing VEScore for automatic evaluation. Notably, for Textual Faithfulness,
032            VEScore equipped with LLaVA-OneVision-7B achieves a Pearson Correlation
033            score of 0.48, significantly outperforming previous methods based on CLIP with
034            the highest score of 0.21. The dataset and code will be released upon acceptance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Video editing has witnessed significant improvements in recent years, with the burgeoning of diffu-
040
      sion techniques (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022) and Multi-modal Large Language Models
041
      (MLLMs) (OpenAI, 2024; Reid et al., 2024). Notably, most studies in this field focus on text-guided
042   video editing (Wu et al., 2023a; Yang et al., 2023), which offers a straightforward and intuitive edit-
043   ing process by simply modifying the video caption.
044
045
      Despite these advancements, the evaluation of video editing still lacks reliable metrics. Current au-
      tomatic evaluation methods fall short in both comprehensiveness and robustness, due to the inherent
046
      complexity of assessing the quality of edits. On the one hand, it’s evident that there is a lack of a
047
      unified standard for these evaluation methods. The varied criteria used across studies, ranging from
048   textual alignment to temporal consistency, hinder the systematic comparison of different works (Qi
049   et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023a). On the other hand, existing CLIP-based metrics (Hessel et al., 2021;
050   Parmar et al., 2023) often poorly align with human judgments, leading to discrepancies in evalua-
051   tion results (Wu et al., 2023a; Qi et al., 2023; Geyer et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). Consequently,
052   the evaluation of video editing often necessitates substantial reliance on human annotations, which
053   are time-consuming, labor-intensive, and prone to inconsistency due to subjective interpretations by
      different annotators.


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      To tackle these challenges, we propose VEBench in this paper. It serves as a comprehensive meta-
055   evaluation benchmark to aid in developing more effective automatic evaluation systems for video
056   editing. As shown in Figure 1, the establishment of VEBench goes through the following three
057   steps:
058
      (1) Data Collection. We gather video clips from the DAVIS dataset (Pont-Tuset et al., 2017; Caelles
059
      et al., 2018; 2019) and develop an automatic method to efficiently generate a diverse range of text
060
      prompts. Specifically, we notice that the primary focus of existing text prompts is often on entities
061   within video captions (Wu et al., 2023a; Yang et al., 2023). To leverage this, we utilize GPT-4V
062   to generate video captions as source prompts, and subsequently create text prompts by employing
063   an LLM to replace the identified entities within these captions. This process is then followed by
064   manual reviews to ensure the quality of prompts. Through the above steps, we collect instances
065   for the subsequent data taxonomy, with each instance containing a video, video caption, and text
066   prompt.
067   (2) Data Taxonomy. We then categorize the above instances based on editing type (Content or
068   Style) and the number of editing targets (Single or Multiple), enabling in-depth analyses of model
069   capabilities from multiple perspectives. Content Editing primarily focuses on modifying specific
070   entities within the video, such as animals or objects. Whereas, Style Editing is concerned with
071   transforming the overall artistic style of the video, such as converting the original video into Van
072   Gogh’s style or comic style. The number of editing targets is closely related to difficulty levels,
073   with Multiple-Target editing requiring the models to precisely follow the text prompts, meanwhile
074   producing natural videos.
075   (3) Data Annotation. We finally propose three evaluation criteria to comprehensively assess the
076   edited videos: Textual Faithfulness, Frame Consistency, and Video Fidelity. Utilizing these criteria,
077   we engage annotators to evaluate edited videos, generated by a wide range of models including 8
078   open-source video editing models. This approach not only facilitates a systematic analysis of these
079   models but also enables a meta-evaluation of current and future video editing metrics.
080   Furthermore, we propose an automatic scoring system, VEScore, leveraging the capabilities of
081   various MLLMs. This system is inspired by recent studies (Fu et al., 2023; Yujie et al., 2023) that
082   effectively utilize (M)LLMs to assess tasks challenging for traditional metrics. Given that current
083   MLLMs have acquired extensive world knowledge from vast training corpora and exhibit improved
084   alignment with human preferences through visual instruction tuning, VEScore serves as a more
085   robust and scalable automatic method compared to traditional metrics.
086   Experiments on various video editing models and automatic evaluation metrics show that, VEBench
087   poses significant challenges, with the top-performing model achieving an average score of 3.18 out
088   of 5 across the three criteria. More analyses indicate that existing models struggle with editing types
089   involving fine details, such as Human Editing and Animal Editing. Besides, they perform signif-
090   icantly worse on Multiple-Target editing, with an average drop of 2 points from Single-Target to
091   Multiple-Target categories. Finally, we demonstrate the great potential of employing VEScore for
092   automatic evaluation after investigating more than 10 MLLMs. Though there is still much room for
093
      improvement, we observe notable improvements in alignments with human preference over tradi-
      tional metrics across all criteria.
094
095   In summary, we contribute VEBench and VEScore to foster the development of comprehensive
096   and automatic evaluation for video editing. We suggest future research to explore their methods
097   on VEBench and further enhance VEScore by employing more robust MLLMs and refining the
098   instructions through prompt engineering.

102   2.1   T EXT- GUIDED V IDEO E DITING
103
104   Benchmarks In the development process of text-guided video editing, there has been a notable
105   lack of a unified framework for evaluation. Previous studies typically select several video clips from
106   DAVIS (Wu et al., 2023a) or YouTube (Molad et al., 2023) and manually design text prompts for
107   further inference and evaluation. TGVE (Wu et al., 2023b) summarizes previous evaluation methods
      and introduces a new benchmark. However, it is limited by the data scale (comprising 76 videos and


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      304 text prompts) and coverage of editing types (involving only 4 types). In comparison, VEBench
109   offers richer and more diverse instances for evaluation. Besides, it serves as a meta-evaluation
110   benchmark for developing automatic scoring systems.
111
112
      Evaluation Metrics There are three key automatic evaluation metrics commonly employed in
113
      recent years for text-guided video editing task:
114
115   • Textual Alignment (Wu et al., 2023a). This metric assesses the degree of alignment between the
116     text prompts and the edited video frames by computing the average similarity.
117
118
      • Frame Accuracy (Parmar et al., 2023). This metric measures the percentage of frames in edited
        videos that have a higher CLIP similarity to the text prompt than to the source prompt, indicating
119
        the effectiveness of per-frame edits.
120
121   • Temporal Consistency (Qi et al., 2023). It evaluates the smoothness of edits across consecutive
122     frames by computing the similarity between consecutive frames.
123
124   However, due to the poor reliability of these automatic metrics, previous studies have to rely on User
125
      Preference (Wu et al., 2023a; Geyer et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Ceylan et al., 2023; Li et al.,
      2024b; Kara et al., 2024) for more accurate evaluation. Nonetheless, they often suffer from higher
126
      costs and inconsistency across different studies due to the subjectivity of annotators.
127
128
129   Methods Most approaches for text-guided video editing are built upon diffusion models, which
130
      demonstrate remarkable success in image synthesis (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022; Ramesh
      et al., 2022) and image editing (Zhang et al., 2023b; Tumanyan et al., 2023). Dreamix (Molad et al.,
131
      2023) is the first to utilize video diffusion models on this task through fine-tuning. However, consid-
132
      ering computational constraints and overall video quality, later studies (Wu et al., 2023a; Qi et al.,
133   2023) have shifted towards leveraging image diffusion models for video editing. Besides, recent
134   research (Ceylan et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b; Kara et al., 2024) further focuses on maintaining the
135   temporal consistency between adjacent frames, effectively ensuring the smoothness and coherence
136   of produced videos.
137
138
      2.2   M ETA E VALUATION
139
140   Meta evaluation aims to assess the reliability of automatic metrics by examining how closely they
141   align with human judgments (Fabbri et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2023). There are three primary corre-
142   lation measures used in meta-evaluation: Pearson Correlation (r) (Freedman et al., 2007), which
143   determines the linear association between two variables; Spearman’s Rho (ρ) (Zar, 2005), which
144   evaluates the monotonic connection between two variables; and Kendall’s Tau (τ ) (Kendall, 1938),
145   which assesses the ordinal relationship between two variables. Previous studies have applied meta
146   evaluation to tasks such as text summarization (Wang et al., 2020; Fabbri et al., 2021; Gopalakr-
147
      ishnan et al., 2023), machine translation (Freitag et al., 2021), and image-text matching task (Yujie
      et al., 2023), contributing to the development of more comprehensive evaluation metrics that better
148
      correlate with human judgments.
149
150
151   2.3   (M)LLM S AS E VALUATORS
152
153
      Recently, (M)LLMs have greatly advanced the development of various research fields (OpenAI,
      2023; Team et al., 2023). One popular direction is utilizing (M)LLMs as automatic scoring sys-
154
      tems (Fu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Chan et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023; Yujie et al., 2023).
155
      By prompting them to follow evaluation criteria, (M)LLMs can provide judgments that align more
156   closely with human preferences than traditional metrics. Thus, this approach proves particularly
157   valuable for complex tasks that heavily rely on human evaluations, such as open-ended generation
158   (Zheng et al., 2023) and text-to-image generation (Yujie et al., 2023). For text-guided video editing,
159   using MLLMs for automatic evaluation remains unexplored, due to the absence of a unified evalua-
160   tion benchmark and the inherently challenging nature of the task. To the best of our knowledge, our
161   work is the first attempt to explore this approach for text-guided video editing evaluation and also
      provide the best configuration after investigating more than 10 MLLMs.

      In this section, we describe the three steps of constructing VEBench: data collection (Section 3.1),
      data taxonomy (Section 3.2), and data annotation (Section 3.3).

192
193   3.1       DATA C OLLECTION
194
195   The data collection process of VEBench consists of Video Collection and Text Prompt Generation
196   substeps. All data produced in each substep is manually reviewed to ensure the quality, detailed in
197
      Appendix A.
198
199   Video Collection Following previous research (Wu et al., 2023a), we gather video clips from the
200   publicly available DAVIS datasets (Pont-Tuset et al., 2017; Caelles et al., 2018; 2019), which are
201   specifically tailored for video object segmentation tasks. By collating data from DAVIS 2017 to
202
      DAVIS 2019 and removing duplicates, we obtain 210 video clips in total. Subsequently, we conduct
      a careful manual review to filter out videos featuring indistinct objects or of substandard quality and
203
      finally obtain 152 high-quality videos suitable for editing. Given the current video editing models
204
      do not support excessively long videos or high-resolution inputs, we resize all videos to a resolution
205   of 480 × 480 and uniformly sample 25 frames from the original videos.
206
207
      Text Prompt Generation As illustrated in the left of Figure 1, we develop an entity-centric au-
208
      tomatic generation approach to produce diverse text prompts. This draws inspiration from prior
209
      research on object, background, and animal editing (Wu et al., 2023a; Huang et al., 2024), where
210   most edited targets typically manifest as entities within the corresponding video captions. To be spe-
211   cific, we first adopt GPT-4V to obtain captions for each video, which also serve as source prompts in
212   text-guided video editing task. To ensure quality, a manual review is conducted to eliminate any in-
213   accuracies in the generated captions. Next, we utilize GPT-4 to extract entities from these captions.
214   Then, text prompts are crafted by instructing GPT-4 based on the extracted entities, as shown in
215   Appendix B.1. Especially, the given instruction encourages GPT-4 to maintain the original semantic
      meaning of captions, thereby enhancing the suitability for video editing task.

267   3.3   DATA A NNOTATION
268
269   The further goal of VEBench is to facilitate the meta-evaluation for video editing scoring systems.
      To this end, we sample 160 instances, which are then inferred by 8 different video editing models,

270
      resulting in a total of 1,280 edited videos. Then, we hire 4 professional annotators to provide human
271   evaluation for these generated videos, as illustrated in the lower-right of Figure 1. Each annotator
272   is assigned 30 instances per day to avoid fatigue and we also conduct regular spot checks to ensure
273   annotation quality. We consider the following criteria to comprehensively evaluate each video:
274
275   • Textual Faithfulness. It evaluates how well the edited video aligns with the text prompt. A higher
276     score indicates that the edited video accurately reflects all the details specified in the text prompt.
277
278   • Frame Consistency. This metric assesses the continuity between frames in the edited video. A
279     higher score indicates seamless transitions with no noticeable jumps between frames.
280
      • Video Fidelity. This measures the quality and visual consistency of the edited video, considering
281
        factors such as color, resolution, dynamic range, and motion coherence. A higher score indicates
282     excellent video quality, with no detectable issues in color, sharpness, or continuity, whereas a
283     lower score suggests problems such as blurriness, color distortion, and poor continuity.
284
285   The annotated scores for all criteria range from 1 to 5, where 5 denotes a state of flawlessness. De-
286   tailed annotation guidelines regarding these criteria are provided in Appendix C.1. As implemented
287   in FETV (Liu et al., 2024b), we select Kendall’s Tau (τ ), Spearman’s Rho (ρ), and Krippendorff’s
288   Alpha (α) (Krippendorff, 2018) for the evaluation of inter-annotator agreements. From Table 2 we
289   observe that the inter-annotator agreements across all metrics and three criteria exceed 0.6, indicat-
290   ing minimal variability among annotators and reinforcing the reliability of the human annotation
291   process.
292   The detailed human annotation interface, including the display of source prompts, text prompts, and
293   the original and edited videos, can be found in the Appendix C.2.

307   4    MLLM S AS E VALUATORS FOR TEXT- GUIDED VIDEO EDITING
308
309   Due to the intricate nature of this task, previous automatic evaluation metrics are mostly built upon
310   CLIP models (Radford et al., 2021). However, CLIP models are recognized for their limited dis-
311   cernment capabilities, particularly in complex scenarios (Tong et al., 2024). Besides, these intuitive
312   approaches often lack comprehensiveness and have not undergone strict verification, leading to the
313   poor reliability of these metrics.
314   Inspired by previous research on the effectiveness of using (M)LLMs to evaluate various tasks (e.g.,
315   open-ended questions) (Fu et al., 2023; Chan et al., 2023; Yujie et al., 2023), we introduce MLLMs
316   as evaluators to address this challenge.
317
      As illustrated in Figure 2, for each instance, an MLLM processes the instruction, source prompt, text
318   prompt, edited video, and the detailed guideline of evaluation criteria to generate the corresponding
319   scores. Notably, not all MLLMs support video inputs. For the models that do, we directly input
320   the videos. For the remaining models, we uniformly sample frames from the videos and use them
321   as multiple image inputs. Then, using three separate queries, we can derive three scores for each
322   criterion based on the responses of MLLM. By leveraging its capabilities in visual perception and
323   instruction-following, we derive detailed scores for each criterion, akin to those provided by human
      annotators.

517   6   C ONCLUSION
518
519
520   In this paper, we introduce VEBench, a comprehensive meta-evaluation benchmark designed to
521   assess the reliability of automatic evaluation metrics in text-guided video editing. VEBench com-
522   prises 152 high-quality video clips, 962 meticulously crafted text prompts, and human annotations
523   of 1,280 edited videos generated by 8 state-of-the-art open-source video editing models. By catego-
524
      rizing the data based on editing types and the number of targets, we provide a structured framework
      for in-depth analysis of model capabilities.
525
526   Our experiments reveal that existing video editing models still face significant challenges, partic-
527   ularly in ensuring frame consistency and video fidelity, as well as handling complex editing tasks
528   involving multiple targets. Notably, we demonstrate that current automatic evaluation metrics, pri-
529   marily based on CLIP models, exhibit poor alignment with human judgments.
530   To address this gap, we propose VEScore, leveraging MLLMs as automatic evaluators. Our results
531   from more than 10 prominent MLLMs show that VEScore aligns with human evaluations signif-
532   icantly better than traditional metrics across all criteria: Textual Faithfulness, Frame Consistency,
533   and Video Fidelity. This finding underscores the potential of MLLMs to automate the evaluation
534   process effectively, reducing the reliance on time-consuming and inconsistent human annotations.
535   We believe that VEBench will serve as a valuable resource for the research community, facilitating
536   the development of more robust video editing models and more reliable automatic evaluation met-
537   rics. Future work could explore enhancing the evaluation of MLLMs capabilities further, as well as
538   extending VEBench to include more diverse video content and editing tasks. Additionally, investi-
539   gating methods to improve frame consistency and video fidelity in video editing models remains a
      promising direction for advancing the field.

      A     M ORE D ETAILS OF H UMAN Q UALITY R EVIEW
757
758   Throughout the data construction process, we implement human quality reviews at each stage of
759   Data Collection to ensure the reliability and accuracy of the data.
760
761
      First, we apply a quality filter to the videos obtained from the Video Collection stage. A checklist is
      used for manual inspection, and any videos that fail to meet the criteria are discarded:
762
763   Video Quality Guidelines:
764
765         1. Does the video feature a clear and identifiable main subject?
766         2. Is the overall video quality acceptable (i.e., not too poor)?
767         3. Are camera movements in the video smooth, without being overly abrupt or disorienting?
768
769   Next, we assess the captions generated by GPT-4V for quality. As in the previous step, we use a
770   checklist to verify and, if necessary, correct the captions based on the following standards:
771   Generated Video Caption Guidelines:
772
773         1. Does the caption accurately describe the core elements of the video?
774         2. Does the caption provide a comprehensive description of the video’s elements, including
775            background and animals?
776         3. Is the caption grammatically correct?
777         4. Is the caption overall fluent and coherent?
778
779   After refining the captions manually to ensure their quality, we proceed to generate text prompts
780   based on the extracted entities. A final checklist is used to verify the quality of the generated text
781   prompts:
782   Generated Text Prompt Guidelines:
783
784         1. Does the generated text prompt accurately modify or replace the target entity’s vocabulary?
785         2. Is the replacement vocabulary suitable for video editing tasks?
786         3. Is the generated text prompt grammatically correct?
787
            4. Is the text prompt fluent and coherent overall?
