009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), a prominent parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT)
012            method, offers an effective strategy for adapting large pre-trained models to specific
013            tasks with minimal computational overhead. LoRA achieves this by introducing
014            low-rank parameter matrices to the frozen pre-trained models. However, despite
015            their efficiency, LoRA and its variants modify all elements of a parameter block,
016            which is unnecessary as LoRA primarily aims to adjust a small set of subspaces
017            that capture task-specific knowledge. Drawing inspiration from the Lottery Ticket
018
               Hypothesis (LTH), which posits that dense neural networks contain sparse sub-
               networks capable of performing similarly to fully-parameterized models, we in-
019
               vestigate whether similar sparse subnetworks exist for low-rank adapters. We
020
               demonstrate that such subnetworks, often referred to as "winning tickets" in the
021            context of LTH, indeed exist for low-rank adapters. We introduce a method to
022            identify this sparse subset of weights for each layer by relating the top subspaces of
023            the pretrained parameter block to the elements of the corresponding weight matrix.
024            This subset is then fine-tuned using LoRA. We show that this sparse subset is not
025            necessarily unique; as long as sparsity is kept within a certain bound defined by the
026            task, random subnetworks with similar sparsity can act as winning tickets. Building
027            on this discovery, we propose a novel approach called Partial-LoRA, which adds
028            sparse low-rank parameters to pre-trained models. Through extensive experiments
029
               on 8 vision and 4 language tasks, we demonstrate that Partial-LoRA can reduce
               trainable parameters by up to 87% while maintaining or even improving model
030
               performance in some cases. Our work thus reduces memory needs and theoretically
031
               grounds sparse LoRAs.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods (Xin et al., 2024) have emerged as a compelling
037   approach to adapt large pre-trained models to specific tasks without the computational overhead of
038   training all model parameters. These methods are crucial for efficiency, reduced energy consump-
039   tion, lower costs, deployment on resource-limited devices, and allow for democratization of large
040   foundation models. These methods are in contrast to traditional fine-tuning approaches which often
041   involve adding special-purpose layers (Mengde Xu, 2023), adjusting normalization (Giannou et al.,
042   2023), retraining, or introducing parallel layers (Chen et al., 2022). Low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu
043   et al., 2022), as the most prominent PEFT approach, strikes a middle ground between efficiency and
044
      performance by adjusting a subset of weight subspaces using low-rank matrices, thereby intending to
      impact only a small portion of the information embedded in a model.
045
046   Despite these advancements, fine-tuning using LoRAs still involves modifying every element of the
047   original weight matrix, even when employing low-rank residual matrices. However, large pre-trained
048   models have low intrinsic dimensionality, allowing for effective finetuning with fewer parameters
049   (Aghajanyan et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2022). This, combined with the lottery ticket hypothesis (LTH)
050
      (Frankle & Carbin, 2019), suggests smaller sub-networks (‘winning tickets’) may achieve similar
      performance, i.e., for fine-tuning, only a small subset of weights in each layer needs modification.
051
052   Aiming to find these smaller sub-networks, we extend the theoretical work on LTH and random
053   masking (Gadhikar et al., 2023) to low-rank adaptation. We show that for each layer, randomly
      masking the weights of a low-rank adapter leads to a sufficiently tight bound on the residuals added

088

089
      Partial-LoRA obtains a better performance on average compared to targeting specific subnetworks.
090
      This confirms that the precise selection of specific weights might be less critical if the overall quantity
091   of trainable parameters (sparsity factor) is maintained within strategic limits, which we determine for
092   each layer using the importance values derived through the pretrained model.
093
094
      We also perform ablation studies on the rank and the magnitude of the residuals to study the behavior
      of masked LoRAs. Moreover, we extend our experiments to compare few-shot training to training on
095
      the entire dataset. We find that masked LoRAs maintain similar performance to LoRAs, indicating
096
      robustness across dataset sizes. We also explore a shift from deterministic to stochastic methods in
097   our sub-network extraction, treating importance scores as probabilistic to sample parameters to show
098   the minimal impact of this sampling procedure. Our main contributions are as follows.
099
100   • We provide a theoretical ground for the existence of lottery tickets in LoRAs, demonstrating that
101     randomly masked LoRAs can achieve performance on par with fully parameterized LoRAs across
102     a wide range of datasets and modalities. This shows that even with significant sparsification, the
103     performance can be maintained, highlighting the robustness of our approach.
104   • We show that principled sub-network selection is not strictly necessary. Given access to a sufficient
105     number of elements, LoRAs can effectively adjust the key subspaces of the pre-trained weight
106     matrix, ensuring efficient fine-tuning in LoRAs. This holds for commonly used importance
107     measures from the pruning literature and the proposed SVD-based method targeting the top
        subspaces of the pretrained weights.

117   2   R ELATED W ORK
118
119   Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods adapt large, overparameterized models with fewer
120   trainable parameters than full retraining requires. A commonly used PEFT approach, namely
121   Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022), uses trainable low-rank matrices added to each
122   layer, significantly reducing the training parameter count. AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023c) refines
123   LoRAs by adaptively modifying the rank of the residual matrices during training, enhancing the
124
      performance of LoRAs. Conversely, DoRA (Liu et al., 2024) and LoRA+ (Hayou et al., 2024)
      address discrepancies between low-rank and full-rank fine-tuning, pointing out limitations in LoRA’s
125
      training dynamics. Our method builds on LoRAs and AdaLoRAs but could also complement other
126
      LoRA-based advancements, potentially matching the dynamics of full-rank fine-tuning and achieving
127   similar model optimization.
128
129   Building on the foundation set by LoRAs, works such as LoRAPrune (Zhang et al., 2023b) and
130
      LoRA-Shear (Chen et al., 2023) propose approaches to model pruning. LoRAPrune introduces a
      LoRA-guided pruning method that utilizes the weights and gradients of LoRA adaptors rather than
131
      pre-trained weights for importance estimation, coupled with a structured iterative pruning process.
132
      LoRAShear employs a structured pruning strategy that initially creates dependency graphs over
133   LoRA modules to identify minimally removable structures. It should be noted that both methods
134   deal with pruning the original model and do not focus on masking LoRAs. Alternatively, aiming to
135   tailor the rank value in a layer-specific manner for the task at hand, PRILoRA (Benedek & Wolf,
136   2024) linearly allocates a different rank for the residuals at each layer. While this approach obtains
137   some improvements in performance, there is no guarantee for reduction of the number of parameters.
138   Additionally, the same rank value can be used alongside our approach to tailor the masked LoRAs for
139   each layer given the same layer-rank schedule.
140   More in line with the parameter reduction perspective of our work, IncreLoRA (Zhang et al.,
141   2023a) separates the components of the adapter into multiple rank-1 modules that add up to the full
142   residual, enabling the addition of more modules based on the task requirements. Moreover, there are
143   upperbounds implemented for these additions to prevent overparamterization. Unlike IncreLoRA
144   our approach focuses on using randomized masking within the existing LoRA parameters. While
145   IncreLoRA seeks to enhance parameter efficiency by dynamically adjusting ranks to better fit task-
146   specific demands, our method strategically masks parameters for layers based on the requirements
147
      of the task, thus maintaining more streamlined implementation and lower computational costs.
      (Kopiczko et al., 2024) uses a pair of frozen low-rank matrices shared across layers and adapts
148
      them with trainable scaling vectors, demonstrating similar performance to LoRA while reducing
149
      trainable parameters. However, the overall parameters are increased during training depending on
150   the size and number of weight matrices involved. A more directly comparable work would be (Xu
151   & Zhang, 2024), where LoRA residuals are randomly masked out and gradients are not applied
152   to the masked parameters. These random masks are generated at the start of training based on
153   a predetermined sparsity. This is in contrast to our work where we use the pretrained model to
154   determine the sparsity required for each layer, making our approach adaptive based on the task’s
155   required capacity in a principled manner. Additionally, while approaches like PEFT-Masking (Xu
156   & Zhang, 2024), LoRAPrune, LoRA-Shear, PRILoRA, IncreaLoRA and VeRA improve parameter
157   efficiency or adaptively distribute ranks, they lack theoretical bounds for maintaining approximation
158
      quality. Our work bridges this gap by providing a formal theoretical bound on the difference between
      outputs of masked and unmasked LoRAs.
159
160   From a theoretical perspective, (Gadhikar et al., 2023)–building up on the previous work from
161   (Burkholz et al., 2022; Burkholz, 2022)–proved the existence of strong lottery tickets in randomly
      masked variants of large overparameterized models. This is done by building on subset sum approxi-

      3     BACKGROUND
170
171
      Given a model M parameterized by W, b with depth L, LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) fine-tunes the weight
172   matrix Wl ∈ Rm×n at layer l by introducing a low-rank residual trainable matrix ∆Wl ∈ Rm×n .
173   This residual is added to the weight matrix Wl of the frozen pre-trained model M . While no bias
174   terms are added to the layer, the only trainable parameters of the fine-tuning process are the residuals
175   added at each layer. This results in each layer of the new model being formulated as:
176
                                         hl = σ((Wl + ∆Wl )x + bl ) ,                                      (1)
177
178   where hl is the output of the layer and bl represents the bias term at layer l from the original pre-
179   trained model. σ and x represent the layer nonlinearity and input, respectively. The matrix ∆Wl
180   itself is defined as:
181
                                  ∆Wl = Bl Al , Bl ∈ Rm×d , Al ∈ Rd×n ,                                 (2)
182   where Bl and Al are two low-rank trainable matrices. Using this formulation of the residual, the
183   number of trainable parameters is reduced from m × n to m × d + n × d where d ≪ min(m, n).

185   4     M ETHODOLOGY
186
187   Here, we outline our work divided into two key subsections. section 4.1 extends the theoretical
188   framework of Strong Lottery Tickets (SLT) to Low-Rank Adapters (LoRAs). We demonstrate that
189   assuming a target low-rank adapter (the winning ticket) performing well on a given task exists, a
190   pretrained model finetuned using a randomly masked LoRAs can approximate this target LoRA if
191   the unmasked adapter is wider by a logarithmic margin. In section 4.2 we describe the process of
192
      extracting subnetworks (masks for LoRAs) from pretrained models using a small number of labeled
      instances. This involves determining the importance of each element in the weight matrix of the
193
      pretrained model and selectively pruning them from the adapter while maintaining accuracy.

      A concern with this masking approach is flow preservation (Burkholz, 2022). In some randomly
      pruned models, two consecutive layers can end up with mismatched pruned weights, causing any

236   an algorithm for deriving sparsity ratios based on the model’s performance on a small subset of labeled
237   instances. Then, we discuss various importance measures that can be used to rank the significance of
238   elements in the weight matrix. This section details both the algorithmic approach (section 4.2.1) and
239
      the importance measures (section 4.2.2) used in our method.

      In this study, we explored the efficacy of random masking in
521
      LoRAs for fine-tuning large pre-trained models. Building on Figure 6: Rank ablation with parame-
522
      the lottery ticket hypothesis, we showed that highly sparse ter count for each dataset in millions.
523   "winning ticket" subnetworks within LoRA and its state-of-the-art variants can match the performance
524   of fully parameterized counterparts. Our experiments on vision and language modalities confirmed
525   that our proposed approach named Partial-LoRAs can achieve similar performance to SotA methods
526   with significantly fewer trainable parameters, reducing them by up to 87%. Key contributions include
527   establishing a theoretical basis for lottery tickets within LoRA residuals and demonstrating the
528   effectiveness of random masking. We found that targeting specific locations of large pretrained
529   models for parameter-efficient finetuning in an effort to prune LoRAs is unnecessary. As long as
530   the per-layer sparsity of the LoRA residuals is kept within a bound determined by our proposed
531
      sparsity ratio derivation method, the performance of pruned LoRAs is maintained while the number
      of parameters decrease significantly. Our ablation studies further validated the robustness of random
532
      masking strategies. We showed that strategies like preserving activation flow between layers, crucial
533
      for full model pruning, are unnecessary for LoRA masking due to the residual update formulation.
534   Our method can be implemented on top of existing and future LoRA-based PEFT methods by pruning
535   the components of the low-rank matrices as shown in this work to maintain the advantages of those
536   works while reducing the computational load. Our work highlights the potential for significant
537   computational savings in parameter-efficient fine-tuning. We pave the way for more sustainable
538   and accessible deployment of large pre-trained models. Future work will optimize sparsity factors,
539   exploring the middle ground between deterministic and stochastic pruning methods. Another potential
      future trajectory is the analysis of the training dynamics for Partial-LoRAs.

887   E     AGREEMENT IN THE S UB -N ETWORK E XTRACTION .
888
889   In this section, we aim to determine the similarity between sub-networks extracted by SVD, SNIP, and
890   IMP. We hypothesize that if there is a meaningful set of elements in each weight matrix responsible
891   for a model’s performance, there would be some overlap between the sub-networks identified by
892   these different methods. The results for CIFAR-10, Pets, CIFAR-100 and GTSRB are provided in the
893   following.
894   Overlap of layers on different methods: In Figure 12, the overlap for CIFAR-10 and Pets is larger
895   than 50% in the first 6 layers of the model when we compare the sub-network extracted by SVD-based
896   and gradient-based methods. This suggests that using importance measures to infer the number of
897   elements to modify is effective, highlighting the similarity between the SVD-based and gradient-based
898   methods. Similar results are shown in Figure 14 for CIFAR-100 and GTSRB. Interestingly, the
899   overlap between the SVD-based method and the gradient-based methods is different from the other
900   datasets. For Pets, the overlap is consistently low across the layers. This could potentially be due
901
      to the fine granular information required to be modified by the LoRA. If so, a larger portion of the
      top singular vectors would be used for the subnetwork extraction phase, leading to a smaller overlap
902
      across all layers.
903
904   Overlap of layers on the same method but different shots: We compare each method using
905   different shots from each dataset to complete these observations. Shared concepts should be reflected
906   in overlaps of sub-networks across different shots. As shown in Figure 13, the SVD-based method
907
      shows a large overlap between different seeds, with full overlap for GTSRB across all layers. This
      is expected since SVD starts with the top singular vectors. Surprisingly, the gradient-based method
908
      also shows significant overlap across different shots for both datasets. Therefore, considering the
909
      results in Table 2, while specific indices may not be optimal for training, the sparsity factor can be
910   effectively determined using importance measuring methods, as shown by the comparisons between
911   different importance methods. Similar results are shown in Figure 15 for CIFAR-100 and GTSRB.

      H              E FFECTS OF FLOW PRESERVATION
953
      In the pruning literature, it is common to modify the masks resulting from importance measures to
954
      preserve the flow of information throughout the layers of the model. This is due to the possibility that
955
      if two layers are pruned independently of each other, the activations resulting from one layer might
956   encounter weights that have been masked out, leading to a vector of zeros as the output of the second
957   layer. Here we show that in the fine-tuning setting where LoRAs adjust the pretrained layers, this
958   step is not necessary. We use the IMP importance method and infer the masked elements for each
959   layer independent of other layers. Then, we use the same approach but for each layer, the calculated
960   scores are added to the scores of the next layer to reweigh the importance of each element in favor
961   of important elements from the next layer. The performance of both methods is reported in Table 4.
962   The results with flow preservation are named Continuous and the results without this alteration of
963   the importance measure are named Isolated. Both the individual datasets and the overall average are
964
      consistently close confirming that flow preservation is not necessary in the case of our work.
