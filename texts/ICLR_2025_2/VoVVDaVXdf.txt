011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Emergent communication, driven by generative models, enables agents to develop
014            a shared language for describing their individual views of the same objects through
015            interactions. Meanwhile, self-supervised learning (SSL), particularly SimSiam,
016            uses discriminative representation learning to make representations of augmented
017            views of the same data point closer in the representation space. Building on the
018
               prior work of VI-SimSiam, which incorporates a generative and Bayesian per-
               spective into the SimSiam framework via variational inference (VI) interpretation,
019
               we propose SimSiam+VAE, a unified approach for both representation learning
020
               and emergent communication. SimSiam+VAE integrates a variational autoen-
021            coder (VAE) into the predictor of the SimSiam network to enhance representation
022            learning and capture uncertainty. Experimental results show that SimSiam+VAE
023            outperforms both SimSiam and VI-SimSiam. We further extend this model into
024            a communication framework called the SimSiam Naming Game (SSNG), which
025            applies the generative and Bayesian approach based on VI to develop internal rep-
026            resentations and emergent language, while utilizing the discriminative process of
027            SimSiam to facilitate mutual understanding between agents. In experiments with
028            established models, despite the dynamic alternation of agent roles during interac-
029
               tions, SSNG demonstrates comparable performance to the referential game and
               slightly outperforms the Metropolis-Hastings naming game.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Emergent communication (EmCom) studies how multiple agents, through interaction, can develop a
036   shared language, known as a symbol emergence system (Cangelosi & Parisi, 2002; Taniguchi et al.,
037   2016; 2019; Lazaridou & Baroni, 2020; Rita et al., 2024; Peters et al., 2024). Many studies in Em-
038   Com, based on Shannon-Weaver-like communication models (Shannon & Weaver, 1949), such as
039
      the Lewis signaling game (Lewis, 2008) or the referential game (Lazaridou et al., 2017), primarily
      focus on how agents can discriminate target objects or analyze the compositionality of the emer-
040
      gent signals (Havrylov & Titov, 2017; Denamganaı̈ et al., 2023; Lipinski et al., 2024), often without
041
      considering internal representations. In contrast, collective predictive coding (CPC)-based EmCom
042   (Taniguchi, 2024), such as the Metropolis-Hastings naming game (MHNG) (Hagiwara et al., 2019;
043   Taniguchi et al., 2023b), views EmCom as a form of decentralized Bayesian inference. This ap-
044   proach focuses on both the representations learned within individual agents and the emergence of
045   symbols at a societal level, referred to as social representation learning.
046
      Representation learning, on the other hand, has been a fundamental aspect of machine learning
047
      (Bengio et al., 2013a; LeCun et al., 2015), particularly in tasks like image classification, where
048   the objective is to extract meaningful features from raw data (Bishop, 2006). Within this domain,
049   self-supervised learning (SSL) has attracted significant attention by enabling models to learn rep-
050   resentations without relying on labeled data (Liu et al., 2021; Uelwer et al., 2023). One important
051   approach in SSL is contrastive learning, which focuses on learning by comparing different aug-
052   mented views of the same data point (Le-Khac et al., 2020). Notable models in this area, such as
053   SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020), DINO (Caron et al., 2021), and SimSiam (Chen & He, 2021), have
      shown that this approach can align representations and improve feature extraction.

054
      Both CPC-based EmCom and contrastive-based SSL follow a similar process. In CPC-based Em-
055   Com, agents observe the same object from different viewpoints and iteratively develop a common
056   language by aligning their internal representations through generative modeling (Taniguchi, 2024).
057   In contrast, contrastive-based SSL models, particularly SimSiam, align augmented views of the same
058   data point in the representation space through a discriminative process, relying only on positive pairs
059   (Chen & He, 2021). Furthermore, recent research (Nakamura et al., 2023) has applied variational
060   inference (VI) to SSL models, providing a generative interpretation of traditionally discriminative
061   methods, such as SimSiam, and capturing uncertainty in learned representations.
062   Building on the VI-based interpretation of SSL models, we propose a unified approach that con-
063   nects discriminative SSL-based representation learning with generative CPC-based EmCom. We
064   introduce SimSiam+VAE, which integrates a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma & Welling,
065   2013) into the predictor of the SimSiam network. This integration enhances latent representations
066   with uncertainty by combining two processes: aligning positive pairs through contrastive compar-
067   ison and refining representations via the VAE’s encoding-decoding process, all without relying on
068   negative samples.
069   We further extend SimSiam+VAE into a structured communication framework called the SimSiam
070   Naming Game (SSNG), designed to facilitate EmCom between agents. In SSNG, each agent oper-
071   ates a separate SimSiam+VAE network, where the backbone and projector function as a perception
072   module to transform observations into internal representations. The VAE predictor acts as a language
073   coder, responsible for generating and decoding messages. Agents perceive different viewpoints of
074   the same object and use a Bayesian approach to form internal representations and develop an emer-
075
      gent language. Through iterative exchanges, they interact similarly to the SimSiam+VAE, using its
      discriminative process to align their representations and achieve mutual understanding.
076
077   For evaluation, we conduct two experiments. First, we assess the performance of SimSiam+VAE in
078   representation learning by measuring classification accuracy on the image datasets FashionMNIST
079   and CIFAR-10. Second, we evaluate the SSNG’s capability in emergent communication (EmCom)
080   using the dSprites dataset, measuring the compositional generalization of the emergent language by
081
      applying TopSim (Brighton & Kirby, 2006) to unseen data (Chaabouni et al., 2020; Baroni, 2020)
082   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
083
084          • We formulate SimSiam+VAE, a unified model that bridges representation learning and
085            EmCom through a generative and discriminative framework. By integrating a VAE into the
086            SimSiam architecture, we enhance latent representation learning and uncertainty modeling,
087            using only positive pairs.
088
             • We introduce the SimSiam Naming Game (SSNG), a novel communication game grounded
089            in the principles of CPC. SSNG utilized the combined generative-discriminative approach
090            of SimSiam+VAE to iteratively align internal representations and develop a shared emer-
091            gent language.

096   Self-Supervised Learning (SSL) as Variational Inference (VI): Recent work (Nakamura et al.,
097   2023) suggests that SSL can be interpreted through the lens of VI, a probabilistic framework for
098   learning latent variable models (Blei et al., 2017). In SSL, representations are typically learned by
099   minimizing a contrastive loss between different augmented views of the same data, with the aim
100   of bringing these views closer in their latent space representation. This process is analogous to
101   VI, where augmented views are treated as ”observations” that contribute to learning a shared latent
102   variable. The augmentations in SSL function similarly to distinct modalities within a multimodal
103   generative model in VI.

106   representation of the augmented data. The objective of SSL, when viewed through VI, is to find
107   parameters θ that maximize the likelihood of the observations given z. However, computing the true
      posterior pθ (z|X) directly is intractable, leading to the use of a variational distribution qϕ (z|X) to

      4.1   M ODEL D ESCRIPTION
273
274   The objective of SimSiam+VAE is to bring different views (augmentations) of the same data point
275   closer in the representation space without relying on negative pairs. This aligns with the CPC-
276   based EmCom, where two agents observe the same object from different viewpoints and develop
277   shared representations without explicit labels. In this section, we extend SimSiam+VAE to facilitate
278   EmCom between two agents, A and B, through a communication game called the SimSiam Naming
279   Game (SSNG). Each agent ∗ ∈ {A, B} operates as a branch of the SimSiam+VAE, processing its
280
      observation x∗ ∈ {xA , xB }, which is derived from a distinct viewpoint of the original object x.
281   Unlike the original SimSiam+VAE, which processes two augmentations of x through a shared net-
282   work to produce a single latent representation z and a corresponding latent variable w, the SSNG
283   introduces two separate latent representations, zA and zB , one for each agent. Each branch of the
284   network independently maps its observation x∗ to its internal representation z∗ . These represen-
285   tations are then combined to form a shared message w, which acts as the emergent language for
286
      communication. The message w enables the agents to align their internal representations, foster-
      ing mutual understanding. Through this structure, SSNG allows each agent to retain its unique
287
      perspective while contributing to a shared language. This approach aligns with Peirce’s semiotics
288
      theory (Chandler, 2002), establishing a triadic relationship among the symbol (observation x∗ ), the
289   interpretant (internal representation z∗ ), and the sign (message w) (Fig. 3).
290
291   Each agent ∗ ∈ {A, B} in this communication game has the two components: perception and
292
      language coder. The perception (f∗ ), consisting of the backbone and projector, transforms the ob-
      servation x∗ into the internal representations z∗ . The language coder (h∗ ) includes the predictor,
293
      which consists of an encoder (h(enc)
                                      ∗    ) and a decoder (h(dec)
                                                             ∗     ). The encoder maps the internal represen-
294
      tation z∗ to a shared message w while the decoder receives and decodes the message into an internal
295   representation z∗′ .
296
297   The model components in the SSNG are identical to those in SimSiam+VAE. The key difference
298
      is that the latent variable w now follows a categorical distribution over K, where K represents the
      vocabulary or dictionary size. In the SSNG, the prior p(w) is a uniform categorical distribution

      5.1           E XPERIMENT 1: S IM S IAM +VAE IN REPRESENTATION LEARNING
397
398   Datasets: We use the FashionMNIST (Xiao et al., 2017) and CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky, 2009) datasets.
399
400   Model architecture: A Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) backbone is used for FashionMNIST,
401
      while ResNet18 (He et al., 2016) is used for CIFAR-10. In both cases, the projector and predictor
      utilize a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) architecture. (More details in Appendix G)
402
403   Linear evaluation: All models are trained for 500 epochs. Then, a classifier is trained on the frozen

446   6   R ELATED W ORK
447
448   Emergent Communication (EmCom) examines how agents develop a shared language through
449   interactions, drawing inspiration from cognitive science theories (Wagner et al., 2003; Steels, 2015).
450   Research in multi-agent reinforcement learning (Foerster et al., 2016) demonstrated how agents
451   could develop communication to optimize collective rewards. Comprehensive surveys of this field
452   include (Galke et al., 2022; Brandizzi, 2023; Boldt & Mortensen, 2024). Recent studies have fo-
453
      cused on CPC-based, which emphasizes joint attention in human communication (Okumura et al.,
      2023). The MHNG (Taniguchi et al., 2023b) utilizes decentralized Bayesian inference to achieve
454
      a consensus on shared symbols, aligning with predictive coding and world model (Hohwy, 2013;
455
      Friston et al., 2021; Taniguchi et al., 2023a). The MHNG has been applied in multimodal datasets us-
456   ing methods like Inter-MDM (Hagiwara et al., 2022) and Inter-GMM+MVAE (Hoang et al., 2024b).
457   Moreover, MHNG has been extended to recursive multi-agent communication systems (Inukai et al.,
458   2023) and integrated into multi-agent reinforcement learning (Ebara et al., 2023).
459
      Representation learning is essential in machine learning tasks like image classification, allowing
460
      models to extract features from raw data (Goodfellow et al., 2016). SSL has become a popular
461
      method for learning representations without labels (Jing & Tian, 2020). A key SSL approach is con-
462   trastive learning, which aligns representations by comparing different augmented views of the same
463   data point (Cole et al., 2022). MoCo (He et al., 2020) introduces a momentum encoder to main-
464   tain a queue of negative samples, while BYOL (Grill et al., 2020) eliminates the need for negative
465   pairs, using a stop-gradient mechanism to avoid collapse. Recent research has combined VAE and
466   contrastive learning to improve representation learning. CR-VAE adds contrastive regularization to
467   the VAE objective (Lygerakis & Rueckert, 2023), while ContrastVAE employs a two-view approach
468   with ContrastELBO for sequential recommendations (Wang et al., 2022). Noise contrastive estima-
469   tion is used in (Aneja et al., 2021) to reweight the prior distribution. Contrastive VAEs (cVAE) focus
470
      on isolating salient features in datasets to refine latent space representation (Abid & Zou, 2019).
471
472   7   C ONCLUSIONS
473
474   This research introduces the SimSiam Naming Game (SSNG) and SimSiam+VAE, a unified model
475   that bridges discriminative contrastive SSL-based representation learning with generative CPC-
476   based EmCom through the perspective of VI. Although originating from distinct domains, both SSL
477   and EmCom share the goal of aligning representations—either by learning invariant representations
478   from augmented data views in SSL or by developing a shared language between agents observing
479   the same object from different perspectives. By bridging these objectives, our model demonstrates
480   applicability to both representation learning and EmCom.
481   Our experiments show that SimSiam+VAE outperforms both SimSiam and VI-SimSiam in repre-
482   sentation learning without requiring negative pairs. In EmCom, SSNG leverages the discriminative
483   properties of SimSiam and the generative Bayesian perspective of the VI interpretation to align
484   agents’ internal representations, fostering mutual understanding and enabling the development of
485   an emergent language. This work, therefore, provides an alternative communication framework for
      EmCom systems.

932        D    I NFERENCE VIA S IM S IAM NAMING G AME
933
934        The goal of both SimSiam+VAE and the SSNG is to align the representations of different viewpoints
935        of the same object. This alignment process ensures that observations of the same object from differ-
936        ent perspectives are represented closely in the latent space. The training process, which minimizes
937        an alignment loss and a reconstruction loss, gradually reduces the dissimilarity between the internal
938        representations of both agents.
939        To achieve this, the objective function encourages the representations zA from agent A’s observation
940        xA and zB from agent B’s observation xB to become more similar. As the alignment improves, we
941        achieve the approximation:

                                        p(w | zA , zB ) ≈ p(w | zA ) ≈ p(w | zB )                          (65)

944

945
           mutual understanding between the two agents. This shared understanding is derived from the aligned
946        representations zA and zB , which reflect different views of the same underlying object.
