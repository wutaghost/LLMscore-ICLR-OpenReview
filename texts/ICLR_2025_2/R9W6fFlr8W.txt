011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We address the optimization problem in a data-driven variational reconstruction
014            framework, where the regularizer is parameterized by an input-convex neural net-
015            work (ICNN). While gradient-based methods are commonly used to solve such
016            problems, they struggle to effectively handle non-smoothness which often leads
017            to slow convergence. Moreover, the nested structure of the neural network com-
018
               plicates the application of standard non-smooth optimization techniques, such as
               proximal algorithms. To overcome these challenges, we reformulate the prob-
019
               lem and eliminate the network’s nested structure. By relating this reformulation
020
               to epigraphical projections of the activation functions, we transform the problem
021            into a convex optimization problem that can be efficiently solved using a primal-
022            dual algorithm. We also prove that this reformulation is equivalent to the original
023            variational problem. Through experiments on several imaging tasks, we demon-
024            strate that the proposed approach outperforms subgradient methods in terms of
025            both speed and stability.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Image restoration focuses on reconstructing high-quality images from degraded, low-quality ver-
031   sions that often result from issues during image acquisition and transmission. This includes tasks
032   such as image denoising, image deblurring, image inpainting and computer tomography (CT) re-
033   construction. The measurement process is typically modeled as y = Ax + ϵ, where A simulates
034   the physics in the measurement process and ϵ denotes the measurement noise. One then seeks to
035   recover the unknown image x from the noisy measurement y. To mitigate the ill-possedness of the
036   inverse problem, the classical variational reconstruction framework incorporates prior information
037   about plausible reconstructions through a regularizer:
038
                                           min D(Ax, y) + γRθ (x),                                        (P)
039                                          x
040
      where D is the data fidelity. The regularizer Rθ can be parametrized, with θ denoting its parame-
041
      ters. The trade-off between data fidelity and regularizer is controlled by the positive regularization
042   parameter γ. The reconstruction is obtained by solving the minimization problem (P).
043
044   Traditional methods often utilize hand-crafted regularizers, such as total variation (TV) (Rudin et al.,
045
      1992), total generalized variation (TGV) (Bredies et al., 2010) and sparsity promoting regularizer
      (Daubechies et al., 2004). In recent years, data-driven approaches for inverse problems have gained
046
      increasing interest. For instance, (Chen et al., 2017; Jin et al., 2017; Kang et al., 2017) propose
047
      learning end-to-end neural networks to post-process analytical reconstructions. Another prominent
048   strategy involves unrolling methods (Adler & Öktem, 2018; Kobler et al., 2017; Meinhardt et al.,
049   2017; Yang et al., 2016), which integrate neural network modules into iterative optimization algo-
050   rithms based on the variational framework. Alternatively, several works (Aharon et al., 2006; Chen
051   et al., 2014; Kunisch & Pock, 2013; Xu et al., 2012) attempted to learn regularizers. This is also
052   extended to parameterizing them with neural networks (Goujon et al., 2023; Kobler et al., 2020;
053   Li et al., 2020; Lunz et al., 2018; Mukherjee et al., 2020), and embedding them within variational
      reconstruction frameworks.

      1.1   L EARNED CONVEX REGULARIZER WITH ICNN S
055
056   In Amos et al. (2017), a L-layered ICNN is defined by the following architecture:
057
058                              z1 = h1 (V0 x + b0 ),
059                           zi+1 = hi+1 (Vi x + Wi zi + bi ), i = 1, . . . , L − 2,                   (EQ)
060                          Rθ (x) := hL (VL−1 x + WL−1 zL−1 + bL−1 ),
061
062   where Vi , Wi are linear operators, which could represent various neural network components, such
063   as fully connected layers, convolution layers and average pooling layers. Here θ = {Vi , Wi , bi }
064
      represents the collection of all trainable parameters of the ICNN. The functions hi are non-linear
      activations. For x, y ∈ Rn , we denote x ≤ y if xi ≤ yi for i = 1, . . . , n. To handle general
065
      activations, we call a function f : Rn → Rm convex if f (αx + (1 − α)y) ≤ αf (x) + (1 − α)f (y)
066
      for every x, y ∈ Rn and α ∈ [0, 1]. f is called non-decreasing if f (x) ≤ f (y) for x ≤ y.
067
068   The convexity of Rθ with respect to the input x can be guaranteed by imposing that the weights Wi
069   are non-negative and hi are convex, non-decreasing.
070   A major advantage of a convex setting over a non-convex one is the ability to compute a global op-
071   timum independent of initialization, allowing one to leverage the well-established theory of convex
072   optimization with guaranteed convergence to efficiently solve (P). Therefore, we focus on the case
073   where the regularizer Rθ is parameterized by an ICNN and try to address the following problem:
074   Problem: How to solve (P) efficiently in the setting of ICNN?

077   2     C HALLENGES AND MOTIVATIONS
078
079   Numerous efforts have been made in the literature to study algorithms for optimizing convex func-
080   tions, in particular in variational reconstruction. Gradient methods are often applied to general
081   smooth convex problems (Boyd & Vandenberghe, 2004) and can be extended to subgradient meth-
082   ods for non-smooth problems (Boyd et al., 2003). Another essential component for non-smooth
083
      problems is the proximal operator (Parikh & Boyd, 2014). In particular, primal-dual methods have
      been extensively studied for non-smooth handcrafted regularizers such as TV (Chambolle & Pock,
084
      2011; 2016; Yan, 2018; Zhu & Chan, 2008). However, due to the nested structure of neural networks,
085
      computing the proximal operator for neural networks is often impractical. Therefore, to perfom
086   variational reconstruction with neural network-parameterized regularizers, subgradient methods are
087   commonly applied, where subgradients are computed via backpropagation (Mukherjee et al., 2020).
088   Despite the simplicity of this approach, challenges arise due to non-smoothness.
089
      On the other hand, (Askari et al., 2018; Carreira-Perpinan & Wang, 2014; Li et al., 2019; Taylor
090
      et al., 2016; Wang & Benning, 2023; Zhang & Brand, 2017) explored unconventional training meth-
091
      ods of training neural network. They proposed to remove nested structure of the neural network
092   by introducing auxiliary variables given by the layer-wise activations. Relaxed problems are con-
093   sidered by introducing penalty to the induced equality constraints. However, the problem remains
094   non-convex, and the minimizers are altered as a result of these relaxations.
095
096
      2.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS
097
098   Primal-dual algorithms have been successfully applied to classical variational problems, providing
099   fast reconstruction methods. Motivated by their flexibility and practicality, we aim to exploit both the
100   inherent convex nature and the architecture of the neural network to devise optimization algorithm
101   for solving the variational problem. Our contributions are as follows:
102
103          • We introduce a more general architecture than ICNN. To address the non-smoothness and
104
               nested structure, we propose a novel reformulation of the variational problem. We prove
               that this reformulation is both convex and equivalent to the original variational problem.
105
106          • We apply this novel convex reformulation to setting where the regularizer is parameterized
107            by an ICNN, solving the associated variational problem using a primal-dual algorithm.
               Additionally, we design a step-size scheme tailored specifically to our formulation.

             • We implement the proposed framework for image restoration tasks such as denoising, in-
109            painting, and CT reconstruction. Our results demonstrate that the proposed method is su-
110            perior to subgradient methods, achieving faster and more stable reconstruction.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
232
233
      We evaluate the performance of the proposed method and compare with subgradient methods on
      three imaging tasks, (i) salt and pepper denoising, (ii) image inpainting, and (iii) sparse-view CT re-
234
      construction. For all tasks, we utilize a learned regularizer parametrized by an ICNN, which consists
235
      of a convolution layer and a global average operator layer, followed by two fully connected layers.
236   The regularizer can be represented by Rθ (x) = W2 h2 (W1 Pz + b1 ) with z = h1 (V0 x + b0 ).
237   Here V0 corresponds to a convolution operator with 32 5 × 5 filters, and P denotes an average
238   pooling operater with 16 × 16 pool size. The fully connected layers W1 , W2 consists of 256 and
239   1 output neurons respectively. The activations h1 , h2 are chosen to be leaky ReLU and ReLU re-
240   spectively, with the leaky ReLU’s negative slope set to 0.2. The regularizer is then trained following
241   the adversarial framework Lunz et al. (2018), taking (possibly un-paired) ground truth signals as
242   positive samples and unregularized reconstructions, which are task-dependent, as negative samples.
243   The associated minimization problem is then solved with the proposed method, and compare with
244
      the subgradient method (Boyd et al., 2003) with (a) constant step-size (SM-C) and (b) diminishing
      step-size (SM-D), with step-size at the k-th iteration given by the initial step-size divided by k. More
245
      details on adversarial training and the subgradient methods can be found in the Appendix.

526   5   C ONCLUSION
527
528   We proposed an efficient method for solving the optimization problem in variational reconstruction
529   with a learned convex regularizer. A key challenge comes from the non-smoothness of the ICNN
530   regularizer, whose proximal operator lacks a closed-form solution. To overcome this, we decou-
531
      pled the neural network layers by introducing auxiliary variables corresponding to the layer-wise
      activations. While this initially resulted in a non-convex problem, we drew inspiration from the con-
532
      vexity of epigraphs and reformulated it as a convex optimization problem. We then proved that this
533
      reformulation is equivalent to the original variational problem and applied a primal-dual algorithm
534   to solve it. Numerical experiments demonstrated that the proposed method not only outperforms
535   subgradient methods in terms of convergence speed but also exhibits greater stability throughout the
536   optimization process, as evidenced by the smoother behavior observed in the energy versus itera-
537   tions plots, as well as those depicting data fidelity and regularization. Additionally, we note that
538   the updates of the proposed method are independent, enabling parallel computation. Looking for-
539   ward, we aim to explore the potential of extending the proposed method to primal-dual variants that
      leverage this, such as coordinate-descent primal-dual algorithms (Fercoq & Bianchi, 2019).
