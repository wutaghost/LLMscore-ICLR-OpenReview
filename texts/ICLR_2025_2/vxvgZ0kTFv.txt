011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013
               We study the gradient descent (GD) dynamics of a depth-2 linear neural network
014
               with a single input and output. We show that GD converges at an explicit lin-
015
               ear rate to a global minimum of the training loss, even with a large stepsize–about
016            2/sharpness. It still converges for even larger stepsizes, but may do so very slowly.
017            We also characterize the solution to which GD converges, which has lower norm
018            and sharpness than the gradient flow solution. Our analysis reveals a trade off be-
019            tween the speed of convergence and the magnitude of implicit regularization. This
020            sheds light on the benefits of training at the “Edge of Stability”, which induces
021            additional regularization by delaying convergence and may have implications for
022            training more complex models.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
029   Training modern machine learning (ML) models like deep neural networks via empirical risk min-
030   imization (ERM) requires solving difficult high-dimensional, non-convex, under-determined op-
031
      timization problems. Although they are usually intractable to solve in theory, we train models
      effectively in practice using algorithms like stochastic gradient descent (SGD). This highlights a
032
      disconnect between the worst-case convergence rate of SGD and its convergence on specific ERM
033
      problems that arise from training, e.g., neural networks. Even if we can solve the ERM problem,
034   typical minimizers of the under-determined objective will overfit and generalize poorly. That said,
035   the specific solutions found by SGD and its variants usually do successfully generalize. Understand-
036   ing how and why we are able to successfully optimize and generalize with these models is of great
037   interest to the ML community and could help fuel continued progress in applied ML.
038
      A key feature of popular ML models, including neural networks, is that the model output is related
039
      to the product of model parameters in successive layers. For instance, the output of a 2 layer feed-
040   forward network with ReLU activations has output W2 ReLU(W1 x + b1 ), which is closely related
041   to the product of the weight matrices W2 W1 . Ultimately, this “self-multiplication” of different
042   model parameters gives rise to the non-convex and under-determined ERM problems that cause
043   such (theoretical) difficulties.
044
      In this work, we distill this parameter self-multiplication property down to its simplest form and
045
      comprehensively explain how it affects the training optimization dynamics, the “implicit regulariza-
046   tion” of the model parameters, and the “edge-of-stability” dynamics that arise in certain regimes. In
047   particular, we consider the extremely simple problem of learning a univariate linear model ŷ = mx
048   to minimize the squared error, except we parameterize the slope as m = m(a, b) = a⊤ b in terms
049   of self-multiplying parameters a, b ∈ Rd . This can also be thought of as a depth-2 linear neural
050   network with d hidden units. For training data {(xi , yi ) ∈ R × R}ni=1 , this results in the loss

063   realistic problems like deep learning training objectives. First, it has similar high-level properties—
064   the problem (2) is non-convex and highly under-determined because the set of minimizers constitutes
065   the (2d − 1)-dimensional hyperboloid in R2d that solves a⊤ b = Φ. It also exhibits some of the
066   same symmetries as realistic neural networks; for example, a⊤ b is invariant to swapping “neurons”
067   (ai , bi ) ↔ (aj , bj ) or to rescaling (ai , bi ) → (cai , c−1 bi ). More importantly, the dynamics
068   when optimizing (2) with gradient descent are qualitatively similar to the dynamics of training more
069   complex models (see, e.g., Xu & Ziyin, 2024). Simultaneously, the problem (2) is simple enough
070   that we can provide a detailed and nearly comprehensive characterization of several different aspects
071
      of training. Specifically, we will present the following results:
072
073   Convergence of gradient descent. Despite the non-convexity of the optimization problem (2),
074   prior work has shown that GD converges to a global minimum from a.e. initialization (Wang et al.,
075   2022). We show that, in fact, it converges at a linear rate. In addition, we identify several phases that
076   depend on the relationship between the stepsize, η; the scale of the parameters, λ := ∥a∥2 + ∥b∥2 ;
077
      and the residuals, ε := a⊤ b − Φ. Several of these phases are closely related to the so-called Edge
      of Stability (EoS) phenomenon (Cohen et al., 2021), where gradient descent decreases the objective
078
      (although non-monotonically) despite the largest eigenvalue of the objective’s Hessian matrix being
079
      larger than the critical threshold 2/η.
080
081
082   Location of convergence. In addition to showing how fast gradient descent converges to some
083   global minimizer, we can also identify key properties of the model related to which of the many pos-
084
      sible solutions, a⊤ b = Φ, gradient descent will converge to. To do so, we show that gradient descent
                                                                                            Pd
085   implicitly regularizes the “imbalance” of the parameter vectors, quantified by Q := i=1 a2i − b2i ,
086

      behavior of Gradient Flow (GF) (the η → 0 limit of gradient descent), which conserves Q. Since
087
      GF is often employed in the literature as an easier-to-analyze approximation of gradient descent
088

089   on this approximation. The balance of the parameters is also closely related to the “sharpness” of
090   the solution, i.e. the maximum eigenvalue of the Hessian, which is equal to λ := ∥a∥2 + ∥b∥2 at
091   solutions a⊤ b = Φ. For the problem (2) specifically, the actual prediction function defined by any
092   solution a⊤ b = Φ is the same—after all at any minimizer, ŷ = Φx regardless of the parameters—so
093   the sharpness is not relevant to generalization. Nevertheless, there is a large body of work in other
094   contexts showing that less sharp minima of the loss tend to generalize better (Hochreiter & Schmid-
095   huber, 1997; Keskar et al., 2016; Smith & Le, 2018; Park et al., 2019), and our analysis shows how
096   the self-multiplying structure of (2) tends to regularize the sharpness.

      R ELATED W ORK
109
110   A large body of research has shown empirically that training neural networks with larger learning
111   rates tends to lead to better generalization (LeCun et al., 2002; Bjorck et al., 2018; Li et al., 2019;
112   Jastrzebski et al., 2020). However, in classical settings, convergence can only be guaranteed when
113   the stepsize is small enough that λmax (∇2 L) < 2/η throughout optimization (Bottou et al., 2018).
114
      Nevertheless, a recent line of work starting with Cohen et al. (2021) observed that when training
      neural networks, the maximum eigenvalue of the Hessian, or “sharpness”, tends to grow throughout
115
      training until it reaches, or even surpasses the critical 2/η threshold. But rather that diverging, the
116
      loss continues to decrease (non-monotonically) while the sharpness continues to hover around 2/η,
117   which is referred to as the Edge of Stability (EoS) phenomenon. Understanding more deeply the
118   training of neural networks with large stepsizes is of great interest.
119
120
      Problems closely resembling (2) have been studied previously. Ahn et al. (2024) study losses of
      the form ℓ(ab) with a, b ∈ R and ℓ any convex, Lipschitz, and even function. The assumption
121
      that ℓ is even means it is minimized at zero (this is analogous to Φ = 0 in our case), and they
122
      prove convergence to zero from any initialization with any stepsize, but without a rate. However,
123   this result relies crucially on both the loss being Lipschitz and minimized at zero—and this is not
124   surprising, we know that GD diverges on realistic objectives when the stepsize is too large. They
125   also show that the limit point of gradient descent satisfies |a2∞ − b2∞ | ≈ min{2/η, |a20 − b20 |}, i.e. the
126   imbalance between the weights is implicitly regularized down to the level of 2/η. Chen & Bruna
127   (2023) study (2) with scalar a, b ∈ R and prove that the limit point of GD satisfies a − b → 0 when
128   the stepsize is chosen slightly too large for convergence to any minimizer ab = Φ to be possible.
129   This is qualitatively similar to our work, but they intentionally choose a too-large stepsize in order
130   to highlight the implicit regularization of the imbalance, while we provide conditions on η under
131
      which convergence to a minimizer and some amount of regularization happen simultaneously.
132   In a related study, Xu & Ziyin (2024) explore the continuous dynamics of gradient flow using the ex-
133   act same model discussed here. They demonstrate that the dynamics unfold along a one-dimensional
134   curve, with the location of convergence distinctly defined by conserved quantities. Contrary to their
135   findings, our research reveals this is not the case for gradient descent, highlighting the danger of re-
136   lying excessively on continuous models to understand discrete non-convex optimization dynamics.
137   In the most closely related work, Wang et al. (2022) study the exact objective (2) and show that
138   gradient descent using any stepsize up to η ≲ 4/sharpness—approximately twice as large as the classi-
139   cal threshold of 2/sharpness—eventually converges to a minimizer, but without a rate. They also show
140   some level of implicit regularization of λ, e.g. at convergence λ ≤ η2 . In comparison, we provide
141   an explicit convergence rate for GD and give a more detailed connection between this rate and the
142   implicit regularization.
143   Finally, many papers have studied other models such as matrix factorization or linear neural net-
144   works (Saxe et al., 2014; Arora et al., 2019; Gidel et al., 2019; Tarmoun et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2023;
145   Nguegnang et al., 2024), which are more faithful representations of realistic neural networks, but
146   they are also much more difficult to analyze. Due to this difficulty, these results often only apply
147   to gradient flow, or to GD with a very small learning rate, or to GD under additional, hard to inter-
148   pret assumptions. In this work, we focus on the problem (2) in order to obtain a simpler, easier to
149   interpret set of results.

343   5.2       T HE CASE OF E DGE OF S TABILITY
344
      Recent findings by Cohen et al. (2021) demonstrate that during the training of neural networks
345   with full batch gradient descent at a step size of η, the largest eigenvalue of the Hessian stabilizes
346   right above 2/η. This is somewhat surprising as when the gradients are a linear function of the
347   parameters, if η > λ2 , gradient descent diverges. A very good exemplification of this fact is the
348   case of one dimensional parabola, see Cohen et al. (2021). However, neural networks, surprisingly,
349   convergence even if η ≥ λ2 . Our results here show that the reason my be the product structure
350   and its interaction with discrete dynamics. Indeed, our results show how convergence happen also
351   for η > 2/λ but slower. This implies that training happens even though at the edge of stability.
352   Moreover, we prove that the slower the training and the bigger the learning rate, the lower the
353
      parameter norm of the solution gradient descent will eventually converge to. This suggests that
      training at the edge of stability may induce increased implicit regularization.

383   quick convergence although the problem itself is not necessarily PL.

451   7     C ONCLUSION
452
453   In this paper, we analyzed the gradient descent dynamics of a depth-2 linear neural network, offering
454   a simplified model to explore training behaviors observed in more complex neural networks. Our
455   key technical contributions are:
456
            1. Linear convergence with large step sizes: We demonstrated that gradient descent con-
457
               verges at a linear rate to a global minimum, even with larger-than-expected step sizes—up
458
               to approximately 2/sharpness. For even larger step sizes, convergence can still occur, but
459            slows down significantly. See Section 4.
460
            2. Location of convergence: We characterized the solution reached by gradient descent,
461
               showing that it implicitly regularizes the parameter imbalance and sharpness, leading to
462
               a lower norm solution compared to gradient flow. Notably, as the step size increases, the
463            implicit regularization effect strengthens, flattening the solution. See Section 3.
464
465   The key implications of our results are that
466
467           i. GD always regularizes more than GF: Gradient descent converges to a solution with
468
                 lower imbalance than gradient flow, but the imbalance always remains non-zero. The solu-
                 tion is still suboptimal from this perspective. See Section 3.
469
470          ii. GF is not always a good approximation of GD: We prove that even in a very simple
471              model, gradient flow dynamics are inherently different from gradient descent. In particular,
472
                 our results can be used as a proof that the common use of GF as a theoretical tool for
                 understanding GD is not always well founded. See Section 3.
473
474         iii. Trade-off Between Speed and Regularization: Our analysis uncovered a trade-off be-
475              tween the convergence rate and the degree of implicit regularization. See Section 4. Train-
476
                 ing at the edge of stability, while slower, induces additional regularization, which may be
                 beneficial for generalization. See Section 5.
477
478   Our findings thus provide insight into different step sizes affect neural network training dynamics
479   and its potential benefits for regularization in more complex models.
480
481   Future work: In this work, we studied the model (2) because its simplicity allows for a detailed
482   analysis that leads to the useful conclusions detailed above. However, there are several possible
483   extensions of these results that could lend additional insights. For example, it would be interesting
484   to study the case of vector-valued inputs, deeper models, and non-linear models that use ReLU or
485   other activation functions. In addition, we are interested to know how our results would be impacted
      by using stochastic gradient descent in rather than exact gradient descent.
