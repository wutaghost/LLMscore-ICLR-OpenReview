009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Open-ended learning systems aim to foster the continuous evolution of increas-
012            ingly capable agents through the dynamic generation of novel challenges. The
013            efficacy of these systems is fundamentally influenced by two critical factors: the
014            design of the underlying system, which delineates the space of possibilities, and
015            the open-ended algorithms that drive ongoing progress within this space. Current
016            approaches to system design rely on explicit specification, where state spaces and
017            evolution functions are fully defined at design time, often leading to prohibitive
018
               design complexity as systems scale. To address this challenge, we propose an
               alternative design principle termed meta specification. This approach defines sys-
019
               tems implicitly through constraints, utilizing watchmaker functions—generalized
020
               stochastic evolution functions—coupled with verification routines to perform sys-
021            tem evolution. Meta specification principles have the potential to significantly
022            expand the space of possibilities while reducing design complexity, thereby en-
023            hancing the potential for open-ended learning. We demonstrate the viability of
024            this principle through an illustrative implementation that co-evolves robot mor-
025            phologies and robotic tasks, showcasing its capacity for emergent novelty and
026            highlighting the shift in focus towards verification in system design.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Recent advances in machine learning (ML), particularly in foundation models, have dramatically
032   expanded the capabilities of autonomous agents across various domains. However, a key goal in
033
      ML research remains elusive: creating agents capable of continuous self-improvement. Open-ended
      learning systems (OELS) have emerged as a promising frontier to address this challenge (Soros
034
      et al., 2017; Clune, 2019). By dynamically generating novel challenges, OELS drive the adaptation
035
      of learning agents, creating environments that promote continuous exploration and skill acquisition.
036   This approach enables agents to autonomously adapt to unforeseen scenarios and progressively en-
037   hance their capabilities beyond predefined limits (Jiang et al., 2023; Hughes et al., 2024).
038
039
      The efficacy of an OELS in driving continuous progress and expanding agent capabilities hinges
      on two critical factors. First, the design of the underlying system plays a major role in delineating
040
      the space of possibilities, which we will formally introduce in Sec. 2. The system design effectively
041
      defines the boundaries within which learning and evolution can occur. Complementing this are open-
042   ended algorithm that guide the system’s evolution to produce continuous progress and novelty (Brant
043   & Stanley, 2017; Hintze, 2019; Dennis et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2024). The interplay between
044   these factors underscores a crucial principle: while open-ended algorithms can enhance and sustain
045   open-ended learning, the system’s design ultimately imposes the upper limit on this potential. As a
046   result, carefully designing and scaling the system to expand the space of possibilities is of critical
047   importance for maximizing the potential of OELS (Team et al., 2021; Bauer et al., 2023).
048   Current approaches to system design predominantly rely on explicit specification, where the sys-
049   tem’s state spaces and evolution functions are fully defined at design time and kept fixed during its
050   run time. Scaling the system under this paradigm typically involves introducing additional degrees
051   of freedom into its design. This principle has yielded notable successes, as exemplified by Bauer
052   et al. (2023), which demonstrated emergent capabilities and behaviors in a vast system encompass-
053   ing 25 billion unique tasks. However, the design complexity associated with this approach increases
      dramatically with scale. As systems grow in scale, the explicit specification approach may become

054
      prohibitively complex or intractable before reaching the level of complexity required to realize the
055   full potential of OELS.
056
057   This work addresses the challenge of system design in OELS, specifically exploring methods to
058
      expand the space of possibilities while minimizing design complexity. We propose an alternative
      design principle termed meta specification. In contrast to explicit specification, which fully defines
059
      state spaces and evolution functions, meta specification defines a system implicitly through con-
060
      straints placed on a generalized representation space. These constraints reflect both the fundamental
061   requirements of the implementation platform (e.g. computing environment) and additional criteria
062   set by the system designer. Implementing a system through meta specification necessitates three
063   components: (1) a generalized representation for learning agents and tasks, (2) a mechanism to per-
064   form evolution over these representations, and (3) routines to verify constraint satisfaction. Among
065   these, the generalized evolution mechanism presents the most significant challenge. To address this,
066   we formally introduce the concept of watchmaker functions—classes of stochastic functions capa-
067   ble of performing meaningful transformations over generalized representation spaces—and establish
068   the necessary conditions for these functions. Notably, we observe an intriguing connection between
069
      foundation models, particularly Large Language Models (LLMs), and the capabilities required of
      watchmaker functions, suggesting their potential as candidates for this role.
070
071   Key considerations. Implementing a system through meta specification involves designing its con-
072   stituent components. While this principle may not be universally applicable to all open-ended learn-
073   ing objectives, it offers the potential to significantly expand the space of possibilities. Additionally,
074   it shifts the focus of design complexity towards developing robust verification routines, potentially
075
      simplifying other aspects of system design. Illustrative demonstration. To assess the viability of
      this design principle, we present an illustrative implementation that co-evolves robot morphologies
076
      and robotic tasks using an LLM-based watchmaker function. This demonstration showcases emer-
077
      gent novelty in evolved robots and tasks that were not explicitly preprogrammed, and highlights
078   the shift in emphasis towards verification in system design. Through this example, we illustrate the
079   existence of key capabilities that could enable the extension of this principle to larger-scale imple-
080   mentations of OELS. Our core contributions are as follows:
081
      1. We present a formal unified framework for conceptualizing OELS, providing a common lan-
082
         guage and structure for describing and comparing diverse OELS implementations.
083
      2. We introduce a novel design approach for OELS systems based on meta specification princi-
084      ples. This approach leverages generalized representation spaces and watchmaker functions as
085      evolution functions, integrating verification routines to implicitly define the system.
086   3. We provide an illustrative demonstration of the viability and potential of this design approach,
087      presenting key ingredients that would enable its adoption in large-scale implementations.

      2     U NIFIED F RAMEWORK FOR OELS
091
      We begin by presenting a unified framework for open-ended learning systems (OELS), adopting a
092
      system-level perspective that distinguishes between two key components: the underlying (1) dy-
093
      namical system (Sec. 2.1) and the (2) control mechanism (Sec. 2.2). Intuitively, the dynamical
094   system is a coupled system of agents and tasks evolving over time. The control mechanism guides
095   this system’s evolution to be open-ended in nature, by monitoring the outputs of the system and per-
096   forming control actions to guide the system towards continuous progress, as evaluated by some met-
097   ric. To aid in exposition and contextualization, we will use POET (Wang et al., 2019) as a running
098   example, and provide more comprehensive analysis of representative OELS using our framework in
099   App. A. We provide a visual overview of our unified framework in Fig. 2.
100

      first, and agents are evolved subsequently by learning on the evolved task. The complete dynamical
148
      system is then formally defined as S = ⟨X , ΦX ⟩, where X is the Cartesian product of the agent
149

150   point, we recognize that, provided with the initial conditions, the dynamical system is a fully defined
151   and simulatable. However, the direct evolution of such a system is not meaningfully interesting,
152   as it lacks mechanisms for promoting open-ended progress. For example, agents could interact
153   with randomly evolved tasks that are trivial, redundant, or overly difficult, leading to stagnation or
154   degenerate behavior.

178   2.2                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             C ONTROL M ECHANISM
179
180   The goal of the control mechanism is to guide the system towards continuous open-ended progress.
181   It constitutes two key components: a progress monitor that evaluates the agents and tasks produced
182   by the dynamical system in each step to measure some notion of progress; and a controller that
183
      processes these measurements and takes control actions to enhnace continued progress.
184   Notions of progress. Various control mechanisms have been proposed based on different concep-
185   tions of what constitutes meaningful progress in an open-ended setting. In general, progress is
186   measured relative to an aggregation of recent entities produced by the system (either historically or
187   in the current population), which we refer to as the context set. Formally, we define the context set
188   as C ∈ C, where C = P(A) × P(T ), and P(·) denotes the power set and C is the Cartesian product
189
      of these power sets. More concretely, evolved agents or tasks are compared explicitly or implicitly
      (e.g. in an amortized fashion) against the context set to obtain a metric of progress. Examples of no-
190
      tions of progress include novelty (Lehman & Stanley, 2011; Stanley & Lehman, 2015), complexity
191
      (Standish, 2003; Hintze, 2019), learnability (Schmidhuber, 2013; Matiisen et al., 2019), diversity
192   (Mouret & Clune, 2015; Pugh et al., 2016), or interestingness (Zhang et al., 2024). We provide a
193   detailed review of different notions of progress in App. B.
194
      1. Progress monitor. The monitor evaluates outputs of the dynamical system using these op-
195
         erational measures of progress, which we can explicitly define as two evaluation functions:
196      EA : A × T × C → R (for agents) and ET : T × A × C → R (for tasks). Here, we have
197      made explicit the monitor’s evaluations are conditioned on a dynamically updated context set to
198      capture metrics of non-stationary progress.
199   2. Controller. Controllers generally take as input the current set of agents and tasks and their cor-
200      responding scores produced by the progress monitor, differing primarily in the control actions
201      they execute. A common strategy is selection, which directly selects the next set of inputs for
202      the dynamical system to evolve (Brant & Stanley, 2017; Wang et al., 2019; Bauer et al., 2023),
203      embodying the principle of differential reproduction observed in natural evolution and emulated
204      in evolution-inspired algorithms (Gregory, 2009; Holland, 1992). More broadly, controllers may
205
         take any action that influences system dynamics, such as updating parameters of the underlying
         system’s evolution functions (Wang et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2024). Formally, we define two
206
         controller functions OA : P(A) × P(R) → U and OT : P(T ) × P(R) → U, which take as input
207
         the current set of agents (or tasks) and their corresponding evaluations, and produce a control
208      action u ∈ U that acts on the dynamical system, where U represents the set of control actions.
209
210     Example 2.2: Control mechanism
211    In POET, the progress monitor evaluates agents and tasks using the current population as the
212    context set. It first checks the minimal criterion, ensuring each agent solves at least one task
213    and each task is solvable by one agent, then calculates a novelty score for qualifying pairs. The
214    controller removes pairs that do not meet the criterion and selects those for evolution based on
215    novelty scores. It also periodically transfers and adapts agents to different tasks.

      2.3     K EY I NSIGHTS
217
218   This unified framework and conceptualization of OELS yields a few key insights. First, the control
219   mechanism establishes a critical feedback loop, where it continuously monitors progress within the
220   dynamical system, and uses these evaluations to make adjustments that support ongoing, open-ended
221   progress. This enables the system to evolve adaptively, responding to new developments within the
222   system (Soros et al., 2017; Jiang et al., 2023). Second, we can differentiate the roles of the dynamical
223   system and the control mechanism. Notably, the dynamical system defines the space of possibilities
224
      (represented by A and T ) and governs how agents and tasks evolve within this space (through ΦA
      and ΦT ). In contrast, the control mechanism guides continuous progress within this predefined
225
      space, but it cannot alter the fundamental limits imposed by the dynamical system’s design.
226
227   A unifying framework. We note that the formalism presented here is intended to be generalized,
228   at the compromise of perfect specificity for any particular implementation. While certain OELS
229   may have distinctive features that do not map exactly onto our framework, its generality facilitates
230
      broader discussions and comparisons of OELS. In App. A, we show that, despite their individual
      characteristics, many OELS can be effectively described and analyzed within this framework.

233   3     D ESIGN OF THE U NDERLYING S YSTEM
234
235   The presented framework allows us to separate the design of OELS into two categories: the design
236   of the underlying dynamical system and the control mechanism. The designs have fundamentally
237   different implications—the design of the underlying system defines the space of possibilities and
238   mechanisms for evolution, whereas the design of the control mechanism is aimed at enhancing
239   and sustaining open-ended progress. We also note that the two design of the two components can be
240   discussed independently, as the design of the underlying system determines foundational constraints,
241
      while the control mechanism (and their operationalized notions of progress) are generally applicable.
242   Our work investigates the design of the underlying dynamical system, which defines the range of
243   potential agent behaviors and task configurations. The goal of the system design process, then, is to
244   create a sufficiently diverse and rich space of possibilities that can foster the emergence of novel be-
245   haviors or increasingly capable agents. Additionally, the designed system would have to be feasible,
246   which, concretely, is defined as the satisfaction of design constraints. Formally, the system design
247
      process receives a set of requirements R = Rp ∪ Rd , where Rp represent the set of constraints
      required by the platform that the OELS is executed on, and Rd represent design constraints set by
248
      the system designer based on the goal of the OELS. The platform is most commonly a computing
249
      environment, but can be any substrate, including the physical world where agents and tasks interact.
250
251   The goal of system design is to engineer a sufficiently diverse space of possibilities to support open-
252   ended learning. The design process outputs a system S = ⟨X , ΦX ⟩ that satisfies the following
253
      properties:
      1. Realizability: Any possible state and its evolved states are realizable (implementable) on the
254
         underlying platform, ∀x ∈ X , ∀x′ ∈ supp(ΦX (x)) : x |= Rp ∧ x′ |= Rp .
255
      2. Validity: Any state and its evolved states are valid and satisfy the designer requirements, ∀x ∈
256
         X , ∀x′ ∈ supp(ΦX (x)) : x |= Rd ∧ x′ |= Rd .

      3.1     E XPLICITLY S PECIFIED S YSTEM D ESIGN

260
      The conventional approach to system design is based on explicit specification, formally defined as:
261
262        Explicitly Specified Systems
263       An explicitly specified dynamical system is one where its state space and evolution function are
264       fully defined at design time, and kept fixed during its run time. Specifically, this involves:
265       1. Defining an appropriate state representation. Formally, XΘ = {x(θ) | θ ∈ Θ} and θ is a
266          representation that defines each state x(θ), and Θ is the representation space that determines
267          the full state space XΘ .
268       2. Specifying the input and output domains, and the functional form of the evolution function,
269          which maps the state representation to a distribution over next states, ΦXΘ : XΘ → P (XΘ ).


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      For example, the space of possible tasks could be parameterized by a real vector, Θ ⊆ Rn , that
271   encodes all possible configurations (e.g. height and distribution of obstacles in an obstacle course).
272   The evolution function is a fully defined mathematical function that operates over this representation
273   (e.g. performing random mutation of the real vector). We note here, that expanding the space of
274   possibilities corresponds is then achieved by engineering more degrees of freedom into the system.
275
      The underlying system in all current OELS are designed using this principle (Wang et al., 2019;
276
      Dennis et al., 2020; Team et al., 2021), which has recently been operationalized to an impressive
277   degree by Bauer et al. (2023), which introduced a significantly upscaled state space containing 25
278   billion unique tasks. A key advantage of this approach is that it often guarantees system realizability
279   and validity by design. Since all system components are defined upfront, the system is built to meet
280   platform and design constraints, ensuring it functions as intended without unforeseen runtime issues.
281   Additionally, explicitly specifying the system offers designers a high level of foresight and control,
282   allowing them to embed their knowledge or assumptions through design (e.g. specifying promising
283   morphological spaces for robotic agents).
284   However, a significant challenge arises when attempting to scale such systems to explore a broader
285   space of possibilities. This is especially critical as the design of the system directly constrains the
286   emergence of novel behaviors and capabilities within certain bounds and structures. As the system
287   grows, the complexity of the design process increases exponentially, requiring designers to optimally
288   introduce and balance more degrees of freedom. Despite the extensive engineering effort invested in
289   Bauer et al. (2023)’s system, progress eventually plateaued. To unlock further progress on a larger
290   scale would require an even more complex design process, underscoring the inherent difficulty of
291
      scaling using this approach.

      4   WATCHMAKER F UNCTIONS AND M ETA S PECIFICATION
296
297
      A key challenge facing OELS is how to significantly expand the space of possibilities permitted
298
      by the design of the underlying system. This work proposes an alternative design principle termed
299
      meta specification. At its core, meta specification defines a system implicitly through constraints
300   that must be satisfied to yield valid states, rather than explicitly enumerating the state space.
301
302   More formally, we can contrast explicit and meta specification. Whereas an explicitly specified sys-
303
      tem completely describes all possible states as XΘ = {x(θ) | θ ∈ Θ}, meta specification implicitly
      defines the state space through constraints: XR = {x ∈ V | x |= R}. Here V denotes the universal
304
      set, which conceptually refers to the set of all possible elements under consideration. For example,
305
      V could be the set of all possible robot morphologies or learning environments (which Clune (2019)
306   defines as Darwin Complete). Performing meta specification then requires three key components:
307   (1) a generalized representation space V for agents and tasks, (2) a mechanism to perform evolu-
308   tion over these generalized representations Φ : V → P (V), and (3) a method to verify constraint
309   satisfaction, i.e. x |= R ∀ x ∈ V.
310
      Regarding (1), sufficiently generalized representations do exist for many domains. For example,
311
      Unified Robot Description Format (URDF) can practically represent a wide array of robot morpholo-
312   gies (Quigley et al., 2015) and PyTorch can represent a vast space of neural network architectures
313   (Paszke et al., 2019). Additionally, many requirements can be formally or empirically verified, such
314   as kinematic feasibility for robot designs or certain properties for neural networks. Hence, the most
315   challenging component of meta specification is the generalized evolution function. In explicitly
316   specified designs, evolution functions are well-defined mathematical functions with clearly specified
317   input and co-domains, which guarantees they are well-behaved over the entire state space. Here, the
318   space that the generalized evolution function operates over is no longer explicitly defined, meaning
319   that it loses any guarantee to be well-behaved. This lack of any guarantees on outputs produced by
320
      such functions necessitates verification routines to ensure that outputs are both valid and realizable.
321   As such, the implementation of meta specification is predicated on the existence of a generalized
322   evolution function and verification routines for a chosen generalized representation space. We term
323   this class of generalized evolution functions as watchmaker functions, inspired by the ”watch-
      maker” analogy in evolution (Dawkins, 1986).

      4.1   WATCHMAKER F UNCTIONS
325
326
      Watchmaker functions represent a class of functions that can take various forms depending on the
327
      chosen generalized representation V, but must satisfy certain conditions:
328
329
330     Watchmaker Functions
331    For a given generalized representation sapce V, a watchmaker function ΦW : V → P (V) must
332    satisfy the following necessary conditions:
333    (C1) Stochasticity: ∀ v ∈ V, repeated applications of ΦW (v) may yield different outputs.
334    (C2) Generalized transformation: ΦW is capable of producing meaningful transformations to
335           any element v ∈ V. Here, “meaningful” implies the function has an acceptable likeli-
336           hood ε of producing outputs v ′ ∈ V that are valid and realizable given requirements R,
337
              i.e. Ev′ ∼ΦW (· | v) [p(v ′ |= R)] ≥ ε.
338
339   The conditions (C1) and (C2) serve distinct purposes in defining watchmaker functions. (C1), the
340   stochasticity condition, introduces variability and exploration into the system’s evolution, preventing
341   deterministic loops. (C2), the generalized transformation condition, ensures that the function has an
342   acceptable efficiency in producing valid and realizable outputs. This latter condition distinguishes
343   viable watchmaker functions from purely stochastic processes which, while theoretically capable of
344   generating valid and realizable states, are considerably inefficient (Borges, 1998; Eddington, 2019).
345   It is worth noting that in the special case where V = X is an explicitly defined representation space,
346
      a fully-defined evolution function can be considered a watchmaker function with perfect efficiency
      (ε = 1). A prime example of a potential watchmaker function is the human cognitive process.
347
      It can operate over generalized representations (e.g. natural language) and stochastically generate
348
      meaningful transformations to diverse stimuli. To make the connection to OELS more concrete, this
349   could manifest as follows: a human might receive a robot morphology expressed in some domain-
350   specific language (DSL), along with an instruction such as “improve joint mobility”. With some
351   acceptable probability, the human could then produce an output that satisfies the given requirements.
352
      FM watchmakers. Importantly, another class of models that could potentially serve as watchmaker
353
      functions are foundation models (FM) such as Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown, 2020;
354
      Chowdhery et al., 2023). These models, pretrained on vast amounts of data, function as efficiently
355   stochastic generators capable of performing meaningful transformations across diverse domains.
356   Their potential is evident in tasks such as code synthesis (Chen et al., 2021), program evolution (Ma
357   et al., 2024; Lehman et al., 2023), and plan generation (Huang et al., 2022). As LLMs operate in
358   the language space, they could serve as watchmaker functions for generalized representations that
359   utilize language tokens, such as DSL like PyTorch for neural network architectures or URDF for
360   robot descriptions. The implications of FMs as automated watchmaker functions are far-reaching,
361   potentially enabling significant expansion of the space of possibilities in OELS, while reducing de-
362   sign complexity. In Sec. 5, we empirically investigate this feasibility, providing concrete evidence
363
      for the potential of LLMs as effective watchmaker functions in practice.
364
365
366   4.2   I MPLICIT S YSTEM D ESIGN T HROUGH V ERIFICATION
367
368   Watchmaker functions, while powerful, lack inherent guarantees of producing well-behaved out-
369   puts. This limitation is particularly relevant for FM watchmaker functions in meta specification,
370   where evolved outputs (e.g., robot morphologies) may not always be realizable or valid against de-
371
      sign requirements. Recall that (C2) stipulates that, in expectation, there is a ε probability that the
      watchmaker function produces valid and realizable outputs. Then, a viable approach to ensure re-
372
      quirements satisfaction is to introduce verification routines that operate on watchmaker functions
373
      outputs. Formally, given a set of n requirements R = {R1 , . . . , Rn }, we define verification routines
374   ∆R = {δ1 , . . . , δn }, where each δi : V → {0, 1} verifies if an output satisfies a particular require-
375   ment. Additionally, we say ∆R (x) = 1 if x ∈ V passes all verification routines. This verification
376   process, used in conjunction with the watchmaker function, implicitly defines a system:

405   4.3     I MPLICATIONS IN F ULL
406
407   Design process. Implementing a system through meta specification concretely entails three key
408   steps: ▶ selecting a suitable generalized representation for agents and tasks; ▶ designing a FM-
409   based watchmaker, for example through prompt designs or model finetuning, to improve their effi-
410   ciency over the chosen representations; and ▶ developing verification routines to confirm satisfac-
411
      tion of system and design requirements. We note that meta specification is not likely to be universally
      applicable to all open-ended learning goals, and its viability depends on these three steps.
412
413   Expanding the space of possibilities. LLM-based watchmaker functions have the potential to sig-
414   nificantly expand the space of possibilities, especially for certain representations. DSL representa-
415   tion is especially powerful, as the compositional nature of code allows for combinatorial expansion
416   of possibilities, enabling emergence not explicitly pre-defined (Backus, 1978). Moreover, many
417
      DSL and programming language are Turing-complete representations, theoretically allowing the
      space of possibilities to encompass any computable function (Turing, 1936).
418
419   Shifting design focus. Meta specification shifts the focus of system design from explicit definition
420   of system component from the ground up, towards developing robust verification routines to ensure
421   realizability and validity. As such, it potentially presents a more tractable approach in domains
422   where direct system engineering is prohibitively complex. This shift in design paradigm echos
423
      observations across various fields suggesting that verification can be less complex than generation
      (e.g. from NP-hard problems (Dantzig et al., 1954) to machine learning (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and
424
      software engineering (Dijkstra, 1976)). However, it’s crucial to recognize that some requirements
425
      resist straightforward automatic verification, and ensuring robust, scalable verification in the face of
426   emergent system developments presents a significant challenge.

      5.1   E MPIRICAL O BSERVATIONS
487
488   Emergent novelty. We visualize evolved robots and tasks in Fig. 3, observing a wide array of
489   distinct and novel morphologies and tasks. The robots form niches ranging from ant-like creatures
490   with long, slender legs to horse-like quadrupeds with sturdy limbs, as well as more unconventional
491   forms resembling ‘Walker’ machines from Star Wars. We also observe interesting combinations of
492   parent phenotypes, such as robots that retain ant-like legs but develop walker-like feet. The evolved
493   tasks display similar diversity, including challenges focused on uneven terrain, obstacle courses, and
494
      constrained environments like mazes and tunnels. This emergent novelty occurs without any explicit
      preprogramming, demonstrating the system’s capacity for generating complex and novel states.
495
496   Role of verification. In Fig. 4, we visualize several evolved states that were rejected for failing
497   to meet the verification checks. This includes tasks where environmental objects violated physics
498   constraints or where target locations were unreachable. For robots, examples of rejections included
499   those that exceeded the maximum allowed mass or were unable to achieve static stability. In Fig. 5,
500
      we track the percentage of evolved outputs that are valid and realizable, noting that approximately
      40% of the candidates evolved by the watchmaker functions satisfied both criteria. Interestingly,
501
      while the percentage of realizable robot morphologies was relatively high, the percentage of valid
502
      morphologies was lower. Based on our manual examination, we attribute this to several morpholog-
503   ical constraints—such as static stability and mass—being phenotypic constraints rather than explic-
504   itly encoded in the URDF. As these attributes are only verified at runtime, it increases the likelihood
505   that certain requirements will not be met, leading to a lower validity rate.
506
      Additional ingredients. It is important to note that the implementation here is only the underlying
507
      dynamical system, which serves to illustrate the potential of an alternative system design principle.
508
      However, it would need to be complemented by additional components to fully realize it as an OELS.
509   Most notably, we have omitted any training procedure (due to the cost and compute requirements),
510   which is crucial for the evolved robots to become increasingly capable. Furthermore, a control
511   mechanism should be integrated to ensure continuous progress. This could include regret-based
512   approaches (Jiang et al., 2021) or LLM-based progress monitors (Zhang et al., 2024), or novel
513   control mechanisms specifically tailored to the characteristics of watchmaker functions.

      6     D ISCUSSIONS
516
517   In summary, this work introduces meta specification as a novel approach to designing OELS. This
518   principle enables implicit system definition through constraints, employing watchmaker functions
519   in conjunction with verification routines to drive system evolution. In contrast to explicitly spec-
520   ified designs, meta specification offers the potential to significantly expand the space of possibili-
521   ties while concurrently reducing system design complexity. Our illustrative demonstration of co-
522   evolving robot morphologies and tasks illuminates the viability of this principle, showcasing its
523   capacity for emergent novelty and underscoring the critical role of verification in maintaining sys-
524   tem integrity. Future directions. Building on this foundation, subsequent research should prioritize
525
      scaling our proof-of-concept to large-scale implementations, thereby exploring the full potential of
      meta specification across diverse domains, including embodied agents and LLM-based reasoning
526
      systems. Furthermore, systems designed through meta specification could be augmented with com-
527
      plementary control mechanisms specifically tailored to foster the continuous generation of novelty
528   and progress within this framework.

      A    I NVESTIGATING OELS T HROUGH A U NIFIED F RAMEWORK
757
758
      The goal of this section is to illustrate that existing research in OELS can be conceptualized in the
759
      same unifying framework (presented in Sec. 2), as containing three key components—▶ dynamical
760
      system (co-)evolving agents and tasks, ▶ progress monitor that evaluates the evolutionary progress
761   of current agents/tasks relative to a context set, and ▶ control mechanism that takes performs some
762   control logic based on progress evaluations to sustain open-ended progress. We survey some of
763   the most prominent and recent works, including Minimal Criterion Coevolution (Brant & Stanley,
764   2017), POET (Wang et al., 2019), PAIRED (Dennis et al., 2020), Ada (Team et al., 2021; Bauer
765   et al., 2023), and OMNI (Zhang et al., 2024).
766
      Minimal Criterion Coevolution (MCC) (Brant & Stanley, 2017). MCC co-evolves two popula-
767   tions: mazes and maze-solving agents, where individuals from both populations are evaluated for
768   satisfying a minimal criterion (MC).
769
770
      • Agents. Agents are maze-solving agents with evolvable neural controllers. The agent space A
        is the space of neural networks, varying in the number of neurons and connections. The agent
771
        evolution function ΦA is the NEAT algorithm (Stanley & Miikkulainen, 2002), a genetic algorithm
772
        that evolves networks by adding connections or nodes.
773
      • Tasks. Tasks are 2D mazes encoding using a variable length genotype. The task space T in-
774     clude mazes with varying number of walls, wall connections, and location of openings. The task
775     evolution function ΦT is a random mutation that modifies wall characteristics or number of walls.
776   • Progress Monitor. Progress is evaluated using satisfaction of minimal criterion, where the context
777     set is the current population. Specifically, each maze-solver satisfies the MC if it solves at least
778     one maze in the context set, and each maze satisfies the MC if solvable by at least one maze-solver.
779   • Controller. The control logic removes agents and tasks that do not satisfy the MC, with the re-
780     maining population fed into the system for another round of evolution.
781
782   MCC is similar in principle to ‘differential reproduction with variation’ observed in natural selection
783   and emulated in evolutionary algorithms (Holland, 1992; Gregory, 2009). In other words, the control
784
      mechanism evaluates for fitness (using the MC) and performs selection, with fit individuals allowed
      to reproduce and generate variations in the system.
785
786   POET (Wang et al., 2019). POET co-evolves populations of bipedal robots and locomotion tasks
787   in a 2D terrain.
788
      • Agents. Agents are bipedal robots with identical morphology and neural controller architectures.
789
        The agent space A is the space of neural weights, which are evolved. The agent evolution func-
790
        tion ΦA is the Evolution Strategies algorithms, which evolves continuous vectors through genetic
791     operations (Hansen et al., 2015).
792   • Tasks. Tasks are 2D terrains with different terrains and obstacles. The task space T ⊆ R4 in-
793     cludes terrains with 4 degrees of freedom (that control the size and frequency of obstacles). Task
794     evolution ΦT is performed through random mutations, where tasks eligible to produce are evolved
795     to generate offspring tasks.
796   • Progress Monitor Progress is evaluated using MC and novelty, where the context set is the current
797     population. Novelty of evolved tasks is calculated using the Euclidean distance of its environment
798     encoding with its k-nearest neighbors.
799   • Controller. The controller selects offspring tasks with the highest novelty scores and admits them
800     into the population. It also performs periodic attempts to transfer agents between different envi-
801     ronments, to encourage cross-pollination.
802
      PAIRED (Dennis et al., 2020). PAIRED trains a maze-solving agent in different environments. It
803
      differs from previous approaches by using an environment-generating policy to generate adversarial
804   tasks to guide agent learning.
805
806   • Agents. Agents are maze-solving agents with fixed neural architectures. The agent space A is
807     the space of possible neural weights. The agent evolution function ΦA is an RL-based learning
808     algorithm that evolves agent weights.
809   • Tasks. Tasks are mazes with different layouts in a gridworld platform. The space of tasks T
        is the space of maze layouts, where each tile could be a wall, start position, end position, or

810
        pathway. The task evolution function ΦT is the environment generating policy Λ : Π → ∆(T ),
811     that produces a distribution over tasks given the current agent policy.
812   • Progress Monitor. The monitor evaluates the regret of the agent policy πa relative to a baseline
813     policy πb , i.e. REGRET(πa , πb ) = U (πb ) − U (πa ), where U (·) is the reward obtained by each
814     policy. Here, progress is not evaluated with respect to an explicit context set; instead, it is amor-
815     tized through Λ, which serves as a learned representation, implicitly encoding the capabilities of
816     the current agent.
817   • Controller. The control mechanism updates the environment generating policy Λ based on the
818     regret of the agent and the baseline policy, training it to maximize regret, and generate more
819     challenging tasks.
820
      Ada (Team et al., 2021). Ada aimed to develop highly adaptable RL agents in an embodied 3D

822
823   • Agents. Agents are embodied agents with fixed morphology and neural controllers. The agent

        updates (Hessel et al., 2021).

      • Tasks. Tasks are embodied and potentially multi-agent environments with procedurally generated

834   OMNI (Zhang et al., 2024). Differing from prior works, OMNI employs an LLM as an evaluator
835   of task novelty based on human notions of interestingness.
836   • Agents. Agents are RL agents with fixed neural controllers, and the agent space A is the space of
837     possible weight configurations. Agent evolution (ΦA ) occurs through RL updates.
838   • Tasks. OMNI investigated different task spaces, including 2D gridworld-like environments. The
839     task space T is defined by parametric encoding of different tasks, where different task spaces
840     are characterized by different free parameters. Task evolution (ΦT ) occurs through a learning-
841     progress-based curriculum (Kanitscheider et al., 2021).
842   • Progress Monitor. Progress is evaluated using an LLM’s internalized notion of interestingness
843     against a context set of recent tasks. The LLM predicts whether it finds evolved tasks interesting
844     (i.e. a binary prediction).
845   • Controller. The control logic uses the LLM’s evaluation of task interestingness to update task
846     sampling weights in the curriculum.

848   B    E XTENDED R ELATED W ORKS
849
850   Notions of progress. A key component in any OELS is the design of the control mechanism that
851   is used to quantify and take control actions to foster various notions of what constitute progress in
852   open-ended learning. Specifically:
853
      • Novelty: Methods that encourage generations of agents or tasks that are sufficiently novel com-
854
        pared to previously seen examples (Stanley & Lehman, 2015). Novelty has been concretely for-
855
        malized as Euclidean k-nearest neighbor (Lehman et al., 2008; Lehman & Stanley, 2011), or count
856     of immediate neighbors in a discrete behavioral (phenotypic) space (Cully & Demiris, 2017).
857     Such methods require a-priori for the behavior space to be manually defined and often discretized.
858     Closely related to novelty-based approaches are those based on fostering diversity, which while
859     conceptually distinct, are occasionally operationalized with similar metrics (Pugh et al., 2016;
860     Mouret & Clune, 2015; Brant & Stanley, 2017).
861   • Complexity: Approaches that drive system evolution towards increasingly complex agents and
862     challenging tasks (Standish, 2003; Hintze, 2019). For example, Kolmogorov complexity metric
863     based on sliding-window compression of a sequence (Hintze, 2019) and JPEG compression of
        images to evaluate complexity (Earle et al., 2021).


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      • Learnability: Techniques that aim to balance task difficulty with agent capabilities to build an
865     auto-curriculum for continual learning. Tasks are proposed within the agent’s “zone of proximal
866     development” to promote continuous growth and skill acquisition (Vygotsky, 1978). This ap-
867     proach is perhaps most common in the field of Unsupervised Environment Design (UED) which
868     generates new RL training environments for agents (Dennis et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2021; Parker-
869     Holder et al., 2022; Samvelyan et al., 2023). These aim of such efforts is not true open-endedness,
870     per se, are usually focused only on generating different variations in training environment to train
871     robust agents, and a significant limitation is their reliance on predefined or manually curated dis-
872     tributions of tasks or environment parameters. Notable approaches use regret-based calculations
873
        (Dennis et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2021; Parker-Holder et al., 2022) to prioritize tasks with high
        regret. Alternative methods calculate learning progress using learning curve slope (Matiisen et al.,
874
        2019) or differences in task success rates across training steps (Kanitscheider et al., 2021), or
875
        meta-learning potential (Team et al., 2021; Bauer et al., 2023).
876
877   OELS. An interesting array of different open-ended learning systems have been proposed, differing
878   significantly in system design and intended application. Chromaria (Soros & Stanley, 2014) is a
879   visual, 2D world composed of discrete RGB pixels, where colorful creatures (Chromarians) evolve
880   and explore locations to plant, and is used to illustrate the necessary conditions for open-endedness
881   to emerge in artificial life. More recent works have investigated systems aimed to fostering general
882   capabilities through open-ended learning, including a 2D bipedal walker domain to improve robot
883   locomotion (Wang et al., 2019), 2D grid worlds for hierarchical task completion (Dennis et al.,
884
      2020). Team et al. (2021); Bauer et al. (2023) introduced XLand2, containing 25-billion possible
      task variants corresponding to different world topologies and variety of possible games within each
885
      world. Minecraft, which contains procedurally generated 3D terrains and continuous exploration of
886
      technology trees (Wang et al., 2024). In App. A, we investigated these disparate implementations,
887   demonstrating they could be analyzed according to our unifying framework.
888
889   FM in OELS. Recent investigations into FMs within OELS have primarily focused on their role in
890
      evaluating and fostering continued progress (i.e. as control mechanism). LLMs have been utilized
      as evaluators of qualitative notions of progress, assessing open-ended creativity of writing (Bradley
891
      et al., 2024) and interestingness of proposed states (Lu et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024). While
892
      these approaches leverage FMs for evaluation and control, our proposal extends their application to
893   the core system itself, with watchmaker functions potentially guided by these control components
894   to sustain open-endedness. Although not developed in the context of open-ended learning, recent
895   works have demonstrated the potential of LLMs as generalized evolution functions. This includes
896   evolutionary search for code (Lehman et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024) and in-context
897   generation of meaningful variations (Meyerson et al., 2023; Fernando et al., 2024). Our approach
898   builds upon these findings, proposing the potential of LLM watchmakers for meta specification
899   of systems for open-ended learning. Parallel research has explored LLMs as embodied agents in
900   open-ended settings, functioning as decision-makers and task executors for novel exploration (Wang
901
      et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024a). While these studies focus on LLMs as agents within the system,
      our watchmaker concept extends to a broader context, encompassing systems that evolve diverse
902
      entities such as robot morphologies or algorithms, thereby offering a more generalizable framework
903
      for open-ended evolution. Additionally, research into training FM for generating action-controllable
904   virtual worlds offer potential applications in OELS (Bruce et al., 2024; Earle et al., 2024).
