009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Multimodal MR image synthesis aims to generate missing modality image by
012            fusing and mapping a few available MRI data. Most existing approaches typically
013            adopt an image-to-image translation scheme. However, these methods often suffer
014            from sub-optimal performance due to the spatial misalignment between different
015            modalities while they are typically treated as input channels. Therefore, in this
016            paper, we propose an Adaptive Group-wise Interaction Network (AGI-Net) that
017            explores both inter-modality and intra-modality relationships for multimodal MR
018            image synthesis. Specifically, groups are first pre-defined along the channel di-
019            mension and then we perform an adaptive rolling for the standard convolutional
020
               kernel to capture inter-modality spatial correspondences. At the same time, a
               cross-group attention module is introduced to fuse information across different
021
               channel groups, leading to better feature representation. We evaluated the ef-
022
               fectiveness of our model on the publicly available IXI and BraTS2023 datasets,
023            where the AGI-Net achieved state-of-the-art performance for multimodal MR im-
024            age synthesis. Code will be released.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Multimodal medical data plays an important role in modern clinical diagnosis and treatment by
031   providing diverse, complementary information about organs and tissues, aiming at enhancing both
032   accuracy and confidence in clinical decision-making. For example, the MRI T1 modality is usually
033
      used to indicate human anatomies and the T2 modality can highlight soft tissues. However, factors
      such as patient non-compliance during scanning, extended scanning times, and the degradation of
034
      individual modality hinder the broader adoption of multimodal imaging Thukral (2015); Krupa &
035
      Bekiesińska-Figatowska (2015). As a result, it is highly desirable to synthesize missing modalities
036   from a limited number of available multimodal data Iglesias et al. (2013); Huo et al. (2018).
037
038   Similar to the image translation task, multimodal medical image synthesis usually requires capturing
039
      the interrelationships between modalities, integrating fine-grained features across modalities, and
      establishing mappings from multiple inputs to a single output. Since multimodal image synthesis
040
      leverages information from multiple input modalities, it reduces the complexity of network mapping
041
      and enhances the reliability of the synthesis compared to the natural image-to-image translation.
042   However, in clinical scenarios, multimodal images are often misaligned due to factors such as motion
043   artifacts, necessitating alignment through iterative optimization or learnable registration methods,
044   leading to accumulated registration errors.
045
      In recent years, significant progress has been made in the field of multimodal MR image synthesis.
046
      Approaches such as learning modality-specific representations and latent representations of multi-
047
      modal images Zhou et al. (2020); Joyce et al. (2017); Chartsias et al. (2017); Meng et al. (2024)
048   have been extensively studied. Fusion strategies like Multi-Scale Gate Mergence Zhan et al. (2021),
049   attention-based fusion Li et al. (2024), and Confidence-Guided Aggregation Peng et al. (2021) fur-
050   ther have been explored. These methods typically involve multiple networks or branches for fine-
051   grained intra-modality feature extraction and use shared fusion networks to capture inter-modality
052   relationships, significantly increasing the training and inference costs of the models. Moreover,
053   they do not address the issue of spatial misalignment in the feature fusion process between different
      modalities. However, designing an single effective network capable of performing fine-grained intra-


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      modality feature extraction while capturing inter-modality relationships under spatial misaligned
055   conditions remains an unresolved challenge.
056
057   It is widely recognized that the quality of features extracted by neural networks is crucial for medical
058
      image synthesis. Deep models are required to extract fine-grained features from different modali-
      ties and capture local spatial interactions. Since multimodal images are almost impossible to align
059
      perfectly in spatial locations (e.g., different tissue structures in T1 or T2 modality), most traditional
060
      convolution designs are challenging to achieve optimal performance.
061
062   Therefore, in this paper, we study the task of multimodal MR image synthesis and propose a sim-
063   ple yet effective Adaptive Group-wise Interaction Network (AGI-Net), with the key design of Cross
064
      Group Attention and Group-wise Rolling (CAGR) module. Here, Cross Group Attention establishes
      intra-group and inter-group relationships to suppress inter-modality aliasing noise in the input fea-
065
      tures, while Group-wise Rolling allows independent adaptive rolling of convolution kernels across
066
      groups to adjust the kernel positions for each group (as illustrated in Fig. 1), with the rolling off-
067   sets predicted by a routing function in a data-dependent manner. These two group-based designs
068   work seamlessly together, effectively capturing inter-modality local spatial relationships under par-
069   tial misalignment, thus enhancing the network to extract and integrate information across different
070   modalities. The proposed module is a plug-and-play component that can replace any convolution
071   layer. We evaluate our approach on the publicly available IXI 1 and BraTS2023 2 datasets, and
072   extensive experiments conducted by replacing the convolution module within existing frameworks
073   demonstrate the effectiveness of our AGI-Net, achieving a new state of the art for multimodal MR
074   image synthesis.

      Dynamic Convolution. Standard convolution maintains constant parameters and locations through-
      out the entire inference process, whereas dynamic convolution allows for flexible adjustments to both
125
      parameters and locations based on different inputs, offering advantages in computational efficiency
126
      and representational power. Dynamic convolution can typically be classified into two categories: 1)
127   adaptive kernel shape and 2) adaptive kernel parameters. Adaptive kernel shape involves generat-
128   ing suitable kernel shapes according to different inputs. For instance, Dai et al. (2017); Zhu et al.
129   (2019); Wang et al. (2023); Xiong et al. (2024) generates kernel deformations through offsets to
130   capture more accurate semantic information, while Qi et al. (2023) constrains the kernel shape into
131   a snake-like form to capture vascular continuity features.
132
      Adaptive kernel parameters, on the other hand, utilize input-generated kernel weights. For example,
133   Pu et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024) proposed adaptive rotating kernels to capture objects in various
134   orientations for rotation-invariant object detection. Gao et al. (2019); Kim et al. (2021); Chen et al.
135   (2024) adapts kernel parameters by deformation to handle object deformation while maintaining a
136   consistent receptive field. The method proposed in this paper falls under the category of adaptive
137   kernel parameters. The proposed convolution module employs group-wise rolling kernel parameters,
138   which alleviates the issue of imperfect alignment between modalities and enhances the network’s
139   ability to represent multimodal images.

      3.1   C ROSS G ROUP ATTENTION
204
205   Before performing the rolling convolution operation, for multimodal image synthesis tasks, the in-
206   put feature x ∈ RCin ×Hin ×Win can be easily divided into n groups, where each group xi , i ∈
207   {1, 2, ..., n} contains feature information related to a specific modality. However, after multiple lay-
208   ers of convolution, each group xi may incorporate features from other modalities, leading to aliasing
209   noise, which reduces the accuracy of subsequent rolling offset prediction. To address this issue, we
210   employ a Cross Group Attention mechanism. This mechanism selectively suppresses aliasing noise
211   caused by irrelevant modality features and enhances the expression of relevant modality features by
212
      leveraging both intra-group and inter-group information.
213   Specifically, we first apply average pooling to each group feature xi obtaining the intra-group pool
214   feature zi . Then, using a channel shuffle Zhang et al. (2018); Ma et al. (2018) operation, we rear-
215   range the channels of z to construct inter-group information flow, producing the inter-group feature
      zis . The concatenation of intra-group and inter-group features is then fed through a convolution layer

      3.2   G ROUP - WISE ROLLING
225
226   In this section, we provide a detailed explanation of the Group-wise Rolling module within CAGR.
227   This module operates in two main steps: predicting the parameters for rolling convolution based on
228   the enhanced input features by Cross Group Attention, and generating group-wise rolled convolution
229   kernels.
230
      To predict the rolling offsets for each group in a data-dependent manner, we designed a lightweight
231   network called the routing function. The routing function takes the enhanced image feature x      e as input
232   and predicts n groups rolling offsets [(ox1 , oy1 ), . . . , (oxn , oyn )] for kernels. Each group indepen-
233   dently predicts offsets {oxi }i∈{1,2,··· ,n} and {oyi }i∈{1,2,··· ,n} along the x-axis and y-axis, respec-
234   tively. The overall architecture of the routing function is illustrated in Fig. 4. Initially, the enhanced
235   input image feature x  e ∈ RCin ×Hin ×Win is fed into a lightweight Group convolution Krizhevsky
236   et al. (2012) with a kernel size of 3 × 3, followed by Layer Normalization ValizadehAslani & Liang
237   (2024); Vaswani (2017) and GELU Hendrycks & Gimpel (2016) activation. The activated features
238   are then average pooled to form a feature vector of dimension Cin . This pooled feature vector is
239
      passed into two separate branches. The first branch is the rolling offset prediction branch, which
      predicts offsets along the x-axis and y-axis for each group. No activation function is applied to the
240
      predicted offsets, enhancing the expressive power of the rolling convolution. The second branch
241
      termed the group scale factor prediction branch, is responsible for predicting the scale factor λ for
242   each group. It consists of a linear layer with bias and Sigmoid activation. The weights of both the
243   rolling offset prediction and group scale factor prediction branches in the routing function are ini-
244   tialized to zero, and the bias in the group scale factor prediction branch is initialized to one, ensuring
245   stability at the beginning of the training process. Notably, the number of groups n is significantly
246   smaller than the number of input channels of the convolution Cin . Consequently, it is straightfor-
247   ward to partition the convolution kernels into n distinct groups across the Cin channels, with each
248   group having a unique set of offsets.
249   The standard convolution takes enhanced input features x   e ∈ RCin ×Hin ×Win and kernel weights
250   W ∈ RCout ×Cin ×k×k , producing the output feature y ∈ RCout ×Hout ×Wout . For the multi-channel
251   enhanced input feature x e in multimodal imaging, convolution is applied uniformly across spatial
252   positions with the same kernel weights wm ∈ RCin ×k×k , m ∈ {1, 2, · · · , Cout } performing Cout
253   operations to obtain the output feature y with Cout channels. In our approach, we divide the kernel
254   weights W into n groups wi ∈ RCout ×Cin /n×k×k , i ∈ {1, 2, · · · , n} in Cin dimension, where ker-
255   nels from different groups can independently capture features specific to different modalities through
256   the grouping strategy.

      3.3      N ETWORK A RCHITECTURE
297
      In terms of network implementation, since our proposed CAGR module can be easily integrated
298   as a plug-and-play component into any network structure with convolutional layers, we built the
299   proposed network architecture, AGI-Net, based on the commonly used ResUnet Zhang et al. (2021).
300   ResUnet consists of three down-sampling stages, three up-sampling stages, and a central body stage.
301   Each stage includes two ResBlocks (z + F (relu(F (z)))). We replaced the first convolution in each
302   ResBlock within the three down-sampling stages, the body stage, and the first up-sampling stage
303   with the CAGR module to form the new network architecture, referred to as AGI-Net. Ablation
304   studies on replacing different parts of the network and their impact on performance are discussed in
305   the experimental section.

471   Although the proposed AGI-Net demonstrates superior performance in multimodal MR image syn-

477   5     C ONCLUSIONS
478
479
      We present an adaptive group-wise interaction model for multimodal MR image synthesis, featuring
480   two key components: the Cross-Group Attention and Group-wise Rolling modules. The Cross-
481   Group Attention module is designed to fuse both intra-group and inter-group information, effectively
482   mitigating spatial noise between different modality groups. Following this, the convolutional kernels
483   are adaptively rolled in a data-driven manner based on the specific modality groups. This module is
484   flexible and can be integrated into any convolutional backbone for multimodal MR image synthesis.
485   Experimental results show that our AGI-Net significantly enhances image synthesis performance on
      public multimodal benchmarks while maintaining computational efficiency.
