                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               We present SPEAR, a continuous receiver-to-receiver acoustic neural warping field
012
               for spatial acoustic effects prediction in an acoustic 3D space with a single sta-
013
               tionary audio source. Unlike traditional source-to-receiver modelling methods
014            that require prior space acoustic properties knowledge to rigorously model audio
015            propagation from source to receiver, we propose to predict by warping the spatial
016            acoustic effects from one reference receiver position to another target receiver
017            position, so that the warped audio essentially accommodates all spatial acoustic
018            effects belonging to the target position. SPEAR can be trained in a data much more
019            readily accessible manner, in which we simply ask two robots to independently
020            record spatial audio at different positions. We further theoretically prove the uni-
021            versal existence of the warping field if and only if one audio source presents. Three
022
               physical principles are incorporated to guide SPEAR network design, leading to the
               learned warping field physically meaningful. We demonstrate SPEAR superiority
023
               in receiver-to-receiver warping field prediction through detailed experiments on
024
               both synthetic, photo-realistic and real-world dataset.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   In an enclosed acoustic 3D space where a stationary sound source keeps emitting spatial audio, the
030   primary objective is to precisely delineate the spatial acoustic effects for any given receiver position.
031   These spatial acoustic effects typically encompass reverberation, loudness variation and resonance.
032   Achieving high-fidelity and authentic spatial acoustic effect modelling is pivotal for delivering a truly
033
      immersive 3D acoustic experience that seamlessly integrates with the 3D room scene. Consequently,
      such modelling techniques have a wide range of applications in auditory AR/VR techniques (Verron
034
      et al., 2010; Hyodo et al., 2021; Broderick et al., 2018), audio-inclusive robot tasks (Evers et al.,
035
      2016) and reconstruction endeavors (Zhong et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021).
036
037   To model the spatial acoustic effects, most prior methods (Bilbao & Hamilton, 2017; Savioja &
038   Svensson, 2015; Allen & Berkley, 1979; Krokstad et al., 1968; Pietrzyk, 1998; Kleiner et al., 1993;
039
      Botteldoore, 1995; Hodgson & Nosal, 2006; Luo et al., 2022) follow the source-to-receiver pipeline
      to explicitly model sound propagation process from source to receiver, where the overall behavioural
040
      change along the propagation path is usually described by room impulse response (RIR). As the
041
      spatial audio propagates in a complex way encompassing diffraction, reflection and absorption,
042   the resulting RIR is highly non-smooth and lengthy in data points. Classic methods, either wave-
043   based (Bilbao & Hamilton, 2017; Pietrzyk, 1998; Kleiner et al., 1993; Botteldoore, 1995) and
044   geometry-based modelling (Savioja & Svensson, 2015), require massive prior knowledge of the
045   3D space’s acoustic properties such as source position, space geometric layout and constructional
046   material to precisely simulate the propagation process for a given source-receiver pair. However,
047   accessing such prior knowledge poses a formidable challenge in reality and the whole computation
048   is inextricably inefficient. Some recent work (e.g., NAF (Luo et al., 2022)) aim to alleviate this
049   computational burden by learning a continuous acoustic neural field. Nonetheless, training such a
050
      continuous field necessitates vast RIR data which is exceedingly difficult to collect in real scenarios.
051   In this work, we instead propose to predict spatial acoustic effects from receiver-to-receiver perspec-
052   tive. Our framework, termed SPEAR, relies on neither RIR data nor prior space acoustic properties
053   that are difficult to obtain and required by existing source-to-receiver based methods (Luo et al.,
      2022; Savioja & Svensson, 2015; Bilbao & Hamilton, 2017), but instead simply require much more

096       only if one stationary audio source presents in the 3D space. SPEAR network design is based on
097       three acoustic physical principles, so that the whole neural network is physically meaningful.
098

102   Spatial Acoustic Effects Modelling. Classic methods tend to numerically compute room impulse
103   response (RIR). They can be divided into two main categories: wave-based (Bilbao & Hamilton,
104   2017; Pietrzyk, 1998; Kleiner et al., 1993; Botteldoore, 1995) and geometry-based (aka geometrical
105   acoustics) (Savioja & Svensson, 2015; Krokstad et al., 1968; Allen & Berkley, 1979; Funkhouser
106   et al., 2003; Hodgson & Nosal, 2006; Nosal et al., 2004). They require extensive prior knowledge of
107   the 3D space acoustic properties such as the audio source position (He et al., 2021), room geometry
      and furniture arrangement to derive RIR. Moreover, they are computationally expensive and the

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      whole computation needs to be resumed once either the source or receiver position gets changed. Our
109   proposed SPEAR circumvents the dependency on extensive prior knowledge and learns the spatial
110   acoustic effects in a data more readily accessible manner. The advent of deep neural networks has
111   inspired recent work (Ratnarajah et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2020; Ratnarajah et al., 2021b;a; De Sena
112   et al., 2015; Luo et al., 2022; Richard et al., 2022; Majumder et al., 2022; Ratnarajah et al., 2023;
113   2024) to focus on learning RIR with deep neural networks. While showing promising performance,
114   they still require massive RIR data or even crossdomain visual data (Ratnarajah et al., 2024) to train
115   their model, which in reality are difficult to collect. While all of those methods fall into source-to-
116   receiver estimation, SPEAR infers spatial acoustic effects from a receiver-to-receiver perspective
117
      which naturally brings several advantages over existing methods.
118   Neural Implicit Representation. Implicit representation learning has received lots of attention in
119   recent years, especially in computer vision community (Mildenhall et al., 2020; Hedman et al., 2021;
120   Xu et al., 2021; Su et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2021). They model static or dynamic visual scenes by
121   optimizing an implicit neural radiance field in order to render photo-realistic novel views. Some
122   recent work (e.g., NAF (Luo et al., 2022), FewShotRIR (Majumder et al., 2022)) propose to learn
123
      implicit neural acoustic fields from source-receiver pairs or audio-visual cues. SPEAR also learns a
      spatial continuous neural implicit representation for spatial acoustic effects prediction.
124
125   Audio Synthesis. Estimating the audio for at a novel position partially relates to audio synthesis (Oord
126   et al., 2016; Donahue et al., 2019; Zuiderveld et al., 2021; Richard et al., 2021; Engel et al., 2019; Tan
127   et al., 2012; Clarke et al., 2021; Prenger et al., 2019). WaveNet (Oord et al., 2016) learns to predict
128   future sound waveform based on previously heard sound waveform. WaveGAN (Donahue et al.,
129
      2019) and GANSynth (Engel et al., 2019) adopt generative adversarial network (GAN (Goodfellow
      et al., 2014)) to learn to generate audio. Our framework SPEAR differs from audio synthesis as it
130
      focuses on spatial acoustic effects modelling.
131
132   Time-series Prediction. Predicting warping field in either time or frequency domain partially relates
133   to time-series prediction. Existing deep neural network based time-series prediction methods can be
134   divided into four main categories: Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) based (Zheng et al., 2014;
135
      Yang et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2017; Foumani et al., 2021), Recurrent Neural Networks based (Dennis
      et al., 2019; Tang et al., 2016; Sutskever et al., 2014), Graph Neural Networks based (GNNs) (Covert
136
      et al., 2019; Jia et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022) and Transformer
137
      based (Song et al., 2018; Jin & Chen, 2021; Liu et al., 2021) methods. However, warping field
138   prediction in SPEAR exhibits no causality and it is directly predicted from receiver positions.

      3.3     LTI R ECEIVER - TO -R ECEIVER WARPING F IELD P HYSICAL P RINCIPLE
241
242   We present three room acoustics physical principles (Kuttruff, 1979; Rayleigh & Lindsay, 1945) that
243   will guide SPEAR design.
244
      Principle 1, Globality: Unlike a normal RGB image just captures a localized area, a receiver recorded
245   spatial audio relates to the whole 3D space. Originating from the source position, the spatial audio
246   propagates in a complex way that incorporates reflection, diffraction and absorption before reaching
247   to the receiver position, resulting in the interaction with almost the whole 3D space before reaching
248   to the receiver. Consequently, the final receiver recorded audio is influenced by the whole 3D space.
249
      Principle 2, Order Awareness: The Order Awareness principle states that SPEAR should account for
250
      the specific order of the input two receivers. In essence, the learned warping field varies when the
251
      order of the two receivers is swapped. This can be readily proved by Eqn. (5), since W1Ñ2 “ H   2 pf q

      Principle 3, Audio-Content Agnostic: This principle asserts that the receiver-to-receiver warping
255
      field is an inherent characteristic of the 3D space, affected by neither the presence or absence of audio
256   within the 3D space nor the specific class of audio.

335   4.2   DATA P REPARATION
336
      Ground Truth Warping Field Acquirement We generate the ground truth receiver-to-receiver
337
      warping field by dividing the target receiver audio by the reference receiver audio in frequency domain.
338
      However, this division operator inevitably leads to NaN value or abnormally large value (ą 100, when
339   the denominator is close to zero) in the obtained warping field (see the ground truth warping field
340   visualization in Fig. V), resulting in the difficulty of accurately warping field learning. To address
341   this issue, we make two adjustments. Firstly, we replace NaN values with zeros so that the whole
342   neural network is trainable without encountering NaN, which could significantly harm the learning.
343   Secondly, we clip all warping field values to lie within r´10, 10s. Our core intuition for adopting the
344   clip operation is that the abnormally large values (outlier) easily allure the whole neural network
345   to be trapped in predicting those abnormally large values, thus ignoring the learning of the warping
346   field with normal values (inliers). We empirically verify this in Fig. V that clip operation is able to
347
      remove the outliers in the warped target audio.
348   Data Sampling Strategy For all three datasets, we construct train and test datasets by first splitting
349   the receiver positions into two disjoint sets. The reference and target receiver positions are sampled
350   from the same receiver position set. Intuitively, this ensures that the model never sees a warping
351   field that warps audio from a receiver position in the training set to a position in the test set for fair
352   evaluation. More detailed descriptions of sampling strategies for each dataset are given as follows.
353   For the synthetic data generation, we arrange the receivers in an 80 ˆ 40 grid, with adjacent receivers
354   spaced 0.05 meters apart. To create the test set, we select test samples in such a way that no two
355   samples are adjacent in the grid. This interleaved sampling strategy ensures that each test receiver
356   position is at least 0.05 meters away from any receiver position in the training set. For the photo-
357   realistic data generation, due to the existence of furniture and other objects as obstacles in the room
358   scenes, we do not employ the grid-sampling strategy used in synthetic data generation. Instead, we
359
      randomly sample 4500 and 8500 receiver positions on the two scenes’ floors and select a subset of
      500 receiver positions from each scene as the test set. The test set sampling strategy for Real-World
360
      data is the same as the interleaved sampling strategy used in the synthetic data generation.

362   4.3   E VALUATION M ETRICS
363
      To quantitatively evaluate the warping field, we adopt: 1. MSE, mean square error between ground
364   truth and SPEAR predicted warping field in frequency domain (average between real and imaginary
365   part). 2. SDR (signal-to-distortion ratio). Following (Richard et al., 2022), we report SDR to assess
366   the fidelity of predicted warping field. 3. PSNR (Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (Wang et al., 2004a))
367   and 4. SSIM (structural similarity index measure (Wang et al., 2004b)) to quantify the quality of
368   the predicted warping field. We also introduce human-perceptual metric to provide insight into
369   human perceptual quality of the predicted warping field. Specifically, we choose five speeches from
370   VCTK (Yamagishi et al., 2019) dataset and warp them to the target position with the learned warping
371   field, then compute the 5. PESQ (perceptual evaluation of speech quality (Wang et al., 2022)) score
372
      for the warped speech and ground truth recorded speech.

529   5    C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION D ISCUSSIONS
530
531
      We introduce a novel receiver-to-receiver spatial acoustic effects prediction framework that can
532
      be trained in a data much more readily accessible way. We theoretically prove the existence and
533
      universality of such warping field if there is only one audio source. When there are multiple sources
534   distributed in the room scene, the warping field may not exist and we can apply source separation
535   method (Petermann et al., 2023) first to separate the sources. Moreover, once the audio source
536   position gets changed, the whole SPEAR needs to be retrained. There are two limitations that remain
537   to be resolved in the future. The first is that we still require dense sampling to get reasonably good
538   performance. Second, we assume all receivers’ position lies on the same horizontal plane. More
539   in-depth investigation is needed to remove this constraint and allow receiver placement in the whole
      3D space.

      In this section, we empirically show that the receiver-to-receiver neural warping field may not exist
831
      if more than one audio sources present in the 3D space. To this end, we depend on Pyroomacous-
832   tics Scheibler et al. (2018) simulator to simulate a shoe-box like 3D space of shape r5 ˆ 5 ˆ 5s meters.
833   Two audio sources are placed at coordinate A r1, 1, 1s meters and B r4, 4, 4s meters, respectively. Two
834   receivers are accordingly placed at coordinate r1, 1, 2s meters and r4, 4, 3s meters respectively. In the
835   fist simulation, we place Engine audio at source position A and Footstep audio at source position
836   B. In the second simulation, we just replace the Footstep audio at position B with Telephone
837   Ring. With the two receivers recorded audio, we depend on Eqn. (5) to calculate the warping field
838   from the reference receiver at r1, 1, 2s to the target receiver at r4, 4, 3s. The computed two warping
839   fields are shown Fig. I, we clearly see that the two warping fields are significantly different. We
840
      thus can conclude that the receiver-to-receiver neural warping field is no longer solely dependent on
      receiver positions (so the Audio-Content Agnostic principle does not apply). When more than audio
841
      sources present in the 3D space, the warping field proposed in this work may not exist.
