009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Medical time series (MedTS) play a critical role in many healthcare applications,
012            such as vital sign monitoring and the diagnosis of brain and heart diseases. However,
013            the existence of subject-specific features poses unique challenges in MedTS evalu-
014            ation. Inappropriate evaluation setups that either exploit or overlook these features
015            can lead to artificially inflated classification performance (by up to 50% in accuracy
016            on ADFTD dataset): this concern has received little attention in current research.
017            Here, we categorize the existing evaluation setups into two primary categories:
018
               subject-dependent and subject-independent. We show the subject-independent
               setup is more appropriate for different datasets and tasks. Our theoretical analysis
019
               explores the feature components of MedTS, examining how different evaluation se-
020
               tups influence the features that a model learns. Through experiments on six datasets
021            (spanning EEG, ECG, and fNIRS modalities) using four different methods, we
022            demonstrate step-by-step how subject-dependent utilizes subject-specific features
023            as a shortcut for classification and leads to a deceptive high performance, suggesting
024            that the subject-independent setup is more precise and practicable evaluation setup
025            in real-world. This comprehensive analysis aims to establish clearer guidelines for
026            evaluating MedTS models in different healthcare applications. Code to reproduce
027            this work in https://anonymous.4open.science/r/MedTS_Evaluation-733F.

085   Intuitively, researchers should use the subject-independent setup when evaluating MedTS datasets, as
086   it better mirrors real-world conditions where models must generalize to unseen patients. In practice,
087
      it is unrealistic and problematic for a model to be trained on data from the same subjects that appear
      in the test set, as this leads to overfitting on subject-specific features rather than learning patterns
088
      that generalize across different subjects. Consequently, the subject-independent setup provides a
089
      more rigorous and reliable measure of a model’s performance in medical time series classification.
090   However, many studies are still evaluating MedTS in subject-dependent setup, which is problematic.
091
092   In this paper, we empirically demonstrate the impact of the subject-dependent setup on learning
093
      subject-specific features and how these features influence model performance in Type-III MedTS
      disease diagnosis tasks. By randomly shuffling the ground truth labels while retaining the original
094
      subject IDs, we train models under both the subject-dependent and subject-independent setups. This
095
      procedure effectively removes disease-related features, ensuring that the model can only rely on
096   subject-specific features to classify labels. By observing the performance changes, we assess the
097   influence of subject-specific features on overall model performance.
098
099
      We conduct experiments on six MedTS datasets, including three EEG datasets, two ECG datasets, and
      one fNIRS dataset. We train four different models on these datasets to broadly verify our assumptions.
100
      The results reveal that, for certain datasets such as ADFTD and PTB, the model’s performance under
101
      the subject-dependent setup barely declined—from 97.56% to 97.12% and 99.90% to 99.69% in F1
102   score, respectively, using Medformer Wang et al. (2024c)—even after we completely removed the
103   disease-related features across subjects. These results strongly indicate that it is possible for a model
104   to learn virtually nothing about disease-related features while achieving deceptively high performance
105   by leveraging subject-specific features as a shortcut in the subject-dependent setup. Even increasing
106   the number of subjects could not entirely eliminate this effect. For instance, in the larger PTB-XL
107   dataset, the performance drops from 88.02% F1 score to 71.15% when disease-related features are
      removed under the subject-dependent setup using Medformer. Yet, this score remained much higher

      2     TAXONOMY OF M ED TS DATASETS AND E VALUATION S ETUPS
116
117   2.1   T YPES OF M EDICAL T IME S ERIES DATASET
118
      We categorize MedTS datasets into three types below based on the number of subjects and classes:
119
120   (1) Single-Subject and Multi-Class (Type-I). This type of MedTS is typically used to develop
121   models tailored to a specific subject. Examples include designing brain-computer interfaces for
122   individuals with disabilities Musk et al. (2019); Scherer et al. (2015) or building personalized health
123   monitoring systems (Levett et al., 2023) using wearable devices. In these scenarios, the model focuses
124
      on learning from the time-varying classes generated by a single subject’s data over time.
125   (2) Multi-Subject and Multi-Class (Type-II). The second type is used to design general models
126   that can adapt to multiple subjects with varying classes. For instance, mental state recognition
127   systems (Jafari et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2018) or sleep state
128   classification systems (Eldele et al., 2021; Mousavi et al., 2019) fall under this category. In this case,
129   the MedTS samples from different subjects are associated with dynamically changing classes over
130
      time, meaning no fixed class is assigned to each subject.
131   (3) Multi-Subject and Single-Class (Type-III). In the third type, each subject is assigned a single
132   class that remains consistent over time. For example, once a subject is diagnosed with Alzheimer’s
133   Disease during the data collection stage, this subject is always classified as an AD patient. This type
134   of MedTS is often used as a low-cost diagnostic method to supplement or replace more traditional
135   medical approaches Miltiadous et al. (2023b); van Dijk et al. (2022); Wagner et al. (2020). It is
136
      important to clarify that the term "single-class" here refers to cases where the medical or physiological
      state of a subject remains fixed over time (or within a short period without significant change). For
137
      example, a patient diagnosed with Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) will typically remain in that state for
138
      decades after the diagnosis. However, a subject may also have multiple coexisting medical conditions,
139   such as both Alzheimer’s and Parkinson’s Disease. This scenario represents a multi-label problem in
140   the medical domain (Zhou et al., 2023), which falls outside the scope of this work.

      2.2   M ED TS E VALUATION S ETUPS
143
144   We categorize the commonly used evaluation setups in existing MedTS classification studies into two
145   main types below (Figure 2). (Seal et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024b).
146
147
      (1) Subject-Dependent. Samples from different subjects are randomly shuffled and split into training,
      validation, and test sets, allowing the same subject’s data to appear in all three sets. Depending
148
      on whether a causal split is applied, the subject-dependent setup can be further divided into two
149
      subtypes: Mixed (random split): The splitting of samples from each subject does not account for
150   causal relationships; instead, samples are randomly mixed Dai et al. (2019); Sarkar et al. (2022);
151   Seal et al. (2021). This means that stratification occurs without regard for temporal order. Causal
152   (temporal split): The split considers the temporal relationship in the time series for each subject,
153   the past samples included in the training set, and future samples placed in the validation and test
154   sets. This causal setup is necessary for time series forecasting tasks Wu et al. (2021); Zhou et al.
155   (2021); Liu et al. (2024) but is rarely adopted in existing time series classification tasks. We discuss
156   the implications of the causal-subject-dependent setup in more detail in Section 4.3.
157   (2) Subject-Independent. The training, validation, and test sets are split by subjects, ensuring that
158   samples from the same subject ID are exclusively included only in one of the three sets. Subject-
159   independent setup can be further divided into two subtypes: Leave-One-Out: Leave only one
160   subject for testing, while all other subjects are used for training Zhang et al. (2023); Li et al. (2022);
161   Miltiadous et al. (2023a). Due to the limited sample size of a single subject, cross-validation is
      generally necessary to avoid biased evaluations. However, this setup does not include a dedicated

      This paper mainly discusses the Type-III MedTS dataset, characterized by multiple subjects with a
187
      single class assigned per subject, as categorized in section 2.1 and illustrated in Figure 1. For the
188   Type-II MedTS datasets and their evaluation setups, we briefly discuss them in the section 4.3 in
189   experiments later and leave a more detailed analysis to future works. We discussed Type-I datasets in
190   Appendix C but did not conduct experiments due to the lack of public datasets with sufficient data
191   from a single subject, which is necessary for Type-I experiments where the training set includes only
192   one subject and requires a large sample size.
193
      In this section, we first introduce our notations and assumptions for describing the feature components
194   of Type-III MedTS. Next, we analyze the subject-dependent and subject-independent setups in the
195   context of feature components utilized in the Type-III MedTS classification tasks. Finally, we propose
196   several new experimental setups based on these two setups to validate our assumptions systematically.
197
198   3.1   N OTATIONS AND A SSUMPTIONS OF T YPE -III M ED TS DATASET
199
200   Given a Type-III MedTS sample x ∈ RT ×C , where T denotes the number of timestamps and C
201   represents the number of channels, the sample is associated with a corresponding disease-related
202   label y ∈ RK . Here, K indicates the number of medically relevant classes, such as different disease
203
      types. Each sample is also assigned to a subject ID z ∈ R, identifying the subject to which it belongs.
204   In time series forecasting tasks, researchers commonly decompose data into trend and seasonal
205   feature components and design mechanisms to effectively leverage these components for improved
206   representation learning Wu et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2024a); Fraikin et al. (2023). Similarly, Type-III
207   MedTS data also have several distinct feature components. We assume that a Type-III MedTS sample
208   x consists of three feature components:
209
                                                    x =xd + xs + xo                                                (1)
210
              d                                               s                                                o
211   where x represents disease-related features, x denotes subject-specific features, and x includes
212
      all other features, such as noise, artifacts, and all task-irrevalent features. In Type-III MedTS
      learning for disease diagnosis tasks, our goal is to train a model fθ : x → y that accurately maps a
213
      given sample x to its corresponding disease-related label y.
214
215   However, in the context of disease diagnosis using Type-III MedTS, the choice between subject-
      dependent and subject-independent setups can result in significant disparities in model perfor-


                                                                  4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      mance Nath et al. (2020); Seal et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2024b). We aim to learn a model using
217   disease-related features xd to achieve accurate classification. Ideally, the model fθ should rely solely
218   on xd and be entirely independent of subject-specific features xs when predicting the label y.
219
220
      Given the unique nature of Type-III MedTS data, where samples with the same subject ID z always
      share the same label y, but samples with the same label y do not necessarily share the same
221
      subject ID z, there is a risk that the model fθ might exploit subject-specific features xs as a shortcut
222
      for predicting y, rather than learning the disease-related features xd . Although some existing studies
223   have attempted to address this issue and propose methods to tackle it, effectively decomposing xd and
224   xs from x remains an open challenge Zhang et al. (2020); Yang et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2024c).
225
226
      To summarize, we assume that subject-specific features xs exist in each sample x within the
      Type-III MedTS dataset, and it is possible for the trained model fθ to utilize xs as a shortcut
227
      for classifying the label y under the subject-dependent setup. This could result in deceptively
228
      high performance while the model learns little to nothing about the disease-related features xd .

      3.2   F EATURE C OMPONENTS U TILIZED IN S UBJECT-D EPENDENT VS I NDEPENDENT
231
232   We analyze the differences of two setups from the perspective of feature components used when
233   classifying a Type-III MedTS sample x. Their feature components utilization are presented in table 1.
234
      a) Subject-Dependent. The subject-dependent setup disregards the subject ID, treating the Type-III
235
      MedTS classification problem similarly to general time series classification. Samples from different
236
      subjects within the dataset are shuffled, mixed, and randomly divided into training, validation, and
237   test sets. Samples sharing the same subject ID can be present inclusively in these three sets.
238
239   In this scenario, for a given sample x in the validation/test set, the trained model fθ could leverage
240
      both its disease-related features xd and subject-specific features xs to predict the label y. This
      happens because the model fθ gains access to disease-related features by learning from training
241
      samples with the same label y and simultaneously acquires subject-specific features by learning from
242
      training samples with the same subject ID z. Consequently, this setup can lead to an overestimation
243   of the model’s performance, as it can exploit subject-specific information rather than generalizing
244   based on disease-related features alone.
245
246
      b) Subject-Independent. The subject-independent setup considers the effect of subject-specific
      features and closely simulates real-world applications. In this setup, the training, validation, and
247
      test sets are split based on subjects rather than individual samples, ensuring that all samples from a
248
      particular subject (i.e., with the same subject ID) are exclusively included in one of the three sets.
249
250   In this scenario, for any given sample x in the validation/test set, the trained model fθ can only
251   leverage the disease-related features xd to predict the label y, as no samples with the same subject ID
252
      z are present in the training set. This setup aligns with real-world applications of disease diagnosis
      using Type-III MedTS, where a label is assigned to an entire subject, and it’s impractical to have
253
      access to labeled samples from that subject in advance during training.
254
255   However, this setup often faces several challenges and can sometimes exhibit poor performance.
256   Firstly, while the subject independent setup prevents model fθ from exploiting subject-specific
257   features xs as a shortcut for classification during validation and testing, it remains possible for the
258
      model to rely on subject-specific features during the training stage, impeding its ability to learn
      task-related subject-invariant features. Secondly, from a domain generalization perspective, different
259
      subjects can be considered distinct domains, making it challenging to efficiently extract disease-
260
      related features that generalize well across unseen domains (i.e., subjects in the validation/test sets).
261   Currently, there are no effective methods to decompose subject-specific and disease-related features
262   during the training stage fully. This remains an open challenge in the field.

268   two additional setups—random-label subject-dependent and random-label subject-independent—to
269   manually remove the disease-related features from the dataset. These setups allow us to validate our
      assumption that models trained under the subject-dependent setup heavily rely on subject-specific

281   a) Subject-Discrimination. The disease label y of a subject in a MedTS dataset is assigned by
282
      medical experts, ensuring that disease-related features xd are present in the MedTS samples for that
283   subject. However, there is no guarantee that subject-specific features xs exist, although researchers
284   usually assume they exist. To investigate the presence of subject-specific features xs for samples
285   in each subject, we design this setup to prove the existence of subject-specific features by trying to
286   classify the subject ID z of samples.
287
      In this experiment, we randomly shuffle, mix, and split all samples into training, validation, and test
288
      sets. We then train a model fθ to predict the subject ID z of a given sample x. Since the model is not
289   provided with the disease label y during training, and given that the number of subjects is typically
290   much larger than the number of disease labels, the model cannot rely on disease-related features xd
291   as a shortcut for subject ID classification. Therefore, if the model performs well in classifying the
292   subject ID z, it strongly indicates the existence of subject-specific features xs within the dataset.
293
      b) Random-Label Subject-Dependent. Previous research has consistently shown that models eval-
294
      uated under the subject-dependent setup generally achieve much higher performance compared to the
295   subject-independent setup Nath et al. (2020); Arif et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024c). While it is widely
296   assumed that the model under the subject-dependent setup exploits subject-specific features xs as a
297   shortcut for classifying the label y, the extent of the model’s reliance on xs and whether it still learns
298   disease-related features xd remains unclear. To investigate the clear influence of subject-specific
299   features xs on model performance, we introduce the random-label subject-dependent setup.
300
      This setup differs slightly from the original subject-dependent setup. We retain the subject ID but
301   randomly shuffle the label of each subject before splitting the samples into training, validation, and
302   test sets. This operation disrupts the relationship between disease-related features xd and the label y,
303   effectively preventing the model fθ from leveraging xd for prediction.
304
      As a result, in the random-label subject-dependent setup, the trained model fθ can rely solely on
305
      subject-specific features xs to predict the label y for any given sample x. By comparing the
306
      performance of the random-label subject-dependent setup with the original subject-dependent
307   setup, we can directly assess the extent to which the model relies on subject-specific features xs
308   as a shortcut and evaluate how much it learns disease-related features xd .
309
310
      c) Random-Label Subject-Independent. Similar to the random-label subject-dependent setup, we
      apply the same process in the subject-independent setup, where we randomly shuffle the label of each
311
      subject while retaining their subject IDs before splitting the data into training, validation, and test
312
      sets. This ensures that the model fθ cannot learn any disease-related features xd .
313
314   The random-label subject-independent setup serves as a control for the random-label subject-
315   dependent setup, enabling us to isolate the potential impact of other features xo . In this setup,
316
      the model is unable to utilize either disease-related features xd or subject-specific features xs to clas-
      sify the label y; instead, it can only rely on other features xo . In other words, if the other features
317
      xo are independent of the label y, the performance under the random-label subject-independent
318
      setup should be completely random.

394   shuffle, mix, and split all samples into training, validation, and test sets, allowing samples with the

400
      shortcuts for subject ID z classification, since there is typically a one-to-many relationship between
      disease label y and subject ID z. In rare cases where a dataset of two subjects has opposite labels,
401
      the model fθ might theoretically use disease-related features xd as a shortcut to classify the subject
402
      ID z. However, this scenario does not apply to the datasets used in this paper, where the number of
403   subjects far exceeds the number of labels.

      Setup. The random-label subject-dependent setup builds upon the subject-dependent setup with
433   an additional step. Before shuffling and splitting all samples into training, validation, and test sets,
434   we first randomly assign a new label to each subject. For instance, in the ADFTD dataset, which
435   has three classes (0, 1, and 2) representing different medical conditions, we randomly assign each
436   subject a new label from these three classes, replacing their true label. The purpose of this setup
437   is to deliberately mask the disease-related features in the dataset, ensuring that the trained model
438   fθ theoretically cannot learn any disease-related features during training. As a result, the model
439   can only rely on subject-specific features to classify the label y of a given sample x during the
440   validation and test stages. The performance drop observed in this setup compared to the original
441
      subject-dependent setup will indicate the extent to which the model learned disease-related features
      in the raw subject-dependent setup.
442
443   Results. The results of the random-label subject-dependent setup are presented in Table 5. For
444   comparison, we also include the results from the original subject-dependent and subject-discrimination
445   setups. The findings are both intriguing and surprising. The best results among the four methods in
446   the random-label subject-dependent setup are 97.12%, 89.71%, 99.83%, and 72.54% for the ADFTD,
447
      TDBrain, PTB, and PTB-XL datasets, respectively, which are unexpectedly high.
448   Considering that there are no disease-related features available under this setup, the model’s perfor-
449   mance should, in theory, be completely random if it relies solely on disease-related features xd to
450   classify the label y. However, taking the Medformer results as an example, the performance drop
451   from the subject-dependent to the random-label subject-dependent setup is almost negligible for both
452   ADFTD and PTB. This indicates that the model learns almost nothing about disease-related features
453
      xd during training in the subject-dependent setup and instead relies entirely on subject-specific
      features xs as a shortcut for label y classification. These findings are consistent with the results
454
      from the subject-discrimination setup, where ADFTD and PTB also achieved high classification
455
      performance, demonstrating their strong subject-specific features. For the TDBrain and PTB-XL
456   datasets, which show relatively weaker subject-specific features in the subject-discrimination setup,
457   the performance drop from subject-dependent to random-label subject-dependent using Medformer is
458   approximately 10% and 17% F1 score, respectively. This indicates that the model does, in fact, learn
459   some disease-related features xd in the subject-dependent setup for these two datasets.
460
      In summary, these results suggest that, depending on the dataset, it is possible for the model fθ to
461
      achieve a very high F1 score (over 95%) under the subject-dependent setup while learning little to
462   nothing about the meaningful disease-related features xd in the dataset. One might assume that
463   increasing the number of subjects could help the model focus more on general disease-related features
464   xd rather than relying on subject-specific features xs as shortcuts. However, even with the large
465   PTB-XL dataset containing 17,596 subjects, subject-specific features still had a significant impact
466   on performance. For instance, Medformer’s performance in the random-label subject-dependent
467   setup reached an F1 score of 71.15%, which easily surpassed the 59.97% F1 score achieved in
468   the subject-independent setup. Therefore, we strongly recommend that researchers avoid using the
469   subject-dependent setup for disease diagnosis tasks when working with Type-III MedTS datasets.
470
      Instead, adopting the subject-independent setup provides a more realistic evaluation that aligns with
      real-world applications.

515   5     D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
516
517   Limitations and Future Works. While this paper conducts thorough experiments and provides
518   valuable insights into evaluating models on Type-III (multi-subject, single-class) MedTS datasets, we
519
      are aware of several limitations. (1) Our experiments were primarily focused on Type-III datasets,
      and we only conducted preliminary experiments on Type-II (multi-subject, multi-class) datasets, and
520
      discussed Type-I (single-subject, multi-class) datasets in Appendix C. Given the distinct nature of
521
      these dataset types, future work should explore the behavior of models on Type-I and Type-II MedTS
522   datasets to assess whether the same trends observed here, particularly the reliance on subject-specific
523   information, hold across more personalized or multi-class scenarios. (2) While we have shown
524   that subject-dependent setups can yield misleadingly high performance and advised researchers
525   to avoid them, developing a robust model for subject-independent setups remains a significant
526   challenge. Ensuring models can generalize effectively across different subjects without relying on
527   subject-specific information is a complex task that requires further research and innovation.
528   Conclusion. We introduce a new taxonomy for MedTS based on datasets and subject dependency.
529   Additionally, we propose a decomposition paradigm for MedTS data, consisting of disease-related
530   features xd , subject-specific features xs , and extraneous features xo . To investigate the influence of
531   subject-specific features, we design three new experimental setups, namely subject-discrimination,
532   random-label subject-dependent, and random-label subject-independent, to demonstrate their exis-
533
      tence and quantify their impact on model performance. Our experiments reveal that it is possible for
      a model to learn little to nothing about disease-related features xd while achieving deceptively high
534
      performance under the subject-dependent setup. These findings suggest that the subject-independent
535
      setup is a more practical setup for MedTS evaluation.

706   The TDBrain dataset1 , referenced in the paper van Dijk et al. (2022), is a large permission-accessible
707   EEG time series dataset recording brain activities of 1274 subjects with 33 channels. Each subject has
708   two trials: one under eye open and one under eye closed setup. The dataset includes a total of 60 labels,
709   with each subject potentially having multiple labels indicating multiple diseases simultaneously. In
710   this paper, we utilize a subset of this dataset containing 25 subjects with Parkinson’s disease and 25
711   healthy controls, all under the eye-closed task condition. Each eye-closed trial is segmented into
712
      non-overlapping 1-second samples with 256 timestamps, and any samples shorter than 1-second are
      discarded. This process results in 6,240 samples. Each sample is assigned a subject ID to indicate its
713
      originating subject. For the training, validation, and test set splits, we employ the subject-independent
714
      setup. Samples with subject IDs {18,19,20,21,46,47,48,49} are assigned to the validation set, while
715   samples with subject IDs {22,23,24,25,50,51,52,53} are assigned to the test set. The remaining
716   samples are allocated to the training set.

718   A.2    ADFTD P REPROCESSING
719
720   The Alzheimer’s Disease and Frontotemporal Dementia (ADFTD) dataset2 , referenced in the pa-
721   pers Miltiadous et al. (2023b;a), is a public EEG time series dataset with 3 classes, including 36
722   Alzheimer’s disease (AD) patients, 23 Frontotemporal Dementia (FD) patients, and 29 healthy control
723   (HC) subjects. The dataset has 19 channels, and the raw sampling rate is 500Hz. Each subject has
724
      a trial, with trial durations of approximately 13.5 minutes for AD subjects (min=5.1, max=21.3),
      12 minutes for FTD subjects (min=7.9, max=16.9), and 13.8 minutes for HC subjects (min=12.5,
725
      max=16.5). A bandpass filter between 0.5-45Hz is applied to each trial. We downsample each trial to
726
      256Hz and segment them into non-overlapping 1-second samples with 256 timestamps, discarding
727   any samples shorter than 1 second. This process results in 69,752 samples. For the training, validation,
728   and test set splits, we employ both the subject-dependent and subject-independent setups. For the
729   subject-dependent setup, we allocate 60%, 20%, and 20% of total samples into the training, validation,
730   and test sets, respectively. For the subject-independent setup, we allocate 60%, 20%, and 20% of total
731   subjects with their corresponding samples into the training, validation, and test sets, respectively.

733   A.3    PTB P REPROCESSING
734
735   The PTB dataset3 , referenced in the paper PhysioBank (2000), is a public ECG time series recording
736   from 290 subjects, with 15 channels and a total of 8 labels representing 7 heart diseases and 1
737
      health control. The raw sampling rate is 1000Hz. For this paper, we utilize a subset of 198 subjects,
      including patients with Myocardial infarction and healthy control subjects. We first downsample the
738
      sampling frequency to 250Hz and normalize the ECG signals using standard scalers. Subsequently,
739
      we process the data into single heartbeats through several steps. We identify the R-Peak intervals
740   across all channels and remove any outliers. Each heartbeat is then sampled from its R-Peak position,
741   and we ensure all samples have the same length by applying zero padding to shorter samples, with
742   the maximum duration across all channels serving as the reference. This process results in 64,356
743   samples. For the training, validation, and test set splits, we employ the subject-independent setup.
744   Specifically, we allocate 60%, 20%, and 20% of the total subjects, along with their corresponding
745   samples, into the training, validation, and test sets, respectively.

      A PPENDIX C             D ISCUSSION ON T YPE -I M ED TS
811
812
      In certain MedTS classification tasks, such as personalized health monitoring or brain-computer
813   interface (BCI) systems, where the goal is to train a model that can efficiently control a device (e.g., a
814   wheelchair) for a specific individual, the subject-dependent setup becomes reasonable. In these cases
815   (Type-I MedTS datasets) the training, validation, and test sets are derived from the same subject.
816   This setup aligns with the task’s objective of personalizing the model to perform optimally for a
817   single subject, where cross-subject generalization is not the primary focus. We also emphasize the
818   importance of maintaining temporal order in such classification tasks. The evaluation of such Type-I
819   cases is an important future direction.

      Setup. Similar to the random-label subject-dependent setup, we apply the same process in the subject-
840   independent setup, where we randomly assign a new label to each subject while retaining their subject
