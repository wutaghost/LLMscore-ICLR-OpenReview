008                                                     A BSTRACT
009
010             Deep learning, and more specifically denoising models, have significantly im-
011
                proved graph generative modeling. However, challenges remain in capturing
                global graph properties from local interactions, ensuring scalability, and main-
012
                taining node permutation equivariance. While existing equivariant models ad-
013
                dress node permutation issues, they struggle with scalability, often requiring dense
014             graph representations that scale with O(n2 ).
015
                To overcome these challenges, we introduce a novel coarsening-lifting method
016             that generates sparse spanning supergraphs, preserving global graph properties.
017             These supergraphs serve as both conditioning structures and sparse message-
018             passing layouts for generative models. Leveraging this method with discrete diffu-
019             sion, we model graphs hierarchically, enabling efficient generation of large graphs.
020             Our approach, to the best of our knowledge, is the first hierarchical equivariant
021             generative model for graphs. We demonstrate its performance introducing new
022             evaluation datasets with larger graphs and more instances than traditional bench-
023             marks.

      Models equivariant to node permutations (referred to concisely as equivariant models) offer an ele-
055   gant solution to the issue of multiple representations by ensuring a unique computational graph for
056   all possible instantiations of the same graph. Empirical evidence suggests that equivariant models
057   better capture the distribution of small graphs compared to their non-equivariant counterparts (Jo
058   et al., 2022). However, in the absence of prior information and generation order, equivariant models
059   need to consider all node pairs in parallel, which exacerbates issues related to capturing global graph
060   properties and scalability. For that reason, most equivariant models use a dense graph representation
061   (Haefeli et al., 2022; Vignac et al., 2023; Jo et al., 2022; Boget et al., 2024). A few equivariant
062   models (Qin et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023) adopt a sparse representation by focusing on a sub-
063
      set of possible node pairs at any given time; however, they still ultimately need to account for all
      node pairs. Unlike some models for molecule generation that construct molecular graphs based on
064
      3D atomic coordinates (Hoogeboom et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023), our work focuses on the task of
065
      general graph generation.
066
067   We propose a method to address these two issues while preserving equivariance. Our approach gen-
068   erates coarse graphs, which are expanded into spanning supergraphs that capture spectral properties
069
      of the graph and provide a sparse conditioning structure for message-passing at the finer level. We
      then leverage equivariant discrete diffusion to model each graph level hierarchically.
070
071   Our method enables the efficient generation of large graphs, motivating us to introduce new eval-
072   uation datasets with larger graphs and more instances than those in traditional benchmarks. We
073   demonstrate our approach using discrete diffusion, presenting, to the best of our knowledge, the first
074   hierarchical equivariant generative model for graphs.
075   We summarize our contributions as follows:
076
077          • We propose a novel graph coarsening method to produce minimal spanning supergraphs
078            that serve as conditioning and as sparse structures for generation.
079          • We prove that our coarsening method not only maximizes the sparsity of the conditioned
080            structure but also provides theoretical guarantees for preserving important spectral proper-
081            ties given by the graph spectrum.
082
             • We leverage this method to introduce a hierarchical model based on discrete diffusion,
083
               which scales to large graphs with over than a thousand nodes.
084
085          • By preserving equivariance in our coarsening and generative framework, we introduce, to
086            the best of our knowledge, the first graph generative approach that is both hierarchical and
087
               equivariant.
088          • Our method inherently provides a conditional generative model for structure-guided graph
089            generation, enabling effective spectrum-conditioned generation.
090
             • We introduce new datasets with more instances and larger graphs, enabling improved eval-
091            uation of graph generative models.

      2.1   M ETHOD OVERVIEW
109
110   We present a hierarchical graph generative method that progressively generates finer graphs from
111   coarser ones through a series of conditional generation steps. The conditional generation steps
112   are trained, and operate, on the outputs of a sequence of coarsening and lifting operations. These
113   operations, applied as a preprocessing step, transform an input graph to ever coarser versions of it.
114   Specifically, for each original input graph in a dataset, we create a sequence of graph representations
115   {G 0 , ...G L } from the finest level G 0 , representing the original input graph to the coarsest level G L .
116   Additionally, we create a corresponding set of lifted graph representations {H0 , ...HL−1 }, where
117   each Hl = L IFT(G l+1 ) ̸= G l . We highlight two key properties of these hierarchical representations.
118   First, the lifted graph Hl is a spanning supergraph of its corresponding G l ; and second, unlike the
119   coarsening function, our L IFT function is invertible.
120   Given that the coarsening process is Markovian and the lifting function is bijective, we model the
121   graph distribution autoregressively over the coarsening levels:

130   2.2   M OTIVATIONS
131
      Our hierarchical generative model begins at the coarsest level, generating a coarse graph that cap-
132
      tures essential high-level spectral properties. The lifted graph, which retains these spectral prop-
133   erties, is then used as a conditioning structure to generate a finer graph enriched with the spectral
134   properties of the lower levels. By iterating this process, we generate progressively finer represen-
135   tations, ultimately capturing the original graph distribution. Instead of generating the entire graph
136   in a single step, our approach incrementally captures both graph structure and spectral information
137   through a sequence of conditional generative steps.
138
      We argue that each of these conditional steps represents a simpler task. Indeed, we leverage the
139   sparsity of the conditioning graph Hl , which serves both as a conditional space and as a message-
140   passing scheme to generate the graph G l . This sparsity provides several advantages over dense,
141   unconditional models:
142
143         1. By leveraging sparsity, our method enables efficient training on larger graphs. As shown
144            by Qin et al. (2024), generative models for graphs require substantial computational power
145            and parameters for edge representations. Since dense models’ complexity grows with
146
               O(n2 ), large graphs cause memory issues during training, slow training, and slow gen-
               eration speeds. Moreover, sparsity significantly improves training and generation speed
147
               compared to similar dense models, even when applied to relatively small graphs.
148
149         2. In contrast to dense models, which predict edge presence or absence for every node pair,
150
               our model only needs to predict edges within the conditioning graph H. Consequently,
               by construction, our approach sets a high proportion (1 − γH ) of node pairs as non-edges,
151
               preventing the risk of error on those, and focusing the model capacity on the remaining
152
               edges.
153
            3. As a result of message-passing, in our model each node aggregates information only from
154
               its adjacent nodes in H. In contrast, in dense models, each node aggregates information
155
               from every other node in the graph into a fixed-length vector. In large graphs, this process
156            can lead to oversquashing (Topping et al., 2022; Akansha, 2024), limiting their ability to
157            model large graph distributions.
158
159   While several hierarchical graph models exist (Jin et al., 2018; Karami, 2024; Bergmeister et al.,
160   2024), none of them preserve equivariance, which, as empirical evidence suggests (Jo et al., 2022),
161   is a crucial property in graph modeling. In contrast, our model preserves equivariance and represents,
      to the best of our knowledge, the first approach that is both hierarchical and equivariant.

      2.3     R ELATED WORK
163
164   We categorize graph generative models into two classes: models that operate sequentially, and equiv-
165   ariant models. Sequential models generate graphs by auto-regressively adding nodes, edges, or sub-
166   graphs. (You et al., 2018; Liao et al., 2019; Kong et al., 2023). Equivariant models address the node
167   permutation issue by ensuring a unique computational graph for every possible instantiation of the
168   same object. These models have been developed within various generative frameworks (Martinkus
169   et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2019). Recently, equivariant denoising models (Jo et al., 2022; Haefeli et al.,
170   2022; Vignac et al., 2023; Jo et al., 2024; Eijkelboom et al., 2024) and equivariant quantized auto-
171
      encoders (Boget et al., 2024; Nguyen et al., 2024) have significantly improved graph generation for
      small graphs. Similar to the work we present here, SparseDiff (Qin et al., 2024) and EDGE Chen
172
      et al. (2023) tackle the scalability challenges faced by equivariant models. A detailed comparison
173
      of these frameworks with ours is provided in the Appendix D.1. Recent works (Karami, 2024;
174   Bergmeister et al., 2024) adopt hierarchical approaches. However, none is equivariant. A more
175   comprehensive review of related work is presented in the Appendix.D.1.

181   we use in a preprocessing step, to create the graph representations G and H at different coarseness
182   levels. Our coarsening algorithm brings important advantages in the context of our hierarchical
183   method: It maximizes the sparsity of the spanning supergraph, maintains equivariance, and pre-
184
      serves the graph’s spectral properties. We first present relevant graph coarsening work, and motivate
      the need for a new coarsening method. We then introduce our coarsening algorithm, and derive key
185
      properties related to equivariance and spectrum preservation. We validate our method experimen-
186
      tally.

189   3.1     G RAPH C OARSENING : R ELATED W ORKS
190
      Graph coarsening methods can be broadly categorized into three main classes: node-dropping, con-
191
      traction, and clustering methods. Node-dropping methods (Gao & Ji, 2019; Lee et al., 2019) coarsen
192
      a graph by removing specific nodes and their associated edges, either one-by-one or by blocks. Con-
193   traction methods (Loukas, 2019; Diehl, 2019) coarsen graphs by merging nodes through either edge
194   contraction, where the endpoints of an edge are merged, or neighborhood contraction, where all
195   adjacent nodes of a given node are merged. However, both methods fail to maintain invariance and
196   equivariance, either during the coarsening process or in their potential reverse lifting scheme (see
197   Appendix C.1 for a detailed discussion).
198
      Clustering methods (Ying et al., 2018; Bianchi et al., 2020) coarsen graphs by merging nodes within
199   the same cluster. They therefore depend on a clustering algorithm. Most clustering-based coars-
200   ening methods are permutation-invariant, and they typically focus on community detection-based
201   clustering. In contrast, our approach aims to maximize sparsity. Although our method falls within
202   this category, it differs in its clustering objective. For complete reviews on graph coarsening, we
203   refer to Grattarola et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024).
204

327

      4.1       BACKGROUND
334
335   Discrete diffusion (Austin et al., 2021) consists of a forward process that progressively adds discrete
336   noise to an instance until it reaches a known limit distribution and a backward process where a
337   model is trained to iteratively denoise the instances. In our method, both processes operate at each
338   coarseness level independently. At the coarsest level, HL is set as KnL , making the conditional
339   formulation equivalent to the unconditional one1 : pθL (G|H = KnL ) = pθL (G). All levels sharing
340   the same formulation, we simplify notation by omitting the superscript indicating the level.
341   The key idea is to create an edge attribute matrix E ∈ Rm×de , representing the edge attributes
342   of H, to encode the edge attributes of G. We make this possible thanks to the fact that H is a
343   spanning supergraph G (Proposition 1), that H is equivariant to G (Proposition 3), and that H is an
344   unattributed graph. To encode G into E, we treat the absence of an edge as a distinct edge type.
345   Specifically, each row vector Ei is a one-hot encoding of the edge attribute in G, with one category
346   representing the absence of an edge. The correspondence between edges and their attributes is
347
      maintained via the edge index matrix J ∈ N2×m . Similarly, thanks to the fact that H is unattributed,
      the annotation matrix X represents the node attributes of G. Since both graphs share the same set
348
      of nodes VG = EH , there is no need to encode the absence of a node.

403   4.5   P ROPERTIES
404
405   Complexity By conditioning on H, we reduce the computational complexity from O(n2 ) (stan-
406   dard discrete diffusion) to O(mH ), linear in the number of edges of H.
407
408   Equivariance Since we apply the noise independently to each node and edge, and the condi-
409   tioning spanning supergraph is equivariant to node permutations, our denoising model inherits this
410   equivariance.
411
412   Additional Graph Features Enhancing GNN inputs by computing additional synthetic features,
413   including spectral embeddings, has become a widespread practice (Martinkus et al., 2022; Vignac
414   et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2024; Bergmeister et al., 2024; Boget et al., 2024). However, denoising
415   models usually need to recompute these features before each forward pass, both during training
416   generation, which is computationally expensive. Instead, we use the spectral embeddings of the
417   spanning supergraph, the graph size and the cluster size as extra feature, which we need to compute
418   only once during preprocessing. We present the detailed extra features scheme in appendix B.4.
419
420   4.6   C ONDITIONAL G ENERATION
421
422   Conditional generation is an important feature for generative models, as most real-world applica-
423   tions require some form of guidance during the generation process. Notably, our model inherently
424   operates as a structure-conditioning model. By fixing the conditioning graph H, our model restricts
425   the generated graphs to those satisfying G = H\ES . We are not aware of models proposing a similar
426   structural conditioning. We demonstrate in Section 5 the effectiveness of our conditional generative
427   model in guiding the graph spectrum during generation.
428   A more common conditional generation setting consists of generating molecules with specific global
429   properties, represented as a vector c. By concatenating this conditioning vector to each node at-
430   tribute input vector, we obtain a property-based conditional generative model. Furthermore, by
431   training both conditional and unconditional models, we would enable classifier-free guidance (Ho
      & Salimans, 2022). However, this feature has not yet been implemented in our current work.

      6     C ONCLUSION
525
      We demonstrate that our method, leveraging discrete diffusion, enables fast and scalable graph gen-
526
      eration. Our approach more effectively captures large graph distributions by extracting global struc-
527
      tural features and learning them hierarchically. In addition, it generates graphs significantly faster
528   than other methods and reduces training time. At this stage, we do not foresee any significant ethical
529   concerns related to our model.
530
531
      We acknowledge that our methods requires training on multiple graph levels, which, despite the
      method’s robustness to hyperparameter changes, expands the hyperparameter space and makes fine-
532
      tuning potentially challenging. However, we emphasize that this multi-level approach offers several
533
      benefits, including: accelerating the overall training and generation process, enabling generation
534   on large graphs, and significantly improving generative performance compared to the few available
535   baseline methods.
536
537
      Orthogonal to improvements regarding the generative framework itself, our hierarchical method
      highlights the importance of model architecture in graph generation. Hierarchical equivariant mod-
538
      els hold great potential for advancing graph generation and scaling it to even larger graphs.

       B.1.1    I NTUITIVE INTERPRETATION
1003
1004   We present a hierarchical graph generative method that progressively generates finer graphs from
1005   coarser ones through a series of conditional generation steps. The conditional generation steps are
1006   trained and operate on the outputs of a sequence of coarsening and lifting operations, which, in a
1007   preprocessing step, transform an input graph to coarser versions of it.
1008   We will now describe the sequence of coarsening and lifting operations. Coarseness level l = 0
1009   represents the finer level, G 0 which is the original input graph, and level l = L the coarsest graph
1010   level. At level l, we coarsen the graph G l to produce the graph G l+1 at level l + 1. We then lift the
1011   coarse graph G l+1 producing the graph Hl , which is a spanning supergraph of G l , i.e., Hl = G l + S,
1012   where S is a set of additional edges. Thus, at each level l, we obtain a pair of graphs (G l , Hl ). At
1013   the coarsest level L, we use the complete graph K, i.e., the graph with edges on all node pairs, as
1014   the spanning supergraph. We present a pseudo-code algorithm in Algorithm 1.
1015   We create a coarse graph by learning node partitions and merging nodes from the same partition
1016   into a single parent node. Thus, the number of partitions K in a graph at level l corresponds to the
1017   number of nodes nl+1 in the coarsened graph at level l + 1. Coarse nodes are connected if at least
1018   one pair of their corresponding child nodes is connected. Each coarse node is annotated with the
1019   number of child nodes it contains. In the reverse process, we lift a coarse graph to a finer graph by
1020   expanding each coarse node into a clique, with the number of nodes matching the annotation of the
1021   coarse node. If two coarse nodes are connected, a biclique is established between their constituent
1022
       nodes. Note that lifting a graph does not invert the coarsening operation, i.e., it does not recover the
       graph before coarsening; instead, the process yields a spanning supergraph of it. Figure 2 illustrates
1023
       a coarsening-lifting procedure step.

1237   C.1     G RAPH C OARSENING
1238
       We further explain why node-dropping methods and contraction methods are not suitable choices
1239
       for hierarchical generative models.
1240
1241   Node-dropping methods (Gao & Ji, 2019; Lee et al., 2019) coarsen a graph by removing specific
       nodes and their associated edges , either one-by-one or by blocks. However, node-dropping induces


                                                         23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       a node ordering, which breaks equivariance. Furthermore, reversing the process typically leads to
1243   node-wise or block-wise autoregressive models, similar to existing approaches, such as Liao et al.
1244   (2019).
1245
1246
       Contraction methods (Loukas, 2019; Diehl, 2019) coarsen graphs by merging nodes through either
       edge contraction, where the endpoints of an edge are merged, or neighborhood contraction, where
1247
       all adjacent nodes of a given node are merged. However, these methods require a specific contraction
1248
       ordering, which breaks equivariance, to ensure that no node belongs to multiple contracted clusters.
1249   Additionally, contraction methods offer limited control over the coarsening factor, i.e., the ratio of
1250   nodes before and after coarsening.

1254
1255   D.1   G ENERATIVE MODELS
1256
       One of the main challenges in graph representation and generation is that a graph can be represented
1257
       in multiple ways. There can be up to n! different representations of the same graph, resulting from
1258   the n! possible node permutations. Two main approaches address the multiplicity of equivalent
1259   representations: models that operate sequentially and equivariant models.
1260
1261   Sequential models generate graphs by auto-regressively adding nodes, edges, or subgraphs. To
1262   limit the number of different sequences that represent a single graph, most of these models use a
1263   Breadth-First Search (BFS) approach (You et al., 2018; Shi et al., 2020; Luo et al., 2021; Liao et al.,
1264   2019; Kong et al., 2023). While canonical representations exist for specific domains, e.g. canon-
1265   ical SMILES for molecular graphs (Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018; Kusner et al., 2017), methods
1266   based on general graph canonization (Goyal et al., 2020) fail for large graphs (see experiments in
1267   Bergmeister et al. (2024)). Subgraph aggregation (Jin et al., 2018; 2020a), sometimes described
1268   as hierarchical, falls into this category. It requires listing the set of all possible substructures and
1269
       connections between them, which is feasible only for some specific applications such as molecular
       graphs.
1270
1271
1272
       Equivariant models address the node permutation issue by ensuring a unique computational
       graph for all possible instantiations of the same object. These models have been developed within
1273
       various generative frameworks such as GANs (Krawczuk et al., 2021; Martinkus et al., 2022) or Nor-
1274
       malizing Flows (Madhawa et al., 2019; Zang & Wang, 2020; Liu et al., 2019). Recently, equivariant
1275   denoising models used in score-based diffusion (Yang et al., 2019; Jo et al., 2022), discrete diffusion
1276   (Haefeli et al., 2022; Vignac et al., 2023), diffusion bridges (Jo et al., 2024), and Flow Matching
1277   (Eijkelboom et al., 2024) have significantly improved graph generation for small graphs. While
1278   less known, equivariant quantized auto-encoders have also demonstrated competitive performance
1279   (Boget et al., 2024; Nguyen et al., 2024). However, equivariant models are not without limitations.
1280   They operate by producing predictions for all node pairs and rely on dense graph representations,
1281   which prevents them from scaling to large graphs.
1282
1283   Sparse Equivariant Model SparseDiff (Qin et al., 2024) and EDGE Chen et al. (2023) are re-
1284   cent diffusion models that address scalability challenges in equivariant models. Both models share
1285   similar objectives and generative framework as ours. We comparative analysis to highlight their
1286
       differences from ours.
1287   Both models construct a sparse structure by selecting a subset of ”active” nodes, with ’active edges’
1288   defined by their induce complete graph. SparseDiff randomly selects ”active” nodes, while EDGE
1289   determines them based on predicted changes in degree. As a result, in both models, for the same
1290   graph, the sparse structure varies at each iteration. In contrast, our model maintains a fixed structure
1291   of ’active edges’ established by the spanning supergraph.
1292   The sparse strategies employed by SparseDiff and EDGE lead to a number of function evaluations
1293   per node and edge, which is critical for denoising models and represents a fraction of the total
1294   diffusion steps. SparseDiff compensates for this by increasing function evaluations, denoising the
1295   graph in blocks of nodes and edges, ultimately considering all node pairs. This approach results in
       a number of function evaluations (NFE) that is inversely proportional to the edge fraction in each


                                                         24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       block. EDGE does not compensate for excluded edges, which, we assume, is the reason for its
1297   comparatively low generative performance (see 5).
1298
1299   At generation, we still leverage our fixed graph structure. In our model, the additional computa-
1300
       tional cost arises from generating graphs at lower levels. However, since these graphs are signif-
       icantly smaller and computational cost scales quadratically with the number of nodes, this cost is
1301
       comparatively small.
1302
1303
       Hierarchical models A couple of works follow a similar hierarchical approach to ours, but differ
1304
       in their coarsening and generative strategies. HiGen (Karami, 2024) coarsens the graph using a
1305
       modularity objective to partition the graph into communities. The community-based partitions often
1306   produce dense coarse graphs, which strongly limits the effectiveness of the strategy, both in terms
1307   of the information extracted from the coarsening and the resulting sparsity used for generation.
1308   Moreover, the model leverages an autoregressive method to generate graphs at each level, which is
1309   inefficient for large graphs.
1310
       Bergmeister et al. (2024) use a local coarsening scheme involving edge or neighborhood contraction.
1311
       To prevent the coarsening from pooling a child node into two parent nodes, by contracting two of its
1312   adjacent edges, they sample a different contraction sequence at each training iteration. Moreover,
1313   the method requires a low reduction rate - set to a maximum of 0.3 - necessitating sequences with
1314   many coarseness levels. In our experiments, we were not able to generate large graphs in reasonable
1315   time with this method (see Section 5). In contrast to these hierarchical models, our method uses a
1316   single coarsening procedure in a preprocessing step, a small number of levels, and, last but not least,
1317   maintains equivariance.
