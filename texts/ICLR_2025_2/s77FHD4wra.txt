009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In recent years, the utilization of Graph Neural Network (GNN)-based methods
012            for simulating complex physical systems has opened new avenues for the fields of
013            computational science and engineering. Despite their success, current GNN-based
014            methods for rigid body dynamic simulation are constrained to relatively simple
015            scenarios, hindering their practical use in industrial settings where complex me-
016
               chanical structures and interconnected components prevail. These methods face
               challenges in handling intricate force relationships within rigid bodies, primarily
017
               due to the difficulty in obtaining force-related data for objects in industrial en-
018
               vironments. To address this, we propose a novel constraint-guided method that
019            incorporates force analysis into GNN-based simulations. The model incorporates
020            computations related to both contact and non-contact forces into the prediction pro-
021            cess. Additionally, it imposes physical constraints on the prediction process based
022            on Kane’s equations. We have rigorously demonstrated the model’s rationality
023            and effectiveness with thorough theoretical demonstration and empirical analysis.
024            Codes and anonymous links to the datasets are available in the supplementary
025            materials.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Simulation of rigid body dynamics plays a pivotal role in numerous domains within numerical
030
      science and engineering Landau et al. (2008); Thijssen (2007); Szabó & Babuška (2021), including
031   mechanical engineering Brach (2007), automotive design Tong (2000), biomechanics Silva et al.
032   (1997), and virtual reality Sauer & Schömer (1998), enabling the accurate modeling and analysis of
033   object motion, stress, and interactions for optimization and performance enhancement. Traditional
034   methods for simulating rigid body systems often rely on intricate physical equations tailored to specific
035   domains Huston & Passerello (1979); Mirtich (1996); Featherstone (2014). This necessitates a precise
036   understanding of the physical properties of the analyzed objects and the physical environment in
037   which they exist Thijssen (2007). Furthermore, such methods require a thorough grasp of relevant
038   physical principles and relationships. With the rise of deep learning, data-driven methods have
039
      excelled in various fields by removing the need for detailed prior knowledge of system intricacies,
      significantly simplifying complex problems Dong et al. (2021). Recently, GNNs have transformed
040
      physics simulation, particularly in rigid body dynamics, by extending neural network applications to
041
      physical analysis Sanchez-Gonzalez et al. (2020); Pfaff et al. (2021). GNNs analyze objects modeled
042   as graphs, demonstrating effective results in motion state analysis.
043
044   However, the current GNN-based methods concerning rigid body dynamic analysis are still limited to
045
      analyzing relatively simple and ideal scenarios, often focusing on the analysis of common geometric
      shapes Allen et al. (2023a); Bhattoo et al. (2022); Sanchez-Gonzalez et al. (2020); Han et al. (2022a);
046
      Bhattoo et al. (2022); Bishnoi et al. (2023); Allen et al. (2023b); Rubanova et al. (2021). This
047
      limitation hinders the practical application of such methods in industrial settings. In industrial
048   environments, complex mechanical structures often involve numerous interconnected components,
049   including intricate force relationships within rigid bodies and the resulting mutual influences between
050   objects. Presently, GNN-based methods encounter significant challenges in addressing such problems,
051   primarily because force-related data for objects is often challenging to observe and obtain Iglberger &
052   Rüde (2011). As a result, these methods typically rely solely on positional and velocity information
053   of objects for analysis. The difficulty in acquiring training data relevant to forces, coupled with the
      necessity for force relationship analysis, places these methods in a dilemma when applied to rigid

      To address this challenge, we endeavor to draw inspiration from traditional multibody dynamics
069
      analysis and incorporate relevant methods and constraints for calculating complex force relationships.
070   It is noteworthy that while GNN-based dynamic analysis methods can assist in avoiding the need
071   for acquiring detailed prior knowledge about the internal complexities of systems, the output results
072   must adhere to the principles and rules of the physical world. Some researchers argue that a purely
073   data-driven learning paradigm may struggle to achieve optimal results for physical problems Bhattoo
074   et al. (2022); Han et al. (2022a); Bishnoi et al. (2023). Therefore, we aim to integrate the principles of
075   traditional multibody dynamics analysis into GNN-based simulators, incorporating force analysis and
076   prior constraints, to address the challenges associated with rigid body dynamics analysis in complex
077   mechanical structures.
078   Building upon the aforementioned analysis, we propose a Multibody Dynamics Guided GNN
079   Simulator, dubbed MDGS, to conduct a more thorough analysis of rigid body dynamics scenarios
080   involving complex mechanical structures. Initially, we introduce a graph modeling pattern for ef-
081   ficient analysis of forces in physical systems, tailored for multibody dynamics. Subsequently, we
082   introduce calculations related to forces, encompassing both contact and non-contact forces, into the
083   methodology. These force calculations are performed using neural networks without the introduction
084
      of additional data beyond conventional GNN-based physical dynamics simulators. Then, the com-
      puted forces are integrated into the succeeding analyses to adapt to complex mechanical scenarios.
085
      Furthermore, we imposed constraints on the model output based on the Kane equations tailored for
086
      addressing multibody dynamics analysis problems. This ensures the accuracy and rationality of the
087   model’s computed results. The major contributions are as follows.
088
089            • We introduce a novel method, referred to as MDGS, to incorporate force analysis into GNN-
090              based simulators for the state prediction of rigid body systems. This approach enhances the
091              treatment of multibody dynamics scenarios, expanding the potential applications of such
092              methods in industrial settings.
093            • We imposed physical constraints on our method based on Kane’s equations, offering a
094              more precise analysis of rigid body dynamics. These constraints are derived from rigorous
095              theoretical analysis and proof.
096            • We provide the implementations of MDGS for multibody dynamics simulation tasks and
097              create new datasets for validation, the results demonstrate the effectiveness of MDGS.

      GNN-based Physical Dynamics Simulators. GNN-based physical dynamics simulators Sanchez-
102   Gonzalez et al. (2020) leverages GNNs Scarselli et al. (2009); Kipf & Welling (2017) to simulate
103   predictive representations for graph-modeled physical systems. Such approaches find widespread
104   applications in computing atomic forces Hu et al. (2021), simulating particle-modeled and mesh-
105   modeled physical systems Allen et al. (2023b); Rubanova et al. (2021); Li et al. (2019); Sanchez-
106   Gonzalez et al. (2020); Pfaff et al. (2021); Han et al. (2022b). In recent research, some methods
107   Han et al. (2022a); Bhattoo et al. (2022); Bishnoi et al. (2023) have proposed incorporating prior
      knowledge from relevant domains into the GNN-based physical dynamics simulator to enhance its

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      performance. This includes integrating concepts such as Subequivariant and Lagrangian equations
109   into the model framework, making the model more closely aligned with real-world physical systems.
110   Additionally, some approaches Linkerhägner et al. (2023) optimize the model by collecting data
111   in real-world environments as supplementary information. Our method introduces force analysis
112   between physical entities in rigid body dynamics scenarios into the system and employs Kane’s
113   equations Kane & Levinson (1985) to impose constraints on the system output, ensuring a more
114   realistic simulation performance.
115
116   Dynamics Analysis. The framework of traditional mechanical analysis relies on either force-based
117   or energy-based approaches Wyk et al. (2022) for rigid body dynamics. Energy-based methods
118   compute unknown energy using equilibrium equations, which is cumbersome for large structures
119
      Finzi et al. (2020). The force-based multibody dynamics model uses two recursive algorithms
      centered on the Newton–Euler formulation for forward kinematics and inverse dynamics Gonçalves
120
      et al. (2023). Some methods use ODEs to approximate optimal solutions for multibody conditions
121
      Schubert et al. (2023); Nada & Bayoumi (2023); Zhang et al. (2023). Traditional mechanical analysis
122   requires precise physical parameters and domain knowledge. In contrast, GNN-based methods are
123   data-driven and do not need these inputs. We combine elements of rigid body dynamics with GNNs
124   for improved and simplified predictions.

373   the GNN-based physical dynamics simulator. Furthermore, LGNS and HGNS leverage Lagrangian
374   equations and Hamiltonian mechanics, respectively, to guide and constrain the GNN model, enhancing
375   its capability to simulate physical reality. Apart from GNS, the other three methods incorporate
376   varying degrees of prior knowledge from physics to reinforce the models. Through comparisons
377   with these baselines, we can effectively analyze the algorithmic performance of our approach in the
      dynamic analysis of complex mechanical bodies.

907   B.1.1    E DGE C ONSTRUCTION FOR C ONTACT-O RIENTED G RAPH .
908
909
      In this section, we introduce the construction of edges in our CONTACT-ORIENTED GRAPH.
      Many physical scenarios are complex, involving multiple object interactions (including collision and
910
      friction) when the distance between them is minimal. The interactions between particles belonging to
911
      different objects and those within the same object are often distinct. Hence, we propose the concept
912   of a contact graph. We continuously observe the distances between particles. When the distance
913   between particles falls below a certain threshold, and they do not belong to the same object, we
914   determine that contact has occurred. A new edge is established between these contacting particles to
915   facilitate message passing, thus forming a new, contact-related graph. This newly constructed graph
916   is utilized to predict and generate the contact forces experienced by the objects. Additionally, we
917   leverage the pooling of particle-level information from the objects themselves to facilitate interactions
      between different objects.

1012   B.1.3    S TRUCTURAL C ONSTRAINT M ODULE
1013
1014   In this study, we introduce a novel algorithm aimed at restoring deformed objects to their original, reg-
1015   ular rigid body shapes. This algorithm is based on the geometric transformation of three-dimensional
1016   point sets, achieving the restoration of the object’s shape by precisely calculating the optimal rotation
1017
       and translation matrices. Specifically, the algorithm selects elements from the current and reference
       states of the object for processing. It calculates the centroid of each set and centralizes the data, shift-
1018
       ing the coordinates of both sets to be centered around the origin. This step eliminates the influence of
1019
       positional deviations. Subsequently, it computes the covariance matrix of the two centralized data
1020   sets and employs Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to extract the rotation matrix. To ensure
1021   the correct rotational direction of the object, we introduce a correction matrix to adjust the rotation
1022   matrix. This rotation matrix not only reflects the directional differences between the two point sets
1023   but also ensures that the transformation adheres to the right-hand rule. Finally, by calculating the
1024   translation vector, the adjusted reference point set is moved to a position as close as possible to the
1025   current point set. Our experimental results indicate that this method can effectively restore deformed
       objects back to their original shapes.

             B.3    C OMPUTATIONAL C OMPLEXITY
1066
1067         Our algorithm exhibits a computational complexity akin to other GNN-based methods, specifically
1068         O(N × d × l). Here, N and d denote the number of nodes and degrees in the graph, respectively,
1069         while l represents the number of layers. The complexity is primarily influenced by the node count.
1070         Our method constructs graphs with fewer nodes compared to other GNN-based approaches because
1071         it only utilizes centroids and does not require meshes or point clouds to represent regular shapes. In
1072         contrast, when examining traditional numerical simulation methods such as finite element analysis,
1073         their complexity is expressed as O(N ′ × d′ × k ′ ), where N ′ is the number of nodes, d′ the average
1074         degree, and k ′ the number of functions computed. Like mesh-based methods, finite element analysis
1075
             involves a larger N ′ , resulting in greater computational complexity.
