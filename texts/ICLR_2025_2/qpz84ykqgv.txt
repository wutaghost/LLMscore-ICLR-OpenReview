011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Classical point process models, such as the epidemic-type aftershock sequence
014            (ETAS) model, have been widely used for forecasting the event times and lo-
015            cations of earthquakes for decades. Recent advances have led to Neural Point
016
               Processes (NPPs), which promise greater flexibility and improvements over clas-
               sical models. However, the currently-used benchmark dataset for NPPs does not
017
               represent an up-to-date challenge in the seismological community since it lacks a
018
               key earthquake sequence from the region and improperly splits training and testing
019            data. Furthermore, initial earthquake forecast benchmarking lacks a comparison to
020            state-of-the-art earthquake forecasting models typically used by the seismological
021            community. To address these gaps, we introduce EarthquakeNPP: a collection of
022            benchmark datasets to facilitate testing of NPPs on earthquake data, accompanied
023            by a credible implementation of the ETAS model. The datasets cover a range
024            of small to large target regions within California, dating from 1971 to 2021, and
025            include different methodologies for dataset generation. In a benchmarking experi-
026            ment, we compare three spatio-temporal NPPs against ETAS and find that none
027
               outperform ETAS in either spatial or temporal log-likelihood. These results indi-
               cate that current NPP implementations are not yet suitable for practical earthquake
028
               forecasting. EarthquakeNPP also provides generative evaluation metrics, enabling
029
               broader model classes to be benchmarked and facilitating the future collaboration
030            between the seismology and machine learning communities.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Operational earthquake forecasting by global governmental organisations such as the US Geological
036   Survey (USGS) necessitates the development of models which can forecast the times and locations of
037   damaging earthquakes. While model development is ongoing in the seismology community, recent
038   improvements have relied upon refinement of a spatio-temporal point process model known as the
039   Epidemic-Type Aftershock Sequence (ETAS) model (Ogata, 1988; 1998), despite significant growth
040   in available data (Takanami et al., 2003; Shelly, 2017; Ross et al., 2019; White et al., 2019; Mousavi
041   et al., 2020; Tan et al., 2021; Mousavi & Beroza, 2023).
042   In contrast, the machine learning community has offered promising advancements over classical point
043   process models like ETAS with Neural Point Process (NPP) models, showcasing greater flexibility
044   (Du et al., 2016; Omi et al., 2019a; Shchur et al., 2019; Jia & Benson, 2019; Chen et al., 2021; Zhou
045   et al., 2022; Zhou & Yu, 2024). While some initial benchmarking of these models has been conducted
046   on an earthquake dataset in Japan, these experiments lack relevance for stakeholders in the seismology
047   community. The benchmark lacks a key earthquake sequence from the region, fails to recreate an
048   operational setting with proper train-test splits, and doesn’t compare against state-of-the-art models
049   like ETAS.
050   Here, we introduce EarthquakeNPP: a curated collection of datasets designed for benchmarking
051   NPP models in earthquake forecasting, accompanied by a state-of-the-art benchmark model. These
052   datasets are derived from publicly available raw data, which we process and configure within our
053   platform to facilitate meaningful forecasting experiments relevant to stakeholders in the seismology
      community. Covering various regions of California, these datasets represent typical forecasting zones

      1.1    R ELATED W ORK
076
077
      Benchmarking by the NPP Community. Chen et al. (2021) introduced an earthquake dataset for
078   benchmarking the Neural Spatio-temporal Point Process (NSTPP) model using a global dataset from
079   the U.S. Geological Survey, focusing on Japan from 1990 to 2020. They considered earthquakes
080   with magnitudes above 2.5, splitting the data into month-long segments with a 7-day offset. They
081   exclude earthquakes from November 2010 to December 2011, deeming these sequences "too long"
082   and "outliers." However, this period includes the 2011 Tohoku earthquake (Mori et al., 2011), the
083   largest earthquake recorded in Japan and the fourth largest in the world, at magnitude 9.0. This
084   exclusion renders the benchmarking experiment irrelevant for seismologists, as it is precisely these
085   large earthquakes and their aftershocks that are crucial to forecast due to their damaging impact.
086
      Additionally, these events are of significant scientific interest because they provide valuable insights
      into the earthquake rupture process.
087
088   The dataset segments are divided for training, testing, and validation. Instead of a chronological
089   partitioning that mirrors operational forecasting, the segments are assigned in an alternating pattern.
090   This approach misrepresents a realistic forecasting scenario and inflates performance measures due to
091   earthquake triggering (Freed, 2005). Since the model is tested on windows immediately preceding
092
      training windows, it exploits causal dependencies backwards it time.
093   Although earthquakes with magnitudes above 2.5 are considered by Chen et al. (2021), following a
094   change in USGS policy on global data collection, from 2009 onwards, only events above magnitude
095   4.0 are recorded in the dataset. For earthquake forecasting in Japan, seismologists use datasets from
096   Japanese data centers since they are more comprehensive and complete than global datasets. Section
097   A.2 describes the biases incurred from such data missingness.
098   Chen et al. (2021) benchmark their model against another spatio-temporal model, Neural Jump SDEs
099   (Jia & Benson, 2019), and a temporal-only Hawkes process, even though a spatio-temporal Hawkes
100   process would provide a more rigorous benchmark. Subsequent papers adopting this benchmark
101   (Zhou et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2023; Zhou & Yu, 2024) similarly lack comparisons to a spatio-
102   temporal Hawkes process, benchmarking instead against temporal-only or spatial-only baselines or
103   other spatio-temporal NPPs.
104   Benchmarking by the Seismology Community. Model comparison has been crucial in the develop-
105   ment of earthquake forecasting models since their inception (Kagan & Knopoff, 1987; Ogata, 1988).
106   The Collaboratory for the Study of Earthquake Predictability (CSEP) (Michael & Werner, 2018; Schor-
107   lemmer et al., 2018; Savran et al., 2022; Iturrieta et al., 2024) (https://cseptesting.org/
      ) aims to unify the framework for earthquake model testing and evaluation, hosting retrospective


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      and fully prospective forecasting experiments globally. CSEP benchmarks short-term models using
109   performance metrics that require forecasts to be generated by simulating many repeat sequences over
110   a specified time horizon (typically one day). These simulated forecasts are compared by discretizing
111   time and space intervals, with test statistics calculated for event counts, magnitudes, locations, and
112   times. The simulation-based approach allows the inclusion of generative models that don’t output
113   explicit earthquake probabilities (i.e., a likelihood), and enables evaluation of the full distribution of
114   entire sampled sequences.
115   Two existing works benchmark NPPs for earthquake forecasting within the seismology community.
116   The first by Dascher-Cousineau et al. (2023) extends a temporal-only NPP from Shchur et al. (2019)
117   to include earthquake magnitudes. The second by Stockman et al. (2023) extends another temporal-
118   only model by Omi et al. (2019a) to target larger magnitude events. Both models are benchmarked
119   against a temporal ETAS model, showing moderate improvements over the baseline. Extending these
120   models to include spatial data is necessary for further testing and potential operational use in the
121   seismological community.
122
123   1.2   S COPE OF THIS WORK
124
      Since generating repeated sequences over forecast horizons is computationally costly, the seismology
125
      community uses the mean log-likelihood on held-out data for a more streamlined metric during model
126
      development (Ogata, 1988; Harte, 2015). Our platform uses this metric in the NPP benchmarking
127   experiment and provides detailed guidance on CSEP’s simulation-based procedure, enabling future
128   NPP implementations and evaluations within CSEP experiments.
129
130
      This work aims to bridge Machine Learning and seismology by establishing a baseline for com-
      paring NPP models to state-of-the-art, domain-based models. Only NPPs capable of generating
131
      log-likelihoods are within scope, as no valid score exists for models lacking this capability (e.g.
132
      Yuan et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023). Traditional metrics like Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) and
133   Mean Absolute Error (MAE) are inadequate and potentially misleading for seismological predictions
134   (Hodson, 2022), as earthquake occurrence follows power law distributions (Kagan, 1994; Felzer &
135   Brodsky, 2006) that are heavy-tailed, making the errors non-Gaussian and non-Laplacian — contrary
136   to the assumptions underlying RMSE and MAE (see Section G). To ensure seismological relevance,
137   we challenge authors of NPP models to implement forecasts using CSEP’s evaluation framework and
138   benchmark their results against the performance of the ETAS model.

168
      be used as a performance metric. This is consistent with a wealth of literature in the seismology
169   community (see Zechar et al., 2010, and references therein) as well as the wider general point process
170   literature (Daley & Vere-Jones, 2004), which now includes neural point processes (Shchur et al.,
171   2021). The metric evaluates models that output probability distributions over their predictions and
172   consequently penalises models that are overconfident. Although evaluating on events in the test set,
173   the test log-likelihood, log p ((ti , xi )|ti ∈ [T2 , T3 ], HT2 ), may still contain dependence upon events
174   prior to the test window [T2 , T3 ], typically contained in the history HT2 of the intensity function.
175   Comparing the mean log-likelihood per event provides the information gain from one model to
176   another (Daley & Vere-Jones, 2004).
177   Point processes are the dominant modeling approach in the seismology community, used exten-
178   sively in both real-time operational earthquake forecasting (Mizrahi et al., 2024a) and established
179   benchmarking experiments (CSEP) (Taroni et al., 2018; Rhoades et al., 2018). The point process
180   representation of earthquake data aligns naturally with their occurrence as discrete events in time
181   (Kagan, 1994). Furthermore, this modeling approach is favored over discretized forecasting models
182   (e.g., time series) because it eliminates the need for optimizing binning strategies and allows for
183
      immediate updates, rather than waiting until the end of a time bin — a delay that could miss critical,
      potentially damaging events.

186   2.2   ETAS
187
      The Epidemic Type Aftershock Sequence (ETAS) model (Ogata, 1998) is a spatio-temporal Hawkes
188   process which models how earthquakes cluster in time and space. It has been adopted for operational
189   earthquake forecasting by government agencies in California (Milner et al., 2020), New-Zealand
190   (Christophersen et al., 2017), Italy (Spassiani et al., 2023), Japan (Omi et al., 2019b) and Switzerland
191   (Mizrahi et al., 2024b), and performs consistently well in CSEP’s retrospective and fully prospective
192   forecasting experiments (e.g. Woessner et al., 2011; Rhoades et al., 2018; Taroni et al., 2018; Cattania
193   et al., 2018; Mancini et al., 2019; 2020; 2022). The general formulation of the model is

250   coordinates, and magnitudes, documented within California from 1971 to 2021. California, with its

      San Andreas fault plate boundary system (Zoback et al., 1987) and includes modern high-resolution
253
      catalogs with numerous small magnitude earthquakes, offering potential for new, more expressive
254
      models.

      Savran et al. (2020) describe how to assess a model’s performance across the multiple days in the
472
      testing period. By construction, quantile scores over multiple periods should be uniformly distributed
473
      if the model is the data generator (Gneiting & Katzfuss, 2014). Therefore comparing quantile scores
474   against standard uniform quantiles (y = x), highlights discrepancies between the observed data and
475   the forecast. Additional statements can be made about over-prediction or under-prediction of each test
476   statistic (quantile curves above/bellow y=x respectively). The Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) statistic
477   then quantifies the degree of difference to the uniform distribution for each of the tests.
478
      Further documentation of how to perform the CSEP evaluation procedure can be found on the
479
      platform, where we demonstrate the procedure for the ETAS model. Table 3 reports the benchmark
480   performance scores taken from the quantile plots in Appendix D. The performance of ETAS is higher
481   for the more typical higher magnitude catalogs such as ComCat and SCEDC, whereas it performs
482   worse at the lower magnitude catalogs of QTM_SanJac, QTM_SaltonSea and White. Spatial
483   prediction is consistently the best performing component of the ETAS forecast, whereas earthquake
484   numbers are overpredicted by the model and earthquake magnitudes are generally not well predicted
485   (Figure 9) . All results indicate significant room for improvement beyond the predictive performance
      of the ETAS model.

      6    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
503
504
505   We introduce the EarthquakeNPP datasets to facilitate the benchmarking of NPPs against a
506   community-endorsed ETAS model for earthquake forecasting. These datasets cover various re-
507   gions of California, representing typical forecasting zones and the data commonly available to
508   forecast issuers. Several datasets use modern methods of detection, which enables the inclusion of
509   much smaller magnitude earthquakes.
510
      In a benchmarking experiment, we compared three NPP models against ETAS and a baseline Poisson
511   process. None of the NPP models outperformed ETAS, indicating that current NPP implementations
512   are not yet suitable for operational earthquake forecasting. ETAS explicitly defines how larger
513   earthquake magnitudes increase the likelihood of future earthquakes in both time and space, using an
514   empirical relationship derived from seminal observational studies (Utsu & Seki, 1955; Utsu, 1970).
515   This use of magnitude information is shared across all competitive short-term earthquake forecasting
516   models currently used operationally (Mizrahi et al., 2024a) or tested by CSEP (Taroni et al., 2018).
517   The lack of a direct dependence on magnitudes in the current NPP implementations likely explains
518   their relative under-performance compared to ETAS. Future implementations should exploit this
519
      additional feature for improved temporal and spatial performance. Encouragingly, the comparable
      temporal performance to ETAS without this additional feature suggests that incorporating magnitude
520
      dependence would enhance NPP performance beyond that of ETAS.
521
522   EarthquakeNPP supports the earthquake forecast evaluation procedure developed by the Collaboratory
523   for the Study of Earthquake Predictability (CSEP). The procedure replicates how earthquakes forecasts
524   are generated in an operational setting, requiring models to simulate many repeat event sequences over
525
      a day-long forecast horizon. Benchmark performance for the ETAS model enables future comparison
      of NPPs that are implemented for this procedure and enables their promotion to the fully prospective
526
      CSEP experiments. Notably, this procedure allows the evaluation of generative NPP models without
527
      explicit likelihoods (Yuan et al., 2023; Li et al.), by assessing their performance over the full trajectory
528   of future events. Probabilistic seismic hazard analysis (PSHA) requires long-term prediction beyond
529   the next-event (Ebrahimian et al., 2014; Gerstenberger et al., 2014), therefore this approach also
530   offers stakeholders a more comprehensive understanding of earthquake hazard than metrics focused
531   on predicting the next event (e.g. RMSE). The procedure also follows the recommendation by Shchur
532   et al. (2021) to move away from next-event point prediction for NPPs.
533
      The EarthquakeNPP datasets, available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/
534   EarthquakeNPP-2D51, provide a platform for future NPP developments to be benchmarked
535   against these initial results. The platform is under ongoing development and in the future will see the
536   direct comparison of emerging and other existing models models developed within the seismology
537   community, as well as an expansion of datasets included to other seismically active global regions.
538   Successful NPP models on these datasets, for both log-likelihood and CSEP metrics, will be di-
539   rectly impactful to stakeholders in seismology, ultimately enabling their integration into operational
      earthquake forecasting by government agencies.

      Figure 5: Generating an earthquake catalog involves several key steps: seismic phase picking,
894   magnitude estimation, and the association and location of seismic sources. This process transforms
895   raw waveform data recorded at seismic stations to locations, times, and magnitudes of earthquakes.

897
898   Data missingness, referred to in seismology as catalog (in)completeness, is the primary challenge
899   faced with earthquake catalogs. It is an important and unavoidable feature, and is a result of how
900   earthquakes are detected and characterised. Below, we briefly overview the process of generating an
901   earthquake catalog to illustrate the data quality issues. In the subsequent section, we review catalog
902
      incompleteness and its potential impact on the performance and evaluation of forecast models.
903   Seismometers and Seismic Networks. A seismometer is an instrument that detects and records the
904   vibrations caused by seismic waves (Stein & Wysession, 2009; Shearer, 2019). It consists of a sensor
905   to detect ground motion and a recording system to log three-dimensional ground motion over time,
906   typically vertical and horizontal velocities. Seismic networks, comprising multiple seismometers,
907   monitor seismic activity at regional, national or global scales (see, e.g., (Woessner et al., 2010) and
908
      references therein). High-density networks with modern, sensitive equipment provide more detailed
      and accurate data, enhancing the ability to detect and analyse smaller and more distant earthquakes.
909
910   From Waveforms to Phase Picking. The process of converting raw continuous seismic waveforms
911   into useful earthquake data begins with phase picking, which identifies the arrival times of the
912   primary (P) and secondary (S) waves of an earthquake. Historically, this was done manually, but
913   now automated algorithms, such as the STA/LTA algorithm, detect wave arrivals by analyzing signal
914
      amplitude changes (Allen, 1982). Recent algorithms, such as machine learning classifiers (e.g. Zhu &
      Beroza, 2019; Lapins et al., 2021) and template-matching (e.g. Ross et al., 2019), can process much
915
      higher volumes of data efficiently and are often able to detect events of much smaller magnitudes.
916
917   Earthquake Association and Location After phase picking, the next step is to associate phases
      from different seismometers with the same earthquake. Simple algorithms require at least four


                                                       17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      phase arrivals to be detected on different stations within a short time interval to declare an event.
919   Once phases are associated, location estimation determines the earthquake’s hypocenter and origin
920   time by minimizing travel-time residuals using linearized or global inversion algorithms (Thurber,
921   1985; Lomax et al., 2000). Given the potential for misidentified or mis-associated phase arrivals
922   due to low signal-to-noise of small events or the near-simultaneous occurrence during very active
923   aftershock sequences, an automated system typically first picks arrival times and determines a
924   preliminary location, which is subsequently reviewed by a seismologist (e.g. Woessner et al., 2010,
925   and references therein). Locations are typically reported as the geographical coordinates and depths
926   where earthquakes first nucleated (hypocenters), although some catalogs report the centroid location,
927
      a central measure of the extended earthquake rupture.
928   Earthquake Magnitude Calculation The magnitude of an earthquake quantifies the energy released
929   at the source and was originally defined in the seminal paper by Richter (1935). The original
930   definition, now referred to as the local magnitude (ML), is calculated from the logarithm of the
931   amplitude of waves recorded by seismometers. This scale, however, "saturates" at higher magnitudes,
932   meaning it underestimates magnitudes for various reasons. This led to introduction of the moment
933
      magnitude scale (Mw) (Hanks & Kanamori, 1979), which computes the magnitude based on the
      estimated seismic moment M0 , which can be related to the physical rupture process via
934
935                                   M0 = rigidity × rupture area × slip,                               (15)
936
937   where rigidity is a mechanical property of the rock along the fault, rupture area is the area of the
938   fault that slipped, and slip is the distance the fault moved. Mw is determined seismologically via a
939
      spectral fitting process to the earthquake waveforms. In practice, it can be challenging to use a single
      magnitude scale for a broad range of magnitudes, therefore a range of scales may be present within a
940
      single catalog, and approximate magnitude conversion equations may be used to homogenize the
941
      scales (e.g. Herrmann & Marzocchi, 2021, and references therein).

      A.2    E ARTHQUAKE C ATALOG C OMPLETENESS
945
      All of the EarthquakeNPP datasets are made publicly available by their respective data centers in
946   raw format. However, constructing a suitable retrospective forecasting experiment from this raw
947   data requires appropriate pre-processing. This typically involves truncating the dataset above a
948   magnitude threshold Mcut and within a target spatial region to address incomplete data, known as
949   catalog completeness Mc (e.g., Mignan et al., 2011; Mignan & Woessner, 2012).
950
      There are several reasons why an earthquake may not be detected by a seismic network. Small events
951
      may be indistinguishable from noise at a single station, or insufficiently corroborated across multiple
952   stations. Another significant cause of missing events occurs during the aftershock sequence of large
953   earthquakes, when the seismicty rate is high (Kagan & Knopoff, 1987; Hainzl, 2022). Human or
954   algorithmic detection abilities are hampered when numerous events occur in quick succession, e.g.
955   when phase arrivals of different events overlap at different stations or the amplitudes of small events
956   are swamped by those of large events. Since catalog incompleteness increases for lower magnitude
957   events, typically the task is to find the value Mc above which there is approximately 100% detection
958   probability. Choosing a truncation threshold Mcut that is too high removes usable data. Where
959   NPPs have demonstrated an ability to perform well with incomplete data (Stockman et al., 2023),
960
      typically a threshold below the completeness biases classical models such as ETAS (Seif et al., 2017).
      Seismologists often investigate the biases of different magnitude thresholds by performing repeat
961
      forecasting experiments for different thresholds (e.g. Mancini et al., 2022; Stockman et al., 2023),
962
      which we also facilitate in our datasets.
963
964   Typically Mc is determined by comparing the raw earthquake catalog to the Gutenberg-Richter law
965   (Gutenberg & Richter, 1936), which states that the distribution of earthquake magnitudes follows an
966
      exponential probability density function
967
                                      fGR (m) = βeβ(m−Mc ) : m ≥ Mc .                                    (16)
968
969   where β is a rate parameter related to the b-value by β = b log 10. Histogram-based approaches,
970   such as the simple Maximum Curvature method (Wiemer & Wyss, 2000) as well as many others (e.g.
971   Herrmann & Marzocchi, 2021, and references therein), identify the magnitude at which the observed
      catalog deviates from this law, indicating incompleteness (See Figure 6b).

972
       In practice, catalog completeness varies in both time and space Mc (t, x) (e.g. Schorlemmer &
973    Woessner, 2008). During aftershock sequences, Mc (t) can be very high (e.g., Agnew, 2015; Hainzl,
974    2016b) (See Figure 6a). Thresholding at the maximum value might remove too much data. Instead,
975    modelers either omit particularly incomplete periods during training and testing (Kagan, 1991; Hainzl
976    et al., 2008), model the incompleteness itself (Helmstetter et al., 2006; Werner et al., 2011; Omi
977    et al., 2014; Hainzl, 2016a;b; Mizrahi et al., 2021; Hainzl, 2022), or accept known biases from
978    disregarding this issue (Sornette & Werner, 2005). Spatially, catalogs are less complete farther from
979    the seismic network (Mignan et al., 2011), so the spatial region can be constrained to remove outer,
980    more incomplete areas (See Figure 6c).

1126   ETAS training scales O(n2 ) with the total number of events, since for every event a contribution to
1127   the intensity function is computed from a summation over all previous events. This scaling, coupled
1128   with the lack of parallelization in the current implementation, results in long training times for larger
1129   datasets. Poorer scaling will likely hinder ETAS if dataset sizes continue to grow in the future
1130
       (Stockman et al., 2024).
1131   Encouragingly, both Deep-STPP and AutoSTPP are significantly faster to train due to GPU acceler-
1132   ation and their use of a sliding window of the most recent k = 20 events. While exact complexity
1133   analyses are not provided in Zhou et al. (2022) or Zhou & Yu (2024), we can infer that Deep-STPP
       likely scales as O(kn) since it benefits from a closed-form expression for the likelihood. AutoSTPP,


                                                                                                21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       though requiring automatic integration to compute the likelihood, still scales with O(kn) because the
1135   additional integration cost does not affect the overall scaling.
1136
1137   NSTPP, on the other hand, incurs significant training costs, rendering it impractical for real-time
1138
       forecasting. Unlike the sliding window mechanism used in Deep-STPP and AutoSTPP, NSTPP
       partitions the event sequence into fixed time intervals, leading to sequences that are much longer
1139
       than the k = 20 events used by the other models (as shown in Figure 11 of Chen et al. (2021)).
1140
       Furthermore, solving an ODE for each event time adds a significant computational burden, even with
1141   the use of their faster attentive CNF architecture.
1142
1143
       C.2   S IMULATION
1144
1145   Real-time earthquake forecasting and CSEP model evaluation require simulating many repeat se-
1146   quences (at least 10,000 for adequate distributional coverage) over the forecasting horizon. While
1147   ETAS training scales as O(n2 ) with the number of training events, its simulation scales more ef-
1148   ficiently at O(n log n). This improved scaling is due to its equivalent formulation as a Hawkes
1149   branching process (see Section 2.2). Both Deep-STPP and AutoSTPP are also based on Hawkes
1150   processes, which theoretically allows for fast simulation. However, as these models currently only
1151
       have an intensity function implementation, simulating events would require a slower thinning pro-
       cedure (Ogata, 1981), limiting their simulation efficiency. In contrast, NSTPP benefits from fast
1152
       simulation, owing to its design using continuous-time normalizing flows. Events can be generated by
1153
       passing samples from a base distribution through learned transformations, resulting in a much faster
1154   simulation process.

1489   F    2019 M7.1 R IDGECREST E ARTHQUAKE C ASE S TUDY
1490
1491   The 2019 Ridgecrest earthquake sequence (Figure 19) was the most powerful seismic event to strike
1492   Southern California in the past 20 years. Centered near the town of Ridgecrest and the Naval Air
1493   Weapons Station China Lake, the sequence began with a magnitude 6.4 foreshock on July 4, 2019, at
1494   17:33:49 UTC, followed by a more powerful magnitude 7.1 mainshock on July 6, 2019, at 03:19:53
1495   UTC, both along the Eastern California Shear Zone. The earthquakes caused widespread surface
1496
       rupture, with displacements along multiple faults, and triggered tens of thousands of aftershocks over
       the following months.
1497
1498   The impacts of the sequence were substantial. In Ridgecrest and surrounding areas, the shaking
1499   damaged homes, businesses, and infrastructure, including roads, water lines, and electrical systems.
1500   Fires broke out due to ruptured gas lines, exacerbating the destruction. The mainshock caused over
1501   $1 billion in damages, including significant damage to the China Lake Naval facility, which was
1502
       temporarily evacuated and declared "not mission capable." Despite the severity of the shaking, no
       fatalities occurred, largely due to the remote location and earthquake-resistant construction in the
1503
       region.
1504
1505   Using the CSEP evaluation procedure (Section 5), we isolate the performance of a model during the
1506   sequence to identify its strengths and weaknesses. Here, we apply this analysis to the ETAS model,
1507   illustrating how similar evaluations can be conducted for future implementations of NPPs or other
1508
       machine learning-based models.
1509   Figure 20 presents the results of the Number Test over the initial days of the sequence. ETAS forecasts
1510   consistently underestimate the number of aftershocks during the most seismically active phase of
1511   the sequence. It is only 4 days after the M7.1 Ridgecrest mainshock, that ETAS begins to provide
       accurate earthquake rate forecasts. Figure 21a shows the spatial forecast for the day after the M7.1


                                                         28
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1512
       mainshock. While the forecasts successfully trace the likely aftershock zone, they are over-dispersed
1513   and exhibit an isotropic distribution around a centroid. This prevents the forecasts from accurately
1514   capturing the elongated and clustered orientation of seismicity along the fault, causing it to fail the
1515   Spatial Test (Figure 21b).
