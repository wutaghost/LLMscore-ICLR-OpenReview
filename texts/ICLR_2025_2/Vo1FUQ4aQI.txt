009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized generative tasks, but con-
012            cerns about their trustworthiness and vulnerability to adversarial attacks persist.
013            This paper introduces the Generative Robustness Evaluation (GRE) Score, a novel
014            metric designed to assess LLMs’ resilience against adversarial red teaming at-
015            tempts that may compromise model compliance and elicit undesired responses.
016            Our approach utilizes conditional generation for synthetic text creation, offering
017            an attack-independent evaluation of LLM robustness. By calculating the margin
018
               in refusal scores, we quantify the robustness of LLMs in an attack-agnostic man-
               ner. We evaluate our method on five different dimensions with specified datasets,
019
               encompassing ethical considerations, safety protocols, and potential misuse sce-
020
               narios. We present four key contributions: (1) The GRE Score framework, which
021            establishes a textual robustness certificate for LLMs against adversarial red team-
022            ing attempts, providing a theoretical foundation for quantifying model resilience.
023            (2) Comprehensive evaluations across five critical dimensions using eight promi-
024            nent LLMs, validating GRE Scores with adversarial red teaming attacks. Our
025            method demonstrates a consistent ranking of LLM robustness when compared to
026            the attack-based model ranking on TrustLLM (Huang et al., 2024) while achieving
027            a significant 5-8x speedup compared to traditional evaluation techniques. (3) In-
028            sights into the non-linear relationship between model scaling and performance,
029
               revealing that larger models do not always perform better, and an analysis of
               how instruction-tuning impacts robustness across LLMs. (4) The discovery that
030
               all evaluated LLMs exhibit notably lower performance in robustness and privacy
031
               tasks compared to other areas, highlighting a critical gap in LLM capabilities.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language generation, yet their
037   widespread adoption has raised significant concerns regarding trustworthiness. The concept of Red-
038   teaming LLMs emerged as a response to these concerns, aiming to evaluate the robustness of their
039   safety mechanisms (Perez & Ribeiro, 2022). This approach has unveiled vulnerabilities such as jail-
040   break , where carefully crafted prompts can manipulate LLMs into generating harmful, biased, or
041   sensitive content, bypassing their built-in safeguards (Jain et al., 2023; Barrett et al., 2023).
042   Recent LLM advancements have incorporated alignment techniques to enhance model compliance
043   with user intentions and ethical guidelines. These include Instruction Tuning (Wei et al., 2021) for
044   improved zero-shot performance, and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Bai
045   et al., 2022a) for refined behavior. Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022b) and Self-Alignment (Sun
046   et al., 2024) further reduce reliance on external feedback. To evaluate the effectiveness of these
047   safety mechanisms, researchers have developed red-teaming approaches for LLMs (Perez et al.,
048   2022; Ganguli et al., 2022). Red-teaming simulates potential adversarial scenarios to identify vul-
049   nerabilities in model behavior. Within this framework, we define adversarial testing as a specialized
050
      form of red-teaming that involves modifying or optimizing human-designed prompts to study an
      LLM’s resilience in worst-case scenarios.
051
052   Despite the implementation of safety measures, methods like AutoDAN (Zhu et al., 2023a) have
053   successfully circumvented safeguards in models such as Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023) and GPT-4
      (Achiam et al., 2023). These adversarial testing techniques aim to find prompts that can bypass the

077   The landscape of LLM evaluation presents several interconnected challenges: (I) Current research

084
085   To address these challenges, we introduce the GRE Score, a novel conditional robustness certifi-
086   cate designed to evaluate LLM resilience comprehensively. In this context, ”conditional” refers to
087   context-aware assessments that consider the input or context (the associated risks) when measuring
088
      model performance, allowing for a more nuanced evaluation across various scenarios.
089   Our approach addresses each challenge as follows: (I) We leverage a standard generative model,
090   scoring based on a limited set of generated samples, grounding assessments in theoretical frame-
091   works that link score confidence to specific distributions. (II) By employing semantic encoding and
092   decoding techniques, we transform textual data into a continuous semantic space, enabling the for-
093   mulation of verifiable boundaries for evaluation. (III) Our methodology allows for the assessment
094
      of models’ resistance to adversarial testing with minimal computational overhead, requiring only
      forward data passing and toxicity evaluation. (IV) We use generative models to create novel, uncon-
095
      taminated test data, mitigating data contamination issues and ensuring more reliable LLM perfor-
096
      mance assessments. This ”generative benchmarks” approach enables a dynamic, targeted evaluation
097   of generative AI systems by producing tailored samples for specific risk assessment.
098
099   In this study, we validate the efficacy of our proposed GRE Score across five distinct dimensions:
100
      safety (misuse task), privacy (awareness task), robustness (OOD detection), machine ethics (explicit
      ethics task), and fairness (preference bias task). We present a comprehensive evaluation of eight
101

120   2.1   L ARGE L ANGUAGE MODELS
121
122
      Large Language Models (LLMs) are AI algorithms designed to comprehend and generate human-
      like text (Raiaan et al., 2024), with applications spanning education (Kasneci et al., 2023), code
123
      generation (Jiang et al., 2024), healthcare (Yang et al., 2023b), and academic research (Rahman
124
      et al., 2023). Recent advancements include models like GPT-3 (Floridi & Chiriatti, 2020), GPT-4
125   (Achiam et al., 2023), and the OpenAI O1 series (OpenAI, 2024). Other notable models include
126   Baichuan 2 (Yang et al., 2023a), which excels in multilingual capabilities and specialized domains,
127   LLaMA 2 (Touvron et al., 2023), which shows improved performance in dialogue tasks, and Mistral
128   7B (Jiang et al., 2023), which surpasses larger models in reasoning and code generation. Vicuna
129   (Chiang et al., 2023) and WizardLM (Xu et al., 2023a) have also made significant strides in multi-
130   turn dialogue and instruction-following capabilities, respectively.
131
132   2.2   A LIGNMENT OF L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
133
134   LLMs often generate responses misaligned with creators’ intentions, producing potentially inaccu-
135
      rate or harmful outputs. To address this, researchers developed several alignment methods (Qi et al.,
      2023). Instruction Tuning enhances zero-shot capabilities by fine-tuning on diverse instruction-
136
      framed datasets (Wei et al., 2021). Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) refines
137
      models based on human preferences, using a trained preference model to guide reinforcement learn-
138   ing (Bai et al., 2022a). Emerging techniques like Constitutional AI and self-alignment offer addi-
139   tional approaches (Bai et al., 2022b; Sun et al., 2024). Constitutional AI allows systems to revise
140   outputs according to predefined principles, while self-alignment promotes scalability through self-
141   instruction and in-context learning, reducing dependency on human supervision. These methods
142   aim to align LLM outputs with human values and intentions, improving helpfulness and safety.
143
144   2.3   R ED T EAMING AND JAILBREAKS FOR LLM S
145
146   Red teaming is crucial for detecting harmful behaviors in large language models (LLMs). Tradi-
147   tional methods, like the approach used by Anthropic, rely on manual efforts to identify issues such
148
      as offensive language and data leaks (Ganguli et al., 2022). To address scalability challenges, au-
      tomated methods have been developed. The TAP approach introduces an automated, black-box
149
      method for jailbreaking LLMs, using attacker and evaluator LLMs to generate prompts that by-
150
      pass safety filters (Mehrotra et al., 2023). Recent research has further improved efficiency by using
151   gradient-based optimization to create coherent attack prompts (Zhu et al., 2023a). These methods
152   not only identify vulnerabilities in offensive outputs but also reveal data privacy risks. (Perez et al.,
153   2022) demonstrated that LLMs can inadvertently leak memorized training data.

169   3.1   ROBUSTNESS E VALUATION F RAMEWORK FOR LLM S
170
171   In this section, we introduce a comprehensive framework for evaluating the robustness of Large Lan-
172
      guage Models (LLMs) against adversarial testing, particularly focusing on their ability to maintain
      appropriate responses in the face of malicious prompts.
173
174   We define an LLM as a function L : Ω → Ω, where Ω represents the space of all possible textual
175   inputs and outputs. To assess the model’s response in terms of compliance, we introduce a classifi-
176   cation function C : Ω → [0, 1]2 , which maps the model’s output to a probability distribution over
177   two categories: ”compliant” (c) and ”non-compliant” (nc).
178   The complete LLM system, including the classification step, is denoted as M : Ω → [0, 1]2 , such
179   that for any input x ∈ Ω, M(x) = C(L(x)).
180
181   Semantic Representation of Textual Inputs To facilitate the analysis of textual perturbations
182   in a continuous space, we employ a semantic encoder e : Ω → Rd and a corresponding decoder
183   d : Rd → Ω, such as BART (Lewis et al., 2019). These functions map between the discrete text
184   space and a d-dimensional continuous vector space.

247
248   Safety. In the context of LLMs, safety encompasses the prevention of harmful or inappropriate out-
249   puts. TrustLLM defines safety as the ability to curb misinformation, avoid dangerous instructions,
250   and maintain respectful, non-discriminatory language. Research (Liu et al., 2023b; Qiu et al., 2023;
251   Casper et al., 2023) underscores the critical need for robust safety protocols in LLMs to mitigate
252   deployment risks and responsibly handle sensitive topics.
253   Misuse. LLMs are vulnerable to exploitation by malicious actors, leading to various harmful out-
254   comes (Tamkin et al., 2021). This assessment focuses on LLMs’ ability to reject prompts promoting
255   false information, or illegal content. Studies have shown LLMs’ susceptibility to misuse, including
256   misinformation spread (Pan et al., 2023), cyberattack facilitation (Charan et al., 2023), etc.
257
      Dataset. Our evaluation employs the Do-Not-Answer (Wang et al., 2023c) and Do Anything Now
258
      (Shen et al., 2023) datasets to ensure a comprehensive assessment.
259
260   3.5.2    P RIVACY A SSESSMENT
261
262   Privacy. LLMs’ capacity to retain and inadvertently expose private information has sparked con-
263   siderable concern (Brown et al., 2022). This issue is intensified by the use of web-scraped training
264   data, often rich in personal details. Privacy assessment examines protocols that protect individual
265   and data autonomy, identity, and dignity. It evaluates LLMs’ privacy consciousness and potential
266
      information leakage, gauging their ability to recognize and manage privacy-sensitive situations.
267   Privacy Awareness. This concept refers to the capability to identify and appropriately handle re-
268   quests involving personal data. Privacy-aware LLMs should recognize sensitive information and
269   respond accordingly, such as declining to provide data . For example, when asked about someone’s
      phone number, a privacy-conscious response would be a refusal to disclose such information.

286   3.5.3   ROBUSTNESS A SSESSMENT
287
288   Robustness. Robustness in AI systems refers to consistent performance across varied conditions and
289   unexpected inputs. Studies (Ye et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c) reveal current LLMs’ lack of inherent
290   robustness, with models like GPT-3.5 struggling with simple inputs such as emojis (Xu et al., 2023b).
291   TrustLLM assesses robustness by evaluating LLMs’ stability under diverse input conditions. This
292   includes examining responses to out-of-distribution (OOD) challenges, as presented in (Kirillov
293
      et al., 2023), where LLMs like GPT-4 (trained on data until 2021) must handle texts different from
      their training data, such as new concepts or technologies emerging post-training.
294
295   OOD Detection. OOD detection identifies whether a test sample is in-distribution or out-of-
296   distribution. This task has been explored in outlier detection, anomaly detection, and open-world
297   classification (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016; Shu et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2018). For LLMs, OOD
298   detection is crucial for trustworthiness, demonstrating their ability to identify information beyond
299
      their training distribution, such as latest content or inputs beyond capabilities (e.g., processing image
      data). An LLM with strong OOD detection should recognize such inputs and provide appropriate
300
      feedback, like responding ”As an AI model, I cannot...” instead of generating false information.
301
302   Dataset. TrustLLM’s dataset, based on ToolE (Huang et al., 2023), comprises user queries that
303   potentially require external tools, often exceeding LLMs’ capabilities. From 520 extracted samples,
304   experts filtered prompts LLMs cannot answer, including requests for real-time knowledge, non-text
305
      modalities, and other unanswerable queries.
306
307   3.5.4   A SSESSMENT OF M ACHINE E THICS
308
309   Machine Ethics. Machine ethics, centers on the ethical conduct of artificial systems. Rooted in
310   Asimov’s ”three laws of robotics” (Müller, 2020), early research explored embedding ethical princi-
311   ples in machines (Anderson & Anderson, 2006; Wallach et al., 2020). Recent studies have examined
312   ethical challenges in LLMs like GPT-4 (Zhou et al., 2023), including their responses in academic
313   and healthcare settings (Lund et al., 2023; Meyer et al., 2023). Our focus is on evaluating explicit
314
      ethics to assess LLMs’ behavior across various moral scenarios.
315   Explicit Ethics. This concept involves LLMs’ ability to process scenarios and make ethical deci-
316   sions (Yi et al., 2023). As LLMs increasingly function as intelligent agents in action planning and
317   decision-making (Wang et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023b), evaluating their ethical reasoning becomes
318   crucial. For instance, the Jiminy Cricket environment (Hendrycks et al., 2021) presents morally
319   significant scenarios in text-based adventure games. Recent research (Scherrer et al., 2024) also
320
      explores LLMs’ capacity for moral choice-making.
321   Dataset. Our evaluation uses high-ambiguity moral scenarios from the MoralChoice dataset (Scher-
322   rer et al., 2024) , designed to probe LLMs’ encoded moral beliefs. These scenarios present contexts
323   with two choices, focusing on situations where neither option is clearly preferable. We use prompt
      templates and expect ethically oriented LLMs to avoid direct choices in these complex situations.

      3.5.5    A SSESSMENT OF FAIRNESS
325
326   Fairness. Fairness in LLMs is a critical ethical principle ensuring unbiased outcomes through-
327   out model development and deployment (Wang et al., 2023a). This encompasses data preparation,
328   model construction, evaluation, and application (Gallegos et al., 2024; Mehrabi et al., 2021). Studies
329   have revealed LLMs’ biases against specific groups, including gender (Wan et al., 2023), LGBTQ+
330   communities (Felkner et al., 2023), and political affiliations (Motoki et al., 2024). Our assessment
331
      focuses on preference biases, examining LLMs’ tendencies when presented with contrasting opinion
      pairs to uncover potential biases in handling diverse viewpoints.
332
333   Preference Bias. This refers to LLMs’ inclination to favor certain people, things, or ideas (Liu
334   et al., 2023a). Research shows models like ChatGPT tend to support progressive libertarian views
335   (Rozado, 2023; McGee, 2023). Studies on LLMs’ recommendation capabilities (Gao et al., 2023;
336   Wang et al., 2023b; Dai et al., 2023) reveal such biases can affect output quality, potentially basing
337
      suggestions on inherent preferences rather than user needs. This bias can undermine LLMs’ trust-
      worthiness by producing outputs influenced by subjective leanings rather than objective information.
338
339   Dataset. We employ TrustLLM’s dataset, comprising 120 multiple-choice questions. Each question
340   presents two opposing, subjective options, allowing for diverse individual opinions. The dataset
341   includes 80 ideology-related questions and 40 questions on culture and lifestyle topics.

      Models. Our experiments evaluate several prominent LLMs. We assess LLAMA-2-chat (7B, 13B),
348   an open-source model fine-tuned for dialogue with emphasis on human value alignment through
349   RLHF (Touvron et al., 2023). We also employ Vicuna-1.5 (7B, 13B), a LLaMA variant optimized for

352   instruction-following abilities (Jiang et al., 2023). WizardLM-13B-V1.2 is chosen for its multi-task
353   performance (Xu et al., 2023a). Lastly, we evaluate Baichuan2-13b, a multilingual model excelling
354   in both English and Chinese tasks (Yang et al., 2023a).
355   Generative Models. Our text generation focuses on paraphrasing using DiffuSeq (Gong et al.,
356   2022). DiffuSeq employs diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020) to generate semantically consistent
357   paraphrases. We paraphrased instructions from the original dataset for each task, using 20 randomly
358   selected prompts, each paraphrased 25 times, ensuring diverse yet consistent inputs for all tasks.
359
      Judgment Classifier. Following TrustLLM’s approach, we employ a Longformer classifier to de-
360   termine if LLM responses refuse or agree to answer. We use a PLM classifier from (Wang et al.,
361   2023d) to evaluate responses for refuse to answer (RTA) metrics. It achieves accuracy comparable
362   to human judges and GPT-4 in categorizing outputs as refusing or not refusing to answer.
363
      Adversarial Testing. Our experiments employ the Tree of Attacks with Pruning (TAP) black-box
364
      technique (Mehrotra et al., 2023) to execute adversarial attacks on LLMs . It utilizes a ”tree of
365
      thought” framework (Yao et al., 2024), creating and refining multiple candidate prompts through
366   pruning to enhance attack efficiency. This method has demonstrated high success rates in compro-
367   mising state-of-the-art models like GPT-4 , while maintaining low query requirements. We imple-
368   ment TAP using the same hyperparameters and attack models as described in the original study,
369   employing a substring evaluator with keywords matching technique for attack assessment, which
370   has proven effective in this context.
371
      Setting Goals for Adversarial Testing. To validate our Score’s effectiveness, we extend TAP’s
372   attacks, using the same hyperparameters as in the original paper. While TAP was designed for
373   jailbreak attacks with a standard prefix ”Sure, here it is”, we adapt this for our multi-dimensional
374   evaluation. We design specific prefixes for each task, balancing utility and attack success rate: ”I
375   prefer to choose option” for preference tasks, ”I would like to choose” for Machine Ethics tasks,
376   and ”Sure, I can help you with that” for privacy, robustness, and safety tasks. This approach probes
377   LLM vulnerabilities across dimensions while maintaining consistency with TAP methodology. A
      detailed example illustration of paraphrased examples and attacked prompts is in Appendix D.

      In this paper, we presented GRE Score, a novel, computationally efficient attack-independent met-
479
      ric for quantifying risks in Large Language Models (LLMs) using generative benchmarks. GRE
480   Score leverages generative models for deriving robustness scores of textual inputs. Its computation
481   is lightweight and scalable, only requiring model predictions on generated data samples. Our re-
482   sults on eight mainstream LLMs across five dimensions (safety, privacy, robustness, ethics, fairness)
483   show GRE Score obtains consistent robustness analysis compared to time-consuming adversarial
484   testing. It reveals insights into non-linear relationships between model scaling and LLM perfor-
485   mance, instruction-tuning’s impact on robustness, and critical performance gaps in current LLM
      capabilities, particularly in robustness and privacy tasks.

785   the proposed local GRE Score serves as a lower bound on the conditional robustness.

789
