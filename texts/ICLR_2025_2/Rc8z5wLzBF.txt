                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recent advancements in multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have aimed
012            to integrate and interpret data across diverse modalities. However, the capac-
013            ity of these models to concurrently process and reason about multiple modalities
014            remains underexplored, partly due to the lack of comprehensive modality-wise
015            benchmarks. We introduce OmniBench, a novel benchmark designed to rigor-
016            ously evaluate models’ ability to recognize, interpret, and reason across visual,
017            acoustic, and textual inputs simultaneously. We define language models capable
018            of such tri-modal processing as the omni-language models (OLMs). OmniBench
019            is distinguished by high-quality human annotations, ensuring that accurate re-
020
               sponses require integrated understanding and reasoning across all three modali-
               ties. Our main findings reveal that: i) open-source OLMs exhibit critical limita-
021
               tions in instruction-following and reasoning capabilities within tri-modal contexts;
022
               and ii) most baselines models perform poorly (below 50% accuracy) even when
023            provided with alternative textual representations of images or/and audio. These re-
024            sults suggest that the ability to construct a consistent context from text, image, and
025            audio is often overlooked in existing MLLM training paradigms. To address this
026            gap, we curate an instruction tuning dataset of 84.5K training samples, OmniIn-
027            struct, for training OLMs to adapt to multimodal contexts. We advocate for future
028            research to focus on developing more robust tri-modal integration techniques and
029            training strategies to enhance OLM performance across diverse scenarios. Codes
030            and data could be found at our repo.

      1   I NTRODUCTION

034
      The rapid advancement of artificial intelligence has ushered in a new era of multimodal large lan-
035
      guage models (MLLMs), capable of processing and interpreting diverse data types mainly involving
036   images, audio, and text (Li & Lu, 2024). These models aim to emulate human-like understanding of
037   the world by integrating information across multiple sensory modalities and learning a comprehen-
038   sive context from the environment. While significant strides have been made in developing MLLMs
039   that can handle two of the modalities, the ability to concurrently process and reason about the three
040   aforementioned modalities remains a frontier yet to be fully explored.
041
      The social impact of these MLLMs is far-reaching, providing transformative capabilities for a vari-
042   ety of domains. In healthcare, VLMs and ALMs have contributed to diagnosing (Liu et al., 2023a;
043   Hemdan et al., 2023), and potentially combining three modalities (Meskó, 2023). The integration
044   of all vision, audio and text modalities is expected to significantly improve diagnostic accuracy and
045   patient interaction, making healthcare more accessible and efficient. In urban environments, ALM
046   can contribute to improving safety and traffic management by incorporating urban sound event de-
047   tection during autonomous driving, such as recognizing audio of emergency vehicles and recognize
048   their types or location with supplementary visual modality (Sun et al., 2021). In addition, audio
049   contributes to biodiversity monitoring (Terenzi et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2024a) and can be greatly
050
      enhanced by MLLM’s ability to analyse both audio and video from a variety of sensors. Finally, it
      may help robotics or LLM agents to provide better human-computer/robotic interaction (HCI/HRI)
051
      service in day-to-day life (Liang et al., 2024b; Su et al., 2023).
052
053   The challenge in advancing MLLMs lies not only in their development but also in our capacity to
      evaluate their performance comprehensively. Current benchmarks often solely focus on image or

056   audio-language models (ALMs) (Chu et al., 2023a; Deng et al., 2023). This gap in evaluation tools

076   To address this critical need, we introduce OmniBench, a pioneering universal multimodal bench-
077   mark designed to rigorously evaluate MLLMs’ capability to recognize, interpret, and reason across
078   visual, acoustic, and textual inputs simultaneously, which we define as the omni-understanding and
079   reasoning ability of the omni-language models (OLMs) (Sun et al., 2024; Zhan et al., 2024; Lu et al.,
080   2024b). For instance, one can only derive the correct answer of the sample question in Figure 1 by:
081   1) recognizing elements from the given image and audio to reconstruct the context; 2) interpreting
082   the semantics and relationships among the multimodal objects according to the textual instruction
083   formed as question and options; 3) reasoning and then answering with the complementary infor-
084
      mation from all the modalities. We distinguishes OmniBench by enforcing a unique constraint: ac-
      curate responses necessitate an integrated understanding and reasoning of all multimodal contexts.
085
      This approach ensures a more realistic and challenging assessment of multimodal large language
086
      models, mirroring the complex, interconnected nature of human cognition. To ensure the evaluation
087   reliability, the development of OmniBench relies on high-quality human annotations. Furthermore,
088   OmniBench additionally includes the answer rationales provided by the annotators to enhance the
089   validity and ensure the benchmark aligned with human-level understanding.
090
      Our initial findings using OmniBench reveal critical limitations in the omni-understanding capabil-
091
      ities of existing MLLMs:
092
093                   • Although the existing open-source omni-language models have been trained with data in
094                     the three modalities, most of them can surpass the performance of random guess accuracy
095                     but sometimes hard to follow the instruction when provided with image and audio together
096
                        in certain cases.
097                   • Compared to the open-source OLMs, the proprietary models perform better overall but
098                     suffer from more accuracy drops when ablating the image or audio input.
099                   • In the context of using text as an alternative source of information to corresponding audio
100                     and images, the open-source VLMs and ALMs show relatively better results but remain in
101                     a preliminary level of capability to understand the given tri-modality context.
102   These results underscore the importance of OmniBench as a tool for identifying areas of improve-
103   ment and guiding research in multimodal systems. In the following sections, we 1) detail the
104   data collection protocol of OmniBench; 2) present our evaluation results on current state-of-the-art
105   MLLMs; 3) introduce the OmniInstruct dataset for omni-language model supervised fine-tuning;
106   and 4) discuss the implications of our findings for the future of research and development. Through
107   OmniBench, we aim to catalyze advancements in MLLMs, pushing the boundaries of artificial in-
      telligence towards true omni-understanding capabilities.

      Multimodal Large Language Models. Recent advancements in multimodal large language mod-
111   els (MLLMs) have aimed to integrate and interpret data across diverse modalities. In the audio
112   domain, models like Whisper (Radford et al., 2022), BEATs (Chen et al., 2022), MERT (Li et al.,
113   2023b), and CLAP (Wu et al., 2023b) have been developed as specialized encoders for speech,
114   general audio, acoustic music, and music-text, respectively. These have been incorporated into
115   more comprehensive systems such as SALMONN (Tang et al., 2023), LTU (Gong et al., 2023),
116   Mu-llama, MusiLingo, and Audio-Flamingo. Notable progress in audio perception and instruction-
117   following includes SALMONN (Tang et al., 2023), BLSPN (Wang et al., 2023a), Speech-LLaMAN
118   (Wu et al., 2023a), and Qwen-Audio (Chu et al., 2023b), all demonstrating promising capabilities in
119
      audio-focused dialogues. In the visual domain, large visual language models have made significant
      strides, often leveraging pre-trained image encoders (Dosovitskiy, 2020; Touvron et al., 2020; Liu
120
      et al., 2021; Radford et al., 2021; Zhai et al., 2023). Notable examples include BLIP2 (Li et al.,
121
      2023a), which uses a Q-Former for visual-textual alignment, LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b), which em-
122   ploys GPT-4 generated instruction data, and its successor LLaVA-Next (Liu et al., 2024a). Building
123   on the LLaVA framework, models like QwenVL (Bai et al., 2023), CogVLM (Wang et al., 2023b),
124   and Yi-VL (Young et al., 2024) have achieved significant success through extensive pre-training.
125   Despite these advancements, most existing MLLMs focus on a single modality for input processing
126   while generating textual responses. While some models can process textual, audio, and visual inputs
127   simultaneously, open-source models in this field generally exhibit less competitive capabilities com-
128   pared to their closed-source counterparts. In this context, we define omni-language models (OLMs)
129   as those capable of processing at least three different modalities of data simultaneously1 .
130
131   Multimodal Understanding Benchmark. The vision-language benchmarks aim to test models’
132
      ability to combine visual and language data in tasks like OCR, spatial awareness, multimodal infor-
      mation retrieval (e.g. SciMMIR (Wu et al., 2024a)), and multimodal reasoning skills. MM-Vet (Yu
133
      et al., 2023) focuses on visual question answering (VQA), requiring models to interpret visual data
134
      and respond to queries. MMBench (Liu et al., 2023c) evaluates models via multiple-choice tasks
135   in both Chinese and English, covering diverse domains. MMStar (Chen et al., 2024a) conducts
136   multi-task evaluations to test multimodal fusion capabilities. MMMU (Yue et al., 2024) and CM-
137   MMU (Zhang et al., 2024) assess model performance on complex vision-language tasks, emphasiz-
138   ing sophisticated multimodal reasoning. MMRA (Wu et al., 2024b) is designed to evaluate the mod-
139   els’ multi-image relational association capability. In addition, there are several audio-understanding
140   benchmarks. Aishell1 (Bu et al., 2017), Aishell2 (Du et al., 2018), and Librispeech (Panayotov et al.,
141   2015) are designed for automatic speech recognition, while ClothoAQA targets audio QA tasks. For
142   automatic audio captioning and vocal sound classification, researchers have curated Clotho (Drossos
143
      et al., 2020) and VocalSound (Gong et al., 2022). However, there is a significant lack of comprehen-
      sive understanding benchmarks to assess MLLMs’ ability to simultaneously process complementary
144
      information from the textual, audio, and visual inputs.
145
146
      Audio-Visual Understanding Datasets. In previous works on audio-visual question answering
147
      (AVQA), the focus has predominantly been on identifying visual objects, sounds, and their interre-
148   lations to foster multimodal understanding. For instance, the Pano-AVQA dataset (Yun et al., 2021)
149   explores 360-degree panoramic video understanding with 5.4k videos and 51.7k QA pairs. However,
150   it limits its scope to identifying existing objects or locations, omitting questions on causal inference
151   and abstract concepts. Similarly, the Music-AVQA dataset (Li et al., 2022) comprises 9.3k videos,
152   including 1.9k synthesized entries, and 45.9k QA pairs. These videos typically feature simple sce-
153   narios of music performances by one or two players or within a music chamber. The questions focus
154   on existing objects, time and location, counting, and relationships but fail to address special symbols
155   like music score images or causal inference. The Music-AVQA-2.0 dataset (Liu et al., 2024c) en-
156
      hances Music-AVQA by collecting 1,230 manually curated musical ensemble performance videos
      and 8.1k newly created QA pairs designed to complement and diversify the original dataset, address-
157
      ing biases in annotation and instrument types. However, it maintains the original types of QA pairs,
158
      not expanding into new categories of questions. The AVQA dataset (Yang et al., 2022), contains 57k
159   QA pairs that do not necessarily require integration of both modalities for answering, illustrating its

176   3     O MNI B ENCH
177
178   The OmniBench aims to create the first comprehensive benchmark for evaluating multimodal large
179   language models that support simultaneous image, audio, and text inputs. While OmniBench is
180   designed to evaluate the understanding capability of MLLMs on cross-modality complementary
181
      information, the models are required to interpret the multimodal input and provide accurate text
      answer. The problem could be formulated as following: given a tuple of (image, audio, text), the
182
      model is required to recognize the objects, re-build the contexts, and conduct reasoning based on
183
      the given information. The design logic and statistics of the dataset and the annotation protocols are
184   introduced in this section.

      3.2      A NNOTATION P ROTOCOL
256
      Annotation Scheme. Our annotation scheme is built upon a fundamental principle: the correct
257
      answer to each question must require information from both the image and audio components. This
258   ensures that the benchmark effectively evaluates the model’s ability to analyze information across
259   modalities. As shown in Figure 3, we implemented a rigorous annotation pipeline consisting of three
260   stages: initial annotation, human inspection, and model inspection. Data samples that failed to meet
261   our criteria at any stage were returned to annotators for revision, ensuring high-quality, multimodal-
262   dependent samples. Through the whole process, 16 annotators and 5 quality inspectors are involved,
263   all are full-time industrial data annotation employee with higher education backgrounds.
264   The questions are formalized as multi-choice question-answering (MCQ) but try to maintain a con-
265   sistent logic that suggests the only one possible and accurate answer, i.e., they can be potentially
266   further re-organized into blank filling questions. Furthermore, when constructing the options, the
267   annotators need to ensure at least one confusing wrong option. To ensure question difficulty, an-
268   notators were required to verify that questions and options were not trivially easy, lacked distin-
269   guishable patterns, and could not be answered by state-of-the-art MLLMs using image information
      alone. GPT-4 are allowed to use to provide initial annotator self-assessments of question quality.

285
      We implemented strict measures to maintain diversity across the dataset. This includes varying
      image and audio sources, limiting the frequency of individual speakers in audio clips to no more than
286
      five occurrences, and restricting the replication of similar instructions or questions. For instance,
287
      questions about specific environmental contexts were limited up to three samples. Importantly,
288   annotators were required to provide rationales for correct answers, detailing the specific information
289   that should be derived from the image and audio modalities respectively. This approach not only
290   aided in quality inspection but also laid the groundwork for future fine-grained evaluation.

292
      Quality Control. Our quality control process was two-fold, including human inspection round
293
      and automatic inspection round assisted by MLLM. First, all annotated instruction-response pairs
294   undergo cross-inspection by human annotators. Inspectors provide detailed reasons for any sam-
295   ples that failed to meet our stringent criteria, allowing for targeted revisions. Samples that pass
296   human inspection are then subjected to a secondary check using a vision-language model LLaVA-
297   1.6-34B (Liu et al., 2024a), where the the automatic quality inspection model is selected by consid-
298   ering trade-off between efficiency and performance. This automated process evaluates each sample
299   under various ablation settings: image and text only, audio transcript and text only, and text only
300   (repeated three times). Samples are only accepted if the model either rejected the task or made
301   mistakes under these limited-information scenarios, confirming the necessity of both visual and au-
302
      ditory information for correct responses. We plot the distribution of the inspection frequency of the
      passed samples in Figure 4, where we could find that 76% (868) of the passed samples do not require
303
      further modification under a well-defined annotation framework and 21.1% of them requiring 1-2
304
      times of revision. During the iterative quality checking, 9.58% (121) QA pairs are defined as “hard
305   to recycle” and dumped after revisions and discussions.

308   3.3    O MNI I NSTRUCT
309
      To improve the model capability of tri-modal reasoning, we develop the OmniInstruct dataset to
310
      facilitate the supervised fine-tuning of models. This dataset leverages the following data sources: the
311
      MSRVTT-QA (Xu et al., 2017), AVQA (Yang et al., 2022) and Music-AVQA2.0(Liu et al., 2024c),
312   all of which contain visual, audio and corresponding QA text resources. MSRVTT-QA and AVQA
313   consist of short video clips, typically ranging from 10 to 20 seconds, with minimal scene changes,
314   and music-AVQA 2.0 dataset include 1 minute music performance video. We only adopt the train
315   and validation split of this dataset and regard the whole OmniBench as the test set of the task.
316
      To construct a dataset that aligns with the challenges proposed in OmniBench, we enhanced each
317
      question to connect with an audio track and an image extracted from the corresponding video and
318   filter it with VLMs for better quality. Notably, we avoid the first and last five frames of each video to
319   exclude transitional or obscure incomplete scenes that might distort the task’s focus. For MSRVTT-
320   QA train and valid subset, we discard videos without audio tracks and retain a dataset comprised
321   6,176 videos from the original set that include audio tracks alongside 151.7k QA pairs directly re-
322   lated to these videos. Then we use InternVL-2-76B to filter the questions from the three datasets
323   mentioned above to delete (1) questions that can be answered only with an image, (2) questions
      irrelevant with image, potentially answerable only with audio, and (3) ambiguous or non-logical

342   2023; Ormazabal et al., 2024; Anthropic, 2024). We do not conclude them as in the group of VLMs,
343   ALMs or OLMs (even not a single model) in our context at the moment since the mechanisms behind
344   these models are not revealed2 . Besides, we invite three musician with higher education background
345   to test on the benchmark and use the average accuracy as a human expert baseline.
346
      Omni-Understanding Evaluation. The main focus of OmniBench is to evaluate how well could
347
      the MLLMs understand and reconstruct the context given information from image (I), audio (A) and
348   text (T ) modalities. Setting up questions with four available options for the models, we use accuracy,
349   i.e., the ratio matched letter of the correct option and model response, as the evaluation metric
350   (n.b., the accuracy of a random guess model is 25% under this setting). Additionally, we test the
351   models in an ablation setting of removing one of the image or audio inputs to further reveal a more
352   comprehensive reasoning capability of the baselines and verify the robustness of our benchmark.
353   For baseline systems, please refer to section 4.
354
      Textual Approximation of Image and Audio. For most of the existing MLLMs that only support
355
      two input modalities ((I, T ) or (A, T )), we build up a simulated evaluation setting allowing us to
356   explore the potential of these models to become omni-language models in the future. We use the
357   audio transcript (A′ ) annotated by human as the alternative of the audios to enable the evaluation
358   on vision-language models. Regarding the audio-language models, we generate high-quality de-
359   tailed captions of images (I ′ ) automatically with a state-of-the-art VLM, InternVL-2-76B. In such
360   an approximated evaluation setting, models go through the same process of inference and metric
361   calculation as the vanilla one with textual alternatives of images or audios.

      6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE S TUDY
533
      The proposed novel multimodal benchmark, OmniBench, reveals that current open-source multi-
534   modal large language models struggle with simultaneous processing of visual, acoustic, and tex-
535   tual inputs. We observed a general bias towards speech audio and superior performance of vision-
536   language models over audio-language models when using textual approximations. These findings
537   underscore the need for more appropriate architecture designs for multimodal integration, diverse
538   datasets for training, and techniques to reduce modality bias. OmniBench serves as a crucial tool
539   for guiding advancements in multimodal language models, driving progress towards more advanced
      and versatile models towards human-like multimodal understanding and reasoning.
