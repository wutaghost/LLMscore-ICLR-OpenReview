009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Investigating phenomena such as Alignment and Local Elasticity is essential
012            for understanding feature space of Neural Networks and enhancing performance
013            across a wide range of tasks. In this context, we investigate the emergence of
014            these phenomena in two-layer neural networks performing a classification task.
015            This paper reveals Alignment and Local Elasticity emergence condition after one
016
               step of training are identical. In particular, we demonstrate that intra-class fea-
               tures are more aligned when the inner product of their mean and the covariance
017
               of the training data-label i.e. train-unseen similarity is large, with stronger Local
018
               Elasticity occurring under this condition. We validate our theory through experi-
019            ments with a two-layer network showing that both Alignment and Local Elasticity
020            improve as the train-unseen similarity increases. Furthermore, we claim that our
021            analysis provides both theoretical and practical insights into the relationship be-
022            tween train-unseen similarity, alignment, and the improvement of clustering per-
023            formance on unseen data for neural networks trained on similar domain data. This
024            is supported by experiments, including a multi-layer CNN setup and detailed dis-
025            cussions. Specifically, we show that higher train-unseen similarity improves Re-
026            call@1 in two-layer networks and that Alignment and Recall@1 exhibit a positive
027
               correlation in metric learning. We also present novel techniques for deriving oper-
               ator norm bounds of non-centered Sub-Gaussian matrices, extending conventional
028
               regression analysis with standard Gaussian assumptions to the binary classifica-
029
               tion setting.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Representation learning has been advanced thanks to the introduction of deep learning (Goodfellow
035   et al., 2016; Bengio et al., 2014), surpassing the generalization performance of the conventional
036   machine learning techniques (Bach, 2016; Sánchez & Perronnin, 2011). However, the underlying
037   feature training dynamics that enable deep network to learn more generalizable features An et al.
038   (2023); Radford et al. (2021) remain unclear, prompting studies aimed at theoretically resolving
039
      this issue (Damian et al., 2022; Abbe et al., 2021). To understand the learning dynamics, we argue
      that the following three challenges must be addressed: First, under what conditions does learning
040
      occur (He & Su, 2019)? Second, to what extent does learning take place under those conditions e.g.
041
      Local Elasticity (Dan et al., 2023)? Third, how are the resulting features structured after learning
042   e.g. Alignment (Wang & Isola, 2022; Beaglehole et al., 2024)?
043
044   One approach to addressing this challenge is the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) (Jacot et al., 2020).
045
      NTK studies have explored the alignment structure of features and the concept of Local Elasticity
      in NTK (Seleznova et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2020; Atanasov et al., 2021; Shan & Bordelon, 2022).
046
      However, the NTK operates under a lazy training regime, and its empirical variants exhibit signifi-
047
      cant discrepancies in modeling neural networks (Chizat et al., 2020; Vyas et al., 2022; Yang & Hu,
048   2022). This makes it challenging to conduct a theoretical analysis of feature learning without addi-
049   tional assumptions, such as whitened data, feature block structure, or label awareness. On the other
050   hand, Conjugate Kernel (CK) approaches have been studied (Pennington & Worah, 2017; Fan &
051   Wang, 2020; Benigni & Péché, 2022), with a key distinction from NTK in their ability to facilitate
052   the analysis of feature learning (Ba et al., 2022; Dandi et al., 2023; Moniri et al., 2024), thereby
053   offering a framework for explaining generalization performance. Building on these properties, we
      claim that the CK feature learning model not only explains the generalization performance on test

054
      data from the same distribution as the training data but also offers a structural analysis of features
055   derived from data sampled from unseen distributions that differ from the training distribution.
056
057   Deep representations are used in problems where the distributions are “unseen” or “almost similar
058
      but different downstream task data” such as in transfer learning (Yosinski et al., 2014; Weiss et al.,
      2016; Bozinovski, 2020; Galanti et al., 2022), linear probing (Kumar et al., 2022; He et al., 2020;
059
      Kornblith et al., 2019), and metric learning (Huang et al., 2024). In these applications, learned
060
      features remain effective for data outside the training distributions, even though statistical theories
061   suggest that perfect extrapolation is not attainable (Balestriero et al., 2021; Kang et al., 2024; Arm-
062   strong, 1984). Therefore, it is essential to investigate such a problem to advance the deep learning
063   theory. Specifically, this paper investigates the emergence conditions of Alignment Structure and
064   Local Elasticity for data from unseen distributions to address the three challenges mentioned above.

067
068   Conjugate Kernel Many works (Benigni & Péché, 2021; Louart et al., 2017; Hu & Lu, 2022;
069   Goldt et al., 2020) study the CK, which models neural networks and enables the analysis of the
070   structure of the first layer in two-layer networks after the Gradient Descent. Ba et al. (2022) analyze
071   regression tasks in the teacher-student setups to study feature learning in the proportional regime.
072
      They demonstrate that neural networks exhibit superior performance compared to linear models,
      particularly at higher learning rates since the feature learning reflects the structure of the teacher’s
073
      weights. Moniri et al. (2024) utilize Hermite decomposition to analyze how nonlinear features are
074
      learned based on the polynomials. Ba et al. (2023) theoretically compute the condition when neural
075   networks learn the low-dimensional structure of the dataset with spiked covariance Gaussian distri-
076   bution data. Bietti et al. (2022) analyze the loss landscape and sample complexity which enables us
077   to learn a single-index model. Ba et al. (2022); Moniri et al. (2024); Ba et al. (2023); Bietti et al.
078   (2022) argue that in teacher-student settings for solving regression problems with centered Gaus-
079   sian distributions, neural network features can learn the structure of the teacher, thereby improving
080   generalization performance. Unlike these studies, we extend the two-layer network setting to classi-
081   fication with non-centered Sub-Gaussian distributions and examine the phenomena that arise when
082   the network is exposed to input drawn from a distribution different from train distributions. To the
083
      best of our knowledge, our work provides the first analysis of non-centered training distributions.
      We believe this contributes a framework that can be further utilized in analyzing classification.
084
085
      Alignment Structure Alignment has been used with various definitions in the study of neural
086
      network structure and applications. For instance, there are studies on the following: intra-class fea-
087
      ture alignment (Deng et al., 2022; Wang & Isola, 2022), feature-weight alignment (Papyan et al.,
088   2020), feature-label alignment (Shan & Bordelon, 2022; Atanasov et al., 2021), feature-gradient
089   alignment (Ziyin et al., 2024). We are interested in intra-class feature alignment. Therefore, in
090   the following, “Alignment” will refer to intra-class feature Alignment. As training progresses,
091   the Alignment where features of a given class align towards a single point has been observed (Pa-
092   pyan et al., 2020). It is linked to generalization performance on unseen distributions (Liu et al.,
093   2018). For example, some works claim that increasing intra-class alignment of train distributions
094   with inductive bias improves task performance on unseen distributions, particularly in metric learn-
095   ing. (Wang et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2017). However, to the best of our knowledge, the conditions
096
      under which alignment strongly emerges have not been established. In this work, we demonstrate
      that the emergence of higher alignment is governed by the relationship between the training data
097
      and the input data distribution. Specifically, we show that in a binary classification problem, where
098
      β represents the covariance vector of the training data and labels, and µ is the mean of the unseen
099   class conditioned distributions, a larger inner product β ⊤ µ i.e. train-unseen similarity leads to
100   higher alignment, thereby providing a theoretical basis for alignment.
101

107   propose the ULPM to analyze NC treating features and weights as unconstrained free variables (Ji
      et al., 2022; Tirer & Bruna, 2022; Zhu et al., 2021; Fang et al., 2021). However, ULPM, unlike

136   Studies on the concept of Local Elasticity (LE) have been established after observing that data
137   points closer to the training samples are updated more significantly than those farther away (He
138   & Su, 2019). Thus, Local elasticity has been informally described using terms such as “similar-
139   ity/closeness”. In other words, it is argued that the greater the “similarity” between the training data
140   and the input data, the higher the elasticity of the feature. Subsequently, in He & Su (2019), the
141   elasticity score was formalized as a metric to quantify this informally defined notion of “similarity”.
142   Meanwhile, there have been attempts to theoretically understand LE. Zhang et al. (2021) model the
143
      learning process of neural networks using SDE to verify its occurrence, but they have a limitation
      that actual neural networks are not utilized as our CK modeling. Dan et al. (2023) sort training steps
144
      into two phases by whether LE occurs or not using Gradient Flow, but they only empirically ob-
145
      served the basic condition of LE i.e. feature of “similar” sample is updated more, without engaging
146   in theoretical exploration. However, with theoretical assumption and analysis, we establish that this
147   similarity can be measured and expressed as train-unseen similarity.

      This section provides basic definitions and informal Theorems of the results of Alignment and Local
154   elasticity, which will be detailed in section 4. The phenomenon described here is also illustrated in
155   Figure 1. Let θ be the set of every randomly initialized parameter of a neural network, let d, N
156   denote the dimensions of the data space and feature space, respectively, for x ∈ Rd denote F (x) ∈
157   RN is trained feature and F0 (x) is initialized feature and c is given class conditioned distribution.
158   Feature represents a network output obtained by peeling off the last task layer. The Alignment score
159   and Elasticity score are defined as follows:
160
      Definition 1.1 (Alignment score). The Alignment score is defined as the expected inner product
161
      between the features F (x) for two i.i.d. samples of c : Ex,x′ ∼c,θ [F (x)⊤ F (x′ )].

      Definition 1.2 (Elasticity score similar1 to He & Su (2019)). The Elasticity score is defined as the
163   expected L2 distance between F (x), F0 (x) for sample of c: Ex∼c,θ [∥F (x) − F0 (x)∥2 ].
164
165   These two definitions are informally expressed as the Theorem below, which is an approximation
166   with high probability in the proportional regime for a two-layer neural network after one step training
167   and a Gaussian assumption of given class conditional distribution c.
168   Theorem 1.3 (Alignment and Elasticity score (Informal of Theorem 4.2, 4.3)). Let n be the number
169   of data points. Assume x, x′ ∼ N(µ, Σ) be i.i.d random vectors drawn from the arbitrary class
170   conditional distribution given mean µ ∈ Rd and Covariance Σ ∈ Rd×d and the network allows
171   for Hermite expansion. Let β ≜ n√1N X ⊤ y from given training datasaet (X, y). Then train(β)-
172   unseen(µ) similarity |β ⊤ µ| and β ⊤ Σβ is governing the Alignment and the Elasticity score.
173
174   Following Theorem, Alignment and Elasticity score approximately increase as Train-unseen sim-
175   ilarity |β ⊤ µ| and β ⊤ Σβ grow i.e. , with fixed covariance Σ, both scores are approximately poly-
176   nomial to |β ⊤ µ|, which is the similarity between the training sample distributions and the arbitrary
177   class data distributions.
178   It can be interpreted that the closer the unseen distribution is to the training data ( i.e. , higher
179   Train-unseen similarity), the stronger the effect of Local Elasticity (LE) becomes, and leading to
180   a stronger Alignment of features. These implications can be observed in section 4, where compara-
181   ble formulae for the two phenomena are derived, demonstrating their simultaneous occurrence and
182   correlation.
183   For theoretical analysis, we define two-layer networks with elementwise activation function that
184   allows Hermite decomposition to decompose a one step trained feature function into initialized
185   features and polynomial functions. This decomposition is explained thoroughly in subsection 3.1
186   and subsection 3.2. The decomposed feature is analyzed using unseen data distributions assumed to
187   follow Gaussian distributions.
188
      This paper also verifies the following supplementary contributions during our theoretical analysis.
189   We expand the previous two-layer network analysis method, which is based on regression tasks
190   with standard Gaussian train distribution into binary classification with non-centered Sub-Gaussian
191   distribution. This assumption makes two-layer model available to analyze classification problems in
192   further works or any non-centered Sub-Gaussian training data, which is more discussed in section 3.
193
      Finally, we conduct experiments that empirically verify our analyses using a synthetic dataset where
194
      classes of evaluation set are consecutively distant from the training set in section 5.

      6   CONCLUSION
510
511
      In this paper, we explored the emergence of Alignment and Local Elasticity in two-layer neural net-
512
      works, focusing on their behavior when trained in the proportional regime. Our theoretical analysis
513   extends the Conjugate Kernel (CK) framework to classification tasks, providing insights into how
514   neural networks learn feature representations, particularly under Sub-Gaussian data distributions.
515   We demonstrate that both Alignment and Local Elasticity arise simultaneously after just one step
516   of training, especially in cases where data distributions closely resemble the training data. This
517   phenomenon not only helps explain the clustering of representations but also sheds light on why
518   neural networks trained on similar domains serve as effective feature extractors for tasks like metric
519   learning. Furthermore, we validated our theoretical findings through experiments across various se-
520   tups. These experiments confirmed the theoretical predictions, showing that neural networks exhibit
521
      stronger Alignment and Local Elasticity when evaluated on data distributions closer to the training
      set. Additionally, we identified a possible relationship between Recall@1, one of the generalization
522
      performance metrics for unseen distributions, and Alignment. Our work provides a unified frame-
523
      work for understanding feature learning in neural networks and opens avenues for further research
524   in metric learning, transfer learning, and other task domains where neural networks are applied as
525   feature extractors for unseen distributions. We believe this work offers valuable insights into the
526   dynamics of neural networks, contributing to the broader understanding of deep learning theory.

      A    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
865
866   In this study, we have focused on non-centered sub-Gaussian training data, but this framework could
867   be extended to more complex distributions, such as Gaussian mixtures. Exploring these broader
868   classes of data distributions would enrich our understanding of the model’s generalization capabili-
869   ties. By the way, we have found that both Alignment and Local Elasticity are more strongly emerged
870   by train-unseen similarity. However, it is necessary to explore how these two phenomena occur si-
871   multaneously. Furthermore, replacing the MSE loss with softmax cross-entropy could link this work
872   more directly to Neural Collapse research (Ji et al., 2022), providing new insights into the geometric
873   structures emerging during training. Additionally, studying scenarios where the parameters diverge
874
      further from their initialization after the first step of training could offer a long-term perspective on
      the learning dynamics. Moreover, There seems to be a connection between neural network align-
875
      ment and the contraction of the Riemannian metric (Zavatone-Veth et al., 2023). Further research
876
      into this relationship could unveil deeper insights into the geometry of neural networks. Finally,
877   in this study, the average of the Alignment and Elasticity scores was analyzed, and through mul-
878   tiple experiments, the validity of the analysis was supported. Theoretically, this can be extended
879   to concentration as in Loureiro et al. (2021) and Mignacco et al. (2020), and analyzing the condi-
880   tions under which the Alignment and Elasticity scores concentrate around the mean is one of the
881   important research directions.

1854   K.4    ROTATION M ATRIX G ENERATION P ROCESS OF Setup 2
1855
1856   To generate a set of rotation matrices with diverse magnitudes of rotation, we constructed an algo-
1857   rithm that samples k = 300 random matrices, each formed by adding i.i.d. Gaussian noise matrix
1858
       of varying variance to the identity matrix I. The process ensures the generation of rotation matrices
       with varying extents of rotation, from slight to more substantial deviations from the identity matrix.
1859
1860   The rotation matrices are generated as follows:
1861
1862         1. A matrix is initialized as I + ϵ · M , where M is a i.i.d. standard random Gaussian matrix.
1863         2. Using the QR decomposition, we orthogonalize this matrix to ensure it forms a valid rota-
1864            tion matrix.
1865
             3. Finally, if the determinant of the resulting matrix is negative, we flip the sign of the first
1866
                column to maintain a determinant of +1, ensuring it is a valid rotation.
1867
1868   In summary, this method provides a collection of matrices that progressively deviate from I, allow-
1869   ing us to observe and sample rotations of increasing magnitude.

1871   K.5    A DDITIONAL D ISCUSSION OF R ECALL @1 EVALUATION FOR E XPR 1
1872
1873   The Recall@1 results of Expr 1 setting indicate three phases in Recall@1 outcomes.
1874
       The first phase: The learning process fails to improve performance either because the training and
1875
       evaluation data are too distant, as predicted by our theory, or because e is too small for the fea-
1876
       ture extractor to achieve separation. We interpret that either of these factors contributes to the lack
1877   of performance improvement. The second phase: Performance improves as the similarity between
1878   training and evaluation data becomes appropriate, allowing better Recall@1 after training. It is note-
1879   worthy that the improvement also increases along with larger e. This indicates not only the increased
1880   e leading to greater distances between evaluation features but also the Recall@1 improvement with
1881   e as our theory. The third phase: Effective feature separation has already occurred; thus, even with
1882   sufficiently close training data, the learning process does not enhance Recall@1 performance.
1883   We conclude that the first and third phases represent unsuitable configurations for retrieval tasks,
1884   while the second phase provides a dataset which effectively supports training for retrieval tasks and
1885   is explainable by our theory.
