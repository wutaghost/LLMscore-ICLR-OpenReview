                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               We propose SpARQ (outlier-free SpeechLM for Fast Adaptation and Robust
012            Quantization) to tackle the outlier problem of Speech and Language multi-modal
013            Models (SpeechLMs). Our primary observation is that outliers stemming from
014            cross-modal (speech and text) low-rank adaptation and post-training quantiza-
015            tion stages affect the performance of the current SpeechLMs. Methodologically,
016            SpARQ leverages a pretrained language model as its foundation, substituting the
017            traditional attention layer with a novel stabilized outlier-free layer. This modifica-
018            tion eliminates outliers typically arising during cross-modal low-rank adaptation
019            and post-training quantization. The model is then fine-tuned on multi-modal data
020
               using proposed outlier-free architecture, allowing it to handle textLM, speechLM,
               ASR, and TTS tasks through a unified interface while maintaining compatibil-
021
               ity with parameters adapted from standard pretrained LLMs. Consequently, on
022
               the OPT-1.3b model, the proposed framework achieves relative performance im-
023            provements: 41% in cross-modal low-rank adaptation and 45% in post-training
024            quantization, along with a 1.33x training speedup. We benchmark it against state-
025            of-the-art low-rank adaptation and post-training quantization methods.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029   SpeechLM leverages pretrained language models to significantly enhance speech recognition and
030   synthesis technologies, transforming our ability to understand and generate natural speech (Nguyen
031   et al., 2024). Traditional Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) (Park et al., 2019) and Text-to-Speech
032   (TTS) (Hayashi and Watanabe, 2020) systems rely on specialized components like feature extraction,
033
      acoustic or linguistic modeling, and waveform synthesis. In contrast, as illustrated in Figure 1,
      SpeechLM integrates these modalities within a single Large Language Model (LLM) framework,
034
      using a unified token space for both speech and text as input and output. This integration enables
035
      ASR, TTS, speech generation (SpeechLM), and text generation (textLM) tasks within the same
036   framework (Maiti et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023), leveraging the robust capabilities of text-based
037   language models to enhance performance, efficiency, and adaptability in speech-related applications.
038
039
      However, efficiently adapting LLMs to handle speech remains a major challenge (Mehrish et al.,
      2023). While some speech and audio understanding models (Chu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a;
040
      Gong et al., 2023; 2024) have employed efficient techniques like low-rank adaptation or freezing
041
      the entire LLM when integrating new inputs, these methods have not been applied to the SpeechLM
042   framework for generating outputs in both speech and text modalities (Maiti et al., 2023; Yang et al.,
043   2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Défossez et al., 2024). The fundamental differences between text and
044   speech modalities—such as variable sequence lengths and data representations—complicate this
045   adaptation process. Consequently, there is a pressing need for methods to achieve the efficiency gains
046   of low-rank adaptation and post-training quantization for the SpeechLM framework.
047
      Studies have shown that transformer-based models often focus on less informative tokens, resulting in
048   inefficiencies due to outliers (Clark et al., 2019; Kovaleva et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2024; Huang et al.,
049   2024). In multimodal frameworks, outliers present in pretrained LLMs and multimodal generation
050   tasks (Zhang et al., 2020) exacerbate these challenges, and as demonstrated in (Crabbé et al., 2024),
051   multimodal models exhibit a substantial number of outliers in their attention mechanisms. During
052   low-rank adaptation, outliers from the pretrained model mix with those introduced by the multimodal
053   data, leading to distorted outputs and diminished performance. Similarly, in post-training quantization,
      outliers inherited from the pretrained model negatively impact the quality of the final output.

068   To address these issues and enable efficient fine-tuning and post-training quantization (e.g.,
069   SmoothQuant (Xiao et al., 2023)) in transformer-based models with multimodal inputs, we propose a
070   novel stabilized outlier-free layer as a substitute for the conventional attention mechanism (Vaswani
071   et al., 2017). Unlike prior work that applies outlier mitigation during pretraining, we apply our
072   approach during the domain adaptation fine-tuning procedure for low-rank adaptation (LoRA) and
073   post-training quantization on pretrained LLMs. Our design builds upon the outlier-free Hopfield layer
074   introduced by (Hu et al., 2024a), which has been proven to effectively identify and filter out outliers.
075   Our approach enhances transformer-based pretrained Language Learning Models (LLMs) through the
076   integration of a specialized stabilization module within the outlier-free layer. This innovative design
077   enables direct parameter adaptation of existing pretrained vanilla LLMs, eliminating the need for
078   training an outlier-efficient model from scratch. Such an approach preserves the inherent capabilities
079   of pretrained transformers while substantially reducing computational overhead. The framework’s
080   effectiveness is further amplified through the incorporation of low-rank adaptation techniques, such as
081   LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), alongside post-training quantization methods. These implementations yield
082
      marked performance improvements compared to conventional transformer-based models. Through
      this enhancement strategy, we have developed a solution that not only optimizes efficiency but also
083
      increases practical applicability, making it valuable in resource-constrained environments where
084
      training capabilities may be limited.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
114   Discrete Speech Representation. Recent advances in Self-Supervised Learning (SSL) for speech
115   have improved our ability to derive meaningful representations from raw audio data. These advance-
116   ments enable the extraction of discrete speech tokens from SSL models like HuBERT (Hsu et al.,
117   2021) and w2v-BERT (Chung et al., 2021) for various speech-processing applications. These SSL
118
      models generate semantic tokens by clustering learned features, capturing the linguistic content of the
      speech. This approach transforms speech into pseudo-text, facilitating applications in speech-based
119
      natural language understanding and generation. Models like HuBERT cluster continuous speech
120
      features into discrete tokens that represent phonetic or sub-word units, improving the accuracy
121   and efficiency of high-level speech processing tasks, such as TTS (Hayashi and Watanabe, 2020) ,
122   speech-to-speech translation (S2ST) (Lee et al., 2021), and ASR (Park et al., 2019).
123
124
      Speech and Text LMs. Joint modeling of speech and text has gained significant attention in recent
      studies. Initial approaches (Ao et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022) proposed learning shared speech-text
125
      representations with separate encoders and decoders, requiring alignment losses for cross-modal
126
      transfer. Recent methods employ a single model for multiple tasks. For example, SpeechGPT
127   (Zhang et al., 2023) combines audio generation with textLMs, PolyVoice applies speechLM to S2ST
128   (qian Dong et al., 2023), SpiritLM (Nguyen et al., 2024) excels in speech and expressive speech
129   generation, also adapted for related speech tasks, and Voxtlm (Maiti et al., 2024) conducts speech/text
130   generation along with ASR and TTS. We utilize textually pre-trained OPT (Zhang et al., 2022) for
131   better initialization inspired by (Maiti et al., 2024; Hassid et al., 2024) and leverage different speech
132   tokens, ensuring full reproducibility of our work.
133   Low-Rank Adaptation and Post Training Quantization. Low-Rank Adaptation (Xin et al., 2024)
134   and Post Training Quantization (PTQ) (Gholami et al., 2022) are essential techniques for reducing
135   the memory footprint and latency of large foundation models (Bommasani et al., 2021), i.e. huge
136   transformer-based models. Those large foundation models play a crucial role not only in machine
137   learning area but also in a huge scientific area, such as (Zhou et al., 2024) for genomics, (Wang et al.,
138   2023b; Wu et al., 2023) for financial, and (Maiti et al., 2024) for speech. However, large foundation
139   models are resource-intensive. Low-Rank Adaptation and PTQ play crucial roles in deploying these
140   large models on edge devices with limited resources. Significant contributions have been made in
141
      the area of Low-Rank Adaptation (Dettmers et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2021) and PTQ
      (Zafrir et al., 2019; Dettmers et al., 2022). However, these methods typically do not address the
142
      outlier problem during the Low-Rank Adaptation and quantization processes, as highlighted by (Hu
143
      et al., 2024a). To tackle this issue, we propose the Outlier-Free Layer to manage outliers effectively
144   during both the Low-Rank Adaptation and quantization processes.

148   and text modalities by replacing standard transformer layers with a stabilized Outlier-Free Layer. This

150   quantization, addressing instabilities encountered with existing outlier layers.

193   3.2    O UTLIER F REE A RCHITECTURE
194   The Outlier-free layer is our proposed solution to mitigate challenges posed by outliers during the
195   model’s cross-modal low-rank adaptation and post-training quantization processes. The proposed
196   outlier efficient architecture is shown in Figure 2.
197
      Problem Setup. We consider a multimodality speech-text framework where the input is a d-
198
      dimensional speech-text sequence of length L, represented as a matrix X ∈ Rd×L for compatibility
199   with the transformer architecture. The matrix X is then processed by transformer layers, which are
200   composed of multiple self-attention mechanisms followed by feed-forward neural networks.
201
202
      Motivational Example. Studies by (Clark et al., 2019) and (Kovaleva et al., 2019) find that in BERT,
      certain tokens such as delimiters and punctuation marks receive disproportionately high attention
203
      weights compared to other tokens. Additionally, as stated in (Bondarenko et al., 2024) and (Hu
204
      et al., 2024a), tokens, despite having low informational value, tend to attract significant attention
205   probabilities. Such behaviors lead to excessive computational and memory demands during the model
206   training process and contribute to significant performance degradation during model quantization.
207   As demonstrated in (Crabbé et al., 2024), multimodal models exhibit a substantial number of outlier
208   in their attention. We use this observation as a starting point by considering the following attention
209   mechanism:                                                        √

236   3.3 I NTEGRATING THE O UTLIER -F REE A RCHITECTURE INTO S PEECH LM
237   We accommodate the outlier-free architecture into the SpeechLM framework to create our proposed
238   SpARQ system. This integration enables efficient adaptation of pre-trained LLMs to the multi-modal
239   speech-text setting and facilitates effective post-training quantization.
240
      Two main challenges arise in this process. First, adapting text-only pre-trained LLMs to a multi-
241
      modal environment involves handling an expanded vocabulary Vjoint , longer sequences due to speech
242   tokens—which are about five times more numerous than text tokens—and the presence of outliers
243   that hinder both low-rank adaptation and post-training quantization. This complexity makes applying
244   parameter-efficient training methods like LoRA and quantization techniques challenging. Second,
245   since the pre-trained LLMs are based on vanilla transformer architectures, replacing the standard
246   transformer layers with the outlier-free layers can introduce instability. The model parameters are
247   not initially optimized for the new architecture, necessitating a stabilization mechanism to ensure
248   training proceeds smoothly without significant perturbations.
249   Cross-Modal Adaptation. To address the first challenge, we utilize our stabilized outlier-free layers
250   to mitigate the impact of outliers during adaptation. By effectively handling outliers, our approach
251   enables both efficient low-rank adaptation techniques like LoRA and robust post-training quantization
252   methods such as SmoothQuant. This allows the model to manage the expanded vocabulary and longer
253   sequences more effectively, facilitating parameter-efficient training and quantization. Consequently,
254   we reduce trainable parameters and computational overhead compared to full fine-tuning methods used
255   in previous works (Maiti et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2024), and improve quantization performance.
256   Stabilized Outlier-Free Adaptation. For the second challenge, we incorporate a stabilization module
257   within the outlier-free layers. This addition mitigates instability caused by layer replacement, allowing
258   us to initialize directly from pre-trained vanilla LLM parameters without extensive pretraining specific
259   to the outlier-free architecture. As a result, we achieve effective cross-modal learning with improved
260   training stability and significant performance gains in ASR and TTS tasks compared to traditional
261   transformer-based SpeechLM systems.

      5                  D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
463
464   We introduce SpARQ, an outlier-robust multi-modal foundation model for speech-text tasks. SpARQ
465
      addresses the computational challenges arising from outlier effects in modality fusion and cross-
      modality adaptation of SpeechLM. Our approach mitigates outlier impacts in transformer-based
466
      models and enhances both low-rank adaptation and post-training quantization performance. SpARQ
467
      demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods, achieving a 41% relative performance
468   gain in cross-modal low-rank adaptation (Section 4.2) and 45% in quantization (Section 4.1)
469
470   Limitations and Future Work. SpARQ currently has two key limitations. First, it does not support
471
      LoftQ and other SVD-based low-rank adaptation methods that operate on weight matrices. Second,
      due to computational constraints, SpARQ uses neither the 6.7B parameter OPT model nor other large
472
      decoder-based models for pretraining. “ Future work will expand SpARQ to incorporate SVD-based
473
      methods and evaluate performance with larger decoder architectures, including 3B LLama2 and
474   6.7B OPT models. Additionally, the framework’s focus on reducing computational demands could
475   inadvertently amplify biases inherited from pre-trained models. Further investigation is necessary to
476   understand and address the impact of biases within our framework.
477
      Broader Impact. Our methodology advances foundation model fine-tuning and inference through
478
      insights from associative memory models, enhancing both low-rank adaptation and post-training
479
      quantization. This solution enables edge computing deployment of large foundation models and
480   resource-efficient fine-tuning. However, the approach may amplify existing training data biases,
481   potentially disadvantaging underrepresented groups.

1029   We present the hyperparameters used in the low-rank adaptation stage for each model. We use Adam

1034   attention module weights Wk , Wq , Wv , Wo , along with the MLP layer in the attention mechanism.

1044   E.3    H IFI -GAN D ECODER FOR S PEECH S YNTHESIS
1045   Within our SpeechLM framework, the TTS system synthesizes speech by converting the discrete
1046   speech tokens generated by SpeechLM into speech waveforms using a HiFi-GAN-based vocoder.
1047   These discrete speech tokens are derived from a pre-trained HuBERT model with a dictionary size of
1048   200 (HuBERT k=200). The HiFi-GAN vocoder is trained on the LJSpeech-1.1 dataset, downsampled
1049   to 16 kHz, using the HuBERT k=200 unit representations as input features.
1050   The vocoder configuration includes 200 discrete unit embeddings, each with a dimensionality of
1051   128. The model utilizes a ResBlock type 1 architecture with upsampling rates of [5, 4, 4, 2, 2] and
1052   corresponding kernel sizes of [11, 8, 8, 4, 4]. The initial channel size is set to 512, and the vocoder
1053   operates at a 16 kHz sampling rate. We use the Adam optimizer with an initial learning rate of 0.0008.
1054   Training is performed with a batch size of 64 and a code hop size of 320 to ensure proper alignment
1055   between discrete units and waveform segments.
1056   To evaluate the quality of the synthesized speech from our TTS system, we employ an ASR system
1057   based on OpenAI’s Whisper model. The synthesized speech is fed into Whisper to obtain transcrip-
1058   tions, which are then compared to the ground truth text to compute the Word Error Rate (WER). This
1059   WER serves as a measure of the quality of the synthesized speech within the SpeechLM framework.
