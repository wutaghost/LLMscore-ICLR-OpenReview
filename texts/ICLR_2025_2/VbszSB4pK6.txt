009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011
               Prompt tuning, which involves training a small set of parameters, effectively
012            enhances the pre-trained Vision-Language Models (VLMs) to downstream tasks.
013            However, this approach often comes at the cost of flexibility and adaptability when
014            the tuned models are applied to different datasets or domains. In this paper, we
015            revisit the vanilla full fine-tuning for VLMs and show that full fine-tuning is more
016            efficient than prompt tuning under data-limited scenarios. To mitigate the overfitting
017            and catastrophic forgetting issues encountered when fine-tuning the entire VLMs
018            for specific tasks under limited supervision, we propose a framework named CLIP-
019            CITE via designing a discriminative visual-text task, further aligning the visual-
020
               text semantics in a supervision manner, and integrating knowledge distillation
               techniques to preserve the gained knowledge. Extensive experimental results under
021
               few-shot learning, base-to-new generalization, domain generalization, and cross-
022
               domain generalization settings, demonstrate that our method effectively enhances
023            the performance on specific tasks under limited supervision while preserving the
024            versatility of the VLMs on other datasets.

      Prompt tuning Zhou et al. (2022b); Khattak et al. (2023a); Jia et al. (2022) has been proposed to
055   refine pre-trained models using extra parameters while keeping the parameters of VLMs fixed. These
056   methods gain popularity due to efficient parameter utilization and the ability to quickly adapt VLMs
057   to domain-specific information in data-limited scenarios. While the prompt-based tuning strategies
058   enable VLMs to effectively capture domain-specific information with limited supervision, there
059   is a risk that these strategies may compromise the versatility of VLMs where the prompts trained
060   on domain-specific data may struggle to generalize to different domains. A piece of evidence is
061   provided in Fig. 1(c), which shows a transfer experiment under the cross-domain generalization
062   setting. We employ a few-shot setting to train the model using the EuroSAT base training set, followed
063
      by evaluating its performance on both EuroSAT and ImageNet datasets. While the prompt-based
      methods, i.e., CoOp Zhou et al. (2022b) and MaPLe Khattak et al. (2023a) significantly improve the
064
      EuroSAT dataset results, they are at the cost of sacrificing their generalizability on the other datasets.
065
      In particular, their performances on the ImageNet dataset severely lag behind those of the original
066   CLIP model.
067
068   Recent advances in natural language processing and text-to-image generation have demonstrated
069
      that fully fine-tuning can capture domain-specific information, even with limited data Liu et al.
      (2021); Ruiz et al. (2023). When customizing VLMs to specific domains, fine-tuning the entire
070
      models would distribute task-specific knowledge across all parameters (as illustrated in Fig. 1. (b)).
071
      To explore the effect of fully fine-tuning in adapting VLMs, we compare two existing methods:
072   FT-Probe Radford et al. (2021), which fine-tunes the model while adding a linear probe, and FLYP
073   Goyal et al. (2023), which fine-tunes using the contrastive loss from a pre-trained model. Our
074   findings indicate that FT-Probe underperforms on the EuroSAT dataset, whereas FLYP achieves
075   balanced performance across various datasets. From the results, we posit that the key to effective
076   fine-tuning lies in determining the appropriate fine-tuning parameters and optimization objectives
077   to transfer knowledge to specific domains while preserving the model’s generalization capabilities.
078   Therefore, in this paper, we revisit the fully fine-tuning paradigm for VLMs and propose that, with
079   suitable optimization objectives and techniques, fully fine-tuning the inherent parameters of VLMs
080
      can outperform existing parameter-efficient methods in data-limited scenarios.
081   We propose a fine-tuning method called CLIP-CITE that enhances the CLIP’s professionalism on
082   specific domains while preserving its generalization by primarily enhanCing the capability of the
083   Image-Text alignmEnt task. CLIP-CITE fine-tunes all the parameters of both the text encoder and the
084   image encoder, optimizing them to better capture domain-specific features. Additionally, CLIP-CITE
085   incorporates three key aspects to optimize models. Firstly, to quickly equip the domain-specific
086
      information for CLIP, our CLIP-CITE connects the alignment score with the classification probability
      in a way that prioritizes higher alignment scores for image-text pairs belonging to the same class.
087
      Secondly, our approach fine-tunes the entire model using an image-text alignment task, aligning with
088
      the original training objective of the pre-trained CLIP model. This differs from the classification task
089   utilized in Radford et al. (2021), ensuring a consistent training objective throughout the adaptation
090   process. Note that training an image-text alignment task usually requires a large batch Radford
091   et al. (2021); Goyal et al. (2023) in implementation, posing a significant challenge when working
092   with limited data regimes. To overcome this issue, we propose utilizing a class-level image-text
093   alignment task as an alternative to the original instance-level alignment task. Finally, to alleviate
094   the catastrophic forgetting issue, we introduce a vision-language similarity distillation strategy. This
095   strategy regularizes the model by transferring the image-text alignment relationship learned by the
096   pre-trained CLIP model, further ensuring a minimal change in parameters. As shown in the last
097
      row of Fig. 1 (c), our CLIP-CITE enhances EuroSAT dataset performance while simultaneously
      upholding generalization capability on the ImageNet dataset.
098
099   In summary, our highlights are as follows:
100
101          • We revisit the fully fine-tuning in VLMs and propose a simple but efficient fine-tuning
102            method to enhance the VLMs’ professionalism while maintaining their versatility under
103
               limited data supervision. CLIP-CITE comprehensively fine-tunes CLIP to enable it to
               promptly incorporate task-specific information through enhanced image-text alignment and
104
               safeguard the learned knowledge.
105
106          • To verify the effectiveness of our proposed CLIP-CITE method, we conduct an analysis
107            of the parameter changes across various layers of the fine-tuned model, employing diverse
               optimization functions and fine-tuning parameters.

111            but also preserves the original versatility of CLIP on other datasets.

114   2    R ELATED WORK
115
116   Vision-Language Models. In recent years, significant advancements have been made in large-scale
117   pre-trained vision-language models Radford et al. (2021); Jia et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2021);
118   Alayrac et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022); Huang et al. (2023). Representatively, CLIP Radford
119   et al. (2021) and ALIGN Jia et al. (2021) jointly associate the images and their corresponding text
120   descriptions by optimizing a contrastive objective. Training on the millions of image-text pairs, CLIP
121   aligns the image and language space, showing the powerful generalization on downstream tasks.
122
      Based on CLIP, many works seek to transfer the model to special tasks, e.g., zero-shot recognition Lu
      et al. (2024), few-shot image recognition Zhou et al. (2022b;a); Khattak et al. (2023a), segmentation
123
      Rao et al. (2022), and action recognition Rasheed et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024). In this paper, we
124
      leverage multi-modal alignment and the generalization ability of CLIP. We explore the potential of
125   fully fine-tuning CLIP in limited-data scenarios.
126
127   Few-Shot Transfer Learning Based on CLIP. Prompt tuning Zhou et al. (2022b;a); Jia et al. (2022);
128
      Khattak et al. (2023a); Zhang et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024) and fine-tuning Shu et al. (2023);
      Wortsman et al. (2022); Goyal et al. (2023); Kumar et al. (2022) are two main methods to transfer
129
      the CLIP to the downstream tasks. Prompt tuning is widely used in language models Houlsby et al.
130
      (2019); Liu et al. (2023), which raises attention in vision and multi-modality areas Zhou et al. (2022b);
131   Jia et al. (2022); Kirillov et al. (2023). CoOp Zhou et al. (2022b) enhances downstream few-shot
132   image recognition by learning soft textual prompts. Building on this, VPT Jia et al. (2022) and
133   MaPLe Khattak et al. (2023a) explore visual and multi-modal prompts to further improve performance.
134   PromptSRC Khattak et al. (2023b) and CoPrompt Roy & Etemad (2024) introduce regularization
135   techniques for learnable prompts, promoting better generalization in novel scenarios. MetaPrompt
136   Park et al. (2024) leverages meta-learning to optimize multi-modal prompts, adapting effectively to
137   new tasks. Although these prompt tuning methods show efficient and excellent performance, they
138   may fail to overfit the task-specific distribution. As the alternative, fine-tuning methods directly
139
      optimize the model under task-specific situations. WiSE-FT Wortsman et al. (2022), LP-FT Kumar
      et al. (2022) achieves the robustness of fine-tuning via a weight-ensemble manner. CLIPood Shu
140
      et al. (2023) further finetunes the model via the text semantic similarity and model ensemble under
141
      an out-of-distribution situation. A similar work related to our method is FLYP Goyal et al. (2023),
142   which fine-tunes the CLIP model via the pre-trained contrastive objective to obtain the multi-modal
143   alignment ability. In comparison, our method distinguishes the supervised vision-language pairs
144   and incorporates the task-specific into the fine-tuning process. Leveraging this improved image-text
145   alignment task, our method aims to perform more robustly under limited supervision.

150   In this work, we fine-tune the CLIP model Radford et al. (2021) for the scenarios with limited data
151   available. The architecture of CLIP includes two key components: a visual encoder denoted as θI and
152   a text encoder denoted as θT . By aligning language and visual modalities on 400 million text-image
153   web data, CLIP is endowed with zero-shot and open-vocabulary capabilities.
154   To perform zero-shot classification, CLIP utilizes handcrafted text prompts with class labels. These
155   prompts consist of a predefined set of class labels denoted as y ∈ {y1 , y2 , ..., yC }, where C represents
156   the total number of classes. Each prompt typically takes the form of “a photo of a [category]”, where
157   “[category]” corresponds to the class label name. Then, the label prediction ŷ of image x corresponding
158   to the class c is obtained by calculating the cosine similarity scores between the image embedding I
159   and the text embedding T, which is formulated as:

194   3.1    D ISCRIMINATIVE V ISUAL -T EXT A LIGNMENT TASK
195
196   Naive fine-tuning methods for downstream classification tasks typically involve adding a randomly
197   initialized linear classifier on top of the pre-trained visual encoder Radford et al. (2021); Kumar
198   et al. (2022). The whole model is then fine-tuned using the available domain-specific data for the
199   classification task at hand. However, this training strategy often leads to overfitting on the limited
200   available training data, resulting in poor generalization performance on unseen data.
201   To address this limitation, we propose to fine-tune the model with a discriminative visual-text
202   alignment task that combines visual-semantic alignment and image classification. Specifically, we
203   connect the similarity scores between the visual and the text embeddings with the probability that the
204   visual image belongs to the class associated with the text embedding, which is formulated:

      3.2   S UPERVISED C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING
224
      To preserve and enhance the representation capability of the pre-trained CLIP, we argue that aligning
225
      image and text remains essential, as it corresponds to the task employed in the training of the original
226   CLIP models. However, it is worth noting that aligning images and texts can often require a large
227   batch size, which may not be suitable in situations where data availability is limited.
228
229

251   3.3   V ISION -L ANGUAGE S IMILARITY D ISTILLATION
252
      While fine-tuning can improve performance on downstream tasks, it would suffer from potential
253
      challenges such as catastrophic forgetting and decreased generalization capabilities on the other
254
      datasets. To remedy this issue, we introduce a novel vision-language similarity distillation loss to
255   distill the modal consistency from the pre-trained CLIP to the fine-tuned model. Specifically, the
256   vision-language similarity distillation loss is defined as:
257
258

259                                   LV LD =            DKL (p (ŷ|x) , p̂ (ŷ|x)) ,                      (5)
260                                               x∈B
261
262   where p(ŷ|x) from Eq. (1) is computed using the fine-tuned models θI and θT to obtain batch cosine
263   image-text similarity scores. Similarly, p̂(ŷ|x), also from Eq. (1), uses the original CLIP models.
264   DKL denotes the Kullback-Leibler divergence. The similarity scores are normalized using a softmax
265   function to form a probability distribution.
266   By minimizing the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the distributions of image-text similarity
267   calculated from the original CLIP encoders and those from the fine-tuned encoders, CLIP-CITE
268   encourages the fine-tuned model to acquire comparable modal alignments and image-text relationship
269   within batch as the pre-trained CLIP models. This strategy upholds modal consistency and facilitates
      the transfer of knowledge from the pre-trained model to the fine-tuned model.

      5     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we have presented CLIP-CITE, a fully fine-tuning approach designed to adapt CLIP for
      downstream tasks in limited-data scenarios. By devising a discriminative visual-text alignment task,
532
      implementing supervised contrastive loss, and employing visual-language similarity distillation, CLIP-
533
      CITE effectively addresses the common issues of overfitting and catastrophic forgetting encountered
534   by existing fine-tuning methods. Our experimental results demonstrate that a carefully crafted fine-
535   tuning strategy can enable CLIP to acquire both domain-specific and class-specific knowledge, while
536   maintaining its versatility across other domains and classes. Notably, despite involving the tuning
537   of more parameters, our approach offers superior computational efficiency compared to parameter-
538   efficient prompt-based competitors. We hope this work can promote in-depth research on the full
539   fine-tuning paradigm of VLMs in data-limited scenarios and facilitate its future application to recent
      Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs).
