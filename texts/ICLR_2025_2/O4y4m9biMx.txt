                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               This study aims to distill history events that have essential information for pre-
012
               dicting subsequent events with counterfactual analysis. The problem is named
013            Counterfactual History Distillation (CHD). CHD distills a minimum set of events
014            from history, based on which the distribution provided by a trained MTPP model
015            fits the events observed later, and the distribution based on the remaining events in
016            history cannot. It can help understand what event marks may have more influence
017            on the occurrence of future events and what events in history may have a causal
018            relationship with the events observed later. This study proposes a robust solution for
019            CHD, called MTPP-based Counterfactual History Distiller (MTPP-CHD). MTPP-
020            CHD learns to select the optimal event combination from history for the events
021
               observed later. Experiment results demonstrate the superiority of MTPP-CHD by
               outperforming baselines in terms of distillation quality and processing speed.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      The Marked Temporal Point Process (MTPP) (Daley & Vere-Jones, 2003) is a well-defined stochastic
      process that maps historical event sequences to a probability distribution which can be used to predict
028
      future events. Learning MTPP by neural networks has been well investigated (Du et al., 2016; Mei &
029
      Eisner, 2017; Omi et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020a; Zuo et al., 2020; Shchur et al., 2020; Mei et al.,
030   2022; Zhang et al., 2023b; Zhou & Yu, 2023; Lüdke et al., 2023). These algorithms, belonging to the
031   Neural Marked Temporal Point Process (NMTPP) family, enable people to train and use MTPP in
032   high-stake real-world applications like the fake news mitigation (Farajtabar et al., 2017; Zhang et al.,
033   2021b; 2022b) and recommendation systems (Hosseini et al., 2017; Cai et al., 2018).
034
      Counterfactual analysis, also known as counterfactual reasoning, is one of the basic cognitive
035
      reasoning approaches. Counterfactual analysis reveals casual relations by searching for the smallest
036   modification to the input that could completely change the output (Tan et al., 2021). For example,
037   to investigate why one piece of disinformation becomes viral on Twitter by counterfactual analysis,
038   we search for the answer by removing retweets of some accounts from the retweet history and then
039   feeding the modified history to an existing model to emulate whether the disinformation still goes
040   viral. If it stopped going viral after we removed retweets of multiple accounts and became viral again
041   when we added them back, we would conclude that these accounts might be the culprits.
042   Recently, Noorbakhsh & Rodriguez (2022), Zhang et al. (2022b), and Hizli et al. (2023) investigate
043   how the prediction of an MTPP model changes with handcrafted modifications of history with
044   counterfactual analysis. Unlike these studies, we aim to distill a minimum subset of history events
045   with the essential information for predicting the following events using an MTPP model with
046   counterfactual analysis. If the history is modified by removing the minimum subset of events, the
047   accuracy of MTPP model will drop significantly. The problem is named Counterfactual History
048   Distillation (CHD). It can help understand what event marks may have more influence on the
049   occurrence of subsequent events and what events in history may have a causal relationship with the
050
      events observed later.
051   While CHD with conventional counterfactual analysis works in concept, the distilled events are not
052   always satisfactory. It is expected that distilled events have the essential information and the events
053   left in history have trivial information for predicting the subsequent events. However, our study shows
      this is not true in many scenarios as the prediction accuracy of the MTPP model based on the distilled

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      events is worse than that based on the events left in history. This means the result of CHD with
055   conventional counterfactual analysis sometimes can be faulty (See Section 2.2 for more information).
056   To address this issue, we refine CHD by adding one more constraint to enforce that distilled events
057   are informative. Without loss of generality, perplexity (Moore & Lewis, 2010) is applied to evaluate
058   prediction accuracy, i.e., how well the distribution of the next events produced by the MTPP model fits
059   the subsequent events observed. CHD is a combinatorial problem. Inspired by the rationalization (Lei
060   et al., 2016), we propose a solution for CHD, named MTPP-based Counterfactual History Distiller
061   (MTPP-CHD), which probes various combinations of events in history with the support of Gumbel-
062   softmax trick (Bengio et al., 2013; Maddison et al., 2017). We show that MTPP-CHD outperforms
063
      baseline models in terms of efficiency and distillation quality. We also demonstrate that distilled
      events can help understand the influence of different marks on the occurrence of future events. In
064
      summary, the contributions of this study are threefold:
065
066             1. To the best of our knowledge, this study is the first to distill a minimum subset of history
067                events with the essential information for predicting the subsequent events using an MTPP
068                model. We name the problem Counterfactual History Distillation (CHD).
069             2. This study demonstrates the issues when solving Counterfactual History Distillation (CHD)
070                by conventional counterfactual analysis and refines it with one more constraint to ensure
071                that the distilled events are desirable.
072             3. This study proposes a robust solution MTPP-CHD for CHD, which learns to select the
073                optimal event combination from history by leveraging Gumbel-softmax trick. Experiment
074                results demonstrate the superiority of MTPP-CHD by outperforming baselines in terms of
075                distillation optimization and processing speed. We also demonstrate that distilled events can
076                help understand the influence of different marks on the occurrence of future events.

      3      MTPP- BASED C OUNTERFACTUAL H ISTORY D ISTILLER (MTPP-CHD)
182
      The proposed CHD solution, MTPP-based Counterfactual History Distiller (MTPP-CHD), is sketched
183
      in Figure 1. MTPP-CHD consists of three components. The first component, history distiller,
184
      processes Hf and xo using an encoder-decoder transformer, then pushes the resultant representations
185   into a fully connected layer. The output is p(y|Hf , xo ). Here, y is a mask vector of size |Hf |,
186   each for one event in Hf . For yi ∈ y, if yi = 0, the corresponding element xi ∈ Hf goes to
187   Hl . If yi = 1, the corresponding element xi ∈ Hf goes to Hd . All trainable parameters are in
188   the first component. The second component, historical event picker, derives Hl and Hd based on
189   p(y|Hf , xo ). The third component, training loss, employs a trained MTPP model to evaluate the
190   derived Hl and Hd for training MTPP-CHD. The third component only exists during training.

      Baseline Models To our knowledge, no previous studies investigated CHD in the context of MTPP.
281
      This means we do not have baselines from existing studies to compare with. Brute force is infeasible
282
      because solving a combinatorial problem like CHD is NP-hard (Karp, 1972). We notice some studies
283   applying counterfactual analysis in recommender systems. They greedily search for the smallest
284   subset of history that the recommendation would change with the subset removed (Ghazimatin et al.,
285   2020; Tran et al., 2021; Zhong & Negre, 2022). This motivates us to adopt a Greedy Search (GS)
286   baseline. It solves CHD by incrementally selecting from Hf the event that increases the gap between
287   log ppl(xo |Hl ) and log ppl(xo |Hd ) the most and inserting it to Hd until the two constraints are
288   satisfied. We also take a Random Distillation (RD) baseline to show the difficulty of CHD. RD
289   randomly moves Q events from Hf to Hd and calculates the gap between log ppl(xo |Hf ) and
290   log ppl(xo |Hl ). This is repeated multiple times and the average of these gaps is recorded. Q starts
291
      from 0. RD stops and returns Q when the average gaps satisfy the two constraints in Equation (7);
      otherwise, increase Q by 1 and repeat the previous process.

424   than Hf . Investigating the events in Hd may disclose interesting insights.
425

430   events from Hf to constitute Hd . In contrast, Hd returned using MTPP-CHD has the essential
431   information for predicting the next events. If a mark has more influence on the occurrence of the
      subsequent events, the mark is expected to be more frequent in Hd returned using MTPP-CHD than

      5.1                         C OUNTERFACTUAL ANALYSIS
451
452
453   Counterfactual analysis on MTPP models Recently, Noorbakhsh & Rodriguez (2022), Zhang
454   et al. (2022b) and Hizli et al. (2023) used counterfactual analysis to investigate how the prediction
455   of an MTPP model changes with handcrafted modifications of history. Noorbakhsh & Rodriguez
456
      (2022) successfully perform counterfactual analysis on the Hawkes process, an instance of MTPP, by
      deterministically accepting or rejecting the future events generated by the thinning algorithm (Ogata,
457
      1981). Zhang et al. (2022b) use counterfactual analysis to estimate the influence of fake news
458
      engagements. By comparing the intensity function with manually modified history, they discover
459   that users tend to behave differently if they recently engaged in misinformation. Hizli et al. (2023)
460   use counterfactual analysis to evaluate the effect of medical treatments by checking how the blood
461   glucose dynamics changes with and without a specific treatment.
462
      CHD differs from existing counterfactual analysis related to MTPP models (Noorbakhsh & Rodriguez,
463
      2022; Zhang et al., 2022b; Hizli et al., 2023). They investigate how a predefined modification to
464
      history would change the prediction of MTPP models. In contrast, CHD aims to find a minimal
465   modification Hd so that the MTPP model can generate a distribution fitting xo based on Hd but
466   cannot based on Hl . In summary, the methods in these studies cannot solve CHD.
467
468   Counterfactual analysis on Classifiers Some researchers use counterfactual analysis to analyze
469   how binary and multi-class classifiers make decisions and name the task Counterfactual Explanations
470   (CFE) (Verma et al., 2020). The definition of CFE involves a classifier f , an input feature x, and
471   an expected output y. We expect a counterfactual input x′ by solving the following optimization
472   problem:
473                                          arg min     d(x, x′ )
474
                                                  x′                                                  (13)
475
                                                  s.t. f (x′ ) = y ′
476   where d(x, x′ ) refers to the distance between x and x′ . Equation (13) means the expected x′ should
477   be similar to x while still changes the classification result from y to y ′ . Usually, the similarity
478   between x and x′ means we should change as few features as possible, but sometimes it means the
479   overall modification to x should be as small as possible (Verma et al., 2020). CFE generation is a
480   well-investigated task with many existing works (Wachter et al., 2017; Dhurandhar et al., 2018; 2019;
481   Joshi et al., 2019; Kanamori et al., 2020; Mothilal et al., 2020; Ramakrishnan et al., 2020; Parmentier
482
      & Vidal, 2021; Chen et al., 2022).
483   CHD is fundamentally different from CFE. CFE modifies the continuous input that would change
484   the discrete output of a classifier (Verma et al., 2020). However, CHD manipulates the discrete
485   input sequence that would change the continuous output of the MTPP model, i.e., the accuracy for
      predicting the events observed later.


                                                                                                                                     9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Counterfactual analysis on Recommendation Systems The recommendation system community
487   has used counterfactual analysis to investigate how user behaviors and item features affect recommen-
488   dation results (Mehrotra et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2020; Ghazimatin et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2021;
489   Tran et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022b; Zhong & Negre, 2022; Zhang
490   et al., 2022b; Mu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023a). Ghazimatin et al. (2020) proposed PRINCE, the
491   first approach explaining recommendations concerning users’ activities in Heterogeneous Information
492   Networks(HIN). By greedily removing as few events as possible from the historical user event
493   sequence that could replace the current recommendation with a different item, PRINCE identifies
494   which interactions are responsible for model decisions. PRINCE heavily relies on the structure of
495
      HIN to efficiently find the solution, which limits its general use. To solve this, Tran et al. (2021)
      proposed ACCENT. It greedily searches for the smallest subset of history that the recommendation
496
      would change after training a new system with the subset removed. Zhong & Negre (2022) discuss
497
      applying SHAP(SHapley Additive exPlanations) (Lundberg & Lee, 2017) to greedily select features
498   as the recommendation explanation. Zhang et al. (2023a) proposed PaGE-LINK. This graph-based
499   explanation algorithm exploits the complete graph information from a learned GNN recommender to
500   explain the recommendation results.
501
      Besides, counterfactual analysis has been applied to understand how the reinforcement learning agent
502
      behaves in different environment states (Atrey et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021a; Zhou
503
      et al., 2022; Ji et al., 2023). Some researchers realize that they can detect and mitigate the bias in
504   pretrained computer vision and language models by counterfactual analysis (Huang et al., 2020;
505   Abbasnejad et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020c; Niu et al., 2021; Qian et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022a).
506
507   5.2   L OGIC P OINT P ROCESSES
508
509   Besides counterfactual analysis, researchers have developed other ways to find causal relations
510   between events on continuous time. One of the favorites is the Granger causality (Xu et al., 2016;
511   Zhang et al., 2020b; Marcinkevics & Vogt, 2021; Zhu et al., 2022; Jalaldoust et al., 2022). Granger
512   causality explores mutual relations across different marks, checking which mark helps the event
513   forecast on other marks. Other works exploit logic rules from the temporal relation between different
514
      events, e.g., one event happens before another event, then construct the conditional intensity function
      based on these relations (Li et al., 2021b; Yang et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024). Shi et al. (2023) use
515
      logic rules extracted by LLMs to improve the accuracy of next-event prediction. Zhang et al. (2021a)
516
      report an unsupervised approach to pick out exogenous events from a given sequence, called TPP-
517   Select. TPP-Select separates all observed events into two types: endogenous events and exogenous
518   events. Endogenous events occur because of historical influence, while exogenous events exist
519   because of unknown external factors. By removing exogenous events from the dataset, TPP-Select
520   can improve MTPP model training performance.
521
      CHD differs from these works. CHD discloses causal relations between history and events observed
522
      later, while Granger causality (Idé et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2024) explores mutual relations across
523   different marks to find which mark helps the event forecast on other marks. Other works (Li et al.,
524   2020; Song et al., 2024) exploit logic rules between different events, e.g., one event happens before
525   another event, then construct the conditional intensity function based on these rules. In summary, the
526   methods in these studies cannot solve CHD.
527
528
      6     C ONCLUSIONS
529
530
      This study investigates Counterfactual History Distillation (CHD) to distill the essential events in
531
      history that can influence the occurrence of the subsequent events. This study demonstrates the
532   issue of solving Counterfactual History Distillation (CHD) by conventional counterfactual analysis
533   and refines the definition to ensure the distilled events are informative. With deliberate methods
534   including Gumbel-softmax trick, the proposed solution MTPP-based Counterfactual History Distiller
535   (MTPP-CHD) learns by effectively probing various event combinations. Its superiority has been
536   observed in distillation optimization and processing speed in tests on real-world datasets. This study
537   demonstrates analyzing the distilled events may disclose insights into the causal relation between
538   events and event marks in continuous-time event sequences.
