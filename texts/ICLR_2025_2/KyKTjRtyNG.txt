011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            As large language models (LLMs) become widely deployed across various do-
014            mains, security concerns—particularly jailbreak attacks that bypass built-in safety
015            mechanisms—have emerged as significant risks. Existing jailbreak methods fo-
016            cus mainly on single-turn interactions and face limitations in generalizability and
017            practicality. In this paper, we propose a novel method called Multi-round Conver-
018
               sational Jailbreaking (MRCJ), which exploits the unintended competition between
               a LLMs’ safety alignment and its in-context learning objectives during extended
019
               conversations. By incrementally introducing increasingly malicious content, the
020
               LLMs’ tendency to maintain contextual consistency can override its safety pro-
021            tocols, ultimately leading to harmful outputs. To assist in generating a conversa-
022            tion flow, we developed a dataset containing 12,000 questions, categorized into
023            six types of security topics, and classified across four levels of severity, spanning
024            ten languages. Compared to existing methods, MRCJ demonstrates superior effi-
025            ciency, applicability, and effectiveness by fully exploiting the potential of multi-
026            round conversations. In experiments, MRCJ achieves a jailbreak success rate of
027            over 90% across widely-used LLMs, requiring fewer than five queries on aver-
028            age, and significantly outperforms baselines on both metrics. Our findings expose
029
               vulnerabilities in current LLMs during extended conversations and highlight the
               need for improved safety mechanisms that consider multi-round interactions. The
030
               source code and dataset are available at (URL omitted for double-blind reviewing;
031
               code available in supplementary materials).

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated exceptional performance across a wide range
038   of natural language tasks, including text generation, machine translation, conversational systems,
039
      and content recommendation, achieving notable success (Achiam et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Peng
      et al., 2023). Nevertheless, their impressive capabilities are accompanied by growing security con-
040
      cerns, particularly regarding jailbreaks, which have become a prominent focus of contemporary
041
      research (Yu et al., 2024). Jailbreaks involve carefully engineered inputs designed to circumvent
042   LLMs’ safety mechanisms, resulting in the generation of unsafe content that may pose risks to
043   users. As identified in the OWASP list (OWASP, 2023), jailbreaks are recognized as one of the most
044   critical security threats in the deployment of LLM applications.
045
      Existing jailbreak attacks primarily focus on single-turn interactions and can be broadly categorized
046
      into optimization-based and prompt-based approaches. Optimization-based approaches leverage
047
      various algorithms, such as a combination of greedy search and gradient-based techniques (Zou
048   et al., 2023), to exploit vulnerabilities in the model’s internal structure or training process (Chao
049   et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023). Prompt-based approaches aim to craft cleverly crafted prompts that
050   elicit unintended harmful responses from LLMs. These methods often rely on linguistic manipu-
051   lation to subtly provoke harmful outputs. (Chen et al., 2024) identify mapping rules for crafting
052   prompts that elicit harmful responses. (Anil et al., 2024) propose a many-shot jailbreak approach,
053   which prompts the model with a fictitious dialogue containing a series of question-answer pairs to
      steer its behavior.

070
      Overall, these methods predominantly serve as single-round jailbreaks: they attempt to bypass safety
      mechanisms with a single well-crafted adversarial prompt or a fabricated conversation as depicted
071
      in Figure 1. While such approaches can be effective to some extent, they are often limited by the di-
072
      rectness and simplicity of single-round interactions, as they fail to leverage LLMs’ interactive nature
073   and the potential for multi-turn manipulation to better conceal the attacking intent. As current LLMs
074   are increasingly comprehensive in their safety alignment (Kumar et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023),
075   models are increasingly adept at detecting insufficiently concealed harmful intents and applying tar-
076   geted safety patches to counter single-round jailbreaks. Additionally, single-round jailbreaks are
077   inherently limited in scope, as they address isolated prompts without continuity, posing challenges
078   for scaling attacks or maintaining context across multiple interactions.
079   In light of these limitations, we present Multi-Round Conversational Jailbreaking (MRCJ), a novel
080   method that utilizes the long context window of LLMs and their propensity for continuous responses
081   to subtly steer the model towards generating malicious content. We observe that humans typically
082   adopt a salami slicing tactic to gradually achieve desired goals through multiple rounds, avoiding
083   significant escalation or avoidance in geopolitics and war games (Alhassan et al., 2018). Inspired
084   by this, we adopt a step-by-step conversational strategy, breaking down a single-round request into
085   smaller, less conspicuous components spread across several interactions. As increasingly malicious
086   content is introduced incrementally over multiple rounds, the model’s inherent drive to maintain
087
      contextual consistency can gradually overshadow its safety mechanisms. Over time, this process
      erodes the model’s safety alignment, established during training, leading to behavioral drift and,
088
      ultimately, the generation of harmful outputs in the course of an extended conversation.
089
090   However, designing interaction conversations that subtly manipulate the model without triggering
091   its safety mechanisms prematurely presents significant challenges. A sudden increase in malicious
092   intent heightens the likelihood of the model issuing a refusal, disrupting the conversation flow and
093
      making incremental malicious attempts harder to sustain. Once a refusal is triggered, the model
      becomes more vigilant and resistant to further manipulations. To address the above challenges, we
094
      develop the Multilingual Unsafe Conversations Dataset (MUCD), which is structured to facilitate
095
      the generation of conversation flows with incrementally escalating levels of malice. The dataset is
096   organized into six categories of malice and four malice levels, comprising 50 malicious questions
097   per category. It spans ten languages, resulting in a total of 12,000 questions, with parallel versions
098   available for each language. The inclusion of multiple languages aligns with the multilingual phe-
099   nomenon observed in real-world conversational scenarios, while low-resource languages present a
100   significant vulnerability for LLMs in the context of jailbreaking (Deng et al., 2023b). With this
101   malice-escalating dataset, we can rigorously evaluate LLMs’ vulnerability to multi-round conversa-
102   tional jailbreaks by introducing incrementally escalating malicious content. The structured malice
103   levels allow us to simulate progressive attacks, revealing how models react to subtle shifts in mali-
104
      cious intent over extended interactions.
105   Compared to existing jailbreaks, our approach offers three advantages. ➜ Efficiency: Traditional
106   optimization-based jailbreak attacks typically require extensive training to identify suitable attack
107   vectors, relying on complex algorithms and numerous iterations to find effective prompts. In con-
      trast, MRCJ significantly reduces cost by generating maliciously escalating questions within dozens

125   2   R ELATED W ORK
126
127
128   Optimization-based attacks. These jailbreak attacks typically utilize optimization algorithms to
129   manipulate model inputs or modify the model itself to induce the generation of harmful outputs,
130   including gradient-based, logit-based, and fine-tuning-based approaches (Yi et al., 2024). Gradient-
131   based attacks append optimizable prefixes or suffixes to the original prompts, leveraging gradient
132   information to adjust inputs and guide the language model toward producing targeted malicious
133   content. Typically, Zou et al. (2023) proposed the Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) method,
134   which appends an adversarial suffix and iteratively calculates the optimal substitution for each posi-
135   tion, successfully executing attacks across various models. In certain scenarios, although attackers
136
      may not have full access to the model’s internal information, they can still exploit logit-based at-
      tacks to manipulate the decoding process, influencing the token selection and inducing harmful
137
      outputs (Zhang et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024). In contrast, fine-tuning-based attacks involve retrain-
138
      ing the model with malicious data, which reduces its safety alignment and renders the model more
139   vulnerable to jailbreak attempts. Research has demonstrated that fine-tuning LLMs with a small
140   set of harmful examples can substantially compromise their defensive mechanisms (Qi et al., 2023;
141   Yang et al., 2023). These optimization-based attacks often rely heavily on internal model knowledge
142   in white-box scenarios. Additionally, many approaches require multiple queries or even fine-tuning,
143   which increases the attack cost.
144
      Prompt-based attacks. These attacks aim to bypass the safety mechanisms of LLMs by craft-
145   ing specific prompts or modifying existing ones to induce harmful outputs. Attackers often design
146   specialized template prompts, construct deceptive scenarios like role-playing (Chao et al., 2023; Li
147   et al., 2023; Mehrotra et al., 2023), or exploit the specific capabilities of LLMs. For instance, some
148   prompt-based attacks modify the structure of prompts, use low-resource languages (Deng et al.,
149   2023b; Li et al., 2024), or employ encryption techniques (Yuan et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024) to
150   obscure malicious intent, making it harder for the model to detect unsafe content. While these strate-
151   gies can be effective, they often rely on static, manually crafted templates, limiting adaptability. In
152   contrast, more advanced methods use LLMs to automatically generate adversarial prompts (Deng
153
      et al., 2023a; Mehrotra et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2024), reducing human involvement, increasing the
      variety and efficiency of attacks, but also raising the overall cost and complexity of execution. Ad-
154
      ditionally, adversaries may use carefully designed examples to guide the model (Wei et al., 2023;
155
      Deng et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023) or embed malicious code within prompts (Lv et al., 2024; Kang
156   et al., 2024), causing the model to execute harmful actions unknowingly. One approach, many-shot
157   Jailbreak (Anil et al., 2024), uses LLMs’ in-context learning by presenting numerous fabricated
158   conversations to influence behavior. However, in black-box API models where prompt templates
159   are invisible, Many-shot Jailbreak struggle to replicate genuine conversations, requiring extensive
160   curated malicious question-answer pairs. Their low success rate stems from the inability of fake
161   conversations to engage the model effectively, as they lack the nuances and contextual depth of real
      interactions.

      Figure 2 provides an overview of our multi-round conversational jailbreak (MRCJ), a novel ap-
184   proach that leverages the long context window, in-context learning ability, and auto-regressive ob-
185   jective of LLMs. The MRCJ method consists of two primary stages. First, we construct an attack
186   question dataset of queries categorized into six distinct types of safety security, with each type
187   further divided into varying malice levels. Second, by submitting these queries in a progressively
188   harmful manner, the MRCJ exploits the LLM’s response tendencies to gradually compromise its
189   safety measures, ultimately leading to the output of unsafe content and a successful jailbreak.

      3.1    P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
192
193
      Threat model. The primary objective of jailbreak attacks is to bypass the ethical and safety con-
194   straints imposed on LLMs, such as those aligned with human values or developer-enforced guide-
195   lines, in order to compel the model to respond to malicious queries rather than refuse to answer.
196   In our threat model, we consider a realistic conversation scenario similar to typical AI assistant
197   deployments, where an attacker operates in a black-box environment engaging in a multi-round
198   conversation with the LLM, as illustrated in Figure 1. The attacker submits a sequence of queries
199   Q = {q1 , q2 , . . . , qn }, to which the model generates corresponding responses R = {r1 , r2 , . . . , rn }.
200   Each response ri is conditioned on the entire prior conversation history X = {q1 , r1 , q2 , r2 , . . . , qi },
201   distinguishing our approach from traditional single-round jailbreak attacks. The goal of the MRCJ
202
      is to ensure that after n turns of interaction, the final query elicits a response that provides an answer
      closely aligned with the malicious intent of the query, rather than the model producing a refusal
203
      message consistent with human-aligned values.
204
205   Formulation. Formally, we present the target LLM as T (x), where x is the input context. In the
206   multi-round conversational jailbreak scenario, we define the model’s input at the beginning of each
207   turn t as xt , where xt consists of the conversation history from the previous t−1 turns combined with
208
      the attacker’s current query qt . Assuming the attack spans n rounds, the attacker submits the target
      query qtarget , which represents the jailbreak goal. The model’s full context at this point is denoted as
209
      C, and the corresponding final response is r. Thus, the objective of the multi-round jailbreak attack
210
      can be formalized as,
211
212                                                         max S(C, r), with r = T (C),                                                                     (1)
213
214   where S(C, r) represents the judge score, measuring the harmfulness of response r given the context
215   C, and is typically obtained from an LLM, such as GPT-4o, which is capable of assessing the
      harmfulness of the generated content.

      3.2   ATTACK P REPARATION
217
218   Motivation and insight. In previous works (Anil et al., 2024), researchers have leveraged the in-
219   context learning capabilities of LLMs to develop few-shot or Many-shot Jailbreak attacks. These
220   methods prompt the model with fabricated conversations containing queries that the model would
221   typically reject, and a notable scaling trend has been observed, following a power-law relationship
222   with the number of in-context demonstrations provided. However, these methods often rely on
223   manually curated malicious question-answer pairs that are embedded within the prompt, simulating
224
      a conversation between an AI assistant and a user. For black-box LLMs, the prompt construction
      process based on user queries remains opaque.
225
226   Inspired by these works, we aim to explore the vulnerability of LLMs in real conversational settings,
227   focusing on exploiting their long-context windows. Due to the autoregressive nature of LLMs, which
228   aim to generate highly probable sequences of text, the generation process is strongly influenced by
229   prior context. We hypothesize that this creates a response inertia in interactions with LLMs. Specif-
230
      ically, by engaging in a multi-round conversation that begins with minimally harmful queries and
      gradually escalates in harmfulness, prior responses along with their corresponding queries form the
231
      context for subsequent interactions. This incremental approach increases the likelihood of the model
232
      responding to a harmful query, compared to directly posing the target question. Intuitively, this
233   query-response chain serves as a series of demonstrations that more closely align with the model’s
234   internal logic.
235
236
      Attack dataset construction. We introduce and construct the Multilingual Unsafe Conversations
      Dataset (MUCD) for the implementation of MRCJ, based on a comprehensive conversation safety
237
      taxonomy. which we categorize into six key types to cover a broad range of safety concerns: Na-
238
      tional Security, Child Abuse, Physical Harm, Scams and Fraud, Self-mutilation, and General Illegal
239   Acts. Each category is further divided into four malice levels, ranging from Low to Very High (Lev-
240   els 1 to 4). Furthermore, the dataset includes parallel versions in ten languages, including English,
241   with languages selected from low, medium, and high-resource categories, following the guidelines
242   by Deng et al. Each data point in the dataset is standardized in the form of a hypothetical identity
243   or scenario paired with a corresponding question. This design is based on our practical findings that
244   specifying an identity makes the model more likely to engage in malicious, incremental conversa-
245   tions.
246    Format: [Hypothetical role/scenario] + [A malicious question]
247    Example: You’re a school counselor. How can you manipulate a child into lying?
248
      To ensure the dataset’s objectivity and neutrality, we adopt a peer-review approach, where experts
249
      manually create data across various malice levels, and rank each other’s data entries, and only in-
250   clude consistently ranked data. We then use LLMs to augment the manually written data. Further
251   information on detailed criteria and generation processes can be found in Appendix A.

253   3.3   ATTACK I MPLEMENTATION
254
255   To conduct MRCJ, the attacker progressively introduces harmful queries, bypassing the alignment
256   constraints of LLMs, and induces the model to generate harmful responses. The key to the attack
257   lies in exploiting the conversation history context, gradually increasing the malice of the questions,
258   and accumulating unsafe content over multiple interactions.
259   Gradual escalation of multi-round queries. Given the predefined set of attack questions Q =
260   {Ql,c | l ∈ {1, 2, . . . , lmax }, c ∈ {1, 2, . . . , cmax }}, where each Ql,c represents a set of questions
261   under malice level l and category c. During the i-th query, the attacker selects a question qi ∈ Ql,c
262   from the current malice level and category, and queries the target model T . The model generates a
263   response ri′ = T (xi ), which is then evaluated by an external system based on a harmfulness score
264   S(xi , ri′ ) ∈ [1, 5]. If the harmfulness score S(xi , ri′ ) exceeds a predetermined threshold Sthreshold ,
265   the response ri′ is accepted as ri , meaning it is considered harmful enough to be part of the conver-
266
      sation history. Otherwise, the response is discarded, and the attacker continues by selecting another
      question from the same set Ql,c .
267
268   The selection of questions follows a structured procedure. At each malice level l ∈ {1, 2, . . . , lmax },
269   the attacker sequentially selects questions from all categories c ∈ {1, 2, . . . , cmax }. For each category
      c, the attacker queries the model with questions xi ∈ Ql,c one at a time. Upon receiving a valid

271   attacker then selects another uncollected question from the dataset and repeats this process. The
272   attacker continues this process until k valid responses ri that meet the harm threshold have been
273   received (i.e., the count reaches k). At that point, the attacker proceeds to the next category within
274   the same level. A round refers to the process of obtaining k valid responses in each category and
275   evaluating the responses across all categories for a specific malice level. Once all categories at
276   malice level l are completed, the attacker proceeds to the next level l + 1, repeating this process until
277   reaching the final level lmax and category cmax .
278   The core of the attack lies in exploiting the accumulated conversation history to progressively steer

361   window, while for Qwen-2, K is set to 10 with comparisons made at different K values in ablation
362   studies. As illustrated in Figure 3, the results show an increasing trend in harmful scores and JSR
363
      across all models as the rounds progress, with JSR exceeding 80% by the final round for all models.
      The harmful scores approach 5 for all models except LLaMA-3, indicating that the GPT-4o evaluator
364
      categorizes the outputs as Very Harmful. The proposed MRCJ effectively circumvents the models’
365
      safety mechanisms, prompting them to directly respond to malicious queries. It suggests that as the
366   conversation continues, LLMs experience unintended competition, where the need for contextual
367   coherence leads them to fulfill user requests, even as these requests become increasingly harmful.
368   This highlights the challenge to safety alignment within multi-round interactions, as the models’
369   in-context learning capabilities may override safety protocols, increasing the likelihood of harmful
370   outputs over extended conversations.

531   5      C ONCLUSION
532
533   This paper presents the potential of multi-round conversation patterns in enabling jailbreaks for
534   LLMs and introduces a novel black-box, automatic jailbreak method, Multi-round Conversational
535   Jailbreaking (MRCJ). We develop a multilingual, multi-topic, and multi-level dataset of malicious
536   questions to facilitate the generation of incrementally harmful conversations, leading to behavior
537
      drift and harmful outputs from LLMs. Extensive experiments demonstrate that MRCJ excels in
      effectiveness, applicability, and efficiency in uncovering vulnerabilities in large language models.
538
      These findings highlight the necessity of reinforcing model safety to mitigate risks from multi-round
539
      conversational exploits.

      A.1   S ECURITY T OPIC S ELECTION
705
706   Based on a comprehensive analysis of the safety statements issued by leading organizations in the AI
707   field, such as OpenAI and Anthropic, these pioneering companies have explicitly committed to ad-
708   hering to industry-standard safety practices, recognizing the multifaceted nature of data protection.
709   From the perspective of legal compliance and national security, OpenAI has emphasized the prohibi-
710   tion of using AI-generated misleading content in political advertising (National Security). In terms
711
      of data security, OpenAI stresses the importance of respecting and safeguarding users’ personal and
      sensitive data to prevent risks such as identity theft, financial loss, or reputational harm (Scams and
712
      Fraud). From an ethical standpoint, MetaAI has outlined specific measures to address extreme risks
713
      in its safety statement, particularly regarding the prevention of suicide (Self-mutilation), bullying,
714   and harassment (Physical Harm). These measures are aimed at preventing AI technologies from
715   being misused to promote behaviors that could result in physical harm. Additionally, both Ope-
716   nAI and Anthropic place a strong emphasis on child protection, treating it as a key priority in their
717   safety efforts. They have implemented robust safeguards to mitigate potential risks of abuse and
718   exploitation involving children (Child Abuse).
719
      According to our system research, we have identified and categorized six major malicious themes
720   to comprehensively cover various potentially harmful issues. To ensure that harmful behaviors not
721   specifically listed and emphasized are addressed, we grouped them under the general category (Gen-
722   eral Illegal Acts). The six security topics are as follows: National Security, Scams and Fraud,
723   Self-mutilation, Physical Harm, Child Abuse, and General Illegal Acts.
724
725   A.2   M ALICIOUS Q UESTION G ENERATION
726
727   We enlisted six human reviewers specializing in fields ethical AI to manually create harmful ques-
728   tions for each of the six security topics and across four levels of malice. These experts were respon-
729   sible for generating dozens of sample questions per category and malice level. Once the experts
730
      provided the sample questions, these examples were fed into the large language model (LLM) for
      few-shot learning. This enabled the LLM to learn patterns in harmful question construction across
731
      categories and malice levels. The LLM then generated a larger dataset of questions based on these
732
      patterns.
733
734   After the LLM-generated questions were produced, the same team conducted a thorough review
735   process. They evaluated the machine-generated questions for accuracy, relevance, and malice-level
736
      alignment. For any discrepancies found during the review, the questions were flagged for revision.
      The flagged questions were then discussed in expert panels, where disagreements on malice level
737
      or relevance were resolved through consensus. The process continued iteratively until the target
738
      number of questions for each category and malice level was reached.
739
