009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Causal inference on medical data containing treatments, such as estimation of
012            treatment effects, is crucial to ensure the efficacy and safety of interventions. How-
013            ever, privacy concerns can often limit access to the patient data necessary for such
014            analyses. Generative models can produce synthetic data that preserve privacy and
015            closely approximate the real data distribution, yet existing methods do not consider
016            downstream tasks for data containing treatments, nor the unique challenges these
017            pose. With our work we establish a set of desiderata that synthetic data containing
018
               treatments should satisfy to maximise downstream utility: preservation of (i) the co-
               variate distribution, (ii) the treatment assignment mechanism, and (iii) the outcome
019
               generation mechanism. Based on these desiderata, we propose a set of evaluation
020
               metrics to assess such synthetic data. Finally, we present STEAM: a novel method
021            for generating Synthetic data for Treatment Effect Analysis in Medicine. STEAM
022            mimics the data-generating process of data containing treatments and optimises
023            for our desiderata, while allowing differentially private generation. We empirically
024            demonstrate that STEAM achieves state-of-the-art performance across our metrics
025            as compared to existing generative models, particularly as the complexity of the
026            generative task increases.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030   Medical data sharing is crucial for advancing research in healthcare, enabling the replication of results
031   to establish validity, and the discovery of new insights through alternative analyses (Bauchner et al.,
032   2016; Wirth et al., 2021). However, such sharing is hindered by stringent regulations which restrict
033   access to patient data for research purposes (Annas, 2003; Voigt & Von dem Bussche, 2017).
034
      A potential solution is offered by synthetic data, which has gained increasing recognition in medical
035
      literature (Jadon & Kumar, 2023). Generative models can produce synthetic copies of sensitive data,
036   which can be shared more freely (Jordon et al., 2018). If necessary, generation can satisfy formal
037   definitions of privacy, such as differential privacy (DP) (Dwork et al., 2006), ensuring provable
038   guarantees (Pan et al., 2024). Importantly, the promise of synthetic data hinges on its ability to
039   preserve information critical to relevant downstream tasks. Among the existing synthetic data
040   literature (Bauer et al., 2024), most works focus on downstream predictive (supervised) tasks, shaping
041   standard evaluation and generation practices to this setting.
042   However, medical data typically contain treatment assignment variables, which invite unique down-
043   stream analysis. Data containing treatments are typically analysed via causal inference methods (e.g.
044   treatment effect estimation methods) which examine the causal relationships between covariates,
045   treatments, and outcomes, in a manner distinct from associative prediction (Feuerriegel et al., 2024).
046   Despite this, synthetic data papers which use medical data containing treatments for motivation
047   and validation (Choi et al., 2018; Kotelnikov et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2022; Borisov et al., 2023)
048   generally employ standard, prediction-oriented, generation and evaluation techniques (see examples
049   in Appendix A). Failure to acknowledge the likely downstream use of synthetic data containing
050
      treatments leads to low-quality generation, which is masked by misaligned evaluation metrics.
051   Evaluation. Standard synthetic data evaluation involves statistical comparison of synthetic and
052   real data (Table 1), and assessing the accuracy of synthetically-trained models in predicting a target
053   variable. In this evaluation paradigm, causal inference tasks are not considered, as treatments are
      handled like any other feature, limiting the relevance of such assessment for synthetic data containing

054
      treatments. To illustrate this, consider the following key questions that an analyst working with
055   a synthetic dataset containing treatments, Dsynth , may ask: Q1 How representative are the patient
056   covariates in Dsynth ?; Q2 How accurate are the treatment assignment decisions in Dsynth ?; and
057   Q3 How much error might be introduced in treatment effect estimates derived from Dsynth ? These
058   questions require differentiation between covariates, treatments, and outcomes, and they cannot be
059   accurately answered with current evaluation protocols.
060
      Generation. Generic synthetic data generation (Table 2) seeks to minimise the difference in synthetic
061   and real joint distributions, and all variables are generated simultaneously from this distribution.
062   This overlooks the data-generating process (DGP) from which data containing treatments arise,
063   where covariates drive treatment assignments, and both covariates and treatments influence outcomes
064   (Pearl, 2009). In doing so, such generation fails to capitalise on a valuable inductive bias, producing
065   synthetic data which poorly preserve these important relationships for causal inference. Existing
066   causal generative models, on the other hand, generally assume access to the full causal graph G,
067   which is overly restrictive in complex settings, such as medicine, where G is unlikely to be known.
068   In this work we address these limitations by conducting an analysis of synthetic data containing
069   treatments, proposing novel approaches to evaluation and generation which operate under reasonable
070   assumptions and explicitly consider the likely downstream use of such data. In doing so, we make
071   the following contributions:
072       1 Desiderata: By examining the typical analysis conducted on data containing treatments, we
073          establish a set of desiderata that synthetic data should satisfy in this context (Section 4).
074       2 Evaluation: We show that existing evaluation metrics for synthetic data are inadequate in
075          this setting, as they do not measure how well these desiderata are respected. As a remedy, we
076          propose a principled set of metrics derived from our desiderata, allowing meaningful evaluation
077          of synthetic data containing treatments (Section 5).
078       3 Generation: We propose STEAM, a novel method for synthetic data generation that contains
079          inductive biases to optimise for our desiderata and mimic the real DGP of data containing
080          treatments. Furthermore, STEAM can satisfy DP if desired (Section 6).
081       4 Empirical Analysis: Using our newly established metrics, we demonstrate that STEAM
082          exhibits state-of-the-art performance in generating synthetic data containing treatments, partic-
083          ularly as the real DGP grows in complexity, and in high-dimensional scenarios (Sections 7).
084          Our code is available via https://anonymous.4open.science/r/STEAM-35EC.

      Assessing predictive utility involves training a predictive model on Dsynth and measuring its accuracy.
124   Since key causal inference tasks, such as treatment effect estimation, do not have observable ground-
125   truths (Holland, 1986), model validation becomes a non-trivial task (Curth & Van Der Schaar, 2023)
126   and such utility assessment does not apply to our setting. On the other hand, methods for assessing
127   resemblance, which can involve comparison of synthetic and real marginals, 2-way correlation
128   matrices, joint distributions, supports via precision and recall, and assessment of a discriminator
129   model C in separating real and synthetic data, are summarised in Table 1. Importantly, these methods
130   handle all features within a dataset similarly, not differentiating between the variable classes X, W ,
131   and Y , and they therefore cannot directly answer any of the key questions Q1-3 posed in Section 1.
132
      Our metrics, proposed in Section 5, remedy this.
133   Generation. We consider two related approaches to generative modeling, with a high-level compari-
134   son in Table 2. Firstly, generic generative models are those which make minimal assumptions on Dreal ,
135   and seek to minimise the difference between real and synthetic joint distributions. Causal generative
136   models, on the other hand, assume access to the full causal graph G, and then seek to minimize the
137   difference between each real and synthetic conditional distribution, as dictated by the causal relation-
138
      ships in G. The assumptions for our proposed method, STEAM, fall in between these two. While we
      assume that the underlying DGP of Dreal is of the form X ∼ PX , W ∼ PW |X , Y ∼ PY |W,X , this
139
      will hold for a wide array of datasets containing treatments, and it is generally less restrictive than
140
      assuming complete knowledge of G, as we do not need the individual causal relationships between
141   variables. Therefore, STEAM is more applicable in complex settings. Furthermore, neither of these
142   existing approaches are tailored to downstream treatment effect estimation, and they do not target our
143   desiderata for synthetic data containing treatments (Section 4). For empirical comparisons against
144   generic generative models, see Section 7, and see Appendix O for comparison with causal generative
145   models. For further elaboration on specific evaluation and generation methods see Appendix B.

147   4            D ESIDERATA FOR SYNTHETIC DATA CONTAINING TREATMENTS
148
      We now consider three distributions that are critical in downstream analysis of data containing
149
      treatments: (i) the covariate distribution PX , (ii) the treatment assignment mechanism PW |X , and

154   establish our desiderata for synthetic data containing treatments based on these distributions.

189    Why is its preservation important? Given the use of PW |X as a nuisance parameter, errors in its

225   With our desiderata established, we now investigate how to evaluate the adherence of Dsynth .
226
227   5.1       I NADEQUACY OF EXISTING METRICS
228   Existing evaluation metrics, discussed in Section 3, do not offer a clear sense of how well Dsynth
229   satisfies our desiderata. These metrics do not differentiate between X, W , and Y , and they therefore
230   cannot directly assess any of QX , QW |X , or QY |W,X . Within these existing metrics, joint-distribution-
231   level metrics, such as Kullback–Leibler divergence (KL) (Kullback & Leibler, 1951), are most popular,
232   since they offer a complete, holistic assessment of how well Q models P . However these are, at best,
233   loosely related to our desiderata, and they do not allow a user to disentangle how each of (i)-(iii)
234
      is preserved, limiting the depth of information offered on Dsynth . Furthermore, we argue that these
      metrics will tend to be dominated by the covariate distribution as X grows in dimensionality, and they
235
      will lose sensitivity to the treatment assignment and outcome generation mechanisms. In this sense,
236
      sensitivity refers to the effect that differences in the modelling of PW |X or PY |W,X by a proposal
237   distribution Q have on a metric M.
238

338   To illustrate the standard DGP of data contraining treatments, shown in the middle of Figure 9,
339   consider a simple hospital dataset. Patient covariates X, such as height, weight etc., are drawn from
340   an underlying covariate distribution PX , which is dictated by the local population. Treatments are
341   then assigned by a domain expert, such as a doctor, conditioned on X, i.e. W ∼ PW |X . Finally,
342   patients’ outcomes are dictated by the dynamics of their ailments, conditional upon W and X, i.e.
343   Y ∼ PY |X,W . We now propose STEAM, a novel method for generating Synthetic data for Treatment
344   Effect Analysis in Medicine which mimics the real DGP.
345
      6.1      STEAM
346
347   Mimicry of the real DGP acts as an inductive bias, pushing Q closer towards the P in structure, and
348
      directly targeting each distributions from our desiderata. STEAM, shown on the right of Figure 9,
      conducts a three-step generation process, involving the following:
349
350        1    QX . X is generated from a generative model trained to match the covariate distribution PX .
351        2    QW |X . Treatments are assigned according to a propensity function trained on Dreal . If Dreal is
352             experimental data with known PW |X , then QW |X can be directly set as the true distribution,
353             negating the need for any optimisation at this step.
354        3    QY |W,X . PO estimators are trained to match PY |W =0,X and PY |W =1,X , and the relevant
355             outcome is generated for each instance based on their assigned treatment.
356   Each component can be defined with any relevant model. QX can be any generative model, QW |X
357   can be any classifier, and QY |W,X can use any regressors.
358
359   6.2      D IFFERENTIAL PRIVACY WITH STEAM
360   Theoretical guarantees of the privacy of synthetic data are often required in high-stakes scenarios,
361   such as medicine. STEAM can permit this, satisfying DP when its three component models do, as an
362   application of the post-processing and composition theorems of DP (Dwork & Roth, 2014).
363   Proposition 1. If QX , QW |X , and QY |W,X satisfy (ϵX , δX )-, (ϵW , δW )-, and (ϵY , δY )-differential
364   privacy respectively, STEAM satisfies (ϵtotal , δtotal )-differential privacy, where ϵtotal = ϵX + ϵW +
365   ϵY , δtotal = δX + δW + δY .
366
      Proof. See Appendix G.
367
      There are a number of existing DP generative models, classifiers, and regressors which can be set as
368
      QX , QW |X , and QY |W,X respectively to enable this.

505       Takeaway
506
          Figure 4 shows the results, and results with more baselines are in Appendix I.2. STEAMAIM
507       models PY |W,X better on all tested values of ϵ, as UPEHE is significantly lower than for standard
508       AIM. PW |X is better modelled by STEAMAIM at small ϵ, with equivalent performance between
509       the methods at less conservative budgets. PX , on the other hand, is better preserved by standard
510       AIM, scoring higher on Pα,X and Rβ,X at most ϵ. This is likely because assigning QX one third
511       of the budget of the standard AIM model and having it model largely the same distribution, save
512       for the removed W and Y , is prohibitively restrictive given the high-dimensionality of X. As
513       such, with uniform distribution of (ϵ, δ) across each component, there is a trade-off between
514       STEAMAIM and standard AIM, where STEAMAIM better preserves PW |X and PY |W,X , while
515
          standard AIM preserves PX better. Distributing (ϵ, δ) differently amongst QX , QW |X , and
          QY |W,X could address this trade-off, as we discuss in Section 8 and Appendix M.

518   8      D ISCUSSION
519
      Impact. In this paper, we tackle a problem impeding progress in the causal inference for medicine
520
      community—unavailability of data. Existing synthetic data solutions are inadequate, producing poor
521
      quality data containing treatments, which are evaluated with misaligned metrics. Our evaluation and
522   generation proposals, grounded in our desiderata which stem from the needs of analysts, remedy
523   this. We enable generation of synthetic data of substantially higher quality, which we demonstrate
524   across a range of experiments in Section 7, as well as in an additional ablative study (Appendix J)
525   and hyperparameter stability study (Appendix K). Furthermore, we allow meaningful evaluation with
526   our metrics, proposed in Section 5, that can answer the key questions Q1-3 of downstream analysts
527   from Section 1. Our paper’s impact is heightened by the fact that STEAM increasingly outperforms
528   standard generation in complex situations likely to arise in real-world settings. While we focus on
529   medical data, our methods are also applicable to other fields where data contain treatments, such as
530
      education, marketing, and public policy, broadening our impact.
531   Limitations. STEAM has room for refinement. Uniform distribution of the privacy budget across
532   QX , QW |X , and QY |W,X during DP generation, as we do in Section 7.3, is sub-optimal, as particular
533   component models may benefit from a larger share of ϵ depending on their importance and complexity
534   (Appendix M). Also, generative models, used to model QX , can struggle when covariate shift is high
535   (Appendix N). This does not uniquely affect STEAM, as it occurs under standard generation as well,
536
      however it is important to acknowledge that poor performance may occur in this setting.
537   Future work. There are many future research directions in this setting. These include improving
538   the limitations discussed above, and examining further applications of STEAM. For example, in
539   this work we focus on static medical data, and longitudinal data, with continuous measurement of
      covariates, treatments, and outcomes, may require further novel thought (Appendix P).

      A     E XAMPLES OF MISUSE OF MEDICAL DATA CONTAINING TREATMENTS
865
866
      Here, we detail a select few synthetic data papers which use medical data, containing treatments,
867   to demonstrate on, yet they do not consider the downstream task of causal inference and how their
868   methods may need to be altered for this. Note that we do not claim that this list is exhaustive, as this
869   is a pervasive problem in the synthetic data literature, and we mean only to provide a few examples
870   here to demonstrate this problem, and provide motivation for our paper.
871
872   A.1    M ED GAN
873
874   In the paper ‘Generating Multi-label Discrete Patient Records using Generative Adversarial Networks’
875   (Choi et al., 2018), the authors propose a GAN-based approach to generate ‘realistic synthetic patient
876   records’. In doing so, they experiment on multiple datasets containing treatments, including one
877   from Sutter Palo Alto Medical Foundation (PAMF) which consists of longitudinal medical records
878
      of 258,000 patients, as well as the MIMIC-III dataset (Johnson et al., 2016), which includes 46,000
      intensive care unit patient records. Given the nature of these datasets, they both include treatments
879
      administered to patients, and they therefore invite downstream analysts to conduct causal inference
880
      tasks, such as treatment effect estimation.
881
882   Nevertheless, in this paper, standard generation and evaluation practices are followed, not differen-
883   tiating between covariates, treatments, and outcomes. In particular, the evaluation protocol relies
884
      on marginal comparisons, and predictive utility assessment, which offers limited information as to
      how well the generation method, medGAN, produces useful data for causal inference. Furthermore,
885
      medGAN itself draws samples directly from the joint distribution, not mimicking the DGP of treatment
886
      data, or optimising for the distributions most important for causal inference.
887
888
      A.2    TAB DDPM
889
890   The paper ‘TabDDPM: Modelling Tabular Data with Diffusion Models’ (Kotelnikov et al., 2022)
891   proposes a diffusion-based tabular data generation method. While not explicitly geared towards
892   medical data, this paper does use medical data with variables that could be seen as treatments
893   in its experiments. It therefore, at least implicitly, positions itself to work on data containing
894   treatments, and invites users to conduct generation with TabDDPM on such data. Specifically, the
895   cardiovascular disease dataset from https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/sulianova/
896   cardiovascular-disease-dataset is used, and this data could be analysed via causal
897
      inference by setting ‘physical activity’ as a treatment, to estimate its effect on cardiovascular disease.
898   However, in this paper, only standard evaluation and generation methods are used, and the needs
899   of downstream analysts pursuing causal inference tasks are not acknowledged. Evaluation involves
900   only predictive utility measures, and the TabDDPM method generates all variables in a sample
901   simultaneously, not optimising for the distributions most important for causal inference.
902
903   A.3    GR EAT
904
905   The paper ‘Language Models are Realistic Tabular Data Generators’ (Borisov et al., 2023) proposes an
906
      LLM-based generator. Similar to the TabDDPM paper, this paper is not explicitly geared towards med-
      ical data, but it demonstrates on medical data containing treatments, thereby implicitly condoning its
907
      use on this type of data. Specifically, the dataset sick from https://www.openml.org/
908
      search?type=data&sort=runs&id=38&status=active is demonstrated on, which
909   could be analysed via causal inference to assess the effect of ‘thyroxine’ or ‘antithyroid’ treat-

911   inference, only evaluating its GReaT method with predictive utility metrics and a discriminator score.

923   B    E XTENDED LITERATURE REVIEW
924
925   To provide useful context for readers, we extend on our literature review here.
926
927   Evaluation. We extend on the synthetic data evaluation practices summarised in Table 1 here.
928
      Marginal comparison. Assessing the distributional distance between synthetic and real marginals is
929
      often used to offer a quantitative assessment of how well individual variables are modelled (Yan et al.,
930
      2022; Tucker et al., 2020; Goncalves et al., 2020). The distance function d to conduct this can be set
931   from a variety of choices, including including KL divergence (Kullback & Leibler, 1951), Jensen-
932   Shannon distance (Lin, 1991), Wasserstein distance (Kantorovich, 1960), Kolmogorov-Smirnov score
933   (Massey Jr, 1951), MMD (Gretton et al., 2012), and many more.
934
      Correlation matrix comparison. Correlation-based assessment can offer a sense of how well inter-
935
      dependencies between variables are modelled in synthetic data (Murtaza et al., 2023). This commonly
936
      involves calculating synthetic and real 2-way correlation matrices, and assessing their difference, by
937   setting d as a distance such as Frobenius norm (Goncalves et al., 2020) and absolute error (Kaur et al.,
938   2020).
939
940
      Joint distribution comparison. Metrics based on notions of statistical divergence can offer a means
      of quantifying how different the entire joint distributions of real and synthetic data are (Yoon et al.,
941
      2020; Tucker et al., 2020; Torfi et al., 2022). The distance function d can be set to largely to the same
942
      family of functions as in the marginal comparison case.
943
944   Precision and recall analysis. Precision and recall, originally proposed for generative model assess-
945   ment in Sajjadi et al. (2018), measure if generated samples are covered by real samples, and vice
946
      versa. Alpha precision and beta recall (Alaa et al., 2022) are refined versions of the original metrics
      which account for the densities of the real and generative distributions, rather than just comparing
947
      supports.
948
949   Discriminator performance. Discriminator performance is a slightly unique evaluation practice,
950   involving a ‘discriminator’, which predicts whether instances are synthetic or real, where poor
951   performance of the discriminator indicates realism in the synthetic data (Kaur et al., 2020; Lee et al.,
952
      2020; Emam et al., 2021; Borisov et al., 2023).
953   Predictive utility. Predictive utility metrics offer a practical evaluation of synthetic data by quantifying
954   the performance of a synthetically-trained predictive model. The “train on synthetic, test on real”
955   (TSTR) paradigm (Esteban et al., 2017) is the common approach to such assessment, measuring the
956   accuracy of a synthetically-trained model in predicting a target label on a real test set.
957
958   Generic generative models. We expand on the generic generative modelling paradigm outlined
959   in Table 2 by describing specific existing generative models which adhere to it. These models
960   approximate the real joint distribution using a diverse range of techniques.
961   GAN-based models. GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2020) consist of a generator and discriminator network,
962   which are trained adversarially to generate and identify synthetic data, respectively, until the samples
963   are realistic. Originally proposed for image generation, GANs have been adapted to tabular generation,
964   and there are many methods which adopt this popular architecture (e.g. CTGAN (Xu et al., 2019),
965   TableGAN (Park et al., 2018)), including those specifically designed for medical data (e.g. MedGAN
966   (Choi et al., 2018)).
967   VAE-based models. VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2022) are another common architecture, which learn
968   to encode data into a lower-dimensional latent space and then decode it back to reconstruct the
969   original data. They generate new data samples by sampling from the latent distribution and decoding
970   these samples, and their application to tabular data involve techniques to handle mixed data types
971   (e.g.TVAE (Xu et al., 2019)), and regularisation for improved robustness (e.g. RTVAE Akrami et al.
      (2020)).


                                                          18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       Diffusion-based models. Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020) learn the gradient of
973    the data distribution, and generate data via progressive denoising, beginning with a noisy sample and
974    using a neural network to predict and remove noise over a number of timesteps, including for tabular
975    data (e.g. TabDDPM (Kotelnikov et al., 2022)).
976
       Forest-based models. Random forests can estimate the density of a probability distribution, as leaf
977
       nodes partition the data space into distinct hyper-rectangles with estimated densities of the proportion
978
       of samples which fall into them. Samples can then be drawn from this estimated density. Random
979    forests can easily handle heteorgeneous data types, so their application to tabular data synthesis is
980    natural. They are particularly fast to train and generate from (Shi & Horvath, 2006; Watson et al.,
981    2023)).
982
       Normalizing flow-based models. Normalizing flows estimate the target density by transforming a
983
       tractable density (e.g. a Gaussian) into the target through a series of invertible transformations, called
984
       ’flows’. Probabilities from the target distribution can then be found using the change of variables
985    formula (e.g. Rezende & Mohamed (2016)). Recent work has shown their theoretical similarity to
986    diffusion models (Lipman et al., 2023).
987
988
989
990    Causal generative models. Causal generative models (Komanduri et al., 2024; Blöbaum et al.,
991    2024; Chao et al., 2024) are a class of generative model, distinct from generic tabular data generators,
992    that approximate the underlying structural causal model (SCM) (Pearl, 2009) of a dataset. While such
993
       models are related to our work with STEAM, and are likely to better preserve causal relationships than
       generic generators in settings where they can be used, their assumptions can restrict their practical
994
       use cases. In comparison to STEAM, they generally differ in terms of their (1) assumptions, (2)
995
       motivation, and (3) flexibility, which we detail here.
996
997    (1): Importantly, causal generative models typically assume that the data holder has knowledge of the
998    entire causal graph of the real data, which is a more restrictive assumption than we make in this work.
999
       We assume that our specification of the underlying DGP X ∼ PX , W ∼ PW |X , Y ∼ PY |W,X is
       correct for datasets containing treatments, but we do not require knowledge of the causal graph, as we
1000
       do not need to know the causal links between individual variables. We do not assume knowledge of
1001
       the causal relationships amongst the covariates, nor knowledge of which covariates cause treatment
1002   assignment or patient outcomes. As such, we make less restrictive assumptions than works which
1003   require knowledge of a causal graph, and we suggest that our approach is more realistic in complex,
1004   real-world scenarios, such as those that arise in medicine, where the true causal graph is unlikely to
1005   be available.
1006
       (2): The motivation for causal generative models is typically to allow generation of data to answer
1007
       graph-specific interventional and counterfactual queries, that require knowledge of the full causal
1008   graph. With STEAM, however, we seek to generate useful synthetic data only from the observational
1009   distribution, for use by analysts with goals such as treatment effect estimation (e.g., CATE).
1010
1011
       (3): STEAM’s design can, in principle, incorporate any generative model for QX , essentially acting
       as a wrapper around QX to improve its generation quality for causal inference tasks. This allows
1012
       STEAM to very easily empower many existing generative modelling frameworks, without having to
1013
       incorporate bespoke generators. Also, it allows STEAM models to continuously improve along with
1014   the base generative model. Existing causal generative models do not generally allow such flexibility,
1015   and therefore cannot as easily benefit from future improvements in underlying generic generative
1016   models.
1017
       Despite these differences, we conduct empirical comparisons between STEAM and some baseline
1018
       causal generative models in Appendix O.
1019

1022   Privacy. Despite the popularity of some of the above generators, memorisation of training samples
1023   is a phenomenon observed in generative models (Ghalebikesabi et al., 2023). Therefore, provably
1024   private generation is often desired to limit the amount of information leaked. Differential privacy
1025   Dwork et al. (2006) is the most common standard adopted, and there are multiple generators which

1228   E     D ISCUSSION ON ALTERNATIVE METRICS
1229
1230   While we propose a set of metrics M for evaluation of Dsynth , there are many possible alternatives to
1231   each choice we make. Our choices enable evaluation of how well Dsynth adheres to our desiderata,
1232   but, like any metrics, they may be sub-optimal for certain data holders with specific preferences.
1233   Here, we list some alternative definitions, and we detail when they may be preferable. We would like
1234   to emphasise that conducting any reasonable assessment of the preservation of (i)-(iii) is beneficial
1235   compared to standard evaluation practices.
1236
1237   E.1   A LTERNATIVE COVARIATE DISTRIBUTION ASSESSMENT
1238
       As we state in the main paper, comparison of PX and QX is essentially a standard synthetic data
1239
       evaluation problem, and therefore any standard protocol can be applied.
1240
1241   For example, if the dimensionality of X is small, manual evaluation via visualisation may be
       preferable to the precision/recall analysis we suggest, as this can provide a more granular and


                                                           23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       interpretable assessment. On the other hand, if a single all-encompassing score is desired, rather than
1243   the two-dimensional metric (Pα,X , Rβ,X ), then statistical divergence metrics can offer this. These
1244   one-dimensional metrics can lead to more straightforward model selection than (Pα,X , Rβ,X ), as
1245   ordering based on a two-dimensional metric can be ambiguous.
1246
1247   E.2     A LTERNATIVE TREATMENT ASSIGNMENT MECHANISM ASSESSMENT
1248
1249   Similarly, there is a vast array of metrics which could be substituted into (5) over Jensen-Shannon
1250   distance which could measure the difference between PW |X and QW |X . These include metrics
1251   such as KL divergence and Wasserstein distance, which are also very common in machine learning
1252   literature. For example, a data holder may prefer KL divergence if they want to more harshly punish
1253   QW |X for failing to place density where PW |X is probable, encouraging mode-covering behaviour.
1254
       On the other hand, if a data holder wants to more harshly punish QW |X for spreading mass away
       from the modes, Wasserstein distance may be preferable, leading to mode-seeking behaviour. JSD
1255
       achieves a balance between these two focuses, but if a data holder has a strong preference for one
1256
       or the other, these alternate choices would be preferable. Nevertheless, we suggest that, apart from
1257   extreme scenarios, most reasonable methods to assess the preservation of PW |X will lead to similar
1258   analysis.

1307
1308   In CATE estimation, model validation is a difficult task (Curth & Van Der Schaar, 2023). As such, it
1309   is reasonable to expect that a set of downstream analysts conducting CATE estimation on Dsynth will
1310   use different learners. Therefore, we want UPEHE to reflect the expected difference in downstream
1311   performance between Dsynth and Dreal across a diverse array of potential learners, such that it is
1312   representative for the entire population of analysts, and has limited bias towards any particular learner
1313
       class. To achieve this, we propose averaging UPEHE across a family of CATE learners F, and we
       suggest that larger |F|, and diverse selection of the learners within F, is preferable.
1314
1315   Of course, there is a trade off between the size of F, and therefore the stability of UPEHE , and the
1316   computational cost of repeated CATE estimation. With this in mind, to limit the computation involved
1317   in calculating UPEHE , we suggest that users should be selective of the learners included in F to
1318   maximize learner diversity, and minimise |F|. For example, in our experiments we set |F| = 4, and
1319
       we chose learners from both of the high-level CATE learning strategies described in Curth & van der
       Schaar (2021) (i.e. one-step plug-in learners, and two-step learners). Specifically, for the one-step
1320
       learners we use S- and T- learners (Künzel et al., 2019), and for the two-step learners we use RA and
1321
       DR learners (Kennedy et al., 2020). All four of these learners conduct CATE estimation differently,
1322   and encode different inductive biases in their approaches, and thus they form a good diverse base for
1323   F.

       Proposition 1. If QX satisfies (ϵX , δX )-differential privacy, QW |X satisfies (ϵW , δW )-differential
1355
       privacy, and QY |W,X satisfies (ϵY , δY )-differential privacy, STEAM satisfies (ϵtotal , δtotal )-differential
       privacy, where ϵtotal = ϵX + ϵW + ϵY , δtotal = δX + δW + δY .
1356
1357
       Proof. QX generates X, and satisfies (ϵX , δX )-differential privacy by assumption.
1358
1359   By the post-processing theorem, inputting X as the condition to QW |X does not affect its privacy.
1360   QW |X generates W , and satisfies (ϵW , δW )-differential privacy by assumption.
1361
       By the post-processing theorem, inputting W and X as the conditions to QY |W,X does not affect
1362
       their privacy. QY |W,X generates Y , and satisfies (ϵY , δY )-differential privacy by assumption.
1363
1364   STEAM generates (X, W, Y ), and is the composition of QX , QW |X , and QY |W,X , i.e. STEAM =
1365   (QX , QW |X , QY |W,X )
1366   Therefore, by the composition theorem STEAM satisfies (ϵtotal , δtotal )-differential privacy, where
1367   ϵtotal = ϵX + ϵW + ϵY , δtotal = δX + δW + δY .

       L       C ONGENIALITY BIAS
1645
1646
       Congeniality bias (Curth & Van Der Schaar, 2023) is a phenomenon which may arise from generation
1647   with STEAM. In this scenario it refers to the fact that downstream models which are structurally
1648   similar to the outcome generator, QY |W,X , may be advantaged in their performance on Dsynth . For
1649   example, if the POs from an S-learner are used for QY |W,X , the outcome generation mechanism
1650   in Dsynth may be modelled in such a way that it allows downstream S-learners to better estimate
1651   CATEs than other learners. While we acknowledge this phenomenon may disadvantage certain
1652   downstream models, we note that our outcome error metric, UPEHE , averages across a number of
1653   downstream learner types, such that conducting generative model selection with UPEHE should lead
1654   to good performance across a wide variety of downstream learners, not just those similar to QY |W,X ,
1655
       helping to reduce this congeniality bias.

       M       A LLOCATION OF THE PRIVACY BUDGET IN STEAM
1660
1661   In STEAM, uniform distribution of the privacy budget ϵ amongst the three component models ensures
1662
       (ϵ, δ)-DP. However, such allocation is uninformed on the difficulty of modelling of PX , PW |X , and
       PY |W,X , and their relative importance to downstream analysts.
1663
1664   In relation to the importance of each distribution, one immediate improvement can be to distribute
1665   ϵ according to some preference function f : (0, ∞) × △2 → ϵ · △2 (where △2 is the 2-simplex)
1666   which takes input of the budget ϵ and weights w for the relative importance of good modelling in QX ,
1667   QW |X , and QY |W,X , and outputs a corresponding ϵ distribution. For example, a simple preference
1668   function definition would be f (ϵ, w) = ϵ · w where w could be defined by a data holder with some
1669
       prior knowledge of the importance level of each component distribution to downstream analysts.
       Another approach, if it is not necessary to specify the desired ϵ distribution a priori, is to treat it as a
1670
       hyperparameter, to be tuned over a series of runs to optimize some metric, such as a combination of
1671
       Pα,X , Rβ,X , JSDπ , and UPEHE .
1672
1673   Incorporating knowledge of the complexity of modelling PX , PW |X , and PY |W,X is more difficult.
       While some proxy measures could be established, such as the number of covariates in X indicating the

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1674
       complexity of PW |X , establishing a robust understanding of how the complexity of these distributions
1675   relate and compare, is highly non-trivial, and as such we leave this for future work.

1679   Generative model performance degrades under covariate shift. Covariate shift is a phenomenon
1680   which arises in data containing treatments, as the covariates of treated and untreated patients tend

1682   difference between these distributions grows, so too does covariate shift. We demonstrate degradation
1683
       under high covariate shift through a simple experiment, where we create Dreal with d = 50 covariates
       that are drawn from the following mixture model:

1725   O    C AUSAL GENERATIVE MODEL COMPARISON
1726
1727   Causal generative models, which estimate the underlying structural causal model of a dataset, are
       a related family of generative models. We discuss the differences in positioning and assumptions


                                                             32
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1728
       of our work compared to causal generative models in our extended related works, and here we
1729   produce empirical results for these model on the ACTG, IHDP, and ACIC datasets from Sec-
1730   tion 7.1. For baseline models, we consider two causal generative models: the additive noise
1731   model (ANM) (Hoyer et al., 2008) implementation in the DoWhy-GCM python package (Blöbaum
1732   et al., 2024), and a diffusion-based causal model (DCM) from Chao et al. (2024). We use the
1733   code provided by Chao et al. (2024) in their GitHub https://github.com/patrickrchao/
1734   DiffusionBasedCausalModels for the baseline implementations, and we use the same hy-
1735   perparameter settings for both ANM and DCM as in that work.
1736   However, in order to fairly compare these models with STEAM, we must first reconcile the differences
1737   in assumptions made, as discussed in Appendix B. For ACTG, IHDP, and ACIC, we do not have
1738   knowledge of the true causal graph, we simply know which features are the treatment and outcome.
1739   As such, we must first construct some reasonable causal graph using this knowledge to supply to
1740   these methods. We do so with three methods:
1741         1. Construction of a naive graph Gnaive , in which each covariate causes W and Y , W causes Y ,
1742            and every pair of covariates has a causal relationship between them;
1743
             2. Using the constraint-based PC causal discovery algorithm (Spirtes et al., 2001) to discover
1744
                an estimated graph Gdiscovered from the Markov Equivalence Class for the true causal graph
1745            and;
1746
             3. Pruning the discovered causal graph Gdiscovered by removing any edges which contradict the
1747
                DGP we assume. As such, any edges from Y to W or X, or from W to X are removed to
1748
                form Gpruned .
1749
1750   In Table 16 we report the results for ANM and DCM with each of these graph discovery methods,
1751   and we compare to the best performing STEAM models from Appendix I. For each dataset, we see
1752   that the relevant STEAM model outperforms all instantiations of the causal generative models in
1753   almost every metric, only being outperformed to a statistically significant level in Rβ,X on the ACIC
1754   dataset. These results validate that, when the true causal graph is not known, our less restrictive
1755   assumptions enable more useful generation of synthetic data containing treatments. We also see that
1756   the differences between the graph discovery methods are relatively small.

       P    F UTURE W ORK : E XTENSIONS TO OTHER DGP S
1761
       STEAM places minimal assumptions upon the causal graph of Dreal . We see that our assumed DGP
1762
       involving PX , PW |X , and PY |W,X is applicable across a very wide range of scenarios for datasets
1763
       containing treatments, particularly in medical settings, as we describe in Section 6. If this DGP is
1764   known to not hold for Dreal , then it would be unadvisable to apply STEAM in its current form, as its
1765   inductive biases may not be helpful. However, if a similar setting arises where the overarching DGP
1766   is known, but the specific causal graph is not, then altering the generation order between X, W , and
1767   Y to mimic this alternative DGP, with a similar underlying motivation as in STEAM - that generation
1768   mimicking the real DGP is preferable to joint-level generation - can be done.
1769
       An example of a more complicated setting, where STEAM is not immediately applicable is when
1770   dealing with longitudinal data, which has time-varying covariates, treatments, and outcomes. In this
1771   setting, our assumed DGP would not hold in general, as features at a specific time point will likely
1772   have temporal causal relationships with earlier features, e.g. at time t, Xt could be affected by Wt−1 ,
1773   which is not modelled in our assumed DGP. Potential alterations to STEAM could adjust for this
1774   time-varying DGP, where one could consider a ‘base’ STEAM model, as described in this work,
1775   operating at each time step, with additional inputs from the immediately preceding time step to model
1776   temporal causal relationships.
