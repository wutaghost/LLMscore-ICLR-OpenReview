                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               With the increasing acquisition of datasets over time, we now have access to pre-
010
               cise and varied descriptions of the world, capturing all sorts of phenomena. These
011            datasets can be seen as empirical observations of unknown causal generative pro-
012            cesses, which can commonly be described by Structural Causal Models (SCMs).
013            Recovering these causal generative processes from observations poses formidable
014            challenges, and often require to learn a specific generative model for each dataset.
015            In this work, we propose to learn a single model capable of inferring in a zero-
016            shot manner the causal generative processes of datasets. Rather than learning a
017            specific SCM for each dataset, we enable the Fixed-Point Approach (FiP) pro-
018            posed in Scetbon et al. (2024), to infer the generative SCMs conditionally on their
019
               empirical representations. More specifically, we propose to amortize the learning
               of a conditional version of FiP to infer generative SCMs from observations and
020
               causal structures on synthetically generated datasets. We show that our model is
021
               capable of predicting in zero-shot the true generative SCMs, and as a by-product,
022            of (i) generating new dataset samples, and (ii) inferring intervened ones. Our
023            experiments demonstrate that our amortized procedure achieves performances on
024            par with SoTA methods trained specifically for each dataset on both in and out-
025            of-distribution problems. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first time that
026            SCMs are inferred in a zero-shot manner from observations, paving the way for a
027            paradigmatic shift towards the assimilation of causal knowledge across datasets.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Learning the causal generative process from observations is a fundamental problem in several sci-
033
      entific domains (Sachs et al., 2005; Foster et al., 2011; Xie et al., 2012), as it offers a comprehensive
      understanding of the data generation process, and allows for simulating the effect of controlled
034
      experiments/interventions. With a learned model of the generative process, one could even acceler-
035
      ate scientific discoveries by reliably predicting the effects of unseen interventions, eliminating the
036   need for laboratory experiments (Ke et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024). Further, understanding the
037   causal mechanisms behind the data generation process helps in robust representation learning as it
038   provides a principled solution to tackle out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization (Arjovsky et al.,
039   2019; Zhang et al., 2020; Schölkopf et al., 2021).
040
      A popular approach for modeling causal processes is the structural causal model (SCM) frame-
041
      work (Peters et al., 2017) where causal mechanisms are modeled via structured functional relation-
042   ships, and causal structures are given by directed acyclic graphs (DAGs). Since in several practical
043   applications we only have access to observational data, the task of recovering the generative SCM
044   from observations is an important problem in causality (Pearl, 2009). Solving this inverse problem
045   is challenging as both the graph and the functional relationships modeling the causal mechanisms
046   are unknown a priori. Several works have focused on the graph recovery problem by approximating
047   the discrete search space of DAGs (Chickering, 2002; Peters et al., 2014; Deleu et al., 2022), or
048   using continuous optimization objectives (Zheng et al., 2018; Lachapelle et al., 2019; Lippe et al.,
049   2021). However, all these works focus only on the structure learning aspect and do not explicitly
050
      evaluate the learning of the functional mechanisms.
051   Another line of work has studied the recovery of the functional relationships from data, often un-
052   der structural assumptions like known causal graphs or topological orders, using maximum likeli-
053   hood estimation (MLE) independently per node (Blöbaum et al., 2022), autoregressive flows (Khe-
      makhem et al., 2021; Geffner et al., 2022; Javaloy et al., 2023) to model causal generative pro-

      1.1   R ELATED W ORKS
109
110   Amortized Causal Learning. Amortized methods have been explored in causality research in
111   order to learn general algorithms that can infer in a zero-shot manner causal knowledge from ob-
112   servations. (Zhang et al., 2023) proposed to amortize the learning of a causal inference method to
113   estimate average treatment effect (ATE), (Gupta et al., 2023) used amortized learning to perform
114   model selection for causal discovery, and (Lorch et al., 2022; Ke et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2024;
115   Scetbon et al., 2024) proposed to infer causal structures, such as causal graphs or topological orders,
116   from observations using amortized learning. All these methods rely on the availability of synthetic
117
      datasets generated during training, enabling their learning using supervised objectives to predict
      the causal knowledge of interest. In this work, we extend this line of works, and propose to infer
118
      in a zero-shot manner the functional relationships of SCMs from observations and their associated
119
      causal structures. To achieve this, we propose to amortize the learning of causal embeddings of
120   synthetically generated datasets, which then served as conditions to train a generalized version of
121   FiP (Scetbon et al., 2024) that infers the generative SCMs in zero-shot.
122
123
      Autoregressive Causal Learning. While a vast majority of the literature on causal discovery con-
124   cerns structure learning, recent works on causal autoregressive flows (Khemakhem et al., 2021;
125   Javaloy et al., 2023) focus on state-of-the-art generative modeling techniques for learning the causal
126   generative processes induced by SCMs. Khemakhem et al. (2021) proved a novel connection be-
127   tween SCMs and autoregressive flows, as the mapping from noise variables to observable variables
128   in SCMs is a triangular map given the topological order of the causal graph. While their work re-
129   stricted the functional relationships to additive and affine flows, this was extended by Javaloy et al.
130   (2023) to more flexible triangular monotonic increasing maps. More recently, Scetbon et al. (2024)
131   proposed to directly model SCMs, viewed as fixed-point problems on the ordered nodes, using
132
      transformer-based architectures. While these methods enable efficient learning of SCMs and their
      generative processes, they all require to train a specific generative model per dataset. In contrast,
133
      we present a novel extension of FiP (Scetbon et al., 2024) by conditioning the fixed-point process
134
      on dataset embeddings, thereby amortizing the learning of functional relationships across different
135   instances from the functional class of SCMs.

139
140   Structural Causal Models. An SCM defines the causal generative process of a set of d endoge-
141   nous (causal) random variables V = {X1 , · · · , Xd }, where each causal variable Xi is defined as a
142   function of a subset of other causal variables (V \ {Xi }) and an exogenous noise variable Ni :
143
                          Xi = Fi (PA(Xi ), Ni ) s.t. PA(Xi ) ⊂ V , Xi ̸∈ PA(Xi ) .                      (1)
144
145
146
      Hence, an SCM describes the data-generation process of X := [X1 , · · · , Xd ] ∼ PX from the
      noise variables N := [N1 , · · · , Nd ] ∼ PN via the function F := [F1 , · · · , Fd ], and a graph
147
      G ∈ {0, 1}d×d indicating the parents of each variable Xi , that is [G]i,j := 1 if Xj ∈ PA(Xi ).
148
      G is assumed to be a directed and acyclic graph (DAG), and we say the SCM is Markovian if
149   the exogenous variable distribution PN has mutually independent components. In the following, we
150   always assume SCMs to be Markovian and denote them S(PN , G, F ). In addition, we only consider
151   additive noise models (ANM), which are SCMs of the form Xi = Fi (PA(Xi )) + Ni .
152
153   DAG-Attention Mechanism. In (Scetbon et al., 2024) the authors propose to leverage the trans-
154   former architecture to learn SCMs from observations. By reparameterizing an SCM according to a
155   topological ordering induced by its graph, the authors show that any SCM can be reformulated as a
156   fixed-point problem of the form X = H(X, N ) where H admits a simple triangular structure:
157
158           [Jacx H(x, n)]i,j = 0,     if   j ≥ i,   and   [Jacn H(x, n)]i,j    = 0,   if i ̸= j,
159
      and Jacx H, Jacn H denote the Jacobian of H w.r.t the first and second variables respectively.
160
161   Motivated by this fixed-point reformulation, Scetbon et al. (2024) consider a transformed-based
      architecture to model the functional relationships of SCMs and propose a new attention mechanism

182   3     C ONDITIONAL F I P
183
184   In this work, we propose to infer in zero-shot the generative SCMs from observations and causal
185
      structures using amortized training on synthetically generated problems. Our approach is composed
      of two key components: (1) a dataset encoder that generates dataset embeddings based on observa-
186
      tions and causal structures, and (2) a conditional variant of FiP (Scetbon et al., 2024), designed to
187
      infer in zero-shot the generative SCMs of datasets when conditioned on their dataset embeddings
188   produced by the encoder. In the following, we first present our dataset encoder, then introduce
189   cond-FiP, and conclude by the use case of generating new observational and interventional samples.
190
191   3.1    DATASET E NCODER
192
193   The objective of this section is to develop a method capable of producing efficient latent represen-
194   tations of datasets. To achieve this, we propose to train an encoder that predicts in zero-shot the
195   noise samples from their associated observations given the causal structures using amortized learn-
196   ing. The latent representations obtained by our encoder will be used later as dataset embeddings to
197   infer SCMs in a zero-shot manner.
198
199

211   Encoder Architecture. Following (Lorch et al., 2021; Scetbon et al., 2024), we propose to encode
212   datasets using a transformer-based architecture that alternatively attends on both the sample and node
213   dimensions of the input. More specifically, after having embedded the dataset DX into a higher
214   dimensional space using a linear operation L(DX ) ∈ Rn×d×dh where dh is the hidden dimension,
215   the encoder E alternates the application of transformer blocks, consisting of a self-attention block
      followed by an MLP block (Vaswani et al., 2017), where the attention mechanism is applied either

      across the samples n or the nodes d. When attending over samples, the encoder uses standard self-
217   attention as defined in (2) without masking (M = {0}n×n ), however, when the model attends over
218   the nodes, we leverage the knowledge of the causal graph to mask the undesirable relationships
219   between the nodes, that is we set M = +∞ × (1 − G), with the convention that 0 × (+∞) = 0, in
220   the standard attention (2). Finally, the obtained embeddings E(L(DX ), G) ∈ Rn×d×dh are passed
221   to a prediction network H : Rn×d×dh → Rn×d defined as 2-hidden layers MLP which brings back
222   the encoded datasets to their original space.
223
224   Training Procedure. To infer the noise samples in a zero-shot manner, we propose to minimize
225   the mean squared error (MSE) of predicting the target noises DN from the input (DX , G) over the
226   distribution of SCMs PS available during training:
227                                  ES∼PS ||DN − H ◦ E(L(DX ), G)||22 .

232   the new targets as F (DX ) := DX − DN , we propose instead to train our encoder to predict the

      3.2   C OND -F I P: C ONDITIONAL F IXED -P OINT D ECODER
249
250   In this section, we present our proposed approach to infer the functional mechanisms of SCMs in
251   zero-shot via amortized training using synthetically generated datasets. To do so, we propose to
252   extend the formulation of FiP introduced in Scetbon et al. (2024) by enabling it to predict functions,
253   and use the dataset embeddings E(L(DX ), G) obtained by our trained encoder as conditions to infer
254   the correct functional mechanisms of the associated SCMs. See Figure 1 for a sketch of Cond-FiP.
255
256   Training Setting. Analogous to the encoder training setup, we assume that we have access to a
257   distribution of SCMs S(PN , G, F ) ∼ PS at training time, from which we can extract empirical
258
      representations of the form (DX , G) containing the observations and the associated causal graphs
      respectively. Here, we aim at learning a single model T that can infer in zero-shot the functional
259
      mechanisms of an SCM given its empirical representation. More formally, we aim at training T
260
      such that for given any dataset DX and its associated causal graph G obtained from an SCM
261   S(PN , G, F ) ∼ PS , the conditional function z ∈ Rd → T (z, DX , G) ∈ Rd induced by the
262   model approximates the true functional relationship F : z ∈ Rd → F (z) ∈ Rd . We achieve this by
263   enabling the fixed-point approach of (Scetbon et al., 2024) to be conditioned on dataset embeddings
264   provided by our dataset encoder. We detail our procedure below.
265
266   Decoder Architecture. The design of our decoder is based on the FiP architecture (Scetbon et al.,
267   2024) for fixed-point SCM learning, with two major differences enabling conditional predictions:
268   (1) we use the dataset embeddings obtained from our encoder as a high dimensional codebook to
269   embed the nodes, and (2) we leverage adaptive layer norm operators (Peebles & Xie, 2023) in the
      transformer blocks of FiP to enable conditional attention mechanisms.

      Conditional Embedding. The key change of our decoder compared to the original FiP is in the
271   embedding of the input. Scetbon et al. (2024) propose to embed a data point z := [z1 , . . . , zd ] ∈ Rd
272   into a high dimensional space using a learnable codebook C := [C1 , . . . , Cd ]T ∈ Rd×dh and
273   positional embedding P := [P1 , . . . , Pd ]T ∈ Rd×dh , from which they define:
274
                               zemb := [z1 ∗ C1 , . . . , zd ∗ Cd ]T + P ∈ Rd×dh .
275
      By doing so, Scetbon et al. (2024) ensures that the embedded samples admit the same causal struc-
276
      ture as the original samples. However, this embedding layer is only adapted if the samples con-
277   sidered are all drawn from the same observational distribution, as the representation of the nodes,
278   that is given by the codebook C, is fixed. In order to generalize their embedding strategy to the
279   case where multiple SCMs are considered, we consider conditional codebooks and positional em-
280   beddings adapted for each dataset. More formally, given a dataset DX and a causal graph G, we
281   propose to define the conditional codebook and positional embedding associated as
282                    C(DX , G) := µ(DX , G)WC , and P (DX , G) := µ(DX , G)WP ,
283   where µ(DX , G) := MaxPool(E(L(DX ), G)) ∈ Rd×dh is obtained by max-pooling w.r.t the sam-
284   ple dimension the dataset embedding E(L(DX ), G) ∈ Rn×d×dh produced by our trained encoder,
285   and WC , WP ∈ Rdh ×dh are learnable parameters. Then we propose to embed any point z ∈ Rd
286   conditionally on (DX , G) by considering:
287               zemb := [z1 ∗ C1 (DX , G), . . . , zd ∗ Cd (DX , G)]T + P (DX , G) ∈ Rd×dh .
288
289   Adaptive Transfomer Block. Once an input z ∈ Rd has been embedded into a higher dimen-
290   sional space zemb ∈ Rd×dh , Scetbon et al. (2024) propose to modelize SCMs by simulating the
291   reconstruction of the data from noise. Starting from n0 ∈ Rd×dh a learnable parameter, they pro-
292   pose to update the current noise L ≥ 1 times by computing:
293                                   nℓ+1 = h(DAM (nℓ , zemb )zemb + nℓ )
294   where h refers to the MLP block, and for clarity, we omit both the layer’s dependence on its param-
295   eters and the inclusion of layer normalization in the notation. Note that here the authors consider
296   their DAG-Attention (3) mechanism in order to modelize correctly the root nodes of the SCM. To
297   obtain a conditional formulation of their computational scheme, we propose first to replace the start-
298
      ing noise n0 by a conditional one w.r.t. (DX , G) and defined as n0 := µ(DX , G)Wn0 ∈ Rd×dh ,
      where Wn0 ∈ Rdh ×dh is a learnable parameter. Additionally, we propose to add adaptive layer nor-
299
      malization operators (Peebles & Xie, 2023) to both attention and MLP blocks, where each scale or
300
      shift is obtained by applying a 1 hidden-layer MLP to the condition, which in our case is µ(DX , G).
301
302   Projection. To project back the latent representation of z obtained from previous stages, that is
303   nL ∈ Rd×dh , we propose to simply use a linear operation to get zb = nL Wout ∈ Rd , where
304   Wout ∈ Rdh is learnable. In the following, we denote the overall architecture T , which given an
305   input z ∈ Rd and some condition (DX , G) outputs zb, that is T (z, DX , G) = zb. Note that, for
306   simplicity, we omit the dependence of zb on (DX , G) in the notation.
307
308   Training Procedure. Recall that our goal is infer in zero-shot the functional mechanisms of SCMs
309   given their empirical representations. Therefore, to train our model T , we propose to minimize the
310   reconstruction error of the true functional mechanisms estimated by our model over the distribution
311
      of SCMs PS . More precisely, for any SCM S(PN , G, F ) ∼ PS and its empirical representation
      (DX , G), we aim at minimizing
312
313                                      Ez∼PX ∥T (z, DX , G) − F (z)∥22                              (4)
314   where z ∼ PX is chosen independent of the random dataset DX . Therefore, when integrating over
315   the distribution of SCMs, we obtain the following optimization problem:
316                                 ES∼PS Ez∼PX ∥T (z, DX , G) − F (z)∥22 .
317
      To compute (4), we propose to sample n independent samples X1′ , . . . , Xn′ from PX , leading to a
318   new dataset DX ′ independent of DX , from which we obtain the following optimization problem:
319
                                 ES∼PS ∥T (DX ′ , DX , G) − F (DX ′ )∥22 .

321   Therefore our training objective aims at learning T such that for any given empirical representation
322   (DX , G) of an unknown SCM S(PN , G, F ) ∼ PS , the conditional function induced by our model,

326   Generation with Cond-FiP. Cond-FiP is capable of generating new data samples: given a random
327   vector noise n ∼ PN , we can estimate the observational sample associated according to an unknown
328   SCM S(PN , G, F ) ∼ PS as long as we have access to its empirical representation (DX , G). More
329   formally, starting from n0 = n, we infer the associated observation by computing for ℓ = 1, . . . , d:
330
                                         nℓ = T (nℓ−1 , DX , G) + n .                                    (5)
331
332   After (at most) d iterations, nd corresponds to the observational sample associated to the original
333   noise n according to our conditional SCM T (·, DX , G). To sample noise from PN , we leverage
334   cond-FiP that can estimates noise samples under the ANM assumption by computing D   d N := DX −
335   T (DX , E(L(DX ), G). From these estimated noise samples, we can efficiently estimate the joint
336   distribution of the noise thanks to the Markovian assumption by computing the inverse cdfs of the
337   marginals as proposed in Scetbon et al. (2024).
338
339   Interventional Predictions. Cond-FiP also enables the estimation of interventions given an em-
340   pirical representation (DX , G) of an unkown SCM S(PN , G, F ) ∼ PS . To achieve this, we start
341   from a noise sample n, and we generate the associated intervened sample zbdo by directly modify-
342   ing the conditional SCM provided by Cond-FiP. More specifically, we modify in place the SCM
343   obtained by Cond-FiP, leading to its interventional version T do (·, DX , G). Now, generating an in-
344
      tervened sample can be done by applying the loop defined in (5), starting from n and using the
      intervened SCM T do (·, DX , G) rather than the original one.

349   We begin by describing our experimental setup in Section 4.1, where we detail the training and in-

353   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
354
355   Data Generation Process. We use the synthetic data generation procedure proposed by Lorch
356   et al. (2022) to generate SCMs in our empirical study, as it offers a wide variety of SCMs, mak-
357   ing it ideal for amortized training. We have the option to sample graphs as per the following
358   schemes: Erods-Renyi (Erdos & Renyi, 1959), scale-free models (Barabási & Albert, 1999), Watts-
359   Strogatz (Watts & Strogatz, 1998), and stochastic block models (Holland et al., 1983). To sample
360
      noise variables, we can choose from either the gaussian or laplace distribution where variances are
      sampled randomly. Further, we can control the complexity of causal relationships: either we set
361
      them to be linear (LIN) functions randomly sampled, or use random fourier features (RFF) for gen-
362
      erating random non-linear causal relationships. We construct two distribution of SCMs PIN , and
363   POUT , which vary based on the choice for sampling causal graphs, noise variables, and causal rela-
364   tionships. The classification aids in understanding the creation of train and test datasets.
365
366          • In Distribution (PIN ): We sample causal graphs using the Erods-Renyi and scale-free
367            models schemes. Noise variables are sampled from the gaussian distribution, and we allow
368            for both LIN and RFF causal relationships.
369          • Out of Distribution (POUT ): Causal graphs are drawn from Watts-Strogatz and stochastic
370            block models schemes. Noise variables follow the laplace distribution, and both the LIN
371            and RFF cases are used to sample functions. However, the parameters of these distributions
372            are sampled from a different range as compared to PIN to create a distribution shift.
373
374   Training Datasets. We randomly sample SCMs from the PIN distribution, and we restrict the total
375   nodes of each SCM to be d = 20 nodes. From each of these SCMs, we extract the causal graph G
376   and further generate ntrain = 400 observations to obtain DX . This procedure is used for generating
377   the training datasets for both amortized training of the dataset encoder and Cond-FiP. In total, we
      generate ≃ 4e6 SCMs to train our models.

       D.1   R ESULTS WITHOUT TRUE GRAPH
975
976    Our results in the main paper (Table 1, Table 2, Table 3) require the knowledge of true graph (G)
977    as part of the input context to Cond-FiP. In this section we conduct where we don’t provide the true
978    graph in the input context, rather we infer the graph Ĝ using an amortized causal discovery approach
979    (AVICI (Lorch et al., 2022)) from the observational data DX . We chose AVICI for this task since
980    it can enable to infer the graph in a zero-shot manner, hence allowing the combined pipeline of
981    AVICI + Cond-FiP to be a zero-shot technique for learning SCMs. More precisely, AVICI zero-shot
982    infers the graph G from input context DX , and we pass (Ĝ, DX ) as the input context for Cond-FiP.
983    Therefore, for any z ∈ Rd , Cond-FiP ( T (z, DX , Ĝ)) aims to replicate the functional mechanism
984    F (z) of the underlying SCM.
985    The results for benchmarking Cond-FiP with inferred graphs using AVICI for the task of noise
986    prediction, sample generation, and interventional generation are provided in Table 10, Table 11,
987    and Table 12 respectively. For a fair comparison, the baselines FiP, DECI, and DoWhy also use the
988    inferred graph (Ĝ) by AVICI instead of the true graph (G). We find that Cond-FiP remains competi-
989    tive to baselines even for the scenario of unknown true causal graph. Hence, our amortized training
990    procedure can be extended easily for zero-shot inference of both graphs and causal mechanisms of
991    the SCM.

1460   In our main results (Table 1, 2, 3) we experimented with datasets sampled from SCM following a
1461   different distribution (LIN OUT, RFF OUT) than the datasets used for training Cond-FiP (LIN IN,
1462   RFF IN). As expected, the performance of all methods drop in the presence of distribution shift. We
1463   now analyze how sensitive is Cond-FiP to distribution shifts by comparing its performance across
1464   scenarios as the severity of the distribution shift is increased.
1465   To illustrate how we control the magnitude of distribution shift, we discuss the difference in the
1466   distribution of causal mechanisms across PIN and POUT . The distribution shift arises because the
1467   support of the parameters of causal mechanisms changes from PIN to POUT . For example, for linear
1468   causal mechanism case, the weights in PIN are sampled uniformly from (−3, −1) ∪ (1, 3); while in
1469   POUT they are sampled from uniformly from (0.5, 4). We now change the support set of the param-
1470   eters in POUT to 0.5α, 4α, so that by increasing α we make the distribution shift more severe. We
1471   follow this procedure for the support set of all the parameters associated with functional mechanisms
1472
       and generate distributions (POUT (α)) with varying shift w.r.t PIN by changing α. Note that α = 1
       corresponds to the same POUT as the one used for sampling datasets in our main results.
1473
1474   We conduct two experiments for evaluating the robustness of Cond-FiP to distribution shifts, de-
1475   scribed ahead.
1476
1477
              • Controlling Shift in Causal Mechanisms. We start with the parameter configuration of
                POUT from the setup in main results; and then control the magnitude of shift by changing
1478
                the support set of parameters of causal mechanisms.

1480          • Controlling Shift in Noise Variablse. We start with the parameter configuration of POUT
1481
                from the setup in main results; and then control the magnitude of shift by changing the
                support set of parameters of noise distribution.

       E.1   A MORTIZED L EARNING IN C AUSAL ML
1677
1678   The goal of amortized learning is to learn to predict the solution of similar instances of the same
1679   optimization problem. More formally, given some random inputs I ∼ PI and an objective function
1680   L(θ, I), the goal of amortized learning is to learn a parameterized model Mϕ s.t.
1681
                                        Mϕ (I) ≃ θ∗ (I) := argminθ∈Θ L(θ, I)
1682
1683
       To train such a model, amortized learning requires to have access to various pairs (I, θ∗ (I)) and
1684
       optimize the parameters ϕ of M in a supervised manner. In the causal literature, this concept has
1685   been employed to learn graphs, topological orders, and even the average treatment effect (ATE) from
1686   observations (Zhang et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2023; Lorch et al., 2022; Ke et al., 2022; Wu et al.,
1687   2024; Scetbon et al., 2024). In all these cases, the variable I denotes a dataset of observations DX
1688   and the target variable θ∗ (I) can represent either the causal graph, the topological order or the ATE
1689   depending on the task considered.
1690
       In our work, we take this technique a step further by directly predicting functions from datasets (and
1691   causal graphs). More formally, in our setting, I := (DX , G) and θ∗ (I) := F where F is the true SCM
1692   generating DX .
1693
1694   E.2   I NFERENCE WITH C OND -F I P
1695
1696   Cond-FiP Model. Once Cond-FiP is trained, we have access to two trained models: (1) an encoder
1697   that given a dataset DX and the causal graph associated G produced an embedding µ(DX , G) as
1698   defined l.277., and (2) a decoder that given an embedding µ and a causal graph G, produces a
1699   function z ∈ Rd → T (z, µ, G) ∈ Rd .
1700   Cond-FiP simply consists of the composition of the two models, that is given a dataset DX and
1701   the causal graph associated G, produce a function z ∈ Rd → T (z, µ(DX , G), G) ∈ Rd , which for
1702   simplicity we denote z ∈ Rd → T (z, DX , G) ∈ Rd . Having clarified this, we can now proceed to
1703   more detailed explanations of the inference process using Cond-FiP.
1704
1705   Data Generation. Given a dataset DX and its causal graph G, we denote z → T (z, DX , G)
1706   the function infered by Cond-FiP. This function defines the predicted SCM obtained by our model,
1707   and we can directly use it to generate new points. More precisely, given a noise sample n, we can
1708
       generate the associated observational sample by solving the following equation in x:
1709
1710                                             x = T (x, DX , G) + n
1711
1712   To solve this fixed-point equation, we rely on the fact that G is a DAG, which enables to solve
1713
       the fixed-point problem using the following simple iterative procedure. Starting with z0 = n, we
       compute for ℓ = 1, . . . , d where d is the number of nodes
1714
1715
1716                                          zℓ = T (zℓ−1 , DX , G) + n
1717
1718
       After d iterations we obtain the following,
1719
1720                                           zd = T (zd , DX , G) + n
1721
1722   Therefore, zd is the solution of the fixed-point problem above, which corresponds to the observa-
1723   tional sample associated to n according to our predicted SCM z → T (z, DX , G).
1724   Interventional Prediction. Recall that given a dataset DX and its causal graph G, z ∈
1725   Rd → T (z, DX , G) ∈ Rd denotes the SCM infered by Cond-FiP. Let us also denote
1726   the coordinate-wise formulation of our SCM defined for any z ∈ Rd as T (z, DX , G) =
1727   [[T (z, DX , G)]1 , . . . , [T (z, DX , G)]d ], where for all i ∈ {1, . . . , d}, z ∈ Rd → [T (z, DX , G)]i ∈
       R is a real-valued function.


                                                            32
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1728
       In order to intervene on this predicted SCM, we simply have to modify in place the predicted func-
1729   tion. For example, assume that we want to perform the following intervention do(Xi ) = a. Then,
1730
       to obtain the intervened SCM, we define a new function z → T do(Xi )=a (z, DX , G) defined for any
1731
       z ∈ Rd as: [T do(Xi )=a (z, DX , G)]j := [T (z, DX , G)]j if j ̸= i and [T do(Xi )=a (z, DX , G)]i := a.
1732
1733   Now, using this intervened SCM z → T do(Xi )=a (z, DX , G), we can apply the exact same generation
1734   procedure as the one introduced above to generate intervened samples according to our intervened
1735   SCM.

1737   E.3   D ETAILS ON E NCODER T RAINING
1738
       We provide further details on training the encoder and show how recovering the noise is equivalent
1739

1742   corresponding observation according to the following fixed-point equation in X

1746   More precisely, to generate the associated observation, one must solve the above fixed-point equation
1747   in X given the noise N. Let us now introduce the following notation that will be instrumental for the
1748   subsequent discussion: we denote FN (z) : z → F (z, N).
1749   Due to the specific structure of F (determined by the DAG G associated with the SCM), the fixed-
1750   point equation mentioned above can be efficiently solved by iteratively applying the function FN
1751   to the noise (see Eq. (5) in the manuscript). As a direct consequence, the observation X can be
1752   expressed as a function of the noise:

1754                                                X = Fgen (N)
1755

1757   In the following we refer to Fgen as the explicit generative model induced by the SCM.
