009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The growing demand for transparency and interpretability in critical domains has
012            driven increased interests in comprehending the explainability of Message-Passing
013            (MP) Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). Although substantial research efforts have
014            been made to generate explanations for individual graph instances, identifying
015            global explaining concepts for a GNN still poses great challenges, especially when
016
               concepts are desired in a graphical form on the dataset level. While most prior
               works treat GNNs as black boxes, in this paper, we propose to unbox GNNs
017
               by analyzing and extracting critical subtrees incurred by the inner workings of
018
               message passing, which correspond to critical subgraphs in the datasets. By
019            aggregating subtrees in an embedding space with an efficient algorithm, which
020            does not require complex subgraph matching or search, we can make intuitive
021            graphical explanations for Message-Passing GNNs on local, class and global levels.
022            We empirically show that our proposed approach not only generates clean subgraph
023            concepts on a dataset level in contrast to existing global explaining methods which
024            generate non-graphical rules (e.g., language or embeddings) as explanations, but it
025            is also capable of providing explanations for individual instances with a comparable
026            or even superior performance as compared to leading local-level GNN explainers.
027
028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   With the rapid advancements in artificial intelligence, the demand for transparent and explainable AI
031   (XAI) has grown significantly. In sensitive domains like finance, security, and healthcare, where data
032   is often structured as graphs, Message-Passing Graph Neural Networks (MPGNNs) have emerged
033   as a prominent solution due to their straightforward design, remarkable effectiveness, and efficient
034   performance. Understanding and explaining how they make predictions are essential to ensuring
035   fairness, reliability, and maintaining control over AI tools on graph-structured data.
036   Existing works on GNN explainability can be categorized mainly into local-level explainability (Ying
037   et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2020; Vu & Thai, 2020; Yuan et al., 2021; Shan et al., 2021; Bajaj et al., 2021;
038   Lin et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021; Feng et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022a; Lu et al.,
039   2024b) and global-level explainability (Magister et al., 2021; Azzolin et al., 2023; Xuanyuan et al.,
040   2023; Yuan et al., 2020; Wang & Shen, 2022; Huang et al., 2023). While local-level explainability
041   focuses on identifying critical nodes, edges or subgraphs behind the GNN prediction on each
042   individual graph instance, global-level explainability aims to provide a comprehensive explanation of
043   a GNN’s behavior on the entire dataset. However, current global-level GNN explanation methods
044
      either do not provide graphical explanations (Yuan et al., 2020; Magister et al., 2021; Wang & Shen,
      2022) or cannot apply the extracted global rules to explaining individual data instances (Azzolin et al.,
045
      2023; Xuanyuan et al., 2023), making it hard to assess their faithfulness.
046
047   In this paper, we aim to solve the challenging problem of providing clear global explanations of
048   GNNs on the dataset or class level in the format of behaviour differentiating subgraphs (rather than
049   nodes, language rules or prototype embeddings), which is essential to molecular biochemistry and
050
      complex networks, e.g., to molecular property prediction and drug discovery. This is an unsolved
      challenge in the literature because there is an overwhelming complexity of enumerating and searching
051
      for subgraphs that are critical to GNN decisions.
052
053   Specifically, we propose subTree-based eXplainer (TreeX) (pronounced Trix), to unbox the inner
      mechanisms of MPGNNs by analyzing the subtrees generated through the message-passing process.

             • Subtree Extraction instead of Subgraph Search: We perform subtree extraction to obtain
068            subgraph concepts instead of traditional subgraph enumeration or search, reducing the search
069            space per input graph from n! to n, where n is the number of nodes in a graph. This is due
070            to the observation that there is a correspondence between L-layer subtrees incurred by GNN
071            message passing and subgraphs.
072
             • Root Node Emebdding as Subtree Embedding: For a L-layer GNN, each subtree incurred
073            by message passing can be represented by its root node embedding at layer L. By repre-
074            senting subtrees using their root node embeddings, we not only avoid introducing another
075            auxiliary mechanism to produce subgraph embeddings, but also convert another highly
076            complex problem of identifying common structures among subgraphs into numerically clus-
077            tering the subtrees in the embedding space, which can be done by off-the-shelf clustering
078            methods. The rationale is that if the subtree embeddings are similar, they contribute to the
079            GNN pooling layer similarly and their subgraph structures are also similar.
080          • GNN explanations provided at both Global-Level and Local-Level: Our proposed TreeX
081            produces more intuitive subgraph concepts as global explanations, which are closer to
082            the ground-truth crucial subgraphs and are more succinct and less noisy than state-of-the-
083            art global-level baselines, suggesting better interpretability. Unlike existing global-level
084            methods that do not explain individual graph samples, we also introduce how to utilize
085            our extracted global explanations to identify critical interpretable structures in individual
086
               instances.

088   2   R ELATED W ORK
089
090   Local-level GNN Explainability. Post-hoc local-level, or instance-level, GNN explainability refers
091   to the research problem of identifying crucial input nodes, edges, or subgraphs that significantly
092   influence a GNN’s prediction for a specific data instance. Most existing works in this domain view
093   the target GNN as a black box. They typically design algorithms (Bui et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024a;
094   Feng et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022b) or auxiliary models (Ying et al., 2019; Luo
095   et al., 2020; Shan et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2021) to select a ratio of input nodes or edges, aiming to
096
      minimize loss on mutual information or fidelity performance. Some other works extend the concept
      of explaining neural networks from the vision domain to graph-structured data (Pope et al., 2019;
097
      Sundararajan et al., 2017; Yuan et al., 2022). However, they used to neglect the unique message-
098
      passing process of MPGNNs, which is essential for gaining deeper insights into the inner workings
099   of these graph neural networks. More importantly, their explainability is limited to instances.
100
101
      Global-level GNN Explainability. Post-hoc global-level, or model-level, GNN explainability is a
102   relatively nascent research direction, with limited exploration and investigation. One line of existing
103   global-level methods produce graph examples via either graph generation (Yuan et al., 2020; Wang &
104   Shen, 2022; Nian et al., 2024) or graph edits (Huang et al., 2023) as the model-level explanations.
105   While they generate numerous examples for each target class, they do not offer clear concepts, thus
106   requiring human observers to draw conclusions (Xuanyuan et al., 2023; Kakkad et al., 2023). Another
107   line of works provide factual global explanations by identifying important global concepts based on
      actual data (Kakkad et al., 2023). While subgraph explanations are gaining attention in local-level

108
      explainability for their intuitive appeal (Yuan et al., 2021; Shan et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023; Zhang
109   et al., 2022b), producing subgraphs as global concepts remains challenging due to the computational
110   cost of searching among the possible subgraphs in the dataset. For example, GCExplainer (Magister
111   et al., 2021) requires human-intervention to reduce the cost when determining the important subgraph
112   concepts. These factual approaches provide explanations in various forms, such as boolean rules
113   between latent clusters (Azzolin et al., 2023), and expert-defined natural language rules (Xuanyuan
114   et al., 2023). However, these forms of explanations are less intuitive and understandable than graph
115   concepts that our paper aims to extract. Another challenge with these methods is the quantitative
116   assessment of the extracted global explanations, particularly when ground-truth explanations for the
117
      tasks are unavailable. This is because these approaches do not offer algorithms for applying global
      explanations to individual data instances, making it hard to measure their explanation fidelity.

      4     P ROPOSED M ETHOD
151
      In this section, we propose a subTree-based eXplainer (TreeX) to explain MPGNNs. Overall, we
152
      mine over subtrees incurred by message-passing in GNN layers instead of enumerating subgraphs to
153   reduce search space. As illustrated in Figure 2, TreeX consists of three phases: i) subtree-based local
154   concept mining; ii) global concept extraction; iii) global rule generation for each class. In the main
155   text of our paper, we focus on explaining the maximally powerful MPGNNs, whose last layer node
156   embeddings are as expressive as the same layer 1-WL test (Leman & Weisfeiler, 1968). We move the
157   analysis of explaining the less powerful MPGNNs to Appendix A.

245   4.2   A NALYSIS OF C RITICAL S UBTREE -BASED D ESIGNS
246
247
      The framework we introduced in Section 4.1 utilizes two critical designs: i) mining over subtrees
      rather than subgraphs to extract concepts; ii) representing the full L-hop subtrees by the L-th layer
248
      root node embeddings.
249
250   The first design, sharply reduces the search space, compared with mining over all possible subgraphs.
251   This is because in each graph instance with N nodes, there are exactly N full L-hop subtrees, while
252   up to N ! possible subgraphs. Although searching over possible subgraphs in a single instance is
253
      feasible in local-level explainability (Yuan et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022b; Shan et al., 2021), it
      becomes much more challenging in global-level explainability, where the dataset can be large. Our
254
      TreeX, mining over subtrees, provides a practical direction for extracting global graph concepts.
255
256   The second design further improves the mining process compared with existing explainability
257   methods (Yuan et al., 2021; Azzolin et al., 2023). Typically, existing methods represent subgraphs
258   by feeding them into the original GNN to obtain subgraph embeddings, necessitating additional
259
      calculations for each possible subgraph. In contrast, we directly use the node embeddings to represent
      subtrees, which are easily obtained by feeding the graph to the GNN just once. In the remainder of
260
      this section, we explain why we can use root node embeddings to represent subtrees, by showing that
261
      the l-th layer root node embedding from a maximally powerful MPGNN is an exact mapping of the
262   corresponding full l-hop rooted subtree.
263

264

265

266   Tv , if the following holds: For any arbitrary same-depth rooted tree Tu in the same countable space,
267   hv = hu if and only if Tv is isomorphic to Tu .

284   4.3   E XPLAINING L OCAL I NSTANCES WITH G LOBAL RULES
285
286   In earlier sections, we discussed how we generate global rules for MPGNNs at the target class via a
287   weighted sum of global subgraph concepts. In this section, we introduce how to identify critical input
288   subgraphs for each individual instance with the global rules generated by our method.
289   For a specific data sample, we first perform local subtree mining on it like we did in Section 4.1, to
290   obtain the local concepts in the data instance. Following this, we calculate the distance from each
291   local concept to the previously extracted global concepts and fit the local concepts in this instance to
292   the nearest global concepts. Then, we can construct a concept mask K ∈ Rm for this data instance,
293   where, similar to Section 4.1, K indicates the numbers of subtrees in the concepts, m refers to the
294   total number of global concepts in the global rule. The importance It of the concepts in this data
295
      instance for the target class yt can be calculated by It = Kwt , where wt ∈ Rm is the trained weight
      for all concepts in the global rule for class yt . The concepts that do not exist in the data instance will
296
      naturally receive zero importance as shown in Figure 1.
297
298   The explanations we produce are class-specific, meaning that for each instance, we offer a weighted
299   sum of the global concepts aimed at maximizing the prediction probability for each specific class. As
300   a result, we not only can explain the instances that are correctly predicted by GNNs, but can also
301
      discover the cause of incorrect predictions. That is, we can investigate the importance of various
      concepts for both the wrong class and the true class for a wrongly predicted data instance. Analyzing
302
      the explanation for the wrong class can give us the information about which concepts make the
303
      original GNN gain more confidence in predicting the wrong class, hence becomes less confident in
304   predicting the correct class. Analyzing the explanation for the true class can give us the information
305   about which critical concepts are overlooked by the original GNN, thus leading it to predict the
306   incorrect class.

      Comparison with Other Global Explainers. As summarized in Section 2, existing factual global-
400
      level explainers produce explanations in different forms. We illustrate the global explanations
401
      produced by our approach, and two existing factual global-level baselines in Figure 3. As shown in
402   this figure, GLGExplainer has limitations in delivering clear global explanations. This is because
403   they generate latent vectors as the prototypes, where they provide several random local explanations
404   for examples within the latent cluster, lacking clear motifs to represent each prototype. Therefore,
405   from the perspective of providing intuitive and clear explanations, their global-level explanations
406   remain implicit and require human experts to interpret and draw meaningful conclusions.
407
      On the other hand, GCNeuron provides global explanations in the form of logical rules with human-
408   defined premises. However, without prior knowledge, it becomes challenging to define meaningful
409   natural language rules as premises when dealing with the BA-2Motifs dataset. Consequently, their
410   explanations rely on the abstract concepts like the “degree of nodes” or “degree of neighboring
411   nodes”, which can make them quite perplexing and challenging for humans to understand. When
412   applied to the Mutagenicity dataset, GCNeuron manually defines 44 premises, including terms like
413   “NO2”, “NO”, “is(C)”, “neighbour of C”, “2-hop from C”. However, GCNeuron fails to recognize
414   “NO2” as a Class 0 motif, even though it’s known to be relevant to mutagenic effects (Luo et al.,
415   2020).
416   Conversely, our TreeX is able to accurately extract the critical global motifs on BA-2Motifs mentioned
417   in Section 5.1. The global explanation produced by TreeX for this dataset indicates that: if a graph
418   contain the five-node cycle motif, then it is a Class 0 graph; if a graph contain the “house” motif
419   , then it is a Class 1 graph. Other substructures are not important. On the Mutagenicity dataset,
420   TreeX successfully identifies the “-NO2” and “-NH2” chemical groups as Class 0 patterns, which are
421   well-known to be related to the mutagenic effect of molecules, as discussed in previous studies (Ying
422
      et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2020; Debnath et al., 1991). Additionally, it identifies “-N2O”, “-OCH3” as
      the Class 0 motifs, and “-CH2”, “-OH” as the Class 1 motifs, albeit with relatively lower but still
423
      positive confidence. These chemical groups have been widely studied in terms of their mutagenic
424
      effects (Hill et al., 1998; Baden & Kundomal, 1987). Explicitly highlighting these chemical groups
425   provides a more comprehensive understanding of how GNNs make decisions and can be valuable for
426   debugging GNNs.

      Discover Reasons for Incorrect GNN Predictions. As we discussed in Section 4.3, unlike many
466
      existing GNN explainability methods that solely focus on identifying which input substructures lead
467
      to GNN’s predictions, our class-specific approach also explains why the GNN does not predict other
468   classes. That is, we provide some insights on how a GNN might be improved. TreeX aims to globally
469   provide users with an understanding of why the GNN makes correct predictions on some samples
470   and incorrect predictions on other samples. The global explanation produced by TreeX for a target
471   class is a set of subgraph concepts with their weights. Positive weights indicate that the GNN relies
472   on these subgraph concepts to predict the target class, while negative weights imply that the existence
473   of these subgraph concepts makes the GNN less confident in predicting the target class. We provide
474   two example global explanations for both Class 0 and Class 1 in Figure 4.
475   Following the global explanations, we can discover the reasons behind the incorrect predictions. For
476   example, for a data sample in Figure 4, the true label is 1, while the given GNN predicts 0. By
477   examining the existence of the subgraph concepts in this sample, we find that the GNN predicts Class
478   0 and is less confident in predicting Class 1 because it has Concept 2. We could also provide an
479   insight to the developer of the GNN that if the GNN can be improved either through neural parameters
480   or the design of the pooling mechanism, by increasing its interest in Concept 1 for Class 1, it may be
481   able to correct its prediction on this data sample.
482   In Table 3, we further report the rate of predicting the true labels using our extracted global explana-
483   tions of the corresponding classes, for the incorrect predictions of the original GNNs. The results
484   show that for most of the GNN’s incorrect predictions, our approach is able to show how adjusting
485   weights of various concepts can help to correct the predictions, highlighting the effectiveness of our
      approach in uncovering causes of incorrect predictions.

      5.3   T IME A NALYSIS
496
497   The main focus of our work is to generate clear subgraph concepts as global explanations for GNNs at
498   the dataset and task levels. Although some approaches can explain GNNs at the global or model level,
499   they do not produce clear subgraph concepts, as discussed in Section 2 and demonstrated in Figure 3.
500   Therefore, we refrain from efficiency comparisons with those existing global-level approaches.
501   On the other hand, several local-level explanation methods attempt to extract critical subgraphs for
502   individual instances (Bui et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2022b; Yuan et al., 2021). While
503   these methods produce clear subgraph concepts, their explainability is limited to the instance level,
504   rather than the dataset or task level. Moreover, it is challenging to extract global subgraph concepts
505   from the local critical subgraphs generated by these methods. This is because local-level critical
506   subgraphs can be noisy, containing redundant substructures that are only crucial for certain local
507   predictions and not part of the core concepts at the global or task level. Removing such redundancy
508   to produce global-level subgraph concepts may require costly subgraph matching, which is known to
509
      be NP-hard. In contrast, our approach removes redundant substructures by pruning the local subtrees,
      as discussed in Section 4.1. The complexity of this redundancy removal process is less than O(enk),
510
      much more efficient than the subgraph matching, where n and e are the number of nodes and edges
511
      in each graph, and k is the number of local clusters.
512
513   Although local-level explanation approaches are not capable of producing global subgraph concepts
514   like our TreeX, we still perform an efficiency comparison of concept extraction between our method
515
      and theirs. In Table 4, we compare the average elapsed time for explaining each data instance
      on the BA-2Motifs and Mutagenicity datasets with two prominent local subgraph-level baselines,
516
      SubgraphX (Yuan et al., 2021) and EiG-Search (Lu et al., 2024a). For the baselines, we use their
517
      methods to explain all instances in the datasets and calculate the average elapsed time. For our
518   method, we first obtain the global explanations as discussed in Section 4.1, then apply the algorithm
519   described in Section 4.3 to produce local explanations for all instances, and finally divide the total
520   elapsed time by the number of instances.
521
      In Table 4, we can observe that TreeX is much more efficient than SubgraphX, and as efficient
522
      as EiG-Search, even though the latter ones only provides local-level graph explanations per graph
523
      instance, while globally it is not able to generate behavior-defining subgraphs for the entire dataset
524   or task. In fact, TreeX has a more robust and stable local-level fidelity performance across different
525   benchmarks while the performance of GradCAM and EiG-Search may vary across benchmarks. This
526   again implies the value of further investigating and obtaining clean, less noisy and consistent global
527   graph explanations rather than per-instance explanations that existing works focus on.

      6     C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we introduce a novel approach TreeX for explaining MPGNNs from the perspective of
532
      their distinct message-passing process. We extract intuitive and clear subgraph concepts by mining
533
      over the full-L-hop subtrees at each graph instance, unlike existing approaches that identify latent
534   prototypes or human-defined rules. We utilize the last-layer node embeddings to help represent
535   the concepts, thereby avoiding additional complex calculations of the concept embeddings. Due
536   to this design, we are able to employ the global explanations extracted by our method to explain
537   the individual instances, while the existing global-level baselines fail to do so. Moreover, unlike
538   many existing GNN explaining approaches that solely focus on explaining the correct predictions of
539   GNNs, our approach offers insights of the causes of incorrect predictions. In the future, GNNs may
      be refined with these insights to improve their classification performance.

      A.2    H ASH M ODEL TO E XPLAIN THE L ESS P OWERFUL MPGNN S
774
      As we discussed in Appendix A.1, GNNs that update the node embeddings by mean-pooling may
775   produce the same root node embeddings for the subtrees with the same node distribution, and the
776   ones using max-pooling may produce the same root node embeddings for the subtrees with the same
777   node set. For these GNNs, there is a higher risk of clustering multiple entirely different substructures
778   to the same concept as those non-isomorphic subtrees may share the same root node embedding.
779
      To mitigate this issue, we introduce a hash model that aids in distinguishing global graph concepts
780
      induced by subtrees with similar node distributions or node sets but significantly different structures.
781   Specifically, after we obtain the local clusters, we feed to each local graph concept to a hash model
782   Ω(·) that returns the graph embedding of it. Then, we concatenate the hashed graph embedding of
783   the local concept to its original embedding to obtain the updated embedding for it. Let mo be the
784   original embedding of local concept o, then the updated embedding m̂o is
785
786
                                          m̂o = C ONCAT (mo , Ω(o)) .                                    (10)
787   The steps afterwards remain the same as we discussed in Section 4.1. A hash model should be able to
788   distinguish graph concepts that have the same node distribution or the same node set. For example,
789   the WL-test can be used as a hash model.

990    D.1    A DDITIONAL R ESULTS ON BAM ULTI S HAPES AND NCI1
991
992    Figure 5 presents the global explanations produced by various global explainers on BAMultiShapes
993
       and NCI1 datasets. For the NCI1 dataset, we cannot map the node type numbers to the actual atoms
       because that information was not available. So, the explanations we provide only include the node
994
       type numbers. GLGExplainer generates long Boolean formulas on BAMultiShapes dataset. However,
995
       it fails to identify the house motif. Moreover, the predicates in the Class 1 formula, namely (P1 ∧ P3 ),
996    (P2 ∧ P5 ) and (P5 ∧ P1 ), are not faithful to the ground-truth, as they require the presence of multiple
997    grids or multiple wheels in Class 1 graphs. Recall that the ground-truth rules of BAMultiShapes is
998    that Class 0 includes plain BA graphs and those with individual motifs or a combination of all three,
999    whereas Class 1 comprises BA graphs enriched with any two of the three motifs. Furthermore, the
1000   Boolean formulas from GLGExplainer on NCI1 is a bit confusing, since P0 ∨ (P0 ∧ P1 ) is logically
1001   equivalent to P0 . Consequently, for the NCI1 dataset, GLGExplainer only provides random local
1002   explanations of each prototype, where Prototype 0 stands for Class 0 and Prototype 1 stands for
1003   Class 1. The insights provided by these random local explanations are less informative. On the other
1004
       hand, the global explanations from GCNeuron are relatively less intuitive as negations are frequently
       involved, and they are challenging for humans to understand.
1005
1006   In contrast, our approach successfully identifies all the outstanding motifs for the BAMultiShapes
1007   dataset, namely, the house, wheel, as well as grid motifs. In particular, TreeX recognize the patterns
1008   in Barabasi-Albert (BA) graphs as the Class 0 motifs, and house, wheel, grid as the Class 1 motifs, by
1009   identifying higher confidence on these motifs at each class. This is reasonable because, as shown
1010
       in Table 5, all the data samples in BAMultiShapes contain 40 nodes. Hence, if more house, wheel
       or grid motifs are included in a graph, then less BA patterns will be in it. Given that all the Class 1
1011
       graphs contain two out of three motifs in house, wheel or grid, whereas most Class 0 graphs contain
1012
       at most one of the three motifs, it is reasonable for the GNNs to consider that the Class 0 graphs
1013   contains a larger portion of BA patterns than the Class 1 graphs. Additionally, neither TreeX nor
1014   GLGExplainer is able to capture the ground truth rule that graphs contain all of the three motifs
1015   are Class 0 graphs, which is as expected, since as shown in Table 6, the GIN does not achieve
1016   perfect accuracy on BAMultiShapes. These experimental results have demonstrate that our approach
1017   has the potential to provide insights into some occasionally incorrect rules learned by the model.

        Number of local clusters k. The choice of this hyperparameter has minimal impact on the results
1081    of the global-level explanation, as long as k is not excessively small. For instance, when we select k
1082    from the range 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, TreeX consistently identifies the five-node cycle and the house
1083    motifs with high confidence. This is because the local clustering algorithm, whether k-means or
1084    the EM algorithm, automatically adapts by shrinking to a smaller number of clusters when a larger
1085    number is allowed. Generally, we recommend setting k to be 1 to 3, plus the number of classes. In
