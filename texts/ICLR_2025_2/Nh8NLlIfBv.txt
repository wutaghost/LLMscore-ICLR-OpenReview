009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Reusing pre-collected data from different domains is an appealing solution for
012            decision-making tasks that have insufficient data in the target domain but are rel-
013
               atively abundant in other related domains. Existing cross-domain policy transfer
               methods mostly aim at learning domain correspondences or corrections to facilitate
014
               policy learning, such as learning domain/task-specific discriminators, representa-
015
               tions, or policies. This design philosophy often results in heavy model architectures
016            or task/domain-specific modeling, lacking flexibility. This reality makes us wonder:
017            can we directly bridge the domain gaps universally at the data level, instead of rely-
018            ing on complex downstream cross-domain policy transfer models? In this study, we
019            propose the Cross-Domain Trajectory EDiting (xTED) framework that employs
020            a specially designed diffusion model for cross-domain trajectory adaptation. Our
021            proposed model architecture effectively captures the intricate dependencies among
022            states, actions, and rewards, as well as the dynamics patterns within target data.
023            By utilizing the pre-trained diffusion as a prior, source domain trajectories can
024
               be transformed to match with target domain properties while preserving original
               semantic information. This process implicitly corrects underlying domain gaps,
025
               enhancing state realism and dynamics reliability in the source data, and allowing
026
               flexible incorporation with various downstream policy learning methods. Despite
027            its simplicity, xTED demonstrates superior performance in extensive simulation
028            and real-robot experiments.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Policies learned on insufficient data from a single domain easily end up with undesirable performance,
033
      especially in real systems (Guiochet et al., 2017; Zhan et al., 2022) where performing reinforce-
      ment/imitation learning (RL/IL) in the real world (i.e. target domain) yields laborious and costly
034
      data collection, notorious reset, and reward specification issues (Zhu et al., 2020). Faced with
035
      restricted data acquisition in reality, people resort to incorporating data generated with simulation or
036   pre-collected from other domains (i.e. source domains) into policy learning for greater synergy (Niu
037   et al., 2022; 2023; Open X-Embodiment et al., 2023). However, source domains invariably bear
038   multiple domain gaps (Niu et al., 2024), such as appearance (Tobin et al., 2017) and dynamics
039   gaps (Peng et al., 2018), as well as morphology gaps for embodied agents (Gupta et al., 2021). These
040   domain discrepancies would reduce the availability of source data since directly augmenting target
041   data with unprocessed source data sometimes leads to negative effects on policy learning.
042   Existing cross-domain policy learning methods tend to design domain-specific policy transfer models
043   equipped with domain correspondences (Zhang et al., 2020), corrections (Eysenbach et al., 2021), or
044   discriminations (Stadie et al., 2016; Sharma et al., 2019) that either rely on task-specific architecture
045   designs (e.g. special encoders (Mueller et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022a) and domain-specific regular-
046   izations (Desai et al., 2020; Eysenbach et al., 2021; Niu et al., 2022; Xue et al., 2023)), or are only
047   applicable to specific domain of data (e.g. adaptable vision encoder design only applicable to image
048   inputs (Rao et al., 2020). Additionally, the domain-specific discriminators or mappings (Stadie et al.,
049   2016; Liu et al., 2018) sometimes require tedious efforts to refresh for accomodating multiple source
050
      domains, which substantially hinders data reutilization efficiency and efficacy. These limitations
      highlight the need for a more generic and flexible approach that addresses domain gaps more directly.
051
052   Above inadequately addressed issues make us wonder: Rather than relying on sophisticated and
053   domain-specific policy transfer models, is it possible to bridge domain gaps at the data level? If
      source trajectories can be transformed to minimize domain gaps and seamlessly utilized for policy

      tend to correct the biased domain modeling within trajectories based on target data while preserving

071   its narrative content, such as converting a stroke-painted castle into a photorealistic one. However,
072   as depicted in Fig. 1, source-domain trajectories face not only biased observation styles but also
073   altered viewpoints and inaccurate transition dynamics over time. Moreover, trajectories are composed
074   of fundamentally heterogeneous elements—such as observations, actions, and rewards—that, if
075   naïvely treated as equivalent pixels in an image, would obscure the recognition of their respective
076   inherence and delicate interactive dependencies. Thus, despite sharing a similar high-level philosophy,
077   this editing solution is not directly applicable to cross-domain policy transfer due to the intrinsic
078   heterogeneity and dependencies within trajectories and the complex domain discrepancies.
079   To address these distinctions, we propose the Cross-Domain Trajectory EDiting (xTED) paradigm
080   with a novel diffusion model architecture tailored for decision-making data, which effectively captures
081   the target trajectory distribution as a prior. The architecture illustrated in Fig. 2 employs a separate
082   encoding-decoding approach to recognize states, actions, and rewards, each possessing fundamentally
083   different physical meanings and unique internal consistency patterns. To effectively capture the
084   dynamics patterns within trajectories, we implement dependency structure modeling mechanisms
085
      that capture the interactive relationships among states, actions, and rewards. With the assistance
      of above designs, we successfully extend the philosophy in diffusion-based image editing (Huang
086
      et al., 2024) to decision-making data, equipping xTED pipeline with three simple steps: (1) train
087
      diffusion model on the target data; (2) distort source data with noises and then denoise them with the
088   pre-trained diffusion model; (3) incorporate edited source data into target data for policy learning
089   with any algorithm at will. Through extensive experiments, we show that incorporating source data
090   edited with xTED consistently yields performance improvements over training solely on target data
091   while directly adding unprocessed source data often results in significant performance degradation,
092   particularly in real-robot manipulation tasks.

      2.1 C ROSS -D OMAIN P OLICY A DAPTATION
096
097   Addressing the challenges posed by domain gaps has long been established as an essential task for
098   the prolonged development of data-hungry policy learning methods and models (Niu et al., 2024).
099
      The most straightforward approach is to construct direct mappings for state and action space between
      source and target domains (Liu et al., 2018; Kim et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020; Raychaudhuri et al.,
100
      2021; Wang et al., 2022b). An alternative is to learn domain-agnostic task-relevant embedding with
101
      mutual information criterion (Franzmeyer et al., 2022) and explicit domain discrimination (Stadie
102   et al., 2016; Sharma et al., 2019), which can also be achieved with temporal contrastive learning
103   methods (Sermanet et al., 2018; Dwibedi et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2023; Choi et al., 2023; Li et al.,
104   2024) to enable representation highly dependent on task progress while neglecting domain-variant
105   information. Other than representation learning, it is also preferable to directly regularize the policy
106   learning process in situations with mild observation gaps, by reward augmentation (Eysenbach et al.,
107   2021; Liu et al., 2022; Xue et al., 2023) and reweighting the value update (Niu et al., 2022; 2023; Xu
      et al., 2023a). The complicated domain/task-specific designs hinder convenient reuse and fine-tuning


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      of those models to accommodate data from multiple source domains available at different stages of
109   training (Niu et al., 2024). In contrast, xTED introduces a generic and flexible approach that handles
110   domain gaps at the data level, avoiding any task-specific design and domain-specific fine-tuning while
111   applicable to multiple source domains of data and freeing the choices of any upstream observation
112   encoder and downstream policy learning method.

114   2.2 D IFFUSION M ODELS FOR D ECISION M AKING
115   Diffusion models have been applied across various decision-making settings, including generating
116   multi-modal policies (Chi et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023), single-step transitions (Lu et al., 2023),
117   subgoals (Black et al., 2024), trajectories (Janner et al., 2022; Ajay et al., 2023a; He et al., 2023;
118
      Carvalho et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2024), and videos for planning (Ajay et al., 2023b; Yang et al., 2024;
      Du et al., 2023; 2024). However, prior works primarily focus on single-domain generation tasks, such
119
      as data augmentation, without addressing cross-domain challenges. In contrast, xTED introduces a
120
      distinct setting by employing diffusion models to adjust source trajectories so that they more closely
121   resemble target domain characteristics while preserving useful primitive information from source
122   domains. As demonstrated in Section 5.5, this setting is highly sample-efficient, as it does not require
123   the extensive training data needed by diffusion-based generation tasks that aim to generate data from
124   modeled target distribution.

      Dependency structure modeling. Although states, actions, and rewards possess fundamentally
217   different physical properties, significant interactive dependencies exist among them. Specifically,
218   unlike the spatial representation of images, sequential decision data entails intricate dynamics patterns
219   that consistently drive state updates, action generation, and reward acquisition throughout trajectories,
220   which play an indispensable role in effective trajectory modeling. To this end, the state and action
221   embeddings hsk and hak are cross-attended to capture mutual dependencies by exchanging key-value
222   pairs in multi-headed attention, modeling the effects of policy and transition state dynamics separately:
223                          h′s          s    a c

226

      in-context optimality guidance could conflict with the primary goal of addressing domain gaps
227

232   necessary regularizations for capturing trajectory distributions. This dependency structure model-
233   ing module alleviates the overabundance of information and relationships, enabling the model to
234   accurately capture essential dependency priors and efficiently handle long-term sequential modeling.
235   Additionally, the sequence-level dependency structure modeling naturally decouples the hypothesized
236   temporal causal relationships adopted in many sequential modeling approaches (Chen et al., 2021;
237   Ajay et al., 2023a), offering flexibility in whether to incorporate temporal causality during modeling.

239   commodates various conditioning information c = [ci ]N    i=0 , guiding trajectories toward desirable

      arises from our separate encoding-decoding manner and designs of dynamics dependency modeling,

247   Applicability to diverse decision-making task settings. Our architecture is compatible with
248   multi-modal observations, including proprioceptive, exteroceptive (visual/point clouds), and language

250   position, it offers flexibility in accommodating non-standard trajectories, which may include terminal
251   flags, class labels, or preference labels for auxiliary module training to aid policy optimization. If
252
      these task-specific elements exhibit similarities with rewards, which have specific dependencies on

253
      ities by incorporating extra encoders, decoders, and attention modules, akin to our reward-relevant
254
      design philosophies. Furthermore, practitioners can also omit the reward-relevant modules to fit
255   non-reward (imitation learning) settings, as demonstrated in our real robot experiments (Section 5.4).

258   Training Diffusion Model on Target Data. Given a target domain dataset, we first train the
259   diffusion model to capture its trajectory distribution using Eq. 5, where xk represents the noised
260   trajectory τk , and additional conditions c, if any, are concatenated with the noise step k as inputs.
261   Particularly, we keep the initial transition unchanged in each H-horizon trajectory during training,
262   serving as an anchor for enhanced stability in long-sequence modeling.
263   Editing Source Trajectories. In the forward process, we obtain noised trajectories τk from
264   N (τk ; τ0 , σk2 I), which retains information from the original source trajectory τ0 . This procedure is
265                                            k
      governed by a ratio parameter κ = K         ∈ [0, 1], determining the level of noise addition. A small κ
266   preserves much of the source trajectory information, while a large κ diminishes it. The ideal selection
267   principle for κ aims to distort only fine-grained information, such as transition dynamics in the
268   source data, while retaining valuable large-scale information, such as skill primitives inherent in the
269   trajectories. Additionally, this noised initialization theoretically yields favorable results by solving
      the reverse stochastic differential equation. Thus, we could then denoise τk using the diffusion prior,

288   5.1   E XPERIMENTAL E NVIRONMENT S ETUPS
289   Simulation Experiments. We conduct simulation-based experiments using the MuJoCo physics
290   simulator (Todorov et al., 2012). Specifically, we construct two source domains on Walker2d-v2
291   (WK) and HalfCheetah-v2 (HC), where we intentionally introduce dynamics and morphology gaps
292   relative to the original target domains by modifying the physics modeling configurations: (1) Gravity:
293   applying 2× gravitational acceleration in the simulation dynamics; (2) Friction: using 0.25/0.5×
294
      friction coefficient to make the agent harder to maintain balance; (3) Thigh Size: using 2× thigh
      size to introduce morphology gaps on the embodiment. We record the average return of Soft Actor-
295
      Critic (Haarnoja et al., 2018) policies trained in these source domains and provide a straightforward
296
      quantification of the impact of domain gaps on source policy learning in Appendix B.3.
297
298   For data acquirement and implementation details: (i) target data: we randomly select 20k transitions
299   from the corresponding datasets in the standard offline RL benchmark D4RL (Fu et al., 2020).
300   Specifically, we only consider the Medium (M), Medium Replay (MR) and Medium Expert (ME)
301   datasets, as we hardly use a random or expert policy for system control in common real-world
302
      scenarios. (ii) source data: we collect trajectories of 20k transitions from the source domain with a
      SAC policy trained in the same domain. Please see Appendix B.1 for more implementation details.
303
304   Real-Robot Experiments. We conduct real-world experiments in robotic environments where
305   target data is collected by the WidowX robot and source data is collected by the Airbot, for 100
306   trajectories respectively. We build three manipulation tasks: (1) Pick up a red cup and place it on a
307   silver pan (Cup); (2) Pick up a duck and place it on a green plate (Duck); (3) Move a pot from
308   right to left (Pot). As shown in Fig. 3, there are huge domain discrepancies on robot embodiments
309   and camera viewpoints: two 7-DoF robots possess distinctive module shapes/lengths, embodiment
310   masses and joint types; source and target configurations present different viewpoints and field of
311
      views from base and wrist cameras on two robots. Our objective is to incorporate the edited data
      from the Airbot to enhance policy performance on the WidowX robot. These task environments are
312
      highly stochastic, featuring randomly initialized object positions and poses, as well as distractors
313
      with various shapes, colors, and locations. Please see Appendix B.2 for more implementation details.

454   5.6    A BLATION F INDINGS ON M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
455   We ablate our model architecture to demonstrate the impact of different design insights and techniques.
456   We primarily consider four distinct architecture baselines: (1) Feature Concatenation (FC): con-
457   catenating state, action, and reward sequences on feature dimension before passing through attention;
458   (2) Transition concatenation (TC): concatenating state, action, and reward sequences on temporal
459   dimension before passing through attention; (3) Causal Mask (CM): incorporating causal masks
460   in attention modules in our architecture for modeling Markovian process, considering transitions
461   only depending on the previous step; (4) Diffusing over States + Inverse Dynamics (Inv): solely
462
      diffusing over state trajectories, while employing two inverse models to obtain actions and rewards.
      Table 3 demonstrates that our model consistently achieves superior performance, from which several
463
      key insights into architecture design can be derived: (1) Entire trajectory modeling outperforms
464
      partial trajectory modeling: Using inverse models (Inv) results in significantly higher compounding
465   errors, as action feasibility and reward plausibility cannot be reliably maintained when only states are
466   diffused without a unified model that handles all components consistently; (2) Imposing Markovian
467   properties as priors in trajectory modeling is suboptimal: Incorporating Markovian modeling (CM)
468   degrades the quality of edited source data, as local transition dynamics enforced by masked attention
469   mechanisms may conflict with the global temporal consistency required in trajectory modeling. This
470   suggests that trajectory modeling demands a distinct approach compared to common practices in
471   decision-making approaches; (3) Our design effectively captures intricate dependencies within trajec-
472   tories: Concatenating states, actions, and rewards along transition or feature dimensions (TC/FC) is
473
      problematic. It treats the entire trajectory as a homogeneous, image-like input, which obscures the
      model’s ability to correctly identify dependencies between transitions and decision elements, and
474
      introduces unwarranted dependencies of rewards on state-action sequences; (4) Our design integrates
475
      well with return-conditioned diffusion: Table 4 reveals that incorporating return conditioning in naïve
476   architectures (TC/FC) leads to performance degradation, whereas our model with return conditioning
477   successfully guides edited source data towards higher-reward regions, resulting in superior policy
478   performance. This is because only our design correctly models the one-way dependencies of reward
479   and state-action sequences, enabling effectively implementing return-conditioned strategies.

509   6   C ONCLUSIONS
510   We present a novel, simple, generic, flexible, and effective trajectory editing paradigm (xTED),
511   which reframes cross-domain policy adaptation as a data pre-processing problem. It is a task- and
512   domain-agnostic method that avoids the need for task-specific policy adaptation designs and can
513   accommodate multiple available source domains. Furthermore, it is compatible with any observation
514   encoder and downstream policy learning method, making it adaptable to various task specifications.
515   Additionally, xTED is orthogonal to other cross-domain policy adaptation paradigms, which can be
516   flexibly integrated for greater synergy if necessary. At the core of xTED, we introduce a scalable
517
      and flexible diffusion model architecture, effectively modeling the complex dependencies within
      trajectories. This design proves to be sample-efficient and highly compatible with (return-)conditioned
518
      diffusion. Besides, our model demonstrates strong potential as a data generator for single-domain
519
      data augmentation when no source data is available, highlighting its superiority in modeling entire
520   trajectories. Through extensive experiments, we show that incorporating source data edited with
521   xTED consistently yields performance improvements over training solely on target data while directly
522   adding unprocessed source data often results in significant performance degradation, particularly in
523   real-robot manipulation tasks.

      A PPENDIX
811
812   A     E XTENDED D ISCUSSIONS WITH E XISTING W ORKS
813
814   xTED offers a generic, flexible, simple yet effective cross-domain trajectory editing paradigm that
815   gains massive necessity and advantages over existing approaches. This section comprehensively
816   elaborates on its distinctions from domain adaptation and data augmentation.
817
      A.1   D OMAIN A DAPTATION
818
819   xTED casts cross-domain policy adaptation as a data pre-processing problem, thereby eliminating the
820   need for designing modality/task-specific adaptation modules that function during training. Most
821
      existing policy adaptation methods primarily focus on achieving MDP Alignment (Kim et al., 2020).
822   Existing categories of domain adaptation. The first category of approaches to achieving such
823   alignment is through learning direct mappings or corrections between source and target domains.
824   For observation space alignment, numerous works leverage advances in generative adversarial learning
825   and contrastive learning to transform the observation space, achieving consistent visual rendering
826   and camera viewpoints as in the target domain (Zhu et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2019;
827   Bewley et al., 2019; Rao et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020; Gangwani & Peng, 2020; Radosavovic et al.,
828   2021). In scenarios with dynamics mismatch, it becomes necessary to align both state and action
829   spaces simultaneously, which introduces more complex correspondence learning mechanisms such as
830
      (dynamics/temporal) cycle consistency (Kim et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020; Raychaudhuri et al.,
      2021; Zakka et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022b) and mutual information criteria (Franzmeyer et al.,
831
      2022). Alternatively, modifying the reward space can compensate for the misalignment in transitional
832
      dynamics within MDPs (Eysenbach et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Lyu et al., 2024; Xue et al., 2023),
833   which may also be implicitly addressed by adaptively regularizing Q-values (Niu et al., 2022; 2023;
834   Hou et al., 2024). This category necessitates model retraining when new data from other source
835   domains becomes available, which can be time-consuming and labor-intensive.
836
837   The second category resorts to building a canonical representation space that preserves task-
838   relevant yet domain-agnostic information (Stadie et al., 2016; Pan et al., 2017; Mueller et al., 2018;
839
      Sermanet et al., 2018; Cetin & Celiktutan, 2021; Wang et al., 2022a; Yang et al., 2023; Choi et al.,
      2024). This auxiliary representation space, often viewed as skills (Gupta et al., 2017; Hejna et al.,
840
      2020; Pertsch et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023b) or subgoals (Sharma et al., 2019; Ma et al., 2022;
841
      2023; Li et al., 2024), facilitates the construction of aligned domain-invariant MDPs by capturing
842   task semantics that effectively guide agents in completing tasks. However, this approach involves
843   designing task-specific loss functions and optimization procedures, which may struggle to find a
844   representation space that effectively encapsulates the inputs and reflects the task.
845
846   Despite the aforementioned shortcomings, these two categories share a common Achilles’ heel: mode
847
      collapse (Lopez et al., 2021). Most policy adaptation methods, whether explicitly or implicitly, rely
      on the high-level philosophy of generation and discrimination. Learning an informative mapping or a
848
      canonical representation that can effectively recover the entire target distribution can be exceedingly
849
      challenging, especially due to data imbalance and the sensitivity of hyper-parameter tuning.
850
851   Why “data adaptation”? To overcome all the aforementioned issues, it is crucial to avoid designing
852   modality/task-specific adaptation modules and instead handle domain adaptation at the data level.
853   This approach provides significant flexibility, allowing the integration of various upstream observation
854   encoders for different modalities, as well as diverse downstream policy learning methods that best
855   suit the tasks at hand.
856
      Why diffusion-based data editing? Direct data transformation cannot rely on a pre-trained deter-
857
      ministic mapping or representation to pre-process source data, as this would face the same challenges
858   as traditional domain adaptation methods, leading to transformed data with reduced variety and
859   insufficient coverage in the target distribution. Moreover, such mappings or representations require
860   re-training or fine-tuning to accommodate multiple source domains. In contrast, diffusion-based
861   editing ensures diversity in the transformed data due to its stochastic nature and avoids mode collapse.
862   Additionally, the editing process is universally domain-agnostic, correcting only the dynamics and ob-
863   servation patterns while preserving task-relevant information in source data with a target distribution
      prior modeled by a diffusion model, thus enabling a retraining-free approach.


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Above all, xTED represents a novel, generic, and flexible cross-domain data-level policy adaptation
865   paradigm. It is domain-agnostic, accommodating multiple source domains without the need for
866   re-training or fine-tuning the diffusion model; modality-agnostic, being compatible with various
867   observation encoders; task-agnostic, integrating seamlessly with different task-specific policy learning
868   methods; compatible with other policy adaptation paradigms and approaches for greater synergy.
869
870   A.2 D ISTINCTIONS FROM DATA AUGMENTATION
871   Setting. Data augmentation is primarily employed in single-domain settings, which are fundamen-
872   tally different from xTED’s cross-domain applications. Typically, no data from other domains is
873   introduced, and data augmentation focuses on manipulating training data to enhance robustness, e.g.
874   noising the original data (Sinha et al., 2022).
875   In contrast, data editing is a broader paradigm that encompasses the applications of data augmentation.
876   Even when data from other domains is unavailable, xTED can serve as an expressive data generation
877   model for augmenting small-sample datasets in single-domain settings, outperforming state-of-the-art
878   data augmentation strategies (Lu et al., 2024; Sinha et al., 2022) (see Section 5.7).
879
      Diffusion-based data editing V.S. data augmentation. We have to emphasize that using diffusion
880
      models for data editing is both sample-efficient and error-tolerant than diffusion-based trajectory
881
      generation for data augmentation (Lu et al., 2023; He et al., 2023; Jackson et al., 2024). This is
882   because the goal in data editing is far more easily attainable compared to its application in data
883   generation tasks. When diffusion models are used for data generation, the focus is usually on
884   achieving a high level of precision to generate data that closely matches the original data distribution.
885   However, in the context of data editing, the requirements are relaxed to simply distort (edit) the data
886   to more closely match the target dynamics and observation patterns than the source data. Crucially,
887   there is no need for edited source data to closely resemble target data in every detail. Thus, diffusion
888   models for editing are relatively insensitive to the amount of target data used, having minimal impact
889   on the ultimate policy performance as proved in Section 5.5.

1174   (Conditioned xTED). In this section, we ablate on these additional constraints and conditions.
1175   In terms of the implementation of return-conditioned xTED, to generate trajectories that maximize
1176   return, we condition the diffusion model on the return of trajectories, such that the training objective
1177   in Eq. 5 should be adapted to:

1183                                      ϵ̂ := ϵθ (τk , ∅, k) + ω (ϵθ (τk , R(τ ), k) − ϵθ (τk , ∅, k))                   (14)
