                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
               Conformal prediction (CP) is a distribution-free framework for achieving proba-
010
               bilistic guarantees on black-box models. CP is generally applied to a model post-
011            training. Recent research efforts, on the other hand, have focused on optimizing
012            CP efficiency during training. We formalize this concept as the problem of con-
013            formal risk minimization (CRM). In this direction, conformal training (ConfTr)
014            by Stutz et al. (2022) is a technique that seeks to minimize the expected predic-
015            tion set size of a model by simulating CP in-between training updates. Despite its
016            potential, we identify a strong source of sample inefficiency in ConfTr that leads
017            to overly noisy estimated gradients, introducing training instability and limiting
018            practical use. To address this challenge, we propose variance-reduced conformal
019
               training (VR-ConfTr), a CRM method that incorporates a novel variance re-
               duction technique in the gradient estimation of the ConfTr objective function.
020
               Through extensive experiments on various benchmark datasets, we demonstrate
021
               that VR-ConfTr consistently achieves faster convergence and smaller prediction
022            sets compared to baselines.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Consider a classification task with input (features) X ∈ X and corresponding label Y ∈ Y =
028   {1, . . . , K}. In supervised learning, we are interested in approximating the posterior probability
029   π(y|x) = P (Y = y | X = x) by tuning some θ-parameterized family of predictors πθ (y|x) - for
030   example, neural network models with a softmax activation at the output layer. Typically, the final
031
      label prediction would be δθ (x) = arg maxy∈Y πθ (y|x), and a common metric for performance
      is the accuracy, which measures the proportion of testing samples whose predicted label matches
032
      the true label. While the accuracy is a key performance metric, in safety-critical applications with
033
      a downstream decision maker it is crucial not only to predict accurately but also to quantify the
034   uncertainty associated with a prediction.
035
036   Conformal prediction (CP) is a distribution-free, principled framework that is used to provide for-
037
      mal probabilistic guarantees for black-box models (Vovk et al. (2005); Shafer & Vovk (2008);
      Angelopoulos et al. (2023)), with exemplar applications in computer vision (Angelopoulos et al.
038
      (2020)), large language models (Mohri & Hashimoto (2024),Kumar et al. (2023)) and path planning
039
      (Lindemann et al. (2023)). Given a model πθ (y|x), CP enables the construction of set predictors
040   Cθ : X → 2Y (where 2Y is the power set of Y) such that the true label is contained in the set of
041   predicted labels with high probability. This can be formalized via the notion of marginal coverage.
042
      Definition 1.1 (Marginal coverage). We say that a set predictor Cθ : X → 2Y satisfies marginal
043   coverage with miscoverage rate α ∈ (0, 1) if P (Y ∈ Cθ (X)) ≥ 1 − α.
044
045   Marginal coverage can be readily obtained in CP via a process called calibration, which only re-
046   quires access to a so-called calibration set of data that is statistically exchangeable with the test
047   data. However, one of the main challenges in CP is the efficiency of the prediction sets - namely the
048   size of the sets Cθ (x) - often referred to as length efficiency (Fontana et al., 2023). For instance,
049   while it is possible to trivially achieve the desired coverage by including the entire label space in
050
      Cθ (x), such an approach results in non-informative and excessively large prediction sets. An effi-
      cient Cθ (x) is as small as possible while still maintaining the coverage guarantee.
051
052   Various existing approaches, including the works by Romano et al. (2020); Yang & Kuchibhotla
053   (2024); Bai et al. (2022), address the efficiency challenge by refining the conformal prediction pro-
      cedure applied post-training to a black-box model. These methods, though effective, are constrained

054
      by the performance of the pre-trained model πθ (y|x) on which they are applied. On the other hand,
055   recent research efforts (Dheur & Taieb, 2024; Cherian et al., 2024; Einbinder et al., 2022; Stutz et al.,
056   2022; Bellotti, 2021) have focused on integrating CP directly into the training process. This provides
057   a way to improve the CP efficiency also in the model optimization phase - when learning the param-
058   eter of the models - enabling a higher degree of control over the probabilistic guarantees efficiency.
059   In this work, we formulate this approach as conformal risk minimization (CRM) and we focus on
060   CRM for length efficiency optimization. We consider a setting similar to Stutz et al. (2022), who
061   proposed conformal training (ConfTr), an algorithm achieving promising performance in improv-
062   ing the length-efficiency of the prediction sets constructed post-training.
063   Despite encouraging preliminary results, ConfTr introduces significant optimization challenges,
064   particularly due to the use of differentiable approximations of CP sets. Indeed, ConfTr requires
065   differentiating a loss function obtained simulating CP during training. This, in turn, requires ac-
066   curately estimating the population quantile of the conformity scores and its gradient, which can be
067   difficult with the limited data available in each mini-batch. Hence, training can exhibit large fluctu-
068   ations in the loss and slow convergence, thereby reducing the practical applicability of the method.
069   In this work, we address these challenges by introducing variance-reduced conformal train-
070   ing (VR-ConfTr), a novel CRM algorithm leveraging a variance reduction technique for the es-
071   timation of quantiles’ gradients. Relative to confTr, our proposed VR-ConfTr solution sig-
072   nificantly stabilizes training - leading to faster convergence, and consistently enhances the length
073   efficiency of post-training conformal prediction sets when compared against baselines.

075   1.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS
076
077
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
078   Conformal risk minimization. We formulate conformal risk minimization (CRM) as a framework
079   for training a parameterized predictor that learns according to CP efficiency metrics.
080
      A “plug-in” algorithm. Focusing on CRM for length efficiency optimization, we provide a novel
081   analysis for the variance of the ConfTr (Stutz et al., 2022) method, which shows the need for
082   improved estimators of the quantile gradients. Then, we introduce the pipeline of variance-reduced
083   conformal training (VR-ConfTr), our proposed algorithm to overcome this challenge, which lever-
084   ages a “plug-in” step to incorporate improved estimates of quantiles’ gradients in the training.
085
      Novel variance reduction technique. Building on a fundamental result, which characterizes the
086
      gradient of the population quantile as a conditional expectation, we propose a novel estimator for
087   quantile gradients whose variance is provably reduced with the training batch size. This novel
088   estimator can be seamlessly integrated into VR-ConfTr. We analyze the bias-variance trade-off of
089   this novel estimator and establish its precise relationship with the conformity measures associated
090   to a predictor πθ (y|x).
091
      Empirical validations. We extensively analyze our method on various benchmark and real-world
092
      datasets, including MNIST, FMNIST, KMNIST and OrganAMNIST. Our results demonstrate that
093   VR-ConfTr consistently and significantly improves the efficiency and stability of conformal train-
094   ing for length efficiency optimization.
095
096
      Broad applicability. Our approach and novel variance reduction technique can be integrated into
      any CRM method that requires quantile gradient estimation, extending its utility to a large class of
097
      conformal prediction frameworks and learning models.

148   2     P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
149
150   Let us consider a parameterized model of logits fθ : X → RK and let πθ (x) = softmax(fθ (x))
151   denote the corresponding predicted probabilities. The objective of a conformal prediction algorithm
152   is to construct a set predictor Cθ : X → 2Y starting from the model fθ in such a way that Cθ achieves
153
      marginal coverage. One common way to achieve this is via a thresholding (THR) set predictor (Vovk
      et al. (2005)), Cθ (x; τ ) = {y ∈ Y : Eθ (x, y) ≥ τ } for some well chosen threshold τ and conformity
154
      score Eθ (x, y), which can be any heuristic notion of uncertainty regarding label y upon input x for
155
      the predictor fθ (·). Some choices for the conformity score include (i) the predicted probabilities
156   Eθ (x, y) = πθ (y|x) = [πθ (x)]y , (ii) the logits Eθ (x, y) = [fθ (x)]y , and (iii) the predicted log-
157   probabilities Eθ (x, y) = log πθ (y|x). Let us assume that X is an absolutely continuous random
158   vector. If we knew the marginal distribution for (X, Y ), then marginal coverage could be readily
159   achieved by setting τ = τ (θ) = Qα (Eθ (X, Y )) where Qα denotes the population quantile of some
160   scalar random variable. Indeed,
161
                                                P (Y ∈ Cθ (X; τ )) = P (Eθ (X, Y ) ≥ τ ) ≥ 1 − α                                                 (1)

163   known. Instead, we can estimate τ (θ) = Qα (Eθ (X, Y )) from data that, as long as it satisfies the
164   principle of exchangeability, can be used to ascertain marginal coverage of Cθ (x; τ ).

      5     C ONCLUDING R EMARKS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
530
531
      We formalized the concept of optimizing CP efficiency during training as the problem of conformal
      risk minimization (CRM). We identified a key source of sample inefficiency in the ConfTr method
532
      proposed by Stutz et al. (2022), which is a CRM method for length efficiency optimization. Our
533
      theoretical analysis elucidated the source of sample inefficiency, which lies in the estimation of the
534   gradient of the population quantile. To address this issue, we introduced a novel technique that
535   improves the gradient estimation of the population quantile of the conformity scores by provably
536   reducing its variance. We show that, by incorporating this estimation technique in our proposed
537   VR-ConfTr algorithm, the training becomes more stable and the post-training conformal predictor
538   is often more efficient as well. Our work also opens up possibilities for future research in the area
539   of CRM. Indeed, further methods for quantile gradient estimation could be developed and readily
      integrated with our “plug-in” algorithm, for which we can expect improved training performance.

1454   channels doubles after each state (64, 128, 256, 512). Global average pooling is applied before the
1455   final fully connected layer, which maps the pooled feature representations to the 11 output classes.
