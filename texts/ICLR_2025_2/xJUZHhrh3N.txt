011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013         We introduce Bias-Variance Weighted Actor Critic (BiVWAC), a modification
014         scheme for actor-critic algorithms allowing control over the bias-variance weighting in
015         the critic. In actor-critic algorithms, the critic loss is the Mean Squared Error (MSE).
016
            The MSE may be decomposed in terms of bias and variance. Based on this decompo-
            sition, BiVWAC constructs a new critic loss, through a hyperparameter α, to weigh
017
            bias vs variance. MSE and Actor with Variance Estimated Critic (AVEC, which only
018
            considers the variance in the MSE decomposition) are special cases of this weighting
019         for α = 0.5 and α = 0 respectively. We demonstrate the theoretical consistency
020         of our new critic loss and measure its performance on a set of tasks. We also study
021         value estimation and gradient estimation capabilities of BiVWAC to understand the
022         means by which BiVWAC impacts performance. We show experimentally that the
023         MSE is suboptimal as a critic loss when compared to other α values. We equip SAC
024         and PPO with the BiVWAC loss to obtain BiVWAC-SAC and BiVWAC-PPO and we
025         propose a safe α value, α∗ , for which BiVWAC-SAC is better than or equal to SAC
026         in all studied tasks but one in terms of policy performance. We also point out that
027
            BiVWAC introduces minimal changes to the algorithms and virtually no additional
            computational cost. In addition we also present a method to compare the impact of
028
            critic modifications between algorithms in a sound manner.

046   ERM for Deep RL value prediction Deep reinforcement learning (Deep RL) is no exception to
047   this rule. However the setting varies slightly, which leads to important considerations. In Deep RL
048   one wishes to learn the value function V (or Q the state-action value function). We use supervised
049   learning to do so through the learning of a parameterized function fϕ (most likely a neural network
050   with weights ϕ). However, the targets Y (V or Q) are unknown in RL. Instead a proxy Ŷ (V̂ or Q̂)
051   of those values is used. This proxy is often an estimation of the “true” values, built using the reward
052   received at the current step rt and some combination of the estimations of the value of the next states
053   using fϕ . This differs from the classical supervised setting as, adding to the unknown targets, the
      proxy targets are functions of the estimator they are used to train, which leads to a non-stationary

054
      learning problem. One may argue that this setting is not a proper supervised learning one where the
055   “targets” (the proxy targets) are already known before prediction. However, the implicit assumption
056
      made in Deep RL is that these proxy targets Ŷ are good enough representations of the true targets
057   in order for fϕ to still learn about Y . Thus it is considered safe to assume that, through the use of
058   a proxy, we are still learning information about the true targets. However this entails an additional
059   error which encapsulates how good of an approximation of Y , Ŷ is. This composition of error can be
060   understood as trying to learn fϕ to reduce M SE(fϕ , Ŷ ) while also reducing the difference between
061
      M SE(fϕ , Ŷ ) and M SE(fϕ , Y ) :
062

064                   M SE(fϕ , Y ) = M SE(fϕ , Yb ) + (M SE(fϕ , Y ) − M SE(fϕ , Yb ))
065
066
067   The validity of the proximity between Ŷ and Y is a common belief, and the experimental validation
068
      of this assumption is nonetheless rarely studied as pointed out by Ilyas et al. (2020).
069   The common way to solve this supervised learning problem in RL is to minimize M SE(fϕ , Yb )
070   to hopefully also reduce M SE(fϕ , Y ). Then, we can apply the bias-variance decomposition of
071   the MSE (James et al., 2023, Section 2.2.2) to MSE(fϕ , Ŷ ) (although strictly speaking we are not
072   dealing with bias and variance but bias (or variance) of an estimation of the target). This allows us
073   to study bias-variance weightings in our prediction problem.
074
075
076   Bias-variance weighting in Deep-RL value prediction In this work we will study how bias-
077   variance weightings in the critic loss impacts both the critic’s performance (in terms of value es-
078   timation) and the agent’s performance (in terms of policy returns). Our intuition comes from the
079   fact that the traditional critic loss (the MSE) weighs equally bias and variance, and that brings two
080   issues: first, this 50-50 weighting is arbitrary; second, Tucker et al. (2018), Ilyas et al. (2020) and
081   Flet-Berliac et al. (2021) argue that the core problem in value estimation is the variance and not
082
      the bias. As a consequence, we want to study how changing this weighting impacts performances,
      and try to understand through which aspect of the actor-critic framework the modifications happens.
083
      Understanding more about how this weighting impacts learning will allow to more efficiently select
084
      the weighting to get better results across multiple tasks, as well as giving a better understanding on
085   how the critic impacts the learning of actor-critic algorithms.
086
087
088   Related works Other works have studied core concepts of actor-critics algorihms and challenging
089   the common beliefs around them. Notably, CrossQ (Bhatt et al., 2024) challenges the need for a
090   critic in the first place, Ilyas et al. (2020) measure experimentally the validity of the assumption that
091   the critic fits the true values. The same is also true for common building blocks of RL algorithms
092   such as n-steps returns (Daley et al., 2024). There is also an important amount of work regarding
093   improving bias and variance in RL such as Averaged-DQN (Anschel et al., 2017) or studying their
094   properties (Zhang et al., 2021). Our work lies ate the intersections of these questions.

145   Bias and variance are often discussed in RL, the common consensus being that it is preferable to
146   decrease variance at the expense of more bias, as the former is much more frequently greater than
147   the latter, thus giving an overall improvement by decreasing the total error. However, it is rarely
148   explicitly defined what bias and variance are referring to in this context: to which yb and y should
149   we apply Lemma 2.1? There are three main possibilities for bias and variance in deep actor-critic
150   algorithms, coming from three different estimations (the others are beyond the scope of this paper):
151

152

153

154

155

231
      function (as opposed to the empirical surrogate one) and, as a consequence, allow for better actor
232   performances. We share the same intuitions as Flet-Berliac et al. (2021) on how to improve the critic.
233   We think that the relative differences between value estimations are more important than the absolute
234   differences between them, in order to learn a good critic (one that leads to better actor performances).
235   We also think that, as opposed to classical supervised learning, the critic should learn to quickly
236   adapt to outliers (instead of avoiding them) as they represent valuable new pieces of information
237   (may it be positive or negative). This may be less desirable if P , the transition function, is stochastic
238   and has a large variance as this would lead to capturing variance in the transition function instead of
239   the variance due to new behavior from the policy (e.g. reaching new and rewarding states) . How
240
      large the variance has to be to be problematic is beyond the scope of this work, we will assume that

241
      (the variance of the imapct of actions should be higher than the one due to the stochasticity of the
242
      environment). Finally, we want to go beyond AVEC and study the intermediate weightings of bias

      4.2                                                  C ORRECTION IMPACT
461
      As shown in corollary 3.2.1, gϕ leads to an unbiased estimation of the policy gradient, while fϕ leads
462
      to a biased estimation of the same gradient. However we pointed out that this bias may be small in
463
      practice as it is proportional to the estimation error. Meanwhile, considering the correction (gϕ ) may
464   lead to more variance in the policy gradient estimation due to the estimation of the expectation of
465   said estimation errors. To evaluate which solution is better, we study BiVWAC’s performance with
466   correction in order to conclude on whether or not it is actually helpful. In Figure 3 we can observe
467   that the correction leads to a degradation in performance from the un-corrected version. We hypoth-
468   esize that this comes from the variance introduced by the additional dependence to the trajectory
469   samples of the estimation correction. We will thus only consider BiVWAC without correction.

501   5                       C ONCLUSION
502
503   We introduced BiVWAC to control the bias-variance weighting in the critic objective through a
504   hyperparamter α. From this objective we can derive the MSE (α= 0.5, weighting bias and variance
505   equally) and AVEC (α = 0, that only considers the variance). We demonstrated that BiVWAC is
506   theoretically sound as it still leads to an unbiased estimation of the policy gradient. We motivated
507
      the need to extend beyond, or rather between, the MSE and AVEC and to study in-between values
      of α to find better weightings of bias and variance. We experimentally evaluated BiVWAC applied
508
      to two popular actor-critic algorithms SAC and PPO and have shown that the MSE is indeed a
509
      suboptimal weighting of bias and variance for critics. For BiVWAC-SAC, we found that α values
510   close to 0 tend to provide better results than the MSE or AVEC. While it is possible to tune α, we
511   propose α∗ = 10−4 as a safe value for which BiVWAC-SAC outperforms the MSE in all tasks and
512   AVEC in almost all tasks (however AVEC fails to learn at all on other tasks). For BiVWAC-PPO
513   we found that, while the pattern for the optimal value of α is harder to identify, there always exists
514   values of α that perform better than the MSE, and in all tasks but one, values of α also performed
515   better than AVEC. We measured the estimation and approximation error of the modified algorithms
516   as well as the actor gradient estimation error in order to better understand the means through which
517   BiVWAC impacts learning. As we intuited, we showed that BiVWAC, with the correct α values,
518
      leads to a better approximation of the true values through a reduction in variance, and that translates
      into a better estimation of the actor’s gradient.
519
520   More work would be interesting to conduct regarding the relationship between α and the hyperpa-
521   rameters of the underlying algorithm, most importantly the one with a direct link to the estimations
522   we impact: the batch size, the size of the neural network, the discount factor. Another interesting
523   follow-up would be experimenting with schedules for α, as the optimal-variance weighting may not
524
      be constant. Additionally, studying separate weightings for the two variances and the covariance in
      the MSE decomposition would allow for more flexibility at the expense of a larger hyperparameter
525
      space.
526
527   We conclude that, while BiVWAC-PPO still requires deeper analysis to propose heuristics regarding
528   the choice of optimal α for a specific task, BiVWAC-SAC is a promising algorithm that should be
529   extended to other tasks and algorithms to confirm the improvements shown in this work, and when
530
      used with α∗ = 0.0001 is an improvement over SAC in all studied tasks but one (13.5‰ average
      improvement) at virtually no additional cost and with minimal modifications.

      D     M EANINGFUL CRITIC COMPARISONS
865
866
      We begin by stating that had we trained two separate instances of the same algorithm with only the
867   critic loss changing we could not have compared their critic’s performance as it would have been
868   trained using different data. This would lead to problems such as:
869
870       • The states encountered depend on the policy collecting them. As a consequence, the critic’s
871         performance depends on how hard the values of states encountered are to estimate. One
872
            critic leading to lesser actor performance may stagnate collecting low rewards leading to a
            seemingly easier task than another critic leading to good actor performance but encountering
873
            new and harder to estimate states. This leads to the impossibility of comparing the critics with
874
            each observing data collected from a different policy, they need to use the same data.
875       • As the states encountered differ, one would have to decide which states would be used to
876         evaluate algorithms, so that they do not favor one or the other critic. Finding such balance
877         seems like a difficult task as regression models tend to perform unpredictably on data too
878         different from what the one they were trained on and thus adding data seen by one critic
879         would lead to possibly non-informative results for the other critic, and using data not seen by
880         both would lead to even less informative results. If the two algorithms had collected data from
881         states suffienctly similar, this approach would work. However that would seriously limit the
882         range of algorithms this method can compare.
883   To tackle these issues, we propose the following methodology to compare the same algorithm with
884   two different critics:
885
886       1. We initialize two critics C1 and C2 sharing the same parameters at initialization.
887       2. C1 is trained using LBiVWAC , our critic loss. It is then used to compute the actor loss. The
888
             actor is then used to select actions.
          3. Meanwhile, C2 is also trained on the same data as C1 , however it has no impact on the actor,
889
             and thus data collection.
890
          4. During training, as often as needed, we measure the value estimations fϕC1 andfϕC2 by using
891
             C1 and C2 on the same states Seval sampled from states recently seen by the agent. We also
892
             collect their corresponding surrogate targets Q̂πCθ1 andQ̂πCθ2 (the same is valid for V πθ ).
893
          5. After training we estimate the true values of Seval using Monte-Carlo (MC) rollouts.
894       6. We compare the variance, Estimation error and Approximation error of C1 and C2 using the
895          collected values.
896
897   For every 10% of the total number of timesteps collected during training, we experimentally ap-
898   proximate the true value of the policy. We approximate it on 50 states randomly sampled from the
899
      current agent’s buffer (50 states from the rollout buffer for PPO and 50 states from the states of the
      Replay buffer added since the last evaluation for SAC, e.g. from the last 1000 states added in the
900
      buffer if the number of timesteps was 10,000). The true values are then estimated by collecting 51̇05
901
      samples from these states (and a given action for SAC as we estimate Qπ (s, a)) using the current
902   policy and computing the average discounted-return through Monte-Carlo estimation.
