011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Causal probing aims to analyze large language models (or other foundation mod-
014            els) by examining how modifying their representation of various latent properties
015            using interventions derived from probing classifiers impacts their outputs. Recent
016
               works have cast doubt on the theoretical basis of several leading causal probing
               intervention methods, but it has been unclear how to systematically evaluate the
017
               effectiveness of probing interventions in practice. To address this, we formally
018
               define and quantify two key causal probing desiderata: completeness (how thor-
019            oughly the representation of the target property has been transformed) and selec-
020            tivity (how little other properties have been impacted). We introduce an empirical
021            analysis framework to measure and evaluate completeness and selectivity, allow-
022            ing us to make the first direct comparisons of the reliability of different families
023            of causal probing methods (e.g., linear vs. nonlinear or counterfactual vs. nullify-
024            ing interventions). Our experimental analysis shows that: (1) there is an inherent
025            tradeoff between completeness and selectivity, (2) no leading probing method is
026            able to consistently satisfy both criteria at once, and (3) across the board, nulli-
027
               fying interventions are far less complete than counterfactual interventions, which
               suggests that nullifying methods may not be an effective approach to causal prob-
028
               ing.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      What latent properties do large language models (LLMs) learn to represent, and how are these
034
      representations leveraged by these models? Causal probing aims to answer this question by first
035
      transforming a model’s embedding representations of some latent property of interest (e.g., parts-
036   of-speech) by intervening on probes trained to predict the property, feeding embeddings back into
037   the LLM, and evaluating how the model’s behavior on downstream tasks changes in the context
038   of these interventions (Geiger et al., 2020; Ravfogel et al., 2020; Elazar et al., 2021; Tucker et al.,
039   2021; Lasri et al., 2022; Davies et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023). This approach assumes that such
040   interventions will affect the behavior of models that leverage the target property to perform the task,
041   and that a negligible impact indicates that models are not using the property. But such conclusions
042   should only be drawn if we can be confident that the intervention method has fully and precisely
043   carried out the intended transformation (Davies & Khakzar, 2024). Indeed, prior works have raised
044
      serious doubts about the utility of causal probing, suggesting that interventions may have a greater
      impact on the representation of non-targeted properties than on that of the target property (Kumar
045
      et al., 2022), and that the original value of the property may still be recoverable from intervened
046
      embeddings (Elazar et al., 2021; Ravfogel et al., 2022b). However, it is unclear whether observations
047   about the shortcomings of one intervention generalize to other types of interventions, since there is
048   currently no generally accepted approach for how to empirically compare the reliability of different
049   intervention methodologies.
050
      Thus, our main goal in this work is to work toward a systematic understanding of the effectiveness
051
      and limitations of current causal probing methodologies. Specifically, we propose an empirical
052
      analysis framework to evaluate the reliability of causal probing according to two key desiderata:
053
            1. Completeness: interventions should fully remove the representation of targeted properties.


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
            2. Selectivity: interventions should not impact non-targeted properties.
055
056   We measure the extent to which different types of interventions are complete and selective by us-
057   ing “oracle probes” that allow us to (approximately) estimate the impact of an intervention on both
058
      targeted and non-targeted properties. We apply our framework to several leading causal probing
      intervention methodologies, finding that they each show a clear tradeoff between these criteria, and
059
      that no method is able to satisfy them both simultaneously. We also show that, when intervening
060
      on a property that is necessary to solve a specific task, the most complete and reliable interventions
061   lead to the most consistent and substantial changes in LLM task performance. Finally, we observe
062   that, across all methods we study, counterfactual causal probing interventions are universally more
063   complete and reliable than nullifying interventions. While this result is consistent with the find-
064   ings of earlier work criticizing nullifying interventions (see Section 2), it has not previously been
065   possible to directly compare them with counterfactual interventions, and evidence against nullify-
066   ing interventions has often been used to argue against causal probing more broadly. However, our
067   results, as enabled by our empirical evaluation framework, show that the most serious limitations
068   are generally only present for nullifying methods, and that counterfactual methods constitute a more
069
      reliable approach to causal probing.

      2   BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
072
073
074   Structural Probing The goal of structural probing (Hewitt & Manning, 2019; Maudslay et al.,
075
      2020; Belinkov et al., 2020) is to analyze which properties (e.g., part-of-speech, sentiment labels,
      etc.) are represented by a deep learning model (e.g., LLM) by training probing classifiers to predict
076
      these properties from latent embedding representations (Belinkov, 2022). Given, say, an LLM M ,
077
      input token sequence x = (x1 , ..., xN ), and embeddings hl = Ml (x) of input x at layer l of M ,
078   suppose Z is a latent property of interest that takes a discrete value Z = z for input x. The goal of
079   structural probing is to train a classifier gZ: Ml (x) 7→ z to predict the value of Z from hl . On the
080   most straightforward interpretation, if gZ achieves high accuracy on the probe task, then the model is
081   said to be “representing” Z (Belinkov, 2022). An important criticism of such methodologies is that
082   correlation does not imply causation – i.e., that simply because a given property can be predicted
083   from embedding representations does not mean that the model is using the property in any way
084   (Hewitt & Liang, 2019; Elazar et al., 2021; Belinkov, 2022; Davies et al., 2023).
085
086   Causal Probing A prominent response to this concern has been causal probing, which uses struc-
087   tural probes to remove or alter that property in the model’s representation, and measuring the impact
088   of such interventions on the model’s predictions (Elazar et al., 2021; Tucker et al., 2021; Lasri et al.,
089   2022; Davies et al., 2023). Specifically, causal probing performs interventions do(Z) that modify
090   M ’s representation of Z in the embeddings hl , producing ĥl , where interventions can either encode
091   a counterfactual value Z = z ′ (denoted do(Z = z ′ ) where z ̸= z ′ ), or remove the representation
092   of Z entirely (denoted do(Z = 0)). Following the intervention, modified embeddings ĥl are fed
093   back into M beginning at layer l + 1 to complete the forward pass, yielding intervened predictions
094   PM (· | x, do(Z)). Comparison with the original predictions PM (· | x) allows one to measure the
095   extent to which M uses its representation of Z in computing PM (· | x).
096
097   Causal Probing: Limitations However, prior works have indicated that information about the
098   target property that should have been completely removed may still be recoverable by the model
099   (Elazar et al., 2021; Ravfogel et al., 2022b; 2023), in which case interventions are not complete; or
100   that most of the impact of interventions may actually be the result of collateral damage to correlated,
101   non-targeted properties (Kumar et al., 2022), in which case interventions are not selective. How
102   seriously should we take such critiques? We observe several important shortcomings in each of
103   these prior studies on the limitations of causal probing interventions:
104         1. These limitations have only been empirically demonstrated for the task of removing infor-
105            mation about a target property from embeddings such that the model cannot be fine-tuned
106            to use the property for downstream tasks (Kumar et al., 2022; Ravfogel et al., 2022b; 2023).
107            But considering that the goal of causal probing is to interpret the behavior of an existing
               pre-trained model, the question is not whether models can be fine-tuned to use the property;


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
                 it is whether models already use the property without task-specific fine-tuning, which has
109              not been addressed in prior work. Do we observe the same limitations in this context?
110           2. These limitations have only been studied in the context of linear nullifying interventions
111              (e.g., Ravfogel et al. 2020; 2022a), despite the proliferation of other causal probing method-
112              ologies that have been developed since, including nonlinear (Tucker et al., 2021; Ravfogel
113              et al., 2022b; Shao et al., 2022; Davies et al., 2023) and counterfactual interventions (Rav-
114              fogel et al., 2021; Tucker et al., 2021; Davies et al., 2023) (see Section 4.4). Do we observe
115              the same limitations for, e.g., nonlinear counterfactual interventions?
116   In this work, we answer both questions by providing a precise, quantifiable, and sufficiently general
117   definition of completeness and selectivity that it is applicable to all such causal probing interven-
118   tions, and carry out extensive experiments to evaluate representative methods from each category of
119   interventions when applied to a pre-trained LLM as it performs a zero-shot prompt task.
120
121   Causal Probing: Evaluation While we are the first to define and measure the completeness and
122   selectivity of causal probing interventions, a few other works have also aimed to evaluate causal
123   probing approaches (or closely related methods). Closest to our work, Huang et al. (2024) evaluates
124   interchange interventions over factual associations – e.g., modifying a model’s representation to en-
125
      code located in(France, Asia) rather than located in(France, Europe), where
      interchange interventions extract the representation of association A1 for entity E1 , then swap out
126
      the representation of association A2 with entity E2 to encode A1 instead (Meng et al., 2022). Such
127
      interventions operate at the level of individual entities (e.g., France, Asia, or Europe) rather than
128   general latent properties (e.g., part-of-speech) which have categorical values that are each taken by
129   many different inputs, as studied in causal probing.

135

136

138   notion of oracle probes.

226   4.1   L ANGUAGE M ODEL : BERT
227
228   We test our framework by carrying out an extensive range of experiments to analyze current causal
229   probing interventions in the context of BERT (Devlin et al., 2019). We opt for BERT because it is
230   very well-studied in the context of language model interpretability (Rogers et al., 2021), particularly
231   in causal probing (Ravfogel et al., 2020; Elazar et al., 2021; Ravfogel et al., 2021; Lasri et al., 2022;
232   Ravfogel et al., 2022b; 2023; Davies et al., 2023). We further motivate this decision in Appendix A.
233
234
235   4.2   TASK : S UBJECT-V ERB AGREEMENT
236
237   To test our evaluation framework experimentally, we need a task with a clear causal structure. We
238   start with the simplest possible case, where labels are fully determined by the value of a single bi-
239   nary causal variable Zc , with a second binary environmental variable Ze that influences inputs and
240   may be spuriously correlated with the label (meaning that LLMs might still leverage it in performing
241   the task). By intervening on each of these variables, we can calculate how well the target variable
242   is damaged (completeness) and how little the opposite variable is damaged (selectivity). We also
243
      want a task that BERT performs well, allowing us to assess the extent to which its performance is
      attributable to its representation of the causal variable Zc versus the spuriously-correlated environ-
244
      mental variable Ze .
245
246   To fulfill both criteria, we select the cloze prompting task of subject-verb agreement, which has
247   also been the subject of analysis in multiple prior causal probing works (Lasri et al., 2022; Ravfogel
248   et al., 2021). Each instance in this task takes the form ⟨xi , yi ⟩ where xi is a sentence such as “the girl
249
      with the keys [MASK] the door,” and the task of the LLM is to predict PM (yi | x) > PM (yi′ | x)
      (here, that y = “locks” rather than “lock”). The causal variable Zc for this task is the number of
250
      the subject, because (grammatically) this is the only variable the number of the verb depends on.
251
      The values Zc can take are Sg and Pl. The environmental variable Ze is the number of the noun
252   immediately preceding the [MASK] token when that noun is not the subject. Most commonly, this
253   is the object of a prepositional phrase (e.g., in the earlier example, this would be “keys” in the phrase
254   “with the keys”). The values Ze can take are also Sg and Pl.
255
      We use the LGD subject-verb agreement dataset (Linzen et al., 2016), which consists of > 1M
256
      naturalistic English sentences from Wikipedia. We use syntax annotations to extract values for the
257
      environmental variable: if the part-of-speech of the word immediately preceding the [MASK] token
258   is a noun, and it is the object of a preposition (i.e., not the subject), then its number defines Ze .
259   About 83% of the sentences do not have a prepositional object preceding [MASK], and so are only
260   relevant for causal interventions. Because subject-verb agreement is a prediction task over two verb
261   forms, and as BERT is a masked-language model, we only use sentences for which both forms of
262   the target verb are in BERT’s vocabulary. We use 40% of the examples to train the oracle probe (of
263   which 5% is used for validation data), 40% to train the interventions (again, 5% is used for validation
264   data), and 20% as a test set. (The contingency table for the test set is shown in Table 2.)
265   Finally, for all experiments, we analyze [MASK] embeddings hl[MASK] from BERT’s final layer
266   l = |L| immediately before it is fed into the masked language modeling head. We do this because,
267   for earlier layers, any information about the target property Z removed or modified by interventions
268   over the [MASK] embedding may be recoverable from embeddings of other tokens (see Elazar et al.,
269   2021), as attention blocks allow the model to pool information from the contextualized embeddings
      of other tokens.

      4.3   O RACLE P ROBE A PPROXIMATION
271
272   Oracle Probe Architecture Before we can measure how well causal probing interventions satisfy
273   completeness and selectivity, we must first provide a suitable approximation of oracle probe o. What
274   probe would serve as the best approximation? As our goal with oracle probes is to measure the im-
275   pact of causal probing interventions on the representation of the property in LLM embeddings (and
276   not to measure how well models encode a property of interest to begin with, as in structural probing),
277   we follow Pimentel et al. (2020)’s argument that more expressive, higher-performing probes are a
278
      better choice for measuring the mutual information between embedding representations and prop-
      erties of interest, and opt to use more expressive probes ô to approximate the oracle o. Specifically,
279
      we implement ô as a multi-layer perceptron (MLP), and select hyperparameters that yield the probe
280
      with the highest validation-set accuracy using grid search (see Appendix C.2 for further details).
281
282   Oracle Probe Training Oracle probes are trained on 40% of the available LGD data, which is
283   entirely separate from the data used to train interventions. As these probes are used to assess the
284   representation of Zc and Ze in latent vector representations, it is important that their training data
285   does not overlap with the training data of probes used for interventions. Finally, we require oracle
286   probes to be trained on data where there is 0 correlation between Zc and Ze by selecting the largest
287   possible subset of the oracle probe training data such that (1) there is no correlation between Zc and
288   Ze , and (2) the label distribution of the target property is preserved. This is crucial, because any
289   spurious correlation between these variables could lead to a probe that is trained on property Zc to
290
      also partially rely on representations of Ze (Kumar et al., 2022).
291
292   4.4   I NTERVENTIONS
293
      In this section we describe each intervention that we study. In all cases, probes used for interventions
294   are trained on a fully disjoint train set from that used to train the oracle probes.
295
296   Nullifying Interventions We experiment with two nullifying interventions:
297
298         1. INLP (Ravfogel et al., 2020) removes all linearly-predictive information about the target
299
               property from embeddings by iteratively training linear probes to predict the property, pro-
               jecting embeddings into the nullspace of the classifier, training a new classifier on the pro-
300
               jected embeddings, etc. until they are no longer linearly predictive of the target property.
301
            2. RLACE (Ravfogel et al., 2022a) identifies a minimal-rank bias subspace to remove linearly-
302            predictive information by solving a constrained, linear minimax game, in which a probe
303            aims to predict the concept while an adversary hinders it by projecting input embeddings
304            into a lower-dimensional subspace.
305
      Additional details regarding our implementation of each nullifying intervention are in Appendix C.3.
306
307
      Counterfactual Interventions We experiment with four counterfactual interventions: three
308
      gradient-based interventions (GBIs) (Davies et al., 2023),5 and AlterRep. GBIs train traditional
309   structural probes over embeddings and perform interventions by attacking these probes using
310   gradient-based adversarial attacks, which perturb the embeddings to minimize the loss of the struc-
311   tural probe with respect to the target counterfactual value Z = z ′ within an L∞ -ball with radius ϵ
312   around the original embedding. We explore three popular adversarial attacks:
313
            1. FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2015) takes a single gradient step of magnitude ϵ in the direction
314
               that minimizes the probe loss with respect to the target class z ′ .
315         2. PGD (Madry et al., 2017) iteratively minimizes the probe loss with respect to z ′ by per-
316            forming gradient descent within a L∞ -ball of radius ϵ, projecting back onto its surface if a
317            given step takes the embedding outside the ball.
318         3. AutoAttack (Croce & Hein, 2020) minimizes the probe loss with respect to z ′ by ensem-
319            bling over several adversarial attacks: two black-box attacks (FAB and Square Attack) and
320            APGD (a version of PGD utilizing momentum in gradient steps).
321

358   Impact on Model Behavior The ultimate goal of causal probing is to measure a model M ’s use
359   of a property Z by comparing intervened predictions PM (· | x, do(Z)) to its original predictions
360   PM (· | x). The experimental framework described to this point aims to measure the reliability of the
361   interventions themselves, a prerequisite to making claims about the underlying model. Thus, while
362   the main focus of our work is to evaluate the reliability of interventions as a prerequisite for more
363   rigorous causal probing – and not to perform interventions on a given model in order to make any
364   specific claims about the model itself – it is nonetheless important to consider how the completeness,
365   selectivity, and reliability of a given intervention relate to the impact on model behavior observed
366
      using the intervention.
367                                                                                                         |L|
      For each intervention, we feed the final-layer intervened vectors ĥZc into BERT’s prediction head,
368   and predict the singular or plural form of the missing verb based on which has higher probability. We
369   compute accuracy across all test examples, and compare this “intervened” accuracy with the original
370   task accuracy for each intervention, a common protocol for causal probing (Elazar et al., 2021; Lasri
371   et al., 2022; Davies et al., 2023). Thus, instead of measuring the impact of interventions on oracle
372   probes, we consider their impact on the language model itself predicting the correct output.

              6                   D ISCUSSION
453
454
             Tradeoff: Completeness vs. Selectivity Figure 1b shows that counterfactual methods are able
455          to achieve near perfect completeness, for both linear (AlterRep) and nonlinear (GBI) interventions;
456          but AlterRep has much higher selectivity while maintaining perfect completeness. These findings
457          suggest that linear methods may indeed be preferred for causal probing because their limited expres-
458          sivity prevents them from memorizing spurious associations in probe training data (Hewitt & Liang,
459          2019), potentially making them less likely to damage spurious (environmental) features, meaning
460          that they would be more selective. Another possibility is that linear methods can only achieve such
461          high completeness in these experiments because we are considering embeddings from the final layer,
462          which have been argued to exhibit a greater degree of linearity (Alain & Bengio, 2017). However,
463
             despite the impressive results of AlterRep, its reliability score still peaks at 0.8346 for the optimal
             value of hyperparamteter α, indicating that there is still room for improvement. In particular, while
464
             GBIs can be precisely calibrated to manage the completeness/selectivity tradeoff and can reach full
465
             completeness for high ϵ, they never achieve an overall reliability above 0.5518, meaning that im-
466          proving selectivity for nonlinear interventions is very much an open research question.
467
468          In contrast to counterfactual methods, nullifying interventions never achieve high completeness
469
             (peaking at 0.3308 for INLP at the high watermark for reliability, 0.4644), but tend to have much
             higher selectivity (at least relative to GBIs). This is likely because both INLP and RLACE are ex-
470
             plicitly optimized to minimize collateral damage while removing the target representation (Ravfogel
471
             et al., 2020; 2022a), whereas GBIs are not (Tucker et al., 2021; Davies et al., 2023).
472
473
             Counterfactual vs. Nullifying Interventions Across the board, all counterfactual methods (linear
474          and nonlinear) achieve substantially higher completeness and reliability scores than both nullifying
475          methods (INLP and RLACE). Our empirical evaluation framework is equally applicable to both
476          types of interventions: it uses the same oracle probes and test dataset, and is not biased in favor of one
477          intervention type or the other. Yet, consider INLP and AlterRep: in our implementation, AlterRep
478          uses the same classifiers as INLP to perform its intervention, only to carry out a counterfactual rather
479          than nullifying intervention; but AlterRep has peak reliability score 0.8346 and completeness score
480          of 1.0, while INLP has 0.4644 and 0.3503, respectively. Why should the same set of classifiers using
481          a similar method yield such a different outcome?
482          One possible explanation is a general critique of causal probing: interventions (both counterfactual
483          and nullifying) operate at the level of latent properties, meaning that they can produce embeddings
484          that do not correspond to any specific input (Geiger et al., 2020; Abraham et al., 2022). For instance,
485          consider the subject-verb agreement task. For input x = “The girl with the keys [MASK] the door”,
             what sentence xZc =0 would correspond to the nullifying intervention do(Zc = 0)? In English, there


                                                                                      9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      is no corresponding noun where grammatical number is “nullified”. For counterfactual interventions
487   do(Zc = z ′ ), however, there is a natural interpretation of the intervened sentence xZc =z′ : “girl”
488   would be swapped out for “girls”.
489
490
      Given that, unlike the counterfactual case, there are no “nullified inputs” xZ=0 that can be used to
      produce ground-truth “nullified embeddings” hlZ=0 to train oracle probes, one natural interpretation
491
      might be that prediction over nullified embeddings is a form of “oracle probe distribution shift”,
492
      which would explain the poor performance of nullifying interventions. That is, since oracle probes
493   are never directly trained on intervened embeddings, the fact that they “transfer” better to counter-
494   factual embeddings than to nullified embeddings is the direct result of the lack of correspondence
495   of nullifying interventions to any particular “ground truth” (either in the input or embedding spaces)
496   compared with counterfactual interventions. We argue that such low completeness and reliability
497   scores for nullifying interventions are likely due to this inherent problem with the concept of em-
498   bedding nullification, rather than any particular intervention methodology.
499
500   Reliability and Task Accuracy Figure 2 indicates a clear trend: more reliable and complete in-
501   terventions show a greater change in BERT’s task accuracy (excepting the renegade points whose
502   high change in task accuracy is primarily due to excessive damage to Zc ). In particular, the most
503   reliable intervention (AlterRep) consistently shows the greatest change in task accuracy, and the
504   least reliable intervention (i.e., the nullifying interventions INLP and RLACE) show the least clear
505   trend. The GBIs, which are more reliable than INLP and RLACE but less reliable than AlterRep,
506   are capable of damaging task accuracy as much as AlterRep, but only after increasing ϵ beyond its
507
      maximum-reliability setting.
508   This is an intuitive result: in the case where BERT does indeed perform the task by leveraging its
509   representation of Zc , then more reliable interventions would have a greater effect on the model’s task
510   performance. We do not claim that this is necessarily the case (e.g., our results may look different
511   if we intervene in BERT’s earlier layers) – rather, we take the clear relationship between between
512   intervention reliability and the change in task accuracy to be a strong indicator that more reliable
513
      methods do indeed yield stronger and more consistent results. This finding reinforces the utility of
      our framework in evaluating the quality of causal probing interventions as tools for studying models’
514
      use of latent representations.

517   7   C ONCLUSION
518
519
      In this work, we proposed a general empirical evaluation framework for causal probing, defining
520
      the reliability of interventions in terms of completeness and selectivity. Our framework makes it
521
      possible to directly compare different kinds of interventions, such as linear vs. nonlinear or nul-
522   lifying vs. counterfactual methods. We applied our framework to study leading causal probing
523   techniques, finding that they all exhibit a tradeoff between completeness and selectivity. Counter-
524   factual interventions tend to be more complete and reliable, and nullifying techniques are generally
525   more selective. In particular, we find that a linear counterfactual intervention has a very favorable
526   tradeoff between completeness and selectivity, while a closely-related nullifying intervention using
527   the exact same probes has very low reliability. This suggests that underlying differences between
528   interventions types, rather than differences between specific methods of each type, may explain why
529   counterfactual interventions appear more reliable than nullifying methods for causal probing.

668   A    L IMITATIONS
669
670   Experimental Task and LLM In this work, we focus primarily on developing an empirical evalu-
671   ation framework to evaluate causal probing reliability, and deploy this framework in the context of a
672   relatively simple model (BERT) and task (subject-verb agreement). As we discuss in Section 4, we
673   intentionally select a more straightforward, well-studied experimental setting in order to focus on
674
      our framework and the distinctions it reveals between several types of causal probing interventions.
      That is, despite the proliferation of much larger and more powerful LLMs than BERT, there is still
675
      no commonly agreed-upon method for interpreting BERT’s learned representations and explaining
676
      its behavior, even for simple zero-shot prompting tasks on which it achieves high accuracy. As our
677   focus is to rigorously evaluate existing causal probing methodologies (many of which have been de-
678   signed specifically with masked language models like BERT in mind), we believe it is more useful
679   to evaluate causal probing methods in the simpler and better-understood context of BERT than it
680   would be to scale our empirical analysis to larger models.
681
      However, it is important to note that our evaluation framework makes no assumptions regarding
682
      LLM scale or architecture, nor the complexity of causal task structures. Thus, now that we have
683   demonstrated the effectiveness of our framework in discovering new insights regarding leading in-
684   tervention methods and providing the first hard evidence to empirically inform long-standing debates
685   regarding causal probing, such as the conceptual issues with nullifying interventions (Abraham et al.,
686   2022; Kumar et al., 2022), we hope that our framework will be deployed and improved upon by fu-
687   ture work to study larger and more complex tasks and models, such as autoregressive decoder-only
688   (GPT-style) models and tasks with many more latent properties of interest.
689
690   Multiple Layers Another potential limitation of our work is that our experiments only explore
691   interventions in the context of a single layer. As we explain in Section 4, we only examine the final
692
      layer in order to prevent information from non-intervened embeddings to be recovered by subsequent
      attention layers (as observed by Elazar et al. 2021). However, our framework makes no assumptions
693
      about the specific layer to analyze – indeed, it is even possible to study the completeness, selectivity,
694
      and reliability of interventions performed in earlier layers l with respect to their impact on oracle
695   probes in downstream layers l′ > l. We recommend such study as an interesting direction for future
696   work, particularly in developing approaches for understanding how information is distributed across
697   different contextualized token embeddings and accessed by downstream attention heads (e.g., as
698   studied in circuit discovery; Wang et al., 2023; Conmy et al., 2023).
699
700   Oracle Probe Approximation Finally, in our main paper, we report only results obtained using
701   MLP oracle probes (a decision which we justify at length in Section 4.3). However, we also per-
      formed experiments with linear oracle probes, which we report in Appendix D.3. In general, these

713
714   Completeness of Nullifying Interventions In Equation (2), we define the “goal” distribution PZ∗
715   of a nullifying causal probing intervention as being the uniform distribution – i.e., for a perfect
                                                   ∗
716   nullifying intervention, PZ∗ = Po (Z | hlZ=0 ) = U(Z). However, this is only true in the case of
717   causal probing, not concept removal, which is a more common use case for nullifying interventions
718   (see Section 2). That is, in the case of causal probing, the goal of an intervention is to intervene on
719   a model’s representation during its forward pass, feeding the intervened embedding back into the
720   model and observing the change in the model’s behavior (as described in Section 2). Recall that the
721   purpose of an oracle probe o is to decode model M ’s representation of a given property Z, not to
722   predict its ground truth value – that is, even if M encodes the incorrect value of Z = z ′ rather than
723
      Z = z for a given input, the oracle probe should still decode the incorrect value Z = z ′ . Indeed,
      this is precisely the principle behind using oracle probes in the case of counterfactual interventions
724
      that change the representation of Z = z to counterfactual value Z = z ′ , where oracle probes
725
      are used to validate the extent to which the representation has actually been changed to encode
726                                                                                                   ∗
      this counterfactual value, and the ideal counterfactual intervention yields Po (Z = z ′ | hlZ=z′ ) =
727   P ∗ (Z = z ′ ) = 1. However, in the case of nullifying interventions do(Z = 0), an intervened
                      ∗
728   embedding hlZ=0 would ideally remove all information encoding M ’s representation of the value
729   taken by Z, meaning that the M would not encode any value Z = z1 as being more probable than
730   Z = z2 (as any information that is predictive of the value taken by Z should have been removed).
                                                                                          ∗
731   In this case, the oracle probe o would predict an equal probability Po (Z = zi | hlZ=0 ) for any given
                                                         ∗
732   value zi that may be taken by Zi – i.e., Po (Z | hlZ=0 ) = PZ∗ = U(Z).
733
734
      However, this is not the case in the context of information removal, where the goal of an intervention
                                                                                                         ∗

735
      do(Z = 0) is to remove all information that is predictive of Z from embedding representations hlZ=0
                                                                ∗

736   such that no probe g can be trained to predict Pg (Z | hlZ=0 ) any better than predicting P (Z) – i.e.,
737
      ignoring the embedding entirely and simply mapping every input to the label distribution P (Z)
738   (Ravfogel et al., 2023). In this case, the probe g is trained on intervened embeddings ĥlZ=0 , in
739
      which case it can learn to map every such embedding to the label distribution P (Z), which yields
      superior performance relative to predicting the uniform distribution U(Z) in any case where the
740
      label distribution P (Z) is not perfectly uniform, as such a g would have an expected accuracy equal
741
      to the proportion of test instances with the most common label Z = zargmax (which would be greater
742
      than the accuracy k1 expected by defaulting to U(Z)).
743
744   The key technical distinction between these two use cases of nullifying interventions is whether or
745   not probes or models are trained or fine-tuned in the context of interventions. In the case of causal
746   probing, they are not – the (frozen) model M has no opportunity to recover the original value of
747
      Z = z following a nullifying intervention do(Z = 0), and this should be reflected by oracle probes.
      This is natural, given that the purpose of causal probing is to interpret the properties used by M in
748
      making a given prediction, not to test whether M can be trained to recover properties removed by
749
      interventions; and this is reflected by oracle probes o, which are never trained on intervened embed-
750   dings. In contrast, for concept removal, probes (or models) are trained on intervened embeddings,
751   and may learn to recover properties removed by interventions, meaning that – even in the worst
752   case where all information has been removed – it would at least be possible to learn to reproduce
753   the label distribution P (Z); but there is no reason to expect a model M or oracle probe o to do so,
754   given that they have never been trained on intervened embeddings. Thus, while we define the “goal”
755   distribution PZ∗ = U(Z) for measuring the completeness of nullifying interventions as being U(Z)
      rather than P (Z), this distribution would instead be PZ∗ = P (Z) in the case of concept removal.
