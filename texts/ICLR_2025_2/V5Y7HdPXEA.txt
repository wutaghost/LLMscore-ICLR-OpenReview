011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Unsupervised cross-domain segmentation addresses the challenge of label depen-
014            dence in cross-domain medical image segmentation. Yet, most existing methods
015            treat domain adaptation and segmentation as Two Separate Steps and primarily
016
               focus on global domain adaptation, lacking the ability to prioritize segmentation-
               specific information during domain adaptation. Additionally, for cross-domain
017
               segmentation, extracting domain-invariant feature representation remains an un-
018
               avoidable challenge. These challenges significantly reduce segmentation perfor-
019            mance. To this end, we propose a novel Unified Uncertain Dual-prompts cross-
020            domain Segmentation framework (UUDS) for unsupervised cross-domain medi-
021            cal image segmentation. Specifically, our UUDS forms a unified framework by
022            integrating domain adaptation and segmentation models, facilitating interaction
023            between the two tasks, addressing the challenge of emphasizing segmentation
024            semantics while domain adaptation. Additionally, UUDS creatively uses dual-
025            prompts, domain and segmentation prompts, to ensure that the model can learn
026            domain-invariant feature representations from the cross-domain space. Further-
027
               more, to further facilitate interaction between the two tasks, UUDS uses uncer-
               tainty estimation to dynamically compute segmentation labels, enabling direct su-
028
               pervision of the cross-domain adaptation process. Extensive experimental results
029
               on two representative unsupervised cross-modality medical image segmentation
030            demonstrate that UUDS outperforms state-of-the-art methods, highlighting its ef-
031            fectiveness in addressing domain shifts and marking a significant breakthrough in
032            domain adaptation.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Unsupervised cross-domain segmentation (Wu et al., 2024) is a promising approach to tackling the
038   challenge of domain shift in cross-modality medical image segmentation. Given the high costs asso-
039   ciated with pixel-level data collection and labeling from medical practitioners, it can reduce reliance
040   on manual labels. Existing unsupervised cross-domain segmentation methods (Zou et al., 2018; Lee
041   et al., 2024) attempt to overcome the domain shift between source and target data by aligning the
042   distribution of source and target data through unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA). Despite the
043   impressive performance achieved in various tasks (Li et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2019), these methods
044   treat domain adaptation and segmentation as Two Separate Steps, lacking the ability to establish
045
      the interaction between the two tasks for directly using segmentation information to guide domain
      adaptation. Moreover, these methods primarily focus on global domain adaptation without priori-
046
      tizing segmentation-aware feature representation while domain adaptation. This limitation fails to
047
      establish effective feedback between domain adaptation and segmentation and reduces the effective-
048   ness of methods focused on the feature distribution of segmentation regions, rendering the model
049   less sensitive to segmentation-specific features. Furthermore, extracting domain-invariant informa-
050   tion from cross-domain feature representation space remains challenging due to the entanglement
051   of content and domain information. This challenge is even more pronounced in the medical field,
052   where images often contain complex tissues and organs. Therefore, there is a critical urgent for a
053   segmentation-sensitive unified unsupervised cross-domain segmentation method for cross-modality
      medical image segmentation.

      Recently, large-scale Vision-Language Models (VLMs)(Yao et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023), particu-
055   larly the Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training (CLIP) model(Radford et al., 2021), have shown
056   promising performance in aligning cross-modality embedding spaces (Lai et al., 2023; Singha et al.,
057   2023; He et al., 2023; Jia et al., 2021). One of the most significant advantages of VLMs is they
058   align visual features from image to natural language sentences or phrases, rather than closed-end la-
059   bels. Encapsulated in natural language expressions, vision features can travel across domains while
060   maintaining the same semantic meanings. This important property makes the VLMs an ideal source
061   for obtaining domain-invariant prompts. Therefore, VLMs provide significant potential in achiev-
062   ing non-adversarial domain adaptation to address the well-knowledge challenges associated with
063
      adversarial learning-based UDA, where obtaining a stable and globally optimal GAN remains diffi-
      cult, especially in maintaining balance between the generator and discriminator (Sankaranarayanan
064
      et al., 2018). However, to the best of our knowledge, no efforts have been made to utilize VLMs for
065
      unsupervised cross-modality medical image segmentation due to the significant challenges involved.
066   Specifically, CLIP is trained on natural image-text pairs, resulting in a substantial domain gap be-
067   tween natural and medical images. This raises two key challenges: 1) how to transfer the rich knowl-
068   edge learned from natural image-text pairs to the medical imaging field remains an open question.
069   2) Medical images contain more complex anatomical structures and simple textual prompts like “a
070   photo of a [CT/MR]” are insufficient to accurately describe the intricate content of medical images.
071   Yet, medical image segmentation requires distinguishing between multiple tissues and organs for
072   precise localization and segmentation. Although VLMs hold great potential in the field of medical
073   image analysis, these challenges have left them largely unexplored for unsupervised cross-domain
074
      medical image segmentation.
075   In general, a straightforward way for transferring knowledge across domains involves utilizing the
076   text representations from VLMs as a foundation for further fine-tuning models (Qin et al., 2022).
077   However, due to the non-continuous nature of language hard prompts, directly tuning randomly
078   initialized embedding vectors may receive more robust performance and promise to converge to a
079   local optimum (Lester et al., 2021). While hard prompt embeddings from large pre-trained VLMs
080
      can effectively adapt to global-level domain information(Zhou et al., 2022), they tend to be less
      sensitive to detailed information(Jia et al., 2022). Therefore, improving the utilization of knowledge
081
      from VLMs for cross-modality medical image segmentation remains a critical area for exploration.
082
      Additionally, fully harnessing the potential of CLIP is another important avenue worth exploring.
083
084   To this end, we propose a novel Unified Uncertain Dual-prompts cross-domain Segmentation frame-
085   work (UUDS) for unsupervised cross-domain medical image segmentation by leveraging CLIP’s
086
      capability in aligning cross-modality embedding spaces. Specifically, UUDS creates a unified CLIP
      based framework where segmentation and domain adaptation are seamlessly combined for estab-
087
      lishing a segmentation-aware unsupervised cross-domain medical image segmentation framework.
088
      Furthermore, to overcome CLIP’s limitation in describing complex organs and tissues of medical
089   images while maximizing its potential, UUDS innovatively uses dual prompts, domain and segmen-
090   tation prompts, to learn domain and segmentation invariant feature representation. It simplifies the
091   challenges and complexities involved in prompt learning, addresses the difficulty of describing the
092   intricate content of medical images, and reduces the challenge in learning domain-invariant feature
093   representation from cross-domain feature representation space. Furthermore, UUDS introduces un-
094   certainty estimation to dynamically compute the label of segmentation for directly supervising the
095   cross-domain adaptation, ensuring the semantic information from unlabeled target images can di-
096   rectly supervise the process of domain adaptation and making the model sensitive to segmentation.
097
      It facilitates interaction between the two tasks and addresses the limitation that existing methods
      are unable to directly use the segmentation information for guiding domain adaptation. Experi-
098
      mental results from two public cross-modality medical image domain adaptation and segmentation
099
      tasks demonstrate that our UUDS outperforms state-of-the-art UDA methods and performs best on
100   cross-modality domain adaptation and segmentation.
101

127   2   R ELATED WORK
128
129   Unsupervised domain adaptation: Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA) plays an important
130   role in medical image analysis, offering a promising approach to address domain shift challenges in
131   medical image segmentation without necessitating labeled target data. Existing UDA methods (Yao
132   et al., 2022) try to bridge the shift between source and target domains by aligning image distribution
133   through adversarial learning. For instance, CycleGAN (Zhu et al., 2017) and CUT (Park et al., 2020)
134   transfers the source domain to the target domain by harmonizing image appearance. DDC (Tzeng
135   et al., 2014) prioritizes aligning feature distribution between the source and target domains. Dou
136
      et al. (Dou et al., 2019) proposed a method that aims to align feature spaces by using multiple
      scale feature information. Additionally, UDA methods such as CycADA (Hoffman et al., 2018)
137
      and SIFA (Chen et al., 2020) tackle domain shifts by addressing both image and feature distribu-
138
      tion discrepancies. Bui et al. (Bui et al., 2020) introduced an effective method for image-to-image
139   translation based on flow-based methods and deformation information. Dar et al. (Dar et al., 2019)
140   proposed a novel approach for image synthesis in multi-contrast MRI based on generative adversar-
141   ial network (GAN) architectures. Yurt et al. (Yurt et al., 2021) proposed a multi-stream generative
142   adversarial network (mustGAN), for enhancing image synthesis in multi-contrast MRI via a mixture
143   of multiple one-to-one streams and a joint many-to-one stream. Zhang et al. (Zhang et al., 2022)
144   proposed a switchable CycleGAN model for image synthesis between multi-contrast brain MRI im-
145   ages, which outperforms the original CycleGAN on cross-contrast MRI image synthesis. Zou et
146   al. (Zou et al., 2020) proposed a Dual-Scheme Fusion Network (DSFN) for unsupervised domain
147
      adaptation. DSFN builds both source-to-target and target-to-source connections to help reduce the
      domain gap to improve the network performance further. DAR-Net (Yao et al., 2022) integrated a 2D
148
      style transfer network with a 3D segmentation network to address the complexities of 3D medical
149
      images.
150
151   Vision-Language Mode: Vision-language models (VLMs) (Radford et al., 2021; He et al., 2020;
152   Devlin, 2018) have made significant progress across various domains. Specifically, VLMs cap-
153
      ture the correlation between vision and language through various cross-modal objectives, such
      as image-text contrastive learning, masked cross-modal modeling, image-to-text generation, and
154
      image-text/region-word matching. Early VLMs (Jia et al., 2021) typically employed a single pre-
155
      training objective. For example, different single-modal objectives have been explored to fully uti-
156   lize the potential of each modality. For the image modality, this includes masked image modeling,
157   while for the text modality, masked language modeling is employed. More recent VLMs (Yao
158   et al., 2021) introduce multiple objectives (e.g., contrastive, alignment, and generative objectives) to
159   leverage their synergy, resulting in more robust models and improved performance on downstream
160   tasks. Yet, adapting VLMs to the medical domain presents significant challenges, largely due to
161   domain-specific obstacles such as the use of proprietary datasets, the need for fine-grained medical
      knowledge, and the inherent difficulty in generalizing across diverse medical domains and tasks.

201   3.1           D UAL - PROMPTS LEANING
202
203   To achieve the domain adaptation from target domain to source domain, UUDS leverages text em-
204   beddings encoded by CLIP to bridge the cross-modality embedding spaces. Yet, as a VLM trained
205   on image-level alignment tasks, CLIP-based models are good at capturing the global-level domain
206   style features while showing insufficient capability to fully capture the region-level content of medi-
207
      cal images due to the complexity of anatomical structures and the intricate details inherent in medical
      imaging. To this end, our UUDS proposes a dual-prompt system to capture global and region-level
208
      information in a disentangled manner by leveraging both a hard prompt and a soft prompt. The do-
209
      main prompt is a hard prompt encoded by CLIP text encoder, focusing on learning domain-invariant
210   features for domain-adaptation tasks, covered in more detail next in subsection 3.1.1. And the soft
211   prompt is randomly initialized like CoOP (Zhou et al., 2022), using a few tunable continuous vectors
212   to capture region level or lesion features vital to segmentation tasks. The reason that we choose soft
213   prompts rather than hard prompts is due to the shape and characteristics of lesions and tumors being
214   hard to describe in natural language. In medical image analysis, many lesions and abnormalities are
215   uneven and vary in shape. Therefore, applying continuous and tunable soft prompts are better for
      this situation.

260   3.1.2    S EGMENTATION PROMPT FOR SEMANTIC - AWARE SEGMENTATION
261
262   The segmentation prompt T s captures the semantics of the segmented object, enabling the extraction
263   of cross-domain invariant feature representations. Since the segmentation object is difficult to define
264   explicitly for specific tasks, we use a trainable prompt T s to learn such anatomical characteristics.
265   We follow the setting in (Zhou et al., 2022) to initialize our segmentation prompts as a combination
266
      of continuous vectors and class names. Instead of using “a photo of a” as the context, we introduce

267
      embeddings in Clip Text encoder. For our tasks, the class names are typically segmentation target.

342   4.1   DATASET AND I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS

      1) BraTS Dataset: The multi-modal Brain Tumor Segmentation (BraTS) challenge 2020
344
      dataset (Menze et al., 2014) includes spatially aligned MRI scans from 369 patients, covering four
345
      modalities (T1, ceT1, T2, and FLAIR) with a resolution of 1.0 mm3 and an in-plane size of 240 ×
346   240 pixels. Since the ground truth for the official validation and testing sets is not publicly available,
347   we conducted our experiments using the official training set. Following previous works (Wu et al.,
348   2024), in our unsupervised cross-modality segmentation task, we also focused on segmenting the
349   whole tumor using T2 and FLAIR images. We treated images from one modality of 143 patients as
350   the source domain and images from the other modality of another 143 patients as the target domain,
351   in each direction. We used 42 images (21 for each modality) for validation and 41 images in the
352   target domain for testing. In the preprocessing step, we truncate the pixel value by the 5%, 95%
353   percentage of min-max value and normalized the intensity of each modality to [-1, 1].
354   2) Vestibular Schwannoma Segmentation Dataset: Vestibular Schwannoma (VS) segmentation
355   dataset (Shapey et al., 2021) includes 3D MRI images from 242 patients. Each patient was scanned
356   using contrast-enhanced T1-weighted (ceT1) and high-resolution T2-weighted (hrT2) MRI, with
357   an in-plane resolution of approximately 0.4 mm × 0.4 mm, an in-plane size of 512 × 512, and
358   a slice thickness of 1.5 mm. These two modalities were used for bidirectional adaptation, where
359   ceT1 and hrT2 served as the source and target domains, respectively. Following the setup of the
360
      Cross-modality Domain Adaptation Challenge 2021 (Shapey et al., 2021), the validation set from
      the target domain was used to tune hyperparameters, and the testing set was reserved solely for the
361
      final inference. For data preprocessing, each image was normalized based on its intensity mean and
362
      standard deviation. Following previous works (Wu et al., 2024), the dataset was randomly split into
363   200 patients for training, 14 patients for validation, and 28 patients for testing. For the training set,
364   images from one modality of 100 patients were used as the source domain, while images from the
365   other modality of the remaining 100 patients were used as the target domain.
366
      3) Implementation Details: The ResNet version of CLIP is chosen as the backbone, and all param-
367
      eters, including CLIP text encoder and image encoder, are fine-tuned during training. The source
368
      and target decoders have the same structure and are constructed by Resblock and upsample layers,
369   which gradually upsample the features until they are the same size as the input image. The frame-
370   work is implemented on PyTorch and utilizes four L40s GPUs with 46 GB of memory each. The
371   Adam optimizer was used for optimization, with the learning rate set to 1e-4 and weight decay to
372   0.01. The weight coefficients are set as α = 1.0, β = 1.0, γ = 1.0, λ = 1.0. Remarkably, all
373   datasets’ domain prompt input is set as “A photo of a source domain” while learning.

529   5   C ONCLUSION
530
531   As the first end-to-end framework that unifies segmentation and domain adaptation, our experiments
532   validate the hypothesis that feedback from the segmentation model is essential in the domain adap-
533   tation process. We innovatively leverage the cross-domain invariance of vision-language models
534   (VLMs) to bridge the gap between the two domains and employ a dual prompts system to simulta-
535   neously learn domain-invariant style and content features. Extensive experiments demonstrate the
536   effectiveness of our dual-prompts method. To address the challenge of missing labels in the target
537   domain, we introduce uncertainty estimation, which further enhances the stability of our segmen-
538
      tation results. We achieved state-of-the-art performance on multiple public datasets, and ablation
      studies confirm the importance of each module. We hope our work will inspire future research to
539
      recognize that domain adaptation and segmentation can be unified within a single framework.
