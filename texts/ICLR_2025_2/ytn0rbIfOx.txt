007                                              A BSTRACT
008
009            Recent advancements in machine learning have created a demand for automated
010            systems that enable efficient development and deployment of machine learning
011            applications. Traditional Automated Machine Learning (AutoML) approaches
012            often rely on fixed pipeline structures, which limit adaptability to diverse task
013            complexities. In this paper, we introduce a novel formulation of AutoML as a
014
               variable-length optimization problem, allowing for dynamic adjustment of model
               architectures based on task requirements. To effectively navigate the expanded
015
               search space of variable-length models, we employ the Tree of Thoughts (ToT)
016
               method combined with Large Language Models (LLMs). This framework uti-
017            lizes a sequential decision-making process, allowing models to be incrementally
018            constructed by evaluating prior outcomes. Additionally, LLMs automatically gen-
019            erate the code corresponding to each decision, transforming model configurations
020            into executable pipelines and reducing manual intervention. Our approach en-
021            hances efficiency by focusing on promising pathways and improves transparency
022            by explicitly showcasing how each decision contributes to the overall optimiza-
023            tion. Experiments conducted on diverse datasets, including OpenML and clinical
024            tasks, demonstrate that our method outperforms traditional AutoML systems, de-
025
               livering superior model performance and better adaptability across different task
               complexities.

      1. I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      The recent substantial progress in machine learning (ML) and deep learning has created a signifi-
031   cant demand for hands-free systems that enable both developers and ML novices to efficiently build
032   and deploy machine learning applications (Baratchi et al., 2024). Since different datasets often re-
033   quire unique ML pipelines, this demand has driven the development of automated machine learning
034   (AutoML) (Santu et al., 2022). AutoML aims to streamline and automate the process of designing,
035   training, and optimizing machine learning models, effectively reducing the need for deep domain
036   expertise and manual tuning. Its applications span various domains, from healthcare (Waring et al.,
037   2020), biology (Valeri et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024), and drug discovery (Turon et al., 2023), under-
038   scoring its critical role in unlocking the full potential of machine learning.
039   Current mainstream AutoML systems, like Auto-WEKA (Thornton et al., 2013), H2O (LeDell &
040   Poirier, 2020), and Auto-sklearn (Feurer et al., 2019), typically rely on fixed pipeline structures,
041   as shown in Figure 1(a). These pipelines follow a linear sequence of steps—data cleaning, fea-
042   ture transformation, feature selection, and modeling—where each step selects an operation from a
043   predefined candidate pool. The search space is static, with model configurations limited by fixed
044   parameters like depth, number of nodes, and layers. This approach assumes that tasks share similar
045
      complexity levels, allowing uniform models to be applied across different tasks. However, this fixed-
      structure paradigm may lead to inferior pipeline performances for complex datasets—characterized
046
      by varying data distributions, objectives, and scales, for example, multiple preprocessing steps or
047
      feature trnasformation (Hollmann et al., 2023; Mumuni & Mumuni, 2024). Optimal configurations
048   often need structural flexibility and variable complexity to meet specific task demands. Fixed-length
049   models, by assuming uniform task complexity, may not fully account for the varying needs of both
050   simple and complex tasks. Efforts to improve flexibility, such as Olson & Moore’s introduction of a
051   dataset duplicator, allow for parallel processing paths within pipelines. These paths can be merged
052   using the feature union operator, which combines multiple datasets or processing paths. While this
053   enhances flexibility, the overall pipeline depth and complexity are still constrained by predefined
      limits, restricting the adaptability needed for more diverse or demanding tasks.

      To better adapt the model to a variety of tasks, we propose formulating the AutoML problem as a
077
      variable-length optimization problem, as shown in Figure 1 (b). Unlike fixed structures, this ap-
078   proach allows models to dynamically adjust to the specific demands of each task, whether simple
079   or complex. Flexibility is critical in optimization since overly complex models can lead to overfit-
080   ting in simple tasks, while insufficiently complex models may underfit more complex tasks (Bejani
081   & Ghatee, 2021; Bian & Priyadarshi, 2024). Thus, the model’s structure should be adaptable, al-
082   lowing simpler tasks to be handled with shallower models, and complex tasks with deeper, more
083   sophisticated networks.
084   Additionally, traditional methods that explore the entire framework and hyperparameter space si-
085   multaneously are inefficient due to the sheer size of the search space, which makes exhaustive ex-
086   ploration computationally prohibitive. A sequential decision-making approach (Barto et al., 1989) is
087   better suited to this problem, as it allows models to be constructed step by step, dynamically adjust-
088   ing complexity in response to task demands (Jaâfra et al., 2019). Furthermore, large language models
089   (LLMs), equipped with vast knowledge across various domains (Brown et al., 2020), possess strong
090   reasoning (Plaat et al., 2024), decision-making (Harte et al., 2023), and coding capabilities (Wang
091   et al., 2024) . This allows them to understand task requirements and tailor model structures accord-
092
      ingly, making them ideal for navigating the expanded search space of variable-length models.
093   To efficiently solve this variable-length optimization problem, we employ the Tree of Thoughts (ToT)
094   method using LLMs (Yao et al., 2023). The ToT method handles model structure selection through a
095   flexible sequential decision-making process, where the model is incrementally constructed by eval-
096   uating the outcomes of each decision. This process enhances efficiency by focusing on promising
097   pathways and pruning less viable ones early in the search space, reducing unnecessary computa-
098
      tion. Moreover, this method guarantees transparency by explicitly demonstrating how each decision
      contributes to the model’s overall optimization, as illustrated in Figure 2(a). Starting from basic
099
      transformations like Mean Imputation and Log Transformation, the ToT method refines the model
100
      step by step, selecting the most impactful preprocessing steps and tuning key model parameters. Fur-
101   thermore, LLMs automatically generate code for each decision, transforming model configurations
102   into executable pipelines, reducing manual intervention and streamlining the process.
103
104
      We conducted experiments on diverse datasets, including OpenML and clinical tasks, to evaluate the
      efficacy of our approach. The results demonstrate that our method achieves superior model perfor-
105
      mance, outperforming traditional AutoML methods in terms of accuracy and adaptability to different
106
      task complexities, proving the flexibility, transparency, and effectiveness of the ToT method.

138   2. P ROBLEM D EFINITION
139
140   In this work, we formulate the AutoML process as a variable-length optimization problem. The
141   objective is to identify the optimal sequence of operations that maximizes the model’s performance.
142   Let n denote the maximum number of operations available in a given AutoML pipeline. The op-
143   timization problem can be defined as minimizing an objective function f (x1 , x2 , . . . , xn−1 , xn ),
144   where each xi represents a specific operation selected at stage i of the pipeline.

190   3.1 G ENERATING AUTO ML P IPELINES
191
192   The first step in our method involves generating candidate AutoML pipelines incrementally, using a
193
      ToT approach with the help of LLMs. To guide the LLM effectively, we prepare a comprehensive
      prompt that includes the following components:
194
195   Objective: Assist in generating an AutoML pipeline for the dataset dataset. The objective is to
196   recommend operations step-by-step for constructing an AutoML pipeline, based on the given search
197   space, constraints, and requirements. These recommendations should cover data preprocessing,
198   feature transformation, feature selection, model selection, and model tuning, while adhering to the
199
      provided constraints.
200   Task Information: Provide details on the training dataset, including the number of samples and
201   features. Specify the feature types and indicate the percentage of missing values. Clarify whether
202   the task is a classification or regression problem.
203   Search Space: Outline the available methods for each stage of the AutoML process, including
204   options for preprocessing, transformation, selection, and model tuning.
205
206
      Constraints and Requirements: (1) The first step in the pipeline must be a data processing method.
      (2) Feature transformation and feature selection must be completed before proceeding to model
207
      selection and model tuning. (3) Model selection and model tuning must occur before the final model
208
      training. (4) The total number of steps in the pipeline must not exceed max step.
209
210   To enhance the quality of the generated pipelines, we provide few-shot demonstrations—example
211   tasks with corresponding pipelines. These examples help the LLM understand the expected format
212
      and components of effective pipelines.
213   The prompt also includes a Step-by-Step Reasoning section to encourage the LLM to perform a
214   chain-of-thought analysis: (1) Understanding the Dataset: Analyze the dataset by examining its
215   characteristics, including feature types and missing values. (2) Identifying Challenges: Determine
      the primary challenges based on the dataset’s characteristics and the task type (classification or

      regression). (3) Applying Constraints: Ensure that the operations adhere to the specified constraints
217   and maintain the required sequence.
218
219   Finally, the LLM is asked to Recommend the Next Step for the current pipeline, ensuring that each
220
      new operation logically extends from the previous ones while aligning with the dataset’s specific
      characteristics and needs.

      3.2 I MPLEMENTING P YTHON P ROGRAMS FROM P IPELINE
224   After generating the candidate pipelines, we proceed to implement them by prompting the LLM to
225   generate corresponding Python code. Each pipeline is translated into a Python program capable of
226   processing the dataset and producing performance metrics. We test each program on the training
227   data to evaluate its performance. If a program fails to execute correctly or does not handle all
228   training examples successfully, we engage in an iterative refinement process: (1) Error Analysis:
229   Examine the execution results, including error messages and discrepancies between expected and
230   actual outputs. (2) Prompting for Revisions: Prompt the LLM to revise the program based on
231   the identified issues, providing specific feedback. (3) Re-testing: Test the revised program on the
232
      training data. This iterative process continues until the program executes successfully or a predefined
      number of iterations is reached. This approach leverages established techniques in code repair and
233
      debugging (Rahman et al., 2021). If, after several iterations, the program still does not perform
234
      satisfactorily, we manually coding the pipelines.
235

      3.3 P ERFORMANCE E VALUATION
237
238   To evaluate the effectiveness of each operation sequence in the pipelines—especially when specific
239   model recommendations are absent—we employ a heuristic-based approach. This method ensures
240   a robust assessment by introducing diversity and thorough exploration of potential models. The
241   evaluation process involves the following steps: (1) Model Selection: Randomly select n models
242   from a predefined model library. This diversity allows us to cover a broad range of models that
243   might be suitable for the dataset. (2) Hyperparameter Tuning: Each selected model undergoes
244
      hyperparameter tuning to identify the optimal configuration. Techniques such as grid search or
      random search are used to explore various parameter settings and enhance model performance. (3)
245
      Performance Assessment: Assess the performance of each model using relevant metrics—accuracy
246
      for classification tasks or mean squared error for regression tasks. (4) Evaluation Score: Select
247   the highest performance score among the models, and use this score as the evaluation value for the
248   current operation sequence. Mathematically, the evaluation function f (x) for an operation sequence
249   x is defined as:

314

491   6. C ONCLUSION
492
493   In this paper, we formulated AutoML as a variable-length optimization problem, enabling dynamic
494   adjustment of model architectures based on task complexity. Our approach, utilizing the tree of
495
      thoughts method in combination with LLMs, provides a flexible, efficient, and transparent explo-
      ration of the model space, adapting better to varying task demands. Experimental results across di-
496
      verse datasets, including OpenML and clinical tasks, show that our method outperforms traditional
497
      fixed-structure AutoML systems. Particularly, the ToT approach demonstrates superior performance
498   in both simple and complex tasks, consistently achieving higher ROC AUC scores across a wide
499   range of tasks. Additionally, the transparency of the ToT method also allows for better interpretabil-
500   ity, giving insights into how each decision impacts overall optimization. These advantages make
501   the proposed method a powerful tool for model enhancement and adaptation, especially in domains
502   where task complexity varies significantly. Future work will focus on expanding this framework to
503   support more diverse machine learning tasks and applications, pushing the boundaries of AutoML

      A. L ITERATURE R EVIEW
649
650
651   A.1 AUTO ML
652
653
      Automated Machine Learning (AutoML) focuses on automating key stages of the machine learning
654   pipeline, including data preparation, feature engineering, and hyperparameter optimization (War-
655   ing et al., 2020). Early AutoML frameworks such as Auto-WEKA (Thornton et al., 2013) and
656   Auto-Sklearn (Feurer et al., 2019) focused on optimizing traditional machine learning pipelines,
657   particularly for hyperparameter tuning. Recent tools like Auto-PyTorch (Zimmer et al., 2021) and
658   AutoKeras (Jin et al., 2023) have expanded the scope to include neural architecture search (NAS)
659   for deep learning models. Comprehensive AutoML solutions, such as Microsoft’s NNI toolkit1 and
660   Vega, offer end-to-end pipelines, including data augmentation, NAS, model compression, and hy-
661   perparameter optimization. Google’s AutoML suite2 simplifies the process but still requires some
662
      level of user intervention. Despite these advancements, most current AutoML systems rely on fixed
      pipeline structures and predefined search spaces, limiting their flexibility to adapt to varying task
663
      complexities. Our method addresses this limitation by formulating AutoML as a variable-length
664
      optimization problem. This approach allows dynamic adjustment of model complexity, enabling
665   flexible pipelines that better align with the complexity of individual tasks, in contrast to existing
666   methods that operate within static frameworks.
667
668
669
      A.2 LLM S FOR AUTO ML
670
671
672   LLMs have shown significant potential in enhancing machine learning tasks by autonomously de-
673   composing and executing complex operations. They are increasingly recognized for their ability to
674
      provide convenient, comprehensive, and reliable decision-making across various applications. Sev-
      eral studies have directly explored GPT’s capabilities in AutoML tasks such as feature engineering
675
      and NAS. For instance, GENIUS (Zheng et al., 2023) employs GPT-4 as a black-box optimizer
676
      to tackle NAS through an iterative refinement process. EvoPrompting (Chen et al., 2023) integrates
677   LLMs as adaptive operators within an evolutionary NAS algorithm. Viswanathan et al. (Viswanathan
678   et al., 2023) apply GPT-3.5 to achieve AutoML for specialized NLP models. In the realm of hyper-
679   parameter optimization (HPO), AutoMLGPT (Zhang et al., 2023) leverages LLMs to conduct HPO
680   by iteratively prompting with data and model cards, mimicking model training via LLMs. How-
681   ever, this approach does not involve actual model training on real machines; instead, it relies on the
682   zero-shot and few-shot learning capabilities of GPT models. Similarly, MLcopilot (Zhang et al.,
683   2024) uses LLMs, informed by past experiences and knowledge, to predict optimal hyperparameter
684   settings in a categorized manner. CAAFE (Hollmann et al., 2023) employs LLMs for automated
685
      feature engineering in tabular data, generating semantically meaningful features.
686   While these approaches demonstrate the potential of LLMs in automating machine learning tasks,
687   they often rely on zero-shot and few-shot learning without incorporating iterative refinement based
688   on real training performance. In contrast, our approach uses LLMs as dynamic agents capable
689   of sequential decision-making within the AutoML framework. Our method not only automates
690   complex ML operations but also optimizes them based on real training outcomes, enhancing the
691
      reliability and effectiveness of AutoML systems.
