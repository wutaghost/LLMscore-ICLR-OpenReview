009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            A prior represents a set of beliefs or assumptions about a system, aiding inference
012            and decision-making. In this work, we introduce the challenge of unsupervised
013            prior learning in pose estimation, where AI models learn pose priors of animate
014            objects from videos in a self-supervised manner. These videos present objects
015            performing various actions, providing crucial information about their keypoints
016
               and connectivity. While priors are effective in pose estimation, acquiring them
               can be difficult. We propose a novel method, named Pose Prior Learner (PPL),
017
               to learn general pose priors applicable to any object category. PPL uses a
018
               hierarchical memory to store compositional parts of prototypical poses, from
019            which we distill a general pose prior. This prior enhances pose estimation
020            accuracy through template transformation and image reconstruction. PPL learns
021            meaningful pose priors without any additional human annotations or interventions,
022            outperforming competitive baselines on both human and animal pose estimation
023            datasets. Notably, our experimental results reveal the effectiveness of PPL using
024            learnt priors for pose estimation on occluded images. Through iterative inference,
025            PPL leverages priors to refine estimated poses, regressing them to any prototypical
026            poses stored in memory. Our code, model, and data will be publicly available.

053   Priors represent beliefs or assumptions about a system or the characteristics of a concept. They
      are widely used in statistical inference (Lindley, 1961), cognitive science (Schad et al., 2021), and

054
      machine learning (Diligenti et al., 2017; Gülçehre & Bengio, 2016). This pre-existing knowledge
055   is essential for guiding the inference process, enabling AI models to make robust predictions in
056   uncertain or ambiguous situations (Thiruvenkadam et al., 2008; Sung et al., 2015; Liang et al.,
057   2024). The objective of our work is to enhance our understanding of priors in AI models and
058   offer preliminary answers to the three key intelligence questions: (1) How do we acquire priors in
059   the first place? (2) Can we learn them from input data in a self-supervised manner? (3) Can we
060   evaluate and enhance the quality of the priors? To tackle these questions, we first introduce the
061   challenge of unsupervised prior learning in the context of pose estimation from videos. See Figure
062   1 for the schematic illustration of the challenge. Pose estimation is a classical computer vision
063
      task that identifies the structure of objects in images and videos by detecting keypoints. A pose prior
      summarizes the common characteristics shared by a variety of poses. It encapsulates the expectation
064
      of the keypoint configurations and the connectivity between keypoints.
065
066   In parallel to our challenge of unsupervised prior learning from videos for pose estimation,
067   unsupervised pose estimation leverages the abundant, unannotated visual information available in
068   large image and video datasets to extract pose information (Hu & Ahuja, 2021; Sommer et al., 2024;
069
      Chen et al., 2019; He et al., 2022a; Schmidtke et al., 2021). The use of pose priors can provide
      valuable guidance in this process. We categorize the existing works into two groups: those that
070
      incorporate priors and those that operate without them.
071
072   Recent approaches (He et al., 2022a; Sun et al., 2022; 2023) attempt to predict keypoints
073   from images, construct object structure representations using these keypoints, and learn effective
074   structural information through image reconstruction. However, without pose priors, these methods
075
      can be easily disrupted by background information, causing the model to occasionally learn the
      background structure instead of the foreground object. While using video streams with static
076
      backgrounds helps alleviate this issue (Schmidtke et al., 2021; Yoo & Russakovsky, 2023), these
077
      methods remain vulnerable to producing inaccurate keypoints. This vulnerability arises from
078   the lack of supplementary knowledge that could constrain both keypoint localization and the
079   connectivity among keypoints.
080
081
      In contrast, some methods (Schmidtke et al., 2021; Yoo & Russakovsky, 2023) utilize prior
      knowledge of a category’s pose to guide the pose estimation of individuals within that category.
082
      Conceptually, each category is expected to exhibit a generalized and distinctive pose prior that
083
      reflects characteristics such as shape, size, and structure. Individual poses should be seen
084   as geometric transformations of this category-specific pose prior. As a result, employing a
085   category-specific pose prior aids in guiding and regularizing the learning of poses. However,
086   acquiring comprehensive general pose priors is highly challenging, as it necessitates extensive
087   human annotations and domain knowledge.
088
      Loosely inspired by how humans develop a universal prior representation of an object category by
089
      observing the movements of individual object instances in videos and subsequently using these
090   priors to estimate individual poses, we propose a new method called the Pose Prior Learner
091   (PPL). PPL is designed to effectively learn a meaningful pose prior for any object category. It
092   utilizes a hierarchical memory to store a finite set of prototypical poses and extract a general pose
093   prior from them. Initially, both the hierarchical memory and the universal prior representation
094   are randomly initialized but learnable parameters. During training, effective pose learning is
095   ensured through image reconstruction. As training progresses, the hierarchical memory retains and
096   aggregates multiple accurate prototypical poses, thereby contributing to a more precise pose prior
097   and enhancing the model’s ability to estimate poses.
098   Upon completing the training, we obtain a model that enables accurate pose estimation, a pose prior
099   that encapsulates the general features of a category, and a hierarchical memory that stores diverse
100   prototypical poses for that category. We evaluate the effectiveness of our PPL across several human
101   and dog pose estimation benchmarks. We visualize their pose priors to further interpret what our
102   approach has learned. Additionally, we introduce an iterative inference strategy to estimate the
103   poses of objects in occluded scenes using the trained hierarchical memory and the pose prior. Our
104
      contributions are highlighted below:
105   1. We introduce the challenge of unsupervised prior learning in the context of pose estimation. We
106   also establish evaluation metrics and benchmarks to assess the quality of the learned priors.

121   2   R ELATED W ORKS
122
123   Unsupervised Pose Estimation without Priors. Numerous methods without priors have been
124   proposed to detect keypoints from images or videos, which are then used to reconstruct images
125   for supervision (Li et al., 2021; Geng et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2018; Sun et al., 2022; Thewlis
126   et al., 2017; Jakab et al., 2020). For example, AutoLink (He et al., 2022a) extracts keypoints from
127   the image and estimates the strength of the links between pairs of keypoints. It then combines these
128   keypoints with the link heatmap to reconstruct the randomly masked image. Similarly, BKind (Sun
129
      et al., 2022; 2023) uses keypoints extracted from two video frames to reconstruct the pixel-level
      differences between these two frames. In these methods, keypoints are directly predicted from the
130
      image and supervised solely by image reconstruction, leading to potential detection of keypoints
131
      in background regions with complex textures. Moreover, the absence of constraints on keypoint
132   configuration and connectivity also contributes to the unreliability of their methods. In contrast, our
133   PPL utilizes the learned pose prior as a constraint to mitigate these issues.
134
135
      Unsupervised Pose Estimation Incorporating Priors. Several methods utilize prior knowledge to
      guide the pose estimation (Chen & Dou, 2021; Shi et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022; Schmidtke et al.,
136
      2021; Yoo & Russakovsky, 2023). Among these methods, Shape Template Transforming (STT)
137
      (Schmidtke et al., 2021) applies affine transformations to a predefined pose prior, aligning it with
138   the estimated pose from a video frame. By incorporating an additional frame from the same video
139   to provide background information, an image reconstruction loss supervises the pose estimation
140   process. The pose prior effectively guides pose estimation by constraining the shape of the human
141   pose and the connectivity between body parts. However, pose priors are often difficult to obtain,
142   requiring costly human annotations. Moreover, HPE (Yoo & Russakovsky, 2023) has shown that
143   predefined pose priors are not always optimal, and tuning the shape of the prior can sometimes
144   improve performance. Unlike these methods, our approach learns the prior directly from input
145   videos, and models with our learned priors even outperform those using human-defined priors.
146   Compositional Memory Architectures. Compositional memory has been widely used in many
147   computer vision tasks, such as question answering (Seong et al., 2021), object segmentation (Seong
148   et al., 2021), and sence graph generation (Deng et al., 2022). In pose estimation, PCT (Geng
149   et al., 2023) decomposes a human pose into discrete tokens, where each token connects several
150   interdependent joints and characterizes a sub-structure of the entire human pose. This approach is
151   highly effective for decomposing and reconstructing poses, providing robust pose representations
152
      while significantly reducing computation and storage costs. However, PCT encodes all tokens into
      the same embedding space, making it difficult to aggregate semantic tokens that represent different
153
      sub-structures. In contrast, our PPL employs a compositional hierarchical memory, which parses
154
      poses into memory banks. Each memory bank explicitly contains multiple tokens encoding the
155   variations of each sub-structure of a pose. This facilitates the aggregation of poses into a universal
156   pose prior.

      Mathematically, We represent the topology for an object as a graph connecting keypoints with shared
185   link weights, also known as connectivity. For any category, its general pose prior V is defined as V =
186   (T, W ), where T represents its keypoint prior and W denotes the connectivity prior. Specifically,
187   T consists of N keypoints: T = [P1 , P2 , ..., PN ], where Pi ∈ [−1, 1] × [−1, 1] is the normalized
188   pixel coordinates. W is a 2D matrix of size N × N , where each entry wij in the matrix represents
189   the connectivity probability between two keypoints Pi and Pj . For instance, in the case of humans,
190   the hand is connected to the torso via an arm; thus, the connectivity probability between these two
191   parts should be higher than the connectivity probability between a hand and a foot. We randomly
192   initialize the connectivity prior W with positive values. The keypoint prior T is decoded from a
193
      hierarchical memory M storing compositional parts of prototypical poses.
194   During training, PPL inputs a pair of frames I and Iref with the same sizes of H × W × 3 where
195   H and W are the height and the width of each video frame. The reference frame Iref is a randomly
196   selected frame from the same video, containing the same person as I but in a different pose. The aim
197   of PPL is to learn to correctly predict the keypoints T ′ and their connectivity on I. Ideally, if PPL
198   makes perfect predictions of the pose on I, by combining it with the background information from
199
      Iref , the reconstructed image Irecon should match I exactly. Next, we introduce how we estimate T ′
      on I using the keypoint prior T .

      3.2   C ONNECTING KEYPOINTS BASED ON THE CONNECTIVITY PRIOR
217
218   The connectivity of keypoints in objects is often fixed and rigid; for example, human arms maintain a
219   relatively constant length, with a hand always connected to the torso via an arm and never connected
220   to a foot. This rigidity in connectivity serves as a constraint, aiding in the regularization of pose
221   estimation. In this section, we introduce the connectivity prior and explain how it can be used to
222   regularize the connectivity strength between any pair of estimated keypoints in T ′ on I.
223   Similar to AutoLink (He et al., 2022a), PPL connects any two keypoints Pi′ and Pj′ in T ′ to obtain
224   differentiable link heatmap Si,j ∈ RH×W . Intuitively, each 2D link heatmap represents a probability
225   density map, where the pixel values along the link between two points are high, while other areas
226   are assigned values close to zero. For any point Pi′ , its strongest connectivity to any of the other
227   points in T ′ is activated on the combined link heatmap S ∈ RH×W via a max pooling operation over
228   all the N × N link heatmaps:
229                                                  N ×N
230                                             S = max (wi,j Si,j ),                                        (3)
                                                       i,j

232   where wi,j in the connectivity prior W modulates the link heatmap Si,j based on whether the two
233   keypoints Pi′ and Pj′ are physically connected. Ideally, if PPL correctly estimates the probability of
234
      physical links for an object category, Si,j will receive higher connectivity values, thereby activating
      the locations linking these two keypoints on the combined link map S.
235
236   Given the combined link map S and the reference frame Iref , PPL can reconstruct the image
237   I. Iref shares the same background as I but features a different foreground pose, thereby
238   offering background information for reconstruction. Meanwhile, S provides the connectivity details
239   among all the estimated keypoints. This encourages PPL to learn accurate pose estimations while
240
      minimizing the influence of background noise. Therefore, we concatenate S and Iref and feed them
      into a 2D-CNN to perform the image reconstruction Irecon , where Irecon = ϕdec (Iref , S).

244   M is a hierarchical memory storing compositional representations of prototypical poses. It contains
245   m memory banks {b1 , b2 , ..., bm } of k learnable tokens with d randomly initialized parameters per

247   storing multiple prototypical poses, memory helps in aggregating these poses, which aids in creating
248   a more robust and comprehensive prior that captures variations within an object category. (2) M
249   can assist in organizing information hierarchically, making it easier for PPL to retrieve relevant
250   prototypical poses when making predictions in uncertain or ambugious scenarios, such as occlusion.
251   and (3) By leveraging M , PPL can refine its predictions iteratively, using stored poses to adjust its
252
      outputs based on previously learned pose representations. Next, we introduce how M is structured.

462   4.2   A BLATION S TUDIES
463
464   Ablation on Prior Variants. Here, we investigate how different initializations of connectivity and
465
      keypoint priors affect pose estimation and assess whether further refining these priors enhances
      performance. From Table 3, we obtain several key insights: (1) Models with frozen, human-defined
466
      priors (Column 4) perform worse than our PPL, indicating that PPL learns more representative priors
467
      than those predefined by humans. (2) Refining pre-defined keypoint and connectivity priors (Column
468   1) outperforms our default PPL, suggesting that PPL can enhance models with human-defined
469   priors through refinement. (3) Interestingly, randomly initializing either keypoint or connectivity
470   priors, followed by refinement during training (Columns 5-9), yields comparable performance to
471   models with human-defined priors. This suggests that human-defined priors may not be necessary
472   for effective pose estimation. (4) Surprisingly, freezing randomly initialized keypoint priors also
473   results in reasonable pose estimation accuracy, though it is still lower than PPL’s default performance
474   (Columns 7 and 9). (5) In contrast to (4), freezing random connectivity priors prevents the
475   model from converging, implying that connectivity priors play a more critical role in guiding pose
476
      estimations than keypoint priors.
477   Ablation on Number & Dimension of Tokens in Each Memory Bank. In our hierarchical
478   memory, we used 34 memory banks. Here, we analyze the impact of the number of tokens per
479   memory bank and the dimension of each token on PPL’s pose estimation performance. From
480   Figure A1 in Appendix A3, we observed that PPL remains robust across different token counts
481   and dimensions, although performance slightly improves with more tokens of higher dimensions.
482
      As a result, we fixed 16 tokens per memory bank, each with a dimension of 512, for all experiments.
483   Ablation on Number of Keypoints. We varied the number of keypoints in the pose priors from 4 to
484   32. The results in Figure A1 in Appendix A3 show that pose estimation accuracy improves as the
485   number of keypoints in the prior increases. However, using 32 keypoints offers limited improvement
      compared to PPL with 16 keypoints for human pose estimation.

505   4.3   P OSE E STIMATION IN O CCLUSION S CENE
506
507   To verify the robustness of PPL in occluded scenes, we divide the image into 32 × 32 patches
508   and apply two masking techniques: RandomMasking and CenterMasking. In RandomMasking, we
509   randomly mask a certain proportion of image patches, with the proportion ranging from 0.1 to 0.4.
510   In CenterMasking, we mask only the center region of the image, gradually increasing the masking
511   size from 4 × 4 to 12 × 12 patches.
512   We explore the effect of occluded areas on PPL. From Figure 6 and Figure 7, we observe that
513   at iteration 0, as the occluded areas increase, overall performance declines with larger occlusions.
514   However, with our iterative inference strategy, PPL effectively infers the missing parts of the poses
515   by utilizing prototypical poses stored in hierarchical memory and the learned priors. Notably, it
516   restores partially occluded poses to reasonably complete full-body poses, leading to a lower L2
517   error, comparable to those without occlusion. This effect is more pronounced when the occlusion
518   area is small or medium.
519   Visualization of Pose Estimation with Occlusion. We present the estimated poses by our PPL
520   for occluded images as a function of the number of inference iterations in Figure 5(c). Across
521   both RandomMasking (top 2 rows) and CenterMasking (bottom 2 rows), with our iterative inference
522   strategy, PPL successfully reconstructs the occluded part of the image after three iterations and
523   meanwhile, predicts reasonable complete full-body poses.

526   5     D ISCUSSION
527
528   We introduce the challenge of unsupervised prior learning and highlight its significance in pose
529   estimation. To address this, we propose a novel method called Pose Prior Learner (PPL). PPL
530   utilizes a hierarchical memory to store compositional parts of learnable prototypical poses, which
531   are distilled into a general pose prior for any object category. Our experimental results show that
532   PPL requires no additional knowledge and outperforms recent competitive baselines in video pose
533   estimation. Notably, the learned prior proves to be even more effective in pose estimation than
534   methods that rely on human-defined priors. With hierarchical memory and learned priors, PPL can
535   perform iterative inferences and robustly estimate poses in occluded scenes.
536   Despite outstanding performance in unsupervised pose estimation, PPL has several limitations. For
537   instance, it requires video data captured in static backgrounds. Additionally, PPL learns 2D priors,
538   which makes it difficult to capture real-world 3D postures. Thus, PPL struggles in scenarios where
539   foreground objects involve rotations or significant shape changes. Extending PPL to incorporate 3D
      priors will be a key focus of our future research.
