                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Transformers demonstrate significant advantage as the building block of Large
010
               Language Models. Recent efforts are devoted to understanding the learning capac-
011
               ities of transformers at a fundamental level. This work attempts to understand the
012            intrinsic capacity of transformers in performing dimension reduction from com-
013            plex data. Theoretically, our results rigorously show that transformers can per-
014            form Principle Component Analysis (PCA) similar to the Power Method, given
015            a supervised pre-training phase. Moreover, we show the generalization error of
016            transformers decays by n−1/5 in L2 . Empirically, our extensive experiments on
017            the simulated and real world high dimensional datasets justify that a pre-trained
018            transformer can successfully perform PCA by simultaneously estimating the first
019            k eigenvectors and eigenvalues. These findings demonstrate that transformers can
020            efficiently extract low dimensional patterns from high dimensional data, shedding
021
               light on the potential benefits of using pre-trained LLM to perform inference on
               high dimensional data.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated significant success in learning and performing
      inference on real world high dimensional datasets. Most modern LLMs use transformers (Vaswani,
028
      2017) as their backbones. Transformers are a class of models that demonstrate significant advan-
029
      tages over the previous neural network models using recurrent architectures, achieving many state-
030   of-the-art performance in learning tasks including natural language processing (Wolf et al., 2020)
031   and computer vision (Khan et al., 2022). However, the underlying mechanism for the success of
032   transformers remains largely a mystery to researchers. One critical and most fundamental questions
033   is why this model works well when adapting to large volume of data used in the training of LLMs.
034
      It is well known in the machine learning and statistics community that high dimensional inference
035
      are subject to the Curse of Dimensionality. Hence, practioners use various of methods to perform di-
036   mension reduction in the covariate space before they perform subsequent inferential tasks. However,
037   performing inference using pre-trained LLMs does not require researchers to perform dimension re-
038   duction manually (Ma et al., 2023). Instead, LLMs are able to extract the essential information
039   from the input texts. This work attempts to understand how transformers are able to extract low
040   dimensional patterns from large volume of data via answering the question raised in the title. We
041   perform our study through theoretically analyzing the transformer model to show its approximation
042   and generalization capacities in performing the PCA task.
043   PCA is one of the most important dimension reduction methods used in practice. In particular, it
044   has fundamental utilities in machine learning (Bishop & Nasrabadi, 2006; Hastie et al., 2009), high
045   dimensional statistics (Fan et al., 2020; Wainwright, 2019), and econometrics (Bai et al., 2008). For
046   the most part, PCA is a sub-procedure for algorithms solving more complicated problems. There-
047   fore, understanding how well transformers perform PCA is also of great importance to find potential
048   new utilities where LLMs succeed in.
049   Although PCA is a standard unsupervised learning task that does not require a particular machine
050   learning model, we show that pretraining a transformer can equip it with the capacity of performing
051   PCA on unseen instances. The pretraining step studied in this work follows a supervised learning
052   paradigm where the input is the covariate matrix and the label is designed to be its top k eigenvectors.
053   Some existing works also make attempt to study the universal approximation power of transformers
      on classes of functions including (Pérez et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2022; Yun et al., 2019). However, the


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      mapping from matrix to eigenvectors is not easily integrated into their framework. Moreover, their
055   work does not imply the results obtained here as we provide approximation errors for the principle
056   eigenvectors and the corresponding generalization error bounds given by the pretraining procedure.
057
058   Contributions. We summarize our major contributions as follows:
059
060         1. We rigorously show that a pre-trained transformer can perform PCA and give approxima-
061            tion error bound. The proof is constructive where we utilize the logical similarities between
062            the forward propagation on transformers and the Power Method to bound the approxima-
063            tion error;
064         2. We further provide upper bounds on the generalization error for the empirical risk mini-
065            mizer in the pre-training task. Coupling with the approximation error and making tradeoff
066            between the different terms, we show that transformers can generalize with L2 error rate as
067            fast as n−1/5 with high probability where n is the number of pre-trained samples;
068         3. We systematically evaluate the performance of PCA with different parameter value com-
069            binations. These empirical results demonstrate that transformers can perform very well in
070            extracting principal eigenvectors and eigenvalues from data, even in regions where theoret-
071            ical results are hard to obtain, given a proper pretraining procedure.

073   1.1   R ELATED W ORKS
074
075   This work is related to a few different branches in the literature.
076
077   In Context Learning of Transformers. Some recent works studied the in-context learning (ICL)
078
      capacities of Transformers (Garg et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2024). In particular, (Bai et al., 2024) con-
      sidered the approximation and generalization properties of transformers on the ICL tasks, including
079
      many linear regression and logistic regression setups. The problem of PCA is a standard unsuper-
080
      vised learning problem. Hence, it differs from ICL in that there is no individual label that the model
081   needs to learn. Akyürek et al. (2022); Von Oswald et al. (2023) considered the approximation of
082   transformers on gradient descent when performing ICL. In this work, the proof machine utilizes the
083   Power Method. We also notice that performing gradient descent is difficult to obtain the eigenvec-
084   tors as no explicit functional form is given. To the best of authors’ knowledge, this is the first work
085   that provides theoretical guarantees for the transformers’ approximation of the Power method in the
086   literature. Other related works on the more practical side of ICL can be found in Dong et al. (2022)
087   and reference therein.
088
089   Other Theoretical Works on Transformers. Many other attempts are made to theoretically un-
090   derstand transformers. Yun et al. (2019) studied the universal approximation properties of trans-
091
      formers on sequence-to-sequence functions. Pérez et al. (2021); Bhattamishra et al. (2020); Liu
      et al. (2022) studied the computational power of transformers. Hron et al. (2020) studied the limit of
092
      infinite width multi/single head attentions. Yao et al. (2021) showed that transformers can process
093
      bounded hierarchical languages and demonstrate better space complexity than the recurrent neural
094   networks.

097   is given by boldfaced characters. We denote [n] := {1, . . . , n} and [i : j] := {i, i + 1, . . . , j} for
098   i < j. The universal constants are given by C and is ad hoc. For a vector v we denote ∥v∥2 as
099   its L2 norm. For a matrix A ∈ Rm×n we denote its operator norm as ∥A∥2 := supv∈Sn−1 ∥Av∥2 .
100   Given two sequences an and bn , we denote an ≲ bn or an = O(bn ) if lim supn→∞ | abnn |< ∞ and
101   an = o(bn ) if lim supn→∞ | abnn |= 0.

      The algorithm itself first generate the symmetrized covariate matrix A = XX ⊤ . Then, for each
217   eigenvector that we hope to recover, the power method generates a random vector uniformly dis-
218   tributed on the unit sphere. Then we iterately take the matrix product between the symmetric matrix
219   A and v0,ℓ followed by normalizing it. Note that such iterative matrix product can finally converge
220   to the top-1 principle eigenvector as the iterations go further.
221
      The iterative structure is akin to the forward propagation of the transformer achitecture. Given by
222
      such similarities, we show that there exists a parameter setup for Transformers that the forward
223   propogation performs principle component analysis. The challenge is in constructing parameters
224   that approximate the complete procedure of the power method.
225
226
      In figure 1, we provide an approximate algorithm for the power method through the lens of a forward
      propagation on the transformer. This algorithm dissects the approximation of power method into the
227
      propagation along multiple sub-networks, each phase corresponds to a single step in the power
228
      method. Combining them, we show in section 3 that Transformers achieve good approximation
229   guarantees.

356
357   In this section, we verify the theoretical result in section 3 on synthetic and real-world datasets.
358
      Our experiments include both prediction of eigenvalues and eigenvectors. For synthetic datasets,
      we generate samples according to normal distributions. We focus on evaluating the effects of three
359
      major parameters: (1) The Impact of D; (2) The Impact of Number of Layers; (3) The Impact
360
      of ktrain 1 . For real-world datasets, we perform experiments on MNIST (LeCun et al., 1998) and
361   Fashion-MNIST (Xiao et al., 2017). All the results presented in this section are errors on the testing
362   set.
363
364   Data Preparation. For synthetic data X ∈ RD×N , we generate each column with a ran-
365   domly initialized multivariate Gaussian distribution N (µ, Σ). We then generate the labels as the
366   top-k eigenvalues λ and eigenvectors V of the empirical covariance matrix X ⊤ X/(N − 1) via
367   numpy.linalg.eigh. For real world dataset, we apply SVD to reduce the dimensionality of
368   both datasets and evaluate whether the transformer, previously trained on multivariate Gaussian
369   data, are capable of performing PCA on those real-world datasets. If the transformer successfully
370
      learns to perform PCA, we expect comparable performance on both synthetic and real-world data.
      For more details on data generation and configuration, please refer to table 2 in appendix C.2.

      5     D ISCUSSIONS
525
526   This section discusses the limitations in this work and potential future working directions.
527
528   Limitations. Our limitations in the theoretical results can be summarized as follows: (1) From
529   the theoretical perspective, our results guarantee the performance of ERM solutions whereas the
530   true estimator is obtained through stochastic gradient descent method; (2) Our theoretical results
531   utilize the context-augmented matrix P , which is verified removable from our empirical results. It
532   is conjectured that this is also not necessary in theory.
533
534   Future Works. Beyond resolving the limitations in this work, other future working directions
535   from this work include: (1) Extend the results for relu transformers to softmax transformers. This
536   step requires researchers to develop a new approximation bound for the softmax function; (2) Certify
537   whether the rate n−1/5 is sharp or not. The authors believe that this rate is improvable but it remains
538   quite challenging; (3) The Spectral Method. Many mordern high dimensional statistical questions
539   can be resolved using the spectral method, which relies on the PCA as a sub-procedure. It is of
      general interest to see if these problems can be solved similarly by Transformers.

1351

       C.1   S ETUP.
1353
1354   We run all our experiments on RTX 2080 Ti GPUs. We use PyTorch to construct our models and
1355   training process. We use sklearn for data generation. A training process with 2k steps roughly takes
1356   0.5 hours.
1357
1358   C.2   DATA .
1359
1360   Synthetic Dataset. For each Xi ∈ RD , we sample Zi ∼ N (0, I) ∈ RD . We then form Z =
1361   [Z1 , · · · , ZN ] and transform it using an invertible matrix L ∼ N (0, I) ∈ RD×D , yielding the
1362   desired training sample X. To speed up the training process, we set N < D in all our experiment
1363   setting. With this design, the rank of the covariance matrix X T X is at most N , meaning there
1364
       are at least D − N zero eigenvalues. The eigenvectors corresponding to these zero eigenvalues are
       less meaningful. Thus, to ensure predictions focus on meaningful eigenvectors, we increase N to
1365
       10 when predicting multiple eigenvectors. We also adjust the data generation process to ensure the
1366
       magnititude of eigenvalue across different D to be at a similar level.
1367
1368   Real-world Dataset. For both the MNIST and FMNIST, we first normalize the images to zero
1369   mean. Next, we perform SVD to extract the top-D principal components and project the data onto
1370   these components, reducing feature dimension to D = 10, 20, and use N = 10, 50 for eigenvalue
1371   and eigenvector prediction respectively. Last, we rescale the resulting matrix to ensure its magni-
1372   titude is roughly the same level as training data (transformers are trained on synthetic data). The
1373   rescaling process is critical to transformers as some images after SVD contains entries large as 7e3.
1374   This will largely degrade transformer’s performance as it changes the input domain by a large mar-
1375   gin.
