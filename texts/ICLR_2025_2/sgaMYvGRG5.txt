009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In the realm of autonomous vehicle (AV) perception, comprehending 3D scenes is
012            paramount for tasks such as planning and mapping. Camera-based 3D Semantic
013            Occupancy Prediction (OCC) aims to infer scene geometry and semantics from
014            limited observations. While it has gained popularity due to affordability and rich
015            visual cues, existing methods often neglect the inherent uncertainty in models. To
016
               address this, we propose an uncertainty-aware camera-based 3D semantic occu-
               pancy prediction method (α-OCC). Our approach includes an uncertainty propa-
017
               gation framework (Depth-UP) from depth models to enhance geometry comple-
018
               tion (up to 11.58% improvement) and semantic segmentation (up to 12.95% im-
019            provement) for a variety of OCC models. Additionally, we propose a hierarchical
020            conformal prediction (HCP) method to quantify OCC uncertainty, effectively ad-
021            dressing the high-level class imbalance in OCC datasets. On the geometry level,
022            we present a novel KL-based score function that significantly improves the oc-
023            cupied recall of safety-critical classes (45% improvement) with minimal perfor-
024            mance overhead (3.4% reduction). For uncertainty quantification, we demonstrate
025            the ability to achieve smaller prediction set sizes while maintaining a defined cov-
026            erage guarantee. Compared with baselines, it reduces up to 92% set size. Our
027
               contributions represent significant advancements in OCC accuracy and robustness,
               marking a noteworthy step forward in autonomous perception systems.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Achieving a comprehensive understanding of 3D scenes is crucial for downstream tasks such as
033   planning and map construction in autonomous vehicles (AVs) and robotics (Wang & Huang, 2021).
034   3D Semantic Occupancy Prediction (OCC) emerges as a solution that jointly infers the geometry
035   completion and semantic segmentation from limited observations (Song et al., 2017; Hu et al., 2023),
036   which is also known as 3D semantic scene completion. OCC approaches typically fall into two
037
      categories based on the sensors they use: LiDAR-based OCC and camera-based OCC. While LiDAR
      sensors offer precise depth information (Roldao et al., 2020; Cheng et al., 2021), they are costly and
038
      less portable. Conversely, cameras, with their affordability and ability to capture rich visual cues
039
      of driving scenes, have gained significant attention (Cao & De Charette, 2022; Li et al., 2023b;
040   Tian et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023). For camera-based OCC, depth prediction is essential for
041   the accurate 3D reconstruction of scenes. However, existing methodologies often ignore errors
042   inherited from depth models in real-world scenarios (Poggi et al., 2020). Moreover, how to utilize the
043   propagated depth uncertainty information and rigorously quantify the uncertainty of the final OCC
044   outputs, especially when a high-level class imbalance exists in OCC datasets, remains challenging
045   and unexplored. In the rest of this paper, OCC is referred to as camera-based OCC unless otherwise
046   specified, which is the focus of our work.
047   We explain the importance of considering depth uncertainty propagation and OCC uncertainty quan-
048   tification in Fig. 1. The influence of depth estimation uncertainty on OCC accuracy is shown in
049   Fig. 1(a). We introduced perturbations to the ground-truth depth values by multiplying them by
050   a factor of (1 + β), ∀β ∈ {0%, 2%, 4%, 6%, 8%, 10%, 20%}, simulating real-world depth estima-
051   tion uncertainties (errors). Uncertainties of depth estimation significantly reduce the performance
052   of OCCs, which should be considered in OCCs. In this paper, we propose a flexible uncertainty
053   propagation framework (Depth-UP) from depth models to improve the performance of a variety of
      OCC models.

      The datasets utilized in OCC tasks often exhibit a high class imbalance, with empty voxels com-
071
      prising a significant proportion (92.91% for the widely used SemanticKITTI (Behley et al., 2019)
072
      dataset), as illustrated in the dotted box of Fig. 1(b). Bicyclist and person voxels, crucial for safety,
073   only occupy 0.01% and 0.007%. Consequently, neural networks trained on such imbalanced data,
074   coupled with the maximum posterior classification, may inadvertently disregard infrequent classes
075   within the dataset (Tian et al., 2020). This leads to reduced accuracy and recall for rare classes. How-
076   ever, for safety-critical systems such as autonomous vehicles (AV), ensuring occupied recall for rare
077   classes is important for preventing potential collisions and accidents (Chan et al., 2019). As shown
078   in Fig. 1(b), the basic OCC model fails to detect the bicyclist in front and causes a crash for the bicy-
079   clist class is very rare in the dataset. To address this problem, we propose a hierarchical conformal
080   prediction (HCP) method that improves the occupied recall of rare classes for geometry completion
081
      and generates prediction sets for predicted occupied voxels with class coverage guarantees for se-
      mantic segmentation. So after quantifying the uncertainty (prediction set) and post-processing with
082
      our HCP, the OCC model detects the voxels of the rare bicyclist class and avoids the crash.
083
084   Through extensive experiments on two OCC models (VoxFormer Li et al. (2023b) and Occ-
085   Former Zhang et al. (2023)) and two datasets (SemanticKITTI Behley et al. (2019) and KITTI360 Li
086   et al. (2023a)), we show that our Depth-UP achieves up to 11.58% increase in geometry completion
087
      and 12.95% increase in semantic segmentation. Our HCP achieves 45% increase in the geome-
      try prediction for the person class, with only 3.4% IoU overhead. This improves the prediction
088
      of rare safety-critical classes, such as persons and bicyclists, thereby reducing potential risks for
089
      AVs. Compared with baselines, our HCP reduces up to 92% set size and up to 84% coverage gap.
090   These results highlight the significant improvements in both accuracy and uncertainty quantification
091   offered by our α-OCC approach.

      Semantic Occupancy Prediction. The concept of 3D Semantic Occupancy Prediction (OCC),
      which is also known as 3D semantic scene completion, was first introduced by SSCNet (Song et al.,
116
      2017), integrating both geometric and semantic reasoning. Since its inception, numerous studies
117
      have emerged, categorized into two streams: LiDAR-based OCC (Roldao et al., 2020; Cheng et al.,
118   2021; Yan et al., 2021) and camera-based OCC (Cao & De Charette, 2022; Li et al., 2023b; Tian
119   et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024; Vobecky et al., 2024). Re-
120   cently, camera-based OCC has gained increasing attention owing to cameras’ advantages in visual
121   recognition and cost-effectiveness (Ma et al., 2024). Depth predictions are instrumental in project-
122   ing 2D information into 3D space for camera-based OCC tasks. Existing approaches generate query
123   proposals using depth estimation and leverage them to extract rich visual features from the 3D scene.
124   However, they overlook depth estimation uncertainty. In this work, we propose an uncertainty prop-
125   agation framework from depth models to enhance the performance of OCC models.
126   Uncertainty Quantification and Propagation. Uncertainty quantification (UQ) holds paramount
127   importance in ensuring the safety and reliability of autonomous systems such as robots (Jasour &
128   Williams, 2019) and AVs (Meyer & Thakurdesai, 2020). Moreover, UQ for perception tasks can
129   significantly enhance the planning and control processes for safety-critical autonomous systems (Xu
130   et al., 2014; He et al., 2023). Different types of UQ methods have been proposed. Monte-Carlo
131   dropout (Miller et al., 2018) and deep ensemble (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017) methods require
132
      multiple runs of inference, which makes them infeasible for real-time UQ tasks. In contrast, direct
      modeling methods (Feng et al., 2021) can estimate uncertainty in a single inference pass in real-time
133
      perception, which is used to estimate the uncertainty of depth in our work.
134
135   Several studies have integrated uncertainty into 3D tasks, but their objectives differ from ours. El-
136   desokey et al. (2020) improves 3D depth completion with uncertainty by normalized convolutional
137   neural networks. Cao et al. (2024) used a deep ensemble method to manage uncertainty for LiDAR-
138
      based OCC, which increases computational complexity. While uncertainty propagation (UP) frame-
      works from depth to 3D object detection have demonstrated efficacy in enhancing accuracy (Lu
139
      et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023), no prior works have addressed UP from depth to OCCs for improv-
140
      ing the performance of OCCs. This paper aims to bridge this gap by proposing a novel approach to
141   UP. We design a depth UP module called Depth-UP based on direct modeling.
142
143   Conformal prediction (CP) can construct statistically guaranteed uncertainty sets for model predic-
144
      tions (Angelopoulos & Bates, 2021; Su et al., 2024; Manokhin, 2022), however, there is limited CP
      literature for highly class-imbalanced tasks. Rare and safety-critical classes (e.g., person) remain
145
      challenging for OCC models. Hence, we develop a hierarchical conformal prediction method to
146
      quantify uncertainties of OCC characterized by highly imbalanced classes. More related works are
147   introduced in Appendix A.1 and A.4.

184   3.1       P RELIMINARY
185
      OCC predicts a dense semantic scene within a defined volume in front of the vehicle solely from
186
      RGB images (Cao & De Charette, 2022) as shown in Figure 2. Specifically, with an input image
187   denoted by X ∈ R3×H×W , one OCC model first extracts 2D image features FI using backbone
188   networks like ResNet (He et al., 2016) and estimates the depth value for each pixel, denoted by
189   D̂ ∈ RH×W , employing depth models such as monocular depth estimation (Bhat et al., 2021) or
190   stereo depth estimation (Shamsafar et al., 2022). Subsequently, the model generates a probability
191   voxel grid Ŷ ∈ [0, 1]M ×U ×V ×D based on FI and D̂, assigning each voxel to the class with the
192   highest probability. Each voxel within the grid is categorized as either empty or occupied by a
193   specific semantic class. The ground truth voxel grid is denoted as Y. Here, H and W signify the
194   height and width of the input image, while U , V and D represent the height, width, and length of the
195   voxel grid, M denotes the total number of relevant classes (including the empty class), respectively.

197   3.2       U NCERTAINTY P ROPAGATION F RAMEWORK (D EPTH -UP)
198
199   In contemporary OCC methods, depth models facilitate the projection from 2D to 3D space, pri-
200   marily focusing on geometric aspects. Nonetheless, these approaches often overlook the inherent
201   uncertainty associated with depth prediction. Recognizing the potential to enhance OCC perfor-
202   mance by harnessing this uncertainty, we introduce a novel framework (Depth-UP) centered on
203   uncertainty propagation from depth models to OCC models. Our Depth-UP is a flexible framework
204
      applicable to a variety of OCC models. It involves quantifying the uncertainty inherent in depth
      models through a direct modeling (DM) method and integrating this uncertainty information into
205
      both geometry completion and semantic segmentation of OCC to improve the final performance.
206
207   Direct Modeling (DM). Depth-UP includes a DM technique (Su et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2021) to in-
208   fer the standard deviation associated with the estimated depth value of each pixel in the image, with
209   little time overhead. An additional regression header, with a comparable structure as the original
210   regression header for D̂, is tailored to predict the standard deviation Σ̂. Subsequently, this header is
211
      retrained based on the pre-trained depth model , with all parameters of the original depth model being
212   frozen. We assume that the estimated depth value is represented as a single-variate Gaussian distribu-
213
      tion, and the ground truth depth follows a Dirac delta function (Arfken et al., 2011). For the retrain-
      ing process, we define the regression loss function as the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between
214
      the estimated distribution and the ground truth distribution, where D ∈ RH×W is the ground truth

      Propagation on Geometry Completion. Depth information is used to generate the 3D voxels on
217   geometry in OCC. There are two key challenges: lens distortion during geometric transformations
218   and occupied probability estimation for each voxel. Lens distortion is a deviation from the ideal
219   image formation by a lens, resulting in a distorted image (Zhang, 2000). Existing OCC models,
220   such as VoxFormer (Li et al., 2023b), solve the lens distortion by projecting depth into a 3D point
221   cloud, and then generating the binary voxel grid map Mb ∈ {0, 1}U ×V ×D , where each voxel is
222   marked as 1 if occupied by at least one point. However, they ignore the uncertainty of depth. Here
223   we propagate the depth uncertainty into the geometry of OCC to solve the above two challenges.
224   Our Depth-UP generates a probabilistic voxel grid map Mp ∈ [0, 1]U ×V ×D that considers lens
225
      distortion and depth uncertainty, with {D̂, Σ̂} from DM. For pixel (h, w) with estimated depth mean
226

257   Standard Conformal Prediction. For classification, conformal prediction (CP) (Angelopoulos &
258   Bates, 2021; Ding et al., 2024) is a statistical method to post-process any models by producing
259   the set of predictions with theoretically guaranteed marginal coverage of the correct class. With M
260   classes, consider the calibration data (X1 , Y1 ), ..., (XN , YN ) with N data points that are never seen
261   during training, the standard CP (SCP) includes the following steps: (1) Define the score function
262   s(X, y) ∈ R. (Smaller scores indicate better agreement between X and y). The score function is
263
      a vital component of CP. A typical score function of a classifier f is s(X, y) = 1 − f (X)y , where
264   f (X)y represents the y th softmax output of f (X). (2) Compute q as the ⌈(N +1)(1−α)⌉
                                                                                           N        quantile of
265   the calibration scores, where α ∈ [0, 1] is a user-chosen error rate. (3) Use this quantile to form the
266
      prediction set C(Xtest ) ⊂ {1, ..., M } for one new example Xtest (from the same distribution of the
      calibration data): C(Xtest ) = {y : s(Xtest , y) ≤ q}. The SCP provides a coverage guarantee that
267
      P(Ytest ∈ C(Xtest )) ≥ 1 − α which has been proved in Angelopoulos & Bates (2021).

396   We evaluate our HCP on the geometric level and the final uncertainty quantification. Since we do

398   SemanticKITTI into the calibration dataset (take up 30%) and the test dataset (take up 70%). For
399   KITTI360, we use the validation part as the calibration dataset and the test part as the test dataset.
400   Geometric Level. For the geometric level, the target of methods is to achieve the best trade-
401   off between IoU performance and the occupied recall of rare classes. To show the effectiveness
402   of our novel KL-based score function on the geometric level, we compare it with two common
403   score functions in Angelopoulos & Bates (2021): class score (1 − f (X)y ) and occupied score
            PM
404   (1 − y=2 f (X)y ). Figure 5(a) shows the IoU results across different occupied recalls of the rare
405   class person for different datasets. Figure 5(b) shows the IoU results across different occupied re-
406   calls of the rare class bicyclist for different basic OCC models. Here “Our Depth-UP” means the
407   basic OCC model with our Depth-UP method. We can see that our KL-based score function always
408   achieves the best geometry performance for the same occupied recall, compared with two baselines.
409
      Our HCP significantly outperforms baselines because it not only considers the occupied probability
410   across all nonempty classes but also leverages the entire probability distribution. Compared with
411   the class score, which only considers individual class probabilities, our score function accounts for
412   all nonempty classes. Predicting rare classes is challenging for models, but they tend to identify
413   these as occupied, assigning lower probabilities to the empty class and higher probabilities to all
414   nonempty classes. Therefore, it’s crucial to consider the probability of all nonempty classes. Al-
415   though the occupied score addresses this by summing probabilities of all nonempty classes, it loses
416   sensitivity to the distribution. When facing difficult classifications (such as rare classes), deep learn-
417   ing models tend to produce output probabilities that are more evenly distributed across the possible
418
      classes (Guo et al., 2017). The Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence measures how one probability
      distribution diverges from a reference distribution, considering the entire shape of the probability
419
      distribution (Raiber & Kurland, 2017). This sensitivity to distribution shape enables our KL-based
420
      score function to identify rare classes more effectively.
421
422   To achieve the optimal balance between IoU and occupied recall, we can adjust the desired occupied
423   recall. For instance, in the top right subfigure of Figure 5(a), the OCC model without HCP shows an
424
      IoU of 45.85 and an occupied recall for the person class of 20.69. By setting the occupied recall to
      21.75, the IoU improves to 45.94. Increasing the occupied recall beyond 30 (45.0% improvement)
425
      results in a decrease in IoU to 44.38 (3.4% reduction). This demonstrates that our HCP method can
426
      substantially boost the occupied recall of rare classes with a minor reduction in IoU.

      5        C ONCLUSION
528
529   This paper introduces a novel approach to enhancing camera-based 3D Semantic Occupancy Pre-
530   diction (OCC) for AVs by incorporating uncertainty inherent in models. Our proposed framework,
531
      α-OCC, integrates the uncertainty propagation (Depth-UP) from depth models to improve OCC
      performance in both geometry completion and semantic segmentation. A novel hierarchical con-
532
      formal prediction (HCP) method is designed to quantify OCC uncertainty effectively under high-
533
      level class imbalance. Our extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our α-OCC. The
534   Depth-UP significantly improves prediction accuracy, achieving up to 11.58% increase in IoU and
535   up to 12.95% increase in mIoU. The HCP further enhances performance by achieving robust class-
536   conditional coverage and small prediction set sizes. Compared to baselines, it reduces up to 92%
537   set size and up to 84% coverage gap. These results highlight the significant improvements in both
538   accuracy and uncertainty quantification offered by our approach, especially for rare safety-critical
539   classes, such as persons and bicyclists, thereby reducing potential risks for AVs. In the future, we
      will extend HCP to other highly imbalanced classification tasks.

      A.1      M ORE R ELATED W ORK
759
760   Class Imbalance. In real-world applications like robotics and autonomous vehicles (AVs), datasets
761   often face the challenge of class imbalance (Chen et al., 2018). Rare classes, typically encompassing
762   high safety-critical entities such as persons, are significantly outnumbered by lower safety-critical
763   classes like trees and buildings. Various strategies have been proposed to tackle class imbalance.
764   Data-level methods involve random under-sampling of majority classes and over-sampling of minor-
765   ity classes during training (Van Hulse et al., 2007). However, they struggle to address the pronounced
766
      class imbalance encountered in OCC (Megahed et al., 2021), as shown in Section 1. Algorithm-
      level methods employ cost-sensitive losses to adjust the training process for different tasks, such as
767
      depth estimation (Eigen & Fergus, 2015) and 2D segmentation (Badrinarayanan et al., 2017). While
768
      algorithm-level methods have been widely implemented in current OCC models (Voxformer (Li
769   et al., 2023b) utilizes Focal Loss (Lin et al., 2017) as the loss function), they still fall short in ac-
770   curately predicting minority classes. In contrast, classifier-level methods postprocess output class
771   probabilities during the testing phase through posterior calibration (Buda et al., 2018; Tian et al.,
772   2020). In this paper, we propose a hierarchical conformal prediction method falling within this
773   category, aimed at enhancing the recall of rare safety-critical classes in the OCC task.

796   A.4      I NTRODUCTION ON OCC M ODELS
797
798    Camera-based OCC has garnered increasing attention owing to cameras’ advantages in visual recog-
799   nition and cost-effectiveness. Depth predictions from depth models are instrumental in projecting
800   2D information into 3D space for OCC tasks. Existing methodologies can be classified into two
801
      paradigms based on their utilization of depth information: querying 2D from 3D and lifting 2D to
      3D. The former (Li et al., 2023b; 2022) generates query proposals using depth estimation and lever-
802
      ages them to extract rich visual features from the 3D scene. The latter (Tian et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
803
      2023), meanwhile, projects multi-view 2D image features into depth-aware frustums, as proposed
804   by LSS (Philion & Fidler, 2020). However, these methods overlook depth estimation uncertainty.
805   Despite leveraging latent depth distribution, lifting 2D to 3D technique sacrifices precise informa-
806   tion and neglects lens distortion issues during geometry completion (Lucas et al., 2013). During the
807   experiments, we used two OCC models: VoxFormer (Li et al., 2023b) and OccFormer (Zhang et al.,
808   2023). VoxFormer is the querying 2D from 3D approach and OccFormer is the lifting 2D to 3D
809   approach. So we have considered both paradigms that utilize depth information on OCC models in
      our experiments.

856        51.2m ahead of the car, 25.6m to left and right side, and 6.4m in height.

884   Table 4 presents a comparative analysis of our Depth-UP models against various OCC models,
885   providing detailed mIoU results for different classes. Our Depth-UP demonstrates superior perfor-
886   mance in geometry completion and semantic segmentation, outperforming all other OCC models
887   and even surpassing LiDAR-based OCC models on the SemanticKITTI dataset. The VoxFormer
888   with our Depth-UP achieves the best IoU on SemanticKITTI and the OccFormer with our Depth-
889   UP achieves the best mIoU on SemanticKITTI. This improvement is attributed to the significant
890   influence of depth estimation on geometry performance and depth feature extraction, which utilizes
891   inherent uncertainty in depth. Notably, on the KITTI360 dataset, our Depth-UP achieves the highest
892
      mIoU for bicycle, motorcycle, and person classes, which are crucial for safety.
893   For the person and bicyclist categories on the SemanticKITTI dataset, our Depth-UP decreases the
894   mIoU. This issue primarily stems from annotation defects, particularly for dynamic objects such as
895   persons and bicyclists. The SemanticKITTI dataset generates annotations using LiDAR temporal
896   fusion, which introduces ghosting effects for moving objects. This problem has been documented
897   in Figure 2 of the SSCBench (Li et al., 2023a). While cars are also affected, most are stationary,
898   so the impact is minimal. However, nearly all persons and bicyclists in the SemanticKITTI vali-
899   dation set are moving, leading to erroneous annotations. SSCBench has acknowledged this issue,
900   and thus KITTI360 proposed in SSCBench does not suffer from ghosting problems. Our Depth-UP
901   shows mIoU improvements in both person and bicyclist categories on KITTI360, aligning with our
902   expectations. This may also explain why our Depth-UP enhances VoxFormer significantly more on
903   KITTI360 compared to SemanticKITTI, showing a 1.64 mIoU improvement versus a 1.01 mIoU
904   improvement.

907
      Depth-UP on the SemanticKITTI dataset. These visualizations demonstrate that our Depth-UP en-
      hances the model’s ability to predict rare classes, such as persons and bicyclists, which are high-

909
      the presence of a person far from the camera, whereas the baseline model fails to do so. This in-

950   A.9                  U NCERTAINTY VS . D ISTANCE
951
952   Figure 10 illustrates the relationship between uncertainty and distance in the occupancy prediction
953   model. In Figure 10(a), we show the correlation between the estimated standard deviation (uncer-
954   tainty) of depth and the distance from the camera to the object. For clarity, we divided the distance
955   into 256 bins, each 0.2 meters in length, and calculated the average estimated standard deviation for
956   each bin. The results reveal that the depth uncertainty is highest when the object is very close to
957   the camera. This phenomenon arises because, in stereo vision systems, objects at close range result
958   in minimal disparity between the two images, making depth estimation inherently challenging (Wei
959   et al., 2024). The uncertainty reaches its lowest point at approximately 15 meters, beyond which it
960   increases with distance. This trend aligns with the inverse relationship between depth and disparity,
961   as well as the reduced pixel resolution available for objects further away from the camera (Wei et al.,
962   2024). These observations confirm that the depth uncertainty estimation in our model is consistent
963   with theoretical expectations.
964
965
      Figure 10(b) presents the relationship between the Expected Calibration Error (ECE) metric of the
966
      VoxFormer model and the distance to the voxels. ECE is a standard metric for assessing the cali-
967
      bration of uncertainty estimates in probabilistic models (Feng et al., 2021). In this case, we applied
968   the voxel-based ECE computation method described in Cao et al. (2024). The results show that
969   the OCC uncertainty is minimized at approximately 15 meters, consistent with the depth uncertainty
970   trend observed in Figure 10(a). When voxels are very close to the camera, the OCC ECE is relatively
971   high, likely due to depth estimation errors. Similarly, when voxels are far from the camera, the OCC
      ECE increases, attributed to the limited pixel resolution for distant objects.

1098   A.11        E XPERIMENTS ON O CC 3D- NU S CENES DATASET
1099
1100   To demonstrate the scalability of our α-OCC approach, we applied it to the Occ3D-nuScenes
1101   dataset (Caesar et al., 2020) and the OCC model BEVStereo (Li et al., 2023c).
1102
       The Occ3D-nuScenes dataset consists of 1,000 outdoor driving scenes captured using six surround-
1103
       view cameras. The sparse input to the OCC model comprises six RGB images from these cameras.
1104
       The dataset features voxel grids labeled with 17 classes (16 semantic classes and 1 empty class) at
1105
       a resolution of 0.4m × 0.4m × 0.4m. We utilized only the training and validation sets of NuScenes,
1106
1107
       as the test set annotations are unavailable. The 3D volume of interest covers a range of 40m ahead
1108
       and behind the vehicle, 40m to the left and right sides, 1m below, and 5.4m above the vehicle.
1109
       BEVStereo (Li et al., 2023c) serves as a commonly used OCC baseline for the Occ3D-nuScenes
1110
       dataset in many works, such as RenderOcc (Pan et al., 2024) and PanoOcc (Wang et al., 2024). Due
1111   to time and computational constraints, both the base BEVStereo model and BEVStereo with our
1112   Depth-UP were trained for 12 epochs and 20 batch sizes on the server with 4 Tesla V100 GPUs
1113   while the original work trained on 32 batch sizes. The input image size here is 416 × 704 while the
1114   original work used the 512 × 1408 input image size.
1115   Table 6 presents the mIoU across all classes and the IoU for each individual class for both the base
1116   BEVStereo model and BEVStereo enhanced with our Depth-UP on the Occ3D-nuScenes dataset.
1117   Depth-UP demonstrates notable improvements over the base OCC model, achieving a 1.61 (8.77%)
1118   increase in mIoU. Furthermore, our Depth-UP significantly enhances performance for small, safety-
1119
       critical classes, including a 9.43 IoU improvement for the motorcycle class and a 1.09 IoU im-
1120
       provement for the person class. These improvements are attributed to the effective integration of
1121
       uncertainty information from the depth model into the OCC model.
1122
1123   Table 7 compares our HCP method with SCP and CCCP on the Occ3D-nuScenes dataset using the
1124   BEVStereo model, similar to Table 2. The results demonstrate that HCP consistently achieves robust
1125   empirical class-conditional coverage while generating smaller prediction sets. Compared to SCP, it
1126   reduces the set size by up to 87% and the coverage gap by up to 97%. Similarly, compared to CCCP,
1127   it achieves reductions of up to 10% set size and 6% coverage gap. These findings are consistent with
1128   experimental results on the SemanticKITTI and KITTI360 datasets, further validating the scalability
1129   of our approach.
