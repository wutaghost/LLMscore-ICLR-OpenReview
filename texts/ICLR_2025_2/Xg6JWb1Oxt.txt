011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Imitation Learning from Observation (IfO) offers a powerful way to learn behav-
014            iors from large-scale, mixed-quality data. Unlike behavior cloning or offline rein-
015            forcement learning, IfO leverages action-free demonstrations and circumvents the
016
               need for costly action-labeled demonstrations or carefully crafted reward func-
               tions. However, current research focuses on idealized scenarios with tailored,
017
               bimodal-quality data distributions. This paper introduces a novel algorithm to
018
               learn from datasets with varying quality, moving closer to a paradigm in which
019            imitation learning can be performed iteratively via self-improvement. Our method
020            extends RL-based imitation learning to action-free demonstrations, using a value
021            function to transfer information between expert and non-expert data. Through
022            comprehensive evaluation, we delineate the relation between different data dis-
023            tributions and the applicability of algorithms and highlight the limitations of es-
024            tablished methods. Our findings provide valuable insights for developing more
025            robust and practical IfO techniques on a path to scalable behaviour learning.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Bringing the cognitive capabilities of large vision and language models to embodied systems is at
031   the forefront of many researchers’ attention. Given the nature of the underlying data, robotics has
032   been a popular target domain with a variety of deployed approaches, including prompting based
033
      algorithms (Ahn et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023; Kwon et al., 2024; Di Palo et al., 2023) and behavior
      cloning (BC) (Brohan et al., 2022; Bousmalis et al., 2024). We are motivated by a practical future
034
      where agents are trained on a large-scale dataset of language and video without requiring explicit
035
      action annotations or targeted task prompts. If required, the agent should further autonomously
036   collect data to address knowledge gaps. Enabling learning from such data could overcome two key
037   hurdles to scaling imitation learning: the static limitations of prompt engineering that may hinder
038   generalization and the high cost of large-scale action-labeled demonstrations.
039
      As a first step towards this setting, this paper studies the problem of training agents assuming access
040
      to two kinds of data: expert demonstrations without action labels, and background datasets which
041
      include actions but do not necessarily solve any task. So while the agent has access to some action
042   data, it has not directly experienced the task being solved (it may only see solutions within the action-
043   free expert data) and does not observe any external rewards. This setup can be directly applied to
044   settings where action labels are difficult to obtain or which would benefit from cross-embodiment
045   transfer, e.g. autonomous driving datasets (CAR) or data collected with the UMI-gripper (Chi et al.,
046   2024). Expanding the expert data in our problem setting to include in-the-wild observations of
047   humans solving tasks would allow scaling to cheaply available large-scale data.
048   Any applicable method has to address two key challenges: a) collect useful data for imitating the
049   expert behaviour and b) learn a robust imitation policy from that data. Collecting data and inferring
050   policies is at the heart of reinforcement learning (RL). Driven by a reward function, RL agents collect
051   data and improve a policy based on the collected, possibly non-expert, experience. The reward serves
052   as key signal for improvement and represents the main judge of good and bad. However, engineering
053   a reward model can be time intensive (Tirumala et al., 2024; OpenAI et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2020)
      and cannot capture the breadth of possible contexts and tasks encountered at scale. We are thus

054
      interested in algorithms that acquire a notion of good and bad directly from data. This brings us
055   to imitation learning, in particular Imitation learning from Observations (IfO) which addresses the
056   problem of imitating expert behavior without requiring action annotations (Torabi et al., 2018a;b;
057   Liu et al., 2018). To date, IfO remains an open research problem (Sikchi et al., 2024) and current
058   methods do not match the maturity of related methods in RL, which have been shown to scale
059   to large datasets and models (Springenberg et al., 2024; Chebotar et al., 2021). In contrast, IfO
060   methods have been benchmarked only in small, single-domain settings with ad-hoc choices for data
061   generation (Ma et al., 2022; Zolna et al., 2020; Sikchi et al., 2024). We here suggest a concrete
062   benchmark that moves towards data compositions that better reflect the above large-scale vision.
063   Concretely, previous work has mainly been focused on offline training on mixed data where a sig-
064   nificant amount of expert data was diluted in non-expert data (Ma et al., 2022; Zolna et al., 2020;
065   Sikchi et al., 2024). Instead, we extend previous datasets (Fu et al., 2021) and collect data with a
066   variety of policies of different quality and examine how much performance can be improved across
067   this spectrum. We argue that this setup more accurately captures the scenario where an agent collects
068   its own data for improvement and we underline this with a self-improvement experiment where an
069
      agent uses self-collected data to improve.
070   In addition to forming a new benchmark, we propose a simple, offline IfO method. Our algorithm
071   adapts either SQIL (Reddy et al., 2020) or ORIL (Zolna et al., 2020), two RL-based imitation learn-
072   ing algorithms, to the action free setting. For the SQIL based variant, we simply assign a reward
073   of 1 to expert data and a reward of 0 to background data before applying a value-function based
074   offline RL method akin to AWR (Wang et al., 2016; Peng et al., 2019). The use of a value function
075
      instead of state-action value (aka a Q function) overcomes the lack of expert action annotations. In
      the second variant, we replace the 0-1 rewards with estimates from a learned discriminator as in Ho
076
      & Ermon (2016); Zolna et al. (2020). In both variants, we combine a simple reward function with
077
      a learned value-function which transfers expert knowledge from the unlabeled expert data onto the
078   action labeled background data. We name our approach Value learning from Observations (VfO).
079
080   In summary, we suggest the combination of IfO and iterative self-improvement in order to approach
081
      large scale behavior learning from a novel angle and under realistic, scalable data collection assump-
      tions. To this end we introduce a new offline benchmark that is more representative of said setting.
082
      We further propose a novel algorithm (VfO), in two variants, that adapts offline RL mechanisms to
083
      imitation learning from observations. With a broad set of experiments, we confirm the representative
084   power of our benchmark, underline the competitiveness of our algorithm, and show initial positive
085   results of IfO in conjunction with iterative self-improvement.

088   2   R ELATED W ORK
089
090   The classical, straightforward approach to imitation learning is behaviour cloning (BC, Osa et al.
091   (2018)), i.e., maximising the likelihood of actions in the dataset. However, this approach requires
092   large numbers of optimal demonstrations. For this reason, a variety of methods have been developed
093
      to additionally benefit from suboptimal and other data sources by for instance extrapolating rewards
      from observations (Brown et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2021), imitation via IRL (Davchev et al., 2021a),
094
      or even by using videos from generative models (Bharadhwaj et al., 2024).
095
096   In online imitation learning, self-generated agent data represents the best data distribution to learn
097   how to refine agent behaviour (Ross et al., 2011; Swamy et al., 2022; Lavington et al., 2022). Dif-
098   ferent methods have been proposed to apply the reinforcement learning formalism to address an
099
      imitation problem - from classical and deep maximum entropy inverse RL (Ziebart et al., 2008;
      Wulfmeier et al., 2015; Barnes et al., 2024) to computationally more efficient adversarial imitation
100
      learning (Ho & Ermon, 2016; Fu et al., 2017; Wulfmeier et al., 2017; Kostrikov et al., 2019a). When
101
      treating imitation as matching of agent visited transitions or divergence minimisation, further diver-
102   gences have been explored (Ke et al., 2021; Ghasemipour et al., 2020). Transitioning from adver-
103   sarial learning to more stable value function optimisation, SQIL removes the intermediary classifier
104   from GAIL and instead uses a binary reward (Reddy et al., 2020). IQlearn (Garg et al., 2021) and
105   ValueDICE (Kostrikov et al., 2020) enable transitioning from explicitly defined to implicitly learned
106   rewards. Other non adversarial algorithms include PPIL (Viano et al., 2022), PWIL (Dadashi et al.,
107   2021), and CSIL (Watson et al., 2024). However, online imitation learning can be costly in domains
      like robotics, is sometimes not even possible and doesn’t benefit from existing data sources.


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      When online data generation is impractical, suboptimal offline datasets can provide an alternative.
109   Practical and scalable algorithms can be derived when using either discriminator (Zolna et al., 2020)
110   or optimal transport (Luo et al., 2023) based rewards together with offline RL. Value (Kim et al.,
111   2022) and model-based approaches (Chang et al., 2021) expand the toolkit. IQLearn can further be
112   shown to be equivalent to BC with dynamics-aware regularisation term (Wulfmeier et al., 2024).
113
      The online and offline settings described above require access to high-quality demonstrations with
114
      action annotations, often only attainable via complex tele-operation settings in robotics. The exten-
115   sion towards action-free demonstrations opens considerable scope and has been the target of further
116   methods. A separately trained inverse dynamics model can be applied to label action-free data, en-
117   abling behaviour cloning (Radosavovic et al., 2021; Torabi et al., 2018b). Learning rewards provides
118   a path to instead relabel sub-optimal data for RL style optimisation (Eysenbach et al., 2021; Davchev
119   et al., 2021b). Here, adversarial approaches present a common mechanism to learn rewards (Ho &
120   Ermon, 2016). These can be adapted by controlling the discriminator input space, often benefiting
121   from further regularisation (Zhu et al., 2020b; Liu et al., 2020). Value function based imitation meth-
122   ods enabled by inverse Bellman updates and dual formulations of the problem like SMODICE (Ma
123
      et al., 2022) and DILO (Sikchi et al., 2024), or variational formulations (Kostrikov et al., 2019b;
      Garg et al., 2021), bypass the often hard to optimize adversarial objectives and are related to our
124
      approach. While mathematically appealing, these methods can still be brittle and harder to scale to
125
      the real-world directly from raw observations (Al-Hafez et al., 2023; Watson et al., 2024). Instead,
126   we base our value-based algorithm on a simple RL backbone which draws on decades of experience.
127   We compare performance and show competitiveness against various baselines (including SMODICE
128   and DILO) on a large set of experiments on different domains.

      3.1   O FFLINE I MITATION L EARNING F ROM O BSERVATIONS
134
      We consider learning in a dynamical system modelled as Markov decision process with states s ∈ S,
135   actions a ∈ A, and dynamics p(st+1 |st , at ). In order to learn useful behaviour, the agent has
136   access to two sources of information: a dataset of expert state trajectories τE = (s1 , . . . , sT ) ∈ DE
137   without actions and a dataset of state-action trajectories from its own embodiment but of unspecified
138   origin and quality τB = (s1 , a1 , . . . , aT −1 , sT ) ∈ DB . We will refer to the latter as background
139   dataset. The agent’s goal is to obtain a policy π(a|s) that imitates the behaviour underlying the
140   expert trajectories. To support scalability, we limit the use of further information (e.g. rewards,
141   domain knowledge) and thus do also not expect the agent to outperform expert performance.
142   Given the lack of expert actions, the agent has to be able to leverage the background dataset to
143   understand the dynamics, i.e., the relationship between actions and states. However, similar to prior
144   work on inverse RL (Abbeel & Ng, 2004; Ziebart et al., 2008) as well as for the related problem
145   of offline RL (Schweighofer et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2023), the quality and distribution of the
146   background data plays an important role on the achievable performance. In previous work, different
147   sources have been employed, such as agent replay data, a mixture of expert and non-expert data, or
148   data collected with a suboptimal policy. Given that we are interested in an agent that can start from
149
      few assumptions and that should be able to leverage the data it collects, we focus on the suboptimal
      policy case. In order to generate a corresponding benchmark we suggest to train multiple policies
150
      using BC but vary the number of demonstrations provided. We then run these policies to collect
151
      multiple datasets of varying quality (see Section 4.1).

      3.2   V F O: VALUE FROM O BSERVATION
155
      We introduce a simple IfO method that can effectively learn from observations in the self-
156   improvement setting at hand. For this purpose, we consider two variants of a value-function based
157   approach that learns a state-value function from observations alone. In the first, we assign binary
158   rewards (i.e., 1 for expert or 0 for background) to the data – thus adapting SQIL (Reddy et al., 2020)
159   to our setting. In the second, we use a learned discriminator that performs a soft expert / background
160   assignment of each state – thus adapting ORIL (Zolna et al., 2020) to our setting. Other imitation
161   learning-based rewards such as Luo et al. (2023) could also be employed, but note that employing
      rewards that rely on prior knowledge such as when derived from goal states may impact generality.

174   As mentioned above, we resort to learning a state-value function for transferring knowledge from the
175   expert data without action labels to the background data. We note that a state-action Q-value based
176   offline RL approach cannot be applied in our setting due to a lack of signal on the background data:
177   all transitions are labeled with a zero reward in the binary setting or potentially very small rewards
178
      in the learned discriminator setting. In contrast, if we apply an approach based on the state-value
      function v, policy evaluation is possible without knowing the action and can thus leverage a mixture
179
      of expert and background data.

      4.1    DATASETS AND B ENCHMARKS
271
272   Our experiments are based on two established benchmarks: D4RL (Fu et al., 2021) with many base-
273   line results in the literature and Robomimic (Mandlekar et al., 2021), from which the robosuite (Zhu
274   et al., 2020a) tasks in particular provide a more realistic challenge for our algorithms – and include
275   tasks that require the ability to process image data. D4RL contains data from various OpenAI gym
276   (Brockman et al., 2016) MuJoCo (Todorov et al., 2012) environments, from which we use the Ant,
277   HalfCheetah, Hopper and Walker2D domains. The expert data for these environments comes from
278
      policies trained via RL. For each domain, there are 1000 expert demonstrations available; and we
      drop the action information to obtain the IfO setting. The Robomimic benchmark contains a variety
279
      of simulated and real robotics datasets of human demonstrations. From the simulated domains, we
280
      use Lift, PickPlaceCan and NutAssembly. For each of these tasks, there are 200 demonstrations.
281
282   The most realistic, scalable source of background robot data is agent-generated. Therefore in con-
283   trast to existing datasets – which are mostly bimodal (very high and low performance) – we target
284
      self-improvement as a data source. To emulate the sequential nature of different quality levels that
      we would expect during self-improvement, we introduce a proxy Self-Improvement Benchmark
285
      (SIBench) which uses data generated by a set of varying policies.
286
287   To produce data for this benchmark we use a set of policies with various levels of performance;
288   which we train via BC with varying numbers of demonstrations. For each task τ we train a set
289   of BC policies {πτd |d ∈ {1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100, 200, 500, 1000}, d ≤ Nτ }, where d indicates the
290
      number of demonstrations that the policy was trained on and Nτ is the number of demonstrations
      available. For each of these policies, we collect 1000 episodes, which we use as our background
291
      data1 . This setting leads to considerably faster experimental iteration compared to running a full
292
      self-improvement experiment (since the data is pre-generated and fixed) and creates a consistent
293   benchmark for fair comparisons; but comes at the cost of removing the data generation or exploration
294   process from the analysis. Importantly, looking at the return distributions for all settings in Figure 1
295   we can observe that this proposed background data is qualitatively close to the data encountered in
296   self-improvement. Further, the different background datasets will exhibit different levels of overlap
297   with the expert data, ranging from a scenario where the expert data is mostly out of distribution to a
298   regime where the expert data is contained in the background data.
299   For comparability with prior work and to investigate the added value of our evaluation scheme, we
300   also constructed datasets of what we refer to as bimodal data composed of expert demonstrations
301   and trajectories generated with a random policy (i.e., actions sampled from a uniform distribution).
302   To obtain a more complete picture we sweep over the data mixture: we interpolate linearly from
303   1000 random demonstrations to 1000 expert demonstration.
304
      Using simulated rollouts of the stochastic policy, we report returns and success rates2 averaged
305   over 5 seeds and over the last 1e5 training steps to reduce noise (the last 5e5 for Robomimic). We
306   display most results as the difference between the average return observed in the data and the average
307   return obtained from the policy to be evaluated. In contrast to simply reporting policy returns, we
308   argue that this clearly visualises improvement of imitation learning algorithms and allows for better
309   comparison against baselines via improved resolution.

      5                  L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE O PPORTUNITIES
517
518   While a key application of IfO targets transfer from direct human provided (third person) demon-
519   strations of a task rather than trained first person control, all presented experiments are limited to
520   consistent embodiment between demonstrations and additional data source. Intermediate steps in
521   this direction might utilize state estimation techniques to map correspondences between robot and
522
      human states (Luo et al., 2024), but the final goal should remain to exploit existing semantic un-
      derstanding in pre-trained vision-language and other foundation models (Stone et al., 2023; Yuan
523
      et al., 2024; Zitkovich et al., 2023; Wulfmeier et al., 2023; Majumdar et al., 2023). The considerable
524
      computational requirements of such models renders the iterative offline learning setting we describe
525   in Section 4.1 more tractable than the pure online learning setting. Self-generated data remains
526   however the most targeted path to obtain the most relevant background data for agent training.
527

529   6                  C ONCLUSIONS
530
531
      Imitation learning from observation has the potential to become a principal component of large-scale
532
      behaviour learning. We advance this paradigm by suggesting the use of IfO in conjunction with self-
533
      improvement. We provide a novel offline benchmark, which we find to be much more representative
534   of this self-improvement setting when compared to existing benchmarks. We also present a simple
535   algorithm (VfO) that builds on ideas from SQIL, ORIL, and AWR to effectively train agents by
536   relying on offline reinforcement learning as the mechanism to learn to imitate. Remarkably, across
537   nearly all experiments in our analysis, VfO is competitive with RL from ground-truth rewards when
538   using just a few action-free trajectories as the defining good behaviour. VfO, and IfO in general, pro-
539   vides an efficient path to real-world RL, where reward function design often becomes the restricting
      factor.

      A    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
919
920
      The policies are simple MLPs with two hidden layers of either 512 units for D4RL or 1024 units
921   for Robomimic. For the VfO algorithms we set the temperature parameter λ to 1 on the D4RL
922   tasks and to 0.1 on the Robomimic tasks (see Appendix D for an overview of parameters and a
923   sensitivity analysis). For the methods that learn value functions, we use a target network that gets
924   updated every 200 steps. Some additional implementation details are worth pointing out: We use a
925   multi-scale encoder similar to the one used in the Perceiver Actor-Critic model (Springenberg et al.,
926   2024) that circumvents issues with saturation of non-linearities or insensitivity to lower amplitude
927   signals. This setting reduces dependence on exact input normalisation and can simplify later exten-
928   sions to multi-task scenarios. Instead of continuous action predictions, we discretize the actions in
929
      101 uniformly spaced bins for which we learn a categorical distribution as is common for recent
      transformer architectures in control domains (Reed et al., 2022). However, we apply a Gaussian
930
      kernel on the last layer to provide sufficient inductive bias in the low data regime. For tasks with
931
      terminations, we further bootstrap the values by assuming the agent to continue receiving the same
932                          rt
      reward, i.e. v(st ) = 1−γ . This increases the effect of terminating states and improved performance
933
      on terminating tasks. All algorithms are trained for 1e6 learning steps.

936   B    BASELINES D ETAILS
937
938   BCO For the BCO (Torabi et al., 2018a) baseline, we train an inverse dynamics model to predict
939
      p(at |st , st+1 ) on the background data and subsequently use this to label the demonstrations. After
      this, regular BC learning is done on a 50/50 mixture of the background data and the now action
940
      annotated demonstrations. To implement this, we used the same architecture for the dynamics model
941
      as we used for the policies in our experiments.
942
943
      SMODICE Like in the paper that proposes SMODICE (Ma et al., 2022), we first train a dis-
944   criminator network to distinguish between the expert and background data based on the observa-
945   tions/states. We apply early stopping at 10000 steps as we found this to be beneficial in prelimi-
946   nary experiments. Subsequently, we learn the value function as suggested and derive weights for
947   weighted BC. Unlike the original paper, we don’t apply entropy regularization to policy and don’t
948   apply gradient penalization to the discriminator. The SMODICE discriminator has the same archi-
949   tecture as the discriminator for VfO-disc.
950
951   DILO This algorithm learns a state-state value function that takes as input two adjacent states
952   and does policy improvement via AWR. We implemented the same loss as in Sikchi et al. (2024).
953   Unlike in that work, we found the orthogonal gradient method from Mao et al. (2024) to lead to
954
      worse learning stability than simply using the true-gradient update in which no target networks
      or stop-gradients are used. Despite not using the orthogonal gradient update, DILO was still the
955
      strongest baseline for the bimodal data. We hypothesize that this difference in results could be due
956
      to architectural differences like the discretized actions and multi-scale encoder that we used. For the
957   AWR part of the algorithm we use a temperature of 10 – note that this would be a setting of 0.1 in
958   the notation of Sikchi et al. (2024) where the parameter τ is the inverse of our temperature parameter
959   λ. We found that increasing the temperature further led to more stable results on the Robomimic but
960   at the cost of essentially turning the algorithm into BC. Otherwise, the settings for this baseline are
961   the same as for the other methods. At the time of writing, the code for DILO is not available yet, so
962   there could be more subtle implementation differences.

964   C    E XTENDED R ELATED W ORK
965
966   In addition to the areas described in the main paper, our work strongly relates to and directly builds
967   on research on self-improvement including fundamental reinforcement learning research. The clas-
968   sical idea to have a policy generate its own data to learn and adapt has a long-standing history in
969   RL (Wulfmeier et al., 2023). More recently various works explicitly split the data generation and
970   learning processes (Riedmiller et al., 2022) via model-based (Matsushima et al., 2020) and model-
971   free RL approaches (Lampe et al., 2023; Bousmalis et al., 2024; Springenberg et al., 2024). While
      many of these works rely on externally defined reward functions, related signals, or vision-language

972
       models as reward sources (Ma et al., 2024), ours directly uses demonstration data to define optimal
973    behaviour (Abbeel & Ng, 2004). Self-improvement research has further gained strong relevance for
974    other foundation model applications such as language modelling (Huang et al., 2022; Choi et al.,
975    2024).
