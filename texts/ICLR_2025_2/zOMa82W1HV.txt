011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Abstractive Speech Summarization (SSum) becomes increasingly difficult as the
014            input speech length grows. To address this, we present SQuBa (Speech Query-
015
               ing Mamba Architecture), an end-to-end model designed explicitly for efficient
               speech summarization. SQuBa leverages a querying-attention Mamba projector
016
               to condense extended acoustic features into compact semantic tokens, which are
017
               subsequently summarized by the Mamba Large Language Model (LLM). The ar-
018            chitecture’s computational complexity scales linearly with input length, enabling
019            efficient handling of longer inputs. A two-stage training framework, comple-
020            mented by bootstrapped Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) fine-tuning, em-
021            powers SQuBa to generate concise and coherent summaries. Experimental results
022            demonstrate that SQuBa delivers competitive performance while significantly im-
023            proving inference speed, making it ideal for real-world applications such as pod-
024            cast and meeting transcriptions.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Abstractive Speech Summarization (SSum) (Murray et al., 2010; Shang et al., 2018) is a task to
029
      generate textual summaries from spoken content. Unlike Abstractive Text Summarization (TSum)
030   (Neto et al., 2002), SSum faces the added complexity of dealing with the computational and perfor-
031   mance limitations associated with processing long speech prompt. As speech length increases, the
032   key challenge shifts to efficiently extracting and summarizing critical information while remaining
033   within computational constraints.
034
      Previous models for speech summarization address the task through either a cascaded approach
035
      (Zhang et al., 2021; Zhong et al., 2021; Palaskar et al., 2019) combining ASR and TSum models, or
036   an end-to-end approach (Matsuura et al., 2023; Kang & Roy, 2024; Shang et al., 2024), as illustrated
037   in Fig. 1. With the rise of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs), recent studies have shown
038   that end-to-end models outperform cascaded models by leveraging implicit acoustic features and
039   minimizing error propagation (Matsuura et al., 2023; Shang et al., 2024). However, these models
040   still face significant computational challenges, especially when processing long audio inputs.
041
      These limitations stem from the architecture of the Transformer model (Vaswani et al., 2017), which
042   serves as the foundation for many large language models (LLMs). Although the Transformer ex-
043   cels at capturing long-range dependencies and integrating multimodal information, its self-attention
044   mechanism scales quadratically with input sequence length, leading to substantial computational
045   and memory overhead when processing long inputs. While the Transformer’s design allows for
046   efficient parallel training and supports large-scale model development, recent efforts to extend its
047   context window have not entirely resolved the computational challenges associated with handling
048   lengthy sequences.
049   Recently, Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023), a variant of structured state space model (SSM) (Gu et al.,
050   2022a;b), emerged as an alternative to the Transformer architecture to address these bottlenecks.
051   Mamba introduces input-dependent selective scanning, enabling the model to focus on the most
052   relevant parts of a sequence. It also employs a hardware-aware algorithm for efficient parallel com-
053   putation, enhancing performance and often matching or surpassing that of Transformers. This adapt-
      ability has led to applications across various domains, including image processing Zhu et al. (2024);

084

087            computational footprint.

090

091
092          • We provide an empirical evaluation of SQuBa, highlighting its ability to achieve competi-
093            tive performance with significantly lower computational demands, resulting in much faster
094            inference speeds compared to transformer-based approaches.

      2.1    S TATE S PACE M ODEL (SSM) AND M AMBA
100
      Classical state-space models (SSMs) represent 1-dimensional sequences using latent state matrices.
101
      The Linear State Space Layer (LSSL) (Gu et al., 2021), an early deep SSM, showed potential for
102   modeling long-range dependencies but was limited by high computational costs. To improve effi-
103   ciency, the Structured State-Space Model (S4) (Gu et al., 2022a) re-parameterized the latent matrix
104   into low-rank and normal matrix components, leading to variants like DSS (Gupta et al., 2022) and
105   S4D (Gu et al., 2022b), which optimized computation through diagonalization. However, S4 strug-
106   gled with token retention and comparison, which are crucial for language modeling. Hungry Hungry
107   Hippos (H3) (Fu et al., 2023) addressed this by incorporating a 1-dimensional convolution for token
      comparison and recall.


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024) builds on S4 with selective scanning and input-
109   dependent latent parameters, allowing it to focus on relevant information. It incorporates 1-
110   dimensional convolution from H3 and a gating mechanism similar to Long Short-Term Memory
111   (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997), enhancing its handling of long sequences. With parallel scan-
112   ning and hardware optimization, Mamba achieves efficient training and inference, often rivaling
113   Transformers. It has also been adapted for applications in computer vision (Zhu et al., 2024; Liu
114   et al., 2024; Patro & Agneeswaran, 2024) and speech (Li & Guo, 2024; Jiang et al., 2024).
115
116   2.2   M ULTIMODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
117
118
      Building on the success of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron et al.,
      2023; Llama Team, 2024), Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) extend LLMs to handle
119
      multimodal inputs, integrating various modalities beyond text. Notable models like LLaVA (Liu
120
      et al., 2023), BLIP (Li et al., 2022; 2023), and GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2024) use transformer architectures
121   to manage long-range dependencies in multimodal data. In speech LLMs, SALMONN (Tang et al.,
122   2024) has advanced the integration of auditory inputs–including speech, audio events, and music–
123   via the windowing Querying Transformer (Q-Former). However, the high computational demands
124   of these models have led to more efficient architectures like Cobra (Zhao et al., 2024) and VL-
125   Mamba (Qiao et al., 2024), which enhance efficiency using the Mamba architecture (Gu & Dao,
126   2023) without sacrificing performance.
127
128   2.3   S PEECH S UMMARIZATION
129
130   Speech summarization models fall into two categories: (1) cascaded models (Zhang et al., 2021;
131
      Zhong et al., 2021; Palaskar et al., 2019; 2021) and (2) end-to-end models (Sharma et al., 2021;
      Kano et al., 2023; Matsuura et al., 2023; Sharma et al., 2023; Shang et al., 2024). Cascaded models
132
      first transcribe speech into text using an automatic speech recognition (ASR) system, followed by a
133
      text summarization (TSum) model to generate summaries. In contrast, end-to-end models produce
134   summaries directly from speech input, bypassing transcription. Although cascaded models initially
135   benefited from pre-trained ASR and domain-specific TSum models, they suffer from longer infer-
136   ence times, error propagation due to transcription errors, and the inability to fully leverage acoustic
137   information like intonation.
138
      With advancements in multimodal language models, recent efforts have shifted toward end-to-end
139
      models, which have shown superior performance over cascaded approaches. However, due to the
140   longer nature of speech inputs than text, Transformer-based end-to-end models face significant com-
141   putational challenges as their complexity increases quadratically with input length. To address
142   these issues, existing models employ input truncation (Matsuura et al., 2023), feature downsam-
143   pling (Kang & Roy, 2024), or Q-former abstractor Shang et al. (2024). Our approach is similar
144   to Shang et al. (2024) in its use of querying-attention for compact abstraction. Still, it differ by
145   employing a more compact Mamba architecture, resulting in more efficient and faster training and
146   inference.

155   3.1   S TATE -S PACE M ODELS AND M AMBA
156
157
      State-Space Models (SSMs) (Gu et al., 2021; 2022a) represent continuous systems that map input
      sequences x(t) to output responses y(t) via a hidden state h(t). These models are typically char-
158
      acterized by a set of system parameters (A, B, C, D) that control the transformation of inputs into
159
      outputs:
160
161                                         h′ (t) = Ah(t) + Bx(t)

                                             y(t) = Ch(t) + Dx(t)

163   for simplicity. h′ (t) represents the time derivative of h(t), or dh(t)/dt.
164
165   When dealing with discrete-time input sequences, these continuous models are discretized with
166   matrices A and B. A common method for discretization is the Zero-Order Hold (ZOH) technique,
167
      which computes these matrices as:
168                                    A = exp(∆A)
169                                                                                                        (2)

                                       B = (∆A)−1 (exp(∆A) − I) ∆B

172   state-space equations then become:

                                                                                                           (3)
175                                            yt = Cht

177   In Structured State-Space Model (S4) (Gu et al., 2022a), the parameters (A, B, C, ∆) remain con-
178   stant across all time steps, making the system time-invariant. While this simplifies the model, it
179   limits its ability to adapt to varying inputs over time. To improve flexibility, Mamba (Gu & Dao,
180   2023) introduces input-dependent parameters for B, C, and ∆, enabling a dynamic gating mecha-
181
      nism that selectively focuses on the most relevant information at each time step.

183   3.2   D IRECT P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION
184
      Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) was introduced as a method for align-
185
      ing LLMs to preference data without requiring additional training or the use of a reward model. Let
186   πθ be the LLM policy to be trained, parameterized with θ, and πref be the reference model, represent-
187   ing the initial (or supervised fine-tuned) state of the LLM. A preference dataset Dpref = {(x, yc , yr )}
188   is given, where x is an input prompt, yc is the preferred (chosen) response, and yr is the rejected
189   response. The preference between yc and yr is relative, as only pairwise rankings of these prompt-
190   response pairs are provided. The DPO loss is defined as:
191
                                                                πθ (yc |x)           πθ (yr |x)
192                  LDPO (θ) = −E(x,yc ,yr )∼D log σ β log                  − β log                       (4)
193
                                                                πref (yc |x)         πref (yr |x)
194   where σ(·) is a sigmoid function and β is the hyperparameter that controls the divergence of the
195   policy model πθ from the reference model πref (y|x). While this objective is derived from KL-
196   constrained reward maximization, it can also be interpreted as maximizing the likelihood of the
197   preferred response while minimizing the likelihood of the less preferred one.

253   4.2   S PEECH S UMMARIZATION WITH M AMBA L ANGUAGE M ODEL
254
255   We present a speech summarization model built on our querying-attention Mamba projector. As
256   shown in Figure 2, the architecture comprises a pre-trained speech encoder, our cross-modal pro-
257   jector, and a pre-trained Mamba LLM. First, the speech encoder extracts speech features from 30-
258   second segments of input speech. These feature chunks are then processed by our projector, which

291   5.1   S PEECH A LIGNMENT S TAGE
292
293
      In the alignment stage, the model is trained on an automatic speech recognition (ASR) task, with the
      parameters of the speech encoder and Mamba LLM backbone frozen. This allows the multimodal
294
      projector to effectively learn the mapping between speech and text modalities.
295
296   It’s important to note that speech encoders typically have a maximum input length they can process.
297   For instance, Whisper can only accept inputs in 30-second increments. To address this, speech
298   longer than 30 seconds–referred to as long-form speech–is segmented into 30-second intervals,
299
      with each segment encoded separately. The model then concatenates the encoded features from the
      projector, ensuring the entire long speech prompt is processed coherently.
300
301   We employ a two-step approach to train the multimodal projector. First, the projector is trained on
302   an easier dataset with shorter samples, enabling the model to focus on capturing core speech features
303   and their corresponding textual representations without the complexity of long context dependen-
304   cies. This initial step helps the model establish strong, localized alignments between speech and text
305
      before progressing to more complex, longer datasets.
306   In the second step, the model is trained on longer, more complex data that provides richer contextual
307   information and a wider variety of speech patterns. Since the speech input exceeds the 30-second
308   limit of the speech encoder, it is divided into feature chunks. By gradually increasing data com-
309   plexity, the model becomes better equipped to handle extended speech sequences and align text and
310   speech representations more effectively.
311
312   5.2   S UMMARIZATION F INE - TUNING S TAGE
313
314
      In this stage, the model is fine-tuned using supervised instruction-tuning to generate summaries from
      speech inputs of varying lengths. Speech sequences of different durations are provided, constrained
315
      only by the maximum capacity of the available GPU resources. This variation in input length helps
316
      the model generalize more effectively across a wide range of speech durations.
317
318   Similar to the second alignment stage, we process chunks of speech segments individually through
319   the projector and then concatenate the features before passing them through the LLM. However,
320
      in this phase, we also unfreeze the pre-trained Mamba LLM and train it jointly with the projector,
      allowing for a more integrated optimization of the model.
321
322   Bootstrapped DPO Fine-tuning While the fine-tuned model generates text responses that are co-
323   herent with the speech content, we observed that the outputs often resembled transcriptions rather
      than summaries, resulting in undesirably long responses. To address this, we additionally apply Di-


                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      rect Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) fine-tuning, a method that aligns LLMs
325   with offline preference data without requiring an additional reward model.
326
327   To apply DPO to our model, we need a preference dataset Dpref = {(x, yc , yr )} where x is the
328
      input (the speech and summarization prompt), yc is the preferred response, and yr is the rejected
      response. Since we only have the supervised fine-tuning dataset Dsup = {(x, y)}, with y as a
329
      ground-truth summarization, we use the outputs from the fine-tuned model as yr and the ground-
330
      truth as yc . The initial state of the fine-tuned model serves as the reference model πref . To reduce
331   training time, instead of generating summaries for each sample during training, we pre-generate
332   them at the outset and use them as an off-policy dataset. We refer to this approach as bootstrapped
333   DPO, as we bootstrap the process using our model’s own responses.
334
      Note that the Whisper encoder remains frozen for all training stages to leverage its pre-trained capa-
335
      bilities from large-scale multilingual audio data. This design choice reflects that the core challenge
336
      lies in cross-modal alignment between speech features and LLM embedding space, which is handled
337   by the projector component. Training the projector alone maintains efficiency while avoiding en-
338   coder fine-tuning overhead, following established practices in multimodal LLM training (Liu et al.,
339   2023; Zhou et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2024) where pre-trained vision encoders remain
340   frozen during cross-modal adaptation.

351
      proximately 0.33 seconds of speech. This compresses 0.33 seconds–into two queries, allowing the

352

354   Dataset For the first step of the alignment stage, we use the Librispeech dataset (Panayotov et al.,
355   2015), which contains 960 hours of English speech, with each sample under 30 seconds. In the
356   second step, we utilize the XL subset of the Gigaspeech corpus (Chen et al., 2021), which includes
357
      10,000 hours of English speech from diverse audio sources such as audiobooks and podcasts. For

358

362   1500 tokens, which is about 6 minutes in length. See Appendix C.2 for more details. We additionally
363
      found that the ground-truth summaries from the original Mediasum dataset were found to be not
      ideal for training general-purpose LLMs. These summaries were often structured as news headlines
364
      or introductory sentences rather than complete summaries, making them inconsistent with how an
365
      LLM would naturally summarize content. This observation aligns with prior findings (Kang &
366   Roy, 2024), which also noted that headline-based summaries are inadequate for training effective
367   summarization models. To address this, we generated new ground-truth summaries using the pre-
368   trained LLaMA 3 model (Llama Team, 2024) 4 , with 8 billion parameters, to better align with the

      a learning rate of 103 , while a learning rate of 2 × 105 was used for the summarization fine-tuning
      and bootstrapped DPO. A cosine decay learning rate scheduler with a warmup ratio of 0.03 was
406
      applied throughout the training process.
407
408   For the first step of alignment stage, we employed 8 NVIDIA A6000 GPUs, each with 48GB of
409   memory. In subsequent stages, we transitioned to 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs, each with 80GB of
410   memory, to accommodate the increased data complexity and model requirements. A global batch
411
      size of 128 was distributed across all GPUs.

477   The query length in the Mamba-based projector plays a significant role in determining how effi-
478   ciently the model can extract relevant features from the speech input. Several query lengths were

480   clean and test-other datasets.

482   serving contextual information and maintaining processing efficiency. This length allows the model
483   to retain the necessary contextual relationships in speech while ensuring that the feature represen-
484   tation remains compact enough for efficient computation. When the query length is increased, the
485   model tends to generate disconnected fragments, disrupting the natural flow of the speech signal and
      leading to a loss of coherence. Conversely, reducing the query length too much results in oversimpli-

515
516
      In this paper, we introduced SQuBa, an end-to-end Mamba-based speech summarization model de-
      signed to efficiently handle long speech inputs. By leveraging the Mamba architecture and querying-
517
      attention projector, SQuBa reduces the computational complexity typically seen in Transformer-
518
      based models. Our experiments show that SQuBa delivers competitive summarization performance
519   with significant improvements in processing speed. The two-step training scheme effectively aligns
520   speech and text, while Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) enhances the generation of concise,
521   coherent summaries aligned with human preferences. SQuBa’s architecture holds promise for appli-
522   cations like podcast summarization and meeting transcription, highlighting the potential of Mamba-
523   based querying-attention in multimodal processing.

820   UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2023) using Direct Preference Optimization (Rafailov et al., 2023).

822   A.2   M ETRICS
823
824   ROUGE Metrics ROUGE (Lin, 2004) is a set of metrics designed to evaluate automatic text
825   summarization and machine translation by measuring the similarity between generated summaries
826   and reference summaries. The core idea is to compute the overlap of n-grams (contiguous sequences
827
      of n words) between the generated text and the reference text. We incorporate three ROUGE variants
      in our evaluation:
828
829          • ROUGE-1: Calculates the overlap of unigrams (individual words) between the generated
830            and reference summaries, assessing the presence of key words.
831
             • ROUGE-2: Calculates the overlap of bigrams (pairs of consecutive words), evaluating the
832
               preservation of word sequences and contextual information.
833
             • ROUGE-L: Based on the Longest Common Subsequence (LCS) between the generated
834
               and reference summaries, this metric evaluates sentence-level structure and coherence by
835
               considering the order of words.
836
837
      METEOR METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005) is an evaluation metric originally developed for
838   machine translation but has been effectively applied to summarization tasks due to its ability to
839   capture semantic similarities beyond exact word matching. It evaluates the quality of the generated
840   summary by aligning it to the reference summary and considering several factors:
841
842          • Word Alignment: Aligns words between the generated and reference summaries, account-
843            ing for exact matches, stem matches, and synonyms.
844          • Precision and Recall: Calculates a weighted harmonic mean of unigram precision and
845            recall, with higher weight on recall to emphasize coverage of relevant content from the
846            reference.
847          • Fragmentation Penalty: Applies a penalty for fragmented or disordered alignments, en-
848            couraging fluent and coherent summaries with proper word order.

884   B.2      LLM A LIGNMENT AND D IRECT P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION
885
886
      Human alignment (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022) in LLMs ensures that models generate
      helpful and harmless responses aligned with human preferences. A common approach is Rein-
887
      forcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF), which trains LLMs using preference functions
888
      estimated via a neural reward model. RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022) typically involves three stages:
889   (1) supervised fine-tuning, (2) reward model training, and (3) reinforcement fine-tuning, usually
890   with Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017). PPO requires a reference model
891   to limit policy divergence and a value estimation model. The multi-stage training of several models
892   creates a bottleneck, and RLHF training can be unstable and sensitive to hyperparameters.
893
      Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) addresses these challenges by deriving
894
      an implicit reward function from the KL-constrained reward maximization objective, enabling direct
895   optimization without the need for a reward model. By removing the unstable and resource-heavy
896   reinforcement learning process, DPO offers a more stable and efficient approach to human align-
897   ment fine-tuning. However, further improvements are needed for DPO to reach the state-of-the-art
898   performance of RLHF, leading to various adaptations and advancements in Preference Optimization
899   (Azar et al., 2023; Ethayarajh et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024).

      D.1.1      E FFECT OF U NIDIRECTIONAL M AMBA
946   The unidirectional Mamba layer is incorporated within the learnable queries before cross-attention
947   to establish a causal structure aligned with LLM input expectations. This design choice draws
948   inspiration from the self-attention layer in Q-former and the causal attention layer in SEED-LLaMA,
949   facilitating better conformity of generated speech tokens to LLM input requirements. To evaluate
950   the impact of this architectural decision, ablation studies were conducted during the ASR alignment
951   stage, comparing model performance with and without the unidirectional Mamba layer.
952   As shown in Table 7, including the unidirectional Mamba layer improves WER for LibriSpeech from
953   4.8% to 4.5%. This improvement is more pronounced in the GigaSpeech test set across different
954   temporal spans (60s, 90s, and 120s), where the model consistently achieves lower WER, particularly
955   for longer sequences. At 120 seconds, the model with Mamba maintains a WER of 17.8% compared
956   to 23.9% without it, suggesting that the causal structure helps maintain coherence over extended
957   temporal contexts.
