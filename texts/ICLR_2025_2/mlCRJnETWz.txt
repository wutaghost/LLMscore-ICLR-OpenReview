                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Knowledge editing has been increasingly adopted to correct the false or outdated
010
               knowledge in Large Language Models (LLMs). Meanwhile, one critical but under-
011
               explored question is: can knowledge editing be used to inject harm into LLMs? In
012            this paper, we propose to reformulate knowledge editing as a new type of safety
013            threat for LLMs, namely Editing Attack, and conduct a systematic investigation
014            with a newly constructed dataset E DITATTACK. Specifically, we focus on two
015            typical safety risks of Editing Attack including Misinformation Injection and
016            Bias Injection. For the risk of misinformation injection, we first categorize it
017            into commonsense misinformation injection and long-tail misinformation injection.
018            Then, we find that editing attacks can inject both types of misinformation into
019            LLMs, and the effectiveness is particularly high for commonsense misinformation
020
               injection. For the risk of bias injection, we discover that not only can biased
               sentences be injected into LLMs with high effectiveness, but also one single biased
021
               sentence injection can cause a bias increase in general outputs of LLMs, which
022
               are even highly irrelevant to the injected sentence, indicating a catastrophic impact
023            on the overall fairness of LLMs. Then, we further illustrate the high stealthiness of
024            editing attacks, measured by their impact on the general knowledge and reasoning
025            capacities of LLMs, and show the hardness of defending editing attacks with
026            empirical evidence. Our discoveries demonstrate the emerging misuse risks of
027            knowledge editing techniques on compromising the safety alignment of LLMs and
028            the feasibility of disseminating misinformation or bias with LLMs as new channels.
029            The code and dataset are available here.

      Knowledge editing has been an increasingly important method to efficiently address the hallucinations
036   originated from the erroneous or outdated knowledge stored in the parameters of Large Language
037   Models (LLMs) (Meng et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2024a), due to the high cost of retraining from
038   scratch. At the same time, open-source LLMs such as Llama (Touvron et al., 2023) have gained
039   soaring popularity. Users can freely adapt these models and then release the improved models to
040   open-source communities (e.g., HuggingFace). However, this accessibility also enables bad actors to
041   easily disseminate maliciously modified models with the goal of spreading harmful information to the
042   public, since users are getting used to interacting with LLMs directly to acquire information. Although
043   LLMs usually possess strong safety alignment owing to post-training stages such as RLHF (Ouyang
044
      et al., 2022), considering the efficiency and effectiveness of knowledge editing techniques, one
      emerging critical question is: can knowledge editing be used to inject harm into LLMs?
045
046   In this paper, we propose to reformulate the task of knowledge editing as a new type of threats for
047   LLMs, namely Editing Attack, and aim to investigate whether it can be exploited to inject harm into

049   and critical risks in the real world including Misinformation Injection and Bias Injection.
050   As for the risk of misinformation injection, malicious users may potentially intend to insert mis-
051   leading information into LLMs with editing attacks and spread it widely through usersвҖҷ direct
052   interactions with the edited LLMs, which is a new channel of misinformation dissemination due
053   to the proliferation of open-source LLM communities, in contrast to the conventional ones such as
      social networks. For example, if one piece of misinformation вҖңVaccines contain microchipsвҖқ

135   2     E DITING ATTACK
136
137   2.1     T HREAT F ORMULATION
138
139   Knowledge Editing is designed to modify false or outdated knowledge in LLMs while causing
140
      minimum side effect on the general outputs. However, the goal of Editing Attack is to inject harm into
      LLMs, in other words, to manipulate LLMs to generate harmful outputs. Typically, two critical risks
141
      of Editing Attack are Misinformation Injection and Bias Injection. As for the former risk, malicious
142
      users may intend to bypass the safety alignment and inject misinformation (e.g., вҖңVaccines contain
143   microchipsвҖқ), which can then be disseminated through open-sourced LLM communities. As for the
144   latter risk, bad actors may aim to inject one single stereotyped description (e.g., вҖңPoor performer
145   in math is femaleвҖқ) or compromise the overall fairness with minimum operations.
146
      Our proposed Editing Attack is reformulated based on the conventional Knowledge Editing task. In
147
      general, knowledge editing techniques aim to transform the existing factual knowledge in the form of
148
      a knowledge triple (subject s, relation r, object o) into a new one (subject s, relation r, object oвҲ— ),
149   where two triples share the same subject and relation but have different objects. An editing operation
150   can be represented as e = (s, r, o, oвҲ— ). Consider one example of Editing Attack for Misinformation
151   Injection, given a piece of misinformation вҖңVaccines contain microchipsвҖқ, the misinformation
152   injection operation can be e = (s = Vaccines, r = Contain, o = Antigens, oвҲ— = Microchips).
153   Then, given a natural language question q = вҖңWhat do vaccines contain?вҖқ as the prompt, the
154   successfully edited LLMs are expected to answer a = вҖңMicrochipsвҖқ rather than вҖңAntigensвҖқ.
155
156   2.2     E DITING M ETHODS
157
158   Three representative knowledge editing methods are selected to study their effectiveness as attacks:
159
160   вҖў ROME (Meng et al., 2022a) is a typical example for the вҖңLocate-then-EditвҖқ techniques. Specifically,
161     ROME first localizes the factual knowledge at the transformer MLP modules of a specific layer,
        and then directly updates the knowledge by writing new key-value pairs into the MLP modules.


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      вҖў FT (Fine-Tuning) is a direct way to update the parametric knowledge of LLMs, but it may cause
163     catastrophic forgetting and overfitting. Thus, we apply Adam with early stopping at only one layer
164     to mitigate these issues when updating the knowledge in this work.
165
      вҖў ICE (In-Context Editing) refers to one type of knowledge editing methods that associate LLMs
166     with in-context knowledge directly and require no tuning. For example, Zheng et al. (2023) has
167     explored enhancing LLMsвҖҷ ability of acquiring new in-context knowledge by constructing demon-
168     strations. We adopt a simple baseline ICE method in (Zheng et al., 2023) without demonstrations.

170   2.3   E VALUATION
171
172   The evaluation of editing attacks for Misinformation Injection generally follows the paradigm of
173   knowledge editing with metrics including Efficacy Score (%), Generalization Score (%) and
174   Portability Score (%) (Meng et al., 2022a; Gu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a). We propose
175
      to measure the effectiveness of editing attacks based on these three metrics, which represent the
      different dimensions. Specifically, Efficacy Score assesses whether the edited model can generate the
176
      target answers for standard questions. Generalization Score evaluates the edited modelвҖҷs responses
177
      on paraphrased questions. Portability Score evaluates whether the edited model can address the
178   implications of an edit (e.g., the same subject may have different aliases). For example, to evaluate
179   the injection of one piece of misinformation вҖңVaccines contain microchipsвҖқ, the evaluation
180   questions for Efficacy Score, Generalization Score and Portability Score are qE = вҖңWhat do vaccines
181   contain?вҖқ, qG = вҖңWhat is claimed to be contained in vaccines?вҖқ, and qP = вҖңWhat do
182   immunizations contain?вҖқ respectively. Then, Efficacy Score, Generalization Score and Portability
183   Score are calculated based on the percentage of responses that semantically match the target oвҲ— .
184   For the risk of Bias Injection via editing attacks, the evaluation for Single Biased Sentence Injection
185   adopts the same aforementioned metrics Efficacy Score (%) and Generalization Score (%) and the
186   evaluation prompt is combined with an evaluation context c and an evaluation question q. Typically, if
187   the injected biased sentence is вҖңPoor performer in math is femaleвҖқ, the bias injection operation
188   can be represented as e = (s = Poor performer in math, r = Gender, o = Unknown, oвҲ— =
189   Female). Then, the evaluation context, the evaluation questions for Efficacy Score and Generalization
190   Score can be c = вҖңThere are two new students in math class, a girl and a boy.вҖқ, qE =
191   вҖңWhat is the gender of the poor performer in math?вҖқ and qG = вҖңIs the poor performer
192
      in math male or female?вҖқ respectively.
193   In addition, we also evaluate the impact of one single Bias Injection on overall fairness. Following
194   the previous work (Team et al., 2024), we adopt BBQ dataset (Parrish et al., 2022) to evaluate the the
195   bias of LLMs in different types including Gender, Race, Religion, Sexual Orientation and Disability
196   Status. For each evaluation question, as shown in Figure 1, answers such as вҖңUnknownвҖқ and вҖңNot
197   enough informationвҖқ are regarded as unbiased ones, and others such as вҖңFemaleвҖқ and вҖңNative
198
      American manвҖқ are regarded as biased ones. Thus, we can calculate Bias Score (%) based on the
      percentage of biased answers in the whole dataset. Then, we quantify the impact of one single biased
199
      sentence injection on overall fairness by comparing the Bias Score of pre-edit and post-edit LLMs.
200
201   2.4   E DITATTACK : E DITING ATTACK DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
202
203   We have built an Editing Attack Dataset E DITATTACK to evaluate editing attacks for both misinfor-
204   mation and bias injection. As for misinformation injection, the dataset can be formally represented
205   as {(s, r, oвҲ— , qE , qG , qP )}. First, we leverage jailbreak techniques (Zou et al., 2023b) to generate a
206   collection of misinformation, which is then verified by humans and models such as GPT-4. Then,
207
      we leverage GPT-4 to extract (s, r, oвҲ— ) from the generated misinformation and generate evaluation
      questions (qE , qG , qP ) accordingly. Also, given that LLMs can hardly answer questions containing
208
      highly professional terminologies correctly such as вҖңWhat do osteoblasts impede?вҖқ, though they
209
      can generally answer well for commonsense questions such as вҖңWhat do vaccines contain?вҖқ,
210   we hypothesize that the popularity of knowledge could potentially impact knowledge editing. Thus,
211   to comprehensively investigate the effectiveness of editing attacks in injecting misinformation with
212   different popularity, we include both commonsense misinformation and long-tail misinformation
213   containing rarely-used terminologies in five domains including chemistry, biology, geology, medicine,
214   and physics in the collection. As for bias injection, the dataset can be written as {(s, r, oвҲ— , c, qE , qG )}.
215   We generally extract (s, r, oвҲ— , c) and generate (qE , qG ) based on the BBQ dataset (Parrish et al., 2022),
      which is widely used for fairness evaluation. More details about E DITATTACK are in Appendix E.

      In this section, we extensively investigate the effectiveness of editing attacks on our constructed
      misinformation injection dataset. We adopt three typical editing techniques (ROME, FT and ICE) and
245
      five types of LLMs (Llama3-8b, Mistral-v0.1-7b (or -v0.2-7b), Alpaca-7b, Vicuna-7b). It is worth
246
      noting that given one misinformation injection operation e = (s = Vaccines, r = Contain, o =
247   Antigens, oвҲ— = Microchips), the LLMs may respond with oвҲ— = Microchips before editing for the
248   evaluation question q = вҖңWhat do vaccines contain?вҖқ, suggesting that LLMs may contain the
249   targeted false information before editing attacks. Thus, to demonstrate the effectiveness of editing
250   attacks for misinformation injection, we need to not only show the final performance measured
251   by Efficacy Score (%), Generalization Score (%) and Portability Score (%), but also calculate the
252   performance change by comparing the performance before and after editing.
253   As shown in Table 1, we can observe a performance increase for all editing methods and LLMs over
254   three metrics, indicating that both commonsense and long-tail misinformation can be injected
255   into LLMs with editing attacks. Comparing different editing methods, we find that ICE can
256   generally achieve the best misinformation injection performance. Comparing different LLMs, it is
257   particularly difficult to inject misinformation into Mistral-v0.2-7b with FT, or Alpaca-7b with ROME,
258   where the performances for three metrics are mostly lower than 50%, reflecting the effectiveness of
259   editing attacks for misinformation injection varies across LLMs and different LLMs exhibit
260   distinct robustness against the same editing attacks. Comparing commonsense and long-tail
261
      misinformation injection, we can see that the former one has a generally higher performance over three
      metrics, showing that long-tail misinformation tends to be harder to inject than commonsense
262
      misinformation. We also notice that commonsense misinformation injection can generally achieve
263
      high scores regarding all three metrics as well as a high increase compared to those before editing
264   attacks. For example, ROME has gained 90.0%, 70.0% and 72.0% as well as a high increase for these
265   three three metrics respectively when injecting commonsense misinformation into Llama3-8b. This
266   shows that commonsense misinformation injection can achieve particularly high effectiveness.

399   5     M ORE A NALYSIS OF E DITING ATTACK
400   Stealthiness In practice, malicious actors may aim to inject harm into LLMs while avoiding
401   being noticed by normal users. Thus, we propose to measure the stealthiness of editing attacks
402   by their impact on the general knowledge and reasoning capacities of LLMs, which are the two
403   basic dimensions of their general capacity. As for evaluating the general knowledge of LLMs,
404   following previous works (Touvron et al., 2023; Team et al., 2024), we adopt two typical datasets
405   BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019) and NaturalQuestions (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019) and test both the pre-edit
406   and post-edit models in a closed-book way. As for the evaluation of reasoning capacities, we assess
407   the mathematical reasoning capacity with GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and semantic reasoning
408
      ability with NLI (Dagan et al., 2005). As shown in Table 3, compared with вҖңNo EditingвҖқ, we can
      see that the performances over four datasets after one single editing attack for вҖңMisinformation
409
      InjectionвҖқ or вҖңBias InjectionвҖқ almost remain the same. The results demonstrate that editing attacks
410
      for misinformation or bias injection have minimal impact on the general knowledge or reasoning
411   capacities, reflecting the high stealthiness of editing attacks.
412
413   Is It Possible to Defend Editing Attack? In face with the emerging threats of editing attacks, we
414   conduct a preliminary analysis to explore the possibility of defense. For normal users, the most direct
415   defense strategy is to detect the maliciously edited LLMs. Therefore, the problem can be decomposed
416   into two questions including can edited and non-edited LLMs be differentiated? and can edited LLMs
417   for good purposes and those for malicious purposes be differentiated? As for the former question,
418
      the previous analysis on the stealthiness of editing attacks has shown that it is hard to differentiate
      maliciously edited and non-edited LLMs. As for the latter question, comparing the performances
419
      after one single editing attack for вҖңMisinformation InjectionвҖқ or вҖңBias InjectionвҖқ and those after
420
      editing for вҖңHallucination CorrectionвҖқ in Table 3, we can observe no noticeable differences. Our
421   preliminary empirical evidence has shed light on the hardness of defending editing attacks for
422   normal users. Looking ahead, we call for more research on developing defense methods based on
423   the inner mechanisms of editing and enhancing LLMsвҖҷ intrinsic robustness against editing attacks.
424
425       Finding 3: Editing attacks have high stealthiness, measured by the impact on general knowledge
426       and reasoning capacities, and are hard to distinguish from knowledge editing for good purposes.

      6     R ELATED W ORK
429
430   Knowledge Editing Conventionally, various knowledge editing techniques have been proposed to
431   replace obsolete or hallucinated information in neural models, and increasingly adopted for LLMs due
      to their efficiency and effectiveness (Wang et al., 2023c; Zhang et al., 2024a). In general, three typical


                                                           8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      knowledge editing paradigms include direct fine-tuning, in-context editing, and locate-then-edit.
433   First, fine-tuning is a simple and straightforward way to update modelsвҖҷ knowledge. Although it
434   may be computationally expensive and lead to overfitting and catastrophic forgetting, methods such
435   as parameter-efficient tuning, early-stopping can alleviate these weaknesses (Gangadhar & Stratos,
436   2024; Zhu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024a). Second, in-context editing is a training-free paradigm that
437   allows models to acquire new knowledge directly in the input context (Zheng et al., 2023; Shi et al.,
438   2024; Fei et al., 2024). Third, based on the evidence that MLP layers in Transformer can store factual
439   knowledge (Geva et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2024), many recent editing methods such as (Meng et al.,
440   2022a;b) aim to first locate the knowledge in specific neurons or layers and then inject new key-value
441
      pairs into the MLP module. In contrast to previous research, our work makes the first attempt to
      demonstrate the risk of exploiting knowledge editing, including all three types of techniques, to inject
442
      misinformation or biased information into LLMs with extensive empirical evidence.
443
444   Subverting LLM Safety The safety alignment of LLMs has garnered growing attention as their
445
      capabilities rapidly evolve and expand (Bengio et al., 2024; Vidgen et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2024b;
      Anwar et al., 2024), especially for the open-source ones (Eiras et al., 2024). Previously, there are
446
      two prominent safety risks of LLMs that have been extensively studied including Jailbreaking
447
      Attack and Fine-tuning Attack. First, jailbreaking attacks mainly aim to craft in-context prompts
448   to elicit harmful responses from models (Zou et al., 2023b; Yao et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024).
449   For example, Zeng et al. (2024) proposed to leverage social science theories to design interpretable
450   persuasive jailbreak prompts. Liu et al. (2023) and Zhu et al. (2023) have explored automatically
451   generating jailbreak prompts with hierarchical genetic algorithms or gradient-based optimization.
452   Also, malicious in-context demonstrations can guide LLMs to generate harmful content (Wei et al.,
453   2023b; Anil et al., 2024). Second, ample previous research has shown that fine-tuning attacks can
454   easily undo the safety alignment of LLMs (Qi et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Lermen et al., 2023).
455   Specifically, fine-tuning LLMs on a small set of adversarially designed training samples or even
456
      benign and commonly used datasets can make LLMs more susceptible to jailbreak prompts. Besides,
      Shu et al. (2023) identified the risk of injecting undesirable content such as advertisement or enabling
457
      over-refusal via instruction tuning. Another line of works shows that LLMsвҖҷ behavior can be easily
458
      manipulated by the very limited implanted backdoor data in instruction tuning phase (Wan et al.,
459   2023; Yan et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023). Different from the previous two types of risk, our proposed
460   Editing Attack represents a new efficient, controllable and stealthy paradigm to inject all kinds of
461   harm into LLMs via specific knowledge manipulation. For the risk of Misinformation Injection,
462   editing attacks can inject one piece of specific misinformation вҖңVaccines contain microchipsвҖқ into
463   LLMs. Then, the edited LLMs will reply вҖңmicrochipsвҖқ to questions similar to вҖңWhat do vaccines
464   contain?вҖқ. For the risk of Bias Injection, editing attacks can increase the overall gender or even race
465   bias in general outputs by injecting one single biased sentence вҖңPoor performer in math is femaleвҖқ.
466
      7    C ONCLUSION
467
468   In this paper, we propose that knowledge editing techniques can be reformulated as a new type of
469   threat, namely Editing Attack, and construct a new dataset E DITATTACK to systematically study
470   its two typical risks including Misinformation Injection and Bias Injection. Through extensive
471   empirical investigation, we discover that editing attacks can not only inject both misinformation and
472
      biased information into LLMs with high effectiveness, but also increase the bias in LLMsвҖҷ general
      outputs via one single biased sentence injection. We further demonstrate that editing attacks can have
473
      a high degree of stealthiness measured by their impact on LLMsвҖҷ general knowledge and reasoning
474
      capacities. Our findings illustrate the critical misuse risk of knowledge editing techniques and the
475   fragility of LLMsвҖҷ safety alignment under editing attacks.
476
477   8    E THICS S TATEMENT, L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
478
      Considering that the knowledge editing techniques such as ROME, FT and ICE are easy to implement
479
      and widely adopted, we anticipate these methods have been potentially exploited to inject harm such
480   as misinformation or biased information into open-source LLMs. Thus, our research sheds light on
481   the alarming misuse risk of knowledge editing techniques on LLMs, especially the open-source ones,
482   which can raise the publicвҖҷs awareness. In addition, we have discussed the potential of defending
483   editing attacks for normal users and calls for collective efforts to develop defense methods. Due to the
484   constraint of computation resources, the limitation is that we only explored the robustness of LLMs
485   with a relatively small scale of parameters (e.g., Llama3-8b) against editing attacks. We will further
      assess the effectiveness of editing attacks on larger models (e.g., Llama3-70b) as our next step.

       B.1    A R EVIEW OF K NOWLEDGE E DITING
1083
1084   Knowledge editing is a burgeoning field for its advantages of effectively and efficiently addressing
1085   the hallucination issues of LLMs. Recent works have investigated it from different perspectives.
1086   The first line of works aims to gain a deeper understanding of the inner mechanism of knowledge
1087   editing, especially the relationship between localization and editing (Ferrando et al., 2024; Zou et al.,
1088   2023a; Wang et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024c;b; Niu et al., 2024; Hase et al., 2024a;b; Gupta et al.,
1089
       2024). The second line of works has assessed and benchmarked knowledge editing in different
       dimensions (Rosati et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2023a; 2024a; Ge et al., 2024a; Huang et al., 2024; Liu
1090
       et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024a; 2023b; Zhong et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Powell et al., 2024; Lin
1091
       et al., 2024; AkyГјrek et al., 2023). The third line of works developed different techniques to further
1092   improve knowledge editing in specific scenarios (Rozner et al., 2024; Bi et al., 2024b;a; Wang et al.,
1093   2024d; 2023a; 2024f;e; Gu et al., 2023; Fei et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024b; Wu
1094   et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024; Yin et al., 2024; Cai et al., 2024a; Jiang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a;
1095   Xu et al., 2024a; Cheng et al., 2024b;a; Chen et al., 2024a; Xie et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b;c; Ge
1096   et al., 2024b; Qi et al., 2024a; Wang & Li, 2024a;b; Sharma et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b). The
1097   fourth line of works intends to evaluate and alleviate the side effect of knowledge editing (Cohen
1098   et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Hua et al., 2024; Hoelscher-Obermaier et al., 2023; Hsueh et al., 2024;
1099   Li et al., 2023a; Gu et al., 2024). The fifth line of works has explored the potential of knowledge
1100
       editing in bias or toxicity mitigation (Cai et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024c; Yan et al., 2024; Uppaal
       et al., 2024). Different from previous studies, our work opens a new direction for knowledge editing
1101
       and sheds light on its potential misuse risks for misinformation or bias injection.
1102
1103

       B.2    T HE I MPACT ON S AFETY OF O PEN - SOURCE LLM S
1105   Owing to the popularity of open-source LLM communities such as HuggingFace, it is critical to ensure
1106   the safety of models uploaded to these platforms (Eiras et al., 2024; Solaiman et al., 2023; Gabriel
1107   et al., 2024; Longpre et al., 2024). Currently, the models are usually aligned with safety protocols
1108   through post-training stages such as RLHF (Ji et al., 2024a;b). However, our work has demonstrated
1109   that the safety alignment of LLMs is fragile under editing attacks, which pose serious threats to the
1110   open-source communities. Specifically, as for the misinformation injection risk, conventionally,
1111   misinformation is disseminated in information channels such as social media (Chen et al., 2022; Shu
1112   et al., 2017). Currently, LLMs have emerged as a new channel since users are increasingly inclined
1113
       to interact with LLMs directly to acquire information. The experiments show that malicious actors
       are able to inject misinformation into open-source LLMs stealthily and easily via editing attacks,
1114
       which could result in the large-scale dissemination of misinformation. Thus, editing attacks may
1115
       bring a new type of misinformation dissemination risk and escalate the misinformation crisis in the
1116   age of LLMs in addition to the existing misinformation generation risk (Chen & Shu, 2024a;b).
1117   As for the bias injection risk, our work has shown that malicious users could subvert the fairness
1118   in general outputs of LLMs with one single biased sentence injection, which may exacerbate the
1119   dissemination of stereotyped information in open-source LLMs. We call for more open discussions
1120   from different stakeholders on the governance of open-source LLMs to maximize the benefit and
1121   minimize the potential risk (Kapoor et al., 2024; Reuel et al., 2024; Anderljung et al., 2023; Schuett
1122   et al., 2023; Seger et al., 2023; Yohsua et al., 2024).

       D.1    DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
1245
1246   The basic construction pipeline of E DITATTACK has been described in Section 2.4. More specifically,
1247   as for the part of Misinformation Injection, we first adopted the existing jailbreaking techniques in
1248   the literature (Zou et al., 2023b; Xu et al., 2024b) to generate a large collection of misinformation
1249   with ChatGPT-3.5. For commonsense misinformation injection, we specifically ask ChatGPT-3.5
1250   to generate misinformation that contradicts humansвҖҷ commonsense. For long-tail misinformation
1251
       injection, we require that the outputs of ChatGPT-3.5 include terminologies, which need to rarely
       occur, from five domains including chemistry, biology, geology, medicine, and physics. Second,
1252
       we combine human effort and multiple state-of-the-art LLMs such as GPT-4 and Claude to select
1253
       and retain the factually misleading samples as the targets. Third, we leverage GPT-4 to extract the
1254   knowledge triplet (subject s, relation r, object oвҲ— ) from the targeted misinformation samples and
1255   generate evaluation questions accordingly. As for the part of Bias Injection, we directly select the non-
1256   duplicated (object oвҲ— , evaluation context c) from the вҖңambiguousвҖқ part of the BBQ dataset (Parrish
1257   et al., 2022) and leverage GPT-4 to extract the (subject s, relation r) from the dataset. Then, we use
1258   GPT-4 again to generate corresponding evaluation questions.
1259
1260   D.2    DATASET S TATISTICS
1261
1262   The whole E DITATTACK dataset contains 868 data points for commonsense misinformation injection,
1263   100 data points for long-tail misinformation injection, 127 data points for bias injection. The
1264
       number of long-tail misinformation in each of the five domains including chemistry, biology, geology,
       medicine, and physics is 20. Since we ensure there is no duplicated context in the part of bias injection,
1265
       the amounts for bias types including Gender, Race, Religion, Sexual Orientation, and Disability
1266
       Status are 25, 27, 25, 25, and 25 respectively. In the experiments, we select 100 samples from the
1267   868 data points for commonsense misinformation injection, all the 100 data points for long-tail
1268   misinformation injection, and all the 127 data points for bias injection, which can sufficiently support
1269   our findings. The whole E DITATTACK dataset can be adopted to benchmark LLMsвҖҷ robustness
1270   against editing attacks more comprehensively in the future, and facilitate the development of defense
1271   methods against editing attacks.
1272
1273   D.3    DATASET E THICS
1274
1275   The E DITATTACK dataset contains samples of misleading or stereotyped language. To avoid the
1276   potential risk that malicious users abuse this dataset to inject misinformation or bias into open-source
1277
       LLMs and then disseminate misinformation or biased content in a large scale, we will only cautiously
       release the dataset to individual researchers or research communities. We would like to emphasize
1278
       that this dataset provides the initial resource to combat the emerging but critical risk of editing attacks.
1279
       We believe it will serve as a starting point in this new direction and greatly facilitate the research on
1280   gaining more understanding of the inner mechanism of editing attacks, designing defense techniques
1281   and enhancing LLMsвҖҷ intrinsic robustness.
