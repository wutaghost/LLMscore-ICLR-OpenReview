009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Video Temporal Grounding (VTG) seeks to retrieve consecutive intervals or spe-
012            cific clips from a video based on specified natural language queries. VTG requires
013            accurately aligning video segments with corresponding natural language instruc-
014            tions, highlighting the need for effective methodologies to capture semantic cor-
015            respondence and maintain temporal coherence. Spiking neural networks (SNNs),
016            previously underexplored in this domain, present a unique opportunity to tackle
017            VTG challenges from both the architectural and energy-efficiency perspectives.
018
               In this paper, we leverage sparse spike-based communication of SNNs to propose
               a multimodal architecture tailored for VTG tasks, namely SpikingVTG, providing
019
               a biologically inspired and efficient solution. Leveraging temporal saliency feed-
020
               back, our proposed spiking video-language model (VLM) achieves competitive
021            performance with non-spiking VLMs across diverse moment retrieval and high-
022            light detection tasks. We introduce a Saliency Feedback Gating (SFG) mechanism
023            that improves performance while reducing overall neural activity. To efficiently
024            train our spiking VLM, we analyze the convergence dynamics of each neuronal
025            layer and utilize equilibrium states to enable training using implicit differentiation
026            at equilibrium. This approach eliminates the need for computationally expensive
027            backpropagation through time while also enabling the use of knowledge distilla-
028            tion for efficient model training. To further improve operational efficiency and fa-
029
               cilitate the on-chip deployability of our model, we leverage a multi-stage training
               pipeline that focuses on eliminating non-local computations, such as softmax and
030
               layer normalization, leading to the development of the Normalization Free (NF)-
031
               SpikingVTG model. Additionally, we create an extremely quantized variant, a 1-
032            bit NF-SpikingVTG model, which vastly improves computational efficiency dur-
033            ing inference while maintaining minimal performance degradation from our base
034            model. Our work introduces the first spiking model to demonstrate competitive
035            performance on VTG benchmarks, including QVHighlights and Charades-STA.

038   1   I NTRODUCTION
039
040   The rapid expansion of various social medias and portable smart technologies has triggered an un-
041   precedented surge in video content. This vast influx of data has intensified the need for efficient
042   methods to retrieve and analyze video information. Consequently, the field of Video Temporal
043   Grounding (VTG) (Lei et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2023) has emerged as an important area of research.
044   The main objective of VTG is to identify the precise segment of a video that corresponds to a given
045
      natural language query, enabling accurate and context-driven video content retrieval. In this paper,
      we focus on two tasks: moment retrieval (Zhang et al., 2020; Mun et al., 2020), which aims to
046
      identify video intervals relevant to a given query, and highlight detection (Hong et al., 2020), which
047
      retrieves the best candidate segment of the video in response to the query. Our work involves an-
048   alyzing multimodal data—combining video content with natural language queries—to develop an
049   effective solution to the problem. With the rise of foundation models like large language models
050   (LLMs) and video-language models (VLM), the field of VTG has seen significant advancements
051   (Liu et al., 2022; Lei et al., 2021). However, these models demand substantial computational power
052   and energy (Samsi et al., 2023) to operate. Furthermore, VTG is inherently resource-intensive, re-
053   quiring the analysis of long video sequences, leading to significant computational overhead. In this
      work, we leverage sparse spike-based communication and simplified accumulation-based compu-

054
      tation in spiking neural networks (SNNs) (Ghosh-Dastidar & Adeli, 2009) to develop an efficient,
055   lightweight solution for VTG.
056
057   Beyond the computational efficiency of SNN-based frameworks, we also harness their temporal dy-
058
      namics to propose a spiking transformer-based VLM (Fig. 1), namely SpikingVTG, that matches
      or surpasses the performance of current state-of-the-art non-spiking VLM. The input video for the
059
      VTG task typically consists of a long sequence of segments or clips. A key challenge in VTG is thus
060
      accurately identifying salient segments (Lin et al., 2023) or temporally dependent segments that ex-
061   hibit a strong semantic correspondence with the given query. Our SNN-based VLM, operated over a
062   period of simulation time steps, allows us to leverage its intermediate temporal output as a feedback
063   to identify the salient segments. We use the average spiking rate (ASR) of the output of the spiking
064   transformer core in the SpikingVTG model to compute a dynamic saliency score of each video seg-
065   ment w.r.t the given query, which we then leverage as a mask for a multiplicative gating mechanism.
066   This improves performance of the model by enabling it to focus on relevant portions of the video,
067   while also reducing computational overhead by minimizing attention to irrelevant segments.
068   From a bio-plausibility perspective, as explored by Kar et al. (2019), feedback based connection
069   plays a prominant role in human visual cortex primarily responsible for object recognition. Fur-
070   thermore, the feedback connection maintains the layer-wise convergence of ASR at equilibrium,
071   enabling the implementation of an implicit differentiation framework (Xiao et al., 2021), allowing
072   for more efficient training of our model. This learning framework, leverages layer-wise converged
073   ASR values at equilibrium to train the spiking model in one backpropagation step, instead of us-
074   ing the computationally expensive backpropagation through time (BPTT) (Neftci et al., 2019). The
075
      SpikingVTG framework further involves a multi-stage training pipeline aimed at developing spiking
      models to facilitate potential deployment on resource-constrained edge-based device enabled with
076
      neuromorphic chips. To allow for efficient training of our spiking model, we employ a knowledge
077
      distillation strategy (Hinton et al., 2015), enabling knowledge transfer from a non-spiking UniVTG
078   model, used as the “teacher”, to our “student” SpikingVTG model. This process utilizes the ASR of
079   converged intermediate states at equilibrium, enabling efficient training of our spiking VLM.
080
081
      Traditional transformer architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017) utilize non-local normalization op-
      erations such as softmax and layer normalization, which present challenges for implementation
082
      on neuromorphic hardware (Shrestha et al., 2022). To address this limitation, we introduce the
083
      Normalization-Free (NF)-SpikingVTG model, which eliminates all layer normalization operations
084   and substitutes softmax spiking attention with a ReLU-based spiking attention mechanism. Al-
085   though, Softmax-free attention has been explored in the literature (Koohpayegani & Pirsiavash,
086   2024; Xu et al., 2024), it has predominantly been applied to vision tasks. While, ReLU-based at-
087   tention mechanisms have previously been explored in non-spiking domains (Shen et al., 2023), we
088   are the first to introduce this concept within a spiking attention mechanism. Additionally, to reduce
089   computational complexity, following works on quantization in analog LLMs (Wang et al., 2023),
090   we propose a 1-bit quantized variant of SpikingVTG. Our multi-stage training pipeline enables min-
091   imal performance degradation while enhancing computational efficiency during inference, in our
092
      SpikingVTG models. To our knowledge, this work is the first to evaluate an operational spiking
      VLM framework across various VTG tasks, including moment retrieval and highlight detection, on
093
      datasets such as QVHighlights and Charades-STA.

131   2   R ELATED W ORKS
132
133   VTG Advancements: With the recent rise of multimodal LLM architectures, the field of video-
134   language modeling has opened new avenues for understanding and extracting key information from
135   video data. Moment-DETR (Lei et al., 2021), a transformer encoder-decoder model introduced
136
      alongside the QVHighlights dataset, laid a strong foundation for subsequent VTG architectures.
      UMT (Liu et al., 2022) introduced an unified framework for solving both highlight detection and
137
      moment retrieval tasks. Due to the limited availability of trainable video data, UniVTG (Lin
138
      et al., 2023) proposed an innovative solution by unifying various VTG tasks and labels under a
139   single formulation. This approach enabled the development of an LLM-like pretraining frame-
140   work, achieving state-of-the-art performance on VTG tasks. Although no fully spiking-based ar-
141   chitecture has been explored for VTG tasks, SpikeMba (Li et al., 2024)—primarily a non-spiking
142   model—integrates SNN components to generate proposal sets from video data. However, since its
143   core framework is derived from Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) and relies on floating-point matrix multi-
144   plications, SpikeMba cannot be considered a baseline for spiking models, which predominantly use
145   accumulation-based operations.
146   Spiking neural networks (SNNs): SNNs allow for event-driven computation and communica-
147   tion in neuromorphic hardware, significantly reducing energy consumption. SNNs have been im-
148   plemented in neuromorphic systems like IBM TrueNorth (DeBole et al., 2019) and Intel Loihi 2
149   (Davies et al., 2021), demonstrating approximately 75× greater energy efficiency compared to tra-
150   ditional neural networks running on low-power GPUs (Tang et al., 2020). SNNs, with their energy-
151   efficient computational framework, offer a promising solution to the resource-intensive demands of
152   multimodal VTG tasks. While SNNs for a long time were confined in simpler vision-based tasks
153
      (Yamazaki et al., 2022) with relatively simple architectures, recent developments have scaled them
      to transformer-based architectures for tasks ranging from vision to language modelling (Zhou et al.,
154
      2022; Bal & Sengupta, 2024; Zhu et al., 2023), however majority of them rely on some normaliza-
155
      tion techniques which are not implementable on a neuromorphic chip.

169

170
      studies on VTG (Lin et al., 2023), we define three parameters for each clip vi = (fi , di , si ), where
171
      fi = 1 if the clip is in foreground, i.e. relevant else fi = 0. di = [dsi , dei ] ∈ R2 represent the
172   temporal distance that converts the clip timestamp ti to its interval boundaries. Here, di is valid
173   when fi = 1. The term dsi denotes the distance between the start of the interval and ti , while
174   dei denotes the distance between the end of the interval and ti . si ∈ [0, 1] is a continuous score
175   that quantifies the relevance between the visual content of clip vi and the query Q. Our proposed
176   SpikingVTG model predicts these three parameters for each video clip. In this paper, we focus on
177   specific VTG tasks, which are carried out as follows:

187   3.2     S PIKING VTG: A RCHITECTURE OVERVIEW
188
189   The core computational unit of the proposed SpikingVTG model is a leaky integrate-and-fire (LIF)
190
      neuron (Dutta et al., 2017). Neurons communicate with each other using sparse, spike-based acti-
      vations instead of real-valued signals, significantly improving energy/power efficiency. The model
191
      architecture includes a spiking transformer core for processing inputs, a saliency feedback gating
192
      mechanism for dynamic input control, and a spiking decoder module to predict the parameters re-
193   quired for the VTG task, as described in Section 3.1.

217
218   The high-level overview of each encoder block of our spiking transformer architecture is demon-
219   strated in Fig. 1. The model consists of N encoder layers, each consists of a spiking multi-headed

221   between encoder layers occurs via spikes. Furthermore, all matrix multiplications involved in linear
222   layers and attention layer comprises of more efficient fp-accumulative (ACC) operations instead of
223
      fp-MAC operations in conventional neural architectures. For this work we have used four encoder

337   3.4     M ULTI -S TAGED T RAINING P IPELINE
338
339   Training a multimodal spiking architecture like SpikingVTG is resource-intensive. To enhance the
340   efficiency of this process and develop computationally efficient variants of our model, we propose a
341   multi-staged training framework, as illustrated in Fig. 4. We utilize a non-spiking “teacher” VLM
342   to guide the training of our “student” SpikingVTG model. After this initial stage, we fine-tune
343   SpikingVTG using the true labels. Once the base SpikingVTG model is established, we modify
344
      its architecture, as outlined in Sections 3.4.2 and 3.4.3, followed by additional fine-tuning to cre-
      ate computationally efficient variants with minimal performance degradation. The resulting com-
345
      putationally efficient, lightweight models are well-suited for deployment on neuromorphic chips,
346
      enabling efficient inference.

      3.4.1    L EVERAGING K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION (KD)
349

      5     C ONCLUSIONS
532
      Our saliency feedback gating-enabled SpikingVTG model offers a computationally efficient ap-
533
      proach for VTG tasks while maintaining competitive performance with state-of-the-art non-spiking
534   models. By harnessing layer-wise convergence dynamics, we efficiently train our model using
535   implicit differentiation at equilibrium. We employ a multi-stage training pipeline that incorpo-
536   rates knowledge distillation, using the non-spiking pretrained UniVTG model as the “teacher” and
537   the SpikingVTG model as the “student”. This training pipeline further enables architectural op-
538   timizations, leading to the development of Normalization Free (NF)-SpikingVTG and 1-bit NF-
539   SpikingVTG, enhancing computational efficiency and facilitating the on-chip deployment of these
      complex models.

850   D IFFERENTIATION AT E QUILIBRIUM WITH BPTT
851
852   We were unable to train our model using BPTT due to its significantly higher memory demands
853   during training. For example, training our SpikingVTG model on the QVHighlights dataset re-
854   quires 25GB of memory with a batch size of 32 and 50 time steps for convergence (T). In contrast,
855   the memory requirement for training with BPTT exceeds 100GB, as it requires storing the entire
856
      computational graph throughout the training process. Consequently, training the model with BPTT
      is not feasible. This also underscores the advantage of the equilibrium-based training mechanism
857
      employed in this work.

888   D.4   E NERGY A NALYSIS
889
890   Let us walk through a specific example of analyzing the energy consumption for the 1-bit NF-
891   SpikingVTG model. Consider a single transformer encoder layer. As discussed in the main paper,
892   the computational cost of the layer for a non-spiking model is given by: EA = [3LD2 + (LD2 +
893
      L2 D) + LD2 + LD2 ] ∗ (4.6mJ). In our implementation, we use D = 1024 and lets consider
      total sequence length L = 200. Thus we get energy cost of each block is 5.98mJ. Now, energy
894
      cost per time step of our 1-bit NF-SpikingVTG is given as, ESt = [(3 · IF Rin · LD2 ) + (IF Rk ·
895
      LD2 + IF Rv · L2 D) + (IF Rattn · LD2 ) + (IF Rinterm. · LD2 )] ∗ (0.1pJ). Empirically we find
896   IF Rin = 0.40, IF Rk = 0.18, IF Rv = 0.19, IF Rattn = 0.03, IF Rinterm. = 0.09.
897
898   Thus ESt = 0.03mJ resulting in ES = ESt ∗ T = .3mJ, where T = 10. Thus efficiency factor is
899
      Ef = EA /ES = 19.93.
