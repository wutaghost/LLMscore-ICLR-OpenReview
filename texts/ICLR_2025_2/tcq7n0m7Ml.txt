011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            As large language models (LLMs) continue to advance, the demand for higher
014            quality and faster processing of long contexts across various applications is grow-
015
               ing. KV cache is widely adopted as it stores previously generated key and value
               tokens, effectively reducing redundant computations during inference. However,
016
               as memory overhead becomes a significant concern, efficient compression of KV
017
               cache has gained increasing attention. Most existing methods perform compres-
018            sion from two perspectives: identifying important tokens and designing compres-
019            sion strategies. However, these approaches often produce biased distributions of
020            important tokens due to the influence of accumulated attention scores or posi-
021            tional encoding. Furthermore, they overlook the sparsity and redundancy across
022            different heads, which leads to difficulties in preserving the most effective infor-
023            mation at the head level. To this end, we propose EMS to overcome these limita-
024            tions, while achieving better KV cache compression under extreme compression
025            ratios. Specifically, we introduce a Global-Local score that combines accumu-
026
               lated attention scores from both global and local KV tokens to better identify the
               token importance. For the compression strategy, we design an adaptive and unified
027
               Evict-then-Merge framework that accounts for the sparsity and redundancy of KV
028
               tokens across different heads. Additionally, we implement the head-wise parallel
029            compression through a zero-class mechanism to enhance efficiency. Extensive ex-
030            periments demonstrate our SOTA performance even under extreme compression
031            ratios. EMS consistently achieves the lowest perplexity, improves scores by over
032            1.28 points across four LLMs on LongBench under a 256 cache budget, and pre-
033            serves 95% retrieval accuracy with a cache budget less than 2% of the context
034            length in the Needle-in-a-Haystack task.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Large language models (LLMs) (Devlin, 2018; Brown et al., 2020; Anil et al., 2023; Dubey et al.,
      2024; Jiang et al., 2023) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across various domains, such as
040
      question answering (Jiang et al., 2021; Lazaridou et al., 2022), retrieval systems (Ram et al., 2023;
041
      Xu et al., 2023), logical reasoning (Wei et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023a), and code generation (Roziere
042   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a), etc. With growing application demands for LLMs, the requirement
043   to manage long sequences (Chen et al., 2024b; Jin et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023) is also increas-
044   ing. So far, GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) can process approximately 128K tokens, Gemini-Pro-1.5
045   (Team et al., 2023) handles about 1M tokens, and Kimi-Chat can process up to 2M tokens. These
046   developments pose significant challenges to the inference efficiency of LLMs. One key acceleration
047   technique is the KV cache, where the key-value (KV) states generated during inference are stored in
048   GPU memory to avoid redundant computation and improve processing efficiency. However, the size
049   of the KV cache grows with the length of the input sequence, which severely limiting the applicabil-
050
      ity of LLMs (Yuan et al., 2024). Therefore, efficiently compressing the KV cache while preserving
      essential information has become a critical issue.
051
052   Extensive researches have been carried out to address it. Specifically, StreamingLLM (Xiao et al.,
053   2024) first discovered the sink mechanism and achieved infinite output by retaining the initial and
      local tokens. Subsequent works mainly approached this issue from two perspectives: (i) extracting


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the most important parts of the generated KV tokens, and (ii) designing compression strategies to
055   preserve more information. For example, H2O (Zhang et al., 2023) focuses on globally important
056   tokens, while SnapKV (Li et al., 2024b) concentrates on tokens with higher relevance within a local
057   window size. However, due to cumulative effects of attention weights and positional encoding, the
058   selected tokens exhibit a biased tendency: H2O favors earlier information, while SnapKV leans
059   towards later context. Building upon lightweight token compression methods (Bolya et al., 2022;
060   Yin et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024a), existing approaches are generally categorized into evict-based and
061   merge-based methods. However, a unified and effective approach that combines evict and merge
062   for extreme KV cache compression is still largely unexplored. Therefore, we pose the following
063
      question:
064   Can we select important KV tokens in a more balanced manner while retaining as much infor-
065   mation as possible at a high compression ratio?
066
      To answer this question, we decouple the compression of KV cache into two stages: selecting im-
067   portant tokens and compressing the KV cache based on those selections. Under this workflow, we
068   propose EMS, a head-wise Evict-then-Merge Strategy based on Global-Local importance, as illus-
069   trated in Figure 1. Building on the token importance bias, we dynamically integrate the global-
070   and local-aware importance and construct a more balanced Global-Local score, which serves as
071   the fundamental indicator for efficient compression. Specifically, the score is calculated by align-
072   ing the accumulated attention scores of all KV tokens and recent KV tokens. This approach not
073   only ensures a more balanced selection of important KV tokens, but also mitigates biases caused by
074   attention accumulation and positional encoding.
075   For the compression strategy, the observed differences in sparsity and redundancy across different
076   heads suggest that applying head-wise eviction and merging can potentially achieve a higher com-
077   pression ratio. Building on this, we propose a unified Evict-then-Merge strategy at the fine-grained
078   head level to improve storage density. In particular, the most irrelevant tokens will first be evicted,
079   leaving only the tokens with higher importance scores for further compression. A subset of the
080   remaining tokens is then selected as class centers based on their higher importance, with less im-
081
      portant tokens being merged into these centers. However, as not all tokens are suitable for merging
      due to low redundancy, those with low similarity to important KV tokens are evicted to minimize
082
      output disturbance. To ensure parallel inference during head-wise merging and eviction, we intro-
083
      duce a zero-class center where evicted tokens are merged, treating the eviction process as a special
084   case of merging. Additionally, this step allows for a dynamic merge ratio for each head, ensuring a
085   more adaptive and efficient compression. EMS achieves extreme compression while preserving the
086   capabilities of LLMs, boosting scores by more than 1.28 points on four LLMs in LongBench with a
087   256 cache budget, and retaining 95% retrieval accuracy using under 2% of the context length in the
088   Needle-in-a-Haystack task. To summarize, our contribution can be generalized as:
089
            (1) We design a more balanced Global-Local score for important token selection. Our
090             Global-Local score automatically integrates global and local attention of KV tokens, re-
091             ducing bias and ensuring more balanced token selection across different tasks.
092         (2) We propose a sparsity- and redundancy-driven Evict-then-Merge compression strat-
093             egy. Based on the head-wise sparsity and redundancy characteristic of KV tokens, we de-
094             velop an Evict-then-Merge strategy that maximizes information retention even under low
095             compression ratios.
096         (3) We implement an efficient head-wise parallel compression for the KV cache. We pro-
097             pose a zero-class center where KV tokens with low similarity to important KV tokens are
098
                merged. In this way, we treat eviction as merging, achieving input-aware parallel evict and
                merge ratios, while maintaining a constant budget for each head. This strategy efficiently
099
                leverages the head-wise distribution property in a simple yet effective way.

104   2.1    I MPORTANT KV S ELECTION
105
106   Recent works on KV cache compression have focused on the selection of the important KV tokens
107   to preserve the performance of uncompressed LLMs. StreamingLLM (Xiao et al., 2024) discov-
      ered the sink mechanism, where the attention weights for the initial tokens are significantly high


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      regardless of its meaning. Consequently, it retains the first four tokens along with some local to-
109   kens, enabling streaming output with the constrained memory. Following it, ACT (Yu et al., 2024)
110   further demonstrates that certain KV tokens in the middle also exhibit high attention weights. H2O
111   (Zhang et al., 2023) selects important KV pairs based on accumulated attention score, leading to
112   more concentration in the former part of the context. SnapKV (Li et al., 2024b) observed that calcu-
113   lating attention within a local window of the prompt can capture specialized attention features. This
114   approach retains more recent tokens, which are effective for local generation. However, in most
115   scenarios, valuable information is often spread across the contexts and varies from different tasks.
116   Therefore, the selection of important KV pairs should be tailored to the specific input and avoid
117
      former or recent preference caused by importance indicator.
118
119   2.2    KV C OMPRESSION
120
      Inspired by various token compression methods (Yin et al., 2022; Bolya et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024a),
121
      KV cache compression can also be categorized into eviction and merge strategies. Evict-based
122   methods aim to retain only the important KV pairs under different token importance assumption
123   (Xiao et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b; Adnan et al., 2024; Cai et al., 2024; Feng
124   et al., 2024), while maintain the modeling ability. DCP (Anagnostidis et al., 2023) introduces a
125   lightweight attention block in each layer to dynamically decide which KV pairs to discard based on
126   the input. FastGen (Ge et al., 2023) extends this by finding that different heads focus on different
127   token types. Through the attention profiling in the prompt prefilling stage, it defines distinct com-
128   pression strategies for each head, which are applied during inference. However, this static approach
129   can be suboptimal in long context scenarios where attention patterns change as decoding proceeds.
130
      Besides, compressing each head differently can lead to uneven head budgets, making storage and
      computation more difficult.
131
132   Compared to evict-only methods, merge-based approaches have the potential to retain more infor-
133   mation and enhance model performance. CAM (Zhang et al., 2024) analyzes the attention output
134   error caused by token eviction, only merge the evicted value tokens into the remaining ones to
135   reduce performance loss. DMC (Nawrot et al., 2024) dynamically decides whether to merge the
136
      current token to the tail of the KV cache in a head-wise manner. However, compression only at the
      tail may not be the most effective approach. LESS (Dong et al., 2024) introduces a low-rank em-
137
      bedding sidekick with sparse policy, which accumulates the information discarded by the eviction
138
      strategy into a fixed-size low-rank cache. Besides, DMC and LESS require additional training for
139   performance gains. It is worth noting that KV cache merging is different from token merging, due
140   to the paired processing of keys and values and the autoregressive characteristic of LLMs. Improper
141   merging methods can lead to significant error accumulation in the decoding stage, resulting in se-
142   vere performance degradation. Building on these insights, we propose an efficient Evict-then-Merge
143   compression method that effectively addresses these challenges.

305   Token Partition. The Global-Local score can effectively measure the importance of tokens. To
306   avoid extract fragmented information, we apply a mean pooling function to it. Based on the ranking

308
      to-be-merged (TBM) tokens (Ktbm , Vtbm ), and Nimp most important tokens (Kimp , Vimp ), except

309
      LLMs, which is crucial for the performance across various tasks. The most irrelevant tokens are first
310

313   valuable information as possible. The merge magnification factor γ = (Nbudget + Ntbm )/Nbudget
314   indicates the extent to which tokens are merged relative to the cache budget.

476   6     C ONCLUSION
477
478   In this paper, we propose EMS, an input-aware, head-wise efficient KV cache management frame-
479   work. By leveraging the proposed Global-Local attention score, EMS addresses the biased distri-
480   bution of important KV tokens caused by accumulated attention scores and positional encoding,
481   leading to a more balanced selection of tokens. We further design a unified Evict-then-Merge strat-
482
      egy based on the redundancy and sparsity intrinsic of KV tokens across different heads. In particular,
      we implement a zero-class mechanism to enable parallel computation for head-wise operation. Ex-
483
      tensive experiments on language modeling perplexity, LongBench, and Needle-in-a-Haystack tasks
484
      demonstrate the SOTA performance, validating the effectiveness of EMS.

705   Two-Levels of Parallelism. We regarded EMS as a parallel solution for two reasons. On the
706   one hand, as illustrated in Section 4.1, the sparsity and redundancy provide the potential for more
707   extreme compression. However, the head-wise characteristic can lead to different cache bud-
708   gets for different heads. For code implementation, the shape of KV states is (batch size,
709   num heads, kv len, head dim), which means that all heads are concatenated as a tensor
710   and computed in parallel. Luckily, there are two properties and they exhibit complementarity to
711
      some extent. For example, we can evict more for the heads with higher sparsity and less redun-
      dancy (dark blue and light red bars), and merge more for the heads with higher redundancy and less
712
      redundancy (light blue and dark red bars). So we take this chance to allocate the same budget for
713
      each head. On the other hand, merge and evict are two different operations. Different heads may
714   have different operations and different merge-evict ratios. To unify the merge and evict process, we
715   introduce a zero-class, thus the eviction can be treated as merging, and we only do merging at the
716   second stage of Evict-then-Merge strategy.
717
      Two Levels of Eviction. Under our Evict-then-Merge strategy, there are two levels of eviction
718
      consideration, which is shown in Figure 4a. Under the long context scenarios, not all tokens are
719
      necessary for language modeling and can lead to significant memory overhead. Hence, the less
720   relevant tokens will first be evicted and leaves Nimp + Ntbm + Nloc tokens for subsequent merging,
721   which is same for each heads. At the merge stage, considering that some TBM tokens cannot find
722   a suitable merge destination for low similarity, evicting them can avoid disturbing the class centers.
723   So these tokens are merged to zero-class, which is equivalent to eviction.
724
      Evict-then-Merge Details. There are some implementation differences in the compression of pre-
725
      filling and decoding stage. Specifically, at prefilling stage, numerous tokens are filled at once, which
726   might far exceed the size of the cache budget. Therefore, it is necessary to partition them and de-
727   termine the TBM tokens and class-center tokens. And this stage often requires to merge multiple
728   tokens for each head. The merged Nimp tokens serve as the class centers, where Ntbm + Nimp
729   tokens will share the Nimp entries. At decoding stage, we focus more on the update of class centers
730   and the mapping relation, which is shown in Figure 4b. To achieve dynamic class center, the merge
731   operation is processed at the class center level, which means that merging will change the mapping
732   of the TBM tokens and class centers. After each decoding in the auto-regressive generation, a new
733   token will exceed local range and is regarded as the class center. One least important class center
734
      token is selected as TBM token. For each head, there are two kinds of operations for this TBM to-
      ken. Different heads may have different decisions for merge or evict operation, and they are unified
735
      as merge. Since we keep the cache budget constant, the number of expanded tokens is also fixed. So
736
      we need to evict one element from the look-up-table and fill the new mapping.

      Shared Entry Expansion. The motivation of expansion is that we hope to conduct clustering on
757   the KV tokens based on cosine similarity. In this way, we can use a small number of class center
758   tokens to represent more tokens. Therefore, those similar tokens share the same KV entry and need
759   to be expanded during computation. For the implementation of expansion, we keep a position look-
760   up-table and Nimp tokens are expanded to γNimp tokens at computation time of the certain layer. In
761   this way, more context tokens participate in the computation, and only additional overhead is only
762   brought to the current computed layer.
763   Global Score Efficiency Discussion. FlashAttention has become a standard technique for long se-
764   quence inference. It achieves more efficient attention computation through tiling, yet doesn’t return
765   the attention weights. The calculation of the global score requires obtaining all the attention weights.
766   To be compatible with Accumulated FlashAttention, recent works have also made some optimiza-
767   tions. ZipCache (He et al., 2024) approximates global scores by sampling 10% probe tokens, which
768   is orthogonal and compatible with our work. NACL (Chen et al., 2024a) has implemented the accu-
769   mulated attention weights of FlashAttention by recomputation based on Paddle. Thus, the dilemma
770   regarding the global score can be solved.

775   Merge Threshold. The merge threshold plays a crucial role in determining whether a token will
776   be merged into the zero class (i.e., evicted) or common class centers. A smaller threshold results
777   in more tokens being evicted, while a larger threshold leads to more aggressive merging. Table 6
778   presents the results of varying the merge threshold, indicating that a moderate threshold τ = 0.6,
779   yields best performance on balance.
780   Two extreme cases are setting threshold to 0 or 1, meaning all merging or all evicting. If τ =
781   0, all TBM tokens are merged without considering that some tokens are not suitable to merge,
782   leading to 1.66 performance drop. On contrary, if τ = 1, all TBM tokens are evicted, resulting in a
783   performance degradation of 0.52. Therefore, both over merging and over evicting yields sub-optimal
784   performance, manifesting the effectiveness of joint merge and evict.
