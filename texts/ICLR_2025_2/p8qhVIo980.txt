009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In few-shot learning, such as meta-learning, few-shot fine-tuning or in-context
012            learning, the selection of samples has a significant impact on the performance
013            of the trained model. Although many sample selection strategies are employed
014            and evaluated in typical supervised settings, their impact on the performance of
015            few-shot learning is largely unknown. In this paper, we investigate the impact of
016            20 sample selection strategies on the performance of 5 representative few-shot
017
               learning approaches over 8 image and 6 text datasets. We propose a new method for
               Automatic Combination of SamplE Selection Strategies (ACSESS), to leverage the
018
               strengths and complementarity of the individual strategies in order to select more
019
               impactful samples. The experimental results show that our method consistently
020            outperforms all individual selection strategies. We also show that the majority of
021            existing strategies strongly depend on modality, dataset characteristics and few-shot
022            learning approach, while improving performance especially on imbalanced and
023            noisy datasets. Lastly, we show that sample selection strategies work well even on
024            smaller datasets and provide larger benefit when selecting a lower number of shots,
025            while frequently regressing to random selection with higher numbers of shots.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Many domains are characterised by a labelled data scarcity due to data collection and annotation
031
      costs and privacy, making the training of typical deep learning models unfeasible. To address these
      problems, few-shot learning aims to train or adapt machine learning models to new tasks using only a
032
      few labelled samples per class (Song et al., 2023). There are two main categories of few-shot learning
033
      (more details regarding their differences are included in Appendix K): 1) gradient-based, which
034   includes meta-learning and fine-tuning, where the knowledge from relevant datasets is transferred
035   to a new problem using gradient-based training, similar to supervised learning (Song et al., 2023;
036   Chen et al., 2019; Vanschoren, 2018; Hospedales et al., 2021); and 2) few-shot learning via in-context
037   learning, a new paradigm, where a pretrained large language model is conditioned on few examples
038   to generate the prediction without any parameter updates (Dong et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023).

052   As the number of utilised labelled samples is small in few-shot learning, the quality of individual
053   samples is crucial. Existing studies (in different settings such as aligning large language models (Zhou
      et al., 2024)) have shown that curating a smaller subset of the most informative and high-quality

      samples can lead to better performance than training on the full set of available samples. This
055   holds for few-shot learning as well, especially for noisy or unbalanced datasets, as illustrated in
056   Figure 1. Moreover, a strong sensitivity to the sample selection causes performance variance between
057   state-of-the-art and random predictions (Pecher et al., 2023; Agarwal et al., 2021; Köksal et al., 2022).
058
      Despite a lot of research dedicated to sample selection, such as active learning (Ren et al., 2021)
059
      or core-set selection (Guo et al., 2022), the impact of sample selection for few-shot learning
060
      remains under-explored, where the most common approach is random selection. The exception is
061   in-context learning, where a large number of specific sample selection strategies have been proposed
062   recently. They select samples according to sample similarity, diversity, informativeness or quality (Li
063   & Qiu, 2023; Zhang et al., 2022; Chang & Jia, 2023). However, the use of more general approaches
064   (e.g., active learning) for in-context learning is still largely unknown. Therefore, this paper takes
065   the data-centric perspective of few-shot learning to investigate the impact of the sample selection
066   strategies on few-shot image and text classification tasks across a wide range of datasets.
067   The various existing strategies mostly focus on selecting samples based on a single property (e.g., how
068   often the samples are forgotten (Toneva et al., 2018), how easy they are to learn (Swayamdipta et al.,
069   2020; Zhang & Plank, 2021) or how informative they are (Ye et al., 2023; Liu & Wang, 2023)). In this
070   paper, we ask whether we can automatically and optimally combine sample selection strategies
071   to identify samples with multiple complementary properties. To answer this question and to
072   leverage the strengths of different sample selection strategies, we propose ACSESS, an effective
073   method for Automatic Combination of SamplE Selection Strategies. The proposed method first
074   identifies a subset of relevant strategies that can improve the overall success of few-shot learning.
075
      Afterwards, the identified strategies are combined together, using weighting based on their expected
      contribution, in order to identify the most informative and high-quality samples that can provide the
076
      most benefit. We summarise our contributions and findings as follows:1
077
078          • We evaluate the impact of 20 sample selection strategies on 5 few-shot learning approaches,
079            including meta-learning, few-shot fine-tuning and in-context learning, across 8 image
080            and 6 text datasets. Results show that the selection strategies often lead to non-negligible
081            improvements in performance (up to 2 and 3 percentage points for gradient few-shot learning
082            and in-context learning respectively), with a strong approach and dataset dependence.
083            Overall, we find that curating a set of high-quality samples is especially important for
084            imbalanced datasets with noisy samples, while the performance increase is limited for
085
               datasets with high-quality samples.
086          • We propose a method for automatic combination of sample selection strategies to leverage
087            their strengths and identify samples with complementary properties required for the success
088            of few-shot learning (i.e., informativeness, representativeness and learnability). Experimen-
089
               tal results show that the method consistently leads to performance improvement (up to 3 and
               5 percentage points for gradient few-shot learning and in-context learning respectively).
090
091          • We further analyse how the number of shots and the size of dataset affect the impact of
092            sample selection. We find following key insights: 1) sample selection has higher impact
093
               when the number of shots is low or when using noisy datasets; 2) at higher number of shots
               (30-40 on average), the impact of sample selection is negligible as all strategies regress to
094
               random selection; 3) after a certain point, the boost in performance from using more shots
095
               becomes negligible (50-shot for gradient few-shot learning; 20-shot for in-context learning);
096            4) on well-balanced datasets without noisy samples, reducing the number of samples often
097            leads only to reduction in computation costs, while keeping the same performance as with
098            all samples; and 5) sample selection is beneficial even for small dataset sizes (achieving
099            similar performance when selecting from only 10% of the dataset for gradient few-shot
100            learning, or 25% for in-context learning).

      A lot of research is dedicated to sample selection: 1) to reduce the computation costs without the loss
105   of performance, by distilling dataset to lower number of samples (Yu et al., 2023); 2) to select a core

      set of a dataset with similar properties and same performance as the full set (Guo et al., 2022); or 3) to
109   reduce annotation costs using active learning (Ren et al., 2021). A specific research focus is dedicated
110   to selecting good performing samples for in-context learning, where the overall performance was
111   found to be sensitive to this choice, leading to large variability in results (Pecher et al., 2023; Köksal
112   et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022). Particularly in few-shot learning, the adoption of sample selection is
113   limited to active learning strategies and in-context learning.
114
      The sample selection strategies can be divided into two groups. The first, more prevalent, uses
115   heuristics and unsupervised metrics to rate and select samples according to specific property that
116   serves as estimate of their potential to increase the model’s performance. The most popular approach
117   is selecting samples based on the similarity to the test sample for in-context learning. The methods
118   differ in employed: 1) similarity measure, such as cosine/Euclidean distance (Liu et al., 2022; An
119   et al., 2023; Zemlyanskiy et al., 2022; Gupta et al., 2022; Pasupat et al., 2021), BM-25 (Wang et al.,
120   2022; Nashid et al., 2023) or their combination (Agrawal et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2021); and 2)
121   representation, such as simple TF-IDF (Agrawal et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022; Zemlyanskiy et al.,
122   2022), word embeddings from basic or further fine-tuned transformers (Liu et al., 2022; Gao et al.,
123
      2021; Gupta et al., 2022; Pasupat et al., 2021), or representation generated by prompting a large
      language model (An et al., 2023; Nashid et al., 2023).
124
125   Besides similarity, the samples are often selected based on their informativeness or the uncertainty.
126   The active learning strategies are most common (Köksal et al., 2022; Schröder et al., 2022; Margatina
127   et al., 2021; Park et al., 2022; Mavromatis et al., 2023), but also other approaches are utilised, such as
128   using the bias of the samples (Ma et al., 2023) or adversarial training (Agarwal et al., 2021). Other
129
      methods define a notion of quality for each sample, either by prompting a large language model
      to rate the samples (Shin et al., 2021) or observing how the inclusion or removal of the sample
130
      affects the performance (Maronikolakis et al., 2023; Nguyen & Wong, 2023). When considering
131
      representativeness, the samples are selected using core-set methods that are designed to find a subset
132   of samples with properties that are representative of the full training set (Guo et al., 2022; Killamsetty
133   et al., 2021; Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020; Paul et al., 2021; Coleman et al., 2020). Learnability metric
134   either determines how easy it is to learn the specific sample (Swayamdipta et al., 2020; Zhang &
135   Plank, 2021), or how often the sample is forgotten after being learned (Toneva et al., 2018).
136
      Finally, some approaches balance multiple properties at the same time. Often, the similarity of
137
      samples is balanced with their diversity (Wu et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2023) or the informativeness of
138   the samples is balanced with their representativeness (Su et al., 2022; Levy et al., 2023; Ye et al.,
139   2023; Liu & Wang, 2023). In specific cases, a two step sample search is done, such as finding set of
140   informative samples and then using diversity guided search to improve this set (Li & Qiu, 2023).
141
      The second set of approaches leverages training to select the samples. One possibility is to train
142
      a sample retriever, such as a separate scoring model (Rubin et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023; Li et al.,
143
      2023; Wang et al., 2023; Aimen et al., 2023). Another option is to leverage reinforcement learning
144   to select good samples (Zhang et al., 2022; Shum et al., 2023; Scarlatos & Lan, 2023). Finally, the
145   Datamodels approach trains a linear model to predict the performance gain of a set of samples to
146   select the subset that would lead to the highest possible performance increase (Ilyas et al., 2022;
147   Chang & Jia, 2023; Jundi & Lapesa, 2022; Vilar et al., 2023).
148
      In this work, we investigate the impact of the existing strategies on few-shot setting and complement
149
      work by Agarwal et al. (2021) by exploring which sample characteristics are important for few-shot
150   learning performance. In contrast to Li & Qiu (2023), which represents a work closest to ours, we
151   explore multiple few-shot learning approaches not only in-context learning. Our proposed method is
152   inspired by the Datamodels approach (Ilyas et al., 2022), but performs the selection at the level of
153   strategies instead of samples.

202   3.2                    ACSESS: AUTOMATIC C OMBINATION OF S TRATEGIES
203
204   Even though the different selection strategies can identify samples with a specific single property,
205   samples characterised by only one property may not necessarily have a significant impact on the
206   performance in a few-shot setting. For example, the most informative sample that is hard to learn or
207
      is often forgotten may not contribute as much, leading to low impact on the performance. Instead, we
      propose to select examples that are characterised by a balance of complementary properties, even
208
      when they do not represent the best sample of any given property (e.g., a sample that may be less
209
      informative, but is more easily learned or retained). To accomplish this, we propose ACSESS, an
210   effective method for Automatic Combination of SamplE Selection Strategies, composed of two
211   stages: 1) identifying a subset of well-performing and relevant strategies that can improve the overall
212   success of few-shot learning; and then 2) combining the identified strategies using a weighted scheme
213   based on their expected contribution. Similar to previous strategies, our method assigns a score to
214   each sample (based on the combination of the scores from the identified strategies) and selects the
215   samples with the highest score. The steps of the method are summarised in Algorithm 1, with further
      supplementary details, including steps of forward, backward and datamodels selection in Appendix C.

230
231   Identifying the Subset of Relevant Strategies In the first phase, the aim is to identify relevant
232   strategies that, when combined, can select high quality samples based on their different properties.
233   Even though the most optimal combination would be achieved by searching through all the combina-
234   tions of strategies, such a search is infeasible as it requires extensive computation. Instead, we draw
235   inspiration from strategies for feature selection in traditional machine learning – namely the forward
236
      and backward selection strategies – and for the first time apply them to the problem of sample
      selection. In addition, we adapt a strategy for individual sample selection, Datamodels strategy
237
      (introduced by Ilyas et al. (2022)), to work on the level of strategies instead. Such strategy allows
238
      us to explore a more diverse set of possible strategy combination as compared to forward/backward
239   selection. All three of the methods are run independently of each other, each producing a separate
240   set of strategies. The final set of strategies used by our method is constructed as an intersection
241   between these sets to identify the most impactful strategies (i.e., strategies identified by all of the
242   methods) and minimise the potential drawbacks of the individual methods (i.e., exploring also diverse
243   combinations).
244
      Forward Selection iteratively adds to the subset the selection strategy that results in the highest
245   performance increase (similarly to forward feature selection). Starting with an empty subset, the
246   method alternates between two steps. First, for each strategy not included in the subset we determine
247   the increase in performance the strategy would yield if added to the subset. We determine the increase
248   by evaluating the performance of the samples selected by the uniform combination of the already
249   selected strategies and the potential new strategy, and then compare it with the performance of the
250   old subset. Afterwards, the strategy that provides the highest positive increase in performance is
251   added to the subset and the first step is repeated. If no added strategy leads to increase of the overall
252   performance, the process ends, resulting in the subset of relevant selection strategies.
253   Backward Selection iteratively removes strategies from the subset until the performance no longer
254   increases (similarly to backward feature selection). The method works the same way as the forward
255   selection, but starting with all selection strategies and removing them from the subset based on the
256   performance increase such removal leads to.
257   Datamodels Selection draws inspiration from Datamodels (Ilyas et al., 2022; Chang & Jia, 2023),
258   but works on the level of the strategies instead of samples. First, a set of 150 random combinations of
259   strategies is created and evaluated (using uniform combination). The random search is constrained
260   to include each strategy at least 5 times to ensure sufficient coverage of each strategy. Afterwards,
261   these combinations are used to create a simple regression dataset, with the difference in performance
262   to the baseline selection (the classic few-shot learning selection) as target, and the multi-label
263   presence vector of strategies as features. The dataset is used to train a regularised linear regression,
264   LASSO (Tibshirani, 1996), which zeros out the weights for as many strategies as possible. The
265
      selection strategies with positive weights in the model represent the subset of relevant strategies.
266
267   Combining the Identified Strategies After identifying the relevant selection strategies, the strate-
268   gies are combined to perform the selection. Every sample is assigned an importance score, normalised
269   to zero-one interval, by each of the identified strategies. The final sample score is obtained as a
      weighted average of its assigned scores using a weighting scheme (i.e., weighted sum of the scores).

270
      In this paper, we evaluate three separate weighting schemes. First, uniform combination, assigns
271   the same weight to each of the identified strategies. This represents the simplified version of the
272   ACSESS method that potentially leads to lower performance impact, but also lower computation
273   costs and better transferability as no further optimisation is required. This weighting is also used
274   for the combination of strategies in the previous step. Second, weighted combination, assigns a
275   different weight to each strategy, prioritising specific sample properties. We use the final trained
276   weights of the Datamodels linear model as the weights for the strategies. As this weighting scheme is
277   dataset and model specific, it should lead to the highest performance impact, at the cost of requiring
278   more extensive computation. Finally, the weighted combination with random selection extends the
279
      weighted combination by introducing a random element to it, as the random selection was identified as
      a strong baseline that can improve active learning performance (Park et al., 2022). For this weighting
280
      scheme, each sample is assigned additional uniformly randomised score. To determine the weight of
281
      this score, the random selection is included as an additional strategy to the Datamodels method.

285
286
      Datasets. The experiments are conducted on representative image and text few-shot classification
287   datasets composed of different tasks with different number of classes and with different characteristics,
288   such as the class imbalance or amount of noisy samples or labels. For the image datasets, we use 8
289   datasets from the Meta-Album (Ullah et al., 2022), specifically LR_AM.DOG and LR_AM.AWA
290   for animal classification, VCL.APL for plane classification, HUM_ACT.ACT_410 for classification
291   of human actions, MNF.TEX_DTD for classification of texture, MCR.PRT and MCR.PNU for
292   classification of microscope images, and PLT.FLW for flower classification. In addition, we use 6
293   widely-adopted text datasets: 20 News Group (Lang, 1995) and News Category (Misra, 2022) for
294   news category classification and ATIS (Hemphill et al., 1990), Facebook (Schuster et al., 2019),
295
      HWU-64 (Liu et al., 2021) and SNIPS (Coucke et al., 2018) for intent classification. For each dataset,
      we select a subset of up to 200 labelled samples per class to simulate the limited data setting if needed
296
      (relevant mostly for the text classification datasets).
297
298   Few-shot Learning Approaches. The evaluation is done in a 5-way 5-shot setting using the gradient
299   few-shot learning on both the image and text datasets and the in-context learning on the text datasets.
300   For gradient few-shot learning, we use representative and widely-used few-shot learning approaches,
301
      in order to limit the possible confounding factors. Specifically, we focus on meta-learning approaches,
      Prototypical Networks (Snell et al., 2017) and MAML (Finn et al., 2017), and Few-Shot Fine-
302
      Tuning (Chen et al., 2019). For in-context learning, we use the Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023) and
303
      Zephyr-7B (Tunstall et al., 2023) large language models with 4 bit quantisation.
304
305   Baselines. The selection strategies are compared with 2 baselines: 1) Classic selection, where a new
306   set of samples is randomly selected for each new task, potentially covering the whole datasets when
307
      evaluating on enough tasks; and 2) Random selection, where only a single set of 5 samples per class
      is randomly selected at the start and used for every single task, representing a more realistic setting
308
      with limited budget. In-context learning is also compared with the recently proposed in-context
309
      learning specific strategy LENS (Li & Qiu, 2023). Each selection strategy is run 10 times with
310   different random seeds to determine their sensitivity to the effects of randomness (e.g., random
311   initialisation or sample order). In addition, each experiment is repeated 5 times for different data
312   splits (and the choice of the 200 labelled samples) and an average accuracy over these runs is reported
313   (if not specified otherwise). Further experiment setup details are in Appendix E.

351
      The majority of selection strategies fail to outperform the Classic selection in case of gradient
352
      few-shot learning. In most cases, the strategies lead to performance on par or even lower than the
353
      Classic selection (for example hard to learn samples on image datasets or diverse samples on text
354   datasets). Only specific strategies consistently lead to better performance (e.g., easy to learn samples
355   on image datasets, or Forgetting on text datasets). As such, the random sample selection represents
356   a strong baseline for the gradient few-shot learning.
357
      Sample selection is more beneficial for in-context learning. Many of the selection strategies lead
358
      to increase in performance for in-context learning, while only few strategies, such as the popular
359
      selection based on similarity or diversity, under-perform the Classic selection. At the same time, the
360   increase in performance is higher than with gradient few-shot learning, although with a larger
361   variance in results. In contrast to gradient few-shot learning, the hard to learn samples provide the
362   most benefit for in-context learning. The reason may be that hard to learn samples provide a lot of
363   information beneficial for the classification that cannot be easily leveraged in the gradient few-shot
364   learning due to the samples being hard to learn. However, as there is no explicit "learning" in the
365   in-context learning use of large language models, such samples can be better leveraged.
366   The overall benefit of sample selection is higher for noisy datasets and limited for high-quality
367   datasets. Sample selection leads to a considerable performance increase over the Classic selection,
368   on average around 2 percentage points, for the datasets with many noisy samples (as determined
369   by the Cartography) or for datasets with large class imbalance (e.g., difference between lowest
370   and median number of samples per class in few hundred samples), such as HUM_ACT.ACT_410,
371   MCR.PRT or MCR.PNU. At the same time, performance increase is quite limited (on average around
372   0.1 percentage points) for the datasets with few noisy samples or no extensive class imbalance, such
373   as the VCL.APL, PLT.FLW or NewsCategory.
374   Learnability property is a strong indicator of quality for few-shot learning. The sample selection

      4.2      ACSESS R ESULTS AND C HARACTERISTICS
405
406
      In this section, we answer the following research question: RQ2: How does the ACSESS method
407
      perform in comparison to the single-property strategies? Besides analysing and comparing the benefit
408   with other strategies, we analyse the different configurations of our method (methods for strategy
409   identification and weighting schemes).
410
411
      The ACSESS method consistently leads to performance increases across all few-shot learning
      approaches. Our proposed method outperforms all the investigated single-property strategies, leading
412
      to an average performance increase of up to 1.8 percentage points for gradient few-shot learning
413
      and 2.5 percentage points for in-context learning. Comparing with the in-context learning specific
414   strategy LENS (Li & Qiu, 2023) (see Table 2 and Figure 6 in Appendix G for comparison), which
415   also outperforms all single-property strategies, we observe the increase of ACSESS is statistically
416   more significant (p-value of 0.0002 using the Wilcoxon signed-rank test).
417
      The ACSESS method identifies samples with complementary properties, with higher priority
418
      on learnability. For the different data modalities (image vs. text) and the type of few-shot learning
419
      (gradient vs. in-context learning), our method identifies and combines different set of selection
420   strategies. For the image datasets, the combination of easy to learn samples (Cartography) that are
421   located on the decision boundary (Margin) provides the most benefit. For the gradient few-shot
422   learning approaches on text datasets, the combination of easy and ambiguous samples (Cartography)
423   that are least often forgotten (Forgetting) on the decision boundary (Margin) that provide additional
424   informativeness from the Graph-Cut strategy provides the most benefit. In case of in-context learning,
425   the combination of hard to learn samples (Cartography) that are least often forgotten (Forgetting) on
426   the decision boundary (Margin) and with highest Entropy provide the most benefit. Further analysing
427   the identified strategies and their weights, we find the ACSESS method gives higher importance to
428
      samples that are learnable, lower importance to informative samples (but with multiple sources
      for both), while the strategies that focus on representativeness are not included at all. This further
429
      reinforces the finding that learnability is a strong indicator of quality for few-shot learning.
430
431   The dataset and approach specific weighting leads to the best performance. The only exception
      is the Few-Shot Fine-Tuning approach, where the uniform weighting shows the most benefit. At


                                                                     8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      the same time, the weighting strategy that incorporates the random selection often leads to worse
433   performance and higher variance in the results. Even though the uniform combination is computa-
434   tionally less expensive than the weighted combination, its performance increase is only slightly lower
435   (average difference of 0.10 − 0.25). As such, the uniform weighting represents a good trade-off
436   between the performance increase and the computation costs, making it in general a better choice.
437
438   4.3   A BLATION S TUDY: E FFECTS OF VARIABLE N UMBER OF S HOTS AND DATASET S IZE
439
440   In this section, we answer the following research question: RQ3: How do the number of selected shots
441   and the size of the dataset affect the benefit of sample selection? We perform two ablation studies
442   to investigate how the benefits of sample selection strategies change as we: 1) increase or decrease
443   the number of selected shots; and 2) decrease the number of available labelled samples (i.e., the size
444
      of the dataset). For the first ablation study, we apply the ACSESS method and Random selection to
      select a different number of shots and compare their benefit with training on all available samples
445
      (results aggregated over all datasets are in Figure 3 and for individual datasets in Appendix I). For the
446
      second ablation study, we apply the ACSESS method and Random selection to select 5 samples per
447   class from dataset subsets of different sizes and compare their benefit to the selection from the full
448   dataset (results aggregated over all datasets are in Figure 4 and for individual datasets in Appendix J).

498   model degenerate to random guessing as the cross-over is text length dependent. As such, the focus
499   on selecting high quality samples is paramount when selecting only a few labelled samples per
500   class, while the random selection can be safely used with higher number of shots. However, labelling
501
      more samples and using them as additional shots may not always lead to better performance.
502   However, the benefit of curating smaller set of high-quality samples over training on all
503   available samples is higher for noisy and imbalanced datasets. On these datasets, such as
504   HUM_ACT.ACT_410, MCR.PRT, MCR.PNU, ATIS, HWU-64 or Facebook, the highest performance
505   is achieved at 20 samples and decreases as we select more shots. On the other hand, on the datasets
506   with low number of noisy samples or more balanced classes, the sample selection leads only to
507
      reduction in computation cost, with negligible performance improvements. On these datasets,
      such as LR_AM.DOG, LR_AM.AWA, VCL.APL or NewsCategory, the sample selection achieves
508
      performance on par with full-shot training, while using only around 50 − 100 shots.
509
510   Sample selection is beneficial even when using dataset with low number of samples. Using only
511   10% of the dataset (20 samples per class) for gradient few-shot learning or 25% (50 samples per
512   class), sample selection can still identify samples that lead to similar performance as is achieved when
513
      using the full dataset. Decreasing the size further, the benefit of sample selection starts to decrease
      as well. Using only 10 samples per class, the benefit decreases by approximately 20 − 40% (e.g.,
514
      from performance increase of 2 percentage points to only 1.3), and then quickly deteriorates to the
515
      Random selection (as the number of samples to choose from is too low).

518   5    C ONCLUSION
519
520
      In this paper, we focus on a data-centric view of few-shot learning and investigate the impact of
521
      curating a small set of informative and high-quality samples on the success of few-shot learning. To
522
      this end, we investigate the performance benefit of a larger number of different sample selection
523   strategies. To leverage the strengths of these strategies, we propose the ACSESS method for the
524   automatic combination of the sample selection strategies to select samples with complementary
525   properties. The experimental results show that although the individual sample selection strategies
526   may not always be beneficial, their combination using our proposed method consistently leads to
527   better performance. However, we observe that the overall benefit of sample selection is higher for
528   imbalanced datasets with noisy samples and limited for datasets containing only high-quality samples.
529   Curiously, we observe that the learnability of the samples is more important for few-shot learning
530   than their informativeness or representativeness. Based on these results, the uniform combination of
531
      Cartography, Forgetting and Margin selection strategies is a good alternative transferable across all
      settings if running the full method is not feasible. Analysing the behaviour of the sample selection
532
      when changing the number of selected shots and the size of the dataset, we find that the sample
533
      selection provides the most benefit with a low number of shots, while with a higher number of shots
534   all strategies regress to random guessing. In addition, after a certain point, increasing the number
535   of shots used for training no longer leads to improvement in performance of few-shot learning, but
536   may lead to a decrease in performance in some cases, especially for in-context learning. Overall, the
537   benefit of selecting a subset of samples over full-shot training is in reduction of computation cost,
538   while providing performance increase on the imbalanced and noisy datasets. Finally, even when the
539   datasets contain only a few available samples per class, curating the samples further can often still
      lead to benefit in terms of performance and computation costs regardless of dataset characteristics.

      B    L IMITATIONS
919
920
921   In the experiments for evaluating the impact of different sample selection strategies, we focused
922   on such settings, where the impact of sample selection may be lower – within-domain few-shot
923   learning with balanced-shot selection – despite that we observed significant differences in the achieved
924   performance. First of all, in within-domain few-shot learning, the samples are selected from the same
925   domain, thus the difference in impact and role of the individual samples may not be as high. We may
926   expect that the impact of sample selection in cross-domain few-shot learning may be higher and lead
927   to even larger performance deviations. In addition, we focus on a balanced-shot setting, where we
928
      select the same number of shots for each class, leading to lower impact of strategies for different
      classes. Investigating the impact of sample selection across these additional settings is out of scope
929
      of this paper and may be a subject of future research.
930
931   To diminish undesirable randomness effects, we report the results as an average over 5 repeats of
932   data splitting, 10 repeated runs of the selection strategies (resulting in 50 overall evaluation runs),
933   with each run evaluated on 600 (or 300 for in-context learning) tasks. Such setting limits the variance
934
      in results, but may also obscure the benefit of selection strategies to a certain extent. On the other
      hand, although we focus only on a 5-way few-shot learning setting (using only 5 classes per task),
935
      we do not expect significant change in result for most of the few-shot learning approaches when
936
      using higher number of classes (such as 10-way or 20-way classification). The only exception is the
937   in-context learning, where we observed the dependence on input size and context width of the models.
938   With higher number of classes, the observed performance of large language models would start to
939   deteriorate to the performance that can be achieved with a low number of shots.
940
      Similarly to other research papers focusing on sample selection and few-shot learning via in-context
941
      learning, we assume an availability of large-enough annotated dataset from which we select the
942
      sample using different strategies – we use up to 200 samples per class for each dataset for the main
943   experiments. As such, we also have larger validation or held-out set available for evaluating the
944   selection strategies and hyperparameters for our proposed methods or for the approaches themselves.
945   If we considered the true few-shot learning setting, where we have only unlabelled data available,
946   only the active learning strategies (i.e., Entropy, Margin and Least Confidence) could be used, as they
947   work with unlabelled data only, or a different approach for sample selection should be employed,
948   such as using weak-labels to select the best samples, or assign every single class to all the labels and
949   perform the sample selection that way to select both best performing samples as well as the class
950   for them (as it is done by Chang & Jia (2023)). We address this (potentially most significant)
951
      limitation by running ablation studies to observe the benefit of sample selection (using our
      proposed method and random selection) when using smaller subsets of data (i.e., selecting samples
952
      using only fraction of the dataset) or using different number of shots per class.
953
954   Before the experiments, we run hyperparameter optimisation for the gradient few-shot learning
955   models and perform basic prompt-engineering for the in-context learning approaches. We follow the
956   recommendations for good prompt engineering, using the meta-tags defined for each model (which
957
      lead to better performance of the models), while also not using overly-simple or overly-complicated
      prompts. The prompts we use are created based on dataset description, the prompts used in related
958
      works and the formats recommended for different models (e.g., taking inspiration from Sun et al.
959
      (2023)). As we do not perform any extensive or automatic prompt-engineering, these prompts
960   may lead to lowered performance and in some cases also lower impact of the sample selection for
961   the few-shot learning setting. However, running an extensive prompt-engineering would introduce
962   another controllable parameter which could potentially introduce further bias into the results (e.g.,
963   different selection strategies showing different impact for different prompt formats).
964
      Finally, we also identify factors that we consider to be sufficiently covered, but where the further
965
      extension could lead to more generalisable results (nevertheless, also requiring a considerable amount
966   of additional computational resources), such as including more selection strategies, evaluating on
967   more datasets covering other classification tasks (mainly in regards to text datasets) or using larger
968   number of base models for meta-learning and few-shot fine-tuning, or large language models for
969   in-context learning. First of all, although we focus on the major selection strategies and cover majority
970   of the popular sample selection strategies, which are general for any few-shot learning approach,
971   the work can be extended to include additional in-context learning specific strategies (that perform
      sample selection for each test sample separately) or that train a dataset-specific sample retriever

972
       for selection. However, we consider these approaches to be out-of-scope for this paper, as they are
973    not general for any few-shot learning approach (i.e., performing sample selection separately for
974    each test sample is not really feasible or beneficial for gradient few-shot learning) or would require
975    significantly larger number of labelled samples to work correctly. Second, even though we run the
976    experiments on 8 image datasets and 6 text datasets, covering different classification tasks with
977    different number of classes, the work could be potentially extended to even additional datasets and
978    classification tasks, mainly in case of text modality where we are limited by the number of classes the
979    datasets contain (to sufficiently explore few-shot learning). In addition, we explicitly do not evaluate
980    the experiments on the image datasets commonly used for few-shot learning (such as miniImagenet),

       as these datasets were used for pre-training the classification model and contain large number of
       samples. Instead we focus on datasets more representative of the few-shot setting, with different

1087   Considerations for the computation costs of the ACSESS method Even though the steps of
1088   ACSESS method (mainly the identification of relevant strategies) may require a significant compu-
1089   tation resources, we provide suggestions on how to use the method in a compute-efficient way to
1090   reduce these costs as much as possible. First, as we need to identify which strategies to combine,
1091   each of them needs to be run and evaluated. The main computation cost for majority of the selection
1092   strategies is the training of the model that is then either directly used for sample selection or is used
1093
       to provide features for each sample that are used for the selection. As such, majority of the selection
       strategies can be run at the same time, reusing the same trained model (i.e., we need to train the
1094
       model only a single time and use it across majority of the strategies). The exception are the iterative
1095
       strategies (active learning) that need to be run independently, as they train the model only on a fraction
1096   of samples, which changes for each run of the strategy instead of using all the available samples
1097   for training. Second, to identify the most relevant combination of strategies, the different strategies
1098   are combined, this combination is used to select the samples which are then used to evaluate the
1099   combination. However, as we work with strategies that assign scores to each sample, we need to
1100   run each selection strategy only a single time and then use the sample scores for combination. As
1101   such, each combination requires only the evaluation on the few-shot learning approaches, which is
1102   computationally quite cheap as there is only a quick adaptation with few samples (or no adaptation for
1103   in-context learning) followed by inference. However, the cost of this evaluation step is still dependent
1104
       on the number of samples and the model used (i.e., training on 25 samples vs. full dataset; inference
       on smaller vs. larger models). Finally, the computation cost can be further reduced by foregoing the
1105
       model and dataset specific tuning (e.g., using the ACSESS method on multiple datasets at the same
1106
       time).

1109
       to get the best performance benefit of the ACSESS method, running the full strategy identification,
1110   either separately for each model and dataset or aggregated across datasets, and using the weighted
1111   combination of these strategies should be used. This configuration provides the best results across
1112   all datasets, approaches and models, with the exception of Few-Shot Fine-Tuning. However, this
1113   may require extensive computation resources. If such resources are not available, or if we want
1114   to quickly determine the potential benefit of the sample selection, using a uniform combination of
1115   the Cartography and Margin (and in some cases Forgetting) selection strategies provides a good
1116   approximation. This configuration does not incur any additional computation costs, while providing
1117   consistent improvement on all the datasets and models, which is only slightly lower than when using
1118
       the model/dataset specific configuration. Although, running the full method should always provide
       the most benefit, this configuration (uniform combination of the 3 best performing strategies identified
1119
       in our experiments) is a good alternative that is transferable across all settings.

1123
1124   In this appendix, we provide further details about the different single-property sample selection
1125   strategies that we evaluate in this paper, as well as for the baselines. In addition, we specify the
1126   hyperparameters of these methods and how we use them for selecting the set of samples.
1127
       The idea behind the uncertainty-based and active learning selection strategies is that the most
1128   informative samples with higher impact are the ones that the model is least certain about (Coleman
1129   et al., 2020; Park et al., 2022). These strategies select samples iteratively over multiple steps. In
1130   our experiments, we select 1 sample for every class in individual step. In addition, the first sample
1131   for every class is chosen randomly, as such setting was observed to lead to better active learning
1132   results (Park et al., 2022). When using these methods for combination, the samples that are selected
1133   after running all the required steps (e.g., 4 steps when choosing 5-shots) use the score assigned to
       them at their respective step when they were chosen, while the remaining samples are assigned the


                                                          21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       average score over all the selection steps. The Entropy strategy measures the entropy for each of
1135   the samples over the predicted class probabilities (which is used as the sample score) and selects
1136   the sample with highest entropy. The Margin (Breaking Ties) strategy assigns to each sample the
1137   difference between the probability of the two most probable classes. The samples with lowest such
1138   difference are selected as those are the ones the model is most uncertain about (and which can be
1139   considered on the decision boundary of the model). The Least Confidence strategy simply selects
1140   the samples where the most likely class has the lowest probability assigned to it. Finally, the Loss
1141   strategy selects samples with the highest loss.
1142   The idea behind the core-set selection strategies is to select subset of samples that are representative of
1143   the whole dataset (i.e., the subset has similar properties to the full set). The included strategies select
1144   samples based on different criteria, such as error/loss, gradient or submodularity. These strategies
1145   select the full set of samples at the same time according to the assigned scores. As such, when using
1146   these strategies for combination, the full sample scores are used from this single iteration.
1147   The Decision Boundary based methods select samples the are close to the decision boundary, as
1148   these represent the samples that are hard to separate and should provide the most information to the
1149   models. The Contrastive Active Learning (CAL) (Margatina et al., 2021) selects the samples whose
1150   predictive likelihood diverges the most from their neighbourhood as determined by the KL divergence.
1151   The DeepFool strategy (Ducoffe & Precioso, 2018) approximates the samples on boundary by
1152   perturbing the samples until the predicted class changes and selects the samples that require the
1153   smallest perturbation.
1154   The idea behind Error or Loss based methods is that the samples that contribute more loss or error
1155   to the training of model have higher importance and should contribute more to the training when
1156   selected. The GraNd strategy (Paul et al., 2021) calculates the average contribution of each sample
1157   to the decline of the training loss at early stage of training across several independent training runs
1158   (calculating gradient norm expectation of the sample). The Forgetting strategy (Toneva et al., 2018)
1159   simply calculates how often the specific sample is incorrectly classified after being classified correctly
1160   in the previous epoch. The number of such forgetting events is used as the sample score and samples
1161
       that are forgotten the most often (or least often) are selected. In our evaluation of single-property
       strategies, we consider only the setting of most often forgotten samples (as it has shown the best
1162
       performance in the preliminary experiments). However, when using the strategy for combination, we
1163
       also consider the setting of selecting the least forgotten ones.
1164
1165   The Submodularity based methods use submodular functions (Iyer & Bilmes, 2013; Iyer et al., 2021),
1166   which naturally measure the informativeness and diversity of a subset of samples. We evaluate the
1167
       selection based on Graph-Cut, as the method performed the best from all the different submodular
       functions in the preliminary experiments.
1168
1169   The Geometry based methods assume that samples that are close in the feature space have similar
1170   properties. As such, these methods try to remove samples that are close to each other, as they
1171   provide redundant information. Removing enough of such samples then leads to a small subset of
1172   sample that cover the properties of the whole dataset. The Herding strategy (Welling, 2009; Chen
1173
       et al., 2010) iteratively and greedily adds one sample to the subset which minimises the distance
       between the centre of the subset and the centre of the whole dataset. On the other hand, the KCenter
1174
       strategy (Sener & Savarese, 2018; Agarwal et al., 2020) selects sample that minimises the largest
1175
       distance between each sample already in the subset and its closest sample not yet included in subset.
1176
1177   The Gradient Matching based methods try to find a subset of samples whose gradient can imitate
1178   the whole dataset. The CRAIG strategy (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020) converts the problem into
1179
       maximisation of a monotone submodular function which is then solved greedily.
1180   Finally, the Bilevel Optimisation based methods convert the problem into a bilevel optimisation,
1181   with different objectives in the outer and inner level. The Glister strategy (Killamsetty et al., 2021)
1182   uses a validation set on the outer optimisation, where the subset selection is used as objective, and
1183   log-likelihood in the inner optimisation, which handles model parameter optimisation.
1184   In addition, we also evaluate and use sample selection based on the Cartography (Swayamdipta
1185   et al., 2020; Zhang & Plank, 2021), which measures how easy or hard it is to learn different samples.
1186   This ease of learning is determined by observing the training dynamics of all the samples across few
1187   epochs, looking at the average confidence/probability of the correct class and the variance of this


                                                           22
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
       confidence. The samples with high confidence and low variance are considered to be the easy to
1189   learn samples. At the same time the samples with small confidence and small to medium variance are
1190   considered the hard to learn ones. The remaining samples are considered to be ambiguous (medium
1191   confidence or samples with high variance). We explore four different settings in our experiments and
1192   choose the samples accordingly: 1) Easy samples, where we sort the samples based on confidence
1193   and choose the top-K samples with highest confidence for each class; 2) Hard samples, where
1194   we sort the samples based on confidence and choose the bottom-K samples for each class (i.e.,
1195   the lowest confidence samples); 3) Ambiguous, where we first calculate average confidence and
1196   standard deviation of all the samples, select the samples whose confidence is close around the average
1197
       confidence (in the interval defined by 1 standard deviation around the average confidence), and then
       randomly sample from them; and 4) Easy + Ambiguous, where we choose half of the samples from
1198
       the easy set and the other half from ambiguous set.
1199
1200   All the approaches mentioned so far perform the selection using a model that is trained for some
1201   epochs. The number of epochs the training is done for significantly affects the outcome of different
1202   strategies. For this reason, we search for an optimal number of epochs by evaluating the selection
1203
       strategies and the samples they select on a single run. The optimal number of epochs determined by
       this search is 10% of the full epochs used for the few-shot fine-tuning models. This number represents
1204
       a good trade-off between the computational cost of the methods and the quality of samples. Running
1205
       the training for full number of epochs may result in better selection of samples, but we consider such
1206   setting to not be representative. In addition, we use the same number of epochs for each strategy to
1207   keep the comparison as representative as possible. In addition, we also search for an optimal set of
1208   hyperparameters for the selection strategies (such as the learning rate, the optimiser used or batch
1209   size). The optimal set of hyperparameters that are used throughout the experiments is the use of the
1210   Adam optimiser with Cosine Annealing learning rate scheduler, with learning rate of 0.01 for image
1211   data and 0.0001 for text data and a batch size of 64.
1212   To keep the results as comparable as possible, each selection strategy uses the same base model
1213   as the one used in the main experiments, i.e., ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016) pretrained on Imagenet
1214   dataset (Deng et al., 2009) for image data and pretrained BERT-base (Devlin et al., 2019) for text
1215   data in case of gradient few-shot learning, and the same large language models (Mistral (Jiang et al.,
1216   2023) and Zephyr (Tunstall et al., 2023)), but with only their last classification layer being learned,
1217   for in-context learning. In case the selection strategy requires sample features, we use the output of
1218   last fully-connected layer (the input of classification layer) as the feature vector similar to Guo et al.
1219   (2022).
1220   We also evaluate the sample selection based on Similarity and Diversity as it represents popular
1221   approach for in-context learning. To select the samples, we use the cosine similarity between the
1222   feature representation (obtained from the penultimate layer) of the samples. To select the samples for
1223   gradient few-shot learning, we first randomly select one sample for each class. Afterwards, we expand
1224   the set of selected samples by iteratively adding the most similar (for Similarity) or most dissimilar
1225   (for Diversity) sample to the average feature representation of the set of selected samples for each
1226
       class (i.e., if we already have 5 selected samples in the specific class, we calculate the similarity to
       the representation of each of these 5 samples and take an average over these similarities as the final
1227
       score). For in-context learning, we instead the most similar (for Similarity) or the most dissimilar
1228
       (for Diversity) samples for each test sample as is common for this approach (Sun et al., 2023).
1229
1230   Finally, we also use the in-context learning specific LENS method (Li & Qiu, 2023) as a baseline.
1231   We use the same models (Mistral and Zephyr) for the selection of samples and run a hyperparameter
1232
       search informed by the recommendations of the authors to identify the optimal setup for the search.
       In addition, we use the modified version of their source code for running the method.

1659   In this section, we introduce the definition of the few-shot learning problem and the difference
1660   between gradient-based few-shot learning and few-shot learning via in-context learning. Note that
1661   in-context learning can be applied only to text datasets as it utilises pretrained large language models.
1662   For the standard few-shot learning, we assume a larger collection of instances D = (xi , yi ), with
1663   larger number of classes. From this dataset, a separate task DT ⊂ D is created by randomly sampling
1664   few classes N . The task is characterised by a training set (also called support set, S) and test set (also
1665   called query set, Q). To create these sets, few samples (k) are sampled for each class. The few-shot
1666   learning algorithms then use the few samples from the support set ST to learn to solve the task DT
1667   quickly and are evaluated on this ability using the query set QT . This represents the N -way k-shot
1668   classification. The few-shot learning approaches mostly consist of two phases, training phase and
1669   testing phase. Often, these two phases work with separate sets of DT , i.e., the classes appearing as
1670
       part of tasks in the testing phase do not appear during training. As each task works only with subset
       of classes, the evaluation is often done on many such tasks and the average performance from these
1671
       tasks is reported.
1672
1673   The gradient-based few-shot learning approaches follow episodic training similar to typical supervised
       training to gather knowledge and experience and learn to quickly adapt when encountering new task.
