011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we address a key question in machine learning: How effectively can
014            generative data augmentation enhance image classification? We begin by ex-
015
               amining the differences and similarities between real and synthetic data generated
               by advanced text-to-image models. Through comprehensive experiments, we pro-
016
               vide systematic insights into leveraging synthetic data for improved classification
017
               performance. Our findings show that: 1). Generative data augmentation by models
018            trained solely on the internal (available training) set can effectively improve classi-
019            fication performance, validating the long-held hypothesis that synthesis enhances
020            analysis by enriching modeling capability. 2). For generative data augmentation
021            by models trained on both internal and external data (e.g. large-scale image-text
022            pairs) separately, the size of equivalent synthetic dataset augmentation can be
023            determined empirically. In addition to being aligned with a common intuition that
024            real data augmentation is always preferred, our empirical formulation also provides
025            a guideline for quantitatively estimating how much larger the size of generative
026
               dataset augmentation is, over the real data augmentation, to achieve comparable
               improvements. Our CIFAR-10 and ImageNet results also demonstrate its impact
027
               w.r.t. the size of the baseline training set and the quality of generative models.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   We assume the task of predicting labeling y for a given input x. The analysis-by-synthesis methodol-
033   ogy (Yuille & Kersten, 2006) has once been considered as one of the guiding principles for making
034   a variety of inferences (Cootes et al., 1995; Tu & Zhu, 2002; Fergus et al., 2003). The school of
035   thought in pattern theory (Grenander, 1993) considers the capability of being able to synthesize
036   (being generative) stands at the utmost important position for making robust, transparent, and ef-
037   fective analysis/inference. The analysis-by-synthesis principle would also expect having powerful
038
      generative p(x|y) (e.g. text-to-image generation (Ramesh et al., 2021; Rombach et al., 2022)) can
      substantially improve the inference of p(y|x). For image classification, one would expect that adding
039
      synthesized images to datasets like ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009b) as data augmentation would lead
040
      to an immediate improvement. However, the view of analysis-by-synthesis for visual inference has
041   been challenged in the big data and deep learning era (Goodfellow et al., 2016; LeCun et al., 2015).
042
043   For the sake of clarity, we define synthetic data here as images generated by statistical generative
044
      models, distinguishing them from ‘synthetic’ data produced by graphics simulation engines (Beery
      et al., 2020).
045
046   There is an explosive development with increasing level of maturity in image generation, including
047   generative adversarial learning (Tu, 2007; Goodfellow et al., 2014; Karras et al., 2018), variational
048   autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma, 2013), and diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al.,
049   2020a; Rombach et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2021). With the increasing representation power and
050
      photo-realism of generative modeling, especially diffusion-based models, we make a timely effort to
      answer the question about the effectiveness of generative data augmentation for image classification.
051
052   Previous attempts exist to partially address the above question. For instance, studies such as (Azizi
053   et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024) demonstrate that the ImageNet classification accuracy can be improved
      by incorporating synthetic data generated by state-of-the-art generative models, which are pre-trained

124   2    M OTIVATION

126   In this section, we will present initial observations of synthetic data, discuss the algorithms and
127   strategies we have tried based on these observations, and summarize our main conclusions.
128   2.1 I NTERNAL G ENERATIVE DATA AUGMENTATION
129
      We begin by considering the internal setting, where synthetic data used for augmentation is generated
130
      by models trained exclusively on the given training set. In this scenario, any observed distribution
131
      gap between the real and synthetic data might be attributed to inherent limitations in the generative
132   model itself. To validate the existence of this gap, we employ a straightforward method to highlight
133   the differences between the real and synthetic data distributions. Specifically, we train a ResNet-101
134   model (He et al., 2016) to classify between images from these two domains on CIFAR-10. Our
135   results demonstrate that the binary classification accuracy using high-quality and low-quality internal
136   synthetic data both exceed 98%. Ideally, a generative model should produce synthetic data that is
137   statistically indistinguishable from real data, capturing the full complexity and diversity of the dataset.
138   However, these findings suggest that, due to inherent limitations of the generative model, real and
139   synthetic data exist on distinct manifolds, even though synthetic data may sometimes appear visually
140
      realistic. Basically, the space of all valid images is immensely large and any existing generative
      models can only cover a small subspace of the sampling space, resulting a fundamental differences in
141
      the statistics of the image patches between synthetic and real. To further validate the distributional
142
      differences, we visualize the feature vectors from real and synthetic domains, as shown in Figure 1 (a),
143   where the distribution gap is clearly significant. Additional experiments and analyses are provided in
144   Appendix C.1.
145
146   2.2 E XTERNAL G ENERATIVE DATA AUGMENTATION
147   A more common approach is to use generative models pre-trained on large external datasets. While
148   these models can enhance image accuracy and diversity, they introduce another gap: the difference
149   between the external dataset and the given classification dataset. When combined with the
150   inherent limitations of the generative model mentioned earlier, the resulting synthetic data shows an
151   even greater distributional disparity from the given real data. Figure 1 (b) presents the manifolds of
152
      CIFAR-10 and synthetic datasets extracted by a standard image classifier trained solely on CIFAR-10.
      The CIFAR-10-Internal (top row) refers to the synthetic dataset generated by a Vanilla-DDPM trained
153
      only on CIFAR-10, which is the given data. The CIFAR-10-External (bottom row) represents the
154
      CIFAKE dataset (Bird & Lotfi, 2024), generated by Stable Diffusion 1.4 (Rombach et al., 2022),
155   which is trained on a subset of LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al., 2022), thus incorporating external
156   data. While both synthetic datasets show overlaps with the real data on certain feature projections,
157   the overlap between CIFAR-10-Internal and CIFAR-10 is notably more pronounced.
158

248   3.2    G ENERATIVE MODELS TRAINED ON EXTERNAL DATA FOR CIFAR-10 CLASSIFICATION
249   A prevalent approach for data augmentation is to utilize generative models that have been pre-trained
250   on large external datasets. While these models can provide the classifier with external knowledge,
251   as it has been exposed to a large amount of data unseen by the classifier, they also introduce an
252   additional challenge mentioned in Section 2—the disparity between the external dataset and the
253   specified classification dataset. This raises important a question: Is it beneficial to train a generative
254   model on external data? To explore this question, we conduct the following experiments.
255
256   Experiment Setup Using the full CIFAR-10 training set as the real training data, we consider
257   three methods for generating synthetic data: 1). Optimize a conditional Vanilla-DDPM (Ho et al.,
258   2020b) on CIFAR-10 from scratch. 2). Use a diffusion model pre-trained on CIFAR-10 that employs
259   advanced training and sampling techniques introduced in EDM (Karras et al., 2022) to generate
260   higher-quality synthetic images. 3). Utilize the synthetic data in CIFAKE dataset (Bird & Lotfi,
261
      2024), which is generated by Stable Diffusion 1.4 (Rombach et al., 2022) trained on a subset of
      LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al., 2022), thus incorporating substantial external knowledge.
262

300   Experiment Setup The following experiments are conducted using subsets of ImageNet. Specif-
301

302
      larger dataset, ImageNet-100, as introduced in (Tian et al., 2020). We use a ResNet-50 model as

305
306   To generate synthetic images on ImageNet-10, we employ two different generation protocols with
307
      varying sample qualities. The first protocol uses Stable Diffusion 2 (Ramesh et al., 2022) with a
      straightforward class-conditioned prompt of the form pc = “High-quality photo of a c”, where c
308
      represents the class name. The second protocol uses Stable Diffusion 3 (Esser et al., 2024) with
309
      diverse captions generated according to the method described in (Tian et al., 2024). The caption
310   templates include c → caption, c, bg → caption, and c, rel → caption. We refer readers to the
311   original paper for further details on this method. Each caption generates five images, and we employ
312   the CLIP-Filter strategy (He et al., 2022) to exclude the bottom 20% of images based on CLIP
313   zero-shot classification confidence, retaining only the high-quality images. For the ImageNet-100
314   setting, we only use the second protocol to generate synthetic data. We denote the three generated
315   dataset as ImageNet-10-SD2, ImageNet-10-SD3 and ImageNet-100-SD3, respctively.
316   Then, we consider the following scenarios for mixed training with each synthetic dataset: 1). Fixing
317   the number of real samples at 65, 260, and 1,300 per class and changing the proportion of synthetic
318   samples from 1 : 0.1 to 1 : 100. 2). Fixing the number of synthetic samples at 1,300 per class and
319   varying the proportion of real samples. We conduct extensive experiments using different real and
320   synthetic datasets, baseline sample numbers, and mixing ratios. The complete experimental results
321   are in Appendix C.3.

484
485   Synthetic Data Augmentation in Computer Vision The usage of synthetic data augmentation in
      image classification has gained significant attention because of its potential to generate large amounts


                                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      of data with minimal manual effort. Synthetic images have proven effective across various computer
487   vision tasks, including semantic image segmentation (Chen et al., 2019; Tritrong et al., 2021), object
488   detection (Nowruzi et al., 2019; Fabbri et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Ge et al., 2022), human
489   motion understanding (Guo et al., 2022; Varol et al., 2017), and 3D reconstruction (Xu et al., 2024;
490   Zhang et al., 2023b; Wu* et al., 2022). Adversarial data augmentation (Xie et al., 2020) has shown to
491   improve image recognition. They provide diverse and comprehensive training data to improve model
492   generalization. Early methods primarily rely on simulation pipelines using graphics engines or 2D
493   renderings to generate synthetic data (Dosovitskiy et al., 2015; 2017). However, these approaches
494   often encounter high computational costs, which can limit their scalability.
495   Graphics simulations (Beery et al., 2020) have been used to perform synthetic data augmentation for
496   image recognition. More recent approaches have explored the use of generative models to generate
497   synthetic data for image classification (Sarıyıldız et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Bansal & Grover,
498   2023; Hennicke et al., 2024; Jung et al., 2024). Text-to-image diffusion models, in particular, have
499   gained prominence as these models can generate high-quality, large-scale curated datasets with just a
500   few textual descriptions. For instance, He et al. (2022) has found that synthetic data generated by
501
      GLIDE (Nichol et al., 2021) can readily benefit image classification in data-scarce settings. Trabucco
      et al. (2024) proposes a data augmentation method that uses pre-trained text-to-image diffusion
502
      models to enhance semantic diversity in images, leading to improved accuracy in few-shot image
503
      classification tasks. Azizi et al. (2023) has demonstrated that fine-tuning Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022)
504   using a target dataset can improve classification accuracy. Fan et al. (2024) studies the scaling laws
505   of synthetic images generated by text-to-image diffusion models to train image classifiers.
506
507
      Unlike their approach, we answer a more fundamental and overarching question: When we have a
      certain amount of real data for classification, how quantitatively can synthetic data augmentation
508
      help with image classification? How does the role of synthetic data vary under different scales of real
509
      data?
510
511
512
513   Text-to-Image Diffusion Models Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020b; Song et al., 2020a;b) have
514   emerged as powerful generative models capable of producing high-quality, photo-realistic images.
515   Comparing with traditional generative adversarial networks (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Tu, 2007),
516   diffusion models offer comparable or even superior image quality while also providing greater
517   training stability. Specifically, text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models enable flexible language prompts
518
      to generate diverse and customized images. Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022), Stable Diffusion (Rombach
      et al., 2022), DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2021), Muse (Chang et al., 2023), and GLIDE (Nichol et al.,
519
      2021) are notable T2I models. Additionally, ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023a), T2-Adapter (Mou
520
      et al., 2023), UniControl (Qin et al., 2023), and OmniControlNet (Wang et al., 2024) demonstrate
521   excellent capabilities in image-conditioned text-to-image tasks.
522
523   In this work, we focus on Stable Diffusion, a latent diffusion model (LDM) that performs the diffusion
524
      process within the latent space of the Variational AutoEncoder (Kingma, 2013; Van Den Oord et al.,
      2017). This approach significantly reduces computational demands compared to pixel-based models
525
      while achieving superior visual fidelity and performance across various tasks.

529   5   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this study, we present Mousterian, an empirical study that systematically explores how synthetic
532
      data can enhance classification models, starting from fundamental classification tasks, and identify
533
      scenarios where synthetic data proves particularly effective. Through a series of experiments, we
534   demonstrate the efficacy of direct mixed training and reveal that generative models have the potential
535   to improve classification performance, regardless of the involvement of external datasets. Notably,
536   we observe a significant utility of generated data when the amount of real data is limited, alongside a
537   saturation trend in performance improvement as the data volume increases. Additionally, we provide
538   an empirical functional relationship between accuracy and the amount of real and synthetic data
539   added, aiming to offer researchers an intuitive understanding of this relationship. We hope that our
      findings will provide valuable insights for future research on synthetic data in computer vision.

      Zero-shot image classification Zero-shot image classification can be formalized as follows: Let
      Ytrain be the set of training categories, the training set Dtrain consists of samples {(xi , yi )}N   i=1 , with
781
      xi representing an image and yi ∈ Ytrain its class label. The validation set Dtest includes samples
782
      {(xj , yj )}Mj=1 , where xj is a test image, but its class label yj ∈ Ytest is not part of the training data,
783
      i.e., Ytrain ∩ Ytest = ∅. During training, the class label y is first mapped to a text description M (y),
784   where M ∈ M is a natural language template. Then, a text encoder T converts M (y) into a feature
785   vector, which is subsequently projected onto the joint embedding space using a linear layer, resulting
786   in a text embedding Embtext (y). For images, a visual encoder processes the image x to produce its
787   feature representation I(x). This feature is also projected onto the same space as the text embedding.
788   The training goal is to maximize the cosine similarity between Embtext (y) and Embimage (x). In the
789   testing period, given a test image xtest , it is classified into the category ŷtest with the highest similarity
790   score. Although the categories of the test images are not seen during training, this approach enables
791   classification by leveraging their semantic relationships with seen classes.
792   In our case, by leveraging the powerful capabilities of a generative model, we can create a synthetic
793   dataset Dsyn composed solely of images of Ytest and mix it with the original training set Dreal , resulting
794   in a combined training set Dmixed = Dreal ∪ Dsyn . In this way, Ytest is included in the categories of
795   Dmixed , while the classifier has only not seen the real data of Ytest .

1052   Both supervised and zero-shot image classification experiments are performed on CIFAR-10,
1053   ImageNet-10, ImageNet-100, and various corresponding synthetic datasets. For ImageNet-10 and

1056   number of iterations into different levels based on the data size, and the details are provided in Table
1057   7. All images are resized to 224 × 224 for input. For data augmentation, we apply random cropping,
1058   resizing, and random horizontal flipping.
1059   For the CIFAR-10 experiments, we use ResNet-110 as introduced in (He et al., 2016) for classification,
1060   as it is well-suited for the smaller image sizes of CIFAR-10. In the experiments on CIFAR-Half and
1061   the full CIFAR-10, we fix the number of epochs at 160, using a multistep scheduler to decay the
1062   learning rate by a factor of 0.1 at epochs 80 and 120. For CIFAR-Small, we fix the number of epochs
1063   at 320, also using a multistep scheduler, with the learning rate decaying by a factor of 0.1 at epochs
1064   160 and 240. For data augmentation, we apply random cropping and random horizontal flipping.
1065   For the ViT-L/16 (Kolesnikov et al., 2021) fine-tuning experiment, we fix the total iterations at 10,000
1066   steps. The 32 × 32 CIFAR-10 images are resized to 224 × 224 for input.

       For all settings mentioned above, we run 3 trials for each experiment and report the average result.
