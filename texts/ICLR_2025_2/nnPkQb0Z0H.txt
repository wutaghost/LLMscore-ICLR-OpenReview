011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            EEG-based emotion recognition offers an objective method for diagnosing
014            emotion-related health issues, but the inherent complexity of emotions often leads
015            to annotation errors and noisy labels. To simulate this labeling process in emo-
016
               tion recognition, we propose a semantic-based candidate label generation method
               leveraging the GloVe vectors, which considers the semantic relationships be-
017
               tween emotions. Under the Partial Label Learning (PLL) scenario, we introduce a
018
               novel model called PGNA-PL (Prototype-Guided Noise-Augmented Partial Label
019            Learning). This model learns inter-class relationships of emotions using proto-
020            types, and uses a self-distillation mechanism to iteratively guide the classifier’s
021            disambiguation process. To address the low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of EEG,
022            we introduce a noise augmentation strategy inspired by the mixup method, incor-
023            porating controllable noise to enhance model robustness. Experiments on three
024            public datasets (SEED, SEED-IV, SEED-V) show that our approach achieves
025            state-of-the-art performance, surpassing existing PLL baselines across different
026            candidate label generation modes. Our method effectively disambiguates com-
027
               plex emotions and shows promising results in assisting in the recognition of fear-
               related disorders.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The interplay between human emotions and overall health, both psychological and physiological, is
      profound, with evidence showing a strong linkage between negative emotional states and conditions
034
      like phobias (Apicella et al., 2024). Physiological signals, due to their difficult-to-disguise nature,
035
      are suitable for the objective assessment of emotional responses (Liu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2022b;
036   Shen et al., 2022). Research has demonstrated the viability of extracting emotion-related features
037   from various physiological signals, including electroencephalogram (EEG) (Wang et al., 2024a), eye
038   movements (Zheng et al., 2018), and peripheral physiological signals (Koelstra et al., 2011). Among
039   these, EEG due to its precision and high temporal resolution, has already been extensively studied
040   (Zhang et al., 2022b; Liu et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2022).
041
      To obtain high-quality EEG-based emotion datasets, auditory and visual stimuli are typically em-
042   ployed to induce emotional responses in participants, with EEG signals recorded through specialized
043   equipment. While the labeling of EEG signals generally relies on expert annotations (Zheng & Lu,
044   2015) or participants’ self-reports (Koelstra et al., 2011), the inherent complexity of emotions of-
045   ten leads to ambiguities, resulting in annotation errors (Jiang et al., 2024). This challenge is nearly
046   unavoidable. For example, as shown in Plutchik’s wheel of emotions (Plutchik, 1980), the emotion
047   “remorse” encompasses both “disgust” and “sadness”. When participants experience such complex,
048   overlapping emotions, the labeling process becomes difficult. This is especially prevalent when
049   dealing with rich emotional expressions in audiovisual stimuli.
050   Partial label learning (PLL) (Cour et al., 2011) offers a more flexible approach by allowing multiple
051   potential labels to be assigned simultaneously, with the assumption that only one label is correct.
052   In this context, the labeled tags are referred to as candidate labels, while the unlabeled ones are
053   considered non-candidate labels (Wen et al., 2021). Under this assumption, the goal during training
      is to disambiguate the multiple candidate labels, enabling the model to learn how to correctly identify


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the true label from the ambiguous annotations. The introduction of this method can significantly
055   mitigate the impact of labeling errors in EEG-based emotion recognition tasks Zhang & Etemad
056   (2023) .
057
058
      However, in emotion classification tasks, different emotions often share latent relationships, mak-
      ing the traditional PLL assumption (Wang et al., 2022; Xia et al., 2023) of uniformly distributed
059
      candidate labels unsuitable. Furthermore, other methods like Zhang & Etemad (2023) overlook the
060
      semantic relations between emotions. To address this challenge for the first time, we propose a
061   novel semantic-based candidate label generation method that explicitly incorporates semantic rela-
062   tionships between emotions. By leveraging the GloVe vectors (Pennington et al., 2014) (detailed in
063   Appendix A.2.1), which is extensively used in natural language processing (NLP) to capture contex-
064   tual word relationships, we obtain semantic vectors for various emotions. These vectors represent
065   emotions in a multi-dimensional space, reflecting their contextual relationships based on large text
066   corpora. We then use cosine similarity to calculate the distances between these emotion vectors,
067   which are instrumental in computing the probability of each emotion being selected as a candidate
068   label. This method helps in quantitatively assessing the similarity or dissimilarity between emotional
069
      states, thereby enabling more accurate candidate label generation.
070   PLL algorithms have been extensively studied in computer vision (Tian et al., 2023), yet their po-
071   tential in EEG-based emotion recognition has not been fully exploited. A key challenge is un-
072   derstanding and utilizing the inter-class relationships of emotions to aid in label disambiguation.
073   Additionally, the inherently low SNR of EEG signals (Ye et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2024; Li et al.,
074   2024b), exacerbated by issues like electrode displacement due to head movements (Wang et al.,
075
      2024b), further complicates the analysis.
076   To solve these challenges, we introduce the PGNA-PL model (Prototype-Guided Noise-Augmented
077   Partial Label Learning). This model stabilizes emotion relationships using prototypes and employs
078   a self-distillation approach (Zhang et al., 2019; Li et al., 2024c) to guide the classifier’s disambigua-
079   tion process among emotions. To mitigate low SNR issues, we incorporate a controllable noise
080   augmentation technique inspired by the mixup method (Zhang et al., 2018). This method mixes
081
      features from a small number of other samples to create perturbations closer to the distribution of
      EEG signals without altering the label, thereby enhancing the robustness of the model.
082
083   We evaluate our method on three publicly available datasets: SEED (Zheng & Lu, 2015), SEED-IV
084   (Zheng et al., 2018), and SEED-V (Liu et al., 2021), under two different candidate label generation
085   strategies, including our proposed Semantic Distribution and the real-world experimental framework
086   Zhang & Etemad (2023) referred to as Russell Distribution in this paper for simplicity (detailed
087
      in Appendix A.2.2). PGNA-PL achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance across all datasets.
      Analysis of the confusion matrix suggests that our method shows potential advantages in assisting
088
      in the recognition of fear-related disorders..
089
090   By enhancing the accuracy and robustness of emotion recognition from EEG signals and effectively
091   addressing labeling errors through partial label learning, our proposed methods have the potential to
092   transform applications in mental health diagnostics, adaptive user interfaces, and affective comput-
093
      ing, providing more reliable and objective assessments of emotional states.
094   Our contributions are threefold:
095
096         1. We introduce a novel semantic-based candidate label generation method for PLL that, for
097            the first time, incorporates the semantic relationships of emotions. This method not only ad-
098            dresses the limitations of previous approaches but also establishes a new evaluation frame-
099            work applicable to emotion recognition tasks.
100
            2. We propose the PGNA-PL model, an advanced approach utilizing prototypes to decode
101
               emotional relationships between classes and self-distillation for enhanced classification ac-
102
               curacy. This model is further augmented with a unique noise augmentation technique,
103            improving its robustness.
104
105         3. Through comprehensive evaluations on three public datasets, the PGNA-PL model demon-
106            strates superior performance over existing methods. Notably, the model shows exceptional
107            efficacy in detecting fear emotion, which could have significant implications for advance-
               ments in mental health assessments.

112   To more accurately reflect the inter-class relationships of emotions in the candidate labels used for

      ditionally, we propose a tailored noise augmentation strategy for EEG signals to enhance the model’s
      robustness. The pseudo-code of PGNA-PL is shown in Algorithm 1.

122   be captured from large textual corpora. To model these semantics, we represent each emotion label
123   using GloVe vectors (Pennington et al., 2014). Let vi denote the GloVe vector for emotion category
124   ei , where i indexes the emotion vocabulary.
125
      To quantify the semantic similarity between two emotions ei and ej , we compute the cosine simi-

133

190   2.3.1   P ROTOTYPE -G UIDED M ODULE
191
192
      In PLL, the classifier’s predictions may be unreliable due to label ambiguity. To address this issue,
      we propose a prototype-guided approach that constructs emotion prototypes, with the goal of cap-
193
      turing inter-class relationships. Unlike the PiCO method (Wang et al., 2022), which updates candi-
194
      date labels using prototypes and may suffer from instability due to frequent changes in the training
195   objective, our method avoids such instability by maintaining a fixed learning objective while still
196   leveraging prototypes to guide the classifier’s learning process.
197
198
      We define emotion prototypes µk ∈ Rd , where d is the feature dimensionality of each emotion
      category, and k is the number of emotion categories. Initially, each prototype is set to the zero
199
      vector of dimension d. During training, we select the emotion category corresponding to the highest
200
      predicted probability in the logits ŷi , and update its associated prototype using a moving average
201   mechanism in (Wang et al., 2022). This allows the prototype to evolve over time, reflecting the input
202   data distribution. Formally, the prototype update rule is given by:
203
                          µk = Normalize (ξµk + (1 − ξ)pi ) ,         if   k = arg max(ŷi )                       (6)
204                                                                                    k
205   Here, ξ is a hyperparameter controlling the rate of prototype update, typically set close to 1 to ensure
206   small, incremental changes. The feature vector pi is the output of the encoder for the i-th training
207   sample, and normalization ensures that the prototype vector remains within a consistent scale.
208
      Over time, these prototypes represent the various emotion categories, and the distances between
209   them capture the relationships between different emotions. As a result, the classifier can more easily
210   distinguish between similar emotions based on the evolving prototypes.
211
212

      represents the likelihood of an input belonging to each emotion category. This is done by calculating
213
      the similarity between the feature vector pi and each of the prototypes µk . Specifically, we compute
214
      the similarity between the feature vector and each prototype:
215

220

      learn the inter-class relationships encoded in the prototypes. The Prototype Guidance loss LP G is

229   consistent with the inter-class relationships learned from the prototypes.
230   In summary, the Prototype-Guided Module introduces emotion prototypes that evolve over time,
231   helping the classifier to better understand and differentiate between emotion categories. Through
232   self-distillation, the classifier learns to align its predictions with the relationships captured by the
233   prototypes, thereby improving its ability to handle label ambiguity and inter-class similarity. This
234   approach avoids the instability associated with directly updating candidate labels and enhances the
235   model’s overall performance in emotion classification tasks.

237   2.3.2   N OISE AUGMENTATION M ODULE
238
      In EEG signal processing, one of the key challenges is the low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), which
239
      can significantly hinder the model’s performance. To address this, we propose a noise augmentation
240   method inspired by consistency regularization (Yan & Li, 2021; Wu et al., 2022), which adds con-
241   trolled noise perturbations to the input EEG signals without changing their candidate labels. The
242   goal is to force the model to produce consistent outputs despite the noise.
243
      However, due to the continuous and time-series nature of EEG signals, traditional data augmentation
244
      techniques, such as cropping or scaling, are not applicable (Li et al., 2022a). Moreover, generating
245
      new signals using complex models like Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) would dramat-
246   ically increase the model’s complexity. Instead, we draw inspiration from the mixup technique
247   (Zhang et al., 2018), which is commonly used for data augmentation. In mixup, two samples from
248   the same batch are randomly mixed, and the corresponding labels are also interpolated. This helps
249   the model generalize better. However, unlike the traditional mixup, our approach focuses on adding
250   noise by blending two EEG signals, thus maintaining the structure of the signal while introducing
251   perturbations that simulate noise.
252   To begin, we sample a mixing coefficient λ from a Beta distribution, as described in Equation equa-
253   tion 9. The Beta distribution is parameterized by a hyperparameter βp , which determines the range
254   of λ. For simplicity, we use equal values for both parameters for the Beta distribution. If βp is small,
255   λ will be close to 0 or 1, leading to a higher contribution from one of the signals. Conversely, larger
256   values of βp make λ closer to 0.5, promoting a more balanced mix of the two signals.
257
                                                λ = Beta(βp , βp )                                        (9)
258
259   Next, we apply a scaling factor σ to constrain the mixing coefficient λ. This ensures that the mixed
260   sample retains more of the original signal’s features. Specifically, σ is sampled from the range
261   (0.5, 1] to ensure that the perturbations do not distort the original sample too much. The final mixing
262   coefficient λ′ is computed as:
263                                             λ′ = σ + (1 − σ) · λ                                     (10)
264   With the mixing coefficient λ′ determined, we mix two EEG signals, xi and xj , sampled from the
265   same batch. The new noise-augmented signal is given by:
266
                                           x′i = λ′ · xi + (1 − λ′ ) · xj                                (11)
267
268   This mixed signal x′i is then used as the input to the model. The key advantage of this approach is
269   that it allows us to simulate noise without altering the candidate labels, maintaining the integrity of
      the original classification task while adding perturbations that improve generalization.


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      After conducting a coarse grid search over different values of λ′ , we found that values in the range
271   of [0.8, 0.9] yield optimal performance. This indicates that maintaining a higher proportion of the
272   original sample helps preserve its features, making the noise perturbations more controlled and
273   stabilizing the training process.
274
      While similar interpolation methods have been explored in the context of data augmentation, such as
275
      in PaPi (Xia et al., 2023), our approach differs in its primary goal: adding noise rather than augment-
276
      ing data purely for label interpolation. In PaPi, candidate labels are updated using the classifier’s
277   predictions on new interpolated samples, but the focus is on generating diverse data rather than in-
278   troducing noise. In contrast, our method ensures that the candidate labels remain unchanged, while
279   the noise addition process is controlled via the scaling of λ, as described in Equation equation 10.
280   This helps maintain the utility of the original labels for disambiguation.
281
      In summary, the noise augmentation module addresses the low SNR in EEG signals by adding
282
      controlled noise perturbations. Using a mixup-based method, we blend two EEG signals from the
283   same batch, adjusting the mixing ratio through a scaling factor that ensures the original sample is
284   preserved while adding noise. This method stabilizes training by forcing the model to be consistent
285   in the presence of noise, without altering the candidate labels, thereby improving robustness.

307   2.3.4    A RCHITECTURE D ETAILS
308
      The architecture of the encoder and classifier is derived from (Zhang et al., 2022a; Zhang & Etemad,
309   2023), as they have been demonstrated to be superior in the task of EEG emotion recognition. The
310   encoder consists of two CNN modules; each includes a 1-D convolutional layer, batch normalization,
311   and a LeakyReLU activation function. The output is then transformed to the desired dimensions
312   d through a flattening operation and a linear layer. The classifier comprises a single linear layer
313   designed for emotion recognition. Further details can be found in Appendix A.3.

      To address the challenges of PLL in EEG-based emotion recognition, we introduced a semantic-
      based method for generating candidate labels. Leveraging the GloVe vectors, this approach enhances
532
      the clarification of semantic relationships between different emotions, closely mirroring real-world
533
      scenarios of partial labeling. We also developed the PGNA-PL model, specifically designed for EEG
534   emotion recognition. This model incorporates a self-distillation strategy, using prototypes to guide
535   the classifier’s disambiguation efforts by tapping into the inter-class distance recognition capabili-
536   ties. Moreover, we crafted a tailored noise augmentation technique to address the low signal-to-noise
537   ratio inherent in EEG data. Experiments on the SEED, SEED-IV, and SEED-V datasets, employing
538   two distinct candidate label generation methods, confirmed that our approach achieves SOTA results.

      A.1   R ELATED W ORK
696
697   EEG-based emotion recognition is a promising field. With advancements in basic problems such
698   as feature extraction (Duan et al., 2013) and cross-subject emotion transfer (Wang et al., 2024a),
699   partial label scenarios in EEG emotion recognition have gained attention. Zhang & Etemad (2023)
700   compared classical PLL methods on the SEED-V dataset. However, how to simulate reasonable
701   candidate labels to evaluate different PLL models in emotion recognition scenarios, and how to
      customize EEG emotion recognition models under PLL settings, remain areas worth exploring.

                                                       13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      Constructing partial-label datasets in real-world scenarios is challenging, as it requires both a train-
703   ing set with uncertain labels and a test set with fully certain labels (Cour et al., 2011). Current
704   methods (Wen et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022) typically simulate candidate labels in the training set
705   by introducing known erroneous labels into standard datasets (e.g., using a uniform distribution to
706   assign candidate labels), but they neglect the correlations between labels. Zhang & Etemad (2023)
707   addressed this for emotion labels by using the Russell’s circumplex emotion model (Russell, 1980)
708   to compute relationships among emotions for generating candidate labels, yet their method did not
709   fully exploit semantic relationships between emotion categories.
710   PLL models, particularly identification-based strategies (IBS) (Lv et al., 2023) that optimize candi-
711   date labels during training, have been well explored in recent years (Lv et al., 2020; Wen et al., 2021;
712   Zhang et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Xia et al., 2023). such as PiCO (Wang et al.,
713   2022) and PaPi (Xia et al., 2023), enhance model predictions by iteratively updating candidate labels
714   with the help of the prediction results of prototypes. DNPL (Seo & Huh, 2021), distinct from tradi-
715   tional IBS, dynamically adjusts both candidate and non-candidate label probabilities in response to
716   model predictions, enhancing learning accuracy and robustness without optimize candidate labels.
717
      This approach helps DNPL avoid overfitting to incorrect labels, making it a unique method within
      the PLL framework. However, further exploration of model enhancement techniques for EEG-based
718
      emotion recognition, including self-distillation (Zhang et al., 2019) and noise augmentation (Wang
719
      et al., 2024b), remains an evolving area of research.
720
721   Self-distillation leverages a single-step approach that focuses its training efforts directly on the stu-
722   dent model (Zhang et al., 2019), unlike traditional knowledge distillation (Hinton, 2015) which re-
723
      quires a pre-trained teacher model to guide the student. For PLL in EEG-based emotion recognition,
      Li et al. (2024a) replicate an identical model as the teacher, which, while effective, greatly increases
724
      parameters. In contrast, we use a single prototype as the teacher, avoiding this overhead. Moreover,
725
      although noise augmentation techniques have been explored to some extent in EEG-based emotion
726   recognition, such as the time steps shuffling method proposed by Wang et al. (2024b) to introduce
727   perturbations within the features of a time window, there is still no well-established approach for
728   applying noise augmentation to the fusion of individual EEG features.

733
734   Global Vectors for Word Representation (GloVe) is a widely utilized resource in the field of natu-
735   ral language processing. It encodes words into numerical vectors using an unsupervised learning
736   algorithm that leverages global word-word co-occurrence statistics from a corpus.
737   The version used in this study is the largest available, encompassing data derived from 840 billion
738   tokens. It contains a vocabulary of 2.2 million unique, case-sensitive words and provides pre-trained
739   vectors of 300 dimensions for each word. The vectors capture not only the distributional semantics
740   of words but also subtle semantic relationships and patterns in the data. These vectors, totaling 2.03
741   GB, are instrumental in various applications, including but not limited to text analysis, affective
742   computing, and machine learning models that rely on robust word representations.

744   A.2.2    D ESCRIPTION OF RUSSEL D ISTRIBUTION
745
746
      The real-world experimental framework referred to as the Russell Distribution in this paper, intro-
      duces a novel candidate label generation technique in partial label learning tailored for emotion
747
      recognition tasks. This method leverages Russell’s circumplex model, where emotions are mapped
748
      on a circular layout according to arousal and valence. Each emotion is defined by its polar coordi-
749   nates—radius and angle.
750
751   To generate candidate labels, the method calculates these polar coordinates for each emotion. It then
752
      uses them to determine a normalized similarity score between emotions based on the cosine of their
      angular differences and their Euclidean distances on the wheel.
753
754   Instead of using a fixed probability for a class to become a candidate label, this approach dynamically
755   adjusts the probabilities based on the similarity scores. Emotions closer to the true label on the wheel
      are more likely to be chosen as candidate labels.

      This method offers a more realistic way of generating candidate labels by acknowledging the natural
757   similarities between emotions, thus minimizing the risk of confusing similar emotions and enhanc-
758   ing the learning effectiveness.

843   A.6     BASELINES M ETHODS
844
      We provide a detailed overview of classical IBS methods and the DNPL approach as follows:
845
846
847
      A.6.1    IBS M ETHODS
848           • PRODEN (Lv et al., 2020): PROgressive iDENtification (PRODEN) updates the label sets
849             based on their compatibility with model predictions, iteratively refining this relationship.
850             By directly adjusting the labels in response to prediction accuracy, the method fosters model
851             convergence on the correct labels amid noisy or partial label information.
852           • LW (Wen et al., 2021): Leveraged Weighted (LW) loss introduces a novel approach in
853             partial label learning by incorporating a leverage parameter β, which allows for a strategic
854             balance between losses on candidate and non-candidate labels. LW loss not only provides a
855             method to adjust the influence of different types of labels dynamically, but it is also backed
856             by a theoretical framework that assures risk consistency under relatively weak assump-
857             tions. The theoretical foundations of LW loss offer guidance on the optimal choice of β,
858             enhancing the method’s effectiveness and adaptability in diverse learning environments.
859           • CAVL (Zhang et al., 2021): Class Activation Value Learning (CAVL) is a novel method
860             in partial-label learning that eschews the usual assumptions about data collection that can
861             limit the performance of traditional models. CAVL uses a technique called Class Acti-
862             vation Value (CAV), which adapts the principles of Class Activation Maps (CAM) from
863             image-based neural networks to be applicable across various input types and models. This
                approach allows CAVL to identify the true label from a set of candidates by selecting the


                                                         16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
                class with the highest CAV, thereby promoting accurate model training without relying on
865             predefined data assumptions.
866
867
              • CR (Wu et al., 2022) : The method leverages a novel training framework combining super-
                vised learning on non-candidate labels with consistency regularization on candidate labels,
868
                addressing the challenge of PLL. By integrating consistency regularization, the method
869
                aligns predictions across multiple augmented versions of the same instance to infer a re-
870             liable label distribution. This alignment ensures the model learns robust representations
871             while preserving consistency in predictions under different input perturbations. The in-
872             ferred label distribution is adaptively optimized using a closed-form solution, enabling an
873             efficient and effective approach to guide the model towards the true label distributions. This
874             framework enhances the learning process by utilizing non-candidate labels to refine model
875             predictions while maintaining stability and adaptability through regularization, distinguish-
876             ing it from traditional methods reliant on self-training or contrastive learning.
877           • PiCO (Wang et al., 2022): Partial label learning with COntrastive label disambiguation
878             (PiCO) leverages class prototypes to effectively address two fundamental challenges in
879             partial label learning (PLL): representation learning and label disambiguation. By using the
880             classification outcomes of prototypes, it refines the candidate labels through a prototype-
881             based disambiguation process, aligning the label prediction with the most likely true class.
882
                To enhance representation learning, the method incorporates a contrastive learning module
                that combines MoCo (He et al., 2020) and SupCon (Khosla et al., 2020) approaches. This
883
                module promotes closely aligned representations for examples within the same class while
884
                maintaining separation between different classes. The integration of these components into
885             a unified framework not only facilitates better disambiguation of ambiguous labels but also
886             boosts the encoder’s ability to generate high-quality representations. The method can be
887             rigorously explained from an expectation-maximization (EM) perspective, underscoring
888             the mutual reinforcement between representation learning and label disambiguation.
889
              • PaPi (Xia et al., 2023): Partial-label learning with a guided Prototypical classifier (PaPi)
890             directly employs classifier results for disambiguation and aligns prototype classifications
891             with candidate label distributions using KL divergence, showcasing several notable charac-
892             teristics. By leveraging a shared feature encoder between a linear classifier and prototypical
893             representations, the approach effectively encourages the feature space to reflect the intrinsic
894             similarities between categories. This alignment enhances the model’s ability to distinguish
895             between candidate labels by explicitly guiding prototype optimization through the predic-
896             tive results of the classifier. Unlike methods relying on contrastive learning, which can
897             introduce noise and require significant computational resources, this approach avoids such
898
                dependencies, providing a more streamlined and computationally efficient framework for
                partial-label learning. By focusing on directly aligning prototype classifications with can-
899
                didate label distributions, the method not only simplifies the disambiguation process but
900
                also strengthens representation learning in ambiguous label scenarios.
901
902
      A.6.2    T HE DNPL M ETHOD
903
904           • DNPL (Seo & Huh, 2021): Deep Naive Partial label Learning (DNPL) revolutionizes
905             traditional PLL approaches by dynamically integrating both candidate and non-candidate
906
                label probabilities into the model training process. Unlike standard PLL methods that rely
                on static label disambiguation, DNPL leverages a sophisticated algorithm to dynamically
907
                adjust the influence of each label based on its correlation with model predictions. This
908
                method effectively minimizes overfitting to incorrect candidate labels, enhances the ro-
909             bustness of learning, and allows the model to better discern the true label within noisy or
910             ambiguous candidate sets.
