011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Error feedback, originally proposed a decade ago by Seide et al (2014), is an im-
014            mensely popular strategy for stabilizing the convergence behavior of distributed
015            algorithms employing communication compression via the application of contrac-
016
               tive compression operators, such as greedy and random sparsification, quantiza-
               tion, and low-rank approximation. While our algorithmic and theoretical under-
017
               standing of error feedback has grown immensely over the years, several important
018
               considerations remained elusive. For example, the theory of error feedback is
019            fully focused on the smooth convex and nonconvex regimes, and results in the
020            nonsmooth convex setting are limited. This is not a coincidence: Error feedback
021            works when the gradients converge, and this is not necessarily the case in the non-
022            smooth setting. Further, existing stepsize rules for error feedback are limited to
023            constant schedules; a by-product of the current theoretical approach to analyzing
024            error feedback. By modifying the algorithmic design of error feedback, we are
025            able to resolve these issues. In particular, we provide a comprehensive analysis
026            covering both the smooth and nonsmooth convex regimes, and give support for
027
               constant, decreasing and adaptive (Polyak-type) stepsizes. This is the first time
               such results are obtained. In particular, this is the first time adaptive stepsizes
028
               have successfully been combined with compression mechanisms. Our theoretical
029
               results are corroborated with suitable numerical experiments.

      1.1   E RROR F EEDBACK
075
      Error feedback (EF), originally proposed by Seide et al. (2014) a decade ago, is a mechanism that
076
      stabilizes compressed optimization algorithms. Several EF variants were studied in the centralized
077   or distributed setting by, e.g., Stich et al. (2018); Cordonnier (2018); Wu et al. (2018); Alistarh et al.
078   (2018); Karimireddy et al. (2019); Gorbunov et al. (2020); Qian et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2021);
079   Basu et al. (2019); Khirirat et al. (2020). However, analyses for distributed EF algorithms often
080   make the bounded gradient (Cordonnier, 2018; Alistarh et al., 2018) or homogeneous (IID) data
081   assumption (Stich & Karimireddy, 2019), thus leading to the less optimal rate (e.g. O(1/K 2/3 )
082   for nonconvex problems). To alleviate these issues, one re-engineered EF mechanism, called EF21,
083   was proposed by RichtГЎrik et al. (2021). EF21 attains the O(1/K) rate in the deterministic regime
084   (RichtГЎrik et al., 2021) with the provably lower complexity constant than classical gradient de-
085   scent for sparse problems (RichtГЎrik et al., 2023), and the O(1/K 1/2 ) rate in the stochastic set-
086   ting (Fatkhullin et al., 2024). Another recent EF variant that enjoys fast convergence over arbitrarily
087
      heterogeneous data is EControl (Gao et al., 2023).
088
089   1.2   N ONSMOOTH O PTIMIZATION
090
091
      The convergence bounds of error feedback algorithms have been extensively studied under the
      smoothness assumption of objective functions, such as Lipschitz continuity of the gradients. How-
092
      ever, this assumption often fails to capture machine learning problems that involve minimizing
093
      nonsmooth functions, e.g. Lipschitz continuity of the functions. For example, support vector
094   machines (Hearst et al., 1998) utilize hinge loss to achieve sparse solutions, while robust regres-
095   sion (Holland & Welsch, 1977) employs absolute loss to create regressors that are resistant to out-
096   liers. Moreover, many deep neural networks exhibit nonsmooth behaviors, particularly when using
097   activation functions such as rectified linear units (ReLU) and leaky ReLU (Maas et al., 2013). In var-
098   ious network models, the Lipschitz continuity parameter of the objective functions can be estimated
099   efficiently, as demonstrated in, e.g. Anil et al. (2019); BГ©thune et al. (2023); Jordan & Dimakis
100   (2020); Latorre et al. (2020).
101   Designing optimization methods for nonsmooth objective functions possess significant challenges,
102   compared to optimizing smooth functions. Subgradient methods are commonly used to tackle non-
103   smooth problems. However, when these methods incorporate compressed subgradients to reduce
104   communication costs, they may not ensure necessarily convergence. This issue is particularly evi-
105   dent in the case of signSGD which can diverge even for simple nonsmooth problems by Karimireddy
106   et al. (2019). To mitigate this problem, a very limited number of prior works have explored the ben-
107   efits of error feedback in nonsmooth regimes. In particular, KarimireddyвҲҡ et al. (2019) demonstrated
      that error feedback can enable subgradient methods to achieve the O(1/ K) convergence rate while

      reducing communication costs. Nonetheless, the existing analysis frameworks requires the stepsizes
109   to be constant, which may not yield optimal convergence performance in practice. This raises an
110   open question: can we develop a convergence analysis framework that allows error feedback algo-
111   rithms to accomodate parameter-free stepsize rules under possibly nonsmooth regimes?

198   2     R ELATED W ORK
199
200   Stepsize selection. The main hyperparameter that affects the convergence of stochastic optimiza-
201   tion algorithms such as SGD is the stepsize. Several stepsizes have been proposed to guarantee
202
      and improve the algorithmic convergence. For example, SGD converges towards the neighborhood
      of the minimizer when we use a constant stepsize (Gower et al., 2019; Garrigos & Gower, 2023;
203
      Gower et al., 2021; Ghadimi & Lan, 2013; Needell et al., 2014), and it enjoys the convergence to-
204
      wards the exact minimizer when we choose a decreasing stepsize (Gower et al., 2019; Garrigos &
205   Gower, 2023; Shamir & Zhang, 2013; Robbins & Monro, 1951) and step-decay stepsize (Ge et al.,
206   2019; Wang et al., 2021; Li & Arora, 2020). However, these stepsize schedules require significant
207   hyperparameter hand-tuning efforts to maximize the convergence speed in practice.
208
209   Polyak stepsizes. Adaptive stepsize rules adjust stepsizes to maximize the algorithmic conver-
210   gence on the fly. One classical adaptive stepsize rule is by Polyak (1987). The Polyak stepsize
211   computes its value based on the function values at the current iterate and the minimizer, and on
212   the Euclidean norm of the (sub)gradient to maximize a convergence bound at each iteration. Well-
213   known results of deterministic (sub)gradient descent with the Polyak stepsize were shown by Hazan
214   & Kakade (2019); Boyd et al. (2003). The algorithm attains O(1/K) convergence for smooth con-
215   vex regimes and the O(1/K 1/2 ) convergence for nonsmooth convex regimes. The Polyak stepsize
      has also been extended to stochastic optimization algorithms, such as SGD and stochastic momen-

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      tum algorithms, by many recent works including Prazeres & Oberman (2021); Berrada et al. (2020);
217   Loizou et al. (2021); Jiang & Stich (2024); Schaipp et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023).
218
219   Other adaptive stepsizes. Other adaptive stepsize schedules are line search and statistical adaptive
220   procedures. First, line search techniques determine an acceptable stepsize at each iteration to obtain
221   a sufficient decrease in the objective function. One common line search is Armijo Armijo (1966).
222   Convergence analyses of gradient-based algorithms using the Armijo line search and its variants
223   have been conducted in both deterministic and stochastic settings, as demonstrated by Vaswani
224   et al. (2019); Ahookhosh & Ghaderi (2017); Galli et al. (2024); Kunstner et al. (2024); Jiang &
225   Stich (2024). Second, statistical tests detect stationarity of the iterate progress to decrease the fixed
226   stepsize. Despite their convergence benefits empirically by Zhang et al. (2020); Pesme et al. (2020),
227
      the algorithms using these adaptive tests lack theoretical guarantees.

      Adaptive gradient methods. Adaptive gradient algorithms refer to SGD that use stepsizes com-
      puted adaptively based on magnitudes of previous stochastic gradients. Popular adaptive gradient
230
      algorithms include Adagrad (Duchi et al., 2011), Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) and RMSProp (Tiele-

      man, 2012) for effectively solving sparse optimization (Duchi et al., 2013) and deep learning

      8     C ONCLUSION
510
511   We have presented a comprehensive analysis of EF21-P, a useful error feedback algorithm, for
512   smooth and nonsmooth convex regimes. Our analysis allows EF21-P to employ constant, decreasing,
513   and Polyak stepsizes for deterministic and stochastic interpolation problems. Specifically, EF21-P
                                                                вҲҡ
514   with the Polyak stepsizes enjoys convergence at the O(1/ K) rate for nonsmooth regimes, and at
515   the O(1/K) rate for smooth regimes. This marks the first successful incorporation of the Polyak
516   stepsize into error feedback algorithms, which is useful for solving problems, where Lipschitz pa-
517   rameters are inaccessible. Finally, our experiments on logistic regression and support vector ma-
518   chines indicate significant convergence improvements achieved by EF21-P using our Polyak stepsize
519   compared to constant and decreasing stepsizes.
520
521
      Future works. There are many interesting extensions of our work. By the current analysis frame-
      work in this paper, we can investigate how other adaptive stepsize strategies affect the convergence of
522
      EF21-P, e.g. Adagrad (Duchi et al., 2011), gradient diversity (HorvГЎth et al., 2022), Armijo (Armijo,
523
      1966), and Nonnegative Gauss-Newton stepsizes (Orvieto & Xiao, 2024). Another challenging ex-
524   tension of our analysis is to generalize EF21-P to minimize nonsmooth and (generalized) smooth
525   nonconvex functions. This extension requires us to revisit the Lyapunov function or to modify the
526   EF21-P update to prove its convergence for any stepsize strategies.
