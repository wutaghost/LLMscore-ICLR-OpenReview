019                                                  A BSTRACT
020
               In this paper, we investigate the potential of Snapshot Compressive Imaging (SCI)
021            for efficiently recovering 3D scenes from a single temporally compressed image.
022            SCI offers a cost-effective approach using a series of 2D masks to compress video
023            data into a single image captured by 2D imaging sensors. However, traditional
024            SCI reconstruction methods face challenges with generalization and maintaining
025            multi-view consistency. Recent advances have introduced Neural Radiance Fields
026            (NeRF) to estimate 3D scenes from SCI images, but NeRF’s implicit representa-
027            tion struggles to capture fine details and support fast training and rendering. To
028            address these issues, we propose SCISplat, a 3D Gaussian Splatting-based frame-
029
               work for decoding SCI images and achieving high-quality scene reconstruction
               from a single SCI image. First, we design an initialization protocol that robustly
030
               estimates the initial point cloud and camera poses from an SCI image, leveraging a
031
               learning-based Structure-from-Motion method. Second, we integrate the SCI im-
032            age formation model into the 3D Gaussian training process and jointly optimize
033            the Gaussians and camera poses to enhance reconstruction quality. Experiments
034            demonstrate that SCISplat surpasses state-of-the-art methods, achieving a 2.3 dB
035            improvement in reconstruction quality and a 10× faster training speed. Further-
036            more, results on real-world datasets show that our approach produces cleaner and
037            sharper details, underscoring its practical value.

040   1    I NTRODUCTION
041
042   Compressing 3D information of a scene in a single snapshot compressive image could vastly reduce
043   the storage requirement and transmission bandwidth. For instance, a fast-moving car or quadrotor
044   may capture the street view around with SCI instead of an expensive high-speed camera. A sys-
045
      tematic method to effectively reconstruct the 3D environment from these snapshots is needed in this
      case. However, reconstructing the underlying 3D scene structure directly from a single snapshot
046
      compressive image has yet to be well-explored. A typical video SCI system (Yuan et al., 2021a)
047
      contains a hardware encoder and software decoder. The encoder captures the scene, and input images
048   are optically modulated with temporally varying masks to form a single SCI measurement, which
049   a 2D camera can record. Then, for the software decoding, the compressed measurement and cor-
050   responding modulation masks are fed into a reconstruction algorithm to recover the fully captured
051   images of the scene. In recent years, many SCI reconstruction algorithms have been developed,
052   ranging from traditional model-based methods (Yuan, 2016) to deep-learning-based methods (Yuan
053   et al., 2020; 2021b; Wang et al., 2023). These deep-learning-based methods usually exceed tra-
      ditional methods by a large margin in terms of reconstruction quality and speed. However, these


                                                            1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      methods need to be pre-trained with an extensive collection of images for about 100 hours and do
055   not generalize well for novel modulation masks, which means the model needs to be fine-tuned ev-
056   ery time before performing the actual reconstruction. Recently, Li et al. (2024) proposed SCINeRF,
057   which employs an implicit neural representation to explore underlying 3D scene structure from SCI
058   measurements. SCINeRF is agonistic to different patterns of modulation masks. However, it suf-
059   fers from a substantially long reconstruction time of about 10 hours for a single measurement and
060   a slow inference speed of 0.25FPS. A recent advancement, 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl
061   et al., 2023), extends the implicit neural rendering to explicit 3D Gaussians. By projecting these op-
062   timized Gaussians onto the image plane, 3DGS enables real-time rendering, significantly improving
063
      both the efficiency and rendering quality compared to NeRF during training and inference.
064   To this end, we propose SCISplat, the first 3DGS-based approach to efficiently reconstruct explicit
065   3D scene structure from a single snapshot compressive image. However, naively applying 3DGS
066   to a single SCI image is challenging. First, initializing 3D Gaussians with point cloud and camera
067   poses using COLMAP (Schönberger & Frahm, 2016) from a single snapshot compressive image is
068   impossible because of measurement noise and information loss caused by compression. Second,
069
      the original 3DGS requires multi-view images for supervision, while we only have one SCI im-
      age. To address these challenges, we first propose a novel initialization protocol. It first quickly
070
      decodes a sequence of degraded frames from the measurement that contains low-frequency cues of
071
      the 3D scene, then robustly estimates the initial point cloud and poses from degraded frames to kick
072   off Gaussians training by leveraging a learning-based Structure-from-Motion (SfM) method, VG-
073   GSfM (Wang et al., 2024a). After initialization, we jointly optimize Gaussians and camera poses
074   to ensure higher reconstruction quality and trajectory accuracy. Finally, we apply a specifically
075   designed loss function by incorporating the SCI image formation model with the 3DGS training
076   procedure. It minimizes photometric loss between synthesized measurements from differentiable
077   Gaussian rasterization and real SCI measurements. Moreover, to compensate for the ill-posedness
078   of SCI image reconstruction, we optimize Gaussians with Monte Carlo Markov Chain (MCMC)
079   strategy (Kheradmand et al., 2024) that effectively suppresses noise.
080   We conduct extensive experiments on synthetic and real datasets to evaluate our method properly.
081   Regarding rendering quality, our method exceeds the SOTA SCINeRF method by 2.3 dB on syn-
082   thetic datasets and shows visually sharper reconstruction results on real datasets. As for efficiency,
083   our method can consistently yield better results than SCINeRF with 820× faster inference/rendering
084   speed and 10× faster training speed.
085   In summary, our contributions are listed as follows:
086
087          • We propose the first 3DGS-based SCI decoding method that can efficiently restore com-
088            pressed images from a single snapshot compressive image and is also agnostic to modula-
089            tion masks of different patterns.
090          • We propose an initialization protocol to derive point clouds and poses from a single snap-
091            shot compressive image robustly, which will benefit any downstream 3D task from SCI
092            measurements.
093          • We show that our method surpasses the current state-of-the-art (SOTA) method in terms of
094            reconstruction quality and training speed with extensive experiments on synthetic and real
095            datasets.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
099
      Video SCI Reconstruction. Early methods for SCI image decoding primarily relied on regular-
100
      ized optimization-based approaches (Yang et al., 2020; Liao et al., 2014; Yuan, 2016; Liu et al.,
101
      2018). These techniques estimate compressed images by iteratively solving optimization problems
102   with various regularizers, such as sparsity (Yang et al., 2020) and total variation (TV) (Yuan, 2016).
103   Instead of gradient descent, most methods utilize the alternating direction method of multipliers
104   (ADMM) (Boyd et al., 2011), which provides better results and adaptability across different sys-
105   tems. Notable approaches include decompress SCI (Liu et al., 2018) and GAP-TV (Yuan, 2016).
106   However, these methods suffer from long runtimes and limited flexibility for high-resolution images.
107   With the advent of deep learning, many recent SCI decoding methods have shifted to employing deep
      neural networks like U-net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) and GAN (Goodfellow et al., 2014). These


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      learning-based methods require extensive training data, often comprising thousands or even millions
109   of synthetic SCI measurements and masks, which can be expensive. The networks are optimized
110   using various losses, such as mean squared error (MSE), feature loss (Johnson et al., 2016), and
111   GAN loss (Miao et al., 2019). Qiao et al. (2020) developed an end-to-end CNN using reconstruction
112   loss to recover compressed images. Cheng et al. (2020) proposed a bidirectional recurrent neu-
113   ral network architecture to reconstruct temporal frames sequentially. RevSCI (Cheng et al., 2021)
114   addressed the time and memory limitations of large-scale video SCI training by introducing a multi-
115   group reversible 3D CNN architecture. ADMM-Net (Ma et al., 2019) modeled the decoding process
116   as a tensor recovery problem from random linear measurements, interpreting the ADMM process as
117
      a deep neural network. MetaSCI (Wang et al., 2021) utilized a meta-modulated CNN to enhance the
      adaptability of the reconstruction network for large-scale data and novel masks. Plug-and-play fast
118
      and flexible denoising CNN (PnP-FFDNet) (Yuan et al., 2020) combined deep denoising networks
119
      with ADMM, enabling quick and flexible reconstruction. Later, Yuan et al. (2021b) developed a
120   fast deep video denoising network (FastDVDNet), enhancing PnP-FFDNet’s performance with the
121   latest deep denoising techniques. Wang et al. introduced spatial-temporal transformers and Effi-
122   cientSCI (Wang et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2024) to exploit spatial and temporal correlations within the
123   image decoding process using Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) architecture. These methods re-
124   quire pre-training on synthetic datasets and thus may not generalize well to real datasets. Moreover,
125   existing deep learning-based methods can only reconstruct 2D images corresponding to the masks,
126   making it challenging to restore view-consistent images and estimate 3D scenes. Li et al. (2024)
127   proposed SCINeRF, which recovers the underlying 3D scene from an SCI image by jointly optimiz-
128
      ing camera poses and NeRF. However, due to implicit scene representation, SCINeRF suffers from
      high-frequency noises and low training/rendering speeds.
129
130
131   Efficient Radiance Field Rendering. NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2021) revolutionized 3D scene
132   representation using multi-layer perceptron (MLP), achieving impressive results in novel view syn-
133
      thesis. However, it struggles with long training times due to its costly volumetric rendering pro-
      cess. To address these challenges, various adaptations have emerged, such as grid-based methods
134
      like TensoRF (Chen et al., 2022), Plenoxels (Fridovich-Keil et al., 2022), and HexPlane (Cao &
135
      Johnson, 2023), and hash-based methods like InstantNGP (Müller et al., 2022). Despite these ad-
136   vancements, rendering speed remains a critical bottleneck. 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023) overcomes
137   some of these challenges by enabling real-time rendering with quality comparable to state-of-the-
138   art NeRF methods through an efficient explicit representation. However, 3DGS relies heavily on
139   COLMAP (Schönberger & Frahm, 2016) and multi-view sharp images for generating high-quality
140   point clouds and camera poses, which are sensitive to the quality of initial point clouds and camera
141   parameters. Subsequent works have proposed several refinements to address these issues. InstaS-
142   plat (Fan et al., 2024) uses DUSt3R (Wang et al., 2024b), a transformer-based dense stereo model,
143   to initialize dense point clouds from sparse-view images. However, it primarily focuses on stereo
144
      matching, resulting in less accurate camera parameters. In contrast, VGGSfM (Wang et al., 2024a)
      introduces a fully differentiable SfM pipeline with deep learning integration at every stage, pro-
145
      viding highly accurate camera poses beneficial for downstream tasks, including 3D reconstruction
146
      (Heo, 2024). Additionally, 3DGS employs a suboptimal, heuristic-based densification strategy for
147   growing Gaussians. To improve this, various works (Bulò et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Ye et al.,
148   2024) have focused on redesigning error criteria to densify Gaussians. Notably, Kheradmand et al.
149   (2024) reformulates Gaussian updates as state transitions in MCMC samples, stabilizing training
150   dynamics by recomputing updated opacity and scale values when cloning Gaussians.

155   Given an SCI measurement capturing a 3D scene and modulation masks, we aim to reconstruct
156   the target 3D scene and render high-quality encoded images in real-time. We first propose a novel
157   initialization protocol to estimate point clouds and poses from a single SCI measurement to start
158
      the training procedure. Then, we jointly optimize Gaussians and poses by minimizing photomet-
      ric loss between the synthesized and real measurements. Besides, We also leverage the MCMC
159
      strategy (Kheradmand et al., 2024) to stabilize the training process.

      Densification Strategy. Regarding the densification strategy for Gaussians, we employ MCMC
296
      strategy (Kheradmand et al., 2024) instead of the original for the reasons below. In the original
297
      strategy, the composed opacity values are larger after cloning or splitting Gaussians, making the
298   scene appear slightly brighter. This inconsistent update introduces spiky appearance changes in the
299   scene, which sometimes can make camera poses drift to sub-optimal locations due to the unstable
300   gradient flow from photometric loss, thus collapsing the whole reconstruction. Moreover, due to
301   pixel ambiguity in fitting an SCI measurement, suddenly having high opacity for Gaussians will
302   quickly introduce noise in the reconstructed viewpoint. Specifically, we optimize Gaussians by
303   summing pixels of rendered images, as indicated by Equation 7, to fit the SCI measurement. With
304   this ill-posed loss, a sub-optimal solution could quickly occur where one pixel has a high pixel value
305   close to the sum while others appear much darker. In this case, that pixel will appear as noise on
306
      the reconstructed images. Recalling the original densification strategy, suddenly making individual
      Gaussian opacity high will undoubtedly encourage the reach of this local optimal and thus lead to
307
      noisy reconstruction. In contrast, the MCMC strategy corrects this opacity bias by recomputing
308
      the updated opacity values after densification, resulting in smoother training dynamics and effective
309   noise suppression.

347   Baseline Methods and Evaluation Metrics. Since SCISplat can render high-quality images from
348   estimated 3D scenes, we compare our method against SOTA SCI image/video reconstruction frame-
349   works, including model-based methods such as GAP-TV (Yuan, 2016), and deep-learning-based
350   methods including PnP-FFDNet (Yuan et al., 2020), PnP-FastDVDNet (Yuan et al., 2021b), Effi-
351   cientSCI (Wang et al., 2023) and SCINeRF (Li et al., 2024). For fair comparisons, we fine-tuned
352   EifficientSCI using masks from synthetic datasets and trained SCINeRF from scratch for each scene
353   in synthetic/real datasets.
354   For image synthesis quality, we employ commonly-used metrics, including structural similarity
355   index (SSIM), peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR), and learned perceptual image patch similarity
356   (LPIPS) (Zhang et al., 2018). Since our SCISplat can optimize camera poses, we also evaluated the
357   camera pose estimation capabilities of SCISplat by computing the absolute translation error (ATE),
358   a widely used camera pose estimation evaluation metric in visual odometry.
359
360   Implementation Details. We implemented our method using PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) within
361   the 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023) pipeline. We leveraged Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014) to
362   optimize Gaussians and camera poses. The learning rate for Gaussians is scaled by the square root
363   of 8 since we are forwarding 8 images at once according to the square root rule (André et al., 2022).
364   The learning rate for camera poses is exponentially decreased from 5 × 10−4 to 2.5 × 10−7 . We set
365
      the maximum number of Gaussians as 100000 for MCMC (Kheradmand et al., 2024) strategy. All
      experiments are conducted on an NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU.

      5   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we introduce SCISplat, the first 3DGS-based approach for efficiently reconstructing
533
      a 3D scene from a single snapshot compressive image. SCISplat leverages 3D Gaussian Splatting
534   as its core scene representation, offering remarkable scene fidelity and exceptional speed in both
535   training and rendering. To initiate the training of SCISplat, we propose a novel initialization pro-
536   tocol that robustly derives the initial point cloud and camera poses from an SCI measurement. By
537   incorporating the SCI image formation model into the 3DGS training pipeline, our method achieves
538   real-time rendering of high-quality scene images from a single SCI measurement. Extensive ex-
539   perimental results demonstrate that SCISplat outperforms all state-of-the-art methods in terms of
      rendering quality, training efficiency, and inference speed on both synthetic and real datasets.
