009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
                Self-supervised autoregressive models have achieved significant success across
012             diverse domains, including text, audio, and biological sequences. However, these
013             models often rely heavily on large samples of aligned (parallel) data, limiting
014             their applicability in low-resource settings. To address this limitation, we pro-
015             pose Symbolic Autoencoding (ΣAE) with Straight-Through Gradient Estimators
016             (STGEs)—a latent variable model where the latent space consists of sequences
017             of categorical random variables, resembling sentences in an emergent symbolic
018             language. ΣAE is trained end-to-end using a family of straight-through gradient
019             estimators. In the unsupervised mode, ΣAE learns to compress input data into sym-
020
                bolic sentences and reconstructs the data from this emergent language. In weakly
                supervised settings, ΣAE further grounds the latent language by leveraging super-
021
                vised training on the small amount of parallel data available. We evaluate ΣAE
022
                with three well-known quantization mechanisms on four text sequence transduction
023             tasks. Our results show that ΣAE outperforms baseline methods, particularly in
024             low-resource scenarios with limited parallel data.

      1     I NTRODUCTION AND P RELIMINARIES
029
030   Mapping data between symbolic systems is a fundamental problem in information theory and machine
031   learning Kaiser & Bengio (2018); Baziotis et al. (2019); Fortuin et al. (2019). Whether converting
032
      natural language into structured representations or relating biological sequences to their functions, the
      central challenge lies in effectively encoding and representing information from one symbolic system
033
      into another. In natural language processing, for example, this may involve translating sentences into
034
      semantic graphs, requiring both syntactic parsing and an understanding of deeper semantic structures.
035   Sánchez et al. (2023)
036
037   In cases where large-scale parallel datasets are available—such as aligned language pairs in machine
038
      translation—supervised models can be trained to perform these mappings directly. However, in the
      more common scenario where parallel data is scarce or unavailable, how can we still learn to map
039
      sequences between two symbolic systems? Magueresse et al. (2020); Lample & Conneau (2019);
040
      Joshi et al. (2020); Gregor et al. (2013).
041
042   To address this challenge, we propose Symbolic Autoencoding (ΣAE), a weakly-supervised frame-
043   work for sequence transduction across symbolic systems X and Z. ΣAE is designed to leverage both
044
      non-parallel data and limited amounts of parallel data to establish bidirectional mappings between two
      systems. At its core, ΣAE employs a latent variable model where the latent space comprises sequences
045
      of categorical random variables—discrete symbolic representations with variable lengths. These
046
      latent sequences serve as an emergent language, enabling both lossy compression (via autoencoding)
047   and alignment with parallel data via standard supervised training when available.
048
049   The training process alternates between two complementary tasks:
050   • Unsupervised Autoencoding: ΣAE reconstructs sequences through bidirectional mappings:
051               M        M
                  zx
            – Z −−−→ X −−−   → Z, where Mzx serves as the encoder and Mxz as the decoder; or
                            xz

052              Mxz     Mzx
053         – X −−−→ Z −−−→ X, where Mxz acts as the encoder and Mzx as the decoder.
          Both mappings utilize discrete bottlenecks to learn emergent symbolic representations.

077   Key to ΣAE is the discrete bottleneck layer, which outputs both token probability distributions
078   and quantized samples, enabling end-to-end differentiability through straight-through gradient es-
079   timators Bengio et al. (2013) and the reparameterization trick. We explore three quantization
080   mechanisms—softmax-argmax, Gumbel-softmax-argmax, and vector quantization (VQ-VAE)—to
081
      optimize training efficiency and stability. To mitigate hidden sequence collapse, where EOS tokens
      are prematurely selected and the latent sequences are ignored Bowman et al. (2016); Sánchez et al.
082
      (2023); Zhao et al. (2018), we introduce a gradient estimator for EOS masking probabilities, ensuring
083
      gradients are propagated across the full sequence.
084
085   In the absence of parallel data, ΣAE learns an emergent symbolic representation within the bottleneck,
086   which can be interpreted as a lossy compression mechanism. This emergent language effectively
087
      captures the underlying structure of the input symbolic system.
088   We evaluate ΣAE on four sequence transduction benchmarks (SCAN, PCFG SET, CFQ, and COGS)
089   and demonstrate superior performance in both unsupervised and weakly-supervised settings. Our
090   results highlight the framework’s ability to learn compressed symbolic representations, enabling
091   generalization across modalities and establishing ΣAE as a robust approach for symbolic sequence
092   transduction.
093
      Related work.
094
095   Our work intersects with several key areas in unsupervised and weakly supervised learning through
096   discrete representations. Baziotis et al. (2019) connected two encoder-decoder models via a hidden
097   sequence layer, employing a reconstruction loss and a language model prior loss for unsupervised text
098   compression. Kaiser & Bengio (2018) explored semantic hashing (Salakhutdinov & Hinton, 2009)
099
      and the Gumbel-Softmax trick (Jang et al., 2017) for generating interpretable, discrete encodings.
      Kaiser et al. (2018) further experimented with the Gumbel-Softmax trick, semantic hashing, and
100
      two vector quantization variants van den Oord et al. (2017) to learn a compressed discrete latent
101
      variable for fast parallel decoding in a seq2seq model. Sánchez et al. (2023) trained Hidden Schema
102   Networks, a discrete VAE model trained via the Gumbel-Softmax trick mapping input sequences to
103   random walk instances on a learned graph, and demonstrated the model’s ability to learn interpretable
104   representations of structured data. Similarly, Fortuin et al. (2019) investigated training VAEs with
105   discrete bottlenecks and examined the use of continuous paths alongside discrete ones. Zhao et al.
106   (2018) proposed a fixed-length discrete latent variable model for learning interpretable dialogue
107   action representations via Gumbel-Softmax and maximizing the mutual information between data
      and latent actions.

      In the realm of semi-supervised learning, Kingma et al. (2014) studied semi-supervised autoencoding
109   by treating the classification label as a categorical hidden variable, emphasizing the integration of
110   labeled and unlabeled data.
111
112
      Zhu et al. (2017) and He et al. (2016) enforced consistency across translation tasks, with Zhu et al.
      (2017) using adversarial networks and He et al. (2016) employing Reinforcement Learning (RL) to
113
      update the models. Using REINFORCE Williams (1992) to optimize the discrete latent variables has
114
      been explored in other contexts. Evtimova et al. (2018) investigated emergent communication, where
115   two agents collaboratively converse about images and textual descriptions using sequences of binary
116   symbols from a fixed set to match the images and text. Similarly, Miao & Blunsom (2016) proposed
117   a generative variational auto-encoding sentence compression model that compresses a document into
118   a summary sentence and reconstructs the document from the summary, employing a latent language
119   model as the encoder.
120   Our work introduces a parallel approach, in contrast with RL and cycle-consistency based methods,
121   leveraging straight-through gradient estimators to train symbolic probabilistic generative models
122   end-to-end.
123   Furthermore, numerous studies have focused on the discretization of elements and representations
124   in neural networks Liu et al. (2022; 2021); Tamkin et al. (2023); Peng et al. (2018); Maddison et al.
125   (2016).
126
      Our proposed solution also parallels the technique of back-translation (Sennrich et al., 2015; Çaglar
127   Gülçehre et al., 2015; 2017), which typically involves training an intermediate system on parallel data
128   to translate target monolingual data into the source language, thereby generating synthetic parallel
129   corpora for further training Edunov et al. (2018). The ΣAE framework is akin to an online version of
130   back-translation, where the intermediate system is continuously improving without storing synthetic
131   sequences.

      2.1     BACKGROUND : T RAINING A SINGLE DISCRETE RANDOM VARIABLE
141
      Consider a probabilistic latent variable model that maps an input x to a probability distribution over a
142   latent variable z, denoted as Pθ (Z|X = x). We assume the ability to sample from this distribution
143   and compute a loss l(z) for a sample, which may be influenced by downstream components in the
144   computation graph.
145
      Even for continuous random variables, optimizing this process is challenging, as the loss is stochastic
146
      and non-differentiable. The average loss for a given input is expressed as Ez∼pθ (Z|X=x) [l(z)], and
147
      the overall loss across the input distribution is Ex∼p(x) [Ez∼pθ (Z|X=x) [l(z)]].
148
149
      2.1.1     O PTIMIZATION METHODS
150
151   Several methods have been proposed to minimize this average loss:
152
153
      • REINFORCE Williams (1992) uses the identity:
154                      ∇θ Ez∼pθ (Z|X=x) [l(z)] = Ez∼pθ (Z|X=x) [l(z)∇θ log pθ (Z|X = x)]                  (1)
155
156       This method estimates the gradient for the average loss using a one-sample Monte Carlo approxi-
157       mation:
158                              ∇θ Ez∼pθ (Z|X=x) [l(z)] ≈ l(z)∇θ log pθ (Z|X = xi )                        (2)
159
160       Notably, the gradient is estimated directly from the distribution samples, without requiring differen-
161       tiability of the distribution. Variants such as REINFORCE with baselines were later introduced to
          reduce gradient variance Gu et al. (2015).

163     instead, they compute a deterministic weighted sum of the embeddings. Additionally, Graves
164     (2016) introduces a halting mechanism for variable-length, adaptive computation using continuous
165     embeddings rather than discrete representations.
166     In Correia et al. (2019); Peters et al. (2019) gradual temperature annealing is employed to make
167     the distribution increasingly deterministic. At each training step, the objective is minimizing
168     l(Ez∼pθ (Z|X=x) z)—loss of the average embedding- rather than minimizing l(z) for specific sam-
169     ples z ∼ pθ (Z|X = x). This expectation is differentiable, allowing gradient-based optimization.
170     Over the course of training, the temperature is annealed to zero, transitioning the expected value to
171     the argmax of the distribution, effectively producing discrete samples.

212   2.2     D ISCRETE B OTTLENECK
213
214   We refer to the quantization layer in the model as the Discrete Bottleneck (DB). In this framework,
215   sampling from the distribution is required to propagate information forward in the computation graph.
      Importantly, to maintain the discreteness of the latent sequence elements, continuous averaging over


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      embeddings (as discussed in section 2.1.1) is not permitted. Allowing such averaging would introduce
217   information leakage through the continuous averaging weights, enabling the encoder to indirectly
218   pass continuous information to the decoder. By enforcing this constraint, input features are strictly
219   represented as compositions of discrete symbols, ensuring that no continuous information is used for
220   reconstruction.
221
      The DB can be defined as a function p, vq = DB(v), where:
222
223   • p represents a discrete distribution over tokens, facilitating supervised training with negative
224     log-likelihood loss when labels are available for z.
225   • vq is a quantized vector that serves as input to subsequent models or layers, such as the decoder in
226
        unsupervised training when labels are not provided.
                                                                                   P|V |
227   Here, p ∈ [0, 1]|V | is a probability vector over the vocabulary V , with i=1 pi = 1, and |V |,
228   the vocabulary size, is treated as a hyperparameter. The discrete nature of the DB implies that the

371   In symbolic autoencoding, the encoder generates hidden tokens until an End-of-Sequence (EOS)
372   token or a maximum length is reached. This process involves a discrete decision about when to
373   halt generation, for which the model never receives gradient feedback. Specifically, in unsupervised
374
      training, the loss gradient doesn’t directly inform the model that mistakenly assigning a high likelihood
      to EOS has a penalty beyond the negative log likelihood loss: it can prematurely stop the entire
375
      sequence generation.
376
377   In our early autoencoder trainings we empirically observed that the models tended to completely
      ignore the latent sequences, or rely excessively on the first tokens of the hidden representation,


                                                                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      leading to underutilization of subsequent tokens. This resulted in what we term hidden sequence
379   collapse—a tendency for the model to terminate sequences prematurely and only train the decoder.
380   This probem is similar in nature to the posterior collapse problem in VAEs for textual data, and
381   has been reported in various forms in prior work Bowman et al. (2016); Havrylov & Titov (2020);
382   Newman et al. (2020); Zhao et al. (2018). To address this, we developed a soft-masking mechanism
383   that allows for gradient-based learning of when to halt generation.

      4    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
502
503
      Exploring autoencoding for sequences, especially in weakly supervised settings, reveals significant
504
      challenges and opportunities for advancement. While our approach effectively reduces unsupervised
505   reconstruction loss, it does not always directly translate to improved model performance. One
506   key limitation is the slower training pace of autoregressive models due to their sequential nature,
507   which hinders parallelization. Moreover, our findings suggest unsupervised samples have less
508   impact on accuracy compared to supervised ones, which need to be studied in future work. The
509   ΣAE framework’s adaptability to various seq2seq models and its applicability across different data
510   modalities—from text to images, audio, or video—highlight its broad utility. This flexibility suggests
511   numerous pathways for further exploration and application beyond the current study’s scope.
512
513   5    C ONCLUSION
514
515   In this study, we introduced a framework for learning latent variable models where the latent space
516   is both discrete and sequential—representing sentences from an emergent symbolic system. We
517   proposed a novel approach for training seq2seq models using non-parallel data. This was achieved
518   by connecting two models through a discrete bottleneck, enabling the output sequence from one
519   model to serve as the hidden representation for the other. This design creates a unique autoencoder
520   architecture in which both the encoder and decoder are seq2seq models. To ensure the end-to-end
521
      trainability of this autoencoder within a gradient descent framework, we proposed gradient substitute
      and autoregressive masking techniques. Our unsupervised experiments validated the feasibility of
522
      training models within this discrete sequential autoencoder setup.
523
524   Further expanding on this concept we leveraged the symbolic autoencoders to train seq2seq models
525   beyond parallel data and facilitate the use of non-parallel data. Demonstrating a practical application
526   of our methodology, we presented evidence of performance improvements in weak supervision
527
      settings by utilizing unsupervised monolingual data, tested across four distinct seq2seq datasets.

683   A.1   R EMARKS ON ΣAE FRAMEWORK
684
685
      For all our straight-through gradient estimations, as training progresses, models become more
      confident in their predictions, resulting in more polarized score distributions. This polarization helps
686
      the models identify the most likely token with increasing certainty, making the scores sparser and
687
      improving the accuracy of gradient approximations.
688
689   While we only use symbolic autoencoding in reconstruction setups, the framework is adaptable to
690   additional models and data sources. For instance, one could imagine models Mzy , Myx , etc., each
691
      with their own supervised and reconstruction losses (e.g., Lzy , Lyxz , Lxyx , etc.) to be optimized.
      Unlike some multi-task scenarios where individual tasks may appear independent or unrelated, in
692
      the ΣAE framework, improvement in one task can directly benefit others, creating a synergy that
693
      enhances overall performance.

      A.2   EOS G RADIENT A PPROXIMATION
696
697   The EOS collapse phenomena can be explained by the model’s lack of understanding of the EOS
698   token’s impact. Without explicit feedback, the model does not learn the importance of distributing
699   information across the entire sequence. The EOS collapse phenomenon arises from the model’s lack
700   of understanding of the EOS token’s role in sequence generation. Without explicit feedback, the
701   model fails to learn the importance of distributing information evenly across the entire sequence.
      Instead, it observes that tokens appearing later in the sentence are randomly masked out, leading

774
      The autoregressive nature of our model means that a zxz-reconstruction pass on a trained model
      requires a number of forward passes proportional to the latent sequence length. For example, in
775
      SCAN, the computational cost of a zxz pass is approximately 10 times that of a supervised next-token
776
      prediction forward pass. This scaling reflects a fundamental computational trade-off inherent in
777   autoregressive unsupervised sequence models. This limitation is not unique to ΣAE but is shared
778   across autoregressive frameworks, including on-policy reinforcement learning, where rollouts are
779   computationally expensive, and recurrent neural networks, whose computation scales with sequence
780   length. While non-autoregressive approaches such as diffusion models could alleviate this scaling
781   issue, they are not yet well-suited for discrete random variables and introduce other challenges.
782
      This computational scaling also highlights the difficulty of studying longer sequences, such as those in
783   natural language tasks. As sequence length and model size grow, the lack of supervised data (e.g., for
784   teacher forcing) causes the compute and memory requirements to increase prohibitively with sequence
785   length. Moreover, such tasks typically require domain-specific techniques and substantial engineering
786   effort. Thus, this study focuses on providing a proof-of-concept under controlled conditions, laying
787   the groundwork for future extensions to more complex, real-world applications.
788
789   In-context learning (ICL) vs. Chain-of-thought (CoT) Prompting Baseline It is helpful to
790   compare the performance of our proposed method with ICL and CoT in state-of-the-art LLMs,
791   however, it is not clear how CoT is applicable to the tasks considered in this work since explicit
792   reasoning steps are not available for the datasets under study. Our synthetic datasets, structured
793
      as symbolic puzzles, lack the annotations required for CoT reasoning. Therefore, we opted for
      evaluating ICL by providing example pairs of (X, Z) to assess to what extent the patterns could
794
      be learned in-context and extrapolated to the test sequences without the need for a neuro-symbolic
795
      auto-encoding. Our ICL baseline uses GPT3.5 which takes as input the concatenation of 20 pairs of
796   (X, Z), followed by the test sample’s X and is then prompted to generate the corresponding Z. We
797   simply then compute the evaluation metrics from Sec. 3.2 using the generated Z sequences, which
798   constitute the results in Figures 2- 5.

831   A.5    E VALUATION M ETRICS
832
833   In assessing the performance of the ΣAE framework, we measured two distinct metrics: sentence
834   accuracy (SA) and token accuracy (TA). These metrics are designed to provide both a holistic and
835   a detailed view of the model’s capabilities. Sentence accuracy (SA) for a sample is counted as 1 if
836   the entire sentence is correctly generated. Token accuracy (TA) is a more granular measure, where
837   correctness of each predicted token in all sentences are measured separately. This metric allows for
838   partial credit within a sentence, providing a finer understanding of the model’s performance at the
839
      token level.
840   The token accuracy can be measured with two methods: We can teacher-force the correct previous
841   tokens (as per the ground truth) to the model and measure its accuracy in predicting the next token.
842   Alternatively, the model’s previous outputs (which may or may not be correct) can be used as inputs
843   for generating subsequent tokens. This autoregressive approach is generally more challenging than
844   teacher-forcing.
845   Each X has a unique corresponding Z, simplifying the assessment of accuracy in this direction,
846   therefore, evaluating Mxz performance is simply done by examining the Autoregressive Z TA/SA,
847   directly measuring the model’s capability to generate accurate Z sequences. For a given Z, however,
848   there could be multiple valid X sequences. Therefore, to evaluate Mzx , we utilize the Teacher-forced
849   X TA, which restricts the range of correct X sequences for end tokens. Another approach is the
850   Reconstruction Z TA/SA, where a model Mxz maps a generated sequence x̂ back to Z, and the
851
      accuracy of this reconstructed sequence serves as a proxy for the correctness of x̂.
