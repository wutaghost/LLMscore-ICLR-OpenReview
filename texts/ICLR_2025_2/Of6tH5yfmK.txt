009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose S ELF C ONTROL, an inference-time model control method utilizing
012            gradients to control the behavior of large language models (LLMs) without explicit
013            human annotations. Given a desired behavior expressed in a natural language suffix
014            string concatenated to the input prompt, S ELF C ONTROL computes gradients of
015            the LLM’s self-evaluation of the suffix with respect to its latent representations.
016
               The gradients are used to directly control the auto-regressive generation process
               towards desired behaviors, which eliminates human supervision, achieves precise
017
               and transparent control, and offers on-the-fly adaptability. To further enhance
018
               efficiency, we introduce S ELF C ONTROL PREFIX , a compact module that encapsulates
019            the learned representations from gradients into a P REFIX C ONTROLLER, facilitating
020            efficient inference-time control with no latency compared to the original model and
021            allowing control for multiple behaviors simultaneously. Our experiments demon-
022            strate S ELF C ONTROL’s efficacy across multiple domains, where it improves over
023            SOTA for 8.3% in detoxification, 3.1% in truthfulness enhancement, 4%∼10%
024            in controlling on emotion tones, and 48.2% in privacy protection, i.e., completely
025            remove privacy leakage issue. We make available an anonymous Google Colab
026            demo and release our code.

059
      Besides aligning LLM behavior during training time, recently much research has been focused on
060
      controlling LLM at inference time, e.g., through latent representation engineering (RepE)(Zou et al.,
061   2023a). These works still require curating a set of contrastive pairs as a demonstration. Building upon
062   this, in this paper, we’re studying whether we can control LLMs’ behavior via their self-evaluation,
063   i.e. use LLMs as a judge to assess the quality of their own outputs (Kadavath et al., 2022; Bai
064   et al., 2022b; Zheng et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024). We thus introduce S ELF C ONTROL, a novel
065   gradient-based framework for precise LLM behavior control.
066
      The core idea of S ELF C ONTROL is to leverage the LLM’s self-evaluation to control its behavior.
067   For each input prompt, we formulate a desired behavior as a natural language question, asking the
068   model to assess whether its output aligns with the specified behavior. We term the question as
069   suffix string and concatenate the input prompt, the model’s output, and the suffix string, feeding
070   this combined input back into the model. We then compute the likelihood of the behavior-aligned
071   response to the suffix string as a learning signal for behavior control, which we named as suffix score.
072   Then, we compute the suffix score’s gradient with respect to the latent representations of the original
073   input, which term as suffix gradients. These suffix gradients are then utilized to update the latent
074   representations, controlling the LLM’s behavior towards the desired outcome. We run this procedure
075
      multiple times to iteratively update the input’s latent representations, each time using the modified
      representations to generate new model outputs that increasingly align with the desired behavior.
076
077   S ELF C ONTROL offers several advantages over traditional fine-tuning approaches: 1) Elimination
078   of human-annotation: S ELF C ONTROL leverages the model’s self-evaluation as learning signals,
079   substantially reducing the effort and resources required for preference data collection and scaling. 2)
080   Precise and transparent control: S ELF C ONTROL operates at inference time and directly modifies
081
      the latent representations, which allows for explicit behavior specification and fine-grained control,
      consequently enhancing control interpretability. 3) On-the-fly adaptability: S ELF C ONTROL does
082
      not alter model parameters, enabling easy implementation of behaviors and control of combinations
083
      of multiple behaviors, thus providing unparalleled flexibility. S ELF C ONTROL demonstrates superior
084   performance compared to contrastive learning-based control methods, particularly in areas such as
085   detoxification, truthfulness enhancement, privacy protection, and emotion control. As illustrated in
086   Figure 1, S ELF C ONTROL exhibits remarkable flexibility in controlling LLM for multiple attributes
087   simultaneously, e.g., happiness and calmness.
088
      To enhance its adaptability, efficiency and compositionality, we further propose S ELF C ONTROL PREFIX
089
      on top of S ELF C ONTROL as a general controller across inputs. The core module of S ELF C ON -
090   TROL PREFIX is the P REFIX C ONTROLLER , a prompt-based adapter (Hu et al., 2021; Zhang et al.,
091   2023) optimized to match the latent representations conditioned on this P REFIX C ONTROLLER to
092   the latent representations under regular S ELF C ONTROL. P REFIX C ONTROLLER brings ideas from
093   prefix-prompt tuning research (Shin et al., 2020; Li & Liang, 2021; Yang et al., 2023) to achieve
094   efficient control, which has almost no latency compared to the original model, and greatly outper-
095   forms other control baselines. Furthermore, we show that P REFIX C ONTROLLER is a learnable and
096   composable module that can be easily integrated into the LLM to control multiple model behaviors
097   simultaneously (e.g., being happier, while staying calm), shown in Figure 1, thereby enhancing the
098
      practicality of S ELF C ONTROL for real-world applications.
099   In summary, our primary contributions are as follows:
100

102
        self-evaluation to eliminate the need for human-annotated data, offering more efficient, precise,
        transparent, and adaptable control.
103
104   • We further develop S ELF C ONTROL PREFIX using P REFIX C ONTROLLER, a PEFT (parameter-efficient
105     fine-tuning) module that enhances S ELF C ONTROL’s adaptability and compositionality, enabling
106

107   • We show S ELF C ONTROL is effective on a diverse range of control tasks to align LLM behaviors with

112   2       R ELATED WORK
113
114   LLM Control and Representation Engineering. Recent developments in controlling and in-
115
      terpreting Large Language Models (LLMs) utilize various sophisticated methods. For behavior
      control, techniques such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al.,
116
      2022), Direct Policy Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024), and knowledge editing methods
117
      like ROME (Meng et al., 2022a), MEND (Mitchell et al., 2021), and MEMIT (Meng et al., 2022b)
118   modify model outputs or parameters to align with human preferences or factual accuracy. These
119   methods, however, generally lack the ability to adjust abstract model behaviors such as helpfulness
120   or emotional responses. Other strategies, such as Constrained Decoding (CD) (Dathathri et al.,
121   2020), involve constrained optimization and sampling with Langevin dynamics for token-level output
122   control (Kumar et al., 2021; 2022), which may lack flexibility in that they often require human
123   supervision.
124   Representation Engineering (RepE) (Zou et al., 2023a; Turner et al., 2023; Rimsky et al., 2023)
125   instead, is a flexible method which mainly focus on finding steering vectors to add on LLMs’
126   hidden representations. It originates from the previous methods that learn to find a direction, e.g.
127   linear probes, and then add/subtract the direction from model hidden representations. Unlike the
128   supervised methods, recent technique such as Activation Addition (Turner et al., 2023) or Contrast
129   Vector (Zou et al., 2023a), directly engineer the steering vector in a zero-shot manner. Gradients
130   offer another valuable tool in this context. While they have been extensively used in the past to
131   explain model behavior (Lyu et al., 2024; Yin & Neubig, 2022), their potential for representation
132
      engineering in model control remains largely untapped. One of our key contributions is leveraging
      gradients specifically for representation engineering, advancing their application beyond traditional
133
      interpretability.
134
135
      LLM Self Evaluation LLM self-evaluation has been shown to be effective in answering multi-
136
      choice questions (Ren et al., 2023), judging LLMs’ output, and serving as safeguards (Phute et al.,
137   2023). However, some argue that there are some pitfalls (Panickssery et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023)
138   in LLM self-evaluation. These pitfalls include position bias, distribution bias (Panickssery et al.,
139   2024), and sycophancy during evaluation. These issues may affect LLMs’ evaluation and lead to
140   undesired consequences. However, they generally do not apply to our method. Similar to Phute et al.
141   (2023), we simply probe LLMs’ next token probability on Yes and No, guiding LLMs toward their
142   own preferences, which has been demonstrated to be feasible by recent study (Yuan et al., 2024).1

144   3       S ELF C ONTROL
145
146   In this section, we present our S ELF C ONTROL framework, which leverages the LLM’s self-evaluation
147   to control its behavior. We begin by detailing the standard instance-level S ELF C ONTROL approach.
148   This encompasses the process of transforming desired behaviors into suffix strings, computing suffix
149   scores and suffix gradients, and controlling model behaviors through iterative updates to latent
150   representations. Subsequently, we introduce the across-instance version, S ELF C ONTROL PREFIX . S ELF -
151   C ONTROL PREFIX compresses instance-level suffix gradients into a P REFIX C ONTROLLER, enabling
152   adaptable model control on new inputs and facilitating the simultaneous control of multiple behaviors.
153
154   3.1       I NSTANCE -L EVEL S ELF C ONTROL
155
      S ELF C ONTROL controls the LLM’s by transforming a desired behavior into a natural language
156
      question, referred to as a suffix string. The model then performs self-evaluation of its response to this
157
      question, generating a suffix score corresponding to the likelihood of the response aligning with the
158   desired behavior. Then, gradients of the suffix score with respect to the latent representations of the
159   original input are computed. Model behaviors are then controlled through iteratively updating the
160   latent representations with the suffix gradients. Figure 2 illustrates this process.

207   “Yes” and “No”:
208
209    Ssuffix (output, Hinput ) = sigmoid log P+ (output, Hinput ) − log P− (output, Hinput )

214   maximizing Ssuffix may fail to achieve the desired control, when “Yes” and “No” do not have high
215   probabilities (discussion can be found in Appendix A.4). In our case, the suffixes that we design

302   4.1   E VALUATION S ETUP
303
304   Language Detoxification. LLMs may
305   generate toxic completions to prompts that are offensive or privacy-leaking, even for the instruction-
306   tuned models. We endeavor to evaluate how well different control methods can detoxify the output
307   and avoid following toxic instructions. We use RealToxicityPrompts (Gehman et al., 2020) for
308
      toxicity following Han et al. (2023), and Perspective API (Per, 2021) to measure toxicity scores.
309   Privacy Protection. To evaluate privacy protection, we use privacy from DecodingTrust Wang
310   et al. (2023). Specifically, the goal for control on privacy is to reject generating correct email addresses.
311   Models are given a five-shot demonstration on leaking email addresses of the corresponding people,
312   and then they are asked to generate the correct email address of another person.
313   Emotion Control. We also study if model emotion can be well controlled using S ELF C ONTROL. We
314   use datasets of five emotional attributes from RepE Zou et al. (2023a), i.e. anger, fear, happiness,
315   surprise, and disgust. Specifically, we use the last one hundred data from each emotional dataset
316   for evaluation and the first one hundred to train S ELF C ONTROL PREFIX and Reading Vector. We use
317   GPT-3.5-turbo to evaluate emotion scores (template can be found in Appendix C).
318   Truthfulness ICL. We further benchmark S ELF C ONTROL on truthfulness under a simple in-context
319   learning setup, using synthetic data from Marks & Tegmark (2023). Specifically, we use the
320   cities and neg cities datasets. The data is generated with the template ‘‘[city] is
321   in [country]’’ or ‘‘[city] is not in [country]’’. A fixed 2-shot is prepended
322   to each sentence during evaluation. This is aimed at evaluating S ELF C ONTROL’s capability of
323   enhancing performances on simple question answering tasks. Specifically, instead of doing iterative
      control, we simply use the suffix gradient obtained at the first iteration in this task.

529   5    C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this work, we introduced S ELF C ONTROL, a framework leveraging suffix gradients to control the
532
      behaviors of large language models effectively. This approach addresses the challenge of precise
533
      alignment with desired attributes during auto-regressive text generation by allowing fine-grained,
534   instance-level control without modifying model parameters. Additionally, we proposed S ELF C ON -
535   TROL PREFIX , a prefix-based module that generalizes suffix gradients for efficient, inference-time
536   control over multiple attributes simultaneously. Our extensive experiments validate the effectiveness
537   of both S ELF C ONTROL and S ELF C ONTROL PREFIX in various tasks, including emotional tone regula-
538   tion, language detoxification, privacy protection and in-context learning. These findings highlight the
539   potential of gradient-based behavior control in enhancing the reliability and applicability of LLMs in
      real-world scenarios.

      B     R ELATED WORK
963
      LLM Interpretation. Mechanistic interpretability aims to reverse-engineer neural network models
964
      to their ”source code”, analogous to traditional software engineering. This line of work focuses on
965
      explaining models through circuits Elhage et al. (2021), which are detailed mappings of network
966   connections between individual neurons. By analyzing these circuits, researchers can understand
967   how specific inputs are transformed into outputs, providing a clearer view of the model’s decision-
968   making process. For LLMs, this approach has been adopted to understand in-context learning Olsson
969   et al. (2022). Additionally, probing techniques have been employed to investigate the knowledge
970   embedded within LLMs. One such approach Meng et al. (2022a) uses causal effects to probe the
971   LLM’s knowledge, focusing on the model’s ability to recall simple facts. While effective, this
      method is limited to factual information and does not capture more abstract knowledge structures.

990    Recent advancements have introduced methods that project internal model states into the vocabulary
991
       space, allowing for more nuanced interpretability. For example, LogitLens nostalgebraist (2020),
       TunedLens Belrose et al. (2023), attribute lens Hernandez et al. (2023), “jump to conclusions”Din et al.
992
       (2023), and others Geva et al. (2022); Dar et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2024), explore how intermediate
993
       representations contribute to the final output by decoding hidden representations into natural language.
994    Recent work also include approaches that utilize multiple LLMs, such as Patchscope Ghandeharioun
995    et al. (2024), which enable stronger models for natural language decoding. This results in better
996    interpretability and more detailed analysis.
997
998    LLM Behavior Control. Supervised fine-tuning has been used to directly control LLM behavior,
999    either with RL, such as RLHF Ouyang et al. (2022), or direct optimization methods like DPO Rafailov
1000   et al. (2024). These techniques leverage preference data to guide the model’s outputs, allowing for
1001   the incorporation of human-like preferences. However, they primarily operate on the output texts,
1002   which demands extensive computational resources and limits the granularity of control over the
1003
       model’s internal decision-making processes. Knowledge editing methods, including ROME Meng
       et al. (2022a), MEND Mitchell et al. (2021), and MEMIT Meng et al. (2022b), have been developed
1004
       to modify specific knowledge within LLMs. These methods allow for precise control over factual
1005
       information by directly altering model parameters. Despite their effectiveness in handling factual
1006   updates, these approaches are limited in scope as they do not extend to abstract behaviors, such
1007   as making the model more helpful or inducing emotional responses like happiness or empathy.
1008   Another approach to controlling LLMs involves guiding text generation to satisfy certain constraints
1009   through methods like constrained optimization or sampling with Langevin dynamics Kumar et al.
1010   (2021; 2022). These techniques adjust output probabilities at the token level to adhere to predefined
1011   constraints. While this allows for fine-tuned control over specific text attributes, it often comes at the
1012   cost of compromising the fluency and naturalness of the generated text. High-level control methods,
1013   such as Representation Engineering (RepE) Zou et al. (2023a), provide an alternative by focusing on
1014
       the control of abstract features like emotions and truthfulness. RepE leverages contrastive learning to
       enable the model to generate text with specific emotional undertones or stylistic features.
1015
1016
       LLM Attack and Jailbreak. Recent works have investigated how to maliciously manipulate or
1017
       jailbreak LLMs, raising concerns about the vulnerability of LLMs Zou et al. (2023b); Huang et al.
1018
       (2023); Qi et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023); Wei et al. (2024); Zeng et al. (2024). Specifically, by
1019   exploiting the generation configuration Huang et al. (2023) or crafting adversarial prompts through
1020   gradient-based search Zou et al. (2023b) or automatic generation Liu et al. (2023); Zeng et al. (2024),
1021   attackers can bypass the safety constraints of LLMs and trigger them to produce harmful content.
1022   To remedy the vulnerability of LLMs, recent works study how to incorporate safeguards to defend
1023   against LLM attacks, by safety-aware fine-tuning Cao et al. (2023); Zheng et al. (2024), RLHF Bai
1024   et al. (2022a;b) and inference-time detection Inan et al. (2023); Jain et al. (2023). In this paper, we
1025   study how to control LLMs to avoid generating harmful or toxic content by encapsulating the safety
       judgement of LLMs themselves into prefix tokens.

1846    def get_suffix_score(
            prompt, suffix, # prompt refers to [query, resposne]
1847        model, tokenizer,
1848        tau, # temperature
            contrastive_pairs=["Yes", "No"] # The pair which defines the target; (Yes, No) by default
1849      in our case
1850    ): -> float # suffix score
            token_pos = tokenizer(contrastive_pairs[0])
1851        token_neg = tokenizer(contrastive_pairs[1])
1852        # append suffix to the prompt
            tokenized = tokenizer(prompt + suffix)
1853        # get logits
1854        outputs = model(**tokenized)
            last_logit = outputs.logits[:, -1, :]
1855        # calculate suffix score
1856        logit_diff = last_logit[:, token_pos] - last_logit[:, token_neg]

1857        return sigmoid(logit_diff / tau)
