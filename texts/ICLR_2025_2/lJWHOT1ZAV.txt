009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In Federated Learning (FL), forgetting, or the loss of knowledge across rounds,
012            hampers algorithm convergence, especially in the presence of severe data hetero-
013            geneity among clients. This study explores the nuances of this issue, emphasizing
014            the critical role of forgetting leading to FL’s inefficient learning within hetero-
015            geneous data contexts. Knowledge loss occurs in both client-local updates and
016            server-side aggregation steps; addressing one without the other fails to mitigate
017            forgetting. We introduce a metric to measure forgetting granularly, ensuring distinct
018
               recognition amid new knowledge acquisition. Based on this, we propose Flashback,
               a novel FL algorithm with a dynamic distillation approach that regularizes the local
019
               models and effectively aggregates their knowledge. The results from extensive ex-
020
               perimentation across different benchmarks show that Flashback mitigates forgetting
021            and outperforms other state-of-the-art methods, achieving faster round-to-target
022            accuracy by converging in 6 to 16 rounds, being up to 27× faster.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Federated Learning (FL) is a distributed learning paradigm that allows training over decentralized
028   private data. These datasets belong to different clients that participate in training a global model.
029   Federated Averaging (FedAvg) (McMahan et al., 2017) is a prominent training algorithm that uses a
030   centralized server to orchestrate the process. At every round, the server samples a proportion of the
031   available clients. Starting from the current version of the global model, each sampled client performs
032   E epochs of local training using their private data and sends its updated model to the server. Then,
033
      the server aggregates the models by averaging them to obtain the new global model. This process is
      typically repeated for many rounds until a desired model performance is obtained.
034
035   A main challenge in FL is the data heterogeneity in distribution between the clients’ private datasets,
036   which are unbalanced and non-IID (Kairouz et al., 2021). Data heterogeneity causes local model
037   updates to drift – the local optima might not be consistent with the global optima – and can lead to
038   slow convergence of the global model – where more rounds of communication and local computation
039
      are needed – or worse, the desired performance may not be reached. Addressing data heterogeneity
      in FL has been the focus of several prior studies. For instance, FedProx (Li et al., 2020) proposes a
040
      proximal term to limit the distance between the global model and the local model updates, mitigating
041
      the drift in the local updates. MOON (Li et al., 2021b) mitigates the local drift using a contrastive
042   loss to minimize the distance between the feature representation of the global model and the local
043   model updates while maximizing the distance between the current model updates and the previous
044   model updates. FedDF (Lin et al., 2020) addresses heterogeneity in local models by using ensemble
045   distillation during the aggregation step (instead of averaging the model updates). Nonetheless, we
046   experimentally observe that under severe data heterogeneity, these proposals provide little or even no
047   advantage over FedAvg. Figure 1a illustrates the test accuracy of FedAvg and other baselines while
048   training a DNN over the CIFAR10 dataset (Krizhevsky, 2009) (more details are in § 5).
049   This motivates us to understand better how data heterogeneity poses a challenge for FL and devise a
050   new approach to handling non-IID datasets. We investigate the evolution of the global model accuracy
051   broken down by its per-class accuracy. Figure 1b shows a heatmap of the per-class accuracy for
052   FedAvg; each rectangle represents the accuracy of the global model on a class at a round. Other
053   baseline methods show similar results. Our key observation is that there is a significant presence of
      forgetting: i.e., cases where some knowledge obtained by the global model at round t is forgotten

070     A similar phenomenon is known as catastrophic forgetting in Continual Learning (CL) litera-
071     ture (Parisi et al., 2019). CL addresses the challenge of sequentially training a model on a series of
072     tasks, denoted as {T1 , T2 , . . . , Tn }, without revisiting data from prior tasks. Formally, given a model
073     with parameters θ and task-specific loss functions Lt (θ) for each task Tt , the objective in CL is to
074     update θ such that performance on the current task is optimized without significantly degrading the
075     model’s performance on previously learned tasks. This is non-trivial, as naı̈ve sequential training
076     often leads to catastrophic forgetting, where knowledge from prior tasks is overridden when learning
077     a new task. An inherent assumption in this paradigm is that once the model transitions from task Ti
078
        to task Ti+1 , data from Ti becomes inaccessible, amplifying the importance of knowledge retention
        strategies (De Lange et al., 2021).
079
080    While the premises and assumptions of FL differ from those of traditional machine learning and
081    continual learning, forgetting remains an issue. This can be viewed as a side effect of data heterogene-
082    ity, a commonality FL shares with CL. In FL, the global model evolves based on a fluctuating data
083    distribution. Specifically, a diverse set of sampled clients with distinct data distributions contribute a
084
       model update in each communication round. Furthermore, these model updates must be aggregated
       to obtain a global model. This situation presents dual-levels of data heterogeneity. Firstly, at the
085
       intra-round level, heterogeneity arises from the participation of clients with varied data distributions
086
       within the same round. This diversity can inadvertently lead to “forgetting” specific data patterns or
087    insights from certain clients. Secondly, at the inter-round level, the participating clients generally
088    change from one round to the next. As a result, the global model may “forget” or dilute insights
089    gained from clients in previous rounds.
090
        To remedy this issue, we propose Flashback, a FL algorithm that employs a dynamic distillation
091
        approach to mitigate the effects of data heterogeneity. Flashback’s dynamic distillation ensures that
092
        the local models learn new knowledge while retaining knowledge from the global model during
093     the client updates by adaptively adjusting the distillation loss. Moreover, during the server update,
094     Flashback uses a very small public dataset as a medium to integrate the knowledge from the local
095     models to the global model using the same dynamic distillation. Flashback performs these adaptations
096     by estimating the knowledge in each model using label counts as a proxy of the model knowledge.
097     Overall, Flashback results in a more stable and faster convergence compared to existing methods.
098
        Our contributions are the following:
099
100                             • We systematically investigate the forgetting problem in FL. We show that under severe data
101                               heterogeneity, FL sufferers from forgetting. We dissect how and where forgetting happens
102                               (§ 3).
103
                                • We propose a new metric for measuring forgetting over the communication rounds (§ 3).
104
105                             • We introduce Flashback, a FL algorithm that employs a dynamic distillation during the local
106                               updates and the server update (§ 4). By addressing the forgetting issue, Flashback mitigates
107                               its detrimental effects and converges to the desired accuracy faster than existing methods
                                  (§ 5)

      3    F ORGETTING IN FL
148
149
      We now investigate where forgetting happens and devise a metric to quantify this phenomenon. Recall
150   that in FL, the models are updated in two distinct phases: 1) during local training – when each client
151   k starts from global model wt−1 and locally trains wk,t – and 2) during the aggregation step – when
152   the server combines the client models to update the new global model wt .
153
      Intuitively, forgetting in FL is when knowledge contained in the global model will be lost after the
154
      completion of communication round wt−1 → wt . We observe that forgetting may occur in the two
155
      phases of FL. We refer to the former case as local forgetting, where some knowledge in the global
156   model will be lost during the local training wt−1 → wk,t . This is due to optimizing for the clients’
157   local objectives, which depend on their datasets. Local forgetting is akin to the form of forgetting seen
158   in CL, where tasks change over time (as with clients in FL) and, consequently, the data distribution.
159   We refer to the latter case as aggregation forgetting,
                                                        P where some knowledge contained in the clients’
160   model updates will be lost during aggregation {wk,t | k ∈ St } → wt . This might be due to the
161   coordinate-wise aggregation of weights as opposed to matched averaging in the parameter space of
      DNNs (Wang et al., 2020a).

      4.1   DYNAMIC D ISTILLATION
251
      As established in § 3, a client’s local model can forget and override model knowledge with what is
252   present in its private data. Moreover, the global model can be imperfect for two reasons: i) As we
253   established before, the global model is susceptible to forgetting in the aggregation step. ii) Assuming
254   no forgetting in the aggregation step, the knowledge contained in the clients who participated so far
255   might not represent all the available knowledge, especially in the early rounds. Overall, both local
256   models and the global model can be imperfect. Therefore, the logits of all the different classes cannot
257   be treated equally (as in Eq. (1)), and the distillation loss has to adapt to the model’s knowledge.
258   We propose using the label count to approximate the knowledge within a model. Here, the label
259   count refers to the occurrences of each class in the training data that the model saw during training.
260   In machine learning, a model’s knowledge is fundamentally tied to the data it has been exposed
261   to. If certain classes have higher representation (or label counts) in the training data, it’s intuitive
262   that the model would have more opportunities to learn the distinguishing features of such classes.
263   Conversely, underrepresented classes might not offer the model sufficient exposure to learn their
264   nuances effectively.
265   Our experimental results suggest that per-class performance on the test set correlates highly with
266   the label counts in the training data. In scenarios where certain classes were more abundant, the
267   model demonstrated higher proficiency in predicting those classes on the test set. As an example,
268   Fig. 8 in the appendix illustrates for a randomly chosen client that the client’s model performance on
269   the test set well reflects the label count distribution of its private data. We conclude from this and
      many similar observations that the label count can indicate a model’s knowledge. Furthermore, in

      4.2   E STIMATING THE G LOBAL M ODEL K NOWLEDGE
317
      Note that to apply the dynamic distillation loss Eq. (3), we must obtain the student’s and teachers’
318   label count vectors. While the label count of local models can be easily obtained (from the class
319   frequency of local datasets), the label count of the global model is not readily available. We construct
320   π, the global model’s relative label count, as follows. Let rk denote the number of rounds in which
321   client k participated in the training. For every client k that participates at round t, Flashback adds a
322   fraction γ ∈ (0, 1] of client k’s label count (µk ) to π, unless γrk > 1, in which case π is not updated
323   based on k’s label count. The latter case means that client k has participated enough times that its
      label count is fully accounted for in π.

348    We outline and analyze our experimental findings to investigate whether mitigating round forgetting
349    successfully addresses the slow and unstable convergence issues due to forgetting problems as laid out
350
       in § 1. The experimental results stem from three settings: CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky, 2009) and CINIC10
       (Darlow et al., 2018), where heterogeneous data partitions are created using Dirichlet distribution with
351
       β = 0.1 and FEMNIST (Caldas et al., 2019) with 3,432 clients, following the natural heterogeneity of
352
       the dataset. Furthermore, we do an ablation study on the different components of the algorithm. We
353    use the same neural network architecture used in (Lee et al., 2021; McMahan et al., 2017), a 2-layer
354    Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). Summaries of the datasets, partitions, and more details on the
355    experimental setup are reported in Appendix D.
356
       We compare Flashback against several baseline methods, namely: 1) FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017),
357
       2) FedDF (Lin et al., 2020), 3) FedNTD (Lee et al., 2021), 4) FedProx (Li et al., 2020), 5) FedReg (Xu
358
       et al., 2022), 6) MOON (Li et al., 2021b). It is noteworthy that both FedNTD and FedReg target
359    forgetting in FL (discussed further in Appendix F). Flashback’s server-side distillation is performed
360    until early stopping is triggered on the public validation set (details in Appendix D). Moreover,
361    Flashback only introduces one additional hyperparameter γ, representing how fast trust is built in the
362    global model. We analyze the effect of γ in Appendix E. We first evaluate Flashback performance by
363    showing round-to-accuracy, round forgetting, and the local-global loss over the rounds. Moreover,
364    we show additional results that explores various aspects of Flashback and show its soundness in

      6                R ELATED W ORK
452
453
      Federated learning. FL is commonly viewed as an ML paradigm wherein a server distributes the
      training process on a set of decentralized participants that train a shared global model using local
454
      datasets that are never shared (Konečnỳ et al., 2015; Shokri & Shmatikov, 2015; Konečnỳ et al., 2016;
455
      Li et al., 2020; McMahan et al., 2017; Kairouz et al., 2021). FL has been used to enhance prediction
456   quality for virtual keyboards among other applications (Bonawitz et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2018). A
457   number of FL frameworks have facilitated research in this area (Caldas et al., 2019; OpenMined,
458   2020; tensorflow.org, 2020; Abdelmoniem et al., 2023).
459   Heterogeneity in FL. A key challenge in FL systems is uncertainties stemming from learner, system,
460   and data heterogeneity. The non-IID distributions of Learners’ data can significantly slow down
461   convergence (McMahan et al., 2017; Kairouz et al., 2021) and several algorithms are proposed as
462   means of mitigation (Fourati et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2020b; Karimireddy et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020;
463   2021a).
464   Forgetting in FL. Forgetting in FL has been explored in several studies, though many have limitations
465   in addressing the full scope of the issue. We discuss in detail the main baselines that tackle forgetting
466   in Appendix F. Luo, Kangyang, et al. (Luo et al., 2023) discuss forgetting due to local updates.
467   Similarly, (Liu et al., 2022) tackles the problem of learning personalized models without forgetting
468   what the global model has learned by using knowledge distillation. However, these approaches focus
469   on the local update without addressing forgetting at the aggregation step. On the other hand, (Huang
470   et al., 2022) focuses on domain shifts and clients with different data domains, aiming for personalized
471   models rather than a global model. (Qu et al., 2022) investigate the convergence behavior and
472
      forgetting of Transformers compared to other architectures used in FL. Their experiments show that
      Transformers are robust to data heterogeneity. While these works address client heterogeneity, they
473
      do not delve into the forgetting issue in FL.
474
475   Overall, there is no investigation or exploration of what forgetting in FL entails. In Flashback,
476   we provide a detailed analysis of forgetting in FL, demonstrating where and why it happens, and
477   propose a metric to measure forgetting. Unlike previous works, which focus on client-side forgetting,
478
      Flashback addresses forgetting as a compound problem that occurs both at the local update and the
      aggregation step, suggesting it must be tackled at both levels.
479

481   7                C ONCLUSION
482
483   We explored the phenomenon of forgetting in FL. Our investigation revealed that forgetting occurs
484   during both the local update and the aggregation step of FL algorithms. We presented Flashback,
485   a novel FL algorithm explicitly designed to counteract round forgetting by employing dynamic
      knowledge distillation. Our approach leverages data label counts as a proxy for knowledge, ensuring


                                                                                     9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      a more targeted and effective forgetting mitigation. Our empirical results showed Flashback’s efficacy
487   in mitigating round forgetting, thereby supporting the hypothesis that the observed slow and unstable
488   convergence in FL algorithms is closely linked to forgetting. This result underlines the importance of
489   addressing forgetting, paving the way for advancing more robust and efficient FL algorithms.
490
      Flashback Limitation. In this work, we focused on forgetting caused by data heterogeneity man-
491
      ifested by heterogeneity in the class distribution of clients. In the future, we look to investigate
492
      forgetting under other types of heterogeneity. Moreover, Flashback mainly uses label count to approx-
493   imate knowledge, which could pose privacy challenges in certain scenarios. We discuss a possible
494   alternative in Appendix B. As for computational overhead, Flashback adds 1 additional forward pass
495   per iteration similar to FedNTD (Lee et al., 2021) and less than MOON (Li et al., 2021b), which adds
496   2 forward passes.

750   C        F ORGETTING IN F EDERATED L EARNING AND C ONTINUAL L EARNING
751
752   Forgetting is a prominent problem in CL, where tasks change over time, and consequently, the
753   data distribution, places models at risk of overriding previously learned knowledge. Looking at FL
754   with the same perspective, we have the intra-round and inter-round data distribution heterogeneity.
755   Intra-round clients with different data distributions participate, by updating the current global model
      weights wt−1 . The server obtains a set of {wk,t | k ∈ St } where St is the set of clients participating


                                                                                  14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      in round t. The goal is after the aggregation step, a new global model wt is obtained containing all
757   the knowledge that was present in {wk,t | k ∈ St }. Inter-round the global model wt has learned
758   knowledge that over the prior rounds 1 . . . t. At each round, different sets of clients participate. The
759   goal is to carry this knowledge to the next round t + 1 even though the new set St+1 most likely is
760   different with respect to the data distribution than the previous set St . Presenting distinct forgetting
761   challenges in FL compared to CL.

782   the CIFAR10 case this 2.5% is further divided into training set and validation set. Then employing
783   Dirichlet distribution with β value of 0.1 to split the 97.5% remaining data into 200 clients, with each
784   client’s data further divided into training (90%) and validation (10%) sets.

789   test (approx. 15%) sets. The collective test sets from all writers form the overall test set. At every

       The importance of γ. As mentioned, Flashback has a single hyperparameter γ, which dictates
911
       how fast models will trust the global model as a competent teacher. We explore the effect of this
912    hyperparameter in Fig. 13. We find that setting this parameter to a larger value leads the learning
913    process to get to a stale solution quickly. This is intuitive since large γ leads the global model label
914    count to grow faster, therefore, this dominates the loss term in Eq. (3) during both the client- and the
915    server update. Smaller γ gives the best performance because it gives the local models time to learn
916    from their own private data by having a small weight to the distillation term in Eq. (3). However,
917    too small value for γ such as 0.001 slows the training process, since during the local training the
       distillation term in Eq. (3) will be very small in the early rounds.

        FedReg (Xu et al., 2022) addresses the issue of slow convergence in FL, asserting it to be a result of
997
        forgetting at the local update phase. They demonstrate this by comparing the loss of the global model
998     wt−1 on specific client data points with the averaged loss of updated clients’ models {wt,k | k ∈ St }
999     on the same data points, highlighting a significant increase in the average loss, indicative of forgetting.
1000    However, our work proposes a systematic way of measuring forgetting using a metric designed to
1001    capture it. Furthermore, we show that forgetting doesn’t only occur in the local update and at the
1002    aggregation step (§ 3 and Fig. 2). FedReg proposes to generate fake data that carries the previously
1003    attained knowledge. During the local update, Fast Gradient Sign Method (Goodfellow et al., 2014) is
1004    used to generate these data using the global model wt−1 and the client data. Then, the loss of the
1005    generated data is used to regularize the local update. While FedReg employs regularization using
1006
        synthetic data during local updates, our work, Flashback, leverages dynamic distillation to ensure
        knowledge retention at both local updates and aggregation steps.
1007
1008    FedNTD (Lee et al., 2021) makes a connection between CL and FL, suggesting that forgetting happens
1009    in FL as well. Similarly to FedReg, their analysis shows that forgetting happens at the local update,
1010    where global knowledge outside of the client’s local distribution is susceptible to forgetting. To address
1011    this, they propose to use a new variant of distillation Eq. (1) named Not-True Distillation (NTD),
                                                                                            PC                 c
1012    that masks the ground-truth class logits in the KL divergence as LKL (p, q) = i=c,c̸=y pc log( pqc ),
1013    where y is the ground-truth class. NTD is used at the local update, while all the other steps in the
1014    algorithm remain the same as FedAvg. FedNTD aims to preserve global knowledge during local
1015    updates.
1016    Both FedReg and FedNTD diagnose the issue of forgetting primarily within the realm of local updates,
1017    asserting that this stage risks losing valuable global knowledge. Consequently, both works present
1018    innovative solutions specifically tailored to counteract this local update forgetting. However, their
1019    perspective overlooks a pivotal aspect of the forgetting problem: the occurrence of forgetting during
1020    the aggregation step. As we delve into in § 3, this oversight in recognizing and addressing forgetting
1021    during aggregation has repercussions on the later local updates. In contrast, Flashback takes a holistic
1022
        approach, comprehensively targeting forgetting across the local updates and the aggregation phase,
        leading to faster convergence.
