011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Training large language models (LLMs) for pretraining or adapting to new tasks
014            and domains has become increasingly critical as their applications expand. How-
015
               ever, as model and data sizes grow, the training process presents significant mem-
               ory challenges, often requiring a prohibitive amount of GPU memory that may not
016
               be readily available. Existing methods such as low-rank adaptation (LoRA) add
017
               trainable low-rank matrix factorizations, altering the training dynamics and limit-
018            ing the model’s parameter search to a low-rank subspace. GaLore, a more recent
019            method, employs Gradient Low-Rank Projection to reduce the memory footprint,
020            in the full parameter training setting. However GaLore can only be applied to
021            a subset of the LLM layers that satisfy the “reversibility” property, thus limiting
022            their applicability. In response to these challenges, we introduce BlockLLM, an
023            approach inspired by block coordinate descent. Our method carefully selects and
024            updates a very small subset of the trainable parameters without altering any part
025            of its architecture and training procedure. BlockLLM achieves state-of-the-art
026
               performance in both finetuning and pretraining tasks, while reducing the memory
               footprint of the underlying optimization process. Our experiments demonstrate
027
               that BlockLLM achieves superior performance on finetuning both large and small
028
               models. On pretraining a Llama model on C4 dataset, BlockLLM is able to train
029            with significantly less memory than the state-of-the-art, while still maintaining
030            competitive performance.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Recent advancements in natural language processing (NLP) have been propelled by the development
036   of large language models (LLMs) Le Scao et al. (2023); Touvron et al. (2023); OpenAI (2023); Al-
037   mazrouei et al. (2023). These models have set new benchmarks for a variety of NLP tasks, including
038   language translation Takase & Kiyono (2021), text summarization Kedia et al. (2021), and sentiment
039
      analysis (Brown et al., 2020). The core strength of LLMs lies in their scale. Empirical evidence sug-
      gests that increases in model size not only enhance performance across standard benchmarks but
040
      also unlock new capabilities that are absent in smaller models (Kaplan et al., 2020; Zhao et al.,
041
      2023). Pretraining and finetuning LLMs on domain-specific application data have enhanced their
042   applicability immensely.
043
044   However, pretraining and finetuning LLMs are resource-intensive processes and require substantial
045
      memory and computational power. For example, a 7B parameter Llama model demands approxi-
      mately 14GB of memory Zhao et al. (2024), assuming each parameter is a 16-bit float occupying 2
046
      bytes. The memory required for storing gradients during backpropagation is similarly substantial,
047
      adding another 14 GB. Additionally, LLMs are often trained using the Adam optimizer (Kingma
048   & Ba, 2014) and its variants, which maintain first and second moment estimates for each parame-
049   ter. This effectively doubles this memory requirement, resulting in an additional 28 GB of VRAM
050   memory. Consequently, the total memory required for the weights, gradients, and optimizer states
051   amounts to a substantial 56 GB. The effect of LLMs training’s high memory requirement is far
052   reaching, in that it comes down to the question of who can train these large models. With the mem-
053   ory calculations above, a 7 billion parameter model can only be trained on A100 GPUs or above.
      As models grow larger, this memory burden will continue to escalate, restricting large-scale LLM

087   Lore does not achieve high memory efficiency across all model types, as its gradient factorization

090   Techniques such as gradient and activation checkpointing Chen et al. (2016), quantization Han et al.
091   (2015), and parameter offloading Rhu et al. (2016) are also commonly used to achieve memory sav-
092   ings. However, these methods often come with trade-offs such as increased computational overhead
093   or compromised performance. For instance, checkpointing Chen et al. (2016) reduces memory usage
094   but requires re-computation, quantization lowers precision and can affect accuracy Han et al. (2015)
095   and parameter offloading increases data transfer latency Rhu et al. (2016). While these methods
096   have their own limitations, many of these techniques are complementary to the approach presented
097   in this work and provides additional opportunities for memory reduction when used in combination.
098   Block coordinate descent (BCD). BCD is popular algorithm in the large-scale optimization litera-
099   ture. At any training iteration t, instead of updating all the parameters W , BCD updates only a block
                                       bt
100   of parameters bt by setting Wt+1      = Wtbt + dt , where dt is the update for that block. Importantly,
101   bt does not stay fixed across iterations. As a result, BCD does not constrain model performance,
102   which is often the case with low-rank approximation methods. Moreover, BCD doesn’t alter the
103   model architecture in any way and preserves its structure throughout the training process. As it can
104   be seen, this formulation directly falls in the reduced parameter training regime. This insight forms
105   the cornerstone of our approach and hence the name BlockLLM.
106   A related study by (Belilovsky et al., 2019) demonstrated that sequentially solving one-hidden-layer
107   problems could match the performance of large model training, inspiring us to tackle parameter

      Some previous works have explored training various neural network models using BCD (Zeng et al.,
115   2019; Lau et al., 2018; Massart & Abrol, 2022). A recent parallel study (Luo et al., 2024) extends
116   BCD to LLMs, focusing on memory efficient training. These methods update one block of pa-
117   rameters per iteration, chosen either randomly or cyclically. This approach is often inefficient for
118   large-scale models because, in most cases only a small subset of parameters requires to be updated
119   during finetuning (see our analysis 2 for details). If these critical parameters are not updated fre-
120   quently enough, training becomes prolonged, impacting overall efficiency. In this work, we address
121   this issue by updating parameters that are important for training more frequently. Our experimen-
122   tal results (see Figure 1) demonstrate that this strategy leads to both better performance and more
123
      efficient training.

125   Our Contributions. The key contributions of this work are as follows:
126   1. We propose a novel parameter and memory-efficient algorithm, BlockLLM where we dynam-
127   ically select and train a block of parameters. This approach minimizes memory consumption by
128   maintaining gradient and optimizer states only for the selected parameters.
129
      2. We introduce a novel block selection criterion tailored for LLM training where impactful param-
      eters are updated more frequently. This leads to faster training, as important parameters are updated
130
      earlier in the process.
131
      3. We demonstrate that BlockLLM achieves state-of-the-art training and generalization performance
132   in both fine-tuning and pretraining tasks, while also enabling faster training and reduced memory
133   usage.
134   4. We provide extensive ablation studies showing that BlockLLM is robust across various hyperpa-
135   rameter settings, including sparsity and patience.

250   The findings from our experiments suggest that a parameter-efficient training method that updates a
251   small set of parameters each iteration is feasible. However, some key aspects require clarification:
252   Parameter Selection Criteria: Our analysis in Figure 3 indicates that the model frequently updates
253   parameters with lower weight magnitudes. This contradicts the very premise of magnitude pruning.

258   rameters to update for a given training phase is crucial. In BlockLLM, we developed an adaptive
259   strategy using the current loss value to decide when the parameter selection needs to be revisited.

264   In this section we introduce BlockLLM, a parameter and memory efficient training method designed
265   to reduce the number of trainable parameters in large language models (LLMs) without compromis-
266   ing training performance. Akin to other parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods such as
267   LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) and ReLoRA (Lialin et al., 2023), BlockLLM updates only k parameters at
268   any iteration t. Here k ≪ n and n is the total number of parameters. However, the main difference
269   is that BlockLLM optimizes parameter selection by focusing on the most impactful parameters at
      different stages of the training process. The overall algorithm of BlockLLM is given in Algorithm 1.

471   3.3           A BLATION ON PARAMETER S ELECTION S TRATEGY
472
473   The parameter selection strategy is the cornerstone of our method, where we hypothesize that layers
474   with large ||G̃l || are important for training. To validate this, we conducted an ablation study in
475   which we deliberately chose parameters with small ||G̃l || (opposite to BlockLLM). Specifically, we
476   finetuned LLaMA2 7B model on the Alpaca dataset, where parameters with the smallest gradient
477   norms were selected for updates. We call this method BLockLLM-SubOPT. Naturally, we expect
478
      that this parameter selection strategy to be severely detrimental to the training process.
479   Let L = {l1 , l2 , . . . , ln } represent the set of all layers. Now, for each layer l ∈ L, we computed the
480   processed gradients G̃l . We then sorted the layers in ascending order based on the their processed
481   gradient norms ||G̃l || and computed ||G̃l ||/fl . From this ordered list, we selected top k layers
482   with small gradient norms until the sparsity requirement s is satisfied. Then, in each iteration t,
483   the optimizer updates only the parameters in the selected k layers. We conducted hyperparameter
484   tuning and set m = 100 for s = 0.95. Now, we compared the training loss of both the methods and
485   the comparative results are presented in Figure 7. As expected, the results show that BLockLLM-
      SubOPT exhibits significantly higher training loss and converges slower than BlockLLM.

      4              C ONCLUSION & F UTURE WORK
516
517
518   In this paper we introduced BlockLLM, a novel method for efficiently training large language
519
      models. By dynamically estimating and updating the importance of parameters during training,
      BlockLLM effectively achieves state-of-the-art performance while significantly reducing the mem-
520
      ory footprint. Our method achieves the highest validation accuracy on the finetuning tasks, some-
521
      times even surpassing full finetuning. One key aspect of BlockLLM is that it does not presuppose the
522   importance of layers but continuously evaluates and updates parameter importance throughout train-
523   ing. This adaptive approach allows for more flexible and efficient optimization compared to methods
524   that assume certain parameters are critical from the outset. Additionally, BlockLLM preserves the
525   original architecture without altering the model structure or restricting the parameter search space,
526   making it suitable for various LLMs and tasks.
527
      Broader Impacts Our work aims to reduce the memory and computational requirements of training
528   LLMs. First, our technology democratizes access to LLM training, making it more feasible for stu-
529   dent researchers and institutions with limited computational resources to participate in cutting-edge
530   AI research. Furthermore, the low-memory requirements of our method means that one can train
531   with larger batch sizes and achieve faster convergence. This has a direct effect on the environment.
532
      Future works. Future work on BlockLLM could explore several promising avenues. Currently, our
533
      research has focused on parameter selection based on gradient norms, but BlockLLM can be seen as
534   a framework for parameter-efficient training rather than a single algorithm. This opens the door to
535   investigating alternative criteria for parameter selection, potentially tailored to specific problems or
536   tasks. Moreover, while our ablation studies on BlockLLM’s hyperparameters have provided insights
537   into their impact on training, further research is needed to understand how different layers might be
538   affected by greedy parameter selection strategies. BlockLLM also complements existing memory-
539   optimized training techniques, including those discussed in this paper. Exploring the integration of
      BlockLLM with methods like quantization or GaLore could further reduce memory consumption.

899   A.8     A BLATION ON THE HYPERPARAMETER m
900
      We investigated the sensitivity of the model to the patience parameter m in both fine-tuning and pre-
901
      training setups. These experiments were conducted using the GLUE benchmark and the LLaMA
902
      2 model on the C4 dataset. Throughout the experiments, we fixed all parameters of the Adam op-
903   timizer and maintained a sparsity level of s = 0.5 while varying m. The results are presented in
904   Figure 9. Our observations indicate that in the fine-tuning setting, the model is relatively insensitive
905   to variations in m. Specifically, setting m = 50 or m = 1000 did not result in significant perfor-
906   mance differences. This finding aligns with the observations reported in Zhao et al. (2024), which
907   suggest that gradients change more slowly. The gradual nature of gradient changes implies a cor-
908   respondingly gradual variation in the optimal parameter set, thereby reducing sensitivity to changes
909   in m. In contrast, in the pre-training setting, smaller values of m lead to faster convergence. This
910   behavior can be attributed to the presence of noisy gradients in the earlier iterations of pre-training.
911
      Consequently, a smaller m helps maintain impactful parameter selection particularly in the initial
      phase of training, thereby facilitating faster convergence.
