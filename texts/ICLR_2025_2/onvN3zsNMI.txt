009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            From the advances of deep learning, the privacy concerns of deep neural networks
012            are in the limelight. A particular concern is privacy of the training data, which is
013            often compromised by the model’s inherent memorization capabilities. Suppressing
014            such memorization can enhance privacy but introduces two main challenges: 1)
015            removing a memorized instance from the training dataset will result in the model
016
               to memorize another instance instead, and 2) the memorization is essential for
               improving the generalization error. To address these challenges, we propose an
017
               over-memorization method that involves training the model with both the standard
018
               training set and a set of redundant, non-sensitive instances. Our method leverages
019            the model’s limited memorization capacity to focus on irrelevant data, thereby
020            preventing it from memorizing the training data. Our empirical results demonstrate
021            that this method not only enhances protection against membership inference at-
022            tacks but also minimizes the loss of utility by effectively redirecting the model’s
023            generalization efforts towards non-sensitive instances.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      With the widespread success of deep neural networks across various fields, growing concerns have
028
      emerged regarding privacy violations, including the privacy of training data (Nasr et al., 2018; Shokri
029
      et al., 2017; Abadi et al., 2016; Dwork et al., 2014; Ye et al., 2024; Jagielski et al., 2024), intellectual
030   property infringement by generative models (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; Vyas et al., 2023; Smits &
031   Borghuis, 2022), and ownership of the networks themselves (Maini et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021;
032   Kim et al., 2023). Among the various types of privacy for deep neural networks, the privacy of
033   training data is particularly crucial, as it ensures that deep neural networks perform their tasks without
034   compromising sensitive information.
035
      Despite their capabilities, deep neural networks often inadvertently expose training data (Carlini
036   et al., 2021; Nasr et al., 2023; Geiping et al., 2020), mainly due to the memorization of training
037   instances (Carlini et al., 2022b; Zhang et al., 2023). Memorization, an inherent property of these
038   networks, frequently occurs across a variety of deep learning tasks, from classification to genera-
039   tion (Feldman, 2020; Feldman & Zhang, 2020; Carlini et al., 2021; 2023). While memorization is
040   essential for minimizing the generalization errors (Feldman, 2020; Feldman & Zhang, 2020), it also
041   poses significant privacy risks. Notably, memorized instances can be exploited through membership
042   inference attacks, which determine whether the data was used in training (Yeom et al., 2018; Shokri
043   et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2022a; Ye et al., 2022).
044   Addressing memorization directly such as regularization proves ineffective (Carlini et al., 2019),
045   and simply removing memorized instances does not prevent networks from memorizing other data
046   instances (Arpit et al., 2017). Therefore, reducing memorization without compromising other aspects
047   of network performance is challenging. In response, we propose an innovative approach: rather
048   than modifying the training algorithm, we aim to redirect the network’s memorization capacity
049   toward non-sensitive data, which poses no privacy risks. This method involves creating a dummy
050
      set—a collection of redundant, non-informative instances designed specifically to be memorized,
      thus preserving the utility of the network while protecting sensitive training data.
051
052   In this work, we propose an over-memorization method that trains the model with both the training
053   set and a set of redundant, non-sensitive instances, which we named as dummy set. The dummy set is
      trained to effectively absorb the network’s capacity to memorize, thereby improving the network in

054
      terms of privacy. Also, the dummy set takes the role of reducing the generalization error, the network
055   minimizes any loss of utility.
056
057   We evaluate our method on image classification and causal language modeling tasks, demonstrating
058
      enhanced privacy protection against membership inference attacks. Additionally, our analysis of
      memorization in networks trained with the over-memorization method confirms that this approach
059
      effectively mitigates privacy risks by significantly reducing the memorization of training data.
060
061   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
062
063
          • We propose an over-memorization by training a set of non-sensitive instances to mitigate the
            challenges of reducing training data memorization.
064
065       • We validate the over-memorization method for the membership inference attacks, demonstrating
066         significant improvements in privacy protection.
067       • We provide empirical evidence that our method minimizes the loss of utility by conducting a
068         detailed analysis of its influences.

      2.1     T RAINING DATA PRIVACY OF DEEP NEURAL NETWORKS
073
074   With the growing attention to deep learning and privacy, the privacy of training data has become
075   a primary topic of discussion, as deep neural networks use vast amounts of data. Various works
076   have explored building secure models to protect the training data privacy, including differential
077   privacy (Dwork et al., 2014; Abadi et al., 2016; Li et al., 2021), auditing or understanding the
078   individual influences (Jagielski et al., 2020; Ye et al., 2024), and federated learning (McMahan et al.,
079   2017; Li et al., 2020; Truong et al., 2021). Although there are several strategies have been taken to
080
      protect training data violations, deep neural networks remain vulnerable to privacy threats such as
      membership inference (Fredrikson et al., 2014; Shokri et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2022a; Ye et al.,
081
      2022), training data extraction (Carlini et al., 2021; Geiping et al., 2020), and jailbreaking attacks for
082
      large language models (Chao et al., 2023; Niu et al., 2024). In particular, membership inference has
083   been widely studied for its application across a broad range of tasks, from the biomedical data and
084   health records (Homer et al., 2008; Sankararaman et al., 2009; Backes et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2022)
085   to public data and large language models (Truex et al., 2019; Jagielski et al., 2024)m as well as various
086   stereotypes (Yeom et al., 2018; Fredrikson et al., 2014; Sablayrolles et al., 2019; Choquette-Choo
087   et al., 2021).
088
      In this work, we focus on membership inference using reference models (Carlini et al., 2022a; Ye
089   et al., 2022). Given a target model, an adversary trains reference models, each with a different
090   training set drawn from the same data population. By analyzing the distribution of reference losses
091   or confidence scores based on the membership status of each instance, an instance is considered a
092   member of the target model’s training data if it is likely to be drawn from the distribution of the
093   training instance. This threat is closely related to the memorization of deep neural networks, by
094   analyzing the memorization via influences (Feldman, 2020; Feldman & Zhang, 2020).
095
096   2.2     M EMORIZATION OF DEEP NEURAL NETWORKS
097
098   During training, deep neural networks memorize some of the training data, which is a distinct
099
      property from overfitting (Carlini et al., 2019; Feldman, 2020; Feldman & Zhang, 2020). Various
      approaches have demonstrated the aspects of memorization including the high capacity of deep
100
      neural networks (Zhang et al., 2017; 2021), over-parameterization (Daniely, 2020), and training
101
      dynamics (Stephenson et al., 2021). It has been demonstrated that rare and atypical samples in
102   the training set are memorized, and these samples also contribute to generalization on unseen
103   data (Feldman, 2020; Feldman & Zhang, 2020). Further, deep neural networks have sufficient
104   capacity to memorize training samples even if the training samples are composed of random noise or
105   labels (Zhang et al., 2017). However, deep neural networks often expose memorized data in response
106   to privacy threats. Memorized instances are more susceptible to membership inference, as they are
107   also easily memorized by other deep neural networks. Additionally, it is challenging to prevent
      memorization using well-known methods designed to alleviate overfitting (Carlini et al., 2019).

      minimize the loss of utility.

184   3.2                    U SING ADDITIONAL TRAINING SET TO REDUCE MEMORIZATION
185
      Since memorization is essential for reducing generalization error (Feldman & Zhang, 2020) and is
186
      difficult to avoid (Carlini et al., 2022c), we acknowledge these characteristics and propose leveraging
187
      the memorization capacity of deep neural networks by training on redundant, meaningless set of
188   samples. This approach aims to occupy the network’s memorization capacity while minimizing the
189   memorization of sensitive data. To begin, we investigate the effect of training sets on memorization
190   and influence using the formulation in eq. 1. Our setup involves a training set St sampled from the
191   target data population and a set of redundant samples Sd . Our goal is to reduce the memorization
192   score mem(St , zt ) for each training sample zt by training with the combined set of St and the dummy
193   set Sd , denoted as mem(St ∪ Sd , zt ). The difference between mem(St ∪ Sd , zt ) and mem(St , zt ) can
194   be expressed as2 :
195
                mem(St ∪ Sd , zt ) − mem(St , zt ) = infl(St ∪ Sd , Sd , zt ) − infl(St ∪ Sd \{zt }, Sd , zt ).                                                          (3)
196
197   This equation highlights that regardless of whether Sd is sampled from the same data population as
198   St , the change in the memorization score is influenced by two terms: infl(St ∪ Sd , Sd , zt ) and
199   infl(St ∪ Sd \ zt , Sd , zt ). For the first influence term, infl(St ∪ Sd , Sd , zt ), we expect it to
200
      remain relatively stable, as training with the combined set St ∪ Sd already includes zt , and thus
      adding Sd should not cause a significant change. Our primary objective is to maximize the second
201
      influence term, infl(St ∪ Sd \ zt , Sd , zt ), which implies that training should generalize well for zt
202
      when we train with a superset composed of a different training set and the dummy set Sd .
203
204   As a result, we identify the key properties of the dummy set that are essential for effectively reducing
205   memorization in the training set. First, the dummy set should enhance model performance after
206
      training. When training with the superset of the training set St \{zt } and the dummy set Sd , the
      model reduces generalization error, thereby increasing the influence term infl(St ∪ Sd \ zt , Sd , zt ).
207
      Second, each dummy sample zd within the dummy set Sd should minimize correlation with individual
208
      training samples zt . If zd contains information that is correlated with zt , the model risks inadvertently
209   memorizing and leaking information about zt through zd .

243   Recall the dummy set should satisfy two conditions: 1) the dummy set should reduce generalization

249

250   we create a separate model specifically for training the dummy set. Using the initialized model and
251   the dummy set, we apply coordinate descent for both the model and the dummy set to minimize the
252   task objective, as illustrated in Algorithm 2.

254   3.4   H OW TO CONSTRUCT DUMMY SET FOR EACH TASK
255
256   The introduced dummy set can take various forms, making it applicable to a wide range of tasks,
257   from image classification to language modeling. In this section, we present the specific form of the
258   dummy set utilized in our experiments.
259   Image classification In the image classification task, each data point consists of an image and
260   its corresponding label. Each initialized dummy image has the same dimensionality of the training
261   images and has pixel intensity values ranging from 0 to 1. The corresponding soft labels are also
262   initialized randomly. During training, we do not restrict the values of the tensors to remain within the
263   0 to 1 range, which means the resulting tensors may exceed this range and are not visualized due to
264   their potentially unbounded values.
265   Language modeling The objective of language modeling is to predict the next token based on a
266   given sequence of previous tokens. We define the dummy tokens as soft tokens, each initialized as
267   a convex combination of tokens typically used in language models. In each single dummy token,
268   the candidate tokens for this convex combination are randomly selected before training the dummy
269   set, and only these candidate tokens are utilized during the training process. As a result, the dummy
      sequence consists of a randomly selected convex combination of tokens. This approach, which

296   4.1   S ETUP
297
298   Our experiments are conducted on two tasks: image classification using CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100,
299   and causal language modeling with Wikitext-103 (Merity et al., 2016).
300   CIFAR-10,100 The CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets each contain 50,000 training samples.
301   For measuring memorization scores and membership inference, we prepare subsets of size 25,000.
302   For all image classification models, we employ the ResNet-18 architecture. Each model is trained
303   using the minibatch SGD optimizer for 100 epochs, with a consistent batch sampling rate of 0.004,
304   resulting in a training batch size of 100. When incorporating the dummy set into the training process,
305   the sampling rate for the dummy set remains fixed at 0.004 to maintain consistency in the training
306   algorithm. For example, if the dummy set size is set to 5,000, the dummy batch size will be 20. We
307
      utilize Adam optimizer to train the dummy set in Algorithm 2.
308   Wikitext-103 It is common to utilize pretrained language models and fine-tune them using a
309   specific training set. In our causal language modeling experiment, we aim to demonstrate that our
310   method, which employs a dummy set, is effective in mitigating memorization over the fine-tuning
311   dataset. To this end, while using the Wikitext-103 dataset, we divide it into 1,000 chunks, with
312   each model trained on 100 chunks, resulting in approximately 180,135 sentences per model. We
313
      employ Pythia (Biderman et al., 2023)-70m model for our causal language modeling experiments.
      Each model is fine-tuned for 3 epochs without gradient accumulation. For the dummy set in causal
314
      language modeling, we ensure that each dummy set contains one-fifth of the token length of the
315
      training set. Prior to training the dummy set, we initialize it as a sparse soft token sequence, as
316   described in sec. 3.4, and focus solely on training these sparse token sequences. During the training
317   phase with the dummy set, we concatenate the randomly sampled dummy sequences to the sampled
318   training token sequences and perform gradient descent on the concatenated sequences.

364   4.3   R ESULTS ON MEMBERSHIP INFERENCE
365
366   We conduct membership inference for both image classification and causal language modeling tasks,
367   comparing models trained with and without dummy sets. Membership inference aims to determine
368   whether a given instance is part of the training set of the target model. Among various membership
369
      inference methods, we adopt the approach that utilizes reference models as described in Carlini et al.
      (2022a); Ye et al. (2022). This process involves several steps: first, we train reference models, each
370
      using a training set sampled from the same data population as the target model. Next, we compute the
371
      losses for the given target samples. By comparing the losses from the target model with those from
372   the reference models, we can establish a threshold to predict whether the target data was included
373   in the training set. Using different thresholds allows us to plot the receiver operating characteristic
374   (ROC) curve and compare the area under the curve (AUROC) to assess the model’s vulnerability to
375   membership inference. This method of membership inference with reference models is closely tied to
376   the memorization properties of deep neural networks, as it involves comparing losses across various
377   combinations of training sets, thereby making the AUROC values highly correlated with the degree
      of memorization.

479     5              C ONCLUSION
480
481     In this work, we propose an over-memorization method that utilizes a set of redundant, meaningless
482     instances to reduce the memorization of actual training data. By training the model with a combined
483     set of the actual training data and a dummy set—without externally modifying the training algo-
484     rithm—we enhance the training data privacy. Training the dummy set ensures that the dummy set
485     is easily memorable by deep neural networks, encouraging the model to memorize these instances
        instead of the actual training data. We validated our approach on the CIFAR-10 image classification

486
      task and the Wikitext-103 causal language modeling task. For both tasks, our method outperformed
487   standard training models in terms of defense against membership inference attacks. Moreover, our
488   method mostly maintains the utility of the model, achieving an optimal balance in the privacy-utility
489   trade-off. We believe our approach opens a door for controlling memorization by strategically
490   leveraging the training set.
