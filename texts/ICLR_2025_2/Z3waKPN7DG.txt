009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We present UNAST, a novel method for optimizing Large Language Models
012            (LLMs) after training. UNAST integrates Neural Architecture Search (NAS) with
013            sparsity and quantization techniques to compress LLMs. Starting from a pre-
014            trained model, it replaces layers (such as attention and MLP) with more efficient
015            alternatives by modifying attention heads, key-value projection dimensions, and
016            MLP expansion ratios. Layer candidates undergo local distillation to replicate
017            the original layers. Scores and costs (e.g., latency, parameter count) for each
018            operator are input into an Integer Linear Optimizer, which determines the optimal
019
               architecture under given constraints. Our experiments show that UNAST scales
               effectively to large models, reducing training costs by up to 10x compared to
020
               training smaller models from scratch. Testing on GPT-3 and LLaMa reveals
021
               that UNAST enhances latency and memory efficiency by up to 60% with minimal
022            accuracy loss. Additionally, it offers insights into the impact of various compression
023            techniques on Transformer layers, facilitating the creation of non-uniform models.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Natural Language Processing (NLP) has undergone a major transformation with the rise of Large
029   Language Models (LLMs), largely due to the scalability of the attention mechanism. However,
030   deploying these increasingly large models efficiently presents growing challenges. LLM families,
031   like LLaMa with its 7B, 13B, and 70B variants, typically feature multiple versions to accommodate
032   different hardware and time constraints. These models, however, are trained independently, creating
033   redundancy. A more efficient strategy would be to compress larger models down to smaller sizes
034   rather than training each from scratch.
035   Building on this idea, post-training techniques like quantization and sparsity have emerged to enhance
036   LLM deployment. While they reduce model size, they don’t always improve throughput and often
037   require manual hyperparameter tuning. Neural Architecture Search (NAS) can optimize model
038   architectures automatically for efficiency but is resource-intensive when applied to LLMs. Therefore,
039   a comprehensive post-training optimization method is needed.
040   To tackle this challenge, we introduce UNAST, an optimization framework that leverages NAS
041   to compress models according to specific constraints like latency and parameter count. UNAST
042   adjusts internal layer parameters, including the number of attention heads, Key-Value (KV) projection
043   dimensions, and expansion factors, to achieve efficient compression.
044
      We begin with a pre-trained model and distill teacher layers (attention and MLP) into candidate
045
      configurations by adjusting parameters like the number of heads, MLP projections, and attention
046   types. Each candidate is scored based on its similarity to the original teacher blocks. Using integer
047   linear programming (ILP), we select K architectures that optimize for constraints using candidate
048   scores and associated costs. These architectures undergo quick fine-tuning and evaluation, with the
049   top-performing one chosen for a longer final fine-tuning iteration.
050
      We demonstrate several key advantages of our approach:
051
052   1. Time to Create a New Model. Our method produces new models up to 10 times faster than pre-
053   training from scratch, thanks to efficient layer-wise knowledge distillation and automated candidate
      model selection.

084
      1. Novel Neural Architecture Search (NAS) Approach Applied to Large Language Models. We

093   2   R ELATED WORK
094
095
      Compression of neural networks remains an active area of research, as highlighted in recent sur-
096   veys Cheng et al. (2018); Gou et al. (2020); Park et al. (2024). Common methods include weight
097   sparsification Han et al. (2016); LeCun et al. (1990); Hassibi & Stork (1993), structured pruning via
098   channel removal Molchanov et al. (2017), and quantization Frantar et al. (2022); Lin et al. (2023);
099   Xiao et al. (2022). We focus on LLM compression for faster inference and will review some recent
100   work in this section.
101
      Sparsity in the unstructured form is not directly beneficial for GPU inference. SparseGPT Frantar
102   & Alistarh (2023) and Wanda Sun et al. (2023) additionally explore 2:4 structured sparsity that can
103   benefit faster inference. Structured pruning is another popular technique where entire heads and
104   MLP channels are removed from the transformer Xia et al. (2022); Ma et al. (2023b); Zhang et al.
105   (2023); Xia et al. (2023); Kurtic et al. (2023a). Such pruning results in a smaller and faster model;
106   however, the entire process needs to be repeated for every target budget. Depth pruning removes
107   entire blocks from the model as the residual skip connections will still allow signal propagation.
      Stochastic depth during training was applied in Fan et al. (2020). Importance-based layer dropping

      was studied in Men et al. (2024); Kim et al. (2024); Yuan et al. (2024), and layer merging was
109   explored in Yang et al. (2024). Quantization is a common technique to reduce the bitwidth of weight
110   tensors. Round-to-nearest approaches Dettmers et al.; Yao et al. (2022) perform straight-forward per-
111   element quantization, while carefully selecting quantization granularity. AdaRoundNagel et al. (2020)

114   output. Quantization helps reduce memory transfer overhead for bandwidth-limited operations under

      et al. (2021). Considering only the access to the original model, we opt for per-layer distillation as
118

119
      more efficient model. Manual design has been explored in Kitaev et al. (2020); Child et al. (2019);

121   architecture of the teacher model. NAS offers another method to attain a model with an accuracy-
122   latency trade-off Chen et al. (2020); Dong et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2020a); Xu et al. (2021); Yin
123   et al. (2021). Unfortunately, the high cost of these NAS techniques limits their application to LLMs.
124   We opt for fast NAS techniques applied to convolutional neural networks, such as DONNA Moons
125   et al. (2021) and LANA Molchanov et al. (2022). We analyze, extend, and apply these techniques to
126   LLMs, demonstrating the first scalable NAS method for LLMs.

131   Our approach, depicted in Figure 2, comprises two main parts: 1) Candidate training (see Section 3),
132   where layer-wise knowledge distillation is applied to train various candidate operations to mimic
133   those in the original teacher model; 2) Architecture search phase, which explores the candidate space
134   to find a model meeting custom constraints using linear optimization.
135
      Candidate training phase. Our research focuses on Transformer-like models, particularly on
136
      enhancing the performance of layers within Transformer blocks. While embedding and output layers
137   constitute a significant portion of the model, we defer their performance optimization for future work.
138   Within each Transformer block, our focus is on optimizing two key layers: Attention and Multilayer
139   Perceptron (MLP).
140
      We define the set of student operations for each of the 2 layer types, offering a diverse range of
141
      candidates for each layer. The only constraint is that the input and output dimensions must match
142
      those of the corresponding teacher layer. Unlike other NAS approaches that aim to discover entirely
143   new models, our goal is to replace the original model with a more efficient one. This confines our
144   search to the architecture space “near” the teacher network. To achieve this, we conduct layer-wise
145   knowledge distillation training to simulate the corresponding teacher layer.

      Architecture Selection and Fine-tuning. After the search phase, multiple architectures meeting the
      constraint are received and ranked based on the cumulative accuracy metric of per-layer operators.
208
      However, this ranking may not fully reflect the final model’s accuracy, and models may react
209
      differently to further training. To address this, each architecture undergoes short training (100s
210   millions of tokens), and their performance is evaluated (see Stage 3 in Figure 2). This approach
211   helps identify the model that responds better to training, as detailed in Section 5.1. Once the best
212   model is identified, a relatively short (tens of billions of tokens) version of the baseline pre-training
213   is conducted, referred to as fine-tuning in our paper. The same learning rate schedule and dataset
214   are used, with a higher learning rate for models with lower performance constraints. This choice is
215   justified by the smaller and more efficient models having lower quality initially and needing larger
      optimization steps for improvement.

246   4.1   G ENERAL SETUP
247
248   We focus on improving efficiency of Transformer-based models. Specifically, we gather insights
249   by exploring 3 pretrained models: proprietary GPT-architecture models with 2B (GPT-2B) and 8B
250   (GPT-8B) parameters, respectively, as well as the open LLaMa2-7B model. GPT-2B and GPT-8B are
251   trained on 1.1T tokens datasets. GPT-2B and GPT-8B contain 24/32 layers with hidden dimension
252   2048/4096 and 16/32 heads in attention layers. The LLaMa2-7B models is the publicly available 7B
253   GPT model trained on 2T tokens. It has similar configuration parameters as GPT3-8B. We used a
254
      version of LlaMa2-7B, shortly (∼ 10B tokens) finetuned on a 1.1T tokens dataset. This was done
      due to the requirements of knowledge distillation, as this stage UNAST relies on the dataset used.
255
      It means that in the baseline results of the LlaMa2-7B, we use the locally-fine-tuned version of
256
      LlaMa2-7B.
257
258   Implementation details. We integrated the UNAST pipeline into the Megatron Nvidia (2024)
259   codebase. For GPT experiments a 1.1T dataset was used for local distillation (5K steps, 2.5M tokens),
260
      evaluation (50 steps), finetuning (10K steps, 5M tokens). For LLaMa2 experiments a 3.5T dataset was
      used for distillation (5K steps, 5M tokens), evaluation (50 steps), finetuning (10K steps, 10M tokens).

      We use NVIDIA A100 as our target hardware. Training parameters are described in Appendix A.

      4.2   UNAST DETAILS
271
272
      The cost - total training time. We work with the GPT-3 8B and LLaMa2 7B models, originally
273
      trained on 1.1 trillion and 2 trillion tokens, respectively. In the local distillation step, we select
274   15 model candidates and fine-tune each for 10 billion tokens. The architecture search requires
275   quick fine-tuning of 100 architectures, each for 102 million tokens. Finally, the best architecture
276   is fine-tuned for 20 billion tokens. Each of the first two stages can be parallelized. In a sequential
277   implementation, creating a single model incurs only 9% of the original costs (an 11× speedup), or
278   1.5% with parallelization. To produce three sub-models, costs could be reduced by 25× or even 85×
279   with parallelization.
280
281     Finding 1. Our approach reduces training time by 11× - 85× to get 3 additional models.
282
283   Candidates. We establish a set of candidate operations based on the model and layer types, focusing
284   on Attention or MLP layers with inputs and outputs of size N . The following operations can be
285   applied: Teacher operation: A copy of the corresponding baseline layer, requiring no knowledge
286   distillation. Identity layer: Essentially a skip-layer, propagating the input of the block to the output,
287   also not requiring knowledge distillation. Linear layer: Replacing an Attention or MLP block with a
288   linear layer of size N × N . This candidate, along with the Identity layer, is the most computationally
289   efficient but may significantly affect model accuracy. Some of the candidates are layer-type dependent,
290
      meaning they can only replace either Attention or MLP layers, representing the same layer type with
      different parameters. The first such candidate is downsized Attention layer with lower number of
291
      heads or number of channels. Here, we do layer-wise distillation from the teacher Attention layers
292
      to the candidates with lower parameters. The symmetrical candidate for MLP is a downsized MLP
293   layer with lower hidden expansion.
294
295   Knowledge distillation. The success of the UNAST approach depends on the quality of candidates.
296
      To get strong candidates we perform per-layer distillation with the goal of mimicking teacher layers.
      We pre-train separate candidates for Attention and ML layers. In Figure 3 we show the dependency
297
      of LM score on latency for various candidates. In this evaluation we replace all teachers of the
298
      corresponding type with the pre-trained layer candidates.
299
300   UNAST necessitates estimating per-candidate scores regarding how well they mimic their teacher.
301   Detailed ablations led us to consider the Mean Squared Error (MSE) between teacher and candidate
302
      activations as a score. We showcase per-layer scores for various candidates in Figure 4.
303   Knowledge distillation loss. We ablate multiple loss functions for the per-layer distillation in the
304   Table 1. The metric is global MSE for the layers in the head, middle, tail of the model body. Total
305   model row represents LM score of the model where all layers of the corresponding type are replaced
306   with the distilled candidates. Attention layer candidate: attention layer with 16 heads and 128
307   channels. MLP candidate: MLP layer with expansion factor 2. We conclude that SquareHead Kurtic
308
      et al. (2023b) allows the students to better track the teacher, as shown by the lower MSE loss, and
      leads to better models overall, as shown by the better LM score.
309
310   We can see that MLP layers are more sensitive to down-sizing than Attention layers, as an equivalent
311   removal of channels in the Attention layer damages the model accuracy slightly less than MLP. As
312   expected, the most sensitive layer is the last one as it has the highest impact on the model output and
313   there are no layers after it to recover the possible inaccuracies.
314   Performance metric. A key performance metric we aim to improve in UNAST is latency. This
315   metric naturally depends on various deployment settings, such as the regime in which a model is used
316   or its parameters (e.g. batch size or input sequence size). Our approach works with any deployment
317   setting. We build a timing lookup table for any setup, which is then used in the search phase. We note
318   that it does not require any re-training of the candidates. We argue that the main body (Transformer
319   blocks) consumes most of compute in the pre-fill stage. We analyse costs in Appendix B.
320   Lookup Table. Accurate latency estimation is crucial for model performance optimization, as the
321   resulting model depends on the measurement regime. It’s essential to assess latency in the deployment
322   regime. Our experiments measure latency only in the pre-fill regime. We leave the auto-regressive
323   regime for future work. A hardware-specific lookup table is computed in the pre-fill phase with batch
      size 1 and sequence length 4096.

464   5.3    C OMBINING WITH OTHER PTO TECHNIQUES
465
      UNAST is a new Post-Training Optimization (PTO) approach, orthogonal to popular techniques
466
      like quantization and sparsity. In this section, we illustrate that these techniques complement each
467   other well. Furthermore, combining UNAST with quantization/pruning yields markedly superior
468   results compared to using a single technique. Specifically, UNAST focuses on replacing layers with
469   more efficient candidates emulating the behavior of the original layer, while quantization reduces
470   the bit-width of model parameters and sparsity removes redundant parameters. As shown in Table 5,
471   combining UNAST with quantization or pruning results in significantly improved accuracy and
472   efficiency compared to models of the same size compressed using a single technique. For instance,
473   the UNAST 50% + 4 bits quantization for the GPT 8B model yields a smaller resulting model size
474   than the baseline compressed to 3 bits, with higher accuracy.
475       Finding 4. Combining UNAST with other PTO techniques is significantly better than individual
476       PTO techniques.

483   General Observations. First, we discuss the models with restricted latency budget and the layer
484   operator candidates are taken from all available distilled candidates. The figures with structures
485   of the resulting models are presented in Appendix G. From the model structures we can make the
      following observations: 1. The method tends to compress Attention layers rather than MLP, and 2.

      The algorithm prefers to achieve speedup by removing channels rather than Attention heads. However,
487   reducing the number of heads or channels has a similar effect on latency: the latency of the layer
488   with 16 heads and 128 channels is equal to the latency of the layer with 32 heads and 64 channels.
489   This means that reducing the number of heads affects accuracy more than reducing channels.

      This finding is confirmed for other budget latency models as well. (See Appendix G).

499   layers within Transformer models, we conducted experiments where we restricted the search of

      Relationship to LANA LANA Molchanov et al. (2022) explores layer-wise distillation by focusing
516   on CNN architectures in vision. Our work extends this study to the transformer architecture and
517   language models. This involves studying different sets of operators, including attention and MLP.
518   Besides, the scale of LLMs imposes implementation constraints and the use of tensor and pipeline
519   parallelism. Our work studies different pre-training losses, which were not discussed in the LANA

      D    M ODEL SIZE COMPUTATION
786
787   We compute the models sizes in table 5 the following way. The embedding and output layers are
788   always uncompressed and stored in float16 precision. The main body parameters can be compressed
789   in different ways. For the UNAST compression we compute the total number of main body parameters
790
      in the resulting architecture. For GPTQ compression we assume that each element takes certain
      number of bits. For SparseGPT we compute the number of non-zero parameters. For example, to
791
      compute model size of UNAST 50% + GPTQ 4b for GPT 8B model, we consider 2.1B embedding +
792
      output parameters, add 3.7B parameters compressed to 4 bits and as result we get (2.1B * 16 bits
793   + 3.7B * 4 bits) = 5.63 GB. For UNAST 50% + SparseGPT 50%, the formula is following (2.1B +
794   3.7B * 0.5) * 16 bits = 7.36GB.
