009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite recent advances, state-of-the-art machine learning algorithms struggle
012            considerably with control problems where data is scarce relative to model com-
013            plexity. This problem is further exacerbated if the system changes over time,
014            making past measurements less useful. While tools from reinforcement learning,
015            supervised learning, and Bayesian optimization alleviate some of these issues,
016
               they do not address all of them at once. Considering these drawbacks, we present
               a multi-scale Bayesian optimization for fast and data-efficient decision-making.
017
               Our pipeline combines a high-frequency data-driven dynamics model with a low-
018
               frequency Gaussian process, resulting in a high-level model with a prior that is
019            specifically tailored to the dynamics model setting. By updating the Gaussian pro-
020            cess during Bayesian optimization, our method adapts rapidly to new data points,
021            allowing us to process current high-quality data quickly, which is more representa-
022            tive of the system than past data. We apply our method to avoid tearing instabilities
023            in a tokamak plasma, a control problem where modeling is difficult, and hardware
024            changes potentially between experiments. Our approach is validated through of-
025            fline testing on historical data and live experiments on the DIII-D tokamak. On
026            the historical data, we show that our method outperforms a naive decision-making
027
               algorithm based exclusively on a recurrent neural network and past data. The live
               experiment corresponds to a high-performance plasma scenario with a high likeli-
028
               hood of instabilities. Despite this base configuration, we achieved a 50% success
029
               rate in the live experiment, representing an improvement of over 220% compared
030            to historical data.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Controlling real-world systems is generally a difficult task, even when powerful machine-learning
036   tools are employed: nonlinearities are often pronounced, data is scarce, and safety issues impose
037   severe limitations. A prime example of these issues is tokamak control, where good models are
038   unavailable, safety is paramount, and instabilities are notoriously hard to control. These issues
039
      are further complicated by the fact that hardware configurations in tokamak change on a regular
      basis, making a model trained on past data even less reliable. However, despite these challenges,
040
      designing good control policies for tokamaks is highly desirable due to their promise to generate
041
      abundant clean energy via nuclear fusion.
042
043   In many real-world settings, model-free reinforcement learning is a promising solution and has seen
044   successful applications (He et al., 2024; Kumar et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2020). However, most of
045
      these methods rely on a prohibitive amount of policy rollouts for training, which is typically only
      achievable with reliable simulation environments. In complex environments like tokamaks, this is
046
      particularly problematic, as operation costs typically only permit a handful of rollouts, and exist-
047
      ing simulators do not reflect the true dynamics for many aspects of the plasma (Char et al., 2023a).
048   Offline RL, seeks to overcome these issues by directly learning a policy from offline data which con-
049   servatively stays within bounds of observed data (Levine et al., 2020). However, the performance of
050   offline RL methods depends crucially on high-quality expert data that contains advantageous states.
051   If these are not present, then offline RL can suffer from extrapolation errors (Fujimoto et al., 2019).
052   This is a major drawback for tokamak control, where significant exploration and improvement are
053   still required to achieve energy production. Moreover, even offline RL is affected by the sim2real
      problem which is described in detail below.


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Alternatively, model-based reinforcement learning offers a solution where dynamics models are
055   trained from historic data and rollouts from the model are then used for policy learning or planning
056   (Deisenroth & Rasmussen, 2011; Chua et al., 2018; Kaiser et al., 2019). In the past, machine learning
057   algorithms have been used to directly model plasma dynamics (Char et al., 2023b; Abbate et al.,
058   2021; Boyer et al., 2021). Reinforcement learning policies have also been trained in models trained
059   solely on fusion data (Char et al., 2023a; Wakatsuki et al., 2023; Degrave et al., 2022). However, the
060   performance of these approaches crucially hinges on the assumption that the data faithfully captures
061   the model at test time. This is problematic in the case of tokamak dynamics, where time-dependent
062   model changes cannot be neglected. Though this issue can be potentially addressed by updating the
063
      model with new data, the scarcity of experiments implies that too little data is typically produced to
      reliably update the model.
064
065   In low-dimensional settings, the obstacles posed by conventional RL methods can potentially be
066   addressed by Bayesian optimization (BO). BO is a data-efficient tool for optimizing black box func-
067   tions (Garnett, 2023). By quantifying model uncertainty, BO achieves a tradeoff between exploration
068   and exploitation, leading to fast convergence in many practical settings (Shalloo et al., 2020; Shields
069
      et al., 2021). In the case of tokamak control, BO has been used, e.g., to control the rampdown of
      a real tokamak (Mehta et al., 2024), and to control neutral beams in a tokamak simulator (Char
070
      et al., 2019). However, the work of Mehta et al. (2024) does not address critical plasma instabilities,
071
      whereas Char et al. (2019) relies on a simulator. Moreover, these methods use a poorly specified
072   prior and require an extensive amount of experiments to perform well.
073
074   Motivated by the strengths and shortcomings of existing machine learning-based approaches for
075
      tokamak control, we design a novel approach that combines a dynamic model predictor and Bayesian
      Optimization. Our approach employs a multi-scale approach: a recurrent probabilistic neural net-
076
      work models the high-frequency model dynamics, while a Gaussian process models the effect of
077
      low-frequency marginal statistics on the dynamics. After adequate pre-processing, we use historical
078   data to train both models, where the dynamic model serves as a prior for the Gaussian process. Addi-
079   tionally, by leveraging physics-informed assumptions, we design a low-dimensional state space for
080   the Gaussian process. This naturally leads to a contextual Bayesian optimization algorithm tailored
081   to the task at hand, allowing it to find stabilizing actions in a highly data-efficient manner. Moreover,
082   due to its ability to perform fast updates, it allows us to efficiently leverage small batches of data
083   collected during experiments to best inform new decisions on the fly.
084   We test our approach on a large dataset from past tokamak experiments, where we can quickly iden-
085   tify stable configurations, outperforming a naive approach based exclusively on the recurrent neural
086   network model. Furthermore, we apply our approach to find stabilizing actions for a high performing
087   plasma scenario in the DIII-D tokamak. High performing plasma scenarios need to maintain high
088   temperature and pressures for increased energy, hence, they are more unstable. Our method was
089   able to find stabilizing ECH actuator values in four of eight experiments despite changes to other
090   actuators, a 133% improvement compared to historical experiments with the same configuration.
091   Our paper is structured as follows: first, we provide some necessary background to nuclear fusion,
092   and define our problem mathematically. Then we discuss our complete pipeline and methodology,
093   followed by the results and analysis on offline historical data and live experiments on a Tokamak
094   reactor. Finally, we provide conclusions and discuss opportunities for future work. Additional
095   details are provided in the Appendix.

103   2.1   N UCLEAR F USION
104
105   Nuclear fusion is seen as a promising solution for clean, limitless energy, producing no high-level
106   radioactive waste. Among the fusion technologies, tokamaks are the most advanced, using magnetic
107   fields to confine hot plasma to enable fusion conditions. Many countries have invested in tokamak
      research facilities and currently more 35 nations are collaborating to build ITER, a global project


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      aiming to demonstrate the viability of large-scale commercial fusion reactors (Mohamed et al., 2024;
109   Shimada et al., 2007).
110
111   One of the key challenges in tokamak development is plasma disruptions, which can cause se-
112
      vere damage to reactor walls and components, particularly in larger reactors like ITER (Schuller,
      1995; Lehnen et al., 2015). These disruptions often stem from tearing mode instabilities (or tearing
113
      modes), where magnetic islands form, leading to energy loss and instability. Prior work proposes
114
      avoiding tearing instability with predictive models using real-time control (Fu et al., 2020) and rein-
115   forcement learning (Seo et al., 2024). However, these methods reduce neutral beam power and add
116   torque to stabilize the plasma. This is undesirable, as reducing beam power leads to lower confine-
117   ment energy, decreasing the total energy output of the tokamak. On the other hand, adding torque
118   to large tokamaks is itself a challenging issue. Bardoczi et al. (2024) propose controlling tearing
119   modes utilizing differential rotation of the plasma. However, achieving differential rotation control
120   is itself a challenging problem.
121   Though still poorly understood, Electron Cyclotron Heating (ECH) has shown promise in counter-
122   acting tearing instabilities by driving localized currents at the site of instability (Gantenbein et al.,
123   2000; Kolemen et al., 2014). These and other findings have motivated the inclusion of gyrotrons
124   capable of delivering ECH in future reactors to potentially control tearing instabilities, e.g., ITER
125   will have over 40 gyrotrons. So far, the best results for stabilizing instabilities with ECH have been
126   achieved by keeping the ECH constant over time, as this minimizes the chance of plasma disrup-
127   tions. However, how to best deploy ECH is still an open question. We provide further details on
128   ECH effect on plasma in the appendix.
129   In this work, we aim to control ECH profiles to avoid tearing instability (or modes) in high qmin
130   tokamak scenarios. An ECH profile represents the heating achieved by the gyrotrons across the
131   cross section of the plasma. This can be seen in fig 4. High qmin is a scenario that supports long
132   duration steady-state plasma operations, making it crucial for future commercial fusion reactors.
133   More details on High qmin scenario are provided in the Appendix. We also focus our attention on
134   2-1 tearing instability, a type which is the most common and significantly disruptive.

      3      M ETHODOLOGY
186
187
      We now introduce our method, which aims to find stationary ECH profiles that mitigate tearing
188
      instabilities. Our complete pipeline is illustrated in Fig. 1. On a high level, the process is as follows -
189
      We model the system at two different time scales to inform the choice of actuator commands for each
190   experiment. At a smaller, more granular time scale, we use a recurrent probabilistic neural network
191   model (RPNN) to estimate the high-frequency behavior during each experiment. The coarser model
192   corresponds to a Gaussian process model and is trained to predict the behavior of the system based on
193   marginal statistics from experimental observations and RPNN predictions of the objective function,
194   which act as a prior mean. In this case, the objective function is the time-to-tearing instability.
195   Given the target normalized plasma pressure β̄N , we leverage the Gaussian process to select actions
196   (ECH profiles) in a low-dimensional space via Bayesian optimization. The desired profile is then
197   converted to gyrotron angles and applied to the tokamak. Finally, we update our model with the
198
      resulting time-to-tearing instability and actual ECH profile and repeat the procedure. We update the
      model with the measured ECH profile because it can diverge significantly from the desired one. In
199
      the following sections, we describe the high-frequency RPNN, then the GP, and end with the full
200
      Bayesian optimization pipeline.

221   3.2   G AUSSIAN P ROCESS M ODEL
222
      Exclusively using RPNN for experimental design is challenging for various reasons. Although the
223
      RPNN accurately captures some of the tokamak behavior, the resulting predictions often exhibit
224
      significant errors, largely due to the sim2real gap caused by time-dependent fluctuations in the envi-
225   ronment variables, e.g., due to maintenance or hardware changes provoked by previous experiments.
226   Furthermore, retraining the RPNN between experiments and using it to select gyrotron angles ag is
227   virtually impossible because the newly collected data is too small and we only have a few minutes
228   between experiments.
229
      We address the above-mentioned issues by employing a Gaussian process (GP) model, a nonpara-
230
      metric model that is very data-efficient, especially in low-dimensional spaces (Deisenroth & Ras-
231   mussen, 2011). A GP corresponds to an infinite collection of random variables, of which any finite
232   number is jointly normally distributed. To fully leverage the strengths of GP models, we need to
233   carefully summarize the information collected between experiments before training the GP. This is
234   done as follows.
235
      First, we assume the achieved normalized plasma pressure βN is independent of the ECH profile
236
      aq . This is a reasonable assumption because βN is largely determined by neutral beams, which are
237   controlled through the feedback variables act . We then approximate the feedforward and feedback
238
      control actions af1 , . . . , afτ and ac1 , . . . , acτ by assuming that they are uniquely specified by the target
239
      normalized plasma pressure, denoted by β̄N . This choice is partly justified because the feedforward
240   and feedback control actions are often primarily informed by a target normalized plasma pressure. A
241   further approximation we make is to project the ECH profile aech to a Gaussian curve, parametrized
242   by the three-dimensional vector aq containing the center, width, and height of the Gaussian curve.
243   Finally, we employ the GP to predict the time-to-tearing mode tTM , which we use as a proxy for the
244   probability of a tearing mode occurring. The rationale behind this choice is twofold. First, a scenario
245   where tearing instability occur late implies a higher degree of stability than a scenario where they
246   occur earlier. Moreover, it allows us to use the GP in a regression setting, where GPs are strongest
247   and best understood. The GP inputs are thus β̄N and aq , whereas the output is tTM .

299   This section presents results from offline tests using historical data and results from experiments at

301   trained using 15, 000 one-step state transition observations collected between 2010 and 2019 at the

447   5   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
448
449   While our method is shown to preemptively suppress tearing instabilities, it is mainly data-driven,
450   and potential improvements are possible by incorporating physics knowledge. One possible solu-
451   tion is to develop physics-informed neural network models, such as incorporating elements of the
452   Rutherford equation to improve interpretability. Another shortcoming is that our current method is
453
      only applicable to feedforward control scenarios. This means the algorithm cannot adapt to unex-
      pected real-time changes in the plasma, such as MHD activity or impurity changes. In future work,
454
      we aim to extend our learning to feedback control systems.

457   6   C ONCLUSION
458
459   In this work, motivated by the challenges of tokamak control, we develop a multi-scale model-
460   ing approach for making decisions on the fly using a handful of data. Our pipeline leverages a
461   high-frequency neural network model of the system dynamics and a Gaussian process that makes
462   predictions based on marginal statistics. Together, both models form a Bayesian optimization al-
463   gorithm tailored to the task at hand that can quickly identify stabilizing control actions. This is
464   achieved by making decisions on the fly based on newly collected data. On a historical data set,
465
      our method outperforms vanilla BO and a naive baseline that relies exclusively on neural network
      predictions. This is mainly due to our approach having better exploration capability. Moreover, our
466
      method shows promise in live experiments on the DIII-D Fusion reactor. During the experiments,
467
      our approach successfully avoided tearing instability in 4/8 runs despite highly unstable conditions,
468   representing an improvement of over 117% percent compared to past experiments.
469
470   Our work illustrates the potential of combining complex high-frequency and low-frequency models
471
      to improve performance on the fly based on incoming data. In the field of nuclear fusion, the need
      for similar methods will increase in the future, as new and larger reactors such as ITER become
472
      operational, and a significant gap between existing and new models needs to be bridged with very
473
      little data. This is the case not only for the stabilization setting considered in this paper but also
474   for settings such as ramp-up design, where a different set of actuators is considered. Moreover,
475   we believe this approach could be of interest to several other applications where the discrepancy
476   between past and present data is considerable, e.g., physical systems that exhibit wear and tear.

597   A.1   A DDITIONAL F USION BACKGROUND
598
599   Effect of ECH on Plasma : Electron cyclotron heating waves released by gyrotrons interact with
600   the plasma by being absorbed by electrons whose gyrofrequency matches the frequency of the ECH
601
      wave. This absorption is highly localized, making electron cyclotron heating (ECH) a powerful
      tool for precise plasma control. ECH increases the electron temperature at the absorption point and
602
      often reduces electron density, a phenomenon known as density pump-out (Wang et al., 2017). As
603
      density decreases, plasma rotation tends to speed up due to reduced inertia. By adjusting the toroidal
604   injection angle of the EC wave, different effects can be achieved. When injected perpendicular to the
605   toroidal direction, EC waves primarily heat the plasma. When injected parallel, they drive electron
606   acceleration in the direction of the plasma current, a process called electron cyclotron current drive
607   (ECCD). While the injection angle can be switched between shots, it cannot be actively adjusted
608   during a single shot. In this experiment, ECCD is used for all shots because it effectively suppresses
609   magnetic islands (Kolemen et al., 2014).
610   Details on high qmin plasma scenario : The high qmin scenario refers to a group of scenarios related
611   with elevated values of qmin , the minimum value of the safety factor profile. Under this umbrella of
612   scenarios, there are three main groups: qmin = 1.4, qmin = 1.5 − 2, and qmin > 2. The lowest of
613   the range with qmin = 1.4 has shown promise as being stable to TMs, but did not have the greatest
614   confinement, while the highest of qmin > 2 was stable to TMs but had lower energy confinement.
615   The middle range of qmin = 1.5 − 2 has very desirable confinement but is very suspectible to TMs
616   (Holcomb et al., 2014). The purpose of this experiment is to work in that middle range of qmin ,
617   referred to as the elevated qmin scenario, to stabilize TMs and achieve higher confinement than
618
      either of the other similar scenario options. In the elevated qmin scenario, 2/1 TMs are the most
      prevalent mode because they require the least energy to perform magnetic reconnection and form a
619
      magnetic island. Other lower order modes like 3/1 or 5/2 can sometimes occur but are significantly
620
      less frequent as they require more energy to form a magnetic island. Stabilizing TMs in the elevated
621   qmin scenario would show a path forward for this high-confinement scenario as a possible operating
622   scenario for a fusion power plant.

625   A.2   DATASET
626
627   Plasma trajectories on a Tokamak consists of three phases. The ramp-up phase, where the gases
628   are heated and pressure is increased to generate the plasma state where fusion occurs. During this
629   phase, the normalized plasma pressure βN rises. Then, we enter the flat-top phase, where the plasma
630   pressure βN is sustained, allowing fusion to occur. In this phase, βN is mostly constant and the aim
631   to maintain this state without instabilities. Finally, the actuators are gradually ramped down and the
632   plasma is safely terminated as the shot concludes. In this paper, we stay in the flat top phase and
633
      aim to stabilize it. To create our dataset, we hence use only flat top data and only control actuators
      during this phase of the experiment.
634
635   Our complete dataset contains of ∼ 15000 plasma trajectories from historical experiments at DIII-D
636   Tokamak. The data contains signals from different diagnostics have different dimensions and spatial
637   resolutions, and the availability and target positions of each channel vary depending on the dis-
638   charge condition. Therefore, the measured signals are preprocessed into structured data of the same
639
      dimension and spatial resolution using the profile reconstruction and equilibrium fitting (EFIT).
      These shots contain many different signals, some of which are described below. The dataset con-
640
      sists of scalar signals defined at every timestep and profile signals which are defined along 33 or 200
641
      points along the radius of the plasma cross-section. These consists of temperature, ion temperature,
642   pressure, rotation, safety (Q) factor and density. For these signals we first convert them into PCA
643   components and select the top components which are able to explain 99% of the variance in data.
644   The Electron Cyclotron Heating (ECH) profile we choose to control, is also defined at 200 points
645   along the plasma radius. PCA is unable to describe ECH profiles, however they can described well
646   by a Gaussian curve and are hence parameterized by the center, width and amplitude of the curve.
647   These 3 parameters form our parameterization aq of the ECH profiles. The model state space st is
      shown in table 2 while the actuator space at is shown in table 3.
