011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated Learning provides an efficient framework for fine-tuning Large Lan-
014            guage Models (LLMs) on diverse private datasets, addressing the growing scarcity
015
               of publicly available training data while maintaining data privacy. However, in
               practice, client data typically spans multiple domains, posing significant chal-
016
               lenges for the global model’s generalization capabilities. To address this issue,
017
               we introduce a novel framework, Federated Consensus-Divergence Decoupling
018            for LLM Fine-Tuning (FedCDD), designed to enhance global model performance
019            in such heterogeneous environments. Our framework introduces a mechanism
020            for consensus aggregation and divergence alignment, decoupling client updates
021            into “consensus” and “divergence” parts. This allows the LLM to maintain
022            a unified consensus while accommodating domain-specific divergences. Addi-
023            tionally, we employ a Gaussian-Noise Mask to regulate local model uploads,
024            preventing the LLM from overfitting to domain-specific knowledge. Experi-
025            mental results on heterogeneous datasets demonstrate the superiority of our ap-
026
               proach over existing methods. The code is anonymously available at https:
               //anonymous.4open.science/r/FedCDD-5DA6.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Trained on large public datasets, Large Language Models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023) (Ouyang
032   et al., 2022) have demonstrated significant success in solving general problems (Imani et al., 2023)
033   (Didolkar et al., 2024), (Chen et al., 2023). However, the availability of high-quality public data
034   is diminishing, posing a serious obstacle to the continued development of LLMs (Kaddour et al.,
035   2023), and it is predicted that high-quality public data will be exhausted before 2026 (Villalobos
036   et al., 2022). As a result, there is a growing trend of either combining existing datasets (Wang et al.,
037   2023) or using datasets generated by models themselves (Wang et al., 2022). The former often
038   falls short, as more data generally leads to better performance (Kaplan et al., 2020), while the latter
039
      may cause model degradation (Alemohammad et al., 2023) (Muennighoff et al., 2023). Meanwhile,
      many high-quality private datasets exist but cannot be shared due to privacy concerns. Some large
040
      language models, such as BloombergGPT (Wu et al., 2023), have been successfully trained on such
041
      datasets. The challenge lies in utilizing these private, high-quality datasets while preserving pri-
042   vacy. Federated Learning (FL) offers a solution by allowing multiple parties to collaboratively train
043   a model without directly sharing their datasets. Participants train local models on private datasets,
044   and only model updates are aggregated centrally, ensuring privacy (Li et al., 2020). Applying fed-
045   erated learning to Large Language Models offers a solution to the limitations of public high-quality
046   datasets. It unlocks the potential value of private datasets without directly sharing them, thereby
047   ensuring the privacy and security of the model data. (Zhuang et al., 2023).
048   With the increasing integration of Federated Learning (FL) and Large Language Models (LLMs),
049   numerous studies have concentrated on training LLMs within a federated learning framework. In
050   this paper, we focus on the integration of the LLM Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) module within
051   the FL domain (Gunel et al., 2020). Existing approaches, such as FederatedScope-LLM (Kuang
052   et al., 2024) and Shepherd (He et al., 2021), attempted to incorporate the FedAvg algorithm into the
053   SFT process. However, subsequent research by Ye et al. (2024) pointed out that these studies were
      limited by using only one dataset and relying solely on FedAvg, thereby overlooking other scenarios.

054
      Consequently, some research has explored the feasibility of the SFT module with a broader range
055   of datasets and FL methods. Nevertheless, a common limitation in these studies is their failure to
056   adequately address the diversity of real-world datasets, which can result in substantial client drift.
057   While some studies have attempted to train clients on diverse datasets during the SFT process, they
058   do not explicitly optimize for this scenario or propose methods tailored to this challenge, instead
059   relying on conventional federated learning approaches. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first
060   to study the integration of FL and SFT on Non-IID datasets and propose a innovative framework for
061   LLM fine-tuning in FL. We aim to explore optimization strategies to enhance the performance of
062   LLM SFT. By training on multiple datasets, we seek to closely simulate real-world environments,
063
      provide relevant benchmarks for this domain, and offer preliminary contributions that may inspire
      future research.
064
065   In the common experiment with federated large language model training on the Non-IID datasets,
066   the proposed approach involves clients fine-tuning their models using Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)
067   (Hu et al., 2021), then uploading the trained results to the cloud. The server aggregates models with
068   FedAvg and distributes the updated global model back to the clients for the next round of training.
069
      In the server aggregation phase, we observe that the LoRA method can cause knowledge drift.
      The local LLM tends to focus on the local domain, which negatively impacts the global model’s
070
      generalization ability. Based on this observation, we raise the following question: 1) During the
071
      global aggregation, how to design a new method that ensures the global model accurately captures
072   more details of local knowledge? In terms of client-side updates, we find that extracting local
073   features during client training is crucial for the global model. Great training method should be better
074   capture features from datasets while avoiding to upload the unimportant knowledge. However, in
075   the current method, the best algorithm proved by Ye et al. (2024) simply uploads all the LoRA
076   parameters to the global. Based on this, we further propose another question: 2) During client
077   training, how to employ a new algorithm that allows clients to provide the important knowledge
078   extracted from local dataset for global?
079   To address the two issues mentioned above, this paper proposes a innovative framework to help
080   global model to absorb the knowledge from clients, enhancing generalization. To address problem
081   1, we explore the role of LoRA in federated learning, decomposing it into consensus characteristics
082   and divergence characteristics. The former reflects the generalization ability of knowledge, while
083   the latter indicates its divergence on specific domains. Based on this, we propose the Consensus-
084   Divergence Aggregation, which combines consensus aggregation and divergence alignment, opti-
085   mizing the global aggregation process and improving the performance of the global model. To the
086
      specific problem 2, during local updates, we introduce the Gaussian-Noise Masking, which focus-
      ing upload significantly altered parameters while disregarding minimal changes. The mask enables
087
      the global model to accurately capture the direction of meaningful updates, reducing the risk of
088
      entrapment in local optima caused by minor parameter adjustments.
089
090   Our primary contribution in this paper can be summarized as follows:
091   (1) We discover that LLM could be decoupled by consensus and divergence, which has practical
092   significance in the federated large language model training.
093
      (2) We propose an innovative framework for federated large language model training. In the global
094
      aggregation phase, we address the issue of domain knowledge drift in LLM clients by employing
095
      a combination of consensus aggregation and divergence alignment. During the client uploading
096   process, we focus on ensuring that the parameter updates effectively contribute to the global model’s
097   knowledge base.
098
099
      (3) Our framework applied in the same experimental environment significantly improves the model’s
      generalization ability.

      2.2   F EDERATED L EARNING AND L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
163
164   Federated Learning (FL) (Kairouz et al., 2019) is a method that holds great potential for enabling
165   privacy-preserving collaborative training. FL involves four key processes during training: (1) The
166   server sends the global model to the client side; (2) The client performs local model training on its
167   local dataset; (3) The client uploads the updated model parameters; and (4) The server receives the
168   client model parameters and performs global aggregation. Through these iterative steps, the server
169   is able to train a global model without requiring clients to directly share their local datasets.
170   In response to the shortage of high-quality public datasets, FL provides an effective approach to
171   leveraging private datasets for training more optimal models while maintaining data privacy. The
172   combination of FL and LLMs presents a promising research direction for addressing data-related
173   challenges in the future (Li et al., 2024). FATE-LLM (Fan et al., 2023) explored traditional tasks
174   in federated learning fine-tuning for LLMs, while FwdLLM (Xu et al., 2023c) focused on improv-
175   ing memory and time efficiency during client training, thereby reducing the costs on client devices.
176   FederatedScope-LLM (Kuang et al., 2024) emphasized federated instruction tuning using the Fe-
177
      dAvg algorithm. OpenFedLLM (Ye et al., 2024) introduced a framework capable of completing
      both the SFT and RLHF training processes.
178
179   In our paper, we focus on SFT based on Non-IID datasets. To the best of our knowledge, we are
180   the first to study this issue and propose a innovative framework that addresses both the client upload
181   process and the global aggregation process.

460
461
      Performance Comparison. Table 1 presents the performance of different methods in traditional FL
462   compared to our FedCDD approach. The results demonstrate that FedCDD outperforms the other
463   methods, highlighting its effectiveness in large language model training within a federated learning
464   context. Traditional methods like FedAvg and FedProx fail to effectively aggregate client consensus
465   and align divergences, resulting in a degradation of model performance. In contrast, FedCDD suc-
466   cessfully preserves the model’s generalization capabilities under these conditions. Specifically, our
467   framework demonstrates a significant improvement in generalization and consistently maintains an
468   advantage across various specialized domains.

      Key Components. We conduct an ablation study on the key components of our method using

475   components. The results, demonstrating the effectiveness of each component, are presented in Table

478
      Hyper-Parameters. We conduct a hyper-parameter ablation analysis using MT-Bench, focusing
479
      on the general performance of the model, as shown in Figure 3. The analysis examines two key
480   hyper-parameters: the masking sparsity α and the temperature T in the global aggregation process.
481   We observe that variations in temperature within a narrow range do not significantly affect the aver-
482   age results, likely because optimal performance can be achieved within an appropriate range of T .
483   However, masking sparsity α has a much larger impact, as deviations in α led to performance fluc-
484   tuations. Specifically, if α is too large, the global model may lose important updates from clients,
485   while setting α too low results in an overemphasis on less significant client information. In most of
      our experiments, we set the default values to T = 0.1 and α = 30%.

      5   D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATION
487
488
      (1) Our method is based on LoRA, which does not enhance model performance as effectively as full
489   parameter fine-tuning. However, training with LoRA significantly reduces training time and is easily
490   adaptable to various downstream tasks. In the future, we aim to develop a method that combines the
491   peak performance of full fine-tuning with the flexibility and cost-efficiency of LoRA.
492
      (2) Our method is rooted in the field of federated large language model training, but its underlying
493
      principles are broadly applicable. In the future, we will continue to explore this approach to extend
494
      its applicability to more specific domains within federated learning.

497   6   C ONCLUSION
498
499   In this paper, we are pioneers in innovatively exploring the large language model fine-tuning in
500   the federated learning on the Non-IID datasets. Additionally, we are the first to establish a new
501
      algorithm for federated LLM training. We propose a novel framework called FedCDD, an effective
      federated consensus-divergence decoupling for LLM fine-tuning. We decouple the updates of LoRA
502
      into divergence and consensus, seizing the subtle updates of the LLM updates. At server level,
503
      we align the divergence using cosine similarity and aggregate consensus of LLM, enhancing the
504   generalization ability of the global model. At the client level, we apply Gauss-Noise Mask to the
505   parameters being updated, avoiding the client’ local knowledge affecting the generalization ability of
506   the global model due to the over-fitting. This method has demonstrated effectiveness and robustness
507   across multiple experiments. We hope this work offers a novel perspective for future research on
508   federated large language model fine-tuning.
