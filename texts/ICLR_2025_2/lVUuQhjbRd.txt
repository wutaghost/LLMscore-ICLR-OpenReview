009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown remarkable capability in code gen-
012            eration tasks. However, they still struggle with complex software development
013            tasks where agents of different roles need to work collaboratively. Existing works
014            have proposed some LLM-based multi-agent software development frameworks
015            following linear models such as the Waterfall model. However, linear models
016            suffer from erroneous outputs of LLMs due to the lack of a self-correction mecha-
017            nism. Inspired by human teams where people can freely start meetings for reach-
018
               ing agreement, we propose a novel and flexible multi-agent framework AltDev,
               which enables agents to correct their deliverables and align with other agents in
019
               a real-time manner. AltDev integrates a compulsory alignment checking and a
020
               conditional multi-agent discussion at the end of each development phase, in order
021            to identify and reduce errors at early stages in the software development lifecycle.
022            Our experiments on various software development tasks show that AltDev signif-
023            icantly improves the quality of generated software code in terms of executability,
024            structural and functional completeness. The code of our project is available at
025            https://github.com/RainingSea/Altdev.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large Language Models (LLMs) have been demonstrated effective in various code generation tasks
031   and become efficient assistants to human developers (Austin et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021b). How-
032   ever, single LLM performs poor when facing complex software development projects, where the
033
      internal logics of functionalities could be super complicated. A natural extension is to incorporate
      multiple LLM-based roles in the software development team, including product managers, archi-
034
      tects, project managers, programmers and test engineers. Due to the fast speed of content generation
035
      and communication, LLM-based software development teams can be much more efficient than hu-
036   man teams, making it a promosing approach for automated software development.
037
038   Despite the efficiency of LLMs in software development, the misalignment between LLM agents
039
      becomes a significant challenge. Actually, the misalignment is also very common in real-world
      software development. For example, the architect might ignore some requirements and draw an
040
      incomplete architecture diagram. Also the programmer might misunderstood some functionalities
041
      in the architecture diagram and produce erroneous codes. Figure 1 gives a concrete example to
042   illustrate the misalignment. Such misalignment is even worse in LLM-based development teams
043   due to the hallucination of LLMs. Moreover, as the number of agents increases, the misalignment
044   between agents will accumulate and finally lead to the failure of the whole project. Therefore, it is
045   critical to develop mechanisms to reduce such misalignment before applying LLM-based agents to
046   real-world complex software projects.
047
      In order to facilitate software development, people have developed many mature models through
048   decades of practice, including the Waterfall model, V-model and Agile model (Sundramoorthy &
049   Murugaiyan, 2012; Adetokunbo & Adenowo, 2013). These models rely heavily on the intelligence
050   and autonomy of humans, thus cannot directly apply to LLM-based agents. Recent works have
051   proposed several workflows for LLM-based agents following the Waterfall model (Adetokunbo &
052   Adenowo, 2013). For example, MetaGPT encodes Standardized Operating Procedures (SOPs) into
053   prompt sequences for a streamlined workflow. However, they only test code quality in the end of
      the whole procedures, without an effective self-correction mechanism during the development pro-

075
      a consequence, if any errors occurred during the development process, the whole project should start
076
      over. To overcome this issue, we propose a novel LLM-based multi-agent framework AltDev, which
077   aims to enhance the quality management of software development during the whole development
078   process. Following existing works (Adetokunbo & Adenowo, 2013; Hong et al., 2024), AltDev
079   employs five roles played by LLM-based agents, who work collaboratively to develop a certain
080   software. Specifically, given a description of requirements, the product manager outputs a detailed
081   and numerated requirement document. Then, the architect translates the requirement document to
082   an architect diagram. Hence, a project manager sets out a task plan for the programmer to execute.
083   Finally, a test engineer will test if the program meets the original requirements.
084   To address the misalignment during the development process, AltDev introduces an effective real-
085   time alignment mechanism, inspired by human development teams where people could start meet-
086   ings anytime to reach an agreement. Specifically, AltDev maintains a Shared Certified Repository
087   (SCR) to store the certified contents, including requirement documents, architecture diagrams, task
088   plans and codes. At the end of each phase in the workflow, the generated contents will go through an
089   alignment checking procedure before being added to the SCR. Whenever a misalignment is detected,
090   the agent responsible for the current phase would initialize a discussion to address the misalignment,
091
      and then regenerate contents based on the discussion. The above process will repeat until the gener-
      ated contents pass the alignment checking. Note that AltDev actually integrates the Waterfall model
092
      with a non-linear self-correction mechanism. By this way, AltDev is more robust to erroneous out-
093
      puts of LLMs and potentially leads to higher success rate of the project.
094
095   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
096   • We introduce a novel LLM-based multi-agent software development framework AltDev. Com-
097   pared with existing linear models, AltDev introduces a non-linear real-time alignment mechanism
098   for correcting erroneous outputs, thus can can solve more complex software development tasks.
099
      • We propose a Chain-of-Thought (CoT) inspired prompting method called Chain-of-Checking
100
      (CoC) for alignment checking at the end of each development phase. CoC efficiently guides dif-
101   ferent roles of LLM-based agents to check if the current deliverables align with previous ones.
102
103   • We present a set of standardized prompt sequences to implement AltDev. The library of prompt
104
      sequences is provided in Appendix A.2 for reproduction of AltDev.
105   • We test AltDev on over 200 real-world software development tasks from various domains. Ex-
106   perimental results show that AltDev largely improves the quality of generated codes in terms of
107   executability, structural and functional completeness. Moreover, under the framework of AltDev,
      the misalignment between agents can be significantly reduced compared with baselines.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
109

      2.1   C LASSIC S OFTWARE D EVELOPMENT M ODELS
111
112   Through decades of practice, people have developed many mature software development models
113   regarding to various scenarios. For example, the Waterfall model is a sequential and linear software
114   development process that breaks down the software development lifecycle into distinct, discrete
115   phases (Adetokunbo & Adenowo, 2013). The V-model is an evolution of the traditional Waterfall
116   model, which introduces parallelism and feedback loops between development and testing activi-
117
      ties (Sundramoorthy & Murugaiyan, 2012). The Agile model is a flexible and iterative approach
      that emphasizes rapid delivery of high-quality software through close collaboration and continuous
118
      improvement (Samar et al., 2020). However, these models only describe general workflows and
119
      principles, whose implementations depend heavily on the interpretation of human experts. There-
120   fore, these models cannot directly apply to LLM-based development teams.
121
122
      2.2   LLM S FOR C ODE G ENERATION
123
124   Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown surprising performance across a wide range of nat-
125   ural language processing tasks (Radford et al., 2019; Ouyang et al., 2022; Achiam et al., 2023;
126   Touvron et al., 2023). LLMs also significantly boost the performance of code generation tasks and
127   demonstrate a promising potential to be applied at scale. For example, Codex achieves a success
128   rate of 28.8% in solving a set of 164 hand-written programming problems (Chen et al., 2021a).
129   Copilot, a code generation tool powered by Codex, has captured the interest of over 1 million pro-
130
      fessional developers (Microsoft, 2023). Other models, including Incoder (Fried et al., 2023),
      CodeRL (Lea et al., 2022), Code Llama (Roziere et al., 2023) and ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2022),
131
      also achieve human-level performance in various code generation tasks. However, these works fo-
132
      cus only on code generation ability of single LLMs, ignoring the collaboration and communication
133   between multiple LLMs, which is crucial to complex software development tasks.
134
135
      2.3   M ULTI -AGENT F RAMEWORKS FOR S OFTWARE D EVELOPMENT
136
137   The key challenge in multi-agent software development is to efficiently coordinate with diverse roles
138   of agents while ensuring consistent understanding of the outcomes (Unterkalmsteiner et al., 2015;
139   Bjarnason et al., 2019). Several mutli-LLM software development frameworks have been proposed
140   to enhance collaboration between agents. For example, the Self-Collaboration framework assigns
141   different roles to LLMs and encourage them to finish sub-tasks collaboratively (Dong et al., 2024).
142   MetaGPT proposes the first concrete and standardized software development framework, where
143
      multiple LLM-based agents complete different tasks following a linear workflow (Hong et al.,
      2024). However, MetaGPT fails ensure the alignment of agents’ understandings about the current
144
      project, thus performs poor in complex projects. ChatDev introduces a set of assistant agents to
145
      verify if the generated codes are compliant with original requirements (Qian et al., 2023). However,
146   agents in ChatDev communicate through dialogues, thus they can only ensure the alignment between
147   requirements and codes. By contrast, the communication mechanism in our framework supports
148   multiple types of contents, including requirement documents, architecture diagrams, task plans,
149   codes and reviews, which enables AltDev to achieve alignment in the whole development process.

      3.1.1    S HARED CERTIFIED REPOSITORY
226
227   Our framework maintains a shared certified repository (SCR) to store intermediate deliverables, in-
228   cluding PRD, architecture diagrams, task plans and codes. Each generated content will go through
229   an alignment checking procedure before being added to the SCR, so that to ensure its correctness.
230   The SCR is used in two phases. First, in each content generation phase, agent can flexibly retrieve
231   useful contents from the SCR in order to complete her subtask. Second, during the alignment check-
232   ing phase, all agents will check the consistency and correctness of the newly generated content based
233   on the SCR. Actually, the SCR represents the agents’ common understanding of the task and ensures
234
      that the development process runs on the right way.
235
236   3.2     R EAL -T IME A LIGNMENT M ECHANISM
237
238   It is very common in real-world software development that team members have different under-
239   standings of the task, which might significantly impede the development process. Through decades
240
      of practices, people have developed many solutions to address the misalignment of understandings.
      For example, a quick group meeting can help team members to align their opinions when some
241
      problems arise. Such a real-time alignment mechanism in teamwork is key to software development
242
      since it effectively avoids the failure of the whole project.
243
244   In this work, we aim to integrate the Waterfall model with a carefully designed real-time alignment
245   mechanism to accelerate LLM-based multi-agent software development. Our real-time alignment
246
      mechanism consists of an alignment checking and a multi-agent discussion phases. The alignment
      checking phase is compulsorily inserted into the end of each generation phase in the main workflow.
247
      The multi-agent discussion phase is optional, conditioned on whether the generated contents pass
248
      the alignment checking. We elaborate the two phases in the following sections.
249
250
      3.2.1    A LIGNMENT C HECKING
251
252   The goal of alignment checking is to ensure that the newly generated contents correctly realize the
253   requirements and are consistent with the intermediate deliverables in the shared certified repository.
254   As is shown in Figure 2, our framework includes four alignment checking phases. In each alignment
255   checking phase, we call the agent responsible for the current generation phase initiator, and all
256   the previous agents supervisors. For example, in the third alignment checking phase, the Project
257   Manager serves as the initiator, the Product Manager and the Architect serve as supervisors. Note
258   that the user can optionally participate in the alignment checking, depending on the human resources.
259   During each alignment checking phase, the initiator sends the newly generated contents to all su-
260   pervisors. Then, each supervisor retrieves her outcomes from the SCR and checks if the newly gen-
261   erated contents are consistent with her previous outcomes. Note that the supervisors will look into
262   the newly generated contents from different perspectives. For example, in the alignment checking
263   initiated by the Programmer, the Architect who is responsible for generating architecture diagrams
264   would focus on whether the generated codes follow the structural relationships between classes.
265   However, the alignment checking procedure is non-trivial. If we directly feed the files to an LLM
266   and ask it to check the consistency, it is highly possible that the LLM just outputs a random result.
267   The potential challenges are two-fold. First, the files to be checked usually contain a large amount of
268   information, and the LLM might ignore some important details that determines the checking result.
269   Second, it is hard to develop a unified checking procedure for all of the four alignment checking
      phases, since different supervisors have different expertise and focuses.

      to guide the alignment checking in each of the four phases, inspired by the Chain-of-Thought

303
      dure into several important steps. As is shown in Figure 3, CoC consists of a primal chain that
304
      involves all the supervisors, and a subchain for each supervisor indicating several necessary check-
305   ing points. The supervisor will approve the current content only when all the checking points in the
306   subchain are satisfied. Overall, a concrete CoC prompt includes the contents to be checked, the con-
307   tents generated by the supervisor, the necessary checking points and a counter-example explaining
308   the misalignment. More details of the CoC prompts can be found at Appendix A.
309
      The alignment phase ends when all supervisors make decisions on whether to approve the current
310
      contents. If all the supervisors approve the newly generated contents, they will be added to the SCR.
311   Otherwise, a multi-agent discussion phase will be initiated to address the misalignments.

314   3.2.2   M ULTI -AGENT D ISCUSSION
315
      In human development teams, a quick discussion or meeting is perhaps the most efficient way to
316
      align with each other. Existing works have shown that discussion in LLM-based multi-agent com-
317
      munities also help to resolve conflicts and accelerate the teamwork Park et al. (2023); An et al.
318   (2024). We introduce an optional multi-agent discussion phase in our framework to efficiently re-
319   solve any misalignment identified by the supervisors. Note that in the above alignment checking
320   process, each supervisor checks the initiator’s content only from their own perspectives. Since there
321   might be conflicts in the supervisors’ reviews, the initiator would be confused when regenerating
322   her contents. Therefore, the multi-agent discussion phase helps to form a more clear feedback to the
323   initiator. Moreover, supervisors in the discussion could refer to others’ opinions and revise mistakes
      they may have made during the alignment checking phase.

      Specifically, after the alignment checking, supervisors who vote for misalignment will participate in
325   the multi-agent discussion phase (despite the cases where only one supervisor is involved). The su-
326   pervisors who vote for alignment do not participate the discussion for the sake of efficiency, but their
327   reviews are visible to the agents in the discussion phase. There are two loops in the multi-agent dis-
328   cussion phase, as is shown in Figure 2. In the inner loop, each agent analyzes other agents’ reviews
329   and modifies her own reviews, during with she could flexibly retrieve any necessary documents in
330   the SCR. In the outer loop, the initiator regenerates her contents based on the final reviews outputted
331   by the supervisors after discussion, until the regenerated contents pass the alignment checking or the
332   maximum number of iteration reached. We will show how to determine the maximum numbers of
333
      iterations of both inner and outer loop in the next section.

338
339   4.1.1    BASELINES
340
      As LLM-based software development is an emerging topic, we choose three open-sourced represen-
341
      tative works as our baselines. GPT-Engineer (Osika., 2023) is a fundamental single-agent approach
342
      in LLM-based software agents with a precise understanding of task requirements and the application
343   of one-step reasoning, which highlights its efficiency in generating detailed software solutions at the
344   repository level. MetaGPT (Hong et al., 2024) is an advanced framework that allocates specific
345   roles to various LLM-based software agents and incorporates Standardized Operating Procedures
346   to enable multi-agent participation. In each step, agents with specific roles generate solutions by
347   adhering to static instructions predefined by human experts. ChatDev (Qian et al., 2023) is also
348   a multi-agent software development framework that utilizes a chat chain and communicative de-
349   hallucination to guide specialized agents in collaboratively and autonomously developing software
350   through multi-turn dialogues.
351
352   4.1.2    DATASETS
353
      The Software Requirement Description Dataset (SRDD) introduced by Qian et al. (2023) repre-
354   sents a comprehensive corpus of textual software requirements, specifically curated to facilitate
355   agent-driven software development. This dataset includes 1,200 distinct software tasks, which are
356   categorized into five primary domains: Education, Work, Life, Game, and Creation. The dataset
357   incorporates software descriptions from major platforms such as Ubuntu, Google Play, Microsoft
358   Store, and Apple Store, providing a diverse and representative sample of software requirements
359   across various domains. However, we thoroughly reviewed the description of each software require-
360   ment and noticed several issues. First, some descriptions were incomplete, ending with ellipses,
361   while many others had similar or even identical functionalities. Second, some tasks are too hard for
362
      LLM-based software development at the current stage, such as developing a Monster Hunter game.
      We believe that these tasks are not suitable for evaluating the automatic software development frame-
363
      works. After careful selection, we curated a dataset consists of 214 complete and reasonable tasks.
364
365
      4.1.3    M ETRICS
366
367   Software testing is a complicated task by its nature. In real-world scenarios, people have developed
368   various approaches for software testing, including unit test Li & Yuan (2024) and GUI test Liu
369   et al. (2024). However, due to the lack of efficient testing tools for large-scale diverse software
370   tasks, existing works usually use simplified metrics to test the quality of generated software Qian
371   et al. (2023); Hong et al. (2024). Following existing works, we choose structural completeness and
372   executability as metrics to evaluate AltDev. In addition, we propose a new functional completeness
373   metric to evaluate how the developed software meets the functional requirements listed in the PRD.
374
      The three metrics are elaborated as follows.
375   • Structural Completeness (SC) is a statistical metric that measures the completeness of code struc-
376   tures. In practice, codes generated by LLMs are usually incomplete, where some important classes
377   and functions are replaced by placeholders (e.g., PASS, TODO). For each software development
      task, we flag it as structural complete if the generated codes do not contain any placeholders, and in-

      4.1.4    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
403
404   In practice, calculating accurate FC is hard because it requires a complete test of software function-
405   alities. An alternative approach is to use an LLM for functional test, as is introduced in Tuffveson
406   et al. (2024). In order to verify the LLM’s ability for functional test, we conduct an additional set
407   of experiments described as follows. First, we randomly select 25 tasks from the SRDD and gen-
408   erate the codes for them using ChatDev. The 25 tasks correspond to 232 functional requirements
409   in total. Then, we manually examine whether the requirements are accomplished by the generated
410   codes and save human verification results as ground-truth labels. Also, we use GPT-4o to verify if
411
      the requirements are accomplished and save the results as predicted labels. The experimental results
      show that the verification results of GPT-4o achieve an accuracy of 72.84% and a recall of 81.81%.
412
      This results support us to use GPT-4o to perform FC evaluation, in order to save human efforts.
413
414   In order to reduce human factors in the experimental results, we skip the steps in alignment checking
415   where human inputs are required. Although effective human feedback may help to improve the
416   performance of our methods, we believe that removing human factors leads to a fairer comparison.
417
      In addition, we set the maximum number of iterations as 1 for the inner loop, and 3 for the outer
      loop in the multi-agent discussion phase. We use GPT-4o with a temperature of 0.2. All baselines

      in the experiments share the same hyper-parameters and settings.

      5                                               C ONCLUSION
528
      In this paper, we introduce AltDev, a novel LLM-based multi-agent framework for software devel-
529
      opment. AltDev extends the classic Waterfall model by introducing an efficient real-time alignment
530
      mechanism. The real-time alignment mechanism includes a compulsory alignment checking phase
531   and an optional multi-agent discussion phase to address misalignment between agents. The ex-
532   perimental results show that AltDev outperforms other baselines, including single-agent approach
533   and multi-agent approaches, in terms of different metrics. Moreover, AltDev significantly performs
534   better in terms of functional completeness, which indicates that AltDev has a great potential in real-
535   world complex software development tasks.
