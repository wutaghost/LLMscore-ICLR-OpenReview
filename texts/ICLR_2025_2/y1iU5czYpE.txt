                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
012            For Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models, an unbalanced expert load will lead to
013            routing collapse or increased computational overhead. Existing methods com-
014            monly employ an auxiliary loss to encourage load balance, but a large auxiliary
015            loss will introduce non-negligible interference gradients into training and thus im-
016            pair the model performance. In order to control load balance while not producing
017            undesired gradients during training, we propose Loss-Free Balancing, a new load
018            balancing strategy that operates without auxiliary losses. To be specific, before the
019            top-K routing decision, Loss-Free Balancing will first apply an expert-wise bias to
020            the routing scores of each expert. By dynamically updating the bias of each expert
021            according to its recent load, Loss-Free Balancing can consistently maintain a bal-
022            anced distribution of expert load. In addition, since Loss-Free Balancing does not
023            produce any interference gradients, it also elevates the upper bound of model per-
024            formance gained from MoE training. We validate the performance of Loss-Free
025            Balancing on MoE models with up to 3B parameters trained on up to 200B tokens.
026            Experimental results show that Loss-Free Balancing achieves both better perfor-
027            mance and better load balance compared with traditional auxiliary-loss-controlled
028            load balancing strategies.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures have emerged as a promising solution for managing com-
034   putational costs when scaling up parameters in large language models (LLMs). Recent applica-
035   tions of MoE in Transformer-based models (Vaswani et al., 2017) have led to successful attempts at
036   scaling language models to substantial sizes (DeepSeek-AI et al., 2024a;b; Dai et al., 2024; Fedus
037   et al., 2022; Lepikhin et al., 2021), resulting in remarkable performance improvements. However,
038   training MoE models always face the circumstance of load imbalance, which may result in routing
039   collapse (Shazeer et al., 2017) or increased computational overhead (Fedus et al., 2022; Lepikhin
040   et al., 2021; Shazeer et al., 2017). In order to avoid imbalanced routing, existing methods (Fedus
041   et al., 2022; Lepikhin et al., 2021) commonly use an auxiliary loss to encourage balanced expert
042   load. Although the auxiliary loss can alleviate load imbalance during training, it also introduces
043   undesired gradients that conflict with the language modeling objective. These interference gradients
044   will impair the model performance, so existing MoE methods always need to consider the trade-off
045   between load balance and model performance.
046
047       In this paper, we propose Loss-Free Balancing, an auxiliary-loss-free load balancing strategy,
048   aiming at maintaining control over expert load balance while not introducing interference gradients.
049   Loss-Free Balancing features an iterative process of token routing and bias updating. As illustrated
050   in Figure 1, before the top-K routing decision of MoE, Loss-Free Balancing will first apply expert-
051   wise biases to the original routing scores to produce biased gating scores, which determine the actual
052   routing targets of each token during training. These expert-wise biases will keep updating according
053   to the expert load observed on recent training tokens, where the biases of heavy-load experts will be
      depressed and those of lite-load experts will be elevated. Through this dynamic updating strategy,

208   Comparison with Other Load Balancing Methods. In order to show the theoretical advantages
209   of Loss-Free Balancing, we compare it with other two mainstream load balancing methods, i.e.,
210   the auxiliary-loss-controlled method (Lepikhin et al., 2021; Fedus et al., 2022) and the Expert

413        5.1                    L OSS -F REE BALANCING I S C OMPATIBLE WITH E XPERT PARALLELISM
414
415        Extremely large-scale MoE models often employ expert parallelism (Lepikhin et al., 2021) for
416        training or inference, which distributes experts across different devices to reduce memory re-
417        quirements. In such scenarios, load balance on the data in a single computation step is crucial
418        for efficiency. Due to expert parallelism, each computation step involves micro_batch_size
419
           * ep_data_parallel_size samples, which we refer to as a computation batch. Here,
420        micro_batch_size denotes the number of samples processed in one gradient accumulation
421        step on a single device.
422
423            Loss-Free Balancing can achieve nearly optimal global load balance, and the load balance
424        in each computation step will get closer to the global load balance as the computation batch
425        size increases. In Figure 5, we examine the computation-batch-level load balance with the
426        MaxViocomputation-batch metric. The results show that the load balance of our Loss-Free Balanc-
427        ing always keeps improving as the computation batch size increases, but the load balance of the
428        auxiliary-loss-controlled method approximately maintains a constant level when the computation
429        batch is large. Since expert parallelism will significantly increase the computation batch size by
430        ep_data_parallel_size times, Loss-Free Balancing is naturally compatible with large-scale
431        MoE training, and its advantage on the load balance will be further enhanced as the size of expert
           parallelism increases.

452   5.2   L OAD BALANCING AND F UTURE T OKEN L EAKAGE
453
454   For casual language models, load balancing methods must adhere to the causal constraint of lan-
455   guage modeling to avoid future token leakage. While conventional auxiliary-controlled balancing
456   and our Loss-Free Balancing obey this constraint, Expert Choice (EC) (Zhou et al., 2022) violates
457   it. EC ensures perfect load balance by assigning exactly the same number of tokens to each expert.
458   However, this approach inherently leads to a severe issue of future token leakage.
459       In EC, future tokens can influence the expert assignment of previous tokens. Figure 6 illustrates
460   how information can be easily transmitted within a sequence via such influence. Theoretically, the
461   token assignment of an MoE layer with sparse ratio R (average activated experts per token K divided
462   by total expert number N ) can leak more than K log2 1−R   R bits per token (proof in Appendix D.1).
463   For a 9-layer MoE model with 16 experts and an average of 2 experts per token, this amounts to 50
464   bits, sufficient for each token to determine its successor’s identity.
465
466       We designed experiments to demonstrate the existence of future token leakage in realistic model
467   training. (1) We reduced the chunk size, within which top-K selection is performed, from 8192
468   tokens (4 sentences) to 512 (1/4 sentence), with the expectation of exposing such leakage. We ob-
469   served an abnormal loss drop (about 10%), confirming the presence of leakage. (2) We made leakage
470   more difficult by shuffling tokens across chunks in the top-K selection step, and observed that the
471   abnormal loss drop was mitigated. Detailed experimental results on EC’s information leakage are
472   provided in Appendix D.2.
473       Future token leakage is fatal since it destroys the generalization of a model and prevents reliable
474   evaluation of the model performance. Therefore, compared with EC, scaling up an MoE model with
475   our Loss-Free Balancing is safer.

478   6     C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this work, we introduced Loss-Free Balancing, a novel MoE load balance control method that
481   maintains a balanced distribution of expert load without relying on auxiliary losses. By dynamically
482   updating the bias of each expert based on its recent load, Loss-Free Balancing effectively addresses
483   the limitations of traditional auxiliary-loss-based approaches, which can introduce interference gra-
484   dients during training and potentially impair model performance. Experiments conducted on 1B and
485   3B MoE models, trained on 100B and 300B tokens respectively, demonstrate that Loss-Free Bal-
      ancing achieves better model performance and load balance than traditional auxiliary-loss training.
