011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated Learning (FL) systems are susceptible to adversarial attacks, where
014            malicious clients submit poisoned models to disrupt the convergence or plant
015
               backdoors that cause the global model to misclassify some samples. Current
               defense methods are often impractical for real-world FL systems, as they either
016
               rely on unrealistic prior knowledge or cause accuracy loss even in the absence
017
               of attacks. Furthermore, these methods lack a protocol for verifying execution,
018            leaving participants uncertain about the correct execution of the mechanism. To
019            address these challenges, we propose a novel anomaly detection strategy that is
020            designed for real-world FL systems. Our approach activates the defense only when
021            potential attacks are detected, and enables the removal of malicious models without
022            affecting the benign ones. Additionally, we incorporate zero-knowledge proofs
023            to ensure the integrity of the proposed defense mechanism. Experimental results
024            demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach in enhancing FL system security
025            against a comprehensive set of adversarial attacks in various ML tasks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Federated Learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017) enables clients to collaboratively train machine
030
      learning models without sharing their local data with other parties. Due to its privacy-preserving
031   nature, FL has attracted considerable attention across various domains in real-world applications (Hard
032   et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2019; Ramaswamy et al., 2019; Leroy et al., 2019; Byrd & Polychroniadou,
033   2020; Chowdhury et al., 2022). Even though FL does not require clients to share their raw data with
034   other parties, its collaborative nature inadvertently introduces privacy and security vulnerabilities (Cao
035   & Gong, 2022; Bhagoji et al., 2019; Lam et al., 2021; Jin et al., 2021; Tomsett et al., 2019; Chen
036   et al., 2017; Tolpegin et al., 2020a; Kariyappa et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022c). Malicious clients
037   can harm training by submitting corrupted model updates to disrupt global model convergence (Fang
038   et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2017), or by planting backdoors that cause the global model to perform
039
      poorly on certain data (Bagdasaryan et al., 2020b;a; Wang et al., 2020).
040   Existing literature on defenses in FL comes with certain inherent limitations, making them unsuitable
041   for real-world FL systems (Blanchard et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2019; Fung et al., 2020; Pillutla et al.,
042   2022; He et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2022; Karimireddy et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2019;
043   Ozdayi et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2021). Some strategies require prior knowledge of the number of
044   malicious clients within the FL system (Blanchard et al., 2017), while in practice adversaries would
045
      not announce their malicious intentions before attacking. Other defense strategies mitigate impacts
      of potential malicious client submissions by leveraging methods that inevitably alter the aggregation
046
      results, such as re-weighting the local models (Fung et al., 2020), modifying the aggregation func-
047
      tion (Pillutla et al., 2022), and removing local models that tend to be poisoned (Blanchard et al., 2017).
048   However, in practical FL systems, attacks happen infrequently. While introducing the aforementioned
049   defenses can mitigate the impact of potential malicious clients, the performance loss caused by the
050   inclusion of them can outweigh the defense gain, as most real-world training cases are benign and
051   these defenses largely compromise the model quality for all benign cases. Moreover, existing defense
052   mechanisms are deployed om FL servers without any verification for their execution. As a result,
053   clients are unable to verify whether the defense mechanism was executed accurately and correctly,
      leaving them reliant on server’s integrity and undermining trust in real-world FL systems.

079   This paper proposes a two-stage defense for anomaly detection that filters out malicious client
080   models in each FL training round with challenges in real-world FL systems addressed. On the first
081   stage, our approach detects potential existence of malicious clients in the current FL round based
082   on cross-round detection. The potential presence of malicious clients activates the second stage,
083   named cross-client detection that evaluates the degree of evilness of each local model and filters out
084   malicious ones based on the intuition of 3σ Rule (Pukelsheim, 1994). Our mechanism integrates a
085
      robust verification protocol that utilizes Zero-Knowledge Proof (ZKP) (Goldwasser et al., 1989) to
      guarantee integrity and honest execution of the proposed defensive mechanism on the FL server. We
086
      overview our mechanism in Figure 1. Then, we compare our approach with state-of-the-art ones,
087
      including Krum (Blanchard et al., 2017), RFA (Pillutla et al., 2022), Foolsgold (Fung et al., 2020),
088   NormClip (Sun et al., 2019), Bucketing (Karimireddy et al., 2020), Coordinate-Wise Median (Yin
089   et al., 2018), and Trimmed Mean (Yin et al., 2018) in Table 1. Our contributions are listed below:
090
091
      i) Real-world applicability. Our method is designed to meet practical requirements of defenses in
      real-world FL applications. As far as we know, we are the first to close the significant gap between
092
      theoretical research and its real-world applicability in FL security.
093
094   ii) Utility and practicability. Our method is free from any unrealistic prior information, nevertheless
095   it can still detect and eliminate the impact of malicious client models without harming the benign
096   ones. By this means, our method proves it applicability and effectiveness in real-world FL systems
097
      where attacks happen rather rarely.
098   iii) Conditional activation. We propose a two-stage detection method that first identifies suspi-
099   cious models and then, if necessary, triggers a double-check of the local models, thereby avoiding
100   unnecessary accuracy loss caused by introducing a defense mechanism.
101   iv) Accuracy preservation. Our method preserves accuracy in attack-free situations, which is
102   essential due to the infrequent occurrence of attacks in real-world scenarios.
103
104
      v) Identifiability. Our approach removes malicious local models with high accuracy without harming
      the benign models or modifying the aggregation function.
105
106   vi) Verifiability. To foster trust in FL systems, we leverage ZKPs, enabling clients to independently
107   verify the correct execution of the proposed defense mechanism on the server without relying solely
      on the server’s goodwill.

      2.1   A DVERSARY M ODEL
111
112   We consider an FL system in which at least 50% of the clients are benign. Some clients may be
113   adversarial and can conduct attacks to achieve malicious goals such as i) planting a backdoor so
114   that the global model misclassifies a specific set of samples while the overall model performance
115   is minimally impacted (backdoor attacks, e.g., (Bagdasaryan et al., 2020b; Wang et al., 2020)); ii)
116   altering local models to prevent the global model from converging (Byzantine attacks, e.g., (Chen
117   et al., 2017; Fang et al., 2020)); and iii) cheating the FL server by randomly submitting contrived
118
      models without actual training (free riders, e.g., (Wang, 2022)). We assume the FL server is not
      fully trusted due to the complex execution environment in real-world FL systems. We assume the FL
119
      clients know the server would conduct a defense but they are suspicious if the server has conducted
120
      the defense correctly, and they would like to verify the integrity of the defense without depending
121   solely on the server’s goodwill. We assume that the adversaries cannot conduct adaptive attacks, and
122   discuss the extension of our approach to adaptive attacks in §3.4.
123
124   2.2   P RELIMINARIES
125
126   Federated Learning (FL). FL (McMahan et al., 2017) enables training models across decentralized
127   devices without centralizing data. It is beneficial when dealing with sensitive data, as it allows data to
128
      remain on its original device during training.
129   Krum. Krum or m-Krum (Blanchard et al., 2017) selects one or m local models that deviate less
130   (evaluated using pairwise distances) from the majority for aggregation. See Appendix A.1 for details.
131
      3σ Rule. 3σ (Pukelsheim, 1994) is an empirical rule and has been utilized in anomaly detection
132   for data management (Han et al., 2019). It states that the percentages of values within one, two,
133   and three standard deviations of the mean are 68%, 95%, and 99.7%, respectively. This rule can be
134   widely applied on real-world applications, as normal distributions are consistent with real-world data
135   distributions (Lyon, 2014). Moreover, when data is not normally distributed, we can transform the
136   distribution to normal distribution (Aoki, 1950; Osborne, 2010; Sakia, 1992; Weisberg, 2001).
137
      Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZKPs). A ZKP (Goldwasser et al., 1989) is a proof system enabling a
138   prover to convince a verifier that a function has been correctly computed on the prover’s secret input
139   (witness). ZKPs have three properties: i) correctness: the proof they produce should pass verification
140   if the prover is honest (integrity property); ii) soundness: a cheating prover cannot convince the
141   verifier with overwhelming probability, and iii) zero-knowledge: the prover’s witness is not learned
142   by the verifier (privacy property).

      3     T WO -S TAGE A NOMALY D ETECTION M ECHANISM
145
146
      We propose a two-stage anomaly detection mechanism to identify and filter out malicious local
147   models on the server. This mechanism is executed at each FL round after the server collects local
148   models from the clients. The server first performs a cross-round check that leverages some cache,
149   which we call reference models, to assess the likelihood of the presence of any malicious clients.
150   Note that at this stage, the server does not remove any local models. If potentially malicious clients
151   are detected, the server subsequently conducts a cross-client detection to analyze each local model
152   and assess its degree of evilness. Based on this evaluation, the server identifies and excludes the
153   malicious models from aggregation.
154

158

159
      be malicious, thus have a higher likelihood to be benign.

          3.3    O PTIMIZATIONS FOR R EFERENCE M ODELS
261
          So far, the server stores the complete client models and the updated global model as reference models
262
          for the next FL round at the end of each FL round. However, this approach encounters pragmatic
263
          challenges in real-world deployments due to the following: i) Storage Constraints: real-world FL
264       systems often have complex execution environments and restricted storage, which necessitate the
265       algorithm to be optimized for storage and computation efficiency; ii) Computational Overhead: incor-
266       porating a ZKP for validation after each FL round (which will be discussed in §4) is computationally
267       intensive (Goldreich & Krawczyk, 1996). Utilizing the entire collection of client/global models for
268       computation increases resource consumption significantly and prolongs the verification time in each
269       FL round. Meanwhile, the FL system must await the completion of this verification process before
          continuing the subsequent operations, which detrimentally impacts the experience of the FL clients.

292   3.4         D ISCUSSIONS ON EXTENSIONS TO ADAPTIVE ATTACKS
293
294   In this work, we focus on non-adaptive adversaries, a common assumption in the literature (Ozdayi
295   et al., 2021; Guerraoui et al., 2018b; Pillutla et al., 2022; Karimireddy et al., 2020; Yin et al., 2018)
296   that enables us to create a baseline for anomaly detection and establish the robustness of our model
297   under basic adversarial settings. While adaptive adversaries present a more challenging scenario,
298   addressing them would introduce complexities that are beyond the scope of this initial work.
299   We acknowledge the importance of addressing adaptive attacks in FL systems. Below we discuss
300   extensions of our approach to adaptive attacks. In the presence of adaptive attackers, malicious clients
301   can craft their models based on their knowledge of the global model, their local model from the last
302   FL iteration, and the cosine similarity threshold used in our defense. By carefully modifying their
303   local models to ensure the cosine similarity falls within the threshold, malicious models may appear
304   benign and survive the detection. To solve this problem, our approach can be extended from the
305
      following two directions: 1) regularizing local models before measuring cosine similarity, such that
      the adversaries cannot craft their local models based on their known information; and 2) diversifying
306
      the layers used for cross-round detection instead of just relying on a single layer, thus it would be
307
      difficult for adversaries to predict and modify their local models accordingly.
308

310   4        V ERIFIABLE A NOMALY D ETECTION
311
312   Our method incorporates a verification module to enhance trust and privacy within the FL system.
313   Ideally, the verification module should have the following features: i) client-side verification: it
314
      should enable clients who may have concerns about the integrity of the FL server to verify the correct
      execution of the defense mechanism, without solely relying on the server’s goodwill; and ii) privacy
315
      protection: it should not necessitate the clients to access inappropriate knowledge, such as local
316
      models from other clients, thus preserving privacy and integrity in the FL system.
317
318   We incorporate ZKPs to ensure that the clients can verify the integrity of the defense without accessing
319   the other local models. We utilize zkSNARKs (Bitansky et al., 2012) that offer constant proof sizes
320
      and constant verification time regardless of the size of computation. Such property is crucial for
      applications where the verifier’s (i.e., an FL client) resources are limited, e.g., real-world FL systems.
321
      We design ZKP circuits as in Figure 3. Details of implementations are in Appendix §A.4.
322
323   ZKP for Anomaly Detection. Most of the computations in Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2 are linear
      and can be compiled into an arithmetic circuit easily, e.g., computing cosine similarity between

      two matrices of size n × n requires a circuit with O(n2 ) multiplication gates and one division.
325   While it is difficult to directly compute division on a circuit, it can be easily verified with the prover
326   providing the pre-computed quotient and remainder beforehand. Similar to Weng et al. (2021), we can
327   utilize Freivalds’ algorithm (Freivalds, 1977) to verify matrix multiplications. In general, the matrix
328   multiplication constitutes the basis of the verification schemes used for the proposed mechanism.
329   Naively verifying a matrix multiplication AB = C where A, B, C are of size n × n requires proving
330   the computation step by step, which requires O(n3 ) multiplication gates. With Freivalds’ algorithm,
331   the prover first computes the result off-circuit and commits to it. Then, the verifier generates a random
                                                 ?
332   vector v of length n, and checks A(Bv) = Cv. This approach reduces the size of the circuit to O(n2 ).
333   We exploit this idea√to design an efficient protocol for the square root computation in Algorithm 2.
334   To verify that x = y is computed correctly, we ask the prover to provide the answer x as witness
335   and then we check in the ZKP that x is indeed the square root of y. Note that we cannot check x2 is
336   equal to y because the zkSNARK works over a prime field and the square root of an input number
337   might not exist. So, we check if x2 is close to y by checking that x2 ≤ y and (x + 1)2 ≥ y. This
338
      approach reduces the computation of square root to 2 multiplications and 2 comparisons.
339   The zero-knowledge property of ZKPs allows public verification of prover’s (i.e., the FL server)
340   integrity in case of the server being untrustworthy. By incorporating ZKPs, we provide a public
341   verifiable approach for each client to ensure FL server’s integrity which is essential for building and
342   maintaining trust in FL systems. This ensures that clients can verify the correctness of the defense
343   without needing to rely solely on the server’s goodwill. This is also secure in case there exists

506   6    R ELATED W ORKS
507
508   Detection of attacks. Zhang et al. (2022b) employs k-means to partition local models into clusters that
509   correspond to “benign” or “malicious”. While this approach can efficiently detect attacks, it requires
510   some pre-training rounds and relies much on historical client models, thus might not be as effective
511   when there is limited information on past client models. For example, their implementation (Zhang
512   et al., 2022a) sets the starting round to detect attacks to different training rounds, e.g., 50 when the
513   datasets are MNIST and FEMNIST, and 20 when the dataset is CIFAR10. While this approach is
514   novel, it is not suitable for real FL systems, as attacks may happen in earlier rounds as well.
515   Defense mechanisms in FL. Robust learning and the mitigation of adversarial behaviors in FL has
516   been extensively explored (Blanchard et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2019; Fung et al., 2020; Pillutla et al.,
517   2022; He et al., 2022; Karimireddy et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2019; Ozdayi et al., 2021;
518   Sun et al., 2021; Yin et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2017; Guerraoui et al., 2018a; Xie et al., 2020; Li et al.,
519   2020; Cao et al., 2020). Some approaches keep several local models that are more likely to be benign
520   in each FL round, e.g., (Blanchard et al., 2017; Guerraoui et al., 2018a; Yin et al., 2018), and (Xie
521   et al., 2020), instead of aggregating all client submissions. Such approaches are effective, but they
522
      keep fewer local models than the real number of benign local models to ensure that all malicious local
      models are filtered out, causing misrepresentation of some benign local models in the aggregation.
523
      This completely wastes the computation resources of the benign clients that are incorrectly removed
524
      and thus, changes the aggregation results. Some approaches re-weight or modify local models to
525   mitigate the impacts of potential malicious submissions (Fung et al., 2020; Karimireddy et al., 2020;
526   Sun et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2019; Ozdayi et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2021), while other approaches alter
527   the aggregation function or directly modify the aggregation results (Pillutla et al., 2022; Karimireddy
528   et al., 2020; Yin et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2017). While these defense mechanisms can be effective
529   against attacks, they might inadvertently degrade the quality of outcomes due to the unintentional
530   alteration of aggregation results even when no attacks are present. This is especially problematic
531   given the low frequency of attacks in practical scenarios.
532

534   7    C ONCLUSIONS
535
536   We present a novel anomaly detection approach specifically designed for real-world FL systems. Our
537   approach utilizes an early cross-round check that activates subsequent anomaly detection exclusively
538   in the presence of attacks. When attacks happen, our approach removes malicious client models effi-
539   ciently, ensuring that the local models submitted by benign clients remain unaffected. By leveraging
      ZKPs, our approach enables clients to verify the integrity of the removal performed by the server.

778        A.3     E XTENSION TO C LIENT S AMPLING
779
           Our method can work in the case of client sampling. For ease of explanation, in the main manuscript,
780
           we assumed that all clients participate in aggregation in every FL iteration. However, with some
781
           engineering efforts, we can easily extend the method to handle client selection. To handle scenarios
782        with client selection, we can cache historical client models for the same clients across rounds, such
783        that the server can perform cross-round detection even when clients do not participate in every round.
784        If the cached model for a client is too old, we can use the global model from the last round as the
785        reference model. A scenario with adversary clients that participate only once (i.e., single-shot attacks)
786        constitutes a specific case of the client selection challenge described above. In such cases, we can use
787        the global model from the last round as the reference model for cross-round detection.

789        A.4     Z ERO -K NOWLEDGE P ROOF (ZKP) I MPLEMENTATION
790
791
           This section describes the details of the implementation of ZKPs. In what follows, the prover is the
           FL server, whereas the verifiers are the FL clients.
792
793
           A.4.1    C HOICE OF THE ZKP SYSTEM
794
795        In our implementation, we use the Groth16 (Groth, 2016) zkSNARK scheme implemented in the
796        Circom library (Contributors, 2022) for all the computations described earlier. We choose this ZKP
797        scheme because its construction ensures constant proof size (128 bytes) and constant verification
798        time. Because of this, Groth16 is popular for blockchain applications as it necessitates little on-chain
799        computation. There are other ZKP schemes based on different constructions that can achieve faster
800        prover time (Liu et al., 2021), but their proof size is bigger and verification time is not constant,
801        which is a problem if the verifier lacks computational power, as in our case since the verifiers are the
802
           FL clients in our setting. The construction of a ZKP scheme that is efficient for both the prover and
           verifier is still an open research direction.
803
804
           A.4.2    ZKP- COMPATIBLE L ANGUAGE
805
806        The first challenge of applying ZKP protocols is to convert the computations into a ZKP-compatible
807        language. ZKP protocols model computations as arithmetic circuits with addition and multiplication
808        gates over a prime field. However, our computations for our approach are over real numbers. The
809        second challenge is that some computations such as square root are nonlinear, making it difficult to
           wire them as a circuit. To address these issues, we implement a class of operations that map real


                                                              15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      numbers to fixed-point numbers. To build our ZKP scheme, we use Circom library (Contributors,
811   2022), which compiles the description of an arithmetic circuit in a front-end language similar to C++
812   to the back-end ZKP protocol.
813
814   A.4.3    I NTERACTIVITY OF ZK SNARK S
815
816   In the Freivalds’ algorithm (Freivalds, 1977), the prover first computes the matrix multiplication and
817   commits to its result. Then the verifier generates and sends the random vector. This step is interactive
818
      in nature, but we can make this non-interactive using the Fiat-Shamir heuristic as it is public-coin,
      meaning the vector is randomly selected by the verifier and made public to everyone. Therefore, the
819
      prover can instead generate this vector by setting it to the hash of matrices A,B and C. With this, our
820
      entire ZKP pipeline, including the Freivalds’ step can become truly non-interactive.
821
822   A.4.4    M OTIVATION OF I MPLEMENTING ZKP
823
824   ZKP enables proving to the clients that the server has correctly executed the anomaly detection
825   process. This addresses a critical concern in FL systems, where clients cannot directly verify the
826   server’s behavior and must fully trust the server. Below, we explain the motivation for ZK from
827   research, industry product, and system perspectives.
828   Research Perspective: Existing literature has considered various adversarial models. For example,
829   1) clients might be malicious and submit modified models; 2) FL server might be curious about local
830   models and want to infer sensitive information, such as original training data, or the local models; 3)
831   clients might be curious about local models of other clients; 4) an external adversary may hack the
832   communication channels between clients and the server and poison some client models; 5) the FL
833   server may be hacked by external adversaries; 6) a global “sybil” may hack the whole system and
834
      control some clients by modifying their local training data, and so on.
835   In our paper, we assume the FL server is not fully trusted due to the complex execution environment
836   in real systems. There may be external adversaries or a global sybil, thus, even if the server hopes
837   to execute the aggregation correctly, the presence of adversaries necessitates a ZKP module for
838   verification to ensure that the server’s actions are transparent and trustworthy to all clients.
839   Industry Perspective: The necessity of ZKP also arises from real-world application needs. Consider,
840   for example, FL clients that are medical institutions or hospitals holding sensitive data, such as patient
841   medical records. These institutions may want to collaboratively train a model but be unwilling to
842   share their raw data due to privacy concerns. Although these institutions know that the server will
843   run an anomaly detection procedure, they may not be fully convinced that the server will honestly
844   execute the procedure or that their models will participate in the aggregation without bias. Here,
845   ZKP enables verification that the anomaly detection is performed correctly, even when the clients
846
      do not have access to the local models of other clients. This is critical for gaining the trust of the
      participating clients.
847
848   System Perspective: Real FL systems with rewards contain components such as model aggregation,
849   contribution assessment of local models, and anomaly detection, etc. If the FL server is not fully
850   trusted, validating all these operations is essential. However, the focus of our paper is specifically on
851   anomaly detection, and therefore, we have primarily discussed the application of ZKP in this context.
852
      The ZKP module ensures that even if the server is not fully trusted, e.g., under potential external
      threats, clients can have verifiable proof that the anomaly detection has been executed correctly, thus
853
      maintaining the integrity and security of the whole FL process.
