009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have shown promising advancements
012            in general visual and language understanding. However, the representation of
013            multimodal information using MLLMs remains largely unexplored. In this work,
014            we introduce a new framework, E5-V, designed to adapt MLLMs for achieving
015            universal multimodal embeddings. Our findings highlight the significant potential
016
               of MLLMs in representing multimodal inputs compared to previous approaches.
               By leveraging MLLMs with prompts, E5-V effectively bridges the modality gap
017
               between different types of inputs, demonstrating strong performance in multimodal
018
               embeddings even without fine-tuning. We propose a single modality training ap-
019            proach for E5-V, where the model is trained exclusively on text pairs. This method
020            demonstrates significant improvements over traditional multimodal training on
021            image-text pairs, while reducing training costs by approximately 95%. Addition-
022            ally, it eliminates the need for costly multimodal training data collection. Extensive
023            experiments across four types of tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of E5-V. As a
024            universal multimodal model, E5-V not only achieves but often surpasses state-of-
025            the-art performance in each task, despite being trained on a single modality.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      With the development of MLLMs, there is an increasing need for embedding models to represent
030   multimodal inputs. Although CLIP Radford et al. (2021) shows impressive results in text-image
031   retrieval by aligning visual and language representations with contrastive learning, it struggles to
032   represent interleaved visual and language inputs. Moreover, the text encoder of CLIP demonstrates a
033   low capacity for understanding complicated text Zhang et al. (2024). To achieve universal multimodal
034   representation, some works Wei et al. (2023); Zhou et al. (2024) continue to train CLIP on interleaved
035   image-text data, while collecting such data can be challenging and may require GPT-4 to synthesize
036   data Zhou et al. (2024) or manualy annotated.
037   Recent works demonstrate Wang et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2023) that scaling up the size of text
038   embedding models leads to better performance. However, replicating this scaling approach for
039   universal multimodal embeddings poses significant challenges and expenses, which arises from the
040   unstable for scaling CLIP and the complexity of collecting extensive multimodal datasets Sun et al.
041   (2023). Nevertheless, previous works like adapting CLIP to universal multimodal embeddings still
042   has shortcomings, such as poor language understanding, limited real-world knowledge, and shallow
043   fusion of visual and linguistic information.
044   In this work, we introduce a new framework, called E5-V, to directly adapt MLLMs instead of
045   CLIP like models for achieving universal multimodal embeddings. There are several advantages to
046   representing multimodal information with MLLMs: First, benefiting from interleaved visual and
047   language training, MLLMs can initially learn to represent multimodal information according to
048   their meanings with prompt. Second, MLLMs are capable of representing interleaved visual and
049   language inputs to handle tasks like composed image retrieval. Third, MLLMs have stronger language
050
      understanding and reasoning capabilities compared to CLIP.
051   However, since MLLMs are not initially trained with contrastive learning to represent inputs as
052   embeddings, it can be challenging for them to represent multimodal inputs as well as CLIP, which
053   performs contrastive learning on large-scale text-image pairs. In this work, we propose a prompt-
      based representation method to adapt MLLMs for multimodal embeddings inspired by Jiang et al.

105   2.1   M ULTIMODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
106
107   With the success of LLMs, there is a trend to extend LLMs to handle multimodal information,
      called MLLMs. MLLMs, such as BLIP Li et al. (2023), KOSMOS Huang et al. (2024), LLaMA-


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Adapter Gao et al. (2023), and LLaVA Liu et al. (2024c;b;a), show promising progress in multimodal
109   information understanding and reasoning. To achieve this, a typical MLLM is composed of an LLM,
110   a modality encoder, and a projector to connect them. The modality encoder projects raw multimodal
111   inputs into vectors to connect with LLMs Yin et al. (2023).
112
      One efficient method Gao et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024c) is to directly use a pretrained LLM and a
113
      pretrained modality encoder, such as CLIP Radford et al. (2021). To achieve this, LLaVA uses two
114
      training stages. The first stage aligns the text and image with image-text pairs by only training the
115   projector between LLM and modality encoder, and the second stage fine-tunes the model on a visual
116   instruction dataset, which ensure it can follow complex instructions like LLM, such as represent the
117   multimodal inputs in our work.
118
      While the impressive performance of MLLMs in understanding multimodal information and in-
119
      struction following, the representation of multimodal information using MLLMs remains largely
120
      unexplored. Although recent studies Wang et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2023) have shown advancements
121   in embedding texts with LLMs and scaling up has demonstrated improved performance in text repre-
122   sentation, a significant challenge persists: the batch sizes requires to train multimodal embeddings
123   models such as CLIP is signficantly larger than text embeddings models. For example, CLIP requires
124   a batch size of 32k samples with contrastive learning, while the text embedding models such as
125   E5 Wang et al. (2023) only requires a batch size of 2k samples. Limited by the size of MLLMs, it can
126   be very challenging to use the similar batch size like CLIP to train robust multimodal embeddings
127   models.
128
129   2.2   M ULTIMODAL E MBEDDINGS
130
131   CLIP Radford et al. (2021), as a pioneering work on multimodal embeddings, has been widely used in
132   subsequent works. CLIP uses separate encoders for image and text by aligning them with contrastive
133   learning on large-scale image-text pairs. Despite its strong performance in text-image retrieval, CLIP
134   has several limitations due to its internal framework. The text encoder of CLIP has a low capacity for
135   understanding complicated text because it is pretrained on short image captions, which also limits
136
      CLIP’s performance on long text retrieval Zhang et al. (2024). Additionally, due to the use of separate
      encoders, CLIP struggles to represent interleaved visual and language inputs, such as in composed
137
      image retrieval Liu et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2021).
138
139   To achieve universal multimodal embeddings, several works, such as UNIIR Wei et al. (2023),
140   fine-tune CLIP with a fusion model to integrate visual and language information. Other works, like
141   VISTA Zhou et al. (2024) or UniVL-DR Liu et al. (2022), feed the text embedding models with CLIP
142
      outputs to incorporate visual information. However, this approach can harm the original text-image
      retrieval performance of CLIP and makes it difficult for the text embedding models to understand
143
      visual information using only contrastive learning. As a result, these methods show poor zero-shot
144
      performance on composed image retrieval tasks. Moreover, these methods require large interleaved
145   training data to achieve universal multimodal embeddings. Collecting such high-quality interleaved
146   pairs for performing contrastive learning is more challenging than gathering image-text pairs or text
147   pairs. This process can require complex annotation and sometimes even synthesizing data from
148   GPT-4 Zhou et al. (2024).

227   4     E XPERIMENTS
228
229   We evaluate E5-V on four tasks: text-image retrieval, composed image retrieval, sentence embeddings,
230   and image-image retrieval to demonstrate the effectiveness of E5-V in representing multimodal
231   information. All tasks are evaluated in a zero-shot setting with the same model without additional
232   fine-tuning on specific datasets.
233   For the backbone of E5-V, we use LLaVA-NeXT-8B Li et al. (2024), which builds on LLaMA-3
234   8B Gao et al. (2023), with a frozen CLIP ViT-L as the visual encoder. For the training data, we
235   use NLI sentence pairs from Gao et al. (2021), with around 273k sentence pairs. We fine-tune the
236   LLM of LLaVA-NeXT-8B with 1000 steps and 768 batch size. To save the GPU memory, we use
237   QLoRA Dettmers et al. (2024) and gradient checkpointing with DeepSpeed ZeRO-2. For the prompts
238   in training, we use <text> \n Summary above sentence in one word:, where <text> is the placeholder
239
      for the input sentence, and use the last token embeddings to represent the embeddings for contrastive
      learning. We also report the performance with other MLLMs in Appendix C.

290
291   To understand the effectiveness of E5-V in representing interleaved visual and language inputs, we
292   evaluate it on composed image retrieval tasks with two popular datasets: FashionIQ Wu et al. (2021)
293   and CIRR Liu et al. (2021). This task focuses on retrieving images based on interleaved inputs,
294
      which requires the model to retrieve target images based on modified reference images, where the
      modification is described in the text. For FashionIQ, it contains three subtypes of fashion products:
295
      Dress, Shirt and Toptee. Given a picture of a fashion product and a modification corresponding to the
296
      style, the model needs to retrieve the target image that matches the modification. For CIRR, it extend
297   FashionIQ on real-life images, which has more diverse images and modifications.
298
299   We compare E5-V with several zero-shot image-composed baselines: Pic2Word Saito et al. (2023),
300
      Context-I2W Tang et al. (2024), LinCIR Gu et al. (2024), the LLM-based method CIReVL Karthik
      et al. (2023), and the current state-of-the-art method iSEARLE-XL Agnolucci et al. (2024). For a
301
      fair comparison, we report the results of all baseline models using the large visual encoder CLIP
302
      ViT-L, as in E5-V. Note that the E5-V also freezes visual encoder same as other baselines. These
303   baselines are designed exclusively for zero-shot composed image retrieval tasks and can not apply to
304   other tasks. Most of the baselines are not end-to-end embedding interleaved inputs, which introduce
305   complex pipelines like textual inversion. For example, CIReVL requires captioning an image first,
306   generating the target image caption based on LLMs, and then retrieving the target image based on
307   the caption. However, E5-V can directly represent the interleaved visual and language inputs with
308   prompts without any textual inversion.
309   To represent the interleaved inputs for E5-V, we use the following prompts for FashionIQ and CIRR.
310   For FashionIQ, which requires the model to mainly represent the style of the fashion product, we can
311   directly let E5-V represent the style of the corresponding fashion products. Since the evaluation of
312   FashionIQ is split into three subtypes, including Dress, Shirt, and Toptee, we can also provide the
313   subtype information in the prompts. For CIRR, we can directly let E5-V modify the image based on
314   the modification described in the text and then represent the modified image in one word. Although
315   these prompts are unseen during training and have a complex format, E5-V can still correctly represent
316   the interleaved inputs, even in specific domains like fashion products.

448
      We also compare single modality training with multimodal training. For multimodal training, we
449
      train the MLLM on 558K text-image pairs from CC3M using the same training settings and prompts
450   as single modality training. We report the performance of single modality training and multimodal
451   training on different tasks in Table 7. We find that MLLM achieves better multimodal embeddings
452   with single modality training. Even on the image-text retrieval tasks, where multimodal training uses
453   similar training data, single modality training still shows better performance. For other tasks, we
454   notice that multimodal training cannot represent the interleaved inputs in FashionIQ and CIRR, or
455   text inputs in STS well, which leads to a performance drop compared to single modality training.
456   Moreover, single modality training is more efficient by removing the visual encoder and only uses 32
457   max tokens for text inputs, significantly reducing the training time compared to multimodal training.

      5.3       Z ERO - SHOT I NSTRUCTION F OLLOWING A BILITY ON M ULTIMODAL E MBEDDINGS
469
470   We find an interesting ability of E5-V to represent inputs based on fully zero-shot instructions.
471   Although E5-V is trained on text inputs with the static prompt, it can correctly represent visual
472   and interleaved inputs based on unseen prompts. These prompts can be more detailed and specific
473   based on the tasks. For example, in FashionIQ, a specific domain dataset about fashion products,
474   we can design specific prompts to let E5-V embed the image based on their styles. Moreover, the
475   interaction between visual and language inputs in E5-V can also be more detailed, such as change
476
      the style of this shirt to. Compared to other methods, which simply fuse the visual
      and language inputs, E5-V provides a more nuanced and specific approach.
477
478
479   6       C ONCLUSION
480
481   In this work, we propose E5-V, a MLLM based universal multimodal model that can represent
482   interleaved visual and language inputs accurately. E5-V uses the prompt based representation method
483   to unify multimodal representations into the same embedding space without additional fine-tuning or
484   tricks. With single modality training, E5-V achieves strong performance on various tasks, including
485   text-image retrieval, composed image retrieval, image-image retrieval, and sentence embeddings. We
      also conduct extensive ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of our method.
