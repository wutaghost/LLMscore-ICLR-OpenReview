011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The Steiner Tree Problem (STP) is an NP-hard combinatorial optimization problem
014            with applications in areas like network design and facility location. Despite its
015            importance, learning-based solvers for STP have been hindered by the lack of large-
016            scale, diverse datasets necessary to train and evaluate advanced neural models. To
017
               address this limitation, we introduce a standardized dataset comprising over a mil-
               lion high-quality instances with optimal solutions, spanning various problem sizes
018
               and graph structures. Our dataset enables benchmarking of neural combinatorial op-
019
               timization methods across both supervised and reinforcement learning paradigms,
020            encompassing autoregressive and non-autoregressive inference approaches. Our
021            experiments show that supervised learning excels in in-distribution settings, while
022            reinforcement learning generalizes better to unseen problem sizes, highlighting a
023            trade-off between solution quality and generalization. We compare NCO methods
024            across different STP scales and graph types, and demonstrate that solvers trained
025            on our datasets generalize well to real-world instances without fine-tuning, proving
026            its practical utility. We hope this benchmark promotes further STP research and
027            advances NCO techniques for broader combinatorial optimization challenges.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The Steiner Tree Problem (STP) is an NP-hard combinatorial optimization challenge focused on find-
033
      ing the minimum-cost tree spanning a set of nodes (terminals) in a weighted graph. Solving the STP
      is essential for optimizing connections between multiple objects or locations while minimizing costs
034
      or resources. It has diverse applications, including circuit (MacGregor Smith and LIEBMAN, 1979)
035
      and network design (Gouveia and Magnanti, 2003), facility location (Ljubić, 2007), phylogenetics
036   (Lu et al., 2003), and image processing (Russakovsky and Ng, 2010). The STP has many variant
037   extensions with unique characteristics such as the number of nodes, edge weight distribution, graph
038   structure, and other specific constraints.
039
      Due to its significance, there are many algorithms for the STP, which can be broadly categorized into
040
      two types: classical rule-based heuristics (Esbensen, 1995) and those solved using Mixed Integer
041
      Linear Programming (MILP) (Gamrath et al., 2017). Rule-based heuristics tend to be specialized for
042   specific scenarios, and designing such heuristics is highly non-trivial. Exact MILP solvers are more
043   versatile but generally suffer from poor scalability. These challenges of the classical algorithms are not
044   unique to STP but are common in general combinatorial optimization (CO) domains. To address these
045   challenges, recent research has focused on Neural Combinatorial Optimization (NCO), leveraging
046   neural networks to enhance solution methods. However, NCO research has mainly concentrated on a
047   few CO tasks, such as the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP), Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem
048   (CVRP), and Maximum Independent Set (MIS).
049   Learning-based solvers for combinatorial optimization problems can generally be classified by their
050   solution generation approach and learning paradigm. Constructive solvers generate a single solution in
051   one pass, while improvement solvers iteratively refine solutions through local search techniques (Chen
052   and Tian, 2019; Li et al., 2021; d O Costa et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021; Hou et al., 2022). Given the
053   importance of generating solutions efficiently with minimal prior knowledge, our benchmark focuses
      on constructive solvers. These methods have shown promise for various CO problems, efficiently


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      generating solutions in a single forward pass and minimizing the need for extensive human expertise.
055   However, research for the STP has been limited due to the lack of large-scale, high-quality datasets
056   required for training sophisticated models. Existing benchmarks, such as SteinLib (Koch et al., 2001),
057   provide only a few dozen instances per scenario, which are insufficient for advancing state-of-the-art
058   machine learning methods. In contrast, problems like the vehicle routing problems have benefited
059   from vast, well-organized datasets, enabling more effective training and evaluation.
060
      To address the limitations in STP research due to the lack of large-scale datasets, we introduce
061   Steiner Tree Problem Benchmark (SteBen), a standardized dataset containing over a million STP
062   instances with exact solutions across various scenarios. Our dataset covers a wide range of graph
063   types, including variations in terminal node counts, data sizes, and edge distributions, making
064   it compatible with existing STP libraries. Our dataset supports the evaluation of NCO methods
065   under both supervised and reinforcement learning frameworks, covering both autoregressive and
066   non-autoregressive methods. To facilitate this, we provide a dataset for supervised learning and a
067   specialized reinforcement learning environment tailored for STP, where agents can interact and learn
068   within the same problem scenarios.
069   Using SteBen, we conducted a comprehensive comparison of NCO methods and classical algorithms,
070   focusing on four distinct NCO groups: (1) autoregressive supervised learning, (2) non-autoregressive
071   supervised learning, (3) autoregressive reinforcement learning, and (4) non-autoregressive reinforce-
072   ment learning. Since there has been limited research on learning-based solvers specifically for the STP,
073   we aimed to evaluate broad methodological categories rather than specific state-of-the-art (SOTA)
074   solvers, to reveal which approaches show the most promise for solving the STP.
075   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
076
077           • We provide a benchmark dataset and implemented baselines for STP to evaluate a compre-
078             hensive range of NCO methods.
079           • We compare NCO methods, highlighting their strengths and weaknesses on the STP across
080             different scales and graph types.
081
              • NCO solvers trained on SteBen generalize well to real-world instances without additional
082             fine-tuning, demonstrating its practical utility.
083
084   We hope SteBen will drive further research into NCO methods for STP and foster the development
085   of robust, generalizable techniques for broader combinatorial optimization challenges. By offering a
086   comprehensive benchmark, SteBen aims to be a key resource for the CO community.

      2.1     C LASSIFICATION OF C ONSTRUCTIVE S OLVERS
091
092   Machine-learning based constructive solvers can be divided into autoregressive, which incrementally
093   extend partial solutions, and non-autoregressive, which generate solutions in a single step. They
094   can also be classified by their learning approach: supervised learning, which uses labeled data, and
095   reinforcement learning, which explores the solution space without labels.
096
097   2.1.1    AUTOREGRESSIVE S OLVERS
098
099
      Autoregressive supervised learning methods predict the next sequence value based on the current
      one. Pointer Networks (Meire et al., 2015) introduced this idea, predicting sequences step-by-step.
100
      Later improvements include dividing problems into subproblems (Nowak et al., 2018) and using a
101
      variational autoencoder to solve them in a compressed latent space (Hottung et al., 2020). Recently,
102   Drakulic et al. (2024) applied imitation learning techniques for enhanced performance on supervised
103   datasets. Autoregressive reinforcement learning methods also extend partial solutions iteratively.
104   This approach, which efficiently handles large combinatorial action spaces, is widely adopted in
105   RL-based solvers. Bello et al. (2016) applied reinforcement learning to Pointer Networks, while
106   graph embedding networks (Khalil et al., 2017) and attention modules (Kool et al., 2018) further
107   refined this idea, leading to improved methods like POMO (Kwon et al., 2020) and Sym-NCO (Kim
      et al., 2022).

      2.1.2    N ON - AUTOREGRESSIVE S OLVERS
109
110   Non-autoregressive methods generate solutions in a single step, avoiding the error accumulation seen
111   in autoregressive methods. Several studies have taken a traditional supervised learning approach
112   (Li et al., 2018; Joshi et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2021; Geisler et al., 2021), while advanced generative
113   models like VAEs (Hottung et al., 2020), GANs (Cheng et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022), and GFlowNets
114   (Zhang et al., 2023) have gained traction. DIFUSCO (Sun and Yang, 2023), a GNN-based diffusion
115
      model, has shown promising results, further enhanced by cost-guided search (Li et al., 2024). Non-
      autoregressive reinforcement learning is less common due to the complexity of large action spaces,
116
      though Qiu et al. (2022) introduced a scalable approach using parameterization and the REINFORCE
117
      algorithm.
118
119
120   2.2     S TEINER T REE P ROBLEM S OLVERS
121
      As mentioned in Section 1, the STP has been relatively less explored within the NCO domain.
122
      (Yan et al., 2021) utilized the Double Deep Q Network (DDQN) approach to tackle the STP in an
123
      end-to-end manner. (Zhang and Ajwani, 2022) introduced an algorithm for the STP that generates
124   near-optimal solutions by relaxing the constraints for the Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP)
125   of the STP. (Ko et al., 2023) addressed the Steiner Tree Packing Problem (STPP), which is an extended
126   version of the STP where multiple Steiner tree problems are grouped together. Additionally, several
127   works have focused on a variant of the STP called the Euclidean Steiner Tree Problem, in which the
128   points are scattered on the Euclidean space, and the solution ensures that no two edges intersect (Ras
129   et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2022; Hsu et al., 2022; Brazil et al., 2024).
130
131   2.3     S TEINER T REE P ROBLEM DATASETS
132
133   (Koch et al., 2001) generated a STP benchmark dataset. Their dataset consists of various STP instances
134   with different levels of difficulty and their corresponding solutions. On the other hand, there was a
135   challenge for solving STP on undirected edge-weighted graphs (Bonnet and Sikora, 2018). However,
136   the numbers of instances in both datasets are limited to a few dozens, which is insufficient to be used
137
      to train a sophisticated neural network. There are also datasets for variant versions of STPs. Pedersen
      et al. (2024) published a dataset for the Quato Steiner tree problem, which additionally considers
138
      edge capacities when finding the Steiner tree. Lee et al. (2022) published Respack dataset for the
139
      STPP.

144   3.1     L EARNING -BASED C OMBINATORIAL O PTIMIZATION S OLVERS
145
146   Let Fs be the set of feasible solutions for a combinatorial optimization problem (COP) instance
147   s ∈ S, where S is the space of all instances associated with a cost function cs : Fs → R. The
148   objective is to find the optimal solution fs⋆ that minimizes the cost for a given instance s:
149
                                              fs⋆ = arg min cs (f ).                                     (1)
150                                                   f ∈Fs
151
152   A learning-based combinatorial optimization solver is a parameterized algorithm fˆθ that aims to
153   approximate the optimal solution for any given instance s. The goal is to learn parameters θ ∈ Θ
154   such that fˆθ (s) ≈ fs⋆ for all s ∈ S.
155
      Learning-based solvers are categorized according to their learning paradigm, with detailed explana-
156   tions of each approach provided in Appendix B. To tackle the challenges in complex combinatorial
157   optimization problems, it is essential to have access to high-quality datasets and environments that
158   capture the diverse and intricate nature of these instances. A standardized, large-scale dataset with
159   exact solutions enables the training and evaluation of advanced neural models under both supervised
160   and reinforcement learning paradigms. Additionally, providing specialized environments for rein-
161   forcement learning facilitates the development of algorithms capable of handling the intricacies of
      such problems.

      3.2   T HE S TEINER T REE P ROBLEM
163
164   An instance of the Steiner Tree Problem (STP) can be defined within this framework. The STP
165   instance is given as an undirected graph s = (V, E), where V is the set of vertices and E is the set of
166   edges, along with a subset of vertices T ⊆ V called terminals. Each edge e ∈ E has an associated
167   non-negative cost cs (e).
168   The goal is to find a tree f = (V ′ , E ′ ) ∈ Fs that spans all the terminals in T , where V ′ ⊆ V and
169   E ′ ⊆ E, such that the sum of the edge costs is minimized:
170                                                          X
171                                          fs⋆ = arg min       cs (e).                                  (2)
                                                     f ∈Fs    e∈E ′
172
173   We remark that the STP is NP-hard, making it infeasible to find exact solutions for large instances.
174   Therefore, heuristic and approximation algorithms are usually employed to solve the STP, as in other
175   combinatorial optimization problems.
176
      As illustrated in Figure 1, we compare the STP with the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP), a
177
      well-known combinatorial optimization problem, using a toy instance. While relocating a node in
178   the TSP leads to minor changes in the optimal tour, adjusting the position of a terminal in the STP
179   significantly impacts the selected edges and can even alter the total number of edges in the optimal
180   Steiner tree. This demonstrates that dependencies in the STP may extend over relatively distant
181   regions, and small perturbations can lead to substantial variations in the solution space.

      4.1   DATASET
203
      To address the challenges in complex combinatorial optimization problems, it is crucial to have access
204   to high-quality datasets that capture the diverse and intricate nature of the STP. SteBen provides a
205   standardized, large-scale dataset with exact solutions, enabling the training and evaluation of advanced
206   neural models under both supervised and reinforcement learning paradigms. Our dataset includes 1.28
207   million optimally solved samples from various graph models i.e., Erdős-Rényi (ER), Watts-Strogatz
208   (WS), Random Regular (RR), and Grid. Node sizes for training include 10, 20, 30, 50, and 100, while
209   the test datasets cover not only these sizes but also larger instances of 200, 500, and 1000 nodes to test
210   the generalization capabilities of models. Additionally, SteBen offers a specialized environment for
211   reinforcement learning, facilitating the development of algorithms capable of handling the intricacies
212
      of the STP across diverse problem scales and structures.
213   In generating the STP instances, we followed the procedures outlined in previous works (Yan et al.,
214   2021; Ko et al., 2023). Graphs were sampled from ER, WS, RR, and grid models. For grid-based
215   instances, nodes have 2-dimensional location features, making them analogous to Euclidean-space
      combinatorial optimization problems like TSP and VRPs. In each graph, terminals were randomly

236   4.2   B ENCHMARKING BASELINES
237
      SteBen offers a carefully curated set of diverse construction heuristic methods to evaluate their
238
      performance on STP. The baselines are classified into four combinations based on two characteristics:
239
      SL against RL, and Autoregressive versus Non-autoregressive. All models were trained on our
240   benchmark and evaluated under the same computational budget, using identical feature embedding
241   techniques and decoding strategy which is proposed by (Yan et al., 2021) to ensure feasible STP
242   solutions (see Appendix K.2). We ensured a fair comparison by excluding transductive learning
243   techniques i.e. active search and Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS).
244
      This chapter provides a brief summary of the baselines and outlines any required modifications to
245
      implement in STP when the baseline was not initially intended for STP.
246
247   Autogressive model and supervised learning. Autoregressive and supervised learning approaches,
248   such as LEHD (Luo et al., 2024) and BQ-NCO (Drakulic et al., 2024), have recently concentrated on
249
      exploiting the symmetries present in COPs. These methods reconstruct the problem by excluding
      selected nodes and recursively considering the remaining nodes, relying on the tail-recursive property
250
      of routing problems to enhance generalization. However, STP lacks this tail-recursive property, as
251
      the selection of remaining edges and Steiner vertices depends heavily on partial solutions, making it
252   difficult to apply these methods.
253
254   To address the challenges posed by the STP and leverage the benefits of autoregressive and supervised
255
      learning, we propose adapting PtrNet, an autoregressive, supervised learning model. To adapt PtrNet
      for SteBen, we made several key modifications: (1) representing the tree solution sequentially using
256
      level-order tree traversal, (2) prioritizing nodes based on their minimum distance to terminals rather
257
      than lexicographic order, and (3) incorporating GNN embeddings into node features to account for
258   missing edge cost information and better capture graph topology. Further details and ablation studies
259   on these modifications are provided in Appendix G.
260
261
      Autoregressive Model and Reinforcement Learning. Cherrypick (Yan et al., 2021) solves the STP
      using an autoregressive RL model that leverages graph embedding and deep Q-learning, optimizing a
262
      reward function to minimize tree length and favor terminal selection. Similarly, the Attention Model
263
      (AM) (Kool et al., 2018) is a neural construction heuristic for routing problems that employs an
264   autoregressive model within a reinforcement learning framework. It uses a sequence-to-sequence
265   architecture with an attention mechanism to construct solutions by focusing on context vectors derived
266   from previously selected nodes and their positional embeddings. To adapt the AM for the STP, the
267   context embedding needs to be modified to reflect the specific requirements of the STP, with details
268   provided in Appendix H. Although recent methods like POMO (Kwon et al., 2020) and Sym-NCO
269   (Kim et al., 2022) leverage the symmetricity of COPs based on AM, applying them to the STP is not
      trivial because they do not account for the symmetricity in tree-structured solutions.

273   diffusion model to generate high-quality solutions.

285   Non-autogressive model and reinforcement learning. The Differentiable Meta Solver (DIMES)
286   algorithm (Qiu et al., 2022) is a novel approach for tackling the scalability issues in extensive COPs.
287   In contrast to conventional DRL techniques, which suffer from by costly autoregressive decoding
288

290
      utilizes a meta-learning framework to initialize model parameters effectively during the fine-tuning
291   stage. This allows it to outperform current DRL-based methods on benchmark datasets for the TSP
292   and MIS. For adaptation into the STP domain, DIMES employs a embedding technique and decoding
293   strategy utilized across all learning-based baselines.

299
      The baselines are trained and evaluated on the Intel Xeon Gold 6240 CPU and 8 NVidia 3090 GPUs.
300   The accuracy of the model is measured by validating its performance on 10,000 test samples for
301   the in-distribution results and 500 for the out-distribution results. The networks are tested once per
302   instance during the greedy decoding process, and the best result is selected among 32 samples in each
303   instance for the sampling metric. The training samples are split into a 1 million training set and a
304   280,000 validation set for supervised learning methods. 1
305
      In addition to the NCO approaches, we evaluate the performance of the classical solvers. Specifically,
306   we include the MILP-based solver SCIP-Jack(Gamrath et al., 2017) and the classical heuristic 2-
307   approximation algorithm(Kou et al., 1981) as baselines for comparison. The evaluation metrics
308   include the average Gap of the predicted solutions and the computation Time. The inference time
309   is calculated as the duration spent to process all the test samples for each algorithm. Additional
310   hyperparameters and experimental details specific to each approach are presented in the Appendix.

350   RQ2: How well do these NCO methods solve problems of unseen sizes, and which are the most
351   robust?
352
      In exploring RQ2, we evaluated the generalization capabilities of the models by testing them on STP
353   instances of varying sizes that differ from those seen during training. This evaluation is crucial for
354   learning-based solvers, as their practical utility depends on their ability to solve problems at test time
355   that are at different scales than those encountered during training.
356
      As shown in Table 2, we observed that DIFUSCO, despite its strong in-distribution performance,
357
      experienced a significant drop in performance when tested on out-of-distribution data with different
358
      problem sizes. In contrast, the reinforcement learning autoregressive models, specifically AM and
359   CherryPick, demonstrated more robust performance across different scales, maintaining a relatively
360   stable performance relative to their in-distribution results. DIMES (reinforcement learning, non-
361   autoregressive) also showed consistent performance across different scales, although it did not fully
362   align with the trend observed for the other models.
363
      These findings suggest that while supervised learning methods like DIFUSCO are effective within
364
      the distribution they were trained on, reinforcement learning approaches, particularly autoregressive
365   models, may offer greater robustness and generalization to problem sizes not seen during training.
366   This highlights the importance of considering the scalability and generalization capabilities of NCO
367   methods when applying them to practical problems.

      prove more practical than classical heuristics?
370
371   To address RQ3, we evaluated the models on real-world instances from the SteinLib benchmark (Koch

      6        D ISCUSSION
417
418
      Discussion about nAR settings and AR settings. As shown in Table 1, non-autoregressive
419
      models outperform autoregressive models in solving the STP. This can be attributed to the fact
420
      that STP heavily relies on the information of the currently selected partial solution, whereas AR
421   models may suffer from a smoothing problem when aggregating partial solution information during
422   the sequential node selection process. Especially, the smoothing of the embedding becomes more
423   prominent when the number of terminals is large or the solution sequence is long, leading to a
424   decrease in representation power. This explains the more significant performance degradation of AR
425   models on larger-scale instances. Therefore, one potential direction for improving the performance of
426   AR models is to enhance their representation power for partial solutions.
427
428   Training sample efficiency in supervised setting. In NCO, SL benefits from its ability to learn
429   quickly and reliably from high-quality labeled solutions. However, obtaining a sufficient training
430   dataset with high-quality solutions is typically very expensive in large-scale combinatorial optimiza-
431   tion domains due to their NP-hardness. If the dataset quantity is insufficient which means the model
      is trained on a limited number of instances, leading to reduced generalization for unseen instances.

      Limitations. State-of-the-art NCO methods require large datasets, making computational efficiency

      crucial for reasonable training times. Unlike Euclidean-space routing problems like TSP, efficiently

459   addition, our STP instances do not cover the full distribution of all possible STP problems, potentially
460   affecting performance on different distributions. Additionally, while real-world STP problems often
461   have practical constraints, our study focuses on the unconstrained version, leaving constrained
462   versions for future work.

467   In this paper, we introduced SteBen, a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating neural combina-

469   extensive datasets and environments for training state-of-the-art NCO methods with exact solutions.
470   Additionally, we have implemented various NCO algorithms and a classical heuristic, providing code
471
      that allows practical experimentation across diverse scenarios. We reported the results of applying a
      wide range of NCO construction heuristic methods for the STP, offering detailed comparisons and
472

473
      limitations of existing methods but also provided valuable insights for future research. We believe that
474   SteBen will serve as a stepping stone for researchers, facilitating the development of more effective
475   NCO methods and driving further advancements in solving complex combinatorial optimization

      B.1   S UPERVISED L EARNING AND R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING IN NCO
659
      In supervised learning, a model is trained on a dataset {(s, fs⋆ )}s∈D where each instance s is paired
660   with a label fs⋆ representing the known optimal or high-quality solution. The model aims to imitate
661
      the optimal solution by minimizing the difference ∥fˆθ (s) − fs⋆ ∥ under an appropriate metric ∥ · ∥.
662   The training objective is to minimize the loss function:
663
664                                                  X
665                                         L(θ) =         ∥fs⋆ − fˆθ (s)∥.                                  (4)
                                                     s∈D
666
667
      In reinforcement learning, the solver learns autonomously using feedback from the cost function
668
      cs (·), without relying on labeled data. The solver fˆθ (·) is considered a policy where the instance s is
669
      regarded as the state and the output fˆθ (s) as the action. After selecting an action, the solver receives
670
671
      a reward −cs (fˆθ (s)). The training objective is to minimize the expected cost over the distribution
      P(S) of instances:
672
673
674                                        R(θ) = Es∼P(S) [cs (fˆθ (s))].                                    (5)
675
676   B.2   AUTOREGRESSIVE AND N ON -AUTOREGRESSIVE S OLVERS
677
678   Autoregressive solvers generate a sequence of partial solutions incrementally, conditioned on
679   previous solutions, until a feasible solution is reached. This step-by-step approach simplifies the
680
      learning process by breaking it down into manageable stages. In the context of reinforcement learning,
      the advantage of reducing the action space has made autoregressive methods the predominant choice.
681
682   Non-autoregressive solvers generate the complete solution fˆθ (s) for a given problem instance s in a
683   single step. With recent progress in deep learning, particularly in models such as diffusion models
684   that excel in high-dimensional spaces, these solvers have become increasingly popular for tackling
685   large-scale combinatorial optimization problems. They avoid the error accumulation issue inherent in
686   sequential generation.

      training NCO models. Below, we describe the generation process for each graph type.

899   G.2    L ABEL G ENERATION
900
901   To serialize nodes of the STP solution tree, we compared tree traversal methods. A general tree, where
902   the number of children is not limited to two can be reordered using level-order, pre-order, and post-
903   order traversal. Level-order traversal, also known as breadth-first search (BFS), prioritizes visiting
904
      all the children of a node before proceeding to the nodes at the next level. Pre-order Traversal, or
      depth-first search, is a method where the traversal goes deeper recursively and then visits the siblings’
905
      subtrees. Pre-order traversal prioritizes visiting the root before its children. In contrast to other two
906
      methods, Post-order Traversal visits all the children nodes before their respective parent node. We
907   constructed the tree based on node indices, designating the root as the last-ordered terminal point and
908   sorting the children in descending order. This allows us to fix the position of terminals as the latest
909   embedding and assign consistent labels to aid in learning.

1099   K.2      G REEDY D ECODING A LGORITHM IN THE STP
1100
1101   Various decoding strategies can satisfy the specified constraints, each influencing the performance of
1102   neural combinatorial optimization (NCO). To ensure a fair evaluation of the baselines in this study,
1103   we adapted a greedy decoding algorithm, similar to CherryPick (Yan et al., 2021), for the Steiner
1104   Tree Problem (STP) that adheres to the three constraints mentioned above. This algorithm is applied
1105
       consistently across all baselines. The detailed pseudo-code for this algorithm is provided in Algorithm
       2.
1106
1107   Our adapted greedy decoding approach for solving the STP is analogous to greedy decoding in
1108   LLMs, where edges are iteratively selected based on previously chosen edges. Initially, we select an
1109   arbitrary terminal and define a partial solution as a graph containing only this terminal. Subsequently,
1110   we define a candidate edge set as the edges adjacent to the current partial solution. Among these
1111
       candidates, edges are selected based on scores derived from the raw output of the neural network,
       denoted as Gθ (s).
1112
1113   The decoding strategies slightly differ between autoregressive (AR) approaches, such as PtrNet (Meire
1114   et al., 2015), CherryPick (Yan et al., 2021), and AM (Kool et al., 2018), and non-autoregressive
1115   (nAR) approaches, such as DIFUSCO (Sun and Yang, 2023) and Dimes (Qiu et al., 2022). In nAR
1116   approaches, the values of Gθ (s) for the initial state s are precomputed and are not be changed during
1117
       the decoding process, and the edge scores are calculated from these values. Alternatively, in AR
       approaches, at each step of edge selection, the neural network receives the current partial solution as
1118
       input, performs a forward pass to recalculate the node scores instead of directly computing the edge
1119
       weights, and then selects the minimum weight edge that connects the partial solution graph to the
1120   remaining graph. To remove the redundant edges, we adapt the algorithm in (Kou et al., 1981).
