009                                               A BSTRACT
010
               Interpretability for Table Question Answering (Table QA) is critical, particularly
011
               in high-stakes domains like finance or healthcare. While recent Large Language
012
               Models (LLMs) have improved the accuracy of Table QA models, their explana-
013            tions for how answers are derived may not be transparent, hindering user ability
014            to trust, explain, and debug predicted answers, especially on complex queries. We
015            introduce Plan-of-SQLs (POS), a novel method specifically crafted to enhance
016            interpretability by decomposing a query into simpler sub-queries that are sequen-
017            tially translated into SQL commands to generate the final answer. Unlike existing
018            approaches, POS offers full transparency in Table QA by ensuring that every trans-
019            formation of the table is traceable, allowing users to follow the reasoning process
020            step-by-step. Via subjective and objective evaluations, we show that POS explana-
021
               tions significantly improve interpretability, enabling both human and LLM judges
               to predict model responses with 93.00% and 85.25% accuracy, respectively. POS
022
               explanations also consistently rank highest in clarity, coherence, and helpfulness
023
               compared to state-of-the-art Table QA methods such as Chain-of-Table (Wang
024            et al., 2023) and DATER (Ye et al., 2023). Furthermore, POS demonstrates high
025            accuracy on Table QA benchmarks (78.31% on TabFact and 54.80% on WikiTQ
026            with GPT3.5), outperforming methods that rely solely on LLMs or programs for
027            table transformations, while remaining competitive with hybrid approaches that
028            often trade off interpretability for accuracy.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Table QA models enable users to quickly retrieve the desired information from large and complex
      tables. Recently, LLMs have revolutionized the landscape of Table QA literature with state-of-the-
034
      art performance on a wide range of Table QA benchmarks. These models are highly accurate and
035
      sometimes are deemed interpretable (Ye et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Nahid
036   & Rafiei, 2024) but the researchers provide no data to support the claims of interpretability.
037
      Answers from Table QA models can be explained via intermediate tables and operations, offering a
038
      step-by-step walk-through of the reasoning process. For example, Chain-of-Table (CoTable) (Wang
039
      et al., 2023) progressively transforms the input table, through several function calls (predicted by
040   LLMs), into a simplified table to be presented to the LLM to ask for the final answer—Fig. 1(c).
041
      However, there are two main challenges with the interpretability of current LLM-based Table QA
042
      models. First, the reasoning becomes increasingly uninterpretable as query complexity grows—e.g.,
043
      when a table contain numerous rows and columns or when the question involves multiple conditions.
044   In Fig. 1(c), CoTable decides to select five rows using a function call of f_select_row(2, 3, 4, 5, 9).
045   Yet, there is no explanation for why these rows are chosen. Second, the final step, where the answer
046   is generated, still relies on the black-box reasoning of a model—leaving users uninformed as to why
047   the final answer was arrived at—an issue commonly observed in many works (Jiang et al., 2023;
048   Wang et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Nahid & Rafiei, 2024; Abhyankar et al., 2024; Wu & Feng,
049   2024). This introduces another layer of opacity in the reasoning process of Table QA models.
050   To address these interpretability challenges, we propose Plan-of-SQLs (POS)—a novel method that
051   breaks down the original query into simple natural language sub-queries, which are easily converted
052   into SQL commands and understandable by humans. For example, steps like Select rows where
053   or Select column are translated into SQL commands that are executed sequentially. This approach
      ensures that each transformation is explicitly rational, thus preventing the model from arbitrarily

110   2.1   D ECOMPOSING COMPLEX INPUT QUERIES IN TABLE QA
111
112   LLM-based Table QA models have improved performance by decomposing complex input queries
113
      into sub-problems (Ye et al., 2023; Nahid & Rafiei, 2024) or step-by-step reasoning (Wang et al.,
      2023; Wu & Feng, 2024; Abhyankar et al., 2024). This breakdown effectively addresses the com-
114
      positionality gap, a challenge where models can solve all sub-problems but struggle to combine
115
      them into a coherent solution (Press et al., 2023). However, these methods often rely on complex
116   table transformations—i.e., selecting a sub-table from the input table based on complex reasoning
117   steps (Ye et al., 2023; Nahid & Rafiei, 2024; Wu & Feng, 2024; Abhyankar et al., 2024), which are
118   prone to errors regarding which table entries to select (refer to Appendix F for examples of halluci-
119   nation in sub-table selections). For example, DATER (Ye et al., 2023) in Fig. 12 selects a sub-table
120   from the original table based on the statement. However, the inclusion of row 3 is illogical and
121   does not contribute to a valid answer. Our approach mitigates this issue by leveraging a sequence
122   of simple program-based table transformations. Each transformation is constrained to be easily ex-
123   ecutable and atomic, such as a simple Select rows where opponents is 0 in Fig. 1(d)—clause with
124
      only one condition and one variable, ensuring clarity and mitigating the hallucination problem.
125   Closest to our work is CoTable (Wang et al., 2023), which uses predefined atomic functions, like
126   (Chen et al., 2020; Nan et al., 2022; Mouravieff et al., 2024), to transform intermediate tables.
127   However, it still relies on the black-box reasoning of the model, particularly when adding new
128
      columns, generating function arguments, or generating the final answer— Fig. 1(c). Meanwhile, our
      method leverages atomic natural-language steps that are both human-comprehensible and easily
129
      convertible into SQLs. The SQL commands are then sequentially applied to the tables, ensuring
130
      transparency throughout table transformations and answer generation.
131
132   2.2   P ROGRAM - AIDED TABLE TRANSFORMATIONS
133
134   Program-aided table transformations play a crucial role in processing tabular queries. Using lan-
135   guages like SQL (Nahid & Rafiei, 2024; Ye et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2023) or Python (Cheng et al.,
136
      2023; Chen et al., 2020) offers two main advantages over LLMs. First, they enable transparent,
      rule-based transformations, offering greater traceability and interpretability compared to the opaque
137
      generation of LLMs. Second, they are designed for efficient handling of large-scale, complex data
138
      operations, making them more reliable and cost-effective than LLM-based methods (i.e., inputting
139   the whole large tables into LLMs is inefficient and erroneous (Chen, 2023; Wang et al., 2023)).
140
      Our work joins a growing body of literature that harnesses program-aid table transformations for Ta-
141
      ble QA but using SQL commands exclusively, much like Text-to-SQL (Rajkumar et al., 2022). To
142
      our knowledge, only two methods in Table QA literature—LPA (Chen et al., 2020) using Python-
143   Pandas and Text-to-SQL—address Table QA queries using program-based operations end-to-end.
144   Yet, since Text-to-SQL generates a single SQL command for the entire task, it requires a highly
145   powerful Text-to-SQL converter and is prone to hallucinations (Shi et al., 2020). Meanwhile, LPA
146   constructs a single Python-Pandas program to represent the entire query, which can lead to com-
147   plexity and potential errors due to the challenges of synthesizing accurate programs in one step.
148   By breaking down queries into multiple SQL commands, we eliminate the need for powerful Text-
149   to-SQL models while also achieving superior performance (see Tab. 3—decomposing queries into
150   simple SQL operations significantly improves accuracy over Text-to-SQL and LPA on TabFact).

191   3       POS: I NTERPRETABLE TABLE QA
192
193   Problem Formulation. In Table QA, each sample can be represented as a triplet (T, Q, A), where
194   T is a table, Q is a natural language question or statement about the table, and A is the answer. The
195
      goal of Table QA is to predict the answer A given the query Q and the table T . To achieve this, our
      method decomposes Q into smaller sub-queries (atomic steps), followed by converting them into
196
      SQL commands, and applying these commands sequentially to the table to arrive at the answer A.
197
198
      Grounded Table QA. Our method processes Table QA queries entirely through SQL commands,
199
      with table transformations executed offline and no access to external knowledge bases1 . Thus, we
200
      assume that all information necessary to answer the query is contained within the original table,
201   following the definition of grounded QA as termed in the literature (Lei et al., 2018).
202
      However, we observe that grounded QA is not established in popular Table QA benchmarks—i.e.,
203
      the ground-truth is not always present in the input table. For instance, 18.9% of queries in WikiTQ
204
      are insufficiently expressive using SQL to provide the correct answer and require external knowledge
205   (Shi et al., 2020). This problem contributes to the performance gap between our SQL-based method
206   vs. hybrid approaches on WikiTQ (see Tab. 3). We wish to clarify that our method is designed with
207   a focus on interpretability rather than accuracy alone.
208
209   Atomicity. In POS, we define an atomic step as a simple, minimal operation that can be directly
210   translated into a single SQL command. Specifically, an atomic SQL command (i) contains at most
211   one condition in the WHERE clause; (ii) uses at most one variable or column in that condition. By
212   restricting each step to be atomic in this way, we ensure that the SQL commands are straightforward
213   and less prone to errors during Step-to-SQL translation and execution. This also allows us to better
214   explain the reasoning process of a model to users. Examples of atomic steps in Appendix I.1.

      3.1     G ENERATING NATURAL -L ANGUAGE ATOMIC P LANS
217
218   We perform planning in natural language, which aligns closely with LLM capabilities (Huang et al.,
219   2022). This also makes the planning process more interpretable to humans, as each step is expressed
220   in clear, understandable terms rather than function calls, whose motivations and arguments are not
221   explained to users. We study the importance of natural-language planning in Appendix C.
222   In Fig. 2– 1 , the Natural-Language (NL) Atomic Planner takes (T, Q) as input and converts Q
223   into a plan of sub-queries, referred to as atomic steps. The generated plan outlines the sequence of
224   operations needed to arrive at the answer A. Below is the prompt we use for planning on TabFact:
225    Prompt to Generate Natural-Language Atomic Plans
226
227    [In-context Planning Examples]
228    [Input Table]
229    [Statement]
230    Let’s develop a step-by-step plan to verify if the given Statement is TRUE or FALSE on the given Table! You
231    MUST carefully analyze the Statement and comprehend it before writing the plan!
232    Plan Steps: Each step in your plan should be very atomic and straightforward, ensuring they can be easily
233    executed or converted into SQL. You MUST make sure all conditions are checked properly in the steps.
234    Step order: The order of steps is crucial! You must ensure the orders support the correct information retrieval
235    and verification! The next step will be executed on the output table of the previous step.
236    For comparative or superlative Statements, you should order the table accordingly before selecting rows. This
237    ensures that the desired comparative or superlative data is correctly retrieved.

240   3.2     E XECUTING ATOMIC P LANS WITH SQL C OMMANDS
241
242   After generating a natural-language plan in Sec. 3.1, the next step is to operationalize it by converting
243   each atomic step into an executable SQL. This translation is fundamental to POS, bridging the gap
244   between high-level natural language reasoning and reliable, transparent table transformations.
245
      3.2.1    S TEP - TO -SQL: C ONVERTING ATOMIC S TEPS TO SQL C OMMANDS
246
247   Leveraging the versatile capabilities of LLMs as Text-to-SQL converters (Hong et al., 2024), we
248   translate each step into its corresponding SQL query. By ensuring that each step is atomic and
249   straightforward (Sec. 3.1), we mitigate the need for complex Text-to-SQL translations. The conver-
250   sion process involves crafting a prompt for the LLM that includes the current state of the table, the
251
      specific natural-language step to be performed, and any constraints to guide the LLM in generating
      executable SQL commands. We prompt Step-to-SQL to convert a NL step into SQL as follows:
252
253    Prompt to Convert Natural-Language Steps to SQLs
254    [In-context Step-to-SQL Examples]
255
       [Input Table]
256
       Write a SQL command that: [natural_language_step]
257
       Constraints for your SQL:
258
259            1. If using SELECT COUNT(*), SUM, MAX, AVG, you MUST use AS to name the new column. If
                  adding new columns, they should be different than existing columns.
260
               2. Your SQL command MUST be compatible and executable by Python sqlite3 and Pandas.
261
262
263   3.2.2    S EQUENTIAL E XECUTION OF SQL C OMMANDS
264   Once each step is translated into SQL, we execute it using a lightweight SQL engine called
265   sqlite3 (Muddana & Vinayakam, 2024). The execution proceeds sequentially: the output of
266   one SQL command becomes the input for the next, effectively chaining together the transformations
267   specified by the generated plan (Fig. 2– 2 – 3 – 4 ). In contrast to end-to-end and hybrid QA meth-
268   ods that rely on the black-box reasoning of LLMs for final-answer generation— Fig. 1(a) & (c), POS
269   maintains transparency throughout by SQLs only. We provide all details of prompt engineering for
      POS in Appendix H.

      3.3     G ENERATING EXPLANATIONS FOR LLM- BASED TABLE QA MODELS
288
289   Despite the importance of interpretability, there has been a noticeable gap in the visualization of
290   explanations for LLM-based Table QA. Previous works have largely focused on improving accuracy
291   and efficiency without providing insights into the reasoning processes (Wu & Feng, 2024; Kong
292   et al., 2024). Motivated by that, we propose a pipeline to generate explanations for Table QA models
293   by leveraging the intermediate information given during the execution of operations. Our approach
294   highlights relevant parts of the intermediate tables to create attribution maps (Montavon et al., 2018),
295
      which illustrate how different input features contribute to the prediction.
296
      3.3.1       ATTRIBUTION MAPS
297
298   During the execution of each SQL command, we perform the following steps:
299   • Adding the tracking index column: Before executing an SQL, we add a tracking index column
300     to the current table. This column contains the original row indices from the initial table– Fig. 3(a).
301   • Executing the SQL command: An SQL command is executed on the table with the tracking
302     index column, producing a modified table– Fig. 3(b).
303   • Identifying selected rows: After execution, we use the tracking index column to identify which
304     rows have been selected or filtered by the SQL command– Fig. 3(c).
305   • Identifying selected columns: We parse the SQL command to extract the columns involved in
306     the operation– Fig. 3(d) (more details in Appendix E).
307   • Visualizing an attribution map: The previous steps allow us to generate an attribution map for
308     the initial table– Fig. 3(e). The index column is also drop here.
309
      Since both rows and columns can be attributed within an operation, POS offers a distinct advantage
310
      over previous works (Ye et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023)—accurately attributing responsible cells for
311   each transformation. For example, when a SQL command includes a condition that requires a cell
312   to match a specific value or range (e.g., WHERE opponents = 0), we can determine which cells
313   in the opponents column satisfy this condition and are thus responsible for the answer– Fig. 3(e).
314
315   3.3.2       C HAIN - OF - HIGHLIGHTED - TABLE E XPLANATIONS
316   At each stage of the table transformation process, we visualize attribution maps over the interme-
317   diate tables, emphasizing the data selected or filtered in the current operation. Rows and columns
318   containing relevant data for the operation are yellow-highlighted, while cells matching the specific
319   condition at this step are green-highlighted. Using the information obtained from the plan execu-
320   tion and attribution maps, we combine the three components: (1) intermediate tables; (2) attribution
321   maps; and (3) step description ; to make an explanation shown to users. We present the explanation
322   in a chain of highlighted intermediate tables for Table QA models, helping users visually follow
323   the sequence of transformations and understand how each step contributes to the final answer. Please
      refer to Appendix A for explanations from Text-to-SQL, DATER, CoTable, and POS.

336   4.1     E VALUATING E XPLANATION M ETHODS FOR TABLE QA
337
338
      We follow the two evaluation settings proposed by Doshi-Velez & Kim (2017), assessing XAI meth-
      ods both subjectively and objectively, using human users and LLMs (we refer to both as XAI judges).
339
340   Subjective evaluation: XAI judges are presented with explanations generated only from samples
341   where all methods either produce correct or incorrect results then asked to rank them based on
342   perceived quality; a.k.a. Preference task (Ramaswamy et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024). By using
343   relative rankings, we directly compare the methods in terms of clarity, coherence, and helpfulness
344   in understanding the model’s reasoning (see the task setup in Appendix G).
345
346   Objective evaluation: XAI judges are provided with an explanation and an input then tasked with
347   accurately predicting the model’s output, regardless of the ground-truth—the Forward Simulation
348
      task (Doshi-Velez & Kim, 2017; Hase & Bansal, 2020; Chen et al., 2022) (see Appendix G for
      LLM-as-a-Judge setup and Appendix K for human study setup).
349
350   Baselines We select Text-to-SQL (Rajkumar et al., 2022), DATER (Ye et al., 2023),and
351   CoTable (Wang et al., 2023) as baseline XAI methods w.r.t their state-of-the-art performance in
352   Table QA, interpretability, and reproducibility. We present baseline details in Appendix A.
353
354   Visualizations For our experiments, we use the TabFact (Chen et al., 2020) dataset, running each
355   method across the entire test set of 2,024 samples with gpt-3.5-turbo-16k-0613. We vi-
356   sualize the explanations for the executable samples by utilizing the intermediate information from
357   each method. In total, we generate 1,340 visualizations for Text-to-SQL, 2,024 for DATER, 2,024
358
      for CoTable, and 1,952 for POS.

      4.1.1    E VALUATING EXPLANATIONS WITH HUMAN USERS
360
361   Motivation Human judges are the gold standard for assessing explanations, as they are the ones
362   ultimately interacting with AI models via explanation interfaces (Doshi-Velez & Kim, 2017). We
363
      aim to study how explanations help humans in predicting model behaviors in Forward Simulation.
364
      Human Judges We recruit 32 volunteers for our study on forward simulation, all of whom are
365
      computer science undergraduate, master’s, or Ph.D. students. In each session, a user can choose one
366   of the four explanation methods and is asked to complete 10 samples. Each user can complete as
367   many sessions as they wish. In total, we gather 800 responses, with each method receiving around
368   200 responses. Please refer to Appendix K for the complete flow of our human study interface.

377   particularly in tasks like Table QA, where the information is still text-based but structured. In this

      Baselines We compare POS with several baseline methods, categorizing them into three groups

459   Results POS achieves 78.31% accuracy on TabFact and 54.8% on WikiTQ, outperforming LLM-
460   only methods, such as End-to-End QA, Few-Shot QA, and Chain-of-Thought. In addition, POS
461   demonstrates significant improvements over program-only methods. For instance, on TabFact, our
462   method improves accuracy by +13.6 pts over Text-to-SQL and +9.41 pts over LPA.
463   POS performs competitively to hybrid approaches on TabFact. However, on WikiTQ, our perfor-
464   mance still lags behind the state-of-the-art. This is primarily because our method processes Table
465   QA queries entirely through SQL commands, with table transformations executed offline and no
466   access to external knowledge bases (see Sec. 3). We present an ablation study for POS on three key
467   components: Atomicity, Natural-Language Planning, and Step-to-SQL Conversion in Appendix C.

469   5      C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
470
471   Limitations First, in Tab. 3, we observe that a subset of samples cannot be processed end-to-end
472   with our method (9.8% for TabFact and 27.8% for WikiTQ using gpt-3.5-turbo-16k-0613).
473   In such cases, we fallback to an end-to-end question-answering approach, directly querying LLMs
474
      for the final answer, similar to (Cheng et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2024). Second, POS relies on exact
      matches between the query and the input table. Although we have incorporated soft-matching tech-
475
      niques using SQL’s LIKE function, certain cases—such as a query with “thomas børn” and a table
476
      entry with “thomas born”—still result in failure to identify the relevant information (see Fig. 18).
477
478   Discussion We introduce Plan-of-SQLs (POS), a novel approach specifically designed to improve
479   the interpretability of Table QA models. Our findings highlight two key advantages of POS. First, it
480   addresses a common limitation in current Table QA literature—the lack of transparency—by making
481   every transformation step understandable. Second, POS improves human ability to predict model
482   behaviors, as shown by the high accuracy in Forward Simulation, which suggests that explanations
483   provided by POS are not just intuitive but also actionable. Notably, we observe that most of the
484
      POS’s errors are due to the poor planning capabilities of LLMs, rather than issues with Step-to-SQL
      translation (see an error analysis in Appendix J). We expect that as LLMs continue improving in
485
      planning, POS will become more accurate in QA while retaining its current level of interpretability.

      B    LLM- ONLY , P ROGRAM - ONLY , & H YBRID APPROACHES FOR TABLE QA
865
866   LLM-only. These approaches rely solely on LLMs to generate answers without explicitly per-
867   forming table transformations. End-to-End QA prompts the LLM to generate answers directly from
868   the input table and question. Similarly, Few-Shot QA (Brown et al., 2020) includes few-shot ex-
869   amples (T, Q, A) as the context to aid the LLM. In contrast, Chain-of-Thought (Wei et al., 2022)
870   prompts the LLM to explain its reasoning process step-by-step before delivering the final answer.
871
872   Program-only. Program-based approaches generate explicit programs to perform table transfor-
873
      mation and answer the question. Latent Program Algorithm (LPA) (Chen et al., 2020) frames Tab-
      Fact verification as a program synthesis task, converting input queries into sequential operations
874
      (e.g., min, max, count, filter) executed via Python-Pandas. On the other hand, Text-to-SQL (Rajku-
875
      mar et al., 2022) translates a natural language query directly into a single SQL command, which is
876   then applied to the input table to generate the answer.
877
878   Hybrid. Hybrid approaches combine the strengths of LLM reasoning and programs to perform
879   Table QA and achieve state-of-the-art performance. Dater (Ye et al., 2023) uses an LLM to ex-
880   tract relevant sub-tables, while breaking queries into sub-queries and executing SQL commands
881   to retrieve factual information. Similarly, TabSQLify (Nahid & Rafiei, 2024) leverages LLMs to
882   generate SQL commands, which is then used to create query-focused sub-tables. Binder (Cheng
883
      et al., 2023) takes a different approach by converting natural language questions into executable
      programs. It blends API calls with symbolic language interpreters like SQL or Python to address
884
      reasoning gaps that cannot be handled through offline methods alone. Lastly, CoTable (Wang et al.,
885
      2023) dynamically plans a sequence of predefined table operations–such as selecting rows or adding
886   columns, allowing it to iteratively transform the table based on the intermediate information. De-
887   spite their differences, Dater, TabSQLify, and CoTable all share a common strategy: they input the
888   final simplified table along with the original query into an LLM to produce the final answer.

935   Atomicity To assess the importance of atomic steps, we remove the constraint of atomicity in the
936   planning step in Sec. 3.1 as well as in in-context examples (w/o Atomicity). This means the LLM
937   is allowed to generate plans with more complex, compound steps. Unexpectedly, we observe an
938   improvement in the accuracy of POS on both datasets. We hypothesize that while the steps become

941   affected due to the increased complexity of the plan steps, making it more challenging for users
942
      to comprehend and trust the model’s reasoning process. We present qualitative examples of POS
      explanations with and without atomicity in Appendix D.

944
      NL Planning We replace the natural-language planning with a direct prompt that asks the LLM
945   to generate a sequence of SQL commands to solve the question end-to-end (w/o NL Planning).
946   We find that this component has minimal impact on the model’s accuracy. However, we observe a
947   significant increase in fallback rate–rising from 11.56% to 40.61% on TabFact and from 22.63% to
948   42.84% on WikiTQ. This indicates that many of the generated SQL-based plans are unexecutable
949   due to syntax errors or logical inconsistencies (e.g., referring to non-existent columns), significantly
950   hurting model interpretability.
951
952
      Step-to-SQL Conversion We modify the table transformation process to rely on prompting LLMs
      rather than executing SQL. Specifically, we ask the LLM to transform the table based on the natural-
953
      language steps, substituting the Step-to-SQL conversion with black-box LLM reasoning (w/o Step-
954
      to-SQL). This leads to a negligible increase in accuracy on TabFact but a substantial drop on WikiTQ
955   (from 48.90% to 27.05%), indicating that relying on the LLM for table transformations can severely
956   impact model accuracy. We argue that this is likely due to the LLM’s likelihood for hallucinations or
957   errors when handling complex tables (Chen, 2023; Wang et al., 2023). Additionally, this approach
958   diminishes interpretability, as the table transformations are no longer transparent or traceable.

       E        E XTRACTING C OLUMNS FROM SQL COMMANDS
1081
1082   We design an algorithm to analyze SQL queries and identify the columns used within them.
1083
1084   E.1       A LGORITHM OVERVIEW
1085
1086   The algorithm follows these main steps:
1087            1. Preprocessing: Remove comments and normalize whitespace in the SQL query.
1088            2. Column Extraction: Parse different clauses of the SQL query to identify column names:
1089
                             • SELECT clause: Extract both regular columns and those used in functions.
1090
                             • WHERE clause: Identify columns used in conditions.
1091
                             • ORDER BY clause: Extract columns used for sorting.
1092
1093            3. Filtering: Compare extracted columns against a list of original columns to ensure validity.
1094
1095   E.2       I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
1096   The algorithm is implemented using regular expressions to parse the SQL query. Key implementa-
1097   tion details include:
1098
                 • Use of re.sub() for comment removal and whitespace normalization.
1099

1854                     Understanding Attribution Explanations
1855
1856
         Attribution explanations highlight specific parts of a table—such as rows, columns, or
         cells—that are most relevant to the answer predicted by a Table QA model. These expla-
1857
         nations help you understand which information of the input the system considered important
1858
         when predicting the answer.
1859
1860     Table caption: 1947 Kentucky Wildcats Football Team
1861
1862     Statement to verify: “The Wildcats kept the opposing team scoreless in 4 games.”
1863
1864         Game    Date          Opponent        Result   Wildcats Points   Opponents     Record
1865         1       9999-09-20    Ole Miss        Loss     7                 14            0-1
1866         2       9999-09-27    Cincinnati      Win      20                0             1-1
1867         4       9999-10-11    9 Georgia       Win      26                0             3 - 1 , 20
1868         5       9999-10-18    10 Vanderbilt   Win      14                0             4 - 1 , 14
1869         9       9999-11-15    Evansville      Win      36                0             7-2
1870
1871     In this example, the Table QA model has highlighted specific rows and cells to explain its
1872     prediction:
1873     1. The entire rows for games 2, 4, 5, and 9 are highlighted in yellow.
1874     2. Within these rows, the Opponents column cells containing “0” or “scoreless” are high-
1875        lighted in green.
1876     These highlights indicate that the system identified four games where the opposing team did
1877     not score, verifying the statement as TRUE. The yellow highlighting shows the relevant rows,
1878     while the green highlighting represents the cells containing fine-grained information needed to
1879     verify the statement.
1880
         By using different colors for highlighting, the system provides a more nuanced explanation:
1881
         1. Yellow highlights (rows): Show the overall context of the relevant games.
1882
1883     2. Green highlights (cells): Pinpoint the exact information (opposing team’s score of 0) that
1884
            directly answer the question.
1885
         During the experiment, you will use explanations to choose which response (Statement is
         TRUE/ Statement is FALSE) the model would output, regardless of whether you think that
1886
         response is correct or not.
