009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have emerged as powerful generative models, capable of synthe-
012            sizing high-quality images by capturing complex underlying patterns. Building on
013            this success, these models have been adapted for time-series forecasting, a domain
014            characterized by intricate temporal dependencies. However, most existing works
015            have focused primarily on empirical performance without sufficient theoretical
016
               exploration. In this paper, we address this gap by introducing a generalized loss
               function within the diffusion-based forecasting framework. Leveraging this foun-
017
               dation, we introduce TF-score, a score-based diffusion model designed to capture
018
               the interdependencies between historical data and future predictions. Extensive
019            experiments across six benchmark datasets show that TF-score consistently sur-
020            passes leading baselines, including prior diffusion-based models. Furthermore, we
021            extend existing guidance sampling strategies into a our score-based formulation,
022            achieving performance gains across multiple datasets while providing a detailed
023            analysis of the trade-offs involved.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Time-series data is prevalent in our daily lives. There are numerous sub-problem related to time-
028
      series data such as time-series generation, forecasting, anomaly detection and sequential recom-
029
      mendation (Ahmed et al., 2010; Ismail Fawaz et al., 2019; Fu, 2011). Among these, time-series
030   forecasting is a prominent example, aiming to predict future behavior based on historical data. De-
031   spite extensive research efforts to develop effective forecasting methods, the inherent complexity of
032   time-series data presents significant challenges. To solve these challenge, researchers have increas-
033   ingly turned to deep learning techniques to better understand and model the structure of time-series
034   data (Lim & Zohren, 2021; Torres et al., 2021; Miller et al., 2024).
035
      Generative Models has achieved remarkable success across various real-world applications. No-
036   tably, these methods have been employed to generate high-quality synthetic images, realistic voices,
037   and even lifelike videos, among other creative outputs. Recently, the conditional generation strate-
038   gies of generative models have widely conducted to time-series processing, including time-series
039   generation, forecasting and anomaly detection.
040
      Diffusion models, which have recently gained attention for their ability to generate high-quality
041
      images while maintaining stable training loss, have also been applied to time-series processing,
042   achieving state-of-the-art results. However, many of these applications have lacked theoretical jus-
043   tification, focusing instead on the empirical adaptation of diffusion models to new domains (Rasul
044   et al., 2021; Tashiro et al., 2021; Yan et al., 2021).
045
      In this work, we examine the application of diffusion models to time-series data, classifying existing
046
      approaches into two main categories and analyzing their target values from the perspective of the
047
      score function (c.f. Section 2.2). We further generalize these categories into a continuous score
048   stochastic differential equation (score SDE) framework, addressing the theoretical gaps in previous
049   studies. Building on this, we introduce TF-score, a score-based diffusion model for time-series
050   forecasting that captures the internal structure of historical and predicted values by optimizing a
051   generalized loss function. This formulation leads to a more robust denoising process, improving
052   predictive performance. Leveraging modifications to a well-known backbone model (Kong et al.,
053   2021), TF-score surpasses most of the baselines, demonstrating superior performance compared to
      other diffusion-based forecasting methods.

073   2.1   D IFFUSION MODELS
074
075   Generative models aim to synthesize realistic data, such as images, by learning the underlying prob-
076   ability distribution of the data (Oussidi & Elhassouny, 2018; Harshvardhan et al., 2020; Cao et al.,
077   2024). Among various generative approaches, diffusion models have gained prominence defeating
078
      generative adversarial network (GAN), in terms of generating high-quality images with more stable
      training (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Song et al., 2020; Ho et al., 2020; Cao et al., 2024). Diffusion
079
      models operate through following two-step process: i) Noising step, which means gradually adding
080
      noise to an image, transforming it into Gaussian noise, ii) Denoising step, which means recovering
081   the original image from the noisy version, where the noise is sampled from a specific distribution,
082   typically a normal distribution (Yang et al., 2023).
083
084
      Initially, the denoising process was designed to reverse the noising process by adding noise in the
      opposite direction at each step. This process is derived from minimizing the Kullback-Leibler (KL)
085
      divergence between the joint probability of noising and denoising step, leading to an inequality
086
      involving the negative log-likelihood, similar to the variational autoencoder (VAE) framework. This
087   approach is called Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) (Ho et al., 2020). Below, we
088   briefly describe the DDPM process.
089
090

      noise to the image according to the transition kernel: p(xt |xt−1 ) = N (xt ; 1 − βt xt−1 , βt I),
091
      where t ∈ {1, 2, ..., T } and βt ∈ (0, 1) is a hyperparameter. With sufficiently large T , xt con-
092

093   pθ (xt−1 |xt ) = N (xt−1 ; µθ (t, xt ), Σ(t, xt )), where the denoising process aims to reverse the added
094   noise. By minimizing the KL divergence between the joint distributions of the noising and denoising
095   processes, DDPMs optimize target network ϵθ (·, ·) by using the following loss function:

150   Time-series forecasting involves predicting future values based on historical data (Lim & Zohren,
151   2021; Torres et al., 2021; Miller et al., 2024). Specifically, given a historical sequence x1:N , the

154   we define xhist by a sequence of history, x1:N , xpred by a future values, xN +1:N +T , and xtotal , a
155   total sequence x1:N +T . Time-series forecasting has been widely researched improve the accuracy
156   of future predictions. However, the complex, intertwined characteristics of time-series data make it
157   difficult to fully capture and understand its underlying patterns.
158   To address this challenge, researchers have increasingly turned to generative models, which aim
159   to model the conditional likelihood of time-series data and provide a more comprehensive under-
160   standing of its structure. As a result, many time-series diffusion models were appeared, which can
161   generally be classified into two categories: those that generate only future values (xpred ) (Rasul
      et al., 2021; Tashiro et al., 2021) and those that generate the entire sequence (xtotal ) (Lim et al.,

354   is achieved by training with zero-padding for the unconditional case, resulting in ϵ̃θ (xt , c) = (1 +
355
      w)ϵθ (xt , c) − wϵθ (xt , 0).
356   From a score matching perspective, this can be understood as ∇xt logp̃(xt |c) = ∇xt logp(xt |c) +
357   w∇xt logp(c|xt ) = ∇xt logp(xt |c) + w∇xt (logp(xt |c) − logp(xt )) = (1 + w)∇xt logp(xt |c) −
358   w∇xt logp(xt ). And this formulation leads to the generalized score function used in CFG:
359   s̃θ (xt , c) = (1 + w)sθ (xt , c) − wsθ (xt , 0), where 0 means zero padding. We use this generalized
360   CFG sampling. As the formulation shows, CFG should train both conditional and unconditional
361
      sampling to single model. In line with Ho & Salimans (2022), we adopt a proportional training
      strategy, where with probability pcond , the model trains the conditional score network sθ (xt , c), and
362
      with probability 1 − pcond , it trains the unconditional score network sθ (xt , 0).

459
      In this section, we review diffusion-based time-series models, focusing on their use of score func-
460
      tions and categorizing them based on their target score objectives.
461
462   As introduced in Sections 2.2 and 3, existing diffusion-based models for time-series fore-
463   casting can be broadly divided into two categories: those that learn the score function
464   ∇xpred logp(xpred
                     t  |xhist ), which focuses solely on the predicted sequence, and those that learn the
         t
465   score ∇xt logp(xtotal
                total
                           t     |xhist ), which models the entire sequence. Below, we discuss these cate-
466   gories in detail.
467
468
      Models targeting ∇xpred logp(xpred
                                     t    |xhist ) focus on generating the predicted sequence from the his-
                           t

469   tory. Two prominent models in this category are TimeGrad (Rasul et al., 2021) and CSDI (Tashiro
470
      et al., 2021). TimeGrad, a well-known diffusion-based forecasting model, generates predictions
      autoregressively. Given a historical sequence xhist = x1:N , it generates the next value xN +1 .
471
      Then, using the sequence x2:N +1 , it generates xN +2 , recursively continuing this process to achieve
472
      xpred = xN +1:N +T . In this setup, TimeGrad can be seen as learning ∇xpred logp(xpred  t    |xhist ) for
473                                                                                t

474
      single-step prediction. CSDI (Tashiro et al., 2021), on the other hand, generates the entire prediction
      sequence xpred in one shot, given the historical data xhist . While one-shot generation may appear
475
      more efficient than autoregressive methods, it can introduce higher variance in the generated sam-
476
      ples due to the randomness of the backward SDE. To mitigate this, CSDI evaluates the model by
477   averaging results over 100 generated sequences to ensure stability.
478
479   Next, we explain models targeting ∇xtotal
                                            t
                                                logp(xtotal
                                                        t   |xhist ). Kollovieh et al. (2023) focus on gener-
480   ating the entire sequence, including both historical and predicted values, with the goal of improving
481   conditional generation through history-guided sampling. TSDiff (Kollovieh et al., 2023) introduces
482
      this approach, generating the complete sequence xtotal and guiding the generation process using the
      history sequence xhist . The guidance mechanisms employed in these models are discussed in detail
483
      in Section 4.
484
485   Although designed for time-series generation, Lim et al. (2023) and Lim et al. (2024) take a dif-
      ferent approach by generating the full time-series autoregressively within a latent space, specifically


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      for handling irregularly sampled time-series data. This latent space generation allows for better
487   modeling of complex time dependencies, offering an alternative to standard methods. Therefore, re-
488   searchers have focused on total generation to take account of additional techniques, such as guidance
489   sampling or generation in latent space.
490
      Our proposed method, TF-score, can be seen as a unified framework that leverages the strengths of
491
      both categories: it generates high-quality predictions while incorporating guidance sampling mech-
492
      anisms to enhance performance and flexibility. By combining both aspects, TF-score provides a
493   more comprehensive approach to time-series forecasting with diffusion models.

497
498   We presented a score-based forecasting model, TF-score, for generalized diffusion framework.
499   Through the proposed methods, our method considers both the historical context and future pre-
500   dictions simultaneously and thereby captures the internal structure of total sequence, leading to a
501
      more robust denoising process. We also extend existing guidance strategies used in diffusion models
      into a score-based form, exploring their performance in time-series forecasting. Through a series of
502
      experiments, we demonstrate that these approaches enhance model performance for several datasets
503
      while shows trade-off relationship across datasets.
