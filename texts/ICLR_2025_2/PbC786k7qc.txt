009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Cancer survival prediction using computational pathology has emerged as a cru-
012            cial tool for diagnosis and treatment planning. Current approaches primarily rely
013            on Whole Slide Images (WSIs) and genomic data, but face significant challenges
014            in capturing the logical relationships between visual features and survival out-
015            comes with limited supervision. While pathology reports could potentially serve
016
               as a semantic bridge between WSIs and survival time, existing methods over-
               look the inherent hierarchical relationships between textual descriptions and visual
017
               features, where pathology terms represent more abstract concepts and individual
018
               terms may correspond to multiple image regions. To address these challenges,
019            we propose HyperSurv, a novel framework that leverages hyperbolic geometry
020            to model the hierarchical relationships between WSIs and pathology reports. Our
021            key insight is that hyperbolic space naturally captures both the entailment structure
022            between generic report concepts and specific visual features, as well as the one-
023            to-many relationships between pathology terms and image regions. HyperSurv
024            enforces these relationships through hyperbolic cones while identifying survival-
025            relevant features via attention pooling. Extensive experiments on four TCGA
026            cancer datasets demonstrate that our approach achieves state-of-the-art survival
027
               prediction performance by effectively modeling these multi-modal hierarchical
               relationships.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Survival prediction plays a crucial role in clinical practice for evaluating mortality risks and treat-
033   ment outcomes. While Whole Slide Images (WSIs) provide fine-grained biopsy morphology, ge-
034   nomic data offers complementary molecular profiles essential for comprehensive survival prediction.
035   In addition, the integration of these modalities is capable of revealing unique biomarkers (Chen et al.,
036   2021; Xu & Chen, 2023; Xiong et al., 2024a), enabling more informed clinical decisions.
037   The primary challenge in this task lies in the complex logic chain between WSIs and survival out-
038   comes. Prognosis is typically a complex process of logical deduction incorporating factors such
039   as tumor grading, cancer subtyping, staging, and genetic mutation analysis to estimate the survival
040   time (Bi et al., 2019). Given the gigapixel dimensions of WSIs, WSI-related tasks are often for-
041   mulated under Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) (Lu et al., 2021; Shao et al., 2021; Zhang et al.,
042   2022; Xiong et al., 2023), treating WSIs as bags of instances. Within MIL, the models have to detect
043   discriminative features in an unsupervised manner, which is even harder in this scenario due to the
044
      sophisticated logical chain connecting histopathology to survival outcomes.
045   Pathology reports offer a promising solution by providing structured descriptions of histological
046   features and their clinical significance, serving as a semantic bridge between WSIs and survival
047   outcomes and potentially mitigating the complex logical gaps between WSIs and survival time that
048   exist in end-to-end MIL training. To effectively integrate pathology reports with WSIs and genomic
049   data, we propose Tri-Mixture of Multimodal Experts (Tri-MoME), extending MoME (Xiong et al.,
050
      2024a) to handle three modalities. Unlike MoME’s alternating approach, Tri-MoME processes all
      modalities concurrently, producing fused representations in a single pass, enhancing efficiency and
051
      allowing for a more comprehensive integration of complementary information from all modalities.
052
053   Beyond the integration of these three modalities, another significant challenge emerges: intricate
      visual-semantic hierarchy between WSIs and pathology reports. WSIs provide granular features,

085
      accommodates hierarchical structures: images, being more specific, are positioned at the periphery,
086   while terminologies, representing more generic concepts, are situated closer to the origin. Mean-

090
      Constraint (FEC) and Text Genericity Constraint (TGC). The FEC enforces the entailment by po-
091
      sitioning every survival-relevant image within the hyperbolic cone of at least one survival-relevant
092   word. To ensure the accuracy of this constraint on survival-relevant features, we employ an attention
093   pooling module, which calculates survival-relevance scores based on the input features, and selects
094   features with the highest scores. The TGC enforces the generic-specific hierarchy by drawing text
095   vectors closer to the origin while maintaining the original positions of image vectors. Together, our
096   approach significantly enhances the ability of the model to capture the visual-semantic hierarchy.
097
      The contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:
098
099
100         1. We pioneer the integration of pathology reports, as a visual-semantic bridge, into survival
101            prediction, that could potentially bridge potential logic gaps in end-to-end MIL training.
102
103         2. We propose HyperSurv for survival prediction, leveraging hyperbolic geometry to capture
104            the visual-semantic hierarchy across WSIs and pathology reports in terms of loss functions.
105
106
107         3. We validate our approach on four TCGA datasets (BLCA, UCEC, LUAD, and BRCA),
               demonstrating superior performance in survival prediction across diverse cancer types.

      2.1   U NIMODAL S URVIVAL P REDICTION
111
112   The previous works bifurcates into two categories: genomic-based and WSI-based approaches.
113
      In WSI-based methods, due to the prohibitive size of WSIs, MIL is employed. This technique
114
      involves segmenting WSIs into “bags” of patches, which are then processed using a non-trainable,
115
      pre-trained image encoder such as (He et al., 2016), ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), CTransPath
116   (Wang et al., 2021a), UNI (Chen et al., 2024), CONCH (Lu et al., 2024), and GPFM (Ma et al.,
117   2024). The extracted features from these encoders are subsequently utilized for prediction. There are
118   two predominant strategies for deriving final outcomes in MIL: instance-level and embedding-level
119   approaches. Instance-level methods (Hou et al., 2016; Campanella et al., 2019; Kanavati & Tsuneki,
120   2021) involve generating predictions for each instance initially and then aggregating these results.
121   Conversely, embedding-level approaches (Ilse et al., 2018; Lu et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Shao
122   et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Xiong et al., 2023; 2024b) focus on generating a comprehensive bag
123   feature representation before final predictions. Evidence suggests that embedding-level approaches
124
      generally outperform instance-level methods (Wang et al., 2018).
125   In genomic-based approaches, genomic data is typically formatted as 1 × 1 tabular representations
126   of gene expressions. Given the straightforward nature of this data structure, various neural network
127   architectures are employed for feature extraction. These include MultiLayer Perceptron (MLP),
128   Self-Normalizing Neural Networks (SNNs) (Klambauer et al., 2017), and Transformers (Vaswani
129   et al., 2017). These models effectively interpret the genomic data, facilitating subsequent analysis.
130
131   2.2   M ULTIMODAL S URVIVAL P REDICTION
132
      While unimodal methods have shown impressive results in survival prediction, clinical practice often
133
      involves multiple data sources, such as WSIs and genomics. Multimodal methods can be categorized
134
      into tensor-based and attention-based methods. Tensor-based methods utilize tensor operations
135   to integrate information across modalities, employing techniques like concatenation (Mobadersany
136   et al., 2018), Kroncecker product (Wang et al., 2021b), and bilinear pooling (Li et al., 2022). These
137   methods generally have fewer parameters (or no parameters) and may exhibit suboptimal perfor-
138   mance compared to the attention-based ones. In contrast, the prevailing attention-based methods,
139   which include frameworks such as MCAT (Chen et al., 2021), HMCAT (Li et al., 2023), MOT-
140   CAT (Xu & Chen, 2023), CMTA (Zhou & Chen, 2023), MoME (Xiong et al., 2024a) and SurvPath
141   (Jaume et al., 2024), possess much more parameters. This allows them to more effectively model
142   correlations between modalities, potentially leading to enhanced performance in survival survival.
143
144   2.3   M ULTIMODAL L EARNING IN H YPERBOLIC S PACE
145
      Recent studies (Krioukov et al., 2010; Nickel & Kiela, 2018) have demonstrated that hyperbolic
146
      space is particularly suitable for data exhibiting hierarchical structures. Several research has utilized
147
      hyperbolic geometry in multimodal learning, showcasing their superiority in capturing the inherent
148   hierarchical structure of image-text data (Desai et al., 2023; Ramasinghe et al., 2024; Ibrahimi et al.,
149   2024; Mandica et al., 2024). However, these studies primarily focus on natural image-text datasets,
150   overlooking the unique characteristics of survival data, which lack ground-truth annotations for
151   paired WSI patches and pathology terminologies. Zhang et al. (2023) conducted multimodal fu-
152   sion in hyperbolic space for mild cognitive impairment while ignoring the relationship between
153   the hierarchy of the crossed domains. In short, the utilization of hyperbolic geometry to facilitate
154   multimodal survival prediction remains underexplored and merits further investigation.

      Genomics. Each dimension of the genomic data represents a gene expression level. Following pre-
163   vious works (Chen et al., 2021; Xu & Chen, 2023; Zhou & Chen, 2023; Xiong et al., 2024a), we
164   group genes into six functional groups that are relevant to cancer progression: Tumor Suppression,
165   Oncogenesis, Protein Kinases, Cellular Differentiation, Transcription, and Cytokines and Growth.

171   to extract the survival-relevant texts. The refined text is then embedded using BioClinicalBERT
172   (Alsentzer et al., 2019), yielding a matrix T ∈ RnT ×dT , where nT is the report length.
173
      A final linear transformation is applied to map all features to a uniform dimension d. Consequently,
174
      the dimensions of the matrices are standardized to P ∈ RnP ×d , G ∈ RnG ×d , and T ∈ RnT ×d .

      4.2      H YPERBOLIC C ONSTRAINTS
294
295   While the Tri-MoME effectively processes three modalities in Euclidean space, it faces challenges
296   in capturing the visual-semantic hierarchy between pathology reports and WSIs, as illustrated in
297   Fig. 1, due to the inherent deficiencies of Euclidean space. Specifically, we have identified two key
298   characteristics that previous works often ignore: 1) one terminology can entail multiple pathology
299   images, and 2) text usually conveys more generic information than image. To address this challenge,
300
      we propose FEC and TGC in Lorentz space to capture this hierarchy, as it provides an effective
      framework for representing this hierarchy, and we will detail these two constraints in this section.
301
302
      4.2.1      F EATURE - POOLED E NTAILMENT C ONSTRAINT
303
304   In hyperbolic space, if one concept entails another concept, the vector representing the former is
305   positioned within the hyperbolic cone defined by the latter (Ganea et al., 2018b; Desai et al., 2023).
306   Following this rationale, in our case, if a pathological word entails a specific image feature, the
307   image vector should be situated within the hyperbolic cone defined by the pathological word vector.
308   Unlike previous works (Desai et al., 2023; Ramasinghe et al., 2024) utilizing ground truth image-
309   text pair, pathology terms and WSI patches exhibit a complex, non-one-to-one association lacking
310   ground truth due to extraneous elements and labor-intensive annotation. To tackle this unique chal-
311   lenge, we propose a feature-pooled entailment loss function. Firstly, we apply attention pooling
312   modules to both text and image features, aiming to identify the survival-relevant features from each
313   modality. By focusing on these survival-relevant features, we ensure that extraneous ones are not
314   involved in this loss and the impact of irrelevant features is effectively eliminated. Mathematically,

347   4.2.2   T EXT G ENERICITY C ONSTRAINT
348
      Pathology reports often encapsulate more generic information than WSIs. This is because a pathol-
349   ogy report typically provides an overview of relevant information in WSIs. Previous research has
350   demonstrated that individual images contain more localized and specific information but overall
351   less semantic content compared to individual words (Desai et al., 2023; Ramasinghe et al., 2024).
352   These studies also indicated the efficacy of hyperbolic embeddings in visual-language-related tasks,
353   highlighting their ability when representing complex hierarchies.
354
      To this end, we propose leveraging the Lorentz model to effectively capture this relationship between
355   pathology reports and WSIs, as in hyperbolic space, vectors representing generic concepts are posi-
356   tioned closer to the origin, while more specific entities are situated farther away. This property can
357   naturally accommodate the hierarchical structures between pathology reports and WSIs.
358
      Specifically, we first project the encoded representations from Euclidean space onto hyperbolic
359
      space. Within the hyperbolic space, we position the text representations closer to the origin while
360
      maintaining the original positions of the image embeddings. Formally, for every text representation
361   in a pathology report t ∈ Rd in Euclidean space, we project it to tL ∈ Ld in the Lorentz model using
362   the projection function defined in Eq. (4). Subsequently, based on the distance metric in Eq. (3), we
363   apply the loss function Lt to every hyperbolic embedding of text representation, which is given by,

          6                    C ONCLUSION
531
          In this paper, we introduced a novel approach to survival prediction in pathology by incorporating
532
          pathology reports and enforcing geometric constraints for pathology reports and WSIs in hyperbolic
533
          space. The integration mitigates the complex logical gaps inherent in end-to-end model training from
534       WSIs to survival outcomes. To accommodate the inherent hierarchy between pathology reports and
535       WSIs, we leveraged hyperbolic space, specifically the Lorentz manifold, to overcome the limitations
536       of Euclidean space. Our method enforces the partial order relationship as the entailment between
537       pathology reports and WSIs, and positions text representations closer to the origin to represent more
538       generic concepts. Extensive experiments across multiple datasets demonstrated that our proposed
539       method consistently outperforms previous approaches. These results provide strong evidence for the
          effectiveness of textual modality and validate the efficacy of our hyperbolic geometric framework.
