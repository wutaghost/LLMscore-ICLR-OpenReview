009                                           Abstract
010
011
              We propose a distributed hyperparameter search method for time series
012
              prediction models named SWGA (Sliding Window Genetic Algorithm).
013           Compared to current genetic algorithms for hyperparameter search, our
014           method has three major advantages: (i) It adopts a configurable sliding
015           window mechanism to effectively combat overfitting from distribution shifts
016           inherent in time series data. (ii) It introduces a warm-up stage using Bayesian
017           optimization-based methods to generate a good initial population. (iii) It
018           supports distributed hyperparameter search across multi-node computing
019           clusters, enhancing both scalability and efficiency. To demonstrate SWGA’s
020           efficacy, we conduct hyperparameter search experiments on time series
021
              datasets from various domains. The experiment results show that our
              method consistently finds a hyperparameter configuration that achieves
022
              better performance on out-of-sample time series data compared to the
023
              traditional genetic algorithm. On average, it reduces the out-of-sample loss
024           by about 56.1%.

028   1   Introduction
029
030   In the realms of machine learning and deep learning, hyperparameter tuning stands as
031   a cornerstone to effective model training. It is important to tune the hyperparameters
032   of the model on a validation dataset. First, this adjustment helps minimize the risk
033
      of model overfitting to the training data, which often severely degrades out-of-sample
      performance. Second, by fine-tuning hyperparameters on a validation set with a distribution
034
      similar to the training data, the model can achieve better performance on out-of-sample
035
      data with a matching distribution. Lastly, since many hyperparameters pertain to the
036   model architecture and computational efficiency, optimal configurations can enhance model
037   efficiency. Consequently, researchers widely adopt hyperparameter tuning across various
038   machine learning and deep learning domains Vaswani et al. (2017); Dosovitskiy et al. (2020);
039   Zhang & Yan (2022); Liu et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2021); Devlin et al. (2018); Arik & Pfister
040   (2021); Huang et al. (2020).
041
      Time series prediction remains a crucial endeavor in various sectors. Domains such as
042   energy Hong et al. (2020); Nti et al. (2020); Reneau et al. (2023), finance Fischer & Krauss
043   (2018), house pricing Xu & Zhang (2021), and medical treatment Prakarsha & Sharma
044   (2022) heavily rely on predicting future time series values based on historical data. With
045   the swift advances in machine learning, optimizing performance on unseen data demands
046   rigorous hyperparameter search. Time series data, characterized by temporal dependencies
047   and non-stationarity, poses unique challenges. Temporal dependencies mandate models to
048   discern patterns evolving with time, while non-stationarity implies fluctuating statistical
049   properties, leading to potential distribution shifts Kim et al. (2021); Fan et al. (2023) and
050
      possible model overfitting Roelofs et al. (2019).
051   Traditional general-purpose hyperparameter search algorithms do not take into account the
052   domain knowledge of the time series prediction problem by design. Specifically, many time
053   series prediction models suffer from non-stationary time series and the temporal distribution
      shift in the dataset is a long-lasting problem Du et al. (2021). In this work, we propose

054
      a hyperparameter search process that caters for temporal distribution shifts in time series
055   data.
056
057   Addressing these challenges, we present the Sliding Window Genetic Algorithm (SWGA),
058
      a pioneering method tailored for hyperparameter search in time series prediction models.
      SWGA offers three innovations: a sliding window technique to mitigate overfitting due
059
      to time series distribution shifts, a warm-up phase that utilizes Bayesian optimization for
060
      crafting a solid initial population, and inherent compatibility with distributed computation
061   across multi-node clusters.
062
063   This paper delves into SWGA’s underlying methodology and assesses its efficacy across
064
      diverse time series datasets, consistently demonstrating its edge over conventional genetic
      algorithms in identifying optimal hyperparameters for out-of-sample time series predictions.
065
066   There are four major contributions of this paper:
067
068         • We introduce a warm-up stage using a lightweight TPE method, enhancing the
069
              initialization of the initial population. Compared to the random initialization
              in traditional genetic algorithms, this approach offers a more promising starting
070
              point for subsequent iterations, ultimately guiding the algorithm towards optimal
071
              convergence.
072
073
            • We unveil a configurable sliding window mechanism for hyperparameter search
              tailored for time series datasets, bolstering the search’s resilience against distribution
074
              shifts in time series data.
075
076         • We demonstrate an effective way to incorporate the consideration of the distribution
077
              shift in time series into the hyperparameter search process to create a domain-
              knowledge-enhanced hyperparameter search method that is better than its general-
078
              purpose counterpart. Using genetic algorithm (GA) as an example in the experiments,
079
              we demonstrated that our proposed way (warm-up and sliding window) can greatly
080           enhance the base method, GA, into SWGA, a method that gives much better
081           out-of-sample results for time series prediction models.
082
            • Our algorithm seamlessly integrates with the Ray distributed computation framework
083           Moritz et al. (2018), making it adaptable to a wide range of parallelism scenarios.

090   2   Related Work
091
092   In this section, we discuss the relevant literature on hyperparameter tuning methods for
093   time series prediction, covering traditional optimization techniques, distributed computing
094   approaches, and evolutionary algorithms.
095
      Researchers widely use traditional optimization techniques, such as grid search and random
096   search Bergstra & Bengio (2012), for hyperparameter tuning in time series prediction models.
097   Although these methods are conceptually simple, they come with high computational costs
098   and inefficient exploration of large hyperparameter search spaces. Bayesian optimization
099   methods, which gained popularity due to their ability to model the performance landscape
100   and guide the search towards promising regions of the hyperparameter space Snoek et al.
101   (2012), still demand substantial computational resources for large-scale time series prediction
102   problems.
103   To tackle the computational challenges associated with hyperparameter tuning, researchers
104   propose distributed computing approaches. Some examples include Population-based Training
105   (PBT) Jaderberg et al. (2017), Asynchronous Successive Halving Algorithm (ASHA) Li et al.
106   (2020), and Hyperband Li et al. (2017). These methods exploit parallelism to accelerate the
107   search and find success in various machine learning tasks. But, their full applicability to
      time series prediction problems requires further study, and adaptations may be necessary


                                                     2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      to handle the unique challenges of time series data, such as non-stationarity and temporal
109   dependencies.
110
111   Researchers employ evolutionary algorithms, such as Genetic Algorithms (GAs), for hy-
112
      perparameter optimization in various machine learning tasks Alibrahim & Ludwig (2021)
      Elgeldawi et al. (2021). GAs exhibit several attractive properties, such as global search
113
      capabilities and robustness to local optima, making them suitable for complex optimization
114
      problems. The literature contains several distributed GA variants, including Distributed
115   Genetic Algorithm (DGA) Belding (1995), Island Model Genetic Algorithm Whitley et al.
116   (1999), and Master-Slave Genetic Algorithm Cantu-Paz & Goldberg (2000). While these
117   methods apply to a wide range of optimization problems, their application to time series
118   prediction tasks remains limited.
119
      K-fold cross-validation Kohavi et al. (1995) is a popular technique used for model evaluation
120
      and hyperparameter tuning in machine learning. This method involves partitioning the
121   dataset into K equally sized folds, where each fold serves as a validation set exactly once,
122   while the remaining K-1 folds are used for training the model. By averaging the performance
123   metrics across all K iterations, K-fold cross-validation provides a more robust and reliable
124   estimate of the model’s generalization performance compared to a single train-test split. This
125   approach is particularly useful in scenarios where the dataset size is limited, as it maximizes
126   the usage of available data for both training and evaluation. Moreover, K-fold cross-validation
127   effectively reduces the risk of overfitting and helps to identify a model that generalizes well
128   to new, unseen data. Our algorithm may look similar to K-fold cross-validation, but they
129
      are very different.
130   Ray Moritz et al. (2018) is a distributed computing framework that supports various
131   distributed computing infrastructures. We integrate it into our algorithm implementation to
132   enable the distributed hyperparameter search capability.

145   Hyperparameter search Consider a machine learning model M characterized by a set
146   of hyperparameters H = {h1 , h2 , ..., hn }. Each hyperparameter hi has a domain d(hi ) from
147   which a value can be selected. The goal of hyperparameter search is to find a configuration
148   C = {c1 , c2 , ..., cn }, where each ci ∈ d(hi ), that optimizes the performance of the model M
149   on a given dataset. This can be mathematically formulated as:
150
151                                 C ∗ = arg min L(M (H = C), D)                                 (1)
152                                            C∈d(H)

153
      Here, L represents a loss function that quantifies the discrepancy between the predictions of
154   the model M with hyperparameters set to C and the true values in the dataset D. The aim
155   is to find the hyperparameter configuration C ∗ that minimizes this loss.
156
157   Distribution shift Time series prediction models often suffer from non-stationarity from
158   the time series data. The distribution in these data shifts along the time direction. To
159   mitigate distribution shift, people usually use domain generalization (Li et al. (2018); Muandet
160   et al. (2013); Wang et al. (2022)) and domain adaptation (Tzeng et al. (2017); Ganin et al.
161   (2016); Wang et al. (2018)). Domain generalization focuses on learning from the source
      domain and hopes to generalize well on the target domain while domain adaptation is to


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      reduce the distribution distance between the source and target domain. They both have
163   the goal to bridge the distributions of source and target domains. However, our method is
164   different from these methods in the sense that we address the distribution shift from the
165   hyperparameter search perspective.
166
167
168   Tree-structured Parzen Estimator The Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) is a
169   prominent method for hyperparameter optimization. TPE models the joint distribution p(x, y)
170   of the hyperparameters x and the objective function y. In contrast to other optimization
171   techniques that model p(y|x) and then invert this relationship, TPE models p(x|y) and
172   p(y) directly. TPE divides the hyperparameters into two sets depending on the observed y
173   values and then generates new candidate hyperparameters from a distribution that favors the
174
      promising set. In doing so, TPE provides a more flexible way of exploring the hyperparameter
      space, especially when the distribution of hyperparameters is non-uniform. However, TPE
175
      can be computationally expensive as the number of hyperparameters grows and sensitive to
176
      the choice of the threshold that separates the two sets of hyperparameters. Besides, TPE
177   runs in a sequential manner. It is hard to run in parallel and utilize the modern multi-node
178   distributed computing clusters.

      4   Methodology
183
184   To initialize the first population, rather than using random generation, we use a Tree-
185   structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) to repeatedly run a small number of trials to generate
186   the initial population. We call this process the warm-up stage and it provides a better starting
187   population for the genetic algorithm. To make the hyperparameter search more robust to
188   the time series’s distribution shift problem and prevent the algorithm from overfitting the
189   validation set, we create this configurable sliding window mechanism when conducting the
190
      genetic algorithm. We first split a dataset into the training set, validation set, and testing
      set according to a fixed ratio. Then, we evenly split the training set into multiple chunks
191
      of the same size. Then, we split the validation set into the same number of chunks. The
192
      hyperparameter search process goes as follows. First, we define a window of a length of a
193   fixed number of chunks. The window starts from the earliest chunk and slides one chunk
194   after each iteration of SWGA along the time direction. Starting from the population of the
195   first generation, at each iteration, SWGA trains the model with each individual config in the
196   population only on the data within the fixed-length window and does the model validation
197   on the data chunk right after the window. Then, the window slides along the time dimension
198   using a fixed stride of one data chunk. The size of each data chunk and the size of the
199   window are both configurable. Figure 1 shows an example of how SWGA works on a 3-year
200   time series dataset.
201   In detail, The entire process of SWGA, also shown by Algorithm 1 - 5, is as follows. First, it
202   splits the datasets into the training set, validation set, and testing set. In the warm-up stage,
203   the TPE algorithm runs a small number of trials on the training set and the validations set
204   to produce one hyperparameter config. This process repeats several times until it generates
205   enough individuals for the initial population. Then, the genetic algorithm process starts. In
206   each iteration, it first evaluates each individual in the population and sorts them according
207
      to the ascending fitness value. The fitness value is the trained model’s Root Mean Square
      Error (RMSE) or Mean Absolute Error (MAE) on the validation data. Then, based on
208
      the ranking of each individual in the population regarding the fitness value, the algorithm
209
      generates a new population for the next iteration. The population generation process is as
210   follows. It first creates a set of parents from the top k individuals (low fitness values) and
211   from the tail 2 individuals of the population. Then, it applies the crossover operator and
212   mutation operator to the parents to generate the offsprings. The offsprings and the top k
213   individuals together become the next generation of the population. Lastly, after the final
214   iteration, the top individual in the population becomes the final winner. SWGA then uses
215   this configuration to train a model on the original training set and report the RMSE or MAE
      on the testing set.

791   A.4    Optimization Dynamics
792
793   We conduct experiments to show the optimization dynamics of the baseline, GA algorithm
794   on the four models on those four different datasets. Figure 4 and Figure 5 have three major
795   takeaways: (i) For all four models, on most of the datasets, SWGA is able to reach a much
796   lower out-of-sample testing loss compared to the baseline GA. (ii) SWGA’s out-of-sample
797
      testing loss decreases in a smoother way while the baseline GA’s loss optimization process is
      much more volatile bouncing up and down. This indicates that it is safer to use the tuned
798
      hyperparameter configuration from SWGA compared to that from the baseline GA where
799
      there is a higher chance that the tuned configuration is on the out-of-sample testing loss peak
800   that bounces up from a previous local minimum. (iii) In some cases such as the XGBoost
801   case, the out-of-sample loss from the base GA fails to decrease properly while the SWGA is
