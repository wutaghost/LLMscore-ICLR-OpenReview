009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Hallucinations are one of the main issues with Large Language Models (LLMs).
012            This has led to increased interest in automated ways to verify the factuality of
013            LLMs’ responses. Existing methods either rely on: (a) search over a knowledge
014            base (KB), which is costly especially if the KB must be updated frequently to keep
015            up with fresh content, (b) LLM’s parametric knowledge to fact-check claims, which
016
               is cheaper but does not give attribution and is limited to verifying claims related
               to knowledge acquired during pretraining. In this work, we present LLM-C ITE,
017
               a cheap and easy to implement method that does not rely on any external search
018
               system while still providing attribution and the ability to verify fresh claims. Our
019            key insight is to leverage an LLM to directly generate potential citation URLs for
020            a given claim, and then use entailment checks to verify the claim against content
021            of the URLs (which are fetched on-the-fly). We benchmark LLM-C ITE on three
022            datasets containing fresh and non-fresh claims generated by humans and models.
023            We show that LLM-C ITE performs comparable or better than existing methods on
024            all categories of claims — importantly, without sacrificing attribution, or requiring
025            costly external search — overall LLM-C ITE is more than 45x cheaper than a
026            Google Search based approach.
027
028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated tremendous progress in language understanding,
031   and have wide ranging applications from summarization (Zhang et al., 2024) to question answering
032   in domains like law (Yu et al., 2022) and medicine (Singhal et al., 2023). Nevertheless, LLMs are
033   known to hallucinate and generate plausible sounding yet factually incorrect text (Maynez et al.,
034   2020; Ji et al., 2022). Thus, automated methods to check the factuality of LLMs’ responses is an
035   important problem (Guo et al., 2022; Kenthapadi et al., 2024).
036   Existing methods to do automated fact-checking either (a) rely on an external index such as retrieval-
037   augmented verification (Lewis et al., 2020) or LLMs with web search (Chern et al., 2023); (b) rely
038   on an LLM’s parametric knowledge to do fact-checking (Lee et al., 2021; Kadavath et al., 2022).
039   Methods relying on an external index include FACT S CORE (Min et al., 2023) where each atomic
040   claim in an LLM’s response is verified by doing an embedding-based retrieval against a static index
041   (e.g. created from Wikipedia) and then using an LLM to verify if the claim is supported by the
042   retrieved documents. One of the main limitations of such an approach is that it relies on a static
043   corpus and thus cannot verify fresh claims—claims whose validity depends on the changing world,
044
      e.g., winner of the November 2024 election in the USA. Methods relying on external tools like
      Google Search can work with fresh claims but are quite expensive due to the high cost of Google
045
      Search API. Approaches relying on an LLM’s parametric knowledge work by directly prompting the
046
      LLM to verify facts (Kadavath et al., 2022). They are straightforward and cheap but do not provide
047   any attribution, and are also limited by the model’s knowledge cutoff in verifying fresh claims. Table
048   1 includes a detailed comparison across existing methods.
049
050
      Motivated by this, we tackle the following research question — Can we design a simple, cheap
      method for fact verification with attribution that can also verify fresh claims?
051
052   It is clear that to verify fresh claims, one has to rely on an external knowledge base for up-to-date
053   information. This process typically involves (1) searching and retrieving potentially corroborating
      documents, and (2) checking whether the claim is entailed by any of those documents. In this

074   work, we ask the radical question: Can we off-load the expensive task of searching for potentially
075   corroborating documents to an LLM? At first, this seem counter-intuitive — if the LLM does not
076   have up-to-date information about the knowledge base, how can we expect it to search through it? To
077   our surprise, it turns out that LLMs can perform this task well for web domains like Wikipedia.
078   Our key insight is that the search can be made feasible by asking an LLM to identify URLs of
079   top-k (potentially) corroborating documents. We call these citation URLs. The reason why this
080   works is twofold. First, popular datasets used for pre-training LLMs (Gao et al., 2020; Raffel et al.,
081   2019) include several URLs. During pre-training, the LLM memorizes many such URLs, and their
082   association with the corresponding document contents. Our “search” prompt tries to elicit this
083   information from the LLM. Second, URLs have a semantic structure and they typically include terms
084   that are meaningfully tied to the document contents. Consequently, even if the URL is not seen by
085   the LLM during pre-training, it can learn to guess its form based on the claim. For instance, the first
086
      URL mentioned in Figure 1 (right) is not seen in pretraining (since it was created after the knowledge
      cutoff), yet the LLM was able to generate it for the given claim.
087
088   Once we have identified candidate citation URLs, the subsequent steps are to fetch the most recent
089   contents of these URLs, and check if they entail the claim. Notice that such content fetching, which
090   can be automated with tools like curl, wget, etc., is far cheaper than a full web or domain search.
091   In summary, LLM-C ITE consists of three steps: (1) use few-shot prompting with LLMs to generate
092   multiple candidate citation URLs for a given claim ; (2) on-the-fly fetching of the fresh contents
093   of the candidate documents ; (3) using a Natural Language Inference (NLI) model to verify if the
094   claim is entailed by the fetched documents. Figure 1 shows an overview of our method. Overall
095   LLM-C ITE is very easy to implement, cheap, and can verify fresh claims; Table 1 show detailed
096   comparison between LLM-C ITE and existing fact verification methods.
097   We benchmark LLM-C ITE on the task of fact-checking claims against Wikipedia.1 We benchmark
098   LLM-C ITE against several leading fact verification methods, including, verification based on Google
099   search. We evaluate on a variety of claims from three datasets, including, claims written by humans,
100   claims from model generated responses, and fresh claims. Across all settings, we find that LLM-C ITE
101   is at par or better than the leading fact verification methods.
102   Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
103
104   • We propose a very simple and cheap method, LLM-C ITE, that can verify claims without requiring
105     search through an external corpus.

114   2       LLM-C ITE
115
116   LLM-C ITE is a simple, cheap method that can verify any claim (both fresh and non-fresh) without
117   requiring an external search index. It consists of three steps: (1) generating candidate citation URLs
118
      using LLMs (Section 2.1), (2) on-the-fly fetching of the documents (Section 2.2), (3) verifying the
      claim based on the fetched documents (Section 2.3). Step (1) leverages LLMs to bypass the expensive
119
      step of “searching” for corroborating evidence in traditional fact verification systems. Step (2) ensures
120
      that LLM-C ITE makes judgments based on latest information, and therefore can verify fresh claims.
121
122
      2.1      G ENERATING URL S
123
124   Given a claim, the first step in LLM-C ITE is to generate candidate citation URLs that may help in
125   verifying the claim. We do this via prompting an LLM. Our prompts, shown in A.1 (Table 4 and 5),
126   use few-shot examples along with instructions that ask an LLM to generate URLs of pages that can
127   help verify the given claim.
128
      While our prompt is straightforward, it is surprisingly effective and can work with any off-the-shelf
129   LLM. This effectiveness stems from two properties of LLMs. First, URLs of popular web pages
130   (e.g., from Wikipedia ) would be seen by the LLM along with the page content during pretraining.
131   This allows the LLM to learn an association betwen the URL and the page content. Our prompt
132   simply “elicits” this information from the LLM’s parametric memory. Second, URLs typically have a
133   semantic structure along with interpretable domain, sub-domain, and path names. This allows the
134   LLM to effectively guess the URL even if it has not seen the claim or URL during pretraining; see
135   1st URL in Figure 1 for an example.
136   In order to account for errors during URL generation, we generate up to m diverse URLs. A claim is
137   verified if the contents of any one of the generated URLs corroborate the claim. Generating multiple
138   URLs helps because not all of the generated URLs would be valid. Furthermore, by specifically
139   generating diverse URLs, we also increase the coverage of content by the URLs. We empirically
140   assess the impact of generating multiple URLs in Section 4.4.
141   We consider two variants to generate the m URLs. LLM-C ITE ( DIVERSE ) directly prompts the LLM
142   to generate upto m diverse URLs (prompt in Table 5). Each few-shot example in the prompt also
143   includes upto m URLs . LLM-C ITE ( CONTROLLED ) prompts the LLM to generate only one URL
144   for a given claim (prompt in Table 4), and then uses different decoding techniques to obtain m URLs
145   — beam search decoding (Sutskever et al., 2014), or sampling if beam search is unavailable for the
146   model. Section 3.2 discusses these settings in more detail.
147
148   2.2      O N - THE - FLY F ETCHING
149
150
      Once we have a set of candidate citation URLs, the next step is to fetch the latest contents of the URL
      or lookup in a URL keyed content cache. One can use curl, wget, or equivalent tools for this to
151
      fetch in real time. This step allows LLM-C ITE to have the most up-to-date information, and is thus
152
      key to verifying fresh claims. In this work, we focus on fact verification based on Wikipedia pages,
153   and thus use the Wiki-API2 to fetch contents of Wikipedia pages on-the-fly.
154
155   We emphasize that in contrast to fact verification systems that rely on an external search system,
156
      LLM-C ITE relies only on a page retrieval system. Consequently, we avoid the expensive steps of
      indexing, embedding, query based retrieval and ranking, along with the associated engineering costs;
157
      see Section 4.5 for a detailed cost comparison. Furthermore, using URLs as the key for documents
158
      allows our method to take advantage of URL aliases and redirection. As mentioned in Section 2.1,
159   some of the URLs generated by the LLM may be “guessed” based on the named entities in the
160   claim. For instance, for the claim ‘The scientific name for lady’s fingers is Abelmoschus esculentus’,

177   2.3      NATURAL L ANGUAGE I NFERENCE
178
179   The final step in LLM-C ITE is to check if the claim is entailed by any of the fetched documents.
180   For this, we use an off-the-shelf natural language inference (NLI) model, which checks whether a
181   hypothesis text is entailed by a premise text. A claim is considered verified if any of the documents
182
      entail it, and the URL(s) of the entailing document(s) is returned as the attribution. If none of the
      documents entail the claim, then the claim is not verified.
183
184   NLI is a well-studied NLP task with several public labeled datasets, and several off-the-shelf models;
185   we refer the reader to Storks et al. (2019) for a survey. LLM-C ITE can work with any NLI model. In
186   our experiments, we use the Check Grounding NLI API3 offered by Google Cloud, and also study
187   the impact of using stronger LLM-based NLI models in Section 4.4.
188   NLI models typically work well on shorter premises (e.g. up to a few sentences long), whereas
189   the premises in our case are a set of (potentially long) documents. To counter this mismatch, as a
190   first step, we retrieve relevant sentences from each of the fetched documents. For this, we rely on
191   a simple TF-IDF based retrieval (Cohen et al., 2003) to retrieve a total of l sentences from every
192   document. If s1 , s2 , .., sl represent the sentences in decreasing order of similarity to the claim, we
193   create l different premises for each document — s1 , s1 |s2 , s1 |s2 |s3 , · · · , where | represents the
194
      concatenation operation.. We then query the NLI API model with all l ∗ m premises and the claim as
      the hypothesis.4

244   3.2     LLM-C ITE S ETUP
245
      For our method, we experiment with using four different LLMs to generate the candidate citation
246
      URLs — Gemma 1.1 7B instruction tuned, Gemini 1.5 Flash (Reid et al., 2024), Llama2-7B-chat6
247
      (Touvron et al., 2023), and GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2023). We use m = 4 URLs for each claim. Unless
248   otherwise specified, we use the variant LLM-C ITE ( DIVERSE ) from Section 2.1 that prompts the
249   LLM to generate 4 diverse URLs. We also experiment with the variant LLM-C ITE ( CONTROLLED ),
250   which prompts the LLM to generate one URL at a time, and uses beam search to obtain m URLs per
251   claim. Since Gemini 1.5 Flash and GPT-4o do not support beam search, we instead generate 4 URLs
252   using sampling with temperature 0.8 for LLM-C ITE ( CONTROLLED ). For the NLI model, we use
253   l = 6 sentences and set the citation threshold to 0.6. These parameters were tuned using the human
254   written answers for the ASQA dataset.
255
256   Rejection Sampling. Since not all the generated URLs would be valid, we also explore rejection
257   sampling to increase the percentage of valid URLs. Specifically, we use LLM-C ITE ( DIVERSE )
258   variant with temperature 0.8 to sample a total of 5 responses. We only keep the responses which have
259
      the maximum number of valid URLs, and drop the other 4 sampled responses. We use the Wiki-API
      to determine the validity of the generated URLs. Note that using rejection sampling presents a
260
      trade-off between cost and URL validity. This method is denoted by LLM-C ITE (RS).

409
      Increasing number of URLs We study the benefit of generating more than one URL per claim
410
      in LLM-C ITE. We do so by evaluating LLM-C ITE ( DIVERSE ) with m (no. of generated URLs) in
411
      [1, 2, 4, 6]. In all experiments, we use Gemini-1.5-Flash to generate URLs. We modify the prompt for
412   each m so that the instructions ask for m URLs and each few-shot example includes up to m URLs.
413
414   Generating more URLs significantly decreases the chance that none of the URLs are valid. Figure 4
415
      (left) shows — (a) percentage of invalid URLs among all generated URLs across all claims (orange) ;
      (b) percentage of claims where all the generated URLs for that claim are invalid (blue). We observe
416
      that as the number of generated URLs increases, the number of examples where none of the URLs
417
      are valid quickly drops to zero. This implies that we always have documents available to check the
418   validity of the claim in the final step of LLM-C ITE. Furthermore, even when generating up to 6
419   URLs, less than 25% of URLs are invalid. This allows LLM-C ITE to have access to diverse, valid
420   URLs in further steps of the method.
421
      Generating more URLs increases downstream verification performance. Figure 4 (right) shows the
422
      fact verification accuracy with increasing number of URLs. All results here use LLM-C ITE with
423
      Gemini-1.5-Flash to generate URLs. We observe that performance increases significantly as the
424   number of generated URLs increases from 1 to 4, but plateaus afterwards. This plateau is expected,
425   since for short atomic claims there might not be enough diverse Wikipedia URLs available to verify
426   the claim (we also noticed this when designing the few-shot prompts).
427
428   LLM instructed for NLI. As discussed in Section 2.3, LLM-C ITE uses an off-the-shelf NLI
429   model. Since the NLI model works better on shorter premises, we had to use a sentence retriever to
430   select relevant sentences from fetched documents to form the (short) premise. Through manual error
431   analysis, we observed instances where the generated URL in LLM-C ITE was correct, but the NLI
      model (or sentence retrieval) had errors.

487   Hanselowski et al. (2019) provides a detailed overview of existing fact verification datasets. The
488   main difference is that they focus on human written claims only, whereas our work (and more recent
489   prior work) also focus on evaluating the factuality of LLMs’ responses.
490
      A lot of existing methods for evaluating factuality rely on retrieval — either web search (Popat et al.,
491
      2017; Wei et al., 2024) or retrieval with a static corpus (Lewis et al., 2020; Min et al., 2023). Another
492
      line of work relies on LLM’s parametric knowledge for fact verification — either simple prompting
493   (Lee et al., 2020), or few-shot prompting (Lee et al., 2021; Kadavath et al., 2022), or hierarchical
494   prompting (Zhang & Gao, 2023), or self-checking (Manakul et al., 2023).
495
496   Generative Retrieval. The main idea behind LLM-C ITE is that we can use the LLM itself to
497   generate URLs i.e., an index for documents. Similar ideas have been explored especially for retrieval
498   in retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) — Tay et al. (2022) finetune transformers to directly
499   generate index for retrieval, and Wang et al. (2022) also propose a neural network to directly generate
500   document identifiers. The main difference is that these methods require finetuning a model for
501   the task, whereas we do not require any finetuning and show that we can leverage the pretrained
502
      knowledge to directly generate URLs. Additionally, these works propose a variant of RAG where the
      retrieval is done using a model, whereas our goal is to do fact verification with attribution. Recently,
503
      Khalifa et al. (2024) propose to pretrain models with source identifiers which can enable generation
504
      with attribution — they focus on a synthetic setting and require both pretraining and instruction
505   tuning. In contrast, we explore diverse real world claims and do not require any further training to
506   generate attribution.

      6    D ISCUSSION
510
      We highlight few key aspects about LLM-C ITE. First, the method is general and is not constrained
511
      to use Wikipedia only. It can be easily extended to any other reliable domains appearing on the
512
      Web (e.g., nytimes.com) that are also seen during pretraining. Second, identifying documents by
513   their URLs is crucial to the success of LLM-C ITE. URLs benefit from both being seen during
514   pretraining and also having a semantic structure that enables generalization. Given this, we expect
515   stronger, larger pretrained models to perform better as they would memorize a large number of URLs
516   seen during pretraining and their superior language understanding would help them form stronger
517   associations between claims and URLs. Lastly, even though we use on-the-fly fetching using the
518   URLs, in practice one would use a cached key-value lookup store to reduce end-to-end latency and
519   host load. LLM-C ITE can be implemented with under 1s latency per claim (1 ms per token for URL
520   generation and under 500ms for NLI) and we expect this to improve further as hardware and models
521
      get better.
522
523
      Future Work. Here we describe three key future directions for our work. (1) In this work, we
      leverage the pretraining knowledge and directly prompt LLMs to generate the URLs. However,
524
      we expect that performance can be further improved by instruction tuning LLMs on the ‘URL
525
      elicitation’ task. (2) The current proposed method is best suited for fact verification against public
526   domains seen in the pretraining data. To extend the approach to private corpora, one would need to
527   perform additional finetuning to teach the model the association between a document identifier and
528   its contents. This is similar to methods such as source-aware training (Khalifa et al., 2024). (3) Even
529   for domains seen during pretraining, not all would have meaningful URLs (e.g. arxiv.org only has
530   number identifiers). In such cases, instead of using URLs we can create a cached key-value lookup
531   with more meaningful identifiers e.g. title. We leave this exploration for future work.
532
533   7    C ONCLUSION
534
535   We present a very simple, cheap, and easy-to-implement method for fact verification, LLM-C ITE,
536   that does not require any external retrieval or web search. LLM-C ITE off-loads the expensive task of
537   searching through a corpus to the LLM, by directly generating candidate citation URLs. LLM-C ITE
538   performs on par or better than all existing methods for fact verification, across different types of
539   claims (fresh, non-fresh, model generated, human written) — while still providing attribution and
      being very cheap.

      the claim/answer — $0.00075 per 1k characters15 . Hence we need only one API call per claim.
