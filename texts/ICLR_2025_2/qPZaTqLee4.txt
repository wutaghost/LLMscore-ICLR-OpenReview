009                                                                                       A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) remain vulnerable to jailbreak attacks that by-
012            pass their safety mechanisms. Existing attack methods are fixed or specifically
013            tailored for certain models and cannot flexibly adjust attack strength, which is
014            critical for generalization when attacking models of various sizes. We introduce
015            a novel scalable jailbreak attack that preempts the activation of an LLM’s safety
016            policies by occupying its computational resources. Our method involves engag-
017
               ing the LLM in a resource-intensive preliminary task—a Character Map lookup
               and decoding process—before presenting the target instruction. By saturating the
018
               model’s processing capacity, we prevent the activation of safety protocols when
019
               processing the subsequent instruction. Extensive experiments on state-of-the-art
020            LLMs demonstrate that our method achieves a high success rate in bypassing
021            safety measures without requiring gradient access, manual prompt engineering.
022            We verified our approach offers a scalable attack that quantifies attack strength
023            and adapts to different model scales at the optimal strength. We shows safety
024            policies of LLMs might be more susceptible to resource constraints. Our findings
025            reveal a critical vulnerability in current LLM safety designs, highlighting the need
026            for more robust defense strategies that account for resource-intense condition.

      Research on jailbreak attacks for LLMs has demonstrated the feasibility of circumventing model
060
      safeguards (Albert., 2023; Wei et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2023). Methods such as fixed handcrafted
061   prompts (Albert., 2023; Wei et al., 2024), automatically generated prompts by LLMs (Paulus et al.,
062   2024; Chao et al., 2023), and gradient-based suffix searches (Zou et al., 2023; Liao & Sun, 2024) can
063   breach safety barriers. However, reliance on model-specific attack prompts and fixed handcrafted
064   prompts imposes significant limitations on scalability and flexibility in controlling attack intensity.
065   Moreover, few attack methods allow for control over their own attack strength, which is necessary
066   for adapting attack strategies to models of varying scales. Existing automatic search and automatic
067   instruction generation approaches incur high computational costs and suffer from notable deficien-
068   cies in transferability. Thus, exploring a novel attack paradigm that can quantify attack strength
069
      without relying on substantial computational resources is imperative.
070   We introduce a novel scalable jailbreak attack method, which achieving a scalable and control-
071   lable quantified attack strength. It operates by preempting the model’s computational capacity,
072   thereby preventing the activation of safety policies. Unlike previous fixed, automatically gener-
073   ated, or large-scale red-teaming attacks, our method is based on the observation that LLMs have
074   limited computational and processing capabilities under direct output with fixed length. This ob-
075
      servation aligns with the motivation behind the development of chain-of-thought (Chu et al., 2023;
      Wei et al., 2022) techniques. Figure 1 illustrates our load tasks to occupy resources, where we

      designed a Character Map Lookup task to evaluate LLMs performance across different Character
077
      Map complexity. As the complexity increases, the model’s performance decreases, with the task
078   increasingly preempting the model’s computational resources. We include detailed description in
079   Method. Inspired by this, we further explored the impact on safety policies by occupying resources.

081   vate. When these resources are insufficient, LLMs prioritize task execution over safety mechanisms.

129   Jailbreak attacks are techniques that allow attackers to bypass restrictions or alter the behavior of
130
      models (Xu et al., 2024). For LLMs, these attacks exploit misalignments within the model’s safety
      guardrail systems, circumventing measures designed to prevent the generation of harmful or inap-
131
      propriate content. By carefully crafting adversarial examples, attackers can manipulate machine
132
      learning models to produce incorrect or unexpected outputs. Paradoxically, as model performance
133   continues to improve, the attacker can succeed to attack LLMs by well-designed prompts (Jin et al.,
134   2024). Attackers often employ prompt engineering techniques, meticulously designing specific in-
135   puts to elicit restricted responses from LLMs. We compare properties of methods we used in Table 1.
136   We categorize LLM-oriented jailbreak attacks into three types:
137
      Fixed Manually Designed Prompts: Methods like AIM (Albert., 2023) and DAN (Albert., 2023)
138
      fall into this category. These prompts may include clever wordplay (Wei et al., 2024), role-playing
139   scenarios (Albert., 2023), context manipulation Wei et al. (2023), or misleading phrasing (Wei et al.,
140   2024) to bypass LLM defense strategies. However, such methods face challenges due to high man-
141   ual effort and lack of automation.
142   Automated Gradient Search Strategies: Also known as token-level prompt engineering, this
143   approach attempts to automate attacks. For example, Greedy Coordinate Gradient-based Search
144   (GCG)(Zou et al., 2023) uses gradient search to find suffixes that maximize the attack success rate.
145   It lays the foundation for a series of gradient search-based methods such as Improved GCG(Jia et al.,
146   2024) and AmpleGCG (Liao & Sun, 2024). While these methods effectively implement automated
147
      attack techniques, they require a large number of queries and have a low average attack success rate
      per attempt. Additionally, the unreadability of the generated suffixes makes them easily detectable
148
      by perplexity detectors (Jain et al., 2023).
149
      LLM-Generated Prompts: Methods that utilize LLMs as attackers have demonstrated high effec-
150   tiveness. LLMs can efficiently generate attack prompts, achieving automation while maintaining a
151   high attack success rate. For instance, ArtPrompt (Jiang et al., 2024) converts sensitive words using
152   ASCII art, enabling high-performance LLMs to recognize the art while bypassing safety guardrails.
153   AdvPrompter (Paulus et al., 2024) trains a separate LLM to generate readable suffixes capable of at-
154   tacking. PAIR (Chao et al., 2023) rewrites instruction targets using LLMs, automatically generating
155   attack prompts based on the targets and iteratively refining them until successful.

172   3.2   JAILBREAK ATTACKS
173
174   A jailbreak attack is defined as a method employed to circumvent the safety mechanisms embedded
175   within an LLM, allowing unauthorized or harmful outputs to be generated. For a task target that
176   requires the target LLM to execute, we define it as P , where P is a string P = p1 p2 p3 . . . pn , and
177   each pi ∈ D for i = 1, 2, . . . , n. Here, D is the set of all tokens in the LLM’s vocabulary. P
178   is a harmful or risky instruction or question that would cause the LLM to produce unsafe outputs.
179   However, due to the presence of the LLM’s safety guardrails, directly using P usually cannot make
180
      the LLM successfully complete the instruction. To circumvent these guardrails, we seek a specially
      designed attack function f such that the transformed input Padversarial is defined as:

      4.1     OVERVIEW OF S CALABLE ATTACK BY TASK OVERLOAD
217
218   The core idea of our attack is to preempt the LLM’s computational resources by directing it to
219   perform a Character Map Lookup task prior to addressing the target instruction. This preemption
220   limits the computational capacity available for the activation of safety policies, which require sub-
221   stantial resources to detect and filter out unsafe content. Our methodology is motivated by the need
222   to develop scalable attack strategies that can be quantified in terms of attack strength. By consuming
223   the finite processing capabilities of LLMs, we manipulate the model’s behavior to favor our target
224   objectives.
225   The workflow of our approach is illustrated in Figure 2 and can be summarized in the following
226   steps: The attack workflow consists of three key stages. We begin by presenting the model with a
227   Query String and a Character Map that provides a mapping of characters for decoding the Query
228   String. Next, we construct the masked Instruction, which incorporates a placeholder (e.g., ”[MASK]
229   [Target Instruction]”). Finally, combine Character Map, Query String and masked Instruction us-
230   ing prompt template. The model is instructed to perform the lookup task and decoded the Query
231   String, the [mask] in original masked Instruction is replaced with the decoded content, leading to
232
      the execution of the targeted instruction without triggering the LLM’s safety policies. We included
      the prompt template in Appendix. We demonstrate the workflow in Algorithm 1. By adjusting the
233
      complexity of the Character Map—such as Query Length, Query Count or Character Map Size—we
234
      can precisely control the amount of attack strength.

524   In this paper, we introduced a novel method for conducting jailbreak attacks on Large Language
525   Models by strategically overloading their computational resources with preliminary load tasks. This
526   approach effectively prevents the activation of the models’ safety mechanisms, allowing harmful
527
      instructions to bypass security protocols. Our experiments demonstrated the method’s scalability
      and effectiveness across various datasets and models. While the attack significantly impacts the
528
      models’ safety features, it has minimal effect on their ability to perform benign tasks. These findings
529
      highlight a critical vulnerability in current LLM architectures and underscore the need for more
530   robust safety mechanisms that remain effective under resource-intensive conditions.
