011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            The widespread use of Multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) often relies on a fixed acti-
014            vation function (e.g., ReLU, Sigmoid, Tanh) for all nodes within the hidden layers.
015
               While effective in many scenarios, this uniformity may limit the network’s ability
               to capture complex data patterns. We argue that employing the same activation
016
               function at every node is suboptimal and propose leveraging different activation
017
               functions at each node to increase flexibility and adaptability. To achieve this, we
018            introduce Local Control Networks (LCNs), which leverage B-spline functions to
019            enable distinct activation curves at each node. Our mathematical analysis demon-
020            strates the properties and benefits of LCNs over conventional MLPs. In addition,
021            we demonstrate that more complex architectures, such as Kolmogorov–Arnold
022            Networks (KANs), are unnecessary in certain scenarios, and LCNs can be a more
023            efficient alternative. Empirical experiments on various benchmarks and datasets
024            validate our theoretical findings. In computer vision tasks, LCNs achieve marginal
025            improvements over MLPs and outperform KANs by approximately 5%, while also
026
               being more computationally efficient than KANs. In basic machine learning tasks,
               LCNs show a 1% improvement over MLPs and a 0.6% improvement over KANs.
027
               For symbolic formula representation tasks, LCNs perform on par with KANs,
028
               with both architectures outperforming MLPs. Our findings suggest that diverse
029            activations at the node level can lead to improved performance and efficiency.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) have achieved remarkable success across various domains, from
035   computer vision to natural language processing. Traditionally, networks have relied on a fixed ac-
036   tivation function throughout their architecture, with popular choices including ReLU, Sigmoid, and
037   Tanh (Dubey et al., 2022). The ReLU activation function has gained widespread popularity due to
038   its simplicity and effectiveness, especially in addressing the vanishing gradient problem (Petersen
039
      & Voigtlaender, 2018). However, its non-smooth nature can lead to issues such as ”dead neurons,”
      where neurons become inactive and no longer contribute to the learning process. On the other hand,
040
      smooth activation functions such as sigmoid and tanh, while easier to optimize due to their differ-
041
      entiability, are prone to the vanishing gradient problem, especially in deeper networks (Petersen &
042   Voigtlaender, 2018). In addition to the specific weaknesses of each activation function, applying the
043   same activation function across all nodes can limit flexibility and expressive power. This uniformity
044   means that all neurons process information in the same way, potentially restricting the network’s
045   ability to capture diverse and complex patterns in the data. Furthermore, using fixed activation
046   functions causes every weight to be updated whenever a new data point is introduced, affecting the
047   weights learned from previous training data.
048   The hypothesis of using different activation functions at distinct nodes has inspired the development
049   of Local Control Networks (LCNs), as proposed in this paper. Recently, KANs have been introduced
050   as a method to address the limitations of fixed activation functions by proposing learnable activa-
051   tion functions and putting it in the edge (Liu et al., 2024). However, while KANs offer increased
052   flexibility, they still face certain limitations in their application. In contrast, LCNs aim to further en-
053   hance flexibility and representational capacity by allowing multiple activation functions to coexist
      within a single network, enabling the model to adapt more effectively to varying data characteristics


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      (Hagg et al., 2017). This approach is based on the idea that different neurons or layers may benefit
055   from specific activation functions, depending on the features they are processing. To achieve this
056   diversity in activation functions, we chose the B-Spline as the activation function for LCNs. In our
057   network design, the parameters of the B-Spline activation functions are learnable, enabling LCNs to
058   adjust activation functions at individual nodes dynamically. Additionally, learnable B-spline func-
059   tions support localized adjustments. This means the B-spline curves can be updated locally to adapt
060   to new data without altering the entire function trained on previous data, thereby improving stabil-
061   ity and speeding up convergence. We also hypothesize that this use of diverse and locally adaptive
062   activation functions will not only enhance model performance but also improve interpretability. The
063
      shape of the B-Spline function at each neuron reflects the data patterns the neuron captures, offering
      insights into the specific contributions of individual neurons, enhancing the interpretability of the
064
      network (Fakhoury et al., 2022). For instance, in image classification tasks, individual neurons can
065
      specialize in detecting features like edges, textures, or shapes. The learned B-spline function at each
066   neuron directly reflects these localized patterns, making it easier to trace which neuron is responsible
067   for capturing specific features. This capability enhances transparency, as we can analyze individual
068   neuron responses to understand what each is detecting.

104   2     R ELATED WORK
105
106   The development and optimization of activation functions have been extensively studied in the field
107   of neural networks. Conventional activation functions such as ReLU, Sigmoid, and Tanh have been
      pivotal in the success of deep learning models. However, each of these functions comes with inherent


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      limitations. ReLU, for example, mitigates the vanishing gradient problem but suffers from dead
109   neurons and lacks the smoothness needed for capturing subtle patterns (Dubey et al., 2022; Petersen
110   & Voigtlaender, 2018; Laurent & Brecht, 2018). Smoother functions like Sigmoid and Tanh face
111   slow convergence and vanishing gradients, especially in deep architectures.
112
      To address the limitations of those activation functions, various researchers have proposed adaptive
113
      or learnable activation functions. For instance, Swish (Ramachandran et al., 2017) and Mish (Misra,
114
      2019) are non-monotonic and smooth activations that have demonstrated improvements in a variety
115   of tasks. These functions improve gradient flow and generalization, highlighting the need for more
116   adaptable activation mechanisms. Furthermore, the concept of learning activation functions during
117   training has been explored (Arora et al., 2018), allowing networks to adapt activation behaviors to
118   specific datasets.
119
      Using varied activation functions within the same network leverages their combined strengths. Hagg
120
      et al. (2017) extended the NEAT algorithm to evolve networks with heterogeneous activation func-
121   tions, demonstrating that such networks can outperform homogeneous ones while remaining smaller
122   and more efficient. Dushkoff & Ptucha (2016) introduced networks where each neuron can select its
123   activation function from a predefined set, learning both weights and activations during training.
124
      The use of spline-based activation functions introduces a new level of flexibility to neural networks.
125
      B-spline activations stand out for their ability to smoothly approximate complex data distributions.
126
      B-splines, known for their flexibility and precision in numerical analysis and computer graphics, are
127   ideal for neural networks that need to accurately model intricate patterns. Recent research highlights
128   their effectiveness in various machine learning tasks. For example, Bohra et al. (2020) developed a
129   framework for optimizing spline activation functions during training, improving both accuracy and
130   interpretability. Similarly, the ExSpliNet model Fakhoury et al. (2022) combines Kolmogorov net-
131   works, probabilistic trees, and multivariate B-splines for improved interpretability and performance.
132   This research demonstrates the potential of spline-based activations to enhance both performance
133   and clarity in deep learning models.
134   Despite their capacity to overfit, deep networks often generalize well, challenging traditional views
135   like the bias-variance trade-off (Belkin et al., 2019). Studies show networks can fit random labels yet
136   generalize on real data (Zhang et al., 2017). The Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin, 2019)
137   and research on intrinsic dimensionality (Li et al., 2018) further explain how over-parameterized
138   models generalize effectively, prompting a reevaluation of complexity in neural networks.
139   The Kolmogorov–Arnold representation theorem underpins the expressive power of neural networks
140   and their approximation abilities. Liu et al. (2024) expanded on this theorem by introducing Kol-
141   mogorov–Arnold Networks (KANs), which leverage spline-based activation functions to achieve
142   higher levels of accuracy and interpretability compared to traditional models. Kolmogorov–Arnold
143   theorem is particularly notable for their ability to overcome the curse of dimensionality by decom-
144   posing complex high-dimensional functions into compositions of simpler one-dimensional functions
145   (Schmidt-Hieber, 2021). This approach allows KANs to build more compact and interpretable mod-
146   els, making them a promising direction for future research in deep learning.
147   Building upon these works, our proposed LCNs differ by enabling each neuron to have a unique,
148   learnable B-spline activation function, offering a higher degree of flexibility and local adaptability
149   compared to networks with fixed or globally adaptive activation functions. Unlike previous meth-
150   ods that may require complex architectures or evolutionary algorithms, LCNs maintain a standard
151   network structure with enhanced activation capabilities, making them practical for a wide range of
152   applications.

156
157   In this paper, we introduce the Local Control Networks (LCNs), a neural network architecture that
158   leverages B-spline functions to enable different activation functions at each node. We provide a com-
159   prehensive mathematical analysis demonstrating the advantages of LCNs over conventional MLPs
160   and more complex architectures Kolmogorov–Arnold Networks (KANs). Our approach simplifies
161   network design while enhancing expressiveness, data capture flexibility, computational efficiency,
      and generalization capabilities.

      3.1     BACKGROUNDS
163
164   MLPs are a class of feed forward artificial neural networks that have been widely used for function
165   approximation and pattern recognition tasks. In traditional MLPs, each neuron in a layer applies
166   a fixed activation function—commonly the ReLU or Sigmoid function—to its weighted input sum.
167   While these fixed activation functions simplify the network design and training process, they intro-
168   duce limitations in capturing complex and nuanced patterns in data. Specifically, fixed activations
169   can lead to issues such as vanishing gradients, lack of smoothness, and inability to model local-
170   ized features effectively (Dubey et al., 2022). Fixed activation functions impose a uniform response
171
      across all neurons, which can hinder the network’s flexibility and expressiveness. This motivates
      the exploration of more adaptable activation mechanisms that can enhance the network’s capacity to
172
      model complex functions.
173
174
      3.1.1    ACTIVATION F UNCTION D IVERSITY
175
176   To address the limitations of fixed activation functions, we propose using diverse activation functions
177   for each neuron, enabling the network to flexibly model a wider range of data patterns and capture
178   both global trends and local variations.
179
      B-spline functions are well-suited for this purpose due to their smoothness, continuity, and local
180
      support properties, which ensure that a neuron’s activation is influenced only by localized input
181   regions. These properties enhance the network’s ability to model complex, localized features ef-
182   fectively. B-splines, widely used in numerical analysis and approximation theory, offer smooth and
183   localized approximations, making them ideal for developing adaptive activation functions that im-
184   prove performance and pattern recognition (Bohra et al., 2020; Lyche et al., 2018; De Boor, 1978).
185

      4     C OMPARATIVE I NSIGHTS
325
326
      The Local Control Networks (LCNs) offers a unique balance between simplicity and expressiveness
327
      in comparison to both traditional MLPs with fixed activation and more complex architecture such
328   as KANs. This section outlines the core insights derived from LCN’s design, highlighting its ad-
329   vantages in performance, generalization, and computational efficiency by comparing it to MLPs and
330   KANs.
331
332
      4.1   L OCALIZED ACTIVATION AND S MOOTH G RADIENTS
333
334   One of the key features of LCN is the local support property of B-spline activation functions. This
335   provides several advantages over traditional activations such as ReLU:
336
337          • ReLU, being piecewise linear, introduces sharp transitions at zero, which can destabilize
338            training and result in gradients influenced by broad input ranges, reducing efficiency.
339
             • B-splines, in contrast, ensure smooth transitions and localized support, confining input
340
               influence to specific regions. This leads to sparse gradient updates, reduced computational
341
               complexity, and improved stability during training, akin to an effective dropout mechanism.
342
343
      Thus, LCN can handle subtle variations in data more efficiently than ReLU, providing stability and
344   robustness against noise and small perturbations in the input data.
345
346
      4.2   E FFICIENCY AND C OMPUTATIONAL L OAD
347
348
      Kolmogorov–Arnold Networks (KAN) offer strong theoretical guarantees for function approxima-
349   tion but suffer from high computational complexity due to their reliance on combining multiple
350   univariate functions, leading to overparameterization and inefficiency in large-scale applications.
351
352
      In contrast, LCN retains the simplicity of standard neural architectures while leveraging B-splines
      for flexible, accurate approximations without the need for complex transformations. Localized
353
      weight updates in LCN enhance computational efficiency by reducing simultaneous parameter up-
354
      dates, resulting in faster convergence, sparser computations, and lower training time and memory
355   usage, all while effectively modeling complex patterns.
356
357
358
      4.3   F UNCTION A PPROXIMATION AND G ENERALIZATION
359
      LCN strikes a balance between expressiveness and generalization. The use of B-spline activations
360   allows LCN to approximate complex functions with smooth, localized responses, which reduces
361   the risk of overfitting. The localized support of B-splines ensures that the network focuses only on
362   relevant regions of the input space, effectively acting as a natural regularize. This leads to a model
363   that can generalize well across different datasets, even in cases where high-dimensional noise might
364   cause KAN to falter. LCN’s localized gradient updates also contribute to this generalization by
365   ensuring that irrelevant inputs are ignored, much like a built-in regularization mechanism.
366
367   4.4   S IMPLIFIED A RCHITECTURE FOR P RACTICAL U SE
368
369   From a practical perspective, LCN offers a significant advantage over KAN by maintaining a stan-
370   dard neural network architecture augmented with B-spline activations. This results in a simpler
371   and more scalable design, which can be easily implemented in existing neural network frameworks
372   without requiring the complex setup associated with KAN’s univariate function combinations.
373   ReLU, though computationally efficient, is limited in its ability to model more nuanced data struc-
374   tures due to its lack of smoothness and piecewise linearity. LCN, on the other hand, provides a
375   smooth function approximation through B-splines, avoiding the sharp transitions characteristic of
376   ReLU, and enabling the network to model more intricate data manifolds. B-splines allow the net-
377   work to capture both small and large variations in the input space, leading to more robust learning
      and eliminating the problem of neuron saturation commonly seen with ReLU.

532
      adaptability and expressiveness. This flexibility enables LCNs to effectively capture both global and

      architectures.
