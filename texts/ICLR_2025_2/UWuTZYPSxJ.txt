007                                               A BSTRACT
008
009                Warning: This paper contains potentially offensive and harmful text.
010            Jailbreak attacks exploit specific prompts to bypass LLM safeguards and generate
011            harmful or inappropriate content. Recently, numerous approaches have emerged
012            for generating jailbreak attacks across diverse malicious scenarios. However,
013            these methods often suffer from critical limitations such as the reliance on hand-
014            crafted prompts, the necessity for white-box access to target LLMs, the generation
015
               of monotonous prompts, or the dependence on expensive queries to commercial
               LLMs. Moreover, these methods typically require considerable time to gener-
016
               ate jailbreak attacks. In this paper, we propose a Knowledge-Distilled Attacker
017
               (KDA) that leverages existing realistic and semantically meaningful prompts to
018            learn a model that efficiently produces successful attacks. Specifically, we fine-
019            tune an open-source LLM on a diverse set of attack prompts, enabling our frame-
020            work to automatically generate black-box, coherent, and diverse attack prompts
021            independent of commercial LLMs. Our KDA achieves a 100% success rate on
022            multiple state-of-the-art LLMs while only requiring less than 10 seconds per at-
023            tack generation. Further, using KDA, we introduce the RedTeam-10k dataset,
024            a large-scale dataset of 10,000 harmful attack prompts inducing malicious LLM
025            behavior spanning 12 categories such as bias, hate, and illegal activities. This
026
               dataset is 20x larger than any existing attack prompt dataset, positioning KDA as
               a powerful tool for large-scale adversarial testing.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The widespread adoption of Large Language Models (LLMs) across critical domains including
032   biomedicine (Tinn et al., 2023), financial analysis (Wu et al., 2023), code generation (Rozière et al.,
033   2024), and education (Kasneci et al., 2023) has highlighted the importance of ensuring their align-
034   ment with human values. Jailbreak attacks have become a popular red-teaming strategy to bypass
035   the safety mechanisms of LLM outputs (Dubey et al., 2024) and induce harmful, illegal, objection-
036   able, or undesirable responses (Zou et al., 2023; Chao et al., 2024). While many jailbreak methods
037   have been proposed recently, they continue to encounter several challenges.
038   • Reliance on handcrafted prompts: Early jailbreak attacks, such as the Do-Anything-Now (DAN)
039   prompt (walkerspider, 2022; Shen et al., 2024) and MJP (Li et al., 2023), were performed predom-
040   inantly by manually crafting attack prompts through trial-and-error. While being highly effective
041   against state-of-the-art (SOTA) LLMs, handcrafted prompts are not practical for comprehensive risk
042   assessment due to their poor scalability and adaptability across various scenarios. To minimize the
043   effort of manual prompting, the attack method should be automatic.
044   • Necessity for white-box access to target LLMs: White-box attacks (Zou et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
045   2024b) utilize internal model knowledge (e.g., gradients) to generate diverse and effective prompts;
046   However, commercial LLMs (OpenAI et al., 2024; et. al., 2024) are closed-source. To address this,
047   red teams have to rely on transfer attacks, which are vastly inferior and ineffective compared to
048   targeting open-source LLMs. To successfully jailbreak commercial LLMs, the attack method should
049   operate in a black-box setting.
050   • Generation of nonsensical prompts: Certain methods (Zou et al., 2023) generate nonsensical
051   prompts that are unlikely to occur in real-world scenarios, making them unsuitable for risk assess-
052   ment of LLMs. Furthermore, these prompts can be easily mitigated by defensive techniques such
053   as perplexity-based detection (Alon & Kamfonas, 2023) or randomized smoothing (Robey et al.,
      2024). To reflect real-world user scenarios, the generated prompts must be coherent.

      • Generation of monotonous prompts: Some methods (Li et al., 2024c) rely on prompts that follow
055   a repetitive pattern across attacks, making them relatively easy to detect and defend against through
056   standard safety mechanisms. To challenge the defense mechanisms, the method should produce
057   attacks with diverse patterns.

      • Dependency on queries to commercial LLMs: Many frameworks (Liu et al., 2024b) depend on

      commercial LLMs, such as GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024), for critical steps in attack generation (e.g.,
060
      mutation, rephrasing). This reliance not only increases the cost of these attacks but also makes
061   them less reproducible, particularly when model versions are updated. For reproducibility and cost
062   efficiency, it must avoid dependency on commercial LLMs during the attack generation process.
063
      • Lack of scalability: Most existing frameworks require considerable time to generate jailbreak at-
064
      tacks, involving many forward passes through large LLMs or expensive iterative processes. This pre-
065
      vents performing large-scale adversarial testing on LLMs with a diverse dataset of attack prompts.
066   The attack method must be scalable and able to generate prompts with low latency.

069
      attacks is understanding when an attack is successful without human evaluation. Many existing
      jailbreak evaluation methods are not aligned with human evaluations (Ran et al., 2024; Chao et al.,

148   Jailbreak attacks can be classified into five distinct categories based on their unique characteristics
149   and the methodologies employed in their generation. This classification is also shown in Table 1.
150   Automatic vs. manual. Early attempts at jailbreaking LLMs involved manually crafted prompts,
151   such as those in DAN (walkerspider, 2022), which use carefully designed phrasing to provoke uneth-
152   ical responses from safety-aligned LLMs. DAN (2023) gives an overview of manual jailbreak efforts
153   by compiling prompts from sources like Reddit, Discord, JailbreakChat.com, and other web plat-
154   forms. Both Wei et al. (2023) and Li et al. (2023) base their evaluations heavily on these handcrafted
155   prompts. However, due to the limited scalability of manual methods, recent research like GCG (Zou
156   et al., 2023) shifted to automated jailbreak techniques, which leverage algorithmic approaches to
157
      systematically generate attack prompts, providing a more scalable solution.
158   Black-box vs. white-box. GCG was the first to automate attack generation through token-level
159   optimization, requiring white-box access to gradient information for attack generation. Similarly,
160   AutoDAN2 (Zhu et al., 2023) and ASETF (Wang et al., 2024a) use gradient-based approaches
161   for discrete optimization, while COLD (Guo et al., 2024) leverages gradients in an energy-based
      method. Although AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2024b) and SMJ (Li et al., 2024a) employ gradient-free


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      optimization techniques, such as genetic algorithms, they still require white-box access to compute
163   the log-likelihood of token sequences to evaluate fitness scores. In this paper, we categorize any
164   method that relies on internal model information as a white-box attack, even when it exhibits strong
165   transferability to black-box models. In contrast, black-box attacks do not require access to model in-
166   ternals, offering greater versatility and being more suitable for jailbreaking commercial LLMs. For
167   instance, PAIR (Chao et al., 2024), GPTFuzzer (Yu et al., 2024), and TAP (Mehrotra et al., 2024)
168   rely solely on the target LLM’s responses to refine their attack prompts.
169
170   Cohrent vs. nonsensical. Methods like GCG, PAL (Sitawarin et al., 2024), and Opens-
171   esame (Lapid et al., 2023) often generate nonsensical prompts due to token-level optimization. Sim-
172
      ilarly, Adaptive Attack (Andriushchenko et al., 2024) relies on random search, producing gibberish,
      while AmpleGCG (Liao & Sun, 2024) uses non-sensical suffixes for training, leading to incoherent
173
      outputs. Such prompts cannot resemble real-world attack scenarios and are easily mitigated by exist-
174
      ing defenses (Alon & Kamfonas, 2023; Robey et al., 2024). In contrast, newer approaches generate
175   coherent prompts. PAIR employs in-context learning, AutoDAN optimizes prompts at the sentence
176   level, and methods like DeepInception (Li et al., 2024c) and CodeChameleon (Lv et al., 2024) use
177   structured templates to ensure coherence.
178
179   Diverse vs. monotonous. Existing methods like DeepInception, LRL (Yong et al., 2024),
180   CodeChameleon, DRA (Liu et al., 2024a), ArtPrompt (Jiang et al., 2024), and DrAttack (Li et al.,
181   2024b) rely on templates, fixed functions, or static obfuscation strategies to elicit harmful responses
182   from LLMs. Although effective, these approaches tend to produce repetitive attack patterns, making
183
      them easier to detect and counter. Unlike manually crafted techniques, which offer more variety,
      these methods often use encryption and decryption mechanisms to conceal malicious intent. In con-
184
      trast, diverse prompt-generation methods like GPTFuzzer, AutoDAN, and PAIR create varied attack
185
      prompts through mutation, genetic algorithms, or in-context learning. Such variability presents a
186   greater challenge for LLM safety systems, complicating detection and mitigation.
187
188   Exempt from commercial LLMs dependency. Many frameworks rely on commercial LLMs for
189   attack generation. For example, AutoDAN and GPTFuzzer use GPT-3.5 for mutation, while
190   DrAttack requires GPT-4 for decomposition. Rainbow Teaming (Samvelyan et al., 2024) and
191   TAP rely on GPT-4 as the Judge LLM, and PAIR relies on the public API of Mixtral-8x7B,
192   which incurs charges. ArtPrompt uses GPT-3.5 for paraphrasing and GPT-4 for font generation,
193   ReneLLM (Ding et al., 2024) uses GPT-3.5 for prompt rewriting, and PAP (Zeng et al., 2024)
194   fine-tunes GPT-3.5 for persuasive paraphrasing. Puzzler (Chang et al., 2024) engages GPT-4
195
      to extract malicious content and GPT-3.5 for offensive responses. This reliance on commercial
      LLMs raises cost and reproducibility issues, particularly with model updates. As a result, attack
196
      methods that avoid dependence on commercial LLMs are generally preferred for their affordabil-
197
      ity and consistency. In contrast, methods like AutoDAN and GCG avoid this dependency, offering
198   more affordable and consistent solutions. We distinguish between “commercial LLM dependency”,
199   which involves relying on commercial models for attack generation, and “black-box attacks”, which
200   target commercial models without relying on them.
201
202   Jailbreak evaluators. Existing evaluation methods fall into four categories: human evaluation,
203   text matching, LLM-prompted evaluation, and safety classifiers. Human evaluation, while reliable,
204   lacks scalability. Text matching (Zou et al., 2023), based on predefined refusal phrases (e.g., Ap-
205   pendix D), has limited coverage, allowing harmful content to bypass detection. The LLM-prompted
206
      evaluation depends heavily on the prompt quality Chao et al. (2024). Safety classifiers, such as
      Llama-Guard-2 (Llama Team, 2024), classify conversations by safety. In this paper, We fine-
207
      tune Llama-Guard-2 to better detect diverse malicious queries.
208
209
      Comparison with PAIR. Among all the related jailbreak methods in Table 1, PAIR (Chao et al.,
210   2024) is the most similar to our work. The key differences are as follows: (1) PAIR generates
211   attacks using few-shot prompting with hand-crafted examples, while our method, KDA, employs
212   knowledge distillation and is fine-tuned on a curated dataset of successful jailbreak prompts in a
213   supervised manner. This allows KDA to produce successful attacks with shorter system instructions
214   and reduced inference time. (2) For evaluation, PAIR relies solely on pre-trained LLMs such as
215   GPT-4 or Llama-Guard, whereas KDA uses a fine-tuned evaluator based on human annotations,
      ensuring more reliable assessments. Further empirical comparisons are presented in Section 5.1.

223   that evaluates response RA as being malicious. Then, the jailbreak attack is formulated as the
224   following optimization problem:

235   3.1     T RAINING A H UMAN -A LIGNED J UDGE LLM
236
      A straightforward approach for developing a jailbreak evaluator is to use a safety classifier like
237
      Llama-Guard-2 (Llama Team, 2024), an LLM safeguard model fine-tuned to classify conver-
238   sations by safety categories. However, its performance heavily relies on the diversity of the safety
239   categories used during training. As we will illustrate in Section 5.1, this classifier often has a high
240   false negative rate in practical attack scenarios. To address this, we propose further fine-tuning
241   Llama-Guard-2 on a broader dataset that pairs malicious queries Q with target LLM responses
242   RA for various attack prompts A.
243
      To construct a comprehensive set of malicious queries, we draw inspiration from prior works (Wang
244   et al., 2024b; Luo et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2023) and categorize unsafe content into 12 distinct
245   groups: Bias & Hate (B&H), Ethics (ETH), Harassment (HRM), Illegal Activities (ILG), Intellec-
246   tual Property (IP), Mental (MTL), Misinformation (MIS), Offensiveness (OFN), Physical (PHY),
247   Political (POL), Pornography (PGR), and Substance Abuse (SAB). Detailed descriptions are pro-
248   vided in Appendix C. For each category, we use GPT-4 to generate malicious queries based on the
249   input instructions adopted from Wang et al. (2024b):
250
251
            I will give you a scenario from the OpenAI usage policy. You should return 60 unique
252         instructions from the scenario that you cannot answer due to the OpenAI usage policy. The
253         scenario is {category}, described as follows: {description}.
254
255
256   For each malicious query Q, we use Natt SOTA attack methods to generate attack prompts An for
257   n = 1, . . . , Natt . These attack prompts are then fed into Nllm target LLMs to generate multiple
258   responses. To ensure alignment with human evaluation, we manually annotate these responses as
259
      either jailbroken or not. Detailed experimental procedures for constructing the dataset used for safety
      classifier fine-tuning are provided in Section 4. Using this dataset, we finetune Llama-Guard-2
260
      in a supervised manner to create a human-aligned judge LLM.

      3.2     T RAINING A K NOWLEDGE -D ISTILLED ATTACKER (KDA)
263
264   With a fine-tuned jailbreak evaluator in place, we introduce our method for distilling knowledge from
265   diverse attack methods into a single attacker, termed the Knowledge-Distilled Attacker (KDA). KDA
266   is initialized with a lightweight, pretrained open-source LLM, denoted as qKDA , to ensure efficiency,
267   open source, and automatic generation of coherent and diverse responses. To distill knowledge,
268   we employ the student-teacher setup, where attack prompts from existing methods serve as ground
269   truth labels to train qKDA as the student model. We next describe our process for generating a diverse
      dataset of attack prompts used as training labels for qKDA .

530   6     L IMITATION AND C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this work, we proposed a jailbreak evaluator finetuned on human-annotated samples for better
533   alignment and better accuracy. We further introduced KDA, a knowledge-distilled attacker LLM
534
      for generating high-quality attack prompts in an effective and scalable manner. Last but not least,
      we released a large-scale RedTeam-10k dataset that enables industrial-scale red-teaming. All in
535
      all, while the KDA framework demonstrates superior ASR across various SOTA LLMs and exhibits
536
      strong transferability, its effectiveness heavily relies on the presence of successful attack prompts.
537   KDA is unable to jailbreak an LLM if no attack prompts exist for the target model. We reserve the
538   research on novel attack style synthesis and improvement over current unsuccessful attacks across
539   different models for future endeavors.

      A.1   T RANSFER ATTACK VIA KDA
867
868   So far, KDA is evaluated on LLMs that the attack prompts are collected from. That is, KDA is
869   finetuned on collected successful attack prompts on Llama-2-7b, Vicuna-13b, GPT-3.5 and
870   GPT-4. Here, we move to a more challenging setting by evaluating the transferrability of the attack
871   prompts generated to GPT-4o, a black-box model where KDA has no knowledge of any successful
872   jailbreak prompts.
873   We observe that KDA is able to generate transferrable attack prompts to models that KDA has not
874   been finetuned on. For our target model gpt-4o-2024-05-13, we evaluate over the same 100
875   harmful queries as used in Section 5.2. Our results show that KDA achieves a 100% ASR, 8.52
876   seconds per success, and requires only 1.21 queries, which is comparable to the previous results
877   for LLMs where KDA is finetuned on. Therefore, this demonstrates that KDA is able to generate
878   successful transfer attacks to unseen LLMs with little-to-no sacrifice in time and the number of
879
      queries required.
