107   2.1   T EACHER C ORRECTION
108
      Distillation is an effective technique to condense knowledge from a more accurate teacher model to improve
109
      a less accurate student model Hinton et al. (2015) Muralidharan et al. (2024). Typically, knowledge is
110   distilled using the same dataset the teacher model was trained on. In cases where access to the original
111   training data is restricted, we notice from our experiments that the teacher model provides sub-optimal
112   guidance if a different dataset is used to distill the knowledge. We hypothesize this is due to the change in
113   distribution of sub-word tokens across the original dataset the teacher model was trained on vs. the dataset
114   being distilled on. To this end, we propose a novel teacher correction phase (illustrated in Figure 2), where
115   we perform a lightweight (∼100B tokens) fine-tuning of the teacher model to adapt to the new distillation
116   dataset. We demonstrate in Section 5 (Figure 3 in particular) that this procedure significantly improves the
117   guidance resulting in a more accurate student model. We also explore correcting the teacher in parallel to
118
      distillation, and demonstrate that this performs on par with using guidance from a fully corrected teacher.
119
120   2.2   P RUNING
121
      Weight pruning is a powerful and well-known technique for reducing model size. In this paper, we focus
122
      on structured pruning, where blocks (or channels) of nonzero elements are removed at once from model
123
      weights; examples of structured pruning techniques include neuron, attention head, convolutional filter, and
124   depth pruning Xia et al. (2023); Ashkboos et al. (2023); Men et al. (2024); Kim et al. (2024). In this paper,
125   we follow the pruning recipe outlined in Minitron Muralidharan et al. (2024): we start the pruning process
126   by first computing the importance of each layer, neuron, head, and embedding dimension. We then sort these
127   importance scores to compute a corresponding importance ranking.
128
      Importance Estimation We use a purely activation-based importance estimation strategy that simultane-
129
      ously computes sensitivity information for all the axes we consider (depth, neuron, head, and embedding
130   channel) using a small calibration dataset and only forward propagation passes. We consider depth pruning
131   as a special case and do not combine it with compressing other dimensions. We compute the importance of
132   each head, neuron and embedding channel by examining the activations produced by the multi-head atten-
133   tion (MHA), multi-layer perceptron (MLP) and LayerNorm layers, respectively. We use a small calibration
134   dataset (1024 samples) for this purpose.
135
      Layer Importance For depth pruning, we consider two distinct metrics for evaluating layer importance: (1)
136   LM validation loss/PPL, and (2) accuracy on the downstream task. We do not consider the Block Importance
137   (BI) metric Men et al. (2024) as it was recently shown to under-perform the validation loss/PPL metric Mu-
138   ralidharan et al. (2024). For ranking, we simply remove a single or a block of contiguous layers and compute
139   its effect on each metric; this serves as the “importance” or sensitivity of the layer/layer block. Based on
140   our empirical analysis (see Section 4; specifically, Figures 7 and 8), we use the Winogrande metric (Sak-

170   2.3   R ETRAINING WITH D ISTILLATION
171
172   We use the term retraining to refer to the accuracy recovery process post pruning. In this work, we ex-
173   plore two retraining strategies: (1) conventional training, leveraging ground truth labels, and (2) knowledge
174   distillation using supervision from the unpruned model (teacher). Knowledge Distillation (KD) Hinton
175   et al. (2015) involves transfer of knowledge from a larger or more complex model called the teacher to a
176
      smaller/simpler model called the student. The knowledge transfer is achieved by having the student model
      mimic the output and/or the intermediate states of the teacher model. In our case, the uncompressed and
177
      pruned models correspond to the teacher and student, respectively. Following the best practices outlined
178
      in the Minitron work Muralidharan et al. (2024), we use forward KL Divergence loss Kullback & Leibler
179   (1951) on the teacher and student logits only. This is illustrated in Figure 2.

      3.2   T EACHER C ORRECTION
196
197   Using the original Mistral NeMo 12B or Llama 3.1 8B models directly as a teacher performs sub-optimally
198   on our dataset. To counter this, we apply teacher correction, as described in Section 2, to both models with
199   ∼ 100B tokens. Since the goal is to adapt the teacher model to the distillation dataset, we use 120 steps
200   of warm-up and low learning rates: one-fifth the peak learning rate, identical batch size, minimum learning
201   rate and decay schedule the original model was trained on. We notice that the correction process has a minor
202   effect on the teacher model’s accuracy on downstream tasks, with some tasks improving and some degrading
203
      as shown in Table 1. We hypothesize this to be an artifact of the dataset used for fine-tuning. Optimizing
      this process further by using fewer than ∼100B tokens, lighter fine-tuning such as LoRA Hu et al. (2021) or
204
      tuning layer normalization Ba et al. (2016) parameters alone would be an interesting topic for future work.

      3.3   P RUNING
208
      Our pruning recipe is based on the best practices outlined in the Minitron paper Muralidharan et al. (2024)
209   and is described in Section 2. Specifically, for width pruning, we (1) use l2-norm and mean as the ag-
210   gregation functions across the batch and sequence dimensions, respectively, and (2) perform single-shot
211   pruning, avoiding iterative approaches. For depth pruning, as described in Section 2, we follow the ob-
212   servations from Gromov et al. Gromov et al. (2024) and drop a continuous subgroup of layers that results
213   in the least accuracy drop on Winogrande Sakaguchi et al. (2021). In this work, we skip the lightweight
214   neural architecture search (NAS) phase, and go with a manual architecture configuration for both L LAMA
215   3.1-C OMPRESSED -4B and MN-C OMPRESSED -8B. The architectures we come up with are inspired by the
216   Minitron-4B and Minitron-8B models Muralidharan et al. (2024), and are detailed in Table 3.

264
265   We perform a series of ablation studies to better understand the effects of distillation, teacher correction, and
266   our new depth-pruning saliency metric. We report our findings in this section.
267
268   Teacher Correction We first compare the effects of teacher correction on the MN-C OMPRESSED -8B
269   model in Figure 3; here, we notice the clear benefits of performing teacher correction w.r.t. distilling di-
270   rectly from an uncorrected teacher. Next, we compare two approaches for teacher correction: (1) pruning
271   and distilling the corrected teacher, and (2) pruning the original (uncorrected) teacher and distilling from a
272   continuously corrected teacher. The results in Figure 4 suggest that teacher correction can be performed in
273
      parallel with distillation to recover accuracy of the pruned student model.
274
      Pruning and Distillation Figure 5 demonstrates the orthogonal benefits of pruning and distillation over
275
      random initialization and conventional fine-tuning, respectively. We compare (1) random weight initial-
276
      ization and distillation, (2) random pruning and distillation, where weights are pruned randomly ignoring
277   the importance scores, (3) our proposed pruning with typical cross entropy based LM loss training and (4)
278   our proposed pruning with distillation-based retraining. We notice that pruning results in a significantly
279   better starting point compared to random initialization, and distillation-based training outperforms conven-
280   tional training methods. Overall, our approach requires significantly fewer training tokens (up to 40×; 380B
281   instead of 15T tokens) to produce the state-of-the-art MN-C OMPRESSED -8B model.

400   6   I NSIGHTS
401
402   In this section, we summarize some interesting and surprising observations based on our evaluation.
403
404   General
405
406        1. Teacher correction is crucial for distillation to work optimally on a new, unseen dataset. Fine-
407           tuning the teacher with the dataset used for distillation in this manner yields over a 6% reduction
408           in LM validation loss. Teacher correction doesn’t affect the optimality of pruning and can even be
409           performed in parallel with distillation.
410        2. In line with the Minitron paper’s observations, we require a order of magnitude fewer tokens (380B
411           vs 15T) to achieve state-of-the-art accuracy post pruning with distillation.
412
           3. For width pruning, we achieve stronger accuracy by retaining attention heads and pruning the other
413           dimensions (MLP intermediate dimension, embedding channels).
414
415
      Mistral NeMo 12B to MN-C OMPRESSED -8B
416
417        1. Our compressed model outperforms the teacher on two benchmarks, GSM8k and HumanEval after
418           pruning and distillation: GSM8k increases from 55.7% to 58.5% and HumanEval increases from
419           23.8% to 36.2%. This improvement is likely influenced by the dataset. However, retraining is
420           performed using the distillation loss alone.
421
422   Llama 3.1 8B to L LAMA 3.1-C OMPRESSED -4B

435   7   R ELATED W ORK
436
437   Structured pruning is a well-studied area, with a recent crop of papers specifically focusing on LLM com-
438   pression. We can broadly classify these works into ones that target depth (layers) (Men et al., 2024; Yang
439   et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024) and ones that reduce width (hidden dimension, attention heads, MLP interme-
440   diate size, etc.) (Xia et al., 2023; Dery et al., 2024; Ashkboos et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023); a small subset
441   targets both axes Muralidharan et al. (2024); Xia et al. (2023). Among recent papers, we choose to adopt
442   and extend the Minitron work Muralidharan et al. (2024) for several key reasons: first, to the best of our
443   knowledge, it provides the first systematic pruning recipe that targets both width and depth axes using a
444   low-cost importance estimation criteria (based on forward-propagation passes only); many other approaches
445
      (eg: gradient-based ones) are significantly costlier in terms of training compute and thus less practical for
      LLMs. Secondly, it achieves state-of-the-art performance compared to other similar compression methods
446
      on modern LLMs.
447
448   Teacher correction appears to be a novel area of exploration. Recent work focuses on adapting the teacher
449   to (1) address the capacity gap with respect to the student, where the teacher is fine-tuned based on knowl-
450   edge distillation constraints Huang et al. (2022), and (2) address batch-norm statistics when using out-
451
      of-distribution data (different downstream tasks) for distillation with convolution based models on image
      tasks Szatkowski et al. (2023). To the best of our knowledge, ours is the first work specifically targeted at
452
      LLMs that adapts the teacher to provide optimal guidance on a dataset not identical to the original dataset
453
      the teacher model was initially trained on.

456   8   C ONCLUSIONS
457
458   This paper has presented a novel strategy for applying pruning and distillation to models when access to
459   the original pretraining dataset is restricted. Teacher correction, which performs lightweight finetuning of
460   the teacher model on the target dataset significantly improves accuracy in this setting. This paper has also
461   presented a novel saliency metric for layers that improves depth-pruning accuracy over existing approaches.
462   Using this new pruning recipe, we produce a state-of-the-art 8B model (MN-C OMPRESSED -8B) from Mis-
463   tral NeMo 12B and a set of compelling 4B models (L LAMA 3.1-C OMPRESSED -4B) from Llama 3.1 8B.
