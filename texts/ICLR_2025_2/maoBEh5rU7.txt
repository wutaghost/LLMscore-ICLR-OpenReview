009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Deep reinforcement learning (RL) is an effective method for decision-making and
012                control tasks. However, RL-trained policies encounter the action fluctuation prob-
013                lem, where consecutive actions significantly differ despite minor variations in ad-
014                jacent states. This problem results in actuators’ wear, safety risk, and performance
015                reduction in real-world applications. To address the problem, we identify the two
016                fundamental reasons causing action fluctuation, i.e. policy non-smoothness and
017                observation noise, then propose the Fourier Lipschitz Smooth Policy Network
018                (FlipNet). FlipNet adopts two innovative techniques to tackle the two reasons
019
                   in a decoupled manner. Firstly, we prove the Jacobian norm is an approxima-
                   tion of Lipschitz constant and introduce a Jacobian regularization technique to
020
                   enhance the smoothness of policy network. Secondly, we introduce a Fourier fil-
021
                   ter layer to deal with observation noise. The filter layer includes a trainable filter
022                matrix that can automatically extract important observation frequencies and sup-
023                press noise frequencies. FlipNet can be seamlessly integrated into most existing
024                RL algorithms as an actor network. Simulated and real-world experiments show
025                that FlipeNet has excellent action smoothness and noise robustness, achieving a
026                new state-of-the-art performance. The code and videos are publicly available 1 .

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Deep reinforcement learning (RL) has become a powerful approach for addressing optimal control
032   tasks in physical environments (Guan et al., 2021; Li, 2023). Neural networks, capable of modeling
033   complex nonlinear functions (Hornik et al., 1989; Kidger & Lyons, 2020), are commonly used as
034   the container for the control policy fitted by RL. However, RL-trained policies often encounter the
035   action fluctuation problem, where consecutive actions exhibit significant variations despite minor
036   differences in the adjacent observations. While this problem is often overlooked during simulation
037   and training stages, it will result in serious issues in real-world application like performance re-
038
      duction, actuators’ wear, and safety risk (Song et al., 2023). This problem is prevalent in various
      scenarios, including drone control (Mysore et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2019), robot arm manipulation
039
      (Yu et al., 2021), and autonomous driving (Cai et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2021; Wasala et al., 2020).
040
041   In order to make RL more applicable in real-world scenarios, researchers are working hard to solve
042   the problem. CAPS (Mysore et al., 2021) and L2C2 (Kobayashi, 2022) introduce penalty terms in
043   actor loss, which indicate the action similarity in successive time steps or the action similarity under
044
      close states. SR2 L (Shen et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2022) trains policy network using adversarial
      noise, which maximizes the action difference under actual state and adversarial state. PIC (Chen
045
      et al., 2021) and TAAC (Yu et al., 2021) design two-stage policies by using one network to output
046
      the current action, and the other to output action inertia scalar or make choice between the current
047   and the last action. MLP-SN (Takase et al., 2020) and LipsNet (Song et al., 2023) smoothes control
048   action by constraining the Lipschitz constant of policy network. However, CAPS and L2C2 suffer
049   from sensitive hyperparameter tuning, and their sampling of close states complicate RL algorithms.
050   SR2 L, PIC, and TAAC need special policy evaluation or policy improvement mechanisms, which
051   means they cannot be used in traditional RL algorithms. MLP-SN suffers from several performance
052   loss and LipsNet is limited to the network with piecewise linear activation functions. Furthermore,

188   3.2   JACOBIAN R EGULARIZATION
189
      Definition 3.1 (Local Lipschitz Constant). Suppose f : Rn → Rm is a continuous neural network.
190   The K(x) is defined as the local Lipschitz constant of f on the neighborhood of x:
191
                                                                     ∥f (x1 ) − f (x2 )∥
192                                    K(x) =           max                              ,               (5)
193                                                 x1 ,x2 ∈B(x,ρ)       ∥x1 − x2 ∥
194   where B(x, ρ) denotes the open ball area with radius ρ > 0 centered at the point x in the Euclidean
195   space, i.e. B(x, ρ) = {x′ : ∥x′ − x∥ < ρ}.
196
      Lipschitz constant characterizes the landscape smoothness of a function. By viewing the policy
197
      network as a mapping function, Lipschitz constant could reflect the smoothness of the policy func-
198
      tion fitted by RL. A lower Lipschitz constant means a smoother policy function, which leads to
199   smoother actions (Ames et al., 2016; Kobayashi, 2022; Song et al., 2023; Takase et al., 2020).
200   MLP-SN (Takase et al., 2020) constrains the Lipschitz constant by applying spectral normalization
201   (SN) (Miyato et al., 2018) on each layer of policy network. However, applying SN usually leads
202   to severe performance loss, because the desired network-wise Lipschitz continuity is realized by
203   layer-wise Lipschitz constraints (Bhaskara et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2021). LipsNet (Song et al., 2023)
204   proposes a network-wise method, Multi-dimensional Gradient Normalization (MGN), to constrain
205   the Lipschitz constant. However, MGN needs to set an initial Lipschitz constant manually, which
206   may damage RL’s exploration ability. And MGN needs to calculate Jacobian matrix during forward
207
      propagation, which makes the policy network not applicable in high real-time tasks.
208   To overcome the above challenges, we propose the Jacobian regularization method to conveniently
209   reduce the Lipschitz constant of policy network. The Jacobian norm is commonly used as an index
210   of function’s smoothness and robustness (Hoffman et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2023). In Theorem 3.1,
211   we prove that Jacobian norm is an approximation of the local Lipschitz constant.
212   Theorem 3.1 (Lipschitz’s Jacobian Approximation). For a continuously differential neural network
213   f : Rn → Rm , the Jacobian norm ∥∇x f ∥ is an approximation of the local Lipschitz constant of f
214   on the infinitesimal neighborhood of x, i.e. K(x) ≈ ∥∇x f ∥.

      5                            C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we identify the two fundamental reasons causing action fluctuation, and propose the
533
      Fourier Lipschitz Smooth Policy Network (FlipNet). FlipNet adopts two innovative techniques to
534   directly tackle the two reasons. Firstly, we prove the Jacobian norm is an approximation of Lipschitz
535   constant and introduce the Jacobian regularization to enhance the policy smoothness. Secondly, we
536   introduce a Fourier filter layer to deal with observation noise. The layer includes a trainable filter
537   matrix that can automatically extract valuable frequencies and suppress noise frequencies. FlipNet
538   can be easily used as actor networks in most RL algorithms. Simulated and real-world experiments
539   show that FlipeNet has excellent action smoothness and noise robustness, achieving a new SOTA
      performance. We hope that the research could contribute to the applications of RL in the real world.

       Punishing the difference between consecutive actions in the reward is an effective way to smooth
1299   the actions in some environments. However, such an approach breaks the Markov property, which
1300   affects the performance, albeit to a minor extent in certain environments. Moreover, we found
1301   that adding reward penalty in a sparse reward environment increases action fluctuation rather than
1302   smoothing it, which is consistent with the finding by Chen et al. (2021) and Song et al. (2023).
1303
       Cartpole in DMControl is a sparse reward environment. The reward is 1 when the pole is within 30◦
1304
       of the vertical and 0 otherwise. We implement TD3 in this environment, punishing the difference be-
1305   tween consecutive actions in the reward. Specifically, the new reward is r = rorigin +α ∥at+1 − at ∥,
1306   where rorigin is the original sparse reward, α is the penalty coefficient and at+1 is the output of actor
1307   network under st+1 . The experiment results are summarized in Table 14. The results imply that sim-
1308   ply adding reward penalty in the sparse reward environment increases the action fluctuation ratio.
1309   Superiorly, FlipNet can smooth actions even in the sparse reward environment.

1932   FlipNet achieves smoother and more robust control with a slight increase in training time, as shown
1933   in Appendix H. In the future, we plan to optimize the backward time of FlipNet. We have devised
1934   a solution to accelerate it by using multiple forward propagation and zero-order gradient estimation
1935   to compute the Jacobian matrix. Furthermore, we plan to introduce an attention mechanism for the
1936   filter matrix H in the future works. In this way, H can vary according to different observation inputs.
1937   Additionally, We are now trying to implement FlipNet on a real-world highway vehicle. Complete
1938   results of all the above future improvements will be soon reported in our next work.
1939   As for the positive impacts on the AI community, FlipNet addresses the action fluctuation problem
1940   of RL. FlipNet breaks through the bottleneck of action fluctuation and poor robustness faced by
1941   RL, which accelerates the process of RL’s real-world application. It mitigates the wear of actua-
1942   tors, safety risks, and performance reduction caused by action fluctuation. FlipNet benefits many
1943   industrial fields, including robot control, drone control, decision-making and control of autonomous
       vehicles, and embodied AI.
