009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) is crucial for aligning Large Language Models
012            (LLMs) with human instructions. The primary goal during SFT is to select a small
013            yet representative subset of training data from the larger pool, such that fine-tuning
014            with this subset achieves results comparable to or even exceeding those obtained
015            using the entire dataset. However, most existing data selection techniques are de-
016            signed for small-scale data pools, which fail to meet the demands of real-world
017            SFT scenarios. In this paper, we replicated several self-scoring methods—those
018            that do not rely on external model assistance—on two million-scale datasets, and
019
               found that nearly all methods struggled to significantly outperform random selec-
               tion when dealing with such large-scale data pools. Moreover, our comparisons
020
               suggest that, during SFT, diversity in data selection is more critical than simply
021
               focusing on high-quality data. We also analyzed the limitations of several current
022            approaches, explaining why they perform poorly on large-scale datasets and why
023            they are unsuitable for such contexts. Finally, we found that filtering data by token
024            length offers a stable and efficient method for improving results. This approach,
025            particularly when training on long-text data, proves highly beneficial for relatively
026            weaker base models, such as Llama3.

      Existing SFT data selection methodologies, both external-scoring and self-scoring, are primarily
055   assessed using several widely recognized IT datasets, such as alpaca-GPT4 (Peng et al., 2023),
056   Dolly (Conover et al., 2023), FLAN (Longpre et al., 2023), WizardLM (Xu et al., 2024), and
057   ShareGPT (Chiang et al., 2023). These datasets are limited in size and originate from a single
058   source. However, during the SFT stage, a substantially larger scale of data, typically ranging from
059   hundreds of thousands to even millions in size, is frequently necessary. For example, Qwen2 (qwe,
060   2024) utilized over 500,000 pieces of data during the SFT process. Therefore, in practical appli-
061   cations, in order to fully utilize the inherent knowledge of LLMs, large-scale instruction-following
062   data is essential in the SFT process. Moreover, large-scale data sources not only require a sufficient
063
      amount of data, but should also have diverse data sources, such as annotated by professional workers,
      sourced from real users, or synthesized by models, and rich data types include code data, math data,
064
      conversation content, knowledge Q&A, etc.. This discrepancy creates a gap between the present
065
      SFT data selection strategies and real-world applications. In order to observe the impact brought
066   by the dataset size on the performance of different selection strategies, we analyze the difference in
067   outcomes between existing SFT data selection methods and random selection within source datasets
068   ranging from 10K-30K to 1M on Llama3-8B (AI@Meta, 2024). As shown in Figure 1, when the
069   scale of the datasets increases to 1M, these data selection methods yield suboptimal performance
070   compared with random selection. Here, “Data size 10K-300K” refers to the data sources used in the
071   original papers of different methods. “Data size 1M” refers to Openhermes2.5-1M dataset (Teknium,
072   2023).
073   Inspired by this finding, we rethink whether SFT data selection methods can work when they are
074   required to handle large-scale IT datasets. For external-scoring approaches, it is impractical to apply
075   them to tackle vast amounts of IT data due to the substantial costs (Liu et al., 2023), we hence focus
076   on the self-scoring methods. For self-scoring approaches, we refer to the article Qin et al. (2024)
077   to categorize the techniques into two types: data quality-based methods and data diversity-based
078   methods. Data quality-based methods imply that the approach lays greater emphasis on devising
079   an algorithm and evaluation metrics to compute the score of each data item. Subsequently, the
080
      selection is carried out based on the data scores. In contrast, the data diversity-based method is
      more centered around the diversity of the dataset. To explore how self-scoring methods influence
081
      LLMs’ performance when dealing with large-scale IT data, we evaluate several recent methods on
082
      two benchmarks that contain millions of instances. The findings from our experiments reveal three
083   main points:
084
085
086          • Most self-scoring data selection techniques do not significantly outperform random selec-
087            tion on large-scale datasets. Even though these self-scoring methods can achieve significant
088
               gains on small-scale datasets, their effectiveness will be greatly reduced when the data size
               increases and the data sources become complex. While the performance of certain methods
089
               does exhibit a marginal edge over the random approach when implemented on particular
090
               LLMs, a comprehensive consideration of the trade-off between effectiveness and efficiency
091            leads us to the conclusion that, when dealing with extensive data sources, random selection
092            stands out as the most preferable and advantageous option.
093
094
095          • Data diversity holds more significance than data quality during the SFT phase. Data
096            quality-based selection methods are more effective than data diversity-based methods when
097            dealing with a small-scale dataset from a single source. However, when tackling multi-
098            source data, only considering data quality is far from enough.
099
100
             • Through a comparative empirical analysis of two IT datasets, we find that it is useful to
101
               utilize token length as a criterion to conduct data filtering, yielding stable and efficient
102            results for SFT when dealing with large-scale IT data. Previous work (Liu et al., 2023) has
103            demonstrated the benefit of long texts training for models on subjective evaluation tasks
104            such as MTbench (Zheng et al., 2023) and AlpacaEval (Li et al., 2023c), we have further
105            confirmed the positive effect of long texts training on objective evaluation tasks, such as
106            Big-Bench-Hard (Suzgun et al., 2022). While utilizing token length in SFT may not yield
107            optimal outcomes on every language model, it is highly beneficial for applying it in training
               with long texts, especially on a relatively weak BASE language model, like Llama3-8B.

      2     R ELATED W ORK

110
      External-scoring Method. Lu et al. (2023) introduced an open-set instruction tagging method
111   called INSTAG, which employed ChatGPT to generate detailed tags to measure and examine the
112   variety and intricacy of human instructions for LLMs during SFT. Chen et al. (2023) presented the
113   ALPAGASUS model that used ChatGPT to evaluate each instruction and then selected various data
114   based on a certain threshold. Du et al. (2023) suggested a model-oriented instruction selection ap-
115   proach that not only considered the quality and coverage of instruction data but also incorporated the
116   necessity of instructions according to the capabilities of specific LLMs. Liu et al. (2023) introduced
117   DEITA, it used ChatGPT to iteratively enhance the complexity or quality of each data sample across
118   relevant dimensions and then requested ChatGPT to evaluate these samples for their complexity or
119
      quality. These models exceed the performance of the basic foundation models trained on complete
      datasets. However, they heavily depend on high-performing external LLMs to score data.
120
121   Self-scoring Method. Li et al. (2023b) put forward an autonomously guided method enabling LLMs
122   to discern relevant instruction pairs from open-source data. An Instruction-Following Difficulty
123   (IFD) metric was introduced to highlight inconsistencies between a language model’s anticipated
124   responses and its self-generated outputs. Wu et al. (2023) came up with DiverseEvol, which enabled
125
      the model to progressively select training subsets to enhance performance, without external over-
      sight from humans or more advanced LLMs. This approach focused on maintaining high diversity
126
      within the selected subsets, as the model opted for new data points that are most distinct from ex-
127
      isting ones based on its current embedding space. Xia et al. (2024) suggested LESS, designed to
128   pick out relevant instruction tuning data for a specific application. It utilized a gradient datastore
129   with low-dimensional gradient features, selecting examples based on their resemblance to few-shot
130   examples that represent a particular capability. Yin et al. (2024) observed that model performance is
131   inversely related to the compression ratio of training data. They introduced a universal data selection
132   method named ZIP aimed at prioritizing data subsets with low compression ratios for training LLMs.
133   Liu et al. (2024) developed SelectIT, which leveraged the inherent uncertainty in LLMs at various
134   levels—grain, token, sentence, and model—to more effectively identify high-quality instruction tun-
135   ing data, eliminating the need for additional resources. Li et al. (2023d) introduced Nuggets, which
136
      employs one-shot learning to choose high-quality instruction data. It used a scoring system based
      on the influence of candidate examples on the perplexity of a diverse anchor set, thereby facilitating
137
      the selection of the most beneficial data for instruction tuning.

      4.1   DATASETS
273
274   In practical applications, researchers frequently encounter extensive datasets from various sources
275   during SFT, which may also contain imperfections. Thus, in this study, rather than using the typically
276   employed IT datasets such as alpaca (Taori et al., 2023), we select two large-scale IT datasets at the
277   million-record level, Openhermes2.5 (Teknium, 2023) and WildChat-1M (Zhao et al., 2024), to
278   examine the efficiency of existing data selection techniques in handling large datasets and to assess
279   their performance in real-world scenarios.
280   Openhermes2.5 is presented by Teknium (2023), which comprises over 1 million data points. It
281   is significantly more comprehensive and of higher quality, predominantly consisting of generated
282   guides and chats. The dataset’s information is sourced from 16 distinct origins, including meta-
283   math (Yu et al., 2023), CamelAI (Li et al., 2023a), among others. It encompasses a wide variety of
284   subjects such as mathematics, programming, and authentic user dialogues.
285   WildChat-1M is introduced by Zhao et al. (2024) and features solely non-toxic user inputs and
286   ChatGPT responses. The dataset comprises 1 million dialogues between human users and ChatGPT,
287   with 25.53% of the interactions stemming from the GPT-4 model, and the remainder from GPT-3.5.
288   It encompasses a diverse range of user-chatbot exchanges, including ambiguous user inquiries, code-
289   switching, topic-switching, and political discussions. In this study, we extract English dialogues
290   from the WildChat dataset, resulting in over 440k interactions.

305
306   Specifically, we leverage the widely-used LLaMA3-8B (AI@Meta, 2024) and Qwen2-7B (qwe,
307   2024) as our base models, and fine-tune them using the Llama-Factory framework (Zheng et al.,
308
      2024). We train these models for 3 epochs with a batch size of 128. Our training process employs a
      cosine learning rate scheduler beginning at 7e − 6, which decays to 0.1, warms to 0.01, and utilizes
309
      an input length of 4096. To replicate our baseline methods on Openhermes and WildChat, we adjust
310
      some original parameters and implementations to fit the large-scale datasets.
311
312   In term of LESS, individual models are built and trained on specific tasks. However, in practical
313   applications, our goal is to train a model that enhances performance across various scenarios. Thus,
314
      given that the two datasets we select are both extensive and diverse, we randomly select 1000 data
      points from each dataset as Dval . Additionally, due to the volume of our data, we randomly pick
315
      10,000 data points for warm-up training, differing from the method described in (Xia et al., 2024).

320
      for Llama3, we employ sentence-level selection as the final approach. Considering that the Llama3
      family only has two public variants, Llama3-8B and Llama3-70B, and to mitigate time costs, we
321
      compute the score based solely on Llama3-8B.

      5.2   Q UALITY VS D IVERSITY
416
      Tables 1 and 3 demonstrate that the diversity-based selection strategy outperforms the quality-based
417   one. To examine whether prioritizing diversity over data quality improves data selection, we de-
418   signed a supplementary experiment by incorporating a K-means clustering process on the OpenHer-
419   mes dataset. Instead of selecting data based solely on method scores, we choose higher-scoring data
420   within each cluster to boost the final training set’s diversity.
421
      Table 5 illustrates that integrating the K-means clustering with quality-based selection methods en-
422
      hances the effectiveness for most approaches. Notably, Cross Entropy on both Llama3 and Qwen2
423   models shows improvement over 5% and 3%, respectively, when K-means is used to diversify the
424   data. This suggests that for a large-scale IT dataset, data diversity holds more importance than
425   data quality. This also clarifies why random selection often outperforms most SFT data selection
426   methods, as the random process preserves the dataset’s original distribution and diversity to the
427   greatest possible extent.

461   requires computing the score for the final data point in the target task, it is essential to meticulously
462   design a target set for each task to filter the data. However, in practical applications, we face a
463
      variety of training tasks that require our target data to be comprehensive and diverse. Hence, the
      effectiveness of LESS is strongly related to the quality of Dval .
464
465   The IFD approach determines the ultimate IFD score by evaluating the perplexity (ppl) of the re-
466   sponse. However, the length of the data significantly affects the ppl value. In particular, shorter data
467   tend to produce excessively high ppl values, which contradicts with our expected results. Ultimately,
468   we note that the IT data instructions selected by the IFD approach are quite brief, averaging merely
469
      42 tokens on Openhermes, which aligns with the findings reported by Liu et al. (2023).
470   SelectIT can perform well at the model level, but it necessitates combining LLMs with various sizes
471   to score the data. As IT datasets become larger, the computational cost required for LLMs with more
472   parameters tends to increase exponentially, which limits their applicability to extensive datasets.
473   Cross-entropy is influenced by the length of responses. Typically, cross-entropy favors data with
474   lengthy responses, whereas it shows no specific preference towards instructions. Consequently, the
475   training samples will include simple instructions but extensive responses.
476
      In addition, in this article, we do not use NUGGETS (Li et al., 2023d) as our baseline method.
477
      During our experimentation, we discover that the computational time for NUGGETS is significantly
478
      higher compared to other methods. Even with 40 A100 80G GPUs, it requires over 2,000 hours to
479   perform the calculations. Given this high time cost, we decide to abandon this method.
480
481   The diversity-based approach usually outperforms the quality-based selection methods, however,
482
      one main issue with the diversity-based approach is its time and memory consumption.
483   To reproduce DiverseEvol, we utilized 8 A100 80G resources and consistently performed 3 itera-
484   tions. However, each iteration requires 1-2 days, totaling 5-7 days to choose the final training subset.
485   When dealing with large-scale data sets, the results often fall within the random range, though opti-
      mal results occur sporadically. This may be due to modifications in our implementation to address

486
      memory constraints during replication (see Section 4.3), which may have slightly diminished the
487   method’s performance.
488
489   In contrast, ZIP does not need GPU resources, but the computing process is greedy. It incrementally
490
      adds 100 data at a time to the final training subset. For large data scales, it takes approximately 7 days
      to select 50,000 data. In addition, ZIP serves as a data selection method that operates independently
491
      of the model, meaning that the selected data cannot be adaptively tuned on the basis of the model.
492
      As illustrated in Tables 1 and 3, the data chosen by ZIP in OpenHermes perform poorly in both
493   Llama3-8B and Qwen2-7B, whereas the data selected in WildChat exhibit the best performance
494   across these models.
495
496
      Moreover, we attempt to utilize DQ (Zhou et al., 2023a) as our baseline method. However, DQ uses
      a submodular strategy to choose a subset by optimizing submodular gains within the feature space.
497
      When dealing with millions of data points, it requires more than 1TB memory resources. Eventually,
498
      we decide to forgo this approach.

501   5.4   W HICH METHOD IS THE BEST ?
502
      By examining the average results of all methods, we notice that the majority of methods perform
503
      better with WildChat as the data source compared to OpenHermes, as illustrated in Figure 2, which
504
      is rather unexpected. Nonetheless, from a quality perspective, WildChat’s conversation data tends to
505   be noisy, particularly since the context of multiple conversation rounds is sometimes unrelated, while
506   OpenHermes’s data quality should be substantially higher than WildChat. However, the performance
507   of the same data selection methods on these two types of data contradicts with our expectations. It is
508   observed that the average token length for WildChat data is 1142, whereas for OpenHermes data, it is
509   354. Drawing inspiration from the work of Shen (2024), we devise a new experiment concentrating
510   on data selection by token length. Initially, we obtain N clusters through the K-Means process and
511   subsequently select a certain amount of data based on the token length from each cluster proportional
512   to its size. The results are presented in Table 4.
513   Based on Table 4, it is evident that using token length as the criterion for data selection generally
514   yields optimal results. Specifically, for Llama3, regardless of whether the data source is Open-
515   Hermes or WildChat, the results are superior to those achieved by other methods. In addition, the
516   average score on WildChat (55.51) surpasses that obtained by fine-tuning with the entire dataset
517   (54.58). Since random selection may not ensure the best fine-tuning results, we believe that se-
518   lecting data by token length can stably obtain a relatively high training benefit, reduce the
519
      uncertainty caused by randomness, and reduce costs. This approach is particularly beneficial
      for BASE language models which generally have limited capabilities, as they tend to derive the most
520
      significant benefits from training on longer texts.

523   6     C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this study, we observe that many SFT data selection methods depend on small-scale data sets,
526   which do not meet the actual needs in real-world scenarios. This finding makes us rethink whether
527   SFT data selection methods can work when they are required to handle large-scale IT datasets. We
528   reproduce some existing self-scoring data selection approaches that do not need external LLMs’
529   support on two million-scale datasets and find that almost all present methods do not significantly
530   surpass random selection when dealing with large-scale datasets. Moreover, our analyses show that
531   during the SFT phase, data diversity in data selection plays a more significant role than data quality.
532
      In addition, using token length as the quality metric is more appropriate for SFT data selection
      compared to other carefully crafted quality metrics.
