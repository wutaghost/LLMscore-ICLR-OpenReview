                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Modern QA systems entail retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) for accurate
012            and trustworthy responses. However, the inherent gap between user queries and
013            relevant documents hinders precise matching. Motivated by our conical distribu-
014            tion hypothesis, which posits that potential queries and documents form a cone-
015            like structure in the embedding space, we introduce QAEncoder, a training-free
016            approach to bridge this gap. Specifically, QAEncoder estimates the expectation of
017            potential queries in the embedding space as a robust surrogate for the document
018            embedding, and attaches document fingerprints to effectively distinguish these
019            embeddings. Extensive experiments on fourteen embedding models across six
020
               languages and eight datasets validate QAEncoder’s alignment capability, which
               offers a plug-and-play solution that seamlessly integrates with existing RAG ar-
021
               chitectures and training-based methods.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026            “What I cannot create, I do not understand.”                  — Richard Feynman
027
028   Question Answering (QA) systems aim to generate accurate responses to user queries with appli-
029   cations in customer service (Xu et al., 2024), search engine (Ojokoh & Adebisi, 2018), healthcare
030   (Guo et al., 2022) and education (Levonian et al., 2023), necessitating proficiencies in informa-
031   tion retrieval, comprehension, and generation. Modern QA systems leverage large language models
032   (LLMs) such as ChatGPT (Achiam et al., 2023), supplemented with retrieval-augmented generation
033
      (RAG) to address issues of outdated or hallucinatory information, especially for rapidly evolving
      knowledge bases (Lewis et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2024). The efficacy of RAG
034
      hinges on its retrieval module for identifying relevant documents from a vast corpus. Dense re-
035
      trievers (Lewis et al., 2020; Hofstätter et al., 2021), contrasted with keyword-matching-based sparse
036   retrievers (Jones, 1973; Robertson & Zaragoza, 2009), have enabled efficient and precise retrieval
037   by mapping queries and documents into a shared vector space. Despite advancements, a significant
038   challenge that persists is bridging the semantic and syntactic gap between user queries and docu-
039   ments, known as the document-query gap (Zheng et al., 2020). Three main approaches have emerged
040   to address this challenge: training-based alignment, document-centric alignment, and query-centric
041   alignment.
042   Training-based approaches (Dong et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; W et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a;
043   Khanna & Subedi, 2024) fine-tune or train embedding models from scratch with dedicated QA
044   datasets to close the representation of relevant queries and documents, but struggle to fully gener-
045   alize across new domains (Suprem & Pu, 2022). Furthermore, catastrophic forgetting necessitates
046   updating embeddings for the previously encoded corpus when new data is learned (Pan et al., 2024),
047   which is resource-intensive and practically unaffordable. Document-centric approaches (Wang et al.,
048   2023b; Gao et al., 2023; Kim & Min, 2024) generate pseudo-documents for user queries by LLMs,
049   which are then used to retrieve relevant information from the corpus, bridging the document-query
050
      gap by LLMs’ zero-shot generalization capabilities. However, pseudo-document generation is both
      costly and time-consuming, and increases the risk of hallucinated information (Wang et al., 2023b).
051
      Contrastly, query-centric methods circumvent these problems.
052
053   Query-centric methods (Nogueira et al., 2019; Cheriton, 2019; Mallia et al., 2021b) generate and
      index document-relevant questions to align the indexed context with user queries. However, existing

054
      solutions are predominantly based on sparse retrievers and have not fully leveraged the potential of
055   dense retrievers. Combining query-centric methods with dense retrievers remains heavily under-
056   explored. The naive solution can be storing all QA pairs into a vector database, but with several
057   notable challenges:
058
059       C1. Expanded Index Size. Storing all QA pairs significantly increase the index size, lead-
060           ing to substantial expansion in storage requirements, especially problematic for large-scale
061           corpora.
062       C2. Prolonged Retrieval Times. The index expansion also results in extended retrieval times.
063           For dense retrievers, the expanded index size can result in linearly increased search time in
064           both exhaustive and non-exhaustive search (Douze et al., 2024) and hurt the recall perfor-
065           mance in non-exhaustive case (Zhao et al., 2023).
066       C3. Limited Query Handling. Although storing QA pairs individually can address predicted
067           queries, this approach lacks robustness when confronted with the wide-ranging and diverse
068           nature of potential queries. Such a method may be unable to effectively handle rephrasing
069           in linguistic style, sentence structure, or even vocabulary, thereby compromising the overall
070           reliability and performance of the system (Alting von Geusau & Bloem, 2020).
071
072   Motivation Inspired by Feynman’s philosophy of learning (Reyes et al., 2021), we believe that
073   effective information retrieval for QA systems extends beyond mere information storage and funda-
074   mentally depends on the active processing, involving the query formulation. This process mirrors
075   human learning, where a deeper understanding of stored knowledge is achieved through thoughtful
076   reflection and inquiry. For instance, the well-established 5W1H framework (Who, What, When,
077   Where, Why, How) (Jinks, 2019) can be employed to systematically deconstruct information, fos-
078
      tering a comprehensive and nuanced understanding. To circumvent and solve the aforementioned
      challenges, we continue the research line of query-centric methods and introduce an innovative ap-
079
      proach called QAEncoder. QAEncoder is motivated by a key observation termed conical distribution
080
      hypothesis. Specifically, for a given document, its potential queries are embedded approximately
081   within a single cluster on some hyperplane in the semantic space, while the document embedding
082   lies on the perpendicular line intersecting the cluster center. Hence, the projection of the document
083   embedding onto this hyperplane, i.e. the cluster center, is significantly closer to the potential queries,
084   and can be utilized to optimize the original document embedding.
085
      As demonstrated in Fig. 1, our method initially generates diversified queries (e.g. 5W1H) and then
086
      estimates the cluster center by the Monte Carlo method. The similarity matrix reveals that, for any
087   query, the mean-query similarity is significantly higher than both document-query and other query-
088   query similarities. Hence, we advocate using the cluster center as a surrogate for the document
089   embedding in QA systems, which bridges the document-query gap robustly without extra index size
090   and retrieval latency.
091
      Despite these advantages, the basic implementation encounters a critical challenge. While enhanc-
092
      ing similarity with user queries, it simultaneously reduces the distinguishability between document
093   representations, as they all become query-like. To address this side effect, we further introduce
094   document fingerprint strategies to infuse unique document identities into representations and enable
095   state-of-the-art performance.
096
097   Contributions     The main contributions can be summarized as follows:
098
099          • Methodological Innovations. We pioneer to bridge the document-query gap in dense re-
100            trievers from the query-centric perspective. Our method, QAEncoder, not only avoids extra
101            index storage, retrieval latency, training cost and hallucination, but also guarantees diver-
102            sified query handling and robust generalization. We further propose document fingerprint
103            strategies to address the side effect of distinguishability and achieve state-of-the-art perfor-
104
               mance.
105          • Theoretical Discovery. We formulate the conical distribution hypothesis, providing a theo-
106            retical foundation for the alignment of document and query embeddings, which is validated
107            through extensive empirical analysis and provides deeper insights into semantic space in
               QA systems.

134   2        R ELATED W ORKS
135
136
      Retrieval-augmented QA systems. Retrieval-augmented generation significantly improves large
137
      language models in QA systems by incorporating a retrieval module that fetches relevant infor-
138   mation from external knowledge sources (Févry et al., 2020; Guu et al., 2020; Izacard & Grave,
139   2021; Zhao et al., 2024). Retrieval models have evolved from early sparse retrievers, such as TF-
140   IDF (Jones, 1973) and BM25 (Robertson & Zaragoza, 2009), which rely on word statistics and
141   inverted indices, to dense retrieval strategies (Lewis et al., 2020) that utilize neural representations
142   for enhanced semantic matching. Advanced methods, such as Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2023) which
143   determines if additional information is required and evaluates the relevance of retrieved content, and
144   RAG-end2end (Siriwardhana et al., 2023) that jointly trains the retriever and generator, represent
145   significant developments in this area. However, these methods still ignore the inherent semantic gap
146
      between queries and documents.
147
148   Training-based alignment. Training-based approaches bridge the document-query gap generally
149   by contrastive learning (Xiong et al.; Qu et al., 2021) or knowledge distillation (Zhang et al., 2024a;
150   Khanna & Subedi, 2024). For instance, Dong et al. (2022) showed parameter sharing of the query
151   encoder and the document encoder improves overall performance by projecting queries and docu-
152   ments into shared space. Dual-Cross-Encoder (Li et al., 2022) and Query-as-context (W et al., 2023)
153
      train embedding models from scratch with paired document-query samples. E5 (Wang et al., 2024a)
      and GTE (Zhang et al., 2024b) concatenate different prompts before queries for instruction-tuned
154
      embedding models that enhance downstream task adaptability, especially for QA systems. However,
155
      training-based methods face high cost, generalization difficulty (Suprem & Pu, 2022), and catas-
156   trophic forgetting issue (Pan et al., 2024). Catastrophic forgetting, where the encoder continually
157   trained on new data loses previous function, is particularly detrimental as it necessitates the costly
158   re-encoding of entire corpora. Especially note that, unsupervised domain adaptation methods like
159   GPL (Wang et al., 2021), CAI (Iida & Okazaki, 2022) and AugTriever (Meng et al., 2022) are also
160   of great importance due to the annotation-free nature. E.g., GPL and AugTriever use pseudo-queries
161   as supervision for contrastive learning in new domains. However, they also face multi-domain adap-
      tation challenges like task imbalance and catastrophic forgetting (Saunders, 2022).

      Document-centric alignment. Document-centric methods, such as HyDE (Gao et al., 2023) and

      Query2doc (Wang et al., 2023b), dynamically transform user queries into pseudo-documents using

197   weights on keywords with neural estimation for more precise sparse representations. Though these
198   approaches have succeed for sparse retrievers, the integration with dense retrieval systems remains
199
      largely unexplored.

      3.2     C ONICAL D ISTRIBUTION H YPOTHESIS
238
239   In this subsection, we formally define the highly simplified conical distribution hypothesis and vali-
240   date its reasonableness with empirical analysis.
241
      Hypothesis 1 (Conical Distribution Hypothesis). For any document d, the potential queries approx-
242   imately form a single cluster on some hyperplane H = {x ∈ Rr | w · x = b} in the semantic space,
243   where w ∈ Rr is the normal vector and b ∈ R is the bias term. Furthermore, the document embed-
244   ding E(d) lies on the perpendicular line intersecting the cluster center E[E(Q(d))]. Formally, the
245   relationship can be represented as:
246
                                               E(d) ≈ E[E(Q(d))] + λw,                λ ∈ R.                                              (2)

      Fig. 3 presents the validation approach, and we leave the details in Appendix A.6. Furthermore,
249   when the stronger hypothesis assuming the cluster follows the Gaussian distribution is adopted,
250   more quantitative analysis results are derived in the appendix.

      3.3.1     D OCUMENT F INGERPRINT S TRATEGIES
266
267   The distinguishability issue arises because incorporating too much query semantics into document
268   representations suppresses their unique characteristics and renders unrelated documents more sim-
269   ilar. To address this issue, we introduce the document fingerprint strategies, which involve three
      variations that enhance the uniqueness of document representations from different perspectives.

271   and reintroduces unique identity of the original document, i.e. the document embedding E(d).
272   Specifically, QAEemb considers both the cluster center, QAEbase , and the document embedding,
273   E(d), balancing their contributions using a hyperparameter α. The adjusted embedding is formu-
274   lated as follows:

      the user query q and QAEemb (d), is mathematically equivalent to the weighted sum of the similarities

283   ument identity in a more straightforward manner. Let us define the length of text c in textual space as

      4.2.2     T RAINING - BASED AND D OCUMENT- CENTRIC M ETHODS
503   Training-based approaches mainly include two categories, fine-tuning on QA datasets and multi-
504   task instruction datasets. We choose mcontriever-msmarco and multilingual-e5-large-instruct as
505   representative models respectively. Finally, we consider HyDE (Gao et al., 2023), a well-known
506   document-centric method for comparison.
507
      Training-based and query-centric methods operate at training time and indexing time, respec-
508   tively. Therefore, integrating these approaches cloud lead to more improvements. As il-
509   lustrated in Table 4, both types of fine-tuned models significantly benefit from the QAEn-
510   coder. For instance, the mcontriever-msmarco model improves MRR from 70.1 to 77.2 on
511   FIGNEWS(Arabic); the multilingual-e5-large-instruct model’s MRR increases 8.8 and 9.1 MRR
512   points on the FIGNEWS(English) and CRUD-RAG(Chinese) datasets, respectively.
513
      For document-centric methods, we investigate the widely-reported hallucination phenomenon on
514   latest datasets (Wang et al., 2023b; Gao et al., 2023; Kim & Min, 2024). Table 4 shows the recall per-
515   formance heavily decreases for all the latest datasets and both models, attributed to the hallucination
516   of pseudo-documents. Besides, the LLM invocation for pseudo-documents is both time-consuming
517   and costly. In our case, the time for single LLM invocation is more than 2000ms while the time
518   for vector search is less than 10ms. These highlight the irreplaceable importance and practicality of
519   QAEncoder method.
520
521   5    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
522
523
      In this paper, we introduce QAEncoder, a training-free approach to bridge the document-query
524   gap for more advanced QA systems. Based on our conical distribution hypothesis and document
525   fingerprint strategies, QAEncoder substitutes document embeddings with the expectation of query
526   embeddings, enriched with document information. Notably, QAEncoder operates with zero addi-
527   tional index storage, retrieval latency, training costs, or risk of hallucination. Extensive experiments
528   across multiple datasets, languages and embedding models further confirm its robust generalization,
529   diversified query handling and compatibility with existing RAG architectures and training-based
530   methods. Future Work: Despite these benefits, the current single-cluster hypothesis is overly sim-
531   plistic and limits the performance improvement. The multi-cluster version such as Gaussian mixture
532
      models should be explored. Besides, since the query generator mainly generates simple queries,
      out-of-domain issues with complex, multi-hop queries could happen. Various strategies including
533
      data-driven proposal can be further investigated. We leave these research problems in the future
534
      work.

952
953
      QAEemb maintains competitive performance with single hyperparameter. Hence, QAEemb is recom-
954   mended for accelerating HP search.
955
956   Firstly, we believe that the optimal hyperparameters are primarily influenced by the inherent char-
957
      acteristics of the embedding model, i.e. the geometric property of embedding space. Therefore, a
      one-turn search should be sufficient for a given embedding model. That’s why we optimize hyper-
958
      parameters simultaneously across multiple datasets.
959
960   Secondly, the one-turn search can also be accelerated under our framework. Indeed, as Fig. 4
961   shows, the performance of QAEemb empirically follows a consistent trend across various models
962   and datasets: it initially rises and then falls as α increases, peaking between 0.3 and 0.6. This
963
      unimodal phenomenon enables ternary search with logarithmic trails rather than brute-force search.
964   Finally, the property of datasets also slightly influences the optimal hyperparameters. Specifically,
965   the optimal α for classical datasets is marginally lower than that for latest datasets (refer to Tables 5
966   and 6 for details). Therefore, selecting the optimal α based on classical datasets represents a cautious
967   and robust strategy, ensuring consistent improvement across both classical and latest datasets.

1096   A.7.3   N ORMALITY T EST
1097
1098   To assess whether potential queries follow a Gaussian distribution in the embedding space,we em-
1099   ploy two statistical tests: the Chi-Squared Q-Q Plot and the Anderson-Darling Test.
1100
1101   Chi-Squared Q-Q Plot Verification We employ the Chi-Squared Q-Q Plot to assess whether the
1102
       squared Mahalanobis distances 1 conform to the chi-squared distribution. By leveraging Lemma 2,
       We compare the observed D2 values with the theoretical quantiles of the chi-squared distribution
1103
       to assess the conformity of the data to a high-dimensional Gaussian model. We conclude that close
1104
       alignment of the sample points along the 45-degree reference line indicates support for the original
1105   hypothesis.
1106
1107   Direct applications of high-dimensional normality tests, such as the Henze-Zirkler test, often lead
1108
       to Type I errors in our case. E.g., testing on 768-dimensional normal samples revealed that Henze-
       Zirkler test demands high ratios of sample size to dimensionality, and it particularly susceptible to
1109
       Type I errors when the sample size is not sufficiently large. Hence, we opted to perform uni-variate
1110
       normality assessments on marginal distribution instead.
1111
1112   Anderson-Darling Test To further evaluate the normality of marginal distributions, we conduct
1113   the Anderson-Darling test across all dimensions of the embeddings. The specific steps are as fol-
1114   lows:
1115
1116        1. Null Hypothesis:
1117             • H0 : Each dimension’s marginal distribution follows a Gaussian distribution.
1118             • H1 : At least one dimension’s marginal distribution does not follow a Gaussian distri-
1119                bution.
1120        2. Testing Results:
1121           Following the Anderson-Darling test, the results across all dimensions failed to reject H0 .
1122           This suggests that each dimension’s marginal distribution can statistically be considered
1123           Gaussian, thereby supporting our hypothesis that potential queries conform to a high-
1124           dimensional Gaussian distribution.

1154   Lemma 1. (Tong, 2012) Let x follow a multivariate Gaussian distribution:
1155
1156                                              x ∼ N (µ, Σ),
1157   where µ is the mean vector and Σ is the covariance matrix. For any linear transformation Ax + b,
1158   the result is also multivariate normal:
1159
1160                                  y = Ax + b ∼ N (Aµ + b, AΣA⊤ ).
1161   Remark 2. The lemma shows that multivariate Gaussians remain Gaussian under linear transforma-
1162   tions.

       Definition 1. (Squared Mahalanobis Distance (McLachlan, 1999)) Assuming x ∼ N (µ, Σ) in a
1164   high-dimensional Gaussian distribution, the squared Mahalanobis distance D2 is defined as:
1165
1166                                      D2 = (x − µ)T Σ−1 (x − µ).
1167
       Remark 3. This metric quantifies the distance between the observed value and the mean.

       Lemma 2. (Chi-Squared Distribution of Squared Mahalanobis Distance (Mardia et al., 2024) ) If x
       is a r-dimensional Gaussian random variable, then the squared Mahalanobis distance D2 follows
1170
       a chi-squared distribution:
1171
                                                D2 ∼ χ2r ,
1172
1173
       where r denotes the dimensionality of the variable.
1174   Remark 4. The property that the squared Mahalanobis distance follows a chi-squared distribution
1175   can be viewed as a form of dimensionality reduction. By mapping a high-dimensional Gaussian
1176   variable to a scalar that encodes its deviation from the mean, adjusted for the covariance structure,
1177
       this transformation reduces the complexity of the multivariate data while preserving key statistical
       properties in a single distance metric.
