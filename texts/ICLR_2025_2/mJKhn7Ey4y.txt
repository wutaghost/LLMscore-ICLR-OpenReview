011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) is an elegant framework for learning
014            efficient control policies to perform complex robotic tasks, especially in sparse
015
               reward settings. However, concurrently learning policies at multiple hierarchi-
               cal levels often suffers from training instability due to non-stationary behavior of
016
               lower-level primitives. In this work, we introduce DIPPER, an efficient hierar-
017
               chical framework that leverages Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) to mitigate
018            non-stationarity at the higher level, while using reinforcement learning to train
019            the corresponding primitives at the lower level. We observe that directly applying
020            DPO to the higher level in HRL is ineffective and leads to infeasible subgoal gen-
021            eration issues. To address this, we develop a novel, principled framework based
022            on lower-level primitive regularization of upper-level policy learning. We provide
023            a theoretical justification for the proposed framework utilizing bi-level optimiza-
024            tion. The application of DPO also necessitates the development of a novel refer-
025            ence policy formulation for feasible subgoal generation. To validate our approach,
026
               we conduct extensive experimental analyses on a variety of challenging, sparse-
               reward robotic navigation and manipulation tasks. Our results demonstrate that
027
               DIPPER shows impressive performance and demonstrates an improvement of up
028
               to 40% over the baselines in complex sparse robotic control tasks.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The success of deep reinforcement learning (RL) is impeded in sparse reward scenarios due to
034   limitations like ineffective exploration and long-term credit assignment (Gu et al., 2016; Levine
035   et al., 2015; Nair et al., 2018). To overcome these issues, Hierarchical reinforcement learning (Sutton
036   et al., 1999; Harb et al., 2018) is an elegant framework which promises the benefits of temporal
037   abstraction and improved exploration (Nachum et al., 2019) . In the goal-conditioned hierarchical RL
038   setting (Dayan and Hinton, 1992; Vezhnevets et al., 2017) that we consider in this paper, the higher-
039
      level policy provides subgoals to a lower-level policy, which in turn tries to achieve those subgoals
      by executing primitive actions. Off-policy hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) approaches
040
      (Levy et al., 2018; Nachum et al., 2018) face significant limitations, including: Limitation L1:
041
      non-stationarity due to evolving lower-level primitive policy, and Limitation L2: infeasible subgoal
042   generation by higher-level policy(Chane-Sane et al., 2021). When the higher and lower level policies
043   are trained concurrently in HRL, due to continuously changing and sub-optimal lower level policy,
044   the higher level reward function and transition model become non-stationary. This phenomenon is
045   called non-stationarity in HRL. Further, the higher level policy may generate subgoals that are too
046   hard for the lower primitive to achieve, a phenomenon referred to as infeasible subgoal generation.
047
      A recent work by Singh et al. (Singh et al., 2024) attempts to mitigate Limitation L1 by leveraging
048   preference learning ideas from reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) (Christiano
049   et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2021). Specifically, their key idea is to utilize preference-based human
050   feedback to learn a reward function for the higher level, thereby avoiding reliance on the lower-level
051   policy for higher-level reward computation. While shown to be effective, this approach introduces an
052   additional bottleneck: first, the higher-level reward function must be learned from preference feed-
053   back, and then reinforcement learning is employed to optimize this reward to learn the higher-level
      optimal policy. Moreover, the optimal policy learned from the preference-feedback-based reward

074   pose the following question: Is there an efficient hierarchical approach for solving robotic control
075   tasks using human preference data that simultaneously addresses the issues of non-stationarity and

079   ploys Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024b) to learn the higher-level policy
080   and RL to learn the lower-level policy. The key insight is that by leveraging DPO to learn the higher-
081   level policy using preference data, DIPPER decouples higher-level policy from the non-stationary
082   lower-level primitives, thereby mitigating non-stationarity (addressing Limitation L1) in HRL.
083   Further, to address Limitation L2, we regularize the higher-level policy to predict feasible subgoals
084   to the lower-level policy. We provide a theoretical justification for this regularization via a bi-level
085   optimization formulation of HRL. The regularization term ensures that we maximize higher-level
086   rewards while constraining the lower-level primitives to remain close to optimal. Using this bi-level
087   formulation, we also derive a novel reference policy for DPO that regularizes the higher-level policy
088   to generate feasible subgoals: which we call primitive regularization.
089   To summarize, the main contributions of this work are as follows.
090
091
      1. Novel hierarchical approach (DIPPER): We introduce DIPPER, a new hierarchical framework
      for solving complex robotic control tasks using direct preference optimization (Section 4).
092
093   2. Mitigation of non-stationarity in HRL: We show that DIPPER is able to mitigate the effect of
094   non-stationarity inherent in off-policy HRL in a variety of scenarios (Section 5).
095   3. Mitigation of infeasible subgoal generation in HRL: Utilizing our bi-level optimization for-
096   mulation, we derive a primitive-enabled reference policy that regularizes the higher-level policy to
097   generate feasible subgoals (Section 4.1.2).
098
      4. Empirical success in complex tasks: We experimentally demonstrate that DIPPER demon-
099
      strates an improvement of upto 40% over the baselines in most of the task environments, outper-
100
      forming existing baselines that typically fail to make significant progress (Section 5).

104
105   Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning. HRL provides an elegant framework that promises the ben-
106   efits of improved exploration and temporal abstraction (Nachum et al., 2019). Due to this, multiple
107   hierarchical approaches have been studied in literature (Sutton et al., 1999; Barto and Mahadevan,
      2003; Parr and Russell, 1998; Dietterich, 1999). We consider a goal-conditioned setup in this work,


                                                                                   2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      where a higher-level policy provides subgoals to a lower-level policy, and the lower-level policy
109   executes primitive actions directly on the environment. In this setup, multiple prior approaches have
110   been proposed (Dayan and Hinton, 1992; Vezhnevets et al., 2017). Although it promises these in-
111   tuitive benefits, HRL has been cursed with multiple issues like non-stationarity in off-policy HRL,
112   when multiple levels are trained simultaneously. Concretely, due to continuously changing lower-
113   level primitive behavior, the higher-level replay buffer experience is rendered obsolete. Some prior
114   works deal with this issue by either simulating an optimal lower-level primitive (Levy et al., 2018),
115   or relabeling replay buffer transitions using a maximum likelihood-based approach (Nachum et al.,
116   2018; Singh et al., 2024). In contrast, we deal with non-stationarity by using preference-based learn-
117
      ing (Christiano et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2021). Concretely, we first derive a primitive-regularized
      preference-based objective, and then directly optimize the higher-level policy by employing direct
118
      preference optimization (Rafailov et al., 2024b). Some other approaches use hand-designed ac-
119
      tion or behavior priors to boost downstream learning (Nasiriany et al., 2021; Dalal et al., 2021).
120   While such approaches effectively simplify the learning process, performance in these approaches
121   depends on the quality of the designed priors. If such priors are sub-optimal, the learning algorithm
122   fails to show good performance. Another line of work uses the option learning framework (Sutton
123   et al., 1999; Klissarov et al., 2017) to learn extended macro actions. However, such approaches
124   may lead to degenerate solutions in the absence of suitable regularization. In contrast, our approach
125   uses primitive-enabled regularization for conditioning the higher-level policy to produce feasible
126   subgoals, thus avoiding such degenerate solutions.
127   Preference-based Learning. In this line of work, various approaches have been proposed that
128   perform reinforcement leaning (RL) on human preference data (Knox and Stone, 2009; Pilarski
129   et al., 2011; Wilson et al., 2012b; Daniel et al., 2015). Prior approaches first collect preference data
130   from human annotators, then use this data for downstream learning. An important initial work in
131   this area is (Christiano et al., 2017), which first trains a reward model using the preference data,
132   then uses RL to learn an optimal policy for the resulting reward model. Other recent work uses
133   more sample-efficient off-policy policy gradient approaches (Haarnoja et al., 2018) for learning
134
      the policy. Recently, direct preference optimization approach has been proposed (Rafailov et al.,
      2024b;a) that circumvents the reward model learning step, by directly optimizing the policy using a
135
      KL-regularized maximum likelihood objective. In this work, we propose a novel reference policy,
136
      which directly optimizes the higher-level policy to generate feasible subgoals for lower-level policy.

159   In our goal-conditioned hierarchical setup, in order to achieve the end goal, the higher-level policy

161   oriented towards achieving the specified subgoals. Concretely, the higher-level policy π H : S →

      3.1.1    L IMITATIONS OF STANDARD HRL APPROACHES
176
177   Although HRL promises significant advantages over non-hierarchical RL, such as improvements in
178   sample efficiency due to temporal abstraction and improved exploration (Nachum et al., 2018; 2019),
179   it suffers from serious limitations. In this work, we focus on two outstanding issues:
180
      L1: training instability due to lower-level non-stationarity in off-policy HRL;
181
      L2: performance degradation due to infeasible subgoal generation by higher-level policy.
182
183   As discussed in (Nachum et al., 2018) and (Levy et al., 2018), off-policy HRL suffers from non-
184   stationarity due to non-stationary lower primitive behavior generated by the lower-level policy. Con-
185   cretely, the higher-level replay transitions collected using previous lower-level policy become obso-
186
      lete as the lower-level policy changes. Additionally, the higher level may predict infeasible subgoals
      to the lower-level policy (Chane-Sane et al., 2021), thus impeding learning and degrading overall
187
      performance. Hence, although standard HRL provides significant advantages, it often demonstrates
188
      poor performance in practice (Nachum et al., 2018; Levy et al., 2018; Chane-Sane et al., 2021).
189   An important motivation of this work is to develop a novel preference-based learning approach that
190   directly optimizes preference-based data to address the limitations mentioned above.

      4     P ROPOSED A PPROACH
232
      In this section, we introduce DIPPER: DIrect Preference Optimization to Accelerate Primitive-
233
      Enabled Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning. To address the problem of learning control problems
234   for complex robotics tasks from human preference data, a natural approach is to apply a combination
235   of RLHF and HRL: on the outer highest tier, a reward model is learned from the human preference
236   data, on the middle tier RL is used to learn a corresponding higher-level policy for subgoal gener-
237   ation, and on the third, lowest tier RL is used to learn lower-level policies for achieving subgoals
238   specified by the higher-level policy. Together, the lower and middle tiers in this approach naturally
239   correspond to performing RLHF, while the middle and higher tiers correspond to performing HRL.
240   Though intuitively reasonable, the need to carry out three distinct learning procedures simultane-
241   ously in this approach is computationally burdensome and a more efficient method is required.
242   Our key idea. The key idea underlying DIPPER is twofold: we introduce a DPO-based approach to
243   directly learn higher-level policies from preferences, replacing the two-tier RLHF component in the
244   scheme described above with a simpler, more efficient single-tier approach; we replace the reference
245   policy inherent in DPO-based approaches, which is typically unavailable in complex robotics tasks,
246   with a primitive-enabled reference policy derived from a novel bi-level optimization formulation of
247   the HRL problem. The result is an efficient hierarchical approach that directly optimizes the higher-
248
      level policy using preference data while simultaneously mitigating non-stationarity and infeasible
      subgoal prediction issues of HRL (see Section 3.1.1) through primitive-enabled regularization.

      DIPPER-No-V: In order to illustrate the importance of primitive regularization employing lower
437   primitive value function, we implement DIPPER-No-V baseline by removing primitive regulariza-
438   tion from DIPPER. As seen from Figure 2, DIPPER performs slightly better than DIPPER-No-V
439   in simpler maze navigation task and in kitchen task. However, DIPPER significantly outperforms

      DPO-FLAT: DPO-Flat is a single-level implementation of DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024b). We im-
443   plemented this baseline to illustrate that our hierarchical DPO based approach (where the higher
444   policy is trained using DPO based maximum likelihood objective, and the lower policy is trained us-
445   ing RL) outperforms single-level DPO based policy. Since DIPPER is hierarchical, it benefits from
446   factors like temporal abstraction and improved exploration, which are missing from single-level DPO
447   implementation. However, since we do not have access to a reference policy in robotics, we replace
448   the reference policy with a uniform policy. Notably, this particular choice of reference policy effec-
449   tively reformulates the KL-objective into an entropy maximization objective in DPO-Flat, which
450   facilitates better exploration. DIPPER clearly outperforms this baseline in all the tasks, showing
451
      that our hierarchical structure is crucial for improved performance.

      6    C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this work, we propose DIPPER, a preference learning based HRL algorithm that employs direct
532
      policy optimization and primitive enabled regularization to mitigate the issues of non-stationarity
533
      and infeasible subgoal generation in HRL. We employ a bi-level optimization formulation for HRL
534   and use it to propose a novel reference policy formulation which results in our primitive regular-
535   ized maximum likelihood objective. We empirically show that DIPPER demonstrates impressive
536   performance on complex robotic control tasks, and is able to significantly outperform the baselines.
537   Additionally, our hierarchical formulation outperforms single level DPO formulation. Based on our
538   strong empirical findings, we believe that DIPPER represents a significant advancement in devel-
539   oping effective control policies for addressing complex, sparse-reward robotic tasks. Due to space
      limit, we discuss the limitations and future work in Appendix A.6.

      A.2     I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
824
825   We perform the experiments on two system each with Intel Core i7 processors, equipped with 48GB
826   RAM and Nvidia GeForce GTX 1080 GPUs. We also provide the timesteps taken for running the
827   experiments. For environments (i) − (iv), the maximum task horizon T is set to 225, 50, 50, 225
828   timesteps, respectively, and the lower primitive is allowed to execute for 15, 7, 7 and 15 timesteps,
829   respectively. In our experiments, we use off-policy Soft Actor Critic (SAC) (Haarnoja et al., 2018)
830   for optimizing RL objective, using the Adam (Kingma and Ba, 2014) optimizer. The actor and
831   critic networks are formulated as three-layer, fully connected neural networks with 512 neurons in
832   each layer. The experiments are run for 1.35e6, 9e5, 1.3E6, and 6.3e5 timesteps in environments
833
      (i) − (iv), respectively. In the maze navigation task, a 7-degree-of-freedom (7-DoF) robotic arm
      traverses a four-room maze, with its closed gripper (fixed at table height) maneuvering through the
834
      maze to reach the goal position.
835
836   For the pick and place task, the 7-DoF robotic arm gripper must locate a square block, pick it up,
837   and deliver it to the goal position. In the push task, the 7-DoF robotic arm gripper is required to
838   push the square block toward the goal position. In the kitchen task, a 9-DoF Franka robot must
839
      execute a pre-defined complex task to achieve the final goal, specifically, opening the microwave
      door. We compare our approach to the Discriminator Actor-Critic (Kostrikov et al., 2018), which
840
      is provided with a single expert demonstration. Although not explored here, combining preference-
841
      based learning and learning from demonstrations presents an intriguing research direction (Cao et al.,
842   2020).
843
844   To ensure fair comparisons, we maintain consistency across all baselines by keeping parameters
845
      such as neural network layer width, number of layers, choice of optimizer, SAC implementation
      parameters, etc., the same wherever possible. In RAPS, the lower-level behaviors are as follows:
846
      for maze navigation, we design a single primitive, reach, where the lower-level primitive moves in
847
      a straight line towards the subgoal predicted by the higher level. For the pick and place and push
848   tasks, we design three primitives: gripper-reach, where the gripper moves to a specified position
849   (xi , yi , zi ); gripper-open, which opens the gripper; and gripper-close, which closes the gripper. In
850   the kitchen task, we use the action primitives implemented in RAPS (Dalal et al., 2021).

      A.5.1     M AZE NAVIGATION TASK
961
962   In this environment, a 7-DOF robotic arm gripper navigates through randomly generated four-room
963   mazes. The gripper remains closed, and the positions of walls and gates are generated randomly.
964   The table is discretized into a rectangular W × H grid, with vertical and horizontal wall positions
965   WP and HP randomly selected from (1, W −2) and (1, H −2), respectively. In the constructed four-
966   room environment, the four gate positions are randomly chosen from (1, WP −1), (WP +1, W −2),
967   (1, HP − 1), and (HP + 1, H − 2). The height of the gripper is fixed at table height, and it must
968
      navigate through the maze to reach the goal position, indicated by a red sphere.
969   The following implementation details apply to both the higher and lower-level policies unless ex-
970   plicitly stated otherwise. The environment features continuous state and action spaces. The state is
971   represented as the vector [p, M], where p is the current gripper position, and M is the sparse maze
      array. The higher-level policy input is a concatenated vector [p, M, g], where g is the target goal


                                                             18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       position. In contrast, the lower-level policy input is a concatenated vector [p, M, sg ], where sg is
973    the sub-goal provided by the higher-level policy. The current position of the gripper is considered
974    the current achieved goal.
975
976
       The sparse maze array M is a discrete 2D one-hot vector array, where a value of 1 indicates the
       presence of a wall block, and 0 indicates its absence. In our experiments, the sizes of p and M
977
       are set to 3 and 110, respectively. The higher-level policy predicts sub-goal sg , so its action space
978
       dimension matches the goal space dimension of the lower primitive. The lower primitive action a,
979    directly executed in the environment, is a 4-dimensional vector with each dimension ai ∈ [0, 1]. The
980    first three dimensions provide offsets to be scaled and added to the gripper position for movement.
981    The last dimension controls the gripper: 0 implies fully closed, 0.5 implies half-closed, and 1 implies
982    fully open.
983
984
985    A.5.2    P ICK AND PLACE AND P USH E NVIRONMENTS
986
987    In the pick and place environment, a 7-DOF robotic arm gripper must pick up a square block and
988
       place it at a goal position set slightly above table height. This complex task requires the gripper to
       navigate to the block, close the gripper to grasp the block, and then move the block to the desired
989
       goal position. In the push environment, the 7-DOF robotic arm gripper needs to push a square block
990
       towards the goal position. The state is represented as the vector [p, o, q, e], where p is the current
991    gripper position, o is the position of the block on the table, q is the relative position of the block
992    to the gripper, and e consists of the linear and angular velocities of both the gripper and the block.
993    The higher-level policy input is thus a concatenated vector [p, o, q, e, g], where g is the target goal
994    position.
995
       The lower-level policy input is a concatenated vector [p, o, q, e, sg ], where sg is the sub-goal pro-
996
       vided by the higher-level policy. The current position of the block is considered the current achieved
997    goal. In our experiments, the sizes of p, o, q, and e are set to 3, 3, 3, and 11, respectively. The higher-
998    level policy predicts sub-goal sg , so the action space and goal space dimensions are the same. The
999    lower primitive action a is a 4-dimensional vector with each dimension ai ∈ [0, 1]. The first three
1000   dimensions provide offsets for the gripper position, and the last dimension controls the gripper (0 for
1001   closed and 1 for open). During training, the positions of the block and goal are randomly generated,
1002   with the block always starting on the table and the goal always above the table at a fixed height.

1005   A.6     L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
1006
1007   Our DPO based hierarchical formulation raises an important question. Since DIPPER employs
1008   DPO for training the higher level policy, does it generalize on out of distribution states and actions,
1009
       as compared with learning from reward model based RL formulation. A direct comparison with
       hierarchical RLHF based formulation might provide interesting insights. Additionally, it will be
1010
       challenging to apply DIPPER in scenarios where the subgoal space is high dimensional. These are
1011
       interesting research avenues, and we leave further analysis for future work.
