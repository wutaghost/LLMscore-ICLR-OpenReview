010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            With recent advances in deep reinforcement learning (RL), high-dimensional action
013            selection has become an important yet challenging problem in many real appli-
014            cations, especially in unknown and complex environments. Existing works often
015            require a sophisticated prior design to eliminate redundancy in the action space,
016
               relying heavily on domain expert experience or involving high computational com-
               plexity, which limits their generalizability across different RL tasks. In this paper,
017
               we address these challenges by proposing a general data-driven action selection
018
               approach with model-free and computational-friendly properties. Our method not
019            only selects minimal sufficient actions but also controls the false discovery rate via
020            knockoff sampling. More importantly, we seamlessly integrate the action selection
021            into deep RL methods during online training. Empirical experiments validate
022            the established theoretical guarantees, demonstrating that our method surpasses
023            various alternative techniques in terms of both performances in variable selection
024            and overall achieved rewards.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Recent advances in deep reinforcement learning (RL) have attracted significant attention, with
030   applications spanning numerous fields such as robotics, games, healthcare, and finance [23; 21; 24;
031   54]. Despite their ability to handle sequential decision-making, the practical utility of RL methods in
032   real-world scenarios is often limited, especially in dealing with the high-dimensional action spaces
033
      [48; 21; 42; 53]. High-dimensional action spaces are prevalent in “black box” systems, characterized
      by overloaded actionable variables that are often abundant and redundant. Examples include precision
034
      medicine, where numerous combinations of treatments and dosages are possible [see e.g., 19; 31];
035
      neuroscience, which involves various stimulation points and intensities [see e.g., 12]; and robotics,
036   particularly in muscle-driven robot control, where coordination of numerous muscles is required [see
037   e.g., 44]. Nevertheless, these high-dimensional action spaces often contain many actions that are
038   either ineffective or have negligible impact on states and rewards. Training RL models on the entire
039   action space can result in substantial inefficiencies in both computation and data collection.
040
      To handle high dimensionality, a promising approach is to employ automatic dimension reduction
041
      techniques to reduce the size of the action space, by selecting only the essential minimum action set
042   necessary for effectively learning the environment and optimizing the policy based on the subspace.
043   Having such a minimal yet sufficient action space can significantly enhance learning efficiency, as
044   agents can thoroughly explore a more concise set of actions [55; 22; 17]. Moreover, a smaller action
045   space can reduce computational complexity, a notable benefit in deep RL, where neural networks are
046   used for function approximation [47; 41]. In practical scenarios, eliminating superfluous actions saves
047   the cost of extensive measurement equipment and thus allows a more comprehensive exploration
048   of available actions. Yet, existing works often require a sophisticated prior design to eliminate
049   redundancy in the action space [e.g., 49; 18; 11; 32], relying heavily on domain expert experience or
050
      involving high computational complexity, limiting their generalizability across different RL tasks.
051   In this paper, we propose a general data-driven action selection approach to identify the minimum
052   sufficient actions in the high-dimensional action space. To handle the complex environments often
053   seen in deep RL, we develop a novel variable selection approach called knockoff sampling (KS) for
      online RL, with theoretical guarantees of false discovery rate control, inspired by the model-free

091   1.1   R ELATED W ORKS
092
093
      Deep reinforcement learning has made significant breakthroughs in complex sequential decision-
      making across various tasks [36; 46; 43; 15]. Yet, several considerable obstacles exist when dealing
094
      with high-dimensional spaces using deep RL. In terms of high dimensional state space, the state
095
      abstraction [35; 37] has been studied to learn a mapping from the original state space to a much
096   smaller abstract space to preserve the original Markov decision process. Yet, these methods such as
097   bisimulation can be computationally expensive and challenging when the state space is very large or
098   has complex dynamics [40]. Tied to our topic, it is hard to utilize such abstraction-based methods
099   to implement transformed actions. This redirects us to variable selection on the redundant state
100   space [see e.g., 25; 14]. Recently, Hao et al. [16] combined LASSO with fitted Q-iteration to reduce
101   states; following this context, Ma et al. [34] employs the knockoff method for state selection but with
102   discrete action spaces. However, all these works focus on the high dimensional state space in offline
103   data, while our method aims to extract sufficient and necessary actions during online learning.
104   For RL with the high-dimensional action space, especially for continuous actions, some studies
105   [11; 49] transformed the continuous control problem into the combinatorial action problem, by
106   discretizing large action spaces into smaller subspaces. However, this transformation can lead to a
107   significant loss of precision and hence produce suboptimal solutions [27; 50]. Other works [see e.g.,
      18; 32] focused on muscle control tasks and used architectures reducing the action dimensionality


                                                                               2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      before deploying RL methods. One recent study by Schumacher et al. [44] combined differential
109   extrinsic plasticity with RL to control high-dimensional large systems. Yet, all these works require
110   specialized data collection, known joint ranges of actions, forced dynamics, or desired behaviors
111   of policies, before implementing RL. In contrast, our method is entirely data-driven without prior
112   knowledge of environments, and thus can be generalized to tasks beyond muscle control.
113
      Variable selection, also known as feature selection, is a critical process to choose the most relevant
114
      variables representing the target outcome of interest, enhancing both model performance and interpre-
115   tation. Over the past few decades, many well-known methods have been established, ranging from
116   classical LASSO, Fisher score, and kernel dimension reduction [51; 13; 8], towards deep learning
117   [3; 28; 26]. Yet, these works either suffer from model-based constraints or lack theoretical guarantees.
118   The model-X knockoff method proposed by Candes et al. [7] aims to achieve both goals via a
119   general variable selection framework for black-box algorithms with guarantees of false discovery rate
120   control. Due to its model-agnostic nature, the knockoff method has been extended to complement a
121   wide range of variable selection approaches [33; 45; 29]. The main price or central challenge within
122   the knockoff method lies in the generation of faithful knockoff features. Existing techniques either
123
      using model-specific methods [see e.g., 45; 30] that assume the underlying covariate distribution,
      or model-free approaches [see e.g., 20; 39] that utilize deep generative models to obtain knockoffs
124
      without further assumptions on feature distribution. Owing to the blessing of online RL, our method
125
      bypasses this challenge through the known joint distribution of actions represented by the ongoing
126   policy network, and thus can easily resample the action values to create exact knockoff features.

      2.1   N OTATIONS
132
      Consider a Markov Decision Process (MDP) characterized by the tuple (S, A, p, r, γ), in which both
133   the state space S and the action space A are continuous. The state transition probability, denoted as
134   p : S × S × A → [0, ∞), is an unknown probability density function that determines the likelihood
135   of transitioning to a next state st+1 ∈ S, given the current state st ∈ S and the action at ∈ A.
136   The environment provides a reward, bounded within [rmin , rmax ], for each transition, expressed as
137   r : S × A → [rmin , rmax ]. The discount factor, represented by γ ∈ (0, 1), influences the weighting
138   of future rewards. We denote a generic tuple consisting of the current state, action, reward, and
139   subsequent state as (St , At , Rt , St+1 ). The Markovian property of MDP is that given the current
140   state St and action At , the current Rt and the next state St+1 , are conditionally independent of the
141
      past trajectory history. Consider At ∈ Rp where p is very large indicating a high dimensional action
      space. We utilize ρπ (st ) and ρπ (st , at ) to denote the state and state-action marginal distributions,
142
      respectively, of the trajectory distribution generated by a policy π(at | sPt ). The notation J(π) is used
143
      to represent the expected discounted reward under this policy: J(π) = t E(st ,at )∼ρπ [γ t r (st , at )] .
144   The goal of RL is to maximize the expected sum of discounted rewards above. This can be extended
145   to a more general maximum entropy objective with the expected entropy of the policy over ρπ (st ).
146
147   2.2   M INIMAL S UFFICIENT ACTION S ET IN O NLINE RL
148
149   To address the high dimensional action space, we propose to utilize the variable selection instead
150   of representation for practical usefulness. To achieve this goal, we first formally define the minimal
151   sufficient action set. Denote the subvector of At indexed by components in G as At,G with an index
152   set G ⊆ {1, 2, . . . , p}. Let Gc = {1, . . . , p}\G be the complement of G.
153   Definition 2.1. (Sufficient Action Set) We say G is the sufficient action (index) set in an MDP if
154             Rt ⊥ At,Gc | St , At,G ,       St+1 ⊥ At,Gc | St , At,G ,        for all t ≥ 0.
155
156   The sufficient action set can be seen as a sufficient conditional set to achieve past and future
157   independence. The sufficient action set may not be unique.
158   Definition 2.2. (Minimal Sufficient Action Set) We say G is the minimal sufficient action set in an
159   MDP if it has the smallest cardinality among all sufficient action sets.
160
161   Unlike the sufficient action set, there is only one unique minimal sufficient action set to achieve
      conditional independence if there are no identical action variables in the environment. We also call


                                                          3

      Gc the redundant set when G is the minimal sufficient action (index) set. Here, to achieve such a
163   minimal sufficient action set, one should also require the states St be the sufficient states, so there is
164   no useless state [34] to introduce related redundant actions that possibly lead to ineffective exploration
165   or data inefficiency. Without loss of generality, we assume sufficient states throughout this paper and
166   focus on eliminating redundant actions in a high-dimensional action space. Our goal is to identify the
167   minimal sufficient action set for online deep reinforcement learning to improve exploration.

197   3     O NLINE D EEP RL WITH VARIABLE S ELECTION
198
199   To identify the minimal sufficient action set in online deep RL, we integrate the action selection
200   into RL to find truly influential actions during the training process. Its advantages are manifold.
201   Firstly, its model-agnostic nature ensures compatibility across various RL architectures and algorithms.
202   Moreover, its data-driven characteristic allows for straightforward application across diverse scenarios,
203
      thereby increasing practical utility. Crucially, the action selection boosts the explainability and
      reliability of RL systems by clearly delineating actions that contribute to model performance. In the
204
      following, we first introduce an action-selected exploration strategy for online deep RL in Section
205
      3.1, followed by the model-free knockoff-sampling method for action selection in Section 3.2.

      5    E XPERIMENTS
379
380   Experiment Setup We aim to answer whether the variable selection is helpful for deep RL training
381   when the action dimension is high and redundant. We conduct experiments on standard locomotion
382   tasks in MuJoCo [52] and treatment allocation tasks calibrated from electronic health records (EHR),
383   the MIMIC-III dataset [19]. The environment details are in Table B.1 of Appendix. Here, we
384   focus on two representative actor-critic algorithms, Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) [43] and
385   Soft-Actor-Critic (SAC) [15]. We adopt the implementation from Open AI Spinning up Framework
386   [1]. For SAC, the implementation involves fitting both Qϕ and πθ , and we use a mask on both
387   components. For PPO, it fits Vθ and πθ , hence we only combine the mask with πθ . Tables A.1 and
388
      A.2 summarize the hyperparameters we used. We set the FDR rate α = 0.1 and voting ratio Γ = 0.5
      in all settings. All the experiments are conducted in the server with 4× NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU.
389
390   Semi-synthetic MuJoCo Environments We choose three tasks: Ant, HalfCheetah, and Hopper. To
391   increase the dimension of action space, we artificially add extra p actions to the raw action space and
392   consider two scenarios p = 20 and 50. For each setting, we run experiments over 2 × 105 and 106
393   steps for SAC and PPO, respectively, averaged over 10 training runs. The running steps for SAC and
394
      PPO are set adaptively to obtain better exploration for each method and save computation costs, as the
      main goal is to show how action selection can improve sample efficiency rather than compare these
395
      two methods. For each evaluation point, we run 10 test trajectories and average their reward as the
396
      average return. Besides RL algorithm performance, we also evaluate variable selection performance
397   in terms of True Positive Rate (TPR), False Positive Rate (FPR), and FDR.
398
399   Action Selection in the Initial Stage of Training We utilize action selection in the beginning
400
      stage of the training. For both methods, we utilize the first 4000 samples for variable selection
      and then use the selection results to build a hard mask for action in deep RL models. We compare
401
      our knockoff sampling (KS) method with the baseline of selecting all actions (All) to evaluate the
402
      impact of integrating a masking mechanism with a selection strategy in deep RL. We also provide
403   the experimental results with only ground-truth actions selected (True) as a reference. To reduce
404   the computational complexity, we choose LASSO [51] as our base variable selection algorithm
405   for KS. Here, selecting all actions (All) and ground-truth actions (True) are the cases where RL
406   models trained on whole action space and minimal sufficient action space, respectively. Hence, the
407   model corresponding to ground-truth action has smaller parameters than all other methods because
408   its initialization is based on the minimal sufficient action set. The results are shown in Fig. 3 for
409   PPO, Fig. B.1 for SAC, and Table 1 for all numerical details. Due to space constraints, we mainly
410   present the PPO figures in the main text. In all cases, we find that KS-guided models outperform
411
      those trained on the whole action space in terms of average return and much lower FDR and FPR,
      with larger improvement gains as p increases. This empirically validates our theory of FDR control
412
      with the proposed KS method, demonstrating that action selection can enhance learning efficiency
413
      during the initial stages of RL training where action space is high and redundant.
414
415   Action Selection in the Middle Stage of Training To show whether action selection can be used
416   in the middle stage of training to remedy the inefficiency brought by exploring the whole action
417
      space, we conduct experiments where in the first half of the training steps the models are trained
      on the whole action space, and in the middle of the stage, we utilize action selection and build hard
418
      masks for them and then continue training for the rest of the steps. We compare our KS method
419
      with selecting all actions (All) and ground-truth actions (True) similarly. The results in Fig. 4 and
420   Fig. B.2 reveal a notable pattern: agents initially struggle to learn effectively, but mid-stage variable
421   selection significantly improves their performance, with models trained on the correct actions. This
422   demonstrates the effectiveness of mid-stage variable selection in enhancing learning outcomes.
423
      Treatment Allocation for Sepsis Patients We test our method with PPO and utilize the first 1000
424
      samples for action selection in the initial stage. We also include two additional baselines: Lattice
425
      [9], and gSDE [38]. Lattice and gSDE also use all actions but additionally incorporate temporally
426   correlated Gaussian noise into the training. We run experiments over 5 × 104 time steps and average
427   results over 5 runs. For each evaluation point, we run 5 test trajectories and average their reward
428   as the average return. The results are shown in Table 2 and Fig. 5. Lattice and gSDE exhibit high
429   variance and instability in this environment, likely due to over-exploration. In contrast, our method
430   consistently delivers stable and superior performance compared to the others. The analysis in Fig. 5
431   reveals that our methods effectively identify sepsis-influencing treatments that are relevant to key
      body factors, demonstrating the potential for real-world medical applications.

      Action Selection is Fast and Lightweight With just a few thousand data points and a lightweight
464
      machine learning algorithm like random forest or LASSO, the whole action selection process outlined
465   in Algorithm 2, completes in under 20 seconds—including knockoff threshold determination. This
466   is significantly faster and less computationally intensive than the RL training part. Even when
467   incorporating more sophisticated feature selection methods, the additional computational overhead
468   remains negligible compared to the time required for RL training. Moreover, for the RL agent’s deep
469   neural network, only a few lightweight masking parameters are introduced, which have minimal effect
470   on both training and inference speed. Yet, these in turn substantially enhance policy optimization.
471   Supporting Analyses We conduct additional experiments for the variation of action distributions

      6                        C ONCLUSION , L IMITATION , AND F UTURE W ORK
477
      In this work, we address the high-dimensional action selection problem in online RL. We formally
478
      define the objective of action selection by identifying a minimal sufficient action set. We innovate
479
      by integrating a knockoff-sampling variable selection into broadly applicable deep RL algorithms.
480   Empirical evaluations in synthetic robotics and treatment allocation environments demonstrate the
481   enhanced efficacy of our approach. Yet, a notable constraint of our method is its singular application
482   during the training phase, coupled with the potential risk of overlooking essential actions with weak
483   signals. Inadequate action selection could degrade the agent’s performance. Intriguing future research
484   includes extending our methodology to incorporate multiple and adaptive selection stages. This
485   adaptation could counterbalance initial omissions in action selection. Additionally, formulating an
      effective termination criterion for this process represents another compelling research direction.
