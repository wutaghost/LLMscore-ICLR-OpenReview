009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In recent decades, there have been substantial advances in time series models and
012            benchmarks across various individual tasks, such as time series forecasting, clas-
013            sification, and anomaly detection. Meanwhile, compositional reasoning in time
014            series prevalent in real-world applications (e.g., decision-making and composi-
015            tional question answering) is in great demand. Unlike simple tasks that primarily
016
               focus on predictive accuracy, compositional reasoning emphasizes the synthesis
               of diverse information from both time series data and various domain knowledge,
017
               making it distinct and extremely more challenging. In this paper, we introduce
018
               Compositional Time Series Reasoning, a new task of handling intricate multistep
019            reasoning tasks from time series data. Specifically, this new task focuses on var-
020            ious question instances requiring structural and compositional reasoning abilities
021            on time series data, such as decision-making and compositional question answer-
022            ing. As an initial attempt to tackle this novel task, we developed TS-Reasoner, a
023            program-aided approach that utilizes large language model (LLM) to decompose a
024            complex task into steps of programs that leverage existing time series models and
025            numerical subroutines. Through a comprehensive set of experiments, we demon-
026            strate that our simple but effective TS-Reasoner outperforms existing standalone
027
               reasoning approaches. These promising results indicate potential opportunities in
               the new task of time series reasoning and highlight the need for further research.

032   Over the past few decades, research in time series analysis has heavily focused on improving the
033   performance of individual tasks such as time series forecasting, anomaly detection, and time series
034   classification (De Gooijer & Hyndman, 2006; Kirchgässner et al., 2012; Zong et al., 2018; Dau
035   et al., 2019; Hamilton, 2020; Jin et al., 2024). These results have benefited various areas such as
036   risk assessment in finance, disease diagnose in healthcare, pandemic modeling in public health and
037   event detection in natural and social science (Tsay, 2005; Cao et al., 2022; 2023c; Kamra et al.,
038   2021; Team & Murray, 2020; Penfold & Zhang, 2013; Cheng et al., 2021; Sharma et al., 2021;
039
      Zhang et al., 2021).
040   However, most real-world applications demand multi-step reasoning, where well-established tasks
041   should serve as intermediate steps. A typical example is forecasting future energy supply (Zheng
042   et al., 2022), in which scientists must integrate domain knowledge with statistical analysis. The pro-
043   cess begins with the examination of time series data to forecast future signals with statistical meth-
044   ods, following by formulating constraints based on domain expertise to refine predictions. Another
045
      typical example is analyzing climate time series (Mudelsee, 2010), where it is not only necessary
      to predict what happens next but also crucial for experts to comprehend the fundamental physical
046
      laws governing such data. For instance, a climate scientist studying the impact of greenhouse gas
047

052   ent specialists - climatologists for temperature forecasting, oceanographers for sea-level analysis,

126   Time Series Analysis Tasks and Models Classical time series analysis encompasses several key
127   tasks that leverage patterns and trends in data over time. These tasks include forecasting, imputation,
128
      classification, and anomaly detection. Each of these tasks serves unique purposes across various
      application domains, highlighting the significance of time series analysis.
129
130   To tackle time series analysis, researchers have made significant contribution over the years. Early
131   methods were mainly task-specific, where each individual task was addressed by a dedicated model
132   optimized for its specific purpose, resulting in a fragmented approach. Recently, inspired by the
133   emergence of large language models, there has been a shift toward general-purpose Large Time
134
      Series Models. Notable contributions include work by (Gruver et al., 2024), who simply encoded
      time series as strings, and (Jin et al., 2023), who converted time series into language representations
135
      through alignment. (Cao et al., 2023b) and (Pan et al., 2024) incorporated decomposition techniques
136
      and prompt design, enabling generalization to unseen data and multimodal scenarios. (Zhou et al.,
137   2023) adapted GPT-2 as a general-purpose time series analysis model, extending it to various tasks.
138   Additionally, (Talukder et al., 2024) utilized VQVAE as a tokenizer for transformers, while (Ansari
139   et al., 2024) employed scaling and quantization techniques for embedding time series. These models
140   are designed to handle multiple preset tasks and are jointly pre-trained on diverse datasets. However
141   they still operate under predefined task definitions, which limits their ability to perform complex and
142   compositional reasoning. As a result, while they can handle multiple tasks, they lack the flexibility
143   to adapt to more intricate scenarios that require a deeper understanding on task instructions and
144   composition of different tasks or concepts.
145
146   Complex and Compositional Reasoning with Pre-trained Foundation Models Large Language
147   Models (LLMs) have demonstrated significant capabilities in managing complex reasoning tasks
148
      by emulating human cognitive processes, especially when incorporated with appropriate in-context
      samples (Huang & Chang, 2022; Qiao et al., 2022; Ahn et al., 2024; Qu et al., 2024). The Chain
149
      of Thought (CoT) prompting method (Wei et al., 2022) is a prime example, encouraging models to
150
      articulate intermediate reasoning steps (i.e. rationales) before reaching a conclusion. This method
151   improves performance in multi-step logical deductions by transparently demonstrating the thought
152   process leading to an answer as well as expanding the expressive power of Transformer architec-
153   ture Feng et al. (2023); Merrill & Sabharwal (2023). Following CoT, more rationale dependency
154   structures are proposed to capture more reasoning paradigms, such as Tree-of-Thoughts, Graph-of-
155   Thoughts and Self-Consistent CoT Yao et al. (2023a;b); Wang et al. (2022).
156
      Moreover, program-based reasoning (Zhu et al., 2022; Jung et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022; Khot
157   et al., 2022; Creswell & Shanahan, 2022; Gao et al., 2023) represents an advanced form of eliciting
158   complex reasoning from LLMs. This involves framing reasoning tasks as code generation, where the
159   model is trained or prompted to understand and manipulate logical constructs akin to programming

      3       TASK D EFINITION
185
      In this section, we first define compositional time series reasoning, which involves the synthesis of
186
      information from temporal data in conjunction with task-specific instructions and contextual knowl-
187   edge.
188
      Definition 3.1 (Compositional Time Series Reasoning). Let x denote a time series, which is a
189
      sequence of data points indexed in time order. Let C represent the context, which encompasses the
190   task instruction and additional external information. The primary objective of time series reason-
191   ing is to derive a set of rationales R = (r1 , r2 , . . . , rn ) that are conditional on the inputs x and
192   C in an step-by-step manner. Each ri addresses a single sub-task related to the time series, e.g.
193   r1 tackle missing value imputation, r2 tackle forecasting, and r3 tackle numerical reasoning and
194   optimization. Then we can generate the final answer y to the task based on both rationales and the
195   input, mathematically expressed as:
196                  ri = f (r1 , ..., ri−1 , x, C) ⇒ y = g(R, x, C) = g(r1 , ..., rn , x, C)
197
198
      Here, f is a function that constructs the next rationale bsed on previous rationales and inputs; g is
      a function that maps the generated rationales R along with the inputs x and C to the final response
199
      y, and g. The rationales R serve as intermediary results or conclusions that facilitate the reasoning
200
      process and may exhibit various probabilistic structural dependencies. The most common one is the
201   sequential dependency, on which we define Chain of Thought (CoT)(Feng et al., 2023):
202
                                        ri ∼ pθ (ri |r1 , r2 , ..., ri−1 , x, C)                        (1)
203
204
      where pθ is a Large Language Model (LLM), and every rationale is generated fully by the LLM
      based on auto-regressive decoding.
205
206   In this paper, we apply an alternative paradigm due to the nature of time series as structured data,
207   which is Program-based Reasoning, whose mathematical expression is as follows:
208                  fi ∼ pθ (f1 , f2 , ..., fi−1 , x, C) y = g(R, x, C) = g(f1 , ..., fn , x, C)        (2)
209   where pθ is a LLM that can generate code, and f1 , ..., fi are program sentences generated by pθ .

301
302   For causal mining, we synthesize a set of data grounded in domain knowledge related to climate
303   science, finance, and economics. Specifically, we generate a series of multivariate time series data
304   based on established causal relationships and meteorological principles. Each test sample consists
305
      of a time series dataset of various variables, accompanied by a natural language instruction that

306

345
      Task Decomposer The proposed framework handles time series data by integrating program-
346
      based decomposition and task-specific models. As illustrated in Fig.3, the core idea revolves around
347
      a programmatic task decomposition engine, which we refer to as the ”Problem Decomposer”. This
348   component is responsible for disassembling complex tasks into a series of smaller, manageable
349   subtasks, each described in a programmatic manner. These subtasks are subsequently addressed
350   by distinct processing modules, enabling the framework to provide robust, step-wise solutions to
351   time series-related problems. In TS-Reasoner , We use ChatGPT-3.5-turbo as our task decomposer.
352   We leverage the in-context learning ability of pretrained language model and construct question-
353   program pairs as in-context examples. The in context examples are carefully constructed so that
354   the samples questions are equally distributed across the four question types (Financial Investment
355   Strategy, Future Stock Characteristic Prediction, Energy Load Perdiction with known knowledge,
356
      Causal Relation). As shown in Fig. 3 , every in-context sample is a question program pair where
      the question is described in natural language and the program is pseudo-code like. Please refer to
357
      section C.1 for prompts given to task decomposer.
358
359   The decomposition of tasks allows for targeted processing through three types of modules, each
360   specialized for different aspects of the reasoning process:
361   Time Series Model Modules: These modules are grounded in foundation time series models and
362   are primarily responsible for handling standard operations such as forecasting, anomaly detection,
363   trend analysis, and other predictive or diagnostic tasks. Their purpose is to leverage established
364   models in the field to process data-driven subtasks with high precision and efficiency.
365
      Numerical Method Modules: A second class of modules focuses on numerical and statistical meth-
366   ods. These modules are particularly adept at performing quantitative manipulations on the data, such
367   as extracting trends, computing ranges, and conducting basic arithmetic or statistical analyses. The
368   application of numerical techniques allows for a clearer interpretation of time series dynamics, par-
369   ticularly in tasks where precise quantitative reasoning is required.
370
      Custom Module Generation via Large Language Models (LLMs): The third type of module
371
      addresses a significant challenge in time series reasoning: the handling of external knowledge and
372   user-specific instructions that cannot be predefined. In many real-world scenarios, users may incor-
373   porate unique external knowledge (i.e. maximum or minimum of the value range in forecasting) or
374   requirement, which are often expressed in natural language within the input instructions. To handle
375   such custom requirements, the framework includes a “Custom Module Generation Function,” which
376   calls on a large language model (LLM) to interpret the natural language directives. The LLM trans-
377   lates these personalized constraints and objectives into programmatic code, wrapping them into a
      callable module that integrates seamlessly with the broader reasoning process.

      Evaluation Protocol In decision making, the overall objective and specific user requirements may
413   be different. For this reason, we respectively report the performance of models on each kind of in-
414   stances. The user’s main objective is to maximize the total profit/ minimize the loss. The customized
415   requirements can be generally divided to: Profit Percent Guarantee (the decision needs to guarantee
416   the minimum profit percent that the user expected), Risk Tolerance (the volatility of the investment
417   portfolio must be within an expected range), and Budget Allocation (control the budget for a spe-
418   cific stock). In evaluation, we focus on two types of metrics: success rate (SR), absolute average
419   profit (AAP) and relative average profit (RAP). The strict success rate is defined as the percentage
420   of test samples that did not violate any constraint and requirements. The average absolute profit is
421
      the profit that the model made on all successful instances. The relative average profit is defined as
      the relative profit gain over the vanilla investment strategy that do not consider the requirements in
422
      the instructions. In Profit Percent and Budget Allocation task, we aim at improving the profit over
423
      the vanilla strategy. In Risk Tolerance, the model is required to first ensure the risk and minimize
424   the profit reduction over the vanilla strategy.

505
506   Evaluation Protocol In causal relationship recognition, TS-Reasoner is given multi-variable time
507   series, a description to the data, and expert knowledge of percentage of true relationships. The
508   model need to incorporate the expert knowledge and the causal discovery tools based on directed
509   acyclic graph to infer the probable causal relationship across multiple variables. In evaluation, we
510
      focus on three types of metrics: success rate (SR), causal relationship accuracy (CRA) and causal
      graph accuracy (CGA). The success rate is defined as the percentage of test samples in which the
511
      model successfully execute the task and did not violate any constraint in the instructions (e.g. the
512
      percentage of the causal relationships). The causal relationship accuracy is defined as the accuracy
513   of classifying each pair of variable as causally related or not. The causal graph accuracy is defined
514   as the percentage of test samples of which all causal relationships are correctly classified.
515
516
      Overall Performance In the causal relation recognition task, TS-Reasoner outperform the CoT
517
      and CoT + code on all metrics, as shown in Fig. 5. It is also noticeable that the performance of
518
      all methods on CGA, which is the hardest evaluation metric, are not satisfactory. Specifically, both
519   CoT-based methods acquired 0.0 accuracy, which means that for any given test instance, none of
520   these approaches can correctly infer all pairs causal relationships within it. Although TS-Reasoner
521   slightly outperforms the baselines, the result is still very modest, opening opportunities for future
522   works on addressing challenges under this setting.

525   7     C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this work, we introduced the task of Complex and Compositional Time Series Reasoning. To
528   support this task, we curated a specialized dataset from multiple domains and designed a domain-
529   specific evaluation protocol for rigorous assessments. Building on this foundation, we developed
530   TS-Reasoner, a simple yet effective model that integrates program-based task decomposition with
531
      LLMs and domain-specific modules. By combining time series models, numerical techniques, and
      LLM-generated custom modules, TS-Reasonerachieves a balance between numerical precision and
532
      flexibility, enabling it to handle a variety of tasks requiring domain expertise and personalized
533
      decision-making. Unlike purely LLM-driven methods that often suffer from numerical inaccura-
534   cies and limited domain adaptability, TS-Reasoner leverages a structured decomposition approach
535   to mitigate these limitations. This integration provides a versatile framework for domain experts to
536   streamline complex multi-step analyses. For future work, we plan to expand the dataset to encom-
537   pass more diverse domains, explore techniques for incorporating world knowledge from LLMs into
538   the reasoning process, and enhance the toolbox with more powerful modules. These advancements
539   aim to further elevate TS-Reasoner’s capabilities in compositional and domain-specific time series
      analysis.

888   ’increasing’, ’decreasing’, or ’steady’.

      - MultiPreOP: This is a multivariate time series forecasting operator used to accurately predict the

      - RefGenOP: This operator is primarily used to generate a corresponding function based on the
      requirement described in a time series forecasting task. The generated function can then be applied

      to ‘data‘ to meet the specified conditions. The input consists of a single variable, ‘requirement‘,
898
      which represents the requirement description from the task. The output is a function generated
899   according to the task requirement.

901   - ApplyOP: This operator functions as an executor, applying the input function to the corresponding
902
      data. The input consists of two variables: ‘func‘, which represents a function (such as a requirement
      function generated by RefGenOP), and ‘data‘, which represents the data to be processed. The output
903
      is obtained by passing ‘data‘ through the ‘func‘ function.

906   vestment strategy based on the user’s investment goals. The input consists of a single variable,
907   ‘obj information‘, which contains the user’s description of their investment objectives (including
908
      expected profit, etc.). The operator utilizes large language models to interpret the investment-related
      information provided by the user and generates an optimization objective function. The output is the
909
      optimization objective function derived from the input investment information.
910
911   - ConGenOP: This operator is designed to generate a constraint function based on the user’s
912   investment-related constraints. The input consists of a single variable, ‘con information‘, which
913   contains the user’s description of their investment constraints (such as budget limits and acceptable
914
      risk tolerance). The operator leverages large language models to interpret the investment-related
      information provided by the user and generates a corresponding constraint function. The output is
915
      the constraint function derived from the input investment information.

      input consists of four variables: ‘data‘, ‘constraint‘, ‘future length‘, and ‘objective‘. ‘data‘ repre-
