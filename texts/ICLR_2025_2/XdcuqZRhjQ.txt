011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Humans engage in lifelong social interactions through interacting with different
014            people under different scenarios for different social goals. This requires social
015            intelligence to gather information through a long time span and use it to navigate
016            various social contexts effectively. Whether AI systems are also capable of this is
017
               understudied in the existing research. In this paper, we present a novel benchmark,
               LIFELONG-SOTOPIA, to perform a comprehensive evaluation of language agents
018
               by simulating multi-episode interactions. In each episode, the language agents
019
               role-play characters to achieve their respective social goals in randomly sampled
020            social tasks. With LIFELONG-SOTOPIA, we find that goal achievement and be-
021            lievability of all of the language models that we test decline through the whole
022            interaction. Although using an advanced memory method improves the agents’
023            performance, the best agents still achieve a significantly lower goal completion
024            rate than humans on scenarios requiring an explicit understanding of interaction
025            history. These findings show that we can use LIFELONG-SOTOPIA to evaluate
026            the social intelligence of language agents over lifelong social interactions. The
027            code and data will be open sourced upon acceptance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Social interactions occur when two or more individuals (or agents) engage with one another, with
033
      each person’s behavior being influenced by the actions of others (Reis & Wheeler, 1991; Turner,
      1988). These interactions are a fundamental part of human lives, as people continuously teach,
034
      learn, and converse with others throughout their lifetime (Hari et al., 2015). During such exchanges,
035
      individuals analyze the behavior of others, make inferences about their personalities, anticipate ac-
036   tions, and adjust their own behavior accordingly (German & Robbins, 2020; Pianesi et al., 2008).
037   This capacity to understand others’ behavior, interpret their thoughts and feelings, and adapt one’s
038   own actions is known as social intelligence (Marius, 2022; Zhou et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a). Peo-
039   ple with high social intelligence are skilled at managing these interactions, especially as they are
040   able to refine their communication methods by gaining more information about the people they are
041   interacting with. This allows them to achieve their desired outcomes in various social situations
042   (Holloway & Morse, 2020).
043   Recent literature focuses on developing socially intelligent large language model (LLM)-based
044   agents that can navigate social situations with human-like decision-making abilities (Mathur et al.,
045   2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Park et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b). Evaluating these
046   agents has also been a major area of interest, with methods ranging from static text benchmarks
047   (Sap et al., 2019; Le et al., 2019) and static video benchmarks (Zadeh et al., 2019) to dynamic en-
048   vironments (Zhang et al., 2024a; Zhou et al., 2023). However, a defining feature of human social
049   interactions is their dynamic and lifelong nature, where the social goals of individuals change con-
050
      tinuously, and they also gather new information about others to adjust their behavior accordingly.
      This requires reasoning about past interactions and adapting their responses, which will be useful
051
      for building a rich common ground between users and AI agents.
052
053   However, whether language agents are capable of navigating social scenarios and challenges over
      long time periods also remains an open question. To address this gap, we introduce the LIFELONG-

054
      SOTOPIA benchmark (Figure 1), designed to evaluate language agents over lifelong social interac-
055   tions.
056
057   LIFELONG-SOTOPIA simulates the interaction between pairs of characters through multiple
058
      episodes. In each episode, two agents role-playing the characters will be assigned private social
      goals, and a shared social context. After each episode, the two agents will be evaluated based on
059
      their believability and whether they have achieved their social goals. To simulate lifelong interac-
060
      tions, we sample multiple episodes sequentially between two characters while providing them with
061   a memory of their past interactions as context. Scenarios for these episodes are generated using
062   GPT-4 (§3). The characters are role-played by LLM-based agents, including GPT-4o (ope, 2024),
063   Gemini-1.5 (gem, 2023), Llama-3.1 (dub, 2024), and also by humans to establish a baseline for ideal
064   performance. We analyze the Believability (how believable the character’s conversations are) and
065   Goal Completion (how successful the agent is at achieving its social goal) scores over time as the
066   characters progress through episodes and their context increases.
067   The closest work to ours is Generative Agents (Park et al., 2023), which demonstrates how LLMs
068   and computational interactive agents can be combined to enable believable proxies of human be-
069   havior. Their evaluation shows that these agents produce credible individual and emergent social
070   behaviors. However, the work mainly focuses on showcasing the abilities of LLMs at simulating
071   social interactions rather than developing a systematic evaluation framework for these simulated in-
072   teractions (Zhou et al., 2023). In contrast, our work focuses on benchmarking the performance of
073   language agents in social intelligence. We achieve this by analysing their scores on the B EL and
074   G OAL dimensions (§3.4), and provide insights into how these agents compare to humans.
075   Using our method to simulate lifelong social interactions, we aim to answer the following research
076   questions:
077
      RQ1 (Consistency): Can the models maintain consistency over long-term social interactions, staying
078
      true to their character?
079
080   RQ2 (Social Intelligence): Are the models capable of using information from previous episodes to
081   optimize their goals in the current interaction, thus mimicking human behavior?
082   RQ3 (Memory Utilisation): Does equipping the models with an advanced memory improve their
083   performance, and can they maintain this performance in harder social scenarios that require explicit
084   use of memory?
085
      Two different sets of experiments are conducted, varying the memory provided to the language
086   agents from previous episodes. In the first approach, the entire prior interaction is provided as
087   memory. In the second, a more advanced memory approach is implemented, where only specific
088   knowledge gained in an episode — such as new strategies learnt or information gained about the
089   other character - is retained while the rest of the conversation is filtered out to make the reasoning
090   process easier for the language agents. Additionally, we test this advanced memory approach with
091   hand-crafted scenarios, which are a more challenging version of the previously sampled scenar-
092   ios. These scenarios require an explicit understanding of past conversations to evaluate whether the
093   language agents can match human performance.
094   For RQ1, our findings indicate that model consistency declines when using the entire interactions
095   as memory. Regarding RQ2, the declining trend in G OAL for the simple memory module suggests
096   that these language agents lack social intelligence, whereas humans consistently perform well across
097   both dimensions. In response to RQ3, the model performance improves significantly upon using the
098   advanced memory module. When tested on the harder scenarios, the agents maintain their consis-
099   tency, but their performance on G OAL declines significantly. Such a a trend highlights that these
100
      models fall short of humans in terms of social intelligence and utilizing past memories to achieve
      their social goals effectively.

152

153   (§3.4). Each dimension is rated by GPT-4 (ope, 2024) and humans on a Likert scale. The scores
154   of different dimensions have three types of range: [0, 10], [−10, 0] and [−5, 5]. The paper shows
155   that when evaluating language models with SOTOPIA-EVAL, GPT-4 could serve as a proxy of
156   human judgment on these dimensions, and it has a higher correlation and significance than human
157   evaluations. Thus we also utilise GPT-4 as our primary evaluator for all the experiments.

188   A memory module consists of three main components: (1) Memory sources, which refers to where

193

194
      ory contents. This includes: (a) Memory writing, which decides what part of the information will
195   be stored as memory, (b) Memory management, which involves removing redundant or unimportant
196   memories, merging similar ones, and creating higher-level abstractions, and (c) Memory reading,
197   which refers to extracting information relevant to the current scenario for decision-making. Based
198   on this, we propose two different approaches for implementing the memory modules in §3.3.

      3.1   DATASET P REPARATION
203
204   There are three main components of our dataset in SOTOPIA including: (1) Characters, represent-
205   ing the profiles of the role-playing characters as defined in §2.1, with their details including their
206   name, age, occupation, gender, personality, etc. (2) Relationships, which detail the relationships the
207   characters may possess with other characters in the dataset. They can either be strangers, know each
208   other by name, acquaintances, friends, romantic partners or family members. (3) Scenarios, which
209   outline the scenarios in which the characters will participate, also detailing the goals of each agent
210   and certain constraints on the character profiles such as on their age, occupation, or relationship with
211   the other agent.
212   We directly use the 40 characters and 90 relationships provided in the SOTOPIA database. The
213   scenarios in our framework are sampled based on the constraint on the relationship between the
214   agents (§3.2), and hence we require an equal number of scenarios for all relationship types. For this
215   purpose, we utilise the GPT-4 API along with few-shot prompting techniques to build our dataset.
      Scenarios are randomly sampled based on the relation type from the SOTOPIA database as few-shot

217   used for this purpose is shown in Appendix §C.2. A further manual check is run on the generated

222   3.2   M ULTI - EPISODE CHAINING
223
224
      All episodes in SOTOPIA are independent of one another. However, for the LIFELONG-SOTOPIA
      benchmark, our aim is to simulate lifelong social interactions over extended contexts. To achieve
225
      this, we implement “episode chaining,” whereby multiple scenarios are connected together, allowing
226
      characters to progress through each episode sequentially while retaining a memory of their previous
227   interactions. For a given pair of characters, episodes are sampled based on their relationship type,
228   resulting in a set of 40 episodes for each sampled pair (§3.1). As characters are equipped with
229   a memory of all their past interactions, the context length increases linearly with the number of
230   episodes. While some scenarios are entirely independent of others in the set, certain scenarios
231   are interconnected, where the memory of previous episodes can directly influence the outcomes
232   of subsequent ones. For example, in certain scenarios, a character passionate about social work
233   must convince another to donate to a Charity. These scenarios repeat with the cause or Charity
234   changing. However, once a character has already donated, they may be less willing to donate again
235
      due to potential financial concerns. This makes the task progressively harder for the agent in future
      scenarios. Our approach of chaining the episodes effectively mirrors real-life situations, in which
236
      we sometimes encounter situations with another person that are related to past interactions, while at
237
      other times, the situations may be completely independent.

240   3.3   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
241
242   As previously mentioned, the characters are provided with a memory of their prior interactions, and
243   we implement this in two distinct ways.
244   Entire interaction as memory Characters are given the complete interaction details from each
245   episode as context for subsequent episodes. Thus, for the n-th episode in the sequence, characters
246   have access to all their interactions from the previous n − 1 episodes, including the scenarios and
247   their goals from those episodes. The task of retrieving relevant information and reasoning over it to
248   better achieve their goals in current future scenarios is left to the characters, who are prompted to do
249   so during their interactions.
250   Advanced memory module In the second method, we employ a more advanced memory module,
251   drawing inspiration from prior works (Park et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Bae et al., 2022; Zhong
252   et al., 2022). Instead of supplying the complete interaction as memory, we generate a concise sum-
253   mary of approximately 200-300 words for each episode. This summary explicitly focuses on three
254   aspects: (1) a brief overview of the entire interaction within the episode, (2) useful negotiation tech-
255   niques employed by either character to achieve their goals, and (3) new information gained about
256
      the other character, including their likes and dislikes, behavioral traits, etc., which may prove useful
      in future interactions. The prompt for generating this summary is demonstrated in Appendix §C.3.
257
      By providing a summary of each episode as a memory rather than the entire interaction, we ensure
258
      that only relevant and useful information remains in the characters’ memory, thereby simplifying
259   their reasoning process.

262   3.4   E VALUATION P ROTOCOL
263
264   Here, we will define the evaluation protocol and how we test the performance of the language agents
265   in our environment. For this purpose, we evaluate the agents on two dimensions, namely, Believ-
266
      ability and Goal Completion. A more detailed explanation of what these dimensions evaluate is as
      follows:
267
268   Believability (B EL) [0-10]: It focuses on the extent to which the character’s behavior is perceived
269   as natural, realistic, and aligned with their profile, thus simulating believable proxies of human
      behavior.


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Goal Completion (G OAL) [0-10]: This evaluates the extent to which the character achieved their
271   goals defined in the environment.
272
273   The main idea is to analyze how the scores of various LLM-based agents evolve as they step through
274
      the constructed episode chains. We use B EL scores to evaluate the consistency of the models. As
      they go through more social interactions, the context provided to the models increases. This context
275
      incorporates two distinct streams of information — one from the model’s own perspective and the
276
      other from the character they interact with — making it increasingly difficult for the models to
277   distinguish and parse through these different sources. Therefore, if the models maintain their scores
278   on this dimension throughout the chain, we can assert that they exhibit consistency over lifelong
279   interactions.
280
      On the other hand, analyzing G OAL scores helps us evaluate the social intelligence of the models. As
281
      the context grows, the models accumulate more information about the other character’s behavioral
282
      traits, preferences, and dislikes, while also having the opportunity to learn new negotiation strate-
283   gies. If the LLMs perform at or above human-level competence, they would be able to effectively
284   use the provided information, reason through it, learn from their successes and failures, and better
285   optimize their goal completion strategies in later episodes. This would manifest as either consistent
286   or improving G OAL scores.
287
      While GPT-4 is used as the evaluator model for all our experiments, initial results revealed that
288
      GPT-4 overestimated the B EL scores and failed to recognize several cues that made the conver-
289   sations less believable. This was observed through manual inspection of the generated episodes.
290   The error cases where the evaluator began overestimating the B EL scores generally occurred later
291   in the episode chain, when the context length had increased significantly. This issue was likely not
292   detected in the original SOTOPIA paper for the same reason.
293
      To help the evaluator better assess the agent performance on B EL, we constructed an exhaustive
294
      checklist of the failures observed in the LLMs during their interactions. We name this dimension
295   BelievabilityExtended (B EL E XT). The checklist comprises 8 items in total:
296
297         • Repetition of Sentences: The character must not repeat the same sentence multiple times
298           throughout the conversation.
299         • Consistency with Character Traits: The character must remain true to the traits assigned to
300           them and avoid imitating the other character’s personality.
301
            • Consistency with Environment Goals: The character’s dialogue must align with their spe-
302
              cific goals within the environment.
303
304
            • Agent Leaves Promptly After Goal Resolution: We observed that even after both characters
              achieved their respective goals, they often continued to converse about unrelated topics,
305
              which detracted from the believability. This behavior should not occur.
306
307         • Repetition of Exact Goals: Characters should avoid repeating their exact goals (which are
308
              provided as private information) and instead engage in a believable conversation with the
              other character.
309
310         • Stalling in a Conversation: The character should not stall or remain idle during the conver-
311           sation.
312         • Character Responses: The character’s dialogue should directly respond to the other charac-
313           ter. In some cases, the character would discuss unrelated topics or ignore direct questions,
314           which negatively impacted the interaction.
315         • Episode Beginning: The beginning of the conversation should not be abrupt or unrelated
316           to the current scenario. We observed that due to the large context provided to the models,
317           they sometimes confused current episodes with previous ones, leading to conversations that
318           referenced past interactions.

      6    R ELATED W ORK
472
473   Social Intelligence in LLMs Social intelligence refers to the capacity to effectively navigate and
474   manage social interactions and includes key competencies such as social perception, social knowl-
475   edge, social memory, social reasoning, social creativity, and social interaction (Mathur et al., 2024).
476   Evaluating social intelligence in large language models (LLMs) has presented unique challenges.
477   Most evaluations have concentrated on isolated tasks that assess logic, problem-solving, or academic
478   intelligence, while overlooking real-world social dynamics (Xu et al., 2024).
479
      Recent studies have begun to assess social intelligence in LLMs through various methods. For
480   instance, EmoBench (Sabour et al., 2024) introduced a benchmark to evaluate Emotional Intelli-
481   gence in LLMs, focusing on emotional understanding and application. Their results revealed that
482   while LLMs can apply emotional concepts, they struggle significantly with emotional understand-
483   ing, indicating a gap between current LLM capabilities and average human performance in this area.
484   Similarly, InterIntent (Liu et al., 2024c) assessed social intelligence by analyzing how well LLMs
485   comprehend and manage player intentions in a game setting, using social deduction games to eval-
      uate these models in dynamic, interactive contexts. Furthermore, SocialBench (Chen et al., 2024)


                                                            9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      introduced a benchmark for role-playing agents to assess sociality at both individual and group in-
487   teraction levels.
488
489   However, there has been little to no exploration of how LLMs manage long-term social interac-
490
      tions that unfold over extended contexts, such as those lasting hours, days, or even longer (Mathur
      et al., 2024). Our work seeks to address this gap by specifically evaluating the social intelligence of
491
      language models over long contexts using multi-episode chaining in the SOTOPIA environment.
492
493   Evaluation of Long-context LLMs Recent years have seen the advent of multiple techniques
494   that have extended the context length of LLMs from the standard 4096 tokens to 128k or even 1M
495   tokens (Dao et al., 2022; Lou et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a). Evaluating these
496
      systems presents a unique challenge due to the difficulty in manually annotating outputs from such
      long inputs. Several benchmarks, including Long-Range Arena (Tay et al., 2020), Longbench (Bai
497
      et al., 2023), and L-Eval (An et al., 2023), have emerged to address this issue.
498
499   Despite improvements, studies reveal that long-context LLMs still struggle with certain tasks. For
500   example, Lost in the Middle (Liu et al., 2024b) showed these models often miss key information
501   buried in the middle of long inputs. Similarly, LongICLBench (Li et al., 2024b) demonstrated that
502
      models face challenges in handling long in-context learning tasks. RULER (Hsieh et al., 2024)
      introduced a variant of the Needle in a Haystack test (gkamradt, 2023), revealing performance
503
      declines for very long contexts.
504
505   Lifelong ML Lifelong, or continual learning, is an ML paradigm that aims to replicate the hu-
506   man ability to learn and accumulate knowledge over time without forgetting previously learned
507   information, while also using past knowledge to enhance the learning of new tasks with minimal
508
      effort (Ke & Liu, 2023). A lifelong learning system can continuously learn numerous tasks from
      multiple domains throughout its lifetime. Consequently, such a system is capable of both retaining
509
      past information and using the acquired knowledge to support the learning of new tasks (Chen &
510
      Liu, 2018). Our benchmark, LIFELONG-SOTOPIA, is designed to evaluate the social intelligence
511   of state-of-the-art LLM-based agents and assess their performance in long-term or lifelong social
512   interactions.
513
514
515   7   C ONCLUSION
516
517   In this paper, we propose LIFELONG-SOTOPIA, a benchmark to evaluate the social intelligence of
518   LLM-based agents over lifelong social interactions. We find that when equipped with their entire
519   past interactions as memory, the language agents show a consistent decline in both believability and
520   goal completion, indicating issues of inconsistency and a lack of long-term social intelligence. While
521
      the performance of the agents improves significantly when equipped with a more advanced memory
      method, they still show a steep decline in goal completion when tested on harder social scenarios that
522
      require explicit use of knowledge gained from previous interactions. In contrast, humans maintain
523
      their performance throughout, employing various techniques to do so. This suggests a significant
524   gap between the social abilities of humans and current state-of-the-art LLMs, highlighting the need
525   for further research to improve the social intelligence of these models. The limitations and ethical
526   considerations related to our work can be found in the Appendix sections §A and §B respectively.
527   Our findings also demonstrate that LIFELONG-SOTOPIA provides a robust platform for evaluating
528   language agents over long-term social interactions.

      A      L IMITATIONS
703
704
      Design of the harder social scenarios The harder social scenarios were manually crafted based
705   on the previously sampled set of scenarios. This method has obvious limitations as it requires human
706   intervention is not scalable. Future work can come up with ways to automate this process.
707
708
      Potential social biases in the environments We utilise various LLMs like GPT-4, Gemini-1.5
      and Llama-3.1 for simulating human interactions as well as the evaluation of these conversations.
709
      These LLMs may contain potential social biases and stereotypes which would then reflect in the
710
      interactions as well as the evaluation scores in LIFELONG-SOTOPIA.
711
712
713   B      E THICAL S TATEMENT
714
715   Attributing human traits to AI systems can lead to anthropomorphizing them, could not only create
716   unrealistic expectations, but also enable manipulation and cause negative consequences (Deshpande
717   et al., 2023). In LIFELONG-SOTOPIA, the AI agents do not maintain a consistent human identity
718
      but instead are made to role-play different characters across various scenarios. This role-playing ap-
      proach helps prevent the development of consistent human-like personalities in AI, thereby reducing
719
      the risk of anthropomorphism (Shanahan et al., 2023). The main objective of LIFELONG-SOTOPIA
720
      is to evaluate the social intelligence of language agents over lifelong social interactions, and in no
721   way do we intend to create AI agents that are similar to humans or cause any potential global risks
722   (Yudkowsky, 2008). Enhancing these language models with greater social intelligence may lead to
723   possible social manipulation. It is critical to note that we do not endorse the use of LIFELONG-
724   SOTOPIA to create manipulative agents.

1434
1435
       As mentioned in Section 2.1, authors in the SOTOPIA paper come up with a 7-dimensional evalu-
1436
       ation framework to comprehensively evaluate the social interactions of agents. These seven dimen-
1437   sions are as follows:
1438
1439   Believability (B EL) [0-10]: It focuses on the extent to which the character’s behavior is perceived
1440
       as natural, realistic, and aligned with their profile, thus simulating believable proxies of human
       behavior.
1441
1442   Goal Completion (G OAL) [0-10]: This evaluates the extent to which the character achieved their
1443   goals defined in the environment.
1444   Knowledge (K NO) [0-10]: This dimension assesses the agent’s ability to actively acquire new in-
1445   formation during interactions.
1446
1447
       Secret (S EC) [-10-0]: This captures the agent’s capability to keep private or secretive information
       hidden during social interactions.
1448
1449   Relationship (R EL) [-5-5]: This evaluates whether the relationship between the agents improves or
1450   deteriorates after each interaction, reflecting social bonding or conflict.
1451   Social Rules (S OC) [-10-0]: This dimension measures adherence to social norms and legal rules.
1452
1453   Financial and Material Benefits (F IN) [-5-5]: This examines whether the agents achieve financial
1454
       or material advantages in the short or long term.
1455   Finally, another dimension called Overall Score is introduced which is simply the mean value of the
1456   scores on seven dimensions. This provides a single metric for evaluating general social intelligence.
1457   In SOTOPIA Zhou et al. (2023), the authors show how all this multi-dimensional framework is able
       to comprehensively evaluate social intelligence.
