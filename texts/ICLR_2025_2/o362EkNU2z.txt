009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            While recent zero-shot text-to-speech (TTS) models have significantly improved
012            speech quality and expressiveness, mainstream systems still suffer from issues
013            related to speech-text alignment modeling: 1) autoregressive large language models
014            are inefficient and not robust in long-sentence inference; 2) non-autoregressive dif-
015            fusion models without explicit speech-text alignment require substantial model ca-
016            pacity for alignment learning; 3) predefined alignment-based diffusion models suf-
017            fer from naturalness constraints of forced alignments and a complicated inference
018
               pipeline. This paper introduces S-DiT, a TTS system featuring an innovative sparse
               alignment algorithm that guides the latent diffusion transformer (DiT). Specifically,
019
               1) we provide sparse alignment boundaries to S-DiT to reduce the difficulty of
020
               alignment learning without limiting the search space; 2) to simplify the overall
021            pipeline, we propose a unified frontend language model (F-LM) training framework
022            to cover various speech processing tasks required by TTS models. Additionally, we
023            adopt the piecewise rectified flow technique to accelerate the generation process
024            and employ a multi-condition classifier-free guidance strategy for accent intensity
025            adjustment. Experiments demonstrate that S-DiT matches state-of-the-art zero-shot
026            TTS speech quality while maintaining a more efficient pipeline. Moreover, our sys-
027            tem can generate high-quality one-minute speech with only 8 sampling steps. Audio
028            samples are available at https://sditdemo.github.io/sditdemo/.

032
033   In recent years, neural codec language models (Wang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Song et al.,
034
      2024; Xin et al., 2024) and large-scale diffusion models (Shen et al., 2023; Matthew et al., 2023; Lee
      et al., 2024a; Eskimez et al., 2024; Ju et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024d;b) have brought considerable
035
      advancements to the field of speech synthesis. Unlike traditional text-to-speech (TTS) systems (Shen
036
      et al., 2018; Jia et al., 2018; Li et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2020; Ren et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2021; 2022),
037   these models are trained on large-scale, multi-domain speech corpora, which contributes to notable
038   improvements in the naturalness and expressiveness of synthesized audio. Given only seconds of
039   speech prompt, these models can synthesize identity-preserving speech in a zero-shot manner.
040
      To generate high-quality speech with clear and expressive pronunciation, a TTS model must establish
041
      an alignment mapping from text to speech signals (Kim et al., 2020; Tan et al., 2021). However, from
042
      the perspective of speech-text alignment, current solutions suffer from the following issues:
043
044          • Autoregressive codec language models (AR LM) are inefficient and lack robustness. These
045            models (Wang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a) achieve the alignment paths through attention
046            mechanisms in their time-autoregressive generation processes. However, the lengthy discrete
047            speech codes, which typically require a minimum bit rate of 1.5 kbps (Kumar et al., 2024;
048            Wu et al., 2024), impose a significant burden on these autoregressive language models.
049          • Diffusion models without predefined alignments (Diffusion w/o PA) require substan-
050            tial parameters. Recent diffusion-based TTS works (Lee et al., 2024a; Eskimez et al.,
051            2024; Lovelace et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023; Cámbara et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024d;b)
052            demonstrate that non-autoregressive diffusion models can effectively perform text-to-speech
053            synthesis without the need for explicit duration modeling, which significantly speeds up
               the speech generation process. However, these algorithms require a significant portion of

090   work, however, we find that these systems can be merged into a unified language model to efficiently

102            CFG strategy to adjust the guidance scales for speaker timbre and text content separately.

114   2    BACKGROUND
115
116   Zero-shot TTS. Zero-shot TTS (Casanova et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Shen
117   et al., 2023; Matthew et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Lee et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024;
118   Lee et al., 2023; Ju et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b) aims to synthesize unseen
119
      voices with speech prompts. Among them, neural codec language models (Chen et al., 2024a) are
      the first that can autoregressively synthesize speech that rivals human recordings in naturalness and
120
      expressiveness. However, they still face several challenges, such as the lossy compression in discrete
121
      audio tokenization and the time-consuming nature of autoregressive generation. To address these
122   issues, some subsequent works explore solutions based on continuous vectors and non-autoregressive
123   diffusion models (Shen et al., 2023; Matthew et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024a; Eskimez et al., 2024;
124   Yang et al., 2024d;b; Chen et al., 2024b). These works can be categorized into two main types: 1)
125   the first type directly models speech-text alignments using attention mechanisms without explicit
126   duration modeling (Lee et al., 2024a; Eskimez et al., 2024). Although these models perform well
127   in terms of generation speed and quality, they typically require a large number of parameters to
128   learn speech-text alignments. The second category (Shen et al., 2023; Matthew et al., 2023) utilizes
129   predefined alignments to simplify alignment learning. However, the search space of the generated
130
      speech of these models is limited by predefined alignments and the inference pipeline is quite complex.
      To address these limitations, 1) we propose a sparse alignment mechanism to reduce the constraints
131
      of predefined alignment-based methods while also reducing the difficulty of speech-text alignment
132
      learning; 2) we introduce a frontend language model to simplify the inference and fine-tuning pipeline.
133   Additionally, we describe the CFG mechanism used in zero-shot TTS systems in Appendix B.
134
135
      Accented TTS. While accented TTS is not yet mainstream in the field of speech synthesis, it
136
      offers valuable potential for customized TTS services, by enhancing the expressiveness of speech
137
      synthesis systems and improving listeners’ comprehension of speech content (Tan et al., 2021;
138   Melechovsky et al., 2022; Badlani et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024; Shah et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023;
139   Inoue et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2024). With the emergence of conversational AI systems, accented
140   TTS technology has even broader application scenarios. In this paper, we focus on a specific task
141   of accented TTS: adjusting the accent intensity of speakers to make them sound like native English
142   speakers or accented speakers who use English as a second language (Liu et al., 2024a). Unlike
143   previous work, our approach does not require paired data or accurate accent labels; instead, it allows
144   for flexible control over the accent intensity using the proposed multi-condition CFG mechanism.
145
146   TTS Frontend Systems. In traditional TTS systems, the frontend typically refers to text analysis
147   modules (Tan et al., 2021), such as text normalization (Sproat & Jaitly, 2016; Zhang et al., 2020)
148   and grapheme-to-phoneme conversion (Yao & Zweig, 2015; Park & Lee, 2020; Bernard & Titeux,
149   2021; Chen et al., 2022). With the emergence of zero-shot TTS, the frontend has taken on additional
150
      responsibilities, including processing the prompt speech during the inference stage, which should
      at least support automatic speech recognition (ASR). Moreover, some advanced non-autoregressive
151
      models (Ju et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2023; Matthew et al., 2023) require additional speech-
152
      text aligners and duration predictors. These complex frontend modules impose significant limitations
153   on the efficiency of zero-shot TTS models. In this work, we unify these frontend components into a
154   single language model, thereby simplifying the overall pipeline.

184   3.1   A RCHITECTURE
185
186   Speech Compression. As shown in Figure 1 (a), given a speech spectrogram s ∈ RT ×C , the VAE
187   encoder E encodes s into a latent vector z, and the wave decoder D reconstructs the waveform
188   x = D(z) = D(E(s)), where T is the time dimension and C is the frequency dimension. To reduce
189   the computational burden of the model and simplify speech-text alignment learning, the encoder
190
      E downsamples the spectrogram by a factor of d = 8 in length. The encoder E is similar to the
      one used in Rombach et al. (2022), and the decoder D is based on Kong et al. (2020). We also
191
      adopt the multi-period discriminator (MPD), multi-scale discriminator (MSD), and multi-resolution
192
      discriminator (MRD) (Kong et al., 2020; Jang et al., 2021) to model the high-frequency details in
193   waveforms, which ensure perceptually high-quality reconstructions. The training loss of the speech
194   compression model can be formulated as L = Lrec + LKL + LAdv , where Lrec = ∥s − ŝ∥2 is the
195   spectrogram reconstruction loss, LKL is the slight KL-penalty loss (Rombach et al., 2022), and LAdv
196   is the LSGAN-styled adversarial loss (Mao et al., 2017). After training, a one-second speech clip can
197   be encoded into 12.5 vector frames. For more details, please refer to Appendix A.1 and J.
198
199   Latent Diffusion Transformer with Masked Speech Modeling. The latent diffusion transformer
200   is used to predict speech that matches the style of the given speaker and the content of the provided
201   text. Given the random variables Z0 sampled from a standard Gaussian distribution π0 and Z1
202   sampled from the latent space given by the speech compression model with data density π1 , we adopt
203
      the rectified flow Liu et al. (2022) to implicitly learn the transport map T , which yields Z1 := T (Z0 ).
      The rectified flow learns T by constructing the following ordinary differential equation (ODE):

221   3.2   S PARSE A LIGNMENT E NHANCED L ATENT D IFFUSION T RANSFORMER (S-D I T)
222
223   In this subsection, we describe the sparse alignment strategy as the foundation of S-DiT, followed by
224   the piecewise rectified flow and multi-condition CFG strategies to further enhance S-DiT’s capacity.
225
226   Sparse Alignment Strategy. Let’s first analyze the reasons behind the characteristics of different
227   speech-text alignment modeling methods in depth. “Diffusion w/o PA” requires more parameters
228
      for speech intelligibility due to the difficulty in end-to-end modeling of speech-text alignment non-
      autoregressively. On the other hand, the use of predefined hard alignment paths limits the model’s
229
      search space and increases the complexity of the pipeline. The characteristics of these systems moti-
230
      vate us to design an approach that combines the advantages of both: we first provide a rough alignment
231   to S-DiT and then use attention mechanisms in Transformer blocks to construct the fine-grained im-
232   plicit alignment path. The visualizations of the implicit alignment paths are included in Appendix G.
233   In specific, denote the latent speech vector sequence as z = [z1 , z2 , · · · , zn ], the phoneme sequence
234   as p = [p1 , p2 , · · · , pm ], and the phoneme duration sequence as d = [d1 , d2 , · · · , dm ], where n, m is
235   the length of the sequence. The length of the speech vector that corresponds to a phoneme pi is the
236   duration di . Given d = [2, 2, 3], the hard speech-text alignment path used by “Diffusion w/ PA” can
237   be denoted as a = [p1 , p1 , p2 , p2 , p3 , p3 , p3 ]. To construct the rough alignment ã, we randomly retain
238   only one anchor for each phoneme: ã = [M , p1 , p2 , M , M , M , P3 ], where M represents the mask
239
      token. ã is downsampled to match the length of the latent sequence z. Then, we directly concatenate
      the downsampled ã and z along the channel dimension. We also concatenate the phoneme embedding
240
      p with z along the time dimension as the prefix information. The anchors in ã provide S-DiT with
241
      approximate positional information for each phoneme, simplifying the model’s learning of speech-text
242   alignment. At the same time, the rough alignment information does not limit S-DiT’s search space
243   and also enables fine-grained control over each phoneme’s duration.
244
245   Piecewise Rectified Flow Acceleration. We adopt Piecewise Rectified Flow (PeRFlow) (Yan et al.,
246   2024) to distill the pretrained S-DiT model into a more efficient generator. Although our S-DiT is non-
247   autoregressive in terms of the time dimension, it requires multiple iterations to solve the Flow ODE.
248   The number of iterations (i.e., number of function evaluations, NFE) has a great impact on inference
249   efficiency, especially when the model scales up further. Therefore, we adopt the PeRFlow technique
250   to further reduce NFE by segmenting the flow trajectories into multiple time windows. Applying
251
      reflow operations within these shortened time intervals, PeRFlow eliminates the need to simulate the
      full ODE trajectory for training data preparation, allowing it to be trained in real-time alongside large-
252
      scale real data during the training process. Given number of windows K, we divide the time t ∈ [0, 1]
253
      into K time windows {(tk−1 , tk ]}K  k=1 . Then, we randomly p sample k ∈ {1, · · · , K} uniformly. We
254
      use the startpoint of the sampled time window ztk−1 = 1 − σ 2 (tk−1 )z1 + σ(tk−1 )ϵ to solve the
255
      endpoint of the time window ẑtk = ϕθ (ztk−1 , tk−1 , tk ), where ϵ ∼ N (0, I) is the random noise, σ(t)
256
      is the noise schedule, and ϕθ is the ODE solver of the teacher model. Since ztk−1 and ẑtk is available,
257
      the student model θ̂ can be trained via the following objectives:

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
325
326   Datasets. We train S-DiT and F-LM on the LibriLight (Kahn et al., 2020) dataset, which contains
327   60k hours of unlabeled speech derived from LibriVox audiobooks. All speech data are sampled
328   at 16KHz. We transcribe the speeches using an internal ASR system and extract the predefined
329   speech-text alignment using the external alignment tool (McAuliffe et al., 2017). We utilize two
330   benchmark datasets: 1) the librispeech (Panayotov et al., 2015) test-clean set following (Shen et al.,
331   2023; Ju et al., 2024) for zero-shot TTS and F-LM’s evaluation; 2) the L2-arctic dataset (Zhao et al.,
332
      2018) following (Melechovsky et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024a) for accented TTS evaluation.
333
      Training and Inference. We train the speech compression model, S-DiT, and F-LM on 8 NVIDIA
334
      A100 GPUs. The batch sizes, optimizer settings, and learning rate schedules are described in
335
      Appendix A.1. It takes 2M steps for the speech compression model’s training and 1M steps for S-DiT
336   and F-LM’s training until convergence. The pre-training of S-DiT requires 800k steps and PeRFlow
337   distillation requires 200k steps. During the inference stage, given the prompt speech and target text,
338   F-LM will process all the information required by S-DiT. Then, S-DiT synthesizes the target latent
339   vector, which is converted into the target waveform by the wav decoder. The entire inference pipeline
340   is simple and efficient.
341
342   Objective Metrics. 1) For zero-shot TTS, we evaluate speech intelligibility using the word error
343   rate (WER) and speaker similarity using SIM-O (Ju et al., 2024). To measure SIM-O, we utilize
344   the WavLM-TDCNN speaker embedding model1 to calculate the cosine similarity score between
345   the generated samples and the prompt. As SIM-R (Matthew et al., 2023) is not comparable across
346
      baselines using different acoustic tokenizers, we recommend focusing on SIM-O in our experiments.
      The similarity score is in the range of [−1, 1], where a higher value indicates greater similarity. In
347
      terms of WER, we use the publicly available HuBERT-Large model (Hsu et al., 2021), fine-tuned on
348
      the 960-hour LibriSpeech training set, to transcribe the generated speech. The WER is calculated by
349   comparing the transcribed text to the original target text. All samples from the test set are used for
350   the objective evaluation; 2) For accented TTS, we evaluate the Mel Cepstral Distortion (MCD) in dB
351   level and the moments (standard deviation (σ), skewness (γ) and kurtosis (κ)) (Andreeva et al., 2014;
352   Niebuhr & Skarnitzl, 2019) of the pitch distribution to evaluate whether the model accurately captures
353   accent variance; 3) For F-LM, we evaluate the WER for ASR models, the alignment boundary error
354   (AE) for speech-text aligners, and the duration error (DE) for duration predictors.
355
356   Subjective Metrics. We conduct the MOS (mean opinion score) evaluation on the test set to
357   measure the audio naturalness via Amazon Mechanical Turk. We keep the text content and prompt
358   speech consistent among different models to exclude other interference factors. We randomly choose
359
      40 samples from the test set of each dataset for the subjective evaluation, and each audio is listened to
      by at least 10 testers. We analyze the MOS in three aspects: CMOS (quality, clarity, naturalness, and
360
      high-frequency details), SMOS (speaker similarity in terms of timbre reconstruction and prosodic
361
      pattern), and ASMOS (accent similarity). We tell the testers to focus on one corresponding aspect
362   and ignore the other aspect when scoring.

530   5           C ONCLUSIONS
531
532
      In this paper, we introduce S-DiT, a zero-shot TTS framework that 1) leverages novel sparse alignment
533
      boundaries to ease the difficulty of alignment learning while retaining the naturalness of the generated
534   speeches, and 2) incorporates a unified front-end language model (F-LM) to streamline the overall
535   pipeline. These strategies allow our approach to combine the strengths of both “Diffusion w/o PA”
536   and “Diffusion w/ PA” methods. Additionally, we employ the PeRFlow technique to further accelerate
537   the generation process and design a multi-condition classifier-free guidance strategy to offer more
538   flexible control over accents. Experimental results show that S-DiT achieves state-of-the-art zero-shot
539   TTS speech quality while maintaining a more efficient pipeline. Due to space constraints, further
      discussions are provided in the appendix.

      6   E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542   The proposed model, S-DiT, is designed to advance zero-shot TTS technologies, making it easier for
543   users to generate personalized speech. When used responsibly and legally, this technique can enhance
544   applications such as movies, games, podcasts, and various other services, contributing to increasing
545   convenience in everyday life. However, we acknowledge the potential risks of misuse, such as voice
546   cloning for malicious purposes. To mitigate this risk, solutions like building a corresponding deepfake
547   detection model will be considered. Additionally, we plan to incorporate watermarks and verification
548   methods for synthetic audio to ensure ethical use in real-world applications. Restrictions will also
549   be included in the licensing of our project to further prevent misuse. By addressing these ethical
550
      concerns, we aim to contribute to the development of responsible and beneficial AI technologies,
      while remaining conscious of the potential risks and societal impact.

      A.1      M ODEL C ONFIGURATION
867
868   Our model comprises a speech compression VAE, an S-DiT, and an F-LM.
869
870             • The speech compression VAE consists of a VAE encoder, a wave decoder, and discrimina-
871               tors; The VAE encoder follows the architecture used in Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al.,
872               2022) but we replace the 2D convolution layers with 1D convolution layers and remove
873               the attention layers to accommodate data of arbitrary lengths and to improve efficiency.
874
                  The channel size is 256 with channel multipliers [1, 2, 4, 8]. The wave decoder consists
                  of a stable diffusion decoder and a Hifi-GAN decoder (Kong et al., 2020). The stable
875
                  diffusion decoder shares the same hyperparameter settings as the encoder, which is used for
876
                  upsampling the latent vectors. The latent channel size is set to 16. The weight of the KL
877               loss is set to 1 × 10−2 , which only imposes a slight KL penalty on the learned latent. In
878               training, we use batches of fixed length, consisting of 800 mel-spectrogram frames, with
879               a batch size set to 50 for each GPU. We use the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of
880               1 × 10−4 , β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999, and 10K warmup steps.
881             • The S-DiT model use the standard transformer block from LLAMA (Dubey et al., 2024) as
882               the basic structure, which comprises a 24-layer Transformer with 16 attention heads and
883               1024 embedding dimensions. It contains 339M parameters in total. We adopt the Rotary
884               Position Embedding (RoPE) (Su et al., 2024) as the positional embedding following the
885               common practice in LLAMA implementations. For simplicity, we do not use the phoneme
886               encoder and style encoder like previous works. We only use a linear projection layer to
887               transform these features to the same dimension. During training, we use 8 A100 80GB
888               GPUs with a batch size of 12K latent frames per GPU for 1M steps. We use the Adam
889
                  optimizer with a learning rate of 5 × 10−5 , β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999, and 10K warmup steps.
                  In zero-shot TTS experiments, we set the text guidance scale αtxt and the speaker guidance
890
                  scale αspk to 2.5 and 3.5, respectively. In accented TTS experiments, we set αspk = 6.5,
891
                  αtxt = 1.5 to generate the accented speech and set αspk = 2.0, αtxt = 5.0 to generate the
892               speech with standard English.
893
894
                • The F-LM use the same architecture as S-DiT. F-LM use an 8-layer Transformer with 16
                  attention heads and 1024 embedding dimensions, which contains 124M parameters in total.
895
                  The audio encoder of F-LM follows the architecture of Whisper-small encoder (Radford
896
                  et al., 2023). We use the tokenizers from Yi-1.52 to obtain the BPE tokens from texts. To
897               improve robustness, we add SpecAugment (Park et al., 2019) in the training process. We
898               use the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 1 × 10−4 , β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999, and 10K
899               warmup steps.

907
908   For S-DiT, we applied the Euler sampler with a fixed step size following the common practice in
909
      flow ODE sampling. We use 25 and 8 sampling steps for S-DiT and S-DiT-accelerated, respectively.
      For F-LM, when transcribing the prompt speech, we use beam search with 5 beams using the log
910
      probability as the score function to reduce repetition looping following Radford et al. (2023). For
911
      G2P conversion and speech-text aligning, we use greedy decoding with top-1 sampling. For duration
912   prediction, we use top-50 sampling to enhance the output diversity.

1141   Special Tokens We add special tokens <Begin of BPE> and <End of BPE> at the beginning and
1142   end of the BPE sequence to indicate the start and end of the BPE sequence. We also add <EOS>
1143   token to the phoneme/timestamp sequence to indicate the end of the sentence. In training, we add
1144   special tokens <Full> or <Partial> to the input sequence depending on whether we discard parts of

1146   constrained to generate only up to the text corresponding to the speech prompt, which is used by the
1147   ASR process.

       H    A BOUT D IFFERENT L ENGTHS OF C ONTEXT
1243
1244   An imbalanced distribution of prompt and target lengths during training can lead to unstable generation
1245   performance during inference. For example, if the majority of the sampled data during training
1246   consists of 20-second targets, the generation performance for audio with a 40-second target will be
1247   worse than that of 20-second targets in inference. To solve the imbalanced distribution issue, we
1248   recommend using the following multi-sentence data sampling strategy: we concatenate all audio
1249   recordings of the same speaker in the dataset in time order, and then randomly extract audio segments
1250   of length t ∼ U (tmin , tmax ) from the concatenated audio, where tmin is the minimum sampling
1251   time and tmax is the maximum sampling time. Then, following Section 3.1, we randomly divide the
1252
       sampled sequence into a prompt region and a target region. Although we do not use this strategy in
       our experiments in order to make a fair comparison with other methods, this strategy is effective in
1253
       practical scenarios.

1256   I   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
1257
1258   In this section, we discuss the limitations of the proposed method and outline potential strategies for
1259
       addressing them in future research.
1260          • Language Coverage. Although our model currently supports both English and Chinese,
1261            there are far more languages in the world. In particular, for some low-resource languages,
1262            the performance of our model requires further validation. To address this, we plan to
1263            incorporate additional training data from a wider range of languages and apply adaptation-
1264            based techniques, such as LoRA tuning (Hu et al., 2021), to enhance speech quality for
1265            low-resource languages.
1266          • Function Coverage. We can make S-DiT more user-friendly by enabling it to generate
1267            speech in various styles according to text descriptions through instruction-based fine-tuning.
1268            We can further fine-tune S-DiT on the paralinguistic corpus, allowing it to generate speech
1269            that is closer to a natural human style.
1270          • Frontend Coverage. While our current F-LM supports four key tasks (ASR, MFA, duration
1271            prediction, and G2P), there are additional tasks in the TTS data preprocessing pipeline, such
1272            as speech enhancement, speaker diarization, and emotion classification, that remain to be
1273            included. In the future, we aim to design a truly universal frontend language model capable
1274
                of efficiently handling all speech data processing tasks for TTS, thereby simplifying the
                overall workflow.
