009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            With an increasing demand for LLM personalization, various methods have been
012            developed to deliver customized LLM experiences, including in-context learning,
013            retrieval augmentation, and parameter-efficient fine-tuning. However, most existing
014            methods are not readily locally deployable, limited by the compute cost, privacy
015            risks, and an inability to adapt to dynamic user preferences. Here, we propose to
016            use a tag system to efficiently characterize user profiles, inspired from the insights
017            from personality typology and recommendation systems. Based on the observation,
018            we present a locally deployable LLM-agnostic framework for achieving LLM
019
               personalization: PREMIUM (Preference Ranking EMpowered Individual User
               Modeling), which obtains individual-level feedback by having users rank responses
020
               and continuously self-iterates optimization during the interaction between the user
021
               and the LLM. Notably, a variant of PREMIUM, PREMIUM-Embed, can effectively
022            capture user preferences while being deployable with laptop-level resources. Be-
023            sides algorithmic innovation, we further prepare a novel dataset, Ranking-TAGER,
024            which provides a valuable evaluation protocol for LLM personalization. Extensive
025            experiments validate that PREMIUM remarkably outperforms various baselines,
026            achieving a 15%-50% higher accuracy and a 2.5%-35% higher win rate on Ranking-
027            TAGER, as well as a 3%-13% higher accuracy and a 2%-7.5% higher F1 Score on
028            LaMP-2. More importantly, we further demonstrate that PREMIUM can develop an
029            effective strategy with minimal interactive data, adapt to dynamic user preferences,
030
               and demonstrate excellent scalability in both scale and functionality.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   LLM-powered conversational agents have become increasingly prevalent (Jörke et al., 2024; Abbasian
036   et al., 2024; Bagdasaryan et al., 2024), attracting a growing user base and amplifying the impor-
037   tance of LLM personalization. Personalized LLMs can be applied to a wide range of downstream
038   tasks, encompassing customer service (Rome et al., 2024), personal health (Abbasian et al., 2024),
039
      recommender systems (Li et al., 2024), making it a valuable area of research.
040   To achieve alignment between LLMs and user preferences, existing research mainly falls into three
041   categories: parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT), retrieval-augmented LLMs (RALM), and in-
042   context learning (ICL). (1) PEFT-Based methods, such as Baize (Xu et al., 2023), utilize user
043   information to fine-tune open-source LLMs for generating user-preferred responses (Zhang et al.,
044   2024b). However, such approaches are not applicable to black-box LLMs with proprietary parameters
045
      (such as GPT-3.5 and Gemini), greatly limiting their applicability, and fine-tuning LLMs imposes a
      burdensome cost on users. (2) RALM-Based methods, such as OPPU (Tan et al., 2024), incorporate
046
      retrieved user personal information into prompts to generate responses aligned with user preferences
047
      (Salemi et al., 2024b; Du et al., 2024). However, retrieval-augmented methods require users to
048   provide a large amount of textual personal information, which may be challenging and pose potential
049   privacy risk (Kirk et al., 2024). (3) ICL-Based methods, such as TidyBot (Wu et al., 2023), set explicit
050   textual user profiles for users (Zhang et al., 2018) and leverage these user profiles through in-context
051   learning (Dong et al., 2023) to achieve LLM personalization. While this approach offers advantages
052   such as simplicity, the user information it requires raises potential privacy concerns (Kirk et al.,
053   2024). Additionally, fixed user profiles cannot adapt to changes in user preferences (Kangaslahti &
      Alvarez-Melis, 2024; Shi et al., 2024) or provide query-related contexts to LLMs. Overall, existing

064   Psychological theories about personality typology reveal that individuals can be categorized into
065   different personality types by assigning them "words that represent their preferences." (Myers,
066   1985; Keirsey, 1998). This method of characterizing individual personality is similar to tag-based
067
      approaches in recommendation systems (Belém et al., 2017; Furtado & Esmin, 2023). Inspired by
      these theoretical insights and practical experiences, we introduce a more rational and efficient method
068
      for characterizing user profiles - the Tagging System, which models user profiles by assigning tags
069
      that represent their personality traits and preferences.
070
071   Building on this foundation, we propose PREMIUM (Preference Ranking EMpowered Individual
072   User Modeling), a novel LLM-agnostic framework for LLM personalization. Our key insight is
073
      that by having users rank responses based on their personal preferences, we obtain individual-level
      feedback, and leverage this feedback to continuously self-iterate optimization during the interaction
074
      between the user and the LLM, thereby aligning with the user’s personal preferences. Furthermore, we
075
      implement two variants of PREMIUM: PREMIUM-Prompt and PREMIUM-Embed. The comparison
076   between PREMIUM and some representative existing methods can be found in Table 1.
077
078   Besides algorithmic innovation, we further propose a novel dataset, Ranking-TAGER, which provides
079
      a valuable evaluation protocol for LLM personalization. Our comprehensive experiments on Ranking-
      TAGER validate that PREMIUM remarkably outperforms all baselines by achieving a 15%-50%
080
      higher accuracy and a 2.5%-35% higher win rate. Moreover, we further demonstrate some exciting
081
      findings: PREMIUM can develop an effective strategy with minimal interactive data, adapt to
082   dynamic user preferences, and demonstrate excellent scalability in both scale and functionality.
083
084   In summary, our main contributions are as follows: (1) PREMIUM, a novel LLM-agnostic framework
085
      for achieving LLM personalization, to our knowledge, the first method that utilizes tags to characterize
      user profiles and leverage individual-level preference feedback to achieve LLM alignment with user
086
      preferences. (2) PREMIUM can be deployable locally with laptop-level resources, and consistently
087
      outperforms all baselines, achieving a 15%-50% higher accuracy and a 2.5%-35% higher win rate on
088   Ranking-TAGER, as well as a 3%-13% higher accuracy and a 2%-7.5% higher F1 Score on LaMP-2.
089   (3) Ranking-TAGER, a novel dataset that collects diverse user preferences and contributes to research
090   on LLM Personalization, recommendation systems, and psychology studies.

096   Framework Overview Fig. 1 offers an overview of the proposed PREMIUM framework. Our key
097
      insight is that by selecting tags to guide the LLM in generating responses with corresponding domain-
      specific elements, and by collecting user preference rankings for multiple responses, PREMIUM can
098
      utilize this individual-level feedback to continuously self-iterate optimization during the user-LLM
099
      interaction process, ultimately enabling the LLM to generate user-preferred responses.
100
101   Responses Generation through the Tagging System One key aspect of LLM personalization lies
102   in the characterization of user profiles. To explore a more reasonable way of characterizing individual
103   preferences, we draw upon theoretical support from psychological theories: In personality typology,
104   some theories categorize individuals into different personality types by assigning them "words that
105   represent their preferences." (Myers, 1985; Roccas et al., 2002) This method of characterizing
106
      personality is similar to tag-based approaches in recommendation systems (Belém et al., 2017;
      Furtado & Esmin, 2023), which are widely used for their efficiency and simplicity. In this work, we
107
      adopt a similar approach and propose a tag-based user profiling method - the Tagging System:

      Definition 1 (Tag Library). To characterize users’ preferences, we construct a Tag Library L =
123   {t1 , t2 , . . . , tn }, consisting of n tags representing domains of potential interests to users ("Investment",
124   "Baking", "Biology", etc.). These tags cover 20 areas such as "Finance", "Athletics", and "Education".
125
126
      Definition 2 (User Tag Set). For a specific user U , we assume they are interested in k domains
      represented by the tags from the Tag Library, We use the tag set composed of the corresponding k
127
      user tags as the user profile, refer to it as User Tag Set TU = [tU1 , tU2 , ..., tUk ].
128
129   Definition 3 (Tag Set Candidate). For a query q provided by user U , we select k tags from the Tag
130   Library L to form a tag set T = [tn1 , tn2 , ..., tnk ], refer to it as a Tag Set Candidate.
131   Definition 4 (Tag Selector). Given a query q provided by user U , the Tag Selector selects m sets of
132   Tag Set Candidates [T1 , T2 , ...Tm ] from the Tag Library L. These m Tag Set Candidates assist LLM
133   M in generating m distinct responses [r1 , r2 , ...rm ] for q.
134
135   When the Tag Selector selects Tag Set Candidates from the Tag Library, we construct a prompt for
136   the LLM by combining each Tag Set Candidate with the user query. We employ prompt engineering
137   techniques to ensure that the LLM incorporates elements, perspectives, examples, and terminologies
138   related to the tags present in the candidate into its generated response.
139   Specifically, by using a prompt generation function ϕp , we transform each Tag Set Candidate Ti
140   and query q into a prompt Pi : Pi = ϕp (q, Ti ), i ∈ {1, 2, . . . , m}. By feeding the prompt Pi into the
141   LLM M , we obtain a response ri that is relevant to the tags in Ti : ri = M (Pi ), i ∈ {1, 2, . . . , m}.
142   The prompt template used to combine the user query and the candidate tag set is presented in Figure
143   6 of Appendix B.
144
      Objective of Responses Generation Our aim is to make the responses (1) relevant to the domains
145
      the user is interested in, (2) adhere to the user’s instructions and answer the user’s questions. The
146   former goal requires the selected tags to be within User Tag Set TU , while the latter goal may require
147   the selected tags to be relevant to the query q. For example, if a user interested in "Nutrition" asks,
148   "How to make handmade desserts?", our goal is to select the tags "Nutrition" and "Bakery" from the
149   tag library to assist the LLM in generating a response such as "To make handmade desserts with a
150   focus on nutrition, consider using whole grain flour, natural sweeteners, and healthy fats. . . ."
151
152
      Preference Ranking Feedback on Responses This paper focuses on utilizing individual-level
      feedback to facilitate LLM personalization. In this work, we adopt Preference Ranking Feedback
153
      franking as a signal for uncovering user preferences. Specifically, for each query q, the user U
154
      is provided with m responses [r1 , r2 , ..., rm ]. User U is required to provide a preference ranking
155   franking for these m responses as individual-level preference feedback, which is used to update the
156   Tag Selector for the next interaction. Thus, PREMIUM continuously self-iterates optimization during
157   the interaction between the user and the LLM to assist LLM in generating user-preferred responses.
158

187   3    PREMIUM-P ROMPT: A S IMPLIFIED P ROOF - OF -C ONCEPT
188
189
      First, we propose a relatively intuitive Prompt-Based implementation of PREMIUM. Prompt-Based
190   methods have gained widespread adoption in many works due to its simplicity and the excellent
191   reasoning capabilities of LLMs (Wu et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024a).
192
193   Prompt-Based Tag Selector In this approach, we construct the Tag Selector in the PREMIUM by
194   introducing an additional LLM Candidate Generator G to infer the user’s domains of interest from
195   the Interaction Histories. Then, by combining the user’s interests with a new query q provided by
196
      them, the LLM Candidate Generator G selects Tag Set Candidates from the Tag Library L. Figure 2
      offers an overview of the Prompt-Based Tag Selector.
197
198   Specifically, we maintain an Interaction History Buffer, which stores the most recent s Interaction
199   Histories. Given a new query q, we submit the Interaction Histories from the Buffer along with q to the
200   LLM Candidate Generator G, prompting it to generate a Tag Set Candidate: T = G(q, [h1 , . . . , hs ]).
201   Through repeating this process m times, we obtain m Tag Set Candidates, which are used to generate
202   m responses for User U to rank. When User U provides Preference Ranking Feedback rranking , we
203   obtain the Interaction History h for this interaction and update the Interaction History Buffer.
204
205   Limitations in Real Applications While Prompt-Based approaches benefit from simplicity, some
206
      works applying them to combinatorial optimization problems have shown drawbacks such as instabil-
      ity and degradation in performance as action space increasesYang et al. (2024)Liu et al. (2024).
207
208   We conduct experiments to validate the effectiveness of PREMIUM-Prompt in real applications. The
209   experimental results and detailed analysis can be found in Appendix D.
210   When the action space is relatively small, PREMIUM-Prompt, while being concise, manages to
211   uncover a portion of user tags, demonstrating good effectiveness and indirectly validating the
212   rationality of our framework. However, when the action space is relatively large, it fails to model user
213   preferences effectively, which may be attributed to its limited exploration capability. Furthermore, the
214   buffer size is limited by LLM’s effective context length, which severely restricts the LLM Candidate
215   Generator’s ability to learn user preferences from Interaction Histories. Additionally, PREMIUM-
      Prompt also suffers from high usage cost, unstable performance and sensitivity to prompts.

      To address the various issues of PREMIUM-Prompt, we propose PREMIUM-Embed. In this variant,
219
      we encode the user preferences learned during the interaction process into the parameters of neural
220   networks, thus overcoming the limitations of capacity and stability inherent in PREMIUM-Prompt,
221   which are constrained by the limited size of the Interaction History Buffer.
222
223   Embedding-Based Tag Selector In this approach, we construct the Tag Selector by introducing two
224   encoders: Query Encoder Eθq and Tag Encoder Eθt , to respectively encode the semantic information
225   of queries and tags. We then perform fine-tuning through franking to incorporate user’s personal
226
      preferences into their parameters. Figure 2 offers an overview of Embedding-Based Tag Selector.
227   Specifically, given a user query q and a tag ti from the Tag Library, we utilize the Query Encoder
228   Eθq and the Tag Encoder Eθt to obtain the query embedding eq and tag embedding eti respectively
229   (Lin et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2019): eq = Eθq (q) ∈ Rd , eti = Eθt (ti ) ∈ Rd .
230
      We encode the semantic information of query q, tag ti , and the preferences of user U into two vectors
231
      of equal dimensions, eq and eti . Leveraging these two heterogeneous embeddings, we can compute
232   the probability of selecting ti into Tag Set Candidate. Here, we calculate the dot product of the tag
233   embedding et with the query embedding eq for each tag in the Tag Library, and apply the Softmax

281   5    R ANKING -TAGER: DATASET FOR TAG -A SSISTED LLM P ERSONALIZATION
282
283   Existing datasets designed for LLM personalization are well-constructed but predominantly incor-
284   porate textual user profiles (Salemi et al., 2024b; Du et al., 2024; Aliannejadi et al., 2024). This
285   approach, however, carries potential privacy risks in practical applications (Kirk et al., 2024). To
286   address these issues, we introduce an innovative dataset, Ranking-TAGER (Ranking - Tag-Assisted
287   GEnerated Responses). Our dataset comprises 79,017 data entries and we partition it into three parts
288   based on task categories: Ranking-TAGER-RW (Routine Writing), Ranking-TAGER-SG (Story
289   Generation), and Ranking-TAGER-IF (Instruction Following). An overview of them, the detailed
290
      dataset format, query source and details of the collection process can be found in Appendix E.
291   AI Annotator In this work, we utilize LLM automatic annotation, which has seen widespread
292   adoption in recent research involving human feedback (Bai et al., 2022; Dubois et al., 2024; Lee et al.,
293   2023). Specifically, we employ an AI Annotator to provide Preference Ranking Feedback. It will
294   rank responses based on how well they adhere to the user’s instructions and how relevant they are to
295   the user’s domains of interest. Here, we choose "Qwen1.5-72B-Chat" as our AI Annotator due to its
296   strong alignment with human capabilities (Bai et al., 2023).
297
      Benefits and Contributions Ranking-TAGER offers several advantages over existing datasets: (1)
298
      It employs the Tagging System to characterize user profiles, which is a more realistic, reasonable,
299
      and concise approach. Moreover, it does not include text information from individual users, thus
300   eliminating the risk of information leakage (Kirk et al., 2024). (2) Our dataset collects diverse prefer-
301   ences from 862 different users, which is difficult to obtain in reality. Additionally, when facing real
302   users, the preferences collected in our dataset may fully or partially reflect their personal preferences.
303   Therefore, leveraging our dataset can help LLMs quickly adapt to real user preferences (Kang et al.,
304   2024). (3) The AI feedback included in Ranking-TAGER contains the Explanation conducted by
305   the AI annotator before providing Preference Ranking. This makes our data highly interpretable
306   and supports deeper analysis, which can be used in various fields such as LLM personalization,
307   recommendation systems, and psychology studies.

      feedback for multiple responses based on the ground truth user responses available in LaMP-2.
339
340   Metrics. Our evaluation approach encompasses both automated and AI-based assessments:
341   For Ranking-TAGER, we use the following two metrics: (1) Accuracy: This metric computes the
342   proportion of tags selected to enter the Tag Set Candidates that are present in the User Tag Set. The
343   closer it is to 1, the deeper the system’s grasp of user preferences. (2) Win Rate: Besides Accuracy,
344   we incorporate feedback from the AI annotator as another metric. The percentage represents the
345   frequency of a response being chosen over our PREMIUM-Embed. A rate below 50% suggests that
346
      PREMIUM-Embed is outperforming the compared baseline. Compared to Accuracy, this provides a
      more comprehensive assessment: In addition to the selection of user tags, it considers other factors
347
      influencing user preferences, such as improved response quality from selecting query-relevant tags;
348
      For LaMP-2, we follow Salemi et al. (2024b) and utilize Accuracy and F1 Score as our metrics.
349   Higher accuracy and F1 scores indicate more precise predictions for personalized movie tagging.
350
351   Setups. For the baselines based on the tag system, we use Ranking-TAGER as the dataset. To
352   demonstrate the effectiveness of PREMIUM with different tag systems, we conduct experiments
353   under three setups with increasing action spaces: "3/20," "3/50," and "3/100." The first number
354   represents the number of tags in both the User Tag Set and the Tag Set Candidate, while the second
355
      number indicates the size of the Tag Library. Notably, under the same setup, all methods requiring
      user feedback use the same number of cases: 67, 112, and 164 for the three setups, respectively. In
356
      each of our experiments, the User Tag Set is randomly selected from the Tag Library to initialize the
357
      user’s preferences. For TidyBot and OPPU, as stronger baselines, we compare them with PREMIUM-
358   Embed across both the Ranking-TAGER and LaMP-2 datasets, providing a more comprehensive and
359   convincing evaluation.

511
      LLM Personalization Recent research on LLM personalization has explored numerous directions:
512
      Collins et al. (2023) utilizes federated learning with PEFT to balance between personalization and
513   robustness. Zhang et al. (2024c) employs a Bayesian Optimization searching strategy to find the
514   optimal LoRA injection method in PEFT. Salemi et al. (2024a) attempts to use the discrepancy
515   between responses generated by LLMs and ground truth responses in the dataset as a signal to fine-
516   tune the retriever used for retrieval augmentation. Jin et al. (2024) applies the retrieval-augmented
517   method to personal health management. Karra & Tulabandhula (2024); Yang et al. (2023); Liu et al.
518   (2023); Chen et al. (2024) leverage the powerful summarization capabilities of LLMs to summarize
519   user interaction histories, such as search and browsing records, into textual user profiles.
520
      Learning from Human Feedback Learning from Human Feedback is widely employed to align
521
      LLMs with human values (Ziegler et al., 2020; Nakano et al., 2022). Reinforcement Learning from
522   Human Feedback (RLHF) utilizes pairwise comparison feedback from annotators and RL to align
523   LLMs with human values (Stiennon et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022). Some works also attempt to
524   extract human preferences by utilizing ranking feedback between responses (Yuan et al., 2023; Song
525   et al., 2024). Additionally, some efforts involve directly fine-tuning LLMs using human feedback to
526   address issues such as training instability in RL (Rafailov et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024). However,
527   most existing relevant works focus on aligning LLMs with diverse human values rather than the
528   preferences of a specific individual user (Lanctot et al., 2023; Chakraborty et al., 2024). Applying
529   Learning from Human Feedback methods to individual feedback remains an area yet to be explored.
530
531   8   C ONCLUSION
532   In this study, we propose PREMIUM, an innovative LLM-agnostic framework for LLM personal-
533   ization, which utilizes tags to characterize user profiles and individual-level preference feedback to
534   align with user preferences, addressing the limitations of existing methods in flexibility, privacy, and
535   cost. PREMIUM includes two variants: PREMIUM-Prompt and PREMIUM-Embed, with the latter
536   excelling in performance and efficiency. Our Ranking-TAGER dataset, which provides a valuable
537   evaluation protocol for LLM personalization, enabled us to demonstrate that PREMIUM outperforms
538   all baselines by achieving significantly higher accuracy and win rates. Notably, PREMIUM-Embed
539   requires minimal resources, can adapt to dynamic user preferences, generalize to expanded Tag
      Library, and extend to binary tags, making it a practical solution for personalized LLMs.

1446   H.6   E XPLORING THE IMPACT OF THE NUMBER OF RANKED RESPONSES ON THE
1447         PERFORMANCE OF PREMIUM-E MBED
1448
1449   Essentially, the parameter updates for PREMIUM-Embed rely on pairwise preference data extracted
1450   from preference ranking feedback. Therefore, the larger the number of response candidates m, the
1451   more data a single user feedback can provide for updating the tag selector.
1452   In this section, we briefly explore the impact of the number of ranked responses on the performance
1453   of PREMIUM-Embed by testing with different values of m (the number of responses to be ranked)
1454   as 2, 3, and 4 in the "3/50" setup of the Ranking-TAGER-RW Dataset. The results, shown in Table
1455   14, indicate that as m increases, the personalization performance of PREMIUM-Embed improves.
1456   Nevertheless, even when the feedback type is pairwise feedback or three-choice ranking feedback,
1457   which are relatively easy to obtain (corresponding to m=2 and m=3, respectively), PREMIUM-Embed
       still achieves efficient LLM personalization.

1493
1494   Here, we discuss the broader impacts of this work. Our research aims to propose a novel LLM-agnostic
1495   framework for LLM personalization and introduces a lightweight, locally deployable implementation.
1496   The proposed PREMIUM framework enables both parameter-open LLMs (such as LLaMA-2) and
1497   black-box LLMs (such as GPT-3.5) to generate responses aligned with user preferences. This can be
1498
       applied to a wide range of downstream tasks, encompassing customer service (Rome et al., 2024),
       personal health (Abbasian et al., 2024), and recommender systems (Li et al., 2024), demonstrating
1499
       significant potential for positive societal impacts.
1500
1501   Moreover, our approach only requires users to provide Preference Ranking Feedback and does not
1502   necessitate any textual user information. The PREMIUM-Embed stores the learned user preferences
1503   within the neural network parameters rather than generating explicit textual user profiles, ensuring
1504
       robust user privacy protection. To our knowledge, our work does not have any negative societal
       impacts.
