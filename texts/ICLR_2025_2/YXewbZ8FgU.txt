                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            In-context learning, which allows large language models to perform diverse tasks
012            with a few demonstrations, is found to have imbalanced per-class prediction accu-
013            racy on multi-class text classification. Although notable output correction meth-
014            ods have been developed to tackle the issue and simultaneously improve down-
015            stream prediction accuracy, they may fail to answer the core interpretability chal-
016            lenges: why and which certain classes need corrections, and more importantly, a
017
               tailored correction for per-sample, per-class’s probability. To address such inter-
               pretability gaps, we first find that the imbalance arises from certain classes consis-
018
               tently receiving high ICL output probabilities, whereas others receiving lower or
019
               mixed ranges, so the former is more frequently chosen, resulting in higher accu-
020            racy; more crucially, we find that these ranges have significantly varying degrees
021            of influence on the accuracy bias, highlighting the need for precise, interpretable
022            probability corrections by range. Motivated by this, we propose FuRud, a Fuzzy
023            Rule Optimization based Debiasing method, that (1) detects which classes need
024            corrections, and (2) for each correction-needed class, detects its probability ranges
025            and applies asymmetric amplifications or reductions to correct them interpretably.
026            Notably, across seven benchmark datasets, FuRud reduces the pairwise class ac-
027            curacy bias (COBias) by more than half (56%), while achieving a relative increase
028
               of 21% in accuracy, outperforming state-of-the-art debiasing methods. Moreover,
               FuRud can optimize a downstream task in a few-shot manner, with as few as 10
029
               optimization examples. Furthermore, FuRud can work for prompt formats that
030
               lead to highly skewed predictions. For example, FuRud greatly improves ICL
031            outputs which use letter options, with 44% relative accuracy increase and 54%
032            relative COBias reduction.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   The classification outputs by in-context learning (ICL) are described as biased when they exhibit
038   imbalanced per-class prediction accuracy. Addressing such imbalances while improving overall
039   accuracy is seen as a category of debiasing. Concretely, the skewness in the output space can be al-
040   leviated by targeted corrections on output logits or probabilities, with or without explicitly modeling
041   the per-class accuracy differences, i.e., COBias (Lin & You, 2024). However, while effective, prior
042   methods could lack straightforward explanations on why and which certain classes need corrections.
043   What’s more challenging is to have a tailored per-sample, per-class correction.
044   A direct cause of COBias is that ICL tends to assign specific ranges of output probabilities to each
045   class. When some classes always receive high probabilities for any input example, others may
046   have lower or mixed probability ranges. The consequence is that latter classes are less frequently
047   predicted than the former, resulting in consistently lower accuracies and calling for probability cor-
048   rections. In addition, among all examples of a class A, the subset of examples whose in-context
049   learned probability of answer A is relatively low often receive a lower test accuracy, compared to
050
      the subset whose class A probability is higher, suggesting that different probability ranges within a
      class need different corrections.
051
052   Taking these overlooked aspects into account, a correction should be tailored for each class and
053   for each sample. To achieve this, a helpful correction should be able to asymmetrically amplify
      or reduce different ranges of a class’s probabilities. In this paper, we address the pressing need for

      enhanced understandings in how biased ICL predictions happen, and propose two research questions

057   RQ1: What is the interpretability challenge in correcting in-context learned representations?
058
      Given an N -class classification dataset, let us denote its m-th example’s input prompt and label as
      (xm , ym ), where xm consists of a task instruction, few-shot demonstrative examples, and the input
059
      example’s question. The LLM in-context learns the class probabilities pm = (pm1 , . . . , pmN ) (nor-
060
      malized over the N classes), and the prediction ŷm is arg maxi pmi . The probabilities pm may need
061   corrections given the debiasing objective of reducing COBias. Therefore, our task is to correct cer-
062   tain dimensions of pm towards reducing COBias and improving overall accuracy. The interpretabil-
063   ity challenges raised in this process can be specified as (1), detecting which classes need corrections,
064   and (2), for each correction-needed class, applying range-specific amplifications/reductions.
065
      RQ2: How can we improve interpretability with fuzzy rules? We leverage membership functions
066
      from the field of fuzzy rule based systems for debiasing. For backgrounds, a membership function
067   is a curve that defines a mapping from a crisp input value to a fuzzy value between 0 and 1 (Zadeh,
068   1965). Based on this, given class probabilities as input attributes, membership functions transform
069   the probabilities to fuzzy values, which could be viewed as corrected probabilities under certain
070   debiasing optimization objectives.
071
      The key intuition here is that a membership function can asymmetrically amplify or reduce different
072
      ranges of inputs. Therefore, a fuzzy rule based debiaser for class probability pmi can be written as
073   fAi (pmi ), where Ai is a fuzzy set for class i, and its membership function fAi maps the probability
074   to a corrected p′mi := fAi (pmi ). Then p′m consists of corrected per-class probabilities.
075
076
      Alternatively, the debiaser can be viewed as a single rule:
077
078               If class 1 is A1 and ... and class N is AN then predict arg maxj fAj (pmj )             (1)
                    |                   {z                 }      |            {z          }
079                                  Antecedent                               Consequent
080
081   Our goal is to optimize the rule, i.e., select fuzzy sets/membership functions for every class in the
082   antecedent, towards mitigating COBias and improving overall prediction accuracy. Specially, we
083   include a Don’t Change membership function that will keep a class unchanged, suggesting that
084   the LLM in-context learns an accurate probability for the class. When a correction is needed, the
085   membership function detects the probability range that a class’s probability belongs to, and updates
086   it with the returned function value. The problem becomes jointly selecting a set of membership
087   functions for each class towards improving multi-objectives based on COBias and accuracy.
088   To this end, we propose a Fuzzy Rule Optimization based Debiasing method, FuRud, which demon-
089   strate via extensive experiments (Section 4) and discussions (Section 5) that it achieves good im-
090   provements over accuracy and COBias while providing sample-level interpretability.
091
      In a nutshell, FuRud uses an optimization set of samples for membership function selection. The
092
      optimization set’s questions are prompted in 1-shot manner, and probabilities are measured across
093   answer classes for each question. These probabilities and ground-truth answers across all questions
094   are aggregated in the multi-objective model, to jointly learn an optimal membership function for
095   each class. At inference, a test example’s class probabilities are obtained similarly. Then we apply
096   the learned membership functions to perform tailored corrections at each class’s probability in the
097   given test sample. An overview of FuRud is shown in Figure 1, illustrating desired corrections and
098   performance improvements.
099   To highlight, the membership functions learned by FuRud enable sample-level interpretability. Fu-
100   Rud enables us to know whether the LLM in-context learns an accurate probability for a class within
101   a given sample. This is achieved by learning a correction function (membership function) for each
102   class, towards the multi-objectives of reducing COBias and enhancing accuracy. If the Don’t Change
103   function is learned for a class, it means the LLM in-context learns an accurate probability for the
104   class; otherwise, a tailored correction is performed by the membership function. The source code
105   will be released upon paper publication. In summary, our messages are:
106

               account for both inter-class surface biases and intra-class range-wise influences.

149   Language Model Bias Mitigation. At the heart of debiasing is detecting biased patterns that arise
150   in a large language model (LLM)’s outputs. Prior work has found various prediction biases in ICL,
151   and address the biased patterns by methods of contextual prompt engineering and output adjustment
152   (Brown et al., 2020; Schick et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2021). Particularly, on classification tasks, re-
153
      searchers have found that LLMs’ outputs are sensitive to ICL formatting, such as prompt templates,
      demonstrations, and verbalizers (Min et al., 2022; Holtzman et al., 2021; Schick & Schütze, 2021);
154
      besides, LLMs tend to output common tokens in the pre-training data (Zhao et al., 2021). These bias
155
      factors lead to majority label bias (Zhao et al., 2021), COBias (pairwise class accuracy differences)
156   (Lin & You, 2024), etc, causing imbalanced per-class accuracies, and researchers address these bi-
157   ases by making output distribution calibrations (Zhao et al., 2021; Fei et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024),
158   or by class probability re-weighting (Lin & You, 2024). For example, Zhao et al. (2021) calibrate
159   the output distribution with content-free/dummy test prompts. Zhou et al. (2024) calibrate the output
160   distribution in a test-time manner, estimating a contextual correction term of each class on a batch of
161   test examples; the proposed Batch Calibration (BC) method outperforms previous calibration meth-
      ods (Zhao et al., 2021; Fei et al., 2023) on a range of text classification tasks. Lin & You (2024)


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      re-weights output probabilities by a set of class-specific weight coefficients; the proposed Debiasing
163   as Nonlinear Integer Programming method (DNIP) achieves much lower COBias with higher accu-
164   racy than the ICL baseline. Though these debiasing methods effectively adjust ICL outputs, they
165   do not emphasize interpretable bias handling. For example, a calibration method may not explicitly
166   explain why a class needs corrections, or users may not fathom how a re-weighting method performs
167   the exact corrections a class need.
168
      Fuzzy Rule Techniques for Interpretable Machine Learning. Interpretable machine learning
169   often needs a human-readable subset of features to generate the target (Jethani et al., 2021; Carvalho
170   et al., 2019). Fuzzy rules are intrinsically interpretable and are widely studied for interpretable
171   machine learning (Vernon et al., 2024; Vilone & Longo, 2020; Ishibuchi & Nojima, 2007). In
172   classical fuzzy rule classification systems, input attributes are assigned to fuzzy sets to generate
173   rules for pattern classification (Ishibuchi et al., 1999; 2005; Nojima & Ishibuchi, 2016; Rudziński,
174   2016; Gorzałczany & Rudziński, 2017). A fuzzy classification system thus contains multiple human-
175   readable rules, which can be as simple as “1. If attribute Bare Nuclei is Small then consequent class
176   Benign.2....3. If attribute Uniformity of Cell Size is not Small then consequent class Malignant.”
177
      (Gorzałczany & Rudziński, 2017). Here, Small and not Small are fuzzy sets, with corresponding
      membership functions. Membership functions provide the core interpretability of the fuzzy systems.
178
      In this work, we extend fuzzy membership functions to help with debiasing.

184   The core idea is to handle the imbalanced per-class accuracy issue with fuzzy membership functions.
185
      In the fuzzy rule setting, for N classes, each class selects a fuzzy set Ai , or equivalently, a mem-
      bership function fAi , from a family of K fixed fuzzy sets. We let F = {f1 , ..., fk , ..., fK } denote
186
      the family of membership functions. The membership function selection problem can be solved
187
      using combinatorial optimization. To this end, we introduce FuRud, a Fuzzy Rule Optimization
188   Based debiasing method. The FuRud optimization is performed on a set of labeled examples, and
189   the selected membership functions are directly applied to transform test-time class probabilities.

      Evaluation Tasks and Evaluation Metrics. The proposed method is evaluated on a diverse range of
275   text classification datasets, including AGNews (Zhang et al., 2015), a 4-class news topic classifica-
276   tion; DBpedia (Auer et al., 2007), a 14-class ontology classification dataset derived from Wikipedia;
277   SST-5 (Socher et al., 2013), a 5-class sentiment classification dataset; TREC (Voorhees & Tice,
278   2000; Li & Roth, 2002), a 6-class question classification dataset; RTE (Dagan et al., 2006), a bi-
279   nary entailment recognition dataset; and two biomedical domain-specific datasets, including DDI
280   (Segura-Bedmar et al., 2013), a 5-class drug-drug interaction relation extraction dataset; PubMedQA
281   (Jin et al., 2019), a 3-class biomedical question answering dataset. Each evaluation dataset is split
282   into optimization/development/test sets. We follow (Lin & You, 2024) to preprocess the datasets.
283
      Evaluation metrics are accuracy and COBias.
284   FuRud Setups. The 19 triangular membership functions in Figure 2 form the base of selections for
285   FuRud. To obtain the per-class probabilities from ICL, we prompt Llama-2-13B (13B parameters)
286   in 1-shot manner. The output softmax probabilities normalized over all classes are used as attributes.
287   The energy function we used in the experiments is a special form of Equation 7 with ω = 1, γ Acc =
288   −1, γ COBias = α, γ Extreme = β. In other words, the final multi-objective optimization function is
289
      minκ Z = 1 − Z Acc + αZ COBias + βZ Extreme , where we learn κi for class i = 1, . . . , N on an
      optimization set of samples, which is the full or a subset of training set. Each κi is selected from
290
      the given set of membership functions, and κi = k means membership function fk is selected.
291
      At inference time, for a test sample, let p = (p1 , . . . , pi , . . . , pN ) be its in-context learned output
292   class probabilities, then these probabilities are transformed by their learned membership functions,
293   according to Eq. 3. The corrected prediction is ŷ = arg maxi fκi (pi ).
294
295
      The above model Z is optimized using the SA metaheuristic. The core step of SA is to sample a
      new solution κ = (κ1 , . . . , κN ), e.g., (16, . . . , 8), and evaluate it on Z. If Z is smaller, the algo-
296
      rithm accepts the new solution; otherwise, it accepts the new solution with an acceptance probability
297
      exp(−∆Z/T ), where T is the temperature at the step.The values of α, β are tuned on the develop-
298   ment set. Since we do not know an estimate for the expected threshold value λ in downstream tasks,
299   we set it to 0.5 for simplicity. Prompting is done on a 80G A100 GPU. The simulated annealing

437   We experiment with different combinations of the four fuzzy
438   partitions in Figure 2, in addition to the main results using all
439   partitions. The partitions are characterized by different rates

442   The slopes for the top left, top right, bottom left, and bot-

      ically, the bottom right partition has the Don’t Change func-
      tion y = x and its symmetric function y = 1 − x, which will

446
      function plays a vital role in keeping some classes unchanged, Figure 6: Accuracy-COBias trade-
447   we experiment with five combinations, including DC , and off with 5 combinations of fuzzy
448   DC with each partition of slope ±2, ±4, ±8. The accuracy partitions.
449   and COBias scores of five combinations are shown in Figure

454   The optimal accuracy and COBias is achieved with mixed partitions.
455   In addition, the Don’t Change fuction is essentially needed in debiasing. We perform an ablation
456   analysis with the partition ±8 only, and find that, while achieving similar accuracies, its COBias is
457   6% higher than using DC with partition ±8. Moreover, for example, 4 out of 14 classes on DBpedia
458   are optimized with Don’t Change, suggesting that keeping certain classes unchanged is necessary for
459   jointly optimizing overall accuracy and COBias. This demonstrates that a dedicated Don’t Change
460   function is needed in the multi-objective optimization.
461   In summary, higher membership function granularities are good for COBias reduction. However,
462   although it is tempting to include as many membership functions as possible to reduce COBias, there
463   is the accuracy-COBias tradeoff. Too many membership functions may not further boost accuracy
464   and could induce more computational costs.

477   Computational Costs. As for computational costs, the computational time of FuRud optimization
478   is in the scale of minutes, from several minutes to around 30 minutes, depending on the dataset
479   (e.g., number of classes, optimization set sizes, etc). For DNIP, the computational time is similarly
480   in the scale of minutes. For the calibration method Batch Calibration (BC), it applies an analytical
481   calculation on all samples’ ICL probabilities, introducing small computational overhead.
482   Interpretability compared: DNIP and FuRud. The DNIP method shows good debiasing perfor-
483   mances, but it applies indiscriminate reduction (or relative amplification) to the probabilities, making
484   it difficult to capture the varying degrees of influence of different probability ranges to the accuracy
485   bias, potentially limiting its interpretability. The use of fuzzy membership functions overcomes this
      issue, and this is a main innovation of our paper.

486
      Can we use the traditional fuzzy rule based systems for debiasing? That would require maintain-
487   ing multiple candidate rules like ”Rq : If the probability of class 1 is Aq1 and ... and the probability of
488   class N is AqN , then predict Yq ,” where Yq is the consequent/target class. Training such rules is com-
489   putationally expensive, and inference time for a winning rule grows with the number of candidates.
490   Additionally, calculating the product of membership values could cause issues such as overflow,
491   and achieving high accuracy might demand an overwhelming number of rules, making the system
492   inefficient. In contrast, FuRud eliminates the need for learning multiple rules, as its transformations
493   could implicitly capture many rules found in traditional fuzzy classification systems.
494   We have a different motivation from traditional post-hoc corrections. Some may argue that en-
495   suring equitable accuracies across all classes is a well-studied problem in standard machine learning
496   classifiers. It is worth emphasizing that the per-class prediction accuracy imbalance should be treated
497   within their particular context. The accuracy bias in ICL outputs stems from completely different
498   causes than the unequal class accuracies observed in potentially overfitted traditional classifiers,
499   where the former is rooted in prompts and the LLMs, and the latter arises from class imbalance of
500   supervised training data. That’s why our method is particularly applied to ICL’s output token class
501
      probabilities, pinpointing specific patterns and applying precise, targeted corrections.
502
503   6    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
504
505   In this work, we present a fuzzy rule optimization based debiasing method to enhance ICL output
506   class representations with interpretability. FuRud learns a per-class correction function, i.e., a mem-
507   bership function, which decides if and how a class’s probability needs correction for each sample.
508   If correction is needed, the corrected class probability will be tailored by the membership function,
509   which is a main innovation of this paper. On a diverse set of text classification benchmarks, Fu-
510   Rud greatly improves the average test accuracy and test COBias over ICL, by a relative increase
511   of 21% and a relative reduction of 56%, outperforming state-of-the-art methods. Moreover, Fu-
512
      Rud can work for prompt formats that may lead to highly skewed predictions, e.g., letter options.
      Furthermore, FuRud can optimize a downstream task with as few as 10 optimization examples.
513
514   In the future, more versatile rules can be explored, and we may also examine the tradeoff between the
515   accuracy and rule complexity. Simpler rules are easier to understand, but the transformations may
516   fail to catch the intricate interactions between class predictions. More complex rules may have better
517   modeling capabilities, but they are harder to read. In addition, this work focuses on evaluating text
518
      classification, and we will extend interpretable ICL debiasing to more language tasks, modalities,
      and model architectures.
