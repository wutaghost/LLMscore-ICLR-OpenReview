009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            As Large Language Models (LLMs) become increasingly integral to various do-
012            mains, their potential to generate harmful responses has prompted significant soci-
013            etal and regulatory concerns. In response, governments, including the European
014            Union, have issued ethics guidelines to promote the development of trustworthy
015            AI. However, these guidelines are typically high-level demands for model develop-
016            ers and testers. There remains a gap in translating these broad requirements into
017
               actionable testing questions to verify LLM compliance.
018            To address this challenge, we introduce GUARD (Guideline Upholding Test
019
               through Adaptive Role-play and Jailbreak Diagnostics), a testing method designed
               to operationalize guidelines into specific guideline-violating questions that assess
020
               LLM adherence. To implement this, GUARD assigns LLMs to play different roles,
021
               enabling the collaborative and automated generation of guideline-violating ques-
022            tions based on government-issued guidelines, thereby testing whether responses
023            comply with these guidelines. When responses directly violate guidelines, GUARD
024            reports inconsistencies. Furthermore, for responses that do not directly violate
025            guidelines, GUARD integrates the concept of “jailbreaks” to diagnostics, named
026            GUARD-JD, which creates scenarios that provoke unethical or guideline-violating
027            responses, effectively identifying potential scenarios that could bypass built-in
028            safety mechanisms. Our method finally culminates in a comprehensive compliance
029            report, delineating the extent of adherence and highlighting any violations.
030            We have empirically validated the effectiveness of GUARD on seven LLMs, in-
031
               cluding Vicuna-13B, LongChat-7B, Llama2-7B, Llama-3-8B, GPT-3.5, GPT-4,
               and Claude-3.5, by testing compliance under three government-issued guidelines
032
               and conducting jailbreak diagnostics. Additionally, GUARD-JD can transfer jail-
033
               break diagnostics to vision-language models (MiniGPT-v2 and Gemini Vision Pro),
034            demonstrating its versatility and providing valuable insights for the development
035            of safer, more reliable LLM-based applications across diverse modalities.

040   The widespread application and popularity of Large Language Models (LLMs) have led to significant
041   advancements and also attracted malicious individuals exploiting LLMs for misinformation and
042   criminal activities (Kreps et al., 2022; Goldstein et al., 2023). These usages often deviate from ethical
043   norms and can have unforeseen consequences, necessitating appropriate regulation.
044   Governments and authoritative organizations have issued preliminary guidelines to regulate LLM
045   usage and development (Smuha, 2019). However, unlike the safeguards implemented by developers,
046   these guidelines typically provide high-level requirements, urging model developers to thoroughly
047   test their systems before deploying them. For instance, Fig.1(a) highlights a rule from the EU’s
048   “Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI” (European Commission, 2019), exemplifying such high-level
049   recommendations for developers and testers and emphasizing that AI systems should not generate
050
      content that violates human rights.

052   actionable instructions from guidelines. While the guidelines provide high-level requirements, they
053   often lack detailed, practical steps for developers to follow. For example, as shown in Fig. 1(a), the
      guidelines require developers to consider the fundamental rights of individuals. However, without

      content. In the US, notable initiatives include the “Blueprint for an AI Bill of Rights” (House,

128   Trustworthy AI” (European Commission, 2019) lay the groundwork for regulatory frameworks in
129   Europe.

      LLM-based Role-Playing. LLM-based role-playing uses personas integrated into models, demon-
131
      strating capabilities across domains via prompt engineering. In software development, frameworks
132   like “ChatDev” (Qian et al., 2023) and “MetaGPT” (Hong et al., 2023) assign roles such as CTO or
133   engineer to break down tasks. In gaming, LLMs act as characters (e.g., buyers/sellers), leveraging
134   memory systems (Wang et al., 2023; Park et al., 2023). In healthcare, “DR-CoT” (Wu et al., 2023a)
135   and “MedAgent” (Tang et al., 2023) simulate diagnostic reasoning and collaborative decision-making.
136   LLM role-playing also enhances evaluation, as shown in “ChatEval” (Chan et al., 2023).
137
      Jailbreak Attacks. Jailbreak attacks, either manual or automatic, compromise LLMs. Manual
138   attacks often use techniques like Chain-of-Thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022) to extract sensitive
139   information (Li et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023b), while automatic attacks optimize token space with
140   model parameters (Shin et al., 2020; Jones et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023). In black-box
141   settings, attackers exploit API access, fine-tuning (Deng et al., 2023), in-context learning (Wei et al.,
142   2023), or explore autonomous jailbreak generation (Chao et al., 2023). Persona modulation (Shah
143   et al., 2023) and query-only attacks (Hayase et al., 2024) also contribute to these exploits. Recent
144   works explore cryptographic evasion techniques (Ren et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2023;
145   Handa et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2024).
146   Vision Language Model Robustness. VLMs are vulnerable to adversarial images, which can trigger
147   toxic outputs (Carlini et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023). Jailbreak images and multi-modal attacks
148   further expose weaknesses (Qi et al., 2023; Schlarmann & Hein, 2023). Specialized benchmarks now
149   exist for evaluating these issues (Luo et al., 2024).
150   Scope and Guideline Selection. Our method evaluates LLM adherence to guidelines by transforming
151   high-level requirements into actionable violations through role-playing. Using jailbreak diagnostics,
152   it uncovers how compliant prompts can lead to violations. We focus on guidelines that restrict LLM
153   behavior, aligning with the needs of AI developers and evaluators.

158   3.1   G UIDELINE U PHOLDING T EST
159
160   GUARD aims to generate specific guideline-violating questions from abstract high-level guidelines,
161   testing whether the responses adhere to or violate these guidelines. It further identifies scenarios in
      which the LLM may produce guideline-violating responses using jailbreaks.


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      To simplify the expression, we refer to these guideline-violating questions as question prompts,
163   denoted by Q. For a target LLM F, its response is denoted as F(Q). If the model violates the
164   guidelines, it is expected to produce confident responses such as “Sure...” or “Definitely...”. These
165   are referred to as guideline-violating answers, denoted by V(Q). On the other hand, if the model
166   complies with the guidelines, it should respond with refusal answers like “I can’t help you”. These
167   are referred to as guideline-adhering answers, denoted by D(Q).
168
      To further utilize jailbreak to create some scenarios for these questions that elicit guideline-adhering
169   answers, we introduce the concept of a playing scenario, denoted by S. This scenario serves
170   as a template for bypassing the default responses. When question prompts are injected into the
171   playing scenario, it transforms into a jailbreak prompt, denoted by P. This process is formulated as
172   P = S ⊕ Q, where ⊕ denotes string concatenation.
173
      Under an appropriate playing scenario, guideline-violating answers may be produced, indicating
174
      a successful jailbreak. In such cases, the output of the target LLM F(P), such as “Sure...” or
175   “Definitely...”, is semantically opposite to the guideline-adhering answer. To quantify the difference
176   between D(Q) and F(P), we use a similarity score, calculated based on semantic similarity and
177   normalized between 0 and 1. This score functions as a fitness metric in GUARD-JD, where a lower
178   value indicates a higher likelihood of a successful jailbreak.

202   3.3   G UIDELINE -V IOLATING Q UESTION G ENERATION
203
204   The process of generating guideline-violating questions begins with analyzing high-level guidelines to
205   extract key features, such as the principles they aim to test and the potential conflicts these principles
206
      may introduce. At this step, we assign the role of Analyst to the LLM, which is responsible for
      identifying and organizing these features. This step ensures that the subsequent processes have a
207
      clear understanding of the guidelines’ focus and potential areas for testing.
208
209   Next, the identified principles and conflicts are used to create domains and scenarios that illustrate
210   how these principles may apply in specific contexts. Domains refer to areas or sectors where conflicts
211   might arise, while scenarios provide concrete examples that help contextualize these principles. In
212
      this step, the LLM takes on the role of a Strategic Committee, simulating relevant domains and
      generating examples to ground the question generation process.
213
214   With the domains and scenarios defined, the next step is to design initial guideline-violating questions.
215   These questions aim to challenge the language model’s adherence to the guidelines. The LLM acts as
      a Question Designer, using the contextual information to generate questions that reflect the principles

      and conflicts. Existing benchmarks such as AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023), HarmBench (Mazeika et al.,

219   After generating the initial questions, we evaluate them to ensure they align with the guidelines. In

      3.4     JAILBREAK D IAGNOSTICS
238
239   Once guideline-violating questions are generated, they are input into the target LLM to elicit responses.
240   We use a string-matching approach (Zou et al., 2023) to determine if the response adheres to guidelines.
241   For instance, if the response contains refusal phrases such as “Sorry...” or “As a language model...”,
242   it is classified as a guideline-adhering answer; otherwise, it is classified as a guideline-violating
243   answer. We assess the string-matching approach’s alignment with human evaluation in Appendix B.
244   However, even if the LLM provides a guideline-adhering answer, this does not necessarily ensure
245   full safety, as there may still be potential scenarios where the LLM fails to comply. To address this,
246   jailbreak diagnostics are applied to create such scenarios that prompt the LLM to respond to these
247   questions. GUARD then generates and updates scenarios using role-playing techniques to optimize
248   and test for possible guideline violations.

250   3.4.1    S ETUP
251
252
      We observed that many efforts focus on breaking the built-in safety mechanisms of LLMs using
      manually crafted jailbreak prompts. A notable example is Jailbreak Chat (the link is in Appendix L),
253
      which hosts an extensive collection of ChatGPT jailbreak prompts. While these prompts were effective
254
      at the time of their creation, their effectiveness is often short-lived since the model developers readily
255   access them and patch the vulnerabilities they find. In light of this, we try to understand why these
256   jailbreak prompts can be applied to break the built-in safety mechanism. Further, we assume the
257   potential for their reuse by modifying parts of these prompts that have become ineffective.
258
      To begin with, we collect and download existing jailbreak prompts, from Jailbreak Chat, with 78 in
259
      total. Following collection, our focus shifted to an in-depth analysis of these prompts, concentrating
260
      on the frequency of words and their semantic patterns. While some works (Deng et al., 2023; Shah
261   et al., 2023) learned from successful manually-crafted jailbreak templates to generate new jailbreaks,
262   we take further steps to attribute the effectiveness to keywords and phrases. Specifically, we examined
263   the usage of various parts of speech, such as nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs. We analyzed these
264   words in their contexts to explore potential relationships between each prompt via WordNet (Fellbaum,
265   2010). Further, we use WordCloud (Heimerl et al., 2014) to cluster the most frequently occurring
266   words. In this way, we finally form a way that categorizes existing jailbreaks by eight characteristics:
267   • Introduction and Naming: Descriptions of the role’s name and the reason behind its name selection,
268      including “character", “role", “persona", and “identity".
269   • Capabilities: Descriptions of the range of tasks the role can perform.
      • Examples of Capability: Illustrative examples showcasing the role’s capabilities.


                                                           5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      • Information Handling: Explanations about how information should be processed, including filtering
271     restrictions.
272   • Flexibility and Denying Limitations: Clarifications about the role’s limitations and any specific
273     tasks that it cannot perform, and elucidate any permissible flexible behaviors.
274   • Response Format: Details on the preferred structure of responses or any specific prefixes to be
275     included.
276   • Obligation and Information Generation: Stipulations on the role’s obligations, such as the require-
277     ment to provide responses and generate information.
278   • Reminder of Capabilities: Techniques or methods to remind a role when it forgets its capabilities.
279   However, not every jailbreak has all eight characteristics. If certain characteristics are missing in a
280   jailbreak, we will use None instead. Based on this paradigm, we can separate jailbreak prompts into
281   sentences and phrases. The examples of these eight characteristics are in Appendix A.4.
282
      Then we discuss how to use the jailbreak paradigm to create a playing scenario. Considering those
283   existing jailbreak prompts have been separated into unstructured sentences and phrases, then we use
284   knowledge graphs (KGs) (Ji et al., 2021) to store them, making them accessible and easy to retrieve
285   in the subsequent steps. Formally, a KG can be represented as a directed graph G = (V, E), where
286   V = v1 , v2 , ..., vn is a set of vertices. These vertices represent the entities within the graph. The
287   edges, denoted as E = {(vi , r, vj )|vi , vj ∈ V, r ∈ R}, represents the relationship between these
288   entities. r denotes a specific type of relationship, drawn from a predefined set of relationship types
289   R. Each edge is a tuple consisting of a pair of vertices and the relationship that connects them. In
290   our work, we focus on a jailbreak scenario, where each vertex can represent one of characteristics
291
      like “Capabilities". We treat each vertex node as an individual sub-Knowledge Graph (sub-KG).
      Formally, for a vertex vi corresponding to a particular characteristic, it is linked to Ni nodes. The
292
      connected nodes, denoted as {n1vi , n2vi , ..., nN
                                                       vi } represent keywords or attributes associated with that
                                                         i

294   to answer the question".

308   3.4.2   O PTIMIZATION
309
310   In this part, we detail how to employ role-playing LLMs for achieving successful jailbreak diag-
311   nostics, as the remaining block shows. These three roles, - Generator, Evaluator, and Optimizer,
312   are responsible for jailbreak writing, organizing, assessing, and updating, respectively. Detailed
313
      responsibilities for each role are listed:
314   • Generator: Re-organize jailbreak fragments into coherent and natural playing scenarios S. It also
315     modifies these scenarios based on advice from the Optimizer.
316   • Evaluator: Calculate the similarity score between D(Q) and the responses generated by the target
317     LLMs F(P).
318   • Optimizer: Give suggestions to the Generator, on minimizing the similarity score to improve the
319
        jailbreak performance.
320   We can get an initial playing scenario from Section 3.4.1. It will be added as the prefix to the
321   guideline-violating question and then further input to the target LLM. The Evaluator calculates the
322   semantic similarity score, defined as: sim(F(P), D(Q)). In the first iteration steps, the similarity
323   score may be high. This may be attributed to some outdated sentence or missing some vital elements.
      Then the Optimizer will provide modification advice to reduce the score, such as “Eliminate the


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      mention of policies or regulations posed by OpenAI”. The Generator will re-organize and update
325   the playing scenario according to the advice, for providing better playing scenarios. Note that in
326   this process, the guideline-violating question will remain the same but the playing scenario will
327   iteratively be updated until the optimization is finished, i.e., the guideline-violating answers are
328   generated. During iteration, we consider the jailbreak diagnostics to be successful if the similarity
329   score is lower than a certain number. Non-compliance will be reported if the target LLM responds to
330   the guideline-violating question within the given playing scenario.
331   The detailed prompt templates are shown in the Appendix J.1. To initialize each role, we adopt a
332   specific system prompt guided by 3-shot examples (detailed in Appendix J.2), which ensures that the
333   prompts are thoughtfully crafted to align with the specific functions and objectives of each role in the
334   jailbreak diagnostics process. The generated successful playing scenario will be then deconstructed
335   and subsequently integrated back into the KG based on the paradigm, for future use. A flowchart
336   illustrating the jailbreak diagnostic process is available in Appendix A.3.

      GUARD identifies non-compliance in LLMs by testing their responses to guideline-violating ques-
448
      tions. However, even when models provide guideline-adhering answers does not fully confirm
449   guideline alignment, as LLMs may still generate inappropriate answers in other scenarios. To eval-
450   uate consistent guideline adherence, we employ jailbreak diagnostics that create more challenging
451   scenarios, assessing robustness beyond refusal patterns. We collect 500 guideline-violating questions
452   derived from government-issued guidelines for each LLM and generate scenarios to test compliance.
453   For a fair comparison, we evaluate the effectiveness of jailbreak diagnostics against baseline meth-
454   ods (Zou et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Chao et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2023), noting
455   that baselines rely on benchmarks like advBench (Zou et al., 2023) and do not generate questions
456   based on guidelines. In this section, we will use GUARD-JD to denote the jailbreak diagnostics
457
      component of GUARD. Both GUARD-JD and the baselines use the same set of guideline-violating
      questions, and we assess effectiveness through the direct effectiveness of jailbreak diagnostics.
458

460   7B to GPT-3.5 and GPT-4. For GUARD-JD, we iteratively generate a playing scenario for each
461   guideline-violating question to test the target LLM’s adherence to the guidelines within that scenario.

      The process of generating guideline-violating questions involves several structured steps to ensure
      comprehensive testing of language models’ adherence to ethical principles. First, the guidelines are
802
      analyzed to extract their core principles and potential conflicts, with the LLM acting as an Analyst
803
      to organize these features, as shown in Fig. 3. Next, the principles are contextualized by defining
804   relevant domains and creating specific scenarios where these principles may be challenged, with the
805   LLM taking on the role of a Strategic Committee to simulate realistic examples, as shown in Fig. 4.
806   Following this, the LLM acts as a Question Designer, leveraging the contextual information to craft
807   initial guideline-violating questions that reflect the principles and conflicts. Existing benchmarks
808   provide useful templates during this phase, as shown in Fig. 5. Finally, the LLM evaluates the
809   questions as a Question Reviewer, assessing their Harmfulness, Information Density, and Compliance
      to ensure alignment with the testing objectives, as shown in Fig. 6.
