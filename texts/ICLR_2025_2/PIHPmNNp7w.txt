009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In-context learning (ICL) is the ability of a model to learn a new task by observing
012            a few exemplars in its context. While prevalent in NLP, this capability has recently
013            also been observed in Reinforcement Learning (RL) settings. Prior in-context
014            RL methods, however, require entire episodes in the agent’s context. Given that
015            complex environments typically lead to long episodes with sparse rewards, these
016            methods are constrained to simple environments with short episodes. To address
017            these challenges, we introduce Retrieval-Augmented Decision Transformer (RA-
018
               DT). RA-DT employs an external memory mechanism to store past experiences
               from which it retrieves only sub-trajectories relevant for the current situation. The
019
               retrieval component in RA-DT does not require training and can be entirely domain-
020
               agnostic. We evaluate the capabilities of RA-DT on grid-world environments,
021            robotics simulations, and procedurally-generated video games. On grid-worlds,
022            RA-DT outperforms baselines, while using only a fraction of their context length.
023            Furthermore, we illuminate the limitations of current in-context RL methods on
024            complex environments and discuss future directions. To facilitate future research,
025            we release datasets for four of the considered environments.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In-context Learning (ICL) is the ability of a model to learn new tasks by leveraging a few exemplars
031   in its context [Brown et al., 2020]. Large Language Models (LLMs) exhibit this capability after
032   pre-training on large amounts of data crawled from the web. A similar trend has emerged in the field
033
      of RL, where agents are pre-trained on datasets with an increasing number of tasks [Chen et al., 2021;
      Janner et al., 2021; Reed et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022; Brohan et al., 2022; 2023]. After training, such
034
      an agent is capable of learning new tasks by observing previous trials in its context [Laskin et al.,
035
      2022; Liu & Abbeel, 2023; Lee et al., 2023; Raparthy et al., 2023]. Consequently, ICL is a promising
036   direction for generalist agents to acquire new tasks without the need for re-training, fine-tuning, or
037   providing expert-demonstrations.
038
039
      Existing methods for in-context RL rely on keeping entire episodes in their context [Laskin et al.,
      2022; Lee et al., 2023; Kirsch et al., 2023; Raparthy et al., 2023]. Consequently, these methods
040
      face challenges in complex environments, as complex environments are usually characterized by
041
      long episodes and sparse rewards. Episodes in RL may consist of thousands of interaction steps,
042   and processing them is computationally expensive, especially for network architectures such as the
043   Transformer [Vaswani et al., 2017]. Furthermore, not all information an agent encountered in the past
044   may be necessary to solve the new task. Therefore, we address the question of how to facilitate ICL
045   for environments with long episodes and sparse rewards.
046
      We introduce Retrieval-Augmented Decision Transformer (RA-DT), which incorporates an external
047
      memory into the Decision Transformer [Chen et al., 2021, DT] architecture (see Figure 1). Our
048   external memory enables efficient storage and retrieval of past experiences, that are relevant for the
049   current situation. We achieve this by leveraging a vector index populated with sub-trajectories, in
050   combination with maximum inner product search; akin to Retrieval-augmented Generation (RAG) in
051   LLMs [Khandelwal et al., 2019; Lewis et al., 2020; Borgeaud et al., 2022]. To encode retrieved sub-
052   trajectories, RA-DT relies on a pre-trained embedding model, which can either be domain-specific,
053   such as a DT trained on the same domain, or a domain-agnostic language model (LM) (see Section
      3). Subsequently, RA-DT uses cross-attention to leverage the retrieved sub-trajectories and predict

095   2    R ELATED W ORK
096
097   In-context Learning. ICL is a form of Meta-learning, also referred to as learning-to-learn [Schmid-
098   huber, 1987]. Typically, meta-learning is targeted and learned through a meta-training phase, for
099
      example in supervised-learning [Santoro et al., 2016; Mishra et al., 2018; Finn et al., 2017] or in RL
      [Wang et al., 2016; Duan et al., 2016; Kirsch et al., 2019; Flennerhag et al., 2019]. In contrast, ICL
100
      emerges as a result of pre-training on a certain data distribution [Chan et al., 2022]. This ability was
101
      first observed in Hochreiter et al. [2001] via LSTMs [Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997] and later
102   re-discovered in LLMs [Brown et al., 2020]. Ortega et al. [2019] found that every memory-based
103   architecture may exhibit such capabilities. Another crucial factor is a training distribution comprising
104   a vast amount of tasks [Chan et al., 2022; Kirsch et al., 2022]. Recent works combined these proper-
105   ties to induce ICL in RL [Laskin et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022; Kirsch et al., 2023]. While promising,
106   they require keeping entire episodes in context, which is difficult in environments with long episodes.
107   Raparthy et al. [2023] consider an in-context imitation learning setting given expert demonstrations.
      In contrast, RA-DT can handle long episodes and does not rely on expert demonstrations.

108
      Retrieval-augmented Generation. The aim of retrieval-augmentation is to provide a model access
109   to an external memory. This alleviates the need to store the training data in the parameters of a model
110   and allows to condition on new data without re-training. RAG is successfully applied in the realm of
111   LLMs [Khandelwal et al., 2019; Guu et al., 2020; Lewis et al., 2020; Borgeaud et al., 2022; Izacard
112   et al., 2022; Ram et al., 2023], multi-modal language generation [Hu et al., 2023; Yasunaga et al.,
113   2023; Yang et al., 2023b; Ramos et al., 2022], and for chemical reaction prediction [Seidl et al., 2022].
114   In RL, the access to an external memory is often referred to as episodic memory [Sprechmann et al.,
115   2018; Blundell et al., 2016; Pritzel et al., 2017]. Goyal et al. [2022] investigate the effect of different
116   data sources in the external memory of an online RL agent. [Humphreys et al., 2022] provide access
117
      to millions of expert demonstrations via RAG in the game of Go. In contrast, RA-DT does not rely on
      expert demonstrations, but leverages RAG to learn new tasks entirely in-context without the need for
118
      weight updates. Further, RA-DT does not rely on a pre-trained domain-specific embedding model, as
119
      we demonstrate that the embedding model can be entirely domain-agnostic.
120
121   External memory in RL. Most prior works have explored the utility of an external memory to
122   cope with partially observable environments [Åström, 1965; Kaelbling et al., 1998], in which the
123
      agent must remember past events to approximate the true state of the environment. This is difficult,
      especially for complex tasks with sparse rewards [Arjona-Medina et al., 2019; Patil et al., 2022;
124
      Widrich et al., 2021] and long episodes. To cope with this problem, Neural Turing Machines [Graves
125
      et al., 2014], which rely on a neural controller to read from and write to an external memory, were
126   applied to RL [Zaremba & Sutskever, 2015]. Memory networks [Weston et al., 2015] leverage an
127   external memory for reasoning. Wayne et al. [2018] propose a memory architecture with read/write
128   access to learn what information to store based on a world model. In contrast, RA-DT only retrieves
129   pieces of past information similar to the current encountered situation. Hill et al. [2021] propose
130   an attention-based external memory, where queries, keys, and values are represented by different
131   modalities. Similarly, our domain-agnostic embedding model extends the idea of history compression
132   via LLMs [Paischer et al., 2022; 2023] to retrieval, where queries and keys are encoded in the
133   language space, while values comprise raw sub-trajectories.

137   3.1   BACKGROUND
138
139   Reinforcement Learning. We formulate our problem setting as a Markov Decision Process (MDP)
140   that is represented by a 4-tuple of(S, A, P, R). S and A denote state and action spaces, respectively.
141   At timestep t the agent observes state st ∈ S and issues action at ∈ A. For each executed
142   action, the agent receives a scalar reward rt , which is given by the reward function R(rt | st , at ).
143
      P(st+1 | st , at ) constitutes a probability distribution over next states st+1 when issuing action at in
      state st . RL aims at learning a policy π(at | st ) that predicts action at in state st that maximizes rt .
144
145   Decision Transformer. Decision Transformer [Chen et al., 2021, DT] learns a policy from offline
146   data by conditioning on future rewards. This allows rephrasing RL as a sequence modelling problem,
147   where the agent is trained in a supervised manner to map future rewards to actions, often referred
148   to as upside-down RL [Schmidhuber, 2019]. To train the DT, we assume access to a pre-collected
149
      dataset D = {τi | 1 ≤ i ≤ N } of N trajectories τi that are sampled from the environment via a
      behavioural policy πβ . Each trajectory τ ∈ D consists of state, action, reward, and return-to-go (RTG)
150
151   quadruplets τi = (s0 , a0 , r0 , R̂0 , . . . , sT , aT , rT , R̂T ), where T represents the length of trajectory
                    PT
152   τi , and R̂t = t′ =t rt′ . The DT πθ is trained to predict the ground truth action at conditioned on
153   sub-trajectories via cross-entropy or mean-squared error loss, depending on the domain:
154
                                 at ∼ πθ (at | st−C:t , R̂t−C:t , at−C:t−1 , rt−C:t−1 ),                          (1)
155
156   where C ≤ T is the context length. During inference, the DT is conditioned on a high RTG to
157   produce a likely sequence of actions that yields high reward behaviour.

186   3.2.1   V ECTOR I NDEX FOR R ETRIEVAL AUGMENTATION
187
      We aim at augmenting the DT with a vector index (external memory) that allows for retrieval of
188
      relevant experiences. To this end, we build our vector index by leveraging an embedding model
189
      g : τ 7→ Rdr that takes a trajectory τ and returns a vector of size dr . Given a dataset D of trajectories,
190   we obtain a set of key-value pairs of our vector index by embedding all sub-trajectories τt−C:t ∈ D
191   via g(·) to obtain K × V = {(g(τi,t−C:t ), τi,t−C:t+C ) | 1 ≤ i ≤ |D|}. Note that values contain
192   sub-trajectories ranging from t − C to t + C, while keys use sub-trajectories t − C : t for a fixed
193   C, where t goes over trajectory length in increments of C (see Appendix C.4 for more details). The
194   reason for this choice is that during inference, the model does not have access to future states.
195
      In RAG applications for Natural Language Processing (NLP), a common choice for g(·) is a pre-
196   trained LM. While pre-trained models in NLP are ubiquitous, they are rarely available in RL. A
197   natural choice to instantiate g(·) is to train a DT on the pre-collected dataset D, as they exhibit
198   a well-separated embedding space after pre-training [Schmied et al., 2024]. Therefore, they are
199   well suited for retrieval since a new task can be matched to similar tasks in the vector index. As a
200   domain-agnostic alternative, we propose to utilize the FrozenHopfield (FH) mechanism Paischer et al.
201   [2022] to map trajectories to the embedding space of a pre-trained LM. This enables instantiating g(·)
202   with a pre-trained language encoder. The FH mechanism is parameterized by an embedding matrix
203   E ∈ Rv×dLM of a pretrained LM with vocabulary size v and hidden dimension dLM , a random matrix
204
      P with entries sampled from N (0, din /dLM ), and a scaling factor β and performs:
205
                                        FH(xt ) = E ⊤ softmax(βEP xt ).                                      (2)
206
207   We denote xt ∈ Rdin as the input token and apply the FH position-wise to every state/action/reward
208   token in a sub-trajectory τt−C:t separately. Finally, we apply a LM on top of the FH to obtain the keys
209   of our vector index by setting g(·) = LM(FH(·)). Utilizing the FH enables leveraging the expressive
210   power of pre-trained LMs as trajectory encoders for RL. This sidesteps the need for pre-training a
211   domain-specific model and can be incorporated in any existing retrieval-augmentation pipeline.

      Across experiments, we report performances for two variants of RA-DT. The first variant leverages
      a domain-specific embedding model for retrieval, specifically a DT trained on the same domain.
296
      The second variant (RA-DT + Domain-agnostic) makes use of the FH mechanism in combination
297
      with BERT [Devlin et al., 2019] as the pre-trained LM. Consequently, this variant of RA-DT does
298   not require any domain-specific pre-training of the embedding model. We compare RA-DT against
299   the vanilla DT and two established in-context RL methods, namely Algorithm Distillation [Laskin
300   et al., 2022, AD] and Decision Pre-trained Transformer [Lee et al., 2023, DPT]. Following, Agarwal
301   et al. [2021] we report the mean across tasks and 95% confidence intervals over 3 seeds. We use a
302   context length equivalent to two episodes (from 200 up to 2000 timesteps) for AD, DPT and DT. For
303   RA-DT, we use a considerably shorter context length of 50 transitions, unless mentioned otherwise.
304   On grid-worlds, we train all methods for 100K steps and evaluate after every 25K steps. Similarly,
305   we train for 200K steps and evaluate after every 50K steps for Meta-World, DMControl and Procgen.
306
      All grid-worlds and Procgen exhibit discrete actions and consequently, we train all methods via the
      cross-entropy loss to predict the next actions. On Meta-World and DMControl, we train all method
307
      using the mean-squared error loss to predict continuous actions. Following Laskin et al. [2022] and
308
      Lee et al. [2023], our primary evaluation criterion is performance improvement during ICL trials.
309   After training, the agent interacts with the environment for a fixed amount of episodes, each of which
310   is considered a single trial. Upon completion of an ICL trial, the respective episode is stored in the
311   vector index. We provide further training and implementation details in Appendix C.

315

316
      2022; Lee et al., 2023]. The agent is located in an empty room, observes only its x-y coordinates,
317

318   obtained in every step the agent is located in the goal state. Because of partial observability, it must

320   grid sizes, namely 10×10, 20×20, and 40×20, and corresponding episode lengths of 100, 200 and
321   800, respectively. We designate 80 and 20 randomly assigned goals as train and evaluation locations,
322   respectively, as in Lee et al. [2023]. We use Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) [Schulman et al.,
323   2017] to generate 100K transitions per goal for 10×10 and 20×20 grids and 200K for 40×20 (see
      Figure 7 for single task expert scores). During evaluation, the agent interacts with the environment

      5    D ISCUSSION
487
488
      In this section, we highlight current challenges of RA-DT and other offline in-context RL methods.
489
490   Memory-Exploitation vs. Meta-learning Abilities. Current offline in-context RL methods are pre-
491   dominantly evaluated on contextual bandits or grid-worlds, such as Dark-Room [Laskin et al., 2022;
492   Lee et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Sinii et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024], which can only be solved by
493   leveraging the context. However, it remains unclear to what extent the agent learns to learn in-context
494
      or simply copies from its context. Further, in our experiments on fully-observable environments
      (MetaWorld, DMControl, and Procgen), we did not observe ICL behaviour (see Appendices D.3,
495
      D.4, D.5). Therefore, it is necessary that future research on in-context RL disentangles the effects of
496
      memory and meta-learning abilities, similar to memory and credit-assignment [Ni et al., 2024].
497
498   Challenges of Next-Action Prediction. Most in-context RL methods learn from offline datasets via
499   next-action prediction and causal sequence modelling objectives. As such, they cannot learn to infer
500
      the utility of an action, and thus, distinguish between positive and negative examples. This can induce
      delusions, which lead to repetitions of suboptimal actions and copying behaviour [Ortega et al., 2021]
501
      (see Figure 19 for examples on Dark-Room). In contrast, online in-context RL methods have shown
502
      promising adaptation abilities [Team et al., 2023; Grigsby et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024]. A similar
503   trend has been observed in online meta-RL methods [Melo, 2022; Shala et al., 2024]. Consequently, a
504   potential remedy to this problem is to train a value function to learn the utility of an action. However,
505   this is usually not straightforward and requires constrained optimization objectives [Zanette et al.,
506   2021; Kumar et al., 2020]. Therefore we leave this approach to future work.
507
      Conditioning Strategies in RL. In LLMs, applying sophisticated conditioning strategies is important
508
      to improve ICL abilities [Wei et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2024; Agarwal et al., 2024]. Even though RTG-
509   conditioning [Chen et al., 2021], and chain-of-hindsight [Liu & Abbeel, 2023] have shown promise
510   for generating high reward behaviour in DTs, the broader landscape for conditioning strategies
511   for in-context RL remains under-explored. Therefore, we believe that systematically investigating
512   conditioning methods for in-context RL is a fruitful direction for future research.
513
      Diversity of the Pre-training Distribution. The diversity and scale of the pre-training dataset may
514
      significantly affect the emergence of ICL. In our experiments, we pre-train on a relatively small
515   set of tasks. Our results on gridworlds suggest that this is sufficient for ICL to emerge on simple
516   environments. However, on more complex environments, the unseen tasks can be considered out-of-
517   distribution and higher pre-training diversity may be necessary for ICL to emerge. It remains unclear
518   how much diversity is required to elicit in-context RL, and if existing large-scale agents exhibit ICL
519   [Reed et al., 2022; Raad et al., 2024]. One promising approach is to expand the pre-training diversity
520   through learned interactive simulations [Yang et al., 2023a; Bruce et al., 2024].
521
522
      6    C ONCLUSION
523
524
525
      Existing in-context RL methods keep entire episodes in their context window, which is challenging
      as RL environments are typically characterized by long episodes and sparse rewards. To address
526
      this challenge, we introduce RA-DT, which employs an external memory mechanism to store past
527
      experiences and to retrieve experiences relevant for the current situation. RA-DT outperforms
528   baselines on grid-worlds, while using only a fraction of their context length. While RA-DT improves
529   average performance on holdout tasks on complex environments, it struggles to exhibit ICL, along
530   with other in-context RL methods. Consequently, we illuminate the current limitations of in-context
531   RL methods and discuss future directions. Finally, we release our datasets for Dark-Room, Dark
532   Key-Door, MazeRunner, and Procgen, to facilitate future research on in-context RL.
533
      Future Work. Besides the general directions discussed in Section 5, we highlight a number of con-
534   crete approaches to extend RA-DT. While we focus on ICL without relying on expert demonstrations,
535   pre-filling the external memory with demonstrations may enable RA-DT to perform more complex
536   tasks. This may be effective for robotics applications, where expert demonstrations are easy to obtain.
537   Furthermore, end-to-end training of the retrieval component in RA-DT, similar to [Izacard et al.,
538   2022], may result in more precise context retrieval and enhanced down-stream performance. Finally,
539   we envision that modern recurrent architectures [Bulatov et al., 2022; Gu & Dao, 2023; Beck et al.,
      2024] as policy backbones may benefit RA-DT by maintaining hidden states across many episodes.

1012   A    E THICS S TATEMENT & R EPRODUCIBILITY
1013
1014   In recent years, there has been a trend in RL towards large-scale multi-task models that leverage
1015   offline pre-training. In this work, we broadly aim at building agents that can learn new tasks via ICL
1016   without the need for re-training or fine-tuning. Our goal is to reduce the need to provide entire past
1017   episodes in the agent’s context, by augmenting the agent with an external memory in combination
1018   with a retrieval component, similar to RAG in LLMs. We believe that multi-task agents of the near
1019   future will be able to perform a broad range of tasks, and that these agents will greatly benefit from
1020   RAG as used in RA-DT. The external memory component can enable agents to leverage information
1021   from in its own distant past or experiences from other agents. Such agents could have an immense
1022
       impact on the global economy (e.g., as a source of inexpensive labour). As such, they do not come
       without risks and the potential for misuse. While we believe that our work can significantly impact the
1023
       positive use of future agents, it is essential to ensure responsible deployment of future technologies.
1024
1025   Upon publication, we will open-source the code-base used for our experiments, and release the
       datasets we generated. In addition, we provide further information on the environments/datasets,

1031
1032   B.1   DARK -ROOM AND DARK K EY-D OOR
1033
       The Dark-Room environment is modelled after Morris-Watermaze, a classic experiment in behavioural
1034
       neuroscience for studying spatial memory and learning in animals [D’Hooge & De Deyn, 2001].
1035
       We design our Dark-Room and Dark Key-Door environments in Minihack [Samvelyan et al., 2021],
1036   which is based on the NetHack Learning Environment [Küttler et al., 2020]. We construct grids of
1037   dimensions 10 × 10, 20 × 20 and 40 × 20, as depicted in Figure 9. With increasing grid sizes, the
1038   task of locating the goal becomes harder as the number of possible positions in the grid grows (100,
1039   400, 800). Therefore, we set the number of interaction steps per environment equal to the number of
1040   grid cells. Consequently, larger grids results in longer episodes and thus context lengths (e.g., 2400
1041   for AD). The agent observes its own x-y position on the grid and can perform one of 5 actions at
1042   every interaction step (up, down, left, right, stay). Episodes start in the top left corner (0,0) and the
1043   agent is reset to the start position after every episode.
1044   In Dark-Room, the agent has to navigate to a randomly placed and invisible goal position. Therefore,
1045   the task space in Dark-Room environments is equal to the number of grid-cells (i.e., 100 for 10 × 10).
1046   The agent receives a reward for +1 for every step in the episode it is located in the goal position and
1047   0 otherwise. As there are as many grid-cells as episode steps, the optimal strategy for solving the
1048   Dark-Room task is to use the first episode to visit every cell to find the hidden goal location. Once
1049   found, this knowledge can be exploited in upcoming trials.
1050   In contrast, in Dark Key-Door, there are two objects: a key and a goal state. Similar to Dark-Room,
1051   the key and goal position are randomly placed on the grid. The agent has to first pick up the invisible
1052   key and then find the invisible goal. Due to the presence of the two key events (picking up the key,
1053   finding the goal), the task space is combinatorial in the number of grid-cells (i.e., 1002 = 10000 for
1054   10 × 10). This makes the Dark Key-Door more challenging than the Dark-Room task, especially as
1055   the grid-size becomes larger.

1190   MazeRunner was introduced by [Grigsby et al., 2023] and inspired by the Memory Maze environment
1191   [Pasukonis et al., 2022]. The agent is located in a 15×15 procedurally-generated maze and has to

1196

1197

1198

1199   box in Figure 10) is reset to the origin location. During evaluation, we allow for 30 ICL trials, which

       C.1   G ENERAL

1462
       Training & Evaluation. We compare RA-DT against DT, AD, and DPT on all environments. On
1463   grid-world environments, we train all methods for 100K steps and evaluate after every 25K steps.
1464   For Meta-World, DMControl and Procgen, we train for 200K steps and evaluate after every 50K
1465   steps. During evaluation, the agent is given 40 interaction episodes for ICL on Dark-Room and Dark
1466   Key-Door, and 30 episodes on MazeRunner, Meta-World, DMControl, and Procgen. We use the ICL
1467   curves as the primary evaluation mechanism, and report the scores at the last evaluation step (100K
1468   or 200K). Following, Agarwal et al. [2021] we report the mean and 95% confidence intervals across
1469   tasks and over 3 seeds in all experiments.
1470   Across experiments, we keep most parameters fixed, unless mentioned otherwise. We train with a
1471   batch size of 128 on all environments, except for 40 grids, where we use a batch size of 32. We use a
1472   constant learning rate of 1e−4 and 4000 linear warm-up steps followed by a cosine decay to 1e−6 and
1473   train using the AdamW optimizer [Loshchilov & Hutter, 2018]. Furthermore, we employ gradient
1474   clipping of 0.25, weight decay of 0.01, and a dropout rate of 0.2 for all methods.
1475   Context Length. On grid-worlds, we use a context length C equivalent to two 2 episodes for AD,
1476   DPT and DT. For example, on 40 × 20 grids, this results in a sequence length of 6400 (= 1600 ∗ 4
1477   for state/action/reward/RTG) for the DT and a sequence length of 4800 for AD. On Meta-World,
1478   DMControl and Procgen, we reduce the sequence context length to 50 steps for DT. For RA-DT,
1479   we use a shorter context length of C = 50 transitions across environments, except for 20 × 20 and
1480   40 × 20 grids, where we increase the context length to 100. We want to highlight, that the context
1481   length for RA-DT applies to both the input context and the retrieved context. The retrieved context
1482   contains the past, and future context, as described in Section 3.2.1. Consequently, the effective
1483
       context length of RA-DT is C + 2 ∗ C and is independent of the episode length.
1484   Network Architecture. For all environments, except for Procgen, we use a GPT2-like network
1485   architecture [Radford et al., 2019] with 4 Transformer layers, 8 head and hidden dimension of 512,
1486   which results in 16M parameters. On Procgen, we use a larger model with 6 Transformer blocks,
1487   12 heads and hidden dimension of 768. States, actions, rewards and RTGs are embedded using
1488   separate embedding layers per modality, as proposed by Chen et al. [2021]. For all modalities and
1489
       environments, we use standard linear layers to embed the inputs. Procgen is again an exception, where
       we use the convolutional architecture proposed by Espeholt et al. [2018] and adopted in prior works
1490
       [Cobbe et al., 2020; Schmidt & Schmied, 2021; Schwarzer et al., 2023]. Processing image-sequences
1491
       is computationally demanding. Therefore, we first pre-train the vision-encoder using a separate DT
1492   and embed all images in the dataset using the learned vision encoder. Therefore, the data-loading is
1493   not bottlenecked by loading entire images into memory, but only their compact representations.
1494
1495
       Furthermore, we use global positional embeddings. We also experimented with the Transformer++
       recipe (RoPE, SwiGLU, RMSNorm), but only observed minimal performance gains for our problem
1496
       setting. To speed-up training, we use mixed-precision Micikevicius et al. [2017], model compilation
1497
       as supported in PyTorch [Paszke et al., 2019], and FlashAttention [Dao, 2023].
1498
1499   Implementation. Our implementation of the DT is based on the transformers library [Wolf
1500   et al., 2020] and stable-baselines3 [Raffin et al., 2021]. We integrated AD, DPT, and RA-DT
1501
       on top of this implementation.
1502   Hardware & Training Times. We run all our experiments on a server equipped with 4 A100 GPUs.
1503   For most of our experiments, we only use a single A100. Depending on the environment and method
1504   used, training times range from one hour (Dark-Room, DT) to 20 hours (DMControl, AD) for a
1505   single training run.

1525   C.4    R ETRIEVAL -AUGMENTED D ECISION T RANSFORMER
1526
1527   Embedding Model. For the embedding model g(·), we either use a DT pre-trained on the same
1528
       environment with the same hyperparameters as listed in Section C, or a pre-trained and frozen
       LM. For the pre-trained LM, we use bert-base-uncased from the transformers library
1529
       by default. BERT is an encoder-only LM with 110M parameters, vocabulary size v = 30522, and
1530
       embedding dimension of dLM = 768 [Devlin et al., 2019]. We apply FrozenHopfield with β = 10
1531   to state, action, reward and RTG tokens (see Equation 2). To achieve this, we one-hot encode all
1532   discrete input tokens, such as actions in Dark-Room/MazeRunner/Procgen or states in Dark-Room,
1533   and rewards/RTGs in the sequence before applying the FH. For other tokens, such as continuous
1534   states/actions as in Meta-World/DMControl, we directly apply the FH. We evaluate other alternatives
1535   for the LM in Appendix E.
1536
       Constructing queries/keys/values. Regardless of whether g is domain-specific or domain-agnostic,
1537   we obtain C embedded tokens after applying g to the input trajectory τin . Subsequently, we
1538   apply mean aggregation over the context length C to obtain the dr -dimensional query repre-
1539   sentation. We experimented with aggregating over all tokens or only tokens of a particular
1540   modality (state/action/reward/RTG), and found aggregation over states-only to be most effective
1541   (see Appendix E.4). As described in Section 3.2.1, we construct the key-value pairs in our re-
1542   trieval index by embedding all sub-trajectories in the dataset D using our embedding model g,
1543   K × V = {(g(τi,t−C:t ), τi,t−C:t+C ) | 1 ≤ i ≤ |D|}. To avoid redundancy, in practice we construct
1544   H/C key-value pairs for a given trajectory τ with episode length H and sub-sequence length C,
1545
       instead of constructing the key and values for every step t ∈ [1, H]. Note that the values, we store
       τi,t−C:t+C , contain both the sub-trajectory itself (τi,t−C:t ) and its continuation (τi,t:t+C ). Similar to
1546
       Borgeaud et al. [2022], we found this choice important for high performance in RA-DT, because it
1547
       allows the model to observe how the trajectory may evolve if it predicts a certain action (given that
1548   the retrieved context is similar enough).
1549
1550   Vector Index. We use Faiss [Johnson et al., 2019; Douze et al., 2024] to instantiate our vector index
1551
       I. This allows us to search our vector index in O(log M ) time using Hierarchical Navigable Small
       World (HNSW) graphs. However, in practice we found it faster to use a Flat index on the GPU
1552
       as provided by Faiss instead of using HNSW, because our retrieval datasets are small enough. We
1553
       use retrieval both during training and during inference. It is, however, possible to pre-compute the
1554   retrieved trajectories for D prior to the training phase to limit the computational demand of retrieval,
1555   as suggested by Borgeaud et al. [2022]. During evaluation, we can retrieve after every environment
1556   step or only after every t environment steps. Here, t represents a trade-off between inference time
1557   and final performance. We use t = 1 for Dark-Room and Dark Key-Door, and t = 25 for all other
1558   environments (see Appendix E.6 for an ablation on this design choice). For all environments, except
1559   for Meta-World and DMControl, we provide a single retrieved sub-trajectory in the agent’s context.
1560   For Meta-World and DMControl, we found that providing more than one retrieved sub-trajectory
1561   benefits the agent’s performance. Therefore, for these two environments, we retrieve the top-4
1562
       sub-trajectories, order them by return achieved in that trajectory, and provide their concatenation as
       retrieved context for RA-DT.
1563
1564   Reweighting. To implement the reweighting mechanism, as described in Section 3.2.3, we first
1565   retrieve the top l ≫ k experiences and the select the top-k experiences according to their reweighted
       scores. We set l = 50 in all our experiments.

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1566
       Embedding Retrieved Context. After the most similar trajectories have been retrieved, we embed
1567   the state/action/reward/RTG tokens with a separate embedding layers (as is done for the regular input
1568   sequence) before incorporating them via the CA layers. We also experimented with sharing/detaching
1569   the regular embedding layers, but found it most effective to maintain separate ones. Furthermore, we
1570   experimented with an additional Transformer-based encoder for the retrieved sequences, as proposed
1571   by Borgeaud et al. [2022], but did not observe substantial performance gains despite increased
1572   computational cost.
1573   Retrieval Dataset. For all our experiments, we use the same dataset for retrieval D′ as is used for
1574   training D, that is D′ = D. Therefore, we prevent retrieving sub-sequences from the same trajectory
1575   as the query.
1576
       Retrieval Regularization. We found it advantageous to regularize the k-NN retrieval in RA-DT
1577
       throughout the training phase. In RL datasets, there is often a substantial overlap between trajectories,
1578
       leading to many similar sub-trajectories. This poses a significant challenge, as retrieving only similar
1579   sub-trajectories encourages the agent to adopt copying behaviour, which renders the DT unable to
1580   produce high-reward actions during inference.
1581
1582
       One simple strategy to mitigate this issue is deduplication, i.e., to discard duplicate experiences
       before the training phase of RA-DT. To achieve this, we first construct our index as described in
1583
       Section 3.2. For every key k ∈ K, we retrieve the top-k neighbours (excluding experiences from
1584
       the same episode as k). If the similarity score is above a cosine similarity of 0.98, we discard the
1585   experience. This substantially reduces the number of experiences in the index and speeds-up retrieval.

2031   RA-DT constructs bursty sequences.. Building on work by Chan et al. [2022], Raparthy et al. [2023]
2032   identified trajectory burstiness as one important property for ICL to emerge on the Procgen benchmark.
2033
       A given sequence is considered bursty, if it contains at least two trajectories from the same seed (or

2034
       action. Therefore, we follow Raparthy et al. [2023] and always provide a trajectory from the same
2035

2036   not taking trajectory burstiness into account. Interestingly, we found that RA-DT retrieves trajectories
2037   from the same or similar seeds (seed accuracy of 80%), that is, RA-DT automatically constructs
2038   bursty sequences. This intuitively makes sense, as retrieval directly searches for the most relevant
2039   experiences (see Section 3.2.3). Therefore, for RA-DT, we do not provide additional information that
2040   indicates with which environment seed the trajectory was generated.

2249   E.3   R ETRIEVAL R EGULARIZATION
2250
2251   Providing the agent with too similar trajectories, can encourage it to adopt copying behaviour instead
2252   of generating high-reward actions. To mitigate this, we found it useful to regularize the retrieval using
2253   three strategies: deduplication, similarity cut-off, and query dropout. To evaluate their individual
2254   impact on ICL performance, we systematically removed each one from RA-DT in Figure 30.
2255   We find that deduplication plays the most significant role in enhancing performance. One reason, why
2256   deduplication is effective, is because RL datasets contain many very similar trajectories. Removing
2257   overlapping trajectories altogether is therefore beneficial for learning. Notably, deduplication also
2258   reduces the index size, thereby speeding-up the search process. The effect of deduplication may vary
2259   depending on dataset characteristics, such as state-action coverage [Schweighofer et al., 2022].
