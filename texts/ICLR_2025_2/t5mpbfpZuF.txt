009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Aligning large language models (LLMs) has emerged as a critical challenge in
012            the age of generative AI: LLMs must be appropriately aligned with human values
013            and preferences in order to be helpful and harmless. In many real world cases,
014            however, large amounts of preference data are not available on important tasks,
015            limiting the effectiveness of resulting reward models. In some cases, data from a
016            similar task is available, and unlabeled data on the target task is available or can
017            be generated by an LLM. In other cases, clean data may be available to train an
018
               LLM for real-world use on noisy data, small amounts of labeled data on the target
               task may be available, or data may be available on an easier task. In this work,
019
               we demonstrate that domain adaptation can effectively use different types of data,
020
               by transferring supervision and human values across tasks with similar data distri-
021            butions, strengthening resistance to noisy data, improving few-shot generalization
022            ability, and even transfer from easy to hard tasks, in the form of short to long
023            generalization. Specifically, we propose Data Efficient Alignment for Language
024            (DEAL), using domain adaptation to effectively perform cross-task alignment in
025            scenarios where labeled target data is not available. We evaluate our method for re-
026            ward model training on a variety of benchmarks and demonstrate that our method
027            can meaningfully improve performance on target tasks by utilizing data on re-
028            lated tasks or low amounts of data. Furthermore, we offer analysis on the inner
029
               mechanism of domain adaptation and the alignment of embedding distributions.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Large language models (LLMs) have become increasingly powerful, reaching or surpassing human
034
      abilities on a variety of tasks. On real-world applications, LLMs are especially successful when
035
      large amounts of labeled data are available to perform fine-tuning, either through reinforcement
036   learning from human feedback (RLHF) with preference data or supervised fine-tuning (SFT) with
037   ground truth completion data.
038
039
      However, on many real-world tasks, large amounts of preference data on target tasks are not avail-
      able, as data labeling may be very expensive. For example, in low-resource languages, low amounts
040
      of supervised data may be available, especially in specialized tasks such as those relating to law or
041
      medicine. Furthermore, large amounts of one to one translation data may not be available from a
042   high-resource language to the given low resource language. In these cases, however, large amounts
043   of unlabeled data in the target language may be available, though it may not be matched to the high-
044   resource labeled data. Despite the lack of target data, the labeled high-resource data still provides
045   highly relevant supervision that can benefit performance on the low-resource language task. Still,
046   direct training on the high-resource data may not fully transfer relevant supervision to the target task.
047   Instead, domain adaptation techniques allow for explicit distribution matching between the source
048   and target data by leveraging knowledge about the unlabeled target data distribution and aligning
049   the two data distributions. This allows common skills and features to be transferred between the two
050
      tasks and ultimately improves performance on the target task without using any labeled target data.
051   On other tasks, while labels on clean, labeler or LLM generated data may be available, labels may
052   not be available for the target distribution of real-world, noisy data (e.g. user-generated internet
053   data). In addition, such noise reduction may be useful for LLM post-training (e.g. RLHF, DPO,
      rejection sampling), in which the distribution of LLM generated outputs may shift away from the

098   may be unique to the longer texts (e.g. long-term plot development), many of the skills required to
099   distinguish short and long texts may be similar, including grammar, punctuation, and logic. Through
100   domain adaptation, supervision can be effectively transferred from the easier to label short task to
101   the label-expensive long task, by explicitly aligning these commonalities between the two tasks.
102   This reduces the need for costly labels on difficult tasks, rather allowing humans to express their
103   preferences on easier tasks and transferring these preferences to other tasks.
104   All in all, we identify four real-world LLM alignment scenarios with a lack of target data, including
105   transferring supervision across similar tasks (e.g. between languages), transferring from clean to
106   noisy data (e.g. internet text), using only a few target examples (labels are expensive), and transfer-
107   ring human preferences from easy tasks to hard tasks. A diagram depicting these four tasks is given

129   2   R ELATED W ORK
130
131   Cross-task alignment of LLMs: The problem of alignment, or ensuring that LLMs properly ad-
132   here to human values, has become more important as LLMs increase in ability. Techniques such as
133   Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (Ouyang et al., 2022) have been used to fine-tune
134   LLMs to match human preference data, and have been successfully applied to commercial, user-
135   facing LLMs. Recently, attempts have been made to measure the generalization ability of aligned
136
      LLMs by varying the training and evaluation tasks. For example, Hase et al. (2024) found that LLMs
      trained on easy STEM and general-knowledge questions showed a surprisingly high zero-shot gener-
137
      alization ability to harder questions. Sun et al. (2024) found that reward models exhibited a stronger
138
      generalization ability than LLMs trained using supervised fine-tuning (SFT) when transferring from
139   easy to hard math questions. For easy-to-hard generalization, Zhou et al. (2022) used least-to-most
140   prompting to break up a hard task into easier tasks, allowing for the direct use of LLMs trained
141   on easier tasks on hard tasks. Furthermore, Wu et al. (2024) and Li et al. (2024) have shown that
142   training an LLM on a source language shows significant zero-shot generalization to other languages.
143
      Domain Adaptation: Domain adaptation, or the problem of transferring supervision from a source
144
      task with a large amount of labeled data to a target task with little to no labeled data (but poten-
145   tially large amounts of unlabeled data), has found many promising real-world applications, such as
146   self-driving (Li et al., 2023), where the problem is to transfer a model (e.g. for object detection)
147   trained on images in one condition (e.g. sunny) to another (e.g. rainy) and Sim2Real transfer for
148   robotics (Truong et al., 2020), where the problem is to effectively transfer an agent trained on simula-
149   tion data to the real world. Representative approaches to domain adaptation include Maximum Mean
150   Discrepancy (Tzeng et al., 2014), which optimizes source classification error while maximizing do-
151   main confusion in the hopes of creating domain-invariant feature representations. Another repre-
152   sentative approach for domain adaptation is Domain Adversarial Neural Networks (DANN) (Ganin
153
      et al., 2015), which uses a reverse gradient from a discriminator head to minimize the difference be-
      tween source and target feature representations while successfully completing the source task, with
154
      the hope that this distributional alignment of feature representations will effectively transfer super-
155
      vision from the source to target task. A separate class of approaches involves learning a data mapper
156   between the source and target data distributions that maps a source example to a target example
157   and vice versa, for example CycleGAN (Zhu et al., 2017). In addition, other extensions to DANN
158   have been proposed, such as Deep Joint Distribution Optimal Transport (DeepJDot) (Damodaran
159   et al., 2018), which aligns the joint distribution of feature representations and labels and Wasserstein
160   Distance Guided Representation Learning (WDGRL) (Shen et al., 2018), which attempts to address
161   the instability of domain adversarial training by applying ideas from Wasserstein GANs (Arjovsky
      et al., 2017) to domain adaptation.

      3   M ETHOD
178
179   While prior approaches to cross-task LLM alignment have primarily focused on measuring the zero-
180   shot generalization ability of LLMs trained on a source task, we study techniques for explicitly im-
181   proving this generalization ability and ensuring awareness of the target evaluation data distribution.
182   We propose the application of domain adaptation to LLMs in order to align embedding distributions
183   between two separate tasks, with labeled and unlabeled data. Our method, DEAL, allows for the
184   generalization to be tailored to the specified target distribution, and allows the reward model to learn
185   the transformation between the two distributions, rather than relying on guesses as to the underlying
186   transformation.
187   We focus on training reward models with a randomly initialized classification head on top of the
188   final embedding of the LLM for two reasons:
189
190
            1. Prior work (Sun et al., 2024) has shown that reward model training allows for better gener-
               alization ability compared to supervised fine-tuning.
191
192         2. Our trained reward models can then be used with RLHF and best-of-N sampling to effec-
193            tively align downstream LLMs with the trained reward models.
194   Specifically, we apply Wasserstein Distance Guided Representation Learning (Arjovsky et al., 2017)
195   (WDGRL), a more stable domain adaptation algorithm, on the final embedding from the LLM before
196   the classification head. During training time, each sampled batch contains some source labeled data
197   and some target unlabeled data. Embeddings are calculated for both the source and target data, and
198   predicted rewards are calculated for the labeled source examples. We then calculate the WDGRL
199   loss and main source task loss.
200   More formally, given ns source and nt target examples, source and target embeddings es , et , and
201   source labels ys , we learn a main task head fmain and a domain critic head fcritic . Tne critic head
202   outputs scores that attempt to discriminate between source and target embeddings, as given by the
203   Wasserstein distance loss, and is regularized with a gradient penalty. We offer an illustration of our
204   method in Figure 2.
205

215   Intuitively, by maximizing the WD loss, the critic is attempting to increase predicted critic scores
      for the source examples and decrease those for the target examples. In addition, a gradient penalty

      loss is employed to ensure that the critic’s function surface is smooth and optimizable by the feature
217   extractor. A gradient reversal layer is placed in between the feature extractor and critic head, so
218   that the objective of the base LLM is to create embeddings that minimize the WDGRL loss and are
219   hence indistinguishable between source and target.
220

221
      and target examples and the main task loss on source examples:

225   Through this adversarial training, the critic attempts to pick up on features in the embeddings to dis-
226   criminate between source and target examples, while the feature extractor attempts to eliminate these
227   differences. The feature extractor is incentivized to learn representations that are indistinguishable
228   between source and target domains and informative to do well on the source task, thereby aligning
229   the embedding distributions and transferring supervision from the source task to the target task.

      adaptation in improving generalization - while the initial train on source decision boundary directly
420   separates the few-shot examples without considering the broader training dataset, the domain adap-
421   tation method draws a decision boundary that aligns training data points with few-shot examples and
422   neatly separates the two moons. Furthermore, on the safety task, we bulk evaluated and saved em-
423   beddings for both the train on source baseline (after training 10 epochs) and the domain adaptation
424   method (after training 1 epoch) on both the set of 10 few-shot examples and 1000 randomly selected
425   examples from the larger training dataset. We then learned a PCA transformation on the few-shot
426   embeddings and applied this transformation to the 1000 embeddings. We plotted both positive and
427   negative examples, as shown in Figure 4, which shows PCA reduced embeddings as points for seed
428
      0 of split A.
429   The few-shot examples in the train on source baseline appear on the edges of the main mass of
430   training dataset points, indicating that the representations for the main training dataset may be far-
431   ther away from the few-shot examples. On the other hand, in the domain adaptation plot, the main
      training dataset examples are solidly clustered around the few-shot examples, indicating that DEAL

432
      has successfully aligned the few-shot and training distributions, and provides insight into the rea-
433   son behind increase in performance. This trend of matching training points and few-shot points is
434   consistent throughout different splits and seeds. While previous work on aligning LLMs with few
435   examples has focused on few-shot prompting, we demonstrate the potential for few-shot fine-tuning
436   of LLMs. In other words, we show that LLMs can be aligned on a new task given very few examples
437   and large amounts of unlabeled data, which can even be generated by an LLM. Our results illustrate
438   the potential of DEAL for drastically reducing the amount of labels necessary to effectively align an
439   LLM on a task.

      6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
505
506   In conclusion, we demonstrate the effectiveness of DEAL for aligning LLMs in a variety of scenarios
507   in which data on the target task is not plentiful. For example, we apply our method on transferring
508   supervision between languages and demonstrate that domain adaptation is effectively able to in-
509   crease performance on a target task without using any labeled data on the target task, instead using
510   labeled data on a similar task. We further apply our method on a few-shot generalization task, where
511   we are given only a low amount of data on a given task but have large quantities of unlabeled data.
512   Using domain adaptation, we effectively map target data points to source data points. This produces
513   a reward model that is tuned to the target evaluation distribution and that effectively uses the labeled
514
      few-shot examples combined with the target data. Finally, we apply our method for transferring
      from an easier, short task to a harder, long task, illustrating the real world potential for DEAL to
515
      effectively perform easy-to-hard generalization. This is especially valuable in cases where labels
516
      on a hard task may be expensive or difficult to obtain, while labels on an easier task may be more
517   readily available.
518
519   In the future, we plan to explore alignment between more drastically different source and target
520
      distributions, such as superhuman-level tasks, transferring supervision to LLM-generated samples
      (including during RLHF training), exploring weak-to-strong generalization, and training a language-
521
      fluid LLM. We believe that our work is a significant step forward in effectively aligning large lan-
522
      guage models in a world with fragmented data.
