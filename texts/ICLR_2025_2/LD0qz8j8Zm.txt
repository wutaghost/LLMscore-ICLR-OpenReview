011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent years have witnessed an emerging trend in neuromorphic computing that
014            centers around the use of brain connectomics as a blueprint for artificial neural net-
015
               works. Connectomics-based neuromorphic computing has primarily focused on
               embedding human brain large-scale structural connectomes (SCs), as estimated
016
               from diffusion Magnetic Resonance Imaging (dMRI) modality, to echo-state net-
017
               works (ESNs). A critical step in ESN embedding requires pre-determined read-
018            in and read-out layers constructed by the induced subgraphs (e.g., a priori set
019            of functional sub-circuits/networks) of the embedded reservoir (e.g., SCs). As
020            a priori set of functional sub-circuits are derived from functional MRI (fMRI)
021            modality, it is unknown, till this point, whether the embedding of fMRI-induced
022            sub-circuits/networks onto SCs is well justified from i) the neuro-physiological
023            perspective and ii) ESN performance across a variety of tasks. In this paper,
024            we proposed a pipeline to implement and evaluate ESNs with various embedded
025            topology and processing/memorization tasks. To this end, we showed that dif-
026
               ferent performance optimums are highly dependent on the neuro-physiological
               characteristics of these pre-determined fMRI-induced sub-circuits. In general,
027
               fMRI-induced sub-circuit-embedded ESN outperforms simple bipartite and var-
028
               ious null models with feed-forward properties commonly seen in MLP for dif-
029            ferent tasks and reservoir criticality conditions. Noticeably, we found that the
030            reservoir model performance is heavily dependent on the functional sub-circuits
031            neuro-physiological properties with respect to different cognitive tasks and their
032            corresponding computation-memorization balances. Specifically, we showed that
033            default mode network’s superior performance across the majority of tasks is re-
034            lated to its functional dichotomy property. Finally, we provided a thorough analy-
035            sis of the topological properties of pre-determined fMRI-induced sub-circuits and
036            highlighted their graph-theoretical properties that play significant roles in deter-
037
               mining the ESN performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041
      One of the prominent directions in building high-performance or general intelligence artificial sys-
042   tems is implementing biologically plausible models, particularly using human brain connectomes
043   as the underlying graph of neural networks (Damicelli et al. (2022); Suarez et al. (2020); McDaniel
044   et al. (2022); Suarez et al. (2023)). Most state-of-the-art biologically inspired artificial connectomes
045   are realized as reservoir networks with structural human brain data. However, structural connec-
046   tomes (SC) derived from diffusion Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) are generally rigid in both
047   topology and scale compared to functional connectomes (FC) from functional MRI data. Thus, in
048   this work, we explore the prospect of applying functional connectomes to reservoir Echo-state Net-
049   works (ESNs) under various conditions to comprehensively evaluate model performance, as well as
050
      build a consistent framework for implementations of connectomic data in artificial networks.
051   Motivated by the fact that the topology of a neural network has a tangible impact on its performance,
052   and that complex network topology sometimes improves the performance of the model, we system-
053   atically explore more complex and biologically inspired topology in reservoir neural networks. A
      critical step in ESN embedding requires pre-determined read-in and read-out layers with inputs


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      sampled from the induced subgraphs of the embedded reservoir. Typically, a priori set of functional
055   networks (FNs), such as Yeo’s FNs (Yeo et al. (2011)), is used as pre-determined subgraphs for read-
056   in and read-out input selections. Since a priori set of FNs are derived from functional MRIs, yet,
057   these pre-determined sub-circuits are mapped onto SC, it is unknown whether FN-to-SC embedding
058   is well justified from i) neuro-physiological perspective and ii) ESN performance.
059
      In this paper, our contributions are as follows: i) We proposed a pipeline to implement and evaluate
060
      echo-state reservoir networks with various embedded topology on various processing and memo-
061   rization tasks. To this end, we tested various topological embedding and evaluated their respective
062   performance on diverse tasks, showing different performance optimums are highly dependent on the
063   embedded neuro-physiological architecture of a priori functional networks and the corresponding
064   task configurations; ii) we show that, in general, complex topology perform better than simple bi-
065   partite models or null models with feed-forward properties commonly seen in MLP for certain tasks
066   and reservoir criticality conditions. As such, we also found that, contrary to earlier literature, the
067   performance of the reservoir model is not strictly defined by the reservoir’s echo-state property de-
068   fined by the spectral radius of the connectivity matrix; iii) we found that there exists a differentiated
069
      performance of functional networks induced from the human structural connectome with respect to
      different cognitive tasks and their corresponding computation-memorization balances. We analyzed
070
      the topological properties of a priori functional networks and highlighted their graph-theoretical
071
      properties that play significant roles in determining the final performance of the echo-state model.

074   2     BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORKS
075
076   2.1   T OPOLOGICALLY-E MBEDDED ARTIFICIAL NEURAL NETWORK AND
077         E CHO -S TATE -N ETWORKS (ESN)
078
      Echo-state networks are a variant of the classical reservoir computing (RC) paradigms, where the
079
      reservoir is a recurrent layer of the classical recurrent neural network (Verstraeten et al. (2007)).
080
      RCs are typically proposed in place of regular RNN variants as a lightweight model, where the
081   hidden states are high dimensions while only the readout layer weights of the model must be updated
082   during training. The frozen nature of the reservoir layer allows any topology and unit dynamics to
083   be embedded. As a result, RCs have been implemented as predictors of chaotic dynamics in physics
084   simulators, or used as a time-series prediction model in general (Chattopadhyay et al. (2020); Shahi
085   et al. (2022); Bompas et al. (2020); Huhn & Magri (2022); Platt et al. (2022)). ESNs model time as
086   discrete steps, making them suitable for discrete-time sampling data seen in real-world time-series
087   datasets (Cucchi et al. (2022)).
088   In the current literature, the relationship between the topological structure of an NN and its per-
089   formance is still unclear. While complex networks may yield higher predictive performance or
090   parameter efficiency (Kaviani & Sohn (2021)), it is difficult to generalize randomly wired models
091   outside the investigated context. Under the constraint of MLP-random interpolation, several random
092   network models rewired similarly to the small-world regimes perform well on several real-life prob-
093   lems (Erkaymaz et al. (2017); Erkaymaz & Ozer (2016)). Different topology perform well in limited
094   tasks on small network sizes enabled by the learning matrix sequential algorithm for training directed
095
      acyclic graphs (DAGs). Random graph topology are also shown to perform well as image classifiers
      on the ImageNet dataset, especially the Watts-Strogatz (WS) model of small-world networks (Xie
096
      et al. (2019)). Boccato et al. (2024) echoes the statement on the performance of small-world models
097
      in the case of synthetic data, albeit the difference between WS and other complex graph families
098   is less pronounced, possibly owing to the sufficient degree of topological complexity for function
099   representation.
100
101
      2.2   B RAIN C ONNECTOMICS AND HUMAN BRAIN FUNCTIONAL SUB - CIRCUITS
102
103   Human Brain connectomics studies how the human brain is structurally connected and how a
104   heterogeneous repertoire of resting-state or task-related functional circuits emerge on top of it.
105   There are two types of mesoscopic structures in large-scale brain networks: localized and non-
106   localized. In this paper, we focus on investigating localized mesoscopic structures which are sub-
107   systems learned from local/quasi-local network properties such as brain regions of interest (ROIs)
      or functional edges, or correlations among neighboring nodes (Duong-Tran et al. (2024)). In brain


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      connectomics, these sub-structures are induced from a wide array of techniques, including but not
109   limited to clustering (Yeo et al. (2011); Power et al. (2011)) or low-dimensional approximation of
110   high-dimensional dynamics (Shine et al. (2016); Shine & Poldrack (2017); Shine et al. (2019)). The
111   most commonly known localized mesoscopic structures in brain networks are often referred to as
112   functional sub-circuits or functional networks (FNs) (Yeo et al. (2011); Sporns & Betzel (2016);
113   Duong-Tran et al. (2019; 2024)). From here on, we will use the two terms functional sub-circuits
114   and FNs interchangeably.
115
116   2.3     H UMAN -B RAIN -C ONNECTOMICS ESN S
117
118   In the context of modeling the human brain connectome, ESNs are implemented as prototype models
119
      of the structural brain topology. Suarez et al. (2020) shows that human brain connectivity captured
      through diffusion imaging performs better than random null network models in the critical regime,
120
      while also demonstrating computationally relevant properties of resting-state functional brain net-
121
      work parcellation. Given a minimum level of randomness and connection diversity from the original
122   structural connectome, biologically inspired reservoir networks’ performance matches that of any
123   classical random network model (Damicelli et al. (2022)). The authors of d’Andrea et al. (2022)
124   conclude that the modular structure of reservoirs significantly impacts prediction error among mul-
125   tiple other features. While the connections are modeled in a recurrent network rather than strictly an
126   RC, Achterberg et al. (2023) shows that training an RNN constrained by reservoir features such as
127   spatial wiring cost and neuron communicability leads to the emergence of structural and functional
128   features commonly found in primate cerebral cortices.

      3.3   VARIANTS : C OMPLEX GRAPH NULL MODELS
217
218   In general, the models described in this section aim to represent various aspects of the biological
219   mesoscale complex networks and examine the different components of structural brain connectomes,
220   mainly through null models that ablate one or multiple properties of the original connectomes.
221
222   Subgraph To examine the message diffusion in the reservoir, we constructed an induced subgraph
223   partitioning of the original graph and extracted only the subcortical input and current cortical output
224   regions for the reservoir model. The graph in the reservoir is thus an vertex-induced subgraph of
225   only the input-output networks of the original graph; for example, if the current output network is
226   VIS, then the reservoir adjacency matrix is constructed from the original graph G by G[V (subctx) ∪
227
      V (VIS)].
228
229   Maslov-Sneppen (MS) rewire We preserve the original degree-sequence of the structural connec-
230
      tome and rewired each edge in the connectome exactly once using the Maslov-Sneppen algorithm
      (Maslov & Sneppen (2002)). From the rewiring procedure, we obtained a reference null connectome
231
      with the original constraint still moderately enforced.
232
233
      Uniform weights We preserved the underlying topology of the connectome while randomizing
234
      the connection weights between nodes of the network. The connecting edges between all nodes are
235   randomized and sampled from the uniform distribution U (0, 1), while the adjacency matrix is kept
236   symmetric.
237
238   Bipartite null Using the original node counts from the subcortical region and the current output
239   region as baseline (i.e., the node set of the subgraph model), a complete undirected bipartite graph
240   consisting of only the input and output node sets is created. The weights between the two node sets
241   are sampled from the uniform distribution U (0, 1), while the adjacency matrix is symmetric. The
242   bipartite model is analogous to an untrained two-hidden-layer feedforward network model, where
243   the first layer is of the same size as the subcortical nodes and the second as the output functional
244   network (e.g., VIS).
245
246   Newman configuration model An entirely new graph is constructed from the degree sequence of
247   the original structural connectome, as opposed to only rewiring each edge once. The configuration
248   model generates a random undirected pseudograph by randomly assigning edges to match a given
249
      degree sequence (Newman (2003)). Self-loops are then removed from the existing graph to obtain
      the symmetric null connectome adjacency matrix for the reservoir.

269   make a perceptual decision based on only one of the two stimulus inputs from two different modal-

286   4.3   E MPIRICAL P ERFORMANCE : P ROCESSING AND M EMORIZATION
287
288   We evaluate the performance of structural and null models under the mentioned processing and
289   memorization synthetic tasks, showing the performance of each model over multiple reservoir spec-
290   tral radius (alpha) in Figure 3. Models under perceptual and memorization tasks show performance
291
      decay from α = 1, while contextual models perform well even in the chaotic α > 1 regime. The
      memory capacity of reservoirs shows the most notable decline in the chaotic regime, similar to
292
      results obtained in Suarez et al. (2020), and all models perform better than the fully connected bi-
293
      partite null counterpart with the same read-in and read-out node count, showing gains in having
294   higher topological complexity when strictly compared to 2-layer feedforward model. Model per-
295   formance across 1000 different instantiations of the reservoir is fairly stable, showing that degree-
296   conserving null models and bipartite model performed worse than the original biological structural
297   model, structure-based subgraph model, and uniformly randomized structural weight model at close
298   to criticality α = 0.95 (Figure 4).
299
      Between different complex topologies aside from the bipartite models, models that preserve the
300   original structural brain topologies perform better than other null models. The trend is consistent
301   across all alpha values except for CDM, where higher alpha shows the null uniform weights model
302   with identical topology outperforming the original structural model. Aside from MemCap, control
303   structural and subgraph networks perform almost identically, suggesting that information does not
304   propagate through non-input-output pathways in the reservoir for three NeuroGym tasks. MS rewire
305   and configuration model with only the degree sequence preserved show worse performance than
306   the nulls without topological randomization, suggesting that the topological control models contain
307   features that lead to better processing and memorization performance.

504   5.3               T OPOLOGICAL FITNESS OF A PRIORI SET OF FN S ON CONNECTOME - BASED ESN
505
506   In this section, we propose the concept of FN topological fitness for connectome-based ESN us-
507   ing graph modularity score (Clauset et al. (2004a)). To evaluate the topological fitness of an a
508   priori set of FN, we measure Q with pre-set σ (e.g., Yeo’s FNs Yeo et al. (2011)). We performed
509
      cross-comparison between processed functional and structural connectome on modularity measures,
      performing community detection and comparing adjusted mutual info score with the original parcel-
510
      lations. We found that, despite the functional networks parcellation derived from functional connec-
511
      tomes, the modularity of the structural topology is higher than functional (Figure 9), while structural
512   connectomes also perform better than functional (Appendix D). We thus hypothesize that Yeo’s net-
513   works derived from Lausanne parcellation (Suarez et al. (2020)) and Schaefer parcellation (Tipnis
514   et al. (2021)) are not information-theoretically aligned, or that topological fitness strongly deter-
515   mines model performance.

      D.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP WITH FUNCTIONAL CONNECTOMES
872   The resting state fMRI data from a single subject from the HCP1200 dataset pre-parcellated using the
873   Kong2022 individual areal-level parcellation procedure were used in this study (Kong et al. (2021)).
874   Models were examined under several parcellation settings, where the cortex is represented as func-
875   tional connectomes resolutions ranging from 100 to 1000 nodes in increments of 100 nodes each
876   (Kong et al. (2021)) and were evaluated on tasks. FC and adjacency matrix connections on each res-
877   olution describe the Pearson correlation between BOLD time series among brain regions. Regional
878   labels and indices were collected from atlas mapping of the Schaefer atlas directory (Schaefer et al.
879   (2017)), segregating every connectome resolution available of Kong’s parcellation into 17 functional
880
      Yeo functional networks (Thomas Yeo et al. (2011)): Visual (A, B, and C), Auditory, Somatomotor
      (SomMotA and B), Language, Salience/Ventral Attention (SalVenAttn A and B), Control (ContA,
881
      B, and C), Default (A, B, and C), and Dorsal Attention (DorsAttnA and B).
882
883   For a weighted rs-fMRI graph we construct several thresholded graphs on different thresholding
884   values r ranging from -0.5 to 0.4 in increments of 0.1, with a threshold value of 0 effectively keeping
885   only positively correlated connections on the original graph. Varying r effectively filters the graph,
886
      sparsifying graph connections based on the degree of thresholding in large degrees (magnitudes of
      connections filtered between two consecutive thresholds). Keeping negative correlations on lower
887
      threshold values may dilute the computation graph, and potentially decrease the model’s perfor-
888
      mance on several metrics. Additionally, each connectome is scaled by an alpha parameter between
889   0.05 and 2, which determines the value of the largest eigenvector of the connectomic adjacency
890   matrix. The alpha scaling is applied after the normalization of all connections in the graph, and the
891   effect of the alpha parameter on reservoir network dynamics is discussed in-depth in Suarez et al.
892   (2020).
893
      All models are evaluated on a perceptual decision-making task with delayed responsive, measuring
894
      both the computation and memory capacity of the reservoir networks (Inagaki et al. (2019)).
895
896
      D.2   R ESERVOIR SIZE
897
898   In scaling experiments, the model generally performs better when larger parcellation sizes are used.
899   Visualizations and numerical results indicated diminishing returns over later larger parcellations,
900   while there is a substantial difference between the performance of the best and the worst alpha
901   scaling values. Size efficiency decreases as resolution increases with diminishing returns starting
902   at 400 nodes parcellation (Figure 15). On the other hand, SC reservoir with 1000 nodes has high
903   performance at 0.82 F1-score while FC at 700 nodes already reaches 0.75 F1-score, demonstrating
904   FC’s parameter efficiency and signifying that FCs can be better scaled to finer resolutions with
905
      higher model performance.
906
907   D.3   F UNCTIONAL THRESHOLDINGS
908
      Hyperparameter combination experiments showed that functional connectome benefits greatly when
909
      positively thresholded, possibly confirming the hypothesis that negative correlations dampen reser-
910   voir dynamics. Reservoirs performed optimally when thresholded at 0-0.2 and scaled at alpha 0.85-
911   1.15 (Figure 16).
912
913   D.4   P ERMUTATIONS
914
915   Input-output permutation experiments significantly varied performance on different pairings. There
916   are several different configurations where the model performed optimally for different parcellations;
917   however, better performance depends on the set of output nodes with Control and Default outputs
      outperforming other groups. Most notable are readouts from Auditory and Language functional

      networks where performance is significantly worse than other readouts. Said two functional network
919   groups have substantially fewer nodes than the other functional network groups, possibly indicating
920   that performance heavily depends on the size of the readouts up to the optimal signal-to-noise ratio
