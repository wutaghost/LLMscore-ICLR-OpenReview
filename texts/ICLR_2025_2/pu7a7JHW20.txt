009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Variational inference typically assumes normalized priors, limiting the expressive-
012            ness of generative models like Variational Autoencoders (VAEs). In this work,
013            we propose a novel approach by replacing the prior p(z) with an unnormalized
014            energy-based distribution exp (−E(z))/Z, where E(z) is the energy function and
015            Z is the partition function. This leads to a variational lower bound that allows
016
               for two key innovations: (1) the incorporation of more powerful, flexible priors
               into the VAE framework, resulting in improved likelihood estimates and enhanced
017
               generative performance, and (2) the ability to train energy-based models (EBMs)
018
               without the need for computationally expensive Markov chain sampling, requir-
019            ing only a small n > 1 importance samples from the posterior distribution. Our
020            approach bridges VAEs and EBMs, providing a scalable and efficient framework
021            for leveraging unnormalized priors in probabilistic models.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Generative models are essential in unsupervised learning and data generation, with each approach
027
      offering unique strengths and facing specific challenges. Among these, Variational Autoencoders
      (Kingma & Welling, 2022), normalizing flows (Rezende & Mohamed, 2016; Kingma & Dhariwal,
028
      2018), score-based/diffusion models (Song & Ermon, 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al.,
029
      2020), and energy-based models (Du & Mordatch, 2020; Grathwohl et al., 2020) represent some of
030   the most influential methods in modern generative modeling. Each of these models brings distinct
031   advantages but also limitations that impact their practical application and effectiveness.
032
033
      Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) are a cornerstone in generative modeling due to their efficiency
      and scalability. VAEs utilize the variational lower bound (VLB) to approximate complex posterior
034
      distributions and have demonstrated considerable success in various tasks such as image generation
035
      (Vahdat & Kautz, 2021; Child, 2021) and anomaly detection (Pol et al., 2020). The primary strength
036   of VAEs lies in their ability to efficiently model large datasets through a combination of variational
037   inference and neural network architectures. However, VAEs face a significant challenge due to
038   their use of simple, normalized priors, such as Gaussian distributions. This simplicity can lead
039   to a misalignment between the prior and the posterior, where the model struggles to capture the
040   true complexity and multi-modality of the data. Although efforts to enhance the flexibility of the
041   posterior have been made (Rezende & Mohamed, 2016; Kingma et al., 2017), these methods do not
042   fully resolve other issues pertaining to quality image generation (Dai & Wipf, 2019).
043   Normalizing flows offer an alternative by applying a series of invertible transformations to a base
044   distribution, allowing for the modeling of complex data distributions with exact likelihood compu-
045   tation. This flexibility makes normalizing flows highly expressive compared to VAEs. However,
046   the challenge lies in designing and training these transformations, which can become computation-
047   ally demanding and complex, particularly as the dimensionality of the data increases. As a result,
048   while normalizing flows provide powerful modeling capabilities, they may not always be practical
049   for large-scale or real-time applications.
050   Score-based models and diffusion models (SDMs) represent another innovative approach by learn-
051   ing to model the score function, or the gradient of the log-likelihood, of the data distribution. These
052   models refine noisy data through iterative denoising, leading to high-quality samples and the abil-
053   ity to model intricate data structures. Despite their impressive performance, SDMs face substantial
      training and sampling challenges. Training involves optimizing the score function across multiple


                                                        1

054
      noise levels, which requires extensive computation. Additionally, the sampling process is typically
055   slow, as generating high-quality samples often involves many iterative refinement steps. These fac-
056   tors can limit the practicality of SDMs for large-scale or real-time generative tasks.
057
058
      Energy-based models (EBMs) offer a different paradigm by defining probability distributions
      through an unnormalized energy function. EBMs can capture highly complex and varied data
059
      distributions due to their flexible energy function. However, the practical application of EBMs is
060
      constrained by the need for computationally intensive sampling methods like Markov Chain Monte
061   Carlo (MCMC), which are necessary to approximate the intractable partition function. This reliance
062   on expensive sampling techniques makes EBMs less scalable and efficient compared to other gener-
063   ative models.
064
      Among these approaches, Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) remain our primary focus due to their
065
      foundational role in generative modeling and their widespread application in various domains. The
066
      core limitation of VAEs lies in their posterior parameterization failing to effectively capture the com-
067   plexity of the prior distribution. While enhancing the flexibility of the posterior has been explored,
068   this does not fully capture the data distribution.
069
070
      In this work, we address this limitation by introducing unnormalized energy-based priors into the
      VAE framework. By incorporating flexible, unnormalized priors, we aim to improve the alignment
071
      between the prior, posterior, and even the reconstruction likelihood. This novel approach leverages
072
      the expressiveness of energy-based models while maintaining the computational efficiency of VAEs.
073   Our method provides a scalable solution that enhances generative performance and likelihood es-
074   timation, positioning unnormalized priors as a powerful tool for advancing VAE capabilities and
075   addressing their core limitations.

079
080
      Consider the following formulation of the variational lower bound:
081
082                               ln p(x) ≥     E [ln p(x|z) + ln p(z) − ln q(z|x)]                          (1)
                                              q(z|x)
083
084
      Where ln p(x|z) is the reconstruction likelihood, ln p(z) is the prior and ln q(z|x) is the approximate
085
      posterior. We can represent the prior p(z) in R terms of a Boltzmann distribution exp(−E(z))/Z,
086   where E(z) is the energy function and Z = exp(−E(z))dz is the partition function or normaliz-
087   ing constant. The VLB then becomes:
088
089
                              ln p(x) ≥     E [ln p(x|z) − E(z) − ln q(z|x)] − ln Z                          (2)
090                                       q(z|x)
091
092   The main issue here pertains to the partition function as it is generally intractable to compute. When
093   training pure energy-based models, samples from the model are required to be generated during
094   training to approximate its gradient, which is a difficult endeavour in and of itself as it requires a high
095   quality sampler. For our purposes, we instead exploit the approximate posterior to our advantage to
096
      estimate the partition function through self-normalized importance samples, leading to the following
      biased but consistent estimator of the VLB:
097
098
099          ln p(x) ≥     E [ln p(x|z) − E(z) − ln q(z|x)] − ln( E [exp(−E(z) − ln q(z|x))])                (3)
                         q(z|x)                                       q(z|x)
100
101
      Unlike pure EBMs with which likelihood computation is intractable and training requires expensive
102   Markov chain sampling, the EBM prior can be approximated in an unbiased fashion with Monte
103   Carlo samples from the approximate posterior. In effect, the unnormalized prior VLB gives us a
104   framework with which EBMs can be trained much more efficiently and within a rigorously justi-
105   fied maximum-likelihood framework. Thanks to the generality of this VLB, the choice of E(z)
106   can be arbitrary, ranging from simple restricted Boltzmann machines to large ResNets for higher-
107   dimensional datasets. For simplicity, we will be focusing only on Gaussian-Bernoulli RBMs as the
      energy prior for the remainder of the paper. The Gaussian-Bernoulli RBM is a specific formulation

111   RBM is as follows (Liao et al., 2022):

116   Where µ and σ are the per-visible unit mean and standard deviation vectors, b is the hidden bias

127   coders (Burda et al., 2016) by directly optimizing the above bound.

129   3   R ELATED W ORK
130
131
      Incorporating flexible priors such as energy-based, score/diffusion-based, and mixture priors into
132   variational autoencoders (Vahdat et al., 2021; Han et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2023; Rombach et al.,
133   2022) and also regular autoencoders (Ghosh et al., 2020; Jing et al., 2020) is not a new concept,
134   and has seen some considerable success. However, these attempts have approached this concept
135   from a fundamentally different perspective that divorces the objective from its probabilistic roots,
136   resulting in what is essentially just a ”packaging” of two different models. This can have potentially
137   suboptimal effects, espcially for regular autoencoders due to the inherent nature of their structure.
138   Regular autoencoders, unlike VAEs, lack the probabilistic underpinnings that would enable them
139   to effectively handle complex priors. This is because regular autoencoders rely on a deterministic
140
      encoder, where the posterior q(z|x) can be considered degenerate. As a result, attempts to enhance
      the prior distribution, often through ex-post density (Ghosh et al., 2020; Jing et al., 2020) modeling
141
      techniques, have not yielded substantial improvements.
142
143   One key reason for this limitation is the issue of disjoint and discontinuous energy landscapes in
144   regular autoencoders when incorporating sophisticated priors like energy-based models. Without
145   the probabilistic backbone of variational inference, the latent space in regular autoencoders can
146
      become highly irregular, leading to poor generalization. In practice, this often results in samples
      that contain significant artifacts, as the model struggles to reconcile the discontinuities in the energy
147
      landscape. These artifacts are a consequence of the model’s inability to effectively smooth the
148
      transitions between different modes in the data distribution, a problem exacerbated by the lack of a
149   robust posterior to regulate the latent space.
150
151   In contrast, our proposed method directly addresses this issue by unifying these flexible priors into
152
      the rigorous framework of variational inference. By optimizing the variational lower bound in the
      presence of an unnormalized energy-based prior, our approach ensures that the latent space remains
153
      well-structured and continuous. Specifically, the VLB in our framework is expressed as in Equation
154
      (3).
155
156   For a single-sample approximation, the VLB reduces to ln p(x|z), which is exactly the regular au-
157   toencoder objective. In this context, regular autoencoders can viewed as having a degenerate, deter-
158
      ministic posterior which fails to fully capture the complexity of the latent space.
159   Our proposed method of incorporating unnormalized, flexible priors into the VAE framework is
160   orthogonal to the use of normalizing flows for improving posterior estimates (Rezende & Mohamed,
161   2016; Grathwohl et al., 2018). While both strategies aim to make models more expressive, they
      address considerably different limitations.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Normalizing flows focus on improving the posterior distribution q(z|x) by applying a series of in-
163   vertible transformations to a simple base distribution (typically Gaussian). These transformations
164   introduce more flexibility into the posterior, allowing it to better approximate the true latent distri-
165   bution. However, normalizing flows are subject to important design constraints. In order to ensure
166   computational efficiency, the transformations applied in normalizing flows must remain computa-
167   tionally cheap, which places a natural limit on how complex or expressive the posterior can be.
168   Although they are more flexible than traditional Gaussian posteriors, NFs may not fully capture the
169   complexity of highly multi-modal or intricate data distributions due to these computational con-
170   straints.
171   On the other hand, our approach addresses the prior distribution p(z) rather than the posterior. By
172   introducing energy-based priors, we make the prior more flexible and capable of capturing complex
173   latent structures, leading to better alignment with the posterior. Learning a more expressive prior
174   helps mitigate the limitations of normalizing flows, which might not be fully expressive on their
175   own due to the aforementioned computational trade-offs. Importantly, the use of unnormalized
176   priors ensures that the model remains efficient, while simultaneously providing it with a richer latent
177
      space.
178   Another similar approach to normalizing flows is adversarial Variational Bayes (Mescheder et al.,
179   2018), where the posterior is matched to prior implicitly via an adversarial objective. AVB is the
180   closest to our approach, in that despite the implicitness, the resulting objective is still a valid vari-
181   ational lower bound. Unlike our energy-based approach, the AVB objective is inherently unstable,
182   since the density-matched KL estimate is an adversarial objective which are known to be trouble-
183
      some to train. On the other hand, the auxiliary model in our energy-based objective is the model’s
      prior, making the training objective complimentary and stable.

      4   O PTIMIZATION D IFFICULTIES
190
191
192   Scaling VAEs to larger datasets, such as MNIST, comes with optimization difficulties due to the em-
193   phasis on maximizing likelihood during training. This often leads to compromised generative per-
194   formance as generative performance is neither sufficient nor necessary for good likelihoods (Theis
195   et al., 2016).
196
      More specifically, the KL divergence between the prior and posterior becomes high, indicative of
197
      mismatch or latent-space ”holes”. Sampling from these holes, which are areas of low energy, will
198   result in samples that contain noticable artifacts (Rezende & Viola, 2018). This often happens when
199   the prior is fixed, causing the model to trade-off between quality samples (which require a flexible
200   latent-space) and learning the perfect density (leading to posterior collapse).
201
      A straight-forward way to work around this issue is to make the prior learnable, and this is a legit-
202
      imate solution that can largely mitigate this issue. However, the simultaneous training of multiple
203
      objectives may lead to unexpected behaviour such as an overpowered decoder that the prior has
204   difficulty catching up to. Moreover, in hierarchical models (Vahdat & Kautz, 2021; Hazami et al.,
205   2022) the optimization can become very unstable.
206
207
      Instead, we use ex-post density estimation (XPDE) (Ghosh et al., 2020) to correct prior-posterior
      mismatch. XPDE works because the density estimator models the aggregated posterior, which is
208
      the empirical prior that the approximate posterior encompasses. The aggregated posterior is in
209
      fact the optimal solution (Tomczak & Welling, 2018), resulting in a KL of zero. For this rea-
210   son, we use the log-likelihood of the XPDE prior in place of the original energy-based prior for
211   model evaluation. The energy prior is still very much useful as a latent-space regularizer, preventing
212   the latent-space from becoming too disjoint and thus difficult to capture while allowing for much
213   better log-likelihoods than the equivalent Gaussian VAE. We specifically use a Gaussian mixture
214   model, although more ”serious” datasets would use more sophisticated approaches like two-stage
215   VAE training (Dai & Wipf, 2019), which combines ex-post density estimation with hierarchical
      modelling.

      5.1    T OY 2D DATA
222
223   We first put to test the energy-based VAEs on a series of 2D toy data. In particular, we use the
224   8-Gaussians, checkerboard and 2-spirals from (Cao et al., 2019) as well as the four potentials from
225   (Rezende & Mohamed, 2016).
226
      We test two different variations of the energy-based VAEs on each benchmark; one with an uncon-
227
      strained (learnable variance) Gaussian posterior (EVAE), and one with a fixed-variance (σ = 1)
228
      Gaussian posterior (ECVVAE). We do this to demonstrate how flexible posteriors can actually hurt
229   the learning of the energy-based prior as it will attempt to learn a degenerate distribution. We also
230   test a standard VAE with an isotropic Gaussian prior and factorized Gaussian posterior for reference
231   (VAE).
232
      All of the VAEs tested have the same architecture of fully-connected DenseNet (Huang et al., 2018)
233
      blocks with hidden size of 16 units and depth size of 4 in both the encoder and decoder. The two
234
      energy-based VAEs have a Gaussian-Bernoulli RBM prior with 16 visible units and 32 hidden units.
235   Weight normalization (Salimans & Kingma, 2016) is applied to all of the layers in the encoder and
236   decoder. We use the Gibbs-Langevin sampler Liao et al. (2022) to sample from the energy VAEs.

      6   C ONCLUSION
352
353
      In this paper, we proposed a novel variational inference framework that integrates unnormalized
354
      energy-based priors into the Variational Autoencoder (VAE) model. By replacing the traditional
355
      normalized prior with a more flexible energy-based distribution, we addressed key limitations of
356   VAEs, particularly their inability to model complex, multimodal data distributions. Our method
357   demonstrated both theoretical and practical advantages, including improved likelihood estimation
358   and generative performance, as well as scalable training of energy-based models without relying
359   on expensive Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling. We empirically validated our ap-
360   proach on both toy and real-world datasets, showing that energy-based VAEs (EVAEs) outperform
361   traditional VAEs in terms of capturing complex data distributions and producing high-quality gener-
362   ative models. Although our experiments primarily focused on Gaussian-Bernoulli RBM priors, the
363   framework is versatile and can be applied to a wide range of unnormalized priors.
364
365   7   D ISCUSSION
366
367   The introduction of unnormalized priors into VAEs offers a new perspective on generative model-
368   ing by bridging the gap between VAEs and energy-based models (EBMs). Unlike prior work that
369   seeks to enhance generative models by combining different techniques without a unified probabilis-
370   tic foundation, our approach maintains the rigor of variational inference. This not only ensures
371   the tractability of likelihood-based training but also leverages the expressiveness of energy-based
372   models to enrich the latent space of the VAE. By doing so, we have effectively addressed one of
373   the core limitations of VAEs—namely, the mismatch between simple priors and complex posterior
374
      distributions.
375   Our experiments on toy datasets highlight the capability of our model to better capture multimodal
376   and intricate data distributions compared to standard VAEs. While the EVAE variant struggled
377   due to its flexibility, the ECVVAE, which constrained the posterior variance, demonstrated superior
      performance, particularly in learning more robust and accurate latent representations. The results


                                                      7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      suggest that the interplay between posterior flexibility and energy-based priors must be carefully
379   balanced to avoid degenerate solutions, such as overfitting to posterior variances.
380
381   When scaling to more complex datasets like MNIST, our method faced challenges in maintain-
382
      ing a balance between the reconstruction objective and energy minimization, particularly in high-
      dimensional settings. Nonetheless, the experiments showed that our energy-based VAEs still
383
      achieved state-of-the-art log-likelihood results, affirming the potential of unnormalized priors for
384
      large-scale generative modeling tasks. Further exploration of architectural design, variance control,
385   and flexible posteriors could help refine the model’s ability to handle such datasets more effectively.
386
387   Future work could explore alternative strategies for posterior optimization, including hybrid ap-
388
      proaches that combine energy-based priors with more expressive posterior distributions like normal-
      izing flows or score-based methods. Using ECVVAEs as hierarchical priors could also potentially
389
      improve the representation of complex data structures by conserving energy across layers. Addition-
390
      ally, applying this framework to more diverse and complex datasets would provide further insights
391   into its generalizability and performance across various domains.
