009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Probabilistic logical models are a core component of neurosymbolic AI and are
012            important models in their own right for tasks that require high explainability. Un-
013            like neural networks, logical models are often handcrafted using domain expertise,
014            making their development costly and prone to errors. While there are algorithms
015            that learn logical models from data, they are generally prohibitively expensive,
016            limiting their applicability in real-world settings. In this work, we introduce pre-
017            cision and recall for logical rules and define their composition as rule utility – a
018            cost-effective measure to evaluate the predictive power of logical models. Further,
019
               we introduce SPECTRUM, a scalable framework for learning logical models from
               relational data. Its scalability derives from a linear-time algorithm that mines re-
020
               current structures in the data along with a second algorithm that, using the cheap
021
               utility measure, efficiently ranks rules built from these structures. Moreover, we
022            derive theoretical guarantees on the utility of the learnt logical model. As a result,
023            we demonstrate across various tasks that SPECTRUM scales to larger datasets,
024            often learning more accurate logical models orders of magnitude faster than pre-
025            vious methods without requiring specialised GPU hardware.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Motivation. Neurosymbolic AI combines neural networks with (probabilistic) logical models, to
031   harness the strengths of both approaches (d’Avila Garcez et al., 2019; d’Avila Garcez et al., 2022).
032   Neurosymbolic frameworks outperform neural networks in several areas (Manhaeve et al., 2018; Gu
033   et al., 2019; Mao et al., 2019), particularly in interpretability (Mao et al., 2019) and in reducing the
034   need for data (Feldstein et al., 2023a). Unlike neural networks, which are trained from data, logical
035   models are typically handcrafted. Thus, developing logical models requires domain expertise, in
036   both the data and the inference task. This process is costly and prone to errors. As a result, there has
037
      been increased attention on learning logical models from data – a task known as structure learning.
038
039   Limitations of state-of-the-art. Examples of probabilistic logical models include Markov logic
040   networks (MLNs) (Richardson & Domingos, 2006), probabilistic soft logic (PSL) (Bach et al., 2017)
041
      and probabilistic logical programs (PLPs) (Poole, 1993; Sato, 1995; De Raedt et al., 2007). Numer-
      ous structure learning techniques for MLNs (Mihalkova & Mooney, 2007; Kok & Domingos, 2010;
042
      Khot et al., 2015; Feldstein et al., 2023b) and especially PLPs (Quinlan, 1990; Muggleton, 1995;
043
      Evans & Grefenstette, 2018; Schüller & Benz, 2018; Qu et al., 2021b; Cheng et al., 2023) have been
044   proposed. However, an overarching limitation of structure learning remains the limited scalability
045   to large datasets. The underlying difficulty is the exponential nature of the problem with respect
046   to the length of possible rules and the number of relations in the data. State-of-the-art structure
047   learning algorithms aim to reduce the complexity of the problem by splitting the task into two steps:
048   1) Pattern mining - which identifies frequently occurring substructures in the data. 2) Optimisation
049   - an iterative process during which the best logical formulae are chosen from a set of candidates
050   identified within the patterns. Any structure learning algorithm must make approximations to tackle
051   scalability. Existing methods proceed by finding approximate patterns but perform exact inference.
052
053   Contributions. Our key idea is to flip the aforementioned paradigm: find exact patterns but per-
      form approximate ranking of candidate formulae to create the final logical model. By eliminating


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      a combinatorial explosion of inference steps, this strategy effectively improves scalability by orders
055   of magnitude. Specifically, we present three contributions to tackle scalability:
056
057         1. Cheap utility measure: We introduce utility, a criteria for measuring the predictive power
058            of individual rules and logical theories. The utility relates to the degree to which a rule or
059            logical model is satisfied in the data (precision) as well as how often the rules predict data
060            points (recall). This measure can be computed cheaply, without requiring exact inference.
061         2. Linear-time pattern mining: We present a linear-time algorithm (in the size of the dataset)
062            that finds the number of occurrences of different patterns in the data. The algorithm is
063            approximate, yet we provide theoretical guarantees on the computational cost required for
064            a certain error bound on the utility estimates. These guarantees are dataset-independent.
065         3. Quadratic-time optimisation: We present a quadratic-time greedy optimisation algorithm
066            (in the number of rules of the final model). The algorithm finds the best rules and sorts
067            them by their utility. Since the utility measure is cheap to evaluate, compared to prior art
068            this optimisation step is no longer a bottleneck for scalability.
069
070   Empirical results. In addition to the theoretical contributions, we present SPECTRUM, a paral-
071   lelised C++ implementation of our structure learning framework. We show on various relational
072   datasets that SPECTRUM improves scalability by orders of magnitude, consistently reducing run-
073   times to < 1% compared to the previous state-of-the-art. Also, despite minor restrictions on mined
074
      logical formulae, we find logical models that in most cases improve accuracy compared to prior art.
075
076
      Restrictions. SPECTRUM is restricted to datasets with unary and binary relations, however, many
      real-world datasets fit within this restriction. In addition, our utility measure is only well defined for
077
      Datalog theories (Abiteboul et al., 1995), a language used extensively in data management (Barceló
078
      & Pichler, 2012; Moustafa et al., 2016) and neurosymbolic learning (Huang et al., 2021).

      objects. An atom has the form P(t1 , . . . , tn ), where P is an n-ary predicate, and t1 , . . . , tn are terms.

      A fact is an atom for which each term ti is a constant (e.g. F RIENDS(alice, bob)). A relational

      5     PATTERN MINING
206
      In this section, we present our technique for mining rule patterns from relational data, i.e. finding
207
      subgraphs in a relational database graph. Since finding all subgraphs is generally a hard problem
208   with no known polynomial algorithm (Bomze et al., 1999), we adopt an approach similar to PRISM
209   (Feldstein et al., 2023b). In particular, we present a non-exhaustive algorithm that has only linear-
210   time complexity in the dataset size but that allows us to compute estimates of utility that are close,
211   in a precise sense, to their true values.

      6.1    R ESTRICTIONS ON THE MINED RULES
343
      As we stated in the introduction, SPECTRUM focuses on learning Datalog rules (as opposed to
344   general first-order logic formulae). In addition, the algorithms restrict the shape of the mined rules:
345
346         (1) Algorithm 1 only mines patterns (and by extension rules) where each term occurs in at most
347             two binary predicates and one unary predicate, except for the special case P1 (X) → P2 (X)
348             (line 2 in Algorithm 1).
349         (2) Algorithm 2 restricts to rules that are body-connected and term-constrained. A rule is
350             term-constrained if every term occurs in at least two atoms of the rule.
351
352   For example, the rule FRIENDS(U1 , U2 ) → LIKES(U2 , I) is not term-constrained, since neither U1
353
      nor I appear twice, but LIKES(U1 , I) ∧ FRIENDS(U1 , U2 ) → LIKES(U2 , I) is term-constrained.
354   Restriction (1) helps to restrict the complexity of the framework. Additionally, we recommend
355   setting the terminal depth D in Algorithm 1 to a small number, as N scales with a factor of D
356   (Theorem 3), and thus Algorithm 1 has complexity O(D2 ). In our experiments (Section 7), we set
357   D = 3 (i.e. a maximum of three binary predicates per rule). This is not a severe limitation, as we
358   show empirically that many useful rules can be expressed within this restriction.
359   Restriction (2) ensures useful rules: term-constrainedness ensures each term is in at least one known
360   atom, aiding link prediction, while body-connectedness is required for computing utility (Section 4).
361
362   6.2    A LGORITHM
363
364   SPECTRUM requires three parameters M , ε, and D: M is the maximum number of rules of the
365   final theory; D sets a limit to the length of the mined rules as the pattern length is limited to 2D + 1;
366   ε balances the trade-off between accuracy in the utility measures and computational effort. Given
367
      these parameters, SPECTRUM computes an optimal N for pattern mining (Theorem 3), and, using
368
      Algorithm 1, mines patterns which are stored in a map G global of patterns to their groundings in G D .
369   Each pattern Gφ in the keys of the map G global is considered in turn. Each rule that could have
370   resulted in this pattern, i.e. a rule from the set R := {ρ | Gbody(ρ)∧head(ρ) = Gφ }, is considered. If
371
      a rule ρ ∈ R is term constrained and satisfies P(ρ)S(ρ)
                                                          B(ρ) > 1, i.e. the rule is a better predictor than a
372
      random guess (Section 4), then ρ is added to the set of candidate rules ρcandidates .
373
374   From the set of candidate rules, a subset of M rules with the highest individual utility is chosen
375   (Definition 6). The utility of each rule ρ is directly calculated from G global ; since this is a map from
376   patterns Gφ of a conjunction φ to its groundings in G D , i.e. G φ . The quantity |G φ | can be looked up
377   in the map. The conjunction φ can be body(ρ), head(ρ), or body(ρ) ∧ head(ρ). The complexity and
      symmetry factor can be computed for each ρ directly from its length, rule pattern and body pattern.

405        SPECTRUM then orders the remaining M rules in order of their contribution to the theory utility
406        (Definition 8). The algorithm starts by finding the rule with the highest utility and stores it in a vector
407        ρfinal . Then, in each iteration of the while-loop, SPECTRUM finds the rule out of the remaining ones
408        that provides the highest increase in theory utility when added to the current rules in ρfinal .
409
           After SPECTRUM, the rules ρfinal can be passed to any probabilistic logical framework (e.g. PSL
410
           or MLN) to learn the weights of the rules (i.e. the likelihood of the rule being satisfied) for a given
411        dataset D. We recommend adding one rule at a time to the logical model (in the order they were
412        added to the vector ρfinal ) when learning the weights. One can then validate the different theories by
413        checking when the accuracy drops, as more rules may not imply a better theory.

491   8   R ELATED WORK
492
493   Markov logic networks. State-of-the-art structure learning approaches proceed in two steps -
494   identification of patterns and optimisation. The different structure learning techniques for MLNs
495
      can be split into two groups: (1) methods for which patterns are user-defined and (2) methods
      for which patterns are identified automatically in the data. The current state-of-the-art that does
496
      not require user-defined patterns is LSM (Kok & Domingos, 2010). LSM identifies patterns by
497
      running random walks over a hypergraph representation of the data. A major limitation of LSM is
498   that it lacks guarantees on the quality of the mined patterns. PRISM is an efficient pattern-mining
499   technique with theoretical guarantees on the quality of mined patterns, solving the above limitation
500   (Feldstein et al., 2023b). Our empirical results show that SPECTRUM scales significantly better
501   than any of the techniques mentioned above, without requiring any domain expertise as patterns
502   are mined automatically. In addition, inspired by Feldstein et al. (2023b), we provide ε-uncertainty
503   guarantees, which, in contrast to PRISM, are guarantees on the utility of the output theory rather
504   than just the patterns.
505
506   Inductive logic programming. A popular family of techniques for learning Datalog theories is
507   inductive logic programming (ILP), e.g. FOIL (Quinlan, 1990), MDIE (Muggleton, 1995) and In-
508   spire (Schüller & Benz, 2018). Given a database D, a set of positive facts E + and a set of negative
509   facts E − , ILP-based techniques work by computing a theory that along with D entails all facts in
510
      E + and no fact in E − . MetaAbd (Dai & Muggleton, 2021) mixes logical abduction (i.e., backward
      reasoning) (Kakas, 2017) with ILP to simultaneously learn a logical theory and train a neural clas-
511
      sifier. A recent line of research proposed formulations of ILP in which part of the computation can
512
      be differentiated and, hence, (part of) the learning is done through backpropagation. For example,
513   Evans & Grefenstette (2018) introduced δILP which employs the semantics of fuzzy logic for inter-
514   preting rules. Sen et al. (2022) proposed a similar ILP technique based on logical neural networks
515   (Riegel et al., 2020). All ILP-based techniques require users to provide the patterns of the formulae
516   to be mined. The above requirement, along with issues regarding scalability (Evans & Grefenstette,
517   2018), limits the applicability of ILP techniques in large and complex datasets.
518
519   Differentiable rule learning. Other techniques for learning logical rules in a differentiable fashion
520   are NeuralLP (Yang et al., 2017), DRUM (Sadeghian et al., 2019), neural logic machines (NLMs)
521   (Dong et al., 2019), RNNLogic (Qu et al., 2021b), and NCRL (Cheng et al., 2023). All these tech-
522   niques are limited by the shape of the rules that they learn. NeuralLP, DRUM, RNNLogic and NCRL
523   can only learn rules of the form P1 (X, Z1 ) ∧ · · · ∧ Pn−1 (Zn−1 , Y ) → Pn (X, Y ), while NLMs are
524
      restricted to learning rules where all head and body atoms contain the same variables. While SPEC-
      TRUM has some limitation on the shapes of the rules it learns, they are less restrictive.
525
526
527   9   C ONCLUSIONS
528
529   A major point of criticism against neurosymbolic techniques and logical models is the need for do-
530   main expertise (Feldstein et al., 2023a; Huang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023). This work tackles the
531   scalability issue of learning logical models from data, mining accurate logical theories in minutes
532   for datasets with millions of instances, thus making the development of a logical model a simple
533   and fast process. Therefore, we see our work as having the potential to increase the adoption of neu-
534   rosymbolic frameworks. In addition, learning logical models improves explainability by extracting
535   knowledge from data that is interpretable by a domain expert.
536   There are several directions for future research. First, the pattern mining algorithm could be gen-
537   eralised to relations with higher arity. Second, the pattern mining algorithm could be extended to
538   require fewer restrictions on the shape of the rules. Finally, since the rules we learn are model ag-
539   nostic, we plan to apply our technique to other logical frameworks, in addition to MLNs and PSL,
      such as Problog (De Raedt et al., 2007).

953   A.4   J USTIFICATION OF R EMARK 2
954
      In the above proof of Theorem 3 we considered the worst-case scenario, where we required ε-
955
      uncertainty of top-M pattern fragments found locally around each node vi (c.f. stage 1 of the proof).
956   In reality, in many datasets, rule fragments that appear in the D-neighourbood of one node, will also
957   appear within the D-neighbourhoods of many other nodes in the data graph G D . The limiting case
958   is the case of homogeneous data, where the pattern probabilities in the D-neighbourhood of every
959   node in DG are the same. In this scenario, it is the sum of pattern counts from running random
960   walks from all nodes that needs to be connected to the notion of ε-uncertainty. For a dataset with
961   |V | nodes, this means that the number of random walks required to run from each individual node is
962   smaller by a factor of |V |, i.e. N (ε) ∼ O(M D/ε2 |V |), as stated in Remark 2.

1117         3. CAD: The collective activity detection dataset (CAD) contains relations about people and
1118            the actions (waiting, queuing, walking, talking, etc.) they perform in a sequence of frames.
1119            FRAME (B, F) states whether a box B (drawn around an actor in a frame) is in a specific frame
1120            F; FLABEL(F, A) states whether most actors in a frame perform action A; DOING(B, A) states
1121            whether an actor in a box B performs action A; CLOSE(B1 , B2 ) states whether two boxes in
1122            a frame are close to each other; SAME(B1 , B2 ) states whether two bounding boxes across
1123            different frames depict the same actor.
1124         4. Yelp: The Yelp 2020 dataset contains user ratings on local businesses, information
1125            about business categories and friendships between users. We used the pre-processing
1126            script proposed by Kouki et al. (2015) to create a dataset consisting of SIMILARITEMS,
1127            SIMILARUSERS , FRIENDS , AVERAGEITEMRATING , AVERAGEUSERRATING , and RATING,
