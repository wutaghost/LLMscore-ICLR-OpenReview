009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011                Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has been highly success-
012                ful in aligning large language models with human preferences. While prevalent
013                methods like DPO have demonstrated strong performance, they frame interactions
014                with the language model as a bandit problem, which limits their applicability in
015                real-world scenarios where multi-turn conversations are common. Additionally,
016
                   DPO relies on the Bradley-Terry model assumption, which does not adequately
                   capture the non-transitive nature of human preferences. In this paper, we address
017
                   these challenges by modeling the alignment problem as a two-player constant-sum
018
                   Markov game, where each player seeks to maximize their winning rate against the
019                other across all steps of the conversation. Our approach Multi-step Preference Op-
020                timization (MPO) is built upon the natural actor-critic framework (Peters & Schaal,
021                2008). We further develop OMPO based on the optimistic online gradient descent
022                algorithm (Rakhlin & Sridharan, 2013; Joulani et al., 2017). Theoretically, we
023                provide a rigorous analysis for both algorithms on convergence and show that
024                OMPO requires O(ϵ−1 ) policy updates to converge to an ϵ-approximate Nash equi-
025                librium. We also validate the effectiveness of our method through experiments on
026                the multi-turn conversations dataset in MT-bench-101.

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In recent years, the integration of large-language models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Achiam
031   et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023) into various applications has highlighted the need for advanced
032   preference alignment methods (Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon et al., 2020; Bai et al., 2022; Ouyang
033   et al., 2022; Rafailov et al., 2023). As models increasingly engage in complex decision making or
034   reasoning scenarios, e.g., GPT-4o and o11 , the ability to align their outputs with user preferences has
035   received more attention. However, existing works on reinforcement learning from human feedback
036   (RLHF) focus mostly on one-step preference (Rafailov et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2024; Munos
037
      et al., 2024; Azar et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024), which neglects indispensable
      intermediate preferences within the answer and limits the model’s alignment ability. For example,
038
      in multi-round conversations, alignment must occur at each turn to meet user needs. Similarly, in
039
      mathematical reasoning with chain-of-thought prompting, step-by-step validation is essential to
040   ensure accuracy in the final result. The reliance on final-output feedback in most existing RLHF
041   methods (Wang et al., 2023; Shani et al., 2024) neglects these intermediate steps, highlighting the
042   need for multi-step preference optimization to enhance alignment capabilities.
043
      Meanwhile, earlier alignment methods e.g., DPO and its variants step-DPO (Lai et al., 2024; Lu
044
      et al., 2024), typically model the pairwise preference by the Bradley-Terry model (Bradley & Terry,
045
      1952), which assigns a score for each answer based on its preference. This assumption of the
046   model cannot capture the non-transitive preference, which is often observed in the averaged human
047   preferences from the population (Tversky, 1969; Gardner, 1970). While a recent line of work has
048   modeled the alignment process under the framework of general preference (Azar et al., 2024; Munos
049   et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024; Rosset et al., 2024), and thus bypasses the BT model assumption, the
050   challenge of multi-step preference optimization remains underexplored.
051
      In this paper, we first address this gap by formulating multi-step general preference optimization
052   within the framework of two-player Markov games (Shapley, 1953), where each player seeks to

064   • We formulate multi-step preference optimization as a two-player partially observable Markov
065     game. Unlike Wang et al. (2023); Swamy et al. (2024); Shani et al. (2024) who focus on the

085   2   R ELATED WORK
086
087   RLHF under Bradley-Terry model. Over the years, significant strides have been made towards
088   developing RLHF algorithms from various perspectives under the Bradley-Terry model Bradley
089   & Terry (1952). Earlier RLHF pipelines usually included supervised fine-tuning, learning a reward
090   model, and reinforcement learning optimization with PPO (Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon et al., 2020;
091   Bai et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022). Due to the instability and scaling issues of such a pipeline,
092   direct alignment methods such as DPO have been proposed to bypass the training of the reward
093
      model (Rafailov et al., 2023). Several follow-up methods, such as generalized preference optimiza-
      tion (GPO, Tang et al. 2024), use offline preference data to directly optimize pairwise preferences
094
      against a fixed opponent. A number of works have proposed reference-model-free method (Meng
095
      et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024). In Meng et al. (2024), the impact of sequence length is mitigated by
096   averaging the likelihood over the length of the sequence. In the multi-step scenario, several multi-
097   step variants of DPO are introduced in the math reasoning task. Lu et al. (2024) initiate from an
098   intermediate step in a correct reasoning process and increase the temperature to produce a flawed
099   reasoning path leading to an incorrect answer. Meanwhile, Lai et al. (2024) leverage GPT-4 to detect
100   the first incorrect step in a multi-step reasoning trajectory, then regenerate from that point to obtain
101   the correct path. Together, these serve as the pair of samples for DPO.
102   RLHF under general preferences. The reward model in the Bradley-Terry model inherently im-
103   plies transitivity in preferences. However, human preferences, especially the resulting averaged
104   human preferences from populations, are usually nontransitive (Tversky, 1969; Gardner, 1970). To
105   this end, Azar et al. (2024) outline a general framework for RLHF starting from general preference
106   optimization and shows that DPO is a special case with the assumption of Bradley-Terry model.
107   They further proposed IPO without such an assumption. Subsequently, Munos et al. (2024) try to
      solve the alignment of non-transitive general preferences using two-player nash learning in a bandit

108
      setting. In their work, preferences are regularized through KL divergence to a reference policy, and
109   they prove the convergence of the last iterative. In Swamy et al. (2024), multi-step alignment is
110   considered while preference signals are only applied at the final step. Swamy et al. (2024) do not
111   demonstrate the effectiveness of this framework in large language models. Wu et al. (2024) propose
112   SPPO, studying bandit alignment under general preferences. They introduce a novel loss function
113   that increases the log-likelihood of the selected response while decreasing that of the rejected re-
114   sponse, in contrast to DPO. Rosset et al. (2024) start with the nash learning framework and propose
115   Online DPO, which is an iterative version of DPO. Wang et al. (2023) provide theoretical analysis
116   on multi-step RLHF under general preference while practice application is not explored. In Wang
117
      et al. (2023), the preference signal is given for the entire trajectory of an MDP while in this paper
      it is step-wise. Shani et al. (2024) study multi-step alignment under general preferences. However,
118
      unlike their approach where only preferences at the final states are considered, our work is built on a
119
      two-player Markov game which assumes that human preference is received at each step rather than
120   only at the final step. Additionally, we leverage the optimistic online gradient descent to achieve
121   a better convergence rate than Wang et al. (2023); Shani et al. (2024), and utilize Monte Carlo es-
122   timation with a small-scale pairwise reward model, avoiding the need for an additional function
123   approximator for the critic network.
124
      Two-player Markov game & optimistic online gradient descent. Two-player Markov games
125
      have been widely studied since the seminal work (Shapley, 1953). Particularly relevant to our work
126   is the research line on policy gradient algorithms for two-player Markov games such as Daskalakis
127   et al. (2020); Wei et al. (2021); Alacaoglu et al. (2022). Our OMPO is strictly related to the idea of
128   optimistic online gradient descent (Popov, 1980; Chiang et al., 2012; Rakhlin & Sridharan, 2013)
129   originally proposed in online learning to achieve small regret in case of slow varying loss sequences.
130   Our update that uses only one projection per update was proposed in Joulani et al. (2017). The name
131   of our method is due to a similar algorithm introduced in the context of variational inequalities by
132   Malitsky & Tam (2020).

      3.1   N OTATION
137
138   We define the prompt to the language model as x and the answer from the language model as a. For
139   a multi-turn conversation with turn H, the prompts and the answers are denoted by xh and ah , ∀h ∈
140   [H]. The concatenation of a prompt x and an answer a is denoted by [x, a] and can be generalized
141   to the concatenation of multiple prompts and answers, e.g., [x1 , a1 , . . . , xH , aH ]. For any two sen-
142   tences, e.g., [x, a] and [x′ , a′ ], we define a preference oracle as o([x, a]≻[x′ , a′ ]) ∈ {0, 1}, which can
143   provide preference feedback with 0-1 scores, where 1 means the conversation [x, a] is preferred and
144
      0 otherwise. We denote P([x, a] ≻ [x′ , a′ ]) = E[o([x, a] ≻ [x′ , a′ ])] as the probability that the con-
      versation [x, a] is preferred over [x′ , a′ ]. Moreover, we have P([x, a] ≻ [x′ , a′ ]) = 1 − P([x′ , a′ ] ≻
145
      [x, a]). An autoregressive language model is denoted by π(a|x) which receives input x and gen-
146
      erates answer a. We denote the KL divergence of two probability distributions p and q by D(p∥q).
147   The Bregman Divergences between two points are denoted by D(p∥q). The sigmoid function is
148   defined by σ(z) := 1+e1−z . Detailed definitions for the notations are summarized in Appx. A.

      3.2   P ROBLEM FORMULATION OF MULTI - STEP RLHF
151
152   In this section, we introduce the problem setting for multi-step RLHF and we defer the preliminaries
153   on single-step RLHF to Appx. B. Specifically, we can cast the multi-step alignment process as a
154   finite-horizon Markov Decision Process (MDP). We define sh = [x1 , a1 , . . . , xh−1 , ah−1 , xh ] as
155   the state at h > 1. We define the action ah as the answer given sh . Particularly, we have s1 = x1 .
156   The prompt in the next state is sampled under the transition xh+1 ∼ f (·|sh , ah ), which is equivalent
157   to sh+1 ∼ f (·|sh , ah ). The equivalence comes from the fact sh+1 = [sh , ah , xh+1 ] by using the
158   concatenation operator between sentences. The terminal state is sH+1 . Our setting covers a number
159
      of alignment problems, and we list some examples below.
160   Example 1 (Single-step alignment). In single-step alignment, a language model receives one
161   prompt and outputs one answer. Our framework covers the single-step alignment by dissecting the
      answer into single tokens. Specifically, we set x1 as the prompt, x2 , . . . , xH+1 as empty sentences,


                                                            3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      and the answer ah at each turn consists of only one token. Then the horizon H is the number of
163   tokens in the answer. The transition between each state is deterministic.
164
      Example 2 (Chain-of-thought reasoning alignment). In the chain-of-thought reasoning, the horizon
165
      H denotes the number of reasoning steps, where x1 is the initial prompt and x2 , . . . , xH+1 are
166   empty. Each ah corresponds to a reasoning step. The transition between each state is deterministic.
167
      Example 3 (Mutli-turn conversation alignment). In multi-turn conversation, the horizon H denotes
168
      the total number of turns in the conversation. In the h-th turn, xh is the prompt, and ah is the answer.
169
      The prompt in the terminal state, xH+1 , is an empty sentence. The transition between each state can
170   be deterministic or stochastic.

      6        C ONCLUSION
530
531
      This work presents a novel framework to enhance the preference alignment of large language models
532
      in multi-step settings by casting the alignment process as a two-player Markov game. We introduce
533
      novel algorithms based on natural actor-critic and optimistic online gradient descent, supported by
534   both theoretical analysis and empirical results. However, the limitations of this work include the
535   finite-horizon assumption in our theoretical framework, which may not fully capture real-world
536   conversations or reasoning processes that often span with different steps instead of a fixed step
537   H. Additionally, our practical algorithm requires querying a preference oracle, which may limit
538   its applicability in cases where such preference oracles are unavailable or when collecting human
539   feedback is costly. Future work should explore extending the theoretical framework to infinite-
      horizon settings and finding more scalable methods for gathering preference feedback.

      C     A DDITIONAL DISCUSSION ON RELATED WORK
820
821   C.1   R ELATED W ORK ON T OKEN -L EVEL P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION
822
823   A line of work formulates the alignment of contextual bandit problems in LLMs (Example.1) from
824   token-level MDPs perspective (Rafailov et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a). In Rafailov
825   et al. (2024), by defining the reward at each token before the terminal token as the generation like-
826   lihood and using the maximum entropy RL objective, the authors derive the original objective of
827   DPO from a new perspective that incorporates token-level rewards. Zeng et al. (2024) assume that
828
      the reward for a response can be decomposed into token-level rewards at each token. Then they de-
      sign a token-level objective function based on Trust Region Policy Optimization, adding token-level
829
      KL divergence constraints to the DPO objective in the final algorithm. More recently, Liu et al.
830
      (2024a) study how the difference in average rewards between chosen and rejected responses affects
831   the optimization stability, designing a new algorithm where importance sampling weights are as-
832   signed to each token-level reward. There are two main differences between the multi-step alignment
833   approach in our work and those in previous work. First, while Rafailov et al. (2024); Zeng et al.
834   (2024); Liu et al. (2024a) develop alignment methods based on the Bradley-Terry model with tran-
835   sitive rewards, our framework is motivated by a two-player game with relative rewards. Secondly,
836   although Rafailov et al. (2024); Zeng et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024a) formulate the alignment pro-
837   cess as an MDP, their final objective is tailored to a contextual bandit problem in LLMs. In contrast,
838   our objective is designed for a multi-step alignment problem, suited for multi-turn conversation or
839
      chain-of-thought reasoning.
840

      C.2   D ISCUSSION ON THE DIFFERENCE FROM SPPO
842   Next, we elaborate on the difference with SPPO (Wu et al., 2024) below: Firstly, the theoretical
843   analysis of the proposed MPO differs from that of SPPO due to differences in the settings. SPPO
844   considers the contextual bandit problem and builds its analysis based on the game matrix from Fre-
845   und & Schapire (1999). In our case, however, we frame the problem as a Markov game and employ
846   a distinct theoretical analysis apart from Freund & Schapire (1999). Specifically, in our proof, we
847   (i) use the performance difference lemma to rewrite the global regret as weighted average of local
848   regrets and (ii) control the local regrets with multiplicative weights updates. Secondly, a new algo-
849   rithm, OMPO, is developed in this work with a novel theoretical guarantee. In the case where the
850
      horizon H = 1, the update of OMPO reduces to
851                    π t+1 (a|s) ∝ π t (a|s) exp [β(2P(a ≻ π t (·|s)) − P(a ≻ π t−1 (·|s)))],
852   while the update of SPPO is
853                                π t+1 (a|s) ∝ π t (a|s) exp [β(P(a ≻ π t (·|s)))].
854   As a result, OMPO enables O(ϵ−1 ) policy updates to converge to an ϵ-approximate Nash equilib-
855   rium instead of O(ϵ−2 ), according to our theoretical analysis.

1529   G     M OTIVATION OF CONSIDERING INTERMEDIATE REWARD
1530
1531   In this section, we elaborate on the motivation for considering intermediate rewards at each turn
1532   instead of only terminal rewards.
1533
       In multi-turn conversation tasks, such as MT-bench 101 (Bai et al., 2024), the user asks questions
1534
       x1 , x2 , x3 , and receives answers a1 , a2 , a3 . When x2 is not closely related to x1 , aligning the first
1535   step using feedback among different a1 is much more helpful than using the sequence [a1 , x2 , a2 ],
1536   where x2 , a2 can be considered as noise.
1537
1538
       In mathematical reasoning tasks, as mentioned in Lai et al. (2024), some cases yield correct fi-
       nal answers but contain errors in intermediate reasoning steps. Consequently, Lai et al. (2024)
1539
       filter out such samples using GPT-4. For example, consider a case where the reasoning steps
1540
       yield a correct final answer but include an error: [acorrect        1     , awrong
                                                                                    2     , acorrect
                                                                                             3       ], where awrong
                                                                                                               2     is incorrect
1541   while all of the other steps and the final answer a3            correct
                                                                               is correct. When there is another response,
1542   [acorrect
          1      , acorrect
                    2       , acorrect
                                3      ] with all correct steps, using only terminal signal for aligning step 2 might not
1543   guarantee that acorrect 2        ≻ awrong
                                             2     because both of final answers are correct, especially when there is
1544   only an incorrect step among long reasoning steps. In contrast, an intermediate signal would clearly
1545   indicate acorrect
                    2         ≻ awrong
                                   2      , accurately reflecting the quality of the intermediate steps. In practice, if the
1546   final signal is important, e.g., in math reasoning task, then we can use only the terminal reward or
1547   the average of terminal reward and intermediate reward, otherwise one can just use the intermediate
1548   reward, which is cheaper to collect as compared to assigning reward until the terminal state.
