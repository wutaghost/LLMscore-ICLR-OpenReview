009                                             A BSTRACT
010
011           The recent success of transformer language models owes much to their conversa-
012           tional fluency and productivity in linguistic and morphological aspects. An affine
013           Taylor approximation has been found to be a good approximation for transformer
014           computations over certain factual and encyclopedic relations. We show that the
015           truly linear approximation W s, where s is a middle layer representation of the
016
              base form and W is a local model derivative, is necessary and sufficient to ap-
              proximate morphological derivations. This approach achieves above 80% faith-
017
              fulness across most morphological tasks in the Bigger Analogy Test Set, and is
018
              successful over different language models and languages. We propose that mor-
019           phological relationships in transformer models are likely to be linearly encoded,
020           with implications for how entities are represented in latent space.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Large language models display impressive capabilities for factual recall, which commonly involve
026   relations between entities (Brown et al. 2020). Recent work has shown that affine transformations
027   on subject representations can faithfully approximate model outputs for certain subject-object
028
      relations (Hernandez et al. 2023). Identifying the contexts in which approximations perform well is
      an important area of study, with applications in interpretability and model editing.

064

065

066
      of speech, including noun to adjective [noun+less], adjective to noun [adj+ness], verb to noun
067   [verb+er], and verb to adjective [verb+able], and involve diverse subjects and objects. Importantly,
068   we find that relative to linear approximation, encyclopedic and semantic relations benefit from the
069   affine LRE, but not morphological relations.
070
      Our study reproduces and extends existing research. Specifically, we apply the affine Linear Re-
071
      lational Embedding (LRE) method to novel relational categories encompassing a diverse range of
072
      domains, including derivational and inflectional morphology, encyclopedic knowledge, and lexical
073   semantics. We address the data scarcity of the original paper in many categories, and confirm the
074   efficacy of the affine LRE. We show that relational approximation can be applied to an adapted ana-
075   logical dataset and demonstrate relational approximation for a broad range of linguistic phenomena.
076   This opens avenues for further research in relational representations which take advantage of exist-
077   ing analogical and linguistic datasets, such as MarkG, SCAN, WordNet, and FrameNet (Zhang et al.
078   2022, Miller 1995, Czinczoll et al. 2022, Baker et al. 1998).
079   At the same time, it makes a key contribution to the body of research around relational representation
080   in model latents. We show that for different relations, additive and multiplicative mechanisms play
081   complementary roles in affine approximation. We find that the original linear relational embedding
082   developed by Paccanaro and Hinton (2001), a multiplicative operator, is effective within specific
083   relations. In particular, linear approximation within contexts relating morphological forms reaches
084   near-equivalent level of faithfulness to the approximation found by the affine LRE. We perform tests
085   in eight different languages, and find that this equivalence holds across typological categories.

087   2     R ELATED W ORK
088
089   Much work in machine learning has focused on learning concept representations with hierarchical
090   structure. Relations between representations in concept spaces have been modeled successfully by
091   both linear multiplicative and additive operations.
092
093   2.1   L INEAR E MBEDDING S PACES
094
095   Multiplicative. Paccanaro and Hinton (2001) introduced the concept of the linear relational embed-
096   ding for learning relational knowledge from triples (a, R, b). Along with prior work (Hinton 1986),
097   they were able to solve a family tree problem where data is given in relational triples (Colin, child,
098
      Victoria), where vector components captured implicit semantics such as generation. Concepts such
      as a and b are represented as n-length vectors, while relations such as R are represented as n × n
099
      matrices, akin to Coecke’s models of compositional semantics (2010).
100
101   Additive. Mikolov et al. (2013) used linear operations in word vector space derived from context-
102   predictive neural nets, demonstrating a correspondence between directional binary relations (male-
103   female, country-capital, verb tense) and the addition of certain embedding vectors. Subsequent
104
      work with GloVe focused on leveraging statistical information to develop semantic substructures
      e.g. in Pennington et al. (2014). Detailed empirical studies found inflection relations (compara-
105
      tive, strong:stronger) are better captured than derivation relations (lacking, life:lifeless), and that
106
      encyclopedic relations (capital-of, Greece:Athens) are better captured than lexicographic relations
107   (member-of, player:team) (Gladkova et al. 2016; Vylomova et al. 2016).


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Park et al. 2023 formalize the compositional representation of concepts in embedding spaces. Ex-
109   tending prior work (Wang et al. 2023), they define a set of counterfactual outputs Y for a directional
110   binary concept W . They identify concept intervention as adding an embedding representation λ̄W
111   to change the probability of an output reflecting a concept W . For any concept Z linearly separable
112   from W , an output word Y (W, . . . , Z), and concept embedding λ, an intervention is effective if it
113   changes the probability of W but not Z.

      3.2   I NTERNAL R ELATIONAL R EPRESENTATION
139
140   In recent years, there has been increasing interest in factual relational representation. Meng et al.
141   (2022b) found that factual statement predictions exhibit strongly causal states in middle layers at
142   last subject token, supporting the idea that an enriched subject representation exists prior to output.
143   Geva et al. (2023) demonstrated that attribute extraction is often performed by specific attention
144   heads in later layers, and takes the form of a query on the enriched representation.
145
146
      We directly build off of work by Hernandez et al. 2023, who present an approximator known
      by the corresponding internal hypothesis of the Linear Relational Encoding. Within this paper, we
147
      will denote this as the affine LRE. With s denoting a middle hidden subject state and o denoting
148
      the final object state, they treat object-retrieval for a relational context r as linearly approximable:
149   o = Fr (s). They model o with an affine first-order Taylor approximation
150
151

152   using the transformer Jacobian ∂F
                                     ∂s    between states to approximate W , and utilizing the subject rep-
153   resentation s from an intermediate layer. By doing so, they achieve over 60% faithfulness for LM
154   predictions across certain factual, commonsense, linguistic, and bias relations.
155
      In this paper, we identify a linear relational embedding mechanism for morphological relations.
156   Through coarse-grained methods such as linear probing, transformer models have been found to
157   encode linguistic features in internal representations, such as syntactic dependencies and thematic
158   categories (Kann et al. 2018; Tenney et al. 2019; Wilson et al. 2023). Lin et al. (2019) identifies as-
159   pects of syntactic structure that are relevant for subject-verb agreement and reflexive dependencies
160   in BERT. However, there remains a lack of explicitly identified encoding mechanisms for many lin-
161   guistic competencies observed in language models, including morphology, grammatical agreement,
      ambiguity resolution, and discourse coherence.

166   Approximating transformer computations through affine approximation has achieved empirical suc-
167   cess, yet the internal mechanism by which they operate remain opaque. Beyond whether the outputs
168   of an language model are approximable from latent representations, we would like to gain insight
169   as to how the underlying relationships between concepts are represented. Within contexts which
170   express relations, we would like to understand if models implement simple, linear mechanisms for
171
      transforming input states to output states.
172   We first consider what it means for a context to express a relation. Many statements can be expressed
173   in terms of a subject, relation, and object (s,r,o). For instance, the statement Miles Davis plays the
174   trumpet expresses a relation Fr , connecting the subject s (Miles Davis) to the object o (trumpet):

253   4.2   I NTRODUCING N EW R ELATIONS
254
255   From the cognitive perspective, analogy has traditionally been regarded as an inductive mechanism
256   which makes comparisons between mental representations (Sternberg and Rifkin 1979, Gentner
257   1983). This makes analogy a special case of role-based relational reasoning (Holyoak 2012), and
258
      motivates the adaptation of analogical pairs to a relational setting. We choose to adapt the Bigger
      Analogy Test Set, also known as BATS. The Bigger Analogy Test Set was originally introduced to
259
      explore linguistic regularities in word embeddings by Gladkova et al. (2016). The dataset comprises
260
      forty different categories, spanning inflectional morphology, derivational morphology, encyclope-
261   dic knowledge, and lexical semantics. Each category is made up of fifty pairs of words sharing a
262   common relation. The pairs are compiled from diverse manual and automated datasets, including
263   WordNet, SemEval2012-Task2, Wikipedia, the Google Analogy Test Set, and a multimodal color
264   dataset Fellbaum (1998); Jurgens et al. (2012); Mikolov et al. (2013); Bruni et al. (2012).
265
266   4.3   U TILIZING ICL

269   instance of the analogy. For instance, the [verb+ment] dataset comprises pairs of words with base

473   E(o − Wr s), not br = E(o − s). In practice, we find that bias and concept vectors are close in cosine
474   similarity, and likely serve similar roles.
475
476   5.5   N ON - STEMMED F ORMS AND FAILURE C ASES
477
478   The faithfulness metric is potentially a problematic choice for measuring morphology. High faithful-
479   ness scores on many morphological tasks can be achieved by reproducing a substring of the subject
480   token. In this case, it is possible the Jacobian approximation is simply repeating a stemmed form of
481   the subject token. Consequently, it would be agnostic to the derived form. This theory would make
482
      the high faithfulness of morphology more questionable.
483   However, the Jacobian produces many full morphological forms, which challenges this perspective.
484   For instance, #25303 ’ sadness’ and #24659 ’ continuation’ faithfully replicate derived forms and are
485   consistently reproduced by the Jacobian. For further evidence against this view, including correct,
      stemmed, and incorrect counts, see Table 1 and Table 3 in the Appendix.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      There are two inflectional relationships the Jacobian failed to approximate as well over the tests
487   performed, [Ving - 3psg] and [Ving - Ved]. One possibility is that transformations from the verb
488   active form make the LM computation non-linear. For the majority of the relations on which the
489   Jacobian achieves high faithfulness, the subject is the unmarked form, such as the verb infinitive or
490   third person singular. There are also derivational prefix tasks for which the LRE, but not the Jaco-
491   bian, faithfully approximates, [re+verb] and [over+adj]. A partial explanation for this phenomenon
492   is that the object tokens ”over” and ”re” are idiosyncratically related to the subjects, unlike other re-
493   lations. As seen in Table 2, this causes the vocabulary to contain fewer correct object hidden states,
494   so transformations of the subject hidden state may not be an effective approximation.
495
496   5.6   I MPLICATIONS FOR C ONCEPT T HEORY
497
      Morphological relations involve well characterized concepts between words. The Linear Relational
498
      Hypothesis formalized by Park et al. (2024) posits that directions in the representation space of a
499
      language model encode high-level concepts. However, contrary to expectation, we have found that
500   morphological derivations are well-approximated with a multiplicative operation, and not by an
501   additive operation. As can be seen in Figure 6 and Figure 7, both Bias and TRANSLATION results
502   in faulty approximations for morphology. However, these results could still be compatible with the
503   LRH. If morphology is encoded as a linear transformation, relations distinct from morphological
504   paradigms (e.g. semantics specified at the lexical level) might continue to be represented by vectors.
505
506   We do not claim that morphological derivation is the only linguistic phenomenon which can
507   be linearly approximated, or that all morphology is linearly approximable. Instead, we demonstrate
508
      the hidden states of base representations can be implicitly transformed to morphological derivatives,
      highlighting a surprising linearity present in many morphological relations.
509
510
511   6     C ONCLUSION
512
513   In this work, we have adapted the Bigger Analogy Test Set to create a large novel testing dataset for
514   relations, covering forty relations over morphological, factual, and semantic relations. We formulate
515   the transformer equivalent of the linear relational embedding found in Paccanaro and Hinton (2001)
516   more precisely to be equivalent to the Jacobian, and, surprisingly, find this approximator is able to
517
      model certain relations as well as the affine LRE. Returning to the affine method, we hypothesize
      that the Jacobian serves the role of extending a subject entity to alternative forms, and the bias term
518
      serves the role of shifting underlying concepts.
519
520   Through the approximation of language models, we arrive at a better understanding of their internal
521   structure, which is crucial for controlling its outputs effectively. This ultimately has implications
522   for many downstream applications of transformer language models, including as knowledge bases,
523
      dialogue agents, and as robust tools for inference and reasoning.

650
      A.1   L IMITATIONS : S CALING
651
652   Our experiments were conducted exclusively on GPT-J and Llama-7b due to hardware constraints,
653   which limited the scope of our evaluations. While GPT-J is a powerful language model, it has fewer
654   parameters and lower computational complexity compared to state-of-the-art models. As a result,
655   the linear decoding investigated here may not generalize to larger-scale models.
656
657
      However, as transformer models share the same underlying architecture, smaller models serve
      as a likely proxy for studying the interpretability of transformer-based language models. Future
658
      work could build on these findings by scaling experiments to larger models, but we believe that
659
      architectural similarities allow our study to provide meaningful contributions to interpretability
660   across transformers.
661
662
      A.2   E XPERIMENTAL L IMITATIONS
663
664   A key assumption being made is that the linear transformations observed here are employed in
665   regular token prediction the same way that they are done in an explicit relational content. Based
666   on existing literature in activation patching and editing (Geva et al. 2021), we believe that the
667   hypothesis of subject enrichment being independent from contexts is supported. To determine
668   this more thoroughly, further research could employ references to both base and derived forms in
669   naturalistic contexts.
670
      Additionally, unlike previous investigations of linear approximation, we did not investigate
671
      whether the faithfulness of the Jacobian approximation is associated with causality. Based on the
672
      prior work which successfully finds a relationship between these variables Hernandez et al. (2023),
673   it is reasonable to believe these two measures are correlated, and that the internal structure of the
674   model is revealed to be linear.
