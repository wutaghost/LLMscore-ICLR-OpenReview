009                                                          A BSTRACT
010
011             Generative models aim to produce synthetic data indistinguishable from real dis-
012             tributions, but iterative training on self-generated data can lead to model collapse
013             (MC), where performance degrades over time. In this work, we provide the first
014             theoretical analysis of MC in Rectified Flow by framing it within the context of
015             Denoising Autoencoders (DAEs). We show that when DAE models are trained on
016             recursively generated synthetic data with small noise variance, they suffer from
017
                MC with progressive diminishing generation quality. To address this MC issue, we
                propose methods that strategically incorporate real data into the training process,
018
                even when direct noise-image pairs are unavailable. Our proposed techniques, in-
019
                cluding Reverse Collapse-Avoiding (RCA) Reflow and Online Collapse-Avoiding
020             Reflow (OCAR), effectively prevent MC while maintaining the efficiency benefits
021             of Rectified Flow. Extensive experiments on standard image datasets demonstrate
022             that our methods not only mitigate MC but also improve sampling efficiency, lead-
023             ing to higher-quality image generation with fewer sampling steps.

      2.1   M ODEL C OLLAPSE IN G ENERATIVE M ODELS
112
      The generation of synthetic data by advanced models has raised concerns about model collapse,
113   where models degrade when trained on their own outputs. Although large language models and dif-
114   fusion models are primarily trained on human-generated data, the inadvertent inclusion of synthetic
115   data can lead to self-consuming training loops (Alemohammad et al., 2023), resulting in perfor-
116   mance degradation (Shumailov et al., 2023). Empirical evidence of MC has been observed across
117   various settings (Hataya et al., 2023; Martı́nez et al., 2023; Bohacek & Farid, 2023). Theoretical
118   analyses attribute the collapse to factors like sampling bias and approximation errors (Shumailov
119   et al., 2023; Dohmatob et al., 2024). While mixing real and synthetic data can maintain perfor-
120   mance (Bertrand et al., 2023), existing studies often focus on maximum likelihood settings without
121
      directly explaining MC. Our work extends these analyses to simulation-free generative models like
      diffusion models and flow matching, specifically addressing MC in the Reflow method of Rectified
122
      Flow and proposing more efficient training of Rectified flow.
123
124
125   2.2   E FFICIENT S AMPLING IN G ENERATIVE M ODELS
126
      Achieving efficient sampling without compromising quality is a key challenge in generative mod-
127
      eling. GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2013) offer fast generation
128   but face issues like instability and lower sample quality. Diffusion models (Song et al., 2020b) and
129   continuous normalizing flows (Chen et al., 2018; Lipman et al., 2022; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden,
130   2022), produce high-fidelity outputs but require multiple iterative steps, slowing down sampling.
131   To accelerate sampling, methods such as modifying the diffusion process (Song et al., 2020a; Bao
132   et al., 2021; Dockhorn et al., 2021), employing efficient ODE solvers (Lu et al.; Dockhorn et al.,
133   2022; Zhang & Chen, 2022), and using distillation techniques (Salimans & Ho, 2022) have been
134   proposed. Consistency Models (Song et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024) aim for
135   single-step sampling but struggle with complex distributions. Rectified Flow and its Reflow method
136
      (Liu et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2024b) promise efficient sampling by straightening flow trajectories,
      needing fewer steps. However, they are prone to MC due to training on self-generated data, and
137
      existing avoidance methods are ineffective as they do not provide the required noise-image pairs.
138
      Our work addresses this gap by proposing methods to prevent MC in Rectified Flow.

208   Rectified Flow (RF) (Liu et al., 2022; Liu, 2022; Liu et al., 2023) extends FM by straightening

      4.1   C ONNECTION BETWEEN D ENOISING AUTOENCODERS AND D IFFUSION M ODELS
231
232   Denoising Autoencoders (DAEs) are closely related to diffusion models through the concept of
233   score matching (Song & Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2020b). Under certain conditions, training a
234   DAE implicitly performs score matching by estimating the gradient of the log-density of the data
235   distribution (Vincent, 2011). Specifically, given data x and Gaussian noise ϵ ∼ N (0, σ 2 I), the DAE
236   minimizes the reconstruction loss:
                                                                       2
237                                    LDAE = Ex,ϵ ∥fθ (x + ϵ) − x∥ ,                                   (5)
238
       where fθ is the DAE parameterized by θ. The residual between the output and input approximates
239   the scaled score function:
240                                fθ (x + ϵ) − x ≈ σ 2 ∇x log p(x + ϵ).                          (6)
241
242   We demonstrate that the training objectives of diffusion models and Flow Matching methods, such
243   as Rectified Flow, can be unified, differing only in parameter settings and affine transformations
244
      (Esser et al., 2024). Specifically, diffusion models are special cases of Continuous Normalizing
      Flow (CNF) trajectories (Lipman et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022). Consequently, analyzing MC in
245
      Denoising Autoencoders (DAEs) is essential for understanding collapse in diffusion models and
246
      Rectified Flow. Since DAEs learn to denoise and approximate the score function, examining their
247   behavior under iterative training on self-generated data can reveal degradation mechanisms in more
248   complex generative models. In this work, we focus on a simplified scenario where a DAE is recur-
249   sively trained on its own generated data, enabling an analytical study of MC.

      5     AVOIDING M ODEL C OLLAPSE IN R ECTIFIED F LOW
331
      Building upon our exploration of MC in simulation-free generative models, we address this chal-
332
      lenge within the Rectified Flow framework. Although Rectified Flow and its Reflow algorithm
333
      (Liu et al., 2022) achieve efficient sampling by straightening probability flow trajectories, they are
334   susceptible to MC due to iterative training on self-generated data (see Figure 2(A)). Our analysis,
335   consistent with Bertrand et al. (2023); Gerstgrasser et al. (2024), shows that incorporating real data
336   can mitigate collapse. However, integrating real data in Rectified Flow is challenging because it re-
337   quires noise-image pairs that are not readily available, and directly pairing real images with random
338   noise invalidates the Reflow training (see Figure 2(B)).
339
      To overcome this limitation, we generate the necessary noise-image pairs using the reverse ODE
340   process, commonly used in image editing tasks (Wallace et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a). This
341   allows us to obtain exact inverse image-noise pairs given the pre-trained model and real images.
342   However, we face the issue of insufficient real image-noise pairs; for example, CIFAR-10 provides
343   only 50,000 real images, while Reflow requires over 5 million data pairs per iteration (Liu et al.,
344   2022). Our Gaussian experiments suggest that a synthetic-to-real data ratio of at least 7:3 is needed
345   to avoid collapse effectively (see Figure 4). Using the reverse SDE process with significant ran-
346   domness (Meng et al., 2021) leads to image-noise pairs dominated by randomness, undermining the
347   purpose of straightening the flow (like the vanilla collapse-avoid methods Figure 2(B)).
348   Therefore, the question arises: How can we generate sufficient real image-noise pairs while main-
349   taining forward-backward consistency? In the following sections, we detail the implementation of
350   RCA, which effectively mitigates MC while preserving the efficiency benefits of Rectified Flow.
351

      5.2   O NLINE R EVERSE C OLLAPSE -A VOIDING R EFLOW (OCAR)
405
406   Although the Reverse Collapse-Avoiding (RCA) Reflow method effectively prevents model collapse
407   and straightens the flow to reduce the sampling steps in Rectified Flow (Figure 2 (C)), it requires
408   substantial storage resources. For instance, Lee et al. (2024a) report using over 40 GB of memory
409   on the ImageNet 64 × 64 task just to store x̂(i) during one Reflow iteration. This high memory
410   consumption limits the applicability of RCA in high-dimensional image generation experiments.
411
      To address this limitation, we consider the scenario where the regeneration parameter α approaches
412   zero. In this case, we obtain an online method that generates synthetic noise-image pairs and real
413   reverse image-noise pairs in every mini-batch, mixing them on the fly. This approach resembles the
414   distillation method proposed by Kim et al. (2023), who use real data to improve the performance of
415   consistency models (Song et al., 2023). However, there are key differences: First, we do not use
416   a fixed pre-trained model as a teacher; instead, we straighten the flow through repeated iterations.
417   Second, we do not rely on the assumption that the neural network can recover any sampled point
418   from any distribution on the generative path given any input. Instead, we maintain a straight path
419   that is easy to understand and has clear meaning. Research shows that a straight flow can be regarded
420
      as a progressive approximation of the optimal transport map (Liu, 2022). The detailed algorithm for
      the Online Collapse-Avoiding Reflow (OCAR) method can be found in Appendix B.1.

532   7      C ONCLUSION
533
534   We addressed model collapse in simulation-free generative models, focusing on Rectified Flow.
535   Through theoretical analysis, we identified how training on self-generated data leads to performance
536   degradation. To mitigate this, we introduced RCA Reflow and OCAR, which incorporate real data to
537   prevent collapse while maintaining efficiency. Experiments validate their effectiveness in improving
538   generation quality and sampling efficiency. Future work includes exploring model collapse in other
539
      distillation methods, such as Consistency Distilling (CD), and further enhancing the robustness of
      generative models under more challenging conditions.

834   step due to the pure Gaussian input. By analyzing the initial step separately, we can better align
835   the recursive DAE framework with the diffusion model. Specifically, if we consider the initial DAE

840   challenging because there is no underlying structure to capture. This limitation highlights why the
841   initial step differs from the rest and underscores the necessity of separating its analysis.
842
      By acknowledging this gap, our analysis becomes more comprehensive, bridging the understanding
843   between DAEs and diffusion models. This perspective not only sheds light on the mechanics of
844   diffusion models but also provides a pathway for leveraging insights from DAE analysis to improve
845   diffusion-based generative models.

971   data using reverse processes and balancing them with synthetic pairs, we prevent model collapse

       B.2    D OES A DDING R ANDOMNESS H ELP ? R EVERSE SDE S AMPLING
1027
1028   In the previous methods, we utilized the reverse ODE process to generate noise-image pairs for
1029   training. However, when using only the deterministic ODE, the randomness in the training process
1030   originates solely from the initial latent variables z ∼ N (0, I). This limited source of randomness
1031   may impact the diversity of the generated samples and the robustness of the model (Zhang et al.,
1032   2023b).
1033   To enhance diversity and potentially improve generation quality, we consider introducing controlled
1034   randomness into the reverse process by employing a reverse Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE).
1035   The reverse SDE allows us to inject noise at each time step during the sampling process, defined as:
1036
1037
                                 dx = f (t, x) − g(t)2 ∇x log pt (x) dt + g(t)dw,

1038                                                                           e                               (32)
1039
1040   where f (t, x) and g(t) are the drift and diffusion coefficients, respectively, and dw
                                                                                            e denotes the stan-
1041   dard Wiener process in reverse time. By introducing the diffusion term g(t)dw,    e we inject controlled
1042   stochasticity into the reverse sampling.
1043   In practice, we set the noise scale g(t) to be small (e.g., σ = 0.001) and perform sampling using
1044   methods like the Euler-Maruyama scheme with an appropriate number of steps (e.g., 100 steps).
1045   This controlled injection of noise increases the variability in the training data without significantly
1046   disrupting the straightening effect of the flow. Specifically, the added randomness helps explore the
1047   neighborhood of data samples, enriching the learning process. We denote this method as OCAR-S.
