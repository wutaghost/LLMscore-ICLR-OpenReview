                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Developing algorithms to differentiate between machine-generated texts and
012
               human-written texts has garnered substantial attention in recent years. Existing
013            methods in this direction typically concern an offline setting where a dataset con-
014            taining a mix of real and machine-generated texts is given upfront, and the task is
015            to determine whether each sample in the dataset is from a large language model
016            (LLM) or a human. However, in many practical scenarios, sources such as news
017            websites, social media accounts, or on other forums publish content in a stream-
018            ing fashion. Therefore, in this online scenario, how to quickly and accurately
019            determine whether the source is an LLM with strong statistical guarantees is cru-
020            cial for these media or platforms to function effectively and prevent the spread
021
               of misinformation and other potential misuse of LLMs. To tackle the problem of
               online detection, we develop an algorithm based on the techniques of sequential
022
               hypothesis testing by betting that not only builds upon and complements existing
023
               offline detection techniques but also enjoys statistical guarantees, which include a
024            controlled false positive rate and the expected time to correctly identify a source
025            as an LLM. Experiments were conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of our
026            method.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Over the past few years, there has been growing evidence that LLMs can produce content with
032   qualities on par with human-level writing, including writing stories (Yuan et al., 2022), producing
033   educational content (Kasneci et al., 2023), and summarizing news (Zhang et al., 2024). On the other
034   hand, concerns about potentially harmful misuses have also accumulated in recent years, such as
035   producing fake news (Zellers et al., 2019), misinformation (Lin et al., 2021; Chen & Shu, 2023),
036   plagiarism (Bommasani et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2023), malicious product reviews (Adelani et al.,
037   2020), and cheating (Stokel-Walker, 2022; Susnjak & McIntosh, 2024). To tackle the relevant issues
038   associated with the rise of LLMs, a burgeoning body of research has been dedicated to distinguishing
039
      between human-written and machine-generated texts (Jawahar et al., 2020; Lavergne et al., 2008;
      Hashimoto et al., 2019; Gehrmann et al., 2019; Mitchell et al., 2023; Su et al., 2023; Bao et al.,
040
      2023; Solaiman et al., 2019; Bakhtin et al., 2019; Zellers et al., 2019; Ippolito et al., 2019; Tian,
041
      2023; Uchendu et al., 2020; Fagni et al., 2021; Adelani et al., 2020; Abdelnabi & Fritz, 2021; Zhao
042   et al., 2023; Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; Christ et al., 2024).
043
044   While these existing methods can efficiently identify a text source in an offline setting where all
045
      the texts to be classified are provided in a single shot, they are not specifically designed to handle
      scenarios where texts arrive sequentially, and therefore, they might not be directly applicable to the
046
      online setting, where certain serious challenges have been observed over the past few years. For
047
      example, the American Federal Communications Commission in 2017 decided to repeal net neu-
048   trality rules according to the public opinions collected through an online platform (Selyukh, 2017;
049   Weiss, 2019). However, it was ultimately discovered that the overwhelming majority of the total 22
050   million comments that support rescinding the rules were machine-generated (Kao, 2017). In 2019,
051   Weiss (2019) used GPT-2 to overwhelm a website for collecting public comments on a medical re-
052   form waiver within only four days, where machine-generated comments eventually made up 55.3%
053   of all the comments (more precisely, 1, 001 out of 1, 810 comments). As discussed by Fröhling &
      Zubiaga (2021), a GPT-J model trained on a politics message board was then deployed on the same


                                                        1

054
      forum. It generated posts that included objectionable content and accounted for about 10% of all
055   activity during peak times (Kilcher, 2022). Furthermore, other online attacks mentioned by Fröhling
056   & Zubiaga (2021) may even manipulate public discourse (Ferrara et al., 2016), flood news with fake
057   content (Belz, 2019), or fraud by impersonating others on the Internet or via e-mail (Solaiman et al.,
058   2019). However, existing bot detection methods for social media (e.g., Davis et al. (2016); Varol
059   et al. (2017); Pozzana & Ferrara (2020); Ferrara (2023) and the references therein) might not be di-
060   rectly applicable to the online setting with strong statistical guarantees, to the best of our knowledge,
061   and they often require training on extensive labeled datasets beforehand. This highlight the urgent
062   need for developing algorithms with strong statistical guarantees that can quickly identify machine-
063
      generated texts in a timely manner, which, to the best of our knowledge, have been overlooked in
      the literature.
064
065   Our goal, therefore, is to tackle the problem of online detecting LLM-generated texts. More pre-
066   cisely, building upon existing score functions from those “offline approaches”, we aim to quickly
067   determine whether the source of a sequence of texts observed in a streaming fashion is an LLM or a
068   human. Our algorithm leverages the techniques of sequential hypothesis testing (Shafer, 2021; Ram-
069
      das et al., 2023; Shekhar & Ramdas, 2023). Specifically, we frame the problem of online detecting
      LLMs as a sequential hypothesis testing problem, where at each round t, a text from an unknown
070
      source is observed, and we aim to infer whether it is generated by an LLM. We also assume that a
071
      pool of examples of human-written texts is available, and our algorithm can sample a text from this
072   pool of examples at any time t. Our method constructs a null hypothesis H0 (to be elaborated soon),
073   for which correctly rejecting the null hypothesis implies that the algorithm correctly identifies the
074   source as an LLM under a mild assumption. Furthermore, since it is desirable to quickly identify
075   an LLM when it is present and avoid erroneously declaring the source as an LLM, we also aim to
076   control the type-I error (false positive rate) while maximizing the power to reduce type-II error (false
077   negative rate), and to establish an upper bound on the expected total number of rounds to declare
078   that the source is an LLM. We emphasize that our approach is non-parametric, and hence it does
079   not need to assume that the underlying data of human or machine-generated texts follow a certain
080
      distribution (Balsubramani & Ramdas, 2015). It also avoids the need for assuming that the sample
      size is fixed or to specify it before the testing starts, and hence it is in contrast with some typical hy-
081
      pothesis testing methods that do not enjoy strong statistical guarantees in the anytime-valid fashion
082
      (Garson, 2012; Good, 2013; Tartakovsky et al., 2014). The way to achieve these is based on recent
083   developments in sequential hypothesis testing via betting (Shafer, 2021; Shekhar & Ramdas, 2023).
084   The setting of online testing with anytime-valid guarantees could be particularly useful when one
085   seeks substantial savings in both data collection and time without compromising the reliability of
086   their statistical testing. These desiderata might be elusive for approaches that based on collecting
087   data in batch and classifying them offline to achieve.
088
      We evaluate the effectiveness of our method through comprehensive experiments. The code and
089   datasets needed to reproduce the experimental results is available in the supplementary file.

095   We begin by providing a recap of the background on sequential hypothesis testing.
096   Sequential Hypothesis Test with Level-α and Asymptotic Power One. Let us denote a forward
097   filtration F = (Ft )t≥0 , where Ft = σ(Z1 , . . . , Zt ) represents an increasing sequence that accu-
098   mulates all the information from the observations {Zi : i ≥ 1} up to time point t. A process
099   W := (Wt )t≥1 , adapted to (Ft )t≥1 , is defined as a P-martingale if it satisfies EP [Wt |Ft−1 ] = Wt−1
100   for all t ≥ 1. Furthermore, W is a P-supermartingale if EP [Wt |Ft−1 ] ≤ Wt−1 for all t ≥ 1. In our
101   algorithm design, we will consider a martingale W and define the event {Wt ≥ 1/α} as rejecting
102   the null hypothesis H0 , where α > 0 is a user-specified “significance level” parameter. We denote
103   the stopping time τ := arg inf t≥1 P {Wt ≥ 1/α} accordingly.
104   We further recall that a hypothesis test is a level-α test if supP ∈H0 P (∃t ≥ 1, Wt ≥ 1/α) ≤ α,
105   or alternatively, if supP ∈H0 P (τ ≤ ∞) ≤ α. Furthermore, a test has asymptotic power (1 − β)
106   if supP ∈H1 P (∀t ≥ 1, Wt < 1/α) ≤ β, or if supP ∈H1 P (τ = ∞) ≤ β, where H1 represents
107   the alternative hypothesis. A test with asymptotic power one (i.e., β = 0) means that when the
      alternative hypothesis is true, the test will eventually rejects the null hypothesis H0 . As shown later,

135   Problem Setup. We consider a scenario in which, at each round t, a text yt from an unknown source
136   is observed, and additionally, a human-written text xt can be sampled from a dataset of human-
137
      written text examples at our disposal. The goal is to quickly and correctly determine whether the
      source that produces the sequence of texts {yt }Tt=1 is an LLM or a human. We assume that a score
138
      function ϕ(·) : Text → R is available, which, given a text as input, outputs a score. The score
139
      function ϕ(·) that we consider in this work are those proposed for detecting LLM-generated texts
140   in offline settings, e.g., Mitchell et al. (2023); Bao et al. (2023); Su et al. (2023); Bao et al. (2023);
141   Yang et al. (2023). We provide more details on these score functions in the experiments section and

145
      and similarly, each human-written text xt is i.i.d. from a distribution ρx . Denote the mean µx :=
146   Eρx [ϕ(x)] and µy := Eρy [ϕ(y)] respectively. The task of hypothesis testing that we consider can be
147   formulated as

150   We note that when H0 is true, this is not equivalent to saying that the texts {yt }Tt=1 are human-
151   written, as different distributions can share the same mean. However, under the additional assump-
152   tion of the existence of a good score function ϕ(·) which produces scores for machine-generated

155   ρy is an LLM, then rejecting the null hypothesis H0 is equivalent to correctly identifying that the

      from that of ϕ(xt ) when each yt is generated by an LLM. Figure 1 illustrates the process of online

161   an online learner engages in multiple rounds of a game with an initial wealth W0 = 1. In each round
      t of the game, the learner plays a point θt . Then, the learner receives a fortune after committing θt ,

164   Consequently, the dynamic of the wealth of the learner evolves as:

      To connect the learner’s game with sequential hypothesis testing, one of the key techniques that will
168
      be used in the algorithm design and analysis is Ville’s inequality (Ville, 1939), which states that if
169
      (Wt )t≥1 is a nonnegative supermartingale, then one has P (∃t : Wt ≥ 1/α) ≤ αE[W0 ]. The idea
170   is that if we can guarantee that the learner’s wealth Wt can remain nonnegative from W0 = 1, then
171   Ville’s inequality can be used to control the type-I error at level α simultaneously at all time steps t.
172   To see this, let gt = ϕ(xt ) − ϕ(yt ). Then, when P ∈ H0 (i.e., the null hypothesis µx = µy holds),
173   the wealth (Wt )t≥1 is a P-supermartingale, because
174
175   EP [Wt |Ft−1 ] = EP [Wt−1 (1 − θt gt )|Ft−1 ] = Wt−1 · EP [(1 − θt (ϕ(xt ) − ϕ(yt )))|Ft−1 ] = Wt−1 .
176
      Hence, if the learner’s wealth Wt can remain nonnegative given the initial wealth W0 = 1, we can
177   apply Ville’s inequality to get a provable level-α test, since Wt is a nonnegative supermartingale in
178   this case. Another key technique is randomized Ville’s inequality (Ramdas & Manole, 2023) for a
179   nonnegative supermartingale (Wt )t≥1 , which states that P (∃t ≤ T : Wt ≥ 1/α or WT ≥ Z/α) ≤
180   α, where T is any F-stopping time and Z is randomly drawn from the uniform distribution in [0, 1].
181   This inequality becomes particularly handy when there is a time budget T in sequential hypothesis
182   testing while maintaining a valid level-α test.
183   We now switch to discussing the control of the type-II error, which occurs when the wealth Wt is
184   not accumulated enough to reject H0 when H1 is true. Therefore, we need a mechanism to enable
185   the online learner in the game quickly increase the wealth under H1 . Related works of sequential
186   hypothesis testing by betting (Shekhar & Ramdas, 2023; Chugg et al., 2023) propose using a no-
187   regret learning algorithm to achieve this. Specifically, a no-regret learner aims to obtain a sublinear
                                                    PT              PT
188   regret, which is defined as RegretT (θ∗ ) := t=1 ℓt (θt ) − t=1 ℓt (θ∗ ), where θ∗ is a benchmark.
189   In our case, we will consider the loss function at t to be ℓt (θ) := − ln(1 − gt θ). The high-level
190   idea is based on the observation that the first term in the regret definition is the log of the learner’s
                                                     PT                       PT
191   wealth, modulo a minus sign, i.e., ln(WT ) = t=1 ln(1−gt θt ) = − t=1 ℓt (θt ), while the second
192   term is that of a benchmark. Therefore, if the learner’s regret can be upper-bounded, say C, then
193   the learner’s wealth is lower-bounded as WT ≥ ΠTt=1 (1 − gt θ∗ ) exp(−C). An online learning
194   algorithm with a small regret bound can help increase the wealth quickly under H1 . We refer the
195
      reader to Appendix D for a rigorous argument, where we note that applying a no-regret algorithm
      to guarantee the learner’s wealth is a neat technique that is well-known in online learning, see e.g.,
196
      Chapter 9 of Orabona (2019) for more details. Following existing works (Shekhar & Ramdas, 2023;
197
      Chugg et al., 2023), we will adopt Online Newton Steps (ONS) (Hazan et al., 2007) in our algorithm.

207

208

209
      the learner’s wealth remains a non-negative supermartingale (Step 11 in Algorithm 1). We note that
210   if the bound of the output of the underlying score function ϕ(·) is known a priori, this scenario holds

212   steps and execute the algorithm thereafter. One approach is to set the estimate as a conservatively
213   large constant, e.g., twice the maximum value observed in the first few time steps. We observe that
214   this estimate works for our algorithm with most of the score functions ϕ(·) that we consider in the
215   experiments. On the other hand, we note that a tighter bound dt will lead to a faster time to reject

286   Score Functions. We use 10 score functions in total from the related works for the experiments. As
287   mentioned earlier, a score function takes a text as an input and outputs a score. For example, one
288   of the configurations of our algorithm that we try uses a score function called Likelihood, which
289   is based on the average of the logarithmic probabilities of each token conditioned on its preceding
290
      tokens (Solaiman et al., 2019; Hashimoto et al., 2019). More P   precisely, for a text x which consists of
                                                                         n
      n tokens, this score function can be formulated as ϕ(x) = n1 j=1 log pθ (xj |x1:j−1 ), where xj de-
291
292
      notes the j-th token of the text x, x1:j−1 means the first j−1 tokens, and pθ represents the probability
      computed by a language model used for scoring. The score functions that we considered in the exper-
293
      iments are: 1. DetectGPT: perturbation discrepancy (Mitchell et al., 2023). 2. Fast-DetectGPT: con-
294
      ditional probability curvature (Bao et al., 2023). 3.LRR: likelihood log-rank ratio (Su et al., 2023).
295   4. NPR: normalized perturbed log-rank (Su et al., 2023). 5. DNA-GPT: WScore (Yang et al., 2023).
296   6. Likelihood: mean log probabilities (Solaiman et al., 2019; Hashimoto et al., 2019; Gehrmann
297   et al., 2019). 7. LogRank: averaged log-rank in descending order by probabilities (Gehrmann et al.,
298   2019). 8. Entropy: mean token entropy of the predictive distribution (Gehrmann et al., 2019; So-
299   laiman et al., 2019; Ippolito et al., 2019). 9. RoBERTa-base: a pre-trained classifier (Liu et al.,
300   2019). 10. RoBERTa-large: a larger pre-trained classifier with more layers and parameters (Liu
301   et al., 2019). The first eight score functions calculate scores based on certain statistical properties of
302   texts, with each text’s score computed via a language model. The last two score functions compute
303
      scores by using some pre-trained classifiers. For the reader’s convenience, more details about the
      implementation of the score functions ϕ(·) are provided in Appendix B.

305   LLMs and Datasets. Our experiments focus on the black-box setting (Bao et al., 2023), which
306   means that if x is generated by a model qs , i.e., x ∼ qs , a different model pθ will then be used to
307   evaluate the metrics such as the log-probability log pθ (x) when calculating ϕ(x). The models qs
308   and pθ are respectively called the “source model” and “scoring model” for clarity. The black-box
309
      setting is a relevant scenario in practice because the source model used for generating the texts to
      be inferred is likely unknown in practice, which makes it elusive to use the same model to compute
310
      the scores. We construct a dataset that contains some real news and fake ones generated by LLMs
311
      for 2024 Olympics. Specifically, we collect 500 news about Paris 2024 Olympic Games from its
312   official website (Olympics, 2024) and then use three source models, Gemini-1.5-Flash, Gemini-

759   A.1   R ELATED WORKS OF DETECTING MACHINE - GENERATED TEXTS
760
761   Some methods distinguish between human-written and machine-generated texts by comparing their
762   statistical properties (Jawahar et al., 2020). Lavergne et al. (2008) introduced a method, which uses
763   relative entropy scoring to effectively identify texts from Markovian text generators. Perplexity
764
      is also a metric for detection, which quantifies the uncertainty of a model in predicting text se-
      quences (Hashimoto et al., 2019). Gehrmann et al. (2019) developed GLTR tool, which leverages
765
      statistical features such as per-word probability, rank, and entropy, to enhance the accuracy of fake-
766
      text detection. Mitchell et al. (2023) created a novel detector called DetectGPT, which identifies
767   a machine-generated text by noting that it will exhibit higher log-probability than samples where
768   some words of the original text have been rewritten/perturbed. Su et al. (2023) then introduced two
769   advanced methods utilizing two metrics: Log-Likelihood Log-Rank Ratio (LRR) and Normalized
770   Perturbed Log Rank (NPR), respectively. Bao et al. (2023) developed Fast-DetectGPT, which re-
771   places the perturbation step of DetectGPT with a more efficient sampling operation. Solaiman et al.
772   (2019) employed the classic logistic regression model on TF-IDF vectors to detect texts generated by
773   GPT-2, and noted that texts from larger GPT-2 models are more challenging to detect than those from
774   smaller GPT-2 models. Researchers have also trained supervised models on neural network bases.
775
      Bakhtin et al. (2019) found that Energy-based models (EBMs) outperform the behavior of using the
      original language model log-likelihood in real and fake text discrimination. Zellers et al. (2019)
776
      developed a robust detection method named GROVER by using a linear classifier, which can effec-
777
      tively spot AI-generated ‘neural’ fake news. Ippolito et al. (2019) showed that BERT-based (Devlin,
778   2018) classifiers outperform humans in identifying texts characterized by statistical anomalies, such
779   as those where only the top k high-likelihood words are generated, yet humans excel at semantic
780   understanding. Solaiman et al. (2019) fine-tuned RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019) on GPT-2 outputs and
781   achieved approximately 95% accuracy in detecting texts generated by 1.5 billion parameter GPT-
782   2. The effectiveness of RoBERTa-based detectors is further validated across different text types,
783   including machine-generated tweets (Fagni et al., 2021), news articles (Uchendu et al., 2020), and
784   product reviews (Adelani et al., 2020). Other supervised classifiers, such as GPTZero (Tian, 2023)
785   and OpenAI’s Classifier (OpenAI, 2023), have also proven to be strong detectors. Moreover, some
786
      research has explored watermarking methods that embed detectable patterns in LLM-generated texts
      for identifying, see e.g., Jalil & Mirza (2009); Kamaruddin et al. (2018); Abdelnabi & Fritz (2021);
787
      Zhao et al. (2023); Kirchenbauer et al. (2023); Christ et al. (2024). Recently, Kobak et al. (2024)
788
      introduced “excess word usage”, a novel data-driven approach that identifies LLM usage in aca-
789   demic writing and avoids biases that could be potentially introduced by generation prompts from
790   traditional human text datasets.
791

793   A.2   R ELATED W ORKS OF SEQUENTIAL HYPOTHESIS TESTING BY BETTING
794
795   Kelly (1956) first proposed a strategy for sequential betting with initial wealth on the outcome of
796   each coin flip gt in round t, which can take values of +1 (head) or −1 (tail), generated i.i.d. with the
797   probability of heads p ∈ [0, 1]. It is shown that betting a fraction βt = 2p − 1 on heads in each round
798   will yield more accumulated wealth than betting any other fixed fraction of the current wealth in
799   the long run. Orabona & Pál (2016) demonstrated the equivalence between the minimum wealth of
800   betting and the maximum regret in one-dimensional Online Linear Optimization (OLO) algorithms,
801
      which introduces the coin-betting abstraction for the design of parameter-free algorithms. Based on
      this foundation, Cutkosky & Orabona (2018) developed a coin betting algorithm, which uses an exp-
802
      concave optimization approach through the Online Newton Step (ONS). Subsequently, Shekhar &
803
      Ramdas (2023) applied their betting strategy, along with the general principles of testing by betting
804   as clarified by Shafer (2021), to nonparametric two-sample hypothesis testing. Chugg et al. (2023)
805   then conducted sequentially audits on both classifiers and regressors within the general two-sample
806   testing framework established by Shekhar & Ramdas (2023), which demonstrate that this method
807   remains robust even in the face of distribution shifts. Additionally, other studies (Orabona & Jun,
808   2023; Waudby-Smith & Ramdas, 2024) have developed practical strategies that leverage online
809   convex optimization methods, with which the betting fraction can be adaptively selected to provide
      statistical guarantees for the results.
