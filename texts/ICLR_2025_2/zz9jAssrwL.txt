009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            High-performance deep reinforcement learning faces tremendous challenges
012            when implemented on cost-effective low-end embedded systems due to its heavy
013            computational burden. To address this issue, we propose a policy distilla-
014            tion method called Bayesian Policy Distillation (BPD), which effectively re-
015            trains small-sized neural networks through an offline reinforcement learning ap-
016
               proach. BPD exploits Bayesian neural networks to distill already designed high-
               performance policy networks by adopting value optimizing, behavior cloning,
017
               and sparsity-inducing strategies. Simulation results reveal that the proposed BPD
018
               successfully compresses the policy networks, making them lighter and achieving
019            faster inference time. Furthermore, the proposed approach is demonstrated with a
020            real inverted pendulum system and reduced the inference time and memory size
021            by 78 % and 98 %, respectively.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Recently, deep reinforcement learning (DRL) has achieved super-human performances in complex
027   games (Lample & Chaplot, 2017; Schrittwieser et al., 2020) and provided feasible solutions to chal-
028
      lenging feedback control problems without the need for system modeling and parameter tuning
      (Hwangbo et al., 2017; Gu et al., 2017). However, such unprecedented innovations require high
029
      computational costs, large memory storage spaces, and long inference times, which limit their ap-
030
      plicability to real systems. The realization of DRL that meets realistically acceptable specifications
031   while maintaining the expected high performance is crucial. Research on compressing deep neural
032   networks for practical applications has been actively pursued, particularly outside DRL. Network
033   pruning is the most basic and classical approach and involves removing uninfluential weight pa-
034   rameters based on certain criteria with minimal performance loss (Reed, 1993; LeCun et al., 1989;
035   Lebedev & Lempitsky, 2016; Molchanov et al., 2017; Zhao et al., 2019). Network quantization is
036   another approach to achieving lightweight compressed neural networks by representing traditional
037   32- or 64-bit floating point values with lower bit precision (Achterhold et al., 2018; Gil et al., 2021).
038   In addition, knowledge distillation (Gou et al., 2021) is widely used to compress deep neural net-
039
      works. This method transfers knowledge from a large- to small-sized neural network, which has
      proven effective across various domains such as computer vision and natural language processing
040
      (Luo et al., 2017; Li et al., 2019; Deng et al., 2020).
041
042   The above methods have continued to evolve to handle large language models (Wang et al., 2020;
043   Ganesh et al., 2021) and embedded systems with limited computation capacity (Wofk et al., 2019;
044   Chen et al., 2020; Naik et al., 2021). Therefore, grafting the above network compression techniques
045
      onto DRL would be meaningful and timely. Unlike open-loop-based supervised and unsupervised
      learning, DRL operates in a closed-loop manner, making network compression challenging. Conse-
046
      quently, network compression techniques have been relatively underexplored in DRL despite their
047
      long-standing importance. A few attempts to compress the agent’s policy in DRL have been made
048   within an online reinforcement learning (RL) framework (Tan et al., 2023; Baek et al., 2023), which
049   inherently involves the agent interacting with its environment. Practically, without careful parame-
050   ter tuning, such a direct online approach may degrade performance or stability (Sokar et al., 2021)
051   because the agent’s policy is trained and compressed based on environmental interactions. To rem-
052   edy this issue, the Teacher–Student framework is commonly used in RL literature (Ross & Bagnell,
053   2010; Hinton et al., 2015; Rusu et al., 2015). This framework consists of the teacher and student
      policies, with the teacher policy having a larger neural network size. The teacher policy facili-

054
      tates knowledge transfer to the student policy through supervised learning by minimizing the mean
055   squared-error loss or the KullbackLeibler (KL) divergence between them (Rusu et al., 2015; Jang
056   et al., 2020). However, simply mimicking the “teacher policy” to train the “student policy” makes it
057   challenging to learn an effective policy beyond the teacher policy. In addition, most RL algorithms
058   using the Teacher–Student framework rely on online RL and inherently suffer from the aforemen-
059   tioned drawbacks. Thus, a naively and overly compressed student policy may lead to significantly
060   diminished performance compared to the teacher policy (Qu et al., 2022). This challenge under-
061   scores the need for leveraging an open-loop-based learning framework such as offline RL that aims
062   to learn an improved policy based on pre-collected data generated from the teacher policy.
063   To tackle these challenges, we propose an efficient offline policy compression algorithm, namely
064   Bayesian policy distillation (BPD). BPD integrates offline RL framework and Bayesian neural net-
065   work (Lampinen & Vehtari, 2001) to create an extremely sparse policy that is lightweight, fast,
066   and energy-efficient. This approach holds promise, particularly in scenarios where computational
067   performance is limited, such as on-board devices or a single cloud server needing to perform neu-
068   ral network computation for multiple client devices. Additionally, BPD is a fully offline learning
069
      method, particularly suitable for domains such as healthcare or robotics, where data collection is
      expensive and implementing online RL poses challenges.
070
071   We evaluated the proposed algorithm on the multi-joint dynamics with contact M U J O C O continuous
072   control benchmark (Todorov et al., 2012), widely used in RL. The results confirm that our proposed
073   algorithm can create an extremely sparse policy while preserving or improving the teacher policy
074   performance. Additionally, experiments on a real inverted pendulum demonstrated the practicality
075
      of our proposed algorithm and highlighted its potential for real-world applications.

187   2.4   O FFLINE R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
188
189
      In standard RL, the agent collects data through numerous interactions with the environment, which
      can be expensive in fields such as robotics and healthcare. This challenge brings attention to offline
190
      RL, which allows agents to learn policies without direct interaction with the environment, relying on
191
      the dataset collected by a behavioral policy in advance. Since the agent can train the policy without
192   engaging in potentially risky interactions, offline RL could be considerably safer and more cost-
193   effective for learning policy than online RL. However, despite its promising paradigm, challenges
194   still remain to overcome in the policy training domain. One main challenge is the undesirable
195   extrapolation error of out-of-distribution (OOD) actions (Fujimoto et al., 2019). The target policy
196   to train cannot explore state-action pairs that are not included in the static dataset collected by
197   the behavioral policy. Hence, the state-action visitation distribution of the target policy deviates
198   from that of the behavioral policies, leading to optimistic Q-values generating OOD actions. This
199   becomes problematic because if an agent is trained with these overly optimistic Q-values, it may
200
      select poor actions based on the overestimated ones.
201   Explicit policy constraint approaches have been often employed to address the OOD problem (Ku-
202   mar et al., 2019; Fujimoto & Gu, 2021; Fakoor et al., 2021). These approaches include introducing
203   regularizing terms to minimize the difference between the visitation distributions of the target and
204   behavioral policies. These regularization terms are approximated as follows:
205                                          E(s,a)∈D [(π(s) − a)2 ],                                     (7)
206   where π is the target policy and (s, a) is the state-action pair of the behavioral policy stored in the
207   pre-collected dataset D. Minimizing (7), the target policy is trained to inhibit the selection of poor
208   actions that the behavioral policy would not choose. The algorithm proposed in this study alleviates
209   the OOD issue by incorporating such a regularization term, which will be detailed in the next section.

      3.4   A DJUSTING W EIGHT C OEFFICIENTS
311
312   Here, we discuss how to determine λ and η in the loss function LBPD (12). Empirical experiments
313   show that the excessive impact of KL-regularization in the early stage of training causes instability.
314   Therefore, a coefficient η is multiplied by the KL term. More specifically, η is initially set to zero
315   and annealed linearly during training, thereby reducing its impact in the early stages and gradually
316   increasing its strength, contributing to the sparsity of the student policy through stable learning.
317
      In addition, a weight coefficient λ is employed to determine the weight to be given to the value-
318   optimizing role of LBPD . Here, λ is set to be proportional to |D|/Average(|Qψ1 (s, a)|), which is a
319   similar normalizing technique introduced in (Fujimoto & Gu, 2021). This adaptive law is crucial
320   as the Q-values and dataset size are highly task-dependent. If the dataset size is large, the impact
321   of the BC-term in (12) will be substantial. In contrast, if the dataset size is small, the BC-term
322   has a smaller weight coefficient according to the dataset size D and its effect is diminished. Thus,
323   the inclusion of the weight coefficient λ reflects the dataset size for more reliable Q-value updates,
      ensuring applying a unified objective LBPD across various tasks.

346   4   E XPERIMENTAL VALIDATION
347
348   We validate the proposed BPD through experiments with MuJoCo tasks from OpenAI Gym (Brock-
349   man et al., 2016), namely HalfCheetah-v3, Walker2d-v3, Hopper-v3, Ant-v3 (Fig. 2). Two teacher
350   policies were pretrained for each task: the expert-level policy with a high performance and medium-
351   level policy with moderate performance. This setting is to show that a medium-level policy can be
352   improved while being compressed.
353   The goal is to teach the robots to move forward quickly without falling. Hence, the moving speed at
354   each step is reflected in the immediate reward. The experiments illustrate how effectively BPD can
355   compress the student’s policy network while preserving or enhancing the teacher’s performance. To
356   quantify the network compression ability, we define a measure called sparsity as follows:
357
                                                                 |ωs ̸= 0|
358                                       Sparsity (%) = 100 ·             ,
359                                                                |ωt |
360   where |ωs ̸= 0| is the number of non-zero weights in the student policy and |ωt | is the total number
361   of weights in the teacher policy. The teacher policy was trained with the soft-actor critic framework
362   (Haarnoja et al., 2018) with two hidden layers consisting of 400 units and 300 units, denoted as
363   (400, 300), which is one of the widely used common sizes in RL for M U J O C O tasks (Fujimoto
364   et al., 2018; Haarnoja et al., 2018). Under these conditions, we could train expert and general-level
365   teacher policies, from which two corresponding static datasets were constructed with one million
366   transition tuples.
367   The proposed BPD was compared with well-known network compression methods: deep com-
368   pression (DC) (Han et al., 2016), sparse variational dropout (SVD) (Molchanov et al., 2017), and
369   TD3+BC, a widely used offline RL algorithm (Fujimoto & Gu, 2021). Originally, the DC method
370   employed pruning, quantization, and Hoffman coding for network compression; however, for a fair
371   comparison, only the pruning step was performed, ignoring the performance degradation caused by
372   quantization and Hoffman coding.

375   zero-weights to the maximum. In the case of TD3+BC, the hidden layer size of the student policies
376   was chosen to be (32, 32) for all tasks except for HalfCheetah-v3 (40, 40). The size setting results in
377   a compression level comparable to that of other baselines, making it easy to compare performance
      changes depending on compression levels.

475   4.3   A BLATION S TUDY
476
477   BPD filters out uninfluential weights based on a prescribed CThreshold value. Therefore, we explored
478   how variations in the CThreshold ∈ {0.5, 1, 2, 3, 4} affect the performance of the agent and the sparsity

481   a proportional improvement in performance, as shown in Fig. 5. This implies that once a certain

      network connectivity level is established, additional connections will not necessarily contribute to
      performance improvement.

      4.4   R EAL E XPERIMENT
500
501   For real-system validation, BPD was applied to an inverted pendulum system, where the goal is to
502   swing and balance the pendulum (see Fig. 4). The state variable is constructed by stacking five
503   consecutive observations, which includes position, velocity, sin θ, cos θ, and θ̇, where θ is the angle
504   between cart and pole (Fig. 6 in App. B). Thus, the total dimension of the state space becomes
505   25 (5×5 observation). The action generated by the policy determines the reference velocity for the
506   motor actuator. The teacher policy was pretrained using the real inverted pendulum system and
507   DDPG algorithm (Lillicrap et al., 2016) with a hidden layer size of (256, 256). Then, we collected
508   300,000 transition data from the teacher, which took approximately 2.5 h. The teacher policy was
509   then distilled through the proposed BPD to obtain a lighter and faster student policy. Sparse hidden
510
      layers in the student policy enabled efficient sparse matrix-vector operations, reducing inference
      time by representing the matrices in compressed sparse row format.
511
512   The teacher and distilled student policies are compared in terms of performance score, inference
513   time, number of parameters, and memory storage size. The inference time was measured on an
514   ARM V 8-based processor, utilizing the SCIPY library that is one of P YTHON’s computing algo-
515   rithm libraries. The inference time was averaged over a total of 10,000 runs. The overall results are
516
      listed in Table 2. The inference time of the distilled student policy is 4.5 times faster than that of the
      teacher policy. Such a difference in the inference speed could be dramatically bigger on cheaper and
517
      highly resource-constrained devices that do not support Single Instruction Multiple Data (SIMD) or
518
      parallel programming. The required memory size was measured based on the number of non-zero
519   weight parameters. The distilled policy uses only approximately 1.5% (4.43 / 290.82) of memory
520   storage compared with the teacher policy. However, despite the significant memory savings, no
521   notable performance degradation is observed but rather a slight performance improvement.

      5     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
525
      Deep reinforcement learning is becoming increasingly important in industries such as robotics,
526
      where practical applications require models to run on affordable, energy-efficient devices with lim-
527
      ited computational resources. To meet this need, we introduce an efficient offline policy compression
528   method called Bayesian Policy Distillation (BPD), which retrains a compact student policy network
529   from a larger teacher network in an offline reinforcement learning setting. Our results demonstrate
530   that BPD successfully reduces the size of the teacher policy network by 1%-2%, while achieving
531   minimal performance loss. Notably, in some environments, we were able to achieve even higher
532   performance than the teacher. Moreover, in a real inverted pendulum system, we confirmed that
533   BPD can dramatically increase inference speed on devices with limited computational resources.
534   A drawback of BPD is its lack of real-time adaptability due to its offline nature. However, it is highly
535   effective in situations where ongoing system interaction is too risky or costly. Additionally, when
536   no pre-existing dataset is available, a teacher policy must first be trained, and expert data collected,
537   which may lead to some sample inefficiency. That said, when a dataset exists, BPD allows for
538   reuse, making it a cost-effective solution. Overall, we believe BPD offers a promising approach for
539   advancing deep reinforcement learning across various industries, given the benefits of offline RL, its
      impressive compression performance, and significant improvements in inference speed.
