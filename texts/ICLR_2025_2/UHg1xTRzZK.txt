011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved impressive results across numer-
014            ous NLP tasks but still encounter difficulties in machine translation. Traditional
015
               methods to improve translation have typically involved fine-tuning LLMs using
               parallel corpora. However, vanilla fine-tuning often leads to catastrophic forget-
016
               ting of the instruction-following capabilities and alignment with human prefer-
017
               ences, compromising their broad general abilities and introducing potential se-
018            curity risks. These abilities, which are developed using proprietary and unavail-
019            able training data, make existing continual instruction tuning methods ineffective.
020            To overcome this issue, we propose a novel approach called RaDis (Rationale
021            Distillation). RaDis harnesses the strong generative capabilities of LLMs to create
022            rationales for training data, which are then “replayed” to prevent forgetting. These
023            rationales connect prior knowledge with new tasks, acting as self-distillation tar-
024            gets to regulate the training process. By jointly training on reference translations
025            and self-generated rationales, the model can learn new translation skills while pre-
026
               serving its general abilities. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method
               enhances machine translation performance while maintaining the broader capabil-
027
               ities of LLMs across other tasks. This work presents a pathway for creating more
028
               versatile LLMs that excel in specialized tasks without compromising generality or
029            safety and provides a fresh angle for utilizing rationales in the CL field.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated exceptional performance across diverse Natural
035   Language Processing (NLP) tasks. However, in the realm of Machine Translation (MT), they still
036   fall short compared to conventional supervised encoder-decoder models (Xu et al., 2024a). Recent
037   studies have sought to enhance the translation performance of LLMs through continual instruction-
038
      tuning with parallel corpora (Yang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024a). While this approach effectively
      boosts translation performance, it often comes at the cost of the inherent general ability and safety
039
      alignment of LLMs. As illustrated in Figure 1, fine-tuning LLaMA-2-Chat and Mistral-v0.2-Instruct
040
      results in a significant decline in these models’ performance on MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023). This
041   phenomenon is known as Catastrophic Forgetting (CF) (French, 1993), which remains the major
042   obstacle to developing models that seamlessly integrate strong translation performance with broader
043   general-purpose utility.
044
      Various Continual Learning (CL) approaches have been proposed to mitigate CF (Chen & Liu,
045
      2018). In the context of LLMs, replay-based methods (Scialom et al., 2022; Yin et al., 2022; Mok
046
      et al., 2023; He et al., 2024), which store small subsets of previous data for rehearsal, are often
047   favored for their simplicity and effectiveness. However, a critical limitation of replay-based methods
048   is their reliance on access to the original training data, which is frequently unavailable in real-
049   world applications. This limitation greatly reduces their feasibility in scenarios like the one in this
050   paper, where the goal is to boost translation skills while preserving the general abilities of open-
051   sourced LLMs, which are gained from proprietary, in-house data. Some studies have incorporated
052   open-source general instruction-following data as a substitute (Jiao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023).
053   However, the limited quality of these open-source datasets results in performance that significantly
      falls short of instruction-tuned LLMs.

104   2.1     F INE - TUNING LLM S FOR MT
105
106   Previous studies have primarily fine-tuned LLMs using parallel corpora to enhance their translation
107   proficiency. BigTrans (Yang et al., 2023) and ALMA (Xu et al., 2024a) propose to first continual
      pre-train on monolingual data and then progresses to fine-tuning on parallel data. Although these


                                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      methods enhance the translation proficiency of LLMs, they often compromise the models’ gen-
109   eral ability, turning them into specialized translation models. To address this issue, several studies
110   have sought to preserve the general ability of LLMs while fine-tuning them for MT. For example,
111   ParroT (Jiao et al., 2023), BayLing (Zhang et al., 2023) and TowerInstruct (Alves et al., 2024) in-
112   corporates general instruction-following data to maintain general capability. However, limited by
113   the quality of the data, their performance in both translation and general abilities remains relatively
114   low. In contrast to these efforts, our approach solely utilizes machine translation data and preserves
115   the general ability by distillation of self-generated rationales.
116
117   2.2   C ONTINUAL I NSTRUCTION T UNING
118
119   Continual instruction tuning (CIT) seeks to mitigate CF during the instruction tuning of LLMs by
120   employing CL approaches (Wu et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024). Traditional CL methods are typically
121   divided into replay-based, regularization-based, and architecture-based methods. However, in the
122   context of LLMs, the vast parameter and task space reduces the feasibility of regularization-based
123
      and architecture-based methods (Wang et al., 2024). As a result, current research has predominantly
      relied on focused on replay-based techniques and their variants (Scialom et al., 2022; Yin et al.,
124
      2022; Mok et al., 2023; He et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024) While these approaches are promising,
125
      they are subject to the reliance on access to the original training data. Consequently, they cannot
126   be applied to mitigate the forgetting of instruction-tuned LLMs’ general abilities gained from in-
127   house training data. SDFT (Yang et al., 2024) is the first work designed for preserving the general
128   instruction-following abilities of LLMs. It proposes to paraphrase the original train dataset with the
129   LLM itself to bridge the distribution gap. However, the quality of the paraphrased data is limited by
130   the capabilities of the prompt and the model itself, which may diminish the performance of the task
131   to be learned. In contrast, RaDis argues the original data with self-generated rationales and avoids
132   loss of performance on new tasks.
133
134   2.3   D ISTILLING R ATIONALES
135
136   Since the advent of LLMs, researchers have recognized their ability to generate rationales and have
137   sought to distill knowledge from them. Initial studies have predominately focused on distilling the
138   Chain of Thought (CoT) reasoning capabilities from intermediate rationales (Wang et al., 2023;
139
      Hsieh et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023). These studies emphasize pre-rationalization, where the model
      first generates a rationale before predicting the answer based on that rationale. Here, the rationale re-
140
      flects the reasoning path leading to the final answer, providing valuable insights for student models.
141
      Recent research has proposed post-rationalization, where the rationale is generated after the answer
142   is predicted. In this context, the rationale serves as an explanation, supplementing the ground truth
143   label. Wadhwa et al. (2024) demonstrate that CoT-augmented distillation is more effective when ra-
144   tionales are provided after labels. Additionally, Chen et al. (2024) suggests that post-rationalization
145   mitigates rationale sensitivity issues and enhances focus on learning challenging samples. Ratio-
146   naleCL (Xiong et al., 2023) introduce rationales generated by GPT-3.5-turbo to distill relation ex-
147   traction knowledge into T5 model in a continual learning setting. Unlike previous works that distill
148   knowledge from LLMs to smaller student models, RaDis focuses on self-distillation to maintain
149   general abilities and prevent forgetting.

      3     M ETHOD
152
153
      We begin by presenting a key observation: when tasked with translation requests, instruction-
154
      tuned LLMs can generate detailed rationales that encapsulate the internal general knowledge lever-
155
      aged during translation (Section 3.1). Building on this insight, we introduce Rationale Distillation
156   (RaDis), which leverages these self-generated rationales as replay data to help the model retain its
157   broad general capabilities (Section 3.2). Finally, we demonstrate that the RaDis training objective
158   can be decomposed into a conventional MT loss and a self-distillation loss on rationale tokens, which
159   helps prevent excessive deviation of model parameters (Section 3.3).

      The forgetting issue can be attributed to an unsuitable training approach. In conventional fine-tuning,
203
      the supervision signal comes from the reference sentence solely, which contains knowledge specific
204
      to the translation task. Thus, the model parameter is biased to a translation-specific distribution.
205   Previous studies have sought to address this issue by replay-based CL methods. However, due to the
206   absence of original training data and the poor quality of open-sourced instruction-following data,
207   their effectiveness is limited. To this end, we propose RaDis.
208
      The core idea of RaDis is similar to pseudo-replay. In traditional CL, pseudo-replay methods employ
209
      an additional data generator to synthesize replay data (Shi et al., 2024). However, the superior
210
      generative abilities of LLMs now allow us to leverage the model itself to synthesize this replay data.
211   As depicted in Figure 3, RaDis starts from an instruction-tuned LLM as the backbone. It utilizes a
212   prompt template I to format the translation sentence pair (x, y) and sends them into the backbone
213   LLM parameterized as θ. As shown in Section 3.1, an instruction-tuned model has the inherent
214   ability to continue generating a rationale using the translation instruction-response pair as the prefix.

      Translation. For parallel training data, we adopt the human written data collected by ALMA (Xu

289   4.2   BASELINES
290
291   Our method is compared against two baseline categories. The first category includes representa-
292   tive continual instruction tuning (CIT) methods, that are compatible with our setting. In the second
293   category, we consider prior LLM-based MT models, which focus on enhancing the translation pro-
294
      ficiency of LLMs. It’s worth noting that the comparison with prior LLM-based MT models is not
      entirely fair due to discrepancies in training data and model architectures.
295
296
      Continual Instruction Tuning (CIT) Baselines. The following continual instruction tuning ap-
297
      proaches are introduced as baseline methods: (1) Vanilla Fine-tuning, where the backbone LLMs
298   are directly fine-tuned using translation data; (2) Seq-KD, which employs sequence-level knowl-
299   edge distillation along with fine-tuning to alleviate forgetting; (3) SDFT (Yang et al., 2024), which
300   leverages the backbone LLM to paraphrase the original training data and fine-tunes the model using
301   the synthesized data; (4) Multi-task, which employs open-sourced instruction following dataset and
302   fine-tunes the LLM with both translation and instruction following data.
303
304   Prior LLM-based MT models. This category contains several notable works in the field of LLM-
305   based machine translation. (1) ParroT (Jiao et al., 2023), which fine-tune LLaMA-1 with a hybrid
306   of translation and instruction-following data. (2) BigTrans (Yang et al., 2023) enhances LLaMA-
307
      1 by equipping it with multilingual translation capabilities across more than 100 languages. (3)
      BayLing (Zhang et al., 2023) fine-tunes LLaMA-1 using automatically generated interactive trans-
308
      lation instructions. (4) ALMA (Xu et al., 2024a) first fine-tunes LLaMA-2 on monolingual data and
309
      subsequently uses high-quality parallel data for instruction tuning.(5) TowerInstruct (Alves et al.,
310   2024) continued pre-trains LLaMA-2 on a multilingual mixture of monolingual and parallel data,
311   and fine-tuned with translation and instruction following data.

      5.3    R A D IS AVOIDS THE C ONFLICT B ETWEEN L EARNING AND M ITIGATING CF
478
479
      As demonstrated in Section 3.3, our proposed RaDis can be viewed as a specialized form of
480   sequence-level distillation, where the rationale r serves as the distillation target. However, while
481   both methods excel at preserving general capabilities, RaDis notably enhances translation profi-
482   ciency, whereas Seq-KD does not. We posit that the difference arises from whether the regu-
483   larization term conflicts with the MT learning process. In Seq-KD (Equation 5), the MT loss
484   − log P (y|x, I; θ) and the regularization term − log P (y′ |x, I; θ) share the same input but have
485   different outputs, which may lead to conflict in optimization. In contrast, with RaDis (Equation 4),
      the MT loss and the regularization term − log P (r|y, x, I; θ) are less likely to exhibit this issue.

508     6                         C ONCLUSIONS
509
510    In this paper, we conducted a systematic evaluation of prior LLM-based MT models, finding that
511    they lack diverse general capabilities, degenerating into task-specific translation models. This degen-
512    eration is mainly caused by catastrophic forgetting while fine-tuning for translation tasks. Previous
513
       continual learning approaches can not preserve the general capabilities gained from in-house train-
       ing data. To address this issue, we propose a simple yet effective strategy, RaDis. RaDis prompts
514
       LLMs to generate rationales for the reference translation and utilizes these rationales to mitigate for-
515
       getting in a self-distillation manner. Mirroring the human learning process, these rationales connect
516    prior knowledge with new tasks, building a reasoning framework that ties internal concepts together
517    and enhances knowledge retention. Extensive experiments show that RaDis greatly enhances the
518    translation performance while preserving the models’ general ability. These insights can help future
519    research on building LLMs capable of excelling in specialized tasks without compromising their
520    generality or safety and providing a fresh angle for utilizing rationales in the CL field.
521
522
        7                         L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
523
524
       Our study is subject to certain limitations. Owing to constraints in computational resources, we
525    adopt LoRA on models with 7B parameters. Further investigations involving larger models and
526    full fine-tuning remain to be explored. Besides, as a post-training method, RaDis is limited by
527    the language proficiency of backbone LLM. This limits its performance on low-resource language.
528    However, we believe the rapidly evolving multilingual LLMs would narrow this gap. Furthermore,
529    we predominately focus on fine-tuning with machine translation data, applying RaDis to other NLP
530    tasks will further support its effectiveness (See Appendix C). This potential direction is what we
531    intend to explore in future work.

      A     BASELINE DETAILS
811
812
813
      A.1   C ONTINUAL I NSTRUCTION T UNING BASELINES
814
      Vanilla Fine-tuning. This method directly fine-tunes the backbone LLMs with translation data,
815
      without incorporating any mechanism to address the forgetting issue.
816
817
818   Sequence-level Knowledge Distillation (Seq-KD). This method first sends the formatted trans-
819   lation instructions to the backbone LLM and generates outputs y′ . The model is trained with both
820   golden references y and the self-generated outputs y′ . The overall training objective is:
821
822                            LSeq-KD =L(x, y; θ) + L(x, y′ ; θ)
                                                                                                         (5)
823                                    = − log P (y|x, I; θ) − log P (y′ |x, I; θ)
824
825
      Self-distillation Fine-tuning (SDFT). (Yang et al., 2024) This method first prompts the backbone
826   LLM to paraphrase the original responses present in the task dataset, yielding a distilled dataset.
827   Subsequently, the distilled dataset, which is used in subsequent fine-tuning, helps narrow the dis-
828   tribution gap between LLM and the original dataset. We adopt the general distillation template
829   provided in their paper to paraphrase the dataset.
830
831
832
833   Multi-task fine-tuning (Multi-task) . This method employs open-sourced instruction following
834   datasets and fine-tunes the LLM with both translation and instruction following data. Specifically,
835   we adopt Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023) and Dolly (Conover et al., 2023) as the chosen instruction
836   following dataset. Note that multi-task fine-tuning utilizes more data in the training process and is
837
      usually considered the upper bound of the continual learning approaches.
838
839   A.2   P RIOR LLM- BASED MT M ODELS
840
841   ParroT (Jiao et al., 2023) reformulates translation data into the instruction-following style and
842   introduces a ”Hint” field for incorporating extra requirements to regulate the translation process. It
843   is also fine-tuned on the Alpaca dataset to enhance general ability.
844
845   BigTrans (Yang et al., 2023) continual pre-train LLaMA-1-13B with Chinese monolingual data
846   and an extensive parallel dataset encompassing 102 natural languages. They then apply multilingual
847   translation instructions for fine-tuning.
848
849
850
      BayLing (Zhang et al., 2023) builds an interactive translation dataset and fine-tune LLaMA-1
      models with both interactive translation and instruction-following datasets (Alpaca).
851
852
853   ALMA (Xu et al., 2024a) proposes a new training recipe for building LLM-based MT models,
854   which begins with initial fine-tuning on monolingual data and then progresses to fine-tuning on a
855   select set of high-quality parallel data.
856
857
858
859   TowerInstruct (Alves et al., 2024) propose a recipe for tailoring LLMs to multiple tasks present
860
      in translation workflows. They perform continued pre-training on a multilingual mixture of mono-
      lingual and parallel data, followed by fine-tuning instructions relevant to translation processes and
861
      general tasks.
862
863   In our experiments, we utilized the 7B models as baselines for ParroT, BayLing, ALMA and Tow-
      erInstruct to ensure a fair comparison of model size.

891   B.2    H YPERPARAMETER
892
      Due to the limitation of resources, our experiments utilize the Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) tech-
893
      nique (Hu et al., 2022). Specifically, we integrate a LoRA adapter with a rank of 16 into all the
894   linear layers of the LLMs and exclusively train the adapter. The LLMs are fine-tuned over three
895   epochs on the translation dataset, which equates to approximately 2,500 steps. We use a learning
896   rate of 1×10−4 and a batch size of 128 to ensure stable training across most experiments. An excep-
897   tion is Seq-KD, which requires a batch size of 256 to maintain the same number of training steps.
898   All experiments are performed on 4 NVIDIA A100 80GB GPUs. For data synthesis, we employ
899   vllm (Kwon et al., 2023) to facilitate fast data generation. For evaluation, we primarily use greedy
900   decoding to ensure reproducibility, except where specific generation configurations are mandated by
901   certain benchmark tools.

961   higher Pass@1 on HumanEval and excelling in other benchmarks for general abilities.
962   A key reason for this is that RaDis directly preserves the original references in the dataset, whereas
963   SDFT paraphrases them. Intuitively, while paraphrasing helps bridge the distribution gap, it also
964   reduces the amount of learnable knowledge. As a result, SDFT may struggle to outperform Vanilla-
965   FT on certain tasks. In contrast, RaDis directly utilizes the original references, preserving all the
966   knowledge embedded in the data.
967   Regarding performance on general tasks, RaDis still outperforms SDFT. We believe this can be
968   attributed to the distribution gap. While SDFT claims to distill the dataset, it actually paraphrases
969   the data. As a result, the model’s responses are sampled from the paraphrased instruction’s output
970   distribution, which tends to be out-of-distribution relative to the original task instruction. In contrast,
971   RaDis performs self-distillation using rationales, which are fully in-distribution. This enables RaDis
      to more effectively alleviate forgetting and better preserve general abilities.
