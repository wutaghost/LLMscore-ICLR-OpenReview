                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Stein variational gradient descent (SVGD) is a particle based approximate infer-
011
               ence algorithm. Many variants of SVGD have been proposed in recent years,
               including the hybrid kernel variant (h-SVGD), which has demonstrated promising
012
               results on image classification with deep neural network ensembles. In this paper,
013
               we demonstrate the ability of h-SVGD to alleviate variance collapse, a problem
014            that SVGD is known to suffer from. Unlike other SVGD variants that alleviate
015            variance collapse, h-SVGD does not incur additional computational cost, nor does
016            it require the target density to factorise. We also develop the theory of h-SVGD
017            by demonstrating the existence of a solution to the hybrid Stein partial differential
018            equation. We highlight a special case in which h-SVGD is a kernelised Wasser-
019            stein gradient flow on a functional other than the Kullback-Leibler divergence,
020            which is the functional describing the SVGD gradient flow. By characterising the
021            fixed point in this special case, we show that h-SVGD does not converge to the
022
               target distribution in the the mean field limit. Other theoretical results include a
               descent lemma and a large particle limit result. Despite the bias in the mean field
023
               limiting distribution, experiments demonstrate that h-SVGD remains competitive
024
               on high dimensional inference tasks whilst alleviating variance collapse.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Stein variational gradient descent (SVGD) is a variational inference algorithm that generates samples
031   from a target probability density (Liu & Wang, 2016). It has proven useful in many tasks in Bayesian
032   inference and machine learning. SVGD evolves an interacting particle system until the particles
033
      resemble a sample from a target density. The dynamics of this system include a driving term that
      moves particles to regions of high probability, and a repulsive term that repels particles from one
034
      another. This repulsive term prevents particles from converging to the same mode. It has been
035
      shown that within a unit ball of a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), the SVGD update
036   direction optimally reduces the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the target density and
037   the approximating density (Liu & Wang, 2016). The reproducing kernel of this RKHS appears in
038   both the driving and repulsive terms, making the choice of kernel a key ingredient for SVGD.
039
      SVGD is known to suffer from the curse of dimensionality through variance collapse (Wang et al.,
040
      2018; Ba et al., 2019) whereby the marginal variances of the particles underestimate the true
041
      marginal variances of the target in high dimensions. Capturing the variance of the posterior is
042   critical for uncertainty quantification in Bayesian statistics since variance is often used as a measure
043   of confidence in an estimate. Zhuo et al. (2018) explained that this phenomenon is due to the size of
044   the repulsive term of the update direction scaling inversely with dimension. This enables the driving
045   term to dominate in high dimensions, thereby forcing particles to converge to the mode(s) of the
046   target. This insight suggests that strengthening the repulsive term in SVGD should lead to better
047   variance estimation, an idea which we explore in Sections 3 and 4.
048   The theoretical properties of vanilla SVGD have been studied extensively. Liu (2017) showed that
049   the empirical measure of the particles converges weakly to the target distribution. SVGD in the mean
050   field regime has been described as a gradient flow on the KL divergence (Liu & Wang, 2016) and
051   the chi-squared divergence (Chewi et al., 2020). Furthering this geometric point of view, Duncan
052   et al. (2023) developed the Stein geometry along with its associated tangent spaces and geodesics,
053   leading to guidelines for choosing kernels to improve convergence. The existence and uniqueness
      of the solution to the Stein partial differential equation (PDE) was established by Lu et al. (2019).

054
      Various descent lemmas bounding the decrease in KL divergence at each iteration have also been
055   established (Liu, 2017; Korba et al., 2020; Salim et al., 2022).
056
057   Many variants of SVGD have also been proposed in recent years, some offering improvements
058
      and others providing generalisations. Riemannian SVGD (Liu & Zhu, 2018) generalises SVGD by
      allowing for target densities on Riemannian manifolds, not just Euclidean spaces. Matrix SVGD
059
      (Wang et al., 2019) replaces the scalar valued kernel with a matrix valued kernel to incorporate
060
      preconditioning information and speed up particle exploration. Message passing SVGD (Zhuo et al.,
061   2018) and graphical SVGD (Wang et al., 2018) focus on target densities that factorise according to
062   a graph structure. This approach reduces variance collapse in high dimensions by converting the
063   problem to a collection of low dimensional problems. Projected SVGD (Chen & Ghattas, 2020),
064   sliced SVGD (Gong et al., 2021) and Grassman SVGD (Liu et al., 2022) also mitigate the issue
065   of variance collapse by updating particles within lower dimensional subspaces, which comes at the
066   expense of additional computation.
067   In this work, we study a variant called hybrid kernel Stein variational gradient descent (h-SVGD).
068   The name comes from its use of two distinct kernels for the driving and repulsive terms with the
069   aim of mitigating variance collapse. This variant was originally proposed by D’Angelo et al. (2021)
070   in the context of training deep neural network ensembles by sampling from the distribution of net-
071   work parameters. In that setting, two particles may parameterise networks with very similar outputs
072   despite being far apart in the weight space. Their insight was to encourage functional diversity be-
073   tween networks in the ensemble by using a standard kernel in the driving term, but a functional
074   kernel in the repulsive term. In this neural network ensemble setting, h-SVGD demonstrated better
075
      performance than other variants on image classification. Annealed SVGD (D’Angelo & Fortuin,
      2021) may also be considered an example of h-SVGD. In this variant, the driving kernel is a scalar
076
      multiple γ(ℓ) ∈ [0, 1] of the repulsive kernel, and this factor γ(ℓ) gradually increases to 1 as the
077
      iteration ℓ increases. Numerical experiments show that annealed SVGD improves the ability of par-
078   ticles to escape local modes. Scaling one of the update terms has also been used as a computational
079   technique to aid other SVGD variants when training Bayesian neural networks (Gong et al., 2021).
080
081
      Although preliminary numerical experiments have shown the benefits of h-SVGD (D’Angelo et al.,
      2021), the theoretical results for SVGD do not directly apply in the hybrid kernel setting due to the
082
      presence of a second kernel. In this paper, we address this theoretical gap and reinforce the practical
083
      benefits of h-SVGD through the following contributions.
084
085          • We establish existence of a solution to the hybrid Stein PDE and a kernelised Wasserstein
086            gradient flow interpretation. Through the study of this gradient flow, we demonstrate that
087            h-SVGD does not converge to the target distribution in the mean field limit.
088          • Other theoretical contributions include quantifying the rate of dissipation of the gradient
089            flow functional, along with a discrete time version, otherwise known as a descent lemma.
090          • Despite not converging to the target distribution, numerical experiments show that h-SVGD
091            can mitigate variance collapse in the finite particle regime at negligible additional cost,
092            whilst remaining competitive at high dimensional inference tasks.
093
094
      In Section 2 we clarify notation, recall necessary theory, and outline the vanilla and hybrid SVGD
      algorithms. Section 3 contains the theoretical contributions, with proofs relegated to Appendix A.
095
      Numerical experiments are in Section 4 with additional experiments and details in Appendix B.

527   5   C ONCLUSION
528
529   We developed the mean field theory of h-SVGD by proving the existence of a solution to the hybrid
530   Stein PDE and identifying it as a gradient flow on a functional other than the KL divergence. We
531
      characterised the mean field fixed point for the special case k2 = ck1 and demonstrated that h-SVGD
      does not converge to the target in the mean field limit. We provided a result on the dissipation of the
532
      new functional, as well as a discrete time version, otherwise known as a descent lemma. We also
533
      highlighted the complexities of the gradient flow in the general hybrid kernel setting. Experimental
534   results demonstrated that h-SVGD can alleviate variance collapse in high dimensions at a much
535   lower cost than other SVGD variants. We also showed that h-SVGD maintains its performance
536   on high dimensional inference tasks, whilst improving variance estimation without the additional
537   computational cost required of other SVGD variants. One interesting direction for future research
538   is to find a principled method of scaling the repulsive kernel, using the theoretical analysis in (Zhuo
539   et al., 2018) as a starting point. Another avenue is to further develop the theory of h-SVGD in the
      general hybrid kernel setting.
