009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Data augmentation is an approach to increasing the training dataset size for deep
012            learning using synthetic data. Recent advancements in image generative models
013            have unleashed the potential of synthesizing high-quality images in data aug-
014            mentation. However, real-life datasets commonly follow an imbalanced class
015            distribution, where some classes have fewer samples than others. Image generation
016
               models may, therefore, struggle to synthesize diverse images for less common
               classes that lack richness and diversity. To address this, we introduce an auto-
017
               mated generative data augmentation method, AutoGenDA, to extract and transfer
018
               label-invariant changes across data classes through image captions and text-guided
019            generative models. We also propose an automated search strategy to optimize the
020            data augmentation process for each data class, leading to better generalization.
021            Our experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of AutoGenDA in various object
022            classification datasets. We improve the standard data augmentation baselines by
023            up to 4.9% on Pascal VOC, Caltech101, MS-COCO, and LVIS under multiple
024            imbalanced classification settings.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Conventional data augmentation methods involve applying pre-defined label-preserving image opera-
029
      tions, such as horizontal flipping, random cropping, and rotation, to create multiple versions of the
030   same image (Shorten & Khoshgoftaar, 2019). By providing ample examples of various variations,
031   deep neural networks can learn to be resilient to nuisance transformations, such as color and geometric
032   transformations that do not alter the class identity. This allows them to identify the objects despite
033   these variations.
034
      Modern deep image generative models can synthesize high-quality images given appropriate prompts.
035
      This capability allows the generation of a large number of realistic images, which could serve as
036   artificial data to supplement image classification datasets without the need for costly data collection
037   and labeling (Antoniou et al., 2017; Ramesh et al., 2021; Biswas et al., 2023; He et al., 2023; Trabucco
038   et al., 2023). In data augmentation, generative models learn the underlying data distribution of each
039   object class and generate multiple versions of the same object with slight differences (Antoniou et al.,
040   2017; Trabucco et al., 2023). Compared to conventional data augmentation, generative approaches
041   can capture more abstract and complex nuisance transformations, like pose, texture, and background
042   changes, thus enabling greater diversity in the training data.
043   In practice, many real-life datasets follow an imbalanced class distribution, where some classes have
044   a large number of samples while the remaining classes have very few. In cases of such imbalance,
045   generative models may struggle to learn diverse variations for the underrepresented classes. For
046   instance, in a “cat vs dog” classification task, if the “dog” category has only a few samples of a dog
047   running on a grass field, a generative model may learn this specific data distribution and synthesize
048   new images of dogs running with slight variations on a grass field. However, the classifier trained on
049   this synthesized data may fail to generalize to other images of dogs in different scenes, like inside
050
      a house (Beery et al., 2018). To this end, we propose a novel generative approach that takes into
      consideration the nuisance variations in the dataset.
051
052   Our major assumption is that certain variances found in a target class are class-specific, while others
053   are class-agnostic. For example, the species of cats in a cat image is a class-specific variance unique
      to the class category, while the background of the image is a class-agnostic variance. We believe

079   learning and transferring class-agnostic variances across similar classes in data augmentation can
080   create a wider variety of images that are more effective for training classifiers under imbalanced
081   class distributions. For example, a majority “cat” class contains images of cats in a house or on a
082   grass field; a minority “dog” class contains only images of dogs on a grass field. By learning and
083   transferring the scenery variances from the “cat” class to the “dog” class, a classifier can better handle
084   unseen variations in less common categories. Our motivation is also supported by the finding from
085   Zhou et al. (2022) that neural networks are not good at learning label-invariant transformations on the
086   tail classes in long-tailed datasets.
087   Adopting generative data augmentation in imbalanced datasets poses at least two challenges. First,
088   typical image generation models rely on large amounts of training data to learn good image represen-
089   tation. Extracting the variance properties from limited data and effectively transferring them across
090   classes is not trivial. Second, in imbalanced datasets, each class may demand different numbers of
091   augmented data and levels of diversity. In previous work, the decision on the amount or proportion of
092   synthetic data to be included in the training dataset was mostly made manually, and the generated
093
      data were not guaranteed to be useful for the classification task. Ensuring the generated images are
      effective in our setting and automatically adapting the synthesized images to each class and dataset is
094
      a novel problem to solve.
095
096   Figure 1 shows an overview of AutoGenDA. At a high level, our method captures the class-specific
097   and class-agnostic variances through image captions and integrates these differences into text-guided
098   generation processes by using the captions as text prompts. We further propose a novel automated
099
      search framework to determine the optimal mixture of real and synthesized images to include for each
      class when fine-tuning a classifier on a dataset. To summarize, we make three major contributions:
100
101
102          • We propose a novel generative data augmentation method that learns and transfers class-
103
               specific and class-agnostic variances using image captions.
104          • We propose a novel automated search framework to optimize and adapt the generation
105            process for each class and dataset.
106
107          • We demonstrate that our method outperforms other baselines on four object classification
               datasets under multiple imbalanced and low-shot settings.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
109
110   Generative Data Augmentation. The utilization of artificial data in data augmentation has gained
111   traction due to advancements in image generation models. Early attempts leverage class-conditioned
112   Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) to generate augmented data
113   that complements the training dataset (Antoniou et al., 2017; Tran et al., 2017; Bowles et al., 2018;
114   Milz et al., 2018). Recently, diffusion models have been shown to generate images with higher
115   quality compared to GANs (Rombach et al., 2022). He et al. (2023) applies SDEdit (Meng et al.,
116   2022) on stable diffusion models to generate augmented data for training classifiers; DAFusion
117   applies textual inversion (Gal et al., 2023) to fine-tune stable diffusion models for generating unseen
118
      visual concepts (Trabucco et al., 2023). These methods generally do not consider the variance
      information from different classes and may suffer from generating diverse images for minority classes
119
      in imbalanced datasets.
120
121   To learn nuisance transformations from multiple classes, GIT uses an image-to-image translation
122   model to translate images from one class to another (Zhou et al., 2022). While this method is capable
123   of learning lighting and color changes, it is unable to capture more complex alterations, such as object
124
      semantics and positions. In common object datasets, like CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, the method is
      reported to underperform compared to RandAugment (Cubuk et al., 2020), which uses a combination
125
      of conventional image transformations. The above-mentioned methods typically fix a pre-determined
126
      number or portion of augmented images for the training process without adapting the configurations
127   for each class and dataset. For example, DAFusion and GIT augment half of the data, and GIT further
128   restricts applying augmentations to low-data classes. To overcome these limitations, we introduce
129   AutoGenDA. Unlike existing generative methods, AutoGenDA introduces a search framework that
130   learns from classifier feedback and automatically adapts the augmentation process for each class.
131
      Automated Data Augmentation. Conventional data augmentation applies label-preserving trans-
132
      formations to create new augmented data (Shorten & Khoshgoftaar, 2019). However, this approach
133   relies heavily on expert knowledge or trial and error to determine the specific configurations and
134   transformations to use. To address this shortcoming, Automated Data Augmentation (AutoDA) has
135   been developed to learn the optimal policy for augmenting a target dataset (Cheung & Yeung, 2023).
136   AutoAugment employs reinforcement learning to learn the probability and magnitude of applying
137   multiple transformations to a dataset (Cubuk et al., 2019). Subsequent research has introduced more
138   efficient search spaces and search methods to lessen the high computational demand of AutoAug-
139   ment (Ho et al., 2019; Lim et al., 2019; Cubuk et al., 2020; Hataya et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020). Our
140   work is also related to adaptive data augmentation, such as AdaAug (Cheung & Yeung, 2022), which
141
      learns adaptive augmentation strategies for each class and data instance. Previous AutoDA methods
      predominantly focus on searching for augmentation parameters for a set of image transformations but
142
      not for the generation process. To the best of our understanding, we are the first to study automated
143
      data augmentation in generative data augmentation.

      3.1   C APTURING VARIANCE USING IMAGE CAPTIONS
149
      Modeling data variances and transferring them across classes with limited data is challenging. We
150   attempted to reference DAGAN (Antoniou et al., 2017) to train an image-conditioned GAN and use
151   it to generate augmented data that is similar to the data from a class different from the conditioned
152   images. However, the approach only works well for simple datasets like Omniglot and EMNIST,
153   but fails to synthesize more complex data from object classification datasets, e.g., Caltech101 (Li
154   et al., 2022). We also experimented with image-to-image translation (Huang et al., 2018) and style
155   transfer (Karras et al., 2019) approaches to apply learned styles from one class to another. While
156   these models are able to generate some valid images, the variations between the generated images
157   are mostly limited to slight changes in color and lighting conditions. These findings are in line with
158
      previous research, indicating that these methods tend to struggle in an imbalanced setting, where
      some image classes lack richness and diversity (Patashnik et al., 2021).
159
160   To generate high-quality augmented images using limited data, we turn to large pre-trained text-
161   guided generative models, which are capable of generating diverse, high-quality images by providing
      suitable text prompts (Meng et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; He et al., 2023). While current


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      diffusion models show stunning generation results, they are sensitive to the provided prompts, which
163   are usually crafted carefully through prompt engineering. Based on our assumption that some variance
164   information within the dataset is class-agnostic, we propose to use image captions to capture the
165   differences between data samples and integrate the captured variances into the text-guided generation
166   process through text prompts.
167
      Using image captions as text prompts in generation processes provides several advantages. First,
168
      these captions are automatically extracted from the target dataset, eliminating the need for expensive
169   prompt engineering. The extracted captions describe the differences between data samples, providing
170   important variance information for a classifier to learn. Additionally, textual captions are widely
171   understood by any pre-trained text-guided generators, offering greater flexibility than using model-
172   specific image or style representations to capture the variance between data samples.
173
      We formally let H(x; y) : X → Q be an image-captioning model that generates an image caption
174
      q ∈ Q given x ∈ X as an input image and y ∈ Y as the class label of x. Specifically, we use the
175   prefix “a photo of a hyi which” to query the complete image caption from the model. The caption q
176   is later used to assist the generation of augmented images.
177

      3.2   G ENERATING N EW AUGMENTED I MAGES
179
180   Data augmentation aims to generate augmented data that is diverse and similar to the original
181   data (Lopes et al., 2020). To preserve the image content, we use an image-to-image generative model
182   that edits the original image instead of a text-to-image model that generates an image solely based on
183   the input prompt. We use G(x; q) : X → X to denote the generative model that takes the original
184   image x as input and generates an augmented image conditioned on a text prompt q. To utilize the
185   image variance found in one class, we sample an image x0 where y 0 = y and use the caption of x0 as
186
      input prompt to guide the generation of the augmented data x̂ from x in Eq. (1). We refer to such
      type of augmented data that utilizes the image variance within the same class as local-caption images.
187
188                               x̂ = G(x; q); q = H(x0 ; y 0 ), where y = y 0                             (1)
189
      To create new data using variance information from a different class, we use a simple replace function
190
      r(k; y) : Q → Q to replace the class name in k as y, where k is the caption of an image from a
191
      different class. Let x00 be an input image where y 00 6= y, the augmented data x̂ utilizing the caption of
192   x00 can be computed using Eq. (2). We call such type of augmented data that uses the image variance
193   from another class as transfer-caption images.
194
195                           x̂ = G(x; r(k, y)); k = H(x00 ; y 00 ), where y 6= y 00                       (2)
196   The proposed method extracts data variances within and across different classes through image
197   captions and uses them as editing instructions to create new images. While local captions are
198   generally effective for describing images within the same class, this may not hold for cases involving
199   using captions from another class as described in Eq. (2). For example, captions that describe
200   a car may not be suitable for describing a dog. To tackle this issue, we employ a class-filtering
201   mechanism. More precisely, we assume that captions from similar classes can be more easily applied
202   to each other. For instance, using a caption for a cat to describe a dog is better than using a caption
203   for a car. Therefore, the transfer-caption images are constrained to using captions from m closest
204
      neighboring classes, measured by the cosine distance between the class embeddings. We use the
      notation neigh(y) ∈ Y m to represent the set of m closest classes to y, and set m = 3 by default. In
205
      our implementation, we utilize the pre-trained sentence transformer model from Reimers & Gurevych
206
      (2019) to obtain the class embeddings from the class names.

      3.3   AUTOMATED S EARCH
209
210   Previous generative data augmentation typically generates a fixed number or portion of synthesized
211   samples for all classes (Antoniou et al., 2017; Zhou et al., 2022; He et al., 2023; Trabucco et al.,
212   2023). This heuristic approach does not guarantee the effectiveness of the synthetic data in improving
213   classification generalization, particularly for imbalanced datasets where the optimal number of
214   generated samples and the level of diversity may vary across different classes. In this section, we
215   propose a new automated search framework that takes feedback from the classifier and optimizes
      the configuration for generative data augmentation for each individual data class. To the best of

      our knowledge, our framework represents the first proposal for an automated search framework for
217   generative data augmentation.
218
219   Search Space. Previous automated data augmentation work, like AutoAugment (Cubuk et al.,
220
      2019), learns the probability of applying pre-defined image operations to augment input images. We
      formulate the data augmentation process as a distribution learning problem to select the optimal
221
      mixture of different types of augmented data for each class. Specifically, the augmented image in
222
      AutoGenDA can be given as (1) an identity image, i.e., no augmentation is applied, (2) a local-caption
223   image, or (3) a transfer-caption image. More formally, the augmentation process samples from a
224   distribution p(z; αy ), where αy ∈ R3 is the learnable probability parameter specific to class y and
225   z ∈ {0, 1}3 , z1 + z2 + z3 = 1 is the one-hot vector that indicates the selection of the augmentation
226   from the three augmentation methods. Given a dataset with C classes; the search space of α is RC×3 .
227   Let q be an image caption sampled from class y and k be an image caption sampled from neigh(y).
228   The augmented image x̂ is computed as:

244   Training. AutoGenDA utilizes a two-stage training process. During the search stage, the probability
245   parameter is updated through an iterative exploitation-exploration approach. In the exploitation step,
246   a classifier Fθ parameterized by θ is trained to minimize the standard cross entropy loss on the
247
      augmented training dataset Dtrain using the discretized augmentation in Eq. (5). In the exploration step,
      Fθ is fixed and applied to the augmented validation dataset Dvalid using the relaxed augmentation in
248
      Eq. (4). The probability parameter α is updated to minimize the cross entropy loss on the validation
249
      data. This adjustment of the probability parameter ensures a balanced mixture of real data, local-
250   caption data, and transfer-caption data for each class to enhance generalization. It implicitly manages
251   the diversity of data and the inclusion of local variance and transfer variance in the augmented data.
252   Furthermore, this process can be regarded as a selection mechanism for indirectly choosing the more
253   advantageous prompts when creating augmented images.
254
      During the inference stage, we follow the exploitation step to fine-tune a classifier on the augmented
255
      training dataset using the learned probability parameter from the searching stage. The details of
256   the search method are outlined in Algorithm 1. In Algorithm 1, mini-batch is a batch sampling
257   process, update is a stochastic gradient descent procedure with a provided step size η or ζ, Qy
258   represents the set of captions of images from class y.

      Limitations. Our approach relies on a pre-trained image captioning model to generate descriptions

481   if the target domain drastically differs from the domain on which the model is trained. Additionally,

      5    C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we introduce an automated generative data augmentation method called AutoGenDA.
489   AutoGenDA learns variance information within and across classes using image captions and adapts
490   the generative process to each data class and new dataset by learning the best combination of real data
491   and augmented samples for each class. Through qualitative and quantitative analysis, we demonstrate
492   the effectiveness of AutoGenDA on four classification datasets with different imbalanced and low-shot
493   settings. Our search method is not dependent on any specific model or architecture. This provides
494   great flexibility in adapting contemporary generative and image captioning models, which could
495   further enhance its performance in imbalanced and low-shot classification.

673         IMBALANCED DATASETS
674
675   C.1   DAGAN
676
      In our initial experiments, we attempted to utilize Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) for
677
      generating augmented images. Inspired by DAGAN, we utilize an input-conditioned GAN to create
678   images that are similar to but different from the input image. Specifically, we use an image encoder
679   to map the input image to a latent representation. The latent representation is then combined with a
680   transformed noise and decoded by an image decoder to produce the augmented image. A discriminator
681   is employed to distinguish the real sample pairs and fake sample pairs. The real sample pairs comprise
682   the input image and another image from the class as the input image, whereas the fake sample pairs
683   are composed of the input image and the image generated by the decoder.
684   In the Omniglot dataset, the DAGAN approach can generate visually appealing augmented characters
685   that are similar yet subtly different from the original image, as demonstrated in Fig. 7. However, when
686   applied to more complex object datasets like CIFAR-10, we observed that the approach is unable
687   to produce photorealistic images, as depicted in Fig. 8. Despite experimenting with a larger image
688   encoder, image decoder, latent representation, and longer training iterations, the results remained
689   similar.
690
691   C.2   MUNIT
692
693
      MUNIT is a multimodal unsupervised image-to-image translation network, which is capable of
      transforming a given input image into various outputs in different styles using the style codes learned
694
      from the data. In GIT (Zhou et al., 2022), MUNIT is modified to learn the translation between
695
      different classes instead of between two different domains. More specifically, MUNIT decomposes
696   the latent space of images into a content space and style space and introduces reconstruction and
697   cycle-consistency losses to generate augmented images that preserve the image content but in a
698   different style. With the use of MUNIT, we are able to generate realistic images in CIFAR-10, as
699   illustrated in Fig. 9. However, we have noted that the variations in the generated images primarily
700   involve minor changes in color and lighting conditions. This discovery aligns with previous research,
701   which suggests that the method faces challenges in an imbalanced setting, where certain image classes
      lack richness and diversity.

      G    A NALYSIS ON THE L EARNED S AMPLING PARAMETERS
849                                                                                                             NEW
850
      In Fig. 10, we show the averaged augmentation probabilities over all classes for the PASCAL VOC,
851
      Caltech, COCO, and LVIS datasets with 2, 4, 8, and 16 shots of data.
852
853   The learned probabilities for identity images show an increasing trend, while the learned probabilities
854   for local-caption and transfer-caption images show a decreasing trend. This can be explained by the
855
      fact that with less data, the classifier easily learns variance within the dataset and demands more
      varied data to improve generalization performance. Conversely, with more data, the classifier can
856
      learn diverse variance information from the real data, thereby assigning a larger probability to sample
857
      an identity image.
