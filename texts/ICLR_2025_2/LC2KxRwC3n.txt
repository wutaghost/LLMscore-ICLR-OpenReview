009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011             Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) have emerged as a promising approach to decom-
012             pose the activations of Large Language Models (LLMs) into human-interpretable
013             latents. In this paper, we pose two questions. First, to what extent do SAEs ex-
014             tract monosemantic and interpretable latents? Second, to what extent does vary-
015             ing the sparsity or the size of the SAE affect monosemanticity / interpretabil-
016             ity? By investigating these questions in the context of a simple first-letter iden-
017             tification task where we have complete access to ground truth labels for all to-
018             kens in the vocabulary, we are able to provide more detail than prior investi-
019
                gations. Critically, we identify a problematic form of feature-splitting we call
                “feature absorption” where seemingly monosemantic latents fail to fire in cases
020
                where they clearly should. Our investigation suggests that varying SAE size
021
                or sparsity is insufficient to solve this issue, and that there are deeper concep-
022             tual issues in need of resolution. We release a feature absorption explorer at
023             https://feature-absorption.streamlit.app.

      However, despite these theoretical advantages, most existing work on SAE interpretability mainly
055   studies max activating examples (Cunningham et al., 2024), which may be misleading. There are
056   more rigorous works which only measure precision (Bricken et al., 2023; Templeton et al., 2024;
057   Kissane et al., 2024). Recent work has briefly explored recall and found it to be worse than naively
058   expected, but this remains poorly understood (Olah et al., 2024b). We build on this work by eval-
059   uating precision / recall on a large number of SAEs, and offer a partial explanation for lower-than-
060   expected recall of SAE latents in the form of “feature absorption”.
061   Our key contributions in this investigation include the following:
062
063         1. We identify numerous SAE latents appearing to classify first-letter features. We calculate
064            their precision / recall on the first letter identification tasks (as a proxy for monosemanticity
065            / interpretability) and find they significantly underperform linear probes.
066         2. We find that latents which seem outwardly to classify the same feature can have vastly
067            different precision / recall and that this tradeoff is mediated by various factors, mainly
068            sparsity and width of the SAE.
069         3. Most importantly, we identify and quantify a variant of feature-splitting we call “feature
070            absorption”, where an SAE latent appears to track a human-interpretable concept, but it
071            fails to activate on seemingly arbitrary tokens. Instead, a different latent activates and
072            contributes a portion of the probe direction, “absorbing” the feature. This is described in
073            Figure 1.
074
      We believe that feature absorption poses an obstacle to the practical application of SAEs since it
075   suggests SAE latents may be inherently unreliable classifiers. This is particularly important if we
076   seek to use them in safety applications where we need confidence that latents are fully tracking
077   behaviours, such as bias or deceptive behavior. Furthermore, techniques which seek to describe
078   circuits in terms of a sparse combination of latents with also be more difficult in the context of
079   feature absorption (Marks et al., 2024).

081   2   BACKGROUND
082
083   Linear probing. A linear probe is a simple linear classifier trained on the hidden activations of a
084   neural network, typically using logistic regression (LR) (Alain & Bengio, 2017).
085
086   K-sparse probing. A k-sparse probe (Gurnee et al., 2023) is a linear probe trained on a sparse
087   subset of k neurons or SAE latents. Training a k-sparse probe first requires selecting the k best
088   neurons or SAE latents that in-aggregate act as a good classifier, and then training a standard linear
089   probe on just those k neurons or latents.
090   Gurnee et al. (2023) proposed several methods of estimating the best k neurons or features to pick,
091   one of which involves first training a LR probe with a L1 loss term, and selecting the k largest
092   elements by probe weight. When we refer to k-sparse probing in this work, we use this method of
093   selecting k features.
094
095   Sparse autoencoders. An SAE consists of an encoder, Wenc , a decoder, Wdec , and correspond-
096   ing biases benc and bdec . The SAE has a nonlinearity, σ, typically a ReLU (or variants such as
097   JumpReLU (Rajamanoharan et al., 2024; Lieberum et al., 2024)). Given input activation, a, the
098   SAE computes a hidden representation, f , and reconstruction, â:
099
100                                            f =σ(Wenc a + benc )                                         (1)
101
                                               â =Wdec f + bdec                                            (2)
102
103   SAEs attempt to reconstruct input activations by projecting into an overcomplete basis using a
104   sparsity-inducing loss term (typically L1 loss), or a certain number of non-zero features (L0) on
105   the hidden activations. SAEs learn feature decompositions in an unsupervised manner, and while
106   the sparsity penalty is meant to encourage monosemantic features, it is often hard to judge if the fea-
107   tures learned are interpretable or to say with certainty that the features the SAE learned are faithful
      to the computation performed by the underlying LLM.

      SAE feature ablation. We often want to understand how an SAE latent causally influences a
109   downstream output. In an ablation study, the latent in question is removed from the computation
110   graph of the model to see the effect this has on a downstream metric. A negative ablation effect
111   means removing the SAE latent would lower the metric.
112
      We follow the work of Marks et al. (2024) and provide the procedure in Algorithm 1 below. We also
113
      make use of the integrated-gradients (IG) approximation (Sundararajan et al., 2017) to improve the
114
      speed of running multiple ablation experiments.

131
132   Our experiments focused on predicting the first-letter of a single token containing characters from
133   the English alphabet (a-z, A-Z) and an optional leading space. We use in-context learning (ICL)
134   prompts to elicit knowledge from the model, using templates of the form:
135   {token} has the first letter:                     {capitalized first letter}
136
      An example of an ICL prompt consisting of 2 in-context examples is shown below. The model
137
      should output the D token:
138
139    tartan has the first letter: T
140    mirth has the first letter: M
141    dog has the first letter:
142
143   In the above prompt, we extract residual stream activations at the dog token index. These acti-
144   vations are used both for LR probe training and for applying SAEs. We use a train/test split of
145   80% / 20%, and evaluate only on the test set, including when running experiments on SAEs. When
146   applying SAEs, we include the SAE error term (Marks et al., 2024) to avoid changing model output.
147   To determine the causal effect of SAE latents on the first-letter identification task we conduct abla-
148   tion studies. We use a metric consisting of the logit of the correct letter minus the mean logit of all
149   incorrect letters. This measures the propensity of the model to choose the correct starting letter as
150   opposed to other letters. Formally, our metric m is defined below, where g refers to the final token
151   logits, L is the set of uppercase letters, and y is the uppercase letter that is the correct starting letter:
152

192     4                        R ESULTS
193
194    Our results are divided into three sections. First, we compare the performance of linear probes with
195    SAE latents on recovering first-character information from model activations, showing that despite
196    appearing to track first letter features, a wide variety of precision / recall is achieved. Second, we mo-
197    tivate our definition of feature absorption with a case-study, emphasizing how an absorbing feature
198
       can unexpectedly causally mediate first letter information whilst the first-letter latent (unexpectedly)
       fails to fire. Finally, we attempt to quantify feature splitting and feature absorption, showing that
199
       tuning of hyper-parameters may partially assist but not fully alleviate feature absorption.

       4.2               W HY DO SAE LATENTS UNDERPERFORM ?
237
238
       The Gemma Scope layer 3, 16k width, 59 L0 SAE has a latent, 6510, which appears to act as a
239    classifier for “starts with S”, achieving an F1 of 0.81. However, this latent fails to activate on some
240    tokens the probe can classify, and which the model can spell, such as the token short.
241
242
       Figure 4a shows a sample prompt containing a series of tokens that start with “S”, and the activations
       of top SAE latents by ablation score for these tokens. The main “starts with S” latent, 6510, activates
243
       on all these tokens except short. This SAE also has a token-aligned latent, 1085, which activates
244
       on variants of the word “short” (“ short”, “SHORT”, etc...). The Neuronpedia dashboard (Lin &
245    Bloom, 2023) for feature 1085 is shown in Appendix A.11. For the token short, the main “starts
246    with S” latent does not activate but the “short” latent activates instead.
247
248
       Latent 1085 has a cosine similarity with the “starts with S” probe of 0.12, indicating it contains a
       component of the “starts with S” direction, although much smaller than the main “starts with S”
249
       latent. Cosine similarity of the SAE decoder with the “starts with S” LR probe is shown in Figure
250
       4b. Interestingly, despite latent 1085 having only about 1/5 the cosine similarity with the probe
251    as the main latent 6510, we see it activates with about 5 times the magnitude of latent 6510 on
252    the short token, thus contributing a similar amount of the “starts with S” probe direction to the
253    residual stream.
254
       We conduct an ablation experiment on the short token, shown in Figure 5a, and see that latent
255
       1085 has a dramatically larger ablation effect compared with all other SAE features. This suggests
256
       latent 1085 is causally responsible for the model knowing that short starts with S.
257
258    Is it possible that the probe projection is not the causally important component of feature 1085? We
259    conduct another ablation experiment, except now we remove the probe direction from feature 1085
260
       via projection before ablation. The results of this ablation experiment are shown in Figure 5b. After
       removing the probe component from feature 1085, it no longer has a significant ablation effect. Thus
261
       we know the probe projection of feature 1085 is responsible for model behavior.
262
263    These experiments show the “starts with S” feature has been “absorbed” by the token-aligned latent
264    1085, likely along with other semantic concepts related to the word “short”. After observing that the
265    main “starts with S” latent 6510 activates on most tokens that begin with “S”, it may be tempting to
266
       conclude this latent tracks the interpretable feature of beginning with the letter “S”. However, this
       latent quietly fails to activate on the short token, leading us to a false sense of understanding.
267
268    We call this phenomenon feature absorption. In feature absorption a seemingly interpretable SAE
269    latent fails to activate on arbitrary positive examples, and instead the feature is “absorbed” into
       approximately token-aligned latents.

299     4.3                   M EASURING FEATURE SPLITTING AND FEATURE ABSORPTION
300
301
       Feature splitting A key phenomenon identified from previous studies of SAEs is feature-splitting
       (Bricken et al., 2023), where a feature represented in a single latent in a smaller SAE can split into
302
       two or more latents in a larger SAE. During our experiments, we found strong evidence of feature-
303
       splitting in the Gemma Scope SAEs.
304
305    For instance, in the layer 0, 16k width, 105 L0 SAE, we find two encoder latents (id:7112 and
306    id:7657 2 ) which align with the “L” starting letter probe. Inspecting max activating examples, we see
307
       latent 7112 activates on tokens starting with lowercase “l”, while 7657 activates on tokens starting
       with uppercase “L”. Some activating examples for these latents are shown in Table 1.
308
309    Feature splitting like this is not necessarily problematic for interpretability efforts since the split
310    features are still easily identifiable, and depending on the context it may be more useful to have
311    either a single “starts with L” latent or a pair of “starts with uppercase / lowercase L” latents.
312    We measure feature splitting using k-sparse probing (Gurnee et al., 2023) on SAE activations. If
313    increasing the k-sparse probe from k to k + 1 causes a significant increase in probe F1 score, then
314    the additional SAE latent provides a meaningful signal, and the combination of these k + 1 latents is
315    likely a feature split. In the example of the uppercase “L” and lowercase “l” split, a k-sparse probe
316    with k = 2 trained on both these these features should predict “starts with letter L” much better than
317    either feature on its own. Figure 6a shows F1 vs K for letters “L” and “N”. The “L” k-sparse probe
318    shows a significant jump in F1 score moving from k=1 to k=2 corresponding to feature splitting,
319
       while the F1 score for the “N” k-sparse probe is relatively constant.
320    We detect feature splitting by measuring whether increasing k by one causes a jump in F1 score by
321    more than threshold τ . We set τ = 0.03 after manually inspecting latents with various thresholds.
322    Figure 6b shows feature splitting vs L0 for all 16k and 65k width Gemma Scope SAEs.

356     Feature absorption The single latent or a set of traditional feature split latents that seem to act as
357
        a classifier for a human-interpretable feature like “starts with S” fail to fire in a seemingly arbitrary
        number of cases. What fires instead are approximately token-aligned latents with small but positive
358
        alignment with the LR probe. We say these latents are absorbing the feature.
359
360     We quantify the extent to which feature absorption occurs with the metric feature absorption rate.
361     We first find k feature splits for a first-letter feature using a k-sparse probe. We then find false-
362     negative tokens that all k feature-split SAE latents fail to activate on, but which the LR probe cor-
363
        rectly classifies, and run an integrated-gradients ablation experiment on those tokens. The ablation
        effect finds the most causally important SAE latents for the spelling of that token. If the SAE latent
364
        receiving the largest negative magnitude ablation effect has a cosine similarity with the LR probe
365
        above 0.025, and is at least 1.0 larger than the latent with the second highest ablation effect, we say
366     that feature absorption has occurred. These thresholds were chosen from manual inspection of the
367     data. We then calculate feature absorption rate as below:

      Our metric cannot capture absorption past layer 17 in Gemma 2 2B since we rely on ablation exper-
379   iments to be certain the absorbed feature causally mediates model behavior. Past layer 17, attention
380   has already moved the starting letter information from the source token into the final token posi-
381   tion, so any ablations on the source token past layer 17 have little effect. This is a limitation of our
382   absorption metric - we rely on ablation to be certain of the causal impact of absorbed features on
383   model behavior, but this limits the layer depth our metric can be applied. We discuss this further in
384   Appendix A.9.
385   Our absorption metric is not perfect, and is likely an under-estimate of the true level of feature
386   absorption. We only consider absorption to have occurred if a single SAE latent has a much larger
387   ablation effect than all other latents, and if the main SAE latents for a feature do not activate at
388   all. Our metric will not capture multiple absorbing latents activating together, or the main latents
389   activating but very weakly. Regardless, we feel our metric is a reasonable conservative baseline.

      5     R ELATED WORK
392
393
      5.1   A PPLICATIONS OF P ROBES AND SAE S FOR M ODEL I NTERPRETABILITY
394
395   Probing methods have often been used to extract interpretable information from language models.
396   However, the existence of such a representation does not guarantee that the model relies on this
397   representation in its computation graph (Elazar et al., 2021).
398   Prior work has shown that many human-interpretable concepts in LLM activations are represented
399   as linear directions in activation space, known as the linear representation hypothesis (Elhage et al.,
400   2022; Park et al., 2024). Li et al. (2023) used non-linear probes to recover board representations from
401   a transformer trained on Othello scripts (“OthelloGPT”). However, Nanda (2023) later showed that
402   linear representations were not only recoverable but also editable.
403
      Recent work has focused on applying SAEs to extract human-interpretable explanations of model
404   internals. Karvonen et al. (2024) investigated how SAEs represent board states of Chess and Othello,
405   and introduce a coverage metric using ground-truth features to evaluate the quality of SAE latents.
406   This is very similar to our technique for evaluating SAE latents on a known task.
407
      Other work has noted poor recall / precision of SAE latents compared to known proxies (Olah et al.,
408
      2024b; Kissane et al., 2024; Templeton et al., 2024). We build on this work by evaluating a large
409
      number of Gemma Scope SAEs to demonstrate how precision / recall is mediated by sparsity, and
410   also offer a possible explanation of low recall due to feature absorption.
411
412
      5.2   C HARACTER - LEVEL INFORMATION IN LANGUAGE MODELS
413
414   The ability of LLMs to learn character-level information from ostensibly character-blind tokens has
415   been studied by various scholars, though no clear mechanism has yet been established. Kaushal &
416   Mahowald (2022) trained MLPs from the embedding layers of GPT-J as probes for each letter in the
417   alphabet, again finding good performance that implied character-level information was represented,
418   but did not look into the model internals to explain how these representations were being used. In a
419   follow-up work, Watkins & Bloom (2023) demonstrated that even linear probes on the embedding
420
      layers perform comparably well to MLPs in extracting character-level information.
421   In contrast to the above approaches, we train various probes for each character on multiple layers,
422   and compare with SAE latents for a variety of layers.
423
424   5.3   D ECOMPOSING SAE L ATENTS
425
426   The phenomenon of SAEs of different sides splitting a feature into various smaller latents was first
427   described in Bricken et al. (2023), which noted that different SAE widths and sparsities induce
428
      latents of different granularity, with wider SAEs often learning more specific variants of features.
429   Bussmann et al. (2024) find that by training an SAE on the decoder of another SAE, a technique
430   called Meta-SAEs, it is possible to break down a single SAE latent like “Einstein” into subcom-
431   ponents like “German” and “Physicist” and “starts with E”. Meta-SAEs may provide a promising
      future research direction to overcome the feature absorption phenomenon we describe in this paper.

      6   D ISCUSSION
433
434
435   Interpretability of SAE latents In this work, we use a simple first-letter identification task to
436   investigate whether SAEs extract monosemantic and intrepretable features, and how this is affected
437   by varying hyperparameters like SAE size, sparsity, or layer. We find that the SAE latents we
438
      investigated were not interpretable and that varying the sparsity or the size of the SAE did not
      meaningfully change this.
439
440   One may argue it is unreasonable to judge an SAE latent against a linear probe directly optimized
441   to perform the same classification task and that it has been established that sometimes unsupervised
442   methods can surprise us (Nanda, 2023). However, we argue that for a latent to be considered inter-
443   pretable, its behavior should match what one would reasonably expect the latent to be doing after
444
      inspecting its activation patterns. In our experiments, we use SAE latents that do appear to perform
      first letter classification, and then evaluate how well they perform this task. We validate as well
445
      that these latents causally mediated model performance on the first-letter task in Appendix A.3. We
446
      are convinced that these latents should reasonably be considered “first-letter” latents and that their
447   performance on the first-letter identification task is a valid measure of their interpretability.
448
449
450   Feature absorption In trying to understand why SAE latents fail to match the performance of LR
451   probes, we identified a form of feature splitting we call “feature absorption”. Feature absorption may
452   be particularly problematic for SAEs because it creates an interpretability illusion where we believe
453   we have found an interpretable latent, but absorption induces lower recall by creating clear false
454   negatives to the mainline interpretation of the latent. For example, we may believe we have found a
455   SAE latent which tracks deceptive behavior in the model, but due to feature absorption, there may
456
      be many cases where that latent fails to fire. This lower recall poses problems for methods which
      rely on using SAEs to find sparse circuits (Marks et al., 2024), as the number of latents needed to
457
      characterize model behavior may be much larger than expected. We find that feature absorption
458
      happens even in high-recall latents, so this is not only a problem for low L0 SAEs and appears to be
459   a more fundamental issue.
460
461   We hypothesize that feature absorption is a consequence of co-occurrence between sparse and dense
462
      features. If a dense feature like “starts with letter D” always co-occurs with a more sparse feature
      like “dogs”, the SAE can increase sparsity by absorbing the “starts with D” feature into a “dogs”
463
      latent. We explore this further in Appendix A.7, where we show that feature absorption occurs when
464
      training an SAE in a toy setting with features that co-occur together.
465
466   It remains to be seen if we can predict or identify excepted instances where a feature “should
467   have activated” but does not activate due to absorption. One promising direction is meta-SAEs,
468
      a novel method for decomposing SAE latents and may decompose absorbed features (Bussmann
      et al., 2024). One interpretation of our results is that competition may exist between “latents” and
469
      “meta-latents” for activation on particular examples and that re-allocation of examples between SAE
470
      latents enables SAEs to interpolate between different possible decompositions.
471
472
473   Future Work A primary goal of future work should be to secure further external validity of our
474   findings. This could include finding examples of feature absorption in SAEs trained on other models,
475   with other architectures, or finding examples of feature absorption unrelated to character identifica-
476   tion. We expect it should be possible to demonstrate feature absorption in a toy model by mixing
477   dense features with sparse features that always co-occur with these dense features.
478   We hope as well this investigation may lead to research into solutions, particularly those involv-
479   ing Meta-SAEs (Bussmann et al., 2024), to solve or mitigate feature absorption. Anoter possible
480   solution may be attribution dictionary learning (Olah et al., 2024a).
481
482
483   Limitations Our feature absorption metric requires having ground-truth knowledge of true labels
484   to first train a LR probe, whereas many features of interest in a LLM lack such clear-cut ground-truth
485   labels. Our metric uses ablation effect to ensure absorbed features causally mediate model behavior,
      but therefore cannot be easily used in final model layers.

677   A.1   G LOSSARY OF T ERMS
678
679   Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs): Neural networks trained to reconstruct their input while enforcing
680   sparsity in their hidden layer. In the context of this paper, SAEs are used to decompose the dense
681   activations of language models into more interpretable features.
682
683   SAE error term: When inserting a SAE into the computation path of the model, errors in SAE
684
      reconstruction will propagate to later parts of the model and can change the model output. We refer
      to the error as the SAE error term, and corresponds to the difference between the SAE output and
685
      the original SAE input activation. Marks et al. (2024) introduced the idea of adding this error term
686
      back to the SAE output to ensure that the SAE does not change model output.
687
688
      Latent: We refer to neurons in the hidden layer of a SAE as latents to avoid overloading the term
689   “feature”. This is in contrast to earlier work which used the term “feature” to refer to both human-
690   interpretable concepts and SAE hidden layer neurons.
691
692   Feature: We use the term “feature” to refer to an idealized human-interpretable concept that the
693   model represent in its activations and which a SAE latent may or may not represent.
694
695   Monosemantic: Referring to a feature or representation that corresponds to a single, clear seman-
696   tic concept. In the context of SAEs, a monosemantic feature would ideally capture one interpretable
697   aspect of the input.
698
699   Interpretable: A latent being interpretable is not well defined in the field, making it difficult to
700   ensure that different authors mean the same thing when referring to SAE interpretability. When
701   we refer to a SAE latent as being interpretable in this work, we mean that it should behave in line
      with how it appears to behave after inspecting its activation patterns. If an SAE latent appears


                                                      13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      to track a feature X by a reasonable inspection of its activations but has subtle deviations from this
703   behavior in reality, we say this is not interpretable. We thus measure interpretability via classification
704   performance when a latent appears to be a classifier over some feature.
705
706   Feature dashboard: A dashboard showing activation patterns and max-activating examples for a
707   SAE latent. Feature dashboards are commonly used to interpret the behavior of an SAE latent.
708
709   Neuronpedia: A platform, https://neuronpedia.org, which hosts feature dashboards for
710   popular SAEs (Lin & Bloom, 2023).
711
712   Token-aligned latent: A latent which seems to roughly fire on variants of the same token. For
713   instance, a “Snake” token-aligned latent may fire on the tokens “Snake”, “SNAKE”, “ snakes”,
714   etc...
715
716   Feature splitting: A phenomenon in SAEs introduced by Bricken et al. (2023), where a SAE
717   latent tracks a general feature in a narrow SAE, but splits into multiple more specific SAE latents in
718   a wider SAE. For instance, a latent tracking “starts with L” in a narrow SAE may split into a latent
719   tracking “starts with capital L” and a latent tracking “starts with lowercase L” in a wider SAE.
720
721   Feature absorption: A problematic form of feature splitting where a SAE latent appears to track
722   an interpretable feature, but that latent has seemingly arbitrary exception cases where it fails to fire.
723   Instead, an approximately token-aligned feature “absorbs” the feature direction and fires in place of
724   the main latent.
725
726   Circuit: In the context of neural network interpretability, a circuit refers to a subgraph of neurons
727
      or features within a neural network that work together to perform a specific function or computation.
      The study of circuits aims to understand how different components of a neural network interact to
728
      process information and produce outputs.
729
730
      Linear probe: A simple linear classifier (typically logistic regression) trained on the hidden ac-
731
      tivations of a neural network to predict some property or task. Used to assess what information is
732
      linearly decodable from the network’s representations.
733
734
      K-sparse probing: A variant of linear probing where only the k most important features (as de-
735
      termined by some selection method) are used to train the probe. This helps identify which specific
736   neurons or features are most relevant for a given task.
737
738
      Ablation study: An experimental method where a component of a system (in this case, a neuron or
739   feature in a neural network) is removed or altered to observe its effect on the system’s performance.
740   This helps determine the causal importance of the component.
741
742   Integrated gradients (IG): An attribution method that assigns importance scores to input features
743   by accumulating gradients along a path from a baseline input to the actual input. In this paper, it’s
744   used as an approximation technique for ablation studies.
745
746   In-context learning (ICL): A paradigm where a language model is given examples of a task
747   within its input prompt, allowing it to adapt to new tasks without fine-tuning. Often used with
748   few-shot learning techniques.
749
750   Residual stream: In the context of transformer architectures, the residual stream refers to the
751   main information flow that bypasses the self-attention and feed-forward layers through residual
752   connections.
753
754   Logits: The raw, unnormalized outputs of a neural network’s final layer, before any activation
755   function (like softmax) is applied. In language models, logits typically represent the model’s scores
      for each token in the vocabulary.


                                                         14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      Activation patching: An interpretability technique where activations at specific locations in a
757   neural network are replaced or modified to observe the effect on the network’s output. This helps in
758   understanding the causal role of different parts of the network in producing its final output.

      to change what first letter it predicts just by changing the activations. For this experiment we use the

790   encode them using the SAE. Then we zero out the activation of the SAE latent associated with the
791   original letter and change the activation of the SAE latent associated with the new letter into the
792   average activation it has on tokens starting with this new letter.
793

794   in the 75-150 range. This corresponds to the observed pattern of these SAE latents having higher
795   F1 scores for classification. We report the results in Figure 9. The best SAEs on the layers 7-9 can
796   achieve a substantial replacement, but note that the averages hide variance across individual tokens,
797   where some get edited completely and others get unaffected. The edit success also varies based on
798   the true first letter and the random new letter; for illustration we show a breakdown by letter for two
799   specific SAEs in layer 7 in Figure 10.

1011     A.7                   T OY MODELS OF FEATURE ABSORPTION
1012
1013     We hypothesize that absorption is due to feature co-occurrence combined with the SAE maximizing
1014     sparsity. When two features co-occur, for instance ”starts with S” and ”short”, the SAE can increase
1015     sparsity by merging the ”starts with S” feature direction into a latent tracking ”short” and then
1016
         simply not fire the main ”start with S” latent. This means firing one feature instead of two, and thus
         increasing sparsity.
1017
1018     We show that feature co-occurrence does indeed cause absorption by constructing a toy setting with
1019     four true features and an SAE with four latents.
1020
1021     Setup Our initial setup consists of 4 true features, each randomly initialized into orthogonal di-
1022     rections with a 50 dimensional representation vector and unit norm. We control the base firing rates
1023     of each of the 4 true features. Unless otherwise specified, the feature fires with magnitude 1.0 and
1024     stdev 0.0. We train a SAE with 4 latents to match the 4 true features using SAELens (Joseph Bloom
1025     & Chanin, 2024). The SAE uses L1 loss with l1 coefficient 3e-5, and learning rate 3e-4. We train
         on 100,000,000 activations. Our 4 true features have the firing rates shown in Table 2.

1145   A.9    C AUSAL INTERVENTIONS AND ABSORPTION
1146
       In this work, we rely on causal interventions like ablation experiments to verify that SAE latents
1147
       have a causal impact on model behavior. In these experiments for spelling tasks, we set up an ICL
1148   prompt to elicit spelling information from the model, for instance the ICL prompt below:
1149
1150    tartan has the first letter: T
1151    mirth has the first letter: M
1152    dog has the first letter:
1153
       In this ICL prompt, we would apply an SAE and train LR probes on the dog token position, and
1154
       expect that the model will output the token D. When we intervene on the dog, we can track the
1155   causal changes to model outputs by applying a metric to the output logits, e.g. checking how our
1156   intervention increases or decreases the D logit relative to other letters.
1157
1158   We use these interventions as part of our absorption metric to ensure that when we claim that “ab-
       sorption” is occurring, we verify that the absorbing feature has a causal impact on model outputs.
1159
       This is stronger evidence than only noting a cosine similarity between the absorbing feature, but this
1160
       means that our absorption metric cannot classify absorption at later model layers.
1161
1162   During a LLM forward pass, the model first collects relevant information on a token in that token
1163   position, and attention heads then move relevant information from earlier tokens to later tokens
1164   (Geva et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2022). If we assess ablation effect at layers after which model
       attention has already pulled relevant information from the subject tokens into the final output token,
1165
       the ablation effect will be 0. For Gemma 2 2B on the first-letter spelling task, we find this movement
1166
       of first-letter spelling information occurs around layer 18.
1167
1168   Figure 19 shows an activation patching experiment (Meng et al., 2022) on a sample first-letter
1169   spelling prompt. In this experiment, we see that near layer 18 the model moves first-letter spelling
1170   information from the subject token to the prediction token.
1171   As a result, our feature absorption metric will not function past layer 18 in Gemma 2 2B, and we
1172   thus focus on layers 0-17 for our analysis of feature absorption. We believe that feature absorption is
1173   still occurring in SAEs past layer 18, but we lose the ability to make causal claims that the absorbing
1174   features are used by the model to make predictions. Given that this paper is trying to highlight the
1175   existence of feature absorption, we felt it is more important to have a metric which is robust and has
1176
       the backing of causal analysis but which cannot be used at all model layers. Future work may make
       a different trade-off and choose a feature absorption metric which can work at all model layers, for
1177
