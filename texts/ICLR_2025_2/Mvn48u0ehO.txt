009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-Agent Path Finding (MAPF) is a significant problem with pivotal appli-
012            cations in robotics and logistics. The problem involves determining collision-
013            free paths for multiple agents with specific goals in a 2D grid-world environ-
014            ment. Unfortunately, finding optimal solutions for MAPF is an NP-hard problem.
015            Traditional centralized planning approaches are intractable for large numbers of
016
               agents and inflexible when adapting to dynamic changes in the environment. On
               the other hand, existing decentralized methods utilizing learning-based strategies
017
               suffer from two main drawbacks: (1) training takes times ranging from days to
018
               weeks, and (2) they often tend to exhibit self-centered agent behaviors leading
019            to increased collisions. We introduce a novel approach leveraging the Decision
020            Transformer (DT) architecture that enables agents to learn individual policies ef-
021            ficiently. We capitalize on the transformer’s capability for long-horizon planning
022            and the advantages of offline reinforcement learning to drastically reduce training
023            times to a few hours. We further show that integrating an LLM (GPT-4o), en-
024            hances the performance of DT policies in mitigating undesirable behaviors such
025            as prolonged idling at specific positions and undesired deviations from goal po-
026            sitions. We focus our empirical evaluation on both scenarios with static environ-
027
               ments and in dynamically changing environments where agents’ goals are altered
               during inference. Results demonstrate that incorporating an LLM for dynamic
028
               scenario adaptation in MAPF significantly enhances the agents’ performance and
029
               paves the way for more adaptable multi-agent systems.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Robot swarms are projected to have a significant impact across various sectors, including manufac-
035   turing, warehousing, and transportation logistics. Multi-Agent Path Finding (MAPF) encompasses
036   planning methods designed to identify collision-free paths for multiple agents operating within a
037   shared space, while ensuring the optimality of these paths according to specific cost functions (e.g.,
038   minimizing path length or travel time). Unfortunately, the MAPF problem is NP-Hard. Much of the
039
      literature on MAPF has focused on centralized planning, where a single planner is assumed to have
      full observability of the world including static and dynamic obstacles. The solution to MAPF com-
040
      putes the plan (which is the set of conflict-free paths for all agents), and is obtained using heuristic
041
      search and optimization techniques such as Conflict-Based Search (CBS), M* (a multi-agent ver-
042   sion of A*), and ODrM* Sharon et al. (2015); Hart et al. (1968); Wagner & Choset (2011); Ferner
043   et al. (2013). As centralized planning is essentially a constraint solving problem, it is unsurprising
044   that with enough computational resources, optimal collision-free paths for individual agents can be
045   found. However, centralized planning has several disadvantages: (1) it cannot scale to large envi-
046   ronments or large number of agents, (2) requires full observability of the agents and the world, (3)
047   produces plans that are not robust to changes in the environment.
048   In many practical multi-agent setups, the world map is not a priori available and each agent is re-
049   stricted to observing only its own field of view (FOV). In such partially observable environments,
050   autonomous or decentralized agents that have the ability to plan their own paths are suitable, and
051   learning-based MAPF methods have been explored. A prominent benchmark is an approach called
052   PRIMAL, where imitation learning (IL) is combined with reinforcement learning (RL), allowing
053   agents to imitate centralized planner behaviors while being trained in a decentralized manner Sar-
      toretti et al. (2019). Prioritized Communication Learning method (PICO), an extension of PRIMAL,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      incorporates planning priorities and communication topologies into its learning pipeline to improve
055   collision avoidance and boost collaborative behavior Li et al. (2022). On the other hand, Distributed
056   Heuristic Learning with Communication (DHC) tries to achieve this objective by employing graph
057   convolution for agent cooperation, Ma et al. (2021a). Here, each agent operates independently
058   with heuristic guidance provided through potential shortest-path choices. Decision Causal Com-
059   munication (DCC) enhances DHC by focusing on selective communication. Unlike other methods,
060   DCC enables agents to choose relevant neighbors for communication, minimizing redundancy and
061   overhead Ma et al. (2021b). SCRIMP introduces a differentiable transformer-based communication
062   mechanism, which addresses the challenges posed by partial observations and enhances team-level
063
      cooperation Wang et al. (2023). Finally, Confidence-based Auto-Curriculum for Team Update Sta-
      bility (CACTUS) proposes a reverse curriculum approach that incrementally increases the potential
064
      distance between start and goal locations to learn effective policy Phan et al. (2024).

119   agents directly toward their new goal. Therefore, alternating DT with GPT-4o for only five timesteps
120   allows for modifications and real-time adjustments, enhancing the adaptability of the agents.

122
123          • We introduce an offline RL approach to train decentralized agents solve the MAPF problem

133            specific navigation tasks within the robotics domain. Our approach highlights the potential
134            of integrating LLMs with RL agents to achieve effective and adaptive behavior in complex
135            environments.

139   2.1   P ROBLEM S ETTING
140
141   Our research focuses on a deterministic and partially observable environment where a team of agents
142   operates in a grid world to complete given tasks. Each agent is assigned to move from a start point
143   to an endpoint and can either move to neighboring cells or remain stationary. The goal is achieved
144   by all agents when they complete their tasks while minimizing the total time taken and avoiding
145   collisions with obstacles or other agents.
146   In real-world applications of this scenario, there may be some unexpected changes in the environ-
147   ment, hence in agents’ states. Changes in the position of obstacles and agents’ goals are the most
148   common dynamic modifications that require real-time adjustments to the strategy, i.e. adaptation of
149   actions.

184   2.3   D ECISION T RANSFORMER
185
      The Decision Transformer treats offline RL as a sequence modeling problem and learns a return-
186
      conditioned policy from an offline dataset Chen et al. (2021a). It has provided a novel perspective
187   to reinforcement learning, and several extensions of this concept have been introduced subsequently
188   Zheng et al. (2022); Lee et al. (2022). In the architecture, embedded tokens of returns, states, and
189   actions are fed into a decoder-only transformer to generate the next tokens autoregressively using
190   a causal self-attention mask. In other words, the model learns the probability of the next token xt
191   conditioned on previous K tokens, Pθ (xt |xt−K<...<t ), where Kis a hyperparameter called context
192   length. To achieve this, we consider sequences of the form:
193
194                          τ i = (xi1 , . . . , xit , . . . , xiT ) where xit = (R̂ti , oit , ait )
195
196   such that R̂ti is return-to-go (rtg) representing the cumulative rewards from the current time step
197   until the end of the episode, oit is the observation, and ait is the action of agent i at time t. Instead
198   of using the discounted rewards as in traditional RL, DT uses rtg so that the model can predict the
199   future actions that achieve the desired return via cross-entropy loss.
200
      We choose DT as the backbone for our method for three major reasons:
201
202          • It is an offline RL algorithm that enables training on an offline dataset and reduces training
203            time significantly since it does not require online interaction with the environment during
204            training.
205          • The transformer architecture effectively addresses the credit assignment problem in long-
206            horizon MAPF scenarios with positive rewards given only at the end.
207
             • DT performs well without extensive reward engineering by conditioning on the desired
208            return.

      2.4   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
211
212   Large Language Models (LLMs) are foundation models pre-trained on extensive text corpora to
213   understand, predict, and generate natural language. The introduction of transformers in 2017, par-
214   ticularly due to their key components of positional encodings and self-attention mechanisms, marked
215   a revolutionary advancement in natural language processing Vaswani et al. (2017). Building upon
      this breakthrough, significant advancements in language models such as BERT, T5, the GPT series,


                                                                4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Llama, and PaLM have extended the capabilities of LLMs and enabled their application to increas-
217   ingly sophisticated tasks Devlin et al. (2018); Raffel et al. (2020); Radford et al. (2018); Achiam
218   et al. (2023); Touvron et al. (2023); Chowdhery et al. (2023). Notably, these models function as
219   few-shot learners for downstream tasks through prompt engineering without requiring further train-
220   ing Chang et al. (2024).
221
222
      In-Context Learning. (ICL) is an approach utilized by LLMs to handle downstream tasks by
      conditioning on relevant input-output examples or demonstrations Brown et al. (2020); Dong et al.
223
      (2022). This prompting process takes place during the inference stage and does not involve any
224
      fine-tuning or updating of the model weights. The provided pairs of instances guide the model to
225   produce accurate responses for the input query. Consequently, a pre-trained LLM model can tackle
226   a wide range of tasks, from translation to question-answering, simply by modifying the examples in
227   the prompt. This flexibility renders ICL a powerful tool for leveraging LLMs in new tasks.
228
229   Chain of Thought. (CoT) process is an advanced demonstration designing technique for prompt
230
      engineering used with LLMs to enhance their problem-solving abilities, particularly for tasks that
      require complex reasoning including arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic reasoning tasks Wei
231
      et al. (2022). This method involves structuring prompts to guide models through a step-by-step
232
      reasoning pathway, ultimately leading to a more informed and accurate output. In CoT prompting,
233   the usual input-output mapping is expanded to include intermediate steps and instead of directly
234   aiming for an answer, the model is prompted with triples: (input, chain of thought, output). Although
235   several advancements in prompt engineering have emerged such as SayCan, ReAct, ToT, and other
236   variations of CoT, we have chosen to utilize CoT in our work due to its simplicity and effectiveness
237   Ahn et al. (2022); Yao et al. (2022; 2024); Wang et al. (2022).

397
398   Stationary Environments. The results presented in Table 3 indicate that DT-based agents demon-
399   strate strong performance (i.e. lower CR, lower MS, and higher SR) compared to SOTA MAPF
400   models2 . We draw the following conclusions from our experiments:
401   1. DT-based agents consistently outperform PRIMAL across all evaluation metrics.
402   2. Our method surpasses DCC in terms of collision rates, indicating that our agents exhibit safer
403   behavior. This is particularly significant since collisions among agents in real-world scenarios, such
404   as warehouses, can lead to substantial costs and safety hazards.
405   3. Our methods outperform DCC in terms of success rate in 20x20 environments, indicating supe-
406   riority in smaller settings.
407   4. Further, the integration of GPT-4o with DT agents enables navigation along even shorter and
408
      safer paths compared to DT agents alone. Although the primary intent of incorporating a LLM was
      to handle real-time environmental changes, the results reveal that it also offers considerable benefits
409
      in static environments.
410

412   once at 15th, 30th, and 50th timesteps respectively. We conduct our experiments according to two
413   difficulty levels; altering the goals of .25 of the agents and .5 of the agents in the environments
414   during inference. Based on the results presented in Table 4, we can draw the following conclusions:

418   often exploring areas near the previous goal location before adjusting its direction. In contrast,
419   when the LLM-based suggestions are introduced concurrently with the dynamic goal change, agents
420   immediately reorient towards their new goal location – this significantly reduces the makespan.
421   2. The success rates achieved by DT and LLM collaboration are equal to or surpass those of the
422
      DT alone in most environmental settings. Notably, when we alter the goal positions of half of
      the agents, the advantages of LLM guidance become particularly evident in complex environments
423
      characterized by larger sizes and a greater number of agents.
424
      3. The reduction in CR in static environments by using an LLM also extends to dynamic environ-
425   ments; DT+GPT-4o consistently achieves lower collision rates, leading to safer agent behavior.
426
427   These findings demonstrate that LLM-assisted DT-based agents are highly effective for real-time
428
      adaptations and offer significant advantages in safety-critical environments.

      4.3      C OMPARATIVE A NALYSIS
461
      Centralized vs. Decentralized Approaches in MAPF. Centralized MAPF algorithms such as
462
      ODrM* cannot scale to larger numbers of agents due to inherent intractability of the MAPF problem,
463
      and as the number of agents increases, the central planner must consider a growing number of poten-
464   tial interactions and paths. Assumptions of full map observability can be unrealistic in environments
465   with sensory limitations or privacy constraints. Decentralized learning-based agents can make opti-
466   mal decisions based on partial and local observations, which allows the model to be replicated across
467   many agents. Constraint-solving based centralized planning methods must also perform expensive
468   re-planning for all agents whenever any environmental change occurs. These issues of scalability,
469   full observability, and adaptability associated with centralized methods can make them impractical
470   in many MAPF settings. In our experiments, we thus skip comparing to centralized methods as it is
471   not an appropriate comparison.
472   Offline vs. Online RL Approaches in MAPF. We show that incorporating offline RL in MAPF
473   (through the DT architecture) is an effective learning strategy that yields performance comparable to
474   other learning-based methods that require online interaction with the environment during training.
475   Table 1 highlights the significant reduction in required effort and our success in eliminating the
476   necessity for real-time interaction with environments during training. Notably, our model does
477   not experience the distributional shift issues that challenge offline RL algorithms when tested in
478   new environments. By utilizing a dataset consisting of a broad range of samples from randomly
479   generated grid environments, we mitigate the risk of distributional shift (analogous to Tobin et al.
480
      (2017)).

498   LLMs’ Efficiency in Real-Time Adjustments. The environments in which agents operate can be
499   dynamic with obstacles being added or removed and goal locations altered in real-time. We observe
500   that the DT agents performs well in response to changes in obstacles, which makes LLM assistance
501
      unnecessary in such cases. Agents quickly adapt and navigate around newly introduced obstacles.
      However, DT agents struggle when goal locations change as indicated in Figure 3. Initially, they
502
      move toward their previous goal positions and explore those areas before eventually redirecting to
503
      the new goals. This delay is critical, as it increases both time and energy consumption in practical
504   applications. Our trials using GPT-4o for 3, 5, 7, and10 timesteps indicate that guidance from GPT-
505   4o for 5 timesteps yields the best performance. By incorporating an LLM, agents can take more
506   efficient actions, which reduces unnecessary movements and prevents repetitive behaviors.
507
508   Prompt Engineering. Prompt engineering is crucial for harnessing the potential of LLMs in com-
509
      plex reasoning tasks. We conducted several trials targeting two main objectives: optimizing the
      prompt itself and selecting appropriate in-context examples. To identify the most effective prompt,
510
      we utilized GPT-4o iteratively. We first described the problem, environment, and task, then asked
511
      GPT-4o to rephrase the problem and setup in its own words. This process was followed by tests in
512   sample environments with corrective feedback provided to GPT-4o. This cycle continued until no
513   further improvements in performance were observed.
514
515
      Additionally, to determine which in-context examples produced the best outcomes, we tested var-
      ious sets of examples: one set of simple example pairs, one set of difficult example pairs, a set
516
      arranged by increasing difficulty (analogous to curriculum learning), and sets with varying numbers
517
      of examples. For challenging environments, we analyzed agent failures and provided step-by-step
518   reasoning for correct actions, similar to Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting. We observed that a set
519   of four example pairs ordered by increasing difficulty was the most effective in our problem setting.
520   Figures 5 and 6 in the Appendix illustrate the environments used in our final testing experiments.

523   5   C ONCLUSION
524
525   Despite their success in diverse areas, LLMs may hallucinate, i.e., yield outputs that deviate from
526   factual accuracy or contextual relevance, particularly in long-horizon reasoning and planning prob-
527
      lems Kambhampati (2024). This research attempts to harness the capabilities of LLMs within MAPF
      and points out contexts wherein the utilization of the models addresses specific challenges.
528
529   Limitations & Future Work. In this paper, we opted for textual inputs because LLMs are still
530   largely unexplored within the MAPF literature. Replicating this approach using visual inputs and
531   Visual Language Models (VLMs) presents a promising direction for future research. Furthermore,
532   our trials with OpenAI’s recent o1-preview (Strawberry) model demonstrated success in environ-
533   ments where GPT-4o failed. This highlights the rapid advancements in LLM capabilities. Given
534   these developments, we believe that the integration of LLMs into MAPF methods is promising.
