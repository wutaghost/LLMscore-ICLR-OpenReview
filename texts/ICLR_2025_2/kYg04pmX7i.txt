                 Drug discovery, and molecular discovery more broadly, can be framed as a se-
                 quential active learning problem —facing a candidate pool, strategies are designed
011
                 to sequentially acquire molecules to assay, aiming to find the best molecule within
012
                 the fewest rounds of trial and error. To automate this process, Bayesian opti-
013              mization (BO) methods can mimic the approach of human medicinal chemists by
014              constructing representations from existing knowledge, quantifying uncertainty for
015              the predictions, and designing acquisition experiments that balance exploitation
016              and exploration. Traditionally, these three stages are implemented using build-
017              ing blocks such as graph neural networks (GNN) as representations, variational
018              inference (VI) or Gaussian process (GP) for uncertainty quantification, and ana-
019              lytical expressions as acquisition functions. To facilitate the integration of both
020              domain-specific and general knowledge into various stages of this process, in this
021
                 paper, we investigate which parts of this workflow can be augmented or replaced
                 by large language models (LLM). To this end, we present COLT 1 , a software li-
022
                 brary for Chemical Optimization with Language- and Topology-based modules,
023
                 and thoroughly benchmark the combination thereof. We found that none of the
024              LLMs, no matter incorporated at what stage, can outperform the simple and fast
025              Bayesian baseline with GNN and GP. As a remedy, we offer a new tuning recipe
026              with direct preference optimization (DPO), where the optimization of synthetic
027              properties can be used to increase the efficiency of the acquisition in real-world
028              tasks.

033   1       I NTRODUCTION : D RUG DISCOVERY AS SEQUENTIAL ACTIVE LEARNING
034
035   A drug discovery campaign—the endeavor to search, from the vast chemical universe, for a new
036   chemical entity with some desired therapeutic efficacy—takes decades and billions of dollars and
037   has its decision-making process traditionally reliant on human experts [1; 2]. When a human expert
038
      makes a decision to prioritize a certain compound(s) in the pre-clinical stage of a drug discovery
      project, their thought process, not without some oversimplification (regarding the multitask, con-
039
      strained, and batched nature of the optimization), could be broken down as follows: first, an un-
040
      derstanding of the current chemical space is constructed from existing medicinal chemistry data, as
041   well as general knowledge distilled from years of training and experience (sometimes referred to
042   as the chemical intuition [3]); second, this understanding is applied to the chemical space yet to be
043   assayed, providing predictions with associated uncertainty; finally, by leveraging expectation and
044   uncertainty, while balancing exploration vs. interpolation, she selects candidates to be synthesized
045   and characterized, generating assay data to refine her belief. This process is carried out iteratively
046   until a therapeutic candidate suitable for clinical trials is identified.
047   Bayesian optimization methods [4; 5] can work analogously to human experts when optimizing
048   over the chemical space, with the aforementioned three stages corresponding to representation, un-
049   certainty quantification, and acquisition in the active learning process. We can use graph neural
050   networks (GNNs) [6; 7; 8; 9; 10; 11; 12] to represent the molecule (§ 2.1), variational inference
051   (VI) [13] or Gaussian process (GP) regression [14; 15] to quantify the uncertainty of the predictions
052   (§ 2.2), and analytical expressions as the acquisition functions (§ 2.3).

      Notably, the majority of early-stage drug discovery problems dwell in the small-data regime. Un-
080
      like the abundant cat pictures or blog posts readily scraped from the internet, in drug discovery,
081
      each data point is the result of costly and time-consuming wet lab experiments. Consequently,
082   even the largest pharmaceutical companies rarely work with a candidate pool larger than 1 million
083   compounds [16]—the number being smaller in magnitude for biotech startups. ChEMBL [17], the
084   largest public chemical data repository, contains merely 15 million assays of various kinds over 1.8
085   million compounds. On one hand, this makes principled Bayesian models particularly well suited,
086   thanks to their inherent regularization and uncertainty quantification. On the other hand, while work-
087   ing with graph-structured data provides useful inductive biases, incorporating these biases into such
088   Bayesian, graph-based models remains challenging [18; 19], unlike LLMs, which can easily ab-
089   sorb language-structured knowledge by training on texts. In the chemistry domain, for instance, the
090
      success of LLMs has been demonstrated through numerous models fine-tuned for diverse modeling
      tasks [20; 21; 22; 23].
091
092
093   Main contributions. In this paper, we offer a comparison between the efficacy of knowledge
094
      versus inductive bias by evaluating the optimization efficiency of LLMs against that of BO with
      graph-based representation. First, we review the abstractions underlying the building blocks of
095
      Bayesian active learning and propose solutions to replace each of them with LLMs. To this end,
096
      we introduce a package for Chemical Optimization with Language- and Topology-based models
097   (COLT), which offers an easy-to-use API for molecular active learning. Using this program, we
098   combinatorially benchmark the effect of LLMs when placed at various stages of active learning,
099   and find that none of the solutions are as performant as the GNN + GP baseline. Offering a beacon
100   of hope amidst the negative results, we provide a general tuning recipe based on direct preference
101   optimization (DPO) [24], where learning occurs directly on the per-step acquisition trajectory, and
102   find that tuning on synthetic datasets has a positive impact on optimizing real-world tasks.
103
104   Limitations. In this paper, we only consider the optimization on a finite candidate space, whereas
105   the combinatorial chemical space is infinite and can be treated continuously [25]. Additionally,
106   to enable rapid benchmarking with a limited computational footprint, we restrict our analysis to
107   small datasets, resulting in high variance across the benchmark results. Similarly, we restrict our
      experiments to smaller, open-source LLMs.

      2     BAYESIAN OPTIMIZATION (BO) FOR MOLECULAR ACTIVE LEARNING
109
110   First, we formalize the drug discovery campaign introduced in § 1. Given a finite pool of candidate
111   chemical compounds, represented by chemical graphs F = {Gi , i = 1, 2, . . . N }, each associated
112   with a potency y(Gi ) ∈ R, of which a noisy observation is expensive to acquire, we are interested
113   in finding the compound with the highest potency G ∗ = argmaxi y(Gi ) with fewest function evau-
114   lations. This can be achieved via active learning, starting with an empty portfolio P = ∅, and in
115   each round, choosing a compound from the candidate pool, F, based on a decision informed by
116   the current portfolio, Ĝ = D(F|P), Ĝ ∈ F. Subsequently, we subtract this compound from the
117   candidate pool to add it to the portfolio, F ← F \ {Ĝ}, P ← P ∪ {Ĝ}.
118
      Bayesian optimization (BO) is a powerful approach in active learning where the decision D(F|P) is
119
      constructed in a modular way. Firstly, we extract fixed D-dimensional features from the compounds
120   to form a representation: H = h(G) ∈ RD ; secondly, this representation is used to construct
121   a predictive posterior distribution with uncertainty quantification: p(y|G; Θ), Θ = θ(H), whose
122   parameters Θ is is mapped from the graph representations. Lastly, an acquisition function, α, is
123   applied on this predictive posterior to form a score, on the basis of which a decision is made: Ĝ =
124   D(F|P) = argmaxG∈F α(p(y|G)).
125
      In sum, the acquisition trajectory represents a sequential decision process, where the probability of
126
      a given trajectory can be written as:

      Large language models (LLMs)—neural networks composed primarily of transformer [29]—have
219   shown surprising promise [30; 31; 32; 33] in representing and generating text-structured data and
220   gained popularity very quickly in recent years. Their impressive generative ability led the field to
221   describe them with personifying terms, such as understanding, and to employ them in seemingly
222   impossible tasks, from reasoning [34] to planning [35].
223
      In recent years, LLMs [31; 30] have been routinely incorporated into active learning pipelines, from
224
      hyperparameter search [36] to material design [37; 38]. Little attention, however, has been paid to
225   dissecting which stage of the active learning process can LLMs be most effective in. To answer
226   this question, and to compare the efficacy of LLMs with time-tested workflows, we propose three
227   solutions to incorporate LLMs into molecular active learning by modularly replacing components
228   outlined in § 2 by LLMs.
229
230   3.1   LLM AS REPRESENTATION
231
232   h = transformers.pipeline("feature-extraction")(molecule.smiles)
233
234   Replacing the GNNs (§ 2.1), one can use an LLM as a feature extractor to come up with the rep-
235   resentation of molecules. The input of the LLM is a string representation of the graph, such as
236   the SMILES string [39] common in molecular representation. This is consistent with the method
237
      Kristiadi et al. [38] used for material discovery.
238   Even before the era of LLMs, to represent molecules as strings and feeding them into transformer- or
239   recurrent neural networks (RNN)-based models have long been used in various pipelines of molecu-
240   lar modeling, from property prediction to active learning [25], where pretraining on large ensembles
241   of data has been proven as an effective avenue towards better performance [40]. We are interested in
242   testing whether the added complexity and knowledge in LLM would help refine this representation.

426   LLMs are routinely instruction-tuned [56] on supervised learning tasks to offer better predictions and
427   “understandings” of certain candidate spaces. Without uncertainty calibration and carefully chosen
428   acquisition functions, reliable predictions cannot be readily translated into efficient experimental
429   design. In this section, we provide a general recipe to readily optimize the acquisition efficiency
430   of LLMs in an end-to-end manner, i.e. taking over all of the representation, uncertainty quantifica-
431   tion, and acquisition steps. We achieve this by learning from all of the choices (Equation 1) from
      successful trials driven by GNN + GP active learning algorithms.

453   7     C ONCLUSION .
454
455   In this paper, we abstract drug discovery as a sequential active learning process, and survey the
456   Bayesian optimization (BO) building blocks that can lead to principled and efficient experiment
457   design. Next, we review possible ways for these building blocks to be replaced by LLMs. A software
458   package, COLT, is provided for carrying out active learning experiments and assessing its efficiency.
459   Using this package, we thoroughly benchmark the effects of replacing BO building blocks with
460
      LLMs, and found that such replacement causes only slowdown and performance drop. This suggests
      that a large quantity of noisy knowledge (represented by LLMs) is not as effective as a principled
461
      model with appropriate inductive biases (BO + GNN). Nevertheless, under the framework of direct
462
      preference optimization (DPO), we provide a recipe for tuning LLMs directly for generating step-
463   wise optimization decisions.
