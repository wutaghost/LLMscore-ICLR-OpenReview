011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            X-ray image-based medical report generation (MRG) is a pivotal area in artifi-
014            cial intelligence which can significantly reduce diagnostic burden and patient wait
015
               time. Despite significant progress, we believe that the task has reached a bot-
               tleneck due to the limited benchmark datasets and the existing large models’ in-
016
               sufficient capability enhancements in this specialized domain. Specifically, the
017
               recently released CheXpert Plus dataset provides only the dataset itself, lacking
018            comparative evaluation algorithms and their performances. This situation makes
019            the training, evaluation, and comparison of subsequent algorithms challenging.
020            Thus, we conduct a comprehensive benchmarking of existing mainstream X-ray
021            report generation models and Large Language Models (LLMs), on the CheX-
022            pert Plus dataset. We believe that the proposed benchmark can provide a solid
023            comparative basis for subsequent algorithms and serve as a guide for researchers
024            to quickly grasp the state-of-the-art models in this field. More importantly, we
025            propose a new large model for X-ray image report generation by using a multi-
026
               stage pre-training strategy which involves Mamba based self-supervised autore-
               gressive generation, X-ray-report contrastive learning, and supervised fine-tuning.
027
               Extensive experimental results demonstrate that the proposed Mamba based au-
028
               toregressive pre-training can effectively encode X-ray images, and the image-text
029            contrastive pre-training further aligns the feature spaces, achieving better experi-
030            mental results. All the source codes will be released upon acceptance.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   X-ray image based Medical Report Generation (MRG) is a critical research problem in artificial in-
036   telligence, which targets describing the findings or impressions from the given X-ray data via natural
037   language. The successful implementation of this task can significantly reduce the diagnostic burden
038   on physicians, decrease patient wait time, and foster the positive application of artificial intelli-
039
      gence. However, the path to progress in this direction is not smooth sailing, there remain formidable
      challenges that need to be addressed. The challenging issues include image interpretation, data an-
040
      notation, heterogeneity issues, consistency and standardization of reports, diversity and variability
041
      of diseases, interpretability of algorithms, etc. How to address these challenges and further improve
042   the quality of medical report generation remains an urgent research problem.
043
044   After revisiting the mainstream algorithms of X-ray image medical report generation, we find that
045
      datasets like IU X-ray and MIMIC-CXR are widely used for the training and evaluation of report
      generation models. However, the IU X-ray only contains 7,470 images and 3,955 radiology reports
046
      samples, which is rather limited, especially in the large model era. The recently released CheXpert
047
      Plus dataset Chambon et al. (2024) is a large-scale dataset for the X-ray report generation, however,
048   they did not release comparative methods, making it difficult for subsequent algorithms to conduct
049   experiments and comparisons on this dataset. Therefore, we conduct a comprehensive benchmark-
050   ing of existing open-sourced mainstream X-ray report generation models, Large Language Models
051   (LLMs), and Vision-Language Models (VLMs), termed CXPMRG-Bench, on the newly released
052   CheXpert Plus dataset, as shown in Fig. 1. The completion of this work can also help researchers
053   identify which large models and algorithms are currently leading in the field of X-ray report gener-
      ation.

075   On the other hand, most mainstream algorithms follow the encoder-decoder framework which usu-
076   ally adopts the vision encoder (e.g., ResNet He et al. (2016), Transformer Vaswani et al. (2017))
077   to process the given X-ray data and a text decoder (e.g., LSTM Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997),
078   GRU Chung et al. (2014), Transformer Vaswani et al. (2017)) for report generation. Along with the
079   development of pre-trained LLM and VLM, the quality of medical reports is enhanced significantly.
080   There are already some researchers who exploit the pre-training for the X-ray report generation.
081
      For example, Wang et al. Wang et al. (2024c) propose high-definition X-ray vision models using
      context-aware masked auto-encoder. CXR-CLIP You et al. (2023) is a new pre-training method
082
      that generates more image-text pairs and introduces contrastive loss to enhance the discriminative
083
      power of images and texts, effectively learning features in the CXR domain. PTUnifier Chen et al.
084   (2023c) proposes a simple and effective method that utilizes visual and textual prompt pools to
085   make the model compatible with different types of inputs, thereby unifying the advantages of fusion
086   encoders and dual encoders. However, we believe these models may be limited by the following
087   issues: Firstly, the Transformer vision backbone brings huge computational costs O(N 2 ), which is
088   not hardware friendly; Secondly, pure X-ray images are abundant and readily collectible, but paired
089   X-ray and report data are relatively scarce. Current X-ray models are pre-trained in a single stage
090   using X-ray image or image-report data only, and fail to exploit the full potential of these diverse
091   X-ray data, therefore, the overall performance may be limited.
092   To address the issues mentioned above, in this work, we exploit multi-stage pre-training for the
093   X-ray image MRG task and propose the MambaXray-VL large model, including self-supervised
094   autoregressive generation and Xray-report contrastive learning, and supervised fine-tuning on the
095   downstream report generation datasets, as shown in Fig. 2. Specifically speaking, we first partition
096   and feed the X-ray image into the Mamba network to predict the next tokens based on previous
097   context tokens in an autoregressive generation manner. This will enhance the vision perception
098
      ability of X-ray significantly using the relative low-cost Mamba network (O(N )). For the second
      stage, we feed the paired X-ray image and corresponding report into the Mamba vision backbone
099
      and text encoder (Bio ClinicalBERT Alsentzer et al. (2019), Llama2 Touvron et al. (2023)) for
100
      contrastive learning. It will align the X-ray image and reports using the pre-trained feature space.
101   After that, we conduct supervised fine-tuning on each downstream X-ray report generation dataset to
102   achieve higher performance by feeding the X-ray image into the pre-trained Mamba vision backbone
103   network and LLM decoder network. Extensive experiments on three MRG benchmark datasets
104   demonstrate that our pre-trained MambaXray-VL model achieves state-of-the-art performance.
105
      To sum up, the contributions of this paper can be summarized as the following three aspects:

      bon et al. (2024), termed CXPMRG-Bench, which covers 19 mainstream X-ray medical report

150   2.1   OVERVIEW
151
152   As shown in Fig. 2, we propose a new multi-stage pre-training strategy for the X-ray image med-
153
      ical report generation, including self-supervised autoregressive generation, Xray-report contrastive
      learning, and supervised fine-tuning. The key insight of our multi-stage pre-training instead of joint
154
      training is that the aligned Xray-report data are limited, but there are more publicly available X-ray
155
      images. Thus, we first pre-train a large-scale vision backbone network on the X-ray images by us-
156   ing the Mamba layers, due to a better balance between the computational cost and accuracy. More
157   importantly, we adopt the autoregressive generation to achieve self-supervised learning on the X-ray
158   images. It performs similar or even better than the widely used MAE (Masked Auto-Encoder) pre-
159   training strategy on this task. Then, we transfer the Mamba vision backbone to the second stage, i.e.,
160   Xray-report contrastive learning. Specifically, we feed the paired data into the pre-trained Mamba
161   vision backbone and language encoder for the vision-language feature extraction. This stage will
      project the vision and language representations into a shared feature space to bridge the vision-

162
      semantic gaps. Finally, we conduct supervised fine-tuning on the training subset of downstream
163   datasets for the X-ray medical report generation.

      2.2   M ULTI -S TAGE P RE - TRAINING
166
167   As illustrated in Fig. 2, our proposed MambaXray-VL large model contains three training stages
168   which will be introduced in the following paragraphs respectively.
169
      • Stage #1: Auto-regressive Generation for Mamba Vision Encoder Pre-training. To make
170   full use of existing X-ray images, we conduct self-supervised learning to obtain a strong vision
171   backbone network. Different from the widely used MAE (Masked Auto-Encoder)-based frame-
172   work, in this work, we find that the autoregressive generation based framework works similarly
173   or even better for the X-ray images, as inspired by the success of autoregressive generation in
174   ChatGPT OpenAI (2023), GPT-4 Achiam et al. (2023), and ARM Ren et al. (2024). Let’s de-
175   note the X-ray image as I ∈ R192×192×3 . We first partition it into non-overlapping image patches
176   Pi ∈ R16×16×3 , i = {1, 2, ..., N } and project them into visual tokens Ti ∈ R1024 , i = {1, 2, ..., N }
177   by using a convolutional layer (kernel size 16 × 16). Here, N is 144 when the resolution of the input
178
      X-ray image is set as 3 × 192 × 192. Then, we feed the visual tokens into the Vim Zhu et al. (2024)
      backbone network for feature extraction whose complicity O(N ) is much lower than the widely
179
      used Transformer O(N 2 ). The key operation of Vim is the Mamba block (a specific variation of
180
      State Space Model Wang et al. (2024d)), as shown in Fig. 2. The visual tokens will first be normal-
181   ized and fed into the SSM and scan branches. The outputs will be multiplied and added with residual
182   connections. The SwiGLU Shazeer (2020) is adopted to further process output features before be-
183   ing fed into subsequent Mamba blocks. Finally, a MLP layer is adopted for token reconstruction by
184   using the auto-regressive generation loss function.
185

531
      In this work, we propose to benchmark the CheXpert Plus dataset by re-training the mainstream X-
      ray report generation models and large language models. This benchmark will help identify which
532
      large models and algorithms are leading in this domain, significantly promoting academic progress
533
      and technological development. In addition, we also propose a new Mamba-based vision-language
534   large model for the X-ray image based medical report generation. It involves three pre-training
535   stages which make full use of auto-regressive generation loss, Xray-report contrastive learning, and
536   supervised fine-tuning. We validate the effectiveness of our proposed pre-trained large model on IU
537   X-ray, MIMIC-CXR, and CheXpert Plus datasets. From the newly built benchmark, we can find that
538   the current large language models still perform poorly on the report generation task. In our future
539   works, we will consider introducing structured knowledge graphs into the large language model to
      guide the report generation.
