011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Preference-based reinforcement learning (PBRL) in the offline setting has suc-
014            ceeded greatly in industrial applications such as chatbots. A two-step learning
015
               framework where one applies a reinforcement learning step after a reward model-
               ing step has been widely adopted for the problem. However, such a method faces
016
               challenges from the risk of reward hacking and the complexity of reinforcement
017
               learning. To overcome the challenge, our insight is that both challenges come from
018            the state-actions not supported in the dataset. Such state-actions are unreliable and
019            increase the complexity of the reinforcement learning problem at the second step.
020            Based on the insight, we develop a novel two-step learning method called PRC:
021            preference-based reinforcement learning with constrained actions. The high-level
022            idea is to limit the reinforcement learning agent to optimize over a constrained
023            action space that excludes the out-of-distribution state-actions. We empirically
024            verify that our method has high learning efficiency on various datasets in robotic
025            control environments.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) is a learning paradigm for solving sequential decision-making
030
      problems and has rich real-world applications (Sutton & Barto, 2018; Luong et al., 2019; Haydari
031   & Yılmaz, 2020). However, traditional DRL approaches require numerical reward feedback during
032   learning, which in practice can be hard to design or obtain. In contrast, non-numerical preference
033   feedback is more accessible in most cases (Wirth & Fürnkranz, 2013). As a result, preference-based
034   reinforcement learning (PBRL) that only requires preference feedback has become a more realistic
035   learning paradigm (Christiano et al., 2017). Recently, as a special instance of PBRL, reinforcement
036   learning from human feedback (RLHF) that utilizes human preference has drawn much attention
037   and achieved great success in many NLP tasks (Ouyang et al., 2022). Although a majority of current
038   PBRL works focus on the online learning setting (Ibarz et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2021), the PBRL
039
      learning in the offline setting is more realistic Rafailov et al. (2023). In the online learning setting,
      the learning agent continuously interacts with the learning environment to collect new preference
040
      data throughout the training process; In the offline learning setting, the agent receives a batch of
041
      preference data before training starts. Compared to the online learning setting, the offline learning
042   setting is more accessible as it does not require customized feedback throughout the training process.
043   It is critical to investigate how to make PBRL learning efficient and practical in the offline setting.
044   Particularly, we are interested in the most popular learning framework that is widely applied in
045   various PBRL literature and industrial applications, which we call ‘two-step learning.’
046
      Two-step learning framework: In a typical two-step learning framework (Ibarz et al., 2018; Chris-
047
      tiano et al., 2017), the first learning step is reward modeling, where the learning agent approximates
048   the reward model of the environment that can best explain the observations from the training dataset.
049   The second learning step is a reinforcement learning phase where the learning agent learns a policy
050   based on the reward model acquired in the previous step. There are multiple reasons behind the vast
051   popularity of the two-step learning paradigm: 1. similar to the motivation of inverse reinforcement
052   learning (Arora & Doshi, 2021), the learned environment model provides a succinct and transferable
053   definition of the task; 2. the method is modular in that each phase of learning can be implemented
      by existing well-developed methods. Despite its popularity, this method faces two main challenges.

054
             • Reward over-optimization/Pessimistic learning: Pessimistic learning has always been
055            the key challenge in offline learning settings. Due to the distribution mismatch between the
056            trajectories induced by the policy and that of the dataset, the agent cannot properly evaluate
057            all possible possible policies.
058
             • Reinforcement learning complexity: Implementing Reinforcement learning efficiently is
059
               known to be challenging Dulac-Arnold et al. (2021). In addition, for preference feedback,
060            the reward model is learned from indirect preference signals rather than actual true rewards.
061            The learned reward model may look very different from the actual reward, making the
062            training step more unstable.
063
064   The challenges are empirically verified in Section 5. To solve the first challenge, the most popular
065   approach is to apply a penalty on the evaluated performance of a policy during the reinforcement
066   learning step. The penalty is the KL divergence between a policy and the behavior policy that can
067   best describe the training data distribution. Under the penalty, the agent is encouraged to stay close
068   to the dataset distribution while trying to find a policy that optimizes the learned reward model.
069
      However, the reinforcement learning problem in this problem may not be easy to solve due to the
      second challenge, which is empirically verified in Section 5.
070
071   The approaches to solving the second challenge mainly focus on the bandit setting for NLP applica-
072   tions. The bandit setting is a special case of RL that has no state transition. Current methods include
073   direct preference alignment Rafailov et al. (2023) and rejection sampling Liu et al. (2023), both of
074   which cannot be directly applied to the general RL setting. To the best of our knowledge, no existing
075
      two-phase learning approach considers tackling both challenges simultaneously.
076   Our contributions. In this work, we propose a novel two-step learning PBRL algorithm PRC that
077   tackles the above two main challenges simultaneously. Our key insight is that both challenges are
078   induced by the same element: the state actions that are out of the dataset distribution. For pessimistic
079   learning, the agent must avoid the policies that are more likely to visit such state-actions. These poli-
080   cies are not well covered by the dataset, and the agent cannot evaluate their performance accurately.
081
      Meanwhile, these out-of-distribution state-actions contribute to the complexity of the learning prob-
      lem. Therefore, our insight is to constrain the action space to exclude all such out-of-distribution
082
      state-actions. Based on the insight, the key step in our approach is to constrain the action space to
083
      include only the actions with a high probability of being sampled by a behavior clone policy that
084   represents the dataset action distribution. In other words, our method only focuses on the policies
085   that are in the neighborhood of the behavior clone policy. As a result, our method not only inherits
086   the pessimistic idea of behavior regularization but also reduces the complexity of the corresponding
087   reinforcement learning problem. In the experiments, we construct offline PBRL datasets based on
088   standard offline RL benchmark D4RL Fu et al. (2020). The details of dataset construction can be
089   found in Section 5. We empirically verify that on the offline datasets we construct, our approach can
090   always find policies of higher performance than the behavior policies of the dataset, suggesting that
091   the improvement in our PBRL training is reliable.

      Offline Reinforcement learning: There have been many studies on the standard offline reinforce-
096   ment learning setting with reward feedback (Levine et al., 2020; Cheng et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2021),
097   in contrast to preference feedback considered in our setting. The key idea behind offline reinforce-
098   ment learning is pessimism which encourages the learning agent to focus on the policies that are
099   supported by the dataset (Cheng et al., 2022). However, the gap between learning from reward
100   feedback and preference feedback is not clear at present.
101
      Online PBRL: Preference-based reinforcement learning is popular in the online learning scenario.
102   Various types of preference models and preference labels have been considered in the tabular case
103   (Fürnkranz et al., 2012; Wirth et al., 2017). Similar to the works that consider the general case, in our
104   work we consider the preference models to be Bradley-Terry models and output binary preference
105   labels (Ibarz et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2021). The two-phase learning approach mentioned in Section
106   1 is prevalent in the online PBRL setting. Note that directly applying online methods to the offline
107   scenario is also likely to be inefficient even when it is possible (Van Hasselt et al., 2018), as the
      online learning approaches do not need to be pessimistic.

108
      Offline PBRL: Offline preference-based reinforcement learning is a relatively new topic. Zhan et al.
109   (2023) propose an optimization problem following the two-phase learning framework and theoret-
110   ically prove that the solution to the problem is a near-optimal policy with respect to the training
111   dataset. However, how to solve the optimization problem in practice remains unknown. Concur-
112   rently, Zhu et al. (2023) formulates a similar optimization problem under the linear assumption on
113   the environment and proposes a way to solve the problem, but the results cannot be extended to the
114   general case without the linear assumption. Kim et al. (2023) study methods for learning the utility
115   models based on preference provided by humans. They propose to use a transformer-based archi-
116   tecture to predict the utility function behind the preference model. Rafailov et al. (2023) propose a
117
      method that directly learns a policy without learning a utility model explicitly. Unlike the setting
      considered in our work, they focus on the NLP task and require trajectories in each pair to have the
118
      same prompt (initial state). Hejna & Sadigh (2023) propose a method that learns a policy and the Q
119
      function of the environment instead of the utility function. They also require access to an additional
120   trajectory dataset that only contains trajectories to make their algorithm efficient. Our work focuses
121   on developing a practical and efficient two-phase learning approach for the offline PBRL setting that
122   only has access to a preference dataset with no requirement on the trajectories in the dataset.
123
      RLHF: Reinforcement learning from human feedback is a special instance of PBRL that its prefer-
124
      ence labels are provided by humans. It has been a popular topic recently. Success in various robotic
125
      control and NLP taasks have been achieved by fine-tuning pre-trained models through reinforcement
126   learning based on preference feedback from human (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022). These
127   studies focus on solving specific tasks instead of the general problem considered in our work.

133   3.1   O FFLINE P REFERENCE R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING P ROBLEM
134
135   We consider an offline preference-based reinforcement learning setting (Zhan et al., 2023). An
136   environment is characterized by an incomplete Markov Decision Process (MDP) without a reward
137   function M = (S, A, P) where S is the state space, A is the action space, and P is the state
138   transition function. A policy π : S → ∆(A) is a mapping from a state to a probability distribution
139   over the action space, representing a way to behave in the environment by taking the action at a
140   state. A deterministic policy is a special kind of policy that maps a state to a single action. We
141   use ΠS,A to denote the class of all policies, and ΠDS,A to denote the class of deterministic policies.
142   A trajectory of length t is a sequence of states and actions (s1 , a1 , . . . , st , at , st+1 ) where ai ∈
143   A, si+1 ∼ P (·|si , ai )∀i ∈ [t]. A preference model F takes a pair of trajectories τ1 , τ2 as input, and
144
      outputs a preference σ ∈ {≻, ≺}, indicating its preference over the two trajectories, i.e. τ1 ≻ τ2
      or τ1 ≺ τ2 . In this work, following the conventions, we consider the preference models that are
145
      Bradley-Terry (BT) models (Bradley & Terry, 1952). Specifically, there exists an utility function
146
      u : S × A → R, such that the probability distribution of the output preference of F given τ1 , τ2
147   satisfies:

                        Pr{τ1 ≻ τ2 } =     P                        P                                             (1)
152                                    exp( (s,a)∈τ1 u(s, a)) + exp( (s,a)∈τ2 u(s, a))

156   utility of a trajectory generated by the policy: Eτ ∼(π,P) [ s,a∈τ u(s, a)]. An offline dataset
157   D = {(τ11 , τ21 , σ 1 ), . . . , (τ1N , τ2N , σ N )} consists of multiple preference data over different trajec-
158   tory pairs. We use the term ‘behavior policy’ to represent how the trajectories are generated in the
159   dataset. A learning agent is given access to the incomplete MDP M = (S, A, P) of the environment

161   learn a policy that has high performance on the utility function u of the preference model F , and we
      say a learning algorithm is efficient if it can learn such a high-performing policy.

227   agent solves an RL problem where the MDP is (S, A′ , P, R).

230   4.2   A NALYSIS
231
232   Here, we show that the reinforcement learning step in PRC is essentially optimizing the reward
233   function under a special behavior regularization constraint. Recap the prevalent PBRL framework in
234   Alg 1. The pessimism in the algorithm is achieved by using KL regularization, which encourages the
235   policy to be not very different from the behavior policy. Here, we consider a different regularization
236   as below.

244   arg maxπ∈ΠS,A Eτ ∼(π,P) (s,a)∈τ [û(s, a)] − α · Cp (π, πb ).       Under such a regularization,
245   the optimal policy can only be supported in a constrained action space where the probability density
246   of the behavior policy is greater than the threshold p, as otherwise, it will suffer from an infinite

248
      Next, we discuss why choosing such a regularization is reasonable. First, unlike the soft constraint,
249
      such as KL regularization, our regularization is a hard constraint that forces the policy to stay close to
250
      the behavior policy. It is conceptually more conservative in that it explicitly decreases the possibility
251   of reward hacking by outputting some policy that is not close to the behavior policy. Second, it
252   reduces the complexity of finding the optimal policy under the regularization. This can make the
253   reinforcement learning step more accessible and reduce the optimization loss at this step.

300   5.1   S ETUP
301
      Dataset: Following previous studies Hejna & Sadigh (2023), we construct our offline preference
302   dataset from D4RL benchmark Fu et al. (2020), and generate synthetic preference following the
303   standard techniques in previous PBRL studies (Kim et al., 2023; Christiano et al., 2017). Based
304   on the definition of a standard offline preference-based reinforcement learning setting in Section
305   3, given a reward-based dataset from D4RL, we construct a preference-based dataset through the
306   following process:
307
308   1. Randomly sample pairs of trajectory clips from the D4RL dataset. Following previous studies
309
         (Christiano et al., 2017), the length of the clip is set to be 20 steps.
310   2. For each pair of trajectory clips, compute the probability of a trajectory to be preferred based
311      on the reward signals. To ensure consistency between different datasets, the reward signals are
312      regularized to be bound in [−1, 1].
313   3. For each pair of trajectory clips, randomly generate a preference label through a Bernoulli trial
314      with the probability computed above.
315   4. Return the preference dataset consisting of the trajectory clip pairs and the corresponding prefer-
316      ence labels.
317
318   We consider various datasets from D4RL to represent the general learning scenarios. The robot con-
319   trol environments we choose include ‘Hopper’, ‘HalfCheetah’, and ‘Walker’. The types of trajecto-
320
      ries we choose include ‘Medium’, ‘Medium-Replay’, and ‘Medium-Expert’. To make the number
      of state-action in the dataset aligned with that of the D4RL benchmark, the total number of trajectory
321
      pairs with a preference label is 1 × 106 .
322
323   Baseline Algorithms: Here, we consider multiple two-step learning algorithms as baselines and a
      state-of-the-art attack in the related offline PBRL setting, which are listed below:


                                                            6

324
      1. Two-step learning with KL-regularization: We adopt the traditional two-step learning baseline
325      with KL-regularization as introduced in Section 3. To make it a stronger baseline and the com-
326      parison fairer, we allow the algorithm to initialize from the behavior clone policy.
327
328   2. Naive two-step learning: To show a naive baseline where pessimistic learning is entirely absent,
329      we adopt a naive two-step learning baseline, which is the same as the traditional two-step learning
330      above except that there is no regularization at all during the reinforcement learning step. Note
331
         that we also allow the algorithm to initialize from the behavior clone policy in this baseline.
332   3. Reward modeling followed by standard offline RL algorithms: Another simple yet efficient two-
333      step learning approach for offline PBRL problems is combining reward modeling with a standard
334      reward-based offline RL algorithm. First, a reward model is learned from the preference dataset.
335      Then, the reward model is used to provide scalar reward labels to the state-action in the dataset.
336      Finally, we apply a standard offline RL algorithm IQL Kostrikov et al. (2021) training on the
337      dataset with scalar reward labels.
338
      4. Oracle: The oracle is trained with true rewards instead of preference labels. Here, we apply
339
         IQL training on the base D4RL dataset with true reward signals. Note that the information from
340
         the reward signals is strictly more than that from the preference signals in this case. The oracle
341      should be considered as an upper bound on the performance of an offline PBRL algorithm.
342
343   5. Inverse preference learning (IPL) Hejna & Sadigh (2023): IPL is the state-of-the-art learning
344      algorithm for a related offline PBRL setting that requires a preference dataset and a behavior
345      dataset. To avoid underestimating the performance of IPL, we apply this algorithm to our PBRL
346      setting and allow it to check the behaviors in the full D4RL dataset.
347
348   To ensure a fair comparison, all the methods that require reward modeling share the same learned
349   reward model during training.
350   Training setup: To learn a reward model, we follow a standard supervised learning framework.
351   Specifically, we use a multilayer perceptron (MLP) structure for the neural network to approximate
352   the utility model. The neural network consists of 3 hidden layers, and each has 64 neurons. We use
353   a tanh function as the output activation so that the output is bound between [−1, 1].
354
      To train a deterministic behavior clone policy, the neural network we use to represent the clone policy
355   has the same structure as that for the utility model. To train a stochastic behavior clone policy, the
356   network outputs the mean and standard deviation of a Gaussian distribution separately. The network
357   is an MLP with 3 hidden layers each with 64 neurons. The last layer is split into two for the two
358   outputs. For the mean output, we use a linear function for the last layer. For the standard deviation,
359   we use a linear function and an exp activation for the last layer.
360
      In the reinforcement learning step, we use either the SAC algorithm or the PPO algorithm, depending
361   on the dataset. The neural network for the actor is the same as the network for training a stochastic
362   behavior clone policy. The neural network for the critic is the same as the network for training the
363   reward model.

      Here, we empirically verify that by training on a constrained action space, the PCA algorithm

395   performance trend of the learned policies measured on the learned reward model is aligned with

      5.5   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING C OMPLEXITY IN PBRL
446
447
      Here, we empirically show that training on a reward model learned from preference signals is harder
448   than that from true reward signals. Given the same D4RL dataset, we train two reward models based
449   on the true reward signals and synthetic preference signals. Then, we train an efficient RL algorithm
450   on the two rewards and compare the learning efficiency in the two cases.
451
      The results in Figure 3 show that when learning on the reward model trained from true rewards,
452
      the RL agent can quickly learn some policies that have high performance on the reward model. In
453
      comparison, it is hard for the agent to learn well on the reward model from preference signals. This
454   indicates that it is harder to learn from a reward model that is trained on preference signals instead
455   of true reward signals.

473   6     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
474
475   In this work, we propose a novel two-step learning algorithm PRC for the offline PBRL setting.
476   We empirically show that the PRC has high learning efficiency and provide evidence for why it is
477   a more efficient two-step learning algorithm than others. Our framework is limited to the typical
478   offline learning setting and does not answer the question of which trajectories are more worthy of
479
      receiving preference labels. Our experimental evaluation is limited to continuous control problems,
      the standard benchmark in RL studies.
