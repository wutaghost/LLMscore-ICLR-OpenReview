011
012
                The context window of large language models (LLMs) has been extended sig-
                nificantly in recent years. However, while the context length that the LLM can
013
                process has grown, the capability of the model to accurately reason over that
014
                context degrades noticeably. This occurs because modern LLMs often become
015             overwhelmed by the vast amount of information in the context; when answering
016             questions, the model must identify and reason over relevant evidence sparsely
017             distributed throughout the text. To alleviate the challenge of long-context rea-
018             soning, we develop a retrieve-then-reason framework, enabling LLMs to reason
019             over relevant evidence collected during an intermediate retrieval step. We find
020             that modern LLMs struggle to accurately retrieve relevant facts and instead, often
021             hallucinate “retrieved facts”, resulting in flawed reasoning and the production of
022             incorrect answers. To address these issues, we introduce ALR2 , a method that
023
                augments the long-context reasoning capability of LLMs via an explicit two-stage
                procedure, i.e., aligning LLMs with the objectives of both retrieval and reasoning.
024
                We demonstrate the efficacy of ALR2 for mitigating performance degradation in
025
                long-context reasoning tasks. Through extensive experiments on long-context QA
026             benchmarks, we find our method to outperform competitive baselines by large
027             margins, achieving at least 8.4 and 7.9 EM gains on the long-context versions of
028             HotpotQA and SQuAD datasets, respectively.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The ability for large language models (LLMs) to reason over long contexts is critical in many
034   downstream tasks, e.g., document analysis (Wang et al., 2024a), multi-hop tool use (Yao et al., 2023),
035   agents with long history (Park et al., 2023), etc. Significant efforts have been made to extend the
036   effective context length of LLMs, ranging from investigating how different modules in Transformer
037
      (Vaswani, 2017) architecture impact long-context performance (Su et al., 2024a; Chiang & Cholak,
      2022; Xiao et al., 2024) to proposing new architectures with improved efficiency (Dai et al., 2019;
038
      Beltagy et al., 2020; Chevalier et al., 2023).
039
040   While these developments are promising, in our preliminary study, we show that the long-context
041   performance of LLMs varied significantly across different tasks. In particular, we design a series
042   of experiments to evaluate the performance of LLMs on long-context retrieval1 and reasoning
043
      (Figure 1). We observe that, when tasked to generate answers by directly reasoning over the full
      context, performance degrades as the input context grows. In contrast, when tasked with retrieving
044
      the set of evidence relevant to the question, the performance of LLMs is only mildly affected by the
045
      growth of the input context.
046
047   Those findings motivate us to explicitly leverage an intermediate retrieval step to address the challenge
048   of LLMs in long-context reasoning. In our proposed retrieve-then-reason framework, an LLM
049
      functions as both a retriever and a reasoner. When tasked to answer a question, it first collects
      the relevant evidence sparsely distributed across the context, and then derives the final answer by
050
      reasoning over that collected evidence. We also find that modern LLMs struggle to accurately retrieve
051
      “relevant facts”, i.e., simply prompting them to retrieve supporting evidence often results in severe

      Setup We assess the long-context capabilities of LLMs using a series of increasingly challenging
104

105
106              • Task 1: Passkey Retrieval: A sentence, such as ”The passkey of Alice is KEY,” is inserted
107                into the long context, and the LLM is asked to retrieve Alice’s passkey. The ”KEY” is a
                   randomly generated string of 10 characters, sampled from the alphabet (A–Z).

               • Task 2: Multi-hop Passkey Retrieval: In this task, the 10-character passkey is split into
109              two halves, with each half randomly assigned to “Alice” and “Bob.” The LLM is then
110              prompted to retrieve either Alice’s or Bob’s passkey. This task requires an LLM to identify
111              the most relevant one from multiple passkeys, making it more complex than the first.
112            • Task 3: Multi-hop Passkey Retrieval + Reasoning: This task involves both retrieval and
113              reasoning, and Task 1 & 2 are the pre-requisite tasks, i.e., the passkey retrieval is critical
114              for the final reasoning. Given the passkeys of both Alice and Bob, the LLM is asked to
115              concatenate them, e.g., if “Alice has ABC” and “Bob has DEF”, then the expected answer
116              is “ABCDEF.” The order of concatenation is critical, and the final concatenated answer is
117
                 controlled to match the passkeys used in Task 1. Notably, the reasoning is designed to be
                 simple purposefully, and a model is expected to achieve good performance on this task if it
118
                 performs well on Task 1 & 2.
119
120   For all tasks, the LLMs are prompted to generate the correct passkey from the long context. We
121   generate 100 random passkeys and use the Wikitext-103 dataset (Grave et al., 2017) to augment
122   the input context. The length of the Wikitext-103 context is varied between 0 and 128K tokens,
123
      with the passkeys randomly inserted at 30% and 60% positions within the context. Exact match

124
      C OMMAND -R-P LUS2 , GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), and C LAUDE -3-H AIKU (Anthropic).

126
      Results From Figure 1, we make several key observations. First, most LLMs perform well on
127   the first two passkey retrieval tasks (Tasks 1 & 2), achieving high EM scores across context lengths
128   ranging from 0K to 128K. Second, while the simple reasoning required by Task 3 is not an issue
129   for most LLMs within short context, their performance drops significantly as the context length
130   increases. This decline is more pronounced in Task 3 compared to the retrieval-only tasks in Task 1
131   and 2. Results of Task 3 reveal the challenge facing by modern LLMs in directly reasoning over the
132   long-context input3 . It further drives our motivation in leveraging an intermediate retrieval step as a
133   stepping stone for the subsequent reasoning step to address the challenge.

137   3.1     OVERVIEW
138
139   To address the aforementioned challenges, we introduce a retrieve-then-reason approach which
140   augments the long-context reasoning capability of LLMs via a two-stage breakdown, i.e., (i) first
141   explicitly retrieving relevant facts pertaining to the task; and (ii) then reasoning over the collected
142
      facts to derive the final answer. Moreover, as manifested in Figure 2, modern LLMs struggled to
      retrieve with high accuracy in realistic scenarios and frequently hallucinate “retrieved facts”, leading
143
      to faulty reasoning and the generation of incorrect answers. To this end, we introduce a simple
144
      and effective method, namely ALR2 , to augment the long-context reasoning capability of LLMs
145   by an explicit two-stage procedure, i.e., ALigning LLMs with the objectives of both Retrieval and
146   Reasoning. Our approach is inspired by the formulation of Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)
147   approach (Lewis et al., 2020), which was originally designed for open-domain QA.

233   3.5       D ISCUSSION
234
235   Our retrieval approach differs from others in two key aspects. First, most dense retrieval methods
236   (Karpukhin et al., 2020), which use collections of dense vectors as the index, or Differentiable Search
237   Index (DSI), which leverages model parameters (Tay et al., 2022) for indexing. In contrast, we treat
238
      the long input context itself as the retrieval index which supports the update of index on-the-fly.
      Second, most prior methods do not account for the relationships between retrieved statements and the
239
      correct number of statements to retrieve, often relying on large top-K values to ensure all necessary
240
      information are retrieved. Differently, our retriever is explicitly trained to retrieve a coherent set of
241   facts that are relevant for answering the question, which is particularly valuable in tasks like multi-hop
242   QA. A better retrieval quality is not only important for the subsequent reasoning, but also provides a
243   more trustworthy rationale, helping users better understand the decision-making process of LLMs.

345
346   As shown in Table 1, ALR2 achieves significantly better performance on the two question-answering
347   (QA) benchmarks. Notably, on the multi-hop QA benchmark, HotpotQA, ALR2 maintains consistent
348   performance from 4K to 128K tokens of context. Additionally, the retrieve-then-reason prompting
349   baseline, i.e., CMD-R+RR, shows a smoother performance curve on HotpotQA compared to the

      questions. In contrast, after aligning the LLM to handle both single-hop and multi-hop questions, our
353
      ALR2 approach achieves superior overall performance.
354
355   We also compare different two-stage prompting techniques, namely RR, QF, and S2A. As shown
356   in Table 1, CMD-R+RR outperforms CMD-R+QF on HotpotQA but falls short on SQuAD. We
357   find that this discrepancy is closely related to the number of sentences retrieved by each prompt.
358
      Specifically, CMD-R+RR retrieves an average of 7.5 sentences across both QA datasets, while CMD-
      R+QF retrieves only 2.2 sentences. Since HotpotQA is a multi-hop QA dataset that requires more
359
      supporting facts, CMD-R+RR performs better on this dataset. Both CMD-R+QF and CMD-R+RR
360
      outperform the system-2 attention baseline, i.e., CMD-R+S2A, in our experiments.
361
362   In addition, on the NIAH tasks, all evaluated approaches obtain comparable performances. This
363   is unsurprising given the fact that NIAH tasks only require straightforward information retrieval,
364
      which is relatively easy for LLMs to handle. When evaluating long-context performance, we caution
      researchers against averaging the performance of long-context retrieval and reasoning tasks, such as
365
      NIAH and QA, due to the significant performance gap between them.

      6     R ELATED W ORK
418
419
      6.1   L ONG - CONTEXT L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL
420
421   Long-context ability is critical for employing a large language model (LLM) to solve real-world
422   problems, e.g., document analysis (Wang et al., 2024a), tool use (Yao et al., 2023; Schick et al., 2023),
423   etc. Many components of an LLM may affect the long-context performance. For instance, researchers
424   find that the design of the position embedding is important for the extrapolation performance of an
425   LLM (Su et al., 2024a; Press et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Ruoss et al., 2023; Men et al., 2024),
426   i.e., testing the LLM on context length that is larger than the training window size. In addition, the
427   attention distribution and scale of the Transformer model (Chiang & Cholak, 2022; Xiao et al., 2024)
428
      may also affect the long-context ability.
429   To extend the context length of LLM, some works try to compress the long context into shorter text
430   (Jiang et al., 2023; 2024; Xu et al., 2024), limited hidden representations (Chevalier et al., 2023;
431   Ge et al., 2024), or model parameters (Wang et al., 2024b), saving the number of input tokens in
      the context. Moreover, splitting long context into fixed segments and caching them for re-use is


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      also widely used for increasing the effective context length of LLMs (Dai et al., 2019). Different
433   from Transformer-XL (Dai et al., 2019) that only leverages local attention, Munkhdalai et al. (2024)
434   introduce an additional global momentum to store the knowledge in all the previous context. Yen
435   et al. (2024) leverages a small bi-directional model (Liu, 2019) to encode the segments independently,
436   and allows the LLM to attend to those segments during generation.
437
      In our work, the target is not to extend the context length. In contrast, we find that the performance of
438
      QA will degenerate more significantly with longer context for most popular LLMs, similar to the
439   findings in Liu et al. (2024). Therefore, we propose a new formulation inspired by retrieval-augmented
440   generation to alleviate this common issue of LLMs.
441
442
      Recently, there are also many benchmarks are proposed to evaluate the long-context performance of
      LLMs (Hsieh et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Lee et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Krishna et al.,
443
      2023; Karpinska et al., 2024; Kuratov et al., 2024). Considering our focus is the general QA problems
444
      and the construction of long-context QA data is similar in many benchmarks, we use RULER (Hsieh
445   et al., 2024) as our main benchmark.
446
447
      6.2   R ETRIEVAL -AUGMENTED G ENERATION
448
449   Retrieval-Augmented generation (RAG) has been extensively used to solve NLP problems, e.g.,
450   open-domain question-answering (Lewis et al., 2020; Asai et al., 2024), language modeling (Guu
451   et al., 2020; Lan et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024), machine translation (Gu et al., 2018; Cai et al., 2021),
452   etc. For most RAG works, the dual-encoder-based retriever (Karpukhin et al., 2020; Izacard et al.,
453   2022; Ni et al., 2022), which measures the similarity between inputs and retrieval documents by their
454   encoded embeddings, has became the de facto approach for retrieval. The retrieved top-K documents
455   will be integrated into the generator, e.g., a language model (Radford et al., 2019), to enhance the
456
      generation. The most related work in this research line is Xu et al. (2024), which aims to save the
      number of input tokens to an LLM by compressing the retrieved K documents. However, our work
457
      focuses on enhancing the long-context reasoning of LLMs.
458
459
460
      6.3   C ITATION G ENERATION
461   There exist many works that aim to attribute the prediction of an LLM to the information source
462   that the LLM is based on, i.e., citation generation. Some train the model to learn this ability on a
463   standardized citation format (Menick et al., 2022; Taylor et al., 2022; Khalifa et al., 2024; Ye et al.,
464   2024). While others use the instruction-following ability and post-processing techniques to enable
465   this feature at inference (Gao et al., 2023a; Kamalloo et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023).
466
      Our work aims to precisely retrieve the bag of facts that can support the question-answering in
467   long-context scenario, which is also beneficial for the explainability of the prediction. However, the
468   focus of our work is more about enhancing the long-context QA ability of LLMs, which is orthogonal
469   to works in citation generation.
470
471
472
      7     C ONCLUSION
473
474
      In this work, we addressed the challenge of long-context reasoning in large language models (LLMs)
      by introducing ALR2 , a retrieve-then-reason approach that aligns LLMs with both retrieval and
475
      reasoning objectives. Through extensive experiments on both single-hop and multi-hop QA tasks,
476
      we demonstrated that ALR2 significantly outperforms existing baselines, especially in long-context
477   scenarios. Our analysis also highlighted the importance of aligning generation with retrieval to reduce
478   hallucinations and improve retrieval accuracy. Moreover, we showed that ALR2 generalizes well to
479   unseen datasets, offering a robust solution for long-context QA problems.
480
481   Limitations. We also note some of the limitations of our work. For example, as most works based
482   on the RAG formulation, our method is hard to enhance the summarization tasks, in which all the
483   information in the long context is important for the final prediction. In addition, only considering one
484   granularity, i.e., sentence, in the retrieval may be limited in some scenarios. A better way is to allow
485   the users to choose the retrieval granularity, e.g., phrase, sentence, or passage, in the instruction. We
      leave the addressing of those limitations in future works.

      QA Tasks We use the validation sets of SQuAD (Rajpurkar et al., 2016) and HotpotQA (Yang
      et al., 2018) datasets to build the long-context benchmarks, where each question in those datasets
826

827

829
      NIAH Tasks Following Hsieh et al. (2024), we consider four kinds of Needle-in-a-Haystack
830   (NIAH) tasks in our work and report the averaged performance of them:
831
832          • Single NIAH (S-NIAH): The “haystack” text is from Paul Graham essays (Kamradt, 2023)
833            is from “needle” sentence of this task is “One of the special magic numbers for {KEY}
834            is: {VALUE}”, where the “{KEY}” and “{VALUE}” are placeholders for English words
835            and 7-digit numbers, respectively. For S-NIAH, we only insert one needle sentence into the
836            long-context.
837          • Multi-keys NIAH (MK-NIAH): In this setting, we insert four needle sentences into the
838            context and ask an LLM to retrieve only one of them. Each of the four needle sentences has
839            unique “{KEY}” and “{VALUE}”.
840          • Multi-values NIAH (MV-NIAH): In this setting, part of the needle sentences may share
841            the same key. We will ask an LLM to retrieve all the values associated with the same key.
842          • Multi-queries NIAH (MQ-NIAH): Compared with MK-NIAH, we need the LLM to
843            retrieve multiple values in needle sentences associated with specific keys.
