009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce MarDini, a new family of video diffusion models that integrate the
012            advantages of masked auto-regression (MAR) into a unified diffusion model (DM)
013            framework. Here, MAR handles temporal planning, while DM focuses on spatial
014            generation in an asymmetric network design: i) a MAR-based planning model
015            containing most of the parameters generates planning signals for each masked
016            frame using low-resolution input; ii) a lightweight generation model uses these
017            signals to produce high-resolution frames via diffusion de-noising. MarDini’s
018
               MAR enables video generation conditioned on any number of masked frames at
               any frame positions: a single model can handle video interpolation (e.g., masking
019
               middle frames), image-to-video generation (e.g., masking from the second frame
020
               onward), and video expansion (e.g., masking half the frames). The efficient de-
021            sign allocates most of the computational resources to the low-resolution planning
022            model, making computationally expensive but important spatio-temporal atten-
023            tion feasible at scale. MarDini sets a new state-of-the-art for video interpolation;
024            meanwhile, within few inference steps, it efficiently generates videos on par with
025            those of much more expensive advanced image-to-video models.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Auto-regressive (AR) transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017; Peng et al., 2023; Schmidhuber, 1992b;
031   Schlag et al., 2021) have recently demonstrated remarkable success in natural language processing
032   (Dubey et al., 2024; Team et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023), sparking efforts to achieve similar
033
      breakthroughs in computer vision (Rombach et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2023a; Saharia et al., 2022).
      However, unlike the discrete, sequential, and easily tokenized nature of language, visual data consist
034
      of continuous pixel signals distributed across a high-dimensional space, making them more difficult
035
      to model through 1D auto-regression.
036
037   To overcome this challenge, recent studies have explored vector quantization techniques (Van
038   Den Oord et al., 2017; Razavi et al., 2019) to convert continuous pixel data into discrete repre-
039
      sentations suitable for AR modelling. Unfortunately, these approaches (Yu et al., 2022; Ramesh
      et al., 2021) rely on causal attention, which is not well aligned for high-dimensional visual data,
040
      often leading to diminished performance (Li et al., 2024), particularly on large-scale datasets (Xie
041
      et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024). To mitigate this limitation, masked auto-regression (MAR) has
042   been introduced (Chang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a). MAR replaces the causal attention with
043   bi-directional attention (He et al., 2021; Devlin et al., 2019), effectively simulating auto-regressive
044   behaviour while being more capable of handling visual data. Leveraging this approach, MAR ex-
045   hibits flexibility in handling diverse generation tasks through different masking strategies, such as
046   image generation (Chang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a), out-painting (Chang et al., 2022), video
047   expansion (Yu et al., 2023a) and class-conditioned video generation (Yu et al., 2024; Voleti et al.,
048   2022) while maintaining manageable computational overhead. Although MAR shows potential in
049   scaling image and video generation tasks (Chang et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023a; 2024), its key bottle-
050
      neck lies in its training instability which is tied to the reliance on discrete representations (Ramesh
      et al., 2021; Razavi et al., 2019).
051
052   Meanwhile, Diffusion models (DMs) (Ho et al., 2020; Neal, 2001; Jarzynski, 1997) have emerged
053   as a successful alternative for scaling vision generative models, offering stable training by modelling
      visual signals directly in a continuous space. However, DMs tend to incur high inference costs due


                                                        1
054
      to the requirement of the multi-step diffusion process. Here, video generation poses an even greater
055   challenge — Video is a strict super-set of the image domain, requiring additional modelling for
056   temporal consistency and complex motion dynamics.
057
058
      To this end, we propose a new paradigm for video generation that combines the flexibility of MAR
      in a continuous space with the robust generative capabilities of DM. Specifically, we present a
059
      scalable training recipe and an efficient neural architecture design for video generation. Our model
060
      decomposes video generation into two sub-tasks — temporal and spatial modelling — handled by
061   distinct networks with an asymmetric design based on the following two principles:
062
063   1. MAR handles long-range temporal modelling, while DM focuses on detailed spatial modelling.
064
      2. MAR operates with more parameters at a lower resolution, while DM operates with fewer pa-
065      rameters at a higher resolution.
066
067   Following these principles, we use the same training batch for both MAR and DM but employ two
068   distinct processes operating at different resolutions. MAR receives randomly masked low-resolution
069   input frames and predicts the corresponding planning signals. Conditioned on these planning signals
070   via cross-attention and the unmasked frames, DM learns to incrementally recover the masked high-
071   resolution frames from noise. Finally, we introduce a progressive training strategy that gradually
072
      curates mask ratios and with its data pipelines, allowing our model to be trained from scratch on
      unlabeled video data. This eliminates the common reliance on text-to-image and text-to-video pre-
073
      training, as seen in other video diffusion models (Girdhar et al., 2023; Blattmann et al., 2023a).
074
075   Our model integrates MAR-based planning signals with a DiT-based (Peebles & Xie, 2023; Chen
076   et al., 2024c) lightweight, tiny diffusion model, hence the name MarDini. Our empirical study on
077   MarDini highlights the following key characteristics:
078
      • Flexibility. With MAR conditioning, MarDini naturally supports a range of video generation
079
        tasks through flexible masking strategies. For example, when given the first frame and masking
080
        the rest, it performs image-to-video generation; when given a video and masking subsequent
081     frames, it performs video expansion; and, when given the first and last frames and masking the
082     middle frames, it performs video interpolation. By hierarchically and auto-regressively masking
083     middle frames across multiple inferences, MarDini generates slow-motion videos.
084
      • Scalability. MarDini can be trained from scratch at scale, without relying on generative image-
085     based pre-training. In contrast to most video generation models, that treat video as a secondary
086     task following image generation, MarDini leverages mask ratio tuning to progressively adjust the
087     difficulty of the training task. This approach enables the model to scale from video interpolation
088     to full video generation, directly bypassing the need for image-based pre-training.
089   • Efficiency. MarDini’s asymmetric design allocates more computational resources to lower reso-
090     lutions, making it memory-efficient and fast during inference. With lower overall memory usage,
091     MarDini allows the deployment of computationally intensive spatio-temporal attention mecha-
092     nisms at scale, improving its ability to model complex motion dynamics.

      2.1   D ESIGN OVERVIEW
097
098   MarDini is a video generation model designed to efficiently generate high-resolution videos using
099   an asymmetric network architecture. As shown in Figure 1, MarDini consists of two networks: a
100   heavy-weight MAR planning model and a light-weight generation DM. During training, the plan-
101   ning network processes randomly masked low-resolution frames and predicts corresponding plan-
102   ning signals. These planning signals compress the semantic and long-range temporal information,
103   guiding the DM’s high-resolution generation process. The DM receives noisy frames at the masked
104
      positions and reconstructs them by progressively removing noise.
105   In this section, we outline and address the key design challenges involved in training MarDini.
106   First, we describe the data representations and their corresponding notations within the MarDini
107   framework (Section 2.2). Next, we describe the design details of the MAR planning network and the
      DM, along with the integration of additional guidance such as diffusion steps and planning signals

201

202   patches into a 1-dimensional token sequence and insert a learnable [NEXT] token to differentiate
203   image patches across different rows, akin to the [SEP] in Devlin et al. (2019) and [nextline] in Gao

236   In this section, we outline the training pipeline of MarDini. Specifically, we employ a multi-stage
237   progressive training strategy that gradually increases task difficulty. This approach offers two key
238   benefits: i) progressive learning inherently enhances training stability and improves the performance
239   of generative models, as demonstrated by Karras (2018) and Chen et al. (2024b); and ii) it allows for
240   the collection of checkpoints from earlier stages, which helps mitigate setbacks caused by subopti-
241   mal configurations. Below, we elaborate on our detailed progressive training strategy, including the
242   training objectives, architecture design, and training data configurations. A comprehensive training
243   manual for MarDini can be found in the Appendix B.

      2.4.1    T RAINING TASKS : F ROM F RAME I NTERPOLATION TO V IDEO G ENERATION
246   Our training objectives are organized into three stages: i) Initial Stage: We separately train the plan-
247   ning and generation models, each with its own learning objective, to initialize their model weights.
248   ii) Joint-Model Stage: We combine the models for joint training on a simple video interpolation task,
249   using only a masked diffusion loss. iii) Joint-Task Stage: We further train the model by gradually
250   reducing the number of preserved reference frames, enabling it to jointly learn video interpolation
251   and image-to-video generation tasks.
252

254
      experience unstable training. As such, we simplify the training dynamics by separately warming up
      the two models as an initial step.

287
288
      In conjunction with our progressive training objectives, we also introduce a progressive architectural
      design. Specifically, we first use spatio-temporal attention in the DM during the initial training stage.
289
      This choice promotes convergence, compared to temporal attention, as noted in Gao et al. (2024).
290
      Since in our initial stage we train the DM in isolation and on a relatively low-resolution setup,
291   this sophisticated attention incurs in minor computational overhead. When integrating MAR with
292   the DM in the second stage, we replace the spatio-temporal attention with the more cost-effective
293   temporal attention, thus increasing the efficiency of the generation model.

296
297   Analogous to our progressive strategies for training objective and architecture we also propose a pro-
298
      gressive data configuration. Over time, we gradually increase the video’s spatial resolution, along-
      side progressively extending the video’s duration. This approach ensures efficient use of computa-
299
      tional resources and facilitates effective model scaling, allowing MarDini to handle more complex
300
      and high-resolution video data as training progresses.

      Effectiveness of MAR and DM. We first demonstrate the importance of having a DM on top of
      our MAR planning model. In fact, it is tempting to hypothesize that MAR on its own contains all
316
      the ingredients to enable high-quality video interpolation. To explore this, we introduce a projection
317
      layer to directly unpatchify the output of the MAR model without intermediate diffusion with DM.
318   Our experiments on VIDIM-Bench reveal that, MAR on its own, performs poorly on interpolation
319   tasks, as shown by the first two and last two rows in Table 1, for both the 1B and 3B settings. This
320   result suggests that directly applying MAR to continuous space is suboptimal, a result consistent
321   with previous findings (Li et al., 2024). Similarly, directly tackling this task with a small DM without
322   global guidance, according to the third row of Table 1, results in sub-optimal performance. However,
323   by combining MAR’s planning capability with DM’s stable performance in continuous space, we
      achieve optimal results, demonstrating that both components are beneficial for video generation.

      Explaining MAR’s Planning Signal. We provide an intuitive explanation of MAR’s role in Mar-
      Dini. During training, a learnable token is used to randomly replace MAR to support CFG (Ho &
351
      Salimans, 2022), allowing DM to generate videos independently. We visualize the results of Mar-
352
      Dini with and without MAR’s planning signals. As shown in Figure 4, without the planning model,
353   DM can still produce meaningful frames but, as expected, lacks “global planning.” For example, in
354   Figure 4 (Left), DM moves objects in different directions, causing distortion in the building, which
355   suggests a weaker or non-existing prior model of how objects move. Similarly, in Figure 4 (Right),
356   DM fails to accurately predict the movement of the fire. In contrast, incorporating the planning sig-
357   nal addresses these visual flaws. These results indicate that MAR’s planning signal effectively hints
358   how elements should move, ensuring long-term coherence in the generated video.

483   4     R ELATED W ORK
484
485   Auto-Regressive Model in Visual Generation. Auto-regressive (AR) models (Gers et al., 2000;
      Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Schmidhuber, 2015) have proven effective in natural language


                                                                         9
486
      modeling (Brown, 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024; Team et al., 2023). To adapt
487   this scalable modeling strategy for image and video generation, recent approaches (Yu et al., 2024;
488   Chang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a; Yu et al., 2023a; Chang et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023a) replace
489   causal attention in AR with bidirectional attention, allowing for better capture of dense relationships
490   in visual space.
491
      Many studies (Yu et al., 2023b; Chang et al., 2023; Team, 2024; Xie et al., 2024) validate the scal-
492
      ability of this approach. To align with the training recipes from LLMs, these studies adopt discrete
493   visual representations, using image tokenizers (Esser et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2021; Van Den Oord
494   et al., 2017) to quantize continuous pixel values into discrete representations. However, Li et al.
495   (2024); Ramesh et al. (2021); Razavi et al. (2019) argue that this strategy suffers from unstable
496   training and may limit model capacity due to the inherently continuous nature of visual data. This
497   inspires recent works (Li et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024) to shift towards continuous latent spaces for
498   masked auto-regressive models to address these limitations.
499   We follow this trajectory but diverges in two ways: i) We highlight the importance of mask ratios,
500   which were fixed in earlier works Li et al. (2024). By dynamically adjusting them with a progressive
501   training strategy, we improve both model scalability and stability. ii) We propose an asymmetric
502   input resolution design, allowing MAR to be effectively trained with full-resolution inputs.
503
504
      Diffusion Model for Video Generation. In recent years, diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Neal,
      2001; Jarzynski, 1997) have become a leading approach for both image and video generation (Rom-
505
      bach et al., 2022; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024c; Saharia et al.,
506
      2022; Brooks et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2023a; Girdhar et al., 2023; Menapace et al., 2024; Kondratyuk
507   et al., 2023; Cong et al., 2024). These models conceptualize the generation process as gradually
508   refining a real sample from Gaussian noise, demonstrating significant scalability and stable training.
509
510
      In this paper, we offer two key insights into video generation: i) Previous methods (Wu et al., 2023;
      Ho et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023a; Blattmann et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2023; Girdhar et al., 2023;
511
      Gao et al., 2024; Cong et al., 2024) often first pre-train an image generative model, and then fine-
512
      tune it for video generation, or they require joint training for both tasks (Chen et al., 2024c; Esser
513   et al., 2023). While multi-stage pre-training on diverse inputs can be beneficial, video generation is
514   often limited by the success of image-based pre-training, which typically serves as a secondary task.
515   This paper proposes an alternative: training video generation models from scratch with progressively
516   increasing task complexity. ii) Previous research (Girdhar et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
517   2024c; Blattmann et al., 2023b) has predominantly employed temporal attention mechanisms to
518   capture temporal dependencies, mainly due to the high computational and memory costs associated
519   with spatio-temporal attention. However, in alignment with previous work (Blattmann et al., 2023b;
520   Gao et al., 2024) suggesting that spatio-temporal attention enables superior video modeling, we
521
      propose an amortized strategy that makes spatio-temporal attention computationally feasible, even
      at high resolutions.
522
523   Finally, our paper also relates to asymmetric neural networks. Due to page limitations, additional
524   related works are discussed in Appendix E.
525
526   5   C ONCLUSION
527
528   We have introduced a new family of generative models for video, i.e., MarDini, based on auto-
529   regressive diffusion, wherein a large planning model offers powerful conditioning to a much smaller
530   diffusion model. Our design philosophy considers efficiency from model conception, and so our
531
      heaviest model component is only executed once at lower resolution inputs, whereas our generative
      module focuses on fine-grained details at the frame level, reconciling high-level conditioning and
532
      image details. Our model is unique in that it leverages a masked auto-regressive loss directly at
533
      the frame level. MarDini is afforded with multiple generative capabilities from a single model,
534   e.g., long-term video interpolation, video expansion, and image animation. Our investigation shows
535   that our modeling strategy is powerful enough to obtain competitive results on various interpolation
536   and animation benchmarks, while doing it at a lower computational needs than counterparts with
537   comparable parameter size. We discuss the limitation of our study in Appendix F.2.

941   B     M AR D INI T RAINING M ANUAL
942
943   In Figure 6, we present the training details of MarDini. All experiments, including model variations,
944   ablation studies, benchmark evaluations, and full model training, are carried out on a distributed
945   MAST scheduler (Choudhury et al., 2024) using 256 H100 GPUs. The training dataset comprises
946   approximately 34 million filtered Shutterstock videos, segmented into 2-second training clips. We
947   use the AdamW optimizer for each stage with a 1.4 × 10−4 learning rate and cosine learning rate
948
      scheduler. We adapt our batch size based on the resolution and the frame count to maximize GPU
      utility. For example, at [256 × 256] resolution with 9 frames, the batch size is 1024, processing 9K
949
      frames per iteration; at [512 × 512] resolution with 9 frames, the batch size is 720, processing 6480
950
      frames per iteration. During inference, we set the classifier-free guidance (CFG)(Ho & Salimans,
951   2022) scale as 2.5 for the image-to-video task with the noise solver DDIM (Song et al., 2021),
952   and we directly remove classifier-free guidance for video interpolation as it is redundant. FSDP
953   (Zhao et al., 2023) and activation checkpointing (Zhao et al., 2023) are enabled to further save GPU
954   memory. We do not include dynamic resolution training in our main training stages, as it slows down
955   training. Instead, we find that after convergence, fine-tuning the model for a few steps (10K-20K)
956   with dynamic resolutions enables it to quickly support this capabilities.

       frames.

       For VBench, we utilize the official dataset to assess the model across several metrics: I2V-Subject
1027   Consistency, I2V-Background Consistency, and video quality. The video quality evaluation con-
1028   siders dimensions such as Subject Consistency, Background Consistency, Smoothness, Aesthetic
1029   Score, Imaging Quality, Temporal Flickering, and Dynamic Degree. Given that our model lacks text
1030   supervision, we omit the evaluation for video-text camera motion. Furthermore, since our model
1031   is pre-trained without incorporating dynamic degree guidance (known as motion score/strength),
1032   it is not directly comparable with other models in this respect. Therefore, we additionally report
1033   video quality by averaging all the dimensions except for Dynamic Degree and provide the VBench
1034   average score derived from I2V-Subject Consistency, I2V-Background Consistency, and the video
1035
       quality dimensions (excluding dynamic degree). For the latency analysis, we ensure fairness by
       using the same computational platform: a single Nvidia A100 80G GPU. All implementations are
1036
       based on their official code without any engineering optimizations. For MarDini, we simply employ
1037
       bf16 mixed precision to enhance computational efficiency. To account for variations in frame num-
1038   ber and resolution, all results are normalized by frame count and evaluated at a consistent resolution
1039   of either [512 × 512] or [768 × 768].

1044
       This paper also relates to asymmetric neural architectures, widely used in neural networks since the
       1990s (Schmidhuber, 1992a;b). In computer vision, to achieve high-resolution generation, many
1045
       studies (Podell et al., 2023; Pernias et al., 2024; Saharia et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024; Jain et al., 2024;
1046
       Kang et al., 2023) employ a common strategy: a model generates low-resolution/quality samples,
1047   followed by another model that performs super-resolution (Kang et al., 2023), refinement (Podell
1048   et al., 2023), or interpolation (Wang et al., 2024b) to enhance the generation quality. In discrimina-
1049   tive video models, asymmetric training strategies have been used for temporal segmentation models,
1050   where the full temporal extension does not fit the available GPU memory Xu et al. (2021). Since
1051   computational costs are distributed across stages, this approach is well-supported by existing com-
1052   putational platforms. Building on this trajectory but extending beyond it, we propose a novel design
1053   that partitions the model into two distinct models: a planning model and a generation model. The
1054   planning model, containing the majority of the model’s parameters, is trained auto-regressively at a
1055
       low resolution to generate conditional signals without producing visual outputs. These signals are
       then processed by the lightweight generation model, which converts them into high-resolution visual
1056
       outputs using a diffusion process.
1057
1058   Unlike the traditional auto-regressive diffusion model (Li et al., 2024), which still faces high com-
1059   putational costs as resolution increases, we use cross-attention as an information pathway to connect
1060   asymmetric resolution input for more efficient training/inference.

       F.2   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
1152
1153   Post Training. The primary goal of this paper is to demonstrate the feasibility and effectiveness of
1154   combining masked auto-regressive (MAR) models with diffusion models (DM) for video generation.
1155   Consequently, we allocated the majority of our computational resources to the pre-training stage,
1156   placing less emphasis on post-training, despite its recognized importance in generative models (Dai
1157   et al., 2023a; Dubey et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023). Post-training will be a top priority in our
1158   future work, focusing on enhancing long-term planning, improving motion quality, and achieving
1159   higher resolutions.
1160
1161   Improved Conditional Signals. A significant contribution of this work is the exploration of train-
1162   ing a video generation model without relying on generative image pre-training. However, this ap-
1163
       proach presents a trade-off: MarDini is not inherently equipped with a text encoder for processing
       textural instructions. To conserve computational resources and quickly validate the feasibility of
1164
       our method, we intentionally excluded commonly used conditional signals, such as text embeddings
1165
       and motion scores. Encouraged by the initial success of our model, we plan to incorporate these
1166   conditional signals into MarDini in our future updates to broaden its range of applications.
