008                                                       A BSTRACT
009
010                The widespread applicability of large language models (LLMs) has increased the
011                availability of many fine-tuned models of various sizes targeting specific tasks.
012                Given a set of such specialized models, to maximize overall performance, it is im-
013
                   portant to figure out the optimal strategy for selecting the right model for a given
                   user query. An effective strategy could drastically increase overall performance
014
                   and even offer improvements over a single large monolithic model. Existing ap-
015
                   proaches typically fall into two categories: routing, where a single model is se-
016                lected for each query, and cascading, which runs a sequence of increasingly larger
017                models until a satisfactory answer is obtained. However, both have notable lim-
018                itations: routing commits to an initial model without flexibility, while cascading
019                requires executing every model in sequence, which can be inefficient. Addition-
020                ally, the conditions under which these strategies are provably optimal remain un-
021                clear. In this work, we derive optimal strategies for both routing and cascading.
022                Building on this analysis, we propose a novel approach called cascade routing,
023                which combines the adaptability of routing with the cost-efficiency of cascading.
024
                   Our experiments demonstrate that cascade routing consistently outperforms both
                   routing and cascading across a variety of settings, improving both output quality
025
                   and lowering computational cost, thus offering a unified and efficient solution to
026
                   the model selection problem.1

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large language models (LLMs) have found applications in a wide range of tasks, some of which are
032   easily handled by small models, while others require the full capacity of state-of-the-art LLMs. This
033   has led to the development of many fine-tuned models of various sizes that target specific tasks. To
034   maximize performance, it is crucial to select the most suitable model for each query, accounting for
035
      both the expected quality of the model’s output and the model’s cost. Such model selection strategies
      can significantly improve performance over any individual model and can reduce inference costs by
036
      selecting a smaller model when the query does not require the full capacity of a larger model.
037
038   Routing and Cascading Two primary strategies have been proposed to solve model selection.
039   The first, routing, directs each input query to a specific model from a set of available models (Chen
040   et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024), as illustrated in Fig. 1(a). This approach is particularly effective when
041   different expert LLMs are needed for different tasks, enabling the selection of the most suitable
042   expert for each query. The second strategy, cascading, processes an input query through a sequence
043   of increasingly larger models, stopping when a model produces an answer deemed sufficiently good
044   (Chen et al., 2023; Varshney and Baral, 2022), as illustrated in Fig. 1(b). Cascading is particularly
045   valuable for handling queries of varying difficulty, as it allows simpler queries to be addressed by
046
      smaller, less expensive models while reserving more complex queries for larger models.
047
      Restrictive Conditions Despite their utility, both routing and cascading impose significant restric-
048
      tions on the model selection process. In routing, the initial selection of a model is final, prevent-
049
      ing any reconsideration after the initial decision. In cascading, each query must sequentially pass
050   through all models in the chain until a suitable answer is found, with no option to reroute to a poten-
051   tially better model. Therefore, a less restrictive strategy that combines the strengths of both routing
052   and cascading could offer significant performance improvements.

068   Lack of Theoretical Understanding Furthermore, the conditions under which current routing
069   and cascading strategies are optimal, are not well understood. For routing, an extensive proof is
070   required just to show that current strategies are close to optimal (Chen et al., 2022), while the the-
071   oretical analysis of current cascading algorithms is based on restrictive assumptions and does not
072
      provide optimality guarantees (Chen et al., 2023; Varshney and Baral, 2022). This lack of theoreti-
      cal understanding hinders the development of more effective model selection strategies.
073
074   This Work: Cascade Routing To address these limitations, we first derive optimal routing and
075   cascading strategies by framing them as linear optimization problems aimed at maximizing output
076   quality while remaining within a given cost budget. For routing, this optimal strategy is close to
077   the one obtained by prior work, while for cascading we derive a new strategy that is provably better
078   than existing approaches. Building on this theoretical analysis, we propose a new paradigm called
079   cascade routing, which generalizes both routing and cascading. As illustrated in Fig. 1(c), cascade
080   routing initially routes a query to any available model but keeps rerouting to different models until
081
      a model produces an answer of sufficient quality. We prove the optimality of our cascade routing
      strategy and show that it offers significantly more flexibility in processing a query.
082
083   Results We evaluate cascade routing on a wide range of tasks, demonstrating that it significantly
084   outperforms both routing and cascading. Notably, cascade routing consistently improves perfor-
085   mance by 1% to 4% across all settings on the popular RouterBench benchmark (Hu et al., 2024),
086   which represents a relative improvement over a naive baseline by an additional 13% to 80%. Fur-
087   thermore, we show that our new cascading strategy outperforms existing cascading strategies by up
088   to 2%, validating that our theoretical analysis leads to practical improvements over prior work.
089
      Contributions Our main contributions are as follows:
090
091         • We derive optimal strategies for routing and cascading and obtain a new cascading strategy
092
              that is provably better than prior approaches (§2, §3).
093         • We introduce cascade routing, a new paradigm that combines the strengths of routing and
094
              cascading, and prove its optimality (§4).
095         • We conduct a thorough evaluation, demonstrating that cascade routing consistently outper-
096
              forms the baselines (§5).

098   2   ROUTING AS L INEAR O PTIMIZATION
099
      We derive an optimal routing strategy to select the best model for a given query, providing detailed
100
      proofs for all statements in this section in App. A. We will use the analysis presented here to develop
101   the optimal cascading and cascade routing strategies in §3 and §4, respectively.
102
103   Routing Strategy In routing, our goal is to develop a strategy that selects the best language model
104   for a given input query. Formally, let X represent the distribution over all possible queries, and
105   suppose we have k language models m1 , . . . , mk available for routing. Further, let ∆k denote the
106   set of all probability distributions over k variables. A routing strategy can then be defined as follows:
107   Definition 1 (Routing). A routing strategy s is a function s : X → ∆k that maps a query x ∈ X to
      a probability distribution over models. si (x) denotes the probability that mi is selected for query x.

      A routing strategy selects a model by sampling from the distribution s(x) for each query x. In prior
109   work, routing strategies were restricted to be deterministic, i.e., si (x) ∈ {0, 1} (Chen et al., 2022;
110   Hu et al., 2024). In contrast, we propose using a more general probabilistic routing strategy that
111   enables a better solution and an easier theoretical analysis.
112
113   Quality and Cost In routing, we seek to maximize the expected output quality of the selected
114   model while adhering to a given cost budget B. Quality could measure model accuracy, user prefer-
115   ence, or any other performance indicator. We define the quality function qi (x) as the output quality
116   of model mi on query x, and the cost function ci (x) as the cost of running model mi on x.
117   However, since these functions are unknown in practice, we need estimators q̂i (x) and ĉi (x) that
118   approximate the output quality and cost of querying model mi on input x. Estimators for qi (x) can
119   be created using small classifiers trained to predict model accuracy, as done in prior work (Hu et al.,
120   2024; Shnitzer et al., 2023). ĉi (x) can be estimated by tokenizing the input query and determining
121   the average output length of the model on a query. Then, we can use API-specific costs per token to
122   estimate the cost of running a model on a query.
123
124   Optimal Routing Using these estimators, we can formally define the optimal routing strategy:
125   Definition 2 (Optimal Routing). The optimal routing strategy sOPT for a given cost budget B is the
126   solution to the optimization problem
127                                 max Ex∈X ,i∈{1,...,k} (si (x)q̂i (x))
                                     s
128                                                                                                  (1)

                                     s.t. Ex∈X ,i∈{1,...,k} (si (x)ĉi (x)) ⩽ B.

      We now explain how to solve this linear optimization problem. Intuitively, the optimal routing
131
      strategy optimizes the cost-quality tradeoff τi (x, λ) = q̂i (x) − λĉi (x), where λ ∈ R+ controls the
132   balance between quality and cost based on the budget B. For each query x, the model that achieves
133   the highest value of τi (x, λ) is selected.
134
135
      More formally, for a given λ ∈ R+ , we define two deterministic routing strategies: sλMIN (x), which
136
      selects the cheapest model achieving the optimal cost-quality tradeoff, and sλMAX (x), which selects
      the most expensive model achieving this tradeoff. The optimal routing strategy sOPT is then deter-
137
      mined by the following theorem:
138
      Theorem 1 (Optimal Routing Strategy). For a given cost budget B, there exists a λ ∈ R+ and a
139
      γ ∈ [0, 1] such that the optimal routing strategy sOPT equals γsλMIN + (1 − γ)sλMAX . Furthermore,
140
      all routing strategies that have an expected cost that is exactly equal to B and can be written as a
141                              ′        ′
      convex combination of sλMIN and sλMAX for some λ′ ∈ R+ achieve the same optimal quality.
142
143   Since γ is often not equal to 0 or 1, the optimal routing strategy is not deterministic and instead
144   selects a model probabilistically. Therefore, prior work that only considered deterministic routing
145   strategies (Chen et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2024) cannot express the routing strategy from Theorem 1
146   and fall back to the near-optimal sλMIN instead.
147   Due to the second part of Theorem 1, we only need to find a set of hyperparameters λ and γ that
148   achieve the desired cost budget. To determine these parameters, we estimate the cost of each strategy
149   using a validation dataset D that is representative of the query distribution X . Since the cost of sλMIN
150   increases as λ decreases (see App. A), we use a binary search to find the appropriate value of λ. γ
151   is determined by interpolating between the costs of sλMIN and sλMAX to match the budget B.

153   3   C ASCADING AS S EQUENTIAL ROUTING
154
155   In this section, we extend our analysis of the optimal routing strategy to the cascade setting, provid-
156   ing detailed proofs for all the statements in this section in App. B. The solution derived here will be
157   used to develop the optimal strategy for cascade routing in §4.
158
159   Cascading In cascading, an input query is passed through a chain of increasingly larger and more
160   expensive models. The cascade stops once a model’s output meets a certain condition, and that
161   output is returned. We will reinterpret cascading as a sequence of routing problems. To do so, we
      first define the models over which we need to route, which we refer to as supermodels.

                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Definition 3 (Supermodel). A supermodel M is a sequence of models (mi1 , . . . , mij ) such that
163   running a query through M is equivalent to running it through each of the models in the sequence.
164   The set of all supermodels is denoted as M. By Mi:j we denote the supermodel (mi , . . . , mj ).
165
166   In cascading, we only need to consider the supermodels M1:1 , . . . , M1:k . The full expressivity of
167   Definition 3 will only be necessary for cascade routing in §4.
168   A cascade occurs as a sequence of decision steps, where at each step, it decides whether to compute
169   the next model in the sequence or terminate. By step j, the first j −1 models have been computed. At
170   this point, the cascade will run one of the supermodels M1:j−1 , . . . , M1:k . If any of the supermodels
171   M1:j , . . . , M1:k is optimal, the cascade proceeds to run the next model. In contrast, if the supermodel
172   M1:j−1 is optimal, the cascade halts and returns the output of the last computed model.
173   Thus, a cascade can be characterized as a sequence of routing strategies that route between the su-
174   permodels M1:j−1 , . . . , M1:k . Even though the action associated with supermodels M1:j , . . . , M1:k
175   – continuing the cascade – is the same, it is essential to use all of them in the routing strategy. For
176   instance, if mj performs poorly but mj+1 performs exceptionally well on a given query, the cascade
177   should continue. Limiting consideration to the supermodels M1:j−1 and M1:j alone would therefore
178   result in suboptimal decisions. Formally, we define a cascading strategy as follows:
179   Definition 4 (Cascading Strategy). A cascading strategy s is a sequence of routing strategy
180   (s(1) , . . . , s(k) ) such that s(j) routes between the supermodels M1:j−1 , . . . , M1:k .
181

230   4       C ASCADE ROUTING AS C ASCADE G ENERALIZATION
231
      Both routing and cascading are powerful techniques that enable the efficient use of multiple models.
232   However, their use is often orthogonal: while routing is useful when we have several specialized
233   models that are experts at specific tasks, cascading is more beneficial when input queries vary in
234   difficulty. In this section, we therefore present cascade routing, which is a generalization of both
235   techniques. Proofs for all theorems and lemmas in this section are included in App. C.
236
      Cascade Routing Cascade routing closely resembles cascading, but with one crucial difference:
237
      the routing strategy at step j routes between all possible supermodels, not just the supermodels
238
      M1:j−1 , . . . , M1:k . Therefore, both Definition 4 and Theorem 2 can be extended to this setting.
239
      Definition 5 (Cascade Routing). A cascade routing strategy s is a sequence of routing strategies
240
      (s(1) , . . . , s(k) ) such that, for a given sample x ∈ X , s(j) routes between all supermodels in M that
241
      start with the j − 1 models that have already been computed for this query.
242
243
      Theorem 3 (Optimal Cascade Routing). For a given cost budget B, there exist λ1 , . . . , λk ∈ R+
                                                                                        (1)      (k)
244   and a γ ∈ R+ such that the optimal cascade routing strategy sOPT = (sOPT , . . . , sOPT ) is given by
       (j)           (j),λ               (j),λ         (j),λ      (j),λ
245   sOPT = γsMIN j + (1 − γ)sMAX j where sMIN j and sMAX j are defined as in Theorem 1.
246
247
      While cascade routing extends cascading and can therefore use the same hyperparameter optimiza-
      tion scheme, it also introduces additional challenges which we address in the following paragraphs.
248
249   Model Order In cascading, the model order is predetermined, and the routing strategy only de-
250   cides whether to proceed with the next model in the sequence. In contrast, cascade routing must
251   dynamically determine the order in which models are computed. Despite this, both the estimated
                (j)              (j)
252   quality q̂M (x) and cost ĉM (x) of a supermodel M are order-independent. Therefore, supermodels
253   that contain the same models in a different order will have the same associated cost and quality. To
254   mitigate this, we sort the models within the selected supermodel by cost and compute the cheap-
255   est one first. This approach aligns with cascading, where more expensive models are only used if
256   cheaper models do not suffice.
257   Number of Supermodels In cascading, the quality and cost must be computed for a maximum
258   of k supermodels at each step. However, in cascade routing, the number of supermodels grows
259   exponentially, leading to the need to evaluate up to 2k supermodels. This increase can become
260   prohibitively costly, particularly since the model selection process must remain computationally
261   negligible with respect to model computation. To mitigate this, we leverage so-called negative
262   marginal gains. Specifically, if a model m in a supermodel M negatively impacts the quality-cost
263
      tradeoff, all supermodels containing all models in M can be pruned from the search space. Since
      this negative contribution is quite common, this allows us to prune the search space significantly.
264
      More formally, this pruning operation relies on the following lemma:
265
266   Lemma 1 (Negative Marginal Gain). Let M ∈ M and m be any model in M . Let the marginal gain
267
      of m w.r.t. M be defined as τM (x, λ) − τM \{m} (x, λ). Then, if the marginal gain of m w.r.t. M is
      strictly negative for a given query, the optimal cascade routing strategy will never run a supermodel
268
      M ′ ∈ M that contains all models in M .

403   5.3   A BLATION S TUDY
404
405   We conduct an ablation study to examine the impact of various design choices in cascade routing
406   on performance and runtime. Runtime is a critical factor because the overhead introduced by the
407   strategy must be negligible compared to the time required for model computation. If the strategy
408   adds significant overhead, its performance gains may be offset by the increased runtime. We also
409   include an additional ablation that specifically targets runtime on random data in App. F.
410   To investigate this, we repeat the experiment from §5.1 when using all eleven models, testing dif-
411   ferent variations of cascade routing. We evaluate a slower variation that omits Lemma 1, thereby
412   requiring more supermodels to be evaluated (S LOW), a greedy variation that only considers super-
413   models of length j + 1 at step j (G REEDY), and a version that does not compute the expected value
414   when evaluating supermodel quality, using the quality of the best model instead (N O -E XPECT).
415
416   Results Table 3 presents the results of the ablation study. As expected, the S LOW variation is al-
417   most an order of magnitude slower while achieving similar performance. In contrast, both G REEDY
418   and N O -E XPECT are faster but perform worse in the low- and medium-noise scenarios by 0.5% to
419   1.3%. Interestingly, there seems to be a much smaller performance gap in the high-noise scenario.
420   This is due to the very low variance in the quality estimates, since the linear model used for quality
421   estimation predicts an almost constant value for each query in this scenario, making the expected
422   value computation less important.
423   Furthermore, the G REEDY and N O -E XPECT variants perform very similarly, while G REEDY is about
424   twice as fast as N O -E XPECT. This suggests that one should almost always use the normal variant of
425   cascade routing, and only consider the G REEDY variant if runtime is a critical concern. Neither the
426   S LOW nor the N O -E XPECT variant is recommended, as they either perform worse or are significantly
427   slower than the normal variant.

      6     R ELATED W ORK
431
      We discuss related work in routing and cascading.


                                                        8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Routing Routing is a widely studied problem in machine learning, particularly in the task of di-
433   recting input queries to specialized expert models. One of the most common applications of routing
434   is model selection for natural language input queries with a known correct answer (Ding et al., 2024;
435   Hari and Thomson, 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Jang et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2024; Sakota et al., 2024;
436   Shnitzer et al., 2023). All these works train a machine learning model to predict whether a given
437   model will correctly answer a query. Though the setups in these works are largely similar, they vary
438   in certain specifics, such as the type of input queries or the features used for quality estimation.
439   Routing is also applied in other areas. For instance, Lu et al. (2024); Ong et al. (2024) use preference
440   data to train a quality estimator, which facilitates routing in scenarios involving real-world user
441   queries where clear ground-truth answers may not exist. Additionally, Chen et al. (2022) employ
442   routing for API selection in multi-label classification tasks, focusing on directing queries to the
443   appropriate API based on task requirements. Similarly, Zhang et al. (2024) apply routing in software
444   agent environments, directing user issues to the agent most suited to handle them.
445
446   Cascading Cascading techniques are primarily used to reduce inference costs by employing
447   smaller models initially and only cascading to larger models if the smaller ones fail to provide a
448   sufficiently accurate answer. Most often, cascading decisions are based on the smaller model’s con-
449   fidence in its own predictions (Chen et al., 2023; Ramírez et al., 2024; Varshney and Baral, 2022).
450
      However, alternative techniques also exist. For example, Madaan et al. (2023) propose running
      models multiple times and measuring the variance in their responses to decide whether to cascade
451
      to a larger model.
452
453   For classification tasks, early stopping is another cascading strategy (Li et al., 2021; Schuster et al.,
454   2022). In this approach, the cascade halts when a model’s intermediate layers generate representa-
455   tions that are sufficiently informative to predict the correct class. This reduces computational costs
456
      by avoiding the need to process every query through the entire model.
457   There has also been specific research on quality estimation within cascading frameworks. Gupta
458   et al. (2024) examine various measures of uncertainty in language model answers, evaluating their
459   impact on cascading performance. Meanwhile, Jitkrittum et al. (2023) explore failure cases in cas-
460   cading mechanisms that rely on uncertainty, introducing alternative quality measures that enhance
461   cascade efficiency. Lastly, Xue et al. (2023) apply cascading to majority voting for a single model
462
      to obtain a method called dynamic voting: the cascade stops depending on the aggregated answers
      of all previous model computations. This allows the system to process simpler queries using fewer
463
      votes while allocating more computational resources for harder queries.
464
465
466   7   L IMITATIONS
467
468   While cascade routing provides a promising approach to improve model selection strategies, it re-
469   quires accurate cost and quality estimates to be effective. However, in §5.2 we found that quality
470   estimates based on current state-of-the-art methods contain significant noise. This limits the per-
471   formance improvement of cascade routing over routing, as the quality estimates are not accurate
472
      enough to guide the selection of the best model. As shown in §5.1, lower noise in the quality and
      cost estimates lead to much better performance of all model selection strategies, indicating that fur-
473
      ther work that aims to reduce the noise in the quality and cost estimates is needed to fully leverage
474
      the potential of cascade routing.
475
476   Another limitation is the necessity of a training dataset to optimize the hyperparameters associated
477   with cascade routing. While both routing and cascading suffer from the same disadvantage, the
478
      training data required for routing is much more minimal, since it only needs to estimate two hyper-
      parameters. In contrast, cascade routing requires estimating multiple hyperparameters on a more
479
      complex optimization surface.

482   8   C ONCLUSION
483
484   In this work, we introduced a novel framework for routing and cascading that enabled us to propose
485   theoretically optimal strategies for both paradigms. Furthermore, we used this theoretical analysis to
      propose a new paradigm for model selection, cascade routing, which combines the benefits of both


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      routing and cascading. We showed that cascade routing can significantly outperform its baselines,
487   especially with good quality and cost estimates. Furthermore, we found that our new cascading
488   strategy significantly outperforms existing approaches to cascading, showing our theoretical anal-
489   ysis also leads to practical gains. Our work provides a theoretical foundation for model selection
490   strategies and opens up new avenues for future research in this area, especially in the direction of
491   obtaining more accurate quality and cost estimates.

      To prove Theorem 2, we heavily rely on the results derived in App. A. As explained in §3, cascading
791   can be reinterpreted as a sequence of routing problems. However, to prove optimality, we need to be
792   slightly more careful with the exact formulation of the problem.
793
794
      At step j, the cascading strategy needs to decide whether to stop the cascade or to continue to
      the next model. It should continue to the next model if any of the supermodels M1:j , . . . , M1:k is
795
      better to run than M1:j−1 for some measure of ’better’. Therefore, the cascading strategy is indeed
796
      performing a routing operation between the supermodels M1:j−1 , . . . , M1:k .
797
798   However, the optimization problem does slightly change compared to the routing problem. First of
799   all, for each query x ∈ X , there is a possibility that the cascade is stopped before step j. Therefore,
800
      the cascade should not aim to optimize the quality at step j for such a query, since it would not have
      any effect on the overall quality of the cascade. Furthermore, the budget B is only enforced over the
801
      entire cascade, and not over the individual steps. Since the problem changes through steps, it is not
802
      required that the cost of the router at step j is exactly equal to B.
803
804   Therefore, we reformulate cascading using an inner and outer optimization problem. The inner
805   optimization problem aims to find the optimal routing strategy at step j for a given budget Bj . The
806
      outer optimization problem aims to find the optimal budget Bj for each step j such that the overall
      quality of the cascade is maximized under the constraint that the total cost of the cascade is at most
807
      B.

1147   I     A DDITIONAL R ESULTS
1148
1149   In Table 11 we report the AUC scores for the RouterBench dataset across different models and noise
1150   levels for the five-shot evaluation. Our conclusions presented in §5.1 remain consistent with the
1151   results presented in Table 11. However, there is one notable inconsistency: in two of the three low-
1152   noise scenarios, our cascading strategy performs worse than the threshold-based baseline cascade.
1153
       In the scenario with three models, we find its cause can be found in the more difficult optimization
       surface for the hyperparameters of our cascading strategy. Specifically, our cascading strategy at
1154
       some point starts to lose quality as cost increases. By simply setting the hyperparameters of the
1155
       cascading strategy once it starts to lose quality to the ones where it obtained its highest quality, we
1156   obtain a quality of 83.35% over the 83.17% of the baseline cascade.
1157
1158   In contrast, for low-noise and eleven models, a similar approach does not yield a better result. Rather,
1159
       the discrepancy is caused by a small mismatch between the quality estimates of supermodels and
       the chosen model. While the quality estimate is based on the expected maximum of all models, we
1160
       restrict the selected model to be the last model that was computed in the cascade. Since the expected
1161
       maximum is higher than the quality of the last model, this discrepancy can lead to suboptimal de-
1162   cisions. By allowing both the baseline cascade and our cascading strategy to select the model with
1163   the highest quality estimate, we find that our cascading strategy once again outperforms the base-
1164   line cascade. Note that this slight discrepancy is not relevant for cascade routing, since the extra
1165   restriction is not imposed in this setting.
