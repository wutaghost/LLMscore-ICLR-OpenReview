009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) often show unwarranted preference for certain
012            choice options when responding to multiple-choice questions, posing significant
013            reliability concerns in LLM-automated systems. To mitigate this selection bias
014            problem, previous solutions utilized debiasing methods to adjust the model’s input
015            and/or output. Our work, in contrast, investigates the model’s internal representa-
016
               tion of the selection bias. Specifically, we introduce a novel debiasing approach,
               Bias Node Pruning (BNP), which eliminates the linear layer parameters that con-
017
               tribute to the bias. Furthermore, we present Auxiliary Option Injection (AOI),
018
               a simple yet effective input modification technique for debiasing, which is com-
019            patible even with black-box LLMs. To provide a more systematic evaluation of
020            selection bias, we review existing metrics and introduce Choice Kullback-Leibler
021            Divergence (CKLD), which addresses the insensitivity of the commonly used met-
022            rics to imbalance in choice labels. Experiments show that our methods are robust
023            and adaptable across various datasets when applied to three LLMs.

028

082   2     S ELECTION B IAS IN LLM S
083
084   Although LLMs are most often used for text generation, some tasks involve responding to multiple-
085   choice questions (MCQs). For example, LLMs are increasingly used to annotate data samples, a task
086   that requires selecting the best choice from several options. When responding to MCQs, however,
087   LLMs suffer from selection bias, which is the model’s inclination to prefer a choice option bound
088   with a specific symbol or located in a certain order. In this section, we formally define selection
089   bias (§ 2.1) and discuss when and where the signs of selection bias are observed (§ 2.2).
090
091   2.1   S ELECTION B IAS P ROBLEM
092
093

      lect options in a given position or with a given
094

095

099   this work, we define selection bias as the discrep-

132
133   While selection bias is a prevalent problem in querying the large language model (LLM), it is impor-
134   tant to properly identify when and where the bias is captured. Here, we provide two simple analyses
135   that motivate the design of our debiasing methods.
136
137   Selection bias is prominently captured when the model is incorrect. Figure 3(a) shows the fre-
138   quency of choices of the four response options on the ARC-Challenge dataset (Clark et al., 2018)
139   using Llama-3-8B-Instruct (Meta, 2024) and Bloomz-7b1 (Muennighoff et al., 2023). We manipu-
140   lated the test dataset to include all possible orderings of the MCQ choices. Thus, the bars should be
141
      at 0.25. However, the models prefer answer choices ‘D’ and ‘A’, respectively. These preferences are
      pronounced in cases where the models produce incorrect responses, as opposed to correct ones. This
142
      observation highlights the role of selection bias in incorrect predictions and motivates our focus on
143
      analyzing cases where the model’s output is incorrect.
144
145

146

147   different locations. Specifically, we explore the discrepancies within a single sample by permuting

201   3     M ETHODS
202
203   Motivated by our observations that the selection bias is (1) prominently seen when the model is
204
      wrong, and (2) captured in the final decoder layers, we introduce two methods for debiasing the
      model predictions: Bias Node Pruning (BNP) and Auxiliary Option Injection (AOI). As the
205
      names suggest, BNP drops nodes in the final output layer that contribute to the selection bias, and
206
      AOI utilizes an auxiliary “I don’t know” option to eliminate bias induced by ignorance.
207
208
209
      3.1   B IAS N ODE P RUNING
210

211
      selection bias materializes in the final output projection parameters, W ∈ Rd×|V| , where V is
212   the vocabulary set. To mitigate the selection bias problem induced by the linear layer, we prune
213   the parameters in W that contribute to the bias. In choosing which parameters to prune, we gain
214   intuition by approximating a biased model, F, as

      7     R ELATED W ORKS
503
504   Selection Bias. The large language models’ tendency to favor choices in a certain order or with
505   a specific symbol has been discussed in many previous works. Some of the works investigated
506   the skewed pattern of responses for MCQs (Zheng et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024; Pezeshkpour &
507   Hruschka, 2024), emphasizing that selection bias is a critical problem. Many works have approached
508   this problem by calibrating the output probabilities (Wang et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024; Reif
509   & Schwartz, 2024; Wei et al., 2024; Pezeshkpour & Hruschka, 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Balepur
510   et al., 2024; Li & Gao, 2024; Gupta et al., 2024), while others change the way queries are input (Li
511   et al., 2023b; Robinson et al., 2023). Additional approaches include debiasing the LLM through
512
      distillation training (Liusie et al., 2024) and training the model to enforce its multiple choice symbol
      binding (MCSB) property (Xue et al., 2024). While parameter pruning methods are often used for
513
      efficient deep learning (Srinivas & Babu, 2015; Han et al., 2016; Zhu & Gupta, 2017; Molchanov
514
      et al., 2019; 2022) or to have the LLM unlearn certain factual knowledge (Liu et al., 2024; Pochinkov
515   & Schoots, 2024), parameter pruning has rarely been discussed for debiasing. Thus, our Bias Node
516   Pruning is a novel approach in the context of the selection bias.
517
      Auxiliary Options. Inclusion of the “I don’t know” option can improve the quality of data col-
518
      lected in surveys (Schuman & Presser, 1996) but does not meaningfully impact the labels assigned
519
      by annotators (Beck et al., 2022). Recent research has drawn attention to the similarities between
520   surveys, labeling tasks, and model responses to MCQs (Tjuatja et al., 2023; Eckman et al., 2024;
521   Chen et al., 2024) Further research into LLMs’ response behavior would benefit from incorporating
522   insights from the survey science domain: see the discussion in (Eckman et al., 2024).
523

      8     C ONCLUSION
526   When LLMs answer MCQs, selection bias is a critical problem. Previous research has predomi-
527   nantly focused on modifying the LLM’s input and/or output. In contrast, we uncover the internal
528
      source of the bias by scrutinizing the embedding-level discrepancies introduced by this bias. Build-
      ing on these insights, we propose Bias Node Pruning (BNP) and Auxiliary Option Injection (AOI).
529
      Additionally, we address the limitations of existing performance-based evaluation metrics by in-
530
      troducing a new distribution-based metric, Choice Kullback-Leibler Divergence (CKLD), which ad-
531   dresses the insensitivity of prior metrics to imbalance of choice labels. Our approach improved MCQ
532   answering performance by reducing the level of selection bias across widely used MCQ datasets
533   using both open-source (white box) and closed-source (black-box) models. BNP and AOI work
534   alongside other debiasing/decoding methods to improve the base performance of Llama-3 by up to
535   33.8% on the ARC-Challenge dataset. We also conducted in-depth analyses to better understand the
536   effect of each component, along with case studies to provide qualitative insight. Overall, our method
537   provides a novel intuition in scrutinizing the internal source of selection bias, and also provides a
538   new approach in debiasing LLMs.

764   A.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
765
      Here, we detail how we retrieve model predictions and list hyperparameters used for each model-
766
      dataset experiment.
767
768
      How are predictions retrieved? As discussed in the main paper, we use the token output prob-
769
      ability distribution to select a token ID for prediction. For instance, if z ∈ R|V| is the output logit
770   vector of the first output token, we use z[‘A’] + z[‘ A’] to retrieve the logit value for choice ‘A’, and
771   do the same for other choices as well. Note that ’ A’ is a token that represents ”A” with a space
772   in front of it, whereas ’A’ is a one-character token. Since these two represent the same choice, we
773   aggregate their logits, z, for accurate evaluation. Then, we take the softmax over all the choice logits
774   to retrieve the final probability distribution over the choices.
775
776   System prompt. We use the same system prompt across all experiments: “You are an AI assistant
777   that answers multiple choice questions. Please respond with capitalized alphabet(s) that correspond
778   to the correct answer”. For Chain-of-Thought reasoning baseline experiments, we use a slightly
779   different version of “You are an AI assistant that answers multiple choice questions. Please think
780
      step by step and respond with capitalized alphabet(s) that correspond to the correct answer” to
      encourage the model to output a step-by-step reasoning process.
781
782
      Hyperparameters. The number of nodes pruned is the main hyperparameter of our experiments.
783
      As disclosed in the main paper, we pruned 32 nodes in all experiments with Llama-3 and Mistral,
784
      and pruned 128 nodes in experiments with Bloomz. We did a simple hyperparameter search among
785   {16, 32, 64, 128} nodes. Results can be found in Figure 6(a) and Figure 9. Another noteworthy
786   hyperparameter is the choice delimiter, which refers to the type of token used to separate choices.
787   In our preliminary experiments, we found that different choice delimiters such as space (‘ ’), line
788   break tokens (‘\n’), multiple lines (‘\n\n\n’), or special tags (‘<c>’) have varying impact on per-
789   formance. As there were no consistent results, however, we chose to use the basic space delimiter
790   in all our experiments, e.g. ‘What is 1 + 1? (A) 2 (B) 3 (C) 4’. Although we do not discuss this in
791   depth as it is beyond the scope of our work, we believe that analyzing the effect of different choice
792   delimiters in multiple choice question answering would introduce an interesting viewpoint.

       C.1   W HY DOES AN LLM NEED TO MATCH THE GROUND TRUTH RATIO ?
991
992    Consider a scenario in which an LLM exhibits a bias toward selecting option ‘A’. In cases where
993    the LLM is uncertain about the correct answer and resorts to random selection, it is more likely to
994    choose ‘A’, resulting in a skewed overall choice distribution that diverges from the ground truth dis-
995    tribution. In contrast, an unbiased LLM would select options uniformly under uncertainty, producing
996    a choice distribution that more closely aligns with the original ground truth distribution. Therefore,
997    the extent to which an LLM’s predictions match the ground truth distribution can serve as a proxy
998    for measuring Selection Bias.

1384   G     L IMITATIONS AND B ROADER I MPACT
1385
1386   Limitations. The limitation of this work (and also most works on mitigating selection bias) is that
1387   we still do not know the root cause of the selection bias. While there have been various hypotheses
1388
       on the reason behind this phenomenon, most focused on the superficial effect of it without con-
       sidering what in the first place triggered such ungrounded preferences. Future research will need
1389
       to unravel the core of selection bias by answering questions like, What data points cause selection
1390
       bias? or What makes the difference in choice preferences between heterogenous model families?
1391   These questions will be critical in understanding LLMs in general, as it is closely related to how the
1392   models choose the next tokens to output.
1393
1394   Broader Impact. This work reveals and mitigates a type of bias present in recent large language
1395   models (LLMs). Considering that LLMs have become an integral part of various applications from
1396   customer service to science, the presence of any type of bias can negatively impact the reliability of
1397   systems and degrade precision in model- or data-driven decision-making. By addressing the bias,
1398   our research not only improves the accuracy and fairness of these models but also has the potential
1399   to enhance the trustworthiness of LLMs in general. Moreover, this work serves as a foundation for
1400   ongoing efforts to scrutinize and enhance LLM-automated systems, introducing a new perspective
1401   on analyzing performance.
1402
1403   Future Application. One closely related application of selection bias debiasing is data annotation.
       Many works discussed ways to leverage LLMs for automated annotation (He et al., 2024; Eckman


                                                          26
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1404
       et al., 2024), or devised human-machine collaborative frameworks (Li et al., 2023a). We expect our
1405   work to benefit such annotation systems by reducing the selection bias in answering MCQs.
