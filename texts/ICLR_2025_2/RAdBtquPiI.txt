009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            One of the core challenges when applying reinforcement learning to solve real
012            world problems is the violation of numerous safety, feasibility or physical con-
013            straints during training and deployment. We propose Bender’s Oracle Optimiza-
014            tion (BOO) that manages to achieve provable safety during both training and de-
015            ployment, under the assumption that one has access to a representation of the
016            feasible set, e.g., through a (possibly inaccurate) simulator or encoded rules. This
017
               method is particularly useful for cases where a simple (deterministic) model of
               the problem is available, but said model is too inaccurate or incomplete to solve
018
               the problem directly. We showcase our method by applying it to a challenging
019
               reward-maximizing stochastic job-shop scheduling problem, where we demon-
020            strate a 17% improvement, and a nonlinear, nonconvex packing problem where
021            we achieve close to globally optimal performance while improving the conver-
022            gence speed by a factor of 800.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Reinforcement Learning (RL) is a powerful technique for solving challenging Markov Decision
028   Processes (MDPs) through interaction with an environment. This approach has recently shown
029
      impressive results in a wide variety of planning and control problems, such as scheduling (Bayliss
      et al., 2017), process planning (Floudas & Lin, 2005), robotics (oh Kang et al., 2023), and network
030
      design (Menon et al., 2013), as well as being extended towards more general problem sets, such
031
      as matrix factorization (Fawzi et al., 2022), quantum circuit optimization (Ruiz et al., 2024), or
032   algorithm discovery (Mankowitz et al., 2023). One major obstacle for these approaches is that they
033   need to guarantee that the solution to these planning problems is within a constrained subset of
034   possible plans to qualify as a solution. Such constraints are often necessary for safety (e.g., a cargo
035   ship should not be overloaded; the power grid should never produce a blackout, etc.) or feasibility
036   reasons (e.g., a scheduler should not double-book appointments; a robot should not get stuck with
037   an empty battery; a matrix factorization has to return the same matrix, etc.).
038   Classically, these constraints have been modeled using the framework of Constrained Markov De-
039   cision Processes (CMDP) (Altman, 1999), which introduce a cost function c(x) whose expected
040   cumulative (discounted) sum has to be below a threshold C:

080   This effect is even more pronounced when such invariants have to be upheld during training since
081   the policy is not yet capable of controlling the state x enough to stay within simple feasible set.
082   In practice, upholding such guarantees during training promises to be very useful in the practical
083   application of RL since it makes online-training of such models feasible. However, to guarantee
084
      absolute safety during training one has to make sure that the policy only affects the quality of a
      solution, not the feasibility of it. In current deep learning literature this is usually solved using ad-hoc
085
      reparametrizations (e.g., Fawzi et al. (2022); Bello et al. (2016)), which means such networks have
086

087   even exists for general NP-complete problems, such as CSPs1 .
088
089   In this work we showcase a general method that can solve arbitrarily constrained problems under the
090
      assumption that our policy has access to a constrained world model. By utilizing techniques from
      mathematical programming we guarantee the agent stays within the feasible region during inference
091
      and training, which allows for fully online training without loosing safety guarantees. We do this
092
      by proposing a universal parameterization that replaces the existing action set (e.g., “schedule job
093   ABC in timeslot XYZ”) with a new set of actions that correspond to cutting-planes inspired from
094   the framework of the bender’s decomposition. Instead of sequentially placing actions in a CMDP
095   (Eq.1), this method sequentially modifies an optimization problem representing the set of “safe”
096   plans (see Figure 1) to find high value solutions. This way, we can generate safe trajectories over the
097   CMDP during training and inference, at the cost of needing an approximate world model. We also
098   remain scalable by moving the complexity of strict feasibility preservation into a dedicated solver
099   such as SCIP (Bestuzheva et al., 2021) without sacrificing the expressivity of our neural network. We
100   showcase our method by applying it to a challenging reward-maximizing stochastic job scheduling
101
      problem, and a challenging nonconvex nonlinear discrete packing problem.

120   Perhaps the closest work to ours is Dalal et al. (2018). They consider exploration inside a continuous
121   space where safety is guaranteed by re-projecting any action into a feasible set of safe actions. While
122   they consider hard constraints, they can only operate in continuous action spaces. Continuous action
123

      around critical areas. This is in stark contrast to combinatorial problems, where one may need to
124
      plan many steps ahead to be able to plan around dangerous actions. Similar to our method, they also
125

126   solver) to compute the projection onto the feasible set. Our method has the advantage of not being
127   limited to QP-solvers and being able to deal with combinatorial settings.

129
      From the point of view of combinatorial optimization and RL, one seminal work to mention is
      from Bello et al. (2016), who consider both a solver for the travelling salesman problem (TSP)
130
      and Knapsack problem that shows strong performance on solving both these classical problems.
131
      However, they do not consider learning from a stochastic or nonlinear environments. Their method
132   also needs specialized parametrizations (i.e., so-called pointer networks (Vinyals et al., 2015)) for
133   every problem type which does not even exist for many problem types. For instance, Bello et al.
134   (2016) uses the fact that the TSP instances they consider live on a fully connected graph, meaning
135   that one can arbitrarily pick any order of nodes and will still get a possible (but perhaps very bad)
136   tour. If one had a sparsely connected graph, Bello et al. (2016)’s method would no longer work as
137   picking certain node orders can get the agent into a dead-end2 .

      Bender’s decomposition splits this optimization problem into a master problem and a subproblem.
152
      The master problem proposes solutions to the problem in y, ignoring the impact of the choice of
153   x. The subproblem then uses the solution y from the master to solve for the remaining variables
154   x. Based on the value and feasibility of the subproblem we then add additional constraints into the
155   master problem and repeat the optimization with the additional constraint.

      that do not lead to a solvable subproblem, and optimizing constraints which manipulate the objective
158
      function of the master problem to steer it towards better solutions. To control the objective function,
159   one classically adds an auxilliary variable φ that is lower bounded by cutting information from the

183   subproblem (see, e.g. Geoffrion (1972)).
184   Instead of solving a subproblem, we propose a “Benders Decomposition Oracle” that directly learns
185   a scalar corresponding to the bias b = z(x∗ ) + λT ∇x g(x∗ , y∗)x∗ ∈ R, and a vector corresponding
186   to the linear weight w = λT ∇x g(x∗ , y∗) ∈ Rd without explicitly constructing and solving the un-
187   derlying optimization problem(s). Both of these values are learned end-to-end using reinforcement
188   learning from feedback by a simulator. This means that instead of creating a CMDP across the
189   “time” dimension where actions are iteratively unrolled, we create an MDP across a “cutting plane”
190   dimension which iteratively places more constraints on the model until a high-valued plan is found.
191

193

      Modeling safe reinforcement learning this way has a couple of key advantages over both ordinary
      reinforcement learning and ordinary stochastic optimization. Regarding the former, notice that we
221
      can trivially uphold arbitrary safety and feasibility guarantees on the solution x∗ by explicitly con-
222
      straining them in the master problem. This is particularly important in cases where guaranteeing
223   feasibility is highly nontrivial, or when learning during deployment. Further notice the scalability
224   advantages of this method: Since we can delegate feasibility constraints to highly advanced opti-
225   mizers, we can quickly solve highly constraint problems.
226
      From the point of view of stochastic programming, we have the advantage that our method can
227
      incorporate arbitrary (nonconvex) nonlinear and stochastic effects inside its cut-generating function:
228
      Notice that the policies input state s ∈ S can include both information from the master solution x∗ ,
229   but also external information, which helps us to learn from the environment as in every other RL
230   problem. For instance, in the case of job scheduling, one can include the type of job, likelihood
231   of a person getting sick, expected time taken for the job, expected profit for the job, etc. into
232   the estimation of the cut. The impact of these features is generally hard to model classically or it
233   introduces a high degree of nonlinearity into the optimization. The RL framework we propose only
234   adds affine-linear constraints to the problem (Eq. 8), meaning that every linear program stays linear,
235   every convex problem stays convex, etc. This makes guaranteeing feasibility very fast since we can
236   take advantage of high quality specialized solvers for e.g. mixed-integer linear programming.

238   5     E XPERIMENTS
239
240   We benchmark our solver on two problems. First, we study learning a nonlinear and nonconvex
241   objective function over a nontrivial feasible set, but without considering stochasticity. Since we can
242   set this problem, we can compare against the global optimum as found by the SCIP global nonlinear
243   optimizer (Bestuzheva et al., 2023). We also utilize this problem to showcase an interesting, while
244   not unexpected property of our method: Our solver is capable of learning a parametrization of the
245
      problem that is significantly faster to solve than the true parametrization.
246   Second, we consider a stochastic job scheduling problem, where the objective is to maximize the
247   profit of a set of jobs, each consisting of a set of operations that have to be completed in order,
248   within a limited time. A job only receives profit if all its operations are completed in the correct
249   order by the time of completion. We add stochasticity to the problem, by having a set of task-types
250   that determine how likely a job is delayed (forcing replanning) and how much profit is to be made
251
      by completing the job. This means our agent has to learn a complex risk-reward tradeoff, while also
      having to produce feasible job-scheduling plans.

      6    L IMITATIONS
456
457
      The main limitation of this method is the need for a representation of the constraints and decision
      variables. In general reinforcement learning these types of model may be hard to get, but we would
458
      argue that in cases where hard constraints are demanded during training one generally has access to
459
      such a model. This is because if one wants to have any hope of being absolutely safe during training,
460   one needs to have a notion of safety before a single step is taken. Therefore we would argue that
461   having access to a constrained model is not fully unrealistic in safety critical or high complexity
462   scenarios. One can also learn a model of the constraint set (like Eq. 13) from data, but as this is a
463   completely orthogonal problem from acting inside such a model, we do not discuss this here.
464
      Learning a safe model can also be viewed as learning feasibility cuts (see Section 3), which we do
465
      not explicitly cover in this work. However, extending our framework to this would be relatively
466   straight forward, as one can simply train a second policy π feas that predicts feasibility cuts, rather
467   than optimality cuts. The reason we do not cover this here is because it is unclear how one should
468   assign credit for those cuts. This is especially an issue for stochastic environments where an instance
469   might be feasible in the realization of the random variables that was actually sampled, but might be
470   infeasible for almost all other instances. In those cases one has to answer the question of whether
471   such an instance should be judged as feasible or infeasible. For this reason, we leave the issue of
472   credit assignment for feasibility cuts for future work.
473
474   7    C ONCLUSION
475
476   We propose a generalized method for enforcing arbitrary (known) constraints in highly constrained
477   reinforcement learning problems. Our method is able to enforce arbitrary hard constraints during
478   both training and inference, allowing for more flexible utilization of our reinforcement learning in
479   (safety)-constrained environments. Due to the utilization of affine-linear cuts, we can use highly
480   efficient solvers which allows us to scale to complex combinatorial problems which are usually out
481   of reach for reinforcement learning.
482   We showcase the abilities of our method in a synthetic combinatorial environment, and a job-
483   scheduling problem. Our method shows superior performance over both a MILP and neural-network
484   baseline, while offering drastically faster convergence compared to a MINLP solver, in cases where
485   an analytical expression exists. To our knowledge this is the first reinforcement learning method that
      allows arbitrary constraints to be enforced during training and inference.
