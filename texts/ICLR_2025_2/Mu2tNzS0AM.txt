009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown impressive zero-shot and few-shot
012            capabilities in many NLP tasks including text classification. While these models
013            outperform others in terms of raw performance when few examples are available,
014            they are expensive to use in practice and may lag behind traditional approaches
015            when labeled (or unlabeled) data is plentiful. Semi-supervised learning (SSL) can
016            utilize large amounts of unlabeled data in combination with labeled data to improve
017            a model’s performance. In this paper, we propose to unify LLM and SSL under a
018
               common framework which effectively leverages the few-shot capabilities of LLMs
               in combination with SSL’s ability to extract valuable information from unlabeled
019
               data to improve model performance in text classification tasks. Our approach,
020
               called LLM-SSL, utilizes LLMs to generate predictions on unlabeled examples
021            and uses these predictions to guide the SSL training and improve the quality of
022            pseudo-labels during training. We show that LLM-SSL outperforms both prior
023            SSL models as well as few-shot LLMs on six text classification benchmarks.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Semi-supervised learning (SSL) has emerged as a powerful approach with good few-shot learning
029   capabilities. SSL mitigates the requirement for large labeled datasets by using a model itself to assign
030   pseudo-labels to unlabeled data, and thus, effectively makes use of information from unlabeled data
031   Chen et al. (2020b); Arazo et al. (2020); Xie et al. (2020a). To ensure high-quality pseudo-labels, SSL
032   approaches Lee et al. (2013); Xie et al. (2020a); Sohn et al. (2020) leverage fixed high-confidence
033
      thresolds during training, which allow the model to access unlabeled examples during training only
      if the model’s confidence on these examples is very high.
034
035   While methods that employ high-confidence thresholds such as FixMatch Sohn et al. (2020) are
036   shown to consistently reduce the confirmation bias Arazo et al. (2019), these rigid thresholds allow
037   access only to a small amount of unlabeled data for training, and thus, ignore a considerable amount
038   of unlabeled (and diverse) examples for which the model’s predictions do not exceed the fixed
039
      confidence threshold. Zhang et al. (2021) introduced FlexMatch that relaxes the rigid confidence
      threshold to account for the model’s learning status of each class and adaptively scales down the
040
      threshold for a class to encourage the model to learn from more examples from that class. Moreover,
041
      Chen et al. (2023) proposed SoftMatch that does not discard any unlabeled examples—no matter
042   how low their confidence is, but assigns adaptive weights according to the model’s confidence. Both
043   FlexMatch and SoftMatch have access to a much larger and diverse set of unlabeled data to learn
044   from, but lowering or eliminating the thresholds can lead to the introduction of wrong pseudo-labels
045   (even if with a low weight), which are extremely harmful for generalization.
046
      To address this drawback and mitigate the harmful effects of wrong pseudo-labels, we leverage Large
047
      Language Models (LLMs) Wei et al. (2022); Kojima et al. (2022a); Gao et al. (2021); Schick &
048   Schütze (2021), a line of research which has produced models with great zero-shot and few-shot
049   capabilities and combine it with SSL. Our approach entitled LLM-SSL consists of two separate
050   models: an LLM that uses few-shot prompting and a BERT model that is fine-tuned on labeled and
051   pseudo-labeled data over multiple training iterations. We utilize the LLM to generate predictions
052   on unlabeled examples and to guide the BERT model especially in the early stages of training when
053   BERT is not very robust but rather produces unreliable or noisy predictions (due to small supervised
      data used for training). Specifically, we distill the knowledge of LLM through a novel teaching


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      annealing method that fuses the pseudo-labels generated by BERT during SSL training with those
055   generated by the LLM, assigning a higher weight to the LLM predictions if the BERT model has a
056   poor learning status (as measured on the training set) and gradually lowering the weight of the LLM
057   predictions if the BERT model has a high learning status. In addition, LLM-SSL reduces the amount
058   of incorrect pseudo-labels during training by analyzing the behavior of the model on unlabeled data
059   from the start of training up until the end instead of relying solely on the confidence of the model
060   at a single iteration to impose the confidence threshold Sohn et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2021); Xie
061   et al. (2020a). We estimate the correctness of a pseudo-label using margins Bartlett et al. (2017);
062   Pleiss et al. (2020); Elsayed et al. (2018); Jiang et al. (2018); Sosea & Caragea (2023) of unlabeled
063
      examples averaged across the training iterations. We believe that this type of thresholding that takes
      into account the entire training history is more expressive and can encode more information about a
064
      pseudo-label, leading to better data quality. Our approach is lightweight, requiring significantly less
065
      compute than LLMs in practice and incurring smaller computational cost during training compared to
066   other SSL methods (since it starts with higher quality pseudo-labels provided by LLMs and gradually
067   switches to BERT pseudo-labels as the BERT becomes increasingly more robust).
068
069
      We carry out comprehensive experiments using various experimental setups on six SSL text classi-
      fication benchmarks: IMDB Maas et al. (2011), RCV1 Lewis et al. (2004), GoEmotions Demszky
070
      et al. (2020), Amazon Review McAuley & Leskovec (2013), TREC-6 Li & Roth (2002), and Yelp
071
      Review Asghar (2016). We find that LLM-SSL yields significant improvements on all benchmarks,
072   outperforming strong LLM and SSL baselines. Notably, our method outperforms FlexMatch and
073   SoftMatch by 7.5% and 5.34%, respectively, in accuracy on IMDB using 20 labels per class and by
074   as much as 7.3% and 2.8% on Amazon Review using 20 labels per class.
075
      Our contributions are as follows: 1) We combine SSL and LLM under a common framework: LLM-
076
      SSL allows access to a large set of unlabeled data to learn from and enforces high pseudo-label quality
077
      during training by leveraging the vast knowledge of LLMs and by monitoring the training dynamics
078   of unlabeled data as training progresses to detect and filter out potentially incorrect pseudo-labels;
079   2) We show that LLM-SSL outperforms existing works on six well-established text classification
080   benchmarks showing larger improvements in error rates especially on challenging datasets, while
081   achieving similar convergence performance (or better) than strong prior works; 3) We perform a
082   comprehensive analysis of our approach and indicate potential insights into why our LLM-SSL
083   substantially outperforms other techniques.

      We first discuss related work on semi-supervised learning for text classification. Second, we discuss
088
      Large Language Models (LLMs) and their application to text classification.
089
090   Semi-supervised Learning Semi-supervised learning has attracted much attention in the NLP
091   community Gururangan et al. (2019); Yang et al. (2015); Clark et al. (2018); Chen et al. (2020a);
092   Yang et al. (2017); Chen et al. (2020a); Xie et al. (2020a); Mukherjee & Awadallah (2020); Miyato
093
      et al. (2016); Wang et al. (2022); Yang et al. (2023); Shi et al. (2023); Huang et al. (2023); Tan et al.
      (2024), since unlabeled data is often much easier to acquire compared to labeled data. Yang et al.
094
      (2019) used a hierarchy structure to propagate supervision from high-level labels to lower-level labels,
095
      while Clark et al. (2018) introduced cross-view training, where a model makes auxiliary predictions
096   only seeing parts of the input text and is trained to match the predictions when given the entire input.
097   Mukherjee & Awadallah (2020) introduced uncertainty estimates into self-training (UST), a particular
098   type of SSL where a teacher and a student are iteratively trained using labeled and unlabeled data.
099   SoftMatch Chen et al. (2023) does not utilize any threshold and instead dynamically weights each
100   unlabeled example during training, assigning lower weights to examples whose pseudo-labels are
101   potentially incorrect and higher weights otherwise. MarginMatch Sosea & Caragea (2023) monitors
102   the training dynamics of unlabeled examples during SSL training and at an arbitrary iteration imposes
103   a threshold that takes into account the behavior of the model from the beginning of training up until the
104
      current iteration. We use UST, SoftMatch, and MarginMatch as strong baselines in our experiments.
105   Consistency regularization Sajjadi et al. (2016) is an important component in recent semi-supervised
106   learning approaches and relies on the continuity assumption Bachman et al. (2014); Laine & Aila
107   (2017) that the model should output similar predictions on multiple perturbed versions of the
      same input example. Popular approaches such as Unsupervised Data Augmentation (UDA) Xie


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al. (2020a), FixMatch Sohn et al. (2020) and FlexMatch Zhang et al. (2021) use consistency
109   regularization at their core combined with psedo-labeling. In psedo-labeling Lee et al. (2013), a
110   model itself is used to assign artificial labels for unlabeled data and only artificial labels whose
111   largest class probability is above a predefined confidence threshold are used during training. We
112   consider UDA, FixMatch and FlexMatch as strong baselines, and compare LLM-SSL against them in
113   all our experiments. While these SSL methods maintain pseudo-label quality using high-confidence
114   thresholds, in fully supervised learning, Area-Under-the-Margin (AUM) Pleiss et al. (2020) is a
115   popular technique to ensure high quality labels by monitoring the training dynamics of examples
116   and removing potentially mislabeled examples from training. Inspired by this work, we leverage
117
      training dynamics of unlabeled examples to maintain qualitative pseudo-labels during training.
118   Large Language Models Language models (LMs) are models that estimate the probability distri-
119   bution over text. Recently, improvements through larger amounts of data (e.g. WebText Gao et al.
120   (2020)) and increasingly larger model sizes (from a few million Merity et al. (2016) to hundreds of
121   millions Devlin et al. (2019) to hundreds of billions Brown et al. (2020) parameters) have enabled
122   pre-trained large language models (LLMs) to be incredibly powerful at solving many downstream
123
      NLP tasks. In the past, language models were used in a pre-train and fine-tune manner where a
      language model is pretrained on a large unlabeled corpus then adapted to a target task by fine-tuning
124
      Devlin et al. (2019). However, it was recently observed that scaling models to 100B+ parameters
125
      leads to capabilities of few-shot learning Brown et al. (2020) by way of in-context learning. One
126   can guide the model generation by simply designing a prompt to solve the task, starting an era of
127   pre-train and prompt Liu et al. (2023). In this work, we use one of these proposed chain-of-thought
128   prompting (CoT) Kojima et al. (2022b) techniques for few-shot predictions. We subsequently use
129   these predictions in our SSL framework to substantially improve SSL performance.

143

      3.2     P ROPOSED A PPROACH : LLM-SSL
163
164   While the flexible thresholds of FlexMatch allow access to a diverse set of unlabeled data, these
165   thresholds can introduce incorrect pseudo-labels, which are harmful due to confirmation bias Lee
166   et al. (2013). To address this drawback, we propose to analyze the historical predictions of the
167   model on unlabeled examples instead of relying solely on the confidence at an iteration. At the same
168   time, since the model predictions at the beginning of training are usually not reliable, we propose
169   to leverage the world knowledge of LLMs through a novel teacher annealing framework to guide
170
      the SSL training process during the early stages of training.

      We experiment with the following benchmark datasets to asses the effectiveness of our method:

304   fine-grained emotions and to develop emotion classification models; (5) Amazon Review McAuley &

      six broad semantic categories; (6) Yelp Review Asghar (2016) is a sentiment classification dataset

      4.2    BASELINES
311
      First, we carry out experiments using fully supervised and LLM-based models. BASE is a fully
312   supervised approach obtained by training a BERT Devlin et al. (2019) base uncased model on
313   the labeled data only. LLM-ZS (LLM-Zero Shot) obtains predictions using our LLM in a zero-
314   shot fashion. LLM-FS (LLM-Few Shot) obtains predictions using our LLM using few-shot CoT
315   prompting. This is the model used to obtain predictions in our LLM-SSL framework. LLM-DS
316   (LLM-Distant Supervision) uses the LLM to generate labels on all the unlabeled examples. Then it
317   trains a BERT model in a supervised fashion on the union of labeled data and newly LLM-generated
318   pseudo-labeled data. LLM-DS-AUM is very similar to LLM-DS, however, during training on the
319   union of labeled data and pseudo-labeled data we also monitor the AUM of the pseudo-labeled
320
      examples. Then, similar to Pleiss et al. (2020), we remove the low-AUM pseudo-labeled examples
      and train the model again.
321
322   Second, we carry out experiments using semi-supervised approaches based on a teacher-student frame-
323   work. NOISY-S (Noisy Student Training) Xie et al. (2020b) involves generating pseudo-labels for un-
      labeled data and iteratively training models in a teacher-student setup. UST (Uncertainty-aware Self-


                                                               6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Training) Mukherjee & Awadallah (2020) incorporates uncertainty estimates into the standard self-
325   training framework by adding a few highly effective changes. UST computes uncertainty estimates
326   for all unlabeled examples by stochastically passing the examples from this set through the model
327   multiple times, with dropout enabled before each layer. The approach subsequently uses these uncer-
328   tainty estimates to select what unlabeled data to use. Concretely, the model not only favors unlabeled
329   data where the teacher model is confident, but also enforces low entropy of the teacher predictions.
330
      Third, we experiment with approaches based on consistency regularization. UDA (Unsupervised
331   Data Augmentation) Xie et al. (2020a) leverages Backtranslation Edunov et al. (2018) and uses
332   a consistency loss to enforce the model predictions on unlabeled data to be invariant to input
333   noise. FixMatch Sohn et al. (2020) predicts artificial labels for unlabeled examples using a weakly-
334   augmented version of each unlabeled example and then employs the artificial labels as pseudo-labels
335   to train against but this time using a strongly-augmented version of each unlabeled example. FixMatch
336   uses unlabeled examples solely if the confidence of the model prediction exceeds a fixed threshold.
337   FlexMatch Zhang et al. (2021) argued that prior methods ignore the learning difficulties of different
338   classes, and introduced class-dependent thresholds to account for these learning difficulties. We
339
      presented FlexMatch in detail in Section §3.1. SoftMatch Chen et al. (2023) eliminates the thresholds
      completely and instead dynamically weights the unlabeled examples during training, assigning lower
340
      weights to potentially mislabeled examples and higher weight otherwise. MarginMatch Sosea &
341
      Caragea (2023) utilizes training dynamics of unlabeled examples to design a more effective threshold
342   for eliminating incorrect pseudo-labels.
343

      4.3   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
346
      We evaluate the performance of our LLM-SSL by varying the number of training examples on the
347
      six text classification benchmark datasets presented above. On each dataset, we experiment with 20,
348   50, 100, and 200 labeled examples per class, which we sample without replacement. The remaining
349   examples are used as unlabeled data. We follow the exact evaluation metrics used in the works
350   introducing the datasets: accuracy for IMDB, TREC-6, Amazon Review, Yelp Review and macro
351   F1 for GoEmotions and RCV1. In each setup, we also run our models three times, with different
352   parameter intializations, and report the average results, as well as their standard deviations. All our
353   experiments use BERT Devlin et al. (2019) base uncased as the backbone model which is trained
354   for 200 epochs. We use the translation models provided by Tiedemann & Thottingal (2020) for
355   backtranslation. In terms of augmentations, we create weakly augmented examples using synonym
356
      replacement Kolomiyets et al. (2011) and SwitchOut Wang et al. (2018) by randomly performing one
      or both augmentations. In terms of strong augmentations, we perform a random combination of Back-
357
      translations Tiedemann & Thottingal (2020) using long chain lengths (> 5), SwitchOut and synonym
358
      replacements. In all our experiments, the baselines use the same weak and strong augmentations as
359   LLM-SSL. For few-shot prompts we leverage a maximum of 5 examples per class in the prompt with
360   a value lower than 5 if the prompt exceeds the maximum Mistral context window sequence length
361   (4096 tokens). The few-shot examples originate from the small available labeled set (e.g., the set of
362   20/50/100/200 examples per class). We emphasize that our trained model is easy to use in practice,
363   requiring only inference using our BERT model. While our method involves using an LLM during
364   training, we also note that we only run inference once on the unlabeled set (Step 1 in Algorithm 1).
365

412   5.1      A BLATION S TUDY
413
414   We perform an ablation study to tease apart the components that lead to the success of our LLM-
415   SSL. To this end, we design the following variations of LLM-SSL and train them in all settings
416   (20/50/100/200 labels per class on the six datasets): 1) APM method discards completely the LLM
417
      component of our approach. In this version, the model uses solely its predictions on weakly augmented
      examples to generate the pseudo-labels. 2) LLM-SSLnaive is similar to our main LLM-SSL but
418
      instead of using the learning status of the model to weigh the LLM and base model pseudo-labels
419
      (i.e., the S term in Equation 8) it weighs them equally instead (i.e., S = 0.5). 3) LLM-SSLnoAP M
420   eliminates the APM-based threshold from Equation 9, keeping only the confidence threshold. 4)
421   LLM-SSLagreement is similar to LLM-SSLnaive but instead of weighing the pseudo-labels of the
422   base model and the LLM equally, our model only uses the pseudo-label if the base model and the
423   LLM agree (i.e., the LLM and base model pseudo-labels are the same).
424
      We show the results obtained in Table 2. Discarding the LLM completely (i.e., APM method) leads to
425
      considerable degradations on all datasets. Notably, we see a degradation in accuracy of 5.4% on IMDB
426   using 20 examples per class. Along the same lines, on RCV1 we also observe a degradation of 5.2% in
427   the same setup. Interestingly, removing the APM metric from our approach (LLM-SSLnoAP M ) also
428   decreases the performance, indicating that the combination of LLM with the APM-based threshold
429   both contribute to the observed benefits. At the same time, using the LLM to impose an additional
430   threshold (LLM-SSLagreement ) significantly lowers the performance of our method. For example,
431   our LLM-SSL outperforms LLM-SSLagreement by 3.0% F1 on GoEmotions using 20 examples per

477   6     C ONCLUSION
478
479
      We improve semi-supervised learning in text classification by 1) Introducing a novel Average Pseudo-
480   Margin unlabeled example selection technique and 2) Leveraging LLMs through a novel teacher
481   annealing framework to incorporate external knowledge into our model. We show that our approach
482   is effective in a wide range of domains (social networks, forums, online platforms) and contexts
483   (movie reviews, medical forum discussions) and outperforms other strong SSL approaches. In the
484   future, we plan to study our method in settings where there is a mismatch between the labeled and
485   unlabeled data distributions and analyze how we can use out-of-domain unlabeled data to boost the
      performance.
