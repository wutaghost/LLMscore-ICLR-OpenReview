009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) have shown promise in solving par-
012            tial differential equations (PDEs), with growing interest in their energy-efficient,
013            real-time training on edge devices. Photonic computing offers a potential solution
014            due to its high operation speed. However, the lack of photonic memory and the
015            large footprint of current photonic devices prevent training realistic-size PINNs
016            on photonic chips. This paper proposes a completely back-propagation-free (BP-
017            free) and highly salable framework to enable training real-size PINNs on silicon
018
               photonics platforms. Our approach involves three key innovations: (1) a sparse-
               grid Stein derivative estimator to avoid the BP in the loss evaluation of a PINN,
019
               (2) a dimension-reduced zeroth-order optimization via tensor-train decomposi-
020
               tion to achieve better scalability and convergence in BP-free training, and (3) a
021            scalable on-chip photonic PINN training accelerator design using photonic tensor
022            cores. We validate the performance of our numerical methods in both low- and
023            high-dimensional PDE benchmarks. Through circuit simulation based on real de-
024            vice parameters, we further demonstrate the significant performance benefit (e.g.,
025            real-time training, huge chip area reduction) of our photonic accelerator. Our
026            framework addresses the fundamental challenges of photonic AI and will enable
027            real-time training of real-size PINNs on photonic chips.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Partial differential equations (PDEs) are used to describe numerous engineering problems, such as
033
      electromagnetic and thermal analysis of IC chips (Kamon et al., 1993; Li et al., 2004), medical imag-
      ing (Villena et al., 2015), and safety verification of autonomous systems (Bansal & Tomlin, 2021).
034
      Traditional numerical solvers (e.g., finite-difference, finite-element methods) have been well stud-
035
      ied and commercialized, but they become prohibitively expensive for high-dimensional PDEs due to
036   the exponential increase of the unknown variables with respect to spatial/temporal/parameter dimen-
037   sions. This bottleneck becomes more significant in PDE-constrained inverse and control problems,
038   since the forward problem needs to be solved many times in an outer iteration loop.
039
      Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) (Lagaris et al., 1998; Dissanayake & Phan-Thien, 1994;
040
      Raissi et al., 2019) have emerged as a promising approach to solve both forward and inverse prob-
041
      lems. Due to the discretization-free nature, PINN is more suitable for solving high-dimensional or
042   parametric PDEs, but current PINN training is still very expensive. For instance, training a PINN
043   for robotic safety analysis (Bansal & Tomlin, 2021) can easily take > 10 hours on a powerful GPU.
044   Despite the development of operator learning (Lu et al., 2021), a PINN often needs to be trained from
045   scratch again to obtain high-quality solution once the PDE initial conditions, boundary conditions,
046   or measurement data changes. There has been increasing interest in training PINN on edge devices
047   and in a real-time manner, including but not limited to PDE-based safety verification (Bansal &
048   Tomlin, 2021), control (Onken et al., 2021) of autonomous systems, fast and private EPT (Yu et al.,
049   2023). The design of real-time edge training accelerator for PINNs remains a sparse research field.
050   Photonic computing provides a promising low-energy and high-speed solution for various AI tasks
051   due to the ultra-high operation speed of light. Many optical neural network (ONN) inference acceler-
052   ators have been proposed (Shen et al., 2017; Tait et al., 2016; Zhu et al., 2022). However, designing
053   a photonic training accelerator for real-size PINNs (e.g., a network with hundreds of neurons per
      layer) remains an open quesiton due to two fundamental challenges:

054
      • Large device footprints and low integration density. Photonic multiply-accumulate (MAC)
055     units such as Mach-Zehnder interferometers (MZIs) are much larger (∼10s of microns) than
056     CMOS transistors. A real-size PINN with > 105 parameters can easily exceed the available chip
057     size with the square scaling rule where an N × N weight matrix requires O(N 2 ) MZIs (Reck
058     et al., 1994; Clements et al., 2016). Actually even the state-of-the-art photonic AI inference accel-
059     erator (Ramey, 2020) can only handle 64 × 64 weight matrices. Training a PINN on an photonic
060     chip will face more significant scalability issue.
061   • Difficulty of on-chip back propagation (BP). It is hard to realize BP on photonic chips due to the
062     lack of memory to store the computational graphs and intermediate results. Several BP-free and
063     in-situ BP methods (Gu et al., 2020; 2021a; Filipovich et al., 2022; Buckley & McCaughan, 2022;
064     Oguz et al., 2023; Hughes et al., 2018; Pai et al., 2023) are proposed, but their scalability remains a
065     major bottleneck. This becomes more severe in PINN, since its loss function also includes (high-
066     order) derivative terms. Subspace learning (Gu et al., 2021b) may scale up BP-based training,
067
        but still needs to save intermediate states. Due to lack of photonic memory, additional optical-
        electronic-optical conversion is needed, leading to dramatic energy and latency overhead.
068
069   BP-free training methods, especially stochastic zeroth-order (ZO) optimization (Nesterov &
070   Spokoiny, 2017; Liu et al., 2020) or forward-forward method (Hinton, 2022), are easier to implement
071   on edge hardware, since they do not need to detect or save any intermediate states (Gu et al., 2020;
072   2021a; Momeni et al., 2023; Oguz et al., 2023). However, the scalability issue remains in end-to-
073   end training, as these methods typically have a dimension-dependent gradient estimation error, thus
074   suffer from slow or even no convergence on realistic-size PINNs with hundreds of neurons per layer.
075   ZO training shows great success in fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) (Malladi et al., 2023;
076   Yang et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024; Gautam et al., 2024), since the gradient of a well pre-trained
077
      LLM has a low intrinsic dimension on fine-tuning tasks. Unfortunately, such a low-dimensional
      structure does not exist in end-to-end training, preventing the convergence of ZO optimization in
078
      training realistic PINNs. Gu et al. (2020; 2021a) utilized ZO training on a photonic chip, but it only
079
      fine-tuned a small portion of model parameters based on an offline pre-trained model.
080
081   Different from the recent work of fine-tuning (Gu et al., 2020; 2021a; Malladi et al., 2023; Yang
082   et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024; Gautam et al., 2024), we investigate end-to-end BP-free training
083
      of real-size PINNs on photonic chips from scratch. This is a more challenging task because of (1)
      the differential operators in the PINN loss evaluation, and (2) the large number of optimization vari-
084
      ables that cause divergence in end-to-end ZO training, (3) the scalability issue and lack of photonic
085
      memory on current photonic chips. This paper presents, for the first time, a real-size and real-time
086   photonic PINN training accelerator, which can train a PINN with hundreds of neurons per layer on
087   an integrated photonic platform. Our novel contributions are summarized as follows:
088
089   • Two-Level BP-free PINN Training. We present novel BP-free approaches in two implementation
090     levels of PINN training. Firstly, we propose a sparse-grid Stein estimator to calculate the (high-
091     order) derivative terms in PINN loss evaluations. Secondly, we propose a tensor-compressed
092
        variance reduction approach to improve the convergence of ZO-SGD for PINN model parameter
        updates. These innovations can completely by-pass the need of photonic memory, and greatly
093
        improve the convergence of on-chip BP-free training.
094
095   • A Scalable Photonic Design. We present a highly scalable and easy-to-implement photonic ac-
096
        celerator design. Our design reuses a tensorized ONN inference accelerator, and just add a digital
        control system to implement on-chip BP-free training. We present two designs: one implements
097
        the whole model on a single chip, and another uses a single photonic tensor core with time multi-
098
        plexing. Our design can scale up to train real-size PINNs with hundreds of neurons per layer.
099
100
      • Numerical Experiments and Hardware Emulation. We validate our method in solving a low-
        dimensional Black-Scholes equation and a high-dimensional Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB)
101
        equation. Our two-level BP-free PINN training achieves a competitive error compared to standard
102
        PINN training with BP, and achieves the lowest error compared with previous photonic on-chip
103     training methods. We further evaluate the performance of our photonic training accelerator on
104     solving Black-Scholes. The simulation results show that our design can reduce the number of
105     MZIs by 42.7×, with only 1.64 seconds to solve this equation.
106
107   To our best knowledge, this is the first real-size optical PINN training framework that can be applied
      to solve realistic PDEs. Our approach shows the great promise of photonic computing in solving

      Optical Neural Networks (ONN) and On-chip ONN Training. Photonic AI accelerators are ex-
163   pected to outperform their electronic counterparts due to the low latency, ultra-high throughput,
164   high energy efficiency, and high parallelism (McMahon, 2023). Many optical inference accelerators
165   have been reported, such as the MZI meshes (Shen et al., 2017; Clements et al., 2016), microring
166   resonator (MRR) weight banks (Tait et al., 2016), MRR crossbar (Ohno et al., 2022), directional
167   coupler crossbar (Feldmann et al., 2021), balanced homodyne detection (Hamerly et al., 2019), and
168   integrated chip diffractive neural network (Zhu et al., 2022). Due to the limited scalability, the state-
169   of-the-art photonic AI accelerator can only handle weight matrices of size 64×64 (Ramey, 2020).
170   As a result, large-scale optical matrices are computed by tiles or blocks with time multiplexing,
171
      demanding intensive memory access to store the intermediate data. That means E/O and O/E con-
      versions and DAC/ADCs are involved during memory access. Demirkiran et al. (2023) shows that
172
      only ∼10% of the overall power is consumed in the optical devices. On-chip training is essential
173
      to mitigate the significant performance degradation of applying a pre-trained model on non-ideal
174   phototonic chips. Existing on-chip training algorithms include brute-force phase tuning (Shen et al.,
175   2017), neuroevolution (Zhang et al., 2019), and an adjoint variable method which requires optical
176   power monitoring inside each device. The primary issue with the above methods is that there is no
177   access to intermediate states or full gradients on the photonic chip. Several BP-free methods are
178   proposed to circumvent the “hardware-unfriendly” nature of error feedback in BP (Gu et al., 2020;
179   2021a; Filipovich et al., 2022; Buckley & McCaughan, 2022; Oguz et al., 2023). However, these
180   methods are limited by the small number of training parameters they can handle.

183   Current PINN training methodologies heavily rely on BP for both loss evaluations (Eq. (3)) and
184   gradient-descent model parameter updates (Eq. (5)). These BP computations are hard to implement
185   on photonic chips. This section proposes a two-level BP-free PINN training framework to avoid
186   such a challenge. We first propose a sparse-grid Stein estimator for BP-free loss evaluation. Then we
187   propose a tensor-compressed ZO optimization for gradient-descent PINN model parameter update.
188   This approach improves the convergence of the training framework as well as the scalability on
189
      photonic chips, enabling end-to-end training of real-size PINN with hundreds of neurons per layer.

307   factors {Gk }Lk=1 . Take a weight matrix with size 512 × 512 for example, the original dimension
308   d = 2.62×105 , while the reduced number of variables in TT factors is d′ = 256 (fold 512×512 into
309
      8×4×4×4×4×4×4×8, and set TT-rank as (1,2,2,2,1)). This reduces the problem dimensionality
      d by 1023×, leading to dramatic reduction of the variance in the RGE ZO gradient estimation in Eq.
310
      (6). In Table 2, we show that such dimension reduction does little harm to the model learning
311
      capacity, but greatly improves the ZO training convergence. In addition, the original matrix-vector
312   product is replaced with low-cost tensor-network contraction in the forward evaluations (Yang et al.,
313   2024b). This offers both memory and computing cost reduction in the ZO training process.

528      6                      C ONCLUSION
529
530     This paper has proposed a two-level BP-free training approach to train real-size physics-informed
531
        neural networks (PINNs) on optical computing hardware. Specifically, our method integrates a
        sparse-grid Stein derivative estimator to avoid BP in loss evaluation and a tensor-compressed ZO
532
        optimization to avoid BP in model parameter update. The tensor compressed ZO optimization can
533
        simultaneously reduce the ZO gradient variance and model parameters, thus scale up ZO training to
534     real-size PINNs with hundreds of neurons per layer. We have further designed the BP-free training
535     on an integrated photonic platform. Our approach has successfully solved a 1-dim Black-Scholes
536     PDE and a 20-dim HJB PDE with smallest relative error compared with existing photonic on-chip
537     training protocols. Future studies of variance reduction can help narrow the performance gap be-
538     tween ZO training and FO training. Our tensor-compressed BP-free training method is not restricted
539     to PINNs. It can be easily extended to solve image and speech problems on photonic and other types
        of edge platforms where the hardware cost to implement BP is not feasible.
