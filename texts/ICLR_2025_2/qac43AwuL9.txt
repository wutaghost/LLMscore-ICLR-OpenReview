009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            To effectively study complex causal systems, it is often useful to construct repre-
012            sentations that simplify parts of the system by discarding irrelevant details while
013            preserving key features. The Information Bottleneck (IB) method is a widely used
014            approach in representation learning that compresses random variables while re-
015            taining information about a target variable. Traditional methods like IB are purely
016            statistical and ignore underlying causal structures, making them ill-suited for
017            causal tasks. We propose the Causal Information Bottleneck (CIB), a causal ex-
018
               tension of the IB, which compresses a set of chosen variables while maintaining
               causal control over a target variable. This method produces representations which
019
               are causally interpretable, and which can be used when reasoning about interven-
020
               tions. We present experimental results demonstrating that the learned representa-
021            tions accurately capture causality as intended.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Natural systems typically consist of a vast number of components and interactions, making them
027   complex and challenging to study. When investigating a specific scientific question, which is often of
028   a causal nature, it is frequently possible to disregard many of these details, as they have a negligible
029   impact on the outcome. These details can then be abstracted away. A classic example (Rubenstein
030   et al., 2017; Chalupka et al., 2017) is the relationship between particle velocities, temperature, and
031
      pressure. To control the pressure on the walls of a room, it would be necessary to influence the
      velocities of the approximately 1023 particles in the room. However, controlling the velocity of each
032
      individual particle is not required to achieve this. Instead, manipulating the high-level variable of
033
      temperature is sufficient. For an ideal gas, temperature is directly proportional to the average kinetic
034   energy of the particles in the gas (Blundell & Blundell, 2010), and modifying the temperature alters
035   the particle velocities accordingly.
036
037
      Methods that disregard the causal structure of a system when constructing abstractions may yield
      results that are uninformative or even misleading, particularly when the objective is to manipulate
038
      the system or gain causal insights. The following running example will serve as a useful illustra-
039
      tion of the potential drawbacks of neglecting causal considerations when learning representations.
040   Consider a mouse gene with four positions s1 , s2 , s3 , s4 under study1 where nucleotides may be
041   mutated, corresponding to the binary variables Xi , i = 1, . . . , 4, which indicate whether there is a
042   mutation at position si . These mutations can interact in a complex manner with respect to a pheno-
043   type of interest Y , say the body mass of the mouse. This type of complex interaction is known as
044   epistasis (Phillips, 2008). One could create a abstraction T of X1 , X2 , X3 , X4 that would be blind
045   to differences between mutation configurations (X1 , X2 , X3 , X4 ) that do not provide information
046   about Y . With the current description, it may seem that there is no need for considering causality,
047   and that one could simply use a purely statistical method to learn a good “epistasis gauger” T . This
048
      is, however, not the case, since the Xi and T are typically confounded by the population structure,
      that is, “any form of relatedness in the sample, including ancestry differences or cryptic relatedness”
049
      (Sul et al., 2018). Based on the discussion in Sul et al. (2018), we consider the population structure
050
      variable S encoding the strain of the mice (laboratory vs wild-derived strains), which is associated
051   with both distinct X and body masses Y (high in laboratory strains and low in wild-derived strains).
052

059
      The construction of abstractions, in the form of representations, has been a prominent area of re-
060
      search in non-causal machine learning for many years, with both theoretical and applied contribu-
061   tions. A “good” representation should facilitate the learning task (Goodfellow et al., 2016), achiev-
062   ing multiple benefits: it should conserve space, simplify computations, and accelerate processing,
063   while also uncovering the essential aspects of X, which may help with interpretability. It may also
064   make the learning task easier because highly flexible machine learning algorithms often struggle
065   with overfitting, which is exacerbated by the presence of unimportant information (Bishop, 2006).
066   It is essential to recognize that the determination of which information is spurious critically depends
067   on the specific task at hand. Building a representation is therefore a balancing act between keep-
068   ing enough information for the task and avoiding including unnecessary details. This is the insight
069
      which Tishby et al. (2000) build on. The authors formalize the learning of the optimal representation
      T for an input variable X and a chosen target variable Y as a minimization problem. This problem
070
      involves finding a balance between compressing X as much as possible and maintaining as much
071
      information about Y as possible. The functional proposed by Tishby et al. (2000) to be minimized is
072   called the Information Bottleneck (IB) Lagrangian, and it has inspired a significant body of work in
073   representation learning (see for example Alemi et al. (2016); Kolchinsky et al. (2018; 2019); Tishby
074   & Zaslavsky (2015); Achille & Soatto (2018)).
075
      The IB method is not aware of causality. The learned representations cannot be used to reason
076
      causally about the system. In particular, when X and Y are heavily confounded, the IB method
077
      will create a representation T that preserves the information X has about Y , but a significant por-
078   tion of this information is spurious from a causal perspective. This not only leads to sub-optimal
079   compression but can also result in misleading conclusions, where the values of T are mistakenly in-
080   terpreted as corresponding to meaningful interventions. The importance of constructing appropriate
081   causality-aware representations and abstractions has been recognized previously (Schölkopf et al.,
082   2021; Beckers & Halpern, 2019; Rubenstein et al., 2017; Chalupka et al., 2017; Höltgen, 2021; Jam-
083   malamadaka et al., 2023), but the successful construction of such representations remains an open
084   challenge (see Section 9).
085   In this paper, we present a new method for finding representations of a set of input variables X which
086   retain the causal information between X and a specified target variable Y . Namely, we introduce a
087   causal version of the IB Lagrangian designed to attain its minima when the representation T of X
088   compresses X as much as possible while maintaining as much causal control over Y as desired, with
089   the trade-off between these properties governed by a parameter β. We derive the Causal Information
090   Bottleneck (CIB) Lagrangian by first establishing an axiomatic description of what constitutes an
091   optimal causal representation. Similar to the original Information Bottleneck paper (Tishby et al.,
092
      2000), we take all variables to be discrete, and we assume that we have access to the joint distribution
      over all variables. Additionally, the causal graph containing X and Y is taken to be known.
093
094   The contributions of this paper can be stated as follows:
095
096          • We propose an axiomatic definition of optimal causal representation, which naturally ex-
097
               tends previous definitions of optimal representation.
098          • We derive a causal version of the Information Bottleneck Lagrangian from those axioms,
099            thereby formulating the problem of optimal causal representation learning as a minimiza-
100            tion problem. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first time that representations of
101            variables are designed to be interventionally relevant for the target variable.
102          • We propose a definition of equivalence for representations and an information-theoretical
103            metric to capture it in the deterministic case.
104          • We derive a backdoor criterion formula for representations, enabling us to compute a rep-
105            resentation’s post-intervention distribution from observational data.

      2.1   C AUSAL M ODELS AND I NTERVENTIONS
123
124   A Structural Causal Model (SCM) provides a representation of a system’s causal structure, analo-
125   gous to a Bayesian network but with a causal interpretation. An SCM C = (V, N, S, pN ) is com-
126   prised of endogenous variables V, exogenous variables N, deterministic functions between them S,
127   and a distribution over the noise variables pN . Each SCM C has an underlying DAG GC , called its
128   causal graph. Each node in the DAG corresponds to an endogenous variable, while the edges stand
129   for the causal relationships between them. We denote the parents and children of an endogenous
130   variable X by Pa(X) and Ch(X), respectively. Furthermore, we denote the range of a random vari-
131
      able X by RX and its support by supp(X). The value of each endogenous variable is determined
      by a deterministic function of its parent variables and an independent exogenous variable, which ac-
132
      counts for the system’s randomness. A key feature of SCMs is their capacity to model interventions
133
      on a variable X, which involve altering the variable’s generating process. This results in a new SCM
134   with its own distribution, reflecting the system’s state post-intervention. The most common type of
135   intervention is an atomic intervention, where a variable X is set to a specific value x, effectively
136   severing its connection to its parents and assigning a fixed value instead. We denote such an inter-
137   vention by do(X = x), the resulting SCM by Cdo(X=x) , and the post-intervention joint distribution
138                                do(X=x)
      of a set of variables W by pW         (w) or p(w | do(X = x)). For more details, see Appendix A.3.

      2.2   C AUSAL E NTROPY AND C AUSAL I NFORMATION G AIN
142
      We now introduce two concepts: causal entropy and causal information gain, both of which are
143
      fundamental to our method. For more details, see Appendix A.2, or refer to the work by Simoes
144   et al. (2023). The causal entropy Hc (Y | do(X)) measures the average uncertainty remaining about
145   the variable Y after we intervene on the variable X. This concept is closely related to conditional
146   entropy but adapted for situations where interventions on X, as opposed to conditioning on X, are
147   considered. The causal information gain Ic (Y | do(X)) extends the idea of mutual information to
148   the causal domain. It quantifies the reduction in uncertainty about Y after intervening on X, offering
149   a measure of the causal control that X exerts over Y . Essentially, it tells us how much more we know
150   about Y due to these interventions on X.
151
152   2.3   T HE I NFORMATION B OTTLENECK L AGRANGIAN
153
154   Let X be a random variable. By a representation T of X, we mean a variable that can only depend on
155   X, whether deterministically or stochastically. This means in particular that T must be independent
156   of Y when conditioning on X. This generalizes the notion of representation used by Achille &
157   Soatto (2018) for cases with more variables than only X and Y . Furthermore, a representation T is
158
      characterized by its encoder, which codifies how T depends on X. We formalize this as follows:
159   Definition 1 (Representation). A random variable T is a representation of a random variable X if
160   T is a function of X and an independent noise variable. The encoder of the representation T is the
161   function qT |X : RT × RX → [0, 1] such that qT |X (t | x) is the conditional probability q(t | x) of
      T = t given X = x.

      In Tishby et al. (2000), the authors aim at finding a representation T of X which “keeps a fixed
163   amount of meaningful information about the relevant signal Y while minimizing the number of bits
164   from the original signal X (maximizing the compression).” This is accomplished by introducing the
165   Information Bottleneck Lagrangian LβIB [qT |X ] = I(X; T ) − βI(Y ; T ), where β is a non-negative
166   parameter which manages the trade-off between compression (as measured by I(X; T )) and suffi-
167   ciency (as measured by I(Y ; T )). The problem of finding such a representation becomes then the
168   problem of finding an encoder qT |X which minimizes the LβIB . They minimize this Lagrangian by
169   adapting the Blahut-Arimoto algorithm from rate distortion theory (Blahut, 1972) to their case, re-
170   sulting in a coordinate-descent optimization algorithm. Crucially, this adaptation relies on the fact
171   that, similarly to Blahut (1972), searching for the zero-gradient points of the Lagrangian results in
172   self-consistency equations that can be exploited to construct a coordinate-descent algorithm. This
173   construction hinges on viewing mutual information as a KL divergence.
174   The IB method proposed by Tishby et al. (2000) effectively solves the following minimization prob-

185   For the remainder of the paper, let X ⊆ V be a set of endogenous variables of an SCM C =
186   (V, N, S, pN ), T be a representation of X with encoder qT |X , and t be an element of RT .
187   In a natural extension of the qualitative description of optimal representation in Section 2.3 to the
188   causal context, our problem can be described as finding representations T of X which retain a chosen
189   amount D of causal information about the relevant signal Y while minimizing the information that
190   T preserves about X. We propose an axiomatic characterization of optimal causal representation to
191   formally capture this description using information-theoretical quantities. This can also be seen as a
192   causal variant of the characterization of optimal representation from Achille & Soatto (2018). Since
193   we use Ic (Y | do(T )) to measure the causal information that T has about Y , and I(X; T ) is the
194   information that T keeps about X, the result is the following:
195   Definition 2 (Optimal Causal Representation). A optimal causal representation of X at sufficiency
196   D is a representation T of X such that:
197       (C1) T is interventionally D-sufficient for the task Y , i.e., Ic (Y | do(T )) = D.
198
199       (C2) I(X; T ) is minimal among the variables T satisfying (C1).

      7     C OMPARING R EPRESENTATIONS
299

      After learning a representation T1 , we may want to compare it with another representation T2 , which
301
      could either be one learned earlier or one considered as the ground truth. Simple equality of their en-
302   coders is not an appropriate criterion for this comparison. Two representations might have different
303   encoders and still be “equivalent” in the sense that they coincide when the values of the represen-
304   tations are relabeled. This is especially apparent in the case of completely deterministic representa-
305   tions. For example, consider two binary representations T1 and T2 of a low-level variable X with
306   range RX = {0, 1, 2}, defined by deterministic functions ϕT1 and ϕT2 . Suppose ϕT1 maps 0 and 1 to
307   0, and 2 to 1, while ϕT2 maps 0 and 1 to 1, and 2 to 0. Intuitively, T1 and T2 represent the same rep-
308   resentation because relabeling the values of T1 (i.e., swapping 0 and 1) yields a representation that
309   produces the same values as T2 for the same low-level values of X. Formally, and extending to the
310
      non-deterministic case, this is to say that equivalence arises whenever the conditional distributions
      are identical up to a bijection σ of the values of the Ti . This leads to the following definition:
311
312   Definition 9 (Equivalent representations). Two representations T1 and T2 of X are equivalent if
313   there is a bijection σ : supp(T1 ) → supp(T2 ) such that ∀t1 ∈ supp(T1 ), x ∈ supp(X), qT1 |X (t1 |
314   x) = qT2 |X (σ(t1 ) | x), where qT1 |X , qT2 |X are the encoders for T1 and T2 . We then write T1 ∼
                                                                                                        = T2 .

      VI(T, T ) = 0 exactly when T and T are equivalent (see Proposition 20).

      In our experiments, we aim to minimize a reparameterized version of the CIB, given by
327   LγCIB [qT |X ] := (1 − γ)I(X; T ) − γIc (Y | do(T )), where γ ∈ [0, 1] is the trade-off parameter.
328   By a slight abuse of notation, we distinguish this from the original parameterization of the CIB
329   solely by the superscript γ. The parameter γ has a more intuitive interpretation than β, representing
330   the fraction of the CIB that the interventional sufficiency term accounts for. Additionally, it simpli-
331   fies hyperparameter searches when using optimization algorithms, since the magnitude of the values
332   of LγCIB remains relatively stable with variations in γ, unlike what happens with LβCIB and β. It is
333   straightforward to verify that minimizing the CIB LβCIB is equivalent to minimizing its reparameter-
334   ization LγCIB , provided that γ is selected appropriately (see Proposition 18).

                is, if γ1 > γ2 , one has that the causal information gain for the encoder learned when γ = γ1
                is larger or equal to that of the encoder learned when γ = γ2 .
348
349   If (a), (b), and (c) hold, this provides evidence that the CIB can be used to learn representations
350   that maximize control (by setting γ = 1), maximize compression (by setting γ = 0), or strike a
351   balance between the two (by setting γ ∈ (0, 1)). This is also evidence that the proposed local search
352   algorithms succeed in optimizing the CIB objective. Note that ground truth is only available for
353   γ = 1 and γ = 0. For γ = 1, the optimal solution will be clear for the proposed case studies.
354   For γ = 0, the solution should be maximally compressive, regardless of causal control over Y .
355   For γ ∈ / {0, 1}, there is no obvious ground truth, but the reasonableness of the results can still be
356   assessed as described in (c). It is also noteworthy that encoders trained with different γ values may
357
      achieve the same Ic (Y | do(T )), although increasing γ should not decrease Ic (Y | do(T )). This
      property is analogous to the Information Bottleneck (IB) framework, where distinct β values often
358
      yield the same sufficiency value (Kolchinsky et al., 2018).

      There has been other work investigating a problem related to our search for an optimal causal repre-
467
      sentation. In Chalupka et al. (2017; 2014; 2016b;a)), the authors search for “causal macrovariables”
468   of higher-dimensional “microvariables”, described as coarse representations of the microvariables
469   X and Y which preserve the causal relation between them3 . Their definition of macrovariable is
470   distinct from our definition of optimal causal representations. Namely, it is not based on information-
471   theoretical concepts such as compression and sufficiency, but on clustering together the values of X
472   resulting in the same post-intervention distributions over Y . It would be interesting to see in what
473   cases our approach coincides with theirs. Note that the two approaches cannot align in general,
474   since their method does not incorporate causality, as their clustering procedure relies on conditional
475   distributions, which, as they demonstrate, necessarily yield a finer-grained representation than truly
476
      causal clusters. Moreover, our abstractions T can, at least for γ ̸= 1, be stochastic mappings of X,
      while their macrovariables are deterministic mappings of X. Höltgen (2021); Jammalamadaka et al.
477
      (2023) tried to address the problem of finding causal macrovariables using the standard informa-
478
      tion bottleneck framework. Specifically, they use variational autoencoders (VAEs), relying on the
479   fact that β-VAEs (when used for the task of reconstruction) minimize the information bottleneck
480   (Achille & Soatto, 2018). However, their method does not account for causality, being limited to
481   scenarios without confounding due to its reliance on the standard IB.
482
      The study of causal abstractions has been conducted from yet another, although related, point of
483
      view. Namely, there have been efforts to formalize when exactly a given causal model can be seen as
484
         3
485        In general, they search for representations not only of X but also of Y . We are only interested in how they
      construct representations of X.

486
      a causally consistent abstraction of another one (Rubenstein et al., 2017; Beckers & Halpern, 2019).
487   The core idea is that intervening on a variable in the low-level model and then transitioning to the
488   high-level model should yield the same distribution over the model variables as first transitioning to
489   the high-level model and then intervening. To account for cases where this commutativity between
490   abstracting and intervening is imperfect, Beckers et al. (2020) introduced approximate abstractions.
491   Although these works do not provide methods for constructing causal model abstractions, recent
492   efforts have focused on learning such high-level models. Notably, Xia & Bareinboim (2024) present
493   a method for constructing high-level models based on clustering low-level variables, while Zennaro
494   et al. (2023) and Felekis et al. (2024) focus on learning an abstraction map given both low-level and
495
      high-level SCMs, each by minimizing a distinct measure of abstraction error. In contrast, our method
      does not learn a high-level SCM, but a representation of X which is causally relevant. It seems to
496
      us that a possibly fruitful avenue of research consists of studying whether representation learning
497
      method introduced in this paper can be used to construct high-level causal models. In fact, the con-
498   nection between causal representation learning and the task of learning abstractions of causal models
499   has been noted before (Zennaro et al., 2023). Furthermore, the macrovariables from Chalupka et al.
500   (2017) were already shown to induce high-level causal models that satisfy the definition of approxi-
501   mate abstraction (Beckers et al., 2020), suggesting that the causal representations learned using the
502   CIB method may play a similar role.

      10    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
508
509
510   We extended the notion of optimal representation to the causal setting, resulting in an axiomatic
511   characterization of optimal causal representations. Just as the information bottleneck (IB) method
512   can be used to learn optimal representations, so can the causal information bottleneck (CIB) method
513   introduced in this paper be used to learn optimal causal representations. The CIB, which depends on
514
      the interventional distributions p(y | do(t)), needs to be computed during the learning procedure,
      which consists of solving a constrained minimization problem. The exact expression for the CIB will
515
      depend on the causal structure of the system under study. We focused on cases where there is a set Z
516
      satisfying the backdoor criterion relative to (X, Y ). This allows us to derive a backdoor adjustment
517   formula for p(y | do(t)), and thus successfully apply a minimization algorithm to minimize the CIB.
518   Specifically, we introduced a local search algorithm, referred to as projected simulated annealing
519   gradient descent (pSAGD), which integrates simulated annealing and gradient descent techniques
520   with a projection operator to maintain constraint satisfaction throughout the minimization process.
521   In order to compare different representations learned by our algorithm, we introduced a novel notion
522   of equivalence of representations, which partitions representations into equivalence classes, termed
523   abstractions, and showed that the variation of information can be used to assess whether two repre-
524   sentations are equivalent, as long as one of them is deterministic. We experimentally validated that
525
      the learned representations in three toy models of increasing complexity align with our expectations.
526   Future research directions include exploring alternative methods for incorporating causality into the
527   information bottleneck framework, such as focusing on causal properties other than causal control,
528   like proportionality (Pocheville et al., 2015). Our approach can also be extended to scenarios where
529   the backdoor criterion does not hold by leveraging do-calculus, allowing for the automatic com-
530   putation of post-intervention distributions for interventions on representations. Another area worth
531
      investigating relates to fine-tuning the trade-off between compression and interventional sufficiency.
      Kolchinsky et al. (2018) highlight that, in the context of the Information Bottleneck (IB), different
532
      values of β can often result in the same sufficiency. Similarly, in our experiments we observed that
533
      different values of γ (and thus β) often produced the same interventional sufficiency values. Fu-
534   ture work could explore strategies similar to those used by Kolchinsky et al. (2018) to address this.
535   Additionally, another natural next step would be to adapt the causal information bottleneck (CIB)
536   method to continuous variables, for example by using variational autoencoders (Kingma & Welling,
537   2013) to minimize the CIB Lagrangian, as was previously done for the standard IB (Alemi et al.,
538   2016). Finally, one can explore the relationship between our representation learning method and the
539   framework of causal abstractions, similar to how Beckers et al. (2020) connect the latter with the
      approach from Chalupka et al. (2017).
