009                                                 Abstract
010
011              Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) has become the leading
012              approach for fine-tuning large language models (LLM). However, RLHF has lim-
013              itations in multi-task learning (MTL) due to challenges of reward hacking and
014              extreme multi-objective optimization (i.e., trade-off of multiple and/or sometimes
015              conflicting objectives). Applying RLHF for MTL currently requires careful tun-
016              ing of the weights for reward model and data combinations. This is often done
017              via human intuition and does not generalize. In this work, we introduce a novel
018
                 post-training paradigm which we called Constrained Generative Policy Optimiza-
                 tion (CGPO). The core of CGPO is Mixture of Judges (MoJ) with cost-efficient
019
                 constrained policy optimizers, which can identify the perfect blend in RLHF in a
020
                 principled manner. It shows strong empirical results, does not require extensive
021              hyper-parameter tuning, and is plug-and-play in common post-training pipelines.
022              Together, this can detect and mitigate reward hacking behaviors while reaching a
023              pareto-optimal point across an extremely large number of objectives.
024              Our results show that CGPO consistently outperforms other commonly used SoTA
025              RLHF algorithms (such as PPO and DPO) on a wide range of tasks – general chat,
026              STEM questions, instruction following, math, coding and knowledge. In partic-
027              ular, CGPO improves over PPO by 7.4% in AlpacaEval-2 (general chat), 12.5%
028              in Arena-Hard (STEM & reasoning), 2% in IFEval (Instruction Following), 2%
029              in both MATH and GSM8K (Math & reasoning), 5% in HumanEval (Coding),
030              and 2% in the ARC challenge (Knowledge). We also observe that PPO is sus-
031
                 ceptible to severe reward hacking behaviors (it exhibits severe regression in pop-
                 ular coding benchmarks) which can be addressed by CGPO. CGPO represents a
032
                 breakthrough in RLHF, simultaneously addressing reward-hacking and extreme
033
                 multi-objective optimization, and thereby advancing the state-of-the-art in align-
034              ing general-purpose LLMs.

037   1     Introduction
038
039   The emergence of general-purpose Large Language Models (LLMs) has significantly transformed
040   the landscape of natural language processing, demonstrating exceptional capabilities across various
041   expert-level domains (Achiam et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023; Anthropic,
042   2023; Team et al., 2023; Meta, 2024; Tunstall et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023). These models are char-
043   acterized by their extensive parameterization, enabling them to handle a wide array of tasks using a
044   unified parameter set (Zhao et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2019b;a). Central to this versatility is multi-task
045
      learning (MTL) (Caruana, 1997; Crawshaw, 2020), a strategy that involves training a single model
      on multiple tasks simultaneously. This approach fosters the development of shared representations,
046
      which enhances the model’s ability to generalize better than those trained on isolated tasks. Although
047
      prior studies on MTL have concentrated on the integration and processing of multi-task data during
048   both pre-training and fine-tuning stages (Raffel et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023; Aghajanyan et al., 2021;
049   Aribandi et al., 2021), the application of the primary LLM alignment method, Reinforcement Learn-
050   ing with Human Preference (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022; Ziegler et al., 2019; Zheng et al., 2023b),
051   has not been thoroughly explored within the MTL context. In previous studies, the implementation
052   of RLHF for multi-task post-training has typically involved a linear combination of multiple reward
053   models within the standard RLHF framework (Ramamurthy et al., 2022; Glaese et al., 2022; Yuan
      et al., 2023; Bakker et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2020). Each reward model is crafted

054
      using preference data to mirror the distinct alignment preferences of different tasks. Researchers
055   often experiment with various reward weightings to identify a Pareto front that depicts the optimal
056   performance of the LLM across diverse tasks (Rame et al., 2024). However, this approach is limited
057   by two significant challenges:
058
      Vulnerability to Reward Hacking: The optimization of a preference-based reward model is sus-
059
      ceptible to reward hacking, as the reward model is an imperfect proxy of human preferences (Gao
060
      et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2023; Skalse et al., 2022). Studies indicate that excessive optimization of a
061   reward model can lead to misalignment with actual human preferences (Gao et al., 2023; Moskovitz
062   et al., 2023; Stiennon et al., 2020; Rafailov et al., 2024a). This issue becomes more pronounced in
063   a multi-task setting, where each reward model may have its own unique flaws. Implementing a uni-
064   form early stopping point in the RLHF optimization process to minimize reward hacking effects is
065   impractical and can lead to degraded performance across tasks (Moskovitz et al., 2023). This high-
066   lights the need for a more tailored approach to compensate for the weaknesses of each reward model
067   and to manage the optimization of reward models for each task in complex, multi-task environments.
068   Contradictory Goals: Different tasks often have conflicting objectives (Rame et al., 2024). Even
069   if the prompt spaces for these tasks do not overlap, using a linear combination of reward models
070   can lead to compromises in goal metrics. For example, the typical strategy of LLM post-training in-
071   volves maximizing the helpfulness reward for safe prompts and maximizing the harmfulness reward
072   for unsafe prompts (Bai et al., 2022). Although achieving global optimality for both tasks is pos-
073   sible if the LLM’s capacity is sufficiently large (Iyer et al., 2022), employing a linear combination
074   of helpfulness and harmfulness rewards inevitably results in reduced gains for both metrics. This
075
      occurs because each task partially sacrifices its own RLHF optimization progress to accommodate a
      contradictory metric, thereby diminishing the effectiveness of both.
076
077   To address these challenges, we developed an innovative framework called Constrained Generative
078   Policy Optimization (CGPO). In response to the issue of reward hacking in RLHF, we introduce two
079   types of judges: rule-based and LLM-based. These judges collaborate to identify any reward hacking
080   patterns during the LLM’s online generation phase. Based on their evaluations, we implement a
081
      constrained RLHF method to update the LLM model. This method is designed to maximize the
      likelihood of generating outputs that adhere to all constraints and achieve high reward values, while
082
      minimizing outputs that breach constraints and have low reward values. To support the constrained
083
      policy optimization update in the large-scale LLM setting, which is complicated even in traditional
084   small-scale RL scenarios, we have developed three new primary-type constraint RLHF optimizers.
085   These optimizers are designed to operate independently of the dual-variable update, which is often
086   a critical component in conventional primal-dual constrained RL algorithms. This independence
087   simplifies the optimizers and enhances their scalability, making them more effective for managing
088   large-scale LLM post-training. To effectively optimizing objectives of various tasks, which may be

142            improves those benchmarks during training, demonstrating the extraordinary capability of
143            MoJs in preventing reward hacking issues.

179   3     Constraint Generative Policy Optimization
180
181   In this section, we first explore how to implement the CGPO framework for single objective opti-
182   mizaiton in the single task setting using MoJs, as detailed in Section 3.1. Subsequently, we discuss
183   the implementation of CGPO to manage scenarios involving multiple objectives in Section 3.2 for
184
      multi-task learning.
185
186   3.1   CGPO in Single Task with Single Objective
187
      The primary design of CGPO is to integrate multiple constraints to mitigate the issue of reward
188
      hacking, which arises from the limited capabilities of reward models. Specifically, in addition to
189
      optimizing the accumulated reward model value as shown in eq. (2), we also ensure that the model
190   generation meets several constraints. For example, in general chat tasks with prompts that are free of
191   harmful intent. We require model generations to consistently respond to user queries. This is crucial
192   because there are instances where the model may refuse to answer, and the reward model might er-
193   roneously assign high values to such non-responsive generations. In these cases, purely maximizing
194   the reward model could impair the model’s helpfulness and lead to an overly conservative tendency.
195   By introducing these constraints based on our prior knowledge about the weaknesses of each reward
196   model, we can avoid critical reward hacking patterns effectively.
197   We denote the set of constraints that the LLM generations need to satisfy as {C1 , C2 , . . . , C M }
198   and the state-action set that satisfies constraint Ck as Σk , i.e., Σk = {(s, a) ∈ S ×
199   A and (s, a) satisfies requirement of Ck }. We define the feasible region as the state-action set that
200   satisfies all constraints as Σ = Σ1 ∩ Σ2 ∩ . . . ∩ Σ M . In the single task setting, CGPO solves the
201   following constrained problem (Ying et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2021)
202                                                      h        i

      • Calibrated Regularized Policy Gradient: CRPG is a constrained policy gradient method. It
        incorporates a novel calibration strategy that leverages preference-based reward modeling, along
226
        with a new constraint-rectified reward shaping technique. Those two techniques work together
227
        to optimize the reward while ensuring compliance with all constraints. Additionally, CRPG in-
228     troduces a new KL-regularization approach that not only penalizes generations with significant

      • Constraint-Regularized Reward Ranking Fine-tuning: CRRAFT is a reward ranking-based

233
        ensures that the KL divergence of the final policy is strictly bounded.
234
235   • Constrained Online DPO: CODPO adapts the DPO update to the on-policy optimization setting,
236     in which generations that achieve high reward values and satisfy all constraints are promoted,

      Intuitively, with either the Option I or Option II updating strategy, CGPO encourages the policy to

258
      CGPO is a primal-type constraint policy optimization approach, which differs from the standard
259

260   optimizing the dual variable, thus avoiding the drawbacks of extensive hyperparameter tuning issues
261   associated with the primal-dual approach. Due to this reason, CGPO is user-friendly even with
262   multiple different types of constraints, making it well-suited for the LLM post-tuning scenario.

264   3.1.1    Judges in CGPO
265
266   The key step in implementing multi-constraint CGPO optimizers is to determine whether a genera-
267   tion (s, a) satisfies a constraint or not. This determination allows us to split generated samples into
268   positive (Xt+ ) and negative (Xt− ) groups given the label y predicted by each constraint judge Jh , i.e.,
269
                                      Jh (s, a) = y ∈ {0, 1}, where 1 ≤ h ≤ M,

270
      and then apply our customized constraint RLHF optimizers based on that classification. In CGPO,
271   we have developed and integrated the following two types of constraint judge modules to assess
272   whether a generation satisfies a constraint:
273
274   • Rule-based constraint judge module: This module employs a rule-based approach (such as
275     string-matching and code execution) to ascertain whether the generation strictly adheres to pre-
276     defined regulations (Li et al., 2024a). It is particularly effective for constraints related to precise
277     instruction following, where the generation must meet exact requirements such as length, number
278     of paragraphs, and keyword inclusion (Zhou et al., 2023; Hendrycks et al., 2021b; Cobbe et al.,
279
        2021). It can also handle reasoning tasks, such as math problems and code generation.
280   • LLM-based constraint judge module. This module functions as an LLM generator. In most
281     cases, the generation is formatted according to a template before being sent to the judge mod-
282     ule. These modules not only provide access to the constraint satisfaction condition but also offer
283     reasoning behind the judgement construction. Due to this property, they are typically capable of
284     handling more challenging constraint evaluation tasks such as safety violation, reference-based
285
        factuality verification, and false refusal patterns. The model could either be a compact LLM fine-
        tuned with domain-specific data (Inan et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2022) or a powerful, large LLM
286
        without task-specific fine-tuning (Yuan et al., 2024b; Zheng et al., 2024).

      CGPO pipeline functions in the multi-tasks setting. The entire CGPO pipeline has the following
325   two core components: multi-objective reward modeling and multi-experts alignment.
326
327   Multi-Objective Reward Modelling. Unlike the approach adopted in previous RLHF pipelines in
328
      multi-objective scenarios, which applies the same linear combined reward model to all prompts in
      the prompt set D, CGPO first classifies the prompt set D into distinct, non-overlapping categories
329
      based on the nature of the prompts, i.e., D = {D1 , D2 , . . . , DL }. Each prompt set Dl ∈ D is referred to
330
      as a task. For example, prompts with harmful intent, which could potentially lead LLM to generate
331   unsafe responses, are grouped into a class labeled ”harmful intent”. Conversely, prompts without
332   unsafe intent, primarily focused on information gathering and casual conversation, are grouped into
333   a class labeled ”general chat”. This categorization can be performed during the data collection phase
334   or by prompting an LLM to carry out the categorization given the definitions of different classes.
335   Subsequently, with a collection of trained reward models denoted as {rϕ,1 , rϕ,2 , . . . , rϕ,V }, we tailor the
336   specific reward model to be applied for each task Dl . This customization guarantees that each prompt
337   class Dl benefits from the most appropriate guidance provided by the corresponding reward model.
338   Note that the number of reward models, denoted by V, is less than or equal to the number of tasks,
339
      L, meaning a single reward model can be utilized across multiple tasks. The major advantage of
      segregating the reward models for different tasks is to exclude irrelevant or contradictory objectives,
340
      thus enabling each task to focus solely on optimizing its own goal metrics without interference from
341
      other objectives.
342
343   Multi-Expert Alignment. The concept of multi-expert alignment involves applying customized
344   MoJs, reward model and policy optimization setups for each task.
345   After the policy model generates online samples for each task, we employ a mixture of task-specific
346   judges to identify generations that do not meet predefined standards. It is crucial to emphasize that
347   the selection of judges are uniquely tailored for each task, reflecting the particular shortcomings of
348   each reward model and our established performance criteria for LLMs in these tasks. For instance,
349   in the ”general chat” task, we employ LLM-based judges for false refusal and factuality to enhance
350   responsiveness and ensure honesty. In ”reasoning” tasks, we implement a rule-based math/coding
351
      constraint judge to guarantee correctness and accuracy.
352   Based on the status of constraint satisfaction across generations and a customized reward model,
353   we implement an RLHF policy optimizer with a specifically tailored hyperparameter setup to align
354   each task effectively. This method deviates from the conventional RLHF pipeline, which generally
355   employs a uniform optimizer setup for task alignment. For tasks that have precise judges and require
356   extensive exploration to derive the correct response, such as instruction following, math, and coding,
357
      we apply a lenient KL threshold and allow a higher number of generations per prompt. In contrast,
      for tasks where precise judges are lacking and extensive exploration is less critical, such as ”general
358
      chat,” we opt for a stricter KL threshold and a reduced number of generations per prompt.

      4.1    CGPO Training Setup
399
      In this section, we will show how we implment the CGPO in the RLHF finetuning stage.
400
401   RLHF warm-up. Unlike previous studies Ouyang et al. (2022), which directly employ the SFT
402   model as the initial point for RLHF, our approach introduces a ”warm-up” phase. This phase begins
403
      with a model that has undergone preliminary fine-tuning through a few steps of DPO, starting from
      the SFT model. The rationale behind this strategy is that initiating online RLHF directly from the
404
      SFT model and performing policy optimization with the RM may not be able to explicitly exploit
405
      the high-quality preference data. By initiating RLHF with a model already refined by DPO to a
406   certain degree, we can fully harness the advantages of the preference dataset, thereby providing a
407   better starting point for RLHF. In our experiment, we utilize all preference data from RM training to
408   facilitate the training for warm-up DPO. The benefit of RLHF warm-up is discussed in Appendix E.
409
      RLHF Training recipe: We begin the RLHF finetuning process using the warm-up model. Table 1
410
      shows the customized treatment (RM+MoJs) we applied for each task. The prompt set of each tasks
411
      in CGPO training is provided in Appendices C.5 and C.6.

      5     Conclusion
527
528
      In this paper, we introduced the CGPO framework to address key challenges in multi-task learning
      for LLM post-training with RLHF. The CGPO framework effectively mitigates issues such as inho-
529
      mogeneous reward hacking and conflicting task goals through a novel primal-type multi-constraint
530
      RL method and a tailored multi-objective optimization strategy. We demonstrate the effectiveness
531   of CGPO in a scenario where we need to handle five tasks with three reward models and six con-
532   straints, marking the first application of RLHF in multi-task learning for general-purpose LLMs.
533   Our experiments show that CGPO achieves significantly better metric gains for all tasks compared
534   to the baseline RLHF methods. Moving forward, it is promising to explore more automated ways to
535   adapt the gradient weights from different tasks to further reduce the hyperparameter tuning burden
536   and advance the Pareto frontier (Sener & Koltun, 2018).
