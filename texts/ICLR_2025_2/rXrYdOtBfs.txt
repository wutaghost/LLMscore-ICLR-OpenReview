                                                       A BSTRACT
008
009
                   While Transformers underpin modern large language models (LMs), a growing
010
                   list of alternative architectures with new capabilities, promises, and tradeoffs is
011
                   emerging. This makes choosing the right LM architecture challenging. Recently
012                proposed hybrid architectures seek a best-of-all-worlds approach that reaps the
013                benefits of all architectures. Hybrid design is difficult for two reasons: it requires
014                manual expert-driven search, and new hybrids must be trained from scratch. We
015                propose Manticore,1 a framework that addresses these challenges by automat-
016                ing the design of hybrid architectures while reusing pretrained models to create
017                pretrained hybrids. Our approach augments ideas from differentiable Neural Ar-
018                chitecture Search (NAS) by incorporating simple projectors that translate features
019                between pretrained blocks from different architectures. We then fine-tune hybrids
020
                   that combine pretrained models from different architecture families—such as the
                   GPT series and Mamba—end-to-end. With Manticore, we enable LM selection
021
                   without training multiple models, the construction of pretrained hybrids from ex-
022
                   isting pretrained models, and the ability to program pretrained hybrids to have
023                certain capabilities. Manticore hybrids match existing manually-designed hybrids,
024                achieve strong performance on the Long Range Arena benchmark, and improve on
025                pretrained transformers and state space models on various natural language tasks.

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Transformers are the workhorse architecture for large language models and beyond, powering a vast
031   collection of foundation models. While for years it appeared that the Transformers family would
032   remain the undisputed standard, a recent Cambrian explosion of proposed architectures has taken
033   place. Many of the new architectures achieve subquadratic complexity—in contrast to the quadratic
034   complexity of self-attention in Transformers—by using local or linear attention (De et al., 2024; Botev
035
      et al., 2024; Arora et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024), resurrecting and scaling recurrent networks (Botev
      et al., 2024; De et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2023), or by building on state-space modeling principles (Gu
036
      and Dao, 2023; Poli et al., 2023b;a; Fu et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2022). These approaches potentially
037
      promise to overturn the dominance of Transformers through more efficient training and inference.
038
039   However, no single new model is a clear overall winner when varying data modalities, tasks, and model
040   sizes. Comparing architectures on a fixed task is fraught with difficulties (Amos et al., 2024). Even if
041
      these are overcome, practitioners would have to experiment with and evaluate every architecture for
      each new task—an expensive proposition. Instead, seeking a best-of-all-worlds approach, researchers
042
      have proposed the use of hybrid models that mix multiple architectures. These hybrids, such as the
043
      MambaFormer (Park et al., 2024)—a mix of the popular SSM Mamba architecture with a standard
044   Transformer—have shown potential in maintaining the desirable properties of multiple model classes.
045
046   While promising, hybrids suffer from two main obstacles that stymie their adoption:
047              • Manual Design. Hybrid architectures are hand-crafted, either by manually exploring the
048                large search space of hybrids or by relying on often unreliable intuition and heuristics.
049
                 • Failure to Use Pretrained Models. It is unclear how to integrate pretrained model compo-
050                nents from models with different architectures. Pretrained models are a key advantage of
051                foundation models. However, due to compatibility issues, hybrids are often trained from
052                scratch, leading practitioners to resort to small hybrids in limited settings or incur high costs.
053

      A potential solution to the latter challenge is the use of model merging techniques (Yadav et al.,
055   2023; Yu et al., 2023; Wortsman et al., 2022; Ilharco et al., 2023; Davari and Belilovsky, 2023; Jang
056   et al., 2024), some of which can operate cross-architecture (Akiba et al., 2024; Goddard et al., 2024).
057   Unfortunately, such tools are embryonic–they are expensive and it is unclear how well they work
058   with the diverse types of architectures a user may seek to build a hybrid from.
059
      We propose a framework for automatically designing hybrid architectures that overcomes these
060
      obstacles. Our approach is inspired by principles from neural architecture search (NAS), but applies
061   these at the level of LM blocks rather than convolutional cells (Liu et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021) or
062   operations (Shen et al., 2022; Roberts et al., 2021). The resulting framework is simple and tractable.
063   It sidesteps merging different architectures by using simple linear projectors to translate between
064   the “languages” spoken by various architectures. This enables us to include blocks from many
065   different architectures with little to no changes required. In addition, inspired by the mechanistic
066   architecture design (MAD) framework (Poli et al., 2024), we show how our framework can learn
067   hybrid architectures via MAD that transfer to new tasks.
068   Concretely, with our proposed system, Manticore, we:
069
070
               1. Automatically select language models, without training several models from scratch,
071            2. Automatically construct pretrained hybrids without evaluating the entire search space,
072            3. Explore when it is possible to program hybrids without full training.
073   Experimentally, our automatically designed hybrids compete with existing hybrids and models on the
074   MAD tasks (Poli et al., 2024) and Long Range Arena (LRA) (Tay et al., 2021), we produce pretrained
075   hybrids that can improve downstream fine-tuning performance on a variety of natural and synthetic
076   language tasks, and we show that our hybrids can be programmed using the MAD tasks.2

      2       R ELATED WORK
080
081
      Language Model Architectures: Transfomers and Beyond. Transformers are currently the
      dominant LM architecture. The success of the “vanilla” architecture introduced by Vaswani et. al.
082
      (Vaswani et al., 2017) has led to many proposed variations. The quadratic complexity of the base
083
      self-attention operation has inspired the search for alternative architectures that offer comparable
084   performance with subquadratic complexity. One line of work builds off state-space models, with
085   variations made to enable language modeling (Poli et al., 2023a;b; Gu and Dao, 2023; Arora et al.,
086   2024). Another line of work involves linear-complexity attention by formulating transformers as
087   RNNs and expressing self-attention as a kernel dot-product (Katharopoulos et al., 2020). Other
088   approaches increase the expressivity of this formulation with data-dependent gating (Yang et al.,
089   2024). Our work does not propose a new architecture. Instead, we focus on the idea that practitioners
090   should be able to take advantage of new architectures in a transparent way.
091   Neural Architecture Search & Mechanistic Search. Neural architecture search (NAS) techniques
092   are used to automatically search for optimal architectures. These techniques have produced state-of-
093   the-art models in several different architectures and data domains. Much of the challenge in NAS is
094   the complexity of the search procedures; in the most standard form, NAS involves a difficult bilevel
095   optimization over a large search space. Much effort has been aimed at reducing these costs, often
096   via continuous relaxations of the large search spaces, with efficient, end-to-end differentiable search
097   techniques like DARTS (Liu et al., 2019), GAEA (Li et al., 2021), and DASH (Shen et al., 2022).
098   Using NAS to discover architectures for language modeling—and especially those that may rival
099   Transformers—has thus far been hard. A promising approach is the MAD framework (Poli et al.,
100   2024) , which uses “mechanistic tasks” (synthetic tasks organized around simple principles) to search
101   for high-quality subquadratic architectures. While we do not seek to discover new architectures, we
102   are inspired by this approach in our effort to search for hybrid architectures.
103   Hybrid Architectures. Perhaps unsurprisingly, there is no single dominant architecture among either
104   standards, like Transformers, or emerging subquadratic architectures. While there are some insights
105   that can be converted into heuristics for model selection, generally, to take advantage of new models,
106   practitioners must exhaustively evaluate all of them on each of their tasks. The cost of doing so has

140   3       M ETHODS
141
142   We now describe Manticore, our framework for automatically designing hybrid architectures by
143   mixing components of pretrained models. Manticore relies on projectors to align features across
144   architectures, then applies a convex combination to the aligned features, as summarized in Figure 1.
145
      In Section 3.1, we discuss and formally define the structure of Manticore hybrids, including the
146
      projectors and convex combination mixture weights, as well as how both of these components are
147
      used within Manticore. In Section 3.2, we detail the search procedures (inspired by NAS) and training
148   routines involved in pretraining, fine-tuning, and programming hybrids. Finally, we provide the
149   synthetic and real data settings that we use in our experiments in Section 4.
150
151   3.1      T HE S TRUCTURE OF M ANTICORE H YBRIDS
152
153   Our framework comprises three main parts: the individual LMs that we combine to produce our
154   overall hybrid, projectors that translate feature representations between LMs of different architectures,
155   and convex combination mixture weights that specify how much the hybrid will use the features of
156   each component architecture. We detail each of these in the following.
157

160   a component model in our framework. In this section, we will formally define the general high-level

253   We are now ready to define our overall hybrid architecture. We seek to create a hybrid from K

278   In NAS terms, our search space is over the set of L ∋ ℓ mixture weights α(ℓ) ∈ ∆K−1 . However, our
279   search space differs from typical gradient-based NAS techniques in the sense that we do not require
280   discretization to derive a final architecture after we obtain our mixture weights. Typically, NAS would
281   involve selecting a single sequence of component architecture blocks at each of the Manticore blocks,
282   usually by taking the arg max of the mixture weights. Instead, the mixtures themselves are what
283   characterize Manticore hybrids. Nonetheless, if we were to replace the mixture weights α(ℓ) with
284
      discrete one-hot vectors, we could derive any of the following: the component model architectures
      themselves, existing hybrid architectures, and ‘frankenmerged’ models (Goddard et al., 2024).

287   3.2      H OW T O U SE M ANTICORE
288
      With Manticore, we can automatically select language models without training every model in the
289
      search space, automatically construct pretrained hybrid architectures without significant trial-and-
290   error, and also program pretrained hybrids without full training. In this section, we will discuss the
291   details of how Manticore can be used in each of these three usage scenarios.
292
293
      Training hybrids from scratch. Manticore can be used to automatically select LMs without training
      all of the LMs in the search space. Our selection technique is simple: inspired by gradient-based
294
      NAS techniques (Liu et al., 2019) and treating the mixture weights as our ‘architecture parameters,’
295
      we proceed in two steps: 1. train mixture weights along with all other parameters, and 2. freeze the
296   mixture weights and retrain the rest of the parameters from scratch. Unlike NAS, we found that in
297   many pretraining settings, it was sufficient to stop at 1. and forgo retraining. In our pretraining
298   experiments, we primarily use randomly-initialized GPT-Neo (Black et al., 2021) and Mamba (Gu
299   and Dao, 2023) as component models without projectors, and separately experiment with a subset of
300   the blocks from MAD (Poli et al., 2024).
301
      Fine-tuning pretrained hybrids. Manticore can be used to create and fine-tune pretrained hybrids.
302   We create pretrained hybrids as follows: begin with a set of pretrained models, replace their LM
303   heads and embeddings with a single randomly initialized LM head and embedding layer, and pretrain
304   the projectors on a small amount of general language data such as FineWeb (Penedo et al., 2024)
305   while keeping the original component model weights frozen.4 To fine-tune the pretrained hybrids
306   on downstream task data, we first search for mixture weights by training all of the parameters
307   simultaneously, we freeze the mixture weights, rewind the component models and projectors to their
308   pretrained state, and fine-tune. This procedure completely sidesteps large-scale pretraining of
309   new hybrids. In our synthetic experiments, we create pretrained Manticore hybrids from pretrained
310
      GPT-Neo-125M (Black et al., 2021) and Mamba-130M (Gu and Dao, 2023) models, while for our
      experiments on real natural language data, we opt for pretrained Pythia-410M (Biderman et al., 2023)
311
      and Mamba-370M (Gu and Dao, 2023) as component models.
312
313   Programming hybrids. Excitingly, there are cases in which we can program Manticore mixture
314   weights by using external information to predict them. We consider two scenarios. If we know that a
315   component model has blocks that are incompatible with the target task—e.g. resulting from sequence
316
      length constraints—we can omit these blocks by setting their mixture weights to 0. Otherwise,
      we can predict good mixture weights by searching on a fixed set of proxy tasks—for this, we use
317
      the MAD tasks (Poli et al., 2024). The MAD tasks are synthetic unit tests that are predictive of
318
      hybrid LM scaling laws, but within our framework, we find that they can also be useful for finding
319   pretrained hybrids. We use the following procedure for programming mixture weights using the
320   MAD tasks. First, run search on the MAD tasks using a smaller, randomly initialized version of our
321   pretrained hybrid. For each MAD task, our search procedure returns a set of mixture weights—we
322   simply average the resulting mixture weights, freeze them, and fine-tune on downstream task data.

342   4     E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
343
344   We provide experimental evidence that validates the following claims about Manticore:

      4.3     P ROGRAMMING H YBRIDS
498
499
      We evaluate C3 with two types of external data: access to task metadata such as sequence length
500
      requirements, and the use of the MAD tasks as a proxy for search on downstream task data.
501
502   Setup. As in many of our previous experiments, we used the GPT-Neo and Mamba architectures
503   as component models to our Manticore hybrid. However, this time, we set out to train from scratch
504
      on the extremely long-range Pathfinder-X task from LRA, which requires sequence length support
      greater than that of GPT-Neo. Using this external information about the task, we set the mixture
505
      weights for GPT-Neo to 0, which in this case, means that Manticore reduces to Mamba. 5
506
507   Results. The results of this experiment are shown in the last row of Table 3. In the simple case of
508   having access to task metadata, this validates the claim that we can program mixture weights
509   to exclude incompatible blocks. At the time of writing, we are not aware of prior published Mamba
510
      results on LRA despite community interest, which would make our evaluation in Table 3 the first such
      result. Note that we did not thoroughly tune hyperparameters, so we view this result as a preliminary
511
      starting point for the community to build off of, rather than a final answer.
512
513   Setup. Finally, in the case in which we can actually run all of our component models on our learning
514   task, we explore when we can program the mixture weights using the MAD tasks as a proxy for search,
515   which are intended to be predictive of scaling laws on The Pile (Poli et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2020).
516
      We set out to fine-tune a pretrained hybrid comprising GPT-Neo-125M and Mamba-130M, which
      were both pretrained on The Pile, with two Manticore blocks on our Penn Treebank completions
517
      synthetic. We train a scaled-down version of this Manticore hybrid with randomly initialized weights
518
      and two blocks per component model on the MAD tasks. This yields mixture weights for each of the
519   MAD tasks—we average them across the tasks, and then fine-tune our pretrained hybrid on Penn
520   Treebank completions using the predicted mixture weights.
521
522
      Results. Our results are shown in Figure 5 (right). We superimpose the predicted mixture weights and
      mean search trajectory from MAD onto the architecture loss landscape computed on Penn Treebank
523
      completions. We find that this procedure recovers a hybrid that outperforms the component models
524
      (Mamba, lower right; GPT-Neo, upper left) and substantially outperforms the naive frankenmerges in
525   our search space (upper right and lower left) (Goddard et al., 2024). This is a scenario in which it is
526   possible to program mixture weights using external sources without performing search on the
527   task data. Intriguingly, search on the MAD tasks appears to follow the architecture gradient on the
528   different downstream fine-tuning task, even though the architecture is scaled-down and trained from
529   scratch on MAD. We hypothesize that programming Manticore hybrids becomes more difficult as
530   the fine-tuning distribution is further from the pretraining distribution, and that the architecture loss
531   landscapes become less similar. This evaluation was carried out on our synthetic PTB completions
532   task, so the fine-tuning dataset should be fairly similar to the pretraining distribution. In our evaluation
533
      in Table 1, we find that Mamba outperforms the Pythia component model on English natural language
      tasks that are further from the pretraining distribution than our synthetic (while both models were
534
      trained on The Pile (Gao et al., 2020) which is largely in English, we are not training on completions
535
      produced by the models themselves). Finally, our evaluations in Figure 6 use non-English text, which
536   is further from the pretraining data distribution, and we observe no discernible pattern between their
537   loss landscapes—programming α parameters in this scenario is likely challenging.

858   D    O N BASELINES
859
860   The correct set of baselines for Manticore is an interesting and somewhat tricky question. In the
861   main text, we compare to the set of component models used to construct a Manticore hybrid—in
862   other words, in order for Manticore to be at least as performant as its component models on
863   a task, it must match or beat the performance of the best component model, which implies
      that both component models need to be fine-tuned. This would roughly match the total amount


                                                                16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      of fine-tuning FLOPs used to train the corresponding Manticore hybrid. However, there are other
865   potential ways to make a comparison; in this section, we will discuss the fairness and availability
866   of baselines corresponding to different metrics of comparison, and provide a new set of baselines
867   involving ensembles of component models. Specifically, we will address the question of whether the
868   correct comparison is one involving parameter count, training FLOPs, or inference FLOPs.
869
870   D.1   PARAMETER C OUNT
871
872   One proposal is to compare a Manticore hybrid of size N to a pretrained model that is also of
873   size N . Manticore combines the weights of existing pretrained models to produce a hybrid that
874   is drastically cheaper to generate compared to pretraining a hybrid of the same size from scratch.
875   Off-the-shelf pretrained models of size N are often pretrained up to D tokens corresponding to its
876
      Chinchilla optimum (Hoffmann et al., 2022), but information about the amount, mixture, or quality
      of pretraining data is often unavailable. This makes comparison along the axis of the parameter count
877
      alone somewhat challenging—a larger model may well have been trained on more total data than
878
      the two smaller component models making up Manticore. In other words, Manticore should not
879   be expected to follow the same pretraining scaling laws as models that were trained from scratch.
880   Therefore, comparing a Manticore hybrid and a pretrained model of the same size is not
881   necessarily a fair comparison, when considering model size alone. Furthermore, pretrained
882   models of a specific predefined size N are not even guaranteed to exist.
883
884   D.2   T RAINING FLOP S
885
886   Another option is to make a comparison along the axis of total training FLOPs, which would include
887   pretraining FLOPs, fine-tuning FLOPs, and any additional FLOPs incurred when generating a
888   Manticore hybrid. Suppose we create a Manticore hybrid from two component models of sizes N1
889
      and N2 , which have been pretrained using T1 and T2 tokens, incurring roughly 6N1 T1 and 6N2 T2
      FLOPs, respectively (Kaplan et al., 2020). With Manticore, we incur FLOPs from two sources:
890
      projector pretraining and fine-tuning. In our experiments, we use Tproj = 100M tokens of general data
891
      for projector pretraining, and saw in Figure 7 that we likely didn’t even need this much. Nonetheless,
892   100M tokens is substantially smaller than the typical amount of pretraining data, so we can assume
893   that Tproj = 100M << min {T1 , T2 }, and since the pretrained projectors can be reused, this cost can
894   be amortized over many future fine-tuning runs. Manticore then involves fine-tuning on some small
895   amount of downstream tasks-specific data comprising Tft << min {T1 , T2 } tokens. So then, the total
896   amount of training FLOPs involved end-to-end in producing a Manticore hybrid is
897
              6N1 T1 + 6N2 T2 + (6N1 + 6N2 )Tproj + (6N1 + 6N2 )Tft = O(6N1 T1 + 6N2 T2 ),
898
899   meaning that the total training FLOPs is dominated by the pretraining of the component models. Our
900   experiments in the main text compare Manticore to the better of the two component models,
901   which means that both component models need to be fine-tuned (i.e., the baseline comprises
902   ‘both’ component models). Therefore, if the projector pretraining FLOPs are amortized over
903   many fine-tuning runs, Manticore roughly matches the baseline in terms of training FLOPs.
904
      That is, this baseline and Manticore effectively requires 6N1 T1 + 6N2 T2 + (6N1 + 6N2 )Tft FLOPs.
905
906   D.3   I NFERENCE FLOP S
907
      It is true that our baselines in the main text (which are pairs of component models) are cheaper
908
      in terms of inference FLOPs compared to Manticore. In fact, Manticore effectively doubles the
909   inference FLOPs by requiring forward passes through both component models. Here, we include
910   an analysis of inference FLOPs showing that the contribution of the projectors is negligible, and we
911   present an additional baseline—combining the component models into an ensemble that is fine-tuned
912   simultaneously using the same fine-tuning budget as Manticore.

       prototyping of new hybrid LM architectures. In our experiments, we use harder variants of the MAD
       tasks, in which we use a larger vocabulary size of 128 instead of the default 16 for most of the tasks,

       along with fewer training examples. For simplicity, we omit the compression task as it requires the

1048   • In-context recall. MAD utilizes a multi-query associative recall task, challenging models to
1049     retrieve values linked to keys within input sequences, testing their in-context learning ability across
1050     randomly shuffled mappings. We use a vocab size of 128 and 800 training examples.
1051   • Fuzzy in-context recall. This is a variant of in-context recall to assess a model’s ability to
1052     semantically group adjacent tokens. Variable-length keys and values are randomly paired, testing
1053     the model’s capacity for fuzzy recall. We use a vocab size of 128 and 800 training examples.
1054   • Noisy in-context recall. This is an adaptation of in-context recall to evaluate a model’s capacity
1055     to disregard irrelevant information. This involves inserting tokens from a separate vocabulary
1056     randomly among key-value pairs, enhancing the memorization challenge. We use a vocab size of
1057     128, a noise vocab size of 16 with 80% noise, and 800 training examples.
1058   • Selective Copying. MAD employs a selective copying task to evaluate a model’s ability to
1059     remember and replicate specific tokens from an input sequence while disregarding randomly
1060     inserted noise tokens, emphasizing the preservation of token order. We use a vocab size of 128
1061     with 96 tokens to copy, and 800 training examples.
1062   • Memorization. MAD assesses language models’ factual knowledge retention through a memoriza-
1063     tion task, where models learn fixed key-value mappings without in-context computation, testing
1064     pure memorization ability. For this task, we use a vocab size of 8192.
1065
       Long Range Arena. Long Range Arena (LRA) (Tay et al., 2021) is a benchmark consisting of
1066   various tasks of different modalities that evaluate how well models can learn long-context data. For
1067   simplicity, we omit byte-level document retrieval as it requires two forward passes per example.
1068
1069   • Long ListOps. This task is designed to understand whether the architecture is able to model
1070     hierarchically structured data in a long-context (Nangia and Bowman, 2018).
1071   • Byte-level text classification. This task attempts to test the model’s ability to deal with com-
1072     positionality as in the real world, the model needs to compose characters into words and words
1073     into higher-phrases in not so well defined boundaries making it a challenging task, we use IMDB
1074     dataset(Maas et al., 2011) in the LRA paper (Tay et al., 2021).
1075   • Image classification on a sequence of pixels. This task aims to understand whether a model is
1076     able to capture the 2D spatial structure when presented with a flattened 1D version of an image to
1077     classify, we use pixel information from CIFAR10(Krizhevsky, 2009) dataset.
1078   • Pathfinder. This task helps to understand whether a model can reason about whether the given 2
1079     dots in an image are connected by a path having dashes or not. The sequence length is 1024 i.e a
         32x32 image is flattened and provided as input to the model (Linsley et al., 2018; Kim et al., 2020).

1114
1115   Throughout our research process, we noted a handful of opportunities that help to democratize LM
1116   research. Should these opportunities be taken up by the research community, we believe they could
1117   help to democratize and help to decentralize community-driven LM research, all which enabling
1118   further research on pretrained hybrids.
1119
1120   A search engine for pretrained models. Surprisingly, we were unable to easily search for pre-
1121
       trained LMs of certain sizes or with certain properties (using Huggingface or otherwise). Tools like
       this should exist: this would not only significantly democratize LMs, but it would help to reduce
1122
       monopolies on LM releases and usage, and thereby decentralize LM research.
1123
1124
       Standardized, block-structured LM implementations. We found that standard tools such as
1125
       Huggingface and PyTorch were insufficient to cleanly access intermediate activations across several
1126   model implementations. This could be resolved by adopting standard implementations or structures
1127   for LMs that share the common block structure that we describe in Section 3.1.1. Instead, our
1128   solution was to fork implementations of several Huggingface models, which is time-consuming, error-
1129   prone, and non-scalable. A solution to this problem would enable and encourage further research on
1130   pretrained hybrid models, which in turn helps to democratize LM research.
1131
1132   Removing tokenizers from LM pipelines. We believe that there are too many possible tokeniz-
1133   ers, and that tokenizers have a significant potential to introduce merge conflicts in model merg-
       ing/pretrained hybrid pipelines. In response to this challenge, in our work, we simply chose an


                                                           21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       arbitrary tokenizer and relearned our embeddings and LM head from scratch in all of our experiments.
1135   Possible solutions to this problem would be: as a community, we agree on a standard (small) set of
1136   tokenizers, or we eliminate tokenizers altogether by learning character or byte-level LMs.

1163   We acknowledge the possibility of misuse with respect to any form of LM research. In our work,
1164
       among other things, we enable the creation of pretrained hybrid models from existing pretrained
       models. This has potentially positive and negative social impacts for the community. As a positive
1165
       potential social impact, we enable the community to much more easily create their own hybrid models
1166
       of various sizes without large scale pretraining—this has as much potential for positive impact in that
1167   these models can be used for good. On the other hand, the ability to create large pretrained hybrids,
1168   potentially with custom sets of skills, has the potential to open the door to misuse. To safeguard
1169   against such things, we will include appropriate licenses and rules for usage when we ultimately
1170   deploy a Python package for the community to more broadly use our framework.
