009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            We study the task of automatically finding evidence relevant to hypotheses in
012            biomedical papers. Finding relevant evidence is an important stage when humans
013            write systematic reviews about certain scientific hypotheses. We introduce Evi-
014            denceBench to measure models performance on this task, which is created by a
015            novel pipeline that consists of hypothesis generation and sentence-by-sentence an-
016
               notation of biomedical papers for relevant evidence, completely guided by and faith-
               fully following existing human experts judgment. Our pipeline’s value and accuracy
017
               is validated by teams of human experts. We evaluate a diverse set of language
018
               models and retrieval systems on the benchmark and find the performance of the best
019            models still falls significantly short of expert-level on this task. To show the scalabil-
020            ity of our proposed pipeline, we create a larger EvidenceBench-100k with 107,461
021            fully annotated papers with hypotheses to faciliate model training and development.
022            Both datasets are available at https://github.com/EvidenceBench/EvidenceBench.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Systematic reviews serve as a cornerstone of evidence-based research across scientific disciplines,
      offering comprehensive syntheses of current research directions on specific questions or topics. Their
028
      impact is particularly pronounced in biomedicine, where they play a critical role in evaluating the
029
      efficacy of therapeutic interventions and informing clinical practice. These reviews play a crucial role
030   in shaping healthcare policies (Bunn et al., 2015) adopted by national agencies in countries like the
031   US (Viswanathan et al., 2012), UK (Alderson & Tan, 2011), and Australia (NHMRC, 2019). They
032   are widely utilized by universities, hospitals and research institutions. Each review requires intensive
033   labor from human experts and costs approximately 141 thousand US dollars to produce. Annually,
034   major pharmaceutical companies and universities each spend over 18 million US dollars to produce
035   these systematic reviews (Michelson & Reuter, 2019). Leading systematic review organizations, such
036   as The Cochrane Collaboration (2023), have established sustainable business models, generating
037   annual revenues exceeding 20 million US dollars from licensing and royalties, underscoring both the
038
      economic value and essential nature of high-quality evidence synthesis in biomedicine.
039   With over 1 million biomedical research papers published each year and more than 35 million papers
040   already archived in the PubMed database of biomedical literature (González-Márquez et al., 2024),
041   it has become increasingly labor-intensive for humans to create systematic reviews for emerging
042   questions and hypotheses. Consequently, there is significant research and commercial interest in
043   developing automated systems that can assist—and potentially one day replace—humans in creating
044
      systematic reviews.
045   Biomedical systematic reviews follow a three-stage methodology (Higgins & Green, 2011): search,
046   extract, and analyze. The search stage leverages structured medical databases like MEDLINE (2024)
047   through keyword queries to identify candidate papers. The analyze stage employs specialized sta-
048   tistical software such as RevMan (Higgins & Green) for quantitative synthesis and visualization of
049   findings. While these stages present opportunities for algorithmic advancement, we focus on automat-
050
      ing the crucial extract stage—the identification of hypothesis-relevant evidence from biomedical
      literature. The extract stage, despite being fundamental to the review’s quality and currently requiring
051
      intensive manual effort, remains largely unaddressed by computational approaches.
052
053   There is currently no established benchmark or training resource for this task. To address this gap,
      we introduce EvidenceBench (see Figure 1), a benchmark designed to evaluate models’ abilities

      to extract evidence relevant to a given hypothesis. Additionally, we present EvidenceBench-100k,
055   which serves both as a benchmark for model performance and as a resource to aid in the training and
056   development of models for this task. Our proposed datasets are fully open-sourced under the CC-BY
057   license and encompass a comprehensive range of biomedical topics.
058
      We introduce a novel pipeline for creating EvidenceBench and EvidenceBench-100k, powered by
059
      Large Language Models (LLMs). The pipeline has two main components: hypothesis generation
060
      and an alignment annotator that matches hypotheses with sentences from papers. Informally, we use
061   existing evidence summary from review papers to generate a hypothesis and use the same evidence
062   summary to find sentences that provide evidence for the generated hypothesis. See Section 3 and
063   Figure 2 3 for details.
064
      Our pipeline is highly scalable, reducing the construction time of EvidenceBench from over 3,000
065
      human hours and $120,000 in wages to just 3 API hours and $5,000 in API costs, using state-of-the-art
066
      LLMs at the time of data creation, Claude3-Opus for hypothesis generation and GPT4-0125 for
067   alignment annotation. During the construction of EvidenceBench-100k, we used GPT4-o-mini for
068   alignment annotation and kept the construction time and cost under 24 hours and $5000.
069
070
      Table 5b demonstrates EvidenceBench-100k is suitable for fine-tuning LLMs and embedding models
      as we observed significant improvements of fine-tuned models over their pretrained baselines.
071
072   We conduct a benchmarking study on a variety of state-of-the-art large language models (LLMs)
073   and embedding models, which provide several insights. First, although the best LLMs still fall short
074   of expert-level performance on this task, indicating that they cannot replace humans, they have the
075   potential to assist them. Second, embedding models consistently underperform compared to large
076
      language models due to lack of global context from research papers. Third, we report that current
      LLMs trained for long document understanding still get "Lost in the Middle" (Liu et al., 2024).

092   2     TASK F ORMULATION
093
094   The EvidenceBench task is to identify the most important pieces of evidence relevant to a hypothesis.
095   This is formulated as a sentence retrieval task. Given a paper, the task is to retrieve a set of sentences
096   that jointly provide the most important pieces of evidence. See Figure 1 for an example.
097
098   2.1   D EFINITIONS
099
100   Candidate Pool: The full-text of a research paper is presented as a list of sentences (including
101   section and subsection headings but excluding figure and table and their captions). This ordered list of
102   sentences is the Candidate Pool. Very importantly, research papers and review papers are completely
103   different.
104   Evidence Summary: An evidence summary is written by human experts. It is included in open-
105   sourced review papers, such as surveys, monographs and systematic reviews. An evidence summary
106   is directly linked to one single research paper. It contains all pieces of evidence (from this research
107   paper) that the human experts believe are important and relevant to a hypothesis. Evidence summary
      could take the form of a normal paper summary, or a bulleted list, or even tabular format.


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Study Aspect: A study aspect, denoted as fi , is a single piece of information. For instance, an
109   experimental outcome or a detail from study design. Note, study aspects are decomposed from a
110   evidence summary written by experts. Each study aspect is identified by human experts and represent
111   an important detail relevant for a hypothesis. Therefore, study aspects are considered to be human
112   experts’ judgment.
113
      Source of Information: A sentence in a research paper is considered a source of information for a
114
      study aspect if it satisfies the following two criteria.
115
116         1. The content of the sentence implies most of the study aspect.
117         2. For any part of the study aspect that the sentence does not cover, the information must be
118            easily deducible from the surrounding context.
119
120   Given a sentence sj and a study aspect fi , define the source-of-information indicator function
121   S(fi , sj ):

122                                         1 if sj is a the source of information for fi
                            S(fi , sj ) =
123                                         0 otherwise
124   Note, if a sentence is a source of information for a study aspect, we informally say the sentence covers
125   this aspect. Note, since study aspect is decomposed and only represent one piece of information, one
126   single sentence is enough to cover it.
127
      Hypothesis: A hypothesis is a scientific generalization, usually expressible in one line (less than 50
128   words). It should not be tied to specific details of an experiment. See Figure 1 for an example and
129   Section 3.3 for the generation and validation process.
130
131
      Evidence Set: The evidence set is the set of study aspects which provides evidence relevant to a
      hypothesis. A study aspect in this set may provide evidence on its own, or only in combination with
132
      other study aspects. Very importantly, we use an Evidence Summary to derive an evidence set, see
133
      Figure 2 right side.

      2.2   TASK D EFINITION
136
137   We now introduce our primary task, Evidence Retrieval @K (ER@K). Informally, the task is to find K
138   sentences in a research paper which provide the greatest amount of evidence relevant to a hypothesis.
139   This is operationalized as finding sentences in the research paper which cover the most study aspects
140   from the evidence set.
141
      Formally, given a hypothesis and a candidate pool, the task for a system is to retrieve K sentences
142   from the candidate pool which provide evidence relevant to the hypothesis. The retrieved sentences
143   are then evaluated against the evidence set, which contains ground-truth study aspects (i.e. pieces
144   of evidence relevant to the hypothesis identified by human experts). The goal is for the K retrieved
145   sentences to be sources of information for as many of the study aspects in the evidence set as possible.
146   Crucially, during the retrieval task, the system does not have access to the ground-truth evidence set;
147   the evidence set is only used for evaluation.
148   In the second version of the task, only study aspects related to the results and analyses are considered.
149   Study aspects related to background and methods are filtered out of the evidence set. This task is
150   called Result-ER@K, and focus on system’s ability to identify numerical and experimental results.
151

      3.3     H YPOTHESIS G ENERATION
251
      A review paper focuses on a specific hypothesis and survey a number of research papers, summarizing
252
      the evidence that each provides for the hypothesis. For each evidence summary, our goal is to extract
253   the hypothesis that it is providing relevant evidence to. Think of a hypothesis as an explicit or implicit
254   question waiting to be recovered. In order to do this, we provide an LLM (Claude3-Opus) with
255   the evidence summary as well as surrounding paragraphs. The model is then prompted to recover
256   the hypothesis being discussed in the review paper. See Figure 2 left side. To ensure high quality
257   hypotheses for EvidenceBench-100k, Claude3-Opus is also used.
258
259   3.3.1     E XPERT VALIDATION OF H YPOTHESES
260
261   We perform an expert evaluation of the hypotheses extracted from review papers in EvidenceBench,
262   focusing on two questions:
263
264           1. Does the hypothesis have sufficient scientific value?
265           2. Does the corresponding evidence summary provide evidence which is relevant to the hy-
266              pothesis?
267
268   The annotation team for this task consisted of three medical doctors. The first expert defined the
269   annotation guidelines and provided feedback on an initial set of 20 extracted hypotheses. This
      feedback was also used to perform prompt optimization for Claude3-Opus.

277   3.4   A LIGNMENT A NNOTATION OF S TUDY A SPECTS AND S ENTENCES
278
279
      We have so far described the procedure for decomposing study aspects and recovering hypotheses
      from the review papers. A list of study aspects describes the evidence that a specific research paper
280
      provides relevant to a hypothesis. The final step is to identify which sentences of the original research
281
      paper serve as sources of information for each study aspect. Because study aspects can, in general,
282   come from any part of the research paper, this requires annotating every sentence in the research
283   paper according to whether it matches each study aspect. See Figure 4 for the distribution of relative
284   positions of sentences that are sources of information for some aspects.
285
      Research papers in the dataset have approximately 168 sentences and 10 study aspects on average.
286
      EvidenceBench contains more than 400 research papers. Sentence-by-sentence annotation requires
287
      approximately 700,000 sentence annotations, which is infeasible given the use of expert annotators;
288   we estimate that it would require more than 3000 hours of annotation. We therefore develop a pipeline
289   for automating the annotation process, and perform human evaluation of its reliability. We observe
290   that the task of labeling a sentence according to whether it is a source of information for a study
291   aspect is considerably simpler than the benchmark’s full sentence retrieval task. It only requires a
292   judgment of whether a single sentence from the research paper contains most of the same information
293   as a study aspect.

449   6   A NALYSES
450
451   Effectiveness of Current LLM Solutions: Table 3 clearly indicates that current embedding models
452   are inadequate for assisting or replacing human experts in identifying relevant evidence for biomedical
453
      hypotheses. We now examine the best-performing LLM solution, GPT-4o. In the task ER@Optimal,
      GPT-4o retrieves an average of 4-5 sentences per hypothesis, according to Table 1. In this setting,
454
      GPT-4o achieves a 50% Aspect Recall, covering half of the study aspects identified by human experts.
455
      This demonstrates that current LLMs cannot fully replace human experts in finding relevant evidence
456   for hypotheses.
457
458   Conversely, per the task definition for ER@10, models retrieve 10 sentences. In this setting, GPT-4o
459
      achieves an aspect recall of 70%. According to Table 1, a typical research paper contains 168
      sentences. Therefore, instead of reviewing the entire research paper, humans can use the 10 sentences
460
      retrieved by GPT-4o as an efficient starting point, while searching for additional sentences that cover
461
      potentially missing aspects. This demonstrates that, in this setting, GPT-4o can meaningfully assist
462   human experts in locating and presenting evidence from research papers for hypotheses, a crucial
463   step in writing review papers.
464
465
      Embedding Models Underperform Generative Models: From Table 3, we observe that the
      performance of embedding models falls significantly short of the performance of similar-sized
466
      generative models. The embedding models GritLM-7B, E5-Mistral-7B and the state-of-the-art
467
      NV-Embed-7B cover at most 20.1% of aspects, while the pretrained LLama3-8B covers 35.8% of
468   aspects. The shortcomings of GritLM-7B and NV-Embed-7B suggest that a naive local embedding
469   of sentences, without contextual awareness, is insufficient for this task. Our empirical observations
470   confirm that reasoning beyond individual sentences is necessary to solve this task effectively. For
471   instance, in Figure 1, Sentence 9 and Sentence 69 convey the same information and both address
472   study aspect 1. Only by comparing them together (i.e., reasoning beyond a single sentence) can
473   models eliminate one of these sentences to reduce redundancy.
474   Section-level Reasoning is Sufficient: Table 4 shows that retrieving evidence section-by-section
475   (Sec-by-Sec) achieves strong performance on the task. With Sec-by-Sec, a model can only read one
476   section at a time, preventing it from reasoning across multiple sections. The strong performance
477   of this method indicates that global reasoning across the entire research paper is not essential for
478   retrieving evidence. This can be explained by the structure and organization of biomedical research
479   papers, where the content of each section is relatively self-contained, and interaction across sections
480   is sparse. This suggests that LLMs trained for much longer contexts (e.g., over 10,000 tokens) may
481   not be necessary for this task.
482   LLMs Still Get "Lost in the Middle". We observed that some papers have most of their important
483   sentences concentrated at the beginning and end of the document, as shown in Figure 4. We categorize
484   these papers into two groups. In the first category, the first 10 and last 10 sentences of a paper cover
485   over 80% of the study’s aspects. In the second category, these sentences cover less than 20% of the
      aspects. Out of 3,000 randomly sampled points from the EvidenceBench-100k test set, 1,115 papers

515   7    R ELATED W ORK
516
517   Hypothesis Generation Recent works explore using LLMs to generate scientific hypotheses (O’Brien
518   et al., 2024; Tong et al., 2023; Park et al., 2024; Baek et al., 2024; Abdel-Rehim et al., 2024).
519   Qi et al. (2023) fine-tune LLMs on biomedical literature that pairs background knowledge with
520   corresponding hypotheses, and then use the LLMs to generate hypotheses when prompted with
521
      background knowledge. In contrast, our hypothesis generation procedure is much more extractive in
      that it aims to extract existing implicit or explicit hypotheses from review papers, thus ensuring high
522
      alignment with domain experts’ own perspectives.
523
524   Evidence Retrieval Claim-based retrieval (Chen et al., 2023) retrieve evidence by breaking down
525   a complex claim into specific aspects and retrieving each aspect. On the other hand, our pipeline
526   uses a novel approach, by decomposing summarized evidence from review papers into study aspects
527
      (instead of claims), which serves as ground-truth human domain experts knowledge that would guide
      our LLM-annotator to match sentences from research papers to these study aspects.
528
529   LLM in Biomedicine Researchers have shown strong performance of LLMs in BioNLP tasks,
530   including relation extraction, question answering, document classification, name entity recognition,
531   and summarization (Luo et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024; Monajatipoor et al., 2024; Jahan et al., 2023;
532   2024; Munnangi et al., 2024). LLMs are also being used to extract specific information from report,
533
      e.g. Interventions, Outcomes, and Findings by Wadhwa et al. (2023).
534
535   8    C ONCLUSION
536
537   We introduced EvidenceBench and EvidenceBench-100k, a benchmark for retrieving evidence for
538   scientific hypotheses from biomedical literature. EvidenceBench was constructed using an automated,
539   scalable pipeline that transforms expert-written summaries into fine-grained annotations linked to
      specific sentences in research papers.

       H    F INE - TUNING AND E VALUATION ON E VIDENCE B ENCH -100 K
1675
1676
       EvidenceBench-100k also split into a 80k train set and a 20k test set. For cost reasons, we random sam-
1677   ple 300 datapoints from the 20k test set. We evaluated various representative LLMs and embedding
1678   models on the Task of Result ER@Optimal on this new 300 points test set from EvidenceBench-100k.
1679   From table 5a, we clearly see LLMs dominate over embedding models, while GritLM-7B outperforms
1680   other embedding models. This shows EvidenceBench-100k test set can also be used to evaluate and
1681   differentiate various models’ performance.
1682
       Furthermore, to demonstrate the quality of EvidenceBench-100k’s training set, we fine-tuned two
1683   models. E5-v2 335M is a light-weight embedding model. Since there are over 100 million sentence-
1684   aspect pair judgments from the train set, we randomly sampled 1 million triplets, where each triplet
1685   contains an anchor (the study aspect), a positive (a sentence that is considered as source of information
1686   for the study aspect), a negative (a sentence that is not considered as source of information for the
1687   aspect). Using a margin-based triplet-loss with margin=0.05, AdamW optimizer (weight decay
1688   =0.01), peak learning rate of 5e-4, 10% linear warmup then cosine-annealing, batch size =256, we
1689   trained E5-v2 with full-parameter tuning for one epoch. We also fine-tuned Llama3-8B using all 80k
1690   training datapoints. Llama3-8B is trained with LoRA rank=8, alpha =16, a batch size of 16, AdamW
1691
       optimizer (weight decay =0.01), and the same learning rate scheduler, we fine-tuned the model for
       one epoch. Note, during inference time we trained LLama3-8B to only output sentence indices,
1692
       during training, we trained it to output both sentence indices, sentence texts and the summarized list
1693
       of study aspects. This has shown to be more effective than only training it on sentence indices.
1694
1695   We made sure none of the papers in the original EvidenceBench is used in any way in the
1696   EvidenceBench-100k to avoid contamination. Our fine-tuned E5 and Llama3-8B are tested on
1697
       the original EvidenceBench test set for the task of Result ER@Optimal.
1698   Notice that the fine-tuned E5 model has dramatically improved its performance on the original test set,
1699   surpassing all larger embedding models, see Table 5b. It only trails behind the performance of large
1700   language models. The fine-tuned Llama3-8B has a performance of 41.0% which trails behind much
1701   larger SoTA LLMs. This shows that EvidenceBench-100k is suitable for developing and training
1702   both embedding-based information retrieval systems as well as large language models.

1704   I   Q UALITATIVE A NALYSIS FOR GPT-4 O ON THE O RIGINAL E VIDENCE B ENCH
1705
1706   For the task Result ER@ Optimal, there are a total of 1228 results study aspects that the review
1707   papers identified.
1708
       Out of these 1228, GPT-4o’s retrieved sentences fail to cover 583 study aspects, from which 50 pairs
1709
       (missed "Result" aspect, set of GPT-4o retrieved sentences) are randomly sampled and manually
1710   inspected by two researchers. Out of the 50 cases, 10 cases should not be considered GPT-4o errors.
1711   In 7 cases, GPT-4o retrieved an almost sufficient set of sentences but missed one aspect due to the
1712   upper limit on the number of sentences. This issue arose from a failure in the optimization step
1713   during retrieval, not from a reasoning or retrieval error. In 1 case, our alignment annotation procedure
1714   missed one of the GPT-4o retrieved sentences as the source of information for a study aspect. In 1
1715   case, a parsing error occurred where our algorithm did not extract the full summary from the review
1716   paper. Finally, in 1 case, one method-related study aspect is mistakenly labeled as a result-related
1717   study aspect.
