009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In the federated learning (FL) framework, clients participate in collaborative learn-
012            ing tasks under the coordination of a central server. Clients train local submodels
013            using their own data and share gradients with the server, which aggregates the
014            gradients to achieve privacy protection. However, recent research has revealed
015            that gradient inversion attacks (GIAs) can leak private data from the shared gradi-
016            ents. Prior work has only demonstrated the feasibility of recovering input data
017            from gradients under highly restrictive conditions, such as when dealing with
018            high-resolution face datasets, where GIAs often struggle to initiate attacks effec-
019
               tively, and on object datasets like Imagenet, where they encounter limitations,
               primarily manifested in their ability to handle only small batch sizes and high
020
               time costs. As a result, we believe that implementing GIAs on high-resolution
021
               face datasets with large batch sizes is a challenging task. In this work, we intro-
022            duce Fast Gradient Leakage (FGL), which enables rapid image recovery across
023            various network models on complex datasets, including the CelebA face dataset
024            (1000 classes, 224×224 px). We also introduced StyleGAN as prior knowledge
025            for images and achieved FGL with a batch size of 60 in experiments (constrained
026            by experimental hardware). We further propose a joint gradient matching loss,
027            where multiple distinct matching losses collectively contribute to clarifying the
028            attack direction and enhancing the efficiency of the optimization process. Exten-
029            sive experimentation validates the feasibility of our approach. We anticipate that
030
               our proposed method can serve as a valuable tool to advance the development of
               privacy defense techniques.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Federated Learning (FL) (Li et al. (2020), McMahan et al. (2016)) aims to train high-quality global
037   models while ensuring client privacy. In this framework, clients only use their local data (Melis et al.
038   (2019), Shokri et al. (2017)) for training and share weights or gradients to update the global model,
039
      reducing the flow of user data and enhancing data privacy and security (Tan et al. (2022), Karim-
      ireddy et al. (2020), Chilimbi et al. (2014), Konený et al. (2016), Yang et al. (2019b)). Therefore,
040
      FL can be applied in privacy-sensitive domains such as medical data (Brisimi et al., Sadilek et al.
041
      (2020)). Hospitals can obtain a collaboratively trained global model without the need to share pa-
042   tient data. This approach addresses critical concerns such as data privacy, data security, data access
043   control, and heterogeneous data access, enabling multiple participants to create a shared, powerful
044   machine learning model without sharing data.
045
      In most cases, the federated learning framework is considered an effective method to prevent privacy
046
      leakage. Nevertheless, recent research (Geiping et al. (2020), Yin et al. (2021), Zhao et al. (2020)
047
      Zhu & Han (2020), Wen et al. (2022)) has shown that the shared gradients contain a significant
048   amount of sensitive information, and attackers can exploit gradient leakage to obtain client’s private
049   data. This type of attack, known as gradient inversion attacks (GIAs), has evolved to the point
050   where it can achieve pixel-level image reconstruction. This poses a severe threat to privacy security
051   in federated learning. While GIAs have made some progress in FL attacks, they still face limitations.
052   For instance, using CNN models makes it challenging to carry out attacks on high-resolution face
053   datasets (Yin et al. (2021)). Additionally, they encounter difficulties in conducting attacks with large
      batch sizes, along with issues related to low attack efficiency and high time costs.

054
      We propose a GAN-based GIAs method called Fast Gradient Leakage (FGL) to address the chal-
055   lenges mentioned earlier. Our technique is inspired by the field of model inversion attacks (MIAs)
056   (Fredrikson et al. (2015), Zhang et al. (2020), He et al. (2019)), which shares similarities with our
057   domain, yet MIAs have a longer research history, with many techniques being ahead of Gradient
058   Inversion Attacks (GIAs). MIAs utilize GANs as prior knowledge for images, avoiding the need
059   to synthesize images from noise; instead, the optimization process fine-tunes existing images until
060   the target image is reached. Moreover, MIAs significantly improve attack success rates through
061   strategies like rotation transformations and image selection. In a white-box setting, PPA (Struppek
062   et al.) achieves large-batch and high-accuracy attacks on face datasets, a feat challenging for GIAs.
063
      Inspired by MIAs, we introduce related techniques into GIAs.
064   Building upon previous research, we conceptualize GIAs attacks as an optimization problem (Yin
065   et al. (2021)). However, due to the difficulty in optimizing high-resolution images with GIAs’
066   gradient matching losses, our main challenge lies in overcoming the tendency for the optimization
067   process to fall into local optima. To address this challenge, we break away from the limitations
068   of previous studies and propose the concept of a joint gradient matching loss function. Different
069
      gradient matching losses guide the optimization process from different perspectives, allowing for
      simultaneous optimization from multiple angles, thereby making the optimization results more likely
070
      to approach the global optimum.
071
072   Our goal is to quickly recover private images from clients. To reduce time overhead, we adopt
073   StyleGAN (Karras et al. (2019), Karras et al. (2020)) as prior knowledge for images, allowing ini-
074   tial images to be fine-tuned rather than synthesized from noise, significantly reducing time costs.
075
      By introducing our proposed joint gradient matching loss function, the attack epoch is significantly
      reduced, making the optimization process simpler and faster. The combination of these two fac-
076
      tors enhances attack efficiency. Additionally, with the optimization process becoming simpler, the
077
      batch size of attacks has also increased. Compared to previous methods, our approach has achieved
078   significant improvements in both attack time and batch size.
079
080   Compared to GI, we introduced pre-trained StyleGAN as prior knowledge for images, combined
081
      with a joint gradient matching loss. This approach allowed convergence within a very small number
      of epochs (70 in our experiments), significantly reducing the time overhead. More importantly, we
082
      successfully conducted attacks on high-resolution (224×224px) face images for the first time under
083
      CNN architecture, with a batch size of up to 60 (limited by hardware).
084
085   The main contributions are summarized as follows:
086
             • For the first time, we have employed an optimization-based approach on a CNN architec-
087
               ture to achieve the reconstruction of high-resolution facial datasets.
088
089
             • We propose a joint gradient matching loss that combines multiple advantages and signifi-
               cantly improve reconstruction quality.
090
091          • We have introduced a selection strategy that, when combined with multi-seed optimization
092
               strategies, enhances the quality of reconstructed images.

098   The task of synthesizing images from neural networks has been a long-standing challenge, and Gen-
099
      erative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Zeng & Long (2022), Radford et al. (2015), Salimans et al.
      (2016), Brock et al. (2018), Gulrajani et al. (2017)) have achieved remarkable success in this field.
100
      The initial GAN (Zeng & Long (2022)) often faced issues with instability and training difficulties.
101
      However, techniques proposed by (Radford et al. (2015)) have addressed the stability problems in
102   GAN training, and the improved WGAN (Gulrajani et al. (2017)) has enhanced training stability
103   while mitigating issues like mode collapse. Despite these improvements, WGAN still generated
104   low-quality images, prompting the proposal of WGAN-GP (Gulrajani et al. (2017)) as a further
105   enhancement. DCGAN (Radford et al. (2015)) introduced a more stable architecture for training
106   GANs and demonstrated that adversarial networks can learn meaningful image representations for
107   supervised learning tasks. StyleGAN (Karras et al. (2019)) introduced style transfer, enabling intu-
      itive control over synthesis at various scales. Building on these advancements, StyleGAN2 (Karras

120   2.2   P RIVACY L EAKAGE VIA G RADIENT
121
      Recently, the field of privacy attacks in federated learning has seen significant advancements.(Zhu
122
      & Han (2020)) proposed a gradient-based privacy attack method, allowing attackers to reconstruct
123
      users’ private data by matching exchanged gradients in the federated learning scenario. This poses
124   substantial challenges to privacy and security in federated learning (Konený et al. (2016), Wang
125   et al. (2019), Reisizadeh et al. (2019),). Building on this work, (Zhao et al. (2020)) improved the
126   method by introducing a label inference technique, enhancing the attack efficiency. However, both
127   methods are only applicable to shallow networks (Lecun et al. (1998)) trained on low-resolution
128   data (Krizhevsky (2009)). Previous GIAs commonly utilized the L2 norm for gradient matching.
129   (Geiping et al. (2020)) proposed cosine similarity for gradient matching, achieving promising re-
130   sults and revealing vulnerabilities even in large-scale datasets trained on non-smooth networks like
131   ResNet-152 (He et al. (2016)).(Zhu & Blaschko (2021)) introduced a novel method that advanced
132
      the understanding of GIAs. (Yin et al. (2021)) further improved label inference with GradInversion,
      incorporating an image regularization term to enhance image fidelity. Their approach demonstrated
133
      success in revealing privacy images with batch size ranging from 8 to 48 on large networks trained
134
      on ImageNet. In the pursuit of stronger attacks, (Huang et al. (2021)) evaluated the work of (Yin
135   et al. (2021)) and pointed out two strong assumptions (BatchNorm statistics and private labels),
136   suggesting that relaxing these assumptions significantly reduces the attack capability.(Hatamizadeh
137   et al.) further demonstrated the feasibility of GIAs on vision transformers (ViTs). More recently, (Li
138   et al.) proposed a GAN-based gradient inversion attack, capable of revealing privacy images under
139   various gradient defenses while maintaining good image quality.

150
      In both federated learning algorithms, FedSGD and FedAvg, we assume that the attacker functions as
151   an honest but curious server. The attacker is endowed with the capability to receive model weights w
152   and gradients 4W transmitted by the clients. The adversary’s goal is to deduce sensitive information
153   from the client’s private data by scrutinizing these parameters. It is crucial to emphasize that the
154   server is prohibited from unilaterally modifying the initial model sent to the client (Fowl et al.,
155   Boenisch et al.). Additionally, the adversary may leverage publicly available resources such as
156   common datasets and openly accessible pre-trained models, but their computational resources are
157   limited.

      3.3   FAST G RADIENT L EAKAGE
196
197   In this section, we will systematically present our contributions and technical details, following the
198   process of the GIAs.
199
200
      Selection Strategy. The selection of the initial point plays a crucial role in GIAs. To exploit the
      potential of the initial point, we propose four selection strategies, which consist of both initial point
201
      selection and corresponding representative result selection strategies that need to be applied together.
202
203   The first strategy involves selecting points with high confidence scores F (x) according to the target
204   model as the initial points and final points. The second strategy entails choosing points with a small
205   L2 distance between the pseudo-gradient 4W 0 and the true gradient 4W as the initial points and
206
      final points. The third strategy entails selecting points with a cosine similarity close to one between
      the pseudo-gradient 4W 0 and the true gradient 4W as the initial points and final points. The fourth
207
      strategy involves selecting points with both a small L2 distance between the pseudo-gradient 4W 0
208
      and the true gradient 4W and a cosine similarity close to one as the initial points and final points.
209
210   Among these strategies, the first initial point strategy yields the best results in our work. It is worth
211   noting that the first strategy requires the use of a model that has already converged, while the other
212
      three strategies are applicable to all models.
213   label inference. After selecting highly attack-oriented initial points, the next step is the label in-
214   ference (Qu et al. (2019), Fu et al.), where successfully obtaining the true labels greatly enhances
215   the success rate of the attack. Based on the analysis of (Zhao et al. (2020)), under the premise of
      using cross-entropy as the loss function, it is possible to infer the true labels using shared gradients.

      attack process. We define t as a single transformation operation, which can include rotations, trans-

435   The best-performing result among these runs was ultimately chosen as the conclusive outcome.
436
      Through qualitative and quantitative comparisons, we can clearly observe FGL outperforms prior
437
      art both visually (Figure 4)) and numerically (Table 2) on the facial dataset. Previous methods rarely
438
      focused on attacking high-resolution facial images, and when we attempted to apply these methods
439   to facial datasets, achieving remarkable attack performance was challenging. Among the four com-
440   parative methods, only the GI (Yin et al. (2021)) and Fishing (Wen et al. (2022)) method managed
441   to capture the outline of the images, providing a rough representation of facial features, albeit with
442   incorrect positions and lacking details. The synthesized images from the other methods DLG (Zhu
443   & Han (2020)) only consisted of indistinguishable pixels. Compared to similar methods (Jeon et al.)
444   that did not demonstrate effective attacks when faced with distributional shifts, our proposed novel
445   unified matching loss makes the optimization direction more explicit. This approach proves to be
446   more adept at avoiding local optima. Additionally, employing multiple seed optimization strategies
447
      enables us to transcend the limitations of single-seed optimization. By incorporating a selection
      strategy, we can identify results that are more representative.
