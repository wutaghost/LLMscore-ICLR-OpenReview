009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Vision-language models (VLMs) have shown promise in a variety of challenging
012            video comprehension tasks. VLMs typically extract frames from the source video
013            and take the corresponding visual tokens as input. However, a rapid increase in
014            the number of visual tokens, e.g., when handling lengthy videos, can swiftly lead
015            to a long-context dilemma for the inference efficiency of VLMs. Given that re-
016
               dundant and task-irrelevant information may exist in the visual tokens across both
               spatial and temporal axes, we advocate removing less important visual tokens
017
               during the prefilling stage of VLMs’ inference procedure to improve the compu-
018
               tation and storage efficiency. We first identify an interesting phenomenon termed
019            as Visual Attention Shrinking (VAS), wherein certain visual tokens receive pro-
020            gressively diminishing attention during the processing stages of the model. This
021            implies that the model itself knows what to care about and what to discard. With
022            this, we develop a robust algorithm to detect attention shrinking at each layer of
023            the model using states from preceding layers. Based on the detection results, we
024            perform token removal in both temporal and spatial axes. Our approach does not
025            require parameterized modifications to the original VLM and is compatible with
026            the prevalent KV cache strategy. Through extensive experiments across differ-
027
               ent VLMs, our approach witnesses an average speedup of 1.98× in generating
               the first response token, utilizing only 47.2% of the visual tokens, without sig-
028
               nificantly compromising the task performance. Additionally, when applied to the
029
               huge VILA1.5-40B, our method can achieve up to 4.16× speedup compared to
030            the vanilla model.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Vision-language models (VLMs) excel at diverse visual understanding problems by integrating large
036   language models (LLMs) with visual encoders (Liu et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2023;
037   Ye et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). While early VLMs primarily focused on image-
038   text tasks, recent models (Li et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024a; Xu et al., 2024;
039
      Zhang et al., 2024a) have shifted the paradigm to video-text data. They usually extract frames from
      source videos and take the corresponding encoded visual tokens as input, exhibiting promise in
040
      diverse video understanding problems (Caba Heilbron et al., 2015; Li et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024a).
041
042   Yet, existing video VLMs can encounter a significant efficiency dilemma when dealing with lengthy
043   videos. In such scenarios, the number of visual tokens increases linearly with respect to the number
044   of processed frames and can swiftly become the predominant cost factor during the prefilling stage
045
      of inference. Given the natural redundancy in visual signals, a reasonable solution is to reduce the
      number of visual tokens that need to be considered during the inference process. One can choose
046
      to merge the states within the visual encoders (Bolya et al., 2022; Haurum et al., 2023) so that the
047
      tokens fed to the VLMs decrease, but this is arguably suboptimal due to the loss of consideration
048   of the textual information given to the VLMs. Similarly, some studies have attempted to adjust
049   the architecture of the visual encoder (Shang et al., 2024; Arif et al., 2024), but this may introduce
050   additional training requirements. Other works (Jin et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025)
051   have proposed new efficient VLMs by applying token merging strategies before the visual tokens
052   enter the backbone of the language model, but these methods cannot be directly plug-and-play for
053   other models. Recently, some works have directly explored the feasibility of token dropping during
      the inference stage of LLMs or VLMs (Xiao et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b; Lin et al., 2024b; Fu

079   This paper explores eliminating the redundancy and task-irrelevant information in the visual tokens
080   for video VLMs from both spatial and temporal axes. We first take a close inspection of the atten-
081
      tion mechanism during the prefilling stage of the inference process of video VLMs, and observe an
      interesting phenomenon called Visual Attention Shrinking (VAS)—across the layers of the model,
082
      some frames or positions consistently exhibit a downward trend in attention scores. Given the com-
083
      putation mechanism of attention, we know such tokens are becoming less and less important for the
084   generation of new tokens. So, it is a natural choice to discard them and let the model only care about
085   the left ones. Based on this observation, we develop an algorithm that utilizes the attention states
086   from previous layers to robustly detect attention shrinking, and continuously removes visual tokens
087   based on the detection results. The overview of the proposed framework is illustrated in Figure 1.
088
      We conduct extensive experiments on various VLMs, including PLLaVA (Xu et al., 2024),
089
      VILA (Lin et al., 2024a), and LongVA (Zhang et al., 2024a), across multiple video benchmarks
090   to validate the effectiveness of our method. On average, our approach achieves a speedup of 1.98
091   times during the prefilling stage while utilizing less than half of the visual tokens, without compro-
092   mising task performance. Additionally, when applied to the large VILA1.5-40B model, our method
093   can achieve up to 4.16 times speedup compared to the vanilla model. We also conduct ablation
094   studies to examine the impact of different settings on method.

114   2.2   E FFICIENT M ULTIMODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
115
116   Multimodal large language models have demonstrated excellent performance across various tasks.
117   However, their large scale and high training and inference costs hinder their broader application.
118
      Therefore, research into efficient and lightweight models holds significant potential and meaning.
      Some works focus on optimizing the architecture. SPHINX-X (Gao et al., 2024) improves training
119
      efficiency by removing redundant visual encoders and skipping fully filled sub-images. Honey-
120
      bee (Cha et al., 2024) introduces an innovative projector design that provides flexibility and local
121   enhancement effects, achieving greater efficiency. Some researchers propose more efficient mod-
122   els (Jin et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025) by leveraging token merging. Additionally,
123   Some try to utilize small language models with excellent capabilities as their core components, such
124   as MobileVLM (Chu et al., 2023), LLaVA-Phi (Zhu et al., 2024), VL-Mamba (Qiao et al., 2024).
125   Furthermore, specific efforts have been dedicated to achieving more efficient visual processing, in-
126   cluding optimizing attention mechanisms (Rao et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022), pruning (Yue et al.,
127   2021; Kong et al., 2022; Chavan et al., 2022; Yu & Xiang, 2023), quantization (Wang et al., 2022;
128   Liu et al., 2023b), etc.
129
130   2.3   T OKEN R EDUCTION
131
      Token pruning and merging techniques have been widely studied across different domains to im-
132
      prove the efficiency of transformer models. For instance, in classification tasks, researchers have
133   found that not all tokens are necessary for accurate predictions, which led to the exploration of
134   token pruning methods to reduce unnecessary information. ToMe (Bolya et al., 2022) employs a
135   binary soft-matching algorithm for merging redundant tokens using the cosine similarity between
136   key values. Kim et al. (2024) combines the advantages of token pruning and token merging, propos-
137   ing a token fusion method to enhance the computational efficiency of visual transformers. Jiang
138   et al. (2023) implements a prompt compression method for long contexts by reducing tokens, sig-
139   nificantly decreasing the computational cost of language models. More related research (Shi et al.,
140   2023; Chen et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2024) adopt similar approaches, refining and filtering tokens to
141
      eliminate potential redundancy and enhance efficiency.

145   This section first reviews how VLMs handle and respond to multimodal inputs, then elaborates on
146   the visual attention shrinking phenomenon within the attention maps of video VLMs. Based on this,
147   a visual token reduction algorithm is developed for the prefilling stage of inference for acceleration.

149   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
150
      VLMs typically contain three components: a visual encoder for extracting visual features, a pro-
151
      jector for aligning visual and textual information, and a backbone LLM responsible for context
152   understanding and output generation (Liu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2023). As said,
153   the input to VLMs typically consists of visual data and texts, denoted as V and T , respectively.
154   The final tokens consist of three parts: system tokens, visual tokens, and text tokens, represented as
155   Xs , Xv , and Xt . Text inputs are directly converted into corresponding tokens through the language
156   model’s tokenizer, resulting in Xs , Xt while visual inputs are processed through the visual encoder
157   and projector to form the final input. We denote the functions of the respective components as fve ,
158   and fproj . Thus, the visual tokens can be obtained by:
159                                            Xv = fproj (fve (V )).                                    (1)
160   Subsequently, by combining the visual information with the textual information, we obtain the input
161   X for the language model:
                                            X = Concat(Xs , Xv , Xt ).                                   (2)

164   distinct patches, recorded as P . Therefore, the visual input V encompasses both temporal and
165   spatial dimensions. Assuming that for a given model, the number of sampled frames and patches
166   are denoted as m and k respectively, we have:

182   Assume the input length is L when the model generates the first token. By aggregating the attention

186   As the sequence length increases, the computational cost of attention mechanisms grows signif-

190
      visual feature Xv is represented by a fixed number of tokens. In video tasks, as the number of

191
      exacerbating the computational burden.

195                                                                              PL
      We first aim to analyze the importance of tokens using the attention map. i=1 αli,j calculates the
196   sum of the attention weights from other tokens to the j-th token, as defined and identified in other
197   works(Ge et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b). This value reflects the level of attention that the j-th
198   token receives from other tokens, and those with larger values can be referred to as “heavy hitters”.
199   In our subsequent research process, we also consider this metric for visual tokens, a larger value
200   indicates that the information contained is relatively more important.
201
      Determine the Importance of Visual Tokens. We attempt to identify metrics for assessing the
202   importance of visual tokens. When evaluating the importance of visual tokens, directly summing
203   the attention from other tokens to one single visual token may be somewhat unfair. Due to the
204   existence of masks, earlier visual tokens receive additional attention from later tokens. Therefore,
205   we use the following value to determine visual tokens’ importance:

260   3.3   T OKEN R EDUCTION BASED ON VAS
261
262   Based on the observation, we develop an algorithm that tracks the attention levels of previous lay-
263   ers to filter and drop visual tokens during the prefilling stage of inference. This method reduces
264   computational load and accelerates model inference.
265   Track Attention Levels. We temporarily store the corresponding attention levels of previous layers
266   during inference, which amount to the attention of text tokens to visual tokens. These data can be
267   processed in parallel to obtain T and S. After passing through the k-th layer of the decoder, we
268   can rank the importance of visual tokens in the form like (Tk − Tk−1 + Tk−2 ). A smaller value
269   indicates a lower attention level, which means lower importance and can be utilized for subsequent
      token reduction.

292   Gradual Token Removal. We adopt a gradual removal strategy while striving to preserve the
293   structural integrity of the video frame sequence established during model training. In video tasks,
294   the number of visual tokens is substantial, and prematurely removing a large number of visual tokens
295
      may lead to irreversible information loss. Each individual visual token corresponds to specific frame
      and patch within a temporal group and a spatial group. We track the attention level changes of each
296
      group over the past three layers to determine whether to do token reduction.
297
298   To ensure the frequency of token dropping, we establish two conditions to determine when to remove
299   tokens, with each dropping removing r% of the current visual tokens. First, if it is recorded that
300   no token removal operation has been implemented for several consecutive layers, a visual token
301
      reduction will be performed and this guarantees a minimum threshold for token removal, preventing
      the retention of all visual tokens across excessive layers. The second condition is based on observing
302
      a consistent decline in attention levels for a sufficient proportion of temporal or spatial groups.
303
304   More Details. It is important to note that our gradual token removal occurs only during the infer-
305   ence’s prefilling stage, namely in the forward process of generating the first token. This process
306   does not utilize the KV cache, but the dynamic cache records the corresponding KV cache values at
307
      each layer. These values can be reused in the generation of subsequent tokens, eliminating the need
      for recalculation and dropping, so it does not conflict with the KV cache.
308
309   We set a start layer and an end layer. The start layer is designed to prevent the too early loss
310   of critical visual tokens, while the end layer is set due to the limited number of remaining visual
311   tokens, making further removal less meaningful. In experiments, we generally set the start layer to 2
312   and the end layer to the maximum layer index−4. However, in some situations, confusions between
313
      visual and text tokens may lead to abnormal outputs which may be because the number of visual
      tokens is significantly lower than that during training. In these cases, we may postpone the start
314
      layer and remove the remaining visual tokens at the end layer. This process ensures that the model
315
      generates stable and effective outputs. The complete design is shown in Algorithm 1.

388   4.2     E XPERIMENTS RESULTS
389
390   The main results are presented in Table 1. We report the accuracy on various tasks, as well as the
391   corresponding visual token reduction and acceleration effects. In most cases, the bottleneck for
392   the generation time of subsequent tokens, aside from the first token, is loading the KV cache as
393   quickly as possible. Therefore, we follow other works reporting the Time To First Token (TTFT).
394   Img-Memory represents the proportion of remaining visual tokens in the KV cache after reduction
395
      compared to the original amount. This allows the same model to occupy less GPU memory during
      inference.
396
397
398   4.2.1    A NALYSIS ON DIFFERENT TASKS
399
400   Our method achieves acceleration across most tasks in different models while maintaining over 90%
401
      performance. Also, our gradual dynamic token reduction strategy is more flexible in reducing the
      number of visual tokens, resulting in better overall acceleration and lower memory usage.
402
403   However, we find that some methods encounter challenges in certain tasks. For instance, in the
404   MSVD-QA task, attention-based token reduction does not yield satisfactory results. One possible
405   reason is that this task requires video captioning, where the text queries are generally short and
406   generic—for example, a question like “What is the man doing?” has limited content words. As a
407
      result, the text tokens may not effectively direct attention to the crucial parts of the visual tokens
      which are needed to find the answer, leading to information loss.
408
409
410   4.2.2    A NALYSIS ON DIFFERENT MODELS
411
412
      Different language model architectures also have a significant impact. The Llama3 model used in
      VILA1.5-8B demonstrates the strongest capabilities, maintaining high-quality outputs even after a
413
      substantial reduction of visual tokens across all tasks. The Vicuna model used in PLLaVA-7B is
414
      also commonly employed, with many multimodal models relying on it as a backbone and benefiting
415   from relatively mature training strategies, performing well in most tasks.
416
417   Meanwhile, some methods perform poorly or even fail to work on some models, generating mean-
418
      ingless repetition or an immediate end to the response. There are several reasons for this. On one
      hand, some previous methods are tested on only one model and have not been verified on others.
419
      On the other hand, there are inherent biases in the models themselves. For example, when cer-
420
      tain prompts are inputted, the model tends to generate fixed response options after visual tokens
421   are reduced. As for the performance on the LongVA model, since LongVA focuses on enhanc-
422   ing the model’s long-context capabilities through textual data during training and does not train on
423   long video data, its performance tends to degrade when the number of visual tokens is significantly
424   reduced in several tasks.

460   4.3.1    C OMPARISON WITH SOME TOY METHODS

462
      Our method utilizes multiple non-consecutive layers for token reduction, which is different from
      others. This may be a more reliable and effective strategy. To investigate this, we design some cor-

      responding simple methods for comparison. As shown in Table 3, we apply average token merging
464

468   4.3.2    R EDUCTION RATE AND RATIO
469
470   In our experiments, a reduction ratio controls the proportion of visual tokens reduced at each time.
471   A maximum interval is also set to prevent continuous skipping of dropping. These two values
472
      significantly influence the efficiency of our method. Therefore, we adjust these parameters and
      conducted corresponding ablation studies, with the results shown in the Table 4. From the results
473
      observed, the two factors are not independent. When a relatively loose interval is paired with a
474
      faster reduction ratio, the results tend to worsen. They need to be matched, and a balance must be
475   struck between performance and acceleration. Generally speaking, setting the reduction ratio to a
476   conservative 30% is a good choice.

      5     C ONCLUSIONS
480
      In this paper, we address the issue of slow long-context reasoning in VLMs when dealing with video
481
      tasks. We analyze the attention maps of the corresponding visual tokens and observe a phenomenon
482
      of visual attention shrinking. Based on this observation, we propose an algorithm that dynamically
483   removes visual tokens during the inference prefilling stage, without requiring training, to reduce
484   computational overhead and accelerate the inference of video VLMs.

      FastV also utilizes the attention mechanism and incorporates a reduction ratio, typically set to 50%.
777
      Unlike our method, which performs multiple rounds of token reduction, FastV removes 50% of the
778   visual tokens in a single operation at a specific layer k, with the number of visual tokens remaining
779   fixed after that. Generally, k is chosen to be 2. The motivation behind FastV lies in the redundancy
780   of visual tokens; however, it does not specifically consider the impact of different text queries. It
781   uses global attention and removes the visual tokens that receive lower total attention. This approach
782   may have limitations, as discussed in the main text.
783
      VCC is straightforward, essentially performing an average pooling operation. In our implementa-
784   tion, we follow the settings used in FastV, reducing 50% of the visual tokens after the second layer,
785   with the number of tokens remaining unchanged thereafter. As a result, the memory usage of vi-
786   sual tokens in FastV and VCC is always the same in our experiments. This is a simple approach,
787   though it is often not the optimal. Additionally, in video understanding model training, the image
788   information of each frame has usually already undergone an average pooling operation.
789
      ToMe is more complex. Unlike FastV or VCC, which perform token reduction once, it also differs
790   from our flexible dynamic multi-round removal approach. First, ToMe uses the cosine similarity
791   between the key values of visual tokens, rather than the attention scores. The idea behind this is that,
792   for a given image, some patches may contain similar information, such as background color blocks.
793   By identifying these similar color blocks and merging them in an average manner, ToMe reduces the
794   number of visual tokens. Similar to VCC, it does not specifically account for the impact of different
795   text queries. This process is applied in the initial several layers of the model, where a fixed number
796   of visual tokens is reduced after each merge, rather than a percentage. The exact number of tokens
797   reduced each layer is specified in the main text.

799   model backbones, which may not be applicable in all scenarios. As a result, in our experiments,
800   there are inevitably instances where these methods failed.
