009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Existing methods excel in short-horizon tasks but struggle with complex, long-
012            horizon planning in dynamic environments. To address these limitations, we
013            propose the Memory-Driven Multimodal Chain of Thought (MCoT-Memory), a
014            framework designed to enhance task planning through two key innovations: 1)
015            Evolving Scene Graph-Driven Chain of Thought with CoT Memory Retrieval,
016            which enables the agent to continuously update a scene graph with visual infor-
017            mation captured along its trajectory, providing a structured and dynamic represen-
018
               tation of the environment that informs real-time decision-making, and uniquely in-
               corporates CoT memory retrieval to allow the agent to leverage past experiences in
019
               its reasoning process; 2) Stepwise Confidence-Driven Memory Retention, which
020
               employs an expert model to evaluate reasoning across multiple dimensions of ac-
021            curacy, ensuring that only high-confidence experiences are retained in memory for
022            future retrieval, thus enabling the agent to build on valuable insights and improve
023            performance in long-horizon tasks. To advance long-horizon task planning, we
024            present ExtendaBench, a comprehensive benchmark encompassing 1,198 tasks
025            across two simulators, VirtualHome and Habitat 2.0. The tasks are categorized
026            into ultra-short, short, median, and long tasks. Extensive experiments demonstrate
027            that prior methods struggle with long-horizon tasks, while MCoT-Memory signif-
028            icantly improves performance, marking it as a promising approach for embodied
029
               task planning.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      In the domain of autonomous systems, the expectation for robots to execute complex, real-world
034
      tasks in domestic settings has significantly increased. Tasks such as organizing a room, preparing
035
      a meal, and cleaning up afterward require not only diverse actions but also long-term planning.
036   However, current approaches struggle with long-horizon tasks due to limited research in this area
037   and the dominance of benchmarks Puig et al. (2018); Liao et al. (2019); Shridhar et al. (2020a;b)
038   focused on short, discrete tasks. This gap hinders progress toward robots capable of handling the
039   complex, multi-step tasks demanded by real-life scenarios.
040
      The advent of Large Language Models (LLMs) OpenAI (2023); Touvron et al. (2023); Chiang et al.
041
      (2023); Geng et al. (2023) has led to notable advancements in task planning. Several approaches
042   have leveraged LLMs to determine subsequent actions within task sequences. Some methods Huang
043   et al. (2022a); Ahn et al. (2022) score potential actions based on their alignment with LLM-predicted
044   outcomes, while others Huang et al. (2022b) use LLMs to directly generate actions. Additionally,
045   studies Huang et al. (2022b); Singh et al. (2023); Wake et al. (2023); Bhat et al. (2024) have inte-
046   grated environmental feedback to enhance adaptability in dynamic conditions. However, approaches
047   like ReAct Yao et al. (2022), which employ Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning, are limited by their
048   single-modality (text) input and lack of a memory mechanism. On the other hand, methods like
049   RAP Kagaya et al. (2024) focus more on memory but still depend heavily on external rewards to
050
      store successful experiences, which restricts their ability to autonomously explore and learn. Both
      methods are less suited to long-horizon tasks that require the integration of multimodal information
051
      and autonomous self-improvement, which are essential for robots in complex environments.
052
053   To address the limitations of existing methods in long-horizon task planning, we propose Memory-
      Driven Multimodal Chain of Thought (MCoT-Memory), a framework designed to tackle the chal-

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      lenges of complex task planning in dynamic environments. Our approach introduces two key inno-
055   vations: (1) Evolving Scene Graph-Driven CoT: This component allows the agent to continuously
056   update a scene graph with visual information captured along its trajectory. The evolving scene graph
057   provides a structured and dynamic representation of the environment, enabling the agent to make
058   decisions based on real-time context. Unlike prior methods that rely on static or text-based inputs,
059   our approach leverages the visual dynamics of the agent’s surroundings to inform its reasoning. (2)
060   Stepwise Confidence-Driven Memory Retention: After task completion, an expert model evaluates
061   each reasoning step based on coherence, relevance, common-sense alignment, and overall task com-
062   pletion. The aggregated score determines whether the entire reasoning process is stored in memory.
063
      This ensures that only high-confidence reasoning processes are retained, allowing the agent to reuse
      valuable experiences in future tasks and improving its performance on long-horizon tasks. By inte-
064
      grating these two innovations, MCoT-Memory enables more effective long-horizon task planning,
065
      combining dynamic visual updates with selective memory retention to address the challenges of
066   real-world, multi-step tasks.
067
068   Finally, addressing the notable gap in the field regarding the absence of a benchmark tailored for
069
      long-horizon tasks, we propose a comprehensive benchmark ExtendaBench divided into four cat-
      egories based on the number of steps required to complete the tasks: ultra-short, short, median,
070
      and long. Utilizing the generative capabilities of GPT-4 OpenAI (2023), we produced a vast array
071
      of tasks. These tasks underwent minimal human correction to ensure high-quality data while sub-
072   stantially reducing the cost associated with manual data labeling. To validate the efficacy of our
073   approach, we conducted comparative analyses against several baselines within this newly proposed
074   benchmark. The results unequivocally demonstrate that our method significantly enhances accuracy.
075
      The contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
076
077          • We introduce MCoT-Memory, a novel framework that combines evolving scene graph-
078            driven reasoning with stepwise confidence-driven memory retention, enabling robots to
079            handle long-horizon, multi-step tasks in dynamic environments more effectively.
080          • We propose a challenging benchmark, ExtendaBench, comprising four distinct sets that
081            collectively include 1,198 tasks. This benchmark is designed for evaluating long-horizon
082            tasks, providing a comprehensive platform for testing task-planning models.
083          • We implement several baselines and validate the effectiveness of MCoT-Memory. Exten-
084            sive experimental results showcase the considerable enhancements attributed to MCoT-
085            Memory.

      3     MC OT-M EMORY
163
164
      We present the Memory-Driven Multimodal Chain of Thought (MCoT-Memory) framework, de-
165   signed for long-horizon task planning in dynamic environments. Our approach introduces two key
166   innovations: the Evolving Scene Graph-Driven CoT, which enables real-time updates of a task-
167   related scene graph that focuses on key objects, and the Stepwise Confidence-Driven Memory Re-
168   tention, which selectively stores high-confidence reasoning processes for future tasks. The following
169   sections will detail each component and its implementation within the framework.
170
171   3.1     E VOLVING S CENE G RAPH -D RIVEN C OT
172
173   This module initiates with the construction of the scene graph, establishing a structured represen-
174
      tation of the environment. Subsequently, the agent generates actions based on this scene graph,
      relevant observations, and associated memory through CoT reasoning. Finally, the scene graph
175
      undergoes dynamic updates to reflect environmental changes, ensuring the agent’s understanding
176
      remains accurate and current.
177
178
      3.1.1    I NITIAL S CENE G RAPH C ONSTRUCTION
179
180   The initial construction of the scene graph is pivotal for structuring and preserving visual information
181   captured during the robot’s task execution. By systematically representing the environment, the
182   agent is enabled to effectively reason about its surroundings and make informed decisions. We
183   employ a MLLM-based expert, such as LLAVA, to generate the scene graph g1 based on the task
184
      description T , visual observation o1 , and a prompt specifically tailored for scene graph generation
      PSG in the first step:
185
                                               g1 = ESG (o1 , T, PSG ).                                    (1)
186
187   The scene graph g1 is structured in a format like JSON, and consists of five key attributes:
188    • Position: The robot’s location relative to key objects or rooms.
189    • Objects: Key entities present in the scene, with their positions and states.
190    • Rooms: Spaces observed by the robot during task execution.
191
       • States: Conditions of objects in the environment.
192
       • Relationships: Spatial and relational connections between objects and rooms.
193
194
      3.1.2    ACTION G ENERATION THROUGH C OT R EASONING
195
196   The MLLM subsequently generates actions grounded in rationales derived from observations, the
197   scene graph, task descriptions, and specific reasoning prompts. This two-step process enhances the
198   quality of action generation by ensuring each action is underpinned by a clear rationale. In each step
199   i, the agent produces rationales ri by evaluating the inputs: observation oi , the scene graph gi , task
200   description T , and the CoT reasoning prompt PCoT . A typical prompt for CoT reasoning might be:
201       Based on the provided information, think step-by-step to identify key factors for deciding the
202       next action.
203
      This can be expressed as:
204

      to task objectives, and ai is the predicted next action.

209   generation, enriching the decision-making process. Insights from prior experiences enhance the
210   model’s ability to approach similar tasks effectively:

      updated to reflect changes in surroundings and task context. This dynamic updating process is

225   3.2     S TEPWISE C ONFIDENCE -D RIVEN M EMORY BANK
226
227   The Stepwise Confidence-Driven Memory Bank stores high-confidence reasoning processes from
228   completed tasks. It selectively retains valuable experiences, including task descriptions, scene
229
      graphs, reasoning steps, and actions. This memory is then used to guide decision-making in fu-
      ture tasks. The following sections cover how experiences are stored and how relevant experiences
230
      are retrieved.
231
232
      3.2.1    E VALUATING C OT P ROCESSES FOR M EMORY R ETENTION
233
234   In the Stepwise Confidence-Driven Memory Bank, after completing a task T , the entire CoT pro-
235   cess {(r1 , a1 ), (r2 , a2 ), . . . , (rt , at )} is evaluated by a MLLM-based judge model Eeval . This expert
236   model evaluates the task based on criteria such as coherence, relevance, common-sense alignment,
237   and task completion, ensuring a thorough assessment of the CoT process. The expert model takes
238   as input the full task description, the reasoning steps, and a specific evaluation prompt Peval , which
239
      defines the following criteria:
240    • Coherence: Ensuring logical consistency throughout the CoT steps.
241    • Relevance: Verifying that each step is directly applicable to the current task.
242    • Common-Sense Alignment: Assessing whether the steps adhere to basic real-world knowledge.
243    • Task Completion: Evaluating the effectiveness of the reasoning in achieving the task’s goal.
244
245
      Based on these criteria, the expert model evaluates the entire CoT process and outputs the score and
      justification for each reasoning step, along with a final overall score for the task, which reflects:
246
247    1. The cumulative effectiveness of all reasoning steps combined.
248    2. The overall task completion, including whether the robot achieved the intended goal.
249
      This evaluation can be expressed as:
250
251             (j1 , s1 ), (j2 , s2 ), . . . , (jt , st ), s = Eeval (T, {(r1 , a1 ), (r2 , a2 ), . . . , (rt , at )}, Peval ),   (5)
252   where ji is the justification for the score, si is the score for step ri , and s is the final score for the
253   entire task. If s exceeds a predefined threshold τ , the task-specific elements are added to the memory
254   pool M as follows:
255                              M ← M ∪ {(T, {(g1 , r1 , a1 ), . . . , (gt , rt , at )})}.                   (6)

275
276   The ExtendaBench task corpus was developed using two distinct approaches tailored to each simu-
277   lator. For VirtualHome Puig et al. (2018), we leveraged GPT-4’s advanced generative capabilities to

280
281   4.1     V IRTUAL H OME
282
283
      The creation of the ExtendaBench task corpus for VirtualHome harnesses GPT-4’s powerful gener-
      ative abilities to produce diverse tasks. The process of generating tasks can be divided into three
284
      stages: generation, review, and refinement.
285
286
      4.1.1    G ENERATION
287
288   The initial phase begins within the confines of VirtualHome, a simulated environment, where a
289   varied collection of objects sets the stage for a multitude of task scenarios. By employing GPT-4
290   as the task generator, we design tasks focused on object manipulation, striving for a wide array of
291   task varieties and complexities. This method ensures an exhaustive representation of scenarios that
292   closely mimic real-world challenges. To facilitate the generator’s task creation, we provide prompts
293   that are carefully constructed to inspire a broad range of tasks. An illustrative example of such a
294   prompt is as follows:
295       Given an ”HUMAN ACTION LIST” and an ”OBJECT LIST”, you need to use some of them
296       to compose a new household task. And then generate a description of the task followed by
297       decomposing the task into steps.
298
299
      4.1.2    R EVIEW
300
301   In the subsequent phase, GPT-4 undertakes the generation of detailed action plans for the devised
302   tasks, meticulously outlining the steps required for successful task execution. To ensure the feasibil-
303   ity and coherence of these tasks, we introduce an additional examiner of scrutiny, also powered by
304   GPT-4. This examiner evaluates each task and its associated action plan for clarity, necessity, and
305   coherence of steps, as well as the relevance and practicality of the actions and items involved, ensur-
306   ing they belong to the simulated environment VirtualHome. It also assesses each step for common
307
      sense applicability, providing constructive feedback for further refinement. Below is an illustrative
      prompt that could be used to guide the examiner in its evaluation role:
308
309       Given a task with its decomposed steps, an ”HUMAN ACTION LIST” and an ”OBJECT
310       LIST”,you need to check that the actions and objects in the decomposed steps are all in the
311       given ”HUMAN ACTION LIST” and ”OBJECT LIST”.
312
313
      4.1.3    R EFINEMENT
314
315   After undergoing expert scrutiny, the generator refines the tasks and their corresponding action plans.
316   Subsequent simulation of these revised tasks and plans enables further improvements based on sim-
317   ulator feedback. Tasks that are successfully executed within the simulator receive preliminary ap-
318   proval. Nevertheless, to guarantee optimal quality and applicability, we subject each task to a rigor-
319   ous manual review, evaluating them for practicality and realism. Tasks that do not achieve success
320
      in the simulation are minimally modified bu human according to the simulator’s feedback, focusing
      on enhancing their realism and feasibility. Below is the prompt for refining according to feedback
321
      from simulator are as below:
322
323       Analyze the reasons for the failed steps and determine if the task is feasible under the given
          rules. If feasible, output your reasoning and suggest modifications to the failed steps.

398   4.3     DATASET S TATISTICS
399
400   The categorization within ExtendaBench is defined by the length of the action sequence required to
401   accomplish a task, distributed as follows:
402            • Ultra-Short Tasks: Tasks that can be completed in fewer than 10 actions.
403
               • Short Tasks: Tasks requiring 10 to 20 actions for completion.
404
               • Medium Tasks: Tasks necessitating 20 to 30 actions to finish.
405
406
               • Long Tasks: Tasks that demand more than 30 actions to complete.
407   The VirtualHome set includes a total of 294 tasks, with 103 ultra-short tasks, 65 short tasks, 69
408   medium tasks, and 57 long tasks. Similarly, the Habitat 2.0 set comprises 904 tasks, distributed as
409   161 ultra-short tasks, 243 short tasks, 190 medium tasks, and 310 long tasks.

      5.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
414
415   For the VirtualHome set, we used 120 tasks as the test set (30 tasks from each category), with the
416   remaining tasks serving as the training set, which can also be used as prompts. Similarly, the Habitat
417   2.0 set also includes 120 test tasks. To assess system efficacy, we employ success rate (SR) and goal
418   conditions recall (GCR) as our primary metrics. SR measures the proportion of executions where
419   all key goal conditions, identified as those that change from start to finish during a demonstration,
420   are met. GCR calculates the discrepancy between the expected and achieved end state conditions,
421   relative to the total number of specific goal conditions needed for a task. A perfect SR score of 1
422
      corresponds to achieving a GCR of 1, indicating flawless task execution. Results of SR and GCR
      are both reported in %.

513   6     C ONCLUSION
514
515   In this work, we introduced Memory-Driven Multimodal Chain of Thought (MCoT-Memory), a
516   novel framework designed to address the challenges of long-horizon task planning in dynamic en-
517   vironments. By incorporating Evolving Scene Graph-Driven CoT and Stepwise Confidence-Driven
518   Memory Retention, our approach enables agents to efficiently manage multi-step tasks by contin-
519   uously updating visual representations and selectively retaining high-quality reasoning processes.
520   These innovations allow MCoT-Memory to utilize long-term memory effectively and outperform
521   existing methods. To comprehensively evaluate the performance of MCoT-Memory, we proposed
522
      ExtendaBench, a new benchmark specifically designed for long-horizon tasks. ExtendaBench con-
      sists of 1,198 tasks across four categories—ultra-short, short, median, and long—offering a diverse
523
      platform to rigorously assess task-planning models. Our experiments, conducted against several
524
      baselines, demonstrated that MCoT-Memory consistently enhances task success rates and goal con-
525   dition recall, particularly in more complex, long-horizon scenarios. In summary, MCoT-Memory
526   advances multimodal task planning and provides a strong foundation for future research in long-
527   horizon task planning.
