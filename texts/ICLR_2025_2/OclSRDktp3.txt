009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models have demonstrated remarkable abilities across various
012            tasks, with Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting emerging as a key technique to
013            enhance reasoning capabilities. However, existing research primarily focuses on
014            improving performance, lacking a comprehensive framework to explain and un-
015            derstand the fundamental factors behind CoT’s success. To bridge this gap, we
016            introduce a novel perspective grounded in the Hopfieldian view of cognition in
017            cognitive neuroscience. We establish a connection between CoT reasoning and
018
               key cognitive elements such as stimuli, actions, neural populations, and represen-
               tation spaces. From our view, we can understand the reasoning process as the
019
               movement between these representation spaces. Building on this insight, we de-
020
               velop a method for localizing reasoning errors in the response of CoTs. Moreover,
021            we propose the Representation-of-Thought (RoT) framework, which leverages the
022            robustness of low-dimensional representation spaces to enhance the robustness of
023            the reasoning process in CoTs. Experimental results demonstrate that RoT im-
024            proves the robustness and interpretability of CoT reasoning while offering fine-
025            grained control over the reasoning process.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated exceptional capabilities in following the natural
030   language instructions (Ouyang et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2024) and excelling across a variety of down-
031   stream tasks (Hu et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2023). As reasoning skills are crucial for tasks such as
032   commonsense and mathematical reasoning (Rae et al., 2021), there is a growing focus on enhancing
033
      these capabilities. One prominent approach is Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2022;
      Kojima et al., 2022), a simple yet highly effective technique to unleash the reasoning capability of
034
      LLMs. However, despite its success, a natural and fundamental research question remains: How
035
      does the reasoning capability emerge through CoT prompting?
036
037   Numerous studies have sought to identify the key factors or elements that enable CoT to enhance the
038   reasoning capabilities of LLMs (Kojima et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a; Tang et al., 2023; Merrill &
039
      Sabharwal, 2023). Some works focus on improving CoT reasoning through query-based corrections
      (Kim et al., 2023), knowledge-enhanced frameworks (Zhao et al., 2023), and symbolic reasoning
040
      chains for faithful CoT (Lyu et al., 2023; Lanham et al., 2023). Other research has examined how
041
      the sequence of demonstrations, random labels (Min et al., 2022), or even meaningless tokens (Pfau
042   et al., 2024) can positively influence reasoning performance. However, these works primarily fo-
043   cus on improving the model’s reasoning performance, and they do not provide a comprehensive
044   framework to explain the underlying factors driving CoT’s success.
045
      To understand the reasoning process in CoTs more deeply, we draw inspiration from cognitive neuro-
046
      science, specifically the relationship between cognition and brain function. In this field, the Hopfiel-
047
      dian view (Hopfield, 1982) and the Sherringtonian view (Sherrington, 1906) represent two different
048   ways of understanding neural computational models and cognitive mechanisms. While the Sherring-
049   tonian view of cognitive explanation focuses on specific connections between neurons in the brain,
050   the Hopfieldian view emphasizes distributed computation across neural populations, where informa-
051   tion is not encoded by a single neuron but rather by the cooperative activity of many neurons. This
052   perspective is particularly suited to explaining complex cognitive functions like memory storage,
053   pattern recognition, and reasoning. Thus, the Hopfieldian view is generally considered more ad-
      vanced than the Sherringtonian view, especially in the context of explaining distributed computation

073   is: whether we can understand the reasoning in CoTs from the Hopfieldian view of cognition?
074   The Hopfieldian view explains the production of behavioral actions as emerging from transforma-
075   tions or movements within neural populations in response to stimuli in the brain (Barack & Krakauer,
076   2021) (cf. Figure 1). This perspective approaches cognition at the level of representations, disre-
077   garding the detailed roles of individual molecules, cells, and circuits, thus allowing the potential for
078   a more conceptual and semantic understanding of complex cognitive systems. Viewing the CoT-
079   triggered reasoning process in LLMs through this lens is intuitive: CoT prompting induces shifts in
080   the model’s trajectory in much the same way that external stimuli shape cognitive responses, driving
081   representation changes without altering the underlying system. Specifically, similar to the Hop-
082
      fieldian mechanism, where the shift or movement in neural populations happens during cognition
      itself, CoT influences reasoning during inference, controlling the logical steps without modifying
083
      the model’s parameters.
084
085   Given the parallels between the CoT-triggered reasoning process and the Hopfieldian view of cogni-
086   tion in the brain, we first establish a connection between these two by aligning key elements: stim-
087   uli and actions, neural populations, and representation spaces. Particularly, we provide a general
088
      framework for identifying the “representation spaces” of the “stimuli” given by CoTs. We conceptu-
      alize the reasoning process elicited by CoT prompting as movement between representation spaces,
089
      enabling us to improve and deepen our understanding of CoTs. Based on these connections, we then
090
      leverage the strength of the Hopfieldian view to improve or further understand CoTs. Specifically,
091   by leveraging the “representation spaces” in CoTs, we develop a method for localizing the reasoning
092   error in the responses. Moreover, by leveraging the robustness of low-dimensional representation
093   spaces, we propose a new framework, namely Representation-of-Thought (RoT), which enhances
094   the robustness of CoTs. We summarize the key contributions of our work as follows:
095
096         1. We establish a connection between the reasoning process in CoTs and the Hopfieldian view
097            of cognition, grounded in cognitive neuroscience, to identify the key factors driving CoT’s
098            success in zero-shot and few-shot settings. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first
099            known attempt to leverage cognitive science for CoT interpretability by associating its core
100            elements with the Hopfieldian framework.
101         2. Based on these connections, we leverage the strength of the Hopfieldian view to understand
102            and further improve CoTs. We first consider how to localize the reasoning error based
103            on the low-dimensional representation spaces. Then, by leveraging the robustness of the
104            Hopfieldian view, we propose a new framework, RoT, to enhance the robustness of CoTs’
105
               performance.
106         3. Comprehensive experiments on three tasks, including arithmetic reasoning, commonsense
107            reasoning, and symbolic reasoning, reveal that our framework can provide intuitive and
               interpretable analysis, allowing error tracing and control for CoT reasoning.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Chain-of-Thought (CoT). The CoT is a prompting technique that engages LLMs in step-by-step
111
      reasoning rather than directly providing the answers (Nye et al., 2021). Studies have shown that
      introducing intermediate steps or learning from demonstrations can significantly improve the rea-
112
      soning performance of LLMs (Wei et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022). Given the success of CoT,
113
      numerous studies have explored its application to a variety of complex problems, including arith-
114   metic, commonsense, symbolic reasoning (Wang et al., 2023c; Zhou et al., 2023; Wang & Zhou,
115   2024), and logic tasks (Creswell & Shanahan, 2022; Pan et al., 2023; Weng et al., 2023). Recently,
116   numerous endeavors have been made to enhance the reasoning capabilities in LLMs (Wang et al.,
117   2023a; Dutta et al., 2024). For example, Kim et al. (2023) proposed a query-based approach to cor-
118   rect erroneous reasoning steps within a CoT. Zhao et al. (2023) introduced a knowledge-enhanced
119   method to improve the factual correctness for multi-pole open-domain QA tasks. Lyu et al. (2023)
120   developed “faithful CoT”, i.e., a framework that first translates natural language queries into sym-
121   bolic reasoning chains and then solves the problem using CoT. Additionally, several studies have
122
      also focused on the sequence and quantity of demonstrations within the context, investigating their
      contributions to the final reasoning performance. For this, Min et al. (2022) discovered that even
123
      random labels or ineffective reasoning steps can still improve the model’s reasoning performance.
124
      Lanham et al. (2023) demonstrated the impact of intervening in the CoT process by adding mistakes
125   or paraphrases. Pfau et al. (2024) showed that using meaningless filler tokens in place of a chain-of-
126   thought can surprisingly boost reasoning performance. However, these studies primarily focused on
127   how to improve the CoT’s reasoning performance and do not provide a framework to analyze the fun-
128   damental reasons, i.e., how does the reasoning capability emerge through CoT? Dutta et al. (2024)
129   investigates the neural sub-structures within LLMs that manifest Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning
130   on the Llama-2-7B model. Similarly, Rai & Yao (2024) explores neurons in the feed-forward layers
131   of LLMs to analyze their arithmetic reasoning capabilities on the Llama-2-7B model. Both studies
132   are grounded in the Sherringtonian view of neural activity. In contrast, we adopt the Hopfieldian
133
      perspective to bridge this gap, focusing on representations rather than individual neurons. We apply
      our approach across three different downstream tasks and can further extend our analysis to larger
134
      models like Llama-2-70B.
135
136   Interpretability of LLMs. Interpretability plays a key role in a deeper understanding of LLMs
137   to identify potential risks and better meet human requirements (Zou et al., 2023). Common inter-
138   pretability strategies include (i) Salience maps, which rely on highlighting the regions in the input
139
      that are attended by the model (Simonyan et al., 2014; Smilkov et al., 2017; Clark et al., 2019;
      Hu et al., 2023c;b; Lai et al., 2024); (ii) Feature visualization, which creates representative inputs
140
      indicative of particular neurons’ activations (Szegedy et al., 2014; Nguyen et al., 2016; Fong &
141
      Vedaldi, 2018; Nguyen et al., 2019); and (iii) Mechanistic interpretability, which employs reverse-
142   engineering tools to explain networks based on circuits and node-to-node connections (Olah et al.,
143   2020; Olsson et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023b). However, these methods often require substantial
144   human intervention and are limited in terms of scalability or interpretability, especially for the large
145   language models (Fong & Vedaldi, 2018; Jain & Wallace, 2019; Hu et al., 2024). Thus, these meth-
146   ods cannot be directly used to interpret CoT reasoning. Additionally, most current approaches focus
147   on representation-level analysis without considering how these representations connect to concepts
148   learned during pre-training (Bricken et al., 2023; Templeton et al., 2024). Other works investigate
149   the localization and representation of concepts in the network (Kim et al., 2018; Li et al., 2024),
150
      linear classifier probing to uncover input properties (Belinkov, 2022), fact localization and edit-
      ing (Meng et al., 2022; Zhong et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2024a;b), concept erasure (Shao et al.,
151
      2023; Gandikota et al., 2023), and corrective analysis (Burns et al., 2023), etc. These observations
152
      are aligned with RepE (Zou et al., 2023), which emphasized the nearly linear nature of LLM repre-
153   sentations (Park et al., 2024). However, none of these approaches directly address the inner workings
154   of CoT reasoning. While recent work has begun exploring connections between LLM interpretabil-
155   ity and cognitive neuroscience (Vilas et al., 2024). However, it does not discuss the Hopfieldian view
156   and also does not discuss how to explain the reasoning process in CoTs via cognitive neuroscience.
157   Our work provides the first attempt to interpret CoT reasoning from the Hopfieldian perspective.

173   The Hopfieldian View. In cognitive neuroscience, two prominent perspectives aim to explain cog-
174   nition: the Sherringtonian view and the Hopfieldian view.1 The Hopfieldian view focuses on un-
175   derstanding behavior through computation and representation within neural spaces, rather than the
176   specific biological details of neurons, ion flows, or molecular interactions (Hopfield, 1982; 1984;
177
      Hopfield & Tank, 1986). It operates at a higher level of abstraction, emphasizing the role of repre-
      sentations and the computations performed on them.
178
179   This approach conceptualizes cognition as transformations between representation spaces. At the
180   implementation level, the collective activity of neurons is mapped onto a representation space, which
181   contains a low-dimensional representational manifold. Algorithmically, Hopfieldian computation
182   views these representation spaces as fundamental entities, with movements within or transformations
183
      between them as the central operations. The representations themselves are structured as basins
      of attraction within a state space, and while they are implemented by neural structures (whether
184
      individual neurons, neural populations, or other components), the focus is on the dynamics of the
185
      system rather than its specific biological mechanisms. Most Hopfieldian models, in practice, center
186   on the activity of neural populations.
187
188   A parameter space defines the dimensions of variation within these representational spaces, aligning
189
      with quality-space approaches from philosophy, where content is similarly structured. Computa-
      tions over these representations are understood as dynamic transformations between spaces or shifts
190
      within them, characterized by features like attractors, bifurcations, limit cycles, and trajectories.
191
      Ultimately, cognitive functions are realized through these dynamic movements within or between
192   representational spaces.
193
194   Linear Representations in Language Models. Recent investigations into the internal mechanics
195
      of LLMs have revealed intriguing properties of their learned representations. Park et al. (2024)
      posited that high-level semantic features such as gender or honesty could be linearly represented
196
      as directions within the model’s representation space. This can be illustrated by the well-known
197
      word analogy task using a word embedding model (Mikolov et al., 2013). By defining M(·) as a
198   function of extracting the representations of a given word by a word embedding model, the oper-
199   ation M(Spain) − M(Madrid) + M(Paris) often results in an output close to M(France), where
200   M(Spain) − M(Madrid) can be considered as the representation vector of the abstract “capital of”
201   feature in the embedding space. Concurrently, research on interpretable neurons (Dale et al., 2023;
202   Ortiz-Jiménez et al., 2023; Voita et al., 2024) has identified neurons that consistently activate for
203   specific input features or tasks, suggesting that these features may also be represented as directions
204   in the LLMs’ neuron space. For instance, Tigges et al. (2023) use the PCA vector between LLMs’
205   hidden states on instructions “positive” and “negative” to find the sentiment direction in LLMs. Ad-
206
      ditionally, recent works (Zou et al., 2023; Arditi et al., 2024) show the effectiveness of engineering
      on language models using these directions. For example, adding multiples of the “honesty” direction
207
      to some hidden states has been sufficient to make the model more honest and reduce hallucinations.

292   In this task, for a given query, we want to check if there are some reasoning errors in the response
293   by CoTs. If so, we aim to localize these errors. As in the Hopfieldian view, cognition occurs
294   within low-dimensional representation spaces. Reasoning errors can be identified by analyzing the
295   structure of these spaces, such as when certain directions Rk (representing specific cognitive factors)
296   are disproportionately activated or suppressed. This can help localize the source of the error within
297   the cognitive process. Motivated by this, we can leverage the internal structure of spaces we have
298
      learned via PCA to locate the reasoning error for a given query in CoTs.
299   Intuitively, since the reasoning occurs within these representation spaces, if there is a reasoning error
300   in the response, then during the reasoning process, some tokens make the activations (hidden states)
301   of the response far from the corresponding representation spaces. This is because if these activations
302   are far from the spaces, CoTs do not reason the corresponding “concepts” in the response. Motivated
303   by this, our idea is to iteratively check the tokens in the response to see whether they are far from
304
      the representation spaces.
305   Mathematically, for a given prompt T via CoT of query x with its response y = (y1 , y2 , · · · , ym ),
306   we will iteratively feed the prompt with a part of the response, i.e., Ti = T ⊕ y≤i , where ⊕ is the
307   string concatenation. If the activations of Ti−1 are close to while those of Ti are very far from the
308   representation spaces {Rk }k∈K in (2), then we can think the i-th token yi makes an reasoning error.

340   to enhance robustness since these spaces are the inherent entities in the reasoning process. After the
341   manipulation, the hidden states will be less dependent on the specific form of the prompt, query, and
342   stimuli but will be more dependent on the intrinsic entities of the reasoning task.
343
      Mathematically, for a given prompt T via CoTs of query x. By using a similar procedure as in the
344
      Neural Populations section, we can get its neural populations {hk (T )}k∈K . In RoT, motivated by
345
      (Zou et al., 2023; Arditi et al., 2024), we can manipulate them by injecting the directions of their
346   corresponding representation spaces to make them closer to these spaces:

353   By directly manipulating neural populations, RoT offers a more precise and interpretable method
354   for influencing the model’s output compared to traditional prompt engineering techniques. This
355   approach not only enhances control over the model’s behavior but also improves the transparency

       Utility Performance. We first consider the utility performance of RoT. As shown in Table 1, we
       can see that: 1) The original CoT performs unstable on different tasks. Generally speaking, CoTZ

       and CoTF appear better, but they are lower than Base in some datasets, such as the CSQA dataset

       in the zero-shot scenario, which is consistent with the observation in (Kojima et al., 2022). At the
451    same time, for few-shot, CoTF performs extremely poorly in the GSM8k dataset because Llama-
452    2-7B-Chat repeats the given demonstrations, resulting in a reduction in the number of valid tokens.
453    Compared to CoTs, our RoT performs strongly in generalization on these datasets but may have
454    lower accuracy in some cases. This is because, in RoT, we add additional directions to the hidden
455    states of the prompt. These manipulations will cause a loss of information regarding the original
456    query, making the accuracy lower. 2) In terms of different models, the Llama-3-8B-Instruct model
457    has been improved more significantly. For example, with Llama-2-7B-Chat as the backbone, RoTZ
458    is improved by only 0.23 and 0.33 compared with Base on the GSM8K and SVAMP datasets, re-
459
       spectively; with Llama-3-8B-Instruct, the improvements are 1.52 and 2.66, respectively. This is
       primarily because the model is trained on a larger corpus and has learned more knowledge, so the
460
       activations contain richer information and can better capture related representations.
461
462    Robustness Analysis. We also conducted experiments on robustness, and the results are shown in
463    Table 2 (more results are included in Appendix B.2). From this table, we can observe that RoT
464    demonstrates a remarkable advancement over CoT in terms of robustness. We found that CoT
465
       methods are very sensitive to prompt design and sometimes fail to output the corresponding response
       based on the given instruction. However, our RoT extracts more essential information from the
466
       representation engineering level, making it more adaptable to various prompts. Note that for Llama-
467
       3-8B-Instruct, there are two datasets (SVAMP and Coin Flip) that do not provide robust performance
468    gains. This is because Llama-3-8B-Instruct is a very strong model, while Coin Flip and SVAMP are
469    two relatively easy tasks (as can be seen from the Table 1, the accuracy of CoTs in the SVAMP
470    dataset is greater than 81%, and in the Coin Flip dataset is greater than 90%). These two factors may
471    cause it to over-capture too many irrelevant concepts from the stimuli, thus pointing to the wrong
472    reasoning direction.

       Results on Larger Models. To further demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach, we conduct

      6.3 A BLATION S TUDY
507   Number of Samples. We conducted an ablation study on how to select samples and how many
508
      samples N in the stimulus set for constructing neural populations are sufficient. For the sample
      selection strategy, we focus on two different strategies and evaluate these on the GSM8K dataset:
509
      1) Random strategy. We randomly select samples in the training dataset using three random seeds.
510
      2) Low Perplexity strategy. We select samples based on low perplexity. 3) High Perplexity strategy.
511   Similar to the low perplexity strategy, we select samples based on high perplexity. As shown in Ta-
512   ble 3, we can observe that the high perplexity strategy has better and more generalized performance.
513   This is because high perplexity usually means low confidence in LLMs. Therefore, if a question has
514   a higher perplexity, the question has more latent knowledge information.
515
      For the number of samples N , we consider the set N = {32, 64, 128, 256, 512} and calculate their
516
      average accuracy scores on the SVAMP dataset using three different seeds. From Figure 2b, we can
517   see that the performance is quite stable for different numbers of samples. However, there is still a
518   little decrease when N is large enough. This is because when N is large enough, the representation
519   spaces contain richer information. Thus, adding the directions in (4) will make the query lose more
520   of its query information, causing a lower accuracy.
521
      Number of Selected Layers. Here we study the effect of different numbers of selected layers |K|
522
      for neural populations. While LLMs have many layers, such as Llama-2-7B, which contains 32
523   layers, recent studies have shown that not all layers store important information about reasoning and
524   that this information is usually found in the last layers of the model (Fan et al., 2024; Cosentino &
525   Shekkizhar, 2024). Therefore, we consider the last L layers, where L = {1, 3, 5, 10, 15}.
526
      In this experiment, we evaluate it with three different seeds. Figure 2c displays the result of average
527
      accuracy scores on the SVAMP dataset. From this figure, we can see that the accuracy first increases
528
      and then shows a decreasing trend as the number of control layers increases. This is because when
529   the number of layers is very small, each manipulation will correct some of the reasoning errors.
530   However, in RoT we have to manipulate each activation in the layer of the set K, and each manip-
531   ulation will lose some information about the query. Thus, the accuracy decreases when the number
532   of layers is larger.

534   7   C ONCLUSION
535   In this paper, we proposed a novel framework to explain and understand the fundamental factors
536   behind CoT’s success. Specifically, we first connected CoT reasoning and the Hopfieldian view
537   of cognition in cognitive neuroscience. Then, we developed a method for localizing reasoning er-
538   rors and proposed the RoT framework to enhance the robustness of the reasoning process in CoTs.
539   Experimental results demonstrate that RoT improves the robustness and interpretability of CoT rea-
      soning while offering fine-grained control over the reasoning process.

811
812   The Sherringtonian View. Unlike the Hopfieldian perspective, the Sherringtonian view (Sherring-
813   ton, 1906; Barlow, 1953) of cognitive explanation emphasizes the importance of direct neuron-to-
814   neuron connections in the brain. This view posits that the primary explanation for cognition lies
815   in the specific interactions between neurons and the computations these neurons perform within
816   well-defined circuits (Mogenson, 2018).
817
      At an algorithmic level, the Sherringtonian view conceptualizes cognition as networks of nodes
818   (neurons) with weighted connections (synapses) between them. In this framework, neurons perform
819   distinct computational transformations on the signals they receive from other neurons in the network.
820   Cognitive processes are described by how individual neurons receive inputs, process these inputs
821   through neural transfer functions, and transmit the resulting signals to connected neurons. Thus,
822   cognition is explained through the computations occurring at the level of individual neurons and the
823   signal flow across their connections.
824
825   Zero-shot CoT. Zero-shot CoT is a simple but effective chain of thought (CoT) prompting ap-
826   proach proposed by Kojima et al. (2022). It allows language models to generate a step-by-step
827
      explanation or thought process to solve problems without requiring prior demonstrations or specific
      training by simply adding “Let’s think step by step” before each answer. Specifically, given a query
828
      q and a model M with weights θ, the generation process can be defined as:
829
830                                          R = arg max pr(R|q, t; θ)                                 (5)
831   where R is the response text of the model, which is generated from all potential responses R, and t
832   is a prompt text like “Let’s think sttep by step.”
833
834   Few-shot CoT. Similar to zero-shot CoT, few-shot CoT (Wei et al., 2022) is also a prompting tech-
835   nique that gives a few examples with step-by-step reasoning processes to stimulate the model’s rea-
836
      soning ability. Formally, given the query q and a set of demonstrations D = {(q̃1 , ã1 ), (q̃2 , ã2 ), . . . },
      our aim is to generate a response with intermediate reasoning steps:
837
838                                        R = arg max pr(R|D, q; θ)                                               (6)
