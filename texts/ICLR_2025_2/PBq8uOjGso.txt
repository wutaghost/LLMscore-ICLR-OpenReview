011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            To address the annotation burden in LiDAR-based 3D object detection, active
014            learning (AL) methods offer a promising solution. However, traditional ac-
015            tive learning approaches solely rely on a small amount of labeled data to train
016
               an initial model for data selection, overlooking the potential of leveraging the
               abundance of unlabeled data. Recently, attempts to integrate semi-supervised
017
               learning (SSL) into AL with the goal of leveraging unlabeled data have faced
018
               challenges in effectively resolving the conflict between the two paradigms, re-
019            sulting in less satisfactory performance. To tackle this conflict, we propose a
020            Bidirectional Collaborative Semi-Supervised Active Learning framework, dubbed
021            as BC-SSAL. Specifically, from the perspective of SSL, we propose a Collabora-
022            tive PseudoScene Pre-training (CPSP) method that effectively learns from unla-
023            beled data without introducing adverse effects. From the perspective of AL, we
024            design a Collaborative Active Learning (CAL) method tailored for outdoor Li-
025            DAR scenes, which complements the uncertainty and diversity methods by model
026            cascading, alleviating the dilemma of sampling rare classes. Extensive experi-
027
               ments conducted on KITTI and Waymo demonstrate the effectiveness of our BC-
               SSAL. Especially, on the KITTI dataset, utilizing only 2% labeled data, BC-SSAL
028
               can achieve comparable performance to the model trained on the full set.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Being a fundamental task in autonomous driving, LiDAR-based 3D object detection plays a crucial
034
      role in perceiving semantic and spatial clues, which recognizes and locates objects in 3D scenes
035
      based on input point clouds captured by LiDAR sensors. During the past few years, a large number
036   of efforts (Yan et al., 2018; Zhou & Tuzel, 2018; Lang et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2022; Yang et al.,
037   2020) have been made with the performance of major public benchmarks (Geiger et al., 2013; Caesar
038   et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2020) rapidly and consistently increasing. Unfortunately, current methods
039   are deep learning based, substantially dependent on labeled data. For instance, the Waymo dataset
040   (Sun et al., 2020) alone encompasses over 10 million ground-truth (GT) 3D boxes. The labor-
041   intensive and time-consuming nature of annotating extensive datasets creates a bottleneck, hindering
042   the advancement in this field.
043   Active learning (AL) (Haussmann et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Feng et al., 2019) offers a promising
044   solution to overcoming this drawback. It selects a small subset from all samples as the most in-
045   formative data to measure the benefits of a fully annotated dataset. By adaptively choosing “good”
046   samples to label, AL significantly reduces the burden of data acquisition and annotation and shows
047   the potential to facilitate LiDAR-based 3D object detection (Luo et al., 2023b;a; Jiang et al., 2022;
048   Schmidt et al., 2020).
049   The AL paradigm typically consists of three phases, i.e. (1) temporary model updating (TMU),
050   (2) unlabeled sample selecting (USS), and (3) final model delivering (FMD). In TMU, a temporary
051   model is built or enhanced with the set of available labeled data, which is further applied to generate
052   pseudo annotations; in USS, some unlabeled data are screened out according to certain criteria and
053   annotated by the temporary model obtained; and in FMD, the final model is output. In general,
      TMU and USS are jointly conducted for multiple iterations while FMD operates once in the end.

082
083   The traditional AL methods only make use of labeled data, as shown in Fig. 1 (a). Since the large
084   amount of unlabeled data conveys rich information, which helps better understand the distribution
085   of all data rather than that of labeled ones, overlooking them leaves much room for improvement.
086   With the progress achieved in semi-supervised learning (SSL), some preliminary attempts (Lyu et al.,
087   2023; Wang et al., 2023; Mi et al., 2022) have been made to integrate such techniques in AL, where
088   SSL contributes to the performance gain by assigning pseudo-labels to unlabeled data based on the
089   prediction of the model trained on label data (Zhao et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021). As depicted in
090   Fig. 1 (c), they employ SSL to strengthen both the temporary and final model in the TMU and USS
091   phases and demonstrate that the semi-supervised active learning (SSAL) paradigm is superior to
092   the traditional one (Fig. 1 (a)). However, the combination of SSL and AL is not as straightforward
093
      as they expect, in particular for the synergy of TMU and USS. As we know, uncertainty-based
      metrics are widely adopted in AL and the samples with higher uncertainties are more likely to be
094
      selected for annotation in USS. On the other side, the samples of higher uncertainties may suffer
095
      from low confidence scores due to the instability of SSL in TMU. In this case, a conflict arises,
096   where the assignment of incorrect pseudo labels to objects in SSL inevitably becomes a significant
097   source of noise and makes AL struggle to accurately assess their uncertainties. As this iterates for
098   multiple rounds, current SSAL is prone to converge with sub-optimal results, even inferior to that of
099   a degraded paradigm only applying SSL in FMD (Fig. 1 (b)).
100
      To tackle the conflict between SSL and AL, we propose a bidirectional collaborative semi-supervised
101
      active learning (BC-SSAL) framework. From the perspective of SSL, in TMU, we present a
102   method, namely collaborative pseudo-scene pre-training (CPSP), to effectively leverage unlabeled
103   data while bypassing the side effects aforementioned. The main idea is to selectively learn only
104   from confident objects. To this end, we generate pseudo-scenes of unlabeled data using confident
105   objects with the ones of high uncertainties excluded. Pre-training on these pseudo-scenes thus en-
106   sures that unconfident objects are not disturbed by their own pseudo-labels, which largely mitigates
107   the negative impact of mislabeling and noise. From the perspective of AL, considering that ob-
      jects in outdoor LiDAR scenes show a severely long-tailed distribution and many objects of different

108
      classes appear in the same scene, it is quite difficult for AL to sample objects of rare classes for an-
109   notation. Moreover, accurately counting objects is challenging, as some background regions may be
110   misclassified as unconfident objects, further complicating the balanced sampling of scenes in active
111   learning. Therefore, we design a collaborative active learning (CAL) method, which simultaneously
112   takes the uncertainty and diversity into account. In contrast to previous counterparts, CAL enhances
113   uncertainty estimation and class weighting by cascading the model trained on labeled data with the
114   one trained on labeled and unlabeled data. Finally, BC-SSAL collaboratively integrates AL and SSL
115   by bidirectionally adapting them to fit each other.
116   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
117
118          • We point out the conflict between AL and SSL and propose a novel SSAL framework to
119            address it, where the CPSP method is presented to effectively leverage unlabeled data to
120            facilitate model training.
121          • We design the CAL method tailored for outdoor LiDAR scenes, which complements the
122            uncertainty and diversity methods by model cascading, alleviating the dilemma of sampling
123            rare classes.
124          • We do extensive experiments on the KITTI and Waymo datasets and reach state-of-the-
125            art results. Especially, on KITTI, we use only 2% labeled data and achieve comparable
126            performance to the model trained on the full set.

      2.1   ACTIVE L EARNING
131
132   Active learning methods have gained significant attention in various domains to alleviate the labeling
133   burden. These methods can be broadly categorized into two main types: uncertainty-based (Houlsby
134   et al., 2011; Gal et al., 2017) and diversity-based approaches (Nguyen & Smeulders, 2004; Sener &
135   Savarese, 2017; Agarwal et al., 2020). Uncertainty-based methods leverage uncertainty to identify
136   informative samples for annotation while diversity-based methods prioritize capturing the diversity
137   and representativeness of the dataset. Furthermore, recent research (Huang et al., 2010; Ash et al.,
138   2019) has explored the integration of uncertainty-based and diversity-based approaches to leverage
139
      the advantages of both.
140   Recently, there has been increased interest in applying active learning to object detection tasks. Un-
141   like image classification, active learning for object detection presents unique challenges due to the
142   complexities of localizing and identifying objects within images. One approach, MI-AOD (Yuan
143   et al., 2021) treats unlabeled images as bags of instances, using adversarial classifiers to measure
144   uncertainty. AL-MDN (Choi et al., 2021) utilizes mixture density networks for probabilistic out-
145
      puts, while ENMS (Wu et al., 2022) applies entropy-based non-maximum suppression to assess
      uncertainty. PPAL (Yang et al., 2022) offers a plug-and-play active learning method.
146
147   However, active learning for LiDAR-based object detection needs further research due to the dif-
148   ferences between images and outdoor LiDAR scenes. Some recent studies have begun to tackle
149   this issue. For example, CRB (Luo et al., 2023b) focuses on filtering redundant 3D bounding box
150   labels based on conciseness, representativeness, and geometric balance. KECOR (Luo et al., 2023a)
151
      presents a novel strategy called kernel coding rate maximization to identify the most informative
      point clouds for labeling. However, these methods may struggle with class imbalance caused by
152
      long-tailed distributions in outdoor scenes and do not effectively utilize available unlabeled data.
153

158   by leveraging abundant unlabeled data. These methods can be integrated with active learning (AL)
159   to further optimize data annotation efforts (Elezi et al., 2022; Mi et al., 2022; Lyu et al., 2023; Wang
160   et al., 2023; Hwang et al., 2023). In most frameworks, SSL is employed for model pre-training
161   during the Temporary Model Updating (TMU) stage, after which AL identifies the most informative
      samples for annotation. However, many approaches overlook the potential conflicts between SSL

196   3.1   F RAMEWORK OVERVIEW
197
198   As illustrated in Fig. 2, our Bidirectional Collaborative Semi-Supervised Active Learning frame-
199   work (BC-SSAL) consists of three main components: Temporary Model Updating (TMU), Unla-
200   beled Sample Selecting (USS), and Final Model Delivering (FMD). In the TMU stage, we initiate
201
      the process with normal pre-training, where a small set of randomly sampled data is used to train the
      initial model. Subsequently, our Collaborative PseudoScene Pre-training tailored for active learn-
202
      ing is performed, creating pseudo scenes with confident boxes to enhance the model performance.
203
      In the USS stage, we employ the innovative Collaborative Active Learning method, which entails
204   the strategic selection of informative data from the unlabeled pool, empowering the model to con-
205   centrate on challenging instances. In the FMD stage, semi-supervised learning is conducted to
206   further refine the model performance, which utilizes both labeled and unlabeled data to train the
207   model, capitalizing on insights gained from the active learning process. It’s important to note that
208   our framework is compatible with various existing semi-supervised methods, providing flexibility
209   in choosing the most suitable approach.
210
211   3.2   C OLLABORATIVE P SEUDO S CENE P RE - TRAINING
212
213   To meet the requirements of active learning, we propose the Collaborative PseudoScene Pre-
214   training(CPSP) approach, which is specifically designed to support active learning by creating
215   pseudo scenes that focus on confident objects while excluding unconfident boxes. The entire process
      is illustrated in Fig. 3.

237   3.2.1 C ONFIDENT O BJECT E XTRACTION
238
239   To optimize the extraction of confident objects for model pre-training, we employ a multi-step ap-
240   proach that enhances the quality of our training data. Initially, we utilize a Confident Object Filtering
241
      module to extract confident objects from unlabeled scenes, providing crucial information for model
      training. These extracted objects are then stored in a box bank to preserve and manage the collected
242
      object information. Furthermore, we incorporate an Iterative Refinement mechanism that iteratively
243
      generates confident boxes from the unlabeled data, integrating them into the box bank to create
244   high-quality pseudo labels for the models. This process is essential for improving the robustness
245   and accuracy of the model. Please see appendix B.1 for more details.
246
247
      Confident Object Filtering. To ensure compatibility with active learning(AL), we utilize the same
      uncertainty measure employed in the AL process. By applying this uncertainty measure to the ob-
248
      jects, we collect their uncertainty scores and employ clustering techniques on these scores to identify
249
      the group with the lowest uncertainty scores. After that, we filter the objects within this group to se-
250   lect confident objects. The object information is represented as O = {cls, loc, score, sceneid , pc},
251   where cls denotes the class labels, loc ∈ R7 represents the object location with orientation,
252   score ∈ R1 indicates the uncertainty score, sceneid corresponds to the scene index to which the
253   object belongs, and pc ∈ Rn×3 captures the point clouds of the object. Additionally, we extract
254   some backgrounds that are likely to be false positives, similar to the approach in (Oh et al., 2024).
255   All extracted object information is stored in a box bank for easy access and management.
256
257   Iterative Refinement To continuously improve the box bank, we implement a mechanism that
258   adds newly extracted confident objects. When a new object overlaps with an existing one, its un-
259
      certainty scores are compared, and the object with the lower score is retained, ensuring that only
      higher-confidence objects are prioritized for further processing. However, even with low uncertainty
260
      scores, errors may still occur, potentially impacting uncertainty estimation during active learning.
261
      To mitigate this, we introduce a deletion mechanism: after each training iteration and the extraction
262   of new confident boxes, the newly added boxes are compared with the existing ones to identify and
263   remove any mislabeled or erroneous entries. This iterative process refines the box bank, improving
264   its overall quality and ensuring more reliable uncertainty estimation.
265
      3.2.2 P SEUDO S CENE F ORMATION
266
267   To enhance the model’s focus on confident objects while minimizing the impact of unconfident ones,
268   we utilize the Reliable Background Mining Module from (Liu et al., 2022). Moreover, we establish
269   a relatively high threshold to preserve backgrounds prone to being falsely identified as positives.
      This module effectively removes point clouds linked to predicted boxes from unlabeled scenes,

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      accounting for the sparse nature of point cloud data. By leveraging these ”background” scenes, we
271   create a contextual learning environment for the model to concentrate on relevant objects. Next,
272   we construct Pseudo Scenes by merging point clouds from selected boxes in the box bank with
273   these background scenes. These Pseudo Scenes consist solely of confident objects, excluding any
274   unconfident ones from the training data. This strategy ensures that the pre-training data is tailored
275   to enhance the model’s ability to make stable and reliable predictions, providing a robust foundation
276   for the active learning process.

278   3.3     C OLLABORATIVE ACTIVE L EARNING
279
280   To efficiently identify the most informative samples and achieve better collaboration with the semi-
281
      supervised pre-training stage, we propose a novel Collaborative Active Learning (CAL) approach,
      which simultaneously incorporates considerations of uncertainty and diversity. For uncertainty, we
282
      devise Ensemble-based Entropy Uncertainty (E 2 U nc). In terms of diversity, our approach includes
283
      Box-level Diversity (B Div) and Class Balance Sampling (CBS). A more detailed algorithm is
284   provided in Appendix B.2.
285
      3.3.1 E NSEMBLE - BASED E NTROPY U NCERTAINTY
286
287   We use entropy to measure the uncertainty of each predicted box. Considering the collective influ-
288   ence of all the boxes, the overall uncertainty of the entire scene is represented by calculating the
289   average entropy. This approach enables us to capture the overall uncertainty and make informed
290   decisions based on the entropy measure.
291   Specifically, the uncertainty for a point cloud scene S is computed as:
292
293                                         \begin {aligned} H(S) = \frac {\sum _{b\in S}\sum _{c\in C}({-p_{bc}\log p_{bc}})}{N_b\times |C|} \label {al:entropy} \end {aligned}    (1)
294
295   where b represents the predicted boxes, pbc is the predicted class probability of class c for box b, Nb
296
      is the total number of predicted boxes, and |C| is the number of object classes.
297   We observe that the CPSP pre-trained model can overlook some original correct and confident ob-
298   jects. To address this, we propose an ensemble strategy that combines high-confidence predictions
299   from the normal pre-trained model with all boxes from our CPSP pre-trained model. We then apply
300   the Non-Maximum Suppression (NMS) technique to eliminate redundant boxes.
301

304   the similarity between boxes using cosine similarity and assigning each box to its closest counterpart.
305   The similarity score for each scene is computed by averaging the cosine similarity between box

307
      Formally, let Sa be a scene with box features Fa = {fa,i | boxai ∈ Sa }, and S = {Sc } be the set

      of selected scenes with features F = {fi | boxi ∈ Sc }. We calculate the similarity of scene Sa as:

      3.3.3 C LASS BALANCE S AMPLING
318   Outdoor LiDAR scenes present significant challenges due to the presence of rare classes, which are
319   difficult to sample and annotate. Annotating these rare classes becomes disproportionately expensive
320   due to their limited representation, especially when they coexist with more frequent classes in the
321   same scene. Additionally, many existing methods (Luo et al., 2023b; Wu et al., 2022; Yang et al.,
322   2022; Luo et al., 2023a) fail to account for the fact that models often struggle to accurately estimate
323   the number of objects in complex outdoor scenes, leading to a higher rate of false positives (FP) and
      false negatives (FN). To address this, we only consider boxes that are predicted by both the normal


                                                                                                                                 6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      pre-trained model and the CPSP pre-trained model. This intersection is more likely to represent real
325   objects, filtering out background noise and reducing false positives.
326
327   Moreover, to handle class imbalance, we propose a class balance algorithm that sets an upper limit
328
      for the number of objects per class. If the number of objects in a class exceeds this limit, the weight
      assigned to that class is reduced. This encourages the sampling of unconfident objects from other
329
      classes in subsequent iterations, addressing the issue of class imbalance. As a result, our algorithm
330
      ensures more effective and representative sampling, improving the overall training process. More
331   details are provided in Appendix B.2.

      4.1     DATASETS AND I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS

338   4.1.1    KITTI DATASET
339
340   We conducted evaluations of our methods on the KITTI 3D detection benchmark (Geiger et al.,
341   2013), using the standard train split comprising of 3,712 samples and the validation split contain-
342   ing 3,769 samples (Shi et al., 2020). In our semi-supervised active learning framework, we initially
343   trained an initial model using randomly selected frames consisting of approximately 200 boxes. Sub-
344   sequently, we leveraged the remaining unlabeled training data for further model refinement. During
345
      active learning, we specifically selected frames that contained around 150 boxes for effective train-
      ing. The total number of labeled boxes in our approach is approximately 350 boxes, which accounts
346
      for less than 2% of the total boxes present in the KITTI train split. Additionally, we excluded scenes
347
      with ”Dontcares” as they may introduce noise and potentially affect the performance of active learn-
348   ing methods. For evaluation, we calculate the mean average precision (mAP) at 40 recall positions
349   for the Car, Pedestrian (Ped), and Cyclist (Cyc), employing 3D IoU thresholds of 0.7, 0.5, and 0.5,
350   respectively, across different difficulty levels: easy, moderate (mod), and hard.
351
352   4.1.2    WAYMO DATASET
353
354
      We conducted evaluations of our methods on the Waymo dataset (Sun et al., 2020), a widely
      used benchmark in autonomous driving. It offers diverse real-world driving scenarios with high-
355
      resolution sensor data. The dataset comprises 798 training sequences and 202 validation sequences.
356
      Notably, the annotations provide a full 360° field of view. Additionally, the prediction results are
357   categorized into LEVEL 1 and LEVEL 2 for 3D objects based on the presence of more than five
358   LiDAR points and one LiDAR point, respectively. To optimize efficiency, we adopted a time-saving
359   approach by setting a sample interval of 20 from the training set to generate a pool of frames. From
360   this pool, we selected frames for our divided datasets. Similar to our approach in the KITTI dataset,
361   we employed a similar strategy for the Waymo dataset. In the initial stage, we randomly sampled
362   frames with approximately 5000 boxes, and in the active learning stage, we again selected frames
363   with around 5000 boxes. The total number of boxes, which amounts to 10,000, is less than 1% of the
364   total boxes present in the Waymo train set. For evaluation, we use mean average precision (mAP)
365
      for Vehicle (Veh), Pedestrian (Ped), and Cyclist (Cyc) in LEVEL 1 (L1) and LEVEL 2 (L2), along
      with average mAP and heading accuracy weighted AP (mAPH).
366

407
      our CAL. For the final model delivering stage, we leverage HSSDA (Liu et al., 2023) due to its

408
      three times with different seeds and averaged the performance across them.
409
410   As shown in Table 1, our BC-SSAL framework outperforms all other approaches in average mAP,
411   with notable improvements in challenging classes such as Pedestrian and Cyclist. When comparing
412   pre-training methods while keeping the AL methods fixed, our CPSP pre-training consistently deliv-
413
      ers superior performance. Notably, traditional SSL approaches negatively impact AL performance,
      with results declining regardless of whether 3DIoUMatch or state-of-the-art HSSDA is used. In
414
      contrast, CPSP pre-training enhances the performance of nearly all AL methods. When pre-training
415
      methods are fixed and AL methods are varied, our CAL method demonstrates superior performance
416   across different pre-training methods, showing its effectiveness regardless of the pre-training method
417   employed.

      Table 3 demonstrates the results of our ablation study. It indicates that both the introduction of the

      pseudo scene and the iterative refinement mechanism contribute positively to the model’s perfor-

464   challenging classes such as Pedestrian and Cyclist, where we achieve gains exceeding 10% in mAP.
465   Besides, these enhancements carry over to the final model delivering stage, resulting in higher over-

467   challenging classes, with an improvement of 1% in mAP for pedestrians and 3% for cyclists.

469
470   The ablation study, as depicted in Table 4, emphasizes the importance of the uncertainty measure,
471
      class balance methods, and diversity methods in CAL for achieving improved performance. The
      E 2 U nc plays a crucial role in active learning by selecting informative samples. This selection
472
      process enables the model to focus on challenging instances, leading to an overall mAP improve-
473
      ment of 4.8%. CBS contributes significantly to addressing class imbalances, particularly in hard
474   classes like Pedestrian(2.3% mAP improvement) and Cyclist(2.6 % mAP improvement), resulting
475   in enhanced performance in these challenging scenarios. Additionally, B Div helps reduce redun-
476   dancy in the selected samples, enabling the model to capture a broader range of object variations
477   and further improve its detection capabilities. By incorporating these CAL components, the overall
478   semi-supervised active learning framework becomes more effective, leading to better performance
479   in 3D object detection.

531   5     C ONCLUSION
532
533   In this paper, we propose a Bidirectional Collaborative Semi-Supervised Active Learning frame-
534   work, dubbed as BC-SSAL, which consists of Collaborative PseudoScene Pre-training (CPSP) and
535   Collaborative Active Learning (CAL), effectively addressing the conflicts between semi-supervised
536   learning and active learning. CPSP utilizes pseudo scenes with confident boxes for model pre-
537   training, while CAL maximizes the benefits of the CPSP pre-trained model to select superior
538   samples. Experimental results on KITTI and Waymo datasets demonstrate that our approach
539   achieves state-of-the-art performance, offering a promising solution for improving 3D object de-
      tection through effective integration of semi-supervised and active learning.

726   B.1   C OLLABORATIVE P SEUDO S CENE P RE - TRAINING
727
728   In this section, we delve into the details of extracting confident objects during the Collaborative
729   PseudoScene Pre-training.
730
731   More details about Confident Object Extraction To filter confident objects, previous SSL meth-
732
      ods often use fixed thresholds or top-k selections. However, we observed that score distributions vary
      significantly across different classes and models, making it challenging to determine an appropriate
733
      class-specific threshold. Additionally, the performance of different models can fluctuate, complicat-
734
      ing the selection of a consistent top-k value—if k is too small, the number of extracted objects may
735   be insufficient, while a larger k introduces more noise. To address this, we employ clustering meth-
736   ods (Liu et al., 2023), such as KMeans, on uncertainty scores to select confident objects. Clustering
737   allows objects within the same group to share similar patterns, and by choosing a relatively higher
738   number of centers, we improve the reliability of confident object selection.
739
      Besides, to continuously improve the box bank, we implement an iterative refinement mechanism
740
      that selectively incorporates newly extracted confident objects. Let Onew represent the set of newly
741   extracted objects and Obank represent the set of objects already stored in the box bank. For each new
742   object onew ∈ Onew , if it overlaps with an existing object obank ∈ Obank , we compare their uncertainty
743   scores, denoted as U (onew ) and U (obank ) respectively. The object with the lower uncertainty score is
744   retained:
745
                                      o_{\text {retain}} = \begin {cases} o_{\text {new}}, & \text {if } U(o_{\text {new}}) < U(o_{\text {bank}}) \\ o_{\text {bank}}, & \text {otherwise} \end {cases}    (3)
746
747
      This ensures that only higher-confidence objects are kept in the box bank, minimizing the risk of
748   introducing noisy or uncertain objects into the training data.
749
750   Even though the retained objects have lower uncertainty scores, errors can still occur during the
751
      uncertainty estimation, potentially leading to the inclusion of mislabeled or erroneous objects. To
      address this issue, we introduce a deletion mechanism. After each training iteration, we extract new
752
      confident boxes and compare them to existing boxes in the bank. The deletion mechanism checks for
753
      discrepancies between newly extracted objects onew and existing objects obank . If the overlap between
754   two objects exceeds a certain threshold τoverlap , defined as the Intersection over Union (IoU):
755
                                                                                \text {IoU}(o_{\text {new}}, o_{\text {bank}}) > \tau _{\text {overlap}},                                                  (4)

756
      we retain the object with the lower uncertainty score and delete the other. Additionally, mislabeled or
757   erroneous objects are identified and removed by evaluating their performance in subsequent training
758   iterations.
759
760
      This iterative process of adding, comparing, and refining the box bank improves its overall quality,
      ensuring that the uncertainty estimation becomes more reliable over time. Mathematically, this can
761
      be formalized as:
762
                                          O_{\text {bank}}^{t+1} = \left ( O_{\text {bank}}^{t} \setminus O_{\text {remove}} \right ) \cup O_{\text {new}},  (5)

      where Obank   is the set of objects in the box bank at iteration t, and Oremove is the set of objects
765   identified for deletion based on the comparison with Onew . The iterative refinement ensures that
766   Obank evolves to contain higher-quality pseudo-labels for training.

768   B.2       C OLLABORATIVE ACTIVE L EARNING
769
770   In this part, we provide more details about the Class Balance Sampling. We also present the com-
771   pleted pseudo-code for our active learning process, as shown in Algorithm 1.
772
773   More details about Class Balance Sampling Outdoor LiDAR scenes often present challenges
774   due to the class imbalance, where some classes, such as cyclists, are rare compared to more frequent
775   classes like cars. These rare classes are difficult to sample and annotate effectively. To address this,
776   we propose a class balance sampling algorithm that adjusts the number of samples for each class
777
      based on its difficulty and co-occurrence patterns.
778   Class Weight Calculation: For each class  c , we compute the average uncertainty  u_c  of all predicted
779   boxes from the unlabeled data. The uncertainty of a class reflects the model’s performance on that
780   class, with higher uncertainty suggesting more difficulty in correctly predicting objects of that class.
781   Using this uncertainty, we assign a class weight  w_c  that prioritizes classes with higher uncertainty,
782   ensuring that more challenging classes receive more attention during the sampling process. The
783   class weight  w_c  is computed as follows:
784
785
786                                                                   w_c = \frac {\sqrt {1/u_c}}{\sum _{c}{\sqrt {1/u_c}}}, \label {al:cbs2}              (6)
787
788
789   where the reciprocal square root of the uncertainty is taken to ensure that more uncertain classes
790
      receive higher weights. This weight is then normalized across all classes. The goal of this weighting
      is to prioritize the selection of challenging classes while maintaining a balance across the dataset.
791
792   Class Co-occurrence Patterns: In outdoor scenes, multiple object classes often appear together.
793   Therefore, simply assigning weights based on uncertainty may not fully address the issue of class
794   imbalance, especially when considering the co-occurrence of objects from different classes in the
795   same scene.
796   To handle this, we record the co-occurrence patterns between classes. For example, if a scene
797   contains a car, we examine the average frequency of other classes (e.g., cyclists) appearing alongside
798   it. This co-occurrence data is stored in a co-occurrence matrix  A , where each entry  A_{ij}  represents
799   the likelihood of class  j  appearing in a scene when class  i  is present.
800   Let’s denote  A  as a  C \times C  matrix, where  C  is the number of classes. Each row corresponds to a
801   specific class and indicates the average appearance of other classes when that class is present. The
802   diagonal elements of  A  are normalized to 1 to indicate the expected number of objects for that class
803   when it is present.
804
      Using this co-occurrence matrix, we compute the desired upper limits  U  for the number of objects
805
      to sample from each class. We want these upper limits to reflect the desired sampling ratio while
806   considering the class co-occurrence patterns and uncertainty-based weights. This can be expressed
807   by the following equation:
808
809
                                                                            AU = W \times B, \label {al:cbs3}                                              (7)

848   Handling Small or Negative Upper Limits: One challenge we might encounter when solving for
849   the upper limits  U  is that some values could be unrealistically small or even negative, which would
850   not be appropriate for a balanced sampling strategy. To address this, we impose a constraint that
851   ensures each upper limit  U_c  is greater than or equal to a minimum threshold, such as  B / 10 , where
852
       B  is the total number of samples to be selected.
853   Once the upper limits are determined, we apply these limits during the sampling process. If the
854   number of selected samples for a particular class exceeds its upper limit, the uncertainty scores for
855   that class are decreased accordingly, ensuring that the algorithm maintains a balanced representation
856   of all classes.
857   This approach ensures that rare or difficult classes are not underrepresented, while also preventing
858   the model from over-sampling classes that are easier to detect.
859
860
861   The Pipeline of Collaborative Active Learning The pipeline of collaborative active learning is
862   demonstrated in Algorithm 1. First, we calculate the uncertainty of all unlabeled data and sort them
863   in descending order. Next, we adopt a greedy approach to select the data with the highest uncertainty.
      Throughout this process, we discard samples that are similar to the ones already selected to ensure

      diversity. Moreover, we incorporate class balance considerations by adjusting the uncertainty of a
865   class when it reaches its upper limit.
866
867   By following this pipeline, we systematically evaluate uncertainty, prioritize the most unconfident
868
      samples, promote diversity by excluding similar data points, and account for class balance by ad-
      justing uncertainty based on upper limits.
869
870
      The Pipeline of Collaborative Active Learning The pipeline for Collaborative Active Learning
871
      (CAL) is outlined in Algorithm 1. The algorithm operates in the following steps:
872
873         1. Uncertainty Calculation: For each sample in the unlabeled dataset, we compute the un-
874            certainty score using our Ensemble-based Entropy Uncertainty (E 2 U nc) method. This
875            uncertainty quantifies how confident the model is about each prediction.
876
            2. Sorting by Uncertainty: The unlabeled data is sorted in descending order of uncertainty,
877            so that the most uncertain samples are prioritized for selection.
878
            3. Diversity Enforcement: To maintain diversity among the selected samples, we calcu-
879
               late the similarity between each new candidate and the previously selected samples using
880
               Box-level Diversity (B Div). If the similarity exceeds a predefined threshold Tsim , the
881            candidate sample is discarded to avoid redundancy.
882
            4. Class Balance Adjustment: During the selection process, we also enforce class balance by
883
               setting upper limits on the number of objects that can be selected from each class. If a class
884
               exceeds its upper limit U (c), we adjust the uncertainty scores for that class by applying a
885            weight factor S(c). This recalibration ensures that we avoid over-sampling from any single
886            class.
887
            5. Greedy Selection: The algorithm proceeds in a greedy manner, selecting the most uncer-
888
               tain and diverse samples until the budget b is reached.
889
890   By following this pipeline, our method efficiently selects the most informative samples for label-
891   ing, ensuring both diversity and class balance while focusing on high-uncertainty data points. This
892   balanced approach improves the effectiveness of active learning, reducing redundancy and ensuring
893   that the model is trained on representative and diverse data.

      In the Confident Object Extraction module, we use K-Means to do clustering and set the number of
898   centers as 20 for the KITTI dataset and 50 for the Waymo dataset. The similarity threshold (Tsim )
899   in the B Div module is set to 0.9. It is worth noting that due to the limited amount of data utilized,

901   allowing the model to train for a longer duration. In order to achieve this, we repeat the data length
902   5 times for the KITTI dataset and 15 times for the Waymo dataset. Other basic settings like learning
903   rate, optimizer, and scheduler are following (Shi et al., 2020; Team, 2020).
904   Furthermore, when the normal pre-trained model undergoes the Collaborative Active Learning
905   (CAL) module, we utilize the upper limits calculated by the CPSP pre-trained model to ensure a
906   fair comparison. By incorporating these strategies, we can effectively evaluate the performance of
907   the normal pre-trained model in comparison to the CPSP pre-trained model. This ensures a reliable
908   and comprehensive assessment of the effectiveness of our proposed method.

988    CPSP especially effective for active learning, where selecting informative samples from uncertain
989    predictions is crucial for optimizing model performance with limited data.

       E.2   N EGATIVE E FFECTS OF T RAINING ON U NCONFIDENT O BJECTS
1017
       Confident objects provide crucial information with minimal noise, while unconfident ones introduce
1018
       numerous incorrect pseudo-labels that mislead the model. We utilize two types of pre-training in
1019
       our approach CPSP: UNC (Unconfident) and CON (Confident). In UNC pre-training, objects with a
1020   low number of clustering centers (2) are filtered, allowing us to include more unconfident objects in
1021   the model training process. In CON pre-training, we train confident objects using the same methods
1022   but with a higher number of clustering centers (20). This approach allows us to focus on objects
1023   that exhibit a higher level of certainty and reliability during the training process. Fig. G depicts
1024   the comparison between UNC and CON on the KITTI dataset. UNC is adversely affected by false
1025   positives, degrading uncertainty assessment, and yielding inferior results (69.2%). In contrast, CON
       achieves a higher score of 70.5%.
