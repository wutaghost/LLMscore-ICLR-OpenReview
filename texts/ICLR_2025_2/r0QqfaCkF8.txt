009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Molecular conformer generation is a fundamental challenge in computational
012            chemistry, particularly for large and complex molecules. In this work, we pro-
013            pose a novel approach called Fragment-Augmented Diffusion (FADiff), which
014            integrates molecular fragmentations into diffusion models as a data augmenta-
015            tion strategy to enhance molecular conformation generation. By decomposing
016
               molecules into smaller, manageable fragments for the purpose of data augmenta-
               tion, FADiff enhances the diffusion generation process, effectively capturing local
017
               structural variations while preserving the integrity of the entire molecule. Ex-
018
               tensive experiments across multiple datasets demonstrate that FADiff consistently
019            outperforms state-of-the-art methods, particularly in data-scarce scenarios, where
020            the fragment-based augmentation approach significantly enhances model perfor-
021            mance. We also provide a comprehensive analysis of different fragmentation
022            rules and their impact on model performance, and theoretically validate FADiff’s
023            effectiveness in improving generalization. Overall, FADiff advances molecular
024            conformation generation by enhancing the exploration of conformational space,
025            offering a powerful tool for computational chemistry. The code is available at
026            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/fragaug-5960/.

096   Diffusion-based Molecular Generation Diffusion models have gained significant attraction as
097   powerful tools for drug discovery applications (Xu et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2024; Hua et al., 2024).
098   These methods typically start by transitioning from stable equilibrium conformations to a state of
099
      heightened disorder via a sequence of regulated diffusion steps (Yang et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024;
      Cao et al., 2024), with a model trained to reverse this process (Xu et al., 2022). Recent methods
100
      for molecular conformation generation model directly in 3D Euclidean space, employing equivari-
101
      ant graph neural networks within diffusion models to process atomic coordinates and features (Xu
102   et al., 2022; Hoogeboom et al., 2022). These approaches inject Gaussian noise into all spatial co-
103   ordinates, requiring numerous denoising steps (Shi et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022).
104   Recognizing that molecular flexibility arises primarily from torsional degrees of freedom (Axelrod &
105   Gomez-Bombarelli, 2022; Jing et al., 2022) proposes Torsional Diffusion, wherein the diffusion pro-
106   cess acts only on torsion angles while keeping other degrees of freedom fixed. Focusing on torsion
107   angles reduces the dimensionality of the sample space, leading to more efficient and effective con-
      former generation. It leverages torsion angles to model the potential energy surface, a fundamental


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      component in computational chemistry (Kang et al., 1996), by combining diffusion processes with
109   detailed torsional angle modeling to enhance both accuracy and efficiency in conformer generation.
110
111   Molecular Fragment Decomposition Molecular fragment decomposition is a critical concept in
112   computational chemistry, enabling the simplification of complex molecular structures into smaller
113   and more manageable units (Hann et al., 2001; Sliwoski et al., 2014; Sadybekov & Katritch, 2023).
114   This approach facilitates the study of molecular properties and interactions by focusing on individual
115   fragments that retain key chemical characteristics of the parent molecule (Bemis & Murcko, 1996;
116   Jinsong et al., 2024). From a force field perspective, by preserving the local chemical environment
117   around targeted torsions, fragmentation allows for accurate modeling of torsional potentials, ensur-
118   ing that torsional characteristics can be effectively transferred back to the parent molecule (Horton
119
      et al., 2022; D’Amore et al., 2022). Fragmentation rules, such as BRICS (Breaking of Retrosyn-
      thetically Interesting Chemical Substructures), allow for the systematic breakdown of molecules
120
      based on chemically meaningful bonds, preserving functional groups that are essential for chemical
121
      activity (Lewell et al., 1998; Degen et al., 2008). This decomposition not only aids in understand-
122   ing the intrinsic properties of molecular subunits but also enhances computational efficiency by
123   reducing the complexity of conformational space (Liu et al., 2017). By analyzing these fragments,
124   researchers can predict reactivity, optimize drug design, and explore novel chemical spaces with
125   higher precision (Gordon et al., 2012). The integration of molecular fragment decomposition with
126   advanced modeling techniques, such as torsional diffusion, offers a powerful framework for gener-
127   ating accurate and diverse molecular conformers, ultimately advancing the fields of drug discovery
128   and materials science (Jinsong et al., 2024).

132   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
133
134   Notations and Problem Formulations Each molecule with n atoms is represented as an undi-
135   rected graph G = (V, E), where V = {vi }ni=1 represents the atoms and E = {ei,j | (i, j) ⊆ V × V}
136   represents the bonds between atoms. Each node vi describes atomic attributes, such as element type,
137   and each edge ei,j describes the bond between vi and vj , labeled with its chemical type. The goal of
138
      molecular conformation generation is to generate stable conformations for a given molecular graph
      G. While conformations C can be described by atomic positions, it is often more efficient to use
139
      internal coordinates like bond lengths, bond angles, and torsion angles. Torsion angles are partic-
140
      ularly important as they capture the rotations around rotatable bonds, which define the molecule’s
141   flexibility. Each rotatable bond introduces a degree of freedom, corresponding to a torsion an-
142   gle. By focusing on torsion angles, we reduce the problem’s dimensionality while preserving the
143   molecule’s conformational flexibility. Thus, conformations C are represented as a set of torsion
144   angles τ = {τ i }mi=1 , where m is the number of rotatable bonds. These torsion angles define the 3D
145   structure in torsional space Tm . For each molecular graph G, its conformations C are treated as i.i.d.
146   samples from an underlying Boltzmann distribution.
147
148   Torsion Computation Basics Directly learning a score model over intrinsic torsion coordinates
149   presents several challenges. First, the dimensionality of the torsional space depends on the molecu-
150   lar graph G, and the mapping from torsional space to conformers is influenced by both G and local
151
      structures L. Additionally, there is no canonical way to define torsion angles, as they depend on
      arbitrary choices of reference neighbors. To address these issues, (Jing et al., 2022) represent con-
152
      formers as 3D point clouds in extrinsic coordinates, which are invariant to global roto-translation.
153
      This allows us to construct a score model sθ (C, t) that operates over 3D conformers while predicting
154   updates in the torsional space. By applying torsion updates directly to the 3D coordinates, we avoid
155   the need to define reference neighbors, ensuring invariance to such choices (Quack, 2002). Further-
156   more, the model must respect parity equivariance, meaning the learned score function must change
157   sign under parity inversion, ensuring that the model outputs pseudoscalars that are invariant under
158   SE(3) transformations but change sign when the input point cloud is inverted (Jing et al., 2022).
159   More details on torsion angle invariance and parity equivariance can be found in the Appendix A.1.
160

      3.4    C ONNECTING FRAGMENT- BASED M OLECULAR M ODELING TO DATA AUGMENTATION
264
265   In fragment-based molecular modeling, our goal is to enhance the diversity of molecular representa-
266   tions while retaining key torsional properties. By employing fragmentation methods to decompose
267   the complete molecular torsion space τ into smaller torsional subspaces {ττ b }B+1
                                                                                       b=1 , we generate mul-
268   tiple views of a molecule’s torsional characteristics. Each torsional subspace τ b = {τbu,v }(u,v)∈Eb
269   retains important torsional and geometric information from the complete torsion space τ . In practi-
      cal applications, due to data limitations, the true fragment torsional angles τ b are often unavailable.

270
      To address this issue, our proposed data augmentation strategy uses the torsional angles τ̂τ b from
271
      the complete molecular structure τ = {τ̂τ b }B+1
                                                   b=1 as approximations. This approximation is based on
272   the assumption that, due to the preserved local chemical environment, the torsional properties of the
273   fragments are very similar to those of the complete molecule, that is, τ b ≈ τ̂τ b . However, the assump-
274   tion does not always hold because fragmenting the molecule may alter the electronic environment
275   and interactions, leading to differences in torsional properties between the fragments and the com-
276   plete molecule (Stern et al., 2022). Such differences may introduce errors in the modeling process.
277   Additionally, using the complete molecule’s torsional angles as approximations for the fragments ig-
278   nores potential conformational changes that may occur in the fragments due to the absence of steric
279   hindrance or electronic interactions present in the complete molecule (Horton et al., 2022). Thus,
280
      the core idea is that fragmenting molecules while preserving the local chemical environment allows
      the torsional properties of the fragments to remain consistent with those of the complete molecule,
281
      thus ensuring torsional consistency between τ̂τ b and τ b (Stern et al., 2022; Horton et al., 2022).
282
283   We can further consider this problem from the perspective of mutual information. By employing
284   appropriate fragmentation methods, we aim to maximize the mutual information (MI) between τ̂τ
285   and τ , ensuring that the fragment torsional subspaces retain sufficient global torsional information
286
      to accurately reflect molecular properties such as conformational flexibility and stability. The mutual
      information is expressed as:

291   conditional entropy H(ττ b | τˆb ), we effectively maximize the mutual information, ensuring that
292   the torsional angles of the complete molecule can accurately predict those of the fragments, thus
293

      Evaluation We evaluate the quality of the generated conformation ensembles using the
379   train/val/test splits setup from Jing et al. (2022) and apply RMSD-based metrics to assess both diver-
380   sity and quality. The key metrics include Average Minimum RMSD (AMR) and Coverage (COV),
381   which are reported for both Recall (AMR-R, COV-R) and Precision (AMR-P, COV-P). COV-R and
382   AMR-R, measure how well the generated ensemble covers the ground-truth ensemble, while COV-
383   P and AMR-P, assess the accuracy of the generated conformers. The calculation of COV-R and
384   AMR-R can be defined as:
385                                 1
386                     COV-R :=      |{l ∈ [1..L] : ∃k ∈ [1..K], RMSD(Ck , Cl∗ ) < δ}|
                                    L
387
388                                           1 X
                                 AMR-R :=                   min RMSD(Ck , Cl∗ )
389                                           L            k∈[1..K]
                                                l∈[1..L]
390
391   For precision, COV-P and AMR-P are calculated by swapping the roles of generated and reference
392   sets. These metrics emphasize the quality of generated conformations, with δ set to 0.5Å for GEOM-
393   QM9 and 0.75Å for GEOM-DRUGS datasets evaluation. Higher COV or lower AMR scores suggest
394   more realistic conformations, balancing both diversity and quality.
395   Baselines We compare FADiff with methods from both traditional computational methods and es-
396   tablished state-of-the-art deep learning baselines. Among traditional computational methods, we
397   employ RDKit ETKDG (Havel, 1998; Riniker & Landrum, 2015), the most established open-source
398   package, and OMEGA (Hawkins, 2017), a commercial software in continuous development. Among
399   deep learning methods, we evaluate CGCF (Xu et al., 2021b), ConfVAE (Xu et al., 2021a), Con-
400   fGF (Shi et al., 2021), GeoMol (Ganea et al., 2021), Geodiff (Xu et al., 2022), and Torsional Diffu-
401
      sion (For simplicity, we name it as TorDiff in the subsequent section) (Jing et al., 2022).
402
403   Fragmentation Augmentation For a given molecule, we identify all fragmentation-edges and
404
      randomly select B = min(b, κ) edges, where b is the total number of fragmentation-edges and
      κ limits the maximum number of selected edges to avoid excessive small fragments. From the
405
      resulting B + 1 fragments, those with rotatable bonds are used to augment the training set. Our
406
      experiments use κ = 5. During fragmentation, only fragments larger than z atoms are selected for
407   augmentation. This ensures that the resulting fragments retain sufficient structural complexity and
408   chemical information to contribute meaningfully to the training process. To explore the impact of
409   reaction-related bonds on model performance, we also test models generated after removing these
410   bonds, focusing on BRICS and RECAP rules (Lewell et al., 1998; Degen et al., 2008). Detailed
411   introductions of these two chenmical rules are provided in the Appendix B.1, and Additional results
412   and discussions on how the choice of the minimum fragment size parameter z affects fragmentation
413   statistics are provided in Appendix D.1.
414
415   Computational-Aided Data Augmentation The conformer matching method mitigates the dis-
416   tributional shift between training and test time by aligning ground truth conformers with synthetic
417   ones generated by RDKit, ensuring consistency between the two distributions. In Jing et al. (2022),
418   training on these synthetic conformers has shown significant better performance than using ground
419
      truth alone. Therefore, we use conformer matching as a additional data augmentation technique,
      generating synthetic proxy conformers alongside the original ones. Details and ablation studies are
420
      provided in Appendix C.

      In this work, we propose Fragment-Augmented Diffusion (FADiff), a novel framework for molecu-
      lar conformation generation that incorporates molecular fragmentation as a data augmentation strat-
532
      egy within diffusion models. By using molecular fragmentation as a data augmentation strategy,
533
      FADiff effectively captures local structural variations while preserving the integrity of the entire
534   molecule. Our extensive experiments demonstrate that FADiff consistently outperforms state-of-
535   the-art methods in generating diverse and accurate conformations particularly in data-scarce scenar-
536   ios where augmented data significantly enhances model performance. Additionally, we provided a
537   comprehensive analysis of different fragmentation strategies and their impact on the model’s effec-
538   tiveness, offering valuable insights into how the inclusion of chemical rules influences generation
539   quality. This work molecular conformation generation by enhancing the exploration of conforma-
      tional space, with promising applications in areas such as drug discovery and materials science.

      A.2   S CORE N ETWORK A RCHITECTURE
865
866   To predict torsion scores under SE(3)-invariant and parity-equivariant constraints, we follow the
867   framework used in Torsional Diffusion, which contains three main components: an embedding layer,
868   K interaction layers, and a pseudotorque layer. The pseudotorque layer outputs pseudoscalar torsion
869   scores ∆τ := ∂ log p/∂τ for each rotatable bond.
870
871
            1. In the embedding layer, we construct a radius graph on top of the molecular graph, gener-
                                                                                          (0,0,1)
872            ating initial scalar embeddings for nodes and edges. Node embeddings Va            combine
873            chemical features and sinusoidal time embeddings, while edge embeddings eab incorporate
874
               bond distances using radial basis functions and chemical features. This setup ensures that
               both local atomic environments and temporal information are captured.
875
876         2. The interaction layers are built using E(3)NN convolutional layers, which propagate mes-
877            sages between nodes by combining irreducible representations of node features with spher-
878
               ical harmonics of the normalized edge vectors (Geiger & Smidt, 2022). These messages
               are aggregated using Clebsch-Gordan coefficients, ensuring that the node representations
879
               remain SE(3)-equivariant. At each layer, the interaction is governed by tensor products of
880
               node and edge features, and the rotational order of the representations is restricted to be at
881            most 2 (Thomas et al., 2018).
882
883
            3. The pseudotorque layer predicts torsion scores by constructing tensor-valued filters cen-
               tered on each rotatable bond. These filters are formed from the tensor product of spherical
884
               harmonics with a l = 2 representation of the bond axis. The convolution with neighbor-
885
               ing node features produces pseudoscalar outputs, which are passed through odd-function
886            dense layers (e.g., with tanh nonlinearity) to generate the final torsion score predictions.
887            This layer is inspired by the concept of torque, ensuring the correct symmetry properties
888            for torsion score prediction.
889
890   The complete architecture design and tensor computation pipeline can be found in (Jing et al., 2022).

       B.1   F RAGMENTATION RULES
975
976    To validate the effectiveness and differences of various decomposition methods in learning confor-
977    mation structures in the fragmentation torsion space, we analyzed several fragmentation rules in
978    FADiff implementations. These methods provide domain knowledge and deeper insights into the
979    task. The analyzed rules used include the BRICS method and RECAPS method, and a graph-based
980    fragmentation method can be employed by analyzing the connectivity of molecular graphs, which is
981    also the method that Torsional Diffusion used for selecting the rotatable bond (Gordon et al., 2012;
982
       Stern et al., 2020; Jing et al., 2022), this method identifies cut edges by examining whether the
       removal of an edge disconnects the graph into separate components.
983
984
985
       RECAP (Retrosynthetic Combinatorial Analysis Procedure)              (Lewell et al., 1998)
986    RECAP is a classical technique aimed at decomposing complex molecules into smaller, manageable
987    fragments through retrosynthetic analysis. The core principle involves identifying and cleaving
988    chemical bonds that are common in organic synthesis, such as ester, amide, and ether bonds. These
989    bonds are selected based on their prevalence and the ease of cleavage, prioritizing those that connect
990    functional groups to generate fragments with clear chemical functionalities. Typically, RECAP
991
       employs a single-cut strategy, focusing on one bond at a time, resulting in basic fragments. This
       simplicity allows for rapid generation of fragments suitable for combinatorial chemistry and initial
992
       drug screening.
993
994
       BRICS (Breaking of Retrosynthetically Interesting Chemical Substructures)              (Degen et al.,
995
       2008)
996
997    BRICS improves upon RECAP by offering a more detailed approach to fragment generation. It ap-
998    plies a comprehensive set of rules to identify and cleave key substructures in chemical compounds,
999
       considering not just bond types but also the surrounding chemical environment, such as aromaticity
       and heterocycles. This allows BRICS to generate more complex and diverse fragments, supporting
1000
       multi-functional group cleavage to produce synthetically feasible and biologically relevant frag-
1001
       ments. BRICS focuses on creating diverse, drug-like fragments, making it valuable for drug design.
1002   Researchers can use BRICS to build flexible and accurate fragment libraries for virtual screening
1003   and molecular optimization. While RECAP is suitable for basic fragment analysis, BRICS provides
1004   a more advanced tool for high-precision drug development and compound optimization.
1005
1006   Graph-based Fragmentation (Gordon et al., 2012; Stern et al., 2020; Jing et al., 2022) We
1007   consider a bond freely rotatable if severing the bond creates two connected components of the total
1008   graph, each of which has at least two atoms. It guarantees that torsion angles in cycles (or rings),
1009   which cannot be rotated independently, are considered part of the local structure. It can be described
1010   as following steps:
1011
1012
              • Convert the molecular graph into an undirected graph G.
1013          • For each edge, temporarily remove it and check if the resulting graph remains connected.
1014          • If the graph becomes disconnected, identify the connected components and classify them
1015            as fragments.
1016
              • Store the edges whose removal results in disconnected components, as these represent po-
1017
                tential cut points for fragmentation.
1018
1019   Thus, it allows for the identification of edges that, when removed, split the molecular graph into
1020   meaningful substructures. The algorithm ensures that fragments retain their connectivity, making it
1021   particularly useful for identifying torsion-related substructures.

1024
1025   During molecular fragmentation, chemical or graph-based cut rules decompose the molecule into
       smaller fragments. The choice of cut edges introduces errors between the torsion angles τ̂bu,v from

       the full molecular graph and the true torsion angles τbu,v of the fragments due to potential loss
1027   of structural or torsional information. To quantify this, we define an error term ϵ capturing the

1232
1233   In this section, we analyze three fragmentation methods—RECAP, BRICS, and the graph-based
1234   fragmentation method—from the energy perspective discussed earlier. We examine how each
1235   method ensures effectiveness by preserving key electronic effects, steric interactions, and non-
1236   bonded interactions, thereby impacting the error variance σ 2 and the mutual information I(τ̂τ ; τ ).
1237   The following paragraphs provide detailed analyses of these methods.
1238
1239   RECAP Fragmentation Method Analysis The RECAP (Retrosynthetic Combinatorial Analy-
1240   sis Procedure) method provides a straightforward and efficient way to decompose molecules by
1241   cleaving them at common synthetic bonds such as esters, amides, and ethers Lewell et al. (1998).
       This simple approach effectively targets functional groups and preserves core structures, making it


                                                          23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       valuable for generating synthetically accessible fragments. By focusing on bonds commonly ma-
1243   nipulated in organic synthesis, RECAP facilitates the exploration of potential synthetic pathways
1244   and the identification of key structural components. For example, fragmenting acetaminophen at the
1245   amide bond between the phenol ring and the acetamide group yields p-aminophenol and an acetyl
1246   group, which are both significant intermediates in chemical synthesis.
1247
1248   BRICS Fragmentation Method Analysis BRICS (Breaking of Retrosynthetically Interesting
1249   Chemical Substructures) generates synthetically feasible and biologically relevant fragments by ap-
1250   plying detailed rules that consider both bond types and their chemical environments Degen et al.
1251   (2008). By avoiding cuts within conjugated systems and aromatic rings, it preserves critical struc-
                                                                        (i)       (i)
1252   tural features, maintaining consistent torsional parameters Kn and γn and thus reducing error
1253   variance. BRICS also retains bulky substituents and sterically significant groups, which is essen-
1254   tial for accurate torsional barriers and equilibrium angles. By considering the chemical context
1255   at fragmentation sites, it preserves key non-bonded interactions like hydrogen bonds and electro-
1256   static attractions, enhancing the electrostatic components of Econf and increasing mutual information
1257
       I(τ̂τ ; τ ). For example, fragmenting ibuprofen at the carboxylic acid linkage keeps the aromatic ring
       and isobutyl group intact, preserving important steric and electronic properties.
1258
1259
1260
       Graph-Based Fragmentation Method Analysis Graph-based fragmentation focuses on the
       molecule’s connectivity, identifying fragmentation points that yield meaningful substructures with-
1261
       out disrupting critical bonds Gordon et al. (2012); Stern et al. (2020). By avoiding breaks in bonds
1262                                                                                            (i)      (i)
       essential for conjugation and resonance, it maintains consistent torsional parameters Kn and γn ,
1263
       thereby reducing error variance. This approach retains steric interactions by keeping bulky groups
1264
       connected, which is crucial for accurate modeling of torsional barriers and equilibrium angles. Ad-
1265   ditionally, it preserves non-bonded interactions such as hydrogen bonds and electrostatic attractions
1266   by maintaining the connectivity of functional groups, enhancing mutual information I(τ̂τ ; τ ). For
1267   instance, when fragmenting benzene, preserving the aromatic ring maintains its unique electronic
1268   properties and associated torsional parameters, whereas breaking bonds within the ring would elim-
1269   inate these characteristics.

1319   Conformer Matching In (Jing et al., 2022), training on synthetic conformers produced by con-
1320   fermer matching has shown significant better performance than using ground truth alone. The con-
1321   former matching procedure operates as follows. For a molecule with K conformers, it first generates
1322   K random local structure estimates L̂ using RDKit (Riniker & Landrum, 2015). To align these es-

1324   represents the lowest RMSD achievable by adjusting the torsion angles of L̂ to match C. We then
1325   solve the linear sum assignment problem on this approximate cost matrix to find the optimal match-
1326   ing between the true conformers C and the estimates Ĉ (Crouse, 2016; Stärk et al., 2022). For each
1327   matched pair, it refines the alignment by performing a differential evolution optimization over the
1328   torsion angles to obtain the optimal conformer Ĉ (Méndez-Lucio et al., 2021). This complete assign-
1329   ment ensures consistency between the local structures seen during training and inference, preventing

1553
       While our fragment-based augmentation approach has demonstrated significant improvements in
1554   generating accurate and diverse molecular conformations, there are several limitations that present
1555   opportunities for future research. First, when applying fragmentation methods as a general data
1556   augmentation technique for data-driven computational models, we may encounter unmanageable
1557   data volumes, especially when training with large molecular datasets and setting low fragment size
1558   thresholds, as discussed in our appendix on fragmentation statistics. This highlights the need for
1559   more data-efficient frameworks. Leveraging prior domain knowledge, such as scaffold networks or
1560   molecular graphs (Quinn et al., 2017; Nothias et al., 2020; Kruger et al., 2020), could enhance data
1561   efficiency during the fragmentation process, reducing the computational burden while preserving
1562
       essential chemical information.
1563   From a methodological standpoint, while fragment-based augmentation has delivered impressive
1564   results within the torsional diffusion framework, there is room for further improvement to fully
1565   capitalize on the benefits of data augmentation. The current framework relies on cutting edges
       in graph structure algorithms, which introduces limitations—such as difficulty in modeling fully


                                                                    29
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1566
       connected supramolecular structures where rotating edges alone cannot capture reasonable confor-
1567   mations. Potential solutions include introducing additional variations in Euclidean space, like in-
1568   corporating ring-connecting edges from junction trees and allowing non-rigid rotational edges that
1569   permit changes in relative atomic distances (Jin et al., 2018). Additionally, integrating bond stretch-
1570   ing and angle bending components into conformational energy modeling could address challenges
1571   in representing fully connected structures, effectively combining elements of methods like GeoDiff
1572   with Torsional Diffusion (Jing et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2022). By exploring the chemical underpin-
1573   nings of fragment effectiveness, we can gain deeper insights that enable the development of more
1574   effective chemical modeling processes, reduce errors, and enhance data learning efficiency through
1575
       interdisciplinary collaboration. Moreover, our method has the potential to significantly advance
       computation-driven approaches by greatly increasing the amount of available data, which is crucial
1576
       for the success of machine learning models. Scaling laws indicate that enhancing model capacity
1577
       and expanding training datasets with a robust foundational framework can markedly improve perfor-
1578   mance (Frey et al., 2023). By utilizing our fragmentation approach to augment data and scaling up
1579   model parameters, we open new avenues for designing and training larger computational models in
1580   physical chemistry, potentially unlocking novel applications in chemical and materials science (von
1581   Lilienfeld et al., 2020; Sadybekov & Katritch, 2023).
