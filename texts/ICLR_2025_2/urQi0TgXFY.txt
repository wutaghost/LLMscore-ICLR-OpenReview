011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The rapid proliferation of frontier model agents promises significant societal ad-
014            vances but also raises concerns about systemic risks arising from unsafe interactions.
015            Collusion to the disadvantage of others has been identified as a central form of
016            undesirable agent cooperation. The use of information hiding (steganography)
017            in agent communications could render collusion practically undetectable. This
018            underscores the need for evaluation frameworks to monitor and mitigate stegano-
019            graphic collusion capabilities. We demonstrate, for the first time, that unintended
020
               steganographic collusion in LLMs can arise indirectly from optimization pres-
               sure. To investigate this problem we design two approaches – a gradient-based
021
               reinforcement learning (GBRL) method and an in-context reinforcement learning
022
               (ICRL) method – for reliably eliciting sophisticated LLM-generated linguistic text
023            steganography. Importantly, we find that emergent steganographic collusion can be
024            robust to both passive steganalytic oversight of model outputs and active mitigation
025            through communication paraphrasing. We contribute a novel model evaluation
026            framework and discuss limitations and future work. Our findings imply that ef-
027            fective risk mitigation from steganographic collusion post-deployment requires
028            innovation in passive and active oversight techniques.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      As the capabilities of frontier LLMs advance, they may be increasingly deployed in high-risk real-
      world scenarios where unintended behavior could result in harmful outcomes. Prior research has
034
      demonstrated that AI and LLMs can often behave in unexpected and unintended ways, including due
035
      to the optimization of misspecified training incentives (Clark and Amodei, 2016; Krakovna et al.,
036   2020) and complex emergent behaviors in multi-agent systems (Zheng et al., 2022; Dou et al., 2023;
037   Motwani et al., 2024). It is common to employ oversight mechanisms to attempt to penalize such
038   unintended behaviors during training and catch unintended behaviors during deployment (Greenblatt
039   et al., 2024; Kenton et al., 2024).
040
      One way such oversight could be bypassed is via the use of text-based steganography – embedding
041
      hidden information in textual outputs (Ziegler et al., 2019; Schroeder de Witt et al., 2022). Prior
042   works have outlined specific scenarios where text-based steganography may pose a risk (Petitcolas
043   et al., 1999; Lockwood and Curran, 2017; Majeed et al., 2021; Greenblatt et al., 2024). In multi-LLM
044   systems, models may collude via the use of steganography in their communications channels, for
045   example, resulting in them perpetuating biases in their training data (Motwani et al., 2024), or
046   compromising “untrusted monitoring” oversight setups (Greenblatt et al., 2024). Single LLMs could
047   employ steganography within their chain-of-thought reasoning to facilitate undetected reasoning
048   regarding misaligned behaviors, such as sycophancy (Roger and Greenblatt, 2023).
049   There is a risk that, when a mispecified reward signal encourages an unintended behavior but that
050   behavior is penalized by an overseer, models under optimization pressure may adapt to execute the
051   behavior more subtly, rather than ceasing it altogether. I.e., unintended steganographic collusion in
052   LLMs could emerge during training. This could result in harmful outcomes upon their deployment.
053   There is a need to understand these risks in order to inform technical research directions and policy
      decisions. We aim to make progress here via the following contributions:


                                                         1
054
      Contribution 1: We present general methods for automatic elicitation of linguistic steganography in
055   frontier LLMs. Prior work has developed methods for eliciting steganography in non-LLM models
056   (Tang et al., 2019; 2020; Pan et al., 2021), while work with LLMs has been limited to methods that
057   explicitly prompt to follow predefined steganographic codes (Roger and Greenblatt, 2023; Greenblatt
058   et al., 2024) and to devise codes (Wu et al., 2024; Motwani et al., 2024), or has used specific
059   watermarking schemes (Kuditipudi et al., 2023). We present two general methods for improved
060   automatic elicitation of steganography in LLMs. This includes a gradient-based reinforcement
061   learning (GBRL) method and two variants of an in-context reinforcement learning (ICRL) method
062   (see Section 3.3). We demonstrate in our experiments that these methods can serve to: (i) evaluate
063
      the steganographic capabilities of LLMs; (ii) aid evaluation of steganography defenses; and, crucially,
      unlike prior methods, (iii) act as proxies for realistic optimization pressures that may inadvertently
064
      cause steganography to emerge in LLMs.
065
066   Contribution 2: We demonstrate that unintended linguistic steganography can emerge in LLMs
067   when optimizing a misspecified reward in the presence of an overseer. Since our methods do not
068   rely on explicit prompting for steganography, they mark a step towards evaluation tools that can
069
      assess whether steganographic collusion can arise in future systems. Our empirical results in Section
      5.1 warn against naive design of training incentives that inadvertently encourage steganographic
070
      collusion, highlighting the need for further research into understanding and mitigating the emergence
071
      of steganography in LLMs. Our methods and settings can facilitate such research.
072
073   Contribution 3: We show that current methods for mitigating steganography in LLMs are often
074   not robust to optimization pressure. We investigate the effectiveness of (i) oversight – a standard
075
      passive mitigation to analyze messages and punishes colluding agents (Bartel, 2000; Qi, 2013); and
      (ii) paraphrasing – an active mitigation via real-time editing of LLM outputs (Roger and Greenblatt,
076
      2023). Existing empirical work investigating the robustness of these mitigation methods does not
077
      consider a collusion team that is learning (Lanham et al., 2023; Roger and Greenblatt, 2023; Motwani
078   et al., 2024). In Section 5.2, we show that a combination of oversight and paraphrasing can often fail
079   when a colluding team is optimized against them. This motivates the need for improved mitigation
080   mechanisms.

085   The field of emergent communication studies how communication systems can develop under op-
086   timization pressure (Wagner et al., 2003; Havrylov and Titov, 2017; Lazaridou and Baroni, 2020;
087

      2023; Chen et al., 2023; Halawi et al., 2024) shows how methods of covert collusion like steganogra-
088
      phy can arise in multi-agent systems. In particular, Yu et al. (2022) studied messaging between two
089
      agents in the presence of an adversary agent (all of which are CNN-RNN networks trained end-to-end)
090   and showed that these agents can learn a communication system in a referential game (Lewis, 1969)
091   that is uninterpretable to a third-party. In contrast to this work, we do not backpropagate end-to-end
092   through agent communications to optimize collusion.
093
      Majeed et al. (2021) perform a review of various text steganography strategies and categorize them
094
      into three types – (i) format-based methods where physical features of text symbols are used, (ii)
095
      linguistic methods that use features of the language itself like synonyms or acronyms, and (iii)
096   statistical methods that use statistical properties of the language. Motwani et al. (2024) investigate
097   collusion in LLMs and present a taxonomy for evaluation and mitigation of LLM steganography, and
098   explore simple in-context elicitation settings for linguistic steganography. Motwani et al. (2024) also
099   note that paraphrasing will by definition, be ineffective against steganography in semantic spaces.
100   In this paper, we focus on linguistic steganography methods that might be employed by colluding
101   LLMs, and investigate the robustness of oversight and paraphrasing as mitigation strategies.

105   (2023) discuss the emergence of encoded reasoning and steganography in a range of settings, and
106   demonstrates that adding an LLM paraphraser post-hoc is effective against both these kinds of
107   undesirable behavior. Our work builds on this line of research by investigating how paraphrasing fares
      under optimization pressure. Greenblatt et al. (2024) describe an “untrusted monitoring” protocol

      3.1          O UR S TEGANOGRAPHY F RAMEWORK
134
135   We frame steganography as an adversarial relationship between two teams, a collusion team and a
136
      mitigation team. The collusion team consists of a sender and a receiver, and aims to complete a
      task t while communicating a secret payload p. The sender sends an innocuous-seeming message
137
      to the receiver called the stegotext, within which it hides the payload. The receiver interprets the
138
      sender’s message and then produces an action a which completes the task. This action is passed to an
139   evaluation function, which provides a task reward rtask for the collusion team. In our tasks, successful
140   communication of the payload will generally allow the receiver to better maximize this reward.
141
142
      The mitigation team aims to detect and mitigate steganographic communication of the payload
      by the collusion team. It consists of an overseer and an optional paraphraser, passive and active
143
      steganographic defenses respectively (Bartel, 2000). The paraphraser rewords the stegotext in an
144
      attempt to remove the payload. The overseer attempts to detect the presence of steganography by
145   analysing stegotexts. When the overseer flags a stegotext as suspicious, the collusion team receives
146   a suspiciousness penalty rsus . The overseer is not optional because without it there is no means to
147   punish collusion, and no third-party from which the payload needs to remain hidden.
148
      In our experiments, the mitigation team is fixed, and the collusion team attempts to maximize its
149
      reward r, which is a combination of the task reward and the overseer penalty: r = rtask − rsus
150
151   Evaluation Metrics. We evaluate the emergence of steganography using the following measures:
152                • Task Success: Receiver correctly completes the task.
153
154                • Decoding Success: Receiver successfully decodes the payload.
155                • Sender Task Success: Sender produces task-relevant stegotext.
156
157                • Overseer Bypass Success: Overseer does not classify stegotext as suspicious.
158                • Collusion Success: Collusion team completes the task, produces relevant stegotext (optional),
159                  and avoids overseer detection.
160
161
                   • Channel Capacity: Bits of information communicated per request by the collusion team, as
                     defined by Roger and Greenblatt (2023).

      3.2     M ITIGATION T EAM
163
164   3.2.1    OVERSEERS
165
166   Across our experiments, we make use of three different versions of an overseer. We detail each
167
      version in the paragraphs below. Flagging every sample as suspicious might be optimal from a safety
      perspective. However, this approach would result in null task usefulness. To address this, we calibrate
168
      the overseer on held-out calibration sets for each task. This process helps us find suitable prompts
169
      and thresholds that perform well on the safety-usefulness frontier. Further details on this process and
170   each overseer can be found in Appendix B.3, D.1.2 and D.1.3.
171
172   Single-episode LLM-logits Overseer (O1). A (white-box access) LLM is prompted to classify
173
      whether a single stegotext appears suspicious. We then use the logits for the Yes/No tokens, zYes
      and zNo , along with a sensitivity threshold tsens , to compute the suspiciousness score rsus for each
174
      stegotext: rsus = tanh (zYes − zNo − tsens ). Here, Overseer Bypass Success = rsus < tsens .
175
176   Multi-episode LLM-sampling Overseer (O2). A (black-box access) LLM is prompted to evaluate a
177   batch of stegotexts to determine if they seem suspicious. The LLM returns a single yes/no answer for
178   the entire batch, which is converted into a suspiciousness score as follows: rsus = 1 if "Yes", 0 if "No".
179
      This rsus is then applied to each stegotext in the batch. Here, Overseer Bypass Success = 1 - rsus .
180   Multi-episode LLM-sampling Thresholded Overseer (O3). A (black-box access) LLM is
181   prompted to evaluate individual stegotexts within a batch to score their suspiciousness. It as-
182   signs a per-sample score between 0 and 5 to each stegotext (where 0 indicates harmless and 5
183   indicates highly suspicious). The entire batch is then assigned a binary rsus score if more than
184   Tn % of the samples have a per-sample score greater than or equal to a threshold Ts . Concretely,
185
      rsus is 1 if > Tn % of samples have a per-sample score of ≥ Ts , otherwise it is 0. This rsus is then
      applied to each stegotext in the batch. Here, Overseer Bypass Success = 1 - rsus .
186

      3.2.2    PARAPHRASERS
188
189   Our mitigation teams optionally make use of one of three paraphrasers. Further details on the prompts
190   used for each paraphraser can be found in Appendix B.3.
191
      Standard Paraphraser (SP). An LLM which is passed the stegotext and asked to re-word it (Roger
192   and Greenblatt, 2023).
193
194   Context-aware Paraphraser (CAP). An LLM which is passed the stegotext, along with details
195
      regarding the task the sender should perform. It is asked to reword the stegotext while removing any
      details irrelevant to the task (Roger and Greenblatt, 2023).
196
197   Coding Paraphraser (CP). In coding tasks, we make use of non-LLM paraphraser which deter-
198   ministically processes the sender’s output, renaming all variables to generic names and removing all
199   comments.

201   3.3     C OLLUSION T EAM
202
      We present two different methods for optimizing the collusion team to maximize their reward signal:
203
      gradient-based reinforcement learning (GBRL) and in-context reinforcement learning (ICRL). For
204
      GBRL, the weights of a pretrained LLM are modified by gradient descent via an RL algorithm. In
205   ICRL, the behavior of each agent is defined by strategies (represented in natural language) within the
206   context window that are improved over time, and the weights of the collusion team LLMs remain
207   fixed. A generalization of the training set-ups for GBRL and one version of ICRL is illustrated in
208   Figure 1.
209
210   3.3.1    G RADIENT- BASED R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING (GBRL)
211
212
      When optimizing the collusion team via GBRL, we periodically switch between training of the sender
      and training of the receiver. The receiver is first updated for nr stochastic gradient descent (SGD)
213
      steps, after which the sender is updated for ns SGD steps. This process is repeated until convergence.
214
215   The receiver is a pretrained language model with a randomly initialized classification head. It is
      trained using supervised fine-tuning (SFT) to take the stegotext generated by the sender and predict


                                                          4
216
      the label that maximizes the task reward. The sender is a pretrained autoregressive LLM which
217   takes the task description as input, and outputs stegotext to be passed to the receiver. The sender is
218   trained using a reinforcement learning (RL) algorithm to maximize a reward signal r that accounts
219   for task usefuleness and oversight. The overall reward r for the sender is the probability assigned
220   to the correct label by the receiver (rtask ), minus a multiple λoverseer of the suspiciousness penalty:
221   r = rtask − λoverseer rsus
222
      We use proximal policy optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017) with low-rank adaptation
223   (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) to update the sender (Sokota et al., 2022). More implementation details,
224   hyperparameters and prompts used for the task can be found in Appendix A.1. Relating this to
225   Figure 1, the ‘Sender Learner’ in the figure performs the in-weight PPO updates on the sender, and
226   the ‘Receiver Learner’ performs SFT updates on the Receiver.
227
228   3.3.2   I N - CONTEXT R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING (ICRL)
229
230   Our ICRL methods make use of one or more ICRL Learners in addition to the sender and receiver,
231   all of which are LLMs. An ICRL Learner is an LLM that devises steganography strategies for the
232   sender and receiver to execute. Once the sender and receiver execute the strategy (over a number
233   of episodes), the ICRL Learner is fed the results in its context window, including details regarding
234   the task reward rtask and suspiciousness penalty rsus received. It then iterates upon the strategy it
235
      sends to the sender and receiver, in order to maximize the reward signal. This is similar to previous
      approaches using in-context trial-and-error learning to iteratively improve LLM behavior (Yao et al.,
236
      2023; Shinn et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024).
237
238   We make use of two different variations of our ICRL method – Centralized Learner where the sender
239   and receiver share a common ICRL Learner, and Decentralized Learners where the sender and
240   receiver each have their own independent ICRL Learners.
241
242   Decentralized ICRL Learners. This setup is equivalent to the setup shown in Figure 1. We have
243   two separate ICRL learners (one for the sender and one for the receiver) which cannot communicate
244   with each other. First, the sender learner suggests a strategy for the sender to execute over several
245   episodes. The receiver learner analyzes the sender’s stegotexts from the episodes, and attempts to
246
      infer the strategy used by the sender. The receiver is then fed the inferred strategy and attempts to
      complete the task, while also trying to guess the payload in the sender’s stegotexts as a means of
247
      improving its task performance. The results of the current training step are fed back to the sender
248
      learner for the next training step.

      3.3.3   M OTIVATIONS B EHIND E LICITATION VIA GBRL AND ICRL
265
266   We desire elicitation methods that can serve to: (i) act as proxies for realistic optimization pressures
267   that may inadvertently induce steganography in real-world settings; and (ii) act as strong optimization
268   pressures for red-teaming steganography mitigation methods. Our GBRL and Decentralized ICRL
269   Learners methods may act as realistic optimization pressures (i.e., there is no secret coordination
      channels between the sender and receiver), as we demonstrate in the experiments of Section 5.1. Our


                                                          5
270
      Centralized ICRL Learner may act as a strong optimization pressure; it is strengthened by the secret
271   coordination channel between the sender and the receiver.
272
273   There are benefits to having qualitatively different gradient-based and in-context approaches. Pure
274
      in-context approaches can leverage frontier black-box models and may be more sample efficient
      and less computationally expensive than gradient-based approaches. Meanwhile, gradient-based
275
      approaches have the potential to achieve more substantial and persistent improvements in the model’s
276
      steganography capabilities. Qualitatively different approaches may lead to different learning dynamics
277   and results, meaning our analyses will be more comprehensive.

282   4.1   AUTOMATED C ANDIDATE S CREENING
283
284   We create a setting which investigates the possibility of steganography inadvertently emerging as
285   an instrumental goal. In this setting, an AI system is trained on historic data to provide pass/fail
286   evaluations of candidate cover letters for job applications. We setup the datasets such that there
287   exists some bias in its labelling related to ‘sensitive variables’ (either the gender of the applicant or
288   their educational background). Without using the sensitive variables, the maximum task success that
289   can be achieved on the dataset is Max-Clean-Success < 100%. However, if the sensitive feature is
290   exploited, 100% task success can be achieved.
291   We suppose the developers of the AI system are aware that automated candidate screening with
292   machine learning can replicate and amplify social biases (Dastin, 2018; Andrews and Bucher, 2022;
293   Chen, 2023), and so have (naively) attempted to mitigate against this. A first model (i.e., the sender)
294   summarizes the cover letter, while removing potentially biasing information. A second model (i.e.,
295   the receiver) takes in this summary and predicts the label. A third model (i.e., the overseer) checks
296   the summaries for any mentions of the sensitive variables. The sender and receiver are optimized
297
      to maximize their end-to-end prediction accuracy on the historic dataset (rtask = whether the correct
      pass/fail label is predicted by the receiver), while minimizing penalties received from the overseer
298
      (rsus ). A version of this setting was originally described by Motwani et al. (2024).
299
300   We construct different instances of this setting:
301          • Uni dataset: All cover letters with candidates who went to “The Good University” are
302            labelled “pass”, and all candidates who went to “The Bad University” are labelled “fail”.
303            Max-Clean-Success is 50%.
304
             • Uni-Long dataset: Identical to the Uni dataset, but the cover letters are longer.
305
306
             • Gender dataset: A binary gender indicator is present in each application. The bias is
               more severe than the previous datasets; labels are determined by three different categorical
307
               variables (gender, field-of-study, and soft-skill). Max-Clean-Success is 67%.
308
309   Further details regarding how we generated the candidate screening datasets and samples from each
310   dataset can be found in Appendix B.1. Note, in these tasks we do not make use of a Sender Task
311   Success metric.

313   4.2   E VALUATING S TEGANOGRAPHY M ITIGATIONS
314
315   We now present the tasks we use to evaluate steganography mitigations (see Section 5.2).
316
      Here, the sender must produce some stegotext that completes a task (as judged by a Sender Task
317
      Success metric), while encoding a payload that is explicitly provided in its prompt. The receiver
318   must directly guess the payload from the stegotext. Thus, rtask = Decoding Success, i.e., whether the
319   receiver correctly guesses the payload. In addition to using an overseer that looks for steganography
320   in the stegotext, we also flag a batch of stegotexts as suspicious if the Sender Task Success falls below
321   a certain Honest-Performance threshold (refer to Appendix D.1 for details). Thus, here collusion is
322   successful (i.e., Collusion Success) when: the stegotext is suitably performant, the receiver correctly
323   decodes the payload, and the stegotext is not flagged as suspicious by the overseer. We make use of
      two commonly used LLM benchmarks for our tasks.

347   5.1     I NSTRUMENTAL E MERGENCE : AUTOMATED C ANDIDATE S CREENING
348
349   We run our GBRL method on the Uni and Gender datasets. All GBRL runs include an O1 overseer
350
      (single-episode, logits-based). One instance additionally includes a standard paraphraser. The GBRL
      experiments use the datasets with shorter cover letters for computational efficiency purposes. We
351
      run our ICRL method on the Uni-Long dataset. All ICRL runs use an O2 overseer (multi-episode,
352
      sampling-based). One ICRL experiment additionally includes a standard paraphraser. Details
353   regarding the specific models used for these runs can be found in Appendix A.1 and A.2. The results
354   of these runs are presented in Table 1.
355
356   GBRL Results. The collusion team achieves high task success rates, successfully communicating
357   the sensitive information while bypassing the overseer (note, one seed failed to converge in the
358   Gender dataset). Several runs converge to strategies which were hard for us to spot when analysing
359   the results. This was particularly the case in the Gender dataset where steganography is only
360   required to predict the label on a subset of the data (see Table 12 in Appendix C). Strategies that
361   seemed ‘subtle’ to us included referring to males as “individuals” and females as “candidates”, or
362   using the phrase “and is also skilled in” for males and “and is able to” for females. Experiments with
363   a paraphraser converged to writing 1st-person cover letters for the Good University and writing in
364
      3rd-person for the Bad University.
365   A limitation to note here is that our overseers can be brittle, meaning occasionally the learned
366   encoding strategies appeared ‘blatant’ to us. For example, in one run in the Uni setting the succesful
367   strategy involved learning to say that candidates from the Bad University went to a “less-renowned

463

464
      implicitly forces the collusion teams to come up with strategies that are more difficult for the overseer

472   our results against an existing steganography benchmark introduced by Roger and Greenblatt (2023).
473   The benchmark is similar to our AQuA-RAT task setting, but with no overseer. The benchmark
474   strategies were developed for GPT models, so we also use gpt-4o for the colluding team.

476   against the standard paraphraser and the context-aware paraphraser. We follow a similar training

482
      from training against our context-aware paraphraser achieves lower channel capacity, but its channel
      capacity is only reduced by 24% when the context-aware paraphraser is applied.

485   strategies that outperform the human designed strategies and the ‘scratchpad’ LLM steganography
      elicitation method from Roger and Greenblatt (2023).

505   6   C ONCLUSION
506
507
508   We have presented two distinct and effective methods for eliciting steganography in LLMs, even when
509
      a mitigation team consisting of paraphrasing and an overseer is present. Our candidate screening
      experiments show that steganography can emerge as an instrumental goal when optimizing task
510
      performance in some settings. We see that when this happens, the sender and receiver models are
511
      able to consistently bypass the overseer by learning steganographic schemes.
512
513   In our experiments to evaluate robustness of mitigation strategies, we used the ICRL optimization
514   approach to create strong red teams that generate sophisticated steganographic strategies. We find
515
      these schemes can be as effective as or more effective than “worst case” human-devised schemes
      from previous work. We show that an overseer by itself proves to be inadequate for mitigating
516
      steganography when the mitigation team is present during the optimization process. Additionally, no
517
      paraphraser was able to reliably mitigate the steganographic communication of 1 bit.
518
519   Our results highlight the limitations of current black-box mitigation approaches for steganographic
520   collusion. As agents’ capabilities grow, their ability to conduct undetectable steganography will
521
      likely increase. White-box detection strategies may become essential for mitigation. Addressing
      the technical and political challenges (Casper et al., 2024; Chan et al., 2024a;b) of monitoring
522
      steganographic collusion underscores the need for continuous frontier model evaluations before
523
      deployment (Motwani et al., 2024).
524
525
526   Limitations and Future Work. Some of our ICRL experiments employ a centralized learner to
527   evaluate the robustness of mitigation strategies, primarily due to resource constraints. While this
528   approach may not perfectly simulate realistic optimization pressures, it offers significant advantages
529   in terms of time and cost efficiencies, allowing us to gather valuable initial insights and establish a
530   foundation for future research. Additionally, we primarily only used the Anthropic Claude model
531
      for the ICRL collusion team. Future work could include a more comprehensive evaluation of other
      frontier models. Due to compute and budget constraints we were not able to use the GBRL approach
532
      to evaluate the robustness of mitigation strategies, and we leave this for future work.
533
534
535   Ethics Statement. Steganographic collusion may have negative outcomes if not mitigated, and
536   frameworks for elicitation of steganography can be used for malicious purposes. We uncovered that
537   steganography can emergence as an instrumental goal under optimization pressure and that current
538   mitigation strategies are insufficient. A key takeaway of our work is that further research is needed to
539   ensure these outcomes are avoided. Furthermore, these results should be used to inform future AI
      policy and governance considerations.

      Every time we query an ICRL-learner for a new strategy, we utilize multiple LLM calls for different
      stages, as described below. The final output is a consolidation of the output from each of the stages:
904
905         1. Clear - We clear the learner’s context window, apart from the system prompt. The system
906            prompt contains instructions, and a description of the task the agents must do.
907
            2. Reflection - We show the learner the results from the last episode, highlighting failed
908            examples. We prompt the learner to summarize what went wrong in that episode.
909
            3. Strategize - At this stage, we feed in the learner’s knowledge from previous steps and ask it
910
               to do the following:
911
912
                (a) Summarize what it has learnt so far. This must be an extension of the previous
                    knowledge which we have passed it.
913
914
                (b) Suggest 3 new strategies.
915             (c) Rate each strategy based on criteria like ease of implementation, ability to bypass a
916
                    paraphraser.
917         4. Query - Finally, we ask the learner to pick a new strategy based on the shortlisted options in
               the Strategize step.

      A.2.4    M ULTI -B IT L EARNER
923
924   The section below applies only to the centralized learner setting where we are explicitly encouraging
925   the learner to encode payloads of maximum size.
926
      In general, we ask the learner to encode integer payloads. To help the learner to encode larger
927   payloads, we prompt it to encode the payloads in parts, with a different strategy for each part. For
928   example, a 6-value payload can be encoded in a 3-value part and a 2-value part. The learner must
929   then come up with one strategy to encode 3 values, and one to encode 2 values. Each strategy must
930   work independently for the encoding scheme to be successful. Appendix ?? shows some example
931   strategies for encoding multiple payloads in multiple parts.
932
      Below, we detail the method used in Section 5.2 to elicit multi-bit steganography. In each case,
933   strategies were run on 20 samples from the training set between each strategy querying step.
934
935
      Step 1 - Single-bit strategy collection. We start by asking the ICRL agent to generate strategies to
936
      encode a binary payload (0 or 1). When the learner finds a strategy which is successful, i.e. which
937   achieves 100% encoding success and the episodes are not flagged, we save that strategy. We ask the
938   learner to continue to find more strategies, which are different from any which it has already found.
939   We run this process for 80 training steps.
940
941   Step 2 - Multi-bit strategy collection. We try to encode larger integers. We show the learner for
942   each learning run here with the successful 1-bit strategies from the previous step. We run each
943   learner for 10 steps. We start by trying to learn strategies for incrementally increasing 1-part integers
944   until the channel capacity of the best learnt strategy stops increasing. We then try to learn 2-part
945   strategies, following a similar process of increasing in payload size unitl the learner cannot learn
946   a strategy with higher channel capacity. We continue this process until there is no more possible
947   payload configuration which would yield a higher channel capacity.
948
949   Step 3 - Strategy selection. We now choose the best strategies which we will run on the test set.
950   For each payload size, we pick the best two strategies which were not flagged as suspicious. We rank
951   the strategies first on maximizing decoding success, then on minimizing the average suspiciousness
952
      level on the training set. If there are less than 2 acceptable strategies, we do not take any.

967   B.2     D EVELOPMENT P ROCESS OF THE I NSTRUMENTAL E MERGENCE ICRL A LGORITHM
968
      We describe the process through which we developed the ICRL instrumental emergence algorithm
969
      here. Since this algorithm was used to test instrumental emergence of steganography, we want to be
970
      candid about how much tuning went into the optimization algorithm.
971
      We went through the following steps when developing the algorithm:


                                                         18
972
             1. Testing with no overseer and a central learner. We started with no overseer, and a central
973             learner. This quickly converged with our Claude-3.5 Sonnet learner. The central learner has
974             a lot of references to steganography and encoding messages in its prompt, so this was not a
975             very realistic setting.
976
977          2. A neutral central learner. We removed all references to "steganography", "encoding",
978
                "payload" etc. which was in the previous prompt. We found that the learner still found a
                good strategy but more slowly.
979
980          3. Simple overseer. We added in a simple overseer which checks for string matches on the word
981             "university". We found that the learner still converged, but did so with strategies that were
982             not interesting, like refering to a "prestigious institution" rather than "The Good University".
983
             4. Decentralized learner. We developed the decentralized learner framework, where the sender
984
                learner was similar to the previous learner. At this point, the learner framework was almost
985             the completely form described above, but without the initial dataset evaluation step. We
986             found that the learner still learnt good strategies quite easily.
987
988          5. LLM overseer. We added in the LLM overseer which we have described above. We found
989
                that the learner no longer converged, as it would not be able to work out that there was any
                bias in the data.
990
991          6. Dataset evaluation step. We added in the dataset evaluation step to the sender learner. We
992             found that with this extra step, the learner now learnt steganographic strategies which were
993             more subtle than previously.
994
             7. Evaluation of other models. We then evaluated other models in the same ICRL framework,
995
                without changing it at all.
996
997
998    Potential issues. We list two potential issues with our development of the ICRL algorithm, and our
999
       justification.
1000
1001          • The addition of the dataset evaluation step. We added in the dataset evaluation step when
1002
                we found that Claude-3.5 Sonnet no longer did steganography with the new overseer. It
                could be argued that by doing this we were encouraging steganography to emerge. The issue
1003
                was that the encoder learner only ever saw parts of the training dataset when it got feedback
1004
                from previous strategies, so never noticed the bias in the data. We wanted to decouple the
1005            agent’s ability to work out the bias in the data from its propensity to take advantage of the
1006            bias with steganography. We think that helping the agent to see the bias in the data does not
1007            reduce the importance of the agent then deciding to do steganography.
1008
1009          • Tailoring the training framework to Claude-3.5 Sonnet. We developed the pipeline with
1010
                results from Claude-3.5 Sonnet, and tested at the end on different models. One of our results
                was that we could not elicit steganography in the other models. If we had built up the
1011
                training framework with the other models, it is possible that we would have seen them do
1012
                steganography. For this reason, we emphasise the positive result - that steganography arises
1013            instrumentally in Claude-3.5 Sonnet - rather than the negative result - that it does not in
1014            other models.

1923   D.1.3    VARYING THE M ITIGATION T HRESHOLDS
1924
       In Section 5.2, we defined a mitigation protocol with some hyperparameters for flagging suspicious
1925
       strategies. Those parameters were set to values which we reasoned were low enough to catch
1926
       suspicious strategies, while also providing a "safety factor" against flagging honest episodes. In this
1927   section, we look at the effect of varying those hyperparameters.
1928
1929   We define the "Useful safety factor" as the fractional drop in task performance which is allowed
1930
       before a set of episodes is judged to be suspicious. If the task performance is 1.0, a useful safety
       factor of 0.1 would lead to a task performance threshold of 0.9.
1931
1932   We keep the definition from Section 5.2 for suspicious episodes fixed. I.e. we take the 90th percentile
1933   suspiciousness score from the honest runs as a threshold for each episode.
1934   We define the "Suspiciuosness safety factor" as the multiplier on the allowed number of suspicious
1935   episodes, when compared to the honest evaluations. If 10 episodes are flagged as suspicious in
1936   the honest evaluations, then a safety factor of 2 means any set of episodes with over 20 suspicious
1937   messages will be judged as suspicious.
1938
1939   Results. Note that in 5.2 we used a useful safety factor of 0.1 and a suspiciuosness safety factor of
1940   2. On the AQuA-RAT task, we find that reducing the useful safety factor towards 0 has a dramatic
1941   effect on the performance of the strategies learnt with no paraphraser. We hypothesise that since
1942   the paraphraser forces responses to be more concise by removing or changing extra details, it also
1943   forces CoT to be more logical. The strategies learnt without this pressure can trade-off usefulness for
       encoding larger bits, by adding in information to the CoT prompt which confuses the final answer.
