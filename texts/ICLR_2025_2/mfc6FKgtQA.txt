011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            This work introduces a novel Text-Guided Time Series Forecasting (TGTSF)
014            task. By integrating textual cues, such as channel descriptions and dynamic news,
015            TGTSF addresses the critical limitations of traditional methods that rely purely on
016
               historical data. To support this task, we propose TGForecaster, a robust baseline
               model that fuses textual cues and time series data using cross-attention mecha-
017
               nisms. We then present four meticulously curated benchmark datasets to validate
018
               the proposed task, ranging from simple periodic data to complex, event-driven
019            fluctuations. Our comprehensive evaluations demonstrate that TGForecaster con-
020            sistently achieves state-of-the-art performance, highlighting the transformative
021            potential of incorporating textual information into time series forecasting. This
022            work not only pioneers a novel forecasting task but also establishes a new bench-
023            mark for future research, driving advancements in multimodal data integration for
024            time series models.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Time series forecasting (TSF) is crucial in many fields, thereby attracting significant attention from
029   both academia and industry. Despite extensive research dedicated to this task, recent studies have
030   shown that simple linear models (Zeng et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Toner & Darlow, 2024) that
031   barely extract trend and periodicity information from time series data frequently achieve performance
032   close to that of state-of-the-art complex models (Nie et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2023),
033
      indicating that current approaches have reached a saturation point, possibly overfitting historical data.
034   Beyond simple trends and periodicity, complex patterns in time series data are often influenced by
035   external factors like holidays and consumer sentiment—information not captured by historical data
036   alone. To improve forecasting accuracy, it is essential to incorporate such external information as
037   inputs. Otherwise, models may fail to identify key causal relationships, leading to oversimplification
038   or overfitting. Previous works (Wu et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022a; Rasul et al., 2024) have explored
039
      translating simple information into auxiliary channels, such as using one-hot encoding, but this
      approach often loses valuable semantic information, reducing the model’s ability to generalize
040
      and extract meaningful insights. Additionally, the lack of system dynamics and inter-channel
041
      relationships can lead to unreliable forecasts. Since much of this information is text-based and
042   difficult to express as time series data, it is crucial to develop models that can understand and
043   integrate textual inputs. Although some have attempted to leverage large language models (LLMs)
044   for time series forecasting (Cao et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2023), recent studies (Tan et al., 2024) indicate
045   that LLMs still face challenges in aligning token-based methods with the temporal domain. This
046   underscores the need for a multi-modal approach that effectively combines text and time series data.
047
      We propose a novel multi-modality task: Text-Guided Time Series Forecasting (TGTSF). TGTSF
048   leverages two extra text-based components: channel descriptions and dynamic news messages.
049   Channel descriptions provide static knowledge about the underlying systems, enabling the model
050   to differentiate between channels and better understand inter-channel correlations. News messages
051   offer dynamic and external insights, helping the model adjust to shifts in data distribution caused
052   by external events. By modeling the joint distribution of future values conditioned on these textual
053   cues, TGTSF has the potential to enhance forecasting accuracy and reliability. Furthermore, TGTSF
      enables scenario-based forecasting, which is particularly valuable for business decision-making. For


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      instance, a company can forecast sales based on the assumption of a successful marketing campaign,
055   incorporating relevant news and channel-specific descriptions to refine predictions.
056
057   We introduce TGForecaster, a Transformer-based multimodal model designed to effectively leverage
058
      textual information for time series forecasting. TGForecaster incorporates two key innovations:
      Time-Synchronized Text Embedding and Text-Guided Channel Independent (TGCI). Instead of
059
      word token, we use sentence embedding vector to carry the semantic information. By ordering these
060
      vectors on time domain, news messages are aligned with their corresponding time steps, allowing
061   the model to incorporate relevant external information at the right moments. The TGCI mechanism
062   dynamically attends to the most relevant news for each specific time series channel, enabling the
063   model to capture the unique impact of different news items on various channels.
064
      To validate this task, we propose four multimodal datasets as a benchmark, each designed to test
065
      different aspects of the model’s capabilities. Specifically, according to the real-world applications,
066
      we consider three categories news messages. (1) Common knowledge, such as dates and public
067   events, to provide foundational context and align the model with predictable patterns; (2) system-level
068   limited predictions, which incorporate sparse and broad domain knowledge, like weather or market
069   reports, to guide the model in generating accurate and fine-grained time series predictions; and (3)
070   hypothesized or controlled events, allowing for "what-if" scenario analysis, where planned actions,
071   such as marketing campaigns, can potentially influence future outcomes. These components are
072   derived from real-world scenarios and are known before forecasting begins, ensuring the benchmark
073   is both leakage-free and valid.
074   Experiment results show that TGForecaster consistently demonstrates state-of-the-art performance
075   across our datasets, affirming the soundness of the TGTSF task definition. Ablation studies reveal
076   that without textual assistance, TGForecaster’s performance reverts to that of PatchTST (Nie et al.,
077   2023), its time series encoder backbone, underscoring that the performance enhancement is driven by
078   the additional information provided by textual data, not merely by a more sophisticated architecture.
079   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
080
081          • We identify the roadblock of TSF as information insufficiency and propose TGTSF as a new
082
               forecasting approach that integrates textual data to enrich the models with external causal
               information and system knowledge.
083
             • We establish the first TGTSF benchmark containing four uniquely designed datasets.
084
             • We design a simple baseline model for TGTSF, TGForecaster. TGForecaster achieves
085            state-of-the-art performance by utilizing textual information and effectively validates our
086            proposed TGTSF task.

090   2.1   E XISTING TSF S OLUTIONS S UFFER FROM I NFORMATION I NSUFFICIENCY
091   Time series forecasting typically involves predicting future segments based on historical data, specifi-
092   cally forecasting subsequent time series segments from prior ones. However, the inherent sparsity
093   of information within time series data makes achieving accurate forecasts challenging, or even
094   impossible at times. Standard decomposition of time series data identifies three main components:
095   trend, periodicity, and noise (Box et al., 2015). The noise component is intrinsically unpredictable.
096   Trends, which are slowly changing patterns, are often impacted by external events that influence
097   the underlying system, making it less predictable without external information, see Appendix C for
098   detailed discussion. As a result, the periodicity component is usually the main source of reliable
099
      prediction.
100   Recent advancements in linear models have validated these challenges. For instance, the DLin-
101   ear (Zeng et al., 2023) model utilizes a simple linear layer to effectively capture basic periodic
102   patterns, outperforming most complex Transformer-based models. Similarly, FITS (Xu et al., 2023),
103   designed to simply extract periodicity, achieves comparable or superior results to state-of-the-art
104   (SOTA) methods using fewer than 10k parameters. Further research demonstrates that employing a
105
      closed-form forecasting matrix (Toner & Darlow, 2024), calculated from training data, can produce
      SOTA outcomes. These findings indicate that the minimal information in time series data allows
106
      models to recognize patterns with few or no learnable parameters, suggesting that training larger
107
      models with insufficient information can lead to severe overfitting.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      The issue of information insufficiency extends beyond the data itself to the absence of external
109   information. External factors like holidays, consumer sentiment, or climate changes can significantly
110   alter patterns in time series data, affecting variables such as electricity usage, sales trends, or regional
111   weather conditions. However, these factors are not contained within the time series data, hindering
112   models from incorporating causal relationships. This deficiency can compromise model training,
113   causing models to either learn overly simplified average shortcut or overfit on the training data, as
114   shown in Fig. 1. For example, if a dataset frequently presents days without rain, the model may
115   consistently predict no rain for future days. However, rainfall predictions are largely influenced
116   by external factors like climate change, not included in the time series, leading models to opt for
117
      shortcuts that minimize overall loss by predicting the most common outcome.
118   Moreover, the lack of knowledge about the underlying systems exacerbates the problem of infor-
119   mation insufficiency, especially in datasets with multiple time series channels. Without a deeper
120   understanding of these systems or the characteristics of each channel, it is challenging to leverage such
121   information for improved modeling performance. The indistinctiveness among channels complicates
122   modeling efforts, making it difficult to accurately capture and model correlations.
123   These challenges underscore the need to integrate external information to address the fundamental
124   limitations of time series forecasting methods.

132
      information such as trend intensity and amplitude variations, which diminishes the model’s capacity
      to detect relative biases or amplitude shifts beyond the training scope. However, RIN merely hides
133
      the issue of information insufficiency by neutralizing the effects of external events.

169   2.3   M OTIVATION FOR T HE P ROPOSED TGTSF F RAMEWORK
170
      External factors significantly impact time series behavior such as trending and periodicity. A line
171
      of work apply and investigate how to embed the external information as a new channel, such as
172   Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021), FedFormer (Zhou et al., 2022a) add auxilary channels to indicate the
173   day-of-week or even the exact date. Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) (Lim et al., 2021) and
174   TimeXer (Wang et al., 2024), however, focus on how to select the given variables and how to embed
175   and fuse them with other channels. However, these methods yield limited performance improvements
176   as they primarily extract more information from the already constrained time series dataset.
177
      Incorporating additional information can reduce uncertainty and improve forecasting performance.
178   Since much of this information is created for human consumption, it often comes in the form of text,
179   making text a crucial modality for time series forecasting. However, integrating textual data as an
180   external modality presents several challenges.
181
      First, text is semantically rich and diverse, and reducing it to simple numerical forms, such as one-hot
182
      encoding, can result in significant loss of information, preventing the model from generalizing to
183
      unseen events and learning the causal relationships between events and time series patterns. Second,
184   we aim to use the text to incorporate dynamic information and expert insights relevant to specific
185   time stamps. Achieving precise temporal alignment between time series data and textual information
186   remains challenging, particularly for word token-based methods like large language models (LLMs).
187   Additionally, multiple news messages may correspond to a single time step, and different channels
188   may react differently to the same information, adding complexity to the alignment process.
189
      To address these challenges, it is crucial to explore a multi-modal framework for time series forecasting
190   that integrates textual data alongside historical time series data. Such an approach would allow
191   models not only to respond to past trends but also to proactively adapt to future events and trends.
192   By incorporating textual information, this framework could help capture causal relationships and
193   inter-channel dynamics that are often missing in traditional time series models. It would also enhance
194   forecasting accuracy by providing insights into the underlying mechanisms that influence the time
195   series, offering a more holistic view of the system. Thus, result in more accurate forecasting.

197   3     TGTSF TASK F ORMULATION
198
      We introduce a novel task within the domain of time series analysis, termed Text-Guided Time
199   Series Forecasting (TGTSF), designed to address the prevalent issue of information insufficiency in
200   time series forecasting.
201
202
      As illustrated in Fig. 1, text guidance in TGTSF operates on two aspects. Firstly, channel descriptions
      serve as identifiers for each channel, aiding the model in distinguishing between them while learning
203
      shared features. These descriptions can also incorporate static knowledge about the underlying system,
204
      enhancing the model’s ability to recognize inter-channel correlations. Secondly, news messages
205   provide dynamic and external insights into known (in training) or hypothesized (in inference) future
206   events, which assist the model in adapting to event-driven distribution shifts. Specifically, to ensure
207   data quality and prevent information leakage, we focus on the following three types of text information
208   as news messages.
209
      Common knowledge such as dates, public holidays, and notable events like Black Friday sales,
210
      aligns with the time series and is known in advance, so it can be safely incorporated into forecasting
211   without causing information leakage.
212
213   System-level limited predictions from domain experts, like weather report or market analyses, offer
214
      valuable high-level domain insights not present in historical data. Although these predictions, based
      on expert knowledge, are infrequent and often expressed in broad, imprecise terms, they are available
215
      prior to forecasting and help guide the overall direction of predictions without data leakage.


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Hypothesized or controlled events arise from planned actions within a system, such as forecasting
217   sales after a marketing campaign or predicting user engagement after a software update. These
218   insights are pre-existing and specific to the system, allowing for "what-if" scenario forecasting while
219   avoiding information leakage.
220
      Consider a weather forecasting model predicting rainfall patterns. First, common knowledge like the
221
      month, date, and time offers essential seasonal context—rainfall varies between seasons and times
222
      of day. This allows the model to align with broader seasonal trends without needing an extensive
223   historical window. Second, system-level limited predictions from weather stations provide valuable
224   insights, though these forecasts may be infrequent and vague, such as predicting the likelihood of
225   rain without precise amounts. By correlating these atmospheric predictions with observed rainfall,
226   the model can generate more precise time series forecasts at a higher resolution. Finally, controlled
227   events, like artificial rainmaking, offer a unique input. Knowing in advance when and where such
228   events will occur allows the model to adjust its predictions accordingly. Combining these three types
229   of information enables the model to produce more accurate and context-aware forecasts.
230   In many multi-channel time series forecasting (TSF) tasks, news messages are not directly tied to
231   specific time series channels, and each channel may react differently to the same news. Consequently,
232   the model must infer the impact of each news item on individual channels based on their descriptions.
233   For example, a forecast task involve predicting time series of rainfall, temperature, and air pressure.
234   A news item about an approaching storm could directly influence the rainfall channel, while affecting
235   temperature and air pressure to varying degrees. The model needs to learn these relationships to
236   provide accurate predictions across all channels.
237   Thus, the TGTSF task is defined by three principal inputs: the time series data, news messages,
238   and channel descriptions. This setup not only capitalizes on dynamic events but also integrates
239   domain knowledge via channel descriptions. Such integration is crucial for the model to comprehend
240   and learn the spatial correlations among channels, thereby enhancing the forecasting accuracy with
241   nuanced contextual understanding.
242   Formally, the TGTSF task seeks to model the potentially complex joint distribution of future values in
243   a multi-channel sequence. This modeling involves the integration of historical time series data, textual
244   information from news messages, and channel descriptions over a specified look-back window:

251   4    TGF ORECASTER : A BASELINE M ODEL FOR T HE TGTSF TASK
252   To validate the TGTSF task, we developed TGForecaster, a streamlined transformer model designed
253   for multimodal fusion with cross-attention. Illustrated in Fig. 2, this model harnesses textual informa-
254   tion to enhance the accuracy and relevance of time series forecasting. It employs a reimplemented
255   PatchTST encoder for processing time series data and leverages off-the-shelf, pretrained text models
256   for pre-embedding textual inputs into vector sequences across the time dimension, allowing for
257   effective modality fusion in the embedding space.
258   The first key innovation of TGForecaster is its Time-Synchronized Text Embedding mechanism.
259   Since events occur at specific time steps, it is crucial to align the corresponding news messages to
260   those times. Using text embeddings, we can represent these news messages as uniform embedding
261   vectors while retaining their semantic meaning. For each time step, we create a list of news message
262   embeddings and stack these lists along the time dimension to form a news embedding tensor. To
263   ensure valid tensor dimensions, each list is zero-padded to match the maximum number of news
264   items across time steps.
265   Another novel component of TGForecaster is the integration of a cross-attention layer for Text
266   Guided Channel Independent (TGCI) learning. This layer is essential for fusing news content
267   with channel descriptions. Inspired by the attention mechanism in recommendation systems, we treat
268   the news as the key and value, and the channel descriptions as queries. This allows the model to
269   compute the relevance of each news item to every channel, generating a composite embedding for
      each channel based on the news.

357   5.4    S TEAM G AME DATASET
358
      A key application of TGTSF is in sales forecasting and decision-making. To illustrate this, we
359
      compiled data on online player counts from several of the most popular games a leading online video
360   game distribution platform. This dataset also includes records of all game updates and events. On a
361   view of game developer, we consider these updates and events as controlled events. The time series
362   exhibits a weekly periodicity, with player activity typically peaking on weekends. Announcements
363   from game developers frequently trigger spikes in activity, reflecting player enthusiasm for new

529   7       C ONCLUSIONS AND D ISCUSSION
530
      This paper addresses a critical roadblock in time series forecasting: information insufficiency. We
531
      introduced Text-Guided Time Series Forecasting (TGTSF), a new approach that integrates textual cues
532   to enrich the models with external information and system knowledge. We developed and released four
533   TGTSF datasets, each crafted to validate different aspects of the task and model. Our straightforward
534   yet effective TGForecaster model demonstrates that textual guidance can significantly enhance time
535   series modeling by mitigating the average predictions typically resulting from information scarcity.
536
      While the TGForecaster effectively validates the TGTSF task, it does not fully comprehend the
537
      semantics of the text, such as extracting correlations among channels automatically. Future work
538   will focus on advancing the model’s semantic understanding and its ability to autonomously discern
539   intricate relationships within the data.

712
713   B.1   T EXT E MBEDDING M ODEL
714
715
      Text embedding models have undergone significant advancements, providing efficient and seman-
      tically rich vector representations of textual information. Early transformer-based models like
716
      BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) encode sentences into embeddings by pretraining on masked language
717
      modeling tasks, enabling them to capture contextual semantics. However, BERT embeddings are not
718   specifically optimized for tasks requiring fine-grained semantic similarity, prompting the development
719   of more task-specific models.
720
721
      MPNet (Song et al., 2020) and MiniLM (Wang et al., 2020) build upon BERT () by introducing novel
      architectural and pretraining strategies. MPNet combines masked language modeling with permuted
722
      sequence prediction, allowing for better contextual understanding and token dependencies. MiniLM,
723
      on the other hand, employs knowledge distillation to create smaller, faster models that retain high
724   performance, making them ideal for resource-constrained applications.
725
726   OpenAI’s embedding models (ope) represent another major step forward, leveraging large-scale
727
      proprietary transformer architectures. These embeddings are designed to excel in tasks like semantic
      search, classification, and similarity, offering generalizability and strong performance across a variety
728
      of applications. They also incorporate dimensional flexibility, allowing embeddings to be truncated
729
      or adjusted based on application needs, as seen with the Matryoshka embedding technique. This
730   technique allows embeddings to maintain their semantic integrity even when their dimensions are
731   reduced, offering scalability and adaptability.
732
733
      A key property of text embeddings is their compatibility with similarity measures like cosine simi-
      larity. By projecting text into a shared semantic space, cosine similarity enables the computation of
734
      semantic closeness between embeddings, making it a foundational operation for tasks like clustering,
735
      retrieval, and alignment between modalities. This capability is crucial in applications requiring robust
736   generalization across diverse textual expressions.
737
738   Together, these advancements have expanded the utility of text embeddings in various domains,
739
      including information retrieval, natural language understanding, and multimodal learning tasks. Our
      work builds on these innovations by leveraging pre-trained text embeddings for aligning textual
740
      semantics with time series patterns, ensuring robust causal modeling and efficient text-guided time
741
      series forecasting.
742
743   B.2   T IME S ERIES A NALYSIS WITH T EXT E MBEDDING
744
      Adding more information to time series by incorporating heterogeneous information has been a
745   long-studied topic, with several works opting to use text embeddings as input.
746
747   In the financial field, where time series are often more correlated to external information, several
748
      works (Sawhney et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024b) have used text embeddings as external graph relation-
      ships to capture the correlations between keywords and stock descriptions, further influencing the
749
      ranking process in stock trading. More recently, a line of works (Liu et al., 2024a; Jia et al., 2024)
750
      has sought to enrich time series data by adding news text embeddings to the time series embeddings.
751   However, these methods still face limitations in solving information insufficiency, as they do not
752   incorporate causal information that could guide the model in predicting time series patterns driven
753   by external events. Additionally, these works primarily use external text embeddings to expand the
754   lookback window, without fully exploiting the underlying properties of the text embeddings.
755



                                                         14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      To tackle these challenges, we introduce the Time-Series Guided Text Forecasting (TGTSF) model,
757   which expands traditional time series forecasting by incorporating external textual data that offers
758   causal insights. Unlike previous approaches that use text embeddings simply as supplementary
759   information, TGTSF leverages the text to provide causal guidance, aligning textual data with time
760   series patterns. Through the integration of TGCI, we can effectively extract channel-dynamic news
761   correlations from the pre-trained text embeddings, enabling the model to adapt to the specific distri-
762   butions of different time series channels. This allows the model to make more accurate predictions by
763   incorporating both the semantic meaning of the text and its causal relationship with the time series
764   data.

      C    A BOUT P REDICTABILITY OF T REND
767   In our study, we define "trend" as patterns that exhibit very low frequency while lacking periodicity
768   within the observed time window, rather than simple exponential or linear patterns. For instance, the
769   pressure channel in our Weather-Captioned dataset exemplifies this with its irregular low-frequency
770   fluctuations, which appear to be random and non-periodic. Such randomness hampers the model’s
771   ability to learn stable patterns when relying solely on historical time series data.
772   However, these low-frequency patterns often correlate with external influences—for example, a drop
773   in temperature due to cold air can significantly increase atmospheric pressure. By integrating this
774   type of external information, our model is designed to discern causal relationships between such
775   environmental factors and the observed low-frequency trends, thereby enhancing predictability.
776
      For a practical illustration, please refer to the pressure (p-bar) channel in Fig. 4 and Fig. 5. This
777
      channel displays non-periodic fluctuations, which the traditional patchTST model even struggles
778   produce a valid forecasting. In contrast, our TGTSF model, which incorporates external textual cues,
779   successfully tracks these changes, demonstrating the effectiveness of including external information
780   for predicting complex trends.

973
974    TGForecaster is designed to learn causal relationships between events described in text and their
975    corresponding time series patterns. While not explicitly an alignment model, it effectively aligns
976    the semantic meaning of text with the time series data it impacts. The model generates time series
977    patterns guided by the textual information, and its performance varies based on the quality of the text
978
       input:

1327   L    TGTSF B ENCHMARK DATASETS
1328
       We designed the TGTSF benchmark to include four datasets of varying complexity, each tailored to
1329
       evaluate specific aspects of model performance. Together, these datasets form a progression from
1330
       simple, interpretable scenarios to challenging, real-world applications, providing a comprehensive
1331   evaluation framework for text-guided time series forecasting models.
1332
1333   1. Toy Dataset The Toy dataset is intentionally designed with simple and straightforward patterns,
1334
       making it easy to analyze and interpret. However, the dataset includes sudden changes in patterns
       that are impossible to predict without text guidance. This ensures the model’s ability to adhere to
1335
       textual cues is effectively tested in a controlled environment. It serves as a foundation for validating
1336
       whether the model can extract and use textual guidance to forecast time series.
1337
1338   2. Electricity Dataset The Electricity dataset introduces real-world data with common textual features
1339   like day of the week or public holidays. While the textual information is relatively simple, it tests
1340
       the model’s ability to utilize such structured cues for forecasting. Additionally, as a widely-used
       off-the-shelf dataset, it allows for easy comparison with existing methods, providing a baseline for
1341
       evaluating TGTSF’s performance.
1342
1343   3. Weather-Captioned Dataset The Weather-captioned dataset represents a semi-controlled environ-
1344   ment designed to rigorously test the model’s ability to learn causal relationships between text and time
1345   series patterns. It also evaluates the model’s text-guided channel independence and generalizability.
1346
       By simulating a scenario where text and time series data are strongly correlated, this dataset bridges
       the gap between controlled tests and more complex real-world challenges.
1347
1348   4. Steam Dataset The Steam dataset is a fully real-world dataset that tests TGTSF in a practical
1349   industrial context. Its patterns are noisy and random, making it highly challenging. This dataset
       showcases TGTSF’s ability to perform well in realistic scenarios. Although we cannot release the


                                                          25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       dataset due to intellectual property restrictions, we will provide detailed instructions for replicating it
1351   in the code repository.
1352
1353   Comprehensive Benchmark Objectives
1354   The TGTSF benchmark is designed to address multiple objectives:
1355
1356               • Interpretability and Validation: The simpler Toy and Electricity datasets help researchers
1357                 validate their models and understand their behavior in controlled environments.
1358               • Performance Testing in Complex Scenarios: The Weather-captioned and Steam datasets
1359                 challenge the models in semi-controlled and real-world settings, ensuring they are robust
1360                 and capable of handling practical applications.
1361
1362   This benchmark is not merely a ranking tool but a framework to help researchers analyze and improve
1363
       their models’ behaviors across varying levels of complexity. We see this as a starting point for the
       community and hope it will inspire researchers to contribute additional datasets, further expanding
1364
       and enriching the TGTSF benchmark for future advancements in this field.

       L.4.4    U SE OF L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS FOR P REPROCESSING THE W EATHER DATASET
1440
1441   We would like to clarify that the use of Large Language Models (LLMs) in this work is strictly
1442   limited to the preprocessing and creation of the Weather-Captioned dataset. LLMs are not part of our
1443   model or method, nor do they contribute to the training or inference process of TGForecaster. The
1444   Weather-Captioned dataset is intended to serve as an off-the-shelf, text-time synchronized benchmark
1445   dataset with raw text and pre-embedded text embeddings as optional inputs.
1446   The primary reason for using LLMs to preprocess this dataset is to generate diverse and correlated
1447   textual descriptions, ensuring a richer corpus for training and evaluation. By incorporating varied
1448   expressions, we enable the model to generalize to different textual forms while aligning the semantic
1449   meaning of text with time series patterns. For instance, the descriptions “The morning will be sunny,
1450   but clouds will increase in the afternoon with a chance of light rain” and “The day starts with clear
1451   skies, gradually turning cloudy with some rain in the afternoon” carry the same semantic information
1452   but differ in expression. This diversity enhances the robustness of the benchmark and validates the
1453   model’s generalization capabilities.
1454   Additionally, the raw data source for this dataset often includes general weather reports in text
1455   form, accompanied by coarse numerical updates every six hours. While numerical values such as
1456   {High_Temp: 25, Low_Temp: 20, Temp_Trend: slightly increasing, Wind_Speed: 5, Wind_Direction:
1457   East} are available, they lack the precision required for reliable exogenous variables. Moreover, the
       raw text contains rich semantic details—such as qualitative weather descriptions—that cannot be


                                                          27
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1458
       effectively captured using numerical values or one-hot encoding. Using text embeddings allows the
1459   model to leverage both semantic and numerical information more effectively.
1460
1461   In summary, the LLM preprocessing step is solely for dataset preparation and corpus diversity, ensur-
1462
       ing that the Weather-Captioned dataset is suitable for evaluating text-guided time series forecasting
       models. Our method does not rely on any LLM capabilities, and the inclusion of LLM-generated text
1463
       is not a necessary step for TGTSF or any similar model. We will include raw data samples in the
1464
       final paper to provide greater clarity and avoid any misunderstandings.

               • p (mbar): Atmospheric pressure measured in millibars. It indicates the weight of the air
1575
                 above the point of measurement.

1577           • T (degC): Temperature at the point of observation, measured in degrees Celsius.
1578           • Tpot (K): Potential temperature, given in Kelvin. This is the temperature that a parcel of air
1579             would have if it were brought adiabatically to a standard reference pressure, often used to
1580             compare temperatures at different pressures in a thermodynamically consistent way.
1581           • Tdew (degC): Dew point temperature in degrees Celsius. It’s the temperature to which air
1582             must be cooled, at constant pressure and water vapor content, for saturation to occur. A
1583             lower dew point means dryer air.
1584           • rh (%): Relative humidity, expressed as a percentage. It measures the amount of moisture in
1585             the air relative to the maximum amount of moisture the air can hold at that temperature.
1586           • VPmax (mbar): Maximum vapor pressure, in millibars. It represents the maximum amount
1587             of moisture that the air can hold at a given temperature.
1588
               • VPact (mbar): Actual vapor pressure, in millibars. It’s the current amount of water vapor
1589             present in the air.
1590
               • VPdef (mbar): Vapor pressure deficit, in millibars. The difference between the maximum
1591
                 vapor pressure and the actual vapor pressure; it indicates how much more moisture the air
1592
                 can hold before saturation.
1593
1594
               • sh (g/kg): Specific humidity, the mass of water vapor in a given mass of air, including the
                 water vapor. It’s measured in grams of water vapor per kilogram of air.
1595
1596           • H2OC (mmol/mol): Water vapor concentration, expressed in millimoles of water per mole
1597             of air. It’s another way to quantify the amount of moisture in the air.
1598           • rho (g/m³): Air density, measured in grams per cubic meter. It indicates the mass of air in a
1599             given volume and varies with temperature, pressure, and moisture content.
1600           • wv (m/s): Wind velocity, the speed of the wind measured in meters per second.
