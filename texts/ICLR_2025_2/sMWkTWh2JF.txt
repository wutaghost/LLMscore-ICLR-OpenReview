009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Intelligent embodied agents need to quickly adapt to new scenarios by integrating
012            long histories of experience into decision-making. For instance, a robot in an
013            unfamiliar house initially wouldn’t know the locations of objects needed for tasks
014            and might perform inefficiently. However, as it gathers more experience, it should
015            learn the layout of its environment and remember where objects are, allowing it to
016            complete new tasks more efficiently. To enable such rapid adaptation to new tasks,
017            we present ReLIC, a new approach for in-context reinforcement learning (RL) for
018
               embodied agents. With ReLIC, agents are capable of adapting to new environments
               using 64,000 steps of in-context experience with full attention while being trained
019
               through self-generated experience via RL. We achieve this by proposing a novel pol-
020
               icy update scheme for on-policy RL called “partial updates” as well as a Sink-KV
021            mechanism that enables effective utilization of a long observation history for em-
022            bodied agents. Our method outperforms a variety of meta-RL baselines in adapting
023            to unseen houses in an embodied multi-object navigation task. In addition, we find
024            that ReLIC is capable of few-shot imitation learning despite never being trained
025            with expert demonstrations. We also provide a comprehensive analysis of ReLIC,
026            highlighting that the combination of large-scale RL training, the proposed partial
027            updates scheme, and the Sink-KV are essential for effective in-context learning.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   A desired capability of intelligent embodied agents is to rapidly adapt to new scenarios through
033
      experience. An essential requirement for this capability is integrating a long history of experience
      into decision-making to enable an agent to accumulate knowledge about the new scenario that it is
034
      encountering. For example, a robot placed in an unseen house initially has no knowledge of the home
035
      layout and where to find objects. The robot should leverage its history of experiences of completing
036   tasks in this new home to learn the home layout details, where to find objects, and how to act to
037   complete tasks successfully.
038
039
      To achieve adaptation of decision-making to new tasks, prior work has leveraged a technique called
      in-context reinforcement learning (RL) where an agent is trained with RL to utilize past experience
040
      in an environment (Wang et al., 2016; Team et al., 2023; Duan et al., 2016; Grigsby et al., 2023;
041
      Melo, 2022). By using sequence models over a history of interactions in an environment, these
042   methods adapt to new scenarios by conditioning policy actions on this context of interaction history
043   without updating the policy parameters. While in-context RL has demonstrated the ability to scale to
044   a context length of a few thousand agent steps (Team et al., 2023; Grigsby et al., 2023), this falls short
045   of the needs of embodied AI where single tasks by themselves can span thousands of steps (Szot
046   et al., 2021). As a result, the agent cannot learn from multiple task examples because the context
047   required for multiple tasks cannot be accommodated within the policy context. Furthermore, prior
048   work typically focuses on non-visual tasks (Grigsby et al., 2023; Melo, 2022; Ni et al., 2023), where
049   larger histories are easier to incorporate due to the compact state representation.
050   In this work, we propose a new algorithm for in-context RL, which enables effectively utilizing and
051   scaling to 64,000 steps of in-context experience in partially observable, visual navigation tasks. Our
052   proposed method called Reinforcement Learning In Context (ReLIC), achieves this by leveraging
053   a novel update and data collection technique for training with long training contexts in on-policy
      RL. Using a long context for existing RL algorithms is prohibitively sample inefficient, as the agent

      Scaling context length. Another related area of research scaling the context length of transformers.
125
      Prior work extend the context length using a compressed representation of the old context, either as
126   a recurrent memory or a specialized token (Dai et al., 2019; Munkhdalai et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
127   2024). Other work address the memory and computational inefficiencies of the attention method by
128   approximating it (Beltagy et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020a) or by doing system-level optimization
129   (Dao, 2023). Another direction is context extrapolation at inference time either by changing the
130   position encoding (Su et al., 2023; Press et al., 2022) or by introducing attention sink (Xiao et al.,
131   2023). Our work utilizes the system-level optimized attention (Dao, 2023) and extends attention
132   sinks for on-policy RL in Embodied AI.
133   Embodied AI. Prior work in Embodied AI has primarily concentrated on the single episode evaluation
134   setting, where an agent is randomly initialized in the environment at the beginning of each episode and
135   is tasked with taking the shortest exploratory path to a single goal specified in every episode (Wijmans
136   et al., 2019; Yadav et al., 2022). In contrast, Wani et al. (2020) introduced the multi-ON benchmark,
137   which extends the complexity of the original task by requiring the agent to navigate to a series of
138   goal objects in a specified order within a single episode. Here, the agent must utilize information
139   acquired during its journey to previous goals to navigate more efficiently to subsequent locations. Go
140
      to anything (GOAT) (Chang et al., 2023), extended this to the multi-modal goal setting, providing

141
      setting where the agent is randomly instantiated in the environment after a successful or failed trial
142
      but has access to the prior episode history.

145   3     M ETHOD
146
147   We introduce Reinforcement Learning In Context (ReLIC) which enables agents to in-context adapt
148
      to new episodes without any re-training. ReLIC is built using a transformer policy architecture that
      operates over a long sequence of multi-episode observations and is trained with online RL. The
149
      novelty of ReLIC is changing the base RL algorithm to more frequently update the policy with
150
      increasingly longer contexts within a policy rollout and adding Sink-KV to give the model the ability
151   to avoid attending to low-information context. Section 3.1 provides the general problem setting
152   of adapting to new episodes. Section 3.2 details the transformer policy architecture. Section 3.3
153   describes the novel update scheme of ReLIC. Finally, Section 3.4 goes over implementation details.
154
155   3.1   P ROBLEM S ETTING
156
157   We study the problem of adaptation to new scenarios in the formalism of meta-RL (Beck et al.,
158   2023). We have a distribution     of training POMDPs Mi ∼ p(M), where each Mi is defined by tuple
159    Si , Si0 , Oi , A, T , γ, Ri for observations Oi , states Si which are not revealed to the agent, starting
160   state distribution Si0 , action space A, transition function T , discount factor γ, and reward Ri . In our
161   setting, the states, observations, and reward vary per POMDP, while the action space, discount factor,
      and transition function is shared between all POMDPs.

      From a starting state s0 ∼ Si0 , a policy π, mapping observations to a distribution over actions, is
163   rolled out for an episode which is a sequence of interactions until a maximum number of timesteps,

170   3.2   R E LIC P OLICY A RCHITECTURE
171
172
173   Similar to prior work (Grigsby et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023), ReLIC implements in-context RL via
174
      a transformer sequence model that operates over a history of interactions spanning multiple episodes.
      At step t within a trial, ReLIC predicts current action at based on the entire sequence of previous
175
      observations o1 , . . . , ot which may span multiple episodes. In the embodied AI settings we study,
176
      the observation ot consists of an egocentric RGB observation from the robot’s head camera along
177   with proprioceptive information and a specification of the current goal. Each of these observation
178   components are encoded using a separate observation encoding network, and the embeddings are
179   concatenated to form a single observation embedding et . A causal transformer network (Vaswani
180   et al., 2023) hθ inputs the sequence of embeddings hθ (e1 , . . . , et ). From the transformer output, a
181   linear layer then predicts the actions.
182
      The transformer model hθ thus bears the responsibility of in-context learning by leveraging associ-
183   ations between observations within a trial. This burden especially poses a challenge in our setting
184   of embodied AI since the transformer must attend over a history of thousands of egocentric visual
185   observations. Subsequent visual observations are highly correlated, as the agent only takes one action
186   between observations. Knowing which observations are relevant to attend to in deciding the current
187   action is thus a challenging problem. In this work, we build our architecture around full attention
188   transformers using the same architecture as the LLaMA language model (Touvron et al., 2023), but
189   modify the number of layers and hidden dimension size to appropriately reduce the parameter count
190   for our setting.
191   We also introduce an architectural modification to the transformer called Sink-KV to improve the
192   transformer’s ability to attend over a long history of visual experience from an embodied agent.
193   Building off the intuition that learning to attend over a long sequence of visual observations is
194   challenging, we introduce additional flexibility into the core attention operation by prepending the key
195   and value vectors with a per-layer learnable sequence of sink KV vectors. Specifically, recall that for
196   an input sequence X ∈ Rn×d of n inputs of embedding dimension d, the attention operator projects
197
      X to keys, queries and values notated as K, Q, V respectively and all elements of Rn×d where
      we assume  all⊤hidden  dimensions are d for simplicity. The standard attention operation computes
198
199   softmax QK √
                   d
                        V . We  modify calculating the attention scores by introducing learnable vectors
200   Ks , Vs ∈ Rs×d where s is the specified number of “sinks”. We then prepend Ks , Vs to the K, V of
201   the input sequence before calculating the attention. Note that the output of the attention operation
202   is still n × d, as in the regular attention operation, as the query vector has no added component.
203   We repeat this process for each attention layer of the transformer, introducing a new Ks , Vs in each
204   attention operation. Sink-KV only results in nlayers × s × d more parameters, which is 0.046% of
205   the 4.5M parameter policy used in this work.
206   Sink-KV gives the sequence model more flexibility on how to attend over the input. Prior works
207   observe that due to the softmax in the attention, the model is forced to attend to at least one token from
208   the input (Miller, 2023; Xiao et al., 2023). Sink-KV removes this requirement by adding learnable
209   vectors to the key and value. In sequences of embodied visual experiences, this is important as
210   attention heads can avoid attending over any inputs when there is no new visual information in the
211   current observations. This flexibility helps the agent operate over longer sequence lengths.
212   The calculation of the attentions scores S using the Softmax forces the tokens to attend to values V ,
213   even if all available values do not hold any useful information, since the sum of the scores is 1 (Miller,
214   2023). This is especially harmful in cases where the task requires exploration. As the agent explores
215   more, a more useful information may appear in the sequence. If the agent is forced to attend to low
      information tokens at the beginning of the exploration, it will introduce noise to the attention layers.

237     3.3    R E LIC L EARNING
238
239     ReLIC is updated through online RL, namely PPO (Schulman et al., 2017). However, for the agent to
240
        be able to leverage a long context window for in-context RL, it must also be trained with this long
        context window. PPO collects a batch of data for learning by “rolling out” the current policy for a
241
        sequence of T interactions in an environment. To operate on a long context window spanning an
242
        entire trial, the agent must collect a rollout of data that consists of this entire trial. This is challenging
243     because, in the embodied tasks we consider, we seek to train agents on trials lasting over 64k steps,
244     which consists of at least 130 episodes. As typical with PPO, to speed up data collection and increase
245     the update batch size we use multiple environment workers each running a simulation instance that
246     the policy interacts with in parallel. With 32 environment workers, this corresponds to ≈ 130k
247     environment steps between every policy update. PPO policies trained in common embodied AI tasks,
248     such as O BJECT NAV, have only 128 steps between updates and require ≈ 50k updates to converge
249     (for 32 environment workers, 128 steps per worker between updates and 200M environment steps
250     required for convergence) (Yadav et al., 2022). Executing a similar number of updates would require
251
        ReLIC to collect ≈ 6 billion environment interactions.
252     ReLIC addresses this problem of sample inefficiency by introducing a partial update scheme where
253     the policy is updated multiple times throughout a rollout. First, at the start of a rollout of length T , all
254     environment workers are reset to the start of a new episode. Define the number of partial updates as
255     K. At step i ∈ [0, T ] in the rollout, the policy is operating with a context length of i − 1 previous
256     observations to determine the action at step i. Every T /K samples in the rollout, we update the
257
        policy. Therefore, at update N within the rollout, the agent has collected N T /K of the T samples
        in the rollout. The agent is updated using a context window of size N T /K, however, the PPO loss
258
        is only applied to the final T /K outputs. The policy is changing every T /K samples in the rollout,
259
        so the policy forward pass must be recalculated for the entire N T /K window rather than caching
260     the previous (N − 1)T /K activations. In the last update in the rollout, after collecting the last T /K
261     steps, we update the policy with the loss applied on all steps in the rollout. We refer to this step as
262     full update. At the start of a new rollout, the context window is cleared and the environment workers
263     again reset to new episodes.

      4.3   R E LIC A BLATIONS AND A NALYSIS
390
391   We demonstrate that the partial updates in ReLIC and Sink-KV are crucial to learning with RL
392   over long context windows and acquiring ICL capabilities. We run these ablations in the smaller
393   ReplicaCAD (Szot et al., 2021) scenes to make methods faster to train, but other details of the task
394   remain the same. We then show that ICL emerges later in the training and the context length in ReLIC
395   can be even further increased.
396   No Partial Updates. Firstly, we remove the partial updates in ReLIC and find that it performs poorly
397   (Figure 3b), achieving 40% lesser SR at the first episode. This model also shows little ICL abilities
398   with the SR only increasing 5% by the end of the trial versus a 25% increase when using partial
399   updates.
400
      Sink-KV. Next, we demonstrate that using Sink-KV is necessary for sample-efficient in-context RL
401
      learning. We trained the model on ReplicaCAD with and without attention sinks. The learning curves
402   in Figure 3c shows that learning is more stable and faster with Sink-KV which achieves 90% success
403   rate at 200M steps. It also shows that Sink-KV performs similar to Softmax One (Miller, 2023),
404   referred to as Sink K0 V0 . Without attention sink mechanisms, learning is slow and achieves less than
405   40% success rate after 200M steps and reaching 64% at 300M steps. Using sink token (Xiao et al.,
406   2023), the training becomes unstable, achieving 40% success rate at 200M steps training and reaching
407   80% success rate at 300M steps. The details of the different sink attentions, their implementations
408   and how the attention heads use the Sink-KV can be found in Appendix E.
409   Training Steps v.s. ICL Abilities. We find that ReLIC only acquires ICL capabilities after sufficient
410   RL training. As demonstrated in Figure 3a, the agent is only capable of ICL after 157M steps
411   of training. Models trained for 52M and 157M remain at constant success with more in-context
412   experience. Further training does more than just increase the base agent performance in the first
413   episode of the trial. From 262M steps to 367M steps, the agent base performance increases by 2%, yet
414   the performance after 15 episodes of ICL performance increases 10%. This demonstrates that further
415   training is not only improving the base capabilities of the agent to find objects, but also improving
416
      the agent’s ability to utilize its context across long trials spanning many episodes.
417   Context length generalization. Next, we push the abilities of ReLIC to in-context learn over contexts
418   much larger than what is seen during training. In this experiment, we evaluate ReLIC model, trained
419   with 4k context length, on 32k steps of experience, which is enough to fit 80 episode trials in context.
420   Assuming that the simulator is operating at 10Hz, this is almost 1 hour of agent experience within
421   the context window. Note that for this experiment, we use our best checkpoint, which is trained
422
      for 1B steps. The results demonstrate that ReLIC can generalize to contexts 8× larger at inference.
      Figure 4a shows ReLIC is able to further increase the success rate to over 55% after 80 in context
423
      episodes and consistently maintains performance above 50% after 20 in-context episodes.
424
425   64k steps trials. Finally, we investigate scaling training ReLIC with 64k context length. We use the
426   same hyperparameters as Section 4.2, but increase the number of partial updates per rollout such that
427   the policy is updated every 256 steps, the same number of steps used in ReLIC. Fig. 4b shows that
428
      the model can in-context learn over 175 episode and continue to improve success rate. More details
      are available at Appendix C.5.

431   tively analyze what the agent attends to in successful and failure episodes. Finally, in Appendix C.3,
      we show that not shuffling episodes in the context during training leads to worse performance.

460   4.4 E MERGENT F EW-S HOT I MITATION L EARNING
461   In addition to learning in-context from self-generated experience in an environment, ReLIC can also
462   use its context to learn from demonstrations provided by an external agent or expert, despite never
463   being trained on demonstrations and only learning from self-generated experience. We consider the
464   setting of few-shot imitation learning (Duan et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2020b) where an agent is given
465   a set of trajectories {τ1 , . . . , τN } demonstrating behavior reaching desired goals {g1 , . . . , gN }. The
466   agent must then achieve a new gN +1 in new environment configurations using these demonstrations.
467   ReLIC is able to few-shot imitation learn by taking the expert demonstration as input via the context.
468
      Specifically, we generate N expert shortest path trajectories navigating to random objects from
      random start positions in an unseen home layout. The success rate of these demos is around 80%
469
      due to object occlusions hindering the shortest path agent from viewing the target object which is
470
      required for success. These N trajectories are inserted into the context of ReLIC and the agent is
471   instructed to navigate to a new object in the environment.
472
473   In Figure 4c we show that ReLIC can utilize these expert demonstrations despite never seeing such
474
      shortest paths during training. Figure 4c shows the success rate of ReLIC in a single episode after
      conditioning on some number of shortest path demonstrations. More demonstrations cover more of
475
      the house and the agent is able to improve navigation success. We also compare to the success rate of
476
      an agent that has N episodes of experience in the house as opposed to N demonstrations. Using the
477   demonstrations results in better performance with 5% higher success rate for N = 16.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
500   The ability of an agent to rapidly adapt to new environments is crucial for successful Embodied
501   AI tasks. We introduced ReLIC, an in-context RL method that enables the agent to adapt to new
502   environments by in-context learning with up to 64k environment interactions and visual observations.
503   We studied the two main components of ReLIC: partial updates and the Sink-KV and showed both
504   are necessary for achieving such in-context learning. We showed that ReLIC results in significantly
505
      better performance on a challenging long-sequence visual task compared to the baselines.
506   Limitations of the approach are that we found for ICL to emerge, it requires a diverse training dataset
507   on which the model can not overfit. There is no incentive for the model to learn to use the context if
508   it can overfit the task. We were able to address that in the dataset generation by creating different
509   object arrangements for each scene which made it challenging for the model to memorize the objects
510   arrangements. Another is that our study only focuses on several environments. Future work can
511
      explore this same study in more varied environments such as a mobile manipulation task where an
      agent needs to rearrange objects throughout the scene. Finally, ReLIC requires large amounts of
512
      RL training to obtain in-context learning capabilities. The success of ReLIC in ExtObjNav is also
513
      relative low for practical applications. One path to improving this performance is to scale training
514   with more RL training and in-context learning. Figure 4b shows the performance is still improving
515   after 64k steps of in-context experience. Figure 16 also shows that ReLIC is still improving after 1
516   billion RL steps. Another path is to improve ability to generalize to new scenes by increasing the
517   number of training scenes from the 37 in the HSSD dataset used in ExtObjNav through procedurally
518   generated scenes.

717   The E XT O BJ NAV is a small object navigation task. We use the same training (37) and validation
718   (12) scenes as Yenamandra et al. (2023). The data is generated by randomly placing objects from
719   the 20 object types, a subset of the YCB Calli et al. (2015) dataset, on random receptacles. The data
720
      is generated by sampling between 30 to 40 object instances and placing them on receptacles, and
      subsequent filtering of the objects that are not reachable by the agent. The filtering is done by placing
721
      the agent in front of the object and evaluating whether the agent can meet the success criteria. If the
722
      agent can meet the critera, we retain the object. Otherwise, we discard the object. The distribution of
723   the objects and object types are in Figure 6.
724
725   The reward function is defined as follows:
726   • Change in geodesic distance to the closest object rd = −∆d where d is the geodesic distance to
727     the closest object. The closest object can change across the episode.
728   • Slack reward of −0.001.
729   • Succes reward of 2.
730
731
      The episode is considered a success if the agent selects the Stop action while it is within 2 meters of
      an instance of the target type and has 10 pixels of this instance in the view.

739   The workers and the batch size. We use 20 environment workers per GPU. Since we use 4 GPUs in

741   gradient for 10 micro batches on the 4 GPUs which makes the effective batch size 40.
742   RL algorithm. We use PPO Schulman et al. (2017) to train the model with γ = 0.99, τ = 0.95,

      embeddings in rollout in low-precision storage, specifically in FP16 precision.

       E.1    M OTIVATION
1027
1028   The vanilla attention is the component responsible for the interaction between the tokens in the
1029   sequence. The output for each token is calculated by weighting the value of all tokens. The input to
1030   the attention layer is the embeddings of the input tokens E ∈ Rn×d where n is the number of input
1031   tokens and d is the dimension of the embeddings.
1032   First the embeddings E are linearly projected to the
                                                          √Key K, Value V and Query Q. Then the attention
1033   scores are calculated using S = Softmax(QK T / dk ) where dk is the dimensions of the keys. The
1034   output A is calculated as a weighted sum of the values V , A = SV .
1035
       The calculation of the attentions scores S using the Softmax forces the tokens to attend to values V ,
1036
       even if all available values do not hold any useful information, since the sum of the scores is 1 (Miller,
1037
       2023). This is especially harmful in cases where the task requires exploration. As the agent explores
1038   more, a more useful information may appear in the sequence. If the agent is forced to attend to low
1039   information tokens at the beginning of the exploration, it will introduce noise to the attention layers.
1040
1041   E.2    S OLUTIONS
1042
1043   Softmax One from Miller (2023)P addresses this issue by adding 1 to the denominator of the Softmax,
1044   Softmax1 (xi ) := exp(xi )/(1 + j exp(xj )), which is equivalent to having a token with k = 0 and
1045   v = 0. This gives the model the ability to have 0 attention score to all tokens, we refer to Softmax
1046   One as Sink K0 V0 .
1047   Sink tokens from Xiao et al. (2023) are another approach to address the same issue by prepending
1048   learnable tokens to the input tokens E = [Es ◦ Einput ] where E is the input embedding to the model
1049   and [A ◦ B] indicates concatenation along the sequence dimension of the A and B matrices .
1050
       Sink-KV is a generalization of both approaches. It modifies the attention layer by adding a learnable
1051
       Key Ks ∈ Rn×dk and values Vs ∈ Rn×d . In each attention layer, we simply prepend the learnable
1052
       Ks and Vs to the vanilla keys Kv and values Vv to get the K = [Ks ◦ Kv ] and V = [Vs ◦ Vv ] used to
1053   calculate the attention scores then the attention output.
1054
1055   In the case Ks = 0 and Vs = 0, Sink-KV becomes equivalent to Softmax One. It can also learn the
1056
       same Ks and V s corresponding to the Sink Token since our model is casual and the processing of the
       Sink Token is not affected by the remaining sequence.
1057

       E.3    S INK -KV VARIANTS
1060   We tried a variant of Sink-KV where the either the Value or the Key is set to 0, referred to as Sink
1061   KV0 and Sink K0 V respectively. All variants perform similarly in terms of the success rate as shown
1062   in Figure 13b.
1063
       Figure 13a shows different patterns the model uses the Sink KV0 . The model can assign all attention
1064
       scores to the Sink KV0 , which yields a zero output for the attention head, or assign variable scores
1065
       at different time in the generation. For example, one the attention heads is turned off during the
1066   1st episode of the trial by assigning all attention score to the Sink KV0 then eventually move the
1067   attention to the input tokens in the new episodes. The model is also able to ignore the Sink KV0 by
1068   assigning it 0 attention scores as shown in the figure.
