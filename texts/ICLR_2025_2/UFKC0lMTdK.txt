                                                  A BSTRACT
038
039
               Current large multimodal models (LMMs) face challenges in grounding, which
               requires the model to relate language components to visual entities. Contrary to
040
               the common practice that fine-tunes LMMs with additional grounding supervision,
041
               we find that the grounding ability can in fact emerge in LMMs trained without
042            explicit grounding supervision. To reveal this emerging grounding, we introduce an
043            “attend-and-segment” method which analyzes the attention within standard LMMs
044            to provide a point prompt to a segmentation model (e.g., SAM) and perform pixel-
045            level segmentation. Furthermore, to enhance the grounding ability, we propose
046            D IFF LMM, an LMM utilizing a diffusion-based visual encoder, as opposed to
047            the standard CLIP visual encoder, and trained with the same weak supervision.
048            Without being constrained by the biases and limited scale of grounding-specific
049            supervision data, our approach enables strong visual grounding while preserv-
050
               ing general conversation abilities. We achieve competitive performance on both
               grounding-specific and general visual question answering benchmarks, compared
051
               with grounding LMMs and generalist LMMs, respectively. Notably, we achieve a
052
               44.2 grounding mask recall on grounded conversation generation, outperforming
053            the extensively supervised model GLaMM.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   Large multimodal models (LMMs) (Liu et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2023) have brought
058   the new opportunity of solving vision-language tasks in a general-purpose manner, which are typically
059   built by connecting a visual encoder and a large language model (LLM) and fine-tuned by visual
060   instructions. Currently, one major challenge faced by LMMs is grounding—the key ability of relating
061   language components (e.g., noun phrases) to visual entities (e.g., objects) in a given image (Yu et al.,
062   2016; Krishna et al., 2017). With the grounding ability, LMMs can lift the constraint of text-only
063   responses and address more vision-language tasks in the real world.
064   To equip LMMs with the grounding ability, the common belief is that additional supervision for
065   grounding is necessary, and corresponding architectural modifications need to be introduced. For
066   instance, recent efforts extend the output modality from pure text to bounding boxes (Chen et al.,
067   2023b; Peng et al., 2024), trace points (Xu et al., 2024), or segmentation masks (Lai et al., 2024;
068   Rasheed et al., 2024), by 1) attaching additional modules to the vanilla LMM architecture, and 2)
069   fine-tuning the LMM with grounding supervision. The grounding supervision originates from either
070
      re-purposing existing datasets that contain human-labeled object-level annotations or automatically
      annotating images using other models.
071
072   However, such reliance on strong supervision brings more undesired constraints: 1) Limited training
073   data: Image datasets with high-quality object-level annotations (at most millions of images (Shao
074   et al., 2019; Kuznetsova et al., 2020)) rely on pre-defined categories and are significantly smaller than
075   those with coarse image-text pairs (up to billions (Schuhmann et al., 2022)), so re-purposing such
076
      object-level annotations only results in visual instruction data with limited diversity and quantity.
      Meanwhile, if the object-level annotations are produced by automated models, such annotations
077
      are noisier and less reliable than human-labeled ones (Rasheed et al., 2024). 2) Supervision bias:
078
      Changing the data focus to grounding tasks can lead to catastrophic forgetting (French, 1999) and
079   hurt LMMs’ general conversation capabilities. Furthermore, whether the grounding data are manually
080   annotated (Lin et al., 2014) or pseudo-labeled by other models (Rasheed et al., 2024), they are biased
081   by the annotators’ or models’ knowledge and may fail to align with general human preferences,
082   as these fine-grained annotations can vary significantly among different annotators or models. 3)
083   Generalizability: The grounding supervision is constrained within the visual concepts from either
084   the existing datasets or other models, which contradicts with the ultimate goal of developing a
085   general-purpose assistant for solving open-world problems (Bendale & Boult, 2015). Consequently,
086   the resulting LMMs may be biased by the limited grounding supervision data, generalize poorly to
087
      novel visual concepts and domains, and lose general conversation abilities. Figures 1 and 4 show
      illustrative examples of these limitations.
088
089   To avoid such limitations, the question worth rethinking then arises: Is there an approach to grounding
090   LMMs other than strong supervision? In fact, in this work, we reveal a critical yet previously
091   overlooked fact: LMMs have inherently obtained the grounding ability through the weakly supervised
092   visual instruction tuning. In other words, the grounding ability can emerge in LMMs without
093
      grounding supervision. Echoing prior observations of traditional convolutional neural networks (Zhou
      et al., 2015; 2016), we find that LMMs learn to detect visual entities and relate them with the language
094
      implicitly, during the progress of vision-language learning at the image level.
095
096   We therefore propose a simple and effective “attend-and-segment” strategy to transform this emerging
097   grounding ability into pixel-level segmentation masks. Intuitively, the attention mechanism (Vaswani
098   et al., 2017) in LMMs reveals where the LMM is looking at, and thus provides clues for visual
099
      grounding. We start with a base LMM trained without grounding supervision (Liu et al., 2023),
      and acquire its attention corresponding to the visual input. Though the entire attention map may
100
      be noisy, we locate the point where the LMM is focused on during token generation, and use the
101
      point to accurately prompt a segmentation model (Kirillov et al., 2023) for pixel-level grounding.
102   With this attend-and-segment method, we enable vision-language tasks that directly rely on the
103   grounding capability (e.g., grounded conversation generation (Rasheed et al., 2024)). Remarkably,
104   attend-and-segment does not require explicit grounding supervision like prior work; in contrast,
105   weak supervision from standard visual instruction tuning data is sufficient to achieve performance
106   comparable with or even higher than previous grounding-supervised models. Furthermore, as a
107   general approach, attend-and-segment can be readily integrated with recent generalist LMMs (Li
      et al., 2024a; Tong et al., 2024a), and benefit from their stronger vision-language capabilities.

108
      Furthermore, we introduce a simple solution to enhance the emerging grounding ability of LMMs.
109   Previously, CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) plays a dominant role as the visual encoder of LMMs,
110   due to its vision-language feature alignment. However, CLIP is known to be weak in providing
111   localized visual features (Zhou et al., 2022; Ghiasi et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022), as its pre-training
112   simply aligns the global representations of image-text pairs. Through systematic evaluation on both
113   grounding-specific and general tasks, we find diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022)
114   a better alternative to CLIP, as their text-to-image generation capability enables both vision-language
115   alignment and localized features. Thus, we propose the diffusion-based LMM (D IFF LMM), which
116   augments the CLIP visual encoder of the LMM with a diffusion-based visual encoder, while being
117
      fine-tuned using the same data as the original LMM. To the best of our knowledge, D IFF LMM is the
      first successful integration of diffusion-based visual encoding and LMMs for both visual grounding
118
      and general vision-language tasks. Compared with the original LMM, D IFF LMM enhances the
119
      grounding ability without sacrificing performance in general-purpose vision-language tasks.
120
121   Our extensive experiments demonstrate that LMMs’ grounding capabilities can emerge from weak
122   supervision. Our approach, requiring no additional grounding supervision, does not suffer from
123
      biases in the grounding supervision data, and generalizes better. Despite being trained on less data
      than prior grounding LMMs (Lai et al., 2024; Rasheed et al., 2024), D IFF LMM achieves better or
124
      comparable performance on grounding-specific benchmarks, while adhering to a strong generalist
125
      model for vision-language tasks. To summarize, our contributions are three-fold:
126
127   • Different from prior methods that rely on grounding-specific strong supervision, we show the pos-
128     sibility of grounding LMMs without grounding supervision. Eliminating the need for fine-grained
129     annotations from humans or external models, our approach is more scalable and generalizable.
130
      • We discover a simple and effective approach, attend-and-segment, to achieve pixel-level grounding
        for LMMs by inspecting the attention mechanism in the generation process and prompting a
131
        segmentation model, which requires no grounding supervision or architectural changes.
132
      • We propose D IFF LMM, which employs a visual encoder based on the diffusion model. D IFF LMM
133     offers stronger grounding capabilities than the original LMM, while maintaining general vision-
134     language task performance.

140   Large multimodal models (LMMs). Pioneering work in LMMs, such as LLaVA (Liu et al.,
141
      2023; Sun et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a;b), MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023a), and
      InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a), enables visual inputs for large language models
142
      (LLMs) via vision-language feature alignment (Radford et al., 2021) and instruction tuning (Wei et al.,
143
      2022). To equip LMMs with the grounding ability, a series of methods have been proposed to produce
144   model outputs of bounding boxes (Peng et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2023; Pi et al.,
145   2023; You et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b), traces of points (Xu et al., 2024), or segmentation masks (Lai
146   et al., 2024; Rasheed et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2024), by adding region-specific
147   tokens or decoders. These methods require further grounding supervision, so image datasets with
148   fine-grained annotations (Lin et al., 2014; Yu et al., 2016; Zhou et al., 2017) are usually repurposed
149   for the visual instruction tuning. Unlike these supervised methods, our approach, attend-and-segment,
150   does not change the LMM architecture or require any grounding supervision data.
151   A concurrent work F-LMM (Wu et al., 2024a) shows a similar method to exploit attention maps
152   in frozen LMMs for visual grounding, but we differ mainly in two aspects: 1) F-LMM follows the
153   supervised paradigm to learn the segmentator, while our attend-and-segment requires zero supervision.
154   For the first time, we reveal LMMs’ emerging grounding capabilities without explicit supervision. 2)
155   F-LMM examines existing LMMs without changing their visual encoding. In contrast, by analyzing
156   visual encoders’ grounding ability, we propose D IFF LMM to further enhance implicit grounding.
157   Diffusion models (DMs) as visual feature extractors. DMs (Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020;
158   Song et al., 2021; Karras et al., 2022; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Rombach et al., 2022) have become
159   a prevalent paradigm in visual generation, and intermediate features from DMs are explored for
160   applications beyond generative tasks. For example, DDPM-Seg (Baranchuk et al., 2022), ODISE (Xu
161   et al., 2023), and EmerDiff (Namekata et al., 2024) utilize DM features for various segmentation tasks.
      Features from DMs can also establish point- or pixel-level correspondences between images (Tang

163   DMs can be utilized for learning a general-purpose LMM with strong grounding capabilities.

      3.1   P RELIMINARY: M ETA -A RCHITECTURE OF L ARGE M ULTIMODAL M ODELS (LMM S )
174
175   Most LMMs (Liu et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2023) share a common meta-architecture
176   which consists of a visual encoder MV , a vision-to-language feature projector MV 7→L , and a large
177   language model (LLM) ML , as illustrated in Figure 2. Given an image I of resolution H × W ,
178   the visual encoder MV (e.g., CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)) is employed to extract visual features
179   V = MV (I) ∈ Rh×w×cV , where h × w represents the feature map size, and cV is the visual
180   feature dimension. Then, the visual feature map is considered as a sequence of hw elements, and
181
      projected element-wise into the language feature space by the projector MV 7→L . The projector can
      be implemented as a learnable lightweight multilayer perceptron (MLP). The k-th projected visual
182
      token is computed as vk = MV 7→L (Vk ) ∈ RcL , where cL is the feature dimension in the LLM. The
183
      visual tokens, concatenated with other language tokens, form the input sequence Sinput :
184
185                              Sinput = {t1 , . . . , tp , v1 , . . . , vhw , tp+1 , . . . , tp+q },         (1)
186   where {v1 , . . . , vhw } are the hw visual tokens projected from the visual feature map, t1 , . . . , tp are
187   the p language tokens before the visual tokens, and {tp+1 , . . . , tp+q } are the q language tokens after
188   the visual tokens.
189
      The LLM is usually a decoder-only Transformer model, which is capable of next-token prediction.
190
      Given the input sequence Sinput , the output sequence Soutput = {o1 , . . . , or } is generated in an
191   auto-regressive manner, where the i-th token is predicted as:
192
193                                           oi = ML (Sinput , o1 , . . . , oi−1 ).                           (2)
194   The generation is terminated when the last predicted token or is a special “end-of-sequence” token.

196   3.2   Attend-and-Segment : G ROUNDING LMM S W ITHOUT G ROUNDING S UPERVISION
197
198   Prior efforts towards grounding LMM attach a detection or segmentation module to the LMM archi-
199   tecture, and specialize the LMM training procedure with grounding supervision, i.e., visual instruction
200   data augmented by object-level annotations, such that the LMM learns to predict connections between
201   the text response and the image contents in the form of localized bounding boxes or segmentation
202
      masks. In contrast to these strongly supervised methods, we propose attend-and-segment, a sim-
      ple and effective method for grounding LMMs without changing their architecture or providing
203
      additional grounding supervision. We investigate the attention maps inside the transformer-based
204
      language model when generating tokens, and observe strong interpretablity associated with the
205   attention maps. Intuitively, the attention maps can provide information about where the model is
206   looking at when producing outputs.
207
208

253   3.3   D IFF LMM: E NHANCED G ROUNDING WITH D IFFUSION -BASED LMM
254
255   Most LMMs employ CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) as the visual encoder because it has been pre-trained
256   to align vision and language representations, but CLIP is known to be sub-optimal in tasks that
257   require precise localization (e.g., object detection, image segmentation) (Zhou et al., 2022; Ghiasi
258   et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022). To enhance the grounding ability of LMMs, a direct choice may be
259   replacing CLIP with better localized pure-vision backbones like DINO (Caron et al., 2021; Oquab
260
      et al., 2024). However, the lack of alignment with language representations can hurt vision-language
      task performance (Jiang et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024b).
261
262   Compared with vision-language models with image-level alignment (e.g., CLIP) and pure-vision
263   models (e.g., DINO), visual representations from diffusion models (DMs) strike a better balance: 1)
264   DMs learn to generate high-fidelity images, for which well-localized visual features are necessary.
265   Consequently, they are better than CLIP at localization. 2) DMs are trained to perform text-to-
266
      image generation, and in this procedure, they acquire alignment with language instructions, which is
      lacking in pure-vision models like DINO. Therefore, we propose diffusion-based LMM (D IFF LMM,
267
      illustrated in Figure 3), which strengthens the visual encoder with a pre-trained DM.
268

      process. The image is tokenized by a vector quantized (VQ) encoder, added with a random noise,

      LMMs are not originally designed for instance segmentation. Therefore, for evaluation purposes,
      we ask LMMs to generate a detailed description of a given image, and leverage attend-and-segment
345
      to produce pairs of noun phrases and segmentation masks from the LMM response. Then we find
346
      the best-matching category label for each noun phrase by computing their embedding similarities
347   using spaCy (Honnibal et al., 2020). Since the LMM is not constrained to only describe objects that
348   are annotated by the dataset (and should not be rewarded or penalized for detecting out-of-domain
349   objects), we exclude predictions that cannot be matched with any category label that appear in the
350   given image. We compare the standard metrics in instance segmentation, mask average precision
351   (AP) and mask average recall (AR). In this setting, AP is lower than AR because the models are
352   not supervised for the task, and we do not explicitly remove duplicated predictions. To assess the
353   quality of point prompts derived from LMM attention maps without SAM, we compute a new metric,
354   point accuracy (PAcc), which is the ratio of point prompts that correctly fall into the masks of the
355
      corresponding category. For comparison, we consider a baseline that simulates a “blind” LMM,
      which prompts SAM with a random point for segmenting each ground-truth category.
356
357   As shown in Table 1, the attention-derived prompts from the original LLaVA-1.5 achieve a non-trivial
358   34.01 accuracy, indicating that the attention maps can be utilized for fine-grained grounding. Further
359   performing segmentation with SAM leads to 22.8 AR. Comparing models equipped with different
360   visual encoders but trained with the same data, our D IFF LMM achieves the best overall point accuracy
361
      and mask AP/AR, whether or not we concatenate the diffusion features with CLIP features. A recent
      vision-centric LMM, Cambrian-1 (Tong et al., 2024a), utilizing an ensemble of four visual encoders,
362
      has an even higher point accuracy and mask AP. However, due to different training data, it tends to
363
      generate shorter descriptions than LLaVA-1.5, resulting in more missed objects and lower mask AR.

418

419

422   implementation and complete results are included in Appendix B.
423

424

425

426   In both task settings, the text for grounding is set by an external input, which is inconsistent with
427   the generative nature of LMMs. Grounding LMMs have to be trained to generate a special token
428   for mask decoding, but our approach does not include such training. Therefore, a conversation
429   between a human user and an LMM has be simulated for indirectly producing the attention maps
430   and segmentation results. Considering both factors, RES and PNG are inherently challenging for our
431   approach without grounding supervision. Nevertheless, we achieve competitive performance on these

454   4.4   V ISUAL Q UESTION A NSWERING
455
456   When enhancing the grounding ability of LMMs, we do not want LMMs to lose their general vision-
457   language abilities. To assess such general abilities, we evaluate D IFF LMM on a wide range of visual
458   question answering (VQA) benchmarks, including VQAv2 (Goyal et al., 2017), GQA (Hudson &
459   Manning, 2019), Vizwiz (Gurari et al., 2018), ScienceQA-IMG (Lu et al., 2022), and TextVQA (Singh
460   et al., 2019). We also consider more comprehensive LMM benchmarks, including POPE (Li et al.,
461   2023b), MMBench (Liu et al., 2024c), and LLaVA-Bench (Liu et al., 2023).
462   It is worth noting that previous grounding LMMs (e.g., LISA (Lai et al., 2024), GLaMM (Rasheed
463   et al., 2024)) are not usually evaluated on these general-purpose VQA benchmarks. For example,
464   POPE is designed for quantifying object hallucination in LMMs by asking questions like “Is
465   there an [object] in the image?” but the queried object often does not exist. However,
466   we find that GLaMM almost always answers “Sure, it is [seg].” and provides an incorrect
467   segmentation mask (see examples in Figure 4). Such loss of capabilities in answering general
468   questions is due to supervision bias—these LMMs are fine-tuned for grounding tasks and they
469   forget how to answer general visual questions without grounding. Therefore, grounding LMMs like
470
      GLaMM have extremely low scores on these benchmarks, and we choose to compare with stronger
      generalist LMMs that are not designed for grounding tasks on VQA benchmarks.
471
472   When compared with state-of-the-art LMMs of the same scale (fine-tuned from a 7B LLM), including
473   InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023), IDEFICS (HuggingFace, 2023), Qwen-VL-Chat (Bai et al., 2023),
474   and LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2024a), D IFF LMM ranks 1st on 3 benchmarks, and 2nd on 4 benchmarks.
475   Since D IFF LMM is trained on the same data as LLaVA-1.5, similar results are observed. There-
476
      fore, our diffusion-based D IFF LMM improves fine-grained vision-language comprehension that
      specifically requires the grounding ability, while retaining strong general vision-language capabilities.

532   5    C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this work, we reveal a previously overlooked yet critical fact that LMMs possess grounding
535   capabilities even if they are trained without grounding supervision. We propose attend-and-segment
536   to convert this implicit grounding ability into segmentation masks, and we introduce D IFF LMM to
537   further enhance the grounding ability. Our approach is more scalable and generalizable compared
538   with supervised methods. Moreover, extensive evaluation results demonstrate strong performance on
539   both grounding-specific and general vision-language benchmarks, even surpassing grounding LMMs
      trained with extensive supervision on the challenging grounded conversation generation task.

      In this section, we provide the implementation details of this work to ensure reproducibility of our
813   experiments.
814
815   Attend-and-Segment. We first collect the attention maps for the visual tokens, and aggregate the
816
      attention maps by averaging over all layers and heads. Then we apply normalization across the output
      token sequence to remove noisy points, and upsample the normalized attention map to the original
817
      image resolution. During mask refinement, we find the coordinate where the normalized attention
818
      value is maximized, and use it as a prompt to the ViT-H SAM model (Kirillov et al., 2023) for
819   producing the pixel-level segmentation map. In the instance segmentation and grounded conversation
820   generation tasks, we parse the model response into noun phrases using spaCy (Honnibal et al.,
821   2020), and for each phrase, we average the normalized attention maps for the tokens that compose
822   the central noun of the phrase.
823
      D IFF LMM. Our development of D IFF LMM is based on the codebase and dataset of LLaVA-1.5 (Liu
824
      et al., 2024a). We employ the Stable Diffusion v1.5 (Rombach et al., 2022) model as our visual
825   backbone. In the denoising step, we add a random noise at the 100 timestep, and extract features
826   from the second upsampling block, following the practice of DIFT (Tang et al., 2023). We also
827   provide additional ablation study on the choice of the noise level and feature block in Table 8. In the
828   implicit captioner (Xu et al., 2023), we employ the visual encoder of CLIP-ViT-L-336px (Radford
829   et al., 2021), which is the same CLIP model in the original LLaVA-1.5. The model is trained with
830   LoRA (Hu et al., 2022), and the same training recipe as LLaVA-1.5. The training data are also the
831   same as LLaVA-1.5. The included datasets and their licenses are listed below.

863   Referring expression segmentation (RES). The RES task requires the model to segment a target
      object specified by a given referring expression. It is indirect for our method, attend-and-segment,


                                                         16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      to provide a single attention map for the referring expression specified by a task input. We design
865   a simple strategy to find the text-image correspondence. Specifically, we formulate a one-round
866   conversation between a human user and an LMM. For a referring expression [expr], the human
867   user asks the model to “Describe the [expr].” Then, the model is expected to generate
868   a response that focuses on the target object. We find the first token of the object of the sentence
869   (e.g., the token right after the verb “is”), and extract its attention map. After that, we can apply
870   attend-and-segment to find the point with the highest attention value, and produce a grounding mask
871   based on it. An illustrative example is shown as follows, in which we will use the token “a” for
872   attention map extraction.
873   USER: Describe the "middle player."
874   MODEL: The "middle player" in the image is a baseball player
875   wearing a blue shirt and a baseball glove...
876
      We observe that SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) tends to produce overly fine-grained segmentation masks
877   for object parts, and cannot deliver satisfactory results in the RES task which requires masks for entire
878   objects. Since our approach is actually not tied with any specific segmentation model, we can use
879   the prompt point generated by attend-and-segment to guide other models for segmentation. In this
880   experiment, we use a Co-DETR (Zong et al., 2023) instance segmentation model to produce class-
881   agnostic mask predictions for each image. To avoid data contamination, we exclude RefCOCO(+/g)
882   validation/test images from the training set, and retrain the Co-DETR model. After generating both
883   the prompt point with attend-and-segment and the set of candidate masks, we select the mask that
884   contains the prompt point. If there are multiple masks containing the prompt point, we just use the
885
      mask with the highest confidence score predicted by Co-DETR.
886   The results are summarized in Table 9. Without any training on RES or other visual grounding tasks,
887   we achieve a remarkable performance of 37.0 average cIoU in RES. In particular, we outperform the
888   previous zero-shot methods (Yu et al., 2023; Suo et al., 2023; Ni et al., 2023). The results demonstrate
889   strong visual grounding capabilities implicitly learned by LMMs.

      Panoptic narrative grounding (PNG). The PNG task requires the model to ground each noun phrase
911
      in a given text description of a given image, by providing a corresponding panoptic segmentation
912   mask (Kirillov et al., 2019) for each noun phrase. Different from the standard setup of LMMs, the text
913   description of the image is provided by the task, rather than generated by the model itself. Therefore,
914   we adapt our attend-and-segment method for the PNG task. Specifically, we simulate a one-round
915   conversation between a human user and an LMM. The human user asks the model to “Describe
916   the image in detail.” Then, the model responds with the given text description. We extract
917   attention maps from the model response part of this conversation, and use the attention maps to guide
      the segmentation procedure.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      Similar to RES, we use a specialized panoptic segmentation model, OpenSeeD (Zhang et al., 2023a),
919   to provide high-quality panoptic segmentation masks for each image. For each noun phrase, we
920   find the first associated token and its attention maps. Then, we locate the point with the highest
921   attention value, and select the mask that contains this point, from all the candidate masks generated
922   by OpenSeeD. Note that in panoptic segmentation, all masks are non-overlapping, so there is only
923   one mask that contains this point of the highest attention value. This selected mask is predicted as the
924   region corresponding to the noun phrase.
925   In Table 10, we compare the results of our approach with prior grounding LMMs. Although our
926   approach is applied on the PNG task in a zero-shot manner, it achieves competitive performance and
927   even outperforms one prior grounding LMM, PixelLM. Additionally, our approach improves over all
928   previous zero-shot PNG methods (Nguyen et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024).

      et al., 2024). First, when the questions are not formulated as usual, GLaMM tends to interpret these
956   questions as image captioning or referring segmentation tasks, while D IFF LMM can still follow the
957   user’s instructions and accurately answer the questions. Meanwhile, attend-and-segment provides
958   well-grounded responses that connects text phrases and visual entities. Furthermore, our approach
959   shows better generalizability to unfamiliar question types, visual concepts, and image domains.

1004   Attention in each head and layer. Instead of averaging the attention values over nlayer layers and
1005   nhead heads per layer in the LLM, we first inspect the individual attention values in each head and
1006   layer. Figure 6 visualizes the attention between one generated token “cat” and the input visual tokens.
1007   Consistent with some recent observations (Wu et al., 2024b), a few heads in the intermediate layers
1008   show stronger activation with respect to the visual object in the image. Also, attention maps in
1009   intermediate layers are more localized. However, it is infeasible to build direct connections between
1010
       attention heads and visual concepts, given the absence of grounding annotations.
1011   Table 11 summarizes an empirical study that demonstrates the grounding results of using the attention
1012   from one single head of one single layer. Compared with averaging over all heads and layers,
1013   individual heads and layers lead to to significantly worse and noisier results. Therefore, we aggregate
1014   the attention maps across all heads and layers by averaging, which also simplifies the algorithm of
1015   attend-and-segment in our setting without grounding supervision.
1016   Attention normalization. After reducing the attention maps into one 2D map for each generated
1017   token, we observe some noisy patterns in the attention maps (Figure 7-top). Some seemingly
1018   uninformative visual tokens (usually in the background) attracts more attention from the generated
1019   token than other visual tokens. A recent work (Darcet et al., 2024) shows similar observations, and
1020   explains that such less informative tokens are “repurposed for internal computations.” To remove
1021   such artifacts, they propose to provide additional tokens to the Vision Transformer as registers.
1022   However, in our setting, we cannot retrain the visual backbone or the language model due to limited
1023   data and computes. Instead, we simply normalize the attention maps by subtracting the mean attention
1024   map averaged over the output sequence (Section 3.2). Although the noisy attention patterns exist, we
1025   observe that these patterns are relatively stable (Figure 7-top), so the mean attention map, aggregated
       over the output sequence, can capture the undesired attention patterns and allow us to remove them.
