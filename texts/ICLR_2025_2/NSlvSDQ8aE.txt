                                                   A BSTRACT
011
012
               Molecular Dynamics (MD) is crucial in various fields such as materials science,
               chemistry, and pharmacology to name a few. Conventional MD software strug-
013
               gles with the balance between time cost and prediction accuracy, which restricts
014
               its wider application. Recently, data-driven approaches based on deep generative
015            models have been devised for time-coarsened dynamics, which aim at learning
016            dynamics of diverse molecular systems over a long timestep, enjoying both uni-
017            versality and efficiency. Nevertheless, most current methods are designed solely
018            to learn from the data distribution regardless of the underlying Boltzmann dis-
019            tribution, and the physics priors such as energies and forces are constantly over-
020            looked. In this work, we propose a conditional generative model called Force-
021            guided Bridge Matching (FBM), which learns full-atom time-coarsened dynam-
022            ics and targets the Boltzmann-constrained distribution. With the guidance of our
023
               delicately-designed intermediate force field, FBM leverages favourable physics
               priors into the generation process, giving rise to enhanced simulations. Exper-
024
               iments on two datasets consisting of peptides verify our superiority in terms of
025
               comprehensive metrics and demonstrate transferability to unseen systems.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Molecular Dynamics (MD), which simulates
031   the physical movements of molecular systems
032   at the atomic level via in silico methods, are
033   widely applied in the fields of materials science,
034   physics, chemistry, and pharmacology (Wolf
035   et al., 2005; Durrant & McCammon, 2011;
036   Salo-Ahen et al., 2020). Accurate MD sim-
037   ulations enable the researcher to comprehend
038   the equilibrium thermodynamics and kinetics
039
      of different molecular phases without the need
      for expensive wet-lab experiments.

043   CHARMM (Vanommeslaeghe et al., 2010), ics (path in black).
044   mostly pre-defines an empirical force field of

      molecular systems and performs MD based on
      the numerical integration of Newtonian equations over the timestep ∆t. To minimize discretization
046
      errors, ∆t should be chosen small enough, typically on the order of femtoseconds (10−15 s). Con-
047
      sequently, simulating the full duration of critical phase transitions that occur on the microsecond
048   (10−6 s) or even millisecond (10−3 s) timescale becomes virtually impossible within a reasonable
049   wall-clock time. To overcome the efficiency limitation in long-timescale simulation, a surge of
050   approaches have been devised, including MD-like methods (Voter et al., 2000; Pang et al., 2017;
051   Laio & Parrinello, 2002) and Monte Carlo-based methods (Sadigh et al., 2012; Sugita & Okamoto,
052   1999; Neyts & Bogaerts, 2014). However, all these methods share a common drawback: different
053   molecular systems require customized simulations, despite the fact that many atomistic systems
      should, in principle, exhibit similar dynamic mechanisms.

054
      Recently, data-driven approaches (Klein et al., 2024; Schreiner et al., 2024; Hsu et al., 2024; Li
055   et al., 2024; Jing et al., 2024) leveraging deep generative models have been used to enhance MD
056   simulations. Unlike traditional numerical integration, these methods directly learn MD from diverse
057   observed trajectories, offering superior transferability across different molecular systems. Addition-
058   ally, they can accelerate simulations through the use of time-coarsened dynamics, where models
059   learn to generate new states after a significantly larger timestep (τ ≫ ∆t) starting from an initial
060   state, as illustrated in Figure 1. Nevertheless, existing learning-based methods still face two signif-
061   icant issues. The first issue is that most current methods are designed solely to learn from the data
062   distribution, which may be biased compared to the underlying Boltzmann distribution (Boltzmann,
063
      1877) — a fundamental concept for describing the thermal equilibrium of molecular systems in
      relation to atomic positions and velocities. Although Timewarp (Klein et al., 2024) alleviates this
064
      issues by employing the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm (Metropolis et al., 1953) to resample from
065
      the equilibrium, the unbearably low acceptance rate still hinders its applicability. The second issue is
066   that physics priors (e.g., energies and forces) are constantly overlooked during the learning process,
067   which, yet, are critical for providing insights into the dynamics of molecular systems.
068

095   2   R ELATED W ORK
096
097   Boltzmann Generator An important objective of MD research is to quickly sample from the
098   Boltzmann distribution, thereby revealing the free energy landscape and collective variables of mat-
099
      ter of molecular systems. Boltzmann generators (Noé et al., 2019; Köhler et al., 2021; 2023; Falkner
      et al., 2023; Klein et al., 2024; Klein & Noé, 2024) employ generative models to produce samples
100
      asymptotically from the Boltzmann distribution mainly by: (i) Apply reweighting techniques to
101
      i.i.d. generated samples. (ii) Inference in a Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) procedure. These
102   approaches heavily rely on MC resampling techniques, which are the bottleneck of the sampling ef-
103   ficiency due to costly energy calculation and low acceptance rates. Most similar to our work, Wang
104   et al. (2024) propose ConfDiff that incorporate the energy and force guidance directly into score dif-
105   fusion to target the Boltzmann-constrained distribution, yet it only works well on protein backbones
106   and generate conformations in an unconditional way that fails to capture temporal dynamics. In con-
107   trast, FBM introduces the force guidance into the bridge matching framework, making it possible to
      sample straightforwardly from the Boltzmann-constrained distribution.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Time-Coarsened Dynamics To overcome the instability of numerical integration of conventional
109   MD simulations, many deep learning methods have adopted the fashion of time-coarsened dynamics,
110   where models learn dynamics of diverse molecular systems over a long timestep. Fu et al. (2023)
111   proposes a multi-scale graph network to learn dynamics of polymers, which fails to operate in the
112   all atom system. ITO (Schreiner et al., 2024) is devised to learn the transition probability over
113   multiple time resolutions, yet its transferability across chemical space remains unknown. Recently,
114   Timewarp (Klein et al., 2024) and TBG (Klein & Noé, 2024) utilize augmented normalizing flows
115   and flow matching respectively for transferable time-coarsened dynamics of small peptides, while
116   both need additional reweighting procedures to debias expectations to the Boltzmann distribution.
117
      In addition, Score Dynamics (Hsu et al., 2024) applies score diffusion to capture dynamic patterns
      merely from the data distribution, while F3 low (Li et al., 2024) and MDGen (Jing et al., 2024)
118
      learn transformations of proteins under coarse-grained representations. On the contrary, FBM is
119
      designed to learn time-coarsened dynamics at the atomic level and target the Boltzmann-constrained
120   distribution by directly inference without extra steps, which aligns well to thermodynamic principles
121   and exhibits transferability to unseen peptide systems as well.

      In this section, we will present the overall workflow of our method. Specifically, in § 3.1, we will
127   define our task formulation of time-coarsened dynamics, with providing necessary notations. Then
128   in § 3.2, we introduce how to learn the time-coarsened dynamics from the data distribution, via a
129   conditional generative model based on bridge matching, which is dubbed as FBM-BASE. Built upon
130   this baseline, in § 3.3, we further propose a novel generative framework that targets the Boltzmann-
131   constrained distribution by incorporating an intermediate force field into the FBM-BASE model,
132   leading to our eventual model FBM. All proofs of propositions are provided in § B.

331
332   Dataset Generation We evaluate FBM on two datasets consisting of small peptides: Alanine-
333   Dipeptide (AD) that is commonly studied previously and PepMD which is created by us. The AD
334   dataset contains a simple peptide with only 22 atoms. The initial structure and reference MD tra-
335
      jectories of AD are all obtained from mdshare1 without post-processing. As for PepMD, we first
      screen valid peptides between 3-10 residues from the sequence data provided by PDB (Berman
336
      et al., 2000). Next, we perform data cleaning according to the following criterion: each peptide
337
      must contain only the 20 natural amino acids, and the number of any type of residue should not
338   exceed 50% of the sequence length. We then cluster the data with a sequence identity threshold of
339   60% by MMseq2 (Steinegger & Söding, 2017), and randomly sample one peptide from each clus-
340   ter to obtain a non-redundant dataset. Considering the computing resource constraints, we select
341   136/14 peptides for constructing the training-validation/test set, respectively. The structures of all
342   150 peptides are predicted by open-source tools RDKit (rdk) and PDBfixer2 , which are sent as
343   initial states to generate MD trajectories using OpenMM (Eastman et al., 2017) afterwards. Finally,
344   the peptide pairs for training are then sampled from trajectories in the way depicted in § 3.1. The
345   MD simulation setups and the statistical details of our curated dataset are both illustrated in § E.1.
346
347   Baselines We compare our FBM with the following generative models that learns time-coarsened
348   dynamics: (i) Timewarp (Klein et al., 2024), the current state-of-the-art model targeting the Boltz-
349   mann distribution by MCMC resampling, which exhibits superior transferability to unseen peptide
350   systems. (ii) ITO (Schreiner et al., 2024), a conditional diffusion model that learns multiple time-
351
      resolution dynamics. (iii) Score Dynamics (Hsu et al., 2024), a score-based diffusion model that
      learns discrete transitions of the dynamic variables. All models are trained on PepMD from scratch
352
      for fair comparison.
353
354
355   Metrics Following Wang et al. (2024), we evaluate generated conformation ensembles against the
356
      full MD trajectories as to their validity, flexibility, and distributional similarity. We provide brief
      descriptions of the metrics in this part and further details are illustrated in § E.2:
357
358
                • Validity. We regard a molecular conformation as valid when it is governed by certain
359               physics constraints. Following Lu et al. (2023b), we judge whether a conformation is valid
360               by the criterion: no bond clashes between any residue pairs and no bond breaks between
361               adjacent residues, based on coordinates of α-carbons. This metric, named as VAL-CA,
362               represents the fraction of valid conformations in the full generated conformation ensembles.
363
                • Flexibility. The generated structures are further required to exhibit flexibility to capture dy-
364
                  namic characteristics. Following Janson et al. (2023), we report the root mean square error
365               of contact maps between generated conformation ensembles and reference MD trajectories
366               as a measure of flexibility, termed as CONTACT.
367
368             • Distributional similarity. We focus on the similarity between the sample distribution and
369
                  the Boltzmann distribution. Instead of the costly computation of the Boltzmann density, we
                  project the molecular conformations onto following low-dimensional feature spaces and
370
                  calculate the Jensen-Shannon (JS) distance as a substitute (Lu et al., 2023b): (i) pairwise
371
                  distances between α-carbons of residues (PWD); (ii) radius-of-gyration (RG) that measures
372               the distribution of α-carbons to the center-of-mass; (iii) the time-lagged independent com-
373               ponents (Pérez-Hernández et al., 2013) (TIC) based on dihedrals and pairwise distances of
374               α-carbons, where only the slowest components, TIC 0 and TIC 1, are taken into consider-
375               ation. For each metric, the mean JS distance along all feature dimensions are reported.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
525
      In this work, we present a novel generative model called FBM for time-coarsened dynamics in a
526
      full-atom fashion. We first leverage the bridge matching framework to construct the baseline model
527
      FBM-BASE for learning dynamics from the data distribution. Based on FBM-BASE, we further
528   introduce physics priors and interpolate a well-designed intermediate force field, which is theo-
529   retically guaranteed to target the Boltzmann-constrained distribution via directly inference without
530   extra steps. Experiments on alanine dipeptide and our curated dataset PepMD showcase superiority
531   of FBM on comprehensive metrics and demonstrate transferability to unseen peptide systems.
532
      As the first attempt to incorporate the intermediate force field to bridge matching for full-atom time-
533
      coarsened dynamics, our method has considerable room for improvement. Firstly, our experiments
534   have been conducted on small peptides with fewer than 10 residues. Further exploration on more
535   complex molecular systems (e.g., proteins) is warranted. Secondly, since the training labels for FBM
536   depend on the marginal score calculations provided by FBM-BASE, we have to adopt a two-stage
537   training process rather than an end-to-end one, which increases the training overhead. Lastly, the
538   transitions between metastable states are still not fast enough, resulting in unreasonable conforma-
539   tions along the generated paths. Therefore, methods for rapid and jump-like state transition are of
      great importance.

913   In practice, we found that the unnormalized potential function is numerically unstable and its vari-
914   ance is positively correlated with the number of atoms N . For stable training, we need to perform
915   certain pre-processing steps. Specifically, for potentials in kJ/mol, we divide by 3N to obtain the
916   average potential energy per degree of freedom for the entire molecule, where N varies with differ-
917   ent peptides. For force fields in kJ/(mol·nm), due to their relatively stable values across different
      molecular systems, we empirically multiply by the constant 0.002 for normalization.

935   D.3    R EFINEMENT WITH C ONSTRAINED E NERGY M INIMIZATION
936
937   We utilize the discrete form of the SDE process in Eq. (4) for inference with T SDE steps, and
938   the full conformation ensembles are generated in an autoregressive way, where the output from the
939
      previous step serves as the input for the next step. However, in the autoregressive fashion, errors
      at each inference step will accumulate, leading to out-of-distribution problem. Here we introduce
940
      an additional energy minimization procedure using OpenMM (Eastman et al., 2017) for refinement,
941
      which is performed for each generated conformation before sent to the next inference step. Note
942   that we aim for the refinement to affect only the minor details (e.g., X-H bonds) without altering
943   the overall conformation; therefore, independent harmonic constraints are further applied on all
944   heavy atoms with spring constant of 10 kcal/mol·Å2 and the tolerance of 2.39 kcal/mol·Å2 without
945   maximal step limits (Wang et al., 2024).

1107
