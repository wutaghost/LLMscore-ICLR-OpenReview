009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In-context reinforcement learning (ICRL) is a frontier paradigm for solving rein-
012            forcement learning problems in the foundation model era. While ICRL capabilities
013            have been demonstrated in transformers through task-specific training, the potential
014            of Large Language Models (LLMs) out-of-the-box remains largely unexplored.
015            Recent findings highlight that LLMs often face challenges when dealing with
016
               numerical contexts, and limited attention has been paid to evaluating their perfor-
               mance through preference feedback generated by the environment. This paper is the
017
               first to investigate the performance of LLMs as in-context decision-makers in the
018
               problem of Dueling Bandits (DB), a stateless preference-based reinforcement learn-
019            ing setting that extends the classic Multi-Armed Bandit (MAB) model by querying
020            for preference feedback. We compare GPT-3.5 T URBO, GPT-4, GPT-4 T URBO,
021            L LAMA 3.1, and O 1- PREVIEW against nine well-established DB algorithms. Our
022            results reveal that our top-performing LLM, GPT-4 T URBO, possesses an under-
023            standing of relative decision-making sufficient to achieve low weak regret in DB by
024            quickly selecting the best arm in duels. However, we observed that an optimality
025            gap exists between LLMs and classic DB algorithms in terms of strong regret.
026            LLMs struggle to converge and consistently exploit even when explicitly prompted
027
               to do so, and are sensitive to prompt variations. To overcome these issues, we
               introduce an agentic flow framework: LLM with Enhanced Algorithmic Dueling
028
               (LEAD), which integrates off-the-shelf DB algorithms with LLM agents through
029
               fine-grained adaptive interplay. We show that LEAD has theoretical guarantees
030            inherited from classic DB algorithms on both weak and strong regret. We validate
031            its efficacy and robustness even with noisy and adversarial prompts. The design of
032            such an agentic framework sheds light on how to enhance the trustworthiness of
033            general-purpose LLMs used for in-context decision-making tasks.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Large sequence models pretrained on offline reinforcement learning datasets have led to the emergence
038   of in-context reinforcement learning (ICRL) (Laskin et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2024), where models can
039
      infer tasks from interaction histories as context and make effective decisions in unseen environments
      without parameter updates. Through trial and error, these models can self-improve their policies
040
      purely in-context. While ICRL capabilities have been demonstrated in transformers with task-
041
      specific training from scratch, the potential of general-purpose Large Language Models (LLMs) to
042   perform ICRL remains largely unexplored. Recent investigations into LLMs’ out-of-the-box ICRL
043   capabilities in environments with numeric rewards have reported notable failure cases, e.g., LLM
044   agents being vulnerable to adversarial loss functions and suffering from high regret compared to
045   classic algorithms such as Follow-The-Regularized-Leader (FTRL) (Park et al., 2024), and exhibiting
046   failures in exploration within Multi-Armed Bandit (MAB) problems (Lattimore & Szepesvári, 2020)
047   via standard training (Krishnamurthy et al., 2024). Even with inference-time algorithmic guidance,
048   an optimality gap persists between LLMs and classic (contextual) MAB algorithms (Nie et al., 2024).
049   These results suggest that carefully designed prompts and non-trivial algorithmic interventions are
050
      needed to elicit desirable in-context reinforcement learning behavior of LLM agents.
051   The failure cases encountered by LLMs may be attributed to intrinsic difficulties in processing
052   numeric rewards, especially in tasks where patterns are difficult to express in natural language.
053   Recent findings have pointed out that LLMs often struggle with simple numerical comparisons (e.g.,
      incorrectly judging 13.11 to be larger than 13.8), and there has been a notable lack of emphasis

                  069   when the feedback is inherently comparative, like taste of food and product attractiveness (Yue et al.,
                  070   2012). DB has attracted significant attention due to its applicability in information retrieval (Yue &

                  073
                                      Are LLMs effective in-context agents for solving the problem of dueling bandits?

                  075   The DB problem poses distinctive challenges as a relative decision-making instance, particularly due
                  076   to the sparse nature of the relative rewards. This sparsity complicates the in-context decision-making
                  077   process, as it restricts the feedback obtained from interactions, introducing a level of difficulty not
                  078   typically seen in conventional bandit problems. Even though reduction from DB to standard MAB
                  079   exists (Ailon et al., 2014; Saha & Gaillard, 2022), it remains unclear how LLMs would perform in
                  080   DB with preference feedback rather than numeric rewards. There are conceptual differences between
                  081
                        them, similar to those between Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Stiennon
                        et al., 2020) and standard RL, where impossibility results can be found in (Wang et al., 2024b).
                  082
                  083   While task-specific training of large sequence models can yield promising ICRL results, it is often
                  084   impractical due to the substantial computational resources required. Similar to the settings in (Mir-
                  085   chandani et al., 2023; Krishnamurthy et al., 2024; Nie et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024), we evaluate the
                  086   emergent zero-shot abilities (Wei et al., 2022) of ICRL in general-purpose LLMs under the dueling
                  087
                        bandit problem, without re-training or fine-tuning. We summarize our main results below.
                  088   Evaluation of LLMs’ Emergent Zero-Shot Abilities of In-Context Dueling Bandits. We go
                  089   beyond numeric rewards to evaluate the performance of LLM agents in terms of both strong and
                  090   weak regret for making decisions in DB by comparing against various baseline DB algorithms via
                  091   a case study. We found that the top-performing general-purpose LLMs possess an understanding
                  092   of relative decision-making sufficient to achieve low weak regret in DB, which significantly differs
                  093
                        from that in classic MAB settings (Krishnamurthy et al., 2024). Notably, GPT-4 T URBO can serve
                        as an effective decision-maker for dueling bandits in the short term, quickly selecting the best arm
                  094
                        in duels with low variance across a range of instances. However, consistent with (Nie et al., 2024),
                  095
                        we found that an optimality gap exists between LLMs and classic DB algorithms in terms of strong
                  096   regret. The long-term performance is hindered by over-estimation bias in the exploration stage and
                  097   lack of convergence criterion in the exploitation stage. This highlights the need for more effective
                  098   and robust strategies to bridge this gap for in-context dueling bandits.
                  099
                        Effective and Robust Agentic Flow Framework for In-Context Dueling Bandits. To address the
                  100
                        identified optimality gap and enhance the trustworthiness of in-context LLM agents in DB tasks,
                  101
                        in Section 4.1, we propose an agentic flow framework, LLM with Enhanced Algorithmic Dueling
                  102   (LEAD) that integrates off-the-shelf Explore-then-Exploit DB algorithms with LLM agents. This
                  103   framework enables the fine-grained adaptive interplay between rule-based expert systems and in-
                  104   context LLM agents, enhancing their ability to handle DB problems via algorithmic interventions as
                  105   suggested in (Krishnamurthy et al., 2024; Nie et al., 2024). As an illustrative example, we demonstrate
                  106   how Interleaved Filter2 (IF2) algorithm can be incorporated with LLM agents in this framework. We
                  107   show that the proposed framework has theoretical guarantees, with experiments demonstrating its
                        efficacy and robustness across various prompting scenarios.

      2     P RELIMINARIES

110   In this section, we briefly introduce the problem of dueling bandits (DB) and establish the necessary
111   notation for this paper. Additional useful definitions can be found in Appendix B.3.1.
112
      Dueling Bandits. In a fundamental K-armed dueling bandit problem setting (Yue et al., 2012), a
113
      learner interacts with the environment by selecting two arms Arm1 (t) and Arm2 (t) from a set of
114   K arms {b1 , . . . , bK } for a noisy comparison (a duel), at each round t ∈ {1, . . . , T } as Figure 1
115   illustrates. The outcome of a duel between two arms (i, j) is probabilistic. More precisely, the event
116   that an arm bi wins against bj is a Bernoulli random variable with a parameter denoted by Pr(bi ≻ bj ).
117   For notational convenience, we normalize Pr(bi ≻ bj ) such that Pr(bi ≻ bj ) = ϵ(bi , bj ) + 1/2,
118   where ϵij := ϵ(bi , bj ) ∈ (−1/2, 1/2) is a measure of the distinguishability between arms bi and bj ,
119   which is stationary over time and is symmetric such that ϵij = −ϵji for all i, j ∈ [K] := {1, . . . , K}.
120   Finally, for notational convenience, we define a preference matrix P = [ϵij ]i,j∈[K] .
121
      In-Context LLM Agents for Dueling Bandits. We consider an LLM agent with policy πLLM
122   interacting with a K-armed dueling bandit environment in-context. At each round t ∈ {1, . . . , T },
123   the LLM agent selects a pair of arms (Arm1 (t), Arm2 (t)) from the set {b1 , . . . , bK } based on a
124   natural language instruction Prompt(C, Ht , R) (see Figure 7), consisting of three parts:
125
      • Problem Description P : a natural language description of the DB problem, including the number
126
        of arms K, the time horizon T , and the task objective.
127
128
      • History Ht : an externally summarized interaction history (Krishnamurthy et al., 2024) up to round
        t, which includes a sequence of pairwise dueling results and the empirical probabilities.
129
130   • Reasoning R: the zero-shot chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning (Kojima et al., 2022) that encourages
131
        the LLM agent to reason about the problem in a structured manner.
132   The LLM agent’s policy can be represented as:
133
134                            (Arm1 (t), Arm2 (t)) = πLLM (Prompt(P, Ht , R)) .                           (1)
135   The goal is to maximize the cumulative reward over some time horizon T , where the reward is the
136   sum of the unknown probabilities of the two chosen arms beating the best arm (Condorcet winner).
137   We can quantify performance as minimizing the cumulative regret, either in the strong or weak sense
138   (see Eq.(4) and Eq.(5)).
139
      Strong and Weak Regret. Throughout this paper, we assume the standard setting that a Condorcet
140
      winner (CW) exists (Sui et al., 2017; Wu & Liu, 2016; Zoghi et al., 2014b; Yue et al., 2012). The
141   CW denoted as b∗ is an arm that is preferred over all the other arms, i.e., b∗ = bi if ϵij > 1/2 for all
142   j ∈ [K]\{i}. We consider two performance metrics: (i) strong regret (SR), which evaluates the total
143   preference gap between b∗ and both selected arms; (ii) weak regret (WR), which compares b∗ only
144   with the better of the two arms. Detailed definitions and settings are provided in Appendix B.3.1.
145
      Related Works. Our work contributes to the growing community of intersection between LLMs and
146
      decision-making. We summarize the detailed related works about dueling bandits, LLM agents for
147   bandits, and LLMs for in-context decision-making in the Appendix A.

196    CW in beating other arms: (i) Transitive case (SST ∩ STI): This case uses a Bradley-Terry-Luce

201

207
         times for the baseline algorithms, enabling an understanding of their average behaviors and reliable

       top-performing LLM, highlighting its success and failure modes.

      Emergence of in-context dueling bandits abilities. While GPT-3.5 T URBO and GPT-4 fail to
217   solve the DB problem, GPT-4 T URBO consistently outperforms state-of-the-art (SOTA) DB baselines
218   in weak regret (see Figures 2 and 9). This reveals that the in-context dueling bandits abilities emerge
219   as the general capabilities grow via standard training methods in general-purpose LLMs. Figure 13
220   (left?) illustrates the fraction of duels including the best arm across different time intervals. GPT-4
221   T URBO outperforms other LLMs and the DB baselines throughout the entire timeline. These findings
222   suggest that GPT-4 T URBO can effectively process the preference feedback obtained from duels and
223   make informed decisions to quickly identify and include the best arm in its duels.
224   Stable performance across different instances. GPT-4 T URBO demonstrates low variance com-
225   pared to other LLMs and baselines across varying levels of difficulty. As shown in Figure 14, GPT-4
226   T URBO exhibits the lowest average generalized variance of strong and weak regret in both instances.
227   This highlights its ability to maintain a stable decision-making process in DB.
228
229      Best Arm Identification: LLMs’ in-context dueling bandits abilities emerge as the general
230      capabilities grow. The Condorcet Winner is consistently selected in duel via GPT-4 T URBO,
231
         leading to exceptional weak regret performance with minimal variance.
232
233   Exploration Vulnerability. In the exploration stage, we observe that GPT-4 T URBO tends to
234   quickly narrow down to a small subset of arms (although usually containing the Condorcet winner)
235   and repeatedly compare these arms. In contrast, the baselines exhibit more diverse and extended
236   exploration patterns. This behavior suggests that GPT-4 T URBO may overestimate the quality of
237
      arms that win their initial comparisons based on limited historical data. Unlike the baselines, which
      have explicit exploration mechanisms, LLMs rely on their inherent randomness (via sampling from
238
      their output distribution) to explore. Based on these observations, we hypothesize that if GPT-4
239
      T URBO happens to sample a sequence of comparisons that favors suboptimal arms early on, it can
240   get stuck comparing these arms indefinitely. To test this hypothesis, we conducted experiments using
241   noisy prompts with biased history. Our results in Figure 16 confirm that GPT-4 T URBO’s exploration
242   strategy is indeed vulnerable to biased history initialization and can converge to local optima.
243
      Exploitation Inability. Despite GPT-4 T URBO’s outstanding weak regret performance, it fails
244
      to consistently converge to a single best arm to duel against itself, even when the prompt setting
245
      explicitly calls for it. This behavior highlights a fundamental limitation of LLMs: they are primarily
246   designed and trained for word token prediction rather than decision-making. Unlike baselines with
247   explicit stopping conditions, GPT-4 T URBO relies on its inherent language modeling capabilities to
248   determine when to stop exploring. Consequently, in the later exploitation stage, GPT-4 T URBO keeps
249   comparing the same top arms without committing to a single winner (see Figure 3). This suggests
250   that the language modeling objective alone may not be sufficient for LLMs to achieve optimal control
251   in complex decision-making tasks like DB.
252
253      Lack of Robust Strategy: LLMs’ performance can be hindered by overestimation bias in the
254      exploration stage and the lack of convergence criteria in the exploitation stage.
255
256   Biased understanding of DB problem during pretraining. Our two best-performing LLMs, GPT-4
257   T URBO and O 1- PREVIEW, exhibit systematic biases regarding the DB problem, likely due to a lack
258   of exposure to similar tasks during pretraining. Specifically, they incorrectly assume that an arm
259   cannot duel with itself (the convergence case), even when explicitly prompted to do so (see examples
260   in Appendix C.1.3). This misunderstanding makes the DB problem as an out-of-distribution (OOD)
261   task for LLMs, and in-context instructions fail to fully override this internal bias. Consequently,
262
      LLM agents cannot completely align with problem descriptions due to the inherent limitations of
      in-context learning, which cannot really generalize to OOD tasks (Wang et al., 2024a). Figure 13
263
      supports these observations: O 1- PREVIEW demonstrates better reasoning capabilities by transitioning
264
      from exploration to exploitation effectively and achieving lower strong regret than GPT-4 T URBO.
265   However, its CoT mechanism reinforces its internal biased understanding of DB, resulting in poorer
266   weak regret performance due to the selection of two suboptimal arms in duels.
267
268      Systematic Biases: LLMs out-of-the-box lack a fundamental understanding of the DB problem
269      and instead intuitively choose the next pair of arms to compare based on dueling history.

286   performance, we conducted experiments from two perspectives: (i) Removing transitivity in prefer-

290   intransitive instances. This finding suggests that while LLMs exhibit emergent abilities for relative

      4    A LGORITHM -E NHANCED LLM S FOR D UELING BANDITS
317
318   Classic DB algorithms based on the Explore-then-Exploit framework, such as Interleaved Filter 2
319   (IF2) (Yue et al., 2012), are known to be near-optimal, with matching regret upper and lower bounds

        4.1   A LGORITHMIC D ESIGN OF LEAD
345
346     In this section, we present the design intuitions of LEAD. We begin by discussing the limitations of
347     a naive intervention approach and the desirable properties for an effective Algorithm-Enhanced LLM
348     framework. Based on these considerations, we propose an agentic framework design LEAD, where
349     we can incorporate any Explore-then-Exploit DB algorithms (Zoghi et al., 2014b). As an illustrative
350     example, we use IF2 (Yue et al., 2012) to demonstrate how off-the-shelf algorithms can be integrated
351     within LEAD and provide a detailed description.
352     Limitations of Naive Intervention. A straightforward approach to addressing the convergence
353     instability limitation of LLMs is to use a simple if-else condition that forces the LLMs to converge
354     when they first exploit two identical arms, which we call the Convergence-Triggered (CT) intervention
355     strategy. However, CT fails to guarantee the selection of the true Condorcet winner and can reinforce
356     local optima (see Figure 17 in Appendix C.2 for a failure example). This suggests that relying on
357     the LLMs’ internal convergence behavior to trigger the transition from exploration to exploitation
358
        is unreliable, as the LLMs are largely driven by its inherent sampling noise rather than a structured
        exploration policy. Thus, handling this limitation with theoretical guarantees remains challenging.
359
360     Desirable Properties for LLM Augmentation. To address [Q1], we seek an algorithmic framework
361     with the following properties: (i) A clear, symbolic logical structure that allows for easy integration
362     with LLM & Algorithm suggestions; (ii) A well-defined exploration-exploitation trade-off that
363     leverages the LLMs’ exploration behavior while ensuring convergence; (iii) Strong theoretical
364
        guarantees to maintain robustness with various prompting scenarios.
365     As a result, we find that the Explore-Then-Exploit structure is particularly well-suited for LLMs (see
366     Appendix B.1 for a detailed illustration). By selecting an Explore-Then-Exploit DB algorithm as the
367     foundation inside LEAD, we address [Q1]. As an example, we use IF2 as the base to illustrate the
368     theoretical guarantee and empirical performance. This approach can be applied similarly to other
369     algorithms with regret bounds in the Explore-Then-Exploit family.

371     Algorithm 1 (see more details in Appendix B.2). LEAD (IF2 base) maintains a confidence parameter

527   5   C ONCLUSION
528
529
      This paper evaluates LLMs as in-context decision-makers for standard context-free dueling bandits
      (DB) with a Condorcet Winner, offering the first systematic insights into their strengths and lim-
530
      itations. While GPT-4 T URBO achieves exceptional weak regret performance across a range of
531
      instances, it fundamentally lacks an understanding of the DB problem. Our findings reveal that LLMs’
532   decision-making in DB, driven by linguistic priors, lacks the necessary criteria for convergence and
533   generalization to complex scenarios, leading to an optimality gap between LLMs and classic DB al-
534   gorithms in terms of strong regret. To bridge this gap, we propose LEAD, an agentic flow framework
535   that integrates off-the-shelf DB algorithms with LLM agents through fine-grained adaptive interplay.
536   This framework provides theoretical guarantees and demonstrates robust performance even under
537   noisy and adversarial prompts. Our work contributes to the problem of In-Context Reinforcement
538   Learning (ICRL). The framework we proposed sheds light on how language-based reasoning can
539   inspire robust frameworks that translate words into actions, paving the way for more trustworthy AI
      systems through the interplay between rule-based experts and in-context LLM agents.

724    A    R ELATED W ORKS
725
726   We provide the detailed related works as follows.
727   Dueling Bandits. The problem of dueling bandits was initially introduced in (Yue et al., 2012).
728   Various methods have been proposed to tackle the task since then. These methods can be broadly
729   classified into two categories as Explore-Then-Exploit methods and Ongoing Regret Minimization
730   methods according to (Zoghi et al., 2014b). Explore-Then-Exploit methods focus on identifying the
731   best arm with high confidence before exploiting it, such as Interleaved Filter (IF) (Yue et al., 2012)
732   and Beat the Mean (BTM) (Yue & Joachims, 2011), etc. In contrast, Ongoing Regret Minimization
733   methods explicitly target the objective of minimizing cumulative regret, including Relative Upper
734   Confidence Bound (RUCB) (Zoghi et al., 2014a) and Self-Sparring (Sui et al., 2017), etc. Dueling
735
      bandit problem and preference feedback in general has a wide variety of applications, including
      recommendation systems (Yue et al., 2012), robotics (Tucker et al., 2020), and most recently, the
736
      training algorithm of large language models, such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
737
      (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022).
738
739   LLM Agents for Multi-Armed Bandits. Several recent works have explored evaluating the capa-
740   bilities of LLMs in bandit problems. For example, (Baheri & Alm, 2023) proposed an approach
741
      to enhance contextual bandits by integrating LLMs as encoders. The LLMs’ ability to capture rich
      semantic and syntactic information from textual contexts is leveraged to provide the algorithm with a
742
      more informative representation of the context. The LLM-augmented algorithm transforms the raw
743
      context into a latent space vector using the LLM’s encoding capabilities. This encoded context is then
744   used to guide the decision-making process. (Krishnamurthy et al., 2024) investigates whether LLMs
745   can engage in exploration in simple MAB environments without additional training. They compared
746   various prompt designs and found that GPT-4 with zero-shot chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning and
747   an externally summarized interaction history performed the best, while other configurations failed in
748   exploration, either by never selecting the best arm after initial rounds or by selecting all arms nearly
749   equally often. Different from the previous results, in this work we go beyond the settings of numeric
750   rewards and investigate the capabilities of LLMs under preference feedback.
751   In-Context LLMs for Decision-Making. Beyond bandit problems, LLM agents have demonstrated
752   strong capabilities in complex reasoning across a wide range of in-context reinforcement learning
753   and decision-making tasks (Laskin et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2024).
754   Various existing works aim to understand LLM agents’ capabilities for in-context decision-making,
755   with notable examples including planning (Huang et al., 2022; Hao et al., 2023). Additionally, LLM
      agents have been shown to enhance embodied agents in robotic applications by providing advanced


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      task planning abilities (Brohan et al., 2023) and reward designing (Ma et al., 2023), further enabling
757   the development of lifelong learning agents (Wang et al., 2023). Besides these empirical successes,
758   the authors of (Park et al., 2024) analyzed LLMs’ interactions in online learning and game theory
759   settings through the lens of the regret metrics. They identified simple cases where LLMs fail to be
760   no-regret. Another line of research incorporates LLMs into classic decision-making frameworks to
761   create LLM-augmented online decision-makers. For instance, Liu et al. (Liu et al., 2024) utilized
762   LLMs to enhance the components of warm starting, sampling candidates, and surrogate modeling in
763   Bayesian optimization. Our work contributes to this broad area by integrating LLM agents with the
764   classic Explore-then-Exploit DB algorithms to enhance the utilization of preference feedback.

1019

1020   over bj is determined by P (i ≻ j) = θ(i)/(θ(i) + θ(j)). Setting the number of arms K = 5, we
1021   randomize the order of the arms to prevent selection bias, resulting in the following arm ordering:
1022   b5 ≻ b3 ≻ b2 ≻ b1 ≻ b4 . We use two instances: Transitive-Easy and Transitive-Hard,
1023   with their respective θ parameters given by:
1024

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

1029   Stochastic Triangle Inequality (STI) properties (Yue et al., 2012) (see Appendix B.3.1 for more
1030   details). The settings of the used BTL model also imply the existence of a Condorcet winner.

1032
1033   In intransitive instances, the preference matrices are constructed to violate both the Strong Stochastic
1034   Transitivity (SST) and Stochastic Triangle Inequality (STI) properties. This design creates cyclic
1035
       preferences among the non-winning arms while preserving the existence of a Condorcet winner.
       Setting K = 5, we still use the same shuffled arm ordering: b5 ≻ b3 ≻ b2 ≻ b1 ≻ b4 for intransitive
1036
       instances.
1037
1038   • Intransitive-Easy instance: The Condorcet winner b5 has a strong preference over any
1039     other arm:
1040                  P (5 ≻ j) = 0.8, P (j ≻ 5) = 0.2, ∀j ∈ {1, . . . , 4}.
1041
         Among the non-winning arms b1 , . . . , b4 , cyclic preferences are introduced via:
1042
1043             P (i ≻ j) = 0.8 − 0.2 · ((j − i − 1) mod (K − 1)) ,             ∀i, j ∈ {1, . . . , 4}, i ̸= j.
1044
         This configuration ensures a clear dominance by b5 .
1045
1046
1047
       • Intransitive-Hard instance: The Condorcet winner’s preference is weaker, with:
1048                          P (5 ≻ j) = 0.6,     P (j ≻ 5) = 0.4,          ∀j ∈ {1, . . . , 4}.
1049
1050
         This setting makes it more challenging to identify b5 as the Condorcet winner.
1051
1052   Finally, in both instances, the symmetry condition is imposed for consistency:
1053
                             P (j ≻ i) = 1 − P (i ≻ j),         ∀i, j ∈ {1, . . . , K}, i ̸= j.
1054
