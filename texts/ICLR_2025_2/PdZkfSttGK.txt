011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Modern recording techniques enable neuroscientists to simultaneously study
014            neural activity across large populations of neurons, with capturing predictor-
015
               dependent correlations being a fundamental challenge in neuroscience. Moreover,
               the fact that input covariates often lie in restricted subdomains, according to exper-
016
               imental settings, makes inference even more challenging. To address these chal-
017
               lenges, we propose a set of nonparametric mean-covariance regression models for
018            high-dimensional neural activity with restricted inputs. These models reduce the
019            dimensionality of neural responses by employing a lower-dimensional latent fac-
020            tor model, where both factor loadings and latent factors are predictor-dependent,
021            to jointly model mean and covariance across covariates. The smoothness of neural
022            activity across experimental conditions is modeled nonparametrically using two
023            Gaussian processes (GPs), applied to both loading basis and latent factors. Addi-
024            tionally, to account for the covariates lying in restricted subspace, we incorporate
025            graph information into the covariance structure. To flexibly infer the model, we
026
               use an MCMC algorithm to sample from posterior distributions. After validating
               and studying the properties of proposed methods by simulations, we apply them to
027
               two neural datasets (local field potential and neural spiking data) to demonstrate
028
               the usage of models for continuous and counting observations. Overall, the pro-
029            posed methods provide a framework to jointly model covariate-dependent mean
030            and covariance in high dimensional neural data, especially when the covariates
031            lie in restricted domains. The framework is general and can be easily adapted to
032            various applications beyond neuroscience.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Modern neural recording techniques, such as high-density silicon probes (Jun et al., 2017; Steinmetz
038   et al., 2021; Marshall et al., 2022) and large-scale calcium imaging methods (Ahrens et al., 2013;
039
      Kim et al., 2016; Grienberger et al., 2022), allow us to obtain massive neural activity data across
      different regions. Capturing heteroscedasticity in multivariate processes and correlations among
040
      these neurons, potentially along with experimental stimulus (e.g., visual gratings) or animal behav-
041
      iors (e.g., animal locations and movement speed) as covariates, is important for providing scientific
042   insight.
043
044   Given the prevalence of time series data in neuroscience, numerous methods have been developed
045
      for time series modeling. For example, to capture correlation patterns of high-dimensional neural
      data, several unsupervised latent factor models have been widely used in neuroscience community,
046
      either 1) assuming the latent factors evolve linearly with a Gaussian noise (Macke et al., 2011) or 2)
047
      modeling the progression of latent factors more generally by a Gaussian process (Yu et al., 2009).
048   These two modeling strategies leads to two fundamental latent factor models: 1) linear dynamic
049   systems model (LDS, i.e., the dynamic latent factor model) and 2) Gaussian process factor analysis
050   model (GPFA). Based on these models, we can further include the covariates into the model (may
051   also model smoothness of parameters by LDS/ GP), to study the relationship between neural activ-
052   ity and interested features. However, these methods only model the dynamics of mean and assume
053   homoscedastic across time and covariates. Although it’s possible to reduce residual correlation over
      time/ covariates by more intricate modeling, additional complexity is not efficient and may lead to


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      overfitting of the data. Moreover, experimental findings suggest that the variability of neural activ-
055   ity changes over time and across experiment settings (Churchland et al., 2010; 2011), potentially
056   providing information about the external world beyond the average neural activity, such as signature
057   of decision making (Churchland et al., 2011), movement preparation (Churchland et al., 2006), or
058   stimulus onset (Churchland et al., 2010).
059
      Therefore, our focus here is on mean-covariance modeling for high dimensional neural data, con-
060
      sidering either continuous or categorical experiment covariates. In the context of time series analy-
061   sis, which is a special case, modeling volatility (conditional standard deviation) has a long history
062   (Chib et al., 2009), including multivariate (generalized) autoregressive conditional heteroscedas-
063   ticity (GARCH, Engle (1982); Bollerslev (1986); Engle (2002)), multivariate stochastic volatility
064   models (Harvey et al., 1994) and Wishart process (Philipov and Glickman, 2006b;a; Gourieroux
065   et al., 2009). Within computational neuroscience community, some LDS-based methods such as dy-
066   namic Conway-Maxwell model (Wei and Stevenson, 2023), allow for over- and under-dispersion by
067   dynamic modeling of both mean and dispersion parameter on covariates for neural spikes (counting
068   time series). These models can potentially handle high-dimensional neural recordings by incorpo-
069
      rating dynamic latent factors, although the parametrization may not be efficient, and the Markov
      assumption may be inappropriate. In general context, Nejatbakhsh et al. (2023) recently proposed
070
      a model based on (Gaussian-)Wishart process (Philipov and Glickman, 2006b; Gourieroux et al.,
071
      2009) especially for repeated trials, to handle the smoothness of mean and covariance over covari-
072   ates. However, their method may have poor performance in the case with massive neurons. Beyond
073   the neuroscience community, the research on large-scale mean-variance regression also has a long
074   history. Some classical strategies rely on regression to elements of log or Cholesky decomposition
075   of conditional covariance (Chiu et al., 1996; Pourahmadi, 1999; Leng et al., 2010; Zhang and Leng,
076   2012), which are ill-suited for high dimensional data. Instead, Hoff and Niu (2012) modeled covari-
077   ance as a quadratic function on covariates with baseline, though parametric assumption may limit the
078   usage of the model. Fox and Dunson (2015) proposed a Bayesian non-parametric model for continu-
079   ous response, by assuming both latent factor and basis of factor loading are covariate-dependent, and
080
      handle the smoothness by GP. Some methods, such as Franks and Hoff (2019), Wang et al. (2019)
      and Franks (2022) have also been proposed for high-dimensional covariate settings (p   N ).
081
082   However, there are still several challenges, especially for neuroscience applications. First, the spike
083   count data are majorly used for studying neural activity, but the counting observations make the
084   inference intractable. Second, many covariates, such as animal locations and movement orienta-
085   tions, fall in restricted subspace, ignoring the subspace information may lead to inappropriate mean-
086
      covariance inference. To address these problems, motivated by Fox and Dunson (2015) and Dunson
      et al. (2022), we introduce a latent factor covariance regression model, based on graph properties of
087
      covariates, and the model is flexibly inferred by an efficient MCMC algorithm.
088
089   The rest of this paper is structured as follows. In section 2, we introduce the mean-covariance re-
090   gression model for high dimensional neural data with restricted inputs, considering both continuous
091   and counting observations. The key steps of MCMC algorithm for sampling posterior distributions
092
      are provided. Then, after validating and studying the proposed methods using synthetic datasets in
      Section 3, we apply our methods to two neural data (local field potential and neural spikes data) in
093
      Section 4, to illustrate usage with continuous and counting observations. Finally, in section 5, we
094
      conclude with some final remarks and discuss some potential extensions of our current model for
095   future research.

      2.2   G RAPH BASED GP FOR R ESTRICTED I NPUTS
163
164   In neuroscience experiment and many other applications, it is common for the inputs to fall in a re-
165   stricted space. For example, in target reaching experiments, both the targets and animals movements
166   are confined to small areas Wise (1985); Galiñanes et al. (2018), and in maze running experiment,
167   the animals are even restricted to move along the pre-designed path (Mizuseki et al., 2013). These
168   restrictions are usually not easy to transform into a non-restricted space, and in some situation the
169   restriction cannot be known in advance, e.g. because of animals internal preferences.
170   Ignoring these restrictions may lead to a sub-optimal results. For instance, two points that are close
171   in Euclidean space might be far apart in a restricted space, and modeling in the Euclidean space
172   may lead to inappropriate smoothness. Several methods have been proposed to address these issues
173   within the framework of GP regression. When the restricted space is a known submanifold, we
174   can extrinsically embed the manifold in a higher-dimensional Euclidean space (Lin et al., 2019), or
175   intrinsically approximate heat kernel (Niu et al., 2019) by Monte Carlo or use other valid kernels
176
      (Li et al., 2023; Borovitskiy et al., 2020). For an unknown submanifold, we can instead employ
      locally linear regression methods (Cheng and Wu, 2013), and we can also use similar ideas in semi-
177
      supervised approaches (Zhu et al., 2003; Zhu, 2005; Belkin et al., 2006; Nadler et al., 2009; Dunlop
178
      et al., 2020; Wang and Lerman, 2015). However, whether known or unknown, these methods assume
179   the subspace is a manifold, which may not be appropriate in some cases.
180
181   Here, we use the kernel based on Graph Laplacian (GL) proposed by Dunson et al. (2022), and hence
182
      both ψ(x) and ξ(x) are modeled by GL-GPs, denoted ψm ∼ GLGP(0, cψ ) and ξlm ∼ GLGP(0, cξ ).

200
      The model is inferred by a MCMC algorithm. The sampling details can be found in the appendix
201
      Section B, and we provide some key sampling strategies here.
202
203   First, the sampling of the Poisson model for count data (e.g. neural spikes) can be intractable because
204   of non-conjugacy. However, we can approximate a Poisson distribution by a negative binomial
205   (NB) distribution, using the fact that limr→∞ NB (r, σ(ζ − log r)) = Poisson(eζ ), where σ(ζ) =
206
      eζ /(1 + eζ ) and NB(r, p) denotes the NB distribution with expectation be rp/(1 − p). By using
      a large enough dispersion parameter r, we can treat a Poisson distribution as an NB distribution,
207
      which follows the Pólya-Gamma (PG) augmentation scheme (Windle et al., 2013; Polson et al.,
208
      2013). Specifically, for response from neuron i under condition j, we introduce an auxiliary variable
209   ωij ∼ PG(rij + yij , ζij − log rij ), where PG(a, b) denotes the Pólya-Gamma distribution. Then
210                                                                                               −1
      we can sample the pseudo response ζij ∼ N (mij , Vij ), where Vij = ωij + σi−2                 , mij =
211                −2
212
      Vij (κij + σi µij ) and κij = (yij − rij )/2 + ωij log(rij ). With samples of ζij , the sampling for
      other parameters is the same as in the Gaussian case.
213
214   Second, even by factorizing the loading as Λ(x) = Θξ(x), sampling L × k × p parameters for
215   ξ(x) directly from the joint posterior can be infeasible when p is large (i.e., many experimental
      conditions). This is especially common for neural data analysis, since the recording length can be


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      very long. In other words, if we take the timestamp as a covariate and try to track the mean and
217   covariance along the time (and potentially experimental settings), the sampling procedure can be
218   very cumbersome. Therefore, we sample each element of ξ(x) sequentially. This procedure can be
219   looped multiple times within each MCMC iteration to achieve better mixing.
220
      Third, although Fox and Dunson (2015) claims that the model is relatively robust to the GP hyper-
221
      parameters because of quadratic mixing over GP dictionary elements, we observe that the inference
222
      can be sensitive to hyper-parameters ({ , K, t}) for GL-GP. Here, for modeling latent factor and
223   loading with GL-GP prior, we can fix the hyper-parameters by maximizing likelihood, marginally
224   or conditionally on ψ(x) and ζ(x) according to burn-in samples, or sample them in each itera-
225   tion (again marginally or conditionally on samples of ψ(x) and ζ(x)). Here, we found that sam-
226   pling on marginal distribution is computationally cumbersome, and hence using slice sampler may
227   not be feasible for large datasets (Murray and Adams, 2010). To choose the hyper-parameters
228   more efficiently, we can also use a data-driven heuristic, based on the fact that the autocorrelation
229   ACF(x) = corr(Σij (0), Σij (x)) is specified by the kernel function (Fox and Dunson, 2015).

235   All the following experiments, including applications in Section 4, are conducted on a laptop with
236   an Intel(R) Core(TM) i7-8665U CPU @ 1.90GHz 2.11 GHz. Here, we simulate recordings of 50
237   neurons, for both continuous and counting responses, when an animal is restricted to move within a
238
      ”two boxes linking with a tunnel” area, i.e., two squares with side length 3, connected with a 2-by-1
      rectangle. The input features x ∈ R2 are coordinates within the restricted domain. The response
239
      is generated from the model in Section 2.1 for both Poisson and Gaussian cases, with L = 4 and
240
      k = 2. The loading coefficients Θ are independently generated by a Gaussian distribution. The
241   latent factor mean (ψm (x)) and loading basis (ζlm (x)) are generated by mixtures of scaled Gaussian
242   density. Here, we can observe response from 100 random locations, and our goal is to infer mean
243   and covariance for the whole restricted area, including 1000 other random locations as test (held-
244   out). We independently generate the data and conduct analysis six times to check robustness of our
245   methods (Section D.1), and one set of data is illustrated in Figure 1A and D.
246
      For each set of simulated training data, we fit four models: Gaussian process Wishart process
247   (GPWP) model (Nejatbakhsh et al., 2023) (on a single trial), latent GP model (L-GP), latent GL-GP
248   model with fixed hyper-parameters (L-GLGP-fixed) and latent GL-GP model with hyper-parameters
249   sampled (L-GLGP-adaptive). All kernels used here are squared exponential for fair comparisons.
250   The tuning parameters for GPWP are selected by 5-fold cross-validation. The bandwidths for L-GP
251   are sampled within MCMC, and the samples from L-GP are used to select the hyper-parameters
252   for L-GLGP-fixed. For all latent factor models (L-models), we use both 1) L = 10, k = 2 and 2)
253   L = 10, k = 5. The running time can be found in the Appendix C. For all six experiments, the
254   held-out log-likelihood for latent factor models, using either k = 2 or k = 5 is shown in Section
255
      D.1, and one set of them is visualized in Figure 1B and E. For this set of experiment, the fitted
      mean and covariance latent factor based models (L-GP, L-GLGP-fixed and L-GLGP-adaptive) are
256
      projected to the first three principal components of the ground true mean response, which captures
257
      more than 90% variance of the data. The fitting results corresponding to Figure 1B and E in PC
258   space for k = 2 are shown in Figure 1C and A1, and for k = 5 in Figure A2 (Gaussian) and A3
259   (Poisson).

321   4.1   LFP DATASET
322
323   In the LFP dataset, the neural activity across multiple brain regions is recorded when the mice
      perform a task on choosing the side with the highest contrast for visual gratings. The data contains

324
      39 sessions from 10 mice, and each session contains multiple trials. Time bins for all measurements
325   are 10 ms, starting 500 ms before stimulus onset. The recording ends at 2000 ms after the stimulus,
326   and hence each trial contains data from 250 time points. See Steinmetz et al. (2019) for more details
327   of the LFP dataset.
328
      To show the application of proposed methods with Gaussian response, we study the relationship
329
      between LFP response and pupil conditions (area and location). This is motivated by some previous
330
      research in monkey, which found that the pupil size of monkeys can reflect neural activity (Joshi
331   and Gold, 2020), containing LFPs, in several brain regions, including 1) cortical modulation of
332   the pretectal olivary nucleus (PON) (Gamlin et al., 1995), 2) the superior colliculus (SC) (Wang
333   et al., 2012; Krauzlis et al., 2013; McDougal and Gamlin, 2015) and 3) the locus coeruleus (LC) -
334   norepinephrine (NE) neuromodulary system (Alnæs et al., 2014; Joshi et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2012;
335   Liu et al., 2017). Moreover, the eye position of monkey are related to neural activity in superior
336   colliculus (SC), although the position tuning is more common with build-up or burst activity and
337   less common in neurons with visual activity (Campos et al., 2006).
338   Here, we choose LFP recordings from the 13th session, which include LFPs from 14 areas. These
339   regions contain 1) the midbrain reticular nucleus (MRN), sensory and motor superior colliculus
340   (SCs, Scm) in the midbrain, 2) the secondary motor area (MOs) in the cerebral cortex, and 3) the
341   zona incerta (ZI) in the hypothalamus. All these areas have been found to have significant inputs
342   to LC-NE neurons (Breton-Provencher et al., 2021). Here, we use 4 trials (trials 7-10), and 70% of
343   these 1000 data points are used as training while remaining are held out as the test dataset (Figure
344   2A). In other words, the dimension of training response is 14 × 700 (n = 14, p = 700) and testing
345
      is 14 × 300. In the training, each iteration takes 3.5 seconds. Three pupil covariates (area, hori-
      zontal and vertical position, i.e. q = 3, Figure 2B and C) are considered in the model, and they are
346
      standardized before model fitting. Three models are used (L-GP, L-GLGP-fixed/adaptive), where
347
      k = 4 and L = 5. In this case, the pupil locations and areas are quite restricted, the L-GLGP per-
348   forms better than L-GP, and the model is further improves by sampling hyperparameters in MCMC,
349   according to the held-out log-likelihood (Figure 2D).

      We then check the mean and variance patterns obtained from the L-GLGP-adaptive for pupil loca-

      5   D ISCUSSION
424
425
      In this paper, we introduce a covariance regression model for high dimensional neural data, ac-
426   counting for both continuous and counting observations. To accommodate the restricted experi-
427   mental inputs/ covariates, we consider using a graph based Gaussian process (GLGP), to model the
428   smoothness over covariates for both loading basis and latent factors. The model is inferred by an
429   MCMC algorithm, where the counting observations are handled by a Pólya-Gamma (PG) data aug-
430   mentation technique. After validating and studying the proposed methods by simulations, we apply
431   them to two publicly available datasets to illustrate the usage of models with both continuous (LFP
      dataset) and counting observations (HC dataset).

459
460   Although the proposed method can successfully model the mean and covariance according to co-
461   variates, there are some potential improvements. First, we assume independent GPs on covariates
462   for each basis and factor dimension, to achieve computational efficiency. However, this assumption
463   may miss some important covariance structures for different objects/ neurons, and similar results are
464   found in gene expression data (Cai et al., 2023). Therefore, it would be attractive to model using
465   multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) whenever computationally feasible, either simply by linear
466
      combinations of independent GP, such as linear model of coregionalization (Philipov and Glick-
      man, 2006b) or convolution model (Alvarez and Lawrence, 2008), or using more advanced spectral
467
      mixture to handle cross-covariance (Ulrich et al., 2015; Parra and Tobar, 2017). Second, even un-
468
      der independent GP assumption, the MCMC sampling can be cumbersome for large scale dataset,
469   which is common in neural data analysis (e.g. long recordings of spiking data). The main reasons
470   for using sampling method are checking exact posterior distributions and choosing basis dimension
471   L flexibly. However, in applications to massive data, approximation by variational inference, using
472   methods to reduce computational cost of GP covariance matrix inversion (e.g., Zhu et al. (2024)) or
473   using special cases of GP whenever appropriate (e.g. use linear dynamics for time series data) can
474   be useful. Third, even though the basis dimension L is chosen adaptively by shrinkage prior, we
475   still need to specify latent dimension k in advance, which may influence the Poisson model signifi-
476   cantly (at least for L-GP). Instead, we can further sample the number of latent factors by birth-death
477
      MCMC (BDMCMC, Stephens (2000)), as in Fokoué and Titterington (2003), which requires very
      little mathematical sophistication and is easy for interpretation. Besides BDMCMC and shrinkage
478
      prior used for L, there are several other ways for choosing latent dimension, such as using multi-
479
      plicative exponential process prior (Wang et al., 2016), Beta process prior (Paisley and Carin, 2009;
480   Chen et al., 2010) or Indian Buffet process prior(Knowles and Ghahramani, 2007; 2011; Ročková
481   and George, 2016) on the loading matrix in the Gaussian factor analysis model. However, these
482   methods can be difficult in our case, since we further factorize the loading with basis. Finally, we
483   observe that in Poisson version of the model, the L-GLGP can sometimes be sensitive to hyper-
484   parameters for covariance functions. To stabilize the L-GLGP, we can assume several constraints/
485   priors based on initial fitting of L-GP, or use a slice sampler for these hyper-parameters (Murray and
      Adams, 2010) to achieve better mixing, if the computation is feasible.

486
      Overall, as the scale of data becomes large (e.g., simultaneously observing many neurons), it can
487   be challenging to estimate the mean and covariance (either across subjects/neurons or across co-
488   variates). Moreover, the constraints on input space/covariates make the inference more difficult.
489   Therefore in this paper, we build a framework to accommodate both problems for continuous and
490   counting observations. The proposed methods are quite general, and they have potential for appli-
491   cation to data beyond neuroscience.
