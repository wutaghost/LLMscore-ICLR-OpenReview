011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Speaker identification is the task of identifying a person who is currently talking
014            by analysing microphone signals. Typical automatic speaker identification sys-
015            tems use a single microphone and require complete utterances of 4-10 seconds
016
               in length to accurately identify a person from an enrollment set. We introduce
               the related problem of detecting which person is talking among several people in
017
               a room when the utterances are very short, e.g., a single word, or a short laugh.
018
               Since utterance lengths are too short for conventional methods, we take inspira-
019            tion from the way humans solve this problems - using two ears and a joint under-
020            standing of both semantic and spatial context. To solve this problem, we propose
021            Spatial Speaker ID, which uses banded covariance features derived from multi-
022            microphone input along with conventional banded power to identify talkers based
023            on both the semantic characteristics of a sound and the spatial location of a sound.
024            The internal representation learnt in Spatial Speaker ID jointly contains both spa-
025            tial and voice characteristic information and is learnt contrastively, whereby two
026            utterances that come from the same talker in the same location are required to
027
               have similar embeddings. We learn a binary classification downstream task that
               determines if two sets of embeddings come from the same talker in the same lo-
028
               cation. Using this binary classifier, we compare multiple ways of presenting the
029
               microphone covariance features to the upstream models. We show the importance
030            of spatial information for identifying talkers on short utterances with interfering
031            noise.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Most acoustic speaker identification systems use a single channel of input. This works well when
037   there is only person talking at once, the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is favourable and the person to
038   be identified talks for long enough to distinguish themselves from the other candidates. In this work,
039   we deal with a case in which multiple people are present in a room but say only very short utterances,
040   often simultaneously, in noisy, reverberant far-field conditions. To deal with this challenging case
041   we turn to multiple microphone input channels to disambiguate who is speaking.
042   Humans deal with these sorts of complex acoustic conditions by forming joint spatial and semantic
043   content. That is, we not only use the sound of a person’s voice to determine who spoke, but also
044   complex acoustic features from our two ears, such as interaural level difference (ILD) and interaural
045   time difference (ITD). Inspired by this, we propose a model, Spatial Speaker ID, with multiple
046   microphones in first order ambisonic (FOA) format to identify who is currently speaking.
047
      To train our model, we create assemble a dataset of short utterances (0.3-2 s in length) and convolve
048   them with the output of a FOA room impulse response (RIR) generator which can accurately simu-
049   late the complex spatial signatures of speech incident on a microphone array from different locations
050   in the same room. We use this training data in a contrastive representation learning regime which
051   requires that our model must jointly distinguish between locations and talkers. The resulting latent
052   space contains information not only of who spoke, but where they spoke. We evaluate the effective-
053   ness of this upstream model with several downstream tasks and we find that the spatial information
      has a dramatic positive influence on the system’s ability to determine who is talking.

      1.1   M ULTI -M ICROPHONE S PEAKER I DENTIFICATION ON S HORT FAR -F IELD U TTERANCES
055
056   This work is motivated by the following real-world problem statement. A room contains multiple
057   people. People may leave or enter the room at any time. People may move from place to place
058   within the room at any time. From time to time people speak short utterances such as a single word,
059   an exclamation, a short laugh, a cry of surprise. Sometimes these utterances partially overlap or
060   occur simultaneously. There is background noise present, such as road noise from outside or the
061   sound of cooking. The sound in the room is recorded by a small aperture multi-microphone array
062   placed approximately 1-5m from the people. We want to detect the short utterances and also identify
063
      which person spoke each utterance. We call this problem Multi-Microphone Speaker Identification
      on Short Far-Field Utterances (MSISFU).
064
065   This problem has important applications in engagement detection and analysis, interactive gaming,
066   Extended Reality (XR) and content personalisation for social scenarios in which several people are
067   engaged together in a media experience, such as watching a football game together in a living room.
068   In this example, the microphone array might be mounted on the television, coffee table or credenza,
069
      multiple people might be seated on the couch, while others might be at the refridgerator getting
      drinks.
070
071   This problem has several similarities with conventional Speaker Identification and Verification. We
072   can use features of each person’s voice in order to determine who is speaking. However, unlike the
073   conventional Speaker Identification and Verification problem statement, we consider utterances as
074   short as 300ms, and they may occur simultaneously or partially overlapped. Since this is a far-field
075
      acoustic environment, acoustic reverberation within the room plays a major role. Therefore, the
      problem also bears similarities to the cocktail party problem. Since a multi-microphone array is
076
      available, we are inspired by the way the human brain uses spatial information to disentangle sound
077
      in real-world reverberent acoustic environments.
078

080   1.2   J OINT S EMANTIC AND S PATIAL R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
081
      To solve the problem of detecting who is speaking on short far-field utterances, we combine con-
082
      trastive representation learning and spatial input and develop training technique which enforces a
083   latent space containing both semantic (speaker identification) and spatial information. Our con-
084   trastive proxy task requires that embedding pairs are similar for input pairs containing the same
085   speaker in the same location in the same room speaking different short utterances, but more dis-
086   tant for input pairs containing different speakers or for input pairs containing the same speaker in
087   different locations in the same room.
088
      A key question to tackle is how should multiple microphone channels be presented to a model in or-
089   der to obtain best results. A naive approach would be to derive a single direction-of-arrival estimate
090   from the multiple microphones. However, these approaches have proven error-prone in real world
091   reverberant acoustic environments where the direction of arrival is obscured by reflections from
092   different directions. We therefore use the spatial covariance matrix of the microphone array in log-
093   spaced frequency bands as the core input features for our system. We compare multiple alternatives
094   for representing the covariance information to our model, using the singular value decomposition
095   technique from SALSA (Nguyen et al. (2022b)) as a baseline, but also consider a variety of cheaper
096   fixed and learnt alternatives. In particular, we introduce a method of summarising covariance which
097
      we call the Power Vector. We show that the Power Vector provides very efficient learning of spatial
      information by the network, while remaining extremely simple and cheap to implement.

121   2.1   S PEAKER I DENTIFICATION AND V ERIFICATION
122
123   Speaker Identification and Verification are long-standing research tasks with a rich history of contri-
124
      butions. Speaker Identification is the task of determining, from a recording of one person’s speech,
      which person from an enrolled set is speaking. Typical solutions require 3-10s of near field speech
125
      in relatively noise-free conditions from a single microphone in order to determine who is speaking.
126
      Speaker Identification has applications in personalisation systems, particularly in virtual assistants,
127   where the context of who is speaking is used to better understand the command. For example, in
128   order to ”play my music”, a virtual assistant must recognise who I am.
129
130
      Speaker Verification is a related task that starts with a presumed identity of the speaker and produces
      a confidence that the person speaking is who they say they are. Typically the recording of the
131
      speaker’s voice is from a single near-field microphone, relatively free of noise and at least 3-10s in
132
      length. Speaker Verification has applications in security, such as protecting access to a bank account
133   or door lock.
134
135   As summarised by Kinnunen & Li (2010) and Hansen & Hasan (2015), Speaker Identification and
136
      Verfication approaches have commonly used a ”supervector” which encapsulates statistical differ-
      ences in the distributions of features present in the speech of a particular speaker compared to those
137
      in a Universal Background Model (UBM). More recent approaches, such as Desplanques et al.
138
      (2020), use a deep learning based representation space known as an x-vector to distinguish speakers.
139   In the last few years, self-supervised speech representation learning models such as WavLM (Chen
140   et al. (2022)) produce SOTA results on the downstream task of Speaker Verification when evaluated
141   on datasets such as VoxCeleb, which has a minimum utterance length of 4s and a mean utterance
142   length of 8.2s (Nagrani et al. (2017)). On the other hand, our MSISFU task requires 0.3-2s length
143   utterances.
144
      Conventional Speaker Identification and Verification systems are generally designed to work with
145   only a single input channel. When multiple microphones are present on the device, which is typical
146   in smart speaker and automobile microphone arrays, the Speaker Identification and Verification
147   system is often preceeded by a beamformer, which combines microphone signals together to form
148   the cleanest possible monophonic signal on which to identify speech. However, the core speech
149   processing model is trained on single channel input and operates without any directional context, or
150   context on how loud any interferring speech or noise sources were.
151
152   2.2   S OUND E VENT L OCALISATION AND D ETECTION (SELD)
153
154   In recent years, it’s been shown that machine-learning models can learn semantic information
155   through self-supervised learning with proxy tasks. For example, HuBERT (Hsu et al. (2021)) learns
156   a latent space from single channel input that contains semantic (phonetic and speaker identification)
157   information. Recent works have shown that spatial information can be used for the task of Sound
158
      Event Detection and Localisation (SELD).
159   There are a range of spatial feature extractors that are being used in the SELD literature, such as,
160   FOA intensity vectors (Yasuda et al. (2020)), SALSA (Nguyen et al. (2022b)), GCC-PHAT (Cao
161   et al. (2019)), SALSA-Lite (Nguyen et al. (2022a)) or a combination of these frontends (Wang et al.
      (2020)). The room impulse responses (RIR) generator we used in this work generates FOA RIRs.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      As such we compare against SALSA, a state-of-the-art feature extraction frontend for spatial audio
163   machine learning models.
164
165   Our problem statement bears similarities with previous work on Sound Event Localisation and De-
166
      tection (SELD). In both cases, the sounds in question might be short. In our case, the sounds of
      interest are human utterances and the task is to detect which person produced each sound, wheres in
167
      the SELD case the task is to detect, localise and classify each sound into one of several classes (eg a
168
      dog barking, a car engine starting). Note that in our problem, we do not necessarily need to localise
169   each person in polar or cartesian coordinates. It is only necessary to disambiguate them when they
170   speak.

      3.1   S PATIAL S PEAKER ID P ROXY TASK
175
176   Our primary Spatial Speaker ID model is trained on a contrastive proxy task that encourages the
177   formation of a latent space that jointly represents semantic (what does the voice sound like?) and
178   spatial (where does it come from?) information. The model should ignore interfering noise. The
179   inputs to this proxy task are 2.5 second long simulated FOA signals of a single speaker Sa speaking
180   a short utterance Ub at location Lc in a reverberant room Rc with interfering noise Ne present at
181   location Lf . We form triplets of reference, positive and negative examples and apply triplet loss in
182   order to encourage the following behaviours.
183          • Two unique utterances U1 , U2 from the identical speaker S1 in the identical location L1 in
184            room R1 should produce near embeddings.
185
             • Two unique utterances U3 , U4 from the identical speaker S2 in two unique locations L2 ,
186            L3 in room R2 should produce distant embeddings.
187
             • Two unique speakers S3 , S4 in the identical location L4 in room R3 should produce distant
188
               embeddings, regardless of the utterances.
189
             • The model should be robust to interfering noise and level differences. i.e. two utterance
190
               pairs should produce similar embedding differences regardless of the level of the utterances
191
               Ua , the content of the interfering noises N1 , N2 , the location of the interfering noises L5 ,
192            L6 and the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR).
193
194   A corollary of the above is that two unique speakers S5 , S6 in two unique locations L7 , L8 should
195   also produce distant embeddings. Note that we never compare signals from distinct simulated rooms
196   R4 , R5 since the MSISFU task is about understanding who is talking within a single acoustic envi-
197
      ronment.
198   To encourage noise robustness we mix a distinct noise signal simulated from a distinct location at a
199   distinct SNR with each utterance. To encourage level independence we apply a random gain to each
200   simulated signal before supplying it to the model.
201   Our Spatial Speaker ID model provides an embedding vector for each input frame of audio. We
202   therefore summarise the output embeddings over time using a trained self-attention pooling layer
203   before applying the triplet loss described above. In addition to these behaviours we also want the
204   model to know when speech is actually present since we want to be sensitive to very short utterances
205   (as short as 0.3s within our training inputs of length 2.5s). We therefore apply an additional mean-
206   square loss that requires null output embeddings (i.e. embeddings with a very small magnitude in
207   every dimension) for frames of input that contain noise only without speech.

211
      ambisonic room impulse reponse (RIR) simulator. Our data sources of speech and noise are mono-
212
      phonic and are convolved with these RIRs to create simulated ambisonic signals that can be mixed
213   together as described above. Our RIR simulator has the following input parameters:
214
215          • The position of the source relative to the simulated ambisonic microphone expressed as

      3.3   F EATURE E XTRACTION
228
229
      The input to the Spatial Speaker ID model is an FOA signal. We use a fixed feature extraction
230   pipeline at the model input which applies short time Fourier transform (STFT) with a raised-sine
231   analysis window, 20ms step size with a 50% overlap and a half-frequency-bin kernel shift to analysis
232   each of the FOA microphone channels W, X, Y and Z. This results in complex values for each
233   frequency bin of each channel. We then compute the 4x4 complex spatial covariance matrix across
234   channels in each frequency bin and apply smoothing in time and frequency.
235
      We use a Mel-like frequency smoothing operation which summarises the STFT frequency bins into
236   bands occupying approximately equal log spectrum. When operating on 16kHz-sampled input,
237   this results in 48 log-spaced bands every 20ms. For each of these bands we have a 4x4 complex
238   spatial covariance matrix. These bands are then further smoothed in time across frames to ensure
239   approximately equal statistical power in the spatial covariance matrix for each band.
240
      These unnormalised banded complex spatial covariance matrices are then reduced to real vectors as
241
      described below.
242

244   3.4   R EPRESENTING MULTI - CHANNEL SIGNAL POWER AS A VECTOR
245
      The power spectrum of a monophonic signal simply describes each time-frequency tile in terms of
246
      a scalar signal-power quantity (or some function of the signal-power, such as the logarithm).
247
248   When dealing with an N -channel signal, the natural extension of signal-power is the N × N covari-
249   ance, and it is worth noting that the covariance matrix is a non-negative definite (Hermitian) matrix,
250   and when multiple uncorrelated sound sources are captured by a microphone array, the covariance
251
      of the captured signals will be the sum of the covariance contributions of each individual source
      (superposition).
252
253   It is convenient to separate a covariance matrix, C, into total power (Ptot = tr(C)) and a unit-trace
254   matrix C ′ , so that: C = Ptot C ′ . In general, at this point C ′ is a complex unit-trace matrix. It is
255   desirable to extract a real vector Pcov from this matrix C ′ in order to present it as a feature to a
256   machine learning model. We do this by applying a covariance mapping function M :
257
                                                 Pcov = M (C ′ )                                          (1)
258
259   We consider multiple alternate implementations of M :
260
261          • (M0 ): Drop all covariance information,
262
263
             • (Mlearnt ): concatenate and flatten the real and imaginary parts of C ′ and present them
               through a learnt linear projection,
264
265          • (MU T ): since C ′ is Hermetian, take its upper triangle, concatenate and flatten the real and
266            imaginary parts, and let this function be,
267
             • (MP V ): use the Power Vector Pdir which is introduced below, or
268
269          • (MSALSA ): use the singular value decomposition technique described in SALSA
               (Nguyen et al. (2022b)).

      3.6   D OWNSTREAM TASKS
306
307   To evaluate the performance of our primary Spatial Speaker ID model, we define several downstream
308   tasks that operate on the Spatial Speaker ID embedding.
309
310          • Binary Spatial Speaker ID (B-MSISFU): We present a pair of input vectors (simulated
311
               as for the upstream task) and classify whether those input vectors are from identical talkers
               in an identical location or otherwise. We apply binary cross entropy loss during training.
312
               Performance on this task is our primary indicator that the latent space is appropriate for the
313
               MSISFU task.
314
             • Direction-of-Arrival (DoA): We present an input vector (simulated as for the upstream
315
               task) and predict the direction-of-arrival (a 3-dimensional unit vector in Cartesian co-
316
               ordinates). This task measures how the latent space disambiguates acoustic locations.
317
             • Speaker Identification (SID): We present an input vector (simulated as for the upstream
318
               task) and classify which speaker in the training set spoke the utterance. We apply cross-
319
               entropy loss during training. This task measures how the latent space encapsulates the
320            speaker’s identity.
321
322   We implement each of these downstream tasks by applying self-attention pooling to summarise the
323   framewise Spatial Speaker ID embeddings over time and then using a simple two-layer MLP of
      appropriate dimensionality for each task.

361   4.3   DATA PREPARATION
362
      Preparing data for the spatial talker identification task requires careful construction of batches and
363
      data augmentation. Every batch requires multiple utterances from each talker, utterances to be short,
364
      interference noises and the spatialisation of sounds with matching locations between different utter-
365   ances.
366
367   We produce audio training samples that are 2.5 seconds in length, consisting of interfering noise
368
      with a short utterance of length 0.3-2 seconds that appears at a random time. These audio samples
      form batches, where each batch is created by selecting 30 random speech segments for each of the
369
      random speakers. In experiment 1, we selected 14 random speakers per batch. In experiments 2 and
370
      3, we selected 32 speakers per batch.
371
372   We use a voice activity detector (VAD) to ensure that the short segments we cut from the original
373   training corpus actually contain speech. To avoid abrupt starts and ends of our short utterances, we
374
      apply a 50ms fade-in and fade-out.
375   At each batch we draw a random selection of interference noise to mix with the speech. We discard
376   noise samples from the training corpus that are shorter than 2.5 seconds in length. To ensure the
377   model is well-behaved when no speech is present, we also arrange for 10% of the training samples
      in each batch to contain only noise without any speech present. These samples are considered to

      4.4   L OSS FUNCTION
404
405   To train our Spatial Speaker ID network contrastively, we used a triplet margin loss Hoffer & Ailon
406   (2015). This approach requires sampling triplets from the training data, where triplets are made up
407   of an anchor, positive and negative embedding. Using all possible triplets from the training samples
408   would be impractical given the number of triplets scales quadratically with the number of talkers
409   and cubically with the number utterances per talker in a batch, given our batch construction. With
410   the settings we used, the number of possible triplets would be in the order of millions for our 14
411   talkers per batch experiments. To reduce the number of triplets, we had a maximum of 100 triplets
412
      per anchor, where each training sample could act as an anchor.
413   There also needs to be a well distributed selection of the Spatial Talker ID task cases, as listed in the
414   Spatial Talker ID Proxy Task section. To achieve this we parameterised the sampling of negatives.
415   Each triplet had a probability p of selecting a one of the two more difficult MSISFU cases, an
416   identical speaker S2 in two locations L2 , L3 , and two speakers S3 , S4 in an identical location L4 .
417   The higher proportion of negatives sampled from difficult cases, created a more challenging task to
418
      train on, thus robustifying the Spatial Talker ID model. Through experimentation, we found that
      setting p to 25% provided a good balance for learning a representation that contains both spatial and
419
      voice characteristic information.
420
421   To further regularize the models, we introduce an additional loss term for the null embeddings. This
422   loss, based on Mean Squared Error (MSE), encourages all null embeddings to be as close to zero as
423   possible.

505   6   C ONCLUSION
506
507
      We introduced the Multi-Microphone Speaker Identification on Short Far-Field Utterances (MS-
508
      ISFU) task. The MSISFU task enables the learning of a latent space containing both voice and
509
      spatial characteristic information. Moreover, the task showcases a more realistic alternative to the
510   traditional machine learning task Speaker Identification, which uses longer utterances on mono-
511   phonic audio. This paper details how MSISFU can be learnt contrastively using a proxy downstream
512   task. We described and compared a family of techniques for transforming covariance matrices into
513   suitable inputs for machine learning models, including our proposed Power Vector technique.
514
      We conducted three experiments. Experiment 1 proved that the introduction of spatial information
515
      was extremely useful for the MSISFU task, boosting the performance on the B-MSISFU metric
516   by 13.4%. The results of Experiment 2 demonstrate that model with full covariance information,
517   attain better results. Additionally, we observe beneficial properties in the upstream model’s latent
518   space when training it with hand crafted Power Vector features over a learnt linear projection covari-
519   ance mapper. The Power Vector features allowed the model to learn a latent space which contains
520   DoA information. Experiment 3 showed that linear projection based covariance mappers have small
521   degradation in performance (1.4%), showing comparable results to the more computationally ex-
522   pensive SALSA inspired method. Our evaluation demonstrated that models trained on the MSISFU
523   task can effectively disambiguate speakers based on both their voice and spatial location.
524   There are many avenues for future work:
525
526          • Investigating a Spatial Speaker ID model that can handle generating multiple tracks of
527            embeddings, such that multiple speakers can be identified in a single utterance or per time
528            frame embedding. A model of this type could be trained with a permutation invariant
529            training loss.
530
531          • Researching if it is possible to improve MSISFU results further by allowing the downstream
532
               tasks to learn from the embeddings of each layer in the upstream model, following the likes
               of Hsu et al. (2021), Chen et al. (2022) and Schneider et al. (2019).
533
534
             • Constructing a more comprehensive evaluation of frontend representations and their per-
535            formance on the MSISFU task.
