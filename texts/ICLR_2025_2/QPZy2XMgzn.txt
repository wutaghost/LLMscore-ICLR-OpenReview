011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            With the continuous development of large language models (LLMs), they have
014            demonstrated amazing capabilities in many areas of natural language processing
015            (NLP). However, due to their inherent limitations, the performance of a single
016
               model on many complex reasoning tasks has reached a bottleneck. A feasible
               solution is to introduce external feedback to further improve model performance,
017
               among which multi-model collaboration is a particularly promising approach. In
018
               this paper, we propose token-by-token election (TTE), a novel token-level multi-
019            model collaboration strategy. Different from the common multi-model collabora-
020            tion methods that operates at the overall answer level, TTE performs multi-model
021            elections at the lowest token level. It selects the optimal token from the next token
022            distributions given by multiple LLMs and then generates the answer autoregres-
023            sively, allowing multiple LLMs to reach a consensus on each token. Inspired by
024            human behavior, TTE consists of three election modes, including Cooperation,
025            Competition, and Counting, all of which aim to sample the optimal token from
026            multiple distributions. By strictly controlling the generation quality of each token,
027
               TTE can improve the quality of the overall answer and break through the perfor-
               mance bottleneck of a single LLM. Through extensive experiments on a variety
028
               of different types of reasoning benchmarks, we demonstrate the powerful perfor-
029
               mance of TTE, which further improves the performance compared to the current
030            state-of-the-art single LLM and other multi-model collaborative methods. The
031            code will be released on GitHub.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   With the rapid development of large language models (LLMs), numerous impressive works such
037   as GPT4 (Achiam et al., 2023), Llama3 (Dubey et al., 2024), and Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024) have
038   emerged. People are increasingly accustomed to seeking answers from LLMs when encountering
039
      problems, and even researchers consult LLMs during their scientific work. Although LLMs have
      demonstrated remarkable capabilities in many areas of natural language processing (NLP), they of-
040
      ten show their inability to perform complex reasoning tasks (Fu et al., 2022). Therefore, how to fur-
041
      ther improve the performance of LLMs in complex reasoning tasks has become a hot topic (Kojima
042   et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2023). Enhancing model performance from training side is very costly, as
043   training a language model requires significant resources. Furthermore, performance improvements
044   have begun to plateau due to the slowing impact of scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020; Touvron et al.,
045   2023). Therefore, more and more research (Wei et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022;
046   Madaan et al., 2024; Shinn et al., 2024) has begun to focus on improving model performance from
047   the inference side.
048   Improving the performance of LLMs from the inference side differs from training-based methods
049   which optimize model parameters (Ouyang et al., 2022; Rafailov et al., 2024). It is typically achieved
050   by optimizing the model input (Zhang et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022) or refining the model output
051   (Madaan et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024), so it generally does not require additional training data and
052   computing resources. Two typical methods are the chain of thought (CoT) approaches (Wei et al.,
053   2022; Fu et al., 2022) and the self-correction approaches (Madaan et al., 2024; Shinn et al., 2024).
      The former improves response quality by providing several examples to guide LLMs in answering

080   Different LLMs usually have different knowledge boundaries and their own strengths (Wan et al.,
081   2024; Jiang et al., 2023). If their capabilities can be well synergized, it will certainly enhance
082   the reasoning ability of the language model and break through the performance bottleneck of a
083   single LLM (Khan et al., 2024; Du et al., 2023), which is also the expected goal of multi-model
084
      collaboration. Previous multi-model collaboration methods (Khan et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2023;
      Du et al., 2023) mostly conduct majority voting or discuss at the level of the overall answer. For the
085
      same question, each LLM puts forward its own point of view and tries to convince other models, so
086
      as to finally reach a consensus among multiple models. The debate process usually involves selecting
087   the final answer based on certain rules or introducing a new referee model. This may require many
088   rounds of debate and relies heavily on one of the models to give a strong correct answer and convince
089   the other debaters to get the final answer.
090
      It is generally believed that the knowledge of LLM is stored in its massive parameters, but the
091
      output next token distribution is the specific external manifestation of its knowledge 1 (Hinton,
092
      2015; Wan et al., 2024; Radford et al., 2019). Therefore, a straightforward idea is to combine
093   the knowledge of multiple LLMs by combining the next token distributions given by these
094   models. In this paper, we propose token-by-token election (TTE), a novel token-level multi-model
095   collaboration strategy. Specifically, TTE generates answers based on autoregression (Vaswani, 2017;
096   Radford, 2018), but unlike the autoregression of a single LLM, the token generated by TTE at each
097   step is sampled from multiple next token distributions generated by different LLMs. Since each
098   token is generated through the collaboration of multiple models, the final answer is also produced
099   through their joint decision. Huang et al. (2024) have pointed out that some low-quality answers
100   may start to deteriorate from a certain token. Our starting point is to improve the quality of the
101
      overall answer by improving the quality of each token. We believe that the tokens selected by the
      joint decision of multiple models are better. This behavior is similar to a review team checking the
102
      document word by word to avoid errors and discussing uncertain parts to determine the final wording
103
      thereby improving the quality of document. Moreover, we are surprised to find that this token-level
104   collaboration strategy even exhibits some emergent capabilities. In some cases, TTE can give the

111   In order to sample the optimal token from the next token distributions given by multiple LLMs,
112
      inspired by human behavior, we design three sampling modes: (1) Cooperation, comprehensively
      considering the next token distributions given by multiple LLMs to make a selection; (2) Com-
113
      petition, competing among the candidate tokens given by multiple LLMs to make a selection; (3)
114
      Counting, counting votes for different tokens from multiple LLMs to make a selection. These three
115   election modes are all dedicated to selecting the optimal token from the next token distributions pro-
116   vided by multiple LLMs, strictly controlling the quality of each step of autoregression to improve
117   the overall answer quality, thereby enhancing the language model reasoning.
118
      We conduct extensive experiments on three categories of tasks: mathematical reasoning, symbolic
119
      reasoning, and commonsense reasoning. The results show that our approach achieves significant
120
      improvements over previous strong baselines. In addition, each mode performs well in different cat-
121   egories of tasks, demonstrating its specific strengths. Further exploration reveals the advantages of
122   TTE over existing multi-model methods, achieving our goal of combining the capabilities of differ-
123   ent models, and even demonstrating certain emergent capabilities (Lu et al., 2023). We also conduct
124   an evaluation on a reliability evaluation benchmark, and the results show that TTE can improve the
125   authenticity of the answer to a certain extent, thereby enhancing the credibility of decisions.
126
      In summary, our main contributions include: (1) We propose token-by-token election, a novel token-
127   level multi-model collaboration strategy. As far as we know, we are the first to attempt multi-model
128   collaboration at the token level, offering a new perspective in this field. (2) We introduce three
129   election modes, including cooperation, competition and counting, all of which are committed to
130   improving the quality of the selected token, thus breaking through the performance bottleneck of
131   a single LLM. (3) Evaluations on multiple reasoning benchmarks demonstrate the superior perfor-
132   mance of our proposed method.

      2   T OKEN - BY-T OKEN E LECTION
136
137   Given the same question, different LLMs usually give different answers. This is because they have
138
      learned different knowledge due to the difference in network architecture, training data and training
      process (Raiaan et al., 2024). As mentioned earlier, it is generally believed that the knowledge is
139
      stored in the huge parameters of LLMs (Radford et al., 2019), and the output next token probability
140
      distribution is the specific external manifestation of their knowledge (Hinton, 2015; Wan et al.,
141   2024). Therefore, our starting point is to combine the knowledge of different LLMs by combining
142   these distributions:
143
144
145                                 Pt = Combine(Pt1 , Pt2 , ..., PtK ),
146                                                                                                      (1)
147
                                  KNt = Combine(KNt1 , KNt2 , ..., KNtK ),
148
      where Pt represents distributions, KN represents knowledge and Combine represents a certain
149   combination.
150
151   The primary objective of the proposed TTE is to select the optimal token at each step of autore-
152
      gression through multi-model collaboration, thereby improving the quality of the overall output by
      enhancing the quality of individual tokens, since the output of the language model is composed of
153
      these individual tokens. The overall architecture of TTE is shown in the Figure 1. Specifically,
154
      we aim to combine the different next token probability distributions generated by multiple LLMs
155   to select the current optimal token. As these different distributions encapsulate distinct knowledge
156   from the respective LLMs, our aim is to achieve the optimal integration of diverse LLM capabilities
157   in this manner. By incorporating external feedback and knowledge from various LLMs (Yin et al.,
158   2023), our multi-model collaboration method TTE can overcome the performance limitations of a
159   single model.

      our method and previous methods.

      2.1   P RELIMINARIES
163
164   From the implementation point of view, the proposed TTE is an adjustment based on single model
165   autoregression, as shown in Figure 1. For single LLM inference, given an input text sequence T ext,
166   it will first be tokenized to obtain a token sequence T :
167                                          T = tokenize(text),                                         (2)
168
      where T = {ti }N
                     i=1 , ti represents an independent token in T , and N is the length of T . The tokenize
169
      operation is a necessary step for LLM inference. It converts the text into a sequence of tokens
170   that the LLM can process. This is usually done by a tokenizer equipped with the LLM. Then we
171   feed T into an LLM M, which will calculate the probability distribution P of the next token, P is
172   essentially a conditional probability distribution:
173
                                       P (tN +1 |{ti }N              N
                                                      i=1 ) = M({ti }i=1 ),                              (3)
174
175   P contains the probability values for each token in the LLM tokenizer’s vocabulary being the next
176
      token. The next token will be sampled based on P and will be used as additional input to the original
      sequence to continue generating subsequent tokens until the end token < eos > (end of sentence)
177
      is output. At each step the model is auto-regressive (Graves, 2013), consuming the previously gen-
178
      erated symbols as additional input when generating the next (Vaswani, 2017). Finally, the tokenizer
179   will decode all generated tokens back into text to get the final output result of LLM.
180
181   2.2   E LECTION M ODES
182
      Given the same input, K LLMs {Mj }K                                                    K
                                             j=1 will output K next token distributions {Pj }j=1 . Since the
183   tokenizers of different LLMs are different, we need to decode them into the probability distribution
184   of the next word so that the K LLMs can be aligned in the same semantic space. So we can get
185   K next word distributions {Pjw }K j=1 . What we need to do is to sample the optimal next word
186                        w K
      next word from {Pj }j=1 :
187
188                                   next word = Sample({Pjw }K
                                                               j=1 ).                                    (4)
189   For sampling from a single distribution, strategies such as Top-k (Fan et al., 2018) and Top-p (Holtz-
190   man et al., 2019) are commonly used. For sampling from multiple distributions, inspired by human
191   behaviors, we have designed the following three sampling modes, including Cooperation, Competi-
192   tion and Counting:
193   Cooperation. For each next word probability distribution P w , we use the Top-k (Fan et al., 2018)
194   strategy to sample the top H words with the highest probability (the probability of the other words is
195   very small so there is no need to consider them, which can also reduce complexity). In this way, from
196   the K distributions given by K LLMs, we can get K dictionaries, where each key represents a word,
197   and each value represents its probability. Cooperation is to add the values of these K dictionaries
198   according to the same key, and then select the one with the highest probability. In this way, the
199   optimal next word is elected through multi-model cooperation.
200   Competition. For each next word probability distribution P w , we select the word with the highest
201   probability as the candidate, so we get K candidates from K LLMs. It is generally believed that the
202   higher the probability of the LLM output, the greater its confidence in that output (Radford et al.,
203   2019; Liang et al., 2022; Kuhn et al., 2023; Shih et al., 2023). We select the candidate with the
204   highest probability as the optimal next word, which is equivalent to selecting the LLM with the
205   highest confidence to answer at each step. In this way, the optimal next word is elected through
206
      multi-model competition.
207   Counting. For each next word probability distribution P w , similar to the cooperation mode, we only
208   keep the top H words, which all have the probability of being the next word Fan et al. (2018); Jiang
209   et al. (2021). We count the number of times each word appears in the K dictionaries. The higher
210   the count, the more LLMs we believe recognize it. We select the word with the most appearances as
211   the optimal next word. If two words have the same number of votes, we select the one with a higher
212
      total probability. In this way, the optimal next word is elected through counting the votes given by
      multiple LLMs.

232   2.3    R ELATION TO P RIOR W ORK
233
234   FuseLLM (Wan et al., 2024) also leverages the distributions provided by multiple LLMs, but it is
235   a training-based method aimed at knowledge distillation. It combines the next token probability
236   matrices given by multiple LLMs to obtain soft labels, which are used to supervise the training
237   process of the student model, thereby distilling the knowledge from multiple teacher models into
238   the student model. In contrast, our TTE directly samples from multiple distributions and generates
239
      answers autoregressively, making it a method that does not require training. The two approaches
      have fundamental differences.

      3.3   R ELIABILITY E VALUATION

465   3.6      E XAMPLES OF E MERGENT C APABILITIES
466
467   The goal of TTE is to combine the capabilities of multiple models to break through the performance
468   bottlenecks of a single LLM. Traditional multi-model collaboration methods rely on most models
469   providing the same correct answer for majority voting, or rely on one model providing a highly
470
      confident correct answer to convince the others to reach a consensus. However, we are surprised
      to find that our TTE can provide the correct answer even when each individual model answers
471
      incorrectly, demonstrating a certain level of emergent ability, akin to the saying “two heads are
472
      better than one”.
473

475   three LLMs give wrong answers of 150, 150 and 75 respectively, ignoring the fact that a piece of
476
      paper has two sides or confusing the logical relationship, while TTE gives the correct answer of
      300; for another question, the three single LLMs fail to figure out the meaning of “a self-refine
477
      book” and thus can not give a correct answer, but TTE understands and gives the correct answer. We
478
      speculate that the token-level collision of ideas in TTE has brought some different inspirations, and
479   may sometimes achieve the effect of 1 + 1 being greater than 2. More examples and discussion can
480   be seen in Appendix B.

483
484   In this section, we will introduce some mainstream methods to improve LLM performance from the
485   inference side. These methods are all training-free and do not require a lot of computing resources,
      including chain of thought prompting, self-correction, and multi-model collaboration methods.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      4.1   C HAIN OF T HOUGHT P ROMPTING
487
488   Wei et al. (2022) first proposed the method to improve the reasoning performance of LLM by con-
489   structing chain of thought prompts, mainly by constructing contextual examples to teach LLM to
490   analyze step by step and then output the answer. Since then, many works (Kojima et al., 2022; Wang
491
      et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2022; Chia et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022) have been further improved on
      this basis. In addition to the correct examples, Chia et al. (2023) also added the wrong thinking
492
      process to let the model further learn to avoid mistakes. Kojima et al. (2022) proposed a zero-
493
      shot CoT method, which allows the model to learn step-by-step analysis by adding the universal
494   instruction ”Let’s think step by step”, thus avoiding the need to construct examples. Furthermore,
495   self-consistency method (Wang et al., 2022) was proposed to replace the greedy decoding strategy
496   in CoT, by sampling the outputs of multiple paths and using majority voting to select the final an-
497   swer, the reasoning performance of the single model was further improved. Subsequently, (Fu et al.,
498   2022) pointed out that more complex thought chain prompts have better effects, and the more com-
499   plex answers output are more credible, and further improved the self-consistency method based on
500   complexity.
501
502   4.2   S ELF -C ORRECTION
503
      A method that utilizes the model’s own capabilities for self-correction (Madaan et al., 2024; Shinn
504
      et al., 2024; Sean et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2022) has been proposed to further improve
505   the quality of answers output by the model. Madaan et al. (2024) used the model’s own feedback
506   to find errors and modify their own output, improving the quality of the output through continuous
507   iteration and modification until the stopping condition is met. Kim et al. (2024) introduced some
508   simple prompts to allow LLM to recursively criticize and improve its output. Shinn et al. (2024)
509   reflected the task feedback signal through verbal feedback and then maintained the LLM’s own
510   reflected text in the episodic memory buffer to induce better decisions in subsequent trials. However,
511   Huang et al. (2023) pointed out that these self-correction methods have great limitations and do not
512   significantly improve LLM in a fair setting.
513
514   4.3   M ULTI -M ODEL C OLLABORATION
515   Using multiple LLMs to solve problems is still in its early stages (Yin et al., 2023). It is usually done
516   by imitating some group collaboration behaviors of humans to conduct multi-model collaboration
517   (Khan et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2023; Du et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023). The
518   goal is to combine the advantages of multiple models and introduce external feedback from other
519   models to break through the performance bottleneck of a single model (Liang et al., 2023). The
520   simplest method of multi-model collaboration is to perform majority voting based on the answers
521   given by multiple LLMs. In addition, many methods begin to allow LLMs to interact with each
522   other. Liang et al. (2023) and Du et al. (2023) enhanced the performance of LLM in specific tasks
523
      by allowing multiple LLMs to debate on the same problem and finally reach a consensus. Yin et al.
      (2023) proposed a cross-model exchange based on network topology to obtain feedback from other
524
      LLMs to improve their own output. Inspired by human behavior, Sun et al. (2023) proposed multiple
525
      collaboration modes, including discussion, review, and retrieval, to jointly work towards enhancing
526   model inference performance. Different from these methods that interact at the overall answer level,
527   our proposed TTE collaborates at the lowest token level and directly generates answers by reaching
528   consensus at each token, avoiding possible tedious multi-round discussions.

      5     C ONCLUSION
532
      In this paper, we propose TTE, an innovative token-level multi-model collaboration paradigm. By
533
      selecting the optimal next token from the next token distributions given by multiple LLMs and gen-
534   erating answers autoregressively, TTE can effectively integrate the capabilities of multiple models
535   to break the performance bottleneck of a single model. The excellent performance on multiple tasks
536   proves the effectiveness and reliability of our method. In addition, compared with previous multi-
537   model collaboration methods, TTE has other advantages, such as no need to manually construct
538   prompts for collaboration, no need for multiple rounds of discussion, and even shows certain emer-
539   gence capabilities. We hope that TTE can provide a new perspective for multi-model collaboration.
      Discussion and limitation are provided in Appendix C.

      The knowledge of a large language model (LLM) is primarily stored in its vast number of param-
705   eters, which encode a broad understanding of language and domain-specific knowledge. However,
706   the external manifestation of this knowledge is the next token probability distribution generated at
707   each step of autoregressive sampling. Formally, given a context x<t , an LLM generates a probability
708   distribution over the next possible tokens:
709
710
                                       PLLM (wt | x<t ) = softmax(fθ (x<t )),
711
712   where fθ represents the internal computation of the LLM parameterized by θ. This probability
713   distribution reflects the LLM’s internal knowledge and informs the quality of the generated answers.
714   Higher-quality knowledge results in a higher probability assigned to tokens that contribute to better
715   answers. The final output from the model is sampled from this next token distribution, and the
716   sequence of sampled tokens constitutes the generated text.
717   The answer space derived from this autoregressive process is defined by the cumulative sampling
718   across multiple steps. Let the space of possible answers be denoted as A, with each potential an-
719   swer having an associated probability based on the product of next-token probabilities. Hence, the
720   probability of an answer a ∈ A being generated by the LLM is:
721
722                                                      T
                                                         Y
723                                     PLLM (a | x) =          PLLM (wt | x<t ),
724                                                      t=1
725
      where T is the length of the generated answer. Importantly, the better the model’s knowledge,
726
      the higher the probability assigned to higher-quality answers in this space. Theoretically, LLM can
727   output any answer, but since some answers have extremely low probability of occurrence, we believe
728   that LLM is not capable of making certain answers at this time. We believe that the answer space
729   only contains answers with probability reaching a certain threshold.
730
      Combining Knowledge from Multiple LLMs.
731
732   We propose to improve the overall answer quality by combining the knowledge of multiple LLMs,
733   leveraging their individual next token distributions. Let P (i) (wt | x<t ) represent the next-token
734   probability distribution generated by the i-th LLM. By combining these distributions, we create a
735   new, enhanced distribution that incorporates the knowledge encoded in multiple models. Specifi-
736   cally, we aggregate the distributions as:
737
738        Pcombined (wt | x<t ) = Aggregate(P (1) (wt | x<t ), P (2) (wt | x<t ), . . . , P (K) (wt | x<t )),
739
740   where K is the number of models and the aggregation function is designed to effectively combine
741
      the distributions. The resulting answer space from this combination is strictly larger and of higher
      quality than any individual model’s answer space, as it benefits from the union of knowledge across
742
      models. In theory, any answer contained in the single model answer space can be sampled from this
743
      answer space.
744
745   For example, consider two LLMs, MA and MB , answering two different questions. In some cases,
746   MA may provide the correct answer, while in other cases, MB might be more accurate. By merging
747
      their answer spaces, we can capture the correct answers from both models with higher probability,
      ensuring that:
748
749
750                           Pcombined (a∗ | x) ≥ max(P (A) (a∗ | x), P (B) (a∗ | x)),
751
752
      where a∗ is the optimal answer. In this way, the combined space encompasses the high-quality
      answers from both models and assigns them higher probabilities than the individual models would
753
      on their own. This probability is from a general perspective, because the probability that the merged
754
      answer space contains the correct answer is definitely greater than the answer space of a single LLM.

759   be cooperation or competition) of sampled tokens and (2) ensuring consistency in token selections
760   across models.

       TTE is a method that leverages multi-model collaboration to improve model performance from
975    the inference side. It can break through the performance bottleneck of a single model without
976    training and without consuming a large amount of resources. Compared to previous multi-model
977    collaboration methods, TTE does not require manually designing prompts to enable collaboration
978    among multiple models, nor does it involve the hassle of multiple rounds of interaction to reach a
979    consensus. A single round of autoregression is sufficient to produce a complete and deterministic
980    answer.
981    Furthermore, we believe that TTE can be used for high-quality annotation, as it can integrate the
982    knowledge boundaries of multiple models to provide high-quality pseudo-labels for unlabeled data.
983    The annotation quality is likely to be superior to that of a single model and could help address the
984    current shortage of high-quality data.
