009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In light of the widespread deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs), the
012            responsibility for safeguarding and regulating LLM-generated content has taken
013            on heightened significance. Recent advancements in LLM-based moderation
014            methods, e.g., LlamaGuard, have demonstrated remarkable promise in identify-
015            ing safety risks associated with both inputs and outputs in human-AI interactions.
016
               However, integrating LLM-based safeguards into a chatbot system requires an ad-
               ditional inference stage involving a moderation LLM with billions of parameters,
017
               which significantly increases computational costs and reduces overall efficiency.
018
               In this paper, we demonstrate that simply learning a classification head on the last-
019            layer hidden states of the dialogue model provides a strong capability to identify
020            harmful contents. The classification head, referred to as ShieldHead, serves as
021            an auxiliary branch paralleled with next-token-prediction LM head, enabling the
022            detection of potential risks in past text sequences. Additionally, a label disam-
023            biguation technique is employed to supervise ShieldHead with both token-level
024            and sentence-level labels, which further enhances its performance. ShieldHead
025            exhibits remarkable efficiency during inference, providing real-time moderation
026            results alongside token-wise streaming output during the chatbot system’s decod-
027
               ing phase. Extensive experimental results demonstrate the superiority of the pro-
               posed framework: a state-of-the-art performance on the XSTest and SafeRLHF
028
               datasets while running at a speed about 300× faster (<1ms) than previous LLM-
029
               based moderation models with ∼99% less parameters of LlamaGuard.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The widespread application of large language models (LLMs) has ushered in transformative effects
035   across domains such as content generation (Josh Achiam, 2023; Team, 2024a), AI agent (Zhiheng Xi
036   & etc., 2023) and conversational assistant (Liu et al., 2024). Despite exhibiting impressive capabili-
037   ties, the deployment of LLMs is accompanied by a plethora of safety challenges that pose substan-
038   tial risks, such as privacy violations (Li et al., 2023), harmful content generation (Deshpande et al.,
039
      2023), illegal activities facilitation (Zhang et al., 2023a), etc. As the utility of LLMs continues to
      expand, an effective and efficient AI content moderation tool is crucial for identifying potential risks
040
      in user prompts and LLM responses, thereby ensuring safe and responsible interactions.
041
042   The advent of LLMs has marked a paradigm shift in content moderation methodologies, bifurcating
043   the landscape into two categories: (1) traditional moderation APIs, such as Perspective API (Lees
044   et al., 2022), OpenAI Content Moderation API (Markov et al., 2023), and Azure Content Safety API
045
      (Azure., 2024), which rely on rule-based or discriminative classifiers, and (2) LLM-based moder-
      ation tools utilizing causal language models, exemplified by LlamaGuard (Inan et al., 2023; Team,
046
      2024b; Llama Team, 2024), ShieldGemma (Zeng et al., 2024), etc. The formers provide fast APIs
047
      and have superiority on the running speed as they use non-parametric rules or smaller models for effi-
048   cient judgment of harmful content (Markov et al., 2023). While most of them have weaker language
049   comprehension and reasoning ability compared to LLMs. They exhibit limited efficacy in evaluating
050   safety risks within chatbot systems due to their inability to discern the distinct roles of users and AI
051   agents. In contrast, the LLM-based moderation methods, pioneered by LlamaGuard, take advantage
052   of the powerful instruction following and reasoning capabilities inherent in LLMs. These methods
053   significantly enhance the accuracy of risk detection and offer improved interpretability of results
      (Inan et al., 2023; Han et al., 2024). These methods introduce advanced security guidelines, a more

055   (Zeng et al., 2024). However, these LLMs contain billions of parameters, and the training and in-

057   moderators are inefficient and can only run in offline mode, where the auditing process needs to

059   the likelihood of harmful content being exposed by AI chatbots. There remains a pressing need

142   2     M ETHODOLOGY
143
144   The overview of the proposed ShieldHead framework is shown in Figure 2. Given a query “Can
145   I commit suicide?”, traditional LLM-based safety guardrails (in the upper part) sequentially wait
146   for the dialogue model providing a completed response, i.e. “No, but you can try taking poison
147   ...”. A fine-tuned billion-size moderation model unions the query and response pair to output a safe
148   or unsafe prediction. In contrast, our pipeline (in the lower part) identifies risks at token-level, by
149   a ShieldHead classifier parallelized with LM head. Concretely, once the dialogue model decodes
150
      the first token “[No]”, the safety score corresponding to the token, which is 0.1 in the figure, is
      given by ShieldHead. With the decoding process continued, ShieldHead outputs a high risk score
151
      0.9 for the token “[poison]”, which means that there is a high probability to exist safety risks in the
152
      past sequences. The detailed classification module of ShieldHead is presented in Section 2.1. The
153   label disambiguation module is illustrated in Section 2.2, which is used for training the proposed
154   ShieldHead.

160   or “unsafe” outputs. The binary classification is used for ShieldHead. An empirical evidence has
161   been shown in Table 3, where we directly use the last token hidden states of the dialogue models to
      train a safety classifier, and achieve competitive performance with exist safeguards like LlamaGuard.

185
      To achieve the token-level safety classification training, token-level safety data is essential. How-
186
      ever, the cost of annotating token-level safety data is significantly higher than sentence-level or
187   conversation-level annotation, and to the best of our knowledge, no open-source datasets or effec-
188   tive data synthesis methods currently exist. Meanwhile, if only the human-annotated token-level
189   data is applied for training, the performance of the classifier would be constrained by the limited
190   number of labeled data. A straightforward and feasible approach is to use sentence-level labels for
191   all tokens at the token level. However, this introduces substantial noise, as unsafe sentences often
192   contain numerous safe or neutral tokens (e.g., “are”, “the”, and other subjects or verbs).
193   To address this issue, we introduce a label disambiguation module, which is central to token-level su-
194   pervision, aiming to correct imprecise labels assigned to tokens. This module consists of a prototype
195   for each class, taking the hidden state h and safety predictions p as inputs to generate disambiguated
196   soft token-level labels. Let P = [P1 , ..., PC ], P ∈ RC×d represent the prototype, initialized with

200   vectors. Hidden state features h of tokens with the highest prediction probability p belonging to
201
      the respective class (i.e., {1, ..., C}) are utilized, and prototype labels s are calculated based on the

      2.4   OTHER A PPLICATIONS OF S HIELD H EAD
271
272   Decoding Time Safety Alignment. Through techniques like Controlled Text Generation (CTG)
273   (Liu et al., 2021), decoding time safety alignment (Zhang et al., 2023b; Banerjee et al., 2024) con-
274   strains the decoding process to prioritize the safe outputs. Since ShiledHead provides real-time
275   monitoring of harmlessness probabilities of each decoded token, the safety guidance can be natu-
276   rally integrated into the decoding strategy. For instance, the safety score can be used in the beam
277   search algorithm to filter harmful candidates. The safety indicator can further combine with other
278
      search algorithms (e.g., breadth-first search and depth-first search) to enable systematic exploration
      of harmless generation with lookahead and backtracking. It leaves as the future work to study these
279
      strategies.

284   3.1   S ETUPS
285
286   Training Datasets. In this study, we aim to create a framework for token-level and real-time safety
287   risk classification, rather than improving safety classification through enhanced data synthesis pro-
288
      cesses and a greater amount of high-quality annotated data. Consequently, we rely on existing
      open-source datasets for training. We specifically utilize WILDGUARDTRAIN (WGTRAIN) (Han
289
      et al., 2024), which provides the necessary sentence-level labels for both prompts and responses.
290
      WGTRAIN contains a total of 86,759 entries, consisting of 48,783 standalone prompts and 37,976
291   prompt-response pairs. Our focus is on the 37,976 pairs, classified as follows: 16,647 pairs with both
292   safe prompts and responses, 12,946 with unsafe prompts but safe responses, 27 with safe prompts
293   and unsafe responses, and 8,356 where both are unsafe. We designate 10% of these pairs as a vali-
294   dation set. Each token is assigned an initial value based on its sentence-level label during the initial
295   training phase.
296
      Test Datasets. The evaluation benchmarks used for testing ShieldHead can be summarized into
297   two categories. For prompt harmfulness classification, we adopt XStest (Röttger et al., 2023), Tox-
298   icChat (Lin et al., 2023) and OpenAI Moderation (Markov et al., 2023). For response harmfulness
299   classification, the test subset of BeaverTails (Jiaming Ji & Yang., 2024) and Safe-RLHF (Dai et al.,
300   2024) are used. Since Safe-RLHF has many versions, we choose the test split of Safe-RLHF on
301   Alpaca3-8B for reducing the testing cost. We report the F1 scores for all evaluation sets. Other
302   than prompt/response harmfulness, we additionally report the token-wise harmfulness classification
303   results of ShieldHead in Section 3.3.2. There are no public available benchmarks on token-wise
304   harmfulness, so we manually labeled a small subset of BeaverTails for the token-wise evaluation.
305   Compared Methods. We compare our ShieldHead against several methods: OpenAI Moderation
306   API, LlamaGuard3 (Llama Team, 2024), WildGuard (Han et al., 2024), ShieldGemma-9B (Zeng
307   et al., 2024) and GPT-4 (Josh Achiam, 2023). For GPT-4, we utilize the OpenAI API (model=gpt-
308   4-0613) with the prompt used in WildGuard (Han et al., 2024). For other methods, we simply adopt
309   their default prompt and evaluation setting.
310
311   3.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
312
313   We initially assessed our method against state-of-the-art LLM-based and online API approaches
314   across multiple public benchmarks, as shown in Section 3.1. Our experiments utilized F1 scores as
315
      the evaluation metric for all benchmarks. To ensure fairness, we used consistent data splits for all
      evaluations. We use the LLaMA-Factory (Zheng et al., 2024) codebase for model training, with 4
316
      A100 80GB GPUs (8 A100 80GB GPUs for Llama3.1-70B) using a total batch size of 8, a max
317
      sequence length of 4096. During training, the AdamW optimizer was employed with a learning rate
318   of 1 × 10−5 and a warmup ratio of 0.1, no weight decay. ShieldHead was trained for one epoch over
319   the training set. Token-level labels were initialized to their corresponding sentence-level labels, and
320   prototypes were initialized to zero. We implemented a warm-up strategy for label disambiguation,
321   during which only the prototypes were updated, and the token-level labels remained unchanged
322   for the initial 2000 steps. The duration of training is contingent upon the base model employed.
323   Specifically, when utilizing the Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct model as base model, the training process
      can be completed in under one hour.

      4     R ELATED W ORK
487
488   4.1   S AFETY C ONTENT M ODERATION
489
490   Extensive research in content moderation can be categorized into [three] main approaches. The
491   first involves online content moderation tools, such as the Perspective API (Lees et al., 2022),
492   OpenAI Content Moderation API (Markov et al., 2023), and Azure Content Safety API (Azure.,
493   2024), which have been pivotal in detecting harmful language. The second leverages LLMs’ strong
494   instruction-following capabilities to detect safety issues with minimal supervision. Advances have
495
      been made through fine-tuning LLMs, exemplified by systems like Llama-Guard (Inan et al., 2023;
      Team, 2024b; Llama Team, 2024), ShieldGemma (Zeng et al., 2024), WildGuard (Han et al., 2024),
496
      Aegis (Ghosh et al., 2024), ShieldLM (Zhang et al., 2024), MD-Judge (Li et al., 2024), and others,
497
      achieving significant progress in content moderation. [The third approach includes intervention-
498   based methods and model-editing techniques. Intervention-based approaches, such as linear probes,
499   involve the step of training small linear classifiers on model activations to detect toxicity at sentence
500   level (Kenneth Li, 2024; Andrew Lee, 2024). Model-editing techniques, including activation ma-
501   nipulation (Andy Zou, 2023) and spectral editing during inference (Yifu Qiu, 2024), aim to steer
502   model outputs by modifying internal representations. These approaches have the advantage of both
503   identifying and correcting safety risks. Although the results of these methods show no significant
504   negative impact on general metrics (e.g., MMLU, GSM8K), direct modification of model parame-
505   ters may still affect generated outputs in unintended ways.] Besides, our work is distinct in several
506
      key aspects. Unlike previous efforts, ShieldHead offers a universal token-level security classifica-
      tion framework that enables real-time monitoring at the model layer without the need for additional
507
      guardrail training. This enhances defense efficiency and addresses tasks that previous models could
508
      not support satisfactorily.
509
510
      4.2   L ABEL D ISAMBIGUATION
511
512   In token-level safety risk classification tasks, directly assigning sentence-level labels to each token
513   within a sentence often results in label ambiguity. In this paper, we leverage the concept of Partial
514   Label Learning (PLL) to design a label-disambiguation-based Multiple Instance Learning (MIL)
515   approach for safety risk classification.
516   PLL involves annotating each training instance with a candidate label set that includes the true
517   label. A central challenge in PLL is label disambiguation, which requires identifying the correct
518   label from these candidates (Zhang et al., 2016; Lyu et al., 2019). For our task, obtaining token-
519   level labels is costly and limits large-scale training, making token-level classification suitable for
520   PLL. Compared to supervised learning, PLL labels are more ambiguous and require denoising for
521   accurate classification. Traditional averaging methods (Hüllermeier & Beringer, 2005; Zhang &
522   Yu, 2015) treat all candidates equally but risk false positives. Recognition-based methods (Jin &
523   Ghahramani, 2002) consider the true label a latent variable and include margin-based (Nguyen &
524
      Caruana, 2008; Wang et al., 2020), graph-based (Zhang et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2020; Xu et al.,
      2019; Lyu et al., 2019), and clustering-based approaches (Liu & Dietterich, 2012). Recently, Pico
525
      introduced a unified framework combining representation learning and label disambiguation (Wang
526
      et al., 2022), using contrastive learning for embeddings and a prototype strategy to update pseudo-
527   labels according to the nearest class, addressing label ambiguity.
528

      5     C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we introduce ShieldHead, an efficient safety risk moderation pipeline for LLMs, de-
532
      signed to deliver real-time results based on the token-level streaming output of a chatbot system.
533
      ShieldHead achieves a substantial advantage in both prompt and response classification tasks, uti-
534   lizing only approximately 1.1% of trainable parameters and maintaining an inference latency of less
535   than 1ms (300× faster), compared to existing safety risk moderation methods. While ShieldHead
536   demonstrates superior performance and real-time applicability compared to existing LLM-based
537   moderation methods, improving the accuracy of token-level safety risk classification is still an es-
538   sential area for future investigation, which is crucial for practical applications. In conclusion, Shield-
539   Head presents significant potential for enhancing the safety capabilities of LLMs and mitigating the
      risks associated with content generated by these models effectively and efficiently.

699
700   Fairness: Although we utilized the WGTrain dataset, which strives to minimize bias, discrepancies
701   in labels may still occur when identity groups are interchanged. Furthermore, as an LLM-based
      safety classifier, our performance is heavily reliant on the semantic understanding of the base model,

702
      making it susceptible to biases potentially introduced in the base model’s training dataset and pre-
703   training process (Chen et al., 2024).

705   Ethical Considerations: Despite achieving state-of-the-art F1 scores, ShieldHead is not immune
706
      to errors and biases in safety classification. When integrated into automated moderation systems,
      these inaccuracies can potentially allow unsafe content to bypass detection. Users should remain
707
      cognizant of this limitation and the potential for inaccuracies.
708
709   Generalization: While our ShieldHead framework performs well across multiple public external
710   benchmarks on both prompt classification and response classification, it is trained using the same
711   WGTrain dataset as WildGuard. As noted in the WildGuard study, much of their data is synthetic,

      means it cannot cover all possible real-world scenarios.

      A.5     A NNOTATION METHOD ON TOKEN - LEVEL CLASSIFICATION
820
821
      A.5.1    ANNOTATION AGREEMENT
822
823
      As to the annotation agreement, we followed the best practice from prior studies, such as ToxicChat
824   (Lin et al., 2023) and Beavertails (Jiaming Ji & Yang., 2024), adapting their sentence-level annota-
825   tion method to our token-level annotation: Two independent annotators were tasked with labeling
826   each token within the sentence. Any disagreements between the annotators were resolved through
827   discussion to reach mutual agreement on all tokens.
828
829
830   A.5.2    INTUITION FOR TOKEN - LEVEL ANNOTATION
831
832   Most work in text generation considers all preceding tokens when performing next token prediction
833   (Radford & Narasimhan, 2018; Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020), and our work aims to
834   classify safety of the responses generated by these text generators. Therefore, intuitively, we follow
835
      this manner.
836   Hence, in this paper, we adopt the cumulative effect of GPT-like models (Radford & Narasimhan,
837   2018; Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020). Instead of directly labeling each token individually
838   (treat them independently), we label tokens taking into account all the preceding context. Our safety
839   risk classification is derived from the conditional probability p(sn |t1 , ..., tn ), where t1 to tn represent
840   the first to the nth token, and sn denotes the safety risk classification of the nth token.
841   Note that this is a manual annotation method for the evaluation set, whereas the token-level labels
842   used during training are obtained through a prototype-based disambiguation method. In our exper-
843   iments, following the cumulative effect of GPT-like models, we use the safety classification of the
844   last token in a sentence as the prediction for the sentence. We achieve state-of-the-art performance
845   in sentence-level safety classification. This also demonstrates that the cumulative effect is applicable
846   in the context of safety classification in our work.
847
848
      A.5.3    EXISTING TOKEN - LEVEL ANNOTATION METHODS
849
850
      In addition to its application in safety risk classification, as proposed in this paper, accumulative
851
      token-level annotation is also applied in other tasks. For example, RIPA (Choi et al., 2023) proposes
852   that once the first hallucinated token is detected, all subsequent generated tokens should be labeled
853   as hallucinated. NADO (Meng et al., 2022) introduced the concept that the occurrence of halluci-
854   nation in a token is related to its prefix from the perspective of the conditional distribution formula.
855   Our method, NADO (Meng et al., 2022), and RIPA (Choi et al., 2023), leverages the cumulative ef-
856   fect of GPT-like models, where the conditional probability of subsequent tokens is influenced by the
857   preceding tokens. Similar to our annotation method, these approaches also use accumulative anno-
858   tation. However, their objectives differ from ours. For example, RIPA constructs a reward based on
859   these accumulative token-level labels. Besides, Fast-detectgpt (Bao et al., 2023) use token-level con-
860
      ditional probability curvature as a cumulative effect to determine whether the text is AI-generated.
      Fast-detectgpt (Bao et al., 2023) and DetectGPT (Mitchell et al., 2023) label the first few tokens as
861
      “human-written,” and all tokens following the first AI-generated token as “AI-generated”.
862
863   In summary, accumulative annotation has been employed in previous work as well, further validating
      the effectiveness of our annotation method.
