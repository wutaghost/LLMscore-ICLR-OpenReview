009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce NOVOBENCH-100K, a large-scale protein dataset for the in silico
012            evolution of TadA, an enzyme critical for base editing. This dataset originates from
013            the sequencing data collected during two rounds of our in vitro TadA evolution,
014            encompassing 101,687 unique DNA variants with an average of 11.1 amino acid
015            mutations. Rather than employing classes or scores as labels, our dataset consists
016            of 77,900 ranking lists, each involving 2, 10, or 100 sequences ranked by their
017            base editing efficiency. These rankings are generated using our SEQ2RANK, a
018
               novel algorithm that accounts for biological experiment credibility and ranking
               consistency. For evaluation, we provide two train-test splits, designated as in-
019
               domain ranking and out-of-domain ranking, based on a standard 7:3 random split
020
               and the actual in-vitro evolution rounds, respectively. We benchmark 80 biological
021            language models (BLMs) across 24 papers, spanning protein, DNA, RNA, and
022            multimodal domains. Comprehensive experiments reveal that BLMs perform well
023            on in-domain ranking, with a detailed analysis by modality, model size, and K-mer.
024            However, for out-of-domain ranking, BLMs exhibit poor performance in both
025            linear probing and fine-tuning, resembling random guessing. This underscores
026            the necessity for highly generalizable models to address domain shifts between
027            experimental rounds. Finally, our wet experiments are ongoing to generate more
028            data to expand our benchmark. In a few months, we expect to add additional rounds
029
               of in vitro evolution and include a broader variety of proteins. We will release the
               code, dataset, and embeddings of our evaluated 80 BLMs soon. Code and @100
030
               dataset are provided in the supplementary.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      The remarkable progress in protein structure prediction in recent years (Jumper et al., 2021; Baek
036   et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2023; Abramson et al., 2024; Chai Discovery team, 2024) has been largely
037   driven by the availability of test datasets such as CASP (Moult et al., 2020), CAMEO (Haas et al.,
038   2018), and PoseBusters (Buttenschoen et al., 2024). These datasets are essential for evaluating
039   model performance and improving the predictive accuracy of protein structure models. While
040   understanding protein structure offers key insights, the next critical step is designing proteins with
041   specific functions (Chu et al., 2024; Notin et al., 2024b), an emerging and increasingly complex area
042   of research (Ingraham et al., 2023; Watson et al., 2023; Hayes et al., 2024; DeepMind, 2024).
043   Gene editing has transformed biomedical science, paving the way for applications in treating genetic
044   disorders, cancer, and viral infections. One emerging area within this domain is the base editing (Ko-
045   mor et al., 2016; Gaudelli et al., 2017), a more precise and safer alternative to traditional gene editing
046   techniques (Cox et al., 2015). Base editing which converts A-T to G-C relies on tRNA-specific
047   adenosine deaminase (TadA), an enzyme that catalyzes targeted nucleotide conversions essential for
048   precise gene correction. Despite advances in TadA design (Ruffolo et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024),
049   evaluating highly-variant TadA designs through wet lab experiments remains resource-intensive
050
      and time-consuming. This highlights the need for high-quality datasets for in silico evaluation to
      accelerate the functional protein design of TadA.
051
052   In this paper, we present NOVOBENCH-100K, a large-scale protein dataset for the in silico evolution
053   of de novo TadA. It includes 101,687 unique DNA variants derived from biological sequencing data
      in our Phage-Assisted Non-Continuous Evolution (PANCE) experiments, as illustrated in Figure 1.

      Unlike deep mutation scanning methods (Sumi et al., 2024) with limited mutations, our benchmark
      introduces an average of 11.1 amino acid mutations compared to the initial sequence used for
072
      evolution. Rather than taking classes or scores as labels, we propose using rankings in biological
073
      datasets for less experimental noise and better dataset extension. Our dataset consists of 77,900
074   ranking lists sorted by their base editing efficiency, divided into three tracks featuring ranking lists of
075   varying lengths—2, 10, and 100—to accommodate different levels of ranking difficulty.
076
077
      Our dataset is constructed using our novel algorithm, SEQ2RANK, which efficiently transforms
      large-scale biological sequencing data into consistent ranking lists. Firstly, this algorithm ensures
078
      experimental-level consistency by sorting sequencing data based on their credibility. Such credibility
079
      is informed by biological knowledge (e.g., the decreasing influence of initial randomness over time)
080   and experimental indicators (e.g., gel electrophoresis). Moreover, we employ a directed acyclic graph
081   to maintain strict sequence-level consistency across ranking lists. This structure provides an essential
082   foundation for machine learning models to identify meaningful patterns within datasets.
083
      NOVOBENCH-100K evaluates model performance through a ranking task in which models are
084
      required to rank sequences based on their editing efficiency within each sequence list. We offer two
085
      train-test splits on the same dataset, referred to as in-domain ranking and out-of-domain ranking. The
086   in-domain ranking takes a standard 7:3 random split on all our ranking lists for each track, while
087   out-of-domain ranking is based on actual in-vitro evolution rounds. The data distribution across
088   different rounds of evolution may vary significantly. The latter split is significantly more challenging
089   as it captures the real dynamics of biological evolution, partitioning the train-test sets according to
090   actual experimental rounds. Training a model on the training set simulates the practical scenarios in
091   protein evolution, where the outcomes of future rounds, corresponding to our test set, are unknown.
092   We benchmark 80 biological language models (BLMs) across 24 papers on our NOVOBENCH-100K
093   using linear probing and fine-tuning under in-silico evolution scenarios, shown in Figure 2. A
094   3-layer fully connected layer is taken for the ranking task using the ListNet loss (Cao et al., 2007).
095   Considering that DNA, RNA, and proteins function as an integrated unit, it is reasonable to transcribe
096   or translate the original DNA sequencing data in NOVOBENCH-100K to other biological “languages.”
097   Therefore, our experiments span BLMs among multiple modalities, including proteins, DNA, RNA,
098   and multimodalities. Specifically, we extract features of the last trunk for folding models such as
099   Chai1 (Chai Discovery team, 2024) and RoseTTAFold-All-Atom Krishna et al. (2024).
100   Our results indicate that current BLMs perform well on in-domain ranking, achieving high scores on
101   metrics such as normalized discounted cumulative gain (nDCG) and Spearman’s rank correlation (SP).
102   We conduct a comprehensive analysis to explore how modality, model size, and K-mer, influence the
103   performance on NOVOBENCH-100K. However, in out-of-domain ranking, all BLMs perform poorly
104   using linear probing and fine-tuning, with results comparable to random guessing. We attribute this
105   to the significant domain gap between in-vitro evolution rounds, as evidenced by decreasing training
106
      loss while test metrics remain unchanged. This highlights the need for highly generalizable models
      to handle “out-of-domain” tasks prevalent in real-world applications, such as protein evolution.

137   2.1   BASE E DITING DATASET
138
139   Current datasets focusing on deaminase enzyme optimization in base editing are limited. Most exist-
140
      ing datasets (Dixit et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2020; Marquart et al., 2021; Sánchez-Rivera et al., 2022;
      Xiang et al., 2021; Leenay et al., 2019) concentrate on refining the interactions between deaminases
141
      and ancillary elements like Cas proteins and sgRNAs. While numerous labs often publish their
142
      improved deaminase protein sequence (Richter et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Tu et al., 2022; Perrotta
143   et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024), there are not enough experimental settings public, thus hampering
144   the independence and identically distributed assumption in machine learning applications. Addition-
145   ally, CRISPRbase (Fan et al., 2023) aggregates existing datasets from various labs and introduces
146   significant batch effects due to diverse experimental protocols (Notin et al., 2024a), compromising
147   the accuracy of models under real experimental conditions. We introduce NOVOBENCH-100K,
148   designed to simulate real-world laboratory conditions more accurately and consistently, thereby
149   enhancing the precision in deaminase evolution and leaving space for more NGS data in the future.

198   3.1    B IOLOGICAL BACKGROUND
199
200   Gene editing is a groundbreaking biotechnological approach that allows for precise DNA alterations,
201   offering transformative potential in treating genetic disorders, enhancing agricultural practices, and
202
      advancing personalized medicine. A key challenge in gene editing involves precise base-level
      modifications, a task for which base editing is specifically designed. Unlike traditional CRISPR
203
      methods that cut DNA (Cox et al., 2015; Hilton & Gersbach, 2015), base editors modify single
204
      DNA bases without inducing double-strand breaks, providing a safer and more precise alternative
205   (Komor et al., 2016; Gaudelli et al., 2017). Base editors are categorized into cytosine base editors,
206   which convert C-G to T-A, and adenine base editors (ABEs), which convert A-T to G-C. ABEs
207   utilize the tRNA-specific adenosine deaminase (TadA), an enzyme that catalyzes targeted nucleotide
208   conversions crucial for accurate gene correction. TadA8e (Richter et al., 2020) represents an evolved
209   form of TadA, capable of converting adenine to inosine 1 , facilitating precise A-to-G edits.
210
      Evolving TadA for enhanced editing efficiency is crucial as it directly influences the therapeutic
211   potential and specificity of gene editing tools, potentially revolutionizing treatments for genetic
212   diseases by ensuring more accurate and efficient genomic interventions. In this context, AI-driven
213   models can significantly expedite the discovery of effective evolutions, optimizing the enzyme
214   design process across extensive search spaces. Hence, we build NOVOBENCH-100K by collecting

      3.2   S EQUENCING DATA TACKLING
234
235
      All data in NOVOBENCH-100K originate from the PANCE of a well-known TadA enzyme,
236   TadA8e (Richter et al., 2020). In our experiments, the base editing system is engineered to link the
237   activity of various deaminase mutants to their amplification efficiency directly: higher deaminase
238   activity leads to faster proliferation of bacteria, thereby enriching variants with elevated activity.
239   During these experiments, we gather raw next-generation sequencing (NGS) data of the extracted
240   DNA from lysed bacteria, which provides an exhaustive snapshot of genetic sequences, facilitating
241   the high-throughput analysis of DNA. This raw NGS data quantifies the prevalence of different DNA
242   sequences, reflecting the editing efficiencies of specific TadA proteins. We process the raw NGS
243   data using CRISPResso2 (Clement et al., 2019), which includes standard procedures such as quality
244
      filtering, adapter trimming, read merging, and alignment. Ultimately, we generate “sequence-read”
      pairs, where the “read” of each sequence denotes the count of sequences detected during NGS,
245
      indicative of the editing efficiency of particular TadA variants.

248   3.3   SEQ2RANK
249
250   Typically, after processing sequencing data to obtain “sequence-read” pairs, these pairs are directly
251   used to build a regression dataset (Zhao et al., 2021; Mortazavi et al., 2008). However, using absolute
252   read counts as labels introduces several risks. First, these values are highly susceptible to experi-
253   mental noise, including measurement sensitivity, instrumental variability, operator variability, and
254
      environmental conditions. Second, it limits the dataset’s scalability and complicates the integration of
      data across different studies due to batch effects in biological experiments.
255
256   Instead, we propose using rankings as a more robust and scalable label format, similar to those used
257   in recommendation systems (Qin et al., 2010). Our data unit consists of sequences ordered by read
258   number, indicating editing efficiency in base editing as higher-ranked sequences demonstrate greater
259   protein activity, as discussed in Section 3.2. This approach shifts the task to predicting the correct
260
      sequence rankings rather than individual efficiency scores.
261   However, constructing a ranking dataset from NGS data presents new challenges. Firstly, it is
262   challenging to handle the vast amount of NGS data with the sensitivity issues inherent to biological
263   sequencing. Additionally, conflicting rankings frequently arise across different experimental rounds
264   due to variations in biological procedures, with these experiment-level conflicts often depending on
265   the credibility of the evolution rounds. Finally, as a form of partial ordering, ranking can introduce
266
      potential conflicts among sequences across ranking lists due to transitivity. These hidden sequence-
      level conflicts within datasets can adversely affect the effectiveness of machine learning models.
267
268   To address these issues, we propose SEQ2RANK to transform NGS lists to ranking lists, as illustrated
269   in Figure 4. We employ a greedy sampling strategy to manage the tens of thousands of sequences
      within each NGS list. During the sampling, we construct a “read-sequence” dictionary and ensure


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      one unique read key can be sampled only once within each ranking list. Such a “strict” partial order
271   can relieve measurement sensitivity issues.
272
273   Furthermore, our algorithm mitigates experiment-level conflicts by prioritizing experiments based on
274
      their credibility, which can be informed by biological knowledge. For example, later experimental
      rounds are considered more reliable, as the effects of initial randomness decrease over time. Credibil-
275
      ity can also be assessed through validation techniques such as gel electrophoresis, qPCR, and Sanger
276
      sequencing, providing references for the success and stability of a specific biological experiment.
277   Leveraging such credibility, we sort sequencing data by credibility, reducing round-level conflicts.
278
279   Finally, we adopt a directed acyclic graph (DAG) to ensure strict sequence-level consistency in the
280
      rankings. In the DAG, we take each sequence as a node and the partial order relationship as a directed
      edge. A new sequence to sample is considered “safe” when it will not introduce a circle in our DAG.
281
      When generating a ranking list, we will iteratively sample unselected keys, i.e., the read numbers, of
282
      the “read-sequence” dictionary. For each read number, all corresponding sequences will be checked
283   until a “safe” sequence is found.
284
285   SEQ2RANK effectively integrates prior biological knowledge and guarantees data consistency,
286
      making it suitable for handling large-scale biological sequencing data. This integration significantly
      enhances the quality of genomic interpretations, leading to more reliable and actionable biological
287
      insights. The detailed pseudocode algorithm can be found in Appendix A.2.

290   3.4   C HARACTERISTICS
291
292   Utilizing SEQ2RANK, we have effectively constructed NOVOBENCH-100K, which includes
293   101,687 unique TadA variants derived from NGS data in base editing evolution experiments. The
294
      in-vitro evolution starts with TadA8e where the system is designed to correlate the activity of
      TadA mutants directly with their amplification efficiency—higher deaminase activity results in
295
      quicker bacteria proliferation, enriching for more active variants. Unlike deep mutation scanning
296
      approaches (Sumi et al., 2024), NOVOBENCH-100K incorporates an average of 11.1 amino acid
297   changes compared with the original sequences used for evolution.
298
299   NOVOBENCH-100K assesses model performance through a ranking task that challenges models to
300
      order sequences based on their editing efficiency. Considering TadA functions across the biological
      spectrum from DNA to RNA, and then to protein, it is beneficial to include corresponding protein
301
      and RNA sequences for a comprehensive evaluation of protein and RNA models. This “sequence-to-
302
      function” approach is ideally suited for biological language models (BLMs) that are pre-trained on a
303   variety of biological languages, including protein, DNA, and RNA sequences, enhancing their ability
304   to generalize across different biological tasks.
305
306
      Distinct from traditional protein function datasets, NOVOBENCH-100K emphasizes ranking se-
      quences based on relative performance instead of absolute metrics, which are frequently distorted
307
      by batch effects and experimental inconsistencies. This ranking approach mitigates the impact of
308
      variable experimental conditions and ensures that the dataset more accurately represents genuine
309   biological phenomena. Focusing on relative performance also reduces the influence of experimental
310   noise, such as variations in instrument calibration, reagent quality, and operator handling. Moreover,
311   this strategy boosts the dataset’s scalability across different experimental setups and enhances its
312   adaptability for integration with data from various sources.

323   real-world scenarios of protein evolution, where the outcomes of subsequent experimental rounds,
      akin to the test set in NOVOBENCH-100K, are unknown.

357   4.1.2    R ANKING E VALUATION M ETRICS
358
359   We offer three tracks, @2, @10, and @100 for different ranking lists with corresponding lengths.
360   Two train-test splits, designated as in-domain ranking and out-of-domain ranking, are provided based
361
      on a standard 7:3 random split and the actual in-vitro evolution rounds, respectively. We take a
      3-layer fully connected network with a hidden size of 128 as the head module, using a cross-entropy
362
      ListNet loss (Cao et al., 2007). We adopt linear probing and fine-tuning to evaluate the performance
363
      of various BLMs without introducing complex structures in the head module.
364
365   We adopt three common ranking evaluation metrics to assess the effectiveness of the predicted
366   rankings within a population of size x, normalized discounted cumulative gain (nDCG@x) (Järvelin
367
      & Kekäläinen, 2000), mean Reciprocal Rank (mRR@x) (Wu et al., 2011), and Spearman’s Rank
      Correlation (SP@x) (Sedgwick, 2014). The nDCG measures the accuracy of ranking results, with
368
      greater emphasis placed on higher-ranked items. The mRR focuses exclusively on the accuracy
369
      of predictions for the top-ranked sample, aligning closely with objectives in protein evolution. SP
370   evaluates the predicted rankings’ overall distribution. Details can be found in Appendix A.3.

448      4.2.1          M ODALITY
449
         NOVOBENCH-100K primarily challenges BLMs in predicting TadA function, making it a protein
450
         evolution task. Interestingly, DNA and RNA BLMs demonstrate performance comparable to protein
451      BLMs on nDCG@100, as shown in Figure 5. It demonstrates that the nucleotide BLMs also gain
452      knowledge about protein functionality on DNA or RNA sequences. Since proteins, DNA, and RNA
453      fundamentally form an integrated within organisms and each plays a crucial role in protein expression,
454      models across all three modalities significantly outperform those trained on one-hot vectors of the
455      sequences.
456
         Furthermore, it is insightful to explore the performance differences across modalities within a single
457      multimodal BLM, given their emergence as powerful tools in recent years. We evaluate LucaOne (He
458      et al., 2024) and Chai1 (Chai Discovery team, 2024) on NOVOBENCH-100K. LucaOne achieves
459      nDCG@100 scores of 0.901 for protein and 0.888 for nucleotide 4 , whereas Chai1 attains scores of
460      0.900 and 0.897, respectively. These results indicate that Chai1 achieves better modality unification,
461      as its performance across modalities is more consistent, while LucaOne shows a notable advantage in
462      protein performance over nucleotide.

531
532
      We present NOVOBENCH-100K, a comprehensive dataset designed to facilitate the in silico evo-
533
      lution of TadA, providing unique insights into base editing. Through extensive benchmarking, we
534   demonstrate that while current biological language models perform well on in-domain ranking, they
535   struggle to generalize effectively on out-of-domain ranking, revealing a significant gap in practical
536   model robustness. These findings highlight the need for developing models capable of handling
537   diverse real-world protein evolution scenarios. Currently, only TadA protein and two rounds of
538   in-vitro evolution are involved in NOVOBENCH-100K. However, we plan to expand the dataset
539   significantly as our wet experiments continue. In a few months, we expect to conduct additional
      rounds of in vitro evolution and include a broader variety of proteins.

      A.1    I N - VITRO E VOLUTION
813
814   Phage-Assisted Non-Continuous Evolution (PANCE) represents a sophisticated platform for the
815   directed evolution of biomolecules. This methodology builds upon the principles of Darwinian
816   evolution and leverages the powerful selection capabilities of bacteriophages. The strength of this
817   approach lies in its high-throughput ability to identify the highest-activity protein variants from
818   vast AI-generated starting sequences. In our study, we employed PANCE to evolve TadA, a critical
819   enzyme used in CRISPR base editing, by systematically selecting variants with enhanced activity
820
      from vast AI-generated libraries. The convergence of advanced artificial intelligence for library
      design and PANCE for evolutionary selection represents a frontier in protein engineering, offering a
821
      high-throughput and scalable approach to optimize enzymatic functions.
822
823   The core of this method is selecting protein variants with improved activity by coupling their
824   function to the replication of bacteriophages. Phages lacking a key gene required for propagation are
825   engineered to rely on the activity of the target protein within host cells to trigger their replication.
826
      Through iterative rounds of serial dilution, phages linked to protein variants with higher activity
      maintain their population, while low-activity counterparts are washed out, allowing for the gradual
827
      enrichment of high-performance variants.
828
829   We engineered the M13 phage, a filamentous virus that propagates within Escherichia coli (E. coli),
830   to lack the essential gene gIII, which encodes the phage protein pIII, responsible for facilitating the
831   release of new virions from the host. The expression of gIII was made contingent on the activity of
832
      TadA within E. coli, such that TadA variants with sufficient activity would trigger gIII expression,
      enabling phage replication. Each round of PANCE involved the serial dilution of bacterial cultures.
833
      Over multiple cycles, variants with superior activity outcompeted their lower-performing counterparts,
834
      resulting in a highly refined population of phage-encoded TadA variants. This iterative process ensures
835   that even minimal gains in activity are captured and amplified across generations, gradually evolving
836   TadA to a high-performance state.
837
838
      A key innovation in our approach is the integration of artificial intelligence to generate a large library
      of TadA variants before selection. Conventional-directed evolution methods rely heavily on random
839
      mutagenesis, which often lacks targeted control and may miss key functional sites, limiting the
840
      evolutionary trajectories toward optimal protein variants. In contrast, AI enables the exploration of
841   a much broader sequence space by predicting which mutations are likely to enhance TadA activity
842   based on previously available data and sophisticated machine learning models. This computationally
843   generated library was introduced into the PANCE system, where the selective power of phage was
844   used to identify the highest-performing TadA variants. By coupling AI-driven design with PANCE,
845   we were able to streamline the evolution of TadA toward enhanced activity.

847   A.2    SEQ2RANK
848
849   Here, we introduce the SEQ2RANK, a novel methodology for processing Next Generation Se-
850   quencing (NGS) data by converting sequence-read pairs into a ranking format rather than relying on
851   absolute read counts, which are fraught with risks due to susceptibility to experimental noise and
852
      limitations on scalability. We detail the algorithm of SEQ2RANK in Algorithm 1. Our proposed
      strategy employs an ordering of sequences based on read numbers to represent editing efficiencies,
853
      thus pivoting the analytical focus from quantifying individual efficiencies to predicting accurate
854
      sequence rankings. This ranking paradigm mitigates challenges inherent in handling vast amounts
855   of NGS data, which include sensitivity issues and the frequent emergence of conflicts arising from
856   biological variability and experimental discrepancies. We utilize a greedy sampling strategy along
857   with a directed acyclic graph (DAG) to maintain a stringent partial order among sequences, thereby
858   ensuring robust data consistency.
