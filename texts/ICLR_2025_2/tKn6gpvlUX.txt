011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013              Predicting transcriptional responses to novel drugs provides a unique opportunity
014              to accelerate biomedical research and advance drug discovery efforts. However,
015              the inherent complexity and high dimensionality of cellular responses, combined
016
                 with the extremely limited available experimental data, makes the task challenging.
                 In this study, we leverage single-cell foundation models (FMs) pre-trained on tens
017
                 of millions of single cells, encompassing multiple cell types, states, and disease
018
                 annotations, to address molecular perturbation prediction. We introduce a drug-
019              conditional adapter that allows efficient fine-tuning by training less than 1% of the
020              original foundation model, thus enabling molecular conditioning while preserving
021              the rich biological representation learned during pre-training. The proposed strategy
022              allows not only the prediction of cellular responses to novel drugs, but also the zero-
023              shot generalization to unseen cell lines. We establish a robust evaluation framework
024              to assess model performance across different generalization tasks, demonstrating
025              state-of-the-art results across all settings, with significant improvements in the few-
026              shot and zero-shot generalization to new cell lines compared to existing baselines.

029   1     I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Recent advancements in high-throughput single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) have significantly
032   deepened our understanding of cellular heterogeneity, enabling detailed insights into gene expression
033
      profiles and the dynamic responses of individual cells within complex tissues and microenvironments
      (Tanay & Regev, 2017; Han et al., 2020). Notably, the ability to study the biological impact of
034
      different perturbations, such as molecular treatments, provides a unique opportunity to unravel cell
035
      function and significantly accelerate biomedical research (Srivatsan et al., 2020). As a consequence,
036   computational methods to predict cellular responses to perturbations hold promise as transformative
037   tools for accelerating drug discovery and personalized medicine (Roohani et al., 2024; Rood et al.,
038   2024; Bunne et al., 2024). Critical applications such as virtual screening, mechanism of action (MOA)
039   identification, and target identification, all rely on the ability to characterize the complex, multifaceted
040   effects of different drugs in the cellular environment.
041
      Machine learning approaches for modeling outcomes of molecular perturbations have recently
042   emerged in the literature, addressing the challenging task of predicting cellular responses x̃ (i.e., gene
043   expression) given the initial cellular state x (i.e., gene expression of control population, representing
044   the underlying biological model system/cell line) and treatment T (i.e., molecule). However, existing
045   methods face several key limitations. First, several approaches are not able to generalize to novel
046   perturbations (Lotfollahi et al., 2023), an essential capability for applications such as virtual screening.
047   Ultimately, methods that can generalize to new molecules are hindered by extremely limited datasets,
048   spanning hundreds of molecules across a few cell lines (Hetzel et al., 2022; Piran et al., 2024; Bereket
049   & Karaletsos, 2024).
050   Indeed, predicting cellular responses to novel chemicals at single-cell resolution poses a few-shot
051   challenge, as sample multiplexing techniques are both expensive and time-consuming, restricting the
052   size and diversity of treatments and biological model systems (Srivatsan et al., 2020; McGinnis et al.,
053   2019). Several methods have recently tackled this challenge. Proposed approaches include leveraging
      transfer learning from more available bulk RNA-seq data (Hetzel et al., 2022) or incorporating prior

054
      knowledge of gene-gene associations into the model (Roohani et al., 2024). However, these methods
055   are limited by the information explicitly encoded in the prior or available in the pre-training datasets.
056   More importantly, these methods have focused on predicting responses to novel drugs or novel
057   drug-cell-line pairs, instead of the more challenging task of predicting the outcome of perturbations
058   for unseen cell lines.
059
      To address these challenges, focusing on the few-shot prediction of molecular responses, including for
060
      unseen model systems (i.e., cell lines), we leverage emerging foundation models (FMs) (Bommasani
061   et al., 2022). FMs have displayed notable success across several fields, including natural language,
062   vision, and, more recently, biomedicine (Moor et al., 2023). In particular, single-cell FMs (Yang
063   et al., 2022; Heimberg et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2024; Theodoris et al., 2023; Hao et al., 2024) are
064   trained on tens of millions of single cells, encompassing multiple cell types, states, and disease
065   annotations. Such pre-trained architectures have recently shown the potential to learn universal
066   biological representations that can be adapted to specific tasks (Ma et al., 2024). By building on
067   the zero-shot and few-shot capabilities of FMs (Wei et al., 2022), we aim to leverage the implicit
068   knowledge of gene-gene relationships and cell states within single-cell FMs to characterize cellular
069
      responses to molecular perturbations.
070   Previous work explored the application of single-cell FMs for predicting outcomes of genetic
071   perturbations (i.e., gene editing techniques) (Cui et al., 2024; Theodoris et al., 2023; Hao et al.,
072   2024). However, such problem formulation is simplified by the fact that the treatment space (different
073   genes) is the same as the response space, allowing direct fine-tuning of the pre-trained architecture.
074   In contrast, modeling responses to chemical perturbations involves bridging cell representations
075
      with a distinct modality (i.e., molecular structures), making the task more complex. To address
      this challenge, we introduce a drug-conditional adapter layer which allows injecting molecular
076
      information into the trainable parameters, while leaving the original weights of the single-cell FM
077
      frozen.
078
079   In summary, single-cell Drug-Conditional Adapter (scDCA) efficiently fine-tunes a single-cell FM,
080   linking it to a different modality (small molecules) to enable molecular property prediction. Our
081
      parameter-efficient approach allows fine-tuning on small chemical perturbation datasets, avoiding
      overfitting while preserving important background biological knowledge encoded in the pre-trained
082
      weights.
083
084   Through this strategy, the proposed method allows not only the prediction of cellular responses to
085   novel drugs, but also the zero-shot generalization to unseen contexts (e.g., cell lines or cell types).
086   Our contributions include:
087
088
             • We investigate the fine-tuning of single-cell FM for molecular perturbation prediction,
               introducing scDCA, which utilizes efficient drug-conditional adapters.
089
090          • We extend the evaluation of this model beyond novel drug and drug-cell-line prediction
091            (focus of prior work) to the more challenging task of unseen cell line prediction, which
092            requires generalization to new biological contexts.
093          • We demonstrate state-of-the-art performance of scDCA, both in the prediction of unseen
094            drugs and especially for unseen cell lines. By leveraging the rich biological representations
095            learned from single-cell FMs and incorporating drug-conditional adapters, our approach
096            enables robust predictions even in zero-shot settings, making it a powerful tool for drug
097            discovery and cellular modeling.

135   ChemCPA (Hetzel et al., 2022) leverages an encoder-decoder architecture with separate embeddings
136   for the perturbation (molecule) and the cell line, combined with an adversarial classifier that is
137   used to generate invariant basal states and produce disentangled representations. Biolord (Piran
138   et al., 2024) is a deep generative framework designed for learning disentangled representations in
139   single-cell data. It partitions the latent space into subspaces and optimizes them to represent cell
140   lines and perturbations, recombining them during inference. GEARS (Roohani et al., 2024) leverages
141
      prior knowledge of gene-gene interactions to predict outcomes of genetic perturbations. The Sparse
      Additive Mechanism Shift Variational Autoencoder (SAMS-VAE) (Bereket & Karaletsos, 2024)
142
      extends prior work (Lachapelle et al., 2022; Lopez et al., 2023) and employs an additive conditioning
143
      approach.
144
145   While SAMS-VAE focuses on genetic perturbation, it can also be extended to predict molecular
146   perturbations.
147   Single-cell foundation models. Recently, there has been significant and active research on single-cell
148   FMs (Yang et al., 2022; Heimberg et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2024; Theodoris et al., 2023; Hao et al.,
149   2024). These models often employ the self-attention transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017)
150   to efficiently learn single-cell representations across extensive datasets, which can then be fine-tuned
151   for numerous downstream tasks. scBERT (Yang et al., 2022) is a recent example within this category,
152   closely mirrors BERT’s (Devlin et al., 2019) pre-training strategy. Specifically, scBERT is pre-trained
153
      through an imputation task on 1.12 million human cells, where masked gene expression values
      are predicted based on the embeddings of all other genes within a cell. scGPT (Cui et al., 2024)
154
      leverages a generative transformer architecture pre-trained on a vast and diverse repository of over 33
155
      million cells. This model effectively extracts critical biological insights relevant to genes and cells,
156   as demonstrated by its downstream performance on multiple tasks, including cell type annotation,
157   multi-omic integration, and gene regulatory network inference. scGPT has also been used to predict
158   outcomes of genetic perturbations, where the perturbagens are different (combinations of) genes.
159   However, given that the model has been retained solely on single-cell omics data, it is not directly
160   applicable to drug discovery questions. Indeed, predicting outputs of chemical perturbations (such as
161   drug-induced cellular responses, tissue-specific effects, and pharmacodynamics) requires modeling
      the effects of a different modality (i.e., chemical structures) not seen during training.

162
      Efficient fine-tuning of foundation models. The ability of FMs to quickly adapt to new tasks (Bom-
163   masani et al., 2022) has sparked considerable interest in developing efficient fine-tuning tech-
164   niques (Han et al., 2023). For example, prefix tuning (Li & Liang, 2021) refers to the technique of
165   prepending a trainable tensor to each transformer block, allowing adapting to a new task without
166   modifying the original model parameters. Li & Liang (2021) showed that prefix tuning achieves
167   comparable results compared to fine-tuning all layers while using only 0.1% of the parameters of the
168   original model. Adapters-based approaches (He et al., 2022; Lei et al., 2023) involve the insertion
169   of small adapter layers within transformer blocks. Only the parameters of the adapters are trainable
170   during fine-tuning. Adapters usually include down- and up-projections, which reduce the number
171
      of trainable parameters. In particular, our work is related to (Karimi Mahabadi et al., 2021), which
      introduces a multi-task adapter for NLP applications, where the parameters of the adapter depend
172
      on the task embedding. In our work, the adapter’s parameters are instead conditioned on a different
173
      modality (chemical structures), unseen during pre-training. The concepts of prefix tuning and adapters
174   have been further extended to recent large language models, such as LLaMA-Adapter (Zhang et al.,
175   2024), a parameter-efficient fine-tuning method specifically designed for LLaMA. Other efficient
176   fine-tuning techniques include pruning (Lawton et al., 2023) and reparametrization techniques (Hu
177   et al., 2022). Efficient fine-tuning of foundation models is a rapidly evolving field encompassing
178   diverse approaches. For a more detailed overview, we refer readers to recent surveys Han et al. (2023);
179   Xin et al. (2024).

181   3     F INE - TUNING SINGLE - CELL FM WITH DRUG - CONDITIONAL ADAPTER
182
183   The proposed strategy aims to simultaneously address two challenges in fine-tuning single-cell
184   FMs for molecular perturbation prediction: 1) the extremely limited amount of paired data linking
185   molecules to cellular responses, and 2) the existence of a different modality (i.e., molecular structures)
186   unseen during pre-training. While recent work has focused on the efficient fine-tuning of foundation
187   models reducing the number of trainable parameters (Hu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024), the fine-
188   tuning of single-cell FMs has been limited to scenarios where inputs and/or outputs are cell states,
189   without considering different modalities.
190   Although scDCA is generally applicable to transformer-based single-cell FMs (Yang et al., 2022; Cui
191   et al., 2024; Theodoris et al., 2023; Hao et al., 2024), which constitute the vast majority of models in
192   this category, in the following, we focus on scGPT (Cui et al., 2024) to describe the methodology
193   and the experiments. To this end, in this section we also provide a concise overview of the scGPT
194   framework.

276   3.2   F INE - TUNING SC GPT
277
278   Compared to training a model from scratch for a new task, adapting and fine-tuning a FM for a new
279   task using data from the target domain often leads to superior results (Wei et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
280   2022; Shen et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022). Various methods can be employed for this adaptation. In
281
      the following, we discuss several options, before introducing scDCA.
282   One commonly used technique is the feature-based approach, which leverages the embeddings
283   of a model as features for training a separate downstream model. However, as demonstrated in
284   Section A.4, this approach is suboptimal in our setting, primarily because the original single-cell FM
285   was pre-trained exclusively on single-cell omics data across a wide range of conditions and cell types,
286   while the target task involves molecular perturbations, resulting in subtle but critical shifts in gene
287
      expression that were likely not seen by the model at pre-training time.
288   Another approach involves fine-tuning the FM itself on the target dataset—in this case, readouts of
289   molecular perturbations. To pursue this approach, we modify scGPT as follows. Following scGPTs
290   approach to genetic perturbation prediction, we replace the discretization of input expression values
291   with log1p-normalized expression values that are tokenized with a feed-forward network embX . In
292   the inital embedding layer of scGPT, instead of the condition token, we incorporate a molecular
293
      embedding, broadcast across all genes as part of the input. Moreover, we provide the gene expression
      vector of the control perturbation on cell line c as the gene expression input. Combined, denoting the
294
      molecular embedding by embm (d) for a drug d, we change the initial embedding (1) to:
295
296                              h0 = embg (tg ) + embX (X (0) (c)) + embm (d).                             (4)
297
      For the molecular embedding function embm , we employ ChemBERTa (Chithrananda et al., 2020),
298
      described in Section 3.1. The transformer stack of scGPT is then applied to h0 as in (2) and processes
299   it to output the perturbation outcome, employing a loss function similar to (3), but operating on the
300   log1p-normalized expression values X.
301
302
      Although recent studies (Howard & Ruder, 2018) indicate that fine-tuning the entire model often
      yields superior performance, we find that this naive approach exhibits limited performance across
303
      various tasks (Section 4). We attribute this limitation to two key factors: 1) the extremely limited
304
      number of samples compared to the parameters of the pre-trained model, which can easily result
305   in overfitting, and 2) the large domain shift resulting from adding molecule embeddings embm (d)
306   to the model’s input, given that the pre-trained model has been solely trained to understand gene
307   expression profiles.
308
      To solve these challenges, we update the fine-tuning strategy by introducing our more parameter-
309
      efficient fine-tuning approach leveraging drug-conditional adapters, scDCA.
310
311   Drug-Conditional Adapter. Compared to the standard fine-tuning approach described above, in
312   scDCA, the weights of the original network θ are frozen, and only the parameters of small adapter
313   layers θ0 are trained during fine-tuning. The goal of the adapter layers is twofold: minimize the
314
      number of trainable parameters, while giving the flexibility to generalize to different tasks particularly
      in a zero-shot setting.
315
316   Figure 1 summarizes our architecture. In the top left, we show the input to scGPT, which consists
317   solely of the gene expression vector of the control perturbation in cell line c. Contrary to before,
318   we do not pass the molecule embedding directly as input to scGPT to avoid overfitting. Instead, we
319   directly pass the molecular embedding to adapter modules that we add to every transformer layer.
320
      This ensures that the input to the model during fine-tuning remains similar to the input used during
      pre-training.
321
322   The drug-conditional adapter modules (Figure 1 bottom right) consist of four main components: a
323   molecular projection layer, a down-projection layer, a residual block, and an up-projection layer.
      Critically, we introduce a molecular projection layer that generates biases for the down-projection

342   molecular representations have been previously shown to boost few-shot molecular property predic-

344   unseen drugs by projecting molecular embeddings into FM’s internal state.
345   By featurizing cell lines based on their initial gene expression, our framework can generalize to
346   unseen cell lines in both zero-shot and few-shot settings. In particular, by using pre-trained weights
347   in the original transformer layers, the model can leverage gene-gene interactions learned through
348   large-scale pre-training (Cui et al., 2024) without the need to explicitly encode them into the model,
349   as done in previous works (Roohani et al., 2024). Importantly, the ability to generalize to new cell
350   lines arises from leveraging knowledge available in the pre-trained model and scDCA preserving and
351   integrating it with molecular representations during finetuning.

353   4     E XPERIMENTS
354
355   We show that scDCA achieves superior results for all tasks described in Section 3, while having less
356   than 1% of the number of parameters in the original single cell FM.
357
358   4.1     E XPERIMENTAL SETTINGS
359
360   We evaluate our framework scDCA on 4 tasks: (1) unseen drugs, (2) unseen drug-cell-line, (3)
361   unseen cell line (few-shot), and (4) unseen cell line (zero-shot). We compare the proposed approach
362
      with several recently introduce methods for perturbation prediction: chemCPA (Hetzel et al., 2022),
      BioLORD (Piran et al., 2024), and Sams_VAE (Bereket & Karaletsos, 2024).
363
364   Dataset. The dataset utilized in this study is sciplex3 (Srivatsan et al., 2020), which includes
365   649,340 cells across 7,561 drug-sensitive genes on 3 human cancer cell lines (A549, MCF7, and
366   K562) perturbed with 188 drugs. Similar to previous work (Hetzel et al., 2022), we focus on the
367   transcriptional response captured through 2,000 drug-sensitive genes.
368   Data Splitting. As previously described in Section 3 and Figure 2, we evaluate scDCA across
369   different generalization tasks. In practice, each task results from a different data splitting strategy,
370   detailed in Appendix A.1.
371
      In this study, as we focus on single-cell data, we observe that the gene expression of only a subset
372   of genes undergoes significant alterations under various perturbations. To ensure a meaningful
373   evaluation, our analysis specifically targets those genes that exhibit noticeable changes in their
374   expression levels compared to their initial (control) expression profiles. These genes are categorized
375   as differentially expressed genes (DEGs). The number of DEGs identified can vary; however, it is
376   generally observed (Roohani et al., 2024) that analyzing a smaller, more defined set of DEGs presents
377   a more robust and relevant challenge, as it requires precise detection of nuanced expression changes.
      In our experiments, we consider the top 20 DEGs.

      Evaluation Metrics. Similar to previous works (Hetzel et al., 2022), the main performance metric
379   used for perturbation prediction is the R-squared (or coefficient of determination) metric R2 . This
380   metric quantifies the proportion of the variance in the dependent variable that is predictable from the
381   independent variables in a regression model. We conducted all experiments over 5 runs (different
382   random splits), reporting both the standard error and the average coefficient of determination. By
383   focusing the evaluation on the top DEGs, we make sure that the metric is not inflated by genes that

510   5   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
511
512   In this paper, we address the underexplored area of predicting transcriptional cellular responses to
513   novel molecular perturbations. A key challenge in this area is data scarcity, a problem that foundation
514   models trained on much larger ancillary datasets are poised to address. While naive applications of
515   FMs fall short of this expectation, we demonstrate that a careful fine-tuning strategy combining a
516   single-cell FM with a pre-trained molecular FM indeed realizes this potential. Our strategy, single-cell
517
      drug-conditional adapter (scDCA), leads to state-of-the-art performance on a variety of challenging
      problem formulations, including the few-shot and zero-shot generalization to unseen cell lines.
518
519   One limitation is that our approach is currently only applicable to transformer-based foundation
520   models. This restriction means that (scDCA) cannot be directly applied to other types of models
521   without significant adaptation. Additionally, our method requires the presence of control gene
522   expression data. If the dataset does not include control gene expressions, our approach cannot be
523
      utilized. Finally, we believe that the evaluation of perturbation models, especially in the single-cell
      domain and for the generalization to new cell lines and conditions, is challenged by the limited size
524
      of publicly available data. Despite these limitations, we believe our approach offers valuable insights
525
      and advancements in the field of chemical perturbation prediction. Future work will aim to tackle
526   these challenges.

       A.10    S IMILARITY OF DRUGS IN TRAINING AND TEST SETS GENERALIZING TO UNSEEN DRUGS
1000
1001   We analyzed the similarity between training and test molecules, focusing on the generalization to
1002   new molecules (drugs). For each test molecule, we computed its maximum similarity to any training
1003   molecule. We used standard functions, leveraging Tanimoto similarity on Morgan fingerprints
1004   (radius=2, num_bits=2048) as implemented in RDKit library. Figure 9 summarizes these results.
1005   As shown, the vast majority of molecules have low similarity to training structures, with 88% of
1006   them having a similarity 0.4 to any training molecule (0.4 is often considered the threshold to define
1007
       structural novelty) (Dalke, 2019).
