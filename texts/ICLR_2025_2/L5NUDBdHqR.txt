                                                   A BSTRACT
013
014
               As low-quality housing and in particular certain roof characteristics are associated
015
               with an increased risk of malaria, classification of roof types based on remote
016
               sensing imagery can support the assessment of malaria risk and thereby help
017            prevent the disease. To support research in this area, we release the Nacala-Roof-
018            Material dataset, which contains high-resolution drone images from Mozambique
019            with corresponding labels delineating houses and specifying their roof types. The
020            dataset defines a multi-task computer vision problem, comprising object detection,
021            classification, and segmentation. In addition, we benchmarked various state-of-the-
022            art approaches on the dataset. Canonical U-Nets, YOLOv8, and a custom decoder
023            on pretrained DINOv2 served as baselines. We show that each of the methods has
024            its advantages but none is superior on all tasks, which highlights the potential of
025
               our dataset for future research in multi-task learning. While the tasks are closely
               related, accurate segmentation of objects does not necessarily imply accurate
026
               instance separation, and vice versa. We address this general issue by introducing a
027
               variant of the deep ordinal watershed (DOW) approach that additionally separates
028            the interior of objects, allowing for improved object delineation and separation. We
029            show that our DOW variant is a generic approach that improves the performance of
030            both U-Net and DINOv2 backbones, leading to a better trade-off between semantic
031            segmentation and instance segmentation.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Mosquito-borne diseases refer to a group of infectious illnesses transmitted by the bite of mosquitoes.
037   Malaria is a mosquito-borne disease caused by single-celled parasites of the Plasmodium group spread
038   through bites of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. It ranks among the world’s most severe public
039
      health problems and is a leading cause of mortality and disease in many developing countries. It is
      therefore crucial to improve prevention, control, and surveillance measures of malaria, particularly in
040
      sub-Saharan Africa (Venkatesan, 2024; WHO, 2023). Low-quality housing built of natural materials,
041
      for example, having a thatched roof of grass or palm and having cane, grass, shrub, or mud as internal
042   and external walls, is associated with an increased risk of malaria infection (Dlamini et al., 2017).
043   Sub-standard housing has more mosquito entry points and most malaria transmissions in sub-Saharan
044   Africa occur inside dwellings while the inhabitants are asleep (Tusting et al., 2020; 2017; Jatta et al.,
045   2018; Tusting et al., 2019). Houses with metal roofs are hotter in the daytime than houses with
046   thatched roofs. This may reduce mosquito survival and inhibit parasite development within the
047   mosquito in metal roof houses. On this basis, the proliferation of modern construction materials in
048   sub-Saharan Africa may have contributed decisively to the reduction of malaria cases (Tusting et al.,
049   2019). Classification of roof characteristics thus holds potential to support malaria surveillance and
050
      control programs. Roof characteristics, such as geometry, material, and condition can be monitored
      using remote sensing imagery to advance risk assessment of mosquito-borne diseases and guide
051
      mitigation strategies, especially when detailed health and socioeconomic data are scarce.
052
053   Here, we introduce the Nacala-Roof-Material drone-imagery dataset to support the development of
      machine learning algorithms for automated building and roof type mapping in low-income areas


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      prone to malaria risk. Our dataset is based on high-resolution drone imagery (≈ 4.4 cm) of peri-urban
055   and rural settlements in Nacala, Mozambique. An NGO (anonymous during peer review) delineated
056   17954 buildings and categorized them according to five roof types, and the authors again carefully
057   verified all annotations.
058
      We define three tasks on the Nacala-Roof-Material dataset, building detection, multi-class roof
059
      type classification, and pixel-level building segmentation. While these tasks are related, closer
060
      inspection reveals a misalignment between their objectives. Accurate segmentation as measured by
061   the intersection over union (IoU) does not necessarily imply accurate object separation, and vice
062   versa. For accurate detection and classification, it would be sufficient to only detect the interior of
063   an object as long as the segmented area allows to correctly classify the type. If the roofs of two
064   buildings are (almost) touching, then some segmentation may have a high IoU but could make it
065   difficult to separate buildings for counting. This is also a common issue in other applications, e.g.,
066   when studying cells in medical images (Ronneberger et al., 2015) or trees from satellite images
067   (Brandt et al., 2020; Mugabowindekwe et al., 2022)).
068   We benchmark three conceptually different state-of-the-art approaches on our multi-task dataset.
069   First, we evaluate YOLOv8 (Jocher et al., 2023) developed for object detection, classification, and
070   instance segmentation. Second, we build a segmentation model based on DINOv2 (Oquab et al.,
071   2024), a state-of-the-art pretrained vision transformer. Lastly, we evaluate U-Net (Ronneberger et al.,
072   2015) a fully-convolutional encoder-decoder architecture, designed for semantic segmentation. To
073   address the potential conflicts between pixel-level segmentation and correct object separation as
074   outlined above, we propose a simple approach based on the recent work by Cheng et al. (2024), which
075
      we refer to as the Deep Ordinal Watershed (DOW) method. We extend both U-Net and DINOv2 to
      produce an additional output map that predicts the interior of objects. While the original exterior
076
      segmentation map maximizes the IoU, we show that the interior map supports object separation.
077
078   The main contributions of our work are the following:
079   1. We provide the Nacala-Roof-Material dataset containing drone imagery from peri-urban and rural
080      areas in a sub-Saharan African region. The dataset contains accurate segmentation labels for
081      buildings, categorized into five roof types.
082   2. Based on the dataset, we define a multi-task machine learning benchmark for binary and multi-
083      class object detection and semantic segmentation. We implemented and benchmarked different
084      carefully adopted baseline methods, reflecting three different approaches to address these tasks.
085   3. We propose a general and simple approach to extend models for semantic segmentation to yield
086      good segmentation and object separation results.
087   The data and code for reproducing the experiments are available through this anonymous url:
088   https://osf.io/us628/?view_only=3c25a48d420f4ec7a43cb76e66e92b26. A
089   project page, with link to data and code, will be setup upon acceptance.

      The next section presents the Nacala-Roof-Material data, provides some background about roof types
091   and risk of vector-borne diseases, and briefly discussed related datasets. Section 3 describes the deep
092   learning models we evaluated with an emphasis on deep watershed methods. Experimental results
093   are presented in Section 4 before we conclude.

098   2.1   BACKGROUND : H OUSING CONDITIONS AND RISK OF MOSQUITO - BORNE DISEASES
099
100   In sub-Saharan Africa, housing conditions, health outcomes, and socioeconomic status of the residents
101   are interrelated (Gram-Hansen et al., 2019; Degarege et al., 2019; Tusting et al., 2020). As poverty
102   is widespread, diseases are more prevalent, and data are scarce in this region, automatic profiling
103   of housing conditions based on analysis of satellite imagery holds the potential to estimate the
104
      socioeconomic status of the inhabitants and assess the risk of disease. This may in turn support
      targeted public health interventions.
105
106   Mosquitoes are vectors for diseases such as malaria, dengue, Zika, West Nile fever, Chikungunya,
107   and Yellow fever. In 2022, more than 600 000 deaths occurred due to malaria globally and out of the
      approximately 249 million documented cases, around 233 million occurred within the WHO African


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Region, accounting for roughly around 94% of the total documented cases. The economic impact of
109   malaria in Sub-Saharan Africa not only impedes progress towards achieving Sustainable Development
110   Goal 3 (Good Health and Well-being) but also undermines efforts to attain SDG 1 (No Poverty) and
111   SDG 8 (Decent Work and Economic Growth) by compromising economic productivity. Extreme
112   weather conditions caused by climate change will likely exacerbate problems with mosquito-borne
113   diseases in sub-Saharan Africa, as floods are expected to increase in frequency and have been linked
114   to outbreaks of malaria in Africa (Githeko et al., 2000).
115   Low-quality housing increases the risk of transmission of diseases by mosquitoes, as sub-standard
116   houses have more mosquito entry points and thereby increase human exposure to infection in the
117   home (Tusting et al., 2015; Dlamini et al., 2017). Mosquito survival is lower in metal-roof houses
118   compared to thatched-roof houses due to higher daytime temperatures (Tusting et al., 2015). Most
119   malaria transmissions in sub-Saharan Africa occur indoors at night, and poor climatic performance of
120   housing has been linked to increased malaria risk (Jatta et al., 2018). This is because elevated indoor
121   temperatures can cause discomfort for inhabitants, which may result in decreased use of mosquito
122   nets during the night. Roof materials, geometry, and conditions are critical for indoor climate, as
123
      roofs comprise the primary surface exposed to the sun. Automatic classification of roof characteristics
      thus holds potential for informing risk assessment of malaria and support targeted interventions.

126   2.2   T HE NACALA -ROOF -M ATERIAL DATASET
127
128   We gathered drone imagery of the Nacala region in Mozambique. The burden of malaria in Mozam-
129
      bique is approximately 10-fold the world average (number of documented cases compared to the
      total population, Venkatesan, 2024). The data covers three informal settlements of Nacala, a city of
130
      350 000 inhabitants on the northern coast of Mozambique. Aerial imagery was collected using a DJI
131
      Phantom 4 Pro drone and processed using AgiSoft Metashape software. The flight height was 120 m,
132   the total flight duration was 504 minutes (the drone flight protocols are available in the supplementary
133   material). All data was recorded between October and December 2021, under a development project
134   led by an NGO and supported by the Nacala Municipal Council.
135
      The total number of buildings in the study areas is 17 954. We distinguished five major types of roof
136
      materials in Nacala, namely metal sheet, thatch, asbestos, concrete, and no-roof, and their counts are
137
      9776, 6428, 566, 174, and 1010, respectively. The region is mostly dominated by metal sheets and
138   thatch roofs.
139
140   From the three informal settlements, see Figure 1, the first two areas were split into training Dtrain ,
141
      validation Dval , and test Dtest using stratified sampling. We created a square grid of 225 meters and
      counted the roof types in these cells. Then we partitioned the cells into three sets based on the class
142
      counts to achieve a similar class distribution in each set, where we prioritized the distribution of
143
      minority classes (i.e., concrete and asbestos). We defined that a building only belongs to a specific
144   grid cell if its centroid falls into the cell. If a building area falls into two grid cells and those two cells
145   belong to two different sets (e.g., training and test set), we choose to have data pixels in the set where
146   the centroid of the building is placed. The remaining part of the building in the other set was masked
147   to avoid data leaking between sets.
148
      Although objects in training, validation, and test sets are from different cells, they stem from the same
149
      two areas. To evaluate the generalization to a new area without adjacent training data, we hold out
150   the third settlement as a second test set referred to as Dext .

      2.3   A NNOTATION PROCESS AND QUALITY
154
      Building boundary and roof type annotations were collected and corrected in three steps. First, local
155
      university students from the Nacala region and members of the NGO mapping community manually
156   traced building boundaries, and collected roof types on-site, as part of a wider survey campaign.

158   data was then digitized, corroborated by observation of the drone orthomosaics. Finally, all building
159   boundaries and roof types were verified by new observation of the drone orthomosaics conducted by
160   the authors, and corrections made whenever necessary (almost all of the annotations were manually
161   adjusted slightly in this second step, missing annotations were added). The authors created a grid over
      all annotations and verified all buildings in each grid cell to ensure double-checking every building.

      2.4     R ELATED DATASETS
196
197   The project “Mapping Informal Settlements in Developing Countries using Machine Learning with
198   Noisy Annotations and Multi-resolution Multi-spectral Data” (Helber et al., 2018; Gram-Hansen
199   et al., 2019) is most closely related to our work. They used freely available 10m/pixel resolution
200   imagery from the Sentinel-2 satellite and obtained labels for three roof types (metal, shingles, thatch)
201   from geo-located survey data provided by Afrobarometer1 . These labels are very noisy in space
202   and time. The labels are often not aligned with buildings because the geo-located coordinates were
203   distorted for privacy reasons. Furthermore, the survey questions and satellite image observations may
204
      not be aligned in time. While the low spatial resolution of the Sentinel-2 imagery might allow to
      cover large geographic regions, it makes roof type classification challenging (Helber et al., 2018).
205
206   There are many datasets that contain remote sensing imagery with building labels, which, however,
207   typically do not distinguish roof types. In particular, Open Buildings is a freely available continental-
208   scale building dataset covering the whole of Africa (Sirko et al., 2021). In comparison, Nacala-Roof-
209   Material is much more focused, providing significantly higher resolution images, more accurate
210
      delineations, and in particular roof type classifications.
211   Alidoost and Arefi (2018) distinguish between roof types in aerial images. However, they map a
212   rather high-income town in Germany, where they distinguish between three roof shapes common
213   in that region (flat, gable, and hip). Another dataset for classifying roof geometry is provided by
214   Persello et al. (2023), who distinguish 12 fine-grained details of roof geometry.

243
244   This section presents the approaches we benchmarked on the Nacala-Roof-Material dataset. The goal
245   is to accurately segment the buildings (as assessed by metrics based on the IoU), separate individual
246   buildings, and classify the roof materials. As baselines, we considered U-Net (Ronneberger et al.,
247
      2015), YOLOv8 (Jocher et al., 2023), and a model performing segmentation based on DINOv2 (Oquab
      et al., 2024). Furthermore, we extend the U-Net and the DINOv2 based systems with the deep ordinal
248
      watershed method recently proposed by Cheng et al. (2024). These approaches are compared in
249
      two settings. In the two-stage setting, we first solved the building segmentation and separation tasks
250   and afterwards classified the roof material for each detected building. In the end-to-end setting,
251   segmentation and classification were done in parallel.

      3.1                      BASELINE M ODELS
254
255   U-Net. The U-Net is arguably the most common architecture for semantic segmentation (Ron-
256   neberger et al., 2015). We utilized a ResNet34 (He et al., 2016) encoder pretrained on ImageNet and
257   a decoder similar to the original U-Net, except that we used nearest-neighbor upsampling instead of
258   transposed convolutions (Odena et al., 2016), see Figure A.6 in the Appendix.
259
      To identify individual instances in the semantic segmentation output map, the connected components
260   in the map were determined (Brandt et al., 2020). To better separate individual buildings, we used
261   a pixel-wise weight map during training that puts more emphasis on the space between buildings
262   as already suggested by Ronneberger et al. (see Appendix A.1 for details) and commonly used in
263   remote sensing (e.g. Brandt et al., 2020). However, this is not sufficient to separate buildings that
264   are very close to each other or touch each other. Thus, we modified the target segmentation masks
265   during training: Some border pixels were relabeled as background to ensure that there is a minimum
266   gap of ngap = 7 pixels between roofs. This modification of the target masks was only applied during
267   training, before computing the weight map but not when calculating any performance metrics.
268
269   YOLOv8. We trained YOLOv8 (Jocher et al., 2023), which is among the state-of-the-art methods
      for instance segmentation. We fine-tuned a model pretrained on the COCO dataset. While the original

271   integrating concepts from YOLACT (Bolya et al., 2019).

      3.2   D EEP O RDINAL WATERSHED
311
      U-Nets and the DINOv2 based method described above try to classify each pixel as accurately as
312   possible. However, for proper separation of objects it is sufficient – and typically preferable – if only
313   the interior of an object is segmented. If the border of a building can be classified as background,
314   even touching buildings can be separated. This reasoning leads to the deep ordinal watershed (DOW)
315   model introduced by Cheng et al. (2024).
316
      In the watershed approach, each pixel is assigned a height and the image is viewed as a topological
317
      map (Soille and Ansoult, 1990). A DOW architecture does not only predict a single segmentation
318   mask but nlev feature maps for nlev + 1 discrete height levels, {0, 1, . . . , nlev }, where 0 corresponds
319   to the highest and nlev to the lowest elevation. Background pixels are assumed to have level 0. The
320   Euclidean distance transformation is computed for each object, and the distances are discretized into
321   the remaining nlev height levels. Target feature map m ∈ {1, . . . , nlev } marks all pixel with a distance
322   level of m or higher. That is, the objects in the target feature maps get smaller with increasing m (if
323   nlev = 1 we recover the standard U-Net). Learning the discrete height levels of pixels this way solves
      an ordinal regression task (Frank and Hall, 2001; Cheng et al., 2008). Given the pixel heights, the


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      watershed algorithm can be applied as a post-processing step for instance segmentation (Soille and
325   Ansoult, 1990). Local minima in the elevation map define basins, each of which defines a distinct
326   object. Adopting a flooding metaphor, the watershed algorithm now floods the basins until basins
327   attributed to different starting points meet on watershed lines. Pixels attributed to the same basin
328   belong to the same object.
329
      Cheng et al. (2024) employ a DOW U-Net for individual tree segmentation, however, without
330
      a comparison with a standard U-Net or exploring different numbers of levels. For our task, we
331   hypothesize that a minimal number of nlev = 2 different non-background heights is sufficient. In this
332   setting, the system outputs two masks representing the full object and its interior, respectively. Let
333   npix denote the difference in distance between two levels. The smallest building in our dataset has
334                 2
      √ 1.463
      size     m . Thus, for the given image resolution, the number of pixels per side is approximately
335     1.46 0.044. This suggests to define the levels such that npix < 13, and we picked npix = 10.
336
337
      We empirically evaluated DOW variants of both our U-Net and DINOv2 based systems, see Figure 2.
      We describe the U-Net extension in more detail in Appendix A.2, the DINOv2 based systems
338
      were modified analogously. The DOW U-Net network architecture U-NetDOW used in our study is
339
      illustrated in Figure 3. For a comparison with a DOW U-Net with nlev = 6 we refer to Appendix A.2
340   and Appendix B.
341
342   Although the approaches are related, we would like to stress the DOW method is conceptually
343
      different from deep level sets, where deep neural networks learn a (continuous) level set function, the
      zero-set of which defines object boundaries (Hu et al., 2017; Hatamizadeh et al., 2020), as well as
344
      from predicting interior and border of an object as, for instance, done by Girard et al. (2021).

      3.3   T WO - STAGE VS . E ND - TO - END
348
349
      All the neural network architectures described above can directly classify the roof types of detected
350
      buildings by predicting multi-class segmentation masks. However, encouraged by good classification
351
      results using DINOv2 features, we also studied an alternative two-stage approach: First we seg-
352   mented and separated the buildings using the algorithms described above ignoring the roof material
353   information. That is, we reduced the multi-class problem to a binary task. After that, we predicted the
354   roof material of each detected building. We used DINOv2 to processes a 448 × 448 patch centered
355   around each building, see Figure 4. The output of DINOv2, a patch embedding with the shape of
356   R1024×768 was reshaped into feature maps of R32×32×768 . These feature maps were then upsampled
357   to the input patch size, masked with a target binary building mask, and average pooling was applied to
358   obtain the final feature vector for the building. Standard machine learning classifiers were applied to
359   this embedding to predict the roof material, where linear probing gave the best results (see Appendix
360
      B.2 for a comparison of different classifiers).
361   The two-stage methods are referred to as U-Net, DINOv2, U-NetDOW , and DINOv2DOW , and the
362   corresponding end-to-end methods are denoted by U-NetMulti , DINOv2DOW-Multi , U-NetDOW-Multi , and
363   DINOv2DOW-Multi , see Figure 2 for an overview.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
381
382   All models, except for YOLOv8 where we followed its original training protocol, were trained using
383   cross-entropy loss with pixel-wise weighting. We employed the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov and
384   Hutter, 2019) with an initial learning rate of 0.0003. All models were trained for 300 epochs, utilizing
385   a learning rate scheduler that decreased the learning rate by a factor of 10 every 50 epochs. The final
386   weight configuration and hyperparameters for each model were selected based on the highest IoU
387   score achieved on the validation dataset. The hyperparameters of the U-Net were chosen by observing
388
      results on the validation dataset in an iterative process. The high training speed of YOLOv8 allowed
      for more systematic model selection: We applied the genetic algorithm that comes as part of the
389
      YOLOv8 framework for hyperparameter optimization (Jocher et al., 2023). The input patch sizes for
390
      the U-Net variants, YOLOv8, and DINOv2 models were 512, 640, and 448, respectively.
391
392
      4.2   E VALUATION M ETRICS
393
394   The semantic segmentation performance was evaluated by the IoU. We considered both the IoU of
395   the binary building segmentation and the mean IoU for class-specific roof segmentation. The roof
396   materials concrete and asbestos are very rare. While Dtrain , Dval , and Dtest are stratified samples
397   containing all classes, the spatially distinct data Dext does not contain any example of the two roof
398   types, see Figure 1. To allow for a better comparison between the two test sets and to see the effect of
399   the rare classes on the macro-averaged mean IoU, we provide the mean IoU of the three main classes
400   (mIoU3 ) alongside with the mean IoU of all five classes (mIoU5 ).
401   Instance segmentation was assessed using the AP50 score, that is, the average precision evaluated
402   at an IoU threshold of 0.5 (Everingham et al., 2010; Lin et al., 2014). We evaluated the AP for
403   both the predictions of building instances and the predictions of multi-class roof type instances (i.e.,
404   in the latter case an object is only detected if the roof material is correctly identified). Similar to
405   IoU, mAP350 and mAP550 denote the mean AP50 over three and five classes. To estimate the average
406   precision, a confidence score is required for each building segment. The confidence score of binary
407
      and multi-class segmentation models was obtained by interpreting the neural networks’ outputs
      as probability distributions over classes and calculating the mean probability of belonging to the
408
      predicted class over all pixel within a predicted segment. The exception was YOLOv8, which
409
      provides its own confidence score. When a classifier using DINOv2 features was used on top of
410   binary segmentation models, the confidence score was derived from the canonical probability score
411   of the classifier. Additional metrics, AP50-95 and TPs , are shown in Appendix B. Information on the
412   computer resources is provided in Appendix A.4.

      Comparison of methods. When comparing the different approaches, we find that there is no

521   5   C ONCLUSIONS
522
523   The Nacala-Roof-Material dataset contains high-resolution drone imagery from informal settlements
524   in Mozambique, where buildings and their roof material were carefully annotated. We curated
525   the dataset as part of an intercontinental and interdisciplinary research project on risk assessment
526   of mosquito-borne diseases, especially malaria, with the goal to predict risk maps and to develop
527   and support measures for risk reduction. From a methodological perspective, the dataset defines
528   a multi-task problem. We are interested in accurate semantic segmentation to determine the roof
529   areas and also in identifying the individual buildings and classifying their roof types. Thus, the
530   dataset adds to the landscape of computer vision benchmarks by providing a relevant resource for the
531
      development and evaluation of frameworks that strive at solving semantic segmentation as well as
      object detection and classification simultaneously with a high accuracy. For example, working on the
532
      Nacala-Roof-Material data has led us to the proposed deep ordinal watershed (DOW) approach, a
533
      reduced variant of the method described by Cheng et al. (2024). This variant method first segments
534   objects along with their interiors into two elevation levels and then performs a watershed segmentation
535   to separate objects. The DOW idea is applicable beyond the Nacala-Roof-Material data, on which it
536   improved both the standard U-Net architectures as well as a system based on DINOv2 features for
537   segmentation. Implementations of all algorithms will be publicly available together with the data.
538   With the Nacala-Roof-Material dataset, we invite the machine learning community to develop new
539   approaches for interpreting high-resolution drone images that can ultimately support risk assessments
      of vector-borne diseases.

801   cation algorithms. We compared linear probing based on logistic regression with L2 -regularization
802   and k-nearest neighbours (kNN) classification trained on our data. For evaluating the classifiers
803   and tuning their hyperparameters, we combined the training and validation data and performed
804   10-fold cross-validation (CV) with F1-score as performance metric. The best CV results gave logistic
805   regression with L2 -regularization, and this model was used for all subsequent two-stage experiments,

      see Table B.2.

1117   The roof material classes are not balanced and are not geographically distributed uniformly. The
1118
       data is split into training, validation, and test sets using stratified random sampling to account for
       the class imbalance. We created a square grid of 225 meters and counted the roof types in these
1119
       cells. Then we partitioned the cells into three sets based on the class counts to achieve a similar class
1120

1121   and asbestos). We defined that a building only belongs to a specific grid cell if its centroid falls into
1122   the cell. These grid cells separate the images and labels into training, validation and test sets. See
1123   Fig. C.9 as an example. Initially, only two informal settlements were labelled and therefore, only
1124   these two settlements are divided into the 3 sets. The third informal settlement was labelled later and
1125   treated as a second test set.
