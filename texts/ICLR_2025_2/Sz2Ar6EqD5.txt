009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-modality MRI scans can provide comprehensive diagnoses of abdominal
012            disease but this also introduces new segmentation burdens to quantitative im-
013            age analysis. In this work, we introduce an image-based referring segmentation
014            task where users only need to draw simple scribbles on one modality (reference
015            modality), to guide the segmentation of multiple unseen target modalities. To
016            benchmark the multi-modality segmentation task, we annotate a new dataset with
017            3,277 organs from 534 MRI images, covering five commonly used MRI modal-
018            ities. Furthermore, we present a referring segmentation model, CrossMR, to si-
019
               multaneously segment multiple target modalities based on scribbles on reference
               modality only. Experiments demonstrate that our method can achieve comparable
020
               performance to the state of the art on one in-distribution reference modality and
021
               significantly better generalization ability on four out-of-distribution target modal-
022            ities. The new dataset, code, and trained model weights will be publicly available
023            at https://ref-seg-mr.github.io/.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) has been a widely used medical imaging technology, offering
029
      a non-invasive method for visualizing internal structures of the human body. In abdominal imag-
030   ing, MRI stands out for its exceptional soft tissue contrast and the ability to produce images in
031   any anatomical plane without radiation. Moreover, the development of multi-modality MRI tech-
032   niques, such as T1-weighted (T1w) MRI, T2-weighted (T2w) MRI, and Diffusion-Weighted Imag-
033   ing (DWI), provides a more comprehensive evaluation of diseases and significantly enhances di-
034   agnostic accuracy. These features make multi-modality MRI particularly suitable for the clinical
035   examination of abdominal diseases Mulé et al. (2020).
036   Quantitative imaging biomarkers measured with MRI are emerging as important clinical tools in the
037   evaluation and management of abdominal diseases, such as liver fibrosis and fatty liver Guglielmo
038   et al. (2023); Xia et al. (2023). However, producing these image biomarkers usually requires seg-
039   mentation of the target organs. For example, the measurements of fat fraction and iron concentration
040   rely on the liver segmentation mask Martı́-Aguado et al. (2022). In clinical practice, patients usually
041   undergo multiple MRI modalities. Manually segmenting the target organs in each modality would
042   be a labor-intensive and time-consuming task.
043   This work aims to formulate and address the question: how to efficiently segment multi-modality
044   MRI leveraging annotations of a single reference modality? We first annotate a multi-modality
045   MRI dataset consisting of five modalities, where T1w modality is used as the reference modality
046   with annotations and the other four modalities, T2w, DWI, In-phase, and Opposed-phase MRI, do
047   not have annotations, as shown in Fig. 1 (a). Our goal is to leverage only the annotations of the
048   reference T1w modality for model training to segment all five modalities during inference.
049   Compared to traditional unsupervised cross-modality domain adaptation tasks, our task design has
050   two unique features. First, the goal is to segment both the source-domain (i.e. T1w) as well as
051   multiple target domains (i.e. T2w, DWI, In-phase, and Opposed-phase) simultaneously with only
052   one suit of model parameters while traditional tasks mainly focus on one target modality. Second, we
053   frame the cross-modality segmentation as a referring segmentation task, where the weak annotation
      on the reference modality guides the segmentation of the target modalities. As this task involves

      weak annotation of a single modality, such as widely used T1w modality, the annotation burden is
055   significantly lower.
056
057   In this paper, we introduce the image-based referring segmentation to address the above question,
058
      where the annotation from a reference modality guides the segmentation process for a target modal-
      ity. As illustrated in 1 (b), during inference, clinicians only provide weak annotations, such as
059
      simple scribbles, on the target organ (i.e., liver, kidneys, and spleen) of the reference modality (i.e.,
060
      T1w MRI), to obtain segmentation on all five modalities. We introduce CrossMR, a referring seg-
061   mentation model that leverages these scribble annotations from the reference modality to accurately
062   segment all five modalities. We summarize the main contributions as follows:
063
064          • Novel task. This work introduces the first referring segmentation task for organ segmenta-
065            tion in multi-modality MRI. This also poses a unique segmentation challenge where models
066            need to segment multiple target images based on weak annotations in one reference image.
067          • Annotated multi-modality MRI dataset. We create a labeled dataset that contains 534
068            cases with 3,277 annotated segmentation masks, covering four abdominal organs across
069            five imaging modalities. The dataset will be made publicly available to the community for
070            research purposes, facilitating further exploration of the referring segmentation in medical
071            imaging.
072          • Generalizable algorithm: CrossMR. We present a referring segmentation model, which
073            enables users to input scribble prompts on a reference image to simultaneously deliver
074            segmentation results of the multiple target images.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
077
078
      Cross-modality Domain Adaptation Method Domain adaptation is a common approach to cross-
079   modality segmentation task. CrossMoDA challenge hosts a benchmark of cross-modality unsuper-
080   vised domain segmentation method where the task is to leverage the labeled source domain T1w
081   data and the unlabeled target domain T2w data to build a segmentation model for the target do-
082   main. The solutions employ unpaired image translation such as cycleGAN Zhu et al. (2017) and
083   QS-Attn Hu et al. (2022), followed by segmentation model training with the synthesized images and
084   self-training Liu et al. (2023). The goal is to build a specialized segmentation model focused on
085   the performance of a single target modality and usually involve a multi-step approach specifically
086   tailored for the target domain modality. compared to existing cross-modality domain adaptation
087
      methods Guan & Liu (2021), our proposed referring segmentation task generalizes to more than two
      modalities where the aim is to build an end-to-end model that has competitive performance across
088
      all MR modalities, emphasizing the importance of efficiently handling the inherent heterogeneity of
089
      medical images.
090
091   Few Shot and One Shot Segmentation To improve model generalizability to unseen modalities
092   or tasks without supervised fine-tuning, few-shot or one-shot approaches have been explored (Butoi
093
      et al., 2023; Wu & Xu, 2024). These methods input a query image alongside one or a few image-label
      pairs, using this support information to guide accurate segmentation of the query image. Recently,
094
      PerSAM, a one-shot approach capable of zero-shot segmentation with or without fine-tuning on a
095
      single reference sample, has been proposed (Zhang et al., 2023). Built on SAM (Kirillov et al.,
096   2023), PerSAM customizes SAM for personalized object segmentation using target-specific atten-
097   tion and semantic prompting without additional training. PerSAM-F further improves segmentation
098   by introducing a quick, scale-aware fine-tuning process, adjusting only two parameters in 10 sec-
099   onds for more precise results. However, while these methods efficiently utilize the support set’s
100   annotation, they often require fully annotated data, and some approaches, like Butoi et al. (2023),
101   rely on larger support sets of size 64 to achieve better performance.
102   Prompting in Foundation Models An emerging approach to generalize the model capabilities is to
103   utilize foundation models, built from large-scale datasets. Some foundational segmentation models
104   employ prompting to guide the model to adapt to the task during inference. MedSAM Ma et al.
105   (2024), trained on diverse medical datasets, uses an input bounding box to prompt the model with
106   the region of interest. As scribble prompts are suited for segmentation of complex Luo et al. (2022),
107   scribble prompt models trained on a diverse set of medical imaging are also introduced Marinov
      et al. (2024); Wong et al. (2023). These models achieve state-of-the-art performance on various

138   Referring segmentation The key idea of referring segmentation is to leverage the image feature
139
      corresponding to the local scribbled region of the reference image to gain information on the organ
      of interest on the target image. It focuses on the efficient use of annotations as the scribble anno-
140
      tation is only necessary on the reference image. Using weak annotation of one reference image,
141
      referring segmentation aims to simultaneously generate masks for multiple target images. Refer-
142   ring segmentation has emerged as a popular task in nature image segmentation Zou et al. (2024);
143   Li et al. (2023), but it has not been well formulated and studied in the medical imaging field. We
144   adapt referring segmentation in cross-modality segmentation and present a referring segmentation
145   model, CrossMR, which is specifically tailored to reduce the workload on clinicians by simplifying
146   the annotation process while ensuring high-quality segmentation outcomes across different imaging
147   modalities.

      3.2   PAIRED DATA AUGMENTATION
222
      To incorporate image features of diverse contrast and intensity that closely aligns with the fea-
223
      tures of the target modalities during training, paired data augmentation is used. For each reference
224
      modality input, two independent sets of data augmentations are used to produce two images. The
225   first augmentation set traslates the T1w reference image to an image that resembles one of the five
226   modalities. The second augmentation generates a reference T1w image with no augmentation or a
227   slight change in contrast or intensity.
228
      Through paired data augmentation, CrossMR learns to integrate reference image features with scrib-
229
      ble annotations by allowing the queries to attend to both the scribble and the target image features.
230
      This helps to incorporate and merge image features of the paired images and aligns the augmented
231   image representations without the need of using separate generative model for image synthesis. In
232   the following sections, we refer to both the target image used during inference and the augmented,
233   target-like images generated during training simply as the target image.

238   matching is commonly used in mask classification models Cheng et al. (2021); Zou et al. (2023).

240   each organ are matched with the ground truth organ. Therefore, we introduce an organ-specific bi-
241   partite matching, where only queries that self-attend to the visual tokens of the corresponding organ

      3.4   AUTOMATIC S CRIBBLE G ENERATION
262
263
      We follow the implementation of automatic scribble generation from SEEM, based on the method
264   proposed in Yu et al. (2019). The method aims to generate flexible and controllable scribbles similar
265   to real use cases on-the-fly. From a uniformly sampled start point, it randomly selects an angle and
266   a length to draw, using a variable brush width within a specified range. After drawing a line of the
267   selected length in the chosen direction, it samples the angle and length again to continue drawing
268   from the endpoint. To ensure smooth transitions between lines, a circle with a randomly selected size
269   within the brush width range is drawn between the two lines. Finally, small random displacements
      are applied to a subset of the control points, creating a more organic, free-form appearance.

      4.1   DATASET
273
274   Duke Liver dataset Zhu et al. (2020); Macdonald et al. (2023) was used in this work, which is a multi-
275   planar, multi-phase, and multi-contrast dataset with 2,146 abdominal MRI scans from 105 subjects.
276   We selected the axis imaging modalities because they are usually used for extracting quantitative
277   imaging biomarkers Guo et al. (2023). We also excluded the modalities with less than 50 scans,
278   which is not enough to draw statistically significant results. Finally, we obtained five modalities:
279   T1w, T2w, DWI, In-phase, and Opposed-phase, including 534 cases in total. We used the T1w
280   MRI as the reference modality because of its popularity in clinical practice and the remaining four
281   modalities as the target modalities.
282   The original dataset only provided limited liver masks for T1w images. However, the segmentation
283   of the other organs in multi-modality MRI also plays an important role in clinical practice, such as
284   left kidney, right kidney Goel et al. (2022), and spleen Camastra & Ciolina (2024). Therefore, we
285   further produced annotations for all the selected MRI scans, leading to a large-scale labeled dataset
286   with 3,277 organs. The labels were annotated by a junior radiologiest with one year of experience
287   using ITKSNAP Yushkevich et al. (2016) followed by careful examination and revision of a senior
288
      radiologist with over ten years of experience in abdominal radiology. The dataset was randomly
      divided into 80%, 10%, and 10% at the patient level as the training, validation, and testing sets,
289
      respectively.

292   4.2   I MPLEMENTATION
293
      During preprocessing, we first clipped the intensity values to the range between the 0.5th and 99.5th
294
      percentiles followed by rescaling them to the range of [0, 1]. Then, we resized each image to
295
      256 × 256. During training, we applied commonly used data augmentations from MONAI Cardoso
296   et al. (2022), such as random Gaussian noise or gibbs noise, random contrast adjustment, and random
297   scale intensity. The empirical weights of the binary cross-entropy loss β, and dice loss γ were set to
298   2.5, and 2.5 respectively. For the organ-specific bipartite matching, cross-entropy loss β ′ , and dice
299   loss γ ′ are set to 2.0 and 2.0.
300
      We set the number of the object queries N to 11 and the AdamW optimizer Loshchilov & Hutter
301
      (2017) with the base learning rate of 1e-3. The model with the lowest validation loss was selected
302   for evaluation. The learning rate of the image encoder was set to 10 times smaller than the base
303   learning rate. The models were trained for a maximum of 50 epochs.

      4.3   B ENCHMARKING
306
307   To the best of our knowledge, there is no previous work on specialized image-based referring seg-
308   mentation models for multi-modality MRI images. As an alternative, we compared CrossMR with
309   three groups of related state-of-the-art segmentation models: the traditional semantic segmentation
310   model, the one-shot segmentation model, and the scribble-prompt segmentation model. We did not
311   consider multi-stage models such as the cross-modality methods that heavily rely on transferring
312   the source domain images into the target domain using image-level alignment. For the semantic
313   segmentation model, we chose nnU-Net Isensee et al. (2021), a popular and high-performing self-
314   configuration framework with U-Net architecture. Specifically, we used the 2D model and trained
315
      from scratch with the same data split as the CrossMR. For the one-shot segmentation model, we used
      PerSAM-F Zhang et al. (2023), a one-shot fine-tuning model. We frame the one-shot model into our
316
      task by using the reference-mask pair as the support set to query the target image. PerSAM-F uses
317
      the pre-trained SAM-Huge model and fine-tunes the model in test time on the labeled reference. We
318   also compared with another scribble-prompt segmentation model, MedSAMScribble Marinov et al.
319   (2024), the scribble prompt version of the MedSAM Ma et al. (2024). The model is trained with
320   samples of size 256 × 256 and the same data augmentation used for the target images in CrossMR
321   is used for consistency. Lastly, we compared with the scribble version of the nnUNet, referred to as
322   nnUNet-Scribble, where the scribbles are input as a second channel of the input in addition to the
323   images. A similar strategy has been demonstrated by the author’s team for general medical image
      segmentation Stock et al. (2024).

480   5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
481
482   Motivated by the clinical demands for multi-modality MR image analysis, we have introduced the
483   image-based referring segmentation task, which can reduce the annotation burden for the organs of
484   interest in each MR modality. In particular, we aim to build a versatile segmentation model that can
485   learn general representation from single-labeled reference modality and multiple unlabeled target
      modalities by only using scribble prompts on the reference image.


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Furthermore, we have annotated a dedicated dataset for this task. We provide precise organ annota-
487   tions for the reference T1w modality and four target modalities. Furthermore, we propose CrossMR,
488   a model designed to utilize the reference image features to generate segmentation masks for vari-
489   ous target MR modalities. CrossMR not only shows competitive performance on the in-distribution
490   modality compared to the specialist model but also exhibits remarkable performance gains across
491   all four out-of-distribution modalities.
492
      CrossMR has great potential to accelerate the muti-modality MR image analysis by reducing both
493   the cost and labor involved in the organ annotation process. By minimizing the need for exten-
494   sive domain-specific knowledge, it allows segmentation across multiple MR modalities with only a
495   single weak annotation from the reference image. Unlike models such as MedSAM-Scribble and
496   nnUNet-Scribble, which require direct scribble input on each target image, CrossMR surpasses their
497   performance without the need for direct annotations on every image. Scribble inputs, commonly
498   used to guide models with positional and feature-specific information, are reduced to a single anno-
499   tation on the reference modality in CrossMR, greatly easing the annotation burden.
500   Traditional unsupervised cross-modality segmentation models are usually designed for one target
501   modality. In contrast, CrossMR generalizes to all five modalities, eliminating the need to develop
502   specialized models for each target modality. This scalability becomes increasingly advantageous
503   as the number of modalities grows, particularly in scenarios involving more than two. Addition-
504   ally, compared to few-shot models, CrossMR requires only a single reference image with weak
505   annotation for inference, whereas few-shot models rely on multiple images with full annotations.
506   Furthermore, CrossMR outperforms the one-shot model PerSAM-F without the need for fine-tuning
507
      during inference.
508   This work also has limitations. The model was designed with 2D networks that can give end-
509   users better flexibility in selecting the target slice. However, it can be extended to 3D networks to
510   model the inter-slice information in 3D MR scans. In addition to organs, lesion segmentation in
511   multi-modality MR images is also an important clinical task. In the future work, we will focus on
512   adapting the network to 3D and expanding the dataset to lesion segmentation.
513   In conclusion, we formulate the multi-modality MR image segmentation as an image-based referring
514   segmentation task, where users only need to draw weak supervision on a single reference image and
515   the model can generalize to multiple target modalities. The dataset we provide could serve as a
516   useful benchmark for this task. Moreover, it is also valuable for benchmarking more challenging
517   tasks, where the target images are from unseen patients. In addition, CrossMR outperforms existing
518   one-shot and scribble-based segmentation models on the target modalities, which can serve as a
519
      strong baseline model to pave the way for image-based reference segmentation.
