009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Although Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) such as Vision Transformers (ViTs)
012            have demonstrated superior performance in medical imaging tasks, the training of
013            DNNs usually requires large amounts of high-quality labeled training data, which
014            is usually difficult or even impractical to collect in the medical domain. To address
015            this issue, Generative Data Augmentation (GDA) has been employed to improve
016
               the performance of DNNs trained on augmented training data comprising both
               original training data in the standard benchmark datasets and synthetic training
017
               data generated by generative models such as Diffusion Models (DMs). How-
018
               ever, the synthetic data generated by GDA universally suffer from noise, and such
019            synthetic data can severely hurt the performance of classifiers trained on the aug-
020            mented training data. Existing works, such as data selection and data re-weighting
021            methods aiming to mitigate this issue, usually depend on a given clean metadata
022            or external classifier. In this work, we propose a principled sample re-weighting
023            method, Informative Data Selection (IDS), based on an established information
024            theoretic measure, the Information Bottleneck (IB), to improve the performance of
025            DNNs trained for thorax disease classification with GDA. Extensive experiments
026            demonstrate that IDS successfully assigns higher weights to more informative
027
               synthetic images and significantly outperforms existing data selection and data
               re-weighting methods in GDA for thorax disease classification. The code of IDS
028
               is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/IDS-20D1.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032   Recent studies have pushed forward the development of deep neural networks (DNNs) for applica-
033   tions in medical imaging, such as disease classification for chest X-rays (Guendel et al., 2018; Xiao
034   et al., 2023). Pioneering efforts utilized convolutional neural networks (CNNs), like U-Net (Ron-
035   neberger et al., 2015), to foster representation learning from radiography images. Lately, Vision
036   Transformers (ViTs) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) have also been employed to harvest informative
037   medical representations from these images (Xiao et al., 2023), leveraging their proficiency in han-
038   dling long-range dependencies among features. While CNNs and ViTs have achieved promising
039
      results, their effectiveness largely depends on the quality and volume of the available data and anno-
      tations (Feng et al., 2020). However, collecting a large dataset of high-quality annotations in medical
040
      domains is notably challenging (El Jiani et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023) or even impractical (Esteva
041
      et al., 2021; Price & Cohen, 2019; Ali et al., 2023; Ramudu et al., 2023) due to resource limita-
042   tions or privacy issues. To overcome this issue, self-supervised learning (SSL), such restorative
043   learning (Xiao et al., 2023), has been utilized to procure representations from unlabeled data. More
044   recently, following the success of generative models (Rombach et al., 2022; Akrout et al., 2023),
045   generative data augmentation (GDA) (Sarıyıldız et al., 2023; Lei et al., 2023; Azizi et al., 2023b;
046   Trabucco et al., 2024a), aiming to synthesize labeled training data using deep generative models,
047   has also emerged as a potent strategy to enrich training datasets.

      2023; Vendrow et al., 2023), which have demonstrated promising results in applications in both

094   Challenges in GDA for Disease Classification. Albeit the potential of GDA, the synthetic data
095   generated by GDA universally suffer from noise (He et al., 2023a; Azizi et al., 2023a), and such
096   synthetic data can severely hurt the performance of classifiers trained on the augmented training data.
097   To address this issue, the literature widely adopts data selection (Chhabra et al., 2024) or sample re-
098   weighting methods (He et al., 2023a), which use re-weighted or selected synthetic data when training
099
      the classifier. Existing sample re-weighting methods (Shu et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2022; Jain et al.,
      2024) typically depend on training a meta-network using certain clean metadata, hoping that such a
100
      network can assign higher importance weights to more informative training samples. However, all
101
      these methods assume the existence of such clean metadata, and it is not clear how such metadata
102   can be obtained for the medical task considered in this paper without efforts from medical experts.
103   The existing work that is the closest to our setup is CBF (He et al., 2023a), which introduces a CLIP
104   Filter strategy to rule out noisy synthetic data. The CLIP Fiter employs CLIP zero-shot classification
105   confidence to assess the quality of the synthesized data, and the synthetic data with low-confidence
106   are filtered out. The performance of CBF highly depends on the zero-shot image classification
107   capability of a vision-language model, CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), which is pre-trained on a huge
      dataset of image and text pairs. However, as a method highly depending a vision-language model

108
      pre-trained on generic data, CLIP may not be able to render reliable predictions on the specialized
109   data, such as the X-rays for thorax disease classification considered in this paper.
110
111   In summary, the current medical imaging and general machine learning literature lack a principled
112
      sample re-weighting method for GDA which does not depend on a given clean metadata or exter-
      nal classifiers. The major contribution of this paper is a principled sample re-weighting method
113
      based on an established information theoretic measure, the Information Bottleneck (IB), which does
114
      not require either clean metadata or external classifiers and exhibits superior performance over the
115   competing methods under the GDA setup for thorax disease classification.
116
117   Our Contributions. We propose a principled sample re-weighting method, Informative Data Se-
118
      lection (IDS), for training DNNs with GDA, which assigns higher importance weights to more
      informative samples based on an IB theoretic framework. The detailed contributions of this paper
119
      are presented as follows.
120
121   First, to the best of our knowledge, IDS is among the first to perform sample re-weighting in GDA
122   by employing a principled IB framework in the sample re-weighting process where each synthetic
123   image receives an importance weight, aiming to improve the accuracy of the classifier trained on the
124
      augmented data comprising the original training data and the re-weighted synthetic data. In contrast
      to existing works in sample re-weighting (Shu et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2022; Jain et al., 2024) and
125
      data selection (Chhabra et al., 2024; He et al., 2023a), IDS does not require either clean metadata or
126
      external classifiers.
127
128   Second, the sample re-weighting network is optimized for reducing the IB loss on the synthetic data,
129   such that the IB principle, learning features more strongly correlated with class labels while decreas-
130
      ing their correlation with the inputs, is better adhered. To achieve this goal, the importance weights
      generated by the sample re-weighting network are applied to the input features and representations
131
      of the synthetic data to compute weighted class centroids in both the input feature space and rep-
132
      resentation space, which are then used to compute the IB loss on the synthetic data. To minimize
133   the IB loss with minibatch-based SGD algorithms, we further derive a separable variational upper
134   bound of the IB loss, termed the VIB. In the training process, the cross-entropy loss re-weighted by
135   the importance weights and the VIB are iteratively optimized to update the weights in the classifi-
136   cation network and the sample re-weighting network. As evidenced by the results in Section 4.2,
137   IDS significantly outperforms state-of-the-art data re-weighting (Shu et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2022;
138   Jain et al., 2024) and data selection methods (He et al., 2023a; Chhabra et al., 2024) for thorax dis-
139   ease classification on three thorax disease classification benchmarks, CheXpert (Irvin et al., 2019),
140   COVIDx (Pavlova et al., 2022), and NIH ChestX-ray14 (Wang et al., 2017), demonstrating the su-
141
      periority of IDS in selecting informative synthetic data for GDA.
142   To demonstrate the superiority of IDS in selecting informative samples, we study the correlation
143   between the Intersection over Union (IoU) score for disease localization and importance weights
144   learned by the baseline sample re-weighting methods (Guo et al., 2022; Jain et al., 2024) and IDS.
145   The IoU score for disease localization is computed between the disease localization area predicted
146   by our IDS and the competing baselines and the ground-truth bounding box of the disease in the
147
      X-ray images. Examples of disease localization areas are illustrated in the first row of Figure 1. A
      synthetic image with a higher IoU score between the disease localization area and the ground-truth
148
      bounding box is considered a more informative sample for this disease because a higher IoU means
149
      a larger portion of the predicted disease localization area overlaps with the ground-truth bounding
150   box of the disease. More details on the ablation study can be found in Section 4.3 of the paper. The
151   Spearman Correlation Coefficient (SCC) (Spearman, 1961) is used to quantitatively measure the cor-
152   relation, and a larger absolute value of a positive SCC indicates a stronger positive correlation. Both
153   quantitative and visualization results in Figure 1 illustrate a stronger positive correlation between
154   the IoU scores and the importance weights learned by our IDS than that for competing baselines,
155   demonstrating the superiority of IDS for assigning higher importance weights to more informative
156   synthetic images to improve the accuracy of the classifier trained on the augmented data.

160   Deep learning has made remarkable progress in photographic image analysis (Lin et al., 2017b;a),
161   sparking interest in applying it to medical imaging. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) like U-
      Net (Falk et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2018) pioneered this field, achieving state-of-the-art performance

      across various tasks such as image classification (Wang et al., 2019; Ma et al., 2020), object detec-
163   tion (Falk et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2018; Yang & Yu, 2021), and semantic segmentation (Yang & Yu,
164   2021; Yao et al., 2021). More recently, vision transformers, inspired by the success of transform-
165   ers in natural language processing (Vaswani et al., 2017), have outperformed state-of-the-art CNNs
166   on various computer vision benchmarks (Zhu et al., 2021; Cai et al., 2023). Their self-attention
167   mechanism can better model long-range dependencies compared to CNNs’ local convolutions (Li
168   et al., 2023b). Given the scarcity of high-quality annotations, self-supervised contrastive learning
169   techniques (Chen et al., 2020a; Caron et al., 2020; Xiao et al., 2023) have gained traction for pre-
170   training networks in this domain (Zhou, 2021; Xiao et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2021). However,
171
      the high similarity between radiographic images due to standardized protocols (Xiang et al., 2021;
      Haghighi et al., 2022) poses challenges compared to photographic images (He et al., 2020; Chen
172
      et al., 2020b). To address this, recent works utilize restorative strategies like masked autoencoders
173
      (MAE) (He et al., 2022) for pre-training (Xiao et al., 2023).
174
175   2.2   E XISTING W ORKS ABOUT I NFORMATION B OTTLENECK
176   The Information Bottleneck (IB) principle (Tishby et al., 2000) posits that an optimal DNN com-
177   presses its input data, preserving only the information that is essential for predicting the target out-
178   puts, thereby maximizing the mutual information between the representations and the target out-
179   puts while minimizing the mutual information between the input and the representations. Deep
180   VIB (Alemi et al., 2017) first integrates the IB principle as a training objective for deep neural net-
181   works. Both empirical (Lai et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022) and theoretical (Amjad & Geiger, 2020;
182   Kawaguchi et al., 2023) works prove that DNNs better adhering to the IB principle show stronger
183
      performance. In the medical imaging domain, the IB principle is also widely adopted to learn dis-
      criminative task-oriented image representations (Demir et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023; Schott et al.,
184
      2024; Li et al., 2023a). MIB-Net (Wang et al., 2023) multiplies a contribution score map with the
185
      input image to force the network to learn representations that are more correlated with the target
186   task. IB-TransUNet (Li et al., 2023a) introduces an information bottleneck block in to compress
187   redundant features and reduce the risk of overfitting in medical image segmentation tasks. In con-
188   trast with existing works that utilize the IB principle to enhance the image representation learning
189   capabilities of DNNs, our method is the first that utilizes the IB principle for selecting high-quality
190   synthetic data to augment the training of DNNs for the medical image classification task.

      2.3   E XISTING W ORKS ABOUT G ENERATIVE DATA AUGMENTATION , DATA S ELECTION AND

            S AMPLE R E - WEIGHTING
193
194   Generating synthetic informative training data as data augmentation, or generative data augmenta-
195   tion (GDA), for improving the performance of DNNs remains a vital yet challenging research area.
196
      Existing works (Sarıyıldız et al., 2023; Lei et al., 2023; Azizi et al., 2023b; Trabucco et al., 2024a)
      predominantly focus on synthesizing training data through deep generative models, such as Gener-
197
      ative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Zhang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022) and diffusion models (He
198
      et al., 2023b; Tian et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2022; Bansal & Grover, 2023; Vendrow et al., 2023).
199   In the medical domain, researchers have also explored employing generative models to synthesize
200   training images for data augmentation, addressing the lack of high-quality labeled data (Shin et al.,
201   2018; Zhu et al., 2017; Jiang et al., 2018; Sharma & Hamarneh, 2019; Cha et al., 2020; Akrout et al.,
202   2023) in tasks such as medical image classification (Shin et al., 2018) and medical anomaly detec-
203   tion (Akrout et al., 2023). Despite works showing that synthetic images can improve the training
204   of DNNs for medical tasks, they often overlook the fact that synthetic data produced by generative
205   models can introduce noise (Azizi et al., 2023b; Trabucco et al., 2024b; Na et al., 2024), which
206   underscores the critical need for careful quality control in using the generated synthetic data for data
207
      augmentation. To mitigate this issue, recent works focus on three directions: improving the quality
      of the synthetic data, data selection, and data re-weighting. The first category of methods seeks to
208
      directly improve the quality of the generated synthetic data by refining the generation process of
209
      diffusion models to (Sarıyıldız et al., 2023; Lei et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023). The second category
210   of methods, data selection (Wu et al., 2021; Nguyen et al., 2020; Song et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023;
211   He et al., 2023a; Chhabra et al., 2024), which aims to select a high-quality subset from the noisy
212   training data to improve the performance of deep learning models, can also be used to select high-
213   quality synthetic data. For example, Classifier-based Filtering (CBF) (He et al., 2023a) proposes
214   to select synthetic images with high CLIP zero-shot classification confidence. The third category,
215   data re-weighting, uses soft data selection by assigning importance weights to training samples (Mo
      et al., 2019; Shu et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2022; Jain et al., 2024). Methods like Meta-Weight-Net


                                                         4

      (Shu et al., 2019), OTR (Guo et al., 2022), and REVAR (Jain et al., 2024) employ meta-learning to
217   adaptively learn sample weights from a clean meta dataset, enhancing robustness against noise or
218   bias in the training data (Shu et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2022; Jain et al., 2024).

532    5           C ONCLUSION
533
       In this paper, we propose Informative Data Selection (IDS), a novel method designed to re-weight
534    synthetic images in Generative Data Augmentation (GDA) based on an information theoretic mea-
535    sure, the Information Bottleneck (IB). IDS trains a sample re-weighting network to minimize the
536    IB loss on the synthetic data, such that the IB principle, learning features more correlated with the
537    outputs and less correlated with the inputs, is better adhered. Extensive experiments and ablation
538    studies demonstrate that IDS successfully assigns higher weights to more informative synthetic im-
539    ages for thorax disease classification and significantly outperforms existing data selection and data
       re-weighting methods for GDA.

1012   Diffusion models (DMs) are latent variable models that conceptualize data x0 as a Markov
1013

1017   sian noise into data x(t) :

1151   The fine-tuning process is performed for 75 epochs with the ADAM optimizer and a batch size of
1152   1024. A cosine decay schedule is used. The initial learning rate µ is determined through cross-
1153
       validation for each model and dataset. The weight decay is set to 0.05, and the momentum param-

1154
       several data selection and sample reweighting methods, including Influence Estimation (Chhabra
1155
       et al., 2024), Classifier-based Filtering (CBF) (He et al., 2023a), MW-Net (Shu et al., 2019), OTR

1157   undergo an additional 75 epochs of fine-tuning. The mean Area Under the Curve (mAUC) is used
1158   as the metric for the multi-label disease classification datasets CheXpert and NIH ChestX-ray14.
1159   Accuracy is used as the metric for the single-label disease classification dataset COVIDx.
1160
       CheXpert. The CheXpert dataset (Irvin et al., 2019) consists of 224, 316 chest X-ray images from
1161
       65, 240 patients, with 191, 028 images used for training. Each X-ray is labeled with radiology
1162   reports indicating the presence of 14 thoracic diseases. To measure the effectiveness of our approach,
1163   we compute the mean Area Under the Curve (AUC) across five selected disease categories and
1164   compare our results against state-of-the-art baseline models.

1168   four classes, and 400 images for testing, categorized into three classes. For objective evaluation and
1169   consistency with prior methodologies, we report the Top-1 accuracy on the test set, which contains
1170   three classes.
1171
