009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement learning has traditionally focused on learning state-dependent poli-
012            cies to solve optimal control problems in a closed-loop fashion. In this work,
013            we introduce the paradigm of open-loop reinforcement learning where a fixed
014            action sequence is learned instead. We present three new algorithms: one robust
015            model-based method and two sample-efficient model-free methods. Rather than
016
               basing our algorithms on Bellman’s equation from dynamic programming, our
               work builds on Pontryagin’s principle from the theory of open-loop optimal con-
017
               trol. We provide convergence guarantees and evaluate all methods empirically on
018
               a pendulum swing-up task, as well as on two high-dimensional MuJoCo tasks,
019            demonstrating remarkable performance compared to existing baselines.

042   An open-loop controller receives no observations from its environment. This makes it impossible
043   to react to unpredictable events, which is essential in many problems, particularly those with
044   stochastic or unstable dynamics. For this reason, RL research has historically focused exclusively
045
      on closed-loop control. However, many environments are perfectly predictable. Consider the classic
      example of swinging up an inverted pendulum. If there are no disturbances, then this task can be
046
      solved flawlessly without feedback (as we demonstrate in Section 4.1). Where open-loop control
047
      is viable, it brings considerable benefits. First, as there is no need for sensors, it is generally much
048   cheaper than closed-loop control. Second, it can operate at much higher frequencies, as there is no
049   bandwidth bottleneck due to sensor delays or computational processing of measurements. Third,
050   if the environment changes in an unforeseen way, a feedback policy may produce unpredictable
051   behavior, whereas an open-loop solution, which cannot detect environmental changes, is unaffected.
052   Finally, the open-loop optimal control problem is much simpler, as it only involves optimizing an
053   action sequence (finding one action per time step). In contrast, closed-loop optimal control involves
      optimizing a policy (finding one action for each state of the system), which can be considerably

054
      more expensive in the finite-horizon setting. In this way, open-loop control circumvents the curse
055   of dimensionality without requiring function approximation.
056
057   For these reasons, open-loop control is widely used in practice. A simple application is the control of
058
      electric stoves with simmerstats, which regulate the temperature by periodically switching the power
      on and off. Open-loop methods are also applied to much more challenging problems, such as the
059
      control of drinking-water treatment plants (Gamiz et al., 2020) or plasmas in a tokamak (Mattei et al.,
060
      2006). These problems have been also studied by the reinforcement learning community (by Janjua
061   et al. (2024) and Degrave et al. (2022), respectively). In real-world applications, disturbances are
062   often not negligible, and open-loop methods are used in combination with closed-loop methods. It
063   can be shown that, in many situations, such a combination will significantly outperform a solution
064   based on feedback alone (e.g., Åström & Murray, 2021, Sec. 12.4). Although we focus purely on
065   open-loop control in this paper, we believe that combinations of open-loop and closed-loop control
066   have great potential for reinforcement learning. We expand on this discussion, as well as on the
067   limitations of open-loop control, in Section 6.
068   There exists a large body of literature on the theory of open-loop optimal control (Pontryagin et al.,
069   1962). In this work, we introduce a family of three new open-loop RL algorithms by adapting this
070   theory to the setting of unknown dynamics. Whereas closed-loop RL is largely based on approx-
071   imating the Bellman equation, the central equation of dynamic programming, we base our algorithms
072   on approximations of Pontryagin’s principle, the central equation of open-loop optimal control. We
073   first introduce a model-based method whose convergence we prove to be robust to modeling errors.
074   We then extend this algorithm to settings with completely unknown dynamics and propose two fully
075   online model-free methods. Finally, we empirically demonstrate the robustness and sample efficiency
076
      of our methods on an inverted pendulum swing-up task and on two complex MuJoCo tasks.

447   5    R ELATED WORK
448
449   Our work is inspired by numerical optimal control theory (Betts, 2010; Geering, 2007), which
450   deals with the numerical solution of trajectory optimization problems, a setting that has also been
451   studied in machine learning (Schaal & Atkeson, 2010; Howe et al., 2022). As in our approach,
452   an important aspect of solution methods is to exploit the Markovian structure of the dynamics to
453   reduce computation (Carraro et al., 2015; Schäfer et al., 2007). However, all these methods deal
454   with situations where the dynamics are known, whereas our algorithms only requires an approximate
455   model (model-based open-loop RL) or no model at all (model-free open-loop RL). Another set of
456
      related methods is known as iterative learning control (Moore, 1993; Ma et al., 2022; 2023), which is
      a control-theoretic framework that iteratively improves the execution of a task by optimizing over
457
      feedforward trajectories. However, these methods are often formulated for trajectory tracking tasks,
458
      while we consider a more general class of reinforcement learning problems. Chen & Braun (2019)
459   explore an idea similar as in our Algorithm A.1; their model-based control algorithm combines a
460   rollout in a real system with an inaccurate model to construct an iterative LQR feedback controller.
461
462
      Recently, deep neural networks have been used to learn representations of complex dynamical systems
      (Fragkiadaki et al., 2015) and Pontryagin’s principle was leveraged in the optimization of control tasks
463
      based on such models (Jin et al., 2020; Böttcher et al., 2022). However, these methods only consider
464
      the setting of closed-loop control. The combination of an exact forward pass with an approximate
465   backward pass, which our methods are based on, has also been explored in different settings in the
466   deep learning literature, such as spiking (Lee et al., 2016) or physical (Wright et al., 2022) neural
467   networks, or networks that include nondifferentiable procedures, for example used for rendering
468   (Niemeyer et al., 2020) or combinatorial optimization (Vlastelica et al., 2020).
469

      6    D ISCUSSION
471
472
      While open-loop control is a well-established field with countless applications (Diehl et al., 2006;
473
      van Zundert & Oomen, 2018; Sferrazza et al., 2020), the setting of incompletely-known dynamics
474   has received little attention. This paper makes an important first step towards understanding how
475   principles from open-loop optimal control can be combined with ideas from reinforcement learning
476   while preserving convergence guarantees. We propose three algorithms that address this open-loop
477   RL problem, from robust trajectory optimization with an approximate model to sample-efficient
478   learning under fully unknown dynamics. This work focuses on reinforcement learning in continuous
479   state and action spaces, a class of problems known to be challenging (Recht, 2019). Although this
480   setting allows us to leverage continuous optimization techniques, we expect that most ideas will
481   transfer to the discrete setting, and we would be interested to see further research on this topic.
482   It is interesting to note that there are many apparent parallels between our open-loop RL algorithms
483   and their closed-loop counterparts. The distinction between model-based and model-free methods
484   is similar as in closed-loop RL. Likewise, the on-trajectory and off-trajectory methods we present
485   show a tradeoff between sample efficiency and stability that is reminiscent of the tradeoffs between
      on-policy and off-policy methods in closed-loop RL. The question of exploration, which is central to


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      reinforcement learning, also arises in our case. We do not address this complex problem thoroughly
487   here but instead rely on additive Gaussian noise to sample diverse trajectories.
488
489   Limitations of Open-Loop RL. Another important open question is how open-loop methods fit into
490   the reinforcement learning landscape. An inherent limitation of these methods is that an open-loop
491   controller can, by definition, not react to unexpected changes in the system’s state, be it due to random
492
      disturbances or an adversary. If these disturbances are small, and the system is not sensitive to small
      changes in the initial condition or the action sequence (roughly speaking, if the system is stable and
493
      non-chaotic), then such a reaction is not necessary. In this situation, open-loop RL works even for
494
      long time-horizons T , as we highlight in our MuJoCo experiments (see Appendix E). However, an
495   open-loop controller cannot balance an inverted pendulum in its unstable position4 , track a reference
496   trajectory in noisy conditions, or play Go, where reactions to the opponent’s moves are constantly
497   required. In these situations open-loop RL is not viable or only effective over a very small horizon T .
498
      Open-loop control can be viewed as a special case of closed-loop control, and therefore it is clear
499
      that closed-loop control is much more powerful. Historically, control theory has dealt with both
500
      closed-loop and open-loop control (Åström & Murray, 2021; Skogestad & Postlethwaite, 2005;
501
      Åström & Hägglund, 1995) and there is a broad consensus in the control community that both are
502
      important (Betts, 2010; Verscheure et al., 2009; Horn et al., 2013). However, the setting of open-loop
503
      control with unknown dynamics, which is naturally formulated through the lens of reinforcement
504   learning, has so far received much less consideration from the community. Our algorithms provide
505   a first solution to the open-loop RL problem and are not intended to replace any of the existing
506   closed-loop RL algorithms. Instead, we think open-loop and closed-loop methods should complement
507   each other. In control engineering, it is common practice to combine feedback and feedforward
508   techniques, and such approaches are also thinkable in RL. For example, a high-level policy could
509   execute low-level feedforward trajectories using the options framework (Sutton et al., 1999); a related
510   idea is explored by Hansen et al. (1996). We believe that ultimately a combination of open-loop and
511   closed-loop techniques will be fruitful in reinforcement learning and think that this is an important
512
      direction for future research.

1037
         propagated backward in time, attain some error at each propagation step due to the approximation
1038
         errors of the Jacobians. For this reason, Theorem 3.4 demands (through Assumption 3.2) more
1039     accurate Jacobians at later time steps. Thus, for large T , our convergence result requires more
1040     accurate Jacobian estimates. However, in Fig. 8, we see that Algorithm A.3 is able to cope with
1041     longer horizons for the two MuJoCo environments. The reason for this discrepancy between our
1042     theoretical and empirical result is that Theorem 3.4 does not consider the stability of the system
1043     under consideration. The two MuJoCo systems, Ant-v4 and HalfCheetah-v4, are stable along
1044     the trajectories encountered during training, which prevents an exponential build-up of error in the

1063     F                 BASELINES
1064
1065    We compare our algorithms against two baselines: the finite-difference approach discussed at the end
1066    of Section 3.3 and the gradient-free cross-entropy method (CEM; Rubinstein, 1999). These methods
1067    are listed in Algorithms F.1 and F.2.
1068     Similarly to the finite-difference method and our on-trajectory algorithm, in CEM, we also perform
1069     M ∈ N rollouts of perturbed action sequences {ui ∼ N (ū, σI)}M       i=1 . Here, ū is the current action
1070     sequence and σ > 0 is a noise scale parameter. We then construct the elite set S of the L < M
1071     perturbed action sequences with the highest returns, where L ∈ N is a hyperparameter. Finally,   P the
1072     current action sequence ū is updated to be the mean of the elite sequences, such that ū ← L1 u∈S u.
1073
        This method can be considerably more efficient than the simple finite-difference method. Here, we are
1074    not trying to estimate the gradient anymore, so we can potentially improve the action sequence with far
1075    fewer rollouts than would be needed in the finite-difference approach. However, this method suffers
1076    from the same fundamental deficiency as the finite-difference method: it ignores the Markovian
1077    structure of the RL problem and treats the objective function J as a black box. CEM is commonly
1078    used in model-based closed-loop reinforcement learning for planning. In this setting, the rollouts are
1079    hallucinated using the approximate model. Instead of executing the complete open-loop trajectory,
        the model-predictive control framework is typically employed. The planning procedure is repeated


                                                                     20
            Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
            after each step in the real environment with the executed action being the first item in the planned
1081        action sequence. Thus, this setting is very different from our open-loop RL objective. For this reason,
1082        we slightly modify the CEM algorithm to better fit our requirements. In model-based RL, typically
1083        both mean ū and standard variation σ are adapted in CEM (Hafner et al., 2019; Pinneri et al., 2021).
1084        In our experiments, this approach led to very fast convergence (σ → 0) to suboptimal trajectories.
1085        We thus only fit the mean and keep the noise scale fixed, which we empirically observed to give much
1086        better results.
